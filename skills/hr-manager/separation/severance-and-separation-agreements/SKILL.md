# Severance And Separation Agreements

Severance and separation agreements define what a departing employee receives and what they give up in return. These agreements serve dual purposes: providing financial support and transition assistance to the departing employee, and obtaining a release of claims that protects the organization from future litigation. The design and execution of these agreements must balance fairness, legal compliance, and risk management. An agreement that is too one-sided may be unenforceable or may signal callousness; one that is poorly drafted may fail to provide the intended protection. Agents often treat severance as a formula and miss that the terms, legal requirements, and communication determine whether the agreement achieves its purposes.

Use this skill before designing severance packages, drafting separation agreements, or evaluating severance practices. The goal is to make the agent design severance that is fair, legally compliant, strategically sound, and clearly communicated.

## Core Rules

### Define A Clear Severance Philosophy And Structure

Severance should be governed by a clear philosophy and structure, not determined ad-hoc for each departure. A defined approach ensures consistency, fairness, and predictability.

Define by:

- establishing the circumstances under which severance is offered, typically involuntary separations not for cause, and not voluntary resignations;
- defining the severance calculation, typically based on tenure, such as weeks of pay per year of service, with minimums and caps;
- ensuring the structure is applied consistently across similar situations, with documented rationale for any variations;
- considering whether the structure aligns with market practice and legal requirements in each jurisdiction;
- reviewing the philosophy periodically to ensure it remains appropriate as the organization and market evolve.

A defined severance structure prevents the appearance of arbitrary or discriminatory treatment, where similar departures receive very different packages. It also simplifies administration and ensures that offers are defensible.

### Ensure The Release Of Claims Is Legally Sound

The primary purpose of a separation agreement, from the organization's perspective, is obtaining a release of claims: the employee's agreement not to sue in exchange for the severance. A release that is not legally sound provides no protection.

Ensure soundness by:

- having the agreement drafted or reviewed by legal counsel, as release requirements vary by jurisdiction;
- ensuring the release covers the intended claims, including known and unknown, without overreaching into unenforceable breadth;
- providing consideration, something of value beyond what the employee is already entitled to, as a release requires new consideration to be binding;
- including required waiting periods and revocation periods, particularly for age discrimination claims under laws like the OWBPA in the United States;
- ensuring that the agreement complies with specific legal requirements, such as plain language, specific formatting, or mandatory disclosures.

A release that fails to meet legal requirements may be unenforceable, leaving the organization exposed to the very claims it sought to release. Legal review is not optional; it is essential for the agreement to serve its purpose.

### Balance Organizational Protection With Employee Fairness

A separation agreement that is entirely one-sided, protecting the organization while offering minimal benefit to the employee, may be unenforceable, may generate resentment, and may signal to remaining employees that the organization exploits vulnerability. Fairness serves both ethics and effectiveness.

Balance by:

- providing severance that is genuinely meaningful, not token, relative to the employee's tenure and circumstances;
- including transition support, such as outplacement services, that genuinely aid the employee's job search;
- ensuring that the obligations imposed on the employee, such as non-disparagement or cooperation, are matched by reciprocal obligations on the organization;
- avoiding coercive tactics, such as undue pressure to sign quickly or threats to withdraw already-earned benefits;
- recognizing that a fair agreement is more likely to be honored and less likely to be challenged.

An employee who feels they were treated fairly in departure is less likely to sue, more likely to speak positively about the organization, and more likely to cooperate with post-departure matters. Fairness is both ethical and strategic.

### Handle Non-Disparagement, Confidentiality, And References Carefully

Separation agreements often include clauses that govern what the employee and organization may say about each other and the departure. These clauses must be carefully drafted to be enforceable and appropriate.

Handle by:

- including mutual non-disparagement clauses, so that both parties agree not to make negative statements, not just the employee;
- ensuring confidentiality clauses are reasonable in scope, protecting genuine confidential information without gagging the employee from discussing their employment generally;
- being aware of legal limitations on non-disparagement and confidentiality, such as protections for whistleblowing or discussing terms of employment under certain laws;
- establishing a clear reference policy, whether the organization provides neutral references, detailed references, or confirmation of employment only;
- ensuring that reference policies are applied consistently, to avoid claims of defamation or retaliation.

Overly broad non-disparagement or confidentiality clauses may be unenforceable or may violate laws that protect employee speech. Clauses should protect legitimate organizational interests without overreaching.

### Be Cautious With Non-Compete And Restrictive Covenants

Non-compete clauses, which restrict the departing employee's ability to work for competitors, are increasingly scrutinized and restricted by law. In many jurisdictions, they are unenforceable except in narrow circumstances. Including unenforceable non-competes creates risk and signals overreach.

Be cautious by:

- understanding the legal status of non-competes in each relevant jurisdiction, as many have significantly restricted or banned them;
- including non-competes only where they are legal, necessary to protect legitimate business interests, and narrowly tailored;
- considering less restrictive alternatives, such as non-solicitation of customers or employees, which may be more enforceable;
- ensuring that any restrictive covenants are supported by adequate consideration;
- not using non-competes punitively or as a standard clause regardless of role or circumstances.

A non-compete that is too broad or in a jurisdiction where it is unenforceable provides no protection and may draw legal challenge or regulatory attention. Use restrictive covenants judiciously and legally.

### Manage The Timing And Presentation Of The Agreement

How and when the separation agreement is presented affects both the employee's experience and the legal enforceability of the release. Pressure, confusion, or inadequate time undermine both fairness and validity.

Manage by:

- presenting the agreement at the time of separation, with clear explanation of what it provides and what it requires;
- giving the employee adequate time to review and consider the agreement, including the legally required consideration period;
- encouraging the employee to consult an attorney, and in some jurisdictions providing a contribution toward legal fees;
- not applying undue pressure to sign immediately, which can invalidate the release and generate resentment;
- being available to answer questions and address concerns about the agreement.

An agreement signed under pressure, without time to review or seek advice, is more likely to be challenged and may be found unenforceable. Presenting the agreement fairly protects both the employee's rights and the agreement's validity.

### Administer Severance Payments And Benefits Correctly

The logistical administration of severance, payments, benefits continuation, and tax treatment must be handled correctly. Errors create financial harm to the employee and potential liability for the organization.

Administer by:

- ensuring severance payments are made according to the agreement and legal requirements, including timing and method;
- handling benefits continuation correctly, such as COBRA notifications and any employer-paid continuation period;
- addressing tax treatment of severance, including withholding and reporting, correctly and transparently;
- managing the timing of final pay, unused vacation or PTO payout, and other entitlements according to legal requirements;
- providing clear documentation of all payments and their tax treatment for the employee's records.

Administrative errors in severance, such as late payment, incorrect tax withholding, or missed benefits notifications, create distress for the departing employee and potential legal liability. Accuracy and timeliness are essential.

### Document The Separation And Retain Records

The separation process, including the agreement, payments, and communications, must be documented and retained. This documentation is the record of what was agreed and provided.

Document:

- the signed separation agreement and all exhibits or attachments;
- the schedule of payments and benefits provided;
- communications regarding the separation, including the presentation of the agreement and any negotiations;
- confirmation that all legal requirements, such as waiting periods and revocation periods, were met;
- any post-separation matters, such as return of property or cooperation requests.

Retention of separation records supports the organization if the agreement or the separation is ever challenged. It also provides reference for future separations, supporting consistency and institutional learning.

## Common Traps

### Ad-Hoc Severance Without Structure

Determining severance individually without a defined philosophy, producing inconsistency and perceived unfairness. Define a clear structure.

### Unenforceable Release

Drafting release language without legal review, failing to meet jurisdictional requirements and providing no protection. Ensure legal soundness.

### One-Sided Agreement

Imposing obligations on the employee with minimal benefit or reciprocal commitment, generating resentment and challenge. Balance protection with fairness.

### Overbroad Restrictive Clauses

Including non-disparagement, confidentiality, or non-compete clauses that are unenforceable or legally restricted. Handle clauses carefully and legally.

### Pressure To Sign

Presenting the agreement with pressure to sign immediately, risking invalidation and resentment. Manage timing and presentation fairly.

### Administrative Errors

Mishandling severance payments, benefits, or tax treatment, creating harm and liability. Administer correctly.

### No Documentation

Failing to document the agreement, payments, and process, leaving no record if challenged. Document and retain records.

### Inconsistent Reference Practices

Providing different references for different departing employees without a consistent policy, risking defamation or retaliation claims. Establish clear reference practices.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is severance governed by a clear, defined philosophy and structure, applied consistently across similar situations?
- [ ] Is the release of claims legally sound, drafted or reviewed by counsel, with adequate consideration and required waiting and revocation periods?
- [ ] Does the agreement balance organizational protection with employee fairness, including meaningful severance and transition support?
- [ ] Are non-disparagement, confidentiality, and reference clauses reasonable, legally compliant, and reciprocal where appropriate?
- [ ] Are non-compete and restrictive covenants used cautiously, only where legal, necessary, and narrowly tailored?
- [ ] Is the agreement presented with adequate time, clear explanation, and encouragement to seek legal advice, without undue pressure?
- [ ] Are severance payments, benefits continuation, and tax treatment administered correctly and transparently?
- [ ] Is the separation thoroughly documented, including the agreement, payments, communications, and compliance with legal requirements?
