# Employee Resource Groups And Affinity Networks

Employee resource groups, also called affinity networks or business resource groups, are voluntary, employee-led groups organized around shared identity, experience, or interest. They can be powerful engines of belonging, development, and organizational insight, but they can also become vehicles for unpaid labor, unmet expectations, and frustration if their purpose, scope, and support are unclear. The agent must understand that ERGs need clear charters, executive sponsorship, appropriate funding, and realistic scope to be effective, that they are not a substitute for leadership-driven DEI strategy, and that overburdening them or under-supporting them damages both the groups and the broader DEI effort.

Use this skill when establishing, supporting, or governing employee resource groups. The goal is to make the agent design ERGs that drive meaningful change while protecting participants from burnout and the organization from unmet expectations.

## Core Rules

### Define A Clear Purpose And Charter

ERGs fail when their purpose is vague. A clear charter defines what the group does, for whom, and with what authority.

- Define the ERG's purpose: is it primarily for community and belonging, professional development, business input, or cultural education? Most ERGs serve multiple purposes, but the emphasis should be clear.
- Establish a charter that defines the mission, scope, membership criteria, leadership structure, and decision-making authority.
- Clarify what the ERG can and cannot do: it is typically advisory and community-building, not a decision-making body for policy or a substitute for HR or management action.
- Review the charter annually and adjust as the group and organization evolve.

### Secure Executive Sponsorship

ERGs need executive sponsors who provide visibility, access, and advocacy. Without sponsorship, ERGs struggle to influence.

- Assign an executive sponsor to each ERG, ideally a senior leader who is not a member of the group's demographic, to model allyship and provide access to leadership circles.
- Define the sponsor's role: advocacy, visibility, resource access, and coaching, not ceremonial endorsement.
- Ensure sponsors are engaged, not just named; their active participation signals organizational commitment.
- Match sponsors thoughtfully, considering their influence, commitment, and ability to open doors.

### Provide Appropriate Funding And Resources

ERGs cannot function without resources. Under-resourcing signals that the organization does not value their work.

- Provide an annual budget for ERG activities, such as events, speakers, and programs.
- Provide administrative support, such as communication tools, event logistics, and meeting space.
- Recognize that ERG leadership is significant work and provide time allocation, such as a percentage of work time, for leaders.
- Ensure funding is equitable across ERGs, avoiding disparities that signal differential value.

### Compensate Or Recognize Significant Labor

ERG leadership and significant contributions are labor. Failing to recognize or compensate it, particularly when it falls disproportionately on underrepresented employees, is inequitable.

- Consider stipends, bonuses, or other recognition for ERG leaders, reflecting the significant time commitment.
- Include ERG leadership in performance evaluation and advancement consideration, so it contributes to, rather than detracts from, career progress.
- Ensure ERG involvement does not become a second job that hinders the primary role performance.
- Recognize that relying on unpaid DEI labor from underrepresented groups reinforces the inequity the ERG exists to address.

### Define Realistic Scope And Boundaries

ERGs are often asked to solve problems outside their appropriate scope, such as investigating harassment, setting policy, or representing all members of their demographic. This creates burnout and unrealistic expectations.

- Clarify that ERGs do not replace HR functions, such as investigation, policy-setting, or individual case resolution.
- Clarify that ERG leaders do not represent or speak for all members of their demographic group.
- Route individual complaints and cases to HR, not to the ERG, to protect both the individual and the ERG.
- Protect ERG leaders from being treated as the organizational experts on all issues related to their identity.

### Create Channels For Business Impact

ERGs provide valuable business insight, from product design to customer understanding to talent strategy. Connect them to business decisions.

- Invite ERG input on relevant business decisions, such as product features, marketing, customer experience, and talent practices.
- Close the loop: tell ERGs how their input was used.
- Ensure input is solicited thoughtfully, with adequate time and context, not as a last-minute checkbox.
- Recognize ERG contributions to business outcomes, connecting their work to organizational success.

### Ensure Equitable Support Across Groups

All ERGs should receive equitable support. Disparities in funding, sponsorship, or visibility signal differential value and create friction.

- Apply consistent standards for charter, funding, and sponsorship across ERGs.
- Be responsive to new groups forming as the workforce diversifies, while ensuring sustainability.
- Avoid creating a hierarchy where some groups are treated as more legitimate than others.

### Measure Effectiveness And Evolve

ERGs should be assessed against their purpose, not assumed to be effective because they exist.

- Track participation, engagement, and satisfaction within ERGs.
- Assess the ERG's contribution to its stated purposes, such as belonging, development, or business input.
- Gather feedback from members and sponsors on what is working and what is not.
- Adjust structure, scope, or support based on findings.

## Common Traps

### Operating Without A Charter

An ERG that begins with enthusiasm and a general sense of purpose but no written charter accumulates scope creep — it becomes a social group, a complaint channel, a training provider, and a policy consultant simultaneously, and none of those functions are resourced or bounded. Members' expectations diverge, leadership burns out, and the sponsor cannot advocate for a group whose purpose no one can articulate crisply. Define purpose, scope, membership, leadership structure, authority, and boundaries in a charter before launch, because a charter is what allows the ERG to say no to scope it cannot support and to focus its limited energy on the outcomes it exists to produce.

### The Sponsor In Name Only

Assigning an executive sponsor who lends their title to the ERG but does not actually engage — does not attend, does not advocate in budget meetings, does not open doors — provides the appearance of senior support without any of its substance, and the absence becomes a signal to the rest of the organization about how seriously leadership takes the group. ERG leaders then spend their political capital trying to get access the sponsor was supposed to provide. Select sponsors who will actively engage, define what active engagement means, and hold sponsors accountable for it, because a sponsor who does not sponsor is worse than no sponsor, because they occupy the role and block someone who would fill it.

### Unpaid ERG Labor On Underrepresented Employees

ERG leadership is real work — organizing events, fielding member concerns, advising the business, representing the group externally — and it is disproportionately performed by employees from underrepresented groups on top of their day jobs, usually without compensation, without recognition in performance evaluation, and often at the cost of the very advancement the ERG exists to support. The trap is structural: the people most burdened by inequity are asked to fix it for free, while their peers who do not carry this load advance faster. Compensate significant ERG leadership labor, include it in performance evaluation, and distribute the advisory burden across majority-group allies, because an ERG that advances the belonging of some by slowing the advancement of its leaders is reproducing inequity, not solving it.

### Using The ERG As A Substitute For HR

When managers or leaders route individual complaints, policy decisions, or sensitive cases to an ERG — asking the women's ERG to weigh in on a harassment complaint, the Black ERG to validate a discipline decision — they overload the ERG with functions it is not staffed, trained, or authorized to perform, and they create liability by treating volunteers as agents of the company. The ERG leaders are placed in impossible positions, asked to represent their entire demographic and to handle confidential matters without the infrastructure to do so safely. Route individual cases to HR, keep ERGs focused on community, development, and business input, and define the boundary explicitly so that no one — leaders or members — confuses an affinity network with a compliance function.

### Last-Minute Checkbox Input

When a business decision is nearly finalized and an ERG is asked for input as a formality, with no time to deliberate and no intention to change the decision regardless of the response, the consultation produces no value and signals disrespect: the ERG's time is treated as a box to check rather than as genuine expertise. Members learn that input is performative and stop engaging, which then deprives the organization of the input it needs when a decision is genuinely open. Solicit ERG input early, when it can still shape the decision, provide the context needed for substantive response, and close the loop on what was heard and what changed, because input that cannot change anything is not input.

### Unequal Support Across Groups

When some ERGs receive robust funding, executive access, and visibility while others — often those representing smaller or less powerful groups — receive nominal support, the differential treatment signals which groups the organization values, reproducing inside the ERG program the very inequity the program exists to address. The signal is read clearly by members and by the broader workforce. Apply consistent standards for funding, sponsorship, and visibility across all ERGs, with transparent criteria, and respond to new groups with the same support established groups receive, because equity among the ERGs is a prerequisite for the ERGs to advance equity in the organization.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Does each ERG have a clear charter defining purpose, scope, membership, leadership, and authority?
- [ ] Is each ERG paired with an engaged executive sponsor who provides visibility, access, and advocacy?
- [ ] Is adequate funding and administrative support provided equitably across ERGs?
- [ ] Is significant ERG labor compensated, recognized, or included in performance evaluation, avoiding disproportionate unpaid burden?
- [ ] Are scope boundaries clear, with individual cases routed to HR and ERG leaders not treated as representatives of their entire demographic?
- [ ] Are channels established for ERG input on business decisions, with thoughtful solicitation and loop-closing?
- [ ] Is support equitable across groups, with responsiveness to new groups?
- [ ] Is ERG effectiveness measured against purpose and adjusted based on findings?
