# Pay Equity And Inclusive Compensation

Pay equity is the measurable test of whether an organization's compensation practices produce fair outcomes across demographic groups. Pay gaps, differences in pay that cannot be explained by legitimate factors, create legal liability, reputational harm, and erosion of trust that no amount of compensation communication can offset. The agent must understand that pay equity requires both statistical analysis to identify gaps and correction of the systems that produce them, that pay equity is an ongoing practice not a one-time project, and that the goal is not just to fix current disparities but to prevent future ones through equitable compensation design. See also the compensation skills for detailed guidance on pay structures and market benchmarking.

Use this skill when conducting pay equity analysis, addressing pay gaps, or designing equitable compensation practices. The goal is to make the agent identify and correct pay disparities and design systems that prevent them.

## Core Rules

### Conduct Regular Pay Equity Analysis

Pay equity is not assessed once and forgotten. Regular analysis identifies gaps before they become legal or reputational crises.

- Conduct pay equity analysis at least annually, or more frequently during periods of rapid growth or organizational change.
- Use regression analysis to compare pay across demographic groups, controlling for legitimate factors such as role, level, experience, tenure, location, and performance.
- Distinguish explained pay differences, accounted for by legitimate factors, from unexplained gaps, which may indicate bias.
- Define the threshold for action, such as unexplained gaps above a certain percentage or statistical significance.
- Engage qualified analysts, and consider legal privilege to protect the analysis from discovery.

### Identify The Root Causes Of Gaps

Pay gaps are symptoms. Effective correction requires understanding the systems that produce them.

- Analyze where gaps emerge: at hire, through starting pay differences; through promotion, through differential advancement; through raises, through differential increase allocation; or through job level, through segregation into lower-paid roles.
- Review starting pay practices, which often embed historical disparities and market differences that compound over time.
- Review promotion and raise processes for bias, particularly subjective elements.
- Review job leveling and classification, which may undervalue roles held predominantly by certain groups.
- See the compensation skills for detailed guidance on pay structure design.

### Correct Current Disparities Responsibly

When unexplained gaps are found, correct them, but do so in a way that is fair, transparent, and sustainable.

- Make pay adjustments to close unexplained gaps, not explained ones, to avoid creating new inequities.
- Fund adjustments as a priority, not as a residual after other compensation budget uses.
- Communicate adjustments appropriately, explaining the rationale without disclosing the analysis details that could create legal exposure.
- Avoid creating new disparities by overcorrecting or by adjusting some groups and not others inconsistently.
- Time adjustments to coincide with regular compensation cycles where possible, to reduce disruption.

### Design Systems To Prevent Future Gaps

Correcting current gaps without fixing the systems that produce them guarantees recurrence. Design for equity.

- Use pay bands or ranges with defined midpoints and controlled discretion, reducing the scope for individual bias in pay setting. See the salary structure skill for detailed guidance.
- Standardize starting pay offers based on the role and band, not on candidate salary history, which embeds past disparities. Many jurisdictions prohibit asking for salary history.
- Apply consistent criteria for raises and promotions, reducing subjective discretion. See the merit increase and performance skills for detailed guidance.
- Monitor pay outcomes by demographic group continuously, not just during annual analysis.
- Review job architecture to ensure roles are valued equitably, addressing any undervaluation of roles held predominantly by certain groups.

### Address Occupational Segregation

Pay gaps often reflect occupational segregation, where different groups are concentrated in different, differently paid roles. Overall pay equity requires addressing this structural issue.

- Analyze representation across roles and levels, not just pay within roles.
- Identify roles or levels where certain groups are underrepresented, particularly in higher-paid or leadership roles.
- Address the pipeline and advancement barriers that produce segregation, through inclusive hiring, development, and promotion practices.
- See the DEI strategy and inclusive hiring skills for detailed guidance.

### Ensure Transparency Within Appropriate Limits

Pay transparency supports equity by making disparities visible, but it must be balanced against privacy and legal considerations.

- Provide pay ranges for roles, which many jurisdictions now require, to reduce information asymmetry that disadvantages certain groups.
- Communicate the compensation philosophy and how pay is determined, so employees understand the basis for their pay.
- Be cautious about disclosing individual pay data, which raises privacy concerns.
- Use transparency to build trust, but ensure the disclosed practices are equitable before disclosing them. See the pay transparency skill for detailed guidance.

### Coordinate With Legal Counsel

Pay equity analysis and correction have legal implications. Coordinate with counsel throughout.

- Consider conducting analysis under legal privilege to protect findings from discovery.
- Understand the legal standards for pay equity in each jurisdiction, which vary.
- Document the rationale for pay decisions and corrections, to support defense if challenged.
- Address disparities proactively; ignoring known disparities increases liability.

## Common Traps

### One-Time Analysis Without Ongoing Monitoring

Treating pay equity as a project rather than a practice, allowing gaps to re-emerge. Monitor continuously.

### Correcting Symptoms Without Fixing Systems

Adjusting pay to close current gaps without addressing the hiring, promotion, and raise processes that produce them, guaranteeing recurrence. Fix the systems.

### Starting Pay Based On Salary History

Basing offers on candidate salary history, embedding past disparities and compounding them. Base offers on the role and band.

### Ignoring Occupational Segregation

Focusing on pay within roles while ignoring that different groups are concentrated in different, differently paid roles. Address segregation.

### Disclosure Without Equitable Practices

Embracing pay transparency before ensuring practices are equitable, exposing disparities to legal and reputational risk. Fix practices before disclosing.

### Inadequate Statistical Analysis

Comparing average pay without controlling for legitimate factors, producing misleading gap figures. Use proper regression analysis.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is pay equity analysis conducted regularly, using regression to control for legitimate factors and identify unexplained gaps?
- [ ] Are the root causes of gaps identified, including starting pay, promotion, raises, and job leveling?
- [ ] Are current unexplained gaps corrected through prioritized, fair, and communicated adjustments?
- [ ] Are systems redesigned to prevent future gaps, including pay bands, standardized starting pay, and consistent raise and promotion criteria?
- [ ] Is occupational segregation analyzed and addressed through inclusive hiring, development, and promotion?
- [ ] Is transparency provided within appropriate limits, with equitable practices ensured before disclosure?
- [ ] Is legal counsel coordinated with throughout, including privilege, documentation, and proactive disparity resolution?
