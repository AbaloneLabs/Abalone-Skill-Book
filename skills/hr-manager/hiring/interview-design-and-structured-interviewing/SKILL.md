# Interview Design And Structured Interviewing

The interview is the most influential and most bias-prone component of selection. It is where interviewers form judgments that often override all other evidence, and where unstructured conversation measures likability and similarity more than capability. Agents often treat interviewing as a skill the hiring manager already has, and miss that unstructured interviews are among the worst predictors of job performance, that inconsistent questions across candidates make comparison impossible, and that a single improper question can create discrimination liability regardless of whether it influenced the decision.

Use this skill before designing an interview process, writing questions, training interviewers, or conducting interviews. The goal is to make the agent build a structured, consistent, evidence-based interview that predicts performance, minimizes bias, and can be defended if challenged.

## Core Rules

### Structure The Interview Around Job-Related Criteria

The interview should be a structured assessment of the competencies and qualifications defined in the job analysis, not a conversation that follows wherever it leads. Structure is the single most effective intervention for reducing bias and improving predictive validity.

Build structure by:

- identifying which competencies the interview will assess, derived from the job analysis;
- writing specific questions for each competency before the interview;
- asking the same core questions of every candidate, with planned follow-up probes;
- using a defined scoring rubric for each question, tied to behavioral indicators;
- training interviewers on the rubric and on how to evaluate responses consistently;
- sequencing the interview logically, from rapport-building to substantive assessment to candidate questions.

Structured does not mean robotic. Interviewers can build rapport and adapt follow-up questions, but the core questions and scoring must be consistent. The more unstructured the interview, the more it measures the interviewer's preferences rather than the candidate's qualifications.

### Choose Question Types That Match What You Are Assessing

Different question types measure different things, and matching the question type to the competency improves validity.

Common question types:

- behavioral questions ask the candidate to describe past experiences, on the principle that past behavior predicts future behavior; they work best when followed up rigorously to verify the candidate's actual role and contribution;
- situational or hypothetical questions present a scenario and ask how the candidate would respond, measuring reasoning and approach; they are useful for roles the candidate has not held before;
- knowledge and technical questions assess specific expertise; they should reflect the actual knowledge the role requires, not trivia;
- work samples and case exercises simulate the actual work; they are among the most predictive methods because they show capability directly;
- portfolio review examines past work product, useful for roles with tangible outputs.

Avoid relying on a single question type. A mix that includes behavioral evidence and a work sample or case exercise provides a more complete and more valid assessment than questions alone.

### Write Behavioral Questions That Elicit Specific Evidence

Behavioral questions work when they elicit specific, verifiable examples rather than generalities or hypotheticals. Poorly written behavioral questions produce vague answers that tell you nothing.

Write behavioral questions that:

- ask about a specific past situation, not a general philosophy;
- prompt the candidate to describe the situation, their specific actions, and the results;
- target a competency defined in the job analysis;
- are open enough to let the candidate choose a relevant example, but specific enough to prevent a rehearsed generic answer;
- are followed by probes to verify detail: what was your specific role, what did you do, what was the outcome, what would you do differently.

The follow-up is where the evidence is confirmed. A candidate who describes a team achievement must be probed to distinguish their individual contribution from the team's. Without follow-up, behavioral questions produce claims of capability rather than evidence of it.

### Train Interviewers On Lawful Interviewing And Bias

Interviewers are the front line of legal compliance and fairness, and most have never been trained. Untrained interviewers ask improper questions, apply inconsistent standards, and introduce bias that the structure is meant to control.

Train interviewers on:

- what questions are unlawful and why, including indirect probes into protected characteristics;
- how to redirect a candidate who volunteers protected information without recording or using it;
- how unconscious bias operates in interviews and how structured scoring counteracts it;
- how to use the scoring rubric consistently and independently;
- the importance of documenting evidence from the response, not conclusions about the candidate;
- how to conduct a fair, respectful interview that reflects well on the organization.

Training is not a one-time event. Refresh it regularly, and hold interviewers accountable for following the process. An interviewer who refuses to use the rubric or who asks improper questions is a liability.

### Avoid Discriminatory And Improper Questions

Certain lines of inquiry create direct discrimination risk regardless of intent or whether the answer influenced the decision. The risk arises from asking, not just from acting on the answer.

Do not ask about:

- race, color, national origin, or ethnicity, including questions about native language, accent, or how a name is pronounced;
- age, date of birth, or graduation year used to infer age;
- sex, gender identity, sexual orientation, marital status, pregnancy, or family and childcare arrangements;
- religion, religious observance, or scheduling assumptions based on faith;
- disability or medical history, including questions about the need for accommodation before a conditional offer;
- genetic information;
- citizenship or national origin beyond what is legally required for work authorization verification;
- arrest records in jurisdictions where they cannot be considered, and conviction records without individualized assessment where required;
- military discharge details beyond what is relevant;
- financial history unless job-related and consistent with applicable law.

Small talk is where improper questions most often arise. Asking about weekend plans, family, or commute arrangements to build rapport can elicit protected information. Keep small talk neutral and limited.

### Score Independently Before Group Discussion

Group interviews where evaluators discuss a candidate before recording their own assessment are dominated by the loudest or most senior voice, and independent observations are lost to groupthink. This is particularly damaging when the first speaker is biased, because their framing anchors the entire group.

Ensure independent scoring by:

- having each interviewer complete their scoring rubric before any group discussion;
- collecting and reviewing individual scores before convening the group;
- identifying and discussing significant discrepancies in scoring, rather than averaging them away;
- documenting the rationale for the final consensus decision.

Independent scoring captures the full range of observations and prevents a single interviewer's bias from overriding others. The discussion should reconcile differences through evidence, not through hierarchy.

### Document Interview Evidence, Not Conclusions

Interview notes should record what the candidate said and did, not the interviewer's conclusions about what kind of person they are. Conclusions without supporting evidence are subjective, inconsistent, and indefensible.

Document:

- the specific question asked and the candidate's response, summarized accurately;
- the evidence provided by the candidate, including their specific role and actions;
- the score assigned and the specific behavioral indicators that justify it;
- any concerns or red flags, with the specific evidence that raised them.

Notes that say "great candidate, strong communicator" are useless for defense or comparison. Notes that say "described a specific instance of mediating a conflict between two team members, identified the root cause, proposed a structured resolution, and both parties confirmed the outcome" provide evidence that can be reviewed and compared.

### Provide Accommodation For Candidates Who Need It

Candidates with disabilities may need accommodations to participate fully in the interview process. Failing to provide them is discrimination and can cause the organization to reject a qualified candidate based on a process barrier rather than on capability.

Provide accommodations such as:

- accessible interview locations for candidates with mobility needs;
- sign language interpreters or captioning for candidates who are deaf or hard of hearing;
- extended time or alternative formats for written exercises for candidates with learning or cognitive disabilities;
- modified scheduling for candidates with medical or treatment schedules;
- alternative formats for materials, such as large print or screen-reader-compatible documents.

Announce the availability of accommodation in the posting and in the interview invitation, and respond promptly to requests. Accommodation in the interview process is part of the obligation to provide equal opportunity, not a special favor. See the accommodations skill for the interactive process framework.

## Common Traps

### Unstructured Conversation Mistaken For An Interview

Treating the interview as a chat to get a feel for the candidate, which measures likability and similarity rather than capability and is the weakest predictor of performance. Structure the interview.

### Inconsistent Questions Across Candidates

Asking different questions of different candidates for the same role, making comparison impossible and inviting bias. Ask the same core questions.

### Improper Questions During Small Talk

Asking about family, weekend plans, or personal background to build rapport, which elicits protected information and creates discrimination risk. Keep small talk neutral.

### Groupthink Before Independent Scoring

Discussing the candidate before scoring independently, so the loudest or most senior voice anchors the group. Score independently first.

### Conclusions Without Evidence In Notes

Recording impressions like "good fit" or "not sure about this one" without the specific evidence that supports the judgment. Document evidence.

### Behavioral Questions Without Follow-Up

Accepting general claims of capability without probing for the candidate's specific role and actions, which produces assertions rather than evidence. Probe for detail.

### Ignoring Accommodation Needs In The Interview

Failing to provide interview accommodations, which screens out qualified candidates with disabilities based on process barriers. Announce and provide accommodations.

### Untrained Interviewers

Allowing hiring managers to interview without training on lawful questions, bias, and scoring, which introduces risk that structure cannot control. Train all interviewers.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is the interview structured around competencies from the job analysis, with the same core questions and scoring rubric for all candidates?
- [ ] Are question types matched to the competencies being assessed, with behavioral questions followed up rigorously to verify individual contribution?
- [ ] Are behavioral questions written to elicit specific past examples, with probes for situation, action, and result?
- [ ] Have all interviewers been trained on lawful interviewing, unconscious bias, and the scoring rubric?
- [ ] Are questions free of inquiries into protected characteristics, including indirect probes and small talk that could elicit protected information?
- [ ] Does each interviewer complete their scoring rubric independently before any group discussion, with discrepancies reconciled through evidence?
- [ ] Do interview notes record specific candidate responses and behavioral evidence rather than conclusions and impressions?
- [ ] Are interview accommodations announced and provided promptly for candidates who need them?
