# Applicant Recordkeeping And Compliance

Hiring records are the evidence that the process was fair, and they are required by law. When a selection decision is challenged, the organization must be able to show who applied, what criteria were used, how each candidate was evaluated, and why each was advanced or rejected. Agents often treat recordkeeping as administrative overhead, and miss that incomplete or inconsistent records transform a defensible process into an indefensible one, that retention periods are legally mandated, and that government reporting obligations require demographic data collected and maintained in specific ways.

Use this skill before establishing applicant tracking practices, defining disposition codes, preparing compliance reports, or responding to an audit or inquiry. The goal is to make the agent maintain records that are complete, consistent, retained for the required period, and usable for both defense and compliance reporting.

## Core Rules

### Define Who Is An Applicant And Track All Of Them

The definition of an applicant determines whose data must be retained and reported. The organization must apply a consistent definition, because inconsistent application creates gaps in the record and vulnerabilities in reporting.

Define and track:

- who constitutes an applicant under the organization's consistent definition, typically those who express interest and whom the organization considers for the position;
- the source through which each applicant was referred, which is required for certain reporting;
- the demographic data for each applicant, where required, collected through voluntary self-identification using prescribed categories;
- the disposition of each applicant at each stage, using consistent disposition codes;
- the rationale for each decision, tied to screening criteria and job-related factors.

The applicant pool is the denominator for adverse impact analysis and the basis for compliance reporting. If the pool is not tracked completely and consistently, neither analysis nor reporting can be relied upon.

### Use Consistent Disposition Codes

Disposition codes classify why an applicant did not advance or was not hired. Consistent codes enable meaningful analysis of the funnel and defensible documentation of decisions. Inconsistent or vague codes make analysis impossible and defense weak.

Use codes that capture:

- the stage at which the applicant exited, such as application, screen, interview, offer;
- the specific reason, such as did not meet minimum qualifications, withdrew, failed assessment, selected another candidate, or salary mismatch;
- whether the reason was candidate-driven or organization-driven;
- enough specificity to support analysis without being so granular that consistency is lost.

Train everyone who enters disposition data to use codes consistently, and audit the codes regularly for accuracy and completeness. A pool where half the rejections are coded as "other" provides no analytical value and weak defensibility.

### Retain Records For The Required Period

Hiring records must be retained for specific periods that vary by jurisdiction and by the organization's obligations. Premature destruction creates legal exposure and may be treated as evidence adverse to the organization.

Retain:

- applications, resumes, interview notes, assessments, and disposition records for the period required by applicable law, commonly one to two years, longer for certain obligations;
- records related to background checks, including consents and reports, for the period specified by the applicable consumer reporting law;
- records of accommodation requests and responses during the hiring process;
- demographic data and supporting documentation for compliance reporting;
- job postings, advertisements, and sourcing records.

Establish a retention schedule and destruction process that ensures records are kept as long as needed and destroyed securely when the period expires. Retaining records longer than necessary creates unnecessary privacy exposure; destroying them too early creates compliance and defense gaps.

### Collect And Maintain Demographic Data For Reporting

Government contractors and other obligated employers must collect demographic data on applicants and employees and report it through prescribed channels. Even non-obligated employers benefit from demographic data for voluntary adverse impact analysis.

Collect and maintain:

- voluntary self-identification data using the prescribed categories for race, ethnicity, sex, and veteran status, where applicable;
- disability self-identification data, using prescribed formats and timing;
- data that is kept separate from the application and not used in selection decisions;
- records of who was invited to self-identify and the response rates, which affect reporting accuracy.

Demographic data must be collected voluntarily, stored separately from the selection decision process, and used only for reporting and analysis. Using self-identified demographic data in selection decisions is both unlawful and destructive of the trust needed for accurate collection.

### Prepare Required Government Reports Accurately And On Time

Government contractors and other obligated employers must file periodic reports, such as EEO-1 reports, VETS reports, and affirmative action plans. These reports require accurate data and timely submission.

Prepare reports by:

- maintaining the data throughout the year, rather than scrambling at reporting time;
- ensuring the data is categorized according to current reporting standards, which change periodically;
- reconciling the data across systems, such as the applicant tracking system and HRIS, to ensure consistency;
- submitting by the required deadlines, recognizing that late or inaccurate filings create compliance violations;
- retaining the supporting documentation for the period required.

Report preparation is not just a clerical task; inaccuracies can trigger audits, and the data in reports is itself evidence that can be examined in a discrimination inquiry. Ensure the people preparing reports understand both the mechanics and the significance.

### Maintain Records That Can Withstand An Audit

Government contractors may be audited by agencies such as the OFCCP, and any employer may face a discrimination charge that requires production of hiring records. The records must be organized, complete, and capable of demonstrating a fair, consistent process.

Ensure records can withstand audit by:

- documenting the job analysis, posting, sourcing, screening, interviewing, assessment, and selection for each requisition;
- maintaining the criteria and rubrics used at each stage;
- recording the specific, criterion-based rationale for each decision;
- tracking the demographic composition of the pool and hires for adverse impact analysis;
- being able to produce a complete narrative of the process for any position, from posting to hire.

An audit or charge is not the time to discover that records are incomplete, inconsistent, or missing. The discipline of maintaining audit-ready records throughout the year is the only reliable defense.

### Protect Applicant Data Privacy

Applicant data is personal data subject to privacy law and the organization's own policies. The data collected during hiring must be protected with the same rigor as employee data.

Protect applicant data by:

- limiting access to those involved in the hiring decision;
- storing data in secure systems with appropriate access controls;
- using data only for the hiring purpose for which it was collected;
- providing notice to applicants about how their data will be used and retained;
- honoring applicant rights regarding their data, such as access, correction, or deletion within legal limits;
- securely disposing of data when the retention period expires.

Applicant data that is shared broadly, used for unrelated purposes, or retained indefinitely creates privacy violations and undermines trust in the hiring process. See the data-privacy skill for the broader framework.

### Conduct Periodic Adverse Impact Analysis

Hiring records should be analyzed periodically to detect adverse impact, not merely stored for defense after a charge is filed. Proactive analysis enables correction before a problem becomes a legal matter.

Analyze by:

- calculating selection rates for each demographic group at each stage of the process;
- applying the four-fifths rule or equivalent to identify stages where a group's selection rate is significantly below that of the highest-selected group;
- investigating the causes of identified disparities, examining criteria, tools, and reviewer behavior;
- taking corrective action and documenting the analysis and actions taken;
- repeating the analysis regularly to track whether corrections are effective.

Adverse impact analysis is both a compliance tool and a fairness tool. It reveals problems that are invisible at the individual decision level but apparent in the aggregate, and it enables the organization to address them proactively.

## Common Traps

### Incomplete Applicant Tracking

Not tracking all applicants or not recording dispositions, leaving the pool undefined and the process indefensible. Track every applicant and every decision.

### Vague Or Inconsistent Disposition Codes

Coding rejections as "other" or "not a fit," which provides no analytical value and weak defense. Use specific, consistent codes.

### Premature Record Destruction

Destroying hiring records before the retention period expires, creating compliance violations and adverse inferences. Follow the retention schedule.

### Demographic Data Used In Selection

Allowing self-identified demographic data to influence selection decisions, which is unlawful and destroys collection trust. Keep demographic data separate from decisions.

### Scrambling At Reporting Time

Not maintaining data throughout the year, leading to inaccurate or late government reports. Maintain data continuously.

### Records That Cannot Support A Narrative

Having records that are too incomplete or inconsistent to reconstruct the process for a requisition, making defense impossible. Maintain audit-ready records.

### No Proactive Adverse Impact Analysis

Storing records without ever analyzing them, missing disparities that could be corrected before they become legal matters. Analyze periodically.

### Applicant Data Treated As Less Sensitive Than Employee Data

Applying weaker privacy protections to applicant data, which is both a legal error and a trust failure. Protect applicant data equally.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is a consistent applicant definition applied, with every applicant tracked from source through final disposition?
- [ ] Are disposition codes specific, consistent, and audited for accuracy and completeness?
- [ ] Are all hiring records, including applications, notes, assessments, and dispositions, retained for the legally required period and destroyed securely thereafter?
- [ ] Is voluntary demographic data collected using prescribed categories, stored separately from selection decisions, and used only for reporting and analysis?
- [ ] Are required government reports, such as EEO-1 and VETS, prepared from continuously maintained data and filed accurately and on time?
- [ ] Can the organization produce a complete, documented narrative of the hiring process for any requisition, from posting through selection, sufficient to withstand an audit?
- [ ] Is applicant data protected with access controls, secure storage, purpose limitation, notice, and rights, equivalent to employee data protection?
- [ ] Is adverse impact analysis conducted periodically at each stage of the funnel, with causes investigated and corrective actions taken and documented?
