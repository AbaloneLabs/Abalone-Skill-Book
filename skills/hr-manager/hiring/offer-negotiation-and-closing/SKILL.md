# Offer, Negotiation, And Closing

The offer stage is where the employment relationship begins to form, and where the organization's commitments become binding. The offer letter, the verbal representations made during negotiation, and the documents the candidate signs all create expectations and obligations that can outlast the relationship itself. Agents often treat the offer as a celebratory finish line, and miss that inflated promises create fraudulent inducement claims, that inconsistent offers across similar candidates create pay inequity, and that the terms set at this stage constrain the organization's flexibility for the entire tenure.

Use this skill before extending an offer, negotiating terms, drafting offer documents, or managing a candidate with competing offers. The goal is to make the agent extend offers that are accurate, consistent, fair, and protective of both the organization and the new relationship.

## Core Rules

### Make The Offer Accurate And Complete

The offer letter is a legal document that defines the initial terms of employment. Inaccuracies, omissions, and overstatements create disputes that can arise months or years later. Every material term should be stated clearly and correctly.

Include in the offer:

- job title, level, and reporting relationship, consistent with the job analysis;
- a summary of primary duties and responsibilities, referencing the job description;
- starting compensation, including base salary or hourly rate, and how it is paid;
- bonus, commission, or variable pay structure, including how it is determined and any conditions;
- benefits summary, with eligibility dates and any waiting periods, without overstating coverage or value;
- work location, schedule, and remote or hybrid arrangements, stated explicitly;
- start date and any contingencies, such as background check, reference verification, visa, or licensing;
- employment status, including whether the role is at-will or subject to a contract, probation period, or notice terms;
- required agreements as conditions of employment, such as confidentiality, non-compete, or invention assignment, disclosed before acceptance;
- expiration date of the offer, giving the candidate a reasonable window to decide.

Accuracy protects both parties. The candidate knows what they are accepting, and the organization has a document that reflects the actual agreement.

### Avoid Promises That Cannot Or Will Not Be Kept

The pressure to close a desired candidate can lead to promises that exceed what the role or the organization can deliver. These promises, whether in writing or made verbally, create legal exposure and early disillusionment.

Avoid:

- implying job security or permanence that the employment status does not provide, especially in at-will relationships;
- promising specific future promotions, career paths, or timelines that are not guaranteed;
- overstating bonus potential by citing maximums without explaining the conditions and historical attainment;
- describing the role's scope, autonomy, or resources in terms that exceed reality;
- assuring remote work flexibility that the policy does not support or that may change;
- verbally contradicting the written offer in ways that create ambiguity about the true terms.

Fraudulent inducement claims arise when a candidate accepts an offer based on material misrepresentations. Even without legal action, unmet promises produce early attrition and damage reputation. Honesty at the offer stage is both a legal protection and a retention strategy.

### Ensure Internal Equity In Offer Decisions

The offer extended to a new hire does not exist in isolation. It will be compared, formally or informally, to the compensation of existing employees in similar roles. Offers that are significantly above or below internal benchmarks create equity issues that ripple through the team.

Ensure equity by:

- benchmarking the offer against the established salary band or structure for the role and level;
- considering the compensation of current employees in comparable roles, to avoid creating unexplained gaps;
- documenting the rationale for the specific offer, including how experience, skills, and market factors justify the level;
- being aware of pay transparency laws that may require disclosure of pay ranges to current employees or the public;
- addressing any compression or inversion issues, where new hires are paid more than experienced current employees, proactively rather than reactively.

An offer that ignores internal equity may close one candidate while creating resentment and retention risk among existing team members. See the compensation skill for salary structure and banding methodology.

### Negotiate Fairly And Consistently

Negotiation is expected, but how the organization negotiates affects both the outcome and the fairness of the process. Inconsistent negotiation practices, where some candidates are given flexibility and others are not, can create pay disparities that correlate with protected characteristics.

Negotiate consistently by:

- establishing guidelines for what is negotiable, such as base salary within a range, start date, or sign-on bonus, and what is not;
- applying the same negotiation framework to all candidates for similar roles, regardless of who asks or how aggressively;
- documenting the final agreed terms and the basis for any deviation from the initial offer;
- being aware that relying on salary history to set pay, even in negotiation, is restricted or prohibited in many jurisdictions and perpetuates existing pay gaps;
- basing the final offer on the candidate's qualifications, market data, and internal equity, not on the candidate's negotiation skill or confidence.

Research shows that negotiation outcomes often correlate with demographic characteristics, not because of merit but because of differences in how candidates are socialized to negotiate. A structured, consistent approach to negotiation reduces the risk that these differences become pay disparities.

### Disclose Required Agreements Before Acceptance

Many roles require the employee to sign agreements as a condition of employment, such as confidentiality, non-compete, non-solicit, invention assignment, or arbitration agreements. These should be disclosed before the offer is accepted, so the candidate can make an informed decision.

Disclose:

- all agreements the candidate will be required to sign, with enough detail about their substance;
- any restrictions the agreements impose, such as non-compete scope, duration, and geographic reach;
- whether the agreements are standard for the role or negotiated;
- any jurisdiction-specific limitations on enforceability, being honest that overly broad restrictions may not be enforceable but still create friction.

Surprising a new hire with restrictive agreements on their first day, after they have resigned from a prior job, is both unfair and a potential legal vulnerability. The candidate should know what they are committing to before they accept.

### Handle Competing Offers Without Desperation

When a desired candidate has competing offers, the temptation is to bid aggressively. While some flexibility is appropriate, bidding wars that exceed the role's value or the organization's budget create unsustainable precedents and internal inequity.

Handle competing offers by:

- knowing your ceiling before negotiation begins, based on the salary band and budget, and not exceeding it under pressure;
- considering non-monetary factors that may differentiate the offer, such as growth opportunity, flexibility, or culture, rather than only increasing pay;
- being honest about what the organization can and cannot offer;
- accepting that not every candidate will be closeable, and that overpaying to win a candidate who will leave for the next higher offer is not a sustainable strategy;
- documenting the final terms and the rationale for any exceptional offer.

A candidate who requires an offer far above the role's value to accept may not be the right fit, and the precedent set by an inflated offer will affect every future negotiation and every internal comparison.

### Manage Visa And Immigration Questions Honestly

When a candidate requires visa sponsorship or work authorization, the offer process must address this accurately. Mishandling visa questions can create discrimination liability and can leave the candidate and the organization in a difficult position.

Handle visa questions by:

- not asking about national origin or citizenship status beyond what is legally required for work authorization verification;
- being clear about whether the organization will sponsor a visa and what type, before the candidate relies on the offer;
- understanding the timeline, cost, and uncertainty of the visa process, and setting realistic expectations;
- including appropriate contingencies in the offer, such as start date dependent on visa approval;
- not making promises about visa outcomes that are outside the organization's control.

Discrimination based on national origin or citizenship status is unlawful, and questions about work authorization must be carefully framed. Coordinate with immigration counsel for sponsorship cases.

### Set The Stage For Onboarding

The offer stage is the bridge to onboarding. A well-managed close sets expectations and prepares the candidate for a successful start, while a poorly managed close creates anxiety and no-show risk.

Bridge to onboarding by:

- communicating regularly between acceptance and start date, to maintain engagement;
- providing pre-boarding information, such as first-day logistics, required documents, and what to expect;
- introducing the hiring manager or a peer contact before the start date;
- confirming that all contingencies, such as background checks and equipment, are on track;
- preparing the team and workspace so the new hire's first day is welcoming rather than chaotic.

Candidates who accept an offer but receive no communication for weeks before starting may question their decision or accept a counter-offer. The period between acceptance and start is part of the hiring process, not a gap.

## Common Traps

### Inflating The Role To Close The Candidate

Overstating scope, security, or career trajectory to win acceptance, creating early disillusionment, attrition, and potential fraudulent inducement claims. Be honest.

### Ignoring Internal Equity

Extending offers without regard to current employee compensation, creating compression, inversion, or inequity that damages retention. Benchmark against internal bands.

### Inconsistent Negotiation Practices

Giving some candidates more flexibility than others based on negotiation style, which can produce pay disparities correlated with demographics. Standardize negotiation guidelines.

### Relying On Salary History

Basing offers on what the candidate earned previously, which perpetuates pay gaps and is restricted or prohibited in many jurisdictions. Base pay on the role, market, and qualifications.

### Surprise Restrictive Agreements

Presenting non-compete or arbitration agreements on the first day after acceptance, when the candidate has already resigned. Disclose before acceptance.

### Bidding Beyond The Role's Value

Exceeding budget and internal equity to win a bidding war, creating unsustainable precedents and resentment. Know your ceiling.

### Visa Misrepresentation

Making promises about visa sponsorship or outcomes that the organization cannot guarantee, leaving the candidate stranded. Be honest and include contingencies.

### No Communication Between Acceptance And Start

Going silent after acceptance, which risks counter-offers, no-shows, and disengagement. Maintain contact through start date.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Does the offer letter accurately state all material terms, including title, compensation, benefits, location, schedule, contingencies, employment status, and required agreements?
- [ ] Are all representations, verbal and written, free of promises about job security, promotions, or career paths that the organization may not keep?
- [ ] Is the offer benchmarked against internal salary bands and current employee compensation to ensure internal equity?
- [ ] Are negotiation practices consistent across candidates, with guidelines for what is negotiable and documentation of agreed terms?
- [ ] Is pay based on the role, market, and qualifications rather than salary history, in compliance with applicable bans?
- [ ] Are required agreements, including restrictive covenants, disclosed before acceptance so the candidate can make an informed decision?
- [ ] Are competing offers managed with a known ceiling and honest communication, without exceeding the role's value or creating inequitable precedents?
- [ ] Are visa and work authorization questions handled lawfully, with honest disclosure of sponsorship and realistic expectations?
- [ ] Is the period between acceptance and start date managed with regular communication and pre-boarding to maintain engagement?
