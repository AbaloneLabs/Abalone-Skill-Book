# Pre-Employment Assessment And Testing

Tests and assessments can be among the most predictive selection tools available, far exceeding unstructured interviews. They can also be among the most dangerous, because a test that produces adverse impact and lacks demonstrated validity is direct evidence of discrimination. Agents often treat tests as objective by nature, and miss that a test's validity is specific to the job and the construct it claims to measure, that personality and culture-fit assessments frequently encode bias, and that validation is an ongoing obligation, not a one-time stamp of approval.

Use this skill before selecting, designing, administering, or interpreting pre-employment tests or assessments. The goal is to make the agent choose and validate instruments that predict performance for the specific role, administer them consistently, and monitor their impact on protected groups.

## Core Rules

### Establish What The Assessment Must Measure Before Choosing A Tool

An assessment should be selected because it measures a specific competency or ability that the job requires, not because it is popular or convenient. The link between the construct measured and the essential functions must be explicit.

Before selecting an assessment:

- identify the specific competency, ability, or characteristic the assessment will measure, derived from the job analysis;
- confirm that this construct predicts performance in this specific role, based on evidence rather than assumption;
- determine what level of the construct is needed for competent performance;
- evaluate whether an assessment is the best way to measure it, or whether other evidence such as work samples or behavioral interviews would be more valid and less intrusive;
- document the rationale for choosing this assessment for this role.

A cognitive ability test that predicts performance in complex roles may be inappropriate and unnecessarily screening for a role where the work does not require the measured ability. The assessment must fit the job, not the reverse.

### Understand And Apply The Validity Framework

Validity is the extent to which the assessment measures what it claims to measure and supports the inferences drawn from its scores for a specific purpose. It is the legal and scientific foundation for using any test in selection.

The main validity strategies are:

- criterion-related validity: statistical evidence that assessment scores correlate with job performance, the strongest basis for a selection test;
- content validity: evidence that the assessment's content represents the actual work of the job, appropriate for work samples and knowledge tests;
- construct validity: evidence that the assessment measures a theoretical construct, such as reasoning ability, that is itself related to job performance.

Validity is not a property of a test in general. A test validated for one role may be invalid for another. Relying on a vendor's general validity claim without confirming it applies to your specific role and context is insufficient. If you cannot demonstrate validity for the specific use, the assessment should not be used.

### Assess Reliability And Fairness Across Groups

A valid test must also be reliable, producing consistent results, and fair, producing comparable results across demographic groups. A test that is valid for the overall population but produces adverse impact for a protected group requires specific scrutiny.

Evaluate:

- the reliability of the instrument, including test-retest and internal consistency, as reported by the vendor or in validation studies;
- whether the assessment produces comparable scores across demographic groups, or whether it produces adverse impact;
- whether any group differences are attributable to the construct being measured or to bias in the instrument;
- whether alternative assessments with less adverse impact are available that achieve the same predictive purpose;
- whether accommodations can be provided that maintain validity while reducing barriers for candidates with disabilities.

A test that produces significant adverse impact is not automatically unlawful, but it triggers the obligation to demonstrate job-relatedness and consistency with business necessity, and to consider less discriminatory alternatives. Ignoring adverse impact does not make it go away.

### Design Work Samples And Simulations That Reflect The Job

Work samples, case exercises, and job simulations are among the most valid and least biased selection methods because they show the candidate doing the actual work. They are content-valid by design when they mirror the essential functions.

Design effective work samples by:

- basing the exercise on actual tasks, problems, or materials from the role, sanitized of confidential information;
- ensuring the exercise reflects the complexity, tools, and conditions of the real work;
- making the exercise long enough to produce meaningful evidence but not so long that it deters qualified candidates or creates scheduling barriers;
- defining clear evaluation criteria and scoring rubrics, tied to the competencies the exercise assesses;
- administering the same exercise, with the same instructions and time, to all candidates;
- providing any accommodations needed so that the exercise tests the competency, not a disability.

A work sample that asks a software developer to write code relevant to the role, a salesperson to conduct a mock discovery call, or an analyst to interpret a dataset, provides direct evidence of capability that no interview question can match. The investment in designing a good exercise pays off in hiring accuracy.

### Use Personality And Culture Assessments With Extreme Caution

Personality inventories and culture-fit assessments are widely marketed but carry significant risk. They can encode cultural and demographic bias, they often measure constructs that are weakly related to job performance, and their use in selection is subject to the same validity and adverse impact requirements as any other test.

Be cautious with:

- instruments that claim to measure culture fit, which often measure conformity to the existing workforce's demographic and cultural characteristics;
- personality dimensions that correlate with protected characteristics or that lack evidence of predicting performance in the specific role;
- instruments that pathologize traits associated with disability, neurodivergence, or cultural difference;
- results interpreted by untrained users who may draw unsupported inferences from scores;
- assessments used to screen out candidates without human review of the full profile.

If a personality assessment is used, it must be validated for the specific role, its adverse impact must be assessed, and its results should inform rather than replace human judgment. A personality score should never be the sole basis for rejecting a candidate.

### Administer Assessments Consistently And Lawfully

Consistency in administration is essential for both validity and fairness. If candidates take the assessment under different conditions, comparisons are compromised and defensibility is undermined.

Administer consistently by:

- giving all candidates the same instructions, time limits, and materials for the same assessment;
- using the same version of the assessment for all candidates in a role, unless an accommodation requires modification;
- administering the assessment at the same stage in the process for all candidates;
- ensuring the testing environment, whether in-person or remote, is controlled and equitable;
- providing required accommodations promptly and without requiring unnecessary medical documentation;
- securing assessment content to prevent coaching, sharing, or leakage that would undermine validity.

Inconsistency in administration is itself a source of bias and a basis for challenge. The assessment process must be as standardized as the assessment instrument.

### Comply With Testing-Specific Legal Requirements

Employment testing is subject to specific legal and regulatory frameworks that go beyond general anti-discrimination law. These requirements vary by jurisdiction but share common principles.

Comply with:

- professional testing standards, including the Uniform Guidelines on Employee Selection Procedures or equivalent, which establish expectations for validation, adverse impact analysis, and recordkeeping;
- state and local laws restricting or banning certain types of assessments, including restrictions on honesty tests, credit checks, and some personality inventories;
- requirements for notice, consent, or disclosure when using algorithmic or AI-based assessment tools;
- data protection requirements for assessment data, including retention limits and candidate rights to access their data;
- recordkeeping requirements, including retention of assessment scores and demographic data for adverse impact analysis.

Testing without awareness of these requirements is reckless. The legal framework for employment testing is well-developed, and ignorance of it is not a defense.

### Monitor Outcomes And Adjust Or Discontinue

Validation and fairness are not one-time determinations. The assessment's performance must be monitored as the role, the workforce, and the applicant pool change.

Monitor by:

- tracking the demographic composition of candidates who pass and fail each assessment;
- calculating adverse impact ratios and investigating significant disparities;
- correlating assessment scores with subsequent job performance to confirm ongoing criterion validity;
- reviewing candidate feedback on the assessment experience;
- discontinuing or revising assessments that lose validity, produce adverse impact that cannot be justified, or deter qualified candidates.

An assessment that was valid and fair when adopted may become problematic as conditions change. Regular review ensures that the assessment continues to serve its purpose without creating unnecessary barriers.

## Common Traps

### Treating Tests As Objective By Nature

Assuming any test is fair because it is standardized, when in fact tests can encode bias and must be validated for the specific role. Validate every assessment.

### Using A Test Validated For One Role On Another

Assuming validity transfers across roles, when validity is specific to the job, the construct, and the context. Confirm validity for each use.

### Ignoring Adverse Impact From Assessments

Using assessments without tracking their impact on protected groups, allowing discriminatory outcomes to persist. Monitor and analyze outcomes.

### Culture-Fit Personality Tests As Bias Vectors

Using personality or culture-fit assessments that measure conformity to existing workforce demographics rather than job-related constructs. Validate or discontinue.

### Inconsistent Administration

Administering the same assessment under different conditions, times, or instructions, undermining validity and defensibility. Standardize administration.

### Overreliance On Test Scores Without Human Judgment

Using a single test score as the sole basis for rejection, without considering other evidence or the full candidate profile. Use tests to inform, not replace, judgment.

### No Ongoing Validation Or Monitoring

Treating validation as a one-time approval and never revisiting whether the assessment remains valid and fair. Monitor continuously.

### Work Samples That Do Not Reflect The Job

Designing exercises that test skills the role does not require or that omit essential functions, reducing validity and fairness. Mirror the actual work.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is each assessment selected because it measures a specific competency derived from the job analysis, with documented rationale for its use in this role?
- [ ] Has the assessment's validity been established for the specific role and purpose, using criterion, content, or construct validity evidence?
- [ ] Has the assessment been evaluated for reliability and for adverse impact across demographic groups, with less discriminatory alternatives considered?
- [ ] Do work samples and simulations reflect the actual essential functions, complexity, and tools of the role, with clear scoring rubrics?
- [ ] Are personality and culture-fit assessments used only when validated for the specific role, with adverse impact assessed and results never the sole basis for rejection?
- [ ] Is the assessment administered consistently to all candidates, with the same instructions, time, version, and stage, and with accommodations provided?
- [ ] Are testing-specific legal requirements met, including professional standards, jurisdictional restrictions, notice and consent, and recordkeeping?
- [ ] Are assessment outcomes monitored for adverse impact and ongoing validity, with assessments revised or discontinued when problems emerge?
