# Job Analysis And Role Definition

Job analysis is the foundation document that every downstream HR decision references. The job description determines who you source, how you screen, what you interview for, what accommodation you must consider, how you classify the worker, and how you defend a termination. Agents often treat the job description as a marketing document or a formality, borrowing from a template, and miss that vague or inflated requirements create adverse impact, that missing essential functions undermine accommodation analysis, and that a role defined by impression rather than evidence cannot be defended in any challenge.

Use this skill before posting a role, before designing a selection process, before responding to an accommodation request, or before reclassifying a position. The goal is to make the agent produce a role definition grounded in the actual work, legally defensible, and operationally useful to hiring managers, interviewers, and the selected candidate.

## Core Rules

### Start From The Work, Not The Person

A job analysis begins with understanding what work needs to be done, not with a picture of the ideal person. When you start from a person, you reproduce their characteristics, including irrelevant ones, and you exclude qualified candidates who do not match the mental image.

Gather information about the actual work through:

- observing employees currently in the role or a similar one;
- interviewing the hiring manager about objectives, deliverables, and constraints;
- interviewing incumbents and peers about what they actually do day to day;
- reviewing prior performance data to identify what distinguishes high from low performers;
- examining the workflow, dependencies, and handoffs the role sits within;
- identifying the problems the role exists to solve and the decisions it must make.

The output should describe the work in terms of functions, responsibilities, and outcomes, not in terms of the personality or background of the person who happened to do it last.

### Define Essential Functions Explicitly

Essential functions are the fundamental job duties that a person holding the position must be able to perform, with or without reasonable accommodation. This determination is not academic: it defines what accommodations must be considered, what criteria are legitimately job-related, and what a termination for performance can be based on.

Essential functions are determined by factors including:

- whether the function is the reason the position exists;
- the number of employees available to perform the function;
- the degree of expertise or skill required to perform it;
- the amount of time spent on the function;
- the consequences of not requiring the person to perform it;
- whether the function is highly specialized and the incumbent is hired for that expertise;
- the work experience of past incumbents in the job.

Marginal functions, those that are incidental or could be reassigned, should be separated. This distinction matters critically for accommodation: an employer must accommodate essential functions through modification or assistive tools, but need not eliminate an essential function, and may reassign marginal functions as an accommodation.

### Distinguish Required From Preferred Qualifications

A common error is listing every desirable attribute as a requirement, which inflates the profile and disproportionately screens out groups who may have the capability but not the exact credential. Every requirement should be justified by the essential functions.

For each qualification, ask:

- is it necessary to perform the essential functions on day one, or could it be learned in a reasonable ramp-up period;
- is there a less restrictive alternative that would achieve the same outcome, such as equivalent experience in lieu of a specific degree;
- does this requirement have disparate impact on protected groups, and if so, is it justified by business necessity and job-relatedness;
- what evidence would demonstrate that a candidate possesses this qualification.

Separate must-have requirements clearly from nice-to-have preferences. This helps qualified candidates self-select accurately and prevents screening criteria from becoming proxies for demographic characteristics. A degree requirement for a role that does not require the knowledge a degree provides is both over-inclusive and legally vulnerable.

### Establish Competencies And Behavioral Indicators

Beyond technical qualifications, roles require competencies: the behaviors and skills that predict success in the specific organizational context. Define each competency with observable behavioral indicators so that interviewers can assess them consistently.

For each competency:

- define what it looks like at the expected level for this role;
- identify positive indicators that demonstrate the competency;
- identify negative indicators that signal its absence;
- connect it to a specific essential function or responsibility;
- avoid vague terms like "strong communication" without defining what strong looks like in this role.

Competencies should be derived from what high performers actually do in this role and organization, not from a generic competency library applied uniformly. The same competency name may mean different things at different levels or in different functions.

### Determine Job Level, Scope, And Authority

The level and scope of the role affect compensation, title, reporting relationships, and expectations. Define these explicitly to prevent scope creep and to ensure the role is positioned correctly in the organization.

Specify:

- the decision-making authority: what the person can decide independently, what requires consultation, what requires approval;
- the scope of impact: whose work is affected, what budget or resources are controlled, what stakeholders are served;
- the reporting line and key internal relationships;
- the autonomy and supervision expected: does the role work independently, lead others, or operate under close direction;
- the complexity and ambiguity the role must navigate;
- how the role compares to existing roles in level and scope, to ensure internal equity.

A role defined without clear scope becomes whatever the person in it makes it, which produces inconsistency, compensation inequity, and disputes about expectations.

### Identify Working Conditions And Physical Requirements

Working conditions and physical demands are part of the job analysis and affect accommodation, safety, and candidate expectations. State them accurately and specifically.

Document:

- work location and schedule, including any requirement for on-site presence, travel, or non-standard hours;
- physical demands such as lifting, standing, sitting, or visual requirements, expressed in terms of frequency and intensity;
- environmental conditions such as exposure to noise, temperature, hazards, or outdoor work;
- any safety-sensitive aspects of the role, such as operating machinery, driving, or working with hazardous materials;
- any regulatory or licensing requirements, such as professional licensure, security clearance, or health certifications.

Physical requirements should reflect the actual essential functions, not generic boilerplate. Inflated physical requirements can screen out individuals with disabilities and create discrimination exposure. State only what the job truly requires.

### Align With Classification And Compliance

The job analysis feeds worker classification, exemption status, and compliance reporting. Get the classification right at the definition stage, because misclassification creates significant legal and financial exposure.

Consider:

- whether the role is properly an employee or could be a contractor, based on the level of control, independence, and integration;
- whether the role is exempt or non-exempt from overtime, based on duties, salary level, and salary basis tests;
- whether the role requires specific licenses, certifications, or registrations;
- whether the role is subject to background check, drug testing, or other screening requirements that are job-related and justified;
- whether the role involves working with vulnerable populations, financial assets, or other areas requiring heightened screening.

Classification should be based on the actual duties and working relationship, not on the label the employer prefers. When the analysis is ambiguous, seek guidance from legal counsel. See the compliance skill for classification standards and the compensation skill for exemption implications.

### Write The Job Description As A Living Document

The job description should reflect the current reality of the role, not a historical snapshot. Roles evolve, and a stale job description creates mismatched expectations, inaccurate hiring, and weak defense in disputes.

Maintain the job description by:

- reviewing and updating it before each hiring cycle;
- revising it when duties, scope, or reporting relationships change significantly;
- confirming it with the incumbent and manager during performance reviews;
- ensuring it remains aligned with the organizational structure;
- versioning changes so the description in effect at any time can be identified.

A job description that no one has reviewed in three years is not a reliable basis for hiring, accommodation, or performance management.

## Common Traps

### Starting From A Template Or A Person

Copying a generic job description or describing the last person in the role produces a document that reflects neither the current work nor the actual requirements. Start from the work.

### Inflating Requirements To Filter The Pool

Adding degree, experience, or certification requirements that the work does not justify narrows the pool, creates adverse impact, and is legally vulnerable. Require only what the job truly needs.

### Failing To Identify Essential Functions

Treating all duties as equally important leaves no basis for accommodation analysis, because you cannot distinguish what must be done from what could be reassigned. Identify and document essential functions explicitly.

### Vague Competencies Without Behavioral Indicators

Listing "communication" or "leadership" without defining what they look like in this role makes assessment subjective and inconsistent. Define observable behavioral indicators.

### Ignoring Classification At The Definition Stage

Defining a role without considering exemption status and employee classification creates downstream legal and financial exposure. Address classification during job analysis.

### Stale Job Descriptions

Allowing job descriptions to drift from reality through neglect, so they no longer reflect the work, the scope, or the requirements. Review and update regularly.

### Physical Requirements As Boilerplate

Pasting generic physical demands that do not reflect the actual job, which can either screen out qualified candidates with disabilities or fail to set accurate expectations. State the real demands.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is the job analysis based on observation, interviews, and workflow analysis of the actual work, rather than a template or a prior incumbent's profile?
- [ ] Are essential functions explicitly identified and distinguished from marginal functions, using the factors that determine essentiality?
- [ ] Are required qualifications separated from preferred qualifications, with each requirement justified by its connection to an essential function?
- [ ] Are competencies defined with observable behavioral indicators, positive and negative, tied to the role's specific context?
- [ ] Are job level, scope, decision authority, and reporting relationships specified and compared to existing roles for internal equity?
- [ ] Are working conditions, physical demands, and environmental factors stated accurately and specifically, reflecting only what the job truly requires?
- [ ] Has the role been assessed for proper classification as employee versus contractor and exempt versus non-exempt, based on actual duties?
- [ ] Is the job description maintained as a living document, reviewed before each hire and updated when the role changes?
- [ ] Can the job description withstand scrutiny as the basis for hiring criteria, accommodation analysis, and performance expectations?
