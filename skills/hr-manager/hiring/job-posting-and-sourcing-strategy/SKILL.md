# Job Posting And Sourcing Strategy

Where you look determines who you find. A job posting is both an advertisement and a legal document: its language signals who is welcome, its placement determines who sees it, and the resulting applicant pool is the raw material from which every selection decision is made. Agents often treat sourcing as a tactical step of posting everywhere and hoping for volume, and miss that biased language deters qualified candidates, that homogeneous sourcing channels produce homogeneous pools, and that a pool built through exclusionary sourcing cannot be cured by a fair selection process downstream.

Use this skill before writing a job posting, choosing sourcing channels, designing referral incentives, or evaluating the diversity and defensibility of an applicant pipeline. The goal is to make the agent attract a broad, qualified, and fairly reached pool through language and channels that do not systematically exclude protected groups.

## Core Rules

### Write Postings That Describe The Work Accurately

A job posting should give candidates enough information to self-assess fit and to make an informed decision about applying. Both overstatement and understatement of the role create problems: inflated postings attract candidates who will be disappointed or leave, while postings that hide demands produce misaligned hires.

A strong posting includes:

- a clear summary of the role's purpose and how it contributes to the organization;
- the essential functions and key responsibilities, in language a candidate can understand;
- required qualifications clearly separated from preferred qualifications;
- work location, schedule, and remote or hybrid arrangements stated explicitly;
- compensation range or band, where required by law or where transparency improves the quality and diversity of the pool;
- benefits highlights that are accurate and not overstated;
- information about the hiring process and timeline;
- an equal opportunity and accommodation statement.

The posting is the candidate's first impression of the organization. Accuracy and transparency build trust and reduce early attrition driven by mismatched expectations.

### Use Inclusive, Neutral Language

Language in a job posting can attract or deter candidates based on characteristics unrelated to the work. Words and phrases that carry demographic connotations signal who belongs and who does not, often without the author's awareness.

Watch for:

- gendered terms that imply a preferred gender, such as "rockstar," "ninja," "dominant," or "competitive" in contexts where they are not job-related;
- age-related language such as "digital native," "recent graduate," "young and energetic," or "seasoned" that signals a preferred age range;
- culturally exclusionary phrases, idioms, or references that assume a specific cultural background;
- unnecessary physical or lifestyle requirements, such as "able-bodied" or demands for social activities that are not essential functions;
- requirements framed as cultural preferences, such as a specific educational pedigree or social background, that serve as proxies for demographic characteristics.

Use neutral, work-focused language. Tools that flag biased terms can help, but the goal is to describe the work and the qualifications, not to project a personality type. Have someone outside the hiring team review the posting for exclusionary signals.

### Choose Sourcing Channels Deliberately

The channels through which you source determine the composition of your applicant pool. Relying on a single channel or on channels that reach only one demographic group produces a pool that lacks diversity, and this lack of diversity at the top of the funnel cannot be fixed by fair selection below.

Diversify sourcing by:

- using multiple channels, including general job boards, industry-specific boards, professional associations, and community organizations;
- including channels that reach underrepresented groups, such as affinity-group job boards, veterans organizations, disability advocacy organizations, and workforce development programs;
- avoiding overreliance on employee referrals as the primary source, because referrals tend to replicate the demographics of the existing workforce;
- considering passive candidate sourcing that reaches people who are not actively searching but may be qualified;
- evaluating the yield from each channel over time, including the diversity and quality of candidates it produces.

The objective is not tokenism but genuine reach: ensuring that qualified candidates from all backgrounds have the opportunity to learn about and apply for the role.

### Manage Referral Programs Carefully

Employee referral programs are efficient and produce candidates who tend to stay longer, but they also replicate the existing demographics of the workforce. When the workforce is not diverse, referrals amplify that homogeneity.

Manage referrals by:

- tracking the demographic composition of referral hires over time;
- supplementing referrals with other sourcing channels rather than relying on referrals alone;
- encouraging referrals from across the workforce, not just from the dominant group;
- being transparent that referrals are one channel among several, not the primary pipeline;
- watching for patterns where referrals consistently come from the same team or demographic group.

If referral hires are significantly less diverse than hires from other channels, the program may be contributing to homogeneity and should be adjusted. This is not about discouraging referrals but about ensuring they do not become a closed network.

### Avoid Practices That Create Adverse Impact In Sourcing

Certain sourcing practices have a disparate impact on protected groups even when no discrimination is intended. Identify and address these proactively.

Practices that can create adverse impact include:

- requiring credentials or experiences that are not job-related and that disproportionately screen out protected groups;
- sourcing exclusively from networks or institutions that serve a narrow demographic;
- using algorithms or automated screening tools that incorporate biased data or proxies for protected characteristics;
- placing postings only in locations or on platforms that reach one demographic group;
- requiring active current employment, which can screen out individuals who are unemployed for reasons connected to protected characteristics;
- conducting closed or invitation-only application processes without a public posting, which limits the pool to those already known.

Analyze the composition of your applicant pool and your hires by source and by demographic group. If the pool or hires from a particular channel are significantly less diverse, investigate and adjust.

### Comply With Posting And Notification Requirements

Many jurisdictions require employers to post jobs publicly, to notify current employees of opportunities, or to meet specific posting and recruitment obligations.

Comply with:

- internal posting requirements, where employees must be notified of openings before external candidates are considered;
- job posting laws that require salary or benefits disclosure in the posting;
- visa and immigration-related recruitment requirements, such as good-faith recruitment efforts before sponsoring a foreign worker;
- government contractor obligations, including affirmative action and outreach requirements;
- recordkeeping requirements for the applicant pool, including who applied, their demographic data where required, and the disposition of each candidate.

Failing to meet posting and notification obligations can create compliance violations and can undermine the defensibility of the hiring process. See the compliance skill for detailed recordkeeping obligations.

### Set Realistic Timelines And Manage Candidate Experience

The speed and transparency of the hiring process signal how the organization values people. A slow, opaque, or disrespectful process deters strong candidates, especially those who have options and those who have experienced bias in hiring before.

Manage the process by:

- setting a target timeline from posting to offer and communicating it;
- keeping candidates informed at each stage, including acknowledgment of application and status updates;
- closing out candidates promptly and respectfully when they are no longer under consideration;
- avoiding ghosting, which damages reputation and can correlate with protected characteristics if the pattern is uneven;
- collecting feedback from candidates on the process and acting on it.

Candidate experience is part of sourcing strategy: candidates who feel respected become ambassadors, and those who feel mistreated share their experience, narrowing your future pool.

### Measure And Adjust Sourcing Effectiveness

Sourcing should be evaluated against outcomes, not just activity. Track what is working and what is not, and adjust.

Measure:

- the volume, quality, and diversity of candidates from each channel;
- the conversion rate from application to hire by source;
- the time and cost per hire by source;
- the retention and performance of hires by source;
- whether the pool reflects the available qualified workforce in the relevant market.

Use data to reallocate effort and budget toward channels that produce diverse, qualified, and durable hires, and away from channels that produce homogeneity or poor outcomes. Sourcing that is not measured cannot be improved.

## Common Traps

### Posting Everywhere Without Strategy

Treating volume as the goal and posting indiscriminately, which produces a large but unqualified or homogeneous pool that is expensive to screen. Choose channels deliberately.

### Biased Language That Deters Qualified Candidates

Using terms that carry demographic connotations, signaling who belongs and who does not, often without awareness. Use neutral, work-focused language.

### Overreliance On Referrals

Allowing referrals to dominate sourcing, which replicates the existing workforce's demographics and narrows the pool to known networks. Diversify channels.

### Exclusionary Channel Selection

Sourcing only from channels that reach one demographic group, producing a pool that lacks diversity from the start. Diversify channels.

### Hidden Compensation That Deters Strong Candidates

Omitting pay range where transparency is required or beneficial, which reduces the quality and diversity of the pool and may violate pay transparency law. Disclose the range.

### Ghosting And Poor Candidate Experience

Failing to communicate with candidates throughout the process, which damages reputation and narrows future sourcing. Keep candidates informed.

### Ignoring Adverse Impact At The Top Of The Funnel

Assuming a fair selection process cures an exclusionary pool, when in fact the composition of the pool constrains every downstream decision. Measure and adjust sourcing.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Does the job posting describe the actual work, separate required from preferred qualifications, state location and schedule, and include required disclosures and statements?
- [ ] Is the posting language neutral and inclusive, free of terms that carry demographic connotations or signal a preferred background?
- [ ] Are sourcing channels diversified to reach qualified candidates across demographic groups, including channels that serve underrepresented populations?
- [ ] Is the referral program managed to avoid amplifying existing workforce homogeneity, with its demographic outcomes tracked?
- [ ] Have sourcing practices been assessed for adverse impact, including credential requirements, channel selection, and automated screening tools?
- [ ] Are posting, notification, and recruitment obligations met, including internal posting, salary transparency, and government contractor requirements?
- [ ] Is the hiring process timely and transparent, with candidates kept informed and treated respectfully at every stage?
- [ ] Is sourcing effectiveness measured by volume, quality, diversity, conversion, cost, and retention, with effort reallocated based on outcomes?
