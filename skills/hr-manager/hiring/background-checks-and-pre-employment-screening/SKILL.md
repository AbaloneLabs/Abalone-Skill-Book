# Background Checks And Pre-Employment Screening

Background checks sit at the intersection of due diligence and privacy law, and they are one of the most heavily regulated areas of hiring. A background check can protect the organization from negligent hiring claims and from real risks, but a check conducted without consent, applied as a blanket exclusion, or used to screen on characteristics unrelated to the job creates legal liability, adverse impact, and the loss of qualified candidates. Agents often treat background checks as a standard final step applied uniformly, and miss that blanket criminal exclusions are unlawful in many jurisdictions, that credit checks require specific justification, and that failing to follow adverse action procedures invalidates the ability to act on findings.

Use this skill before conducting any pre-employment screening, before making a decision based on background findings, or before establishing background check policies. The goal is to make the agent screen only for what is job-related, obtain required consents, apply individualized assessment, and follow the procedural protections that law requires.

## Core Rules

### Screen Only For What Is Job-Related And Justified

Each type of background check should be justified by a specific connection to the role. Conducting every available check on every candidate is both unnecessary and legally risky, because checks that are not job-related create privacy intrusion and adverse impact without corresponding benefit.

Justify each check type:

- criminal history: relevant for roles involving vulnerable populations, financial trust, security, or safety; not relevant as a blanket screen for all roles;
- credit history: relevant for roles with financial responsibility, access to funds, or fiduciary duty; not justified for most other roles and restricted by law in many jurisdictions;
- employment verification: relevant to confirm claimed experience and titles;
- reference checks: relevant to gather performance evidence from prior supervisors;
- education and credential verification: relevant for roles requiring specific qualifications;
- drug testing: relevant for safety-sensitive roles and where required by regulation; restricted or regulated in many jurisdictions;
- social media review: rarely justified, as it exposes protected information and creates discrimination risk.

Document the rationale for each check type used for each role category. If you cannot articulate why this check is needed for this role, do not conduct it.

### Obtain Required Consent And Disclosure

Most background checks, particularly those conducted through a consumer reporting agency, require specific, written consent obtained through a clear and standalone disclosure. Failing to obtain proper consent can result in statutory penalties and the inability to use the findings.

For consumer-report-based checks:

- provide a clear and conspicuous disclosure, in a standalone document, that a consumer report may be obtained;
- obtain written authorization before the check is conducted;
- if an investigative consumer report, including personal interviews with references, is conducted, provide additional disclosure of the nature and scope of the investigation;
- comply with any state-specific requirements for disclosure format, timing, or additional information;
- provide a copy of any consumer rights summary required by law.

The disclosure must not be buried in a lengthy application or combined with other documents in a way that obscures it. Consent obtained improperly is not valid, and acting on a report obtained without valid consent compounds the violation.

### Apply Individualized Assessment To Criminal History

Blanket exclusions based on criminal history are both poor risk management and legally deficient in many jurisdictions. The trend in law and best practice is to require individualized assessment that considers the relationship between the offense and the role.

Individualized assessment considers:

- the nature and gravity of the offense or conduct;
- the time that has elapsed since the offense, conviction, or completion of sentence;
- the nature of the job sought, including the duties, environment, and the risk the offense poses in that context;
- evidence of rehabilitation, such as employment history, education, training, or community involvement since the offense;
- whether the offense bears a direct relationship to the essential functions of the role.

Provide the candidate an opportunity to respond before adverse action is taken, including an opportunity to challenge the accuracy of the record, to provide context, and to present evidence of rehabilitation. A policy that automatically disqualifies anyone with any criminal record cannot be defended and excludes qualified candidates who pose no relevant risk.

### Follow Adverse Action Procedures For Report-Based Decisions

When a decision not to hire is based wholly or partly on a consumer report, specific adverse action procedures must be followed before the decision is finalized. These procedures give the candidate the opportunity to review and correct the report before suffering the adverse consequence.

Follow pre-adverse-action procedures:

- provide the candidate with a copy of the consumer report and a written summary of their rights under the applicable law;
- wait a reasonable period, allowing the candidate to dispute inaccuracies or explain the findings;
- if the decision to reject is maintained, send a formal adverse action notice stating the decision, the reasons, the reporting agency's contact information, and the candidate's right to dispute and to obtain an additional copy of the report.

Skipping or shortcutting these procedures can result in statutory damages and the loss of the ability to rely on the report. The procedures apply even when the report is accurate, because the candidate has the right to provide context before the decision is final.

### Comply With Ban-The-Box And Salary History Laws

An increasing number of jurisdictions restrict when criminal history can be inquired into and prohibit asking about salary history. These laws reshape the timing and content of background screening.

Comply with:

- ban-the-box laws that delay criminal history inquiries until after a conditional offer or later stage in the process;
- salary history bans that prohibit asking about or relying on prior compensation to set pay;
- clean slate laws that limit consideration of sealed, expunged, or old offenses;
- restrictions on credit check use, including the role categories and jurisdictions where they are permitted.

These requirements vary significantly by jurisdiction and change frequently. Maintain awareness of the requirements in every jurisdiction where you hire, and update practices accordingly. A practice that is lawful in one state may be unlawful in another.

### Conduct Reference Checks Professionally And Lawfully

Reference checks can provide valuable performance evidence, but they must be conducted in a way that respects privacy, avoids defamation, and complies with policies and laws.

Conduct references by:

- obtaining the candidate's consent before contacting references, particularly current employers;
- asking job-related questions about performance, strengths, areas for development, and eligibility for rehire;
- avoiding questions that could elicit protected information or that seek opinions on characteristics unrelated to the role;
- documenting the reference's responses accurately and factually;
- recognizing that many employers limit references to dates of employment and title, which reduces but does not eliminate their value;
- using the information as one data point among several, not as a sole determinant.

Reference checks that wander into personal opinions, protected characteristics, or off-topic areas create risk. Stay focused on job-related performance evidence.

### Handle Background Data As Sensitive Personal Data

Background check results contain sensitive personal information, including criminal history, credit data, and medical information from drug tests. This data must be handled with strict access controls and retention limits.

Protect background data by:

- limiting access to those with a legitimate need to know the results for the hiring decision;
- storing results securely and separately from the general personnel file, if the candidate is hired;
- retaining results only for the period required, then securely disposing of them;
- ensuring vendors conducting checks have adequate data security and comply with data protection law;
- never using background data for any purpose other than the hiring decision for which it was obtained.

Background data that is mishandled, over-retained, or accessed by unauthorized personnel is both a privacy violation and a source of legal liability. See the data-privacy skill for the broader framework.

### Never Use Social Media Screening As An Informal Background Check

Reviewing candidates' social media profiles is tempting and dangerous. It exposes the organization to protected information that cannot be unseen, creates discrimination risk, and rarely produces job-related evidence.

The risks of social media screening include:

- exposure to protected characteristics such as race, religion, pregnancy, disability, and political affiliation, which then taints the decision;
- inability to prove that protected information seen on social media did not influence the decision;
- inaccurate or misleading information, including fake profiles or content taken out of context;
- privacy intrusion that damages candidate experience and reputation.

If social media screening is conducted at all, it should be by a third party that redacts protected information and reports only job-related findings, at a late stage in the process, with the candidate's awareness. In most cases, it should not be conducted at all.

## Common Traps

### Blanket Criminal History Exclusions

Automatically disqualifying anyone with any criminal record, which is unlawful in many jurisdictions and excludes qualified candidates who pose no relevant risk. Apply individualized assessment.

### Conducting Every Check On Every Candidate

Running all available background checks regardless of the role, creating unnecessary privacy intrusion and adverse impact. Screen only for what is job-related.

### Improper Or Missing Consent

Failing to provide a standalone disclosure or obtain proper written authorization before conducting a check, invalidating the process. Follow consent requirements precisely.

### Skipping Adverse Action Procedures

Rejecting a candidate based on a report without providing pre-adverse-action notice and an opportunity to dispute, which violates law and risks statutory penalties. Follow the full procedure.

### Ignoring Ban-The-Box And Salary History Laws

Asking about criminal history too early or relying on salary history, in violation of jurisdiction-specific requirements. Stay current with the laws where you hire.

### Social Media Screening As Informal Vetting

Reviewing candidates' personal social media, which exposes protected information and creates discrimination risk that cannot be cured. Avoid or use a redacted third-party process.

### Mishandling Background Data

Storing sensitive background check results in general files with broad access, creating privacy violations and liability. Handle as sensitive data with strict controls.

### Overreliance On References That Say Little

Treating limited reference responses as red flags, when many employers restrict references to dates and title. Interpret limited references in context.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is each type of background check justified by a specific, documented connection to the essential functions of the role?
- [ ] Is required consent obtained through a clear, standalone disclosure before any consumer-report-based check is conducted?
- [ ] Is criminal history assessed through individualized assessment considering nature, time, job relationship, and rehabilitation, rather than blanket exclusion?
- [ ] Are pre-adverse-action and adverse-action procedures followed when a decision is based on a consumer report, including providing the report and an opportunity to dispute?
- [ ] Are ban-the-box, salary history, credit check, and clean slate requirements met for every jurisdiction where hiring occurs?
- [ ] Are reference checks conducted professionally, with consent, focused on job-related performance, and documented factually?
- [ ] Is background check data handled as sensitive personal data, with access limited, stored securely, retained only as long as needed, and disposed of properly?
- [ ] Is social media screening avoided or conducted only through a redacted third-party process at a late stage with candidate awareness?
