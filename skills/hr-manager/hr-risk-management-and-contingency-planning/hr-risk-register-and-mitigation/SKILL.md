# HR Risk Register And Mitigation

An HR risk register is the structured inventory of people-related risks the organization faces, assessed for likelihood and impact, with defined mitigations, owners, and monitoring. Its purpose is to make workforce risk visible and managed rather than latent and reactive. The recurring failure is to produce a register that is comprehensive in appearance but inert in practice — a compliance document completed once, filed, and never used to drive mitigation — so the risks it lists continue to materialize while the register records that they were identified. A second failure is to define risks so generically (key person risk, compliance risk) that they cannot be assessed or mitigated, producing a list that names categories without enabling action. A third is to assess risks once and never revisit, so the register drifts out of alignment with the risks the organization actually faces as conditions change. This skill covers the construction and maintenance of an HR risk register that drives mitigation, and the disciplines that keep it a living instrument rather than a filed document.

Use this skill when building or revising an HR risk register, identifying and assessing people-related risks, defining mitigations and controls, or diagnosing why an existing register fails to drive action. The goal is to make the agent produce a register that surfaces and mitigates real workforce risk, not one that documents risk for compliance.

## Core Rules

### Define Risks Specifically Enough To Assess And Mitigate

A risk defined generically — compliance risk, talent risk — cannot be assessed for likelihood or impact, and cannot be mitigated, because it names a category rather than a risk. Specific definition is the foundation of a usable register.

- Define each risk as a specific event or condition with consequence: not key person risk, but departure of the sole maintainer of the legacy payroll system without documented knowledge; not compliance risk, but misclassification of contractors in a jurisdiction that has increased enforcement.
- State the risk in terms of cause, event, and consequence, because mitigation targets the cause and the consequence, and a risk without a stated mechanism cannot be mitigated at the right point.
- Avoid bundling distinct risks into a single entry, because bundled risks have different likelihoods, impacts, and mitigations, and bundling produces an entry that is accurate for none of them.
- Test each risk definition by asking what specific mitigation would address it; if none can be named, the risk is defined too generically.

### Assess Likelihood And Impact Honestly, Including Low-Probability High-Impact Risks

Risk assessment requires honest estimation of likelihood and impact, including for risks that are low-probability but high-impact, which organizations systematically under-weight because they have not recently materialized.

- Assess likelihood and impact separately and honestly, resisting the optimism that downgrades risks that have not recently occurred, because recency bias produces under-assessment of rare but severe risks.
- Give high-impact low-probability risks the weight their impact demands, because the organization that survives mostly cannot survive the catastrophic tail event, and tail risks are where organizational failure lives.
- Use evidence (incident history, benchmark data, leading indicators) to inform assessment, because gut-feel assessment systematically under-weights unfamiliar risks.
- Re-assess as conditions change, because a risk's likelihood and impact shift with the environment, and a static assessment becomes wrong over time.

### Assign Mitigation Owners With Authority And Accountability

A risk without an owner is a risk without mitigation. Each risk must have a named owner with the authority and accountability to mitigate it, and the owner must be at the right level for the risk's significance.

- Assign a single accountable owner to each risk, because shared ownership is no ownership, and risks without a clear owner are not mitigated.
- Ensure the owner has the authority to implement the mitigation, because an owner without authority is a name on a register rather than a mitigator.
- Match the owner's level to the risk's significance, because assigning a significant risk to a junior owner without escalation authority ensures the risk is not mitigated at the level it requires.
- Distinguish the risk owner (accountable for mitigation) from control owners (responsible for specific controls), because complex risks require multiple controls and a single point of accountability for their aggregate effect.

### Define Mitigations As Controls With Monitoring, Not As Aspirations

A mitigation stated as an aspiration — improve manager training, enhance compliance — is not a mitigation, because it cannot be verified or monitored. Mitigations must be defined as specific controls with monitoring that evidences whether they are working.

- Define each mitigation as a specific control or set of controls, because a control can be implemented, verified, and monitored while an aspiration cannot.
- Define the monitoring that will evidence whether the control is working (audit results, incident rates, training completion, control testing), because a control without monitoring is assumed rather than confirmed.
- Distinguish preventive controls (reduce likelihood), detective controls (surface occurrence), and corrective controls (reduce consequence), because a robust mitigation uses all three.
- Recognize residual risk — the risk that remains after mitigation — explicitly, because every control leaves residual risk, and unacknowledged residual risk is unmanaged risk.

### Prioritize By Risk, Not By Ease Or Familiarity

Organizations systematically over-mitigate familiar, easy risks and under-mitigate unfamiliar, difficult ones, because the familiar and easy are comfortable to address while the unfamiliar and difficult are uncomfortable. Prioritization must follow risk, not comfort.

- Prioritize by likelihood-times-impact, not by ease of mitigation or familiarity, because the purpose is to reduce the largest exposures, not to complete the most comfortable work.
- Be alert to the systematic neglect of difficult, unglamorous, or cross-functional risks, because these are often the highest-impact and the most under-mitigated.
- Re-prioritize as assessments change, because a risk that was low-priority can become high-priority as conditions shift, and static prioritization misses the shift.
- Resist the pressure to show mitigation activity on easy risks as a proxy for risk management, because activity on easy risks does not reduce the risks that matter.

### Maintain The Register As A Living Instrument

A register completed once and filed is a compliance artifact, not a risk management tool. The register must be maintained on a rhythm that reflects the pace at which risks change.

- Review the register on a defined cadence (quarterly for most risks, more frequently for fast-moving ones), because risks change and a static register drifts.
- Trigger off-cycle review when conditions change (regulatory shifts, reorganizations, incidents, market changes), because the events that change risk profiles do not follow the review calendar.
- Capture near-misses and incidents and feed them back into the register, because near-misses are evidence about likelihood and control effectiveness that should update the assessment.
- Retire risks that have been genuinely eliminated and add risks that have emerged, because a register that only grows or only shrinks is not tracking reality.

### Connect The Register To Governance And Decision-Making

A register that does not connect to governance — to the decisions about where to invest mitigation effort, to escalation of risks beyond tolerance, to leadership visibility of the largest exposures — is a document, not a management system.

- Report the highest risks to leadership on a rhythm, because leadership visibility is what secures attention and resources for the largest exposures.
- Define risk tolerance and escalate risks that exceed it, because tolerance defines the boundary beyond which mitigation is mandatory rather than discretionary.
- Connect the register to budget and resource decisions, because mitigation requires resources, and a register disconnected from resourcing produces identified but unmitigated risk.
- Use the register to test whether the organization's mitigation investment matches its risk profile, because misalignment between exposure and investment is a sign the register is not driving decisions.

## Common Traps

### The Static Compliance Document

The register is completed for a compliance deadline, filed, and not revisited until the next deadline, while the risks it lists continue to develop and new risks emerge unrecorded. The trap is that a register maintained for compliance rather than for management becomes stale the day after it is filed, because risks change continuously and a static register describes a past risk profile that no longer matches the present, so the organization relies on a document that is increasingly wrong. The compliance framing also ensures that the register is optimized for completion rather than for mitigation, because the incentive is to have a register, not to reduce risk, and the result is a document that satisfies the requirement while the underlying risks persist or worsen. Maintaining the register on a rhythm, triggered by conditions as well as calendar, is what keeps it a risk management instrument, because a register that does not track changing risk is a register that misleads the organization about its exposure.

### Generic Risk Definitions

The register lists risks as categories — compliance risk, talent risk, reputational risk — without defining the specific events, causes, and consequences each entry represents. The trap is that a category cannot be assessed for likelihood or impact, because it bundles many distinct risks with different profiles, and it cannot be mitigated, because mitigation targets a specific mechanism and a category has no specific mechanism. An entry of compliance risk could mean anything from misclassification to wage-and-hour violation to leave-law non-compliance, each with different likelihood, impact, and mitigation, so the entry is accurate for none of them and actionable for none of them. Defining risks as specific events with stated causes and consequences is what makes them assessable and mitigable, because a risk that cannot be assessed or mitigated is not a managed risk; it is a labeled one.

### Owner Without Authority

Each risk has a named owner, but the owner is junior, lacks budget, or has no authority to implement the mitigation, so the ownership is nominal and the mitigation does not occur. The trap is that nominal ownership satisfies the register's requirement for an accountable party while ensuring that no one with the authority to mitigate is actually accountable, because the seniority and authority to address significant risks sits above the named owner, and the named owner cannot escalate effectively. The register reports that every risk has an owner, which is technically true and practically meaningless, because an owner without authority is a name rather than a mitigator, and the risk persists under the appearance of management. Matching owner level and authority to the risk's significance is what makes ownership real, because accountability without authority is a structure designed to ensure that risks are named but not reduced.

### Aspiration Instead Of Control

Mitigations are stated as aspirations — improve awareness, enhance training, strengthen oversight — rather than as specific controls with monitoring, and the register reports that mitigations are in place while nothing verifiable has been implemented. The trap is that an aspiration cannot be implemented, verified, or monitored, so it produces the appearance of mitigation without the substance, and the risk continues at its pre-mitigation likelihood and impact while the register records that it has been addressed. The organization discovers the gap when the risk materializes and the aspiration provides no protection, because there was no control to detect, prevent, or reduce the consequence, only a stated intention to improve. Defining mitigations as specific controls with monitoring that evidences effectiveness is what makes mitigation real, because a mitigation that cannot be verified is a mitigation that may not exist, and the register's credibility depends on mitigations that are confirmed rather than assumed.

### Over-Mitigating The Familiar, Under-Mitigating The Severe

The organization invests mitigation effort in risks that are familiar, easy, or recently materialized, while severe but unfamiliar or difficult risks remain under-mitigated, because the familiar and easy are comfortable to address and the severe and difficult are uncomfortable. The trap is that this produces a portfolio of mitigation that looks active but is misaligned with the risk profile, because the largest exposures — often cross-functional, unglamorous, or involving difficult conversations — are precisely the ones that are under-addressed, and the organization is busy on the small risks while the large ones develop unmitigated. The misalignment is invisible as long as the severe risks do not materialize, and when they do, the discovery that they were under-mitigated while effort went elsewhere is catastrophic. Prioritizing by likelihood-times-impact, and being alert to the systematic neglect of difficult risks, is what aligns mitigation with exposure, because the purpose of risk management is to reduce the largest exposures, not to complete the most comfortable work.

### Ignoring Residual Risk

The register lists mitigations and treats the risk as addressed, without acknowledging or monitoring the residual risk that remains after mitigation, because every control leaves some exposure. The trap is that unacknowledged residual risk is unmanaged risk, because the organization believes the risk has been addressed when it has only been reduced, and the remaining exposure continues to develop without monitoring or ownership, materializing as a surprise in an area the register recorded as handled. The misalignment between the register's record of mitigation and the actual residual exposure is the gap in which failures grow, because the confidence that mitigation provides displaces the vigilance that residual risk requires. Acknowledging residual risk explicitly, monitoring it, and escalating when it exceeds tolerance is what manages the exposure that mitigation cannot eliminate, because a risk that has been mitigated is not a risk that has been eliminated, and treating them the same produces the surprise that the register was meant to prevent.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Are risks defined as specific events with causes and consequences, rather than generic categories that cannot be assessed or mitigated?
- [ ] Are likelihood and impact assessed honestly, including for low-probability high-impact tail risks, using evidence rather than gut feel?
- [ ] Does each risk have a single accountable owner with the authority and seniority to implement the mitigation?
- [ ] Are mitigations defined as specific controls (preventive, detective, corrective) with monitoring that evidences effectiveness, rather than aspirations?
- [ ] Is residual risk — the exposure remaining after mitigation — explicitly acknowledged, monitored, and escalated when it exceeds tolerance?
- [ ] Is prioritization driven by likelihood-times-impact, with attention to the systematic neglect of difficult, cross-functional, or unglamorous severe risks?
- [ ] Is the register maintained on a rhythm (calendar and event-triggered), with near-misses and incidents fed back to update assessments?
- [ ] Is the register connected to governance — leadership visibility of the highest risks, defined tolerance and escalation, and alignment with mitigation investment?
