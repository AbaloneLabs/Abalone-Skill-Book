# Workplace Accessibility And Universal Design

Reactive accommodation addresses barriers one employee at a time, after the barrier has caused harm. Universal design and proactive accessibility address barriers at the source, making the workplace usable by everyone, reducing the need for individual accommodation, and signaling that disability inclusion is a design principle rather than an exception process. The agent must understand that inaccessible workplaces create hidden costs: accommodation requests that could have been avoided, talent that is turned away, and legal exposure under accessibility laws. Building accessibility into facilities, technology, and processes is both a compliance strategy and a talent strategy.

Use this skill when assessing or improving workplace accessibility. The goal is to make the agent identify and remove barriers proactively, comply with accessibility standards, and embed accessibility into workplace design.

## Core Rules

### Treat Accessibility As Proactive Design, Not Reactive Exception

The shift from reactive accommodation to proactive accessibility changes the question from "how do we accommodate this individual?" to "how do we build a workplace that works for everyone?"

- Assess facilities, technology, and processes for accessibility before an accommodation request forces the issue.
- Design new facilities, systems, and processes to accessibility standards from the outset, rather than retrofitting later.
- Recognize that proactive accessibility reduces the volume and burden of individual accommodation requests.
- Recognize that accessible design benefits employees beyond those with disabilities, including aging employees, those with temporary injuries, and those with situational limitations.

### Assess Physical Accessibility Comprehensively

Physical accessibility extends beyond wheelchair access to the full range of mobility, sensory, and cognitive needs. Assess comprehensively.

- Assess building access: entrances, routes, doorways, elevators, and emergency egress, including for mobility devices.
- Assess workspaces: desk height, adjustability, clear floor space, and reach ranges.
- Assess restrooms, break rooms, and common areas for accessibility.
- Assess signage, wayfinding, and emergency notification systems for visual and auditory accessibility.
- Assess parking, transportation access, and drop-off areas.
- Use accessibility checklists and, ideally, consult users with disabilities or accessibility specialists.

### Ensure Digital Accessibility

Digital accessibility, ensuring that technology, applications, and content are usable by employees with disabilities, is increasingly critical as work depends on digital tools. Inaccessible technology is both a barrier and a legal risk.

- Ensure internal applications, intranets, and tools meet accessibility standards, such as the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines, adapted for internal use.
- Ensure employee-facing content, documents, presentations, and training, is accessible, including alternative text for images, captioning for video, and readable document structure.
- Assess third-party tools and software for accessibility before procurement, building accessibility requirements into vendor selection.
- Provide accessible alternatives where tools cannot be made fully accessible.
- Test with assistive technologies, such as screen readers and voice control, and involve users with disabilities in testing.

### Build Accessibility Into Procurement And Design Processes

Accessibility is most cost-effective when built in from the start, rather than retrofitted. Embed accessibility into procurement and design.

- Include accessibility requirements in requests for proposals and vendor contracts.
- Require vendors to demonstrate accessibility compliance and provide accessibility documentation.
- Build accessibility review into the design and development lifecycle for internal systems.
- Assign accessibility responsibility to specific roles, rather than assuming it will be handled.

### Address Procedural And Process Accessibility

Accessibility extends beyond physical and digital to the processes and procedures that govern work. Inaccessible processes create barriers even when facilities and technology are accessible.

- Ensure application and hiring processes are accessible, including application systems, assessments, and interviews.
- Ensure training and onboarding are accessible, including materials, delivery methods, and assessments.
- Ensure communication processes are accessible, including meetings, presentations, and written communication.
- Ensure performance management processes are accessible, including goal-setting, feedback, and evaluation methods.

### Comply With Accessibility Laws And Standards

Accessibility is governed by laws and standards that set minimum requirements. Compliance is the floor, not the ceiling.

- Identify the accessibility laws that apply, including those governing physical accessibility, digital accessibility, and employment.
- Apply the relevant accessibility standards, such as building accessibility standards and digital accessibility guidelines.
- Understand that compliance with minimum standards does not guarantee usability; aim for genuine accessibility, not technical compliance.
- Stay current with evolving standards, particularly in digital accessibility.

### Maintain Accessibility Over Time

Accessibility degrades over time as facilities are modified, technology is updated, and processes change. Maintain accessibility through ongoing attention.

- Reassess accessibility when facilities are renovated, technology is changed, or processes are redesigned.
- Build accessibility review into change management, so that changes do not introduce new barriers.
- Provide accessibility training to those who design, procure, and maintain facilities, technology, and processes.
- Establish a process for employees to report accessibility barriers and track resolution.

### Involve Users With Disabilities

The most effective accessibility assessments involve the people who experience the barriers. Involve users with disabilities in assessment, design, and testing.

- Consult employees with disabilities, through accommodation feedback, accessibility committees, or surveys.
- Involve users with disabilities in testing facilities, technology, and processes.
- Treat accessibility feedback as expert input, not as complaint, and respond constructively.
- Compensate employees for accessibility consultation that goes beyond their role, recognizing the expertise they provide.

## Common Traps

### Waiting For Accommodation Requests

Addressing barriers only when an individual requests accommodation, rather than proactively. Assess and design for accessibility.

### Compliance Over Usability

Meeting technical standards without ensuring genuine usability, leaving barriers in place. Aim for real accessibility.

### Ignoring Digital Accessibility

Focusing on physical accessibility while neglecting inaccessible technology, applications, and content. Address digital accessibility.

### Procuring Inaccessible Tools

Purchasing software and systems without accessibility requirements, inheriting barriers from vendors. Build accessibility into procurement.

### One-Time Assessment

Treating accessibility as a one-time project rather than an ongoing practice, allowing barriers to re-emerge. Maintain accessibility over time.

### Designing Without Users

Assessing and designing accessibility without involving users with disabilities, missing real barriers. Involve users.

### Process Barriers

Making facilities and technology accessible while leaving hiring, training, or management processes inaccessible. Address procedural accessibility.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is accessibility treated as proactive design, reducing the need for reactive accommodation?
- [ ] Is physical accessibility assessed comprehensively, covering mobility, sensory, and cognitive needs?
- [ ] Is digital accessibility ensured for applications, content, and tools, including third-party procurement?
- [ ] Are accessibility requirements embedded in procurement and design processes?
- [ ] Are hiring, training, communication, and management processes accessible?
- [ ] Does the workplace comply with applicable accessibility laws and standards, aiming for usability, not just compliance?
- [ ] Is accessibility maintained over time through change management and reassessment?
- [ ] Are users with disabilities involved in assessment, design, and testing?
