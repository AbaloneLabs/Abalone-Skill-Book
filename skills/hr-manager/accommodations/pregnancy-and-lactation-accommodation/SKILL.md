# Pregnancy And Lactation Accommodation

Pregnancy accommodation has emerged as a distinct legal domain, with federal and state laws requiring employers to provide accommodations for pregnancy, childbirth, and related conditions, often without the need to prove disability. Lactation accommodation is separately required. The agent must understand that pregnancy is not merely a condition to be tolerated but one that the employer must affirmatively accommodate, that lactation support is a non-negotiable legal requirement, and that pregnancy discrimination, in hiring, assignments, evaluation, or termination, persists as a significant liability, often driven by assumptions about pregnant employees' commitment or capacity.

Use this skill when handling pregnancy or lactation accommodation. The goal is to make the agent provide required accommodations, support nursing employees, and prevent pregnancy discrimination.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Affirmative Duty To Accommodate Pregnancy

Pregnancy accommodation laws require employers to provide reasonable accommodations for limitations related to pregnancy, childbirth, or related conditions, without requiring the employee to prove that pregnancy is a disability.

- The duty is triggered by known pregnancy-related limitations, even without a formal request.
- Common accommodations include: seating, more frequent or longer breaks, modified duties, assistance with manual labor, temporary transfer to a less strenuous position, schedule changes, and time off.
- Do not require medical documentation for obvious accommodations, such as more frequent breaks or seating; require documentation only for less obvious or extended accommodations, and only what is needed.
- Do not force the employee onto leave when an accommodation would allow continued work.

### Provide Lactation Accommodation As Required

Lactation accommodation, providing reasonable break time and a private space for nursing employees, is required by law and is essential for supporting nursing parents returning to work.

- Provide reasonable break time for lactation, as frequently as needed by the nursing employee, for up to the period required by law, typically one year after birth, and longer in some jurisdictions.
- Provide a private space, not a bathroom, that is shielded from view and free from intrusion.
- Ensure the space is accessible, clean, and functional, with a chair, a flat surface, and an electrical outlet, and ideally a refrigerator and a sink nearby.
- If the space is shared, ensure it is available when needed, through scheduling or priority access.
- Compensate lactation breaks as working time if the employee is not fully relieved of duty, or according to the applicable rules for break time.

### Address Pregnancy-Related Limitations Promptly

Pregnancy involves a range of limitations beyond the pregnancy itself, including morning sickness, medical appointments, high-risk pregnancy, postpartum recovery, and related conditions. Address these promptly and supportively.

- Accommodate medical appointment attendance without requiring the employee to use PTO or make up the time punitively.
- Accommodate limitations such as lifting restrictions, the need to sit, or dietary needs, through modified duties or equipment.
- Address related conditions, such as gestational diabetes or postpartum depression, through the accommodation and leave frameworks.
- Do not treat pregnancy-related limitations as performance issues or as signs of reduced commitment.

### Prevent Pregnancy Discrimination In Employment Decisions

Pregnancy discrimination, treating an employee adversely because of pregnancy, childbirth, or related conditions, is prohibited in all aspects of employment. It is often driven by assumptions about the employee's commitment, capacity, or future availability.

- Do not make hiring decisions based on actual or potential pregnancy, including asking about pregnancy or family plans in interviews.
- Do not make assignment decisions based on assumptions about what a pregnant employee can handle or about future availability.
- Evaluate pregnant employees based on actual performance, not on assumptions about their focus, energy, or commitment.
- Do not pass over pregnant employees for promotion, training, or high-visibility projects based on assumptions about their leave or return.
- Do not terminate, lay off, or demote based on pregnancy, or on the leave or accommodation needs it generates.

### Manage The Interaction With Leave And Disability Law

Pregnancy, childbirth, and related conditions may qualify for medical leave, disability accommodation, and parental leave, each of which interacts with pregnancy accommodation. Coordinate these frameworks.

- Provide pregnancy accommodation first, to enable continued work; do not default to leave when accommodation is possible.
- When the employee needs leave, for childbirth or serious health conditions, administer it under the applicable leave law.
- For conditions that constitute a disability, such as pregnancy-related impairments that substantially limit a major life activity, engage in the disability accommodation interactive process.
- Coordinate the frameworks so that the employee receives the most protective treatment available.

### Train Managers On Pregnancy And Lactation Support

Managers are critical to effective pregnancy and lactation accommodation. Untrained managers, operating on assumptions or discomfort, create liability and drive talent loss.

- Train managers on the affirmative duty to accommodate pregnancy and on the prohibition of pregnancy discrimination.
- Train managers on lactation accommodation requirements and on supporting nursing employees without making them feel burdensome.
- Train managers to recognize and route accommodation requests, and to avoid assumptions about pregnant employees' capacity or commitment.
- Hold managers accountable for the treatment and advancement of pregnant employees and new parents.

### Maintain Confidentiality And Prevent Retaliation

Pregnancy and lactation information is sensitive and must be handled confidentially. Employees who request accommodation are protected from retaliation.

- Keep pregnancy and lactation information confidential, sharing only with those who need to know.
- Treat accommodation requests as protected activity; do not penalize the employee for requesting or using accommodation.
- Monitor the treatment of pregnant employees and nursing parents for signs of bias or retaliation.
- Address any adverse treatment promptly, as a serious violation.

## Common Traps

### Requiring Disability Proof For Pregnancy

Demanding that the employee prove pregnancy is a disability before providing obvious accommodations. Accommodate known limitations.

### Forcing Leave Over Accommodation

Defaulting to leave when modified duties or schedule changes would allow continued work. Accommodate first.

### Inadequate Lactation Space Or Time

Providing a bathroom or inadequate space, limiting break frequency or duration, or making the employee feel burdensome. Provide proper space and time.

### Assumption-Driven Decisions

Making hiring, assignment, or promotion decisions based on assumptions about pregnant employees' commitment or future availability. Base decisions on performance.

### Passing Over Pregnant Employees

Excluding pregnant employees from opportunities based on their leave or accommodation needs. Preserve opportunities.

### No Manager Training

Leaving managers to handle pregnancy and lactation based on assumptions or discomfort. Train managers.

### Retaliation For Accommodation

Penalizing employees who request or use accommodation, or allowing bias to affect their treatment. Prevent retaliation.
