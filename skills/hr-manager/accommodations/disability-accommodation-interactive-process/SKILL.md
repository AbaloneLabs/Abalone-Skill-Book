# Disability Accommodation Interactive Process

The duty to provide reasonable accommodation for disabilities is an affirmative obligation: the employer must engage in a good-faith interactive process to identify and implement an accommodation that enables the employee to perform the essential functions, unless it would impose an undue hardship. The most common failures are not recognizing an accommodation request, demanding more medical information than necessary, denying accommodations based on assumptions rather than analysis, or failing to engage in genuine dialogue. The agent must understand that the interactive process is a conversation, not a formality, that the employer's preference for how to accommodate is not controlling, and that the duty is ongoing, not satisfied by a one-time response.

Use this skill when handling disability accommodation requests. The goal is to make the agent engage in an effective interactive process, identify reasonable accommodations, and meet the organization's obligations.

## Core Rules

### Recognize That A Request Need Not Use Legal Terms

An accommodation request does not require the employee to mention the law, use the word accommodation, or provide a diagnosis. A request is any communication that suggests a disability-related need for adjustment.

- Recognize requests in statements such as "I'm having trouble with..." or "I need a different chair because of my back" or "My medication makes it hard to come in early."
- Train managers to recognize and route requests, as they are often the first point of contact.
- When in doubt whether a statement is a request, treat it as one and initiate the interactive process.

### Engage In A Genuine Interactive Process

The interactive process is a collaborative dialogue to identify the limitation and the potential accommodations. It is not a form to be completed or a hurdle to be cleared.

- Initiate the process promptly after a request is received.
- Meet with the employee to understand the limitation, the impact on job performance, and the accommodations the employee believes would help.
- Approach the process with an open mind, not with a predetermined outcome.
- Document the discussions, the options considered, and the decisions.
- The process may involve multiple rounds of discussion, particularly if the first accommodation does not work.

### Request Medical Documentation Within Proper Limits

The employer may request medical documentation to understand the limitation and identify accommodations, but the request must be limited to what is needed.

- Request documentation of the functional limitation and the need for accommodation, not the diagnosis or full medical history.
- Specify what information is needed: the nature of the limitation, how it affects the essential functions, the expected duration, and the suggested accommodations.
- Provide the employee with the job description, including essential functions, so the healthcare provider can assess the impact.
- If the documentation is insufficient, specify what additional information is needed and give the employee an opportunity to provide it.
- Do not demand independent medical examinations unless the documentation is inadequate and an exam is job-related and consistent with business necessity.

### Identify The Essential Functions Accurately

Accommodation is about enabling the employee to perform the essential functions, not all functions. Misidentifying marginal functions as essential leads to denying accommodations that should be granted.

- Identify the essential functions based on: whether the position exists to perform that function, the time spent on it, the consequences of not requiring it, the experience of past incumbents, and the work of others in similar roles.
- Distinguish essential functions, which must be performed, from marginal functions, which can be reassigned or eliminated.
- Do not inflate the essential functions to defeat an accommodation request.
- Document the essential functions in the job description, prepared before the accommodation analysis, not retrofitted to it.

### Identify And Implement Reasonable Accommodations

A reasonable accommodation is one that enables the employee to perform the essential functions without imposing an undue hardship. The employer must identify and implement an effective accommodation, though it may choose among effective options.

- Consider accommodations in categories: modifications to the work environment or schedule, modification or reassignment of marginal functions, acquisition or modification of equipment, adjustment of policies, reassignment to a vacant position, and leave.
- The employer may choose among effective accommodations; it is not required to provide the employee's preferred accommodation if another is effective.
- The employer is not required to remove an essential function, lower production standards, or excuse a violation of conduct rules.
- Implement the chosen accommodation promptly and communicate it clearly.
- Assess effectiveness after implementation; if the accommodation is not working, return to the interactive process.

### Assess Undue Hardship With Real Evidence

An accommodation that imposes an undue hardship is not required. But undue hardship is a high bar, requiring significant difficulty or expense, and must be assessed with real evidence, not assertion.

- Assess undue hardship based on the nature and cost of the accommodation, the resources and size of the employer, and the impact on operations.
- A claim of undue hardship must be supported by evidence, such as cost data, operational impact, or disruption to other employees.
- Disruption to other employees, such as increased workload or schedule changes, can contribute to undue hardship but must be significant, not merely inconvenient.
- Do not claim undue hardship reflexively to avoid providing an accommodation; document the analysis.

### Consider Reassignment As A Last Resort

If no accommodation enables the employee to perform the essential functions of their current role, reassignment to a vacant position may be required.

- Reassignment is considered only after accommodation in the current role is not possible.
- The employee must be qualified for the vacant position, with or without accommodation.
- Reassignment is to a vacant position, not to a position held by someone else, and typically to an equivalent position if one is vacant.
- The employee is not entitled to the reassignment of their choice but to a vacant position for which they are qualified.

### Manage Ongoing And Changing Accommodation Needs

Accommodation needs change over time. The duty is ongoing, not satisfied by a one-time accommodation.

- Check in periodically with the employee to assess whether the accommodation remains effective.
- Respond to requests for changed accommodations through the interactive process.
- Recognize that conditions fluctuate; an accommodation that worked may need adjustment.
- Do not assume that an accommodation, once granted, is permanent and unchangeable, nor that it can be revoked without renewed analysis.

### Maintain Confidentiality

Medical information obtained through the interactive process must be kept confidential and stored separately from the personnel file.

- Store medical documentation in confidential medical files, separate from the personnel file.
- Share information only with those who need to know to implement the accommodation, such as the employee's manager, who needs to know the accommodation but not the underlying diagnosis.
- Do not disclose the disability or the medical details to coworkers; explain the accommodation, if necessary, in terms that protect privacy.

## Common Traps

### Missing The Request Because It Did Not Sound Like One

The most common accommodation failure is not recognizing that a request was made, because employees rarely invoke the law or the word "accommodation" — they say "I'm struggling with the stairs" or "my back needs a better chair" or "my medication makes mornings hard," and managers hear a complaint or a performance issue rather than a request that triggers legal obligations. The trap is that the statement sounds conversational, so it is treated conversationally, but the legal duty attaches the moment the employer is on notice of a disability-related need, and failing to recognize the request means the interactive process never begins, the clock on the obligation runs, and the organization is later unable to show it engaged. Train managers to recognize that any statement connecting a medical condition to a work difficulty is a request, route it immediately, and when in doubt treat it as a request, because the cost of engaging unnecessarily is trivial and the cost of missing a request is liability for a process that never started.

### Predetermined Denial Disguised As A Process

Approaching the interactive process with the decision already made — treating the meeting as a formality to deliver a "no" — is a failure to engage in good faith that is independent of whether the denial was substantively correct, because the duty is to engage genuinely with an open mind, not to run a process whose outcome was predetermined. The trap is that the manager already believes the accommodation is infeasible, so the "process" is conducted to confirm that belief, but a court reviews whether alternatives were genuinely considered, and a process that considered only the option the manager wanted to reject is not a process at all. Enter the interactive process genuinely open to outcomes, document real consideration of multiple alternatives, and let the analysis drive the decision, because a predetermined denial dressed as a process is worse than no process — it shows the organization knew the obligation and performed around it.

### Overbroad Medical Requests That Invade Privacy

Demanding a diagnosis, treatment plan, or full medical history when the employer needs only the functional limitation and its impact on essential functions — "we need your complete medical records to evaluate this" — exceeds the employer's rights, invades the employee's privacy, and can itself constitute a violation, because the employer may request only the information needed to understand the limitation and identify accommodations. The trap is that more information feels safer and more thorough, but overbroad requests chill employees from seeking accommodation, collect protected medical information the employer has no right to, and create records that become evidence of overreach. Request only functional information — the nature of the limitation, how it affects essential functions, the expected duration, and suggested accommodations — and specify exactly what is needed, because the right to information is limited to what is necessary, and collecting more is itself a failure.

### Inflating Essential Functions To Defeat The Accommodation

Defining marginal or occasional functions as "essential" so that the employee cannot perform them and the accommodation can be denied — inflating the job description after the request to manufacture infeasibility — is a manipulation of the essential functions analysis that courts and agencies recognize and penalize, because essential functions must reflect the actual job, not a description built to defeat accommodation. The trap is that the inflated description feels accurate to a manager who genuinely believes the function matters, but the analysis examines whether the position exists to perform that function, the time spent, and the experience of past incumbents, and a retrofitted description does not survive that scrutiny. Identify essential functions based on the actual job, documented in the job description before the accommodation analysis, and never retrofit the description to the request, because an inflated description is evidence that the organization manipulated the analysis to reach a predetermined denial.

### Reflexive Undue Hardship Claims Without Evidence

Claiming "undue hardship" reflexively whenever an accommodation seems inconvenient or costly — without documented analysis of cost relative to resources, operational impact, or alternatives — fails the legal standard, which requires significant difficulty or expense assessed in context, because undue hardship is a demanding standard, not a synonym for "inconvenient." The trap is that the cost or disruption feels significant to the manager facing it, but the standard is relative to the employer's overall resources, and what feels like hardship to a department is often trivial to the organization, so the reflexive claim is exposed as unsupported assertion. Assess undue hardship with real evidence — cost data, operational analysis, resource comparison — and document it as if for review, because an undue hardship claim without evidence is an assertion, and assertions do not meet the standard.

### Choosing An Ineffective Accommodation The Employer Prefers

Selecting the accommodation the employer finds easiest or cheapest, rather than one that actually enables the employee to perform the essential functions, leaves the employee unable to work and constitutes a failure to accommodate, because the duty is to provide an effective accommodation, not merely any accommodation, and the employer's preference does not control if the chosen option does not work. The trap is that the preferred option satisfies the employer's sense of having "done something," but an ineffective accommodation is no accommodation — it leaves the barrier in place while creating the appearance of compliance. Choose an accommodation that actually enables performance, assess effectiveness after implementation, and return to the interactive process if it is not working, because providing an accommodation that does not work is providing no accommodation at all, and the appearance of compliance without effectiveness is the most dangerous kind of failure.

### Failing To Consider Reassignment When It Is Available

Denying accommodation outright when no accommodation in the current role works, without considering reassignment to a vacant position for which the employee is qualified, skips a required step in the accommodation analysis, because reassignment is a form of accommodation that must be considered as a last resort when accommodation in the current role is not possible. The trap is that reassignment feels like a burden or a precedent, so it is dismissed without analysis, but the duty to consider reassignment is well-established, and a failure to consider it is a failure to complete the interactive process. Consider reassignment when accommodation in the current role is not feasible, identify vacant positions for which the employee is qualified, and document the analysis, because skipping reassignment is skipping a required step, and a denial that did not consider reassignment is incomplete.

### Treating Accommodation As A One-Time Event

Treating the accommodation as permanent and unchangeable, or revoking it without renewed analysis, ignores that the duty is ongoing and that conditions fluctuate — an accommodation that worked may stop working, a condition may improve, or new accommodations may become available, and the interactive process continues throughout the employment. The trap is that the accommodation feels resolved once implemented, so it is filed away, but conditions change, and an unreviewed accommodation may become ineffective, or a revoked accommodation may have been revoked without the analysis the original decision required. Check in periodically, respond to requests for changed accommodations through the interactive process, and never revoke an accommodation without renewed analysis, because the duty does not end at implementation, and an accommodation treated as permanent is an accommodation that stops being effective without anyone noticing.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Are accommodation requests recognized even when they do not use legal terms, and are managers trained to route them?
- [ ] Is the interactive process genuine, prompt, and documented, with an open mind to outcomes?
- [ ] Is medical documentation requested within proper limits, focused on functional limitations, not diagnosis?
- [ ] Are essential functions identified accurately, distinguishing them from marginal functions?
- [ ] Is a reasonable, effective accommodation identified and implemented, with the employer choosing among effective options?
- [ ] Is undue hardship assessed with real evidence, not reflexively claimed?
- [ ] Is reassignment to a vacant position considered when accommodation in the current role is not possible?
- [ ] Are ongoing and changing needs managed, and is medical information kept confidential?
