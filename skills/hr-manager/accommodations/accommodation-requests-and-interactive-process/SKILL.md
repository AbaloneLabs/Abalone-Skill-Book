# Accommodation Requests And The Interactive Process

Accommodation is the legal and ethical obligation to adjust work, tools, schedules, or environment so that a qualified person with a disability, religious belief, pregnancy, or related condition can perform the essential functions of their role. It is also one of the most mishandled areas of HR, because the obligation is not to grant a specific requested accommodation but to engage in a good-faith interactive process to find an effective one. Agents often treat accommodation as a yes-or-no decision on the employee's first request and miss that refusing to engage, demanding excessive medical detail, or denying without exploring alternatives are themselves violations, independent of whether any accommodation was ultimately owed.

Use this skill before responding to any accommodation request, running the interactive process, assessing undue hardship, or making an accommodation decision. The goal is to make the agent engage the process properly, explore options in good faith, base decisions on evidence, and document the reasoning. This skill is decision support, not legal advice; route complex or contested cases to employment counsel.

## Core Rules

### Recognize The Request Early, Without Requiring Magic Words

An accommodation request does not need to use the word accommodation, cite a statute, or come with a medical certificate. The obligation is triggered when the employer knows or should know that a medical, religious, or pregnancy-related need may require adjustment.

Recognize requests from signals such as:

- an employee stating they need a change due to a health condition, injury, or treatment;
- a statement that a religious belief, practice, or observance conflicts with a work requirement;
- a pregnancy-related limitation or need for adjustment;
- a request for equipment, schedule, or modification to perform the job;
- medical documentation submitted in connection with attendance or performance issues;
- observable changes in capability following injury, illness, or treatment;
- a candidate requesting adjustment to the interview or testing process.

Train managers to recognize these signals and route them to HR promptly, because the duty attaches when the organization knows, not when a formal request is logged. Missing or ignoring the signal does not avoid the obligation; it creates a failure-to-engage violation.

### Confirm The Employee Is Qualified And Identify The Essential Functions

Accommodation is owed to a qualified individual: someone who can perform the essential functions of the role, with or without accommodation. The essential functions analysis defines the scope of what must be accommodated and what can be required.

Determine:

- the essential functions of the role, based on job analysis, not assumption, considering time spent, consequences of non-performance, and whether the function is fundamental or marginal;
- whether the employee can perform those essential functions with or without accommodation;
- whether the employee meets the qualification standards for the role.

If a function is marginal rather than essential, removing or modifying it may be the accommodation. If a function is truly essential and cannot be performed even with accommodation, the employee may not be qualified, but this conclusion must be evidence-based and reached only after genuine exploration, not assumed at the outset to deny the request.

### Request Only The Medical Information Needed To Respond

Medical information in accommodation is a tool to understand the need and evaluate options, not a license to investigate the employee's health. Over-collection violates privacy and can itself be a violation.

Request only:

- confirmation that an impairment or condition exists, where not obvious;
- how the condition affects the ability to perform specific job functions;
- what accommodation may address the limitation;
- the expected duration, if temporary or fluctuating.

Do not request a diagnosis beyond what is necessary, a full medical record, or detailed treatment information. Use the proper forms or a focused inquiry. Store medical information separately from the personnel file with restricted access. If the condition or need is obvious, such as a visible mobility impairment, do not demand documentation of the obvious.

### Engage The Interactive Process In Good Faith

The interactive process is the core obligation. It is a dialogue to identify the barrier and find an effective accommodation. The employer must participate genuinely, not go through the motions to reach a pre-decided denial.

Engage the process by:

- responding promptly to the request and acknowledging the obligation;
- meeting with the employee to understand the limitation and the specific barrier;
- discussing the employee's requested accommodation and their reasoning;
- identifying and evaluating alternative accommodations;
- consulting resources: medical providers, accommodation experts, vocational specialists, and the employee's own input;
- assessing each option against effectiveness, essential functions, and undue hardship;
- communicating the decision and the reasoning;
- documenting the process throughout.

The process may conclude that no effective accommodation exists without undue hardship, but that conclusion must follow genuine exploration. A refusal to engage, or a denial issued before alternatives are considered, is a violation regardless of the ultimate answer.

### Evaluate Effectiveness, Not Preference

The obligation is to provide an effective accommodation, not the employee's preferred accommodation or the employer's most convenient one. Effectiveness is the standard.

An accommodation is effective when it:

- enables the employee to perform the essential functions of the role;
- provides meaningful equal opportunity and access;
- addresses the specific limitation identified.

If the employee's preferred option is effective, it should generally be provided unless it imposes undue hardship. If an alternative is equally effective and less burdensome, it may be chosen, but the employee's preference carries weight and should not be overridden for mere convenience. If the employer's proposed alternative is not actually effective, it does not satisfy the obligation. Document why a chosen accommodation is effective and why a rejected one was not.

### Assess Undue Hardship With Real Evidence

Undue hardship is the defense that excuses the employer from a particular accommodation, but it requires evidence, not assertion. The standard considers cost, disruption, operational impact, and the employer's resources, among other factors.

Assess undue hardship by considering:

- the cost of the accommodation relative to the employer's size and resources, including available funding or tax credits;
- the impact on operations, workflow, other employees, and customers;
- whether the accommodation is unduly disruptive, extensive, or fundamentally alters the nature of the operation;
- whether it poses a direct threat to health or safety that cannot be eliminated;
- the cumulative impact if the same accommodation were granted repeatedly;
- whether less burdensome alternatives exist.

Hardship is not mere inconvenience, minor cost, or manager preference. It is a significant difficulty or expense. A claim of undue hardship that is not supported by analysis will not hold, and denying an accommodation on unsupported hardship grounds is a violation. Document the hardship analysis with specifics.

### Consider The Full Range Of Accommodation Types

Accommodation is broader than equipment or schedule changes. Consider the full range, since the right option may not be the first one considered.

Types include:

- modifications to facilities, equipment, or software;
- schedule changes, modified hours, remote work, or flexible breaks;
- reassignment to a vacant position when accommodation in the current role is not possible;
- modification of policies, such as allowing a beard, head covering, or schedule exception;
- job restructuring that reallocates marginal functions;
- leave as an accommodation when other options are ineffective;
- modified duty or transitional return after injury or illness;
- adjustment of performance metrics or methods that do not alter essential functions.

Reassignment to a vacant role is a last-resort accommodation when no accommodation in the current role works; it is not a first option, and the vacant role must be one the employee is qualified to perform. Leave as an accommodation may overlap with protected medical leave but is a distinct analysis; coordinate with the leave skill.

### Handle Religion And Pregnancy Accommodations Distinctly

While the interactive process framework is similar, religion and pregnancy accommodations have specific characteristics.

For religion:

- the definition of religion and belief is broad, covering sincerely held beliefs and practices, not only organized religion;
- the undue hardship standard has historically been lower than for disability in some jurisdictions, though this is evolving;
- the employer must generally accommodate unless the burden is more than de minimis, though recent legal developments are tightening this;
- the employer should not question the sincerity or centrality of the belief without genuine, evidence-based reason.

For pregnancy and related conditions:

- accommodations may include seating, breaks, schedule modification, lifting limits, and leave;
- some jurisdictions require accommodation of pregnancy without the employee proving impairment, treating pregnancy itself as a condition to be accommodated;
- lactation accommodation, including time and private space, is separately required and is not discretionary.

Apply the correct standard for each category. Do not apply disability frameworks rigidly to religion or pregnancy where the law differs.

### Document The Process And The Decision

The accommodation file is the evidence that the organization engaged properly and decided on a sound basis. It must be built during the process.

Maintain:

- the request as received and when;
- the essential functions analysis;
- the medical or supporting information obtained, stored separately;
- the interactive process: meetings, options discussed, resources consulted;
- the effectiveness analysis for each option;
- the undue hardship analysis where relied upon;
- the decision and the reasoning;
- the implementation steps and any follow-up;
- the review or re-evaluation trigger if circumstances change.

Accommodation is not always permanent. Conditions, roles, and effectiveness change, so build in review. An accommodation that worked may need adjustment, and a denied request may need revisiting if circumstances shift. Coordinate with the leave skill when accommodation and leave overlap, and the performance skill when accommodation affects performance expectations.

## Common Traps

### Failing To Recognize The Request

Waiting for a formal request or specific language before engaging misses the obligation, which attaches when the employer knows or should know. Train managers to recognize signals.

### Refusing To Engage The Interactive Process

A flat denial without dialogue, exploration, or alternatives is a failure-to-engage violation, regardless of whether accommodation was ultimately owed.

### Demanding Excessive Medical Information

Requesting diagnosis, full records, or treatment details beyond what is needed violates privacy and can itself be a violation. Request only what is necessary.

### Denying Based On Assumption Rather Than Analysis

Assuming the employee cannot perform, or that an accommodation would not work, without genuine exploration and evidence. Investigate and evaluate before concluding.

### Confusing Preference With Effectiveness

Rejecting an effective accommodation because it is not the employer's preference, or forcing a less effective alternative for convenience. Effectiveness, not preference, is the standard.

### Claiming Undue Hardship Without Evidence

Asserting hardship based on inconvenience, minor cost, or manager discomfort, without the analysis and evidence the standard requires. Unsupported hardship claims fail.

### Overlooking Reassignment As A Last Resort

Failing to consider a vacant role when accommodation in the current position is not possible, or treating reassignment as a first option when other accommodations remain unexplored.

### Treating Accommodation As One-Time And Permanent

Circumstances change. An accommodation that was effective may need revision, and a denied request may become viable. Build in review.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Was the accommodation request recognized promptly from the signals, without requiring formal language or a medical certificate?
- [ ] Are the essential functions of the role identified through job analysis, distinguishing essential from marginal functions?
- [ ] Is medical information requested only as necessary, focused on the limitation and the accommodation, and stored separately with restricted access?
- [ ] Is the interactive process engaged in good faith, with prompt response, dialogue, option identification, and resource consultation?
- [ ] Is each accommodation option evaluated for effectiveness against the essential functions, with the employee's preference given appropriate weight?
- [ ] Is undue hardship assessed with real evidence on cost, disruption, operational impact, and alternatives, not mere assertion?
- [ ] Has the full range of accommodation types been considered, including equipment, schedule, policy modification, leave, and reassignment as a last resort?
- [ ] Are religion and pregnancy accommodations handled under their distinct standards, including lactation accommodation as required?
- [ ] Is the entire process documented: request, functions analysis, information obtained, interactive dialogue, effectiveness and hardship analysis, decision, and implementation?
- [ ] Is the accommodation reviewed when circumstances change, and is re-evaluation built in rather than treating the decision as permanent?
