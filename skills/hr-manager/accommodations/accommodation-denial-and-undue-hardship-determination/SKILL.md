# Accommodation Denial and Undue Hardship Determination

Most accommodation requests are granted through the interactive process, but some cannot be — the requested accommodation poses an undue hardship, a direct threat, or is not feasible. Denying an accommodation is a high-stakes decision because it triggers legal scrutiny and, if mishandled, creates liability for discrimination or failure to accommodate. The discipline is to approach denial as a last resort after exhausting alternatives, to ground the undue hardship determination in documented evidence rather than assertion, to follow the procedural requirements rigorously, and to communicate the denial in a way that preserves the relationship and the legal position.

## Core Rules

### Treat Denial as a Last Resort After Exhausting Alternatives

Before denying an accommodation, exhaust the alternatives. Return to the interactive process: has the full range of possible accommodations been explored, not just the one the employee requested? Are there alternative accommodations that would meet the employee's needs without the hardship? Has reassignment to a vacant position been considered (a required step under the ADA when no accommodation enables the current role)? Has the employee been engaged in discussing alternatives? Denial should follow genuine exploration, not be the first response to a difficult request. A denial that is later shown to have preceded alternative exploration is vulnerable, because the failure to explore alternatives is itself a failure to accommodate. Document the alternatives explored and the reasons each was infeasible.

### Ground Undue Hardship in Documented Evidence

Undue hardship — the legal standard for declining a disability accommodation — is defined as significant difficulty or expense, and the determination must be grounded in documented evidence, not assertion. For financial hardship, document the cost relative to the organization's resources (the ADA considers the employer's overall resources, not just the department's, which makes financial hardship difficult to establish for most organizations). For operational hardship, document the disruption to operations, the impact on other employees, the effect on the business, and why the accommodation cannot be managed. Vague assertions of "hardship" or "disruption" are insufficient and vulnerable. Gather the evidence before the denial decision, and document the analysis. The undue hardship determination is a legal conclusion that must withstand scrutiny — build it on evidence.

### Evaluate Direct Threat Carefully and Conservatively

A direct threat — a significant risk of substantial harm that cannot be eliminated by reasonable accommodation — is a narrow basis for denial, applicable when an accommodation would pose a danger to the employee or others. Evaluate direct threat carefully and conservatively, based on objective medical and risk evidence, not on speculation or stereotypes. The determination should involve medical guidance (often through the employee's provider or an independent medical examiner) and, where relevant, safety expertise. Direct threat findings based on assumptions about a condition (e.g., assuming an employee with a mental health condition is dangerous) rather than evidence are discriminatory. Reserve direct threat for cases with clear, documented risk, and remember that the obligation is to determine whether the risk can be eliminated by accommodation before relying on direct threat.

### Follow Procedural Requirements Rigorously

Accommodation denial carries procedural requirements, and procedural failures can create liability independent of the merits. Engage in the interactive process fully before denying. Base the decision on individualized assessment, not blanket policies. Document the request, the interactive process, the alternatives explored, the hardship analysis, and the decision rationale. Communicate the decision to the employee clearly and respectfully, explaining the basis. Provide any required notices. If the employee disputes the denial, engage the dispute resolution process (often involving HR leadership, legal counsel, or external review). Procedural rigor protects the decision: a denial that follows the process, documents the analysis, and communicates clearly is far more defensible than one that skips steps, even if the substantive conclusion is the same.

### Document the Decision Rationale Thoroughly

Thorough documentation is the foundation of a defensible denial. Document the request as made by the employee, the information gathered (medical certification, operational impact data, cost analysis), the interactive process (discussions, alternatives explored, the employee's response), the undue hardship or direct threat analysis (with evidence), the alternatives considered and why each was infeasible, the decision, and the basis. Prepare this documentation as if it will be reviewed by a regulator or a court, because it may be. Avoid conclusory documentation ("denied due to hardship") in favor of specific, evidence-based documentation ("denied because the requested accommodation would require X, which poses Y operational impact, as documented by Z"). Thorough documentation contemporaneous with the decision is the strongest defense.

### Communicate the Denial to Preserve the Relationship and Position

How a denial is communicated shapes both the employee relationship and the legal position. Communicate the denial directly and respectfully, in a conversation (not just a letter), explaining the basis in terms the employee can understand. Acknowledge the employee's needs and the genuine effort to find an accommodation. Be transparent about the reasons — vague denials breed suspicion and disputes. Discuss next steps, including whether there are other accommodations to explore, whether reassignment is possible, and what support the organization can offer. A denial communicated with respect and transparency, following genuine effort, is more likely to be accepted (or at least not litigated) than one communicated coldly or vaguely. Preserve the relationship even while denying the request.

### Coordinate With Legal Counsel on High-Risk Denials

Accommodation denials carry legal risk, and high-risk denials — those involving serious conditions, significant operational impact, potential direct threat, or employees in protected classes who may perceive retaliation — warrant legal counsel involvement. Engage counsel before the decision, not after, to review the analysis, the documentation, and the communication. Counsel can identify vulnerabilities, suggest alternatives, and ensure the legal position is sound. Treat counsel involvement as a quality check on a high-stakes decision, not as an admission of difficulty. The cost of counsel review is far less than the cost of a mishandled denial that becomes litigation.

## Common Traps

### Denial as First Response

Denying before exhausting alternatives is a failure to accommodate. Treat denial as a last resort after genuine exploration of the full range of accommodations and reassignment.

### Hardship Based on Assertion, Not Evidence

Vague claims of "hardship" are insufficient and vulnerable. Ground the determination in documented cost and operational impact evidence.

### Direct Threat Based on Stereotypes

Assumptions about dangerousness based on a condition are discriminatory. Base direct threat on objective medical and risk evidence, and consider whether risk can be eliminated.

### Procedural Steps Skipped

Failing to engage in the interactive process or document the analysis creates liability independent of the merits. Follow procedural requirements rigorously.

### Conclusory Documentation

"Denied due to hardship" does not withstand scrutiny. Document the request, process, alternatives, analysis, and decision specifically and evidence-based.

### Cold or Vague Communication

Vague or cold denials breed suspicion and disputes. Communicate respectfully, transparently, and with a discussion of next steps.

### High-Risk Denials Without Counsel

Denials involving serious conditions or potential direct threat without counsel review carry unnecessary risk. Engage counsel before the decision.

## Self-Check

- Have I treated denial as a last resort, exhausting the full range of accommodations and reassignment before deciding to deny?
- Is my undue hardship determination grounded in documented cost and operational impact evidence, relative to organizational resources?
- If relying on direct threat, is it based on objective medical and risk evidence, with consideration of whether risk can be eliminated?
- Have I followed all procedural requirements — interactive process, individualized assessment, documentation, communication?
- Is my documentation thorough and contemporaneous — request, information, process, alternatives, analysis, decision — prepared as if for review?
- Have I communicated the denial respectfully and transparently, acknowledging the employee's needs and discussing next steps?
- Have I engaged legal counsel for high-risk denials before the decision?
