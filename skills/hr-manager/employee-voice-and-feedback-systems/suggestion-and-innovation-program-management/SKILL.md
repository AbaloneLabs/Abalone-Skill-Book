# Suggestion And Innovation Program Management

Suggestion and innovation programs invite employees to propose ideas for improvement, efficiency, or new value. Their appeal is obvious: the workforce holds knowledge leadership lacks, and a channel to surface it should produce value. In practice these programs fail at high rates, and the failure is rarely in the collection of ideas — employees will submit ideas — but in the evaluation, decision, and execution that follow. The recurring failure is the black box: ideas go in, nothing observable comes out, and the employees who contributed conclude that the program is performative, which suppresses future participation and breeds cynicism toward the broader idea of voice. A second failure is to harvest ideas without resourcing execution, so even good ideas die between approval and implementation. A third is to run the program as a contest for novelty rather than a system for improvement, rewarding flashy ideas over useful ones. This skill covers the design of suggestion and innovation programs as end-to-end systems whose value is realized in execution, not in collection.

Use this skill when designing or revising a suggestion or innovation program, building the evaluation and decision process, connecting the program to execution capacity, or diagnosing why an existing program produces declining participation. The goal is to make the agent design a program that evaluates and executes ideas, not one that merely collects them.

## Core Rules

### Design The Whole Pipeline, Not Just The Front End

A suggestion program is a pipeline: submission, triage, evaluation, decision, resourcing, execution, and feedback to the submitter. Designing the submission channel and neglecting the rest produces a program whose front end works and whose back end fails, which is the failure mode that destroys participation.

- Map the full pipeline before launch and assign ownership to each stage, because unowned stages accumulate work without progress.
- Define the triage criteria that route ideas (quick wins to local execution, larger ideas to formal evaluation, out-of-scope ideas to a clear decline), because without triage every idea enters the same slow queue.
- Define the evaluation criteria (impact, feasibility, alignment, cost) and the decision authority, because evaluation without criteria is opinion and decision without authority is paralysis.
- Define the execution path — who builds, who resources, who reports completion — because approval without execution is the most common point of failure.

### Ensure Every Submission Receives A Visible Response

The single behavior that sustains participation is that every submitter learns what happened to their idea. The black box — where ideas disappear — is the single behavior that destroys it.

- Commit to a response on every submission within a defined timeframe, because the absence of response is read as dismissal and suppresses future participation.
- Communicate the decision and its rationale, including for declined ideas, because a decline with a reason is respected while silence is resented.
- Track response time and response rate as program health metrics, because slow or missing responses are the leading indicator of declining participation.
- Make the status of ideas visible to submitters throughout the process, because opacity breeds the assumption of neglect whether or not neglect is occurring.

### Resource Execution, Or Do Not Approve

Approving ideas that the organization has no capacity or intention to execute produces a portfolio of approved-but-dead ideas that destroys the program's credibility more thoroughly than declining them. Approval must be connected to execution capacity.

- Connect approval to resourced execution capacity, because an approved idea without an owner, budget, or timeline is an idea that will not happen.
- Prefer a smaller number of approved-and-executed ideas over a large portfolio of approved-and-stalled ones, because execution credibility is the program's most valuable asset.
- Define the execution path and timeline at approval, so that approval is a commitment rather than an aspiration.
- Be willing to decline good ideas that cannot be resourced, because an honest decline preserves credibility while a false approval destroys it.

### Distinguish Quick Wins From Strategic Bets

A program that treats every idea through the same heavy process either over-processes quick wins (delaying cheap improvements) or under-processes strategic bets (approving complex ideas without rigor). The process should fit the idea.

- Create a fast path for quick wins — local improvements that are low-cost and low-risk — so that cheap, useful ideas are executed without bureaucratic delay.
- Create a rigorous path for strategic bets — ideas with significant cost, risk, or strategic implication — so that complex ideas receive the evaluation their scale demands.
- Route ideas to the right path at triage, because forcing all ideas through one path fails both ends of the spectrum.
- Recognize that most value often comes from many quick wins rather than few big bets, and resource the quick-win path accordingly.

### Evaluate On Impact And Feasibility, Not Novelty Or Appeal

Programs that reward novelty or presentation appeal produce flashy winners and missed substance. Evaluation should be on the impact the idea would create and the feasibility of executing it, judged against consistent criteria.

- Evaluate on impact (what value would this create), feasibility (can we execute it at acceptable cost and risk), and alignment (does it fit our strategy and constraints), because these are the criteria that determine whether the idea is worth doing.
- Guard against bias toward ideas that are well-presented, well-connected, or novel, because these biases produce winners that are appealing rather than valuable.
- Use evaluators who understand the domain, because evaluation by people distant from the work produces decisions disconnected from reality.
- Document the evaluation so decisions can be explained and reviewed, because undocumented evaluation reads as arbitrary.

### Protect Against Appropriation And Give Credit

A program that takes employees' ideas and executes them without credit or recognition teaches the workforce that contributing is a way to be exploited rather than valued. Credit and recognition are part of the program's integrity.

- Give visible credit to the originator of ideas that are implemented, because recognition sustains participation and is ethically owed.
- Define how originators are involved in execution where appropriate, because the originator often holds context that aids implementation.
- Avoid structures where managers claim credit for subordinates' ideas, because appropriation destroys trust and suppresses the upward flow of ideas.
- Recognize that for some employees, the recognition matters more than any reward, and design recognition accordingly.

### Measure Outcomes, Not Submissions

A program measured by submission volume optimizes for collection; a program measured by implemented ideas and created value optimizes for impact. The metric chosen determines what the program produces.

- Measure implemented ideas and their outcomes (cost saved, revenue gained, experience improved), because these are the measures of whether the program creates value.
- Treat submission volume as an input metric, not a success metric, because high submission volume with low implementation is a sign of a failing pipeline, not a successful one.
- Track participation breadth (how many employees, from how many functions and levels), because a program dominated by a few voices is not capturing the workforce's knowledge.
- Review the portfolio for bias — whose ideas get approved, whose get declined — because systematic bias in evaluation destroys participation from the excluded groups.

## Common Traps

### The Black Box

The program collects ideas through a submission channel, and ideas disappear into an evaluation process whose status, criteria, and outcomes are invisible to submitters, who receive no response or a response so delayed it reads as dismissal. The trap is that the black box teaches the workforce that the program is performative — that ideas go in and nothing comes out — which suppresses future participation and breeds cynicism toward voice more broadly, because employees who contributed and were ignored conclude that the channel is inert. The decline is gradual and then sudden: participation erodes cycle over cycle until the program is sustained only by the few who have not yet learned it is pointless, and leadership concludes that the workforce has no ideas when the workforce has learned not to share them. Visible response on every submission, with status tracking and rationale for decisions, is what sustains participation, because the willingness to contribute depends entirely on the evidence that contribution produces response.

### Approval Without Execution

The program approves ideas — sometimes many ideas, to demonstrate responsiveness — but the approved ideas have no owner, no budget, and no timeline, so they sit in a portfolio of approved-but-dead commitments that never reach implementation. The trap is that approval without execution is worse than decline, because it creates the appearance of commitment while delivering nothing, and the submitters whose ideas were approved and then abandoned experience a deeper cynicism than those whose ideas were declined, because they were given hope and then ignored. The credibility cost of a portfolio of dead approvals is severe, because it teaches that even success in the program produces nothing, and the program's most valuable asset — the belief that approved ideas are implemented — is destroyed. Connecting approval to resourced execution, and preferring fewer approved-and-executed ideas over many approved-and-stalled ones, is what preserves credibility, because execution is the proof that the program is real.

### The Novelty Contest

The program is structured as a contest that rewards the most novel, well-presented, or appealing ideas, with winners celebrated at a showcase, while useful but unglamorous improvements receive no attention or reward. The trap is that rewarding novelty and presentation produces winners who are appealing rather than valuable, and the program optimizes for the showcase rather than for impact, so the ideas that would create the most value — often incremental process improvements — are neglected because they are not impressive. The contest framing also discourages the submission of useful small ideas, because employees learn that only novel ideas win, and the program captures the flashy and misses the substantive. Evaluating on impact and feasibility, and recognizing implemented value regardless of glamour, is what aligns the program with its purpose, because a suggestion program exists to create value, not to produce showcase winners.

### The One-Size Process

Every idea, from a quick local improvement to a complex strategic bet, passes through the same heavy evaluation process, so quick wins are delayed by bureaucracy and strategic bets are approved without the rigor their scale demands. The trap is that one process cannot fit the full spectrum of ideas: a process light enough for quick wins is too thin for strategic bets, and a process rigorous enough for strategic bets is too heavy for quick wins, so forcing all ideas through one process fails both ends. Quick wins either die in the queue or get executed informally outside the program, and strategic bets get approved on the same thin evaluation that fits a quick win, producing commitments the organization has not properly assessed. Routing ideas to fit-for-purpose paths at triage is what allows both quick wins and strategic bets to be handled well, because the process must fit the idea, not the reverse.

### Appropriation Of Subordinates' Ideas

A manager submits a subordinate's idea under their own name, or the idea is implemented with credit flowing to the manager rather than the originator, and the workforce learns that contributing upward is a way to have one's work claimed by others. The trap is that appropriation destroys the trust on which upward idea flow depends, because employees will not share ideas if doing so produces credit for someone else, and the program that was meant to surface frontline knowledge instead becomes a channel through which knowledge is extracted and credit is captured by those already visible. The damage extends beyond the individual case, because the pattern is observed and it suppresses contribution from everyone who fears the same appropriation. Visible credit to originators, and structures that prevent managers from claiming subordinates' ideas, are what protect the integrity of the program, because a program that exploits its contributors is a program that loses them.

### Measuring Submissions Instead Of Outcomes

The program reports submission volume as its success metric — how many ideas collected, how many participants — and leadership treats rising submission counts as evidence of a thriving program, while implementation rates and created value go unmeasured or decline. The trap is that submission volume measures collection, not value, and a program optimized for submissions produces submissions, not implemented improvements, so the program grows in activity while shrinking in impact. High submission volume with low implementation is in fact a sign of a failing pipeline — ideas going in faster than they can be evaluated or executed — but it is reported as success because the metric chosen rewards the front end rather than the back end. Measuring implemented ideas and their outcomes is what aligns the program with value creation, because a suggestion program's purpose is not to collect ideas; it is to create value by acting on them.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is the full pipeline — submission, triage, evaluation, decision, execution, feedback — designed and owned before launch, not just the submission channel?
- [ ] Does every submission receive a visible response within a defined timeframe, with status tracking and decision rationale including for declines?
- [ ] Is approval connected to resourced execution capacity, with a smaller portfolio of executed ideas preferred over a large portfolio of approved-but-stalled ones?
- [ ] Are there fit-for-purpose paths for quick wins and strategic bets, rather than one process forced on every idea?
- [ ] Is evaluation based on impact, feasibility, and alignment, with domain-knowledgeable evaluators and documented rationale, guarding against novelty or presentation bias?
- [ ] Is credit given visibly to originators, with structures preventing appropriation of subordinates' ideas by managers?
- [ ] Are success metrics focused on implemented ideas and created value, with submission volume treated as an input metric and participation breadth tracked?
- [ ] Is the portfolio audited for bias in whose ideas are approved, to ensure the program captures the workforce's knowledge rather than a narrow subset?
