# Ongoing Feedback And Coaching

Annual reviews alone cannot develop people or correct problems in time to matter. Feedback that arrives once a year is too late to change behavior, too infrequent to build on strengths, and too disconnected from specific events to be useful. Ongoing feedback and coaching are the daily practice through which performance is shaped, growth is guided, and problems are caught early. Agents often treat feedback as an event rather than a habit, and miss that feedback delayed is feedback wasted, that feedback without specificity changes nothing, and that coaching that tells rather than asks produces dependence rather than growth.

Use this skill before delivering feedback, coaching an employee, establishing feedback practices in a team, or evaluating whether feedback is timely and effective. The goal is to make the agent provide feedback that is timely, specific, and balanced, and coaching that builds capability rather than dependency.

## Core Rules

### Give Feedback Close To The Event

Feedback is most effective when delivered soon after the behavior or outcome it addresses. Delay diminishes the connection between the feedback and the event, reduces the emotional and practical impact, and allows patterns to solidify before they are addressed.

Give timely feedback by:

- delivering positive feedback as soon as you notice the behavior, reinforcing it while it is fresh;
- addressing concerns or problems promptly, before they repeat or escalate;
- not saving feedback for the next formal review or scheduled meeting if the moment is now;
- finding a private and appropriate setting for sensitive feedback, but not letting the search for the perfect moment become an excuse for delay.

Timeliness does not mean delivering feedback in the heat of emotion. If you are angry or upset, take time to calm down. But calm should take hours or a day, not weeks.

### Be Specific About Behavior And Impact

Feedback that says "you're doing great" or "you need to step up" provides no actionable information. Effective feedback identifies the specific behavior and its impact, giving the person a clear understanding of what to continue or change.

Be specific by:

- describing the observable behavior, not the personality or character you infer from it;
- citing the specific instance, with enough context to be clear;
- explaining the impact of the behavior, on results, on the team, on stakeholders, or on the person's own goals;
- avoiding labels like "unprofessional" or "not a team player" without the specific behaviors that led to the label;
- being concrete about what you would like to see more or less of.

"You interrupted Sarah three times in the meeting, which made it difficult for her to finish her points and may discourage her from contributing" is specific and actionable. "You need to be more respectful" is not.

### Balance Positive And Constructive Feedback

People need to know what they are doing well, not just what to improve. A diet of only criticism erodes confidence and motivation, while a diet of only praise leaves people blind to their development areas.

Balance by:

- genuinely looking for and acknowledging strengths and achievements, not just scanning for problems;
- ensuring that constructive feedback is not the only feedback a person receives;
- being authentic in positive feedback, not delivering it as a perfunctory warm-up before criticism;
- recognizing that different people need different ratios of positive to constructive feedback, depending on their experience, confidence, and current challenges;
- avoiding the "feedback sandwich" of praise-criticism-praise, which teaches people to dread praise as the precursor to criticism.

Balance is not a formula. It is an honest effort to see and address the full picture, recognizing strengths while improving weaknesses.

### Focus On Behavior, Not Personality

Feedback that attacks personality or character produces defensiveness and damage, and is often inaccurate because it infers internal qualities from external behavior. Feedback that addresses behavior is actionable, because behavior can be changed.

Focus on behavior by:

- describing what the person did, not who they are;
- avoiding generalizations from a single instance to a character trait;
- being aware of language that implies fixed attributes, such as "you are lazy" or "you are not strategic";
- reframing personality-based observations into behavioral terms, such as "I've noticed the last three reports were submitted after the deadline" rather than "you're unreliable";
- recognizing that behavior is influenced by context, resources, and constraints, and exploring those factors rather than attributing everything to the person.

People can change their behavior; they cannot easily change their personality. Feedback aimed at behavior is empowering; feedback aimed at personality is demoralizing.

### Ask Questions And Listen In Coaching

Coaching is not telling someone what to do. It is helping them develop their own understanding and solutions, building capability that lasts beyond the coaching conversation. Telling produces dependency; asking builds autonomy.

Coach by asking:

- open-ended questions that prompt reflection, such as "What do you think is driving that result?" or "What would you do differently next time?";
- questions that help the person analyze the situation, rather than questions that lead to your predetermined answer;
- questions that explore options, such as "What approaches have you considered?" or "What support would help?";
- questions that build commitment, such as "What will you do, and by when?";
- and then listening, genuinely, to the answers, rather than using the question as a pause before delivering your own solution.

Coaching takes longer than telling in the moment, but it develops judgment and capability that reduce the need for future intervention. The investment compounds.

### Create Regular Check-In Rhythms

Feedback and coaching should not depend on the manager remembering to do them. Regular rhythms ensure that they happen consistently and that no one goes too long without attention.

Establish rhythms by:

- scheduling regular one-on-one meetings, weekly or biweekly, and protecting them from cancellation;
- using check-ins to discuss progress, obstacles, development, and well-being, not just status updates;
- ensuring that every person receives attention, not just those who are struggling or those who are high-performing;
- being flexible in format, adapting to what the person needs, whether it is problem-solving, career discussion, or simply connection;
- treating the rhythm as a commitment, not an optional activity that is the first thing cut when busy.

The frequency and quality of one-on-ones is one of the strongest predictors of employee engagement and retention. Consistency matters more than perfection in any single conversation.

### Build A Feedback Culture Beyond The Manager

Feedback from a manager is one source. A culture where peers, stakeholders, and customers give and receive feedback openly provides richer, more timely, and more credible input.

Build culture by:

- modeling receptivity to feedback yourself, including asking for and acting on feedback from your team;
- encouraging peer feedback and recognition, through formal mechanisms or informal norms;
- training people to give feedback effectively, so that it is specific, timely, and respectful;
- normalizing feedback as a regular part of work, not an exceptional event;
- addressing behaviors that discourage feedback, such as defensiveness, retaliation, or dismissiveness when feedback is given.

A culture where feedback flows in all directions, including upward, is more adaptive and higher-performing than one where feedback only comes from the manager downward.

### Address Performance Problems Early Through Feedback

Many performance problems that escalate to formal action could have been resolved through timely, specific feedback before they became serious. Feedback is the first and most important tool for addressing performance concerns.

Address early by:

- raising concerns as soon as a pattern emerges, not waiting for it to become serious;
- being clear about the gap between expected and actual performance;
- exploring the causes, which may include lack of clarity, resources, training, or personal circumstances;
- agreeing on specific actions and a timeline for improvement;
- documenting the conversation and the agreed actions, in case the problem persists and formal action becomes necessary.

Early feedback gives the person a fair chance to improve before consequences escalate. It also documents that the concern was raised, which is essential if formal performance management becomes necessary later.

## Common Traps

### Delayed Feedback That Loses The Teaching Moment

Waiting for the formal review or the "right moment" to deliver feedback — deferring feedback until the quarterly review, or waiting for the perfect setting — until the feedback is stale and the behavior has solidified, because feedback delivered weeks or months after the event has lost its teaching power, the person cannot connect the feedback to the specific behavior, and the behavior that prompted the feedback has become a habit that is now harder to change. The trap is that the "right moment" never arrives, so feedback is perpetually deferred, but feedback deferred is feedback diminished, and the person who receives stale feedback cannot act on it because the moment has passed. Deliver feedback close to the event, while the behavior and its impact are fresh, because timely feedback teaches and delayed feedback confuses, and the teaching moment is the moment the behavior occurs.

### Vague Feedback That Provides No Actionable Guidance

Saying "good job" or "do better" without specifics — feedback that contains no description of the behavior, no description of the impact, and no description of what to continue or change — provides no actionable guidance, because the person does not know what they did well or poorly, only that the manager has a general impression, and an impression cannot be acted on, repeated, or corrected. The trap is that vague feedback feels safer because it avoids specificity that might provoke disagreement, but safety is purchased with uselessness, and vague feedback is feedback that changes nothing. Be specific about the observable behavior and its impact, because specific feedback tells the person exactly what to continue or change, and vague feedback tells them only that the manager has an opinion.

### Criticism-Only Feedback That Erodes Confidence

Delivering only negative feedback — a feedback relationship that consists entirely of corrections, with no acknowledgment of what the person does well — erodes confidence and motivation, because the person concludes that nothing they do is valued, and a person who receives only criticism either stops trying (because effort is never recognized) or becomes defensive (because every interaction is an attack). The trap is that positive feedback feels unnecessary because the person is "just doing their job," so only problems are raised, but a relationship built entirely on correction is a relationship that demoralizes, and the person who hears only criticism disengages. Balance constructive feedback with genuine, specific positive feedback, tailored to the individual, because recognition sustains the effort that correction guides, and feedback without balance produces compliance without engagement.

### Personality-Based Feedback That Produces Defensiveness

Attacking character rather than behavior — "you're not a team player" or "you lack initiative" — produces defensiveness and damage, because personality feedback feels like an attack on identity rather than behavior, and a person whose identity is attacked defends the identity rather than hearing the feedback, and the conversation becomes a conflict about who the person is rather than what the person did. The trap is that personality language feels like honest directness, so it is used, but character feedback is not more honest than behavioral feedback, it is less useful, because it provokes defense rather than change, and the behavior that prompted the feedback goes unaddressed. Focus on observable behavior and its impact, not on personality or character, because behavioral feedback invites change and personality feedback invites conflict, and the goal is change.

### Telling Instead Of Coaching That Creates Dependency

Providing solutions rather than asking questions — telling the person what to do rather than helping them think through the problem — creates dependency rather than capability, because the person who is told the answer does not develop the ability to find the answer, and they return to the manager for the next answer, and the manager who tells rather than coaches produces employees who cannot operate independently. The trap is that telling is faster than coaching, so it feels efficient, but speed in the moment is purchased with dependency over time, and the manager who tells becomes the bottleneck for every decision. Ask open questions and listen, building the person's own capability to reason and decide, because coaching develops capability and telling develops dependency, and the goal is employees who can operate without the manager.

### Inconsistent Or Cancelled Check-Ins That Signal Low Priority

Treating one-on-ones as optional — cancelling them when busy, rescheduling indefinitely, or holding them only when there is a problem — ensures that some people receive no regular attention, because the check-in rhythm that should guarantee every person regular access to their manager is treated as disposable, and the employees whose check-ins are cancelled learn that they are low priority, and the manager who does not hold regular check-ins has no relationship to draw on when difficult feedback is needed. The trap is that check-ins feel like overhead when there is nothing specific to discuss, so they are cancelled, but the check-in with nothing to discuss is the check-in that builds the relationship, and the relationship is what makes difficult conversations possible later. Protect the check-in rhythm consistently for every person, because a protected rhythm ensures every person receives attention, and attention is the foundation of trust and feedback.

### Feedback Only From The Manager That Misses The Full Picture

Relying on a single source of feedback — the manager alone — misses the richer input available from peers, direct reports, and stakeholders, because the manager sees one slice of the person's work, and the slice the manager sees may not represent the whole, and the person who is excellent with peers but struggling with the manager, or vice versa, receives feedback that reflects only the manager's perspective. The trap is that the manager feels like the authoritative source, so other input is not gathered, but a single perspective is a partial perspective, and feedback from one source is more likely to be wrong than feedback from many. Build a multi-directional feedback culture, with the manager modeling receptivity and peers encouraged to give input, because the full picture requires multiple perspectives, and a single perspective is a distorted picture.

### Saving Problems For Formal Action That Escalates Avoidably

Letting issues accumulate until they require formal performance management — avoiding the difficult early feedback conversation and waiting until the problem is serious enough to justify formal action — converts a manageable problem into a formal one, because early feedback could have resolved the issue before it escalated, and the person who is surprised by formal action was never given the fair chance to improve that timely feedback provides, and the formal action feels arbitrary because the person did not see it coming. The trap is that early feedback feels uncomfortable, so it is avoided, but avoidance converts discomfort into escalation, and the problem that could have been a conversation becomes a formal process. Address performance problems early through feedback, exploring causes and agreeing on actions, because early feedback resolves problems that late feedback escalates, and the conversation avoided today is the formal action required tomorrow.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is feedback delivered close to the event, while the behavior and its impact are fresh?
- [ ] Is feedback specific about observable behavior and its impact, avoiding vague labels or personality judgments?
- [ ] Is there a genuine balance of positive and constructive feedback, tailored to the individual?
- [ ] Does coaching use open questions and active listening to build the person's own capability, rather than telling them what to do?
- [ ] Are there regular, protected check-in rhythms that ensure every person receives consistent attention?
- [ ] Does feedback flow in multiple directions, with the manager modeling receptivity and peers encouraged to give input?
- [ ] Are performance problems addressed early through feedback, with causes explored and actions agreed, before escalation to formal action?
- [ ] Are feedback conversations about performance concerns documented, in case formal action becomes necessary?
