# Competency Framework Design

A competency framework defines the behaviors, skills, and knowledge that the organization expects at each role and level. It is the connective tissue between hiring, performance, development, and promotion, providing a common language for what good looks like. A framework that is vague, unobservable, or inconsistently applied becomes paperwork that no one references, while one that is well-designed enables fair and consistent people decisions. Agents often treat competency design as a listing exercise and miss that competencies that cannot be observed cannot be assessed, that frameworks that ignore level differentiation flatten career progression, and that a framework disconnected from actual people processes creates parallel systems that contradict each other.

Use this skill before designing or revising a competency framework, defining role expectations, or evaluating whether a framework supports fair and consistent people decisions. The goal is to make the agent design a framework that is clear, observable, differentiated by level, and integrated with the people processes that depend on it.

## Core Rules

### Define Competencies In Observable Behavioral Terms

A competency that says "demonstrates leadership" or "shows initiative" tells people nothing about what they actually need to do. Competencies must be defined in terms of observable behaviors that can be seen, described, and assessed.

Define observably by:

- describing the specific behaviors that exemplify the competency, not abstract qualities;
- using action language, such as "identifies problems and proposes solutions" rather than "is proactive";
- providing examples of what the behavior looks like in practice, in different roles or contexts;
- ensuring that two independent observers, seeing the same behavior, would agree on whether it meets the standard;
- avoiding internal states or personality traits that cannot be observed, such as "is passionate" or "has high emotional intelligence," unless broken down into observable behaviors.

If a competency cannot be observed, it cannot be assessed fairly. Two managers will interpret it differently, producing inconsistent evaluations and perceived unfairness.

### Differentiate Expectations By Level

A competency framework that applies the same definition of a competency to all levels fails to distinguish growth and progression. The expectation of "communication" for an individual contributor is different from that for a senior leader, and the framework must reflect that.

Differentiate by:

- defining what the competency looks like at each career level, from entry through senior leadership;
- showing progression in scope, complexity, autonomy, and impact as levels increase;
- ensuring that the differences between levels are meaningful and clear, not just incremental wording changes;
- using the level definitions to inform promotion decisions, so that advancement is based on demonstrated capability at the next level;
- reviewing level definitions for consistency across functions, so that a level means a similar thing regardless of role.

Without level differentiation, promotion becomes based on tenure or manager discretion rather than demonstrated growth in capability. The framework should make the path of progression visible.

### Distinguish Technical Competencies From Behavioral Competencies

Roles require both technical skills, specific to the function, and behavioral competencies, common across the organization. Conflating or neglecting either produces incomplete expectations.

Distinguish by:

- identifying the technical competencies specific to each role or function, such as financial modeling, software architecture, or regulatory knowledge;
- identifying the behavioral competencies expected across the organization, such as collaboration, communication, or ethical judgment;
- defining how the two interact, recognizing that technical excellence without behavioral effectiveness, or vice versa, produces incomplete performance;
- weighting the two appropriately for the role, recognizing that the balance shifts at different levels, with senior roles often requiring more behavioral and strategic competency.

A brilliant technical specialist who cannot collaborate may be valuable in a specific role but is not ready for leadership. A charismatic leader without technical credibility will struggle to guide experts. The framework should address both.

### Ensure The Framework Is Comprehensive But Not Overwhelming

A framework with too few competencies misses important expectations; one with too many becomes unmanageable and no one can attend to them all. The right scope covers the critical behaviors without overwhelming.

Ensure scope by:

- focusing on the competencies that truly differentiate performance and align with organizational values;
- typically keeping behavioral competencies to a manageable number, around six to ten;
- grouping related behaviors under a competency rather than creating separate competencies for each;
- avoiding redundancy, where two competencies overlap significantly;
- reviewing periodically to ensure the framework remains relevant as the organization evolves.

A framework of thirty competencies will not be used. A framework of five well-chosen, clearly defined competencies will shape behavior and decisions.

### Integrate The Framework With All People Processes

A competency framework that exists in a document but is not used in hiring, performance, development, and promotion is wasted effort. The framework must be the common reference across all people processes.

Integrate by:

- using competencies in job descriptions and hiring criteria, so that selection targets the defined behaviors;
- incorporating competencies into performance evaluation, alongside goal achievement;
- referencing competencies in development planning, identifying which behaviors to strengthen;
- using competencies as criteria for promotion, ensuring advancement is based on demonstrated capability;
- ensuring that the language of the framework appears consistently across all processes, so that people encounter the same expectations everywhere.

Integration ensures consistency. A person should hear the same language about expectations whether they are being hired, evaluated, developed, or considered for promotion.

### Validate The Framework With Those Who Use It

A framework designed in isolation, without input from the people who will be assessed against it and the managers who will apply it, is likely to miss the realities of specific roles and to be rejected as impractical.

Validate by:

- involving employees and managers in defining and reviewing competencies, to ensure they reflect real work;
- testing the framework by applying it to sample scenarios or real cases, to see if it produces sensible assessments;
- gathering feedback on whether the definitions are clear, observable, and applicable to different roles;
- revising based on feedback, rather than treating the initial design as final;
- reviewing periodically as roles and the organization evolve.

A framework that people understand and see as fair will be accepted and used. One that feels imposed or disconnected from reality will be ignored or resented.

### Provide Tools And Training For Application

Even a well-designed framework will be applied inconsistently if managers and employees do not know how to use it. Tools and training are essential for consistent application.

Provide by:

- creating guides or rubrics that help managers assess competencies, with examples of behaviors at each level;
- training managers and employees on the framework, what each competency means, and how to use it;
- providing examples and calibration opportunities, so that assessments are consistent across managers;
- offering self-assessment tools, so that employees can reflect on their own development against the framework;
- making the framework and tools easily accessible, not buried in a system no one opens.

The framework is a tool, and like any tool, it requires skill to use. Training and support transform it from a document into a living practice.

### Review And Evolve The Framework As The Organization Changes

Organizations evolve, strategies shift, and roles transform. A competency framework that reflected the organization five years ago may no longer fit. Regular review ensures continued relevance.

Review by:

- assessing whether the competencies still reflect what the organization needs and values;
- examining whether new competencies are needed, such as digital fluency, adaptability, or inclusion;
- checking whether existing definitions remain accurate for evolving roles;
- gathering input from leaders, managers, and employees on what may be missing or outdated;
- making revisions deliberately, with communication and training, rather than letting the framework drift into irrelevance.

A living framework evolves with the organization. A static one becomes an artifact that no one references, undermining the consistency it was designed to create.

## Common Traps

### Vague, Unobservable Competencies That Make Assessment Arbitrary

Defining competencies as abstract qualities like "leadership," "initiative," or "communication" without behavioral descriptions — qualities that two managers would assess differently for the same employee — makes fair assessment impossible, because the competency has no observable definition, and without observable definition the assessment is the manager's subjective impression dressed in the language of a framework, and two managers will reach different conclusions about the same employee for reasons that have nothing to do with the employee. The trap is that abstract competencies feel aspirational and comprehensive, so they are adopted, but a competency that cannot be observed cannot be assessed fairly, and the framework that uses them produces the arbitrary, impression-based ratings it was designed to eliminate. Define competencies in observable behavioral terms with action language and examples that two independent observers would assess consistently, because the entire value of a competency framework is consistency, and consistency requires observability.

### No Level Differentiation That Flattens Career Progression

Applying the same competency definition to all levels — the same "leadership" description for an individual contributor, a team lead, and a director — flattens career progression and makes promotion criteria unclear, because the progression that should distinguish levels is absent from the framework, and an employee cannot see what "more" looks like because the framework says the same thing at every level. The trap is that a single definition feels clean and simple, so level differentiation is skipped, but without differentiation the framework cannot describe growth, and promotion decisions revert to impression because the framework provides no level-based criteria. Differentiate competency expectations by career level, showing meaningful progression in scope, complexity, autonomy, and impact, because career progression must be visible in the framework, and a framework that looks the same at every level cannot guide development or promotion.

### Technical Or Behavioral Neglect That Produces Incomplete Expectations

Focusing only on technical skills or only on behavioral competencies — a framework that is all technical depth with no collaboration or leadership, or all soft skills with no role-specific capability — produces incomplete expectations, because roles require both, and a framework that covers only one dimension either ignores how work gets done (behavioral) or whether the person can do the work (technical), and employees assessed on only one dimension receive feedback that misses half their job. The trap is that one dimension feels sufficient, so the other is omitted, but roles are multi-dimensional, and a framework that captures only one dimension cannot evaluate the whole person or the whole role. Address both technical and behavioral competencies, weighted appropriately for each role, because a complete framework reflects the complete demands of the role, and a partial framework produces partial and misleading assessment.

### Too Many Competencies That Overwhelm And Are Ignored

Creating an exhaustive competency list — fifteen or twenty competencies that cover every conceivable quality — overwhelms managers and employees and is ignored, because no one can assess or develop against twenty dimensions, and a framework that tries to cover everything ends up covering nothing, as managers retreat to the few competencies they remember and ignore the rest. The trap is that comprehensiveness feels thorough, so competencies accumulate, but a framework that cannot be used is worse than no framework, because it creates the illusion of structure while producing arbitrary application. Focus on the critical few competencies that differentiate performance in each role, because a framework that covers the vital dimensions is used, and a framework that is used is infinitely more valuable than a comprehensive one that is ignored.

### Framework Disconnected From People Processes

Designing a competency framework that is not used in hiring, performance, development, or promotion — a framework that exists in a document but does not appear in job postings, interview rubrics, performance reviews, development plans, or promotion criteria — wastes the effort, because the framework's value comes from integration, and a framework that is not integrated is a document, and a document does not shape behavior or decisions. The trap is that the framework is treated as a deliverable rather than a tool, so it is published and forgotten, but a framework that is not wired into the processes where people decisions are made has no effect on those decisions. Integrate the framework across all people processes — hiring, performance, development, promotion — using consistent language, because the framework's power comes from consistency across the employee lifecycle, and a framework that exists outside the lifecycle is inert.

### Designed In Isolation And Rejected By Users

Creating the framework without input from the employees and managers who will use it — designing it in HR and imposing it on the organization — produces impractical or rejected definitions, because the people who do the work know what the work requires, and a framework designed without them will miss the realities of the role and will be experienced as imposed, and imposed frameworks are resisted or ignored. The trap is that design by experts feels efficient, so input is skipped, but a framework that does not reflect the lived reality of the roles it describes will be rejected by the people it governs, and rejection means the framework produces noise rather than consistency. Validate the framework with employees and managers, test it against real scenarios, and revise based on feedback, because a framework that the users helped build is a framework the users will use, and a framework that is used is the only framework that matters.

### No Training Or Tools Leading To Inconsistent Application

Providing a framework without guides, rubrics, or training — publishing the competency definitions and assuming managers will apply them consistently — leads to inconsistent application, because competencies require interpretation, and without training and calibration each manager interprets differently, and the consistency the framework was designed to produce is undermined by the inconsistency of its application. The trap is that the framework feels self-explanatory, so training is skipped, but two managers given the same framework without calibration will assess the same employee differently, and the framework becomes a source of inconsistency rather than consistency. Provide tools, rubrics, examples, and training, and create calibration opportunities, because a framework is only as consistent as the people applying it, and untrained application produces the arbitrary ratings the framework was meant to eliminate.

### Static Framework That Becomes Outdated

Never reviewing or updating the framework — keeping the same competencies and definitions as the organization, strategy, and roles evolve — ensures that the framework becomes outdated and irrelevant, because the competencies that mattered five years ago may not be the competencies that matter now, and a framework that does not reflect current roles and strategy provides guidance toward an organization that no longer exists. The trap is that the framework feels finished once designed, so it is not revisited, but organizations change, and a framework that does not change with the organization drifts into irrelevance, and an irrelevant framework is worse than no framework because it provides false confidence. Review and evolve the framework periodically, assessing whether the competencies still reflect what the organization needs, because a living framework evolves with the organization, and a static framework is an artifact that misleads.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Are competencies defined in observable behavioral terms, with action language and examples that two independent observers would assess consistently?
- [ ] Are competency expectations differentiated by career level, showing meaningful progression in scope, complexity, autonomy, and impact?
- [ ] Does the framework distinguish and appropriately weight technical and behavioral competencies for each role?
- [ ] Is the framework comprehensive but manageable, focusing on the critical differentiating competencies without overwhelming?
- [ ] Is the framework integrated with hiring, performance, development, and promotion, using consistent language across all processes?
- [ ] Was the framework validated with employees and managers, tested against real scenarios, and revised based on feedback?
- [ ] Are tools, rubrics, and training provided to ensure consistent application across managers?
- [ ] Is the framework reviewed and evolved periodically to remain relevant as roles and the organization change?
