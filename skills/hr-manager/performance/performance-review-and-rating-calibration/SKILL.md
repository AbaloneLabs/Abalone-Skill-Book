# Performance Review And Rating Calibration

Performance reviews convert a year of work into a rating and a conversation, and both carry consequences: compensation, promotion, development, and sometimes termination. A review that is poorly conducted, inconsistent across peers, or unsupported by evidence produces unfair outcomes, erodes trust, and exposes the organization to legal risk. Agents often treat the review as a paperwork step and miss that ratings that are not calibrated across managers are meaningless, that feedback delivered without specifics changes nothing, and that a rating that cannot be traced to documented evidence is indefensible if challenged.

Use this skill before conducting reviews, running calibration sessions, writing feedback, or evaluating the fairness of a rating process. The goal is to make the agent produce ratings that are evidence-based, calibrated for consistency, and delivered with feedback that is specific, balanced, and actionable.

## Core Rules

### Gather Evidence Before The Review, Not During

A review built on impressions formed in the last few weeks, or on vague memories, is inaccurate and unfair. Performance must be assessed against documented evidence collected throughout the period.

Gather evidence by:

- reviewing the goals or objectives set at the beginning of the period;
- collecting specific examples of achievements, contributions, and areas for improvement, with dates and context;
- seeking input from peers, stakeholders, or customers where the role involves cross-functional work;
- reviewing quantitative metrics, project outcomes, and quality indicators;
- identifying patterns over time, rather than relying on a single incident or the most recent events.

The recency effect, where recent events disproportionately influence the rating, is one of the most common biases in performance review. Documenting evidence throughout the period counters it.

### Rate Against Pre-Defined Criteria, Not Relative Impression

Ratings based on a manager's general impression of a person, without reference to defined standards, are subjective and inconsistent. Ratings must be assessed against criteria that are understood in advance.

Rate against criteria by:

- using the goal or objective statements and their success criteria as the primary basis;
- referencing competency or behavioral frameworks where they exist, with clear definitions of what each level looks like;
- assessing each goal or competency separately before forming an overall rating;
- documenting the specific evidence that supports each rating level;
- being explicit about the rating scale and what each level means, avoiding vague labels like "meets expectations" without definition.

Rating scales that are not defined produce inconsistent application. A "3" in one manager's view may be a "4" in another's. Defined criteria and calibration address this.

### Calibrate Ratings Across Managers And Teams

Without calibration, ratings reflect each manager's personal standards and tendencies, making cross-team comparisons meaningless and producing systemic unfairness. Calibration sessions align standards across the organization.

Run calibration by:

- convening managers to review ratings across teams before they are finalized;
- comparing the distribution of ratings across teams, looking for managers who are consistently lenient, harsh, or clustering in the middle;
- discussing specific cases, particularly borderline ratings, to align on what the standard means in practice;
- challenging ratings that lack supporting evidence or that deviate significantly from peer distributions without justification;
- documenting calibration decisions and the rationale for any adjustments.

Calibration is not about forcing a curve or quota. It is about ensuring that a given rating means the same thing regardless of who the manager is. A person who is a "4" in one team should be a "4" in another for comparable performance.

### Write Specific, Balanced, And Actionable Feedback

Feedback that says "good job" or "needs improvement" without specifics provides no guidance for the future. Effective feedback identifies what was done well, what could be improved, and what to do differently.

Write feedback that:

- cites specific examples and behaviors, not general characterizations;
- balances recognition of strengths with identification of development areas;
- connects feedback to the goals and criteria, showing how the rating was derived;
- describes the impact of behaviors, not just the behaviors themselves;
- provides clear, actionable suggestions for improvement or continued growth;
- avoids personal attributes or personality judgments, focusing on observable behaviors and outcomes.

Feedback that is all positive leaves the person with no direction for growth. Feedback that is all negative demoralizes and may be perceived as unfair. Balance builds credibility and motivation.

### Prepare For The Review Conversation

The review conversation is where the rating and feedback are delivered and discussed. Delivering it without preparation risks a defensive, unproductive, or damaging conversation.

Prepare by:

- reviewing the evidence, rating rationale, and feedback notes before the conversation;
- anticipating the employee's reactions, particularly to areas of improvement or a lower-than-expected rating;
- planning how to deliver difficult messages clearly and respectfully;
- ensuring the conversation allows time for the employee's perspective and questions;
- being ready to listen, not just to deliver;
- scheduling adequate time and a private setting, not a rushed hallway conversation.

The conversation should be a dialogue, not a pronouncement. The employee may have context the manager lacks, and hearing it may adjust the manager's understanding.

### Deliver Difficult Messages Honestly And Respectfully

Avoiding difficult feedback to spare feelings or avoid conflict is a disservice. The person does not learn what to improve, and the organization retains a performance problem unaddressed. But difficult feedback delivered poorly damages the relationship and produces defensiveness rather than growth.

Deliver honestly by:

- stating the assessment directly, without softening it to the point of incomprehension;
- providing the specific evidence and examples that support it;
- focusing on behaviors and outcomes, not personality or character;
- acknowledging the person's perspective and listening to their response;
- framing the feedback in terms of development and support, not just criticism;
- avoiding surprises by ensuring that performance issues have been raised during the period, not first disclosed in the annual review.

A performance issue that appears for the first time in a review, having never been raised during the year, is unfair and often indefensible. Ongoing feedback throughout the period is essential.

### Document The Review And Outcomes

The review conversation and its outcomes must be documented to provide a record for both the employee and the organization.

Document:

- the final ratings, with the supporting evidence and rationale;
- the feedback delivered, in the specific terms discussed;
- the employee's response, including any disagreements or additional context;
- agreed-upon actions, development plans, or goals for the next period;
- any commitments made by the manager, such as support, resources, or opportunities;
- signatures or acknowledgments, with a process for the employee to add comments.

Documentation protects the employee by ensuring commitments are recorded, and protects the organization by providing a defensible record if the rating or its consequences are challenged.

### Examine Rating Patterns For Bias

Even with calibration, systemic patterns in ratings can reveal bias that individual calibration sessions miss. Analyzing aggregate data can surface disparities that need investigation.

Examine:

- whether ratings differ systematically by gender, race, age, or other demographic characteristics, after controlling for role and tenure;
- whether certain types of roles or teams receive systematically higher or lower ratings;
- whether ratings correlate with factors unrelated to performance, such as visibility, office location, or personal relationships with managers;
- whether the distribution of top ratings is concentrated among a small group or widely distributed.

Patterns do not prove bias in any individual case, but they indicate where to investigate. Disparities that persist after controlling for legitimate factors warrant scrutiny and corrective action.

## Common Traps

### Recency Effect That Distorts The Full-Period Picture

Overweighting recent events and forgetting earlier performance — letting the last few weeks dominate a rating that should reflect a full year — produces ratings that are inaccurate and unfair, because a person who performed well for ten months and stumbled in the last two receives a poor rating, while a person who coasted for ten months and rallied in the last two receives a strong rating, and neither rating reflects the actual performance over the period. The trap is that recent events are vivid and accessible in memory while earlier events have faded, so the vivid dominates the faded, but vividness is not importance, and a rating built on vividness is a rating built on distortion. Gather evidence throughout the period, documenting examples with dates, so that the rating reflects the full period rather than the last few weeks, because the full-period picture is the fair picture, and the recency-distorted picture is the unfair one.

### Halo And Horns Effect That Contaminates All Areas

Letting a single strong or weak characteristic, or a general overall impression, dominate the assessment of all areas — the person who is excellent at presentations receives high ratings on collaboration and quality, or the person who is weak at one thing receives low ratings on everything — produces ratings that are contaminated, because the halo effect lets one positive characteristic inflate the assessment of unrelated areas, and the horns effect lets one negative characteristic deflate them, and the contaminated ratings do not reflect the person's actual performance on each area. The trap is that a general impression feels like an efficient summary, so it is applied across all areas, but a general impression is not area-specific evidence, and applying it across areas produces ratings that are all the same regardless of actual area-level performance. Assess each area against its own evidence before forming an overall rating, because area-specific evidence produces area-specific ratings, and a general impression produces contaminated ratings that miss both strengths and weaknesses.

### Leniency, Severity, And Central Tendency That Make Ratings Meaningless

Managers who rate everyone high (leniency), everyone low (severity), or everyone in the middle (central tendency) — making ratings meaningless for differentiation — because the manager who is lenient gives everyone a top rating, so top ratings lose their meaning, and the manager who is severe gives everyone a low rating, so low ratings lose their meaning, and the manager who clusters in the middle gives everyone an average rating, so no one is differentiated, and in all three cases the ratings cannot be used for promotion, compensation, or development decisions because they do not distinguish between people. The trap is that leniency feels kind, severity feels rigorous, and central tendency feels safe, so each manager gravitates to their tendency, but a rating scale where everyone gets the same rating is a scale that provides no information, and a scale that provides no information is useless for decisions. Calibrate across managers, examining distributions for leniency, severity, and central tendency, because calibration reveals the tendencies that the individual manager cannot see in their own ratings, and revealed tendencies can be corrected.

### Feedback Without Specifics That Provides No Direction

Saying "good job" or "needs work" without examples — feedback that contains no specific behavior, no specific impact, and no specific direction — provides no guidance for improvement, because the person does not know what they did well or poorly, only that the manager has a general impression, and a general impression cannot be acted on, repeated, or corrected. The trap is that vague feedback feels safer because it avoids the specificity that might provoke disagreement, but safety is purchased with uselessness, and vague feedback is feedback that changes nothing. Write specific, evidence-based feedback that cites examples and connects to goals and criteria, because specific feedback tells the person exactly what to continue or change, and vague feedback tells them only that the manager has an opinion.

### Surprise Negative Feedback In The Review That Is Unfair And Indefensible

Raising performance problems for the first time in the annual review — issues that were never raised during the period, appearing for the first time in the review — is unfair and often indefensible, because the person was never given the chance to improve that timely feedback provides, and the surprise issue in the review feels arbitrary and punitive, and the organization cannot defend a rating based on issues that were never communicated, because the record shows that the person was never told. The trap is that raising issues during the period feels uncomfortable, so they are deferred to the review, but deferral converts a manageable conversation into an unfair surprise, and the surprise is the evidence of unfairness. Provide ongoing feedback throughout the period, so that the review contains no surprises, because a review with no surprises is fair and defensible, and a review with surprises is neither.

### Avoiding Difficult Messages That Leaves Problems Unaddressed

Softening or omitting negative feedback to avoid conflict — delivering feedback so gently that the person does not realize there is a problem, or omitting the negative entirely to spare feelings — leaves the person without direction and the performance problem unaddressed, because the person who does not hear the feedback cannot act on it, and the problem persists and often worsens, and the manager who avoided the difficult conversation faces the same problem next year, now worse. The trap is that softened feedback feels kinder, so it is delivered softly, but kindness that leaves the person unaware of a problem is not kindness, it is avoidance, and avoidance produces a person who is blindsided when the consequences of the unaddressed problem arrive. Deliver difficult messages honestly and respectfully, with specific evidence and a focus on development, because honest feedback gives the person the chance to improve, and softened or omitted feedback takes that chance away.

### Ratings Without Calibration That Produce Systemic Unfairness

Allowing each manager's personal standards to govern — each manager using their own definition of what each rating level means — makes cross-team comparisons meaningless and produces systemic unfairness, because a "4" from a lenient manager is not the same as a "4" from a severe manager, and a person rated "3" by a severe manager may have performed better than a person rated "4" by a lenient manager, and compensation and promotion decisions based on uncalibrated ratings reward the lucky employees of lenient managers and punish the unlucky employees of severe managers. The trap is that each manager's ratings feel consistent within their team, so calibration seems unnecessary, but consistency within a team is meaningless if the standards differ across teams, and uncalibrated ratings produce decisions that are unfair in effect regardless of intent. Calibrate ratings across managers and teams before finalizing, because calibration aligns standards, and aligned standards are the foundation of fair cross-team comparisons.

### Undocumented Ratings That Cannot Be Defended

Assigning ratings without recording the evidence and rationale — ratings based on impression with no documented support — leaves the organization unable to defend the decision if challenged, because a rating without documented evidence is a rating that rests on the manager's say-so, and say-so is not a defense, and the person who challenges the rating, the lawyer who depooses the manager, and the regulator who investigates the pattern all ask for the evidence, and evidence that was not documented cannot be produced. The trap is that documentation feels like bureaucracy, so it is deferred, but the rating is a decision with consequences (compensation, promotion, termination), and a decision with consequences that is not documented is a decision that cannot be defended. Document ratings with their supporting evidence and rationale, because the documentation is the defense, and an undocumented rating is an indefensible rating.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is the rating based on documented evidence collected throughout the period, countering the recency effect?
- [ ] Are ratings assessed against pre-defined criteria and goals, with each area evaluated separately before an overall rating?
- [ ] Have calibration sessions aligned standards across managers and teams, with the distribution examined for leniency, severity, or central tendency?
- [ ] Is the feedback specific, balanced, and actionable, citing examples and connecting to goals and criteria?
- [ ] Has the review conversation been prepared for, with anticipated reactions and a plan for delivering difficult messages?
- [ ] Are difficult messages delivered honestly and respectfully, with no surprises about issues not previously raised?
- [ ] Is the review documented with ratings, evidence, feedback, employee response, agreed actions, and commitments?
- [ ] Have rating patterns been examined for demographic or systemic bias, with disparities investigated?
