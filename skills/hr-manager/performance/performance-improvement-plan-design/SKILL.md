# Performance Improvement Plan Design

A performance improvement plan (PIP) is a formal intervention for an employee whose performance has fallen below expectations. It can serve two purposes: a genuine effort to help the person improve and succeed, or a documented path toward separation when improvement is unlikely. The distinction matters profoundly. A PIP that is presented as development but is actually a predetermined exit is dishonest, damages trust across the organization, and can appear retaliatory or discriminatory. A PIP that is genuine but poorly designed gives the person no real chance to improve, producing failure that may be the plan's fault rather than the person's. Agents often treat the PIP as a formality and miss that its design, fairness, and documentation determine whether it develops people or exposes the organization to risk.

Use this skill before designing a PIP, placing someone on one, or evaluating whether a PIP process is fair and defensible. The goal is to make the agent design a PIP that is clear, genuine, supported, and documented, serving either as a fair opportunity to improve or as a defensible record supporting separation.

## Core Rules

### Clarify The Purpose Before Designing The Plan

Before designing a PIP, be honest about its purpose. Is this a genuine effort to help the person improve, with a real possibility of success? Or is the performance gap such that separation is the likely outcome, and the PIP is providing due process before that step? The design differs based on the answer, and concealing the purpose from the employee is unethical.

Clarify purpose by:

- assessing whether the performance gap is closable with reasonable effort and support;
- considering whether the issues are skill-based, which are often addressable, or fit-based, which may not be;
- being transparent with the employee about the seriousness of the situation and the consequences of not improving;
- not presenting a PIP as a development opportunity if the decision to separate has already been made;
- consulting with HR and legal counsel to ensure the approach is consistent with policy and law.

A PIP that is a sham, designed to fail, is worse than no PIP. It wastes time, damages morale, and can be used as evidence of bad faith if the separation is challenged.

### Define Specific, Measurable Improvement Expectations

A PIP that says "improve performance" without specifics gives the person no clear target and makes it impossible to assess whether improvement has occurred. The expectations must be concrete and measurable.

Define expectations by:

- stating the specific performance gaps, with examples and evidence;
- defining the level of performance required, in measurable terms where possible;
- setting clear milestones and a timeline, with check-in points;
- ensuring the expectations are reasonable and achievable, not designed to be impossible;
- documenting the expectations in writing, shared with and acknowledged by the employee.

The expectations should be a clear statement of what acceptable performance looks like, not a moving target. The person should know exactly what they need to do to succeed.

### Provide Genuine Support And Resources

A PIP that demands improvement without providing support is unfair and likely to fail. If the organization wants the person to improve, it must invest in their improvement.

Provide support by:

- identifying what the person needs to succeed, whether training, mentoring, clearer direction, tools, or time;
- providing or arranging for those resources, and documenting what was provided;
- assigning a manager or mentor to provide regular guidance and feedback during the PIP;
- removing or addressing obstacles that may be contributing to the performance gap;
- being realistic about the support the organization can provide, and not promising resources that will not materialize.

The level of support signals whether the PIP is genuine. A PIP with no support is a signal that the organization has already given up.

### Set A Reasonable Timeline

The timeline must be long enough for genuine improvement to occur and be demonstrated, but not so long that it becomes an open-ended limbo. The right length depends on the role and the nature of the performance gap.

Set a timeline by:

- considering how long it would take a person to learn and demonstrate the required improvement;
- typically setting PIPs of 30, 60, or 90 days, depending on the complexity of the role and the gaps;
- defining interim milestones within the period, so progress can be assessed before the end;
- being clear about what happens at the end: successful completion, extension, or consequences;
- avoiding timelines that are too short to allow improvement, which signal a predetermined outcome.

A two-week PIP for a complex role is almost certainly designed to fail. A six-month PIP may be too long to maintain focus and urgency. Calibrate to the situation.

### Conduct Regular Check-Ins During The PIP

A PIP is not set-and-forget. The person needs regular feedback on their progress, and the manager needs to assess whether the plan is working or needs adjustment.

Conduct check-ins by:

- scheduling regular meetings, weekly or biweekly, throughout the PIP period;
- reviewing progress against the specific milestones and expectations;
- providing feedback on what is improving and what still needs work;
- documenting each check-in, including the date, discussion, progress assessment, and any adjustments;
- addressing obstacles or additional support needs that arise during the period;
- being willing to adjust the plan if circumstances change, while maintaining the core expectations.

Check-ins demonstrate that the organization is engaged in the person's success, provide a record of the process, and ensure that the final assessment is based on documented progress, not a single end-point judgment.

### Document Everything Thoroughly

The PIP and its execution must be documented meticulously. If the person improves, the documentation records the success. If they do not, and separation follows, the documentation is the evidence that the organization provided a fair opportunity.

Document:

- the performance gaps that led to the PIP, with specific examples and evidence;
- the improvement expectations, timeline, and support provided;
- the employee's acknowledgment of the plan;
- each check-in, with dates, discussions, and progress assessments;
- any adjustments to the plan and the rationale;
- the final assessment and outcome, with evidence;
- any subsequent actions, whether successful completion, extension, or separation.

Documentation is not about building a case against the person. It is about creating a fair and transparent record that protects both the employee, by ensuring commitments are honored, and the organization, by demonstrating due process.

### Ensure Consistency And Non-Discrimination

PIP decisions must be applied consistently and must not discriminate. A PIP that is applied to one person for behavior that is tolerated in others is unfair and potentially discriminatory.

Ensure consistency by:

- examining whether similar performance issues in other employees have been addressed similarly;
- being alert to patterns where PIPs are disproportionately applied to protected groups;
- ensuring that the decision to place someone on a PIP is based on performance, not on personality conflicts, retaliation, or discriminatory factors;
- documenting the objective basis for the PIP decision;
- involving HR in the decision to place someone on a PIP, to provide oversight and consistency.

If a PIP follows protected activity, such as a complaint of discrimination or harassment, it may appear retaliatory regardless of its merit. Document the performance basis carefully and consult HR and legal counsel.

### Handle The Outcome Fairly

At the end of the PIP, the outcome must be determined fairly, based on the documented evidence, not on a predetermined conclusion.

Handle outcomes by:

- assessing whether the specific improvement expectations were met, using the documented evidence;
- if improvement occurred, formally concluding the PIP and acknowledging the success, while maintaining normal performance expectations going forward;
- if partial improvement occurred, considering whether an extension is warranted and fair;
- if improvement did not occur, proceeding with the consequences outlined in the plan, which may include reassignment or separation;
- communicating the outcome clearly and documenting the decision and rationale.

A person who successfully completes a PIP should not be stigmatized or held to a different standard going forward. The PIP is concluded, and normal performance management resumes.

## Common Traps

### The Sham PIP

Designing a PIP with impossible expectations or no support, as a predetermined path to separation. Be genuine or be honest about the purpose.

### Vague Improvement Expectations

Saying "improve performance" without specifics, making assessment impossible and the plan unfair. Define measurable expectations.

### No Support Provided

Demanding improvement without providing training, resources, or guidance, setting the person up to fail. Provide genuine support.

### Set-And-Forget PIP

Placing someone on a PIP and not checking in until the end, providing no guidance or feedback during the period. Conduct regular check-ins.

### Timeline Designed To Fail

Setting a timeline too short for genuine improvement, signaling a predetermined outcome. Set a reasonable timeline.

### PIP As Retaliation

Placing someone on a PIP following a complaint or protected activity, which may appear retaliatory. Document the objective performance basis and consult HR.

### Inconsistent Application

Applying PIPs to some employees for behavior tolerated in others, creating unfairness and potential discrimination. Ensure consistency.

### No Documentation

Conducting a PIP without documenting the expectations, support, check-ins, and outcomes, leaving no defensible record. Document everything.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is the purpose of the PIP clarified honestly, with transparency to the employee about the seriousness and consequences?
- [ ] Are the improvement expectations specific, measurable, reasonable, and documented, with clear milestones and timeline?
- [ ] Is genuine support provided, including training, resources, guidance, and obstacle removal, with the support documented?
- [ ] Is the timeline reasonable for the role and the gaps, with interim milestones and clarity about end outcomes?
- [ ] Are regular check-ins conducted and documented, with feedback on progress and adjustments as needed?
- [ ] Is the entire PIP process documented, including gaps, expectations, support, check-ins, adjustments, and final assessment?
- [ ] Has the PIP decision been examined for consistency and non-discrimination, with HR involved and objective basis documented?
- [ ] Is the outcome determined fairly based on documented evidence, with successful completion acknowledged and consequences applied consistently?
