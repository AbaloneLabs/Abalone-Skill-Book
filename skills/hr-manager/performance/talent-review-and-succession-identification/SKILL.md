# Talent Review And Succession Identification

Talent reviews and succession planning identify who is ready for bigger roles, who needs development, and who is at risk of leaving. These assessments shape promotion, development investment, and retention focus, and their consequences are significant for individuals and the organization. A talent review that relies on manager impressions, excludes people based on unconscious bias, or identifies successors who are not genuinely ready produces failed promotions, lost talent, and leadership gaps when key people leave. Agents often treat talent identification as a discussion of favorites and miss that without structured criteria, calibration, and evidence, it becomes a popularity contest that perpetuates homogeneity and misses potential.

Use this skill before conducting a talent review, identifying high-potential employees, building a succession pipeline, or evaluating the fairness of talent decisions. The goal is to make the agent identify and develop talent based on structured criteria, calibrated assessment, and evidence, aligned with future organizational needs.

## Core Rules

### Start From Future Business Needs, Not Past Performance

Succession planning answers the question: who can lead this organization in the future? The answer depends on what the organization will need, not just on who has performed well in the current structure. Future strategy, market changes, and organizational evolution should shape the leadership profile sought.

Start from future needs by:

- identifying the critical roles where succession is needed, based on business strategy and risk;
- defining the competencies and experiences required for those roles in the future context;
- assessing not just current performance but potential, learning agility, and adaptability;
- considering whether the future role requires different capabilities than the current one;
- avoiding the assumption that the best performer in the current role is automatically the best successor.

The best technical specialist may not be the best people leader. The best operator may not be the best strategist for a changing market. Assess against future requirements.

### Use Structured Criteria For Potential And Readiness

High potential and readiness for advancement are not the same as current performance. A person may be a strong performer in their current role without having the potential or readiness for a significantly different or larger role. Conflating the two leads to failed promotions.

Use structured criteria by:

- distinguishing current performance from future potential, and assessing both;
- defining what "potential" means in the organization, such as learning agility, aspiration, engagement, and ability to operate at higher levels of complexity;
- defining "readiness" as the assessment of how close the person is to being able to succeed in the target role;
- using a consistent framework, such as a 9-box of performance versus potential, with clear definitions of each dimension;
- ensuring that the criteria are understood and applied consistently by all assessors.

A 9-box or similar tool is only useful if the dimensions are clearly defined and the placement is based on evidence, not impression. Without definition, it becomes a subjective sorting exercise.

### Calibrate Talent Assessments Across Managers

As with performance ratings, talent assessments made by individual managers reflect personal perspectives and relationships. Without calibration, talent identification is inconsistent and biased.

Calibrate by:

- convening a talent review session where managers present and discuss their assessments;
- challenging placements, particularly high-potential designations, asking for the evidence;
- comparing the distribution of talent ratings across teams for patterns of leniency or bias;
- ensuring that high-potential identification is not concentrated in the image of the current leadership;
- documenting the calibration discussion and any adjustments.

Calibration surfaces people who may be invisible to senior leadership because their manager is less vocal, or whose potential is seen differently by different assessors. It broadens the talent pool and reduces bias.

### Base Assessments On Evidence, Not Impression

Talent assessments based on "I have a good feeling about her" or "he has leadership material" are subjective and unreliable. They should be grounded in evidence of behavior, results, and demonstrated capability.

Base on evidence by:

- drawing on performance data, feedback from multiple sources, and examples of behavior in challenging situations;
- looking for evidence of the specific potential criteria, such as times the person adapted to ambiguity, learned a new area quickly, or influenced beyond their authority;
- using structured assessments, such as 360-degree feedback, assessment centers, or behavioral interviews, for higher-stakes decisions;
- documenting the specific evidence that supports each assessment;
- being honest about gaps in evidence, and seeking additional input rather than filling gaps with assumption.

Evidence-based assessment is more accurate and more defensible. It also helps the person understand what they need to demonstrate to be seen as ready for advancement.

### Identify And Mitigate Bias In Talent Identification

Talent identification is particularly susceptible to bias, because potential is more subjective than performance, and because people tend to see potential in those who resemble themselves. Without active mitigation, talent pools become homogeneous and qualified people are overlooked.

Mitigate bias by:

- examining the demographic composition of the high-potential pool and succession slate, and investigating homogeneity;
- being aware of affinity bias, where managers favor those similar to themselves;
- being aware of "mini-me" bias, where successors are selected in the image of the current leader;
- ensuring that criteria do not inadvertently disadvantage certain groups, such as requiring relocation when that may disproportionately affect some employees;
- actively seeking out talent that may be less visible, such as those in remote locations, less prominent roles, or who are less skilled at self-promotion.

Homogeneity in the talent pipeline becomes homogeneity in future leadership. Active effort to identify and develop diverse talent is essential for both fairness and organizational effectiveness.

### Build Development Plans For Identified Talent

Identifying talent without developing it produces a succession chart that looks reassuring but has no substance behind it. Each identified successor needs a development plan that builds the readiness the assessment identified as lacking.

Build development plans by:

- identifying the specific gaps between the person's current capabilities and the target role requirements;
- designing development experiences that close those gaps, such as stretch assignments, lateral moves, mentoring, or formal development programs;
- ensuring the plan is actionable, with specific experiences, timelines, and accountability;
- reviewing progress regularly and adjusting the plan;
- not overloading high-potential employees with development on top of full workloads, which produces burnout rather than growth.

A succession plan that lists names without development plans is a wish list. The development investment is what turns potential into readiness.

### Assess And Address Retention Risk

Talent reviews should identify not only who is ready for advancement but who is at risk of leaving. Losing key talent, particularly those identified as high-potential, undermines the succession pipeline.

Assess retention risk by:

- identifying key people whose departure would create significant difficulty;
- assessing indicators of flight risk, such as disengagement, market demand for their skills, or lack of growth opportunities;
- understanding each person's motivations, career aspirations, and satisfaction;
- developing targeted retention strategies, such as growth opportunities, recognition, compensation review, or role adjustment;
- addressing systemic issues that drive turnover, not just individual cases.

Retention is not about preventing all turnover, which is natural and sometimes healthy. It is about retaining the talent the organization needs, by understanding and addressing the factors that would drive them to leave.

### Revisit And Update The Succession Plan Regularly

A succession plan created once and filed away is quickly outdated. People develop, leave, or change aspirations; business needs evolve; and new talent emerges.

Revisit by:

- reviewing the succession plan at least annually, and more frequently for the most critical roles;
- updating readiness assessments based on development progress and new evidence;
- adding newly identified talent and removing those who are no longer viable successors;
- adjusting for changes in business strategy or organizational structure;
- ensuring that the plan reflects current reality, not a snapshot from a previous year.

A living succession plan is a management tool. A static one is a document that creates false confidence.

## Common Traps

### Promoting The Best Performer Automatically

Assuming the top performer in the current role is the best successor, when the future role requires different capabilities. Assess against future needs.

### Potential Confused With Performance

Treating strong current performance as evidence of high potential, without assessing learning agility and adaptability. Distinguish the two.

### Impression-Based Assessment

Identifying high-potential talent based on gut feeling or personal chemistry, without evidence. Use structured criteria and documented evidence.

### No Calibration

Allowing each manager's personal view to determine talent identification, producing inconsistency and bias. Calibrate across managers.

### Homogeneous Talent Pools

Allowing affinity and mini-me bias to concentrate high-potential identification in the image of current leadership. Actively mitigate bias and seek diverse talent.

### Names Without Development

Listing successors without development plans, creating a succession chart with no substance. Build actionable development for each identified talent.

### Ignoring Retention Risk

Identifying talent without assessing or addressing flight risk, losing the very people the plan depends on. Assess and address retention.

### The Static Succession Plan

Creating the plan once and not updating it, so it becomes outdated and misleading. Revisit regularly.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Does succession planning start from future business needs and the competencies required for future roles, not just past performance?
- [ ] Are potential and readiness assessed using structured, clearly defined criteria, distinct from current performance?
- [ ] Are talent assessments calibrated across managers, with placements challenged and evidence required?
- [ ] Are assessments based on documented evidence of behavior, results, and demonstrated capability, not impression?
- [ ] Has bias been actively mitigated, with the demographic composition of the talent pool examined and diverse talent actively sought?
- [ ] Does each identified successor have an actionable development plan targeting specific gaps, with progress reviewed?
- [ ] Is retention risk assessed for key talent, with targeted strategies to address flight risk and systemic turnover drivers?
- [ ] Is the succession plan revisited and updated at least annually, reflecting current reality rather than a past snapshot?
