# Performance Management And Evaluation

Performance management is the system through which an organization sets expectations, measures contribution, develops people, and makes decisions about pay, promotion, and separation. It is also one of the most legally exposed areas of HR, because performance ratings and documentation are the primary evidence in disputes over promotions, pay equity, and terminations. Agents often treat performance management as an annual rating exercise and miss that it is a continuous process whose real output is behavior change, capability growth, and a defensible record of expectations and outcomes.

Use this skill before designing a performance process, setting objectives, calibrating ratings, running a review cycle, managing underperformance, or advising a leader on a difficult performance decision. The goal is to make the agent build a fair, consistent, well-documented process that improves performance while producing evidence that can withstand scrutiny.

## Core Rules

### Treat Performance Management As Continuous, Not Annual

A once-a-year review is not performance management; it is a historical autopsy. By the time the annual review arrives, the behaviors it describes are months old, the feedback is too late to change anything, and the rating feels arbitrary because the ongoing conversation never happened.

Build a continuous process:

- goal setting at the start of the period, with alignment to team and business objectives;
- regular check-ins, at minimum monthly, where progress, blockers, and adjustments are discussed;
- real-time feedback tied to specific events, both reinforcing and corrective, given close to the behavior;
- mid-period reviews to recalibrate goals that have shifted and surface concerns early;
- a formal review that synthesizes the ongoing conversation rather than introducing surprises.

If the formal review contains feedback the employee is hearing for the first time, the process has failed. The review should confirm and formalize what has already been discussed.

### Set Clear, Measurable Objectives Aligned To Business Goals

Performance cannot be managed against expectations that were never defined. Objectives create the standard against which contribution is measured.

Effective objectives:

- connect to team and business goals so the employee understands why their work matters;
- are specific and measurable, with clear indicators of success and quality;
- distinguish outcome goals from behavioral goals, since both matter;
- are achievable but stretching, calibrated to role and level;
- define what is in the employee's control versus what depends on factors outside it;
- are documented and agreed at the start of the period, not constructed retroactively;
- are updated when business conditions change, with the change recorded.

Vague objectives like "be a team player" or "drive results" cannot be evaluated fairly and invite bias, because the manager will fill the ambiguity with subjective impression. See the workforce-planning skill for aligning individual goals to business drivers.

### Separate Performance From Potential And From Personality

These are distinct dimensions and conflating them distorts decisions.

Distinguish:

- performance: what the employee delivered in the current role, against current objectives;
- potential: capability and readiness for broader or more senior scope;
- behaviors: how the work was done, including collaboration, judgment, and values alignment;
- personality and style: preferences and demeanor, which should not be rated as performance unless they directly affect job results or team effectiveness.

Rating someone low because they are quiet, or high because they are likable, is not performance management. Rate what the role requires and what the employee delivered.

### Use Multiple Sources Of Evidence

A single manager's view is incomplete and biased by proximity, recency, and personal dynamic. Strengthen evaluation with evidence from multiple sources.

Gather:

- the employee's self-assessment against their objectives;
- input from peers and cross-functional partners through structured feedback, not informal gossip;
- stakeholder feedback for roles with external or client-facing scope;
- objective output metrics where they exist and are reliable;
- the manager's own observations, documented contemporaneously;
- 360-degree feedback for development and behavioral assessment, used carefully and with context.

Calibrate the weight of each source. Peer feedback reflects relationship dynamics as much as performance. Metrics can be gamed or distorted by factors outside the employee's control. Triangulate rather than rely on any single source.

### Calibrate Ratings Across Managers To Ensure Consistency

Without calibration, the same performance can receive different ratings depending on who the manager is. This destroys fairness, pay equity, and the credibility of the entire system.

Run calibration sessions where:

- managers present proposed ratings with the evidence behind them;
- ratings are compared across teams at similar levels and roles;
- managers challenge each other on standards and evidence;
- outliers, both high and low, are tested for justification;
- rating distribution is reviewed for adverse impact across protected groups;
- final ratings are adjusted based on the discussion and the rationale recorded.

Calibration is not about forcing a curve. It is about ensuring that a "meets expectations" means the same thing regardless of team. Without it, the rating is the manager's opinion, not an organizational standard.

### Check For Bias And Adverse Impact In Ratings

Performance ratings are a major driver of pay, promotion, and retention decisions. If those ratings carry bias, every downstream decision inherits it.

Review:

- rating distributions by gender, race, age, disability status, and other protected characteristics;
- whether subjective criteria are disproportionately driving lower ratings for certain groups;
- whether behavioral feedback differs in tone or specificity across groups, such as personality-based criticism directed more at one group;
- whether managers rate employees who are similar to them more favorably;
- whether employees returning from leave receive lower ratings due to absence rather than performance;
- whether accommodation users are rated on the impact of their accommodation rather than their performance.

Where adverse impact appears, investigate the cause: the criteria, the manager, the process, or the evidence. Do not assume the rating is correct simply because a manager produced it. See the diversity skill for broader analysis.

### Manage Underperformance Through A Structured, Documented Process

Addressing poor performance is where performance management becomes highest-risk. Done well, it either improves performance or builds a defensible basis for separation. Done poorly, it creates legal exposure and destroys trust.

When managing underperformance:

- identify the specific gap between expectations and performance, with examples and dates;
- give clear, direct feedback in a private conversation, not through hints or gradual distancing;
- document the conversation, the expectations, and the agreed actions;
- provide support: training, resources, clearer priorities, removal of obstacles;
- set a defined improvement period with specific, measurable targets;
- hold regular check-ins during the improvement period to track progress;
- document progress or continued shortfall contemporaneously;
- make a clear decision at the end: performance improved and sustained, or separation initiated.

A performance improvement plan is a tool, not a ritual. It should genuinely intend to help the employee improve, not be a paperwork preamble to a pre-decided termination. If the decision is already made, a PIP used as theater undermines the process and can be evidence of bad faith. Coordinate with the separation skill when underperformance may lead to termination.

### Connect Performance To Development, Not Only To Consequences

If performance management only produces ratings and consequences, employees experience it as judgment and gaming. The developmental purpose must be real.

For each employee:

- identify strengths to build on, not only gaps to close;
- connect performance insights to development goals and learning;
- discuss career aspirations and how current performance supports or hinders them;
- provide stretch opportunities calibrated to readiness;
- recognize and reinforce progress, not only flag shortfalls.

Performance management that develops people retains them. Performance management that only judges them drives attrition of exactly the people the organization wants to keep. See the development skill for the career and succession connection.

### Document Consistently So Decisions Are Defensible

Performance documentation is the evidence the organization will rely on if a decision is challenged. It must be created during the process, not reconstructed after a dispute.

Maintain:

- the objectives set at the start of the period;
- contemporaneous notes from check-ins and feedback conversations;
- the self-assessment, peer feedback, and manager assessment;
- calibration discussion outcomes and rating rationale;
- any performance improvement plan, with targets, support, check-ins, and outcomes;
- the final rating and the basis for it.

Documentation should be specific, factual, and dated. "Not performing well" is not documentation. "Missed the Q2 delivery deadline by three weeks due to unflagged scope issues, despite reminders on May 10 and May 24" is documentation. Vague or missing records make a fair process look indefensible.

## Common Traps

### The Annual Review Surprise

Delivering all feedback once a year, including negative feedback the employee never heard before, is unfair, unactionable, and a primary driver of disengagement and disputes.

### Objectives Set And Forgotten

Goals defined in January and never revisited become irrelevant by midyear, yet may still drive the rating. Revisit and adjust objectives as conditions change.

### Rating Personality Instead Of Performance

Rewarding charisma or penalizing introversion rather than evaluating contribution and job-relevant behavior introduces bias and rewards the wrong things.

### No Calibration Across Managers

Without calibration, a lenient manager's "exceeds" and a strict manager's "meets" describe the same performance, destroying pay equity and credibility.

### Using A PIP As Termination Theater

A PIP designed to fail, with no real support or intention to improve, is transparent to the employee and undermines the integrity of the process. It can also be evidence of pretext if the termination is later challenged.

### Ignoring Adverse Impact In Ratings

If ratings differ systematically by protected group and no one investigates, every pay, promotion, and termination decision inherits the bias.

### Feedback That Is Vague Or Undated

"Needs to improve communication" with no examples, dates, or specifics cannot be acted on by the employee or defended by the organization.

### Penalizing Leave Or Accommodation Through Ratings

Lowering a rating because someone was on protected leave, or rating the effect of an accommodation rather than performance, is discrimination. Adjust expectations and evaluate fairly.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is performance management a continuous process with goal setting, regular check-ins, real-time feedback, and a formal review that synthesizes rather than surprises?
- [ ] Are objectives specific, measurable, aligned to business goals, documented, and updated when conditions change?
- [ ] Are performance, potential, behaviors, and personality distinguished rather than conflated?
- [ ] Does evaluation draw on multiple evidence sources, with the weight of each calibrated?
- [ ] Are ratings calibrated across managers so the same standard applies regardless of team?
- [ ] Are rating distributions checked for adverse impact across protected groups, with causes investigated?
- [ ] Is underperformance managed through a structured, documented process with clear expectations, genuine support, measurable targets, and a fair decision?
- [ ] Does performance management connect to development, strengths, career aspirations, and growth, not only consequences?
- [ ] Is documentation specific, factual, dated, and created contemporaneously so decisions are defensible?
- [ ] Are employees on leave or using accommodations rated on performance, not on absence or accommodation impact?
