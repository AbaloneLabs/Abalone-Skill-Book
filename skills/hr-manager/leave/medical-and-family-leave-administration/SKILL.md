# Medical And Family Leave Administration

Medical and family leave administration is a compliance-heavy domain where errors create direct legal liability. The most common failures are not recognizing leave eligibility, demanding more medical information than the law allows, counting leave improperly, denying intermittent leave, or treating leave-related absences as performance issues. The agent must understand that leave rights are employee protections, that the employer's role is to administer them faithfully, and that interference with leave rights, or retaliation for taking leave, creates liability regardless of intent. Leave administration requires precision, consistency, and documentation.

Use this skill when administering medical, family, or parental leave. The goal is to make the agent administer leave correctly, protect employee rights, and avoid interference or retaliation.

## Core Rules

### Determine Eligibility Accurately And Promptly

Leave eligibility depends on specific criteria that must be assessed accurately. Do not assume ineligibility based on tenure, hours, or location without checking.

- Assess whether the employee meets the tenure and hours-worked requirements for the applicable leave law.
- Assess whether the employer is covered by the applicable leave law, based on size and jurisdiction.
- Assess whether the reason for leave qualifies: serious health condition, birth or adoption, family member's serious health condition, qualifying exigency, or military caregiver leave.
- Respond to leave requests promptly, providing the eligibility determination and the rights and obligations notice.

### Manage Medical Certification Within Legal Limits

Employers may require medical certification to support leave for a serious health condition, but the certification process is tightly regulated. Demanding more than the law allows is interference.

- Provide the certification form and allow the employee at least 15 calendar days to return it.
- Request only the information the law permits: the date the condition began, the expected duration, and the medical facts supporting the condition. Do not request diagnosis, treatment details, or confidential medical information beyond what is permitted.
- If the certification is incomplete or insufficient, specify in writing what additional information is needed and give the employee an opportunity to cure.
- Do not contact the employee's healthcare provider directly without the employee's permission; use a healthcare provider, HR professional, or other designated official, not the employee's direct supervisor.
- Recertify only at the intervals permitted by law, typically every 30 days for ongoing conditions, unless circumstances change.

### Designate Leave Clearly And Count It Accurately

Leave designation and counting errors lead to employees being denied leave they are entitled to or being given more than they are owed. Designate and count precisely.

- Designate qualifying leave in writing, prospectively, within the timeframes required by law.
- Count leave accurately against the employee's entitlement, using the correct measurement period and method.
- For intermittent leave, count only the hours actually taken as leave, based on the employee's usual schedule.
- Do not count time that should not be counted, such as time the employee would not otherwise have been scheduled to work.
- Track the leave balance and communicate it to the employee.

### Handle Intermittent And Reduced-Schedule Leave Correctly

Intermittent and reduced-schedule leave for the employee's or a family member's serious health condition, or for a qualifying exigency, is protected. Mishandling intermittent leave is a common source of liability.

- Approve intermittent leave for chronic conditions, treatments, or episodic conditions that qualify, without requiring a full-day absence.
- Accept the employee's reporting of intermittent leave, and do not require certification for each instance if the condition is already certified.
- Do not treat intermittent leave absences as performance issues, counting them against attendance or using them in discipline.
- Manage intermittent leave to prevent abuse through recertification and, in limited cases, second opinions, not through denial or discipline.

### Coordinate Leave With Paid Time Off And Benefits

Leave is typically unpaid under the core medical leave law, but it interacts with paid leave, disability benefits, and health benefits in specific ways.

- Allow or require the employee to substitute accrued paid leave for unpaid leave, consistent with the law and policy.
- Coordinate with state or disability benefits where applicable.
- Maintain health benefits during leave on the same terms as if the employee were working.
- Recover benefit premiums only where permitted by law and only if the employee fails to return for reasons other than continuing serious health condition or other qualifying reasons.
- Clearly communicate the benefit obligations and the premium payment process.

### Manage The Return-To-Work Process

Employees returning from leave have restoration rights. The return-to-work process must honor these rights while ensuring the employee can perform the job.

- Restore the employee to the same or an equivalent position, with equivalent pay, benefits, and terms.
- An equivalent position must be virtually identical, not merely similar, in pay, benefits, shift, schedule, location, and responsibilities.
- For employees unable to perform the essential functions, engage in the interactive process for accommodation, as covered in the accommodations skill.
- Do not require a full release for all duties if the employee can perform the essential functions, with or without accommodation; require only a fitness-for-duty certification for the specific position, where permitted.
- Do not delay restoration while awaiting a position to open, unless the employee would not otherwise have been employed.

### Prevent Interference And Retaliation

Interference with leave rights and retaliation for taking leave are prohibited, regardless of the employer's intent. Both create liability.

- Do not discourage, interfere with, or deny leave to which the employee is entitled.
- Do not count leave absences against attendance, performance, or discipline.
- Do not change the employee's terms or conditions of employment because of leave, except as permitted for key employees in limited circumstances.
- Treat leave-related absences as neutral in performance evaluation, not as a negative factor.
- Monitor the employee's treatment after return to detect retaliation.

### Document Consistently And Maintain Records

Leave administration requires consistent documentation for compliance and defense.

- Maintain records of leave eligibility, designation, certification, and counting.
- Maintain medical certifications and correspondence separately from the personnel file, in confidential medical files.
- Document the return-to-work process and restoration.
- Retain records for the period required by law.

## Common Traps

### Assuming Ineligibility Without Checking

Denying leave based on assumptions about tenure, hours, or coverage, without accurate assessment. Determine eligibility on the facts.

### Overbroad Medical Information Requests

Requesting diagnosis, treatment details, or confidential medical information beyond what the law permits. Request only permitted information.

### Counting Leave Improperly

Miscounting intermittent leave, counting non-leave time, or failing to track the balance accurately. Count leave precisely.

### Treating Intermittent Leave As Performance

Counting intermittent leave absences against attendance or using them in discipline. Treat leave absences as neutral.

### Failing To Restore To An Equivalent Position

Returning the employee to a similar but not equivalent position, with different pay, schedule, or responsibilities. Restore to the same or equivalent position.

### Requiring An Overbroad Release

Demanding a full release for all duties rather than fitness for the specific position. Require only what is permitted.

### Retaliating After Return

Changing the employee's treatment, assignments, or evaluation because of the leave. Monitor and prevent retaliation.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is eligibility determined accurately and promptly, based on tenure, hours, coverage, and qualifying reason?
- [ ] Is medical certification managed within legal limits, requesting only permitted information and allowing cure for incomplete certifications?
- [ ] Is leave designated prospectively and counted accurately, including intermittent leave?
- [ ] Is intermittent and reduced-schedule leave handled correctly, without treating absences as performance issues?
- [ ] Is leave coordinated with paid time off, disability benefits, and health benefits, with premiums managed lawfully?
- [ ] Is the employee restored to the same or an equivalent position, with the interactive process engaged if needed?
- [ ] Are interference and retaliation prevented, with leave absences treated as neutral?
- [ ] Are records documented consistently and retained according to the schedule?
