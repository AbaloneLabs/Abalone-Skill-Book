# Remote Work Equipment, Stipend, and Expense Policy

Remote and hybrid work shifts the physical workspace from employer-provided office to employee-provided home, and this shift raises practical and legal questions about equipment, expenses, and stipends. Who provides the computer, the chair, the internet connectivity? Does the organization reimburse home office expenses, and if so, how? Are stipends taxable? A clear equipment and expense policy prevents disputes, ensures remote employees have what they need to be productive, and manages the cost and compliance dimensions. The discipline is to decide the equipment and expense model deliberately, to structure stipends or reimbursements consistently and compliantly, to handle the tax implications, and to manage equipment lifecycle and return.

## Core Rules

### Decide the Equipment Model Deliberately

There are several models for remote work equipment, and the choice should be deliberate. The organization-provided model: the employer supplies the computer, peripherals, and sometimes furniture, maintaining standardization and security. The BYOD or stipend model: the employee uses their own equipment or receives a stipend to acquire it, with the organization defining minimum specifications. The hybrid model: the organization provides the computer and security-critical equipment, while the employee provides furniture and pays for connectivity. Each model has implications for security (organization-provided is more secure), cost (stipends shift cost but may be taxable), standardization (organization-provided ensures consistency), and employee experience. Choose based on the organization's security needs, cost tolerance, and culture, and apply consistently to avoid inequity.

### Structure Stipends or Reimbursements Consistently

If the organization provides financial support for home office expenses, structure it consistently. Options include a flat monthly stipend (simple, predictable, but may over- or under-compensate depending on actual expenses), reimbursement of actual expenses up to a cap (accurate but administratively heavy), or a one-time home office setup allowance (supports initial setup but not ongoing costs). Whatever the structure, apply it consistently across remote employees at the same level, to avoid inequity and disputes. Define what expenses are eligible (internet, phone, furniture, utilities portion) and what documentation is required. Inconsistent or ad hoc stipend decisions — one manager approving a stipend, another not — create inequity that damages trust.

### Handle the Tax Implications of Stipends and Reimbursements

The tax treatment of remote work financial support varies by jurisdiction and by structure, and mishandling creates tax liability for both employee and employer. In the U.S., under the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act, unreimbursed employee home office expenses are generally no longer deductible, making reimbursement more valuable to employees. Reimbursements under an accountable plan (business connection, substantiation, return of excess within a reasonable time) are not taxable to the employee. Flat stipends without substantiation are generally taxable as wages. Some jurisdictions (California, Illinois, and others) require employers to reimburse employees for all necessary business expenses, including a portion of home internet and utilities for remote work. Understand the requirements in each jurisdiction where remote employees work, and structure the financial support to be both compliant and tax-efficient.

### Define Minimum Equipment and Security Specifications

Whether the organization provides equipment or employees use their own, define minimum specifications for security and functionality: computer hardware requirements, operating system currency, antivirus and security software, internet speed and reliability, and physical workspace safety. For organization-provided equipment, these specifications are built into the standard configuration. For BYOD, the specifications are requirements the employee must meet. Enforce the specifications through IT assessment (checking device compliance before remote access is granted) and through policy. Remote equipment that does not meet security specifications creates organizational risk — a personal computer with outdated security accessing company data is a vulnerability. Define, communicate, and enforce the specifications.

### Manage Equipment Lifecycle and Return

Organization-provided remote equipment has a lifecycle: deployment, maintenance, upgrade, and return. Manage this lifecycle deliberately: deploy equipment with an asset record (what was issued, to whom, when), maintain it through remote IT support, upgrade on a defined cycle, and recover it at termination or role change. The return process is often neglected — remote employees who leave may retain organization equipment, creating asset loss and security risk. Build equipment return into the offboarding process, with a clear timeline, shipping logistics (the organization should pay for return shipping), and confirmation of receipt. For equipment that is not returned, have a process for follow-up and, if necessary, deduction from final pay (where legally permitted) or escalation. Equipment lifecycle management protects the organization's assets and data.

### Address Workspace Safety and Workers' Compensation

Remote work does not eliminate the employer's obligations around workspace safety and workers' compensation. A remote employee injured while working may have a workers' compensation claim, and the employer's obligations extend to the home workspace (within the course and scope of work). Address workspace safety through guidance (ergonomic setup, electrical safety, trip hazard awareness) and, where feasible, ergonomic assessment or support. Document the safety guidance provided. When a remote worker reports an injury, investigate as you would an on-site injury, in coordination with workers' compensation counsel. Do not assume that remote work eliminates workplace safety obligations — it relocates them to a setting the employer controls less, which requires clear guidance and careful claim handling.

### Coordinate Across IT, Finance, HR, and Legal

Remote work equipment and expense policy sits at the intersection of IT (equipment, security), finance (stipends, reimbursements, tax), HR (policy, equity, employee experience), and legal (expense reimbursement requirements, tax compliance, workers' compensation). Coordinate across these functions to design and administer the policy coherently. IT defines specifications and manages equipment; finance structures stipends and handles tax; HR owns the policy and equity; legal advises on jurisdictional requirements. Without coordination, the policy develops gaps — IT specifications that finance won't fund, stipends that legal flags as non-compliant, safety guidance that HR hasn't communicated. Bring the functions together for policy design and for periodic review.

## Common Traps

### Equipment Model Chosen By Drift

The organization never decides on an equipment model — it just happens. The first remote hires were told to "use whatever you have" (BYOD by default), later hires received company laptops because IT got nervous about security, and managers in different regions apply different rules. Within a year, the organization has three equipment models operating simultaneously, none documented, and no one knows who has what. The trap is that equipment policy seems like a tactical detail not worth a formal decision, so it accretes through individual ad hoc choices that are impossible to audit or enforce. When a security incident traces back to an unmanaged personal device, the organization discovers it has no equipment model to point to, only a history of uncoordinated decisions. The equipment model must be chosen deliberately, documented, and applied consistently from the start, because retrofitting consistency onto drift is far harder than deciding once.

### The Manager Who Becomes A Stipend Authority

Without a centralized stipend structure, individual managers negotiate equipment support with their remote reports. One manager, eager to accommodate a valued hire, approves a generous home office stipend; another, more cost-conscious, denies the same request. The employees compare notes (they always do), and the organization now has an equity problem: identical roles receive different support based on manager generosity, not organizational policy. The trap is that each manager's decision seems reasonable in isolation — they are responding to their team's needs — but the aggregate creates inequity that damages trust and, where the pattern correlates with a protected characteristic, creates legal exposure. Stipend decisions must be centralized in policy, not delegated to manager discretion, because manager-level decisions cannot see the cross-team pattern they create.

### Taxable Stipends Treated As Tax-Free Perks

A flat monthly stipend for "home office" is paid without substantiation and without tax treatment, because it feels like a reimbursement and finance did not flag it. In reality, a flat stipend without an accountable plan (business connection, substantiation, return of excess) is taxable wages, and the organization has been under-withholding and under-reporting for months or years. The liability compounds: back taxes, penalties, interest, and the cost of amending prior returns. The trap is that the stipend looks and feels like a non-taxable reimbursement to everyone involved — it is for business expenses, after all — so no one questions the treatment until an audit or a sharp-eyed payroll provider raises it. The distinction between an accountable-plan reimbursement (non-taxable) and a flat stipend (taxable) turns on documentation that must be built into the process from the start, not retrofitted after the liability accrues.

### Personal Devices With No Security Floor

An employee works on a personal laptop that has not been updated in two years, runs no antivirus, and is shared with a teenager who downloads games. This device accesses company email, documents, and customer data daily. The organization has no visibility into this risk because it never defined minimum security specifications for BYOD, and the employee has no reason to upgrade because no one told them their device is a liability. The trap is that BYOD shifts the device to the employee but the risk remains with the employer — a breach traced to an unmanaged personal device is the employer's breach, with the employer's notification obligations and the employer's liability. Defining specifications is necessary but not sufficient; they must be enforced through IT assessment before remote access is granted, because an unenforced specification is just a suggestion that the most vulnerable devices will ignore.

### Equipment That Vanishes At Termination

A remote employee resigns. The offboarding checklist includes "return laptop," but there is no shipping logistics, no timeline, no asset confirmation, and no follow-up when nothing arrives. Six months later, IT audits assets and discovers the laptop is gone — along with the phone, the monitor, and the headset. The trap is that remote equipment return is treated as a line item on a checklist rather than a process with logistics and accountability. Unlike an office, where the laptop is handed to IT on the last day, remote return requires shipping (which the organization must arrange and pay for), tracking, and confirmation. Without this, the organization absorbs asset loss silently, and the security risk of an unreturned device with active credentials persists until someone notices — which, without asset tracking, may be never.

### Remote Safety Assumed Away

The organization provides ergonomic chairs and desk assessments in the office but sends remote employees a laptop and nothing else, assuming that home workspace safety is the employee's concern. But workers' compensation coverage follows the work, not the location: an employee injured at their home desk during working hours may have a compensable claim, and the employer's obligation to provide a safe workspace does not vanish because the workspace is remote. The trap is the assumption that "we don't control the home office, so we are not responsible" — which is both legally mistaken and practically harmful, because an employee working at a kitchen counter on a barstool for eight hours is developing an injury the employer will eventually pay for. Remote safety guidance, ergonomic support, and documented safety expectations are not optional courtesies; they are the remote equivalent of the obligations the organization meets without question in the office.

### Policy Built In One Function's Silo

IT writes the equipment policy alone, focusing on security, and mandates company-provided devices — which finance has not budgeted for. Finance writes the stipend policy alone, focusing on cost, and sets stipend amounts that do not cover the equipment IT requires. HR writes the equity language alone, without knowing what IT and finance have already decided. The result is a set of contradictory policies that no one can execute, because each function optimized for its own concern without seeing the others. The trap is that each function's draft looks complete in isolation, so no one realizes the gaps until implementation fails. Remote equipment and expense policy sits at the intersection of IT, finance, HR, and legal, and it must be designed jointly, because the constraints are interlocking and no single function holds them all.

## Self-Check

- Have I chosen the equipment model (organization-provided, BYOD, hybrid) deliberately based on security, cost, standardization, and experience?
- Am I structuring stipends or reimbursements consistently across remote employees at the same level, with defined eligibility and documentation?
- Have I handled the tax implications — accountable plan reimbursements, taxable stipends, jurisdictional expense reimbursement requirements?
- Have I defined and enforced minimum equipment and security specifications, whether organization-provided or BYOD?
- Am I managing the equipment lifecycle — deployment, maintenance, upgrade, and return — with asset records and offboarding recovery?
- Am I addressing workspace safety and workers' compensation for remote employees through guidance, documentation, and careful claim handling?
- Have I coordinated across IT, finance, HR, and legal for coherent policy design and periodic review?
