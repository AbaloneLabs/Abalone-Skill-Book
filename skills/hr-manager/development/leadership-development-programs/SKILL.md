# Leadership Development Programs

Leadership development programs invest significantly in building the leadership pipeline, selecting participants, providing learning experiences, and preparing them for bigger roles. These programs are expensive, visible, and consequential for the careers of those selected and the future of the organization. A well-designed program builds genuine leadership capability and strengthens the pipeline. A poorly designed one credentials participants without developing them, signals that potential is concentrated in a narrow group, or produces graduates who are unprepared for the realities of leadership. Agents often treat leadership development as a curriculum of courses and miss that leadership is developed through experience, challenge, and reflection, with courses as one component, and that selection, experiential design, and follow-through determine whether the investment produces leaders.

Use this skill before designing, selecting participants for, or evaluating a leadership development program. The goal is to make the agent design programs that select fairly, develop through experience as well as learning, and produce measurable leadership capability.

## Core Rules

### Ground The Program In The Organization's Leadership Model

A leadership development program must be grounded in a clear model of what leadership looks like in the organization. Without this foundation, the program teaches generic leadership concepts that may not fit the organization's context, culture, or strategy.

Ground by:

- defining the leadership competencies and behaviors that the organization needs, now and in the future;
- ensuring the program curriculum and experiences target those specific competencies;
- connecting the program to the organization's strategy, values, and culture, so that leadership is developed in context;
- using the competency framework as the basis for assessment, development planning, and evaluation;
- revisiting the model as strategy and context evolve, so the program remains relevant.

A program built on a generic leadership textbook may teach concepts, but it will not develop the specific leaders the organization needs. The model provides the target that all design decisions serve.

### Select Participants Fairly And Transparently

Selection for a leadership development program is a high-stakes decision, as it signals who is considered high-potential and invests organizational resources. Unfair, opaque, or biased selection damages morale, perpetuates homogeneity, and undermines the program's credibility.

Select fairly by:

- using clear, published criteria for selection, based on potential and development readiness, not just current performance;
- involving multiple assessors and a calibration process, to reduce individual bias;
- examining the demographic composition of each cohort, and investigating homogeneity;
- being transparent about the selection process and criteria, so that those not selected understand what they need to develop;
- providing alternative development opportunities for those not selected, so that the program is not the only path to growth.

A program whose participants all resemble current leadership sends a powerful message about who is considered leadership material. Intentional, fair selection broadens the pipeline and signals that the organization develops diverse talent.

### Balance Formal Learning With Experiential Development

Leadership is learned primarily through experience, challenge, and reflection, not through courses. Formal learning provides frameworks, knowledge, and language, but capability is built through application. A program that is all classroom produces graduates who know about leadership but cannot lead.

Balance by:

- using formal learning to introduce frameworks, tools, and concepts, not as the primary development vehicle;
- designing stretch assignments, action learning projects, or rotations that challenge participants to apply learning in real situations;
- providing reflection mechanisms, such as journals, peer discussion, or coaching, that help participants extract learning from experience;
- ensuring that experiences are genuinely developmental, with real stakes, real responsibility, and real feedback;
- sequencing learning and experience so that each reinforces the other.

The 70-20-10 model, suggesting that development comes roughly 70% from experience, 20% from others, and 10% from formal learning, is a useful reminder that the classroom is the smallest component. The program design should reflect this.

### Use Assessment And Feedback For Development

Assessment and feedback are powerful development tools in a leadership program, providing participants with insight into their strengths, gaps, and blind spots. Used well, they accelerate self-awareness and targeted development. Used poorly, they create anxiety or are ignored.

Use assessment by:

- including 360-degree feedback or similar multi-source assessment, to provide candid input on leadership behaviors;
- using personality or style assessments to build self-awareness, while being cautious about over-reliance on any single instrument;
- ensuring that assessment results are used for development, not for evaluation or selection decisions within the program;
- providing coaching or facilitation to help participants interpret and act on feedback;
- creating individual development plans that target the gaps the assessment reveals.

Assessment that is delivered without interpretation or follow-up is wasted. The value comes from the reflection, planning, and action that the assessment prompts.

### Provide Ongoing Support And Accountability

A leadership program that is an event, however intensive, will fade without ongoing support. Development is a process that continues after the formal program ends. Sustained support and accountability convert the program experience into lasting capability.

Provide support by:

- assigning coaches, mentors, or peer groups that continue beyond the formal program;
- building in follow-up sessions, refreshers, or alumni networks that reinforce learning;
- connecting the program to ongoing development planning and performance management;
- holding participants accountable for applying their development, through check-ins with sponsors or managers;
- tracking participants' progression and development over time, not just immediately after the program.

A program that ends with a graduation ceremony and a certificate, with no follow-up, produces a credential. A program with ongoing support produces development. The difference is in the sustained effort.

### Engage Senior Leaders As Sponsors And Teachers

The involvement of senior leaders signals the program's importance and provides participants with exposure to real leadership challenges. Leaders who teach, sponsor, or share their experiences add credibility and relevance that external faculty cannot.

Engage by:

- having senior leaders teach sessions, share their leadership journeys, or sponsor action learning projects;
- ensuring that leader involvement is genuine, not a token appearance, with real engagement and candor;
- connecting participants with leaders as mentors or sponsors, building relationships that support ongoing development;
- using leader involvement to reinforce the connection between the program and the organization's strategy and culture;
- preparing leaders for their role, so that their contribution is valuable rather than perfunctory.

A program taught entirely by external faculty may be polished but disconnected from the organization's reality. Senior leader involvement grounds the program in the organization's actual leadership challenges and builds the relationships that support career growth.

### Measure Outcomes At Multiple Levels

Leadership development programs are significant investments that should be evaluated for effectiveness. Measurement should assess whether the program is producing the intended outcomes, at multiple levels and over time.

Measure by:

- assessing participant reaction and learning, as basic quality indicators;
- evaluating behavior change, through follow-up 360 feedback or manager observation, months after the program;
- tracking participant progression: promotions, role expansions, or increased scope;
- assessing the pipeline impact: whether the program is filling the leadership pipeline and reducing reliance on external hiring;
- examining whether outcomes are distributed equitably across participant groups;
- using findings to improve the program design and delivery.

A program that produces high satisfaction scores but no behavior change or pipeline impact is not effective, regardless of how much participants enjoyed it. Measurement at multiple levels, over time, reveals true effectiveness.

### Avoid Creating An Exclusive In-Group

Leadership programs risk creating a perception of an elite in-group, those selected versus those not, which can damage morale and culture if not managed carefully. The program should develop leaders without creating division.

Avoid by:

- being transparent about selection criteria and process, to reduce perceptions of favoritism;
- providing development opportunities for those not in the program, so that it is not the only path to growth;
- framing the program as one stage in a development journey, not as a permanent designation;
- avoiding labels or status markers that create lasting in-group and out-group distinctions;
- ensuring that program graduates are not automatically promoted or favored, but are assessed on demonstrated capability.

A program that creates a permanent "high-potential" caste alienates those not selected and can produce entitlement in those who are. Development should be inclusive and ongoing, not a one-time designation that divides.

## Common Traps

### Generic Curriculum

Teaching generic leadership concepts unconnected to the organization's leadership model and context. Ground in the organization's model.

### Unfair Or Opaque Selection

Selecting participants through closed processes or based on relationships, perpetuating homogeneity. Select fairly and transparently.

### Classroom-Only Design

Delivering leadership development primarily through courses, producing knowledge without capability. Balance learning with experience.

### Assessment Without Follow-Up

Delivering 360 or personality results without coaching or development planning, wasting the insight. Provide interpretation and action planning.

### Event Without Sustainment

Running the program as an intensive event with no ongoing support, so development fades. Provide ongoing support and accountability.

### No Leader Involvement

Teaching entirely with external faculty, disconnecting the program from organizational reality. Engage senior leaders genuinely.

### Satisfaction-Only Measurement

Evaluating only whether participants liked the program, not whether it changed behavior or filled the pipeline. Measure at multiple levels over time.

### Elite In-Group Creation

Allowing the program to create a permanent high-potential caste that divides the organization. Manage perceptions and provide alternative paths.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is the program grounded in the organization's leadership model, targeting specific competencies and connected to strategy?
- [ ] Is participant selection fair, transparent, and calibrated, with the demographic composition of cohorts examined?
- [ ] Does the program balance formal learning with experiential development through stretch assignments and reflection?
- [ ] Are assessments and feedback used for development, with coaching and individual development plans?
- [ ] Is ongoing support provided through coaching, mentoring, follow-up, and connection to development planning?
- [ ] Are senior leaders genuinely engaged as teachers, sponsors, and mentors, connecting the program to organizational reality?
- [ ] Are outcomes measured at multiple levels, including behavior change, progression, pipeline impact, and equity?
- [ ] Does the program avoid creating an exclusive in-group, with transparent selection and alternative development paths?
