# Workplace Space Planning And Occupancy Strategy

Workplace space planning is an HR-adjacent decision that binds the organization to a footprint, a cost, and a way of working for years, and the agent is usually asked to recommend a space strategy while the actual attendance patterns, the work modes the space must support, and the cost of getting it wrong are all uncertain. The judgment problem is that space is expensive and slow to change, that hybrid work has made historical headcount-per-seat ratios obsolete, and that the right answer depends on how the workforce really uses the office — which most organizations measure poorly or not at all. The harm of a bad decision is a workplace that is half-empty and costly, or cramped and frustrating, or laid out for a way of working that no longer exists.

The agent tends to miss this because space planning is treated as a real-estate or facilities task driven by headcount and budget, rather than as an HR task driven by how work actually happens, and because utilization data is scarce, stale, or self-reported. The harm is organizations that renew leases for space they no longer need, build open plans that destroy focus work, or adopt hot-desking that employees experience as a loss. Use this skill to slow the decision down enough to expose the real attendance and work-mode data, then make the recommendation appropriately conservative when footprint cost, employee experience, and multi-year lock-in are at stake.

## Core Rules

### Base The Strategy On Measured Utilization, Not Headcount
Space decisions must be based on how space is actually used, measured, not on headcount multiplied by a historical ratio. The agent should gather utilization data (badge swipes, seat sensors, meeting room bookings, observational counts) across enough time to capture weekly and seasonal variation, and should distinguish peak, average, and trough attendance. A strategy built on headcount assumes everyone is present every day, which post-hybrid is almost always wrong, and produces overbuilt, empty space.

### Map The Work Modes The Space Must Support
Different work demands different space: focused individual work needs quiet and enclosure, collaboration needs shared and visible space, social and informal interaction needs unstructured space, and learning needs flexible space. The agent should map the mix of work modes the organization's actual work requires, estimate the share of time spent in each, and size space types to that mix. A space planned for one mode (typically collaboration) under-serves the others (typically focus), and the work that cannot get done in the office gets done at home, defeating the purpose of the office.

### Decide The Hybrid Model Before The Space Model
The space strategy must follow the hybrid work model, not precede it. The agent should confirm the organization's hybrid policy (required days, team-anchor days, fully flexible, fully remote, office-first) before sizing space, because each model implies a different attendance pattern and a different space need. Designing space before the hybrid model is fixed produces a workplace built for an assumption that may change, locking the organization into the wrong footprint.

### Choose Seating Strategy To Match Culture And Work
Seating strategy — assigned seats, neighborhoods, hot desking, hoteling — must match the organization's culture and work patterns, not just its density goals. The agent should weigh the cost savings of shared seating against the experience costs (loss of belonging, time spent finding and setting up a desk, hygiene concerns, perceived status loss), and should prefer assigned seats or neighborhoods where stable presence and belonging matter, and shared seating where mobility is genuine. Imposed hot desking is one of the most common drivers of return-to-office resentment.

### Right-Size Meeting And Focus Space, Not Just Desks
Meeting room shortages and focus-space absence are the most common office complaints, and both are under-provisioned when planning focuses on desks. The agent should size meeting rooms to actual booking demand (with variety of sizes, since large rooms are often booked by small groups), provide enclosed focus spaces for concentrated work, and provide informal collaboration zones. A workplace with enough desks but no available meeting rooms or quiet spaces fails the work it was built to support.

### Plan For Flexibility And Future Change
Space decisions lock in for years, and the work patterns they serve will change. The agent should build in flexibility (movable furniture, adaptable rooms, spaces that can shift between modes), avoid over-customized build-outs that resist reconfiguration, and phase commitments where possible (subleasing, shorter leases, modular furniture). A rigid, highly-built-out space cannot adapt when attendance or work patterns shift, and the organization pays for the mismatch.

### Connect Space Decisions To Cost, Experience, And Equity
Space decisions trade off real-estate cost, employee experience, and equity across groups. The agent should make the tradeoffs explicit: footprint reduction saves cost but may degrade experience; hot desking may treat lower-status employees worse than leaders with reserved offices; remote-friendly space decisions may advantage some groups over others. A space strategy that optimizes cost while ignoring experience and equity produces resentment and attrition that cost more than the space saved.

## Common Traps

### Headcount-Based Planning In A Hybrid World
The organization sizes space by multiplying headcount by a pre-hybrid square-footage standard, assumes daily attendance, and overbuilds. The trap is that the formula is well-established. The false signal is that the headcount is known. The harm is a half-empty, costly workplace that proves the office is unnecessary, in a self-fulfilling prophecy driven by planning that ignored actual attendance.

### Measuring Utilization Once And Treating It As Truth
A single utilization study, often during an atypical period, is used to set a multi-year space strategy. The trap is that the data exists. The false signal is that the decision is data-driven. The harm is the strategy is built on a snapshot that misses seasonal, weekly, and policy-driven variation, and the space fits one moment and no other.

### Open Plan Destroying Focus Work
The organization adopts a dense open plan to maximize collaboration and reduce cost, and focus work becomes impossible in the office. The trap is that open plan looks collaborative and modern. The false signal is that the space is flexible. The harm is the work that requires concentration moves home, the office becomes a place only for meetings, and the collaboration the open plan was meant to enable is undermined by the absence of anyone choosing to be there to focus.

### Imposed Hot Desking As A Cost Decision
Hot desking is imposed primarily to cut footprint cost, without regard for culture or work pattern. The trap is that shared seating is efficient. The false signal is that utilization improves. The harm is employees experience the office as a loss of place and status, return-to-office friction rises, and the resentment costs more in attrition than the space saved.

### Under-Provisioning Meeting And Focus Space
Planning focuses on desk count and ignores meeting room and focus space ratios. The trap is that desks are the headline metric. The false signal is that there are enough seats. The harm is meeting rooms are perpetually unavailable, focus work happens in noisy open areas or not at all, and the office fails at the collaboration and concentration it was meant to support.

### Rigid Build-Out Locking In A Work Pattern
The space is highly customized for one work pattern, and when the pattern changes the space cannot adapt. The trap is that the build-out is tailored. The false signal is that the space is purpose-built. The harm is expensive reconfiguration or a workplace that no longer fits how the organization works, because flexibility was sacrificed to customization.

### Cost Optimization Ignoring Experience And Equity
Space decisions optimize real-estate cost and ignore the experience and equity consequences. The trap is that cost savings are measurable. The false signal is that the strategy is disciplined. The harm is resentment, perceived status inequality (leaders keep offices while others hot desk), and attrition that exceeds the savings, because space is experienced as a statement of how the organization values its people.

### Presenting Real-Estate Preference As Workforce Strategy
Space decisions are often judgment calls, but the agent should not present a real-estate preference as a workforce necessity. State what is known (utilization, cost, work modes), what is inferred (future attendance), and what is a values judgment about experience and equity, so leadership decides with the tradeoffs visible.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is the space strategy based on measured utilization across sufficient time, not headcount multiplied by a historical ratio?
- [ ] Has the mix of work modes (focus, collaboration, social, learning) been mapped and used to size space types?
- [ ] Is the hybrid work model confirmed before the space model is designed?
- [ ] Does the seating strategy match the organization's culture and work patterns, with hot desking adopted only where mobility is genuine?
- [ ] Are meeting rooms and focus spaces right-sized to actual demand, with variety of room sizes, not just desk count?
- [ ] Is flexibility built in, with adaptable spaces and phased commitments, to handle future change in attendance and work patterns?
- [ ] Are the cost, experience, and equity tradeoffs made explicit, including how shared seating affects different employee groups?
- [ ] Does the output distinguish space decisions that serve how work happens from those that serve real-estate cost alone?
- [ ] Are the utilization data, work-mode mix, seating model, and space ratios specific enough for facilities and HR to implement without re-deciding?
- [ ] Is uncertainty about future attendance and work-pattern change named, with the tradeoffs that would change the recommendation made explicit?
