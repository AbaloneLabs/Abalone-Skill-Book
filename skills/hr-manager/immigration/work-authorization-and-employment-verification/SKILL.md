# Work Authorization And Employment Verification

Employment eligibility verification is a legal requirement that every employer must fulfill, yet it is also one of the most common sources of immigration-related fines and penalties. The agent must understand that verification is a strict compliance process with specific rules about what documents are acceptable, how they must be reviewed, and how records must be retained, that over-documentation, requesting more or different documents than required, is a form of discrimination, and that verification errors, from late completion to improper retention, create financial liability regardless of whether the employee is actually authorized. Verification requires precision, consistency, and training, not judgment calls.

Use this skill when conducting employment eligibility verification, managing verification systems, or auditing verification records. The goal is to make the agent handle verification with precision and consistency, avoiding both compliance errors and discriminatory practices.

## Core Rules

### Follow The Verification Process Exactly

Employment verification follows a specific process with deadlines, required sections, and document rules. Deviation creates liability.

- Complete verification within the legally required timeframe of the first day of work for the employee section, and within the required period for the employer section.
- Ensure the employee completes their section accurately, including attestation of work authorization and signature.
- The employer or authorized representative must physically or virtually examine the original documents, complete the employer section, and sign the attestation.
- Do not backdate or predate verification forms; complete them within the required windows.

### Accept Only Acceptable Documents From The Correct Lists

Verification rules specify acceptable documents in defined categories. Requesting the wrong documents or too many documents is discriminatory.

- Present the employee with the list of acceptable documents and allow them to choose which to present from List A, or from List B plus List C.
- Do not specify which documents the employee must present, request more documents than required, or refuse otherwise acceptable documents.
- Do not request documents based on the employee's national origin, citizenship status, or appearance, which is documentary discrimination.
- Accept documents that reasonably appear genuine and relate to the person, without requiring investigation beyond reasonable inspection.

### Do Not Re-Verify Unless Required

Unnecessary re-verification of work authorization is discriminatory and creates liability.

- Do not re-verify employees who have presented permanent work authorization documents, such as citizens or lawful permanent residents.
- Re-verify only when the employee's work authorization expires, and only for the specific document that is expiring.
- Do not treat employees differently based on citizenship status beyond what the law requires for verification.

### Manage E-Verify Or Equivalent Systems Correctly

Where electronic verification systems are required or used, follow the specific procedures and protections.

- Enroll in and use electronic verification systems where required by law or contract.
- Follow the system's procedures for case creation, result handling, and resolution of tentative nonconfirmations.
- Do not take adverse action against an employee based solely on a tentative nonconfirmation; follow the resolution process, including the employee's right to contest.
- Protect employee data in the verification system and follow notification requirements.

### Retain Verification Records According To The Rules

Verification records must be retained for specific periods and must be available for inspection. Retention errors create liability.

- Retain verification records for the required duration, typically the later of three years after hire or one year after termination, though rules vary by jurisdiction.
- Store records securely, whether paper or electronic, and organize them for retrieval.
- Do not retain records longer than required without a legitimate business reason, as excess retention creates unnecessary exposure.
- Make records available for government inspection upon proper request.

### Conduct Periodic Internal Audits

Verification errors accumulate over time and across many hires. Periodic audits catch and correct them before an external audit.

- Conduct regular internal audits of verification records, checking for completeness, timeliness, and accuracy.
- Correct errors through proper procedures, such as adding missing information with the current date, not backdating.
- Identify patterns of errors, such as consistent late completion or document issues at specific locations, and address them through training.
- Consider conducting audits under legal privilege, as self-identified errors may need to be reported.

### Train All Personnel Involved In Verification

Verification is often done by managers or administrative staff who are not immigration specialists. Training is essential.

- Train all personnel who complete verification on the process, acceptable documents, and anti-discrimination requirements.
- Provide refresher training periodically, as rules and forms change.
- Emphasize that requesting more or different documents than required is discriminatory, even if well-intentioned.
- Provide resources for questions, so that uncertainty does not lead to ad hoc decisions.

### Avoid Discrimination In The Verification Process

Verification is a frequent source of national origin and citizenship status discrimination, often unintentional.

- Do not request specific documents, more documents than required, or different documents from people who look or sound foreign.
- Do not pre-screen for work authorization before hire beyond what the law permits, such as asking about citizenship status in interviews.
- Treat all hires consistently in the verification process, regardless of national origin, citizenship, or appearance.
- Be aware that overly cautious verification, driven by fear of fines, can itself become discriminatory practice.

## Common Traps

### Over-Documentation

Requesting specific documents or more documents than required, which is discriminatory even when well-intentioned. Allow employee choice from the acceptable lists.

### Late Or Early Completion

Completing verification forms outside the required timeframes, creating technical violations. Follow the deadlines exactly.

### Backdating Or Correcting Improperly

Backdating forms or improperly correcting errors, creating false records. Correct through proper procedures with current dates.

### Unnecessary Re-Verification

Re-verifying permanent work authorization or treating employees differently based on citizenship, which is discriminatory. Re-verify only when authorization expires.

### Adverse Action On Tentative Nonconfirmation

Terminating or taking adverse action based solely on a tentative nonconfirmation, before the resolution process is complete. Follow the process.

### Inconsistent Practices Across Locations

Allowing different verification practices at different locations or by different managers, creating inconsistency and liability. Standardize and train.

### Retention Errors

Retaining records too long, not long enough, or in disorganized form. Follow retention rules and organize for retrieval.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is the verification process followed exactly, with timely completion of all sections by the correct parties?
- [ ] Are only acceptable documents requested from the correct lists, with employee choice respected and no over-documentation?
- [ ] Is re-verification limited to expiring authorization, without discriminating based on citizenship status?
- [ ] Are electronic verification systems used correctly where required, with tentative nonconfirmations resolved through the proper process?
- [ ] Are verification records retained for the required period, stored securely, and available for inspection?
- [ ] Are periodic internal audits conducted to identify and correct errors, potentially under privilege?
- [ ] Are all personnel involved in verification trained on process, acceptable documents, and anti-discrimination requirements?
- [ ] Is the process free from national origin or citizenship discrimination, with consistent treatment of all hires?
