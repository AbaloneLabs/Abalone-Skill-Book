# Immigration Compliance And Government Inquiries

Immigration compliance is subject to government oversight through audits, site visits, and inquiries that can occur with little notice. The agent must understand that compliance is not assessed during normal operations but during these high-pressure events, that the organization's practices are judged by the records it has maintained and the consistency of its processes, and that mishandling a government inquiry, through disorganization, inconsistent responses, or failure to engage counsel, can escalate a routine review into a significant enforcement matter. Immigration compliance requires maintaining audit-ready practices at all times and having a clear response protocol for government contact.

Use this skill when managing immigration compliance programs or responding to government audits, site visits, or inquiries. The goal is to make the agent maintain audit-ready compliance and respond to government contact with preparation, coordination, and professionalism.

## Core Rules

### Maintain Audit-Ready Compliance At All Times

Compliance is not something that can be fixed when an audit notice arrives. Maintain practices as if an audit could occur any day.

- Maintain complete, organized files for all sponsored employees, including petitions, supporting documents, and correspondence.
- Maintain public access files, where required, with all necessary documentation, inspection-ready at all times.
- Maintain verification records complete, timely, and organized for retrieval.
- Track all deadlines, expirations, and obligations systematically, not in individual email inboxes.
- Conduct periodic self-audits, under counsel, to identify and correct issues proactively.

### Establish A Response Protocol Before Contact Occurs

Government inquiries can arrive with little notice. Have a protocol in place so the response is organized, not chaotic.

- Designate a primary point of contact for immigration matters, typically a senior HR leader or immigration specialist.
- Establish a protocol for receiving and routing government inquiries, including immediate notification of legal counsel.
- Train reception, security, and front-line staff on how to handle government agents who arrive for site visits, including verifying credentials and contacting the designated point of contact.
- Define roles: who speaks with agents, who gathers documents, who coordinates with counsel, and who communicates with leadership.

### Engage Legal Counsel Immediately

Government inquiries regarding immigration should involve legal counsel from the first contact. Do not attempt to handle inquiries without counsel.

- Notify immigration counsel immediately upon receiving any audit notice, site visit, or inquiry.
- Do not provide documents, statements, or access without consulting counsel, unless legally required to do so immediately.
- Let counsel guide the scope of the response, the documents to produce, and the communications with agents.
- Maintain privilege over compliance analyses and self-audit results by conducting them through counsel.

### Handle Site Visits Professionally And Within Bounds

Government site visits can be intimidating. Handle them professionally while protecting the organization's rights.

- Verify the agent's credentials and identity before providing information or access.
- Contact the designated point of contact and legal counsel immediately.
- Be cooperative and professional, but do not provide information beyond what is requested and legally required.
- Do not allow unrestricted access to facilities, employee files, or employees without proper process.
- Document the visit, including what was requested, what was provided, and what was discussed.

### Organize Document Production Carefully

When documents are requested, produce them carefully, completely, and within scope.

- Produce the specific documents requested, organized and labeled, not a disorganized dump of files.
- Do not produce documents beyond the scope of the request without counsel's guidance.
- Review documents before production, with counsel, to identify issues and prepare explanations.
- Maintain a record of what was produced and when.

### Ensure Consistency Between Records And Practice

Audits compare what the records say with what the organization actually does. Inconsistency creates exposure.

- Ensure that sponsored employees are actually performing the roles described in their petitions, at the locations and wages specified.
- Ensure that verification records reflect actual hiring practices.
- Address any discrepancies between records and reality before an audit reveals them.
- Do not maintain petition descriptions that do not match the actual work, as this creates fraud exposure.

### Communicate With Sponsored Employees Appropriately

Government inquiries may involve contact with sponsored employees. Manage this communication carefully.

- Inform sponsored employees that a site visit or inquiry may occur and that they should be honest and accurate.
- Do not coach employees to provide specific answers, which can constitute obstruction.
- Ensure employees know their rights, including the right to their own counsel.
- Provide support to employees who may be anxious about government contact.

### Address Findings And Implement Corrective Action

If an audit or inquiry identifies violations, address them promptly and comprehensively.

- Work with counsel to understand the findings and their implications.
- Implement corrective action to fix identified issues and prevent recurrence.
- Consider self-disclosure of violations where appropriate, as it may mitigate penalties.
- Document the corrective action taken, for future reference and to demonstrate good faith.

## Common Traps

### No Protocol For Government Contact

Being unprepared when a government agent arrives, leading to disorganized or harmful responses. Establish a protocol in advance.

### Handling Inquiries Without Counsel

Attempting to manage audits or site visits without legal counsel, increasing the risk of harmful disclosures. Engage counsel immediately.

### Over-Producing Documents

Providing more documents or access than requested or required, creating unnecessary exposure. Produce within scope with counsel guidance.

### Records That Do Not Match Practice

Maintaining petition descriptions or records that do not match actual roles, locations, or wages, creating fraud exposure. Ensure consistency.

### Coaching Employees

Instructing employees on what to say to government agents, which can constitute obstruction. Inform without coaching.

### Ignoring Self-Audit Findings

Conducting self-audits but failing to address identified issues, allowing problems to persist until an external audit. Act on findings.

### Disorganized Document Production

Producing disorganized or incomplete documents, creating the impression of noncompliance. Organize and review production carefully.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is compliance maintained as audit-ready at all times, with complete organized files and systematic tracking?
- [ ] Is a response protocol established, with a designated point of contact and trained front-line staff?
- [ ] Is legal counsel engaged immediately upon any government contact, guiding the response?
- [ ] Are site visits handled professionally, with credential verification, counsel notification, and scope control?
- [ ] Is document production organized, complete, within scope, and reviewed by counsel before production?
- [ ] Are records consistent with actual practice, including roles, locations, and wages for sponsored employees?
- [ ] Are sponsored employees informed appropriately about potential government contact, without coaching?
- [ ] Are audit findings addressed promptly with corrective action and documentation?
