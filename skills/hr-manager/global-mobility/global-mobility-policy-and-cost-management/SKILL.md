# Global Mobility Policy and Cost Management

Global mobility — moving employees across borders for assignments — is one of the most expensive and administratively complex HR programs. A single long-term international assignment can cost three to five times the employee's base salary once relocation, housing, tax equalization, schooling, and travel are included. Without a clear policy framework, assignment costs balloon, assignments are structured inconsistently, and the organization cannot demonstrate the return on these significant investments. The discipline is to design a coherent global mobility policy that defines assignment types and their support, to manage costs through structured compensation and tax approaches, to ensure compliance across multiple jurisdictions, and to evaluate assignments against business purpose so that mobility serves strategy rather than habit.

## Core Rules

### Define Assignment Types and Their Support Levels Clearly

Global mobility is not a single program but a portfolio of assignment types, each with different costs, durations, and purposes. Define the types your organization uses: long-term assignments (typically one to five years, full expatriate package), short-term assignments (three to twelve months, lighter package), commuter assignments (employee works in one country, lives in another), permanent transfers (employee relocates permanently, localizes compensation), and business travel (frequent short trips without formal relocation). For each type, define the support package: relocation, housing, tax treatment, schooling, home leave, and repatriation. Clear type definitions prevent the most common failure — an assignment structured as one type but costing like another because support was granted ad hoc.

### Structure Assignment Compensation Deliberately

Assignment compensation can be structured in several ways, and the choice shapes cost, tax treatment, and the employee's experience. The home-based approach (build the package around the employee's home-country compensation, with allowances and tax equalization) maintains home-country equity and eases repatriation but is expensive and complex. The host-based approach (pay the employee on the host-country scale, "local plus" allowances) is less expensive and simpler but can create retention issues if the employee compares unfavorably to home-based expatriates. The net-based approach (guarantee a target net income regardless of location) is clean for the employee but complex administratively. Choose the approach based on assignment type, level, and business purpose, and apply it consistently within each type. Mixing approaches within the same assignment type creates inequity and disputes.

### Manage Tax Equalization and Compliance Proactively

International assignments create tax complexity in both home and host countries, and mishandled tax creates both cost and compliance risk. Tax equalization — ensuring the employee pays approximately the same tax as they would have at home, with the employer handling the cross-border complexity — is the standard approach for long-term assignments. Design the equalization methodology carefully (what income is equalized, how home country tax is calculated, how host country tax is handled, how the employee's share is withheld). Coordinate with tax advisors in both countries, and with payroll to implement withholding correctly. Track the many compliance obligations: certificate of coverage for social security totalization, permanent establishment risk for the employer, immigration status linked to tax residency, and reporting obligations. Tax mishandling is one of the most expensive mobility failures.

### Control Cost Through Structured Allowances and Benchmarking

Assignment costs escalate when allowances are granted case by case rather than governed by policy. Control cost by structuring each allowance within the policy: housing (capped at a percentage of compensation or benchmarked to host location), cost-of-living adjustment (based on a recognized index, not negotiated), hardship premium (defined tiers for difficult locations), education allowance (capped, with defined eligible schools), and home leave (defined trips per year, not open-ended). Benchmark total assignment cost against market data periodically to ensure the package is competitive without being excessive. Review the total cost of each assignment annually against budget, and flag assignments whose costs are trending significantly over budget for review. Cost discipline is not about cutting necessary support but about preventing unmanaged escalation.

### Evaluate Assignments Against Business Purpose

Every assignment should have a clear business purpose: filling a skills gap, developing a future leader, transferring knowledge, entering a market, or supporting a client. Document the purpose before the assignment begins, and define the expected outcome and timeline. This serves two functions: it prevents "assignment creep" (assignments that continue past their purpose because no one reviews them) and it enables return-on-investment assessment at repatriation. Assignments without a clear purpose tend to continue indefinitely and to produce disappointing outcomes, because no one defined what success would look like. Build assignment review into the annual planning cycle, and wind down assignments whose purpose has been fulfilled or has evaporated.

### Plan Repatriation From the Outset

Repatriation — the return of the employee after the assignment — is the most neglected phase of global mobility, and poor repatriation is a leading cause of expatriate departure. Plan repatriation from the outset of the assignment, not at the end. Define the expected return role (or at least the process for identifying it), maintain the employee's connection to the home organization through regular engagement, and begin the return role search twelve to eighteen months before the assignment ends. Avoid the common failure of returning the expatriate to a role beneath their growth, which signals that the assignment was not valued and drives departure. A well-planned repatriation captures the assignment's development value; a poorly planned one wastes it.

### Ensure Immigration and Legal Compliance Across Jurisdictions

Every international assignment involves immigration (work authorization in the host country), and many involve additional legal considerations (employment law, data privacy, permanent establishment). Coordinate immigration proactively — visa and work permit processing can take months, and starting late can delay the assignment or force the employee to begin in a non-compliant status. Engage immigration counsel for complex cases. Ensure the assignment documentation (assignment letter, host-country employment agreement if required) is in place before the assignment begins. Track compliance status throughout the assignment, including renewal dates for time-limited authorizations. Non-compliance creates risk for both the employee (potential deportation, future immigration barriers) and the employer (fines, reputational damage, future immigration difficulties).

### Govern the Program With Clear Ownership and Data

Global mobility touches HR, finance, tax, legal, immigration, and the business — and without clear ownership, it falls into gaps. Assign program ownership to a defined function (global mobility team, or a designated HR owner for smaller organizations), with clear interfaces to the other stakeholders. Maintain assignment data centrally: who is on assignment, assignment type, host and home location, start and expected end date, total cost, business purpose, and compliance status. This data enables program reporting, cost analysis, compliance monitoring, and planning. Programs without central data cannot answer basic questions from leadership ("How many expatriates do we have, and what do they cost?") and cannot be managed strategically.

## Common Traps

### Assignment Structured as One Type, Costing Like Another

Without clear type definitions, an assignment labeled as short-term accumulates long-term support, or a commuter assignment becomes a de facto long-term assignment. Define types and their support, and structure assignments within the framework.

### Mixing Compensation Approaches Within a Type

Home-based, host-based, and net-based approaches each have merits, but mixing them within the same assignment type creates inequity and disputes. Choose the approach per type and apply consistently.

### Tax Equalization Mishandled

Poorly designed equalization methodology, uncoordinated withholding, or missed compliance obligations create cost and risk. Design the methodology carefully, coordinate with advisors and payroll, and track obligations in both countries.

### Allowances Granted Case by Case

Ad hoc allowances cause cost escalation and inconsistency. Structure each allowance within policy, benchmark total cost, and review assignments annually against budget.

### Assignment Without Clear Purpose Continuing Indefinitely

Assignments without a documented purpose and timeline tend to continue past their value. Document the purpose, define the expected outcome, review annually, and wind down fulfilled assignments.

### Repatriation Planned at the End

Repatriation neglected until the assignment ends produces disappointing return roles and expatriate departure. Plan repatriation from the outset, maintain home connection, and begin the return role search early.

### Immigration Started Late, Forcing Non-Compliant Starts

Late immigration processing delays assignments or forces non-compliant starts. Process immigration proactively, engage counsel for complex cases, and track renewal dates throughout.

### No Central Program Ownership or Data

Without clear ownership and central data, mobility falls into gaps and cannot be managed strategically. Assign ownership, maintain assignment data centrally, and enable program reporting.

## Self-Check

- Have I defined assignment types (long-term, short-term, commuter, permanent transfer, business travel) with their support packages, and structured assignments within the framework?
- Have I chosen compensation approaches (home-based, host-based, net-based) deliberately per type and applied them consistently?
- Have I designed tax equalization methodology, coordinated withholding with payroll, and tracked compliance obligations in both countries?
- Am I controlling cost through structured allowances, benchmarking, and annual cost review against budget?
- Does every assignment have a documented business purpose, expected outcome, and timeline, with annual review and wind-down of fulfilled assignments?
- Am I planning repatriation from the outset — return role process, home connection, early search — rather than at the end?
- Am I processing immigration proactively, engaging counsel for complex cases, and tracking compliance status and renewals?
- Have I assigned clear program ownership and maintained central assignment data for reporting, cost analysis, and planning?
