# HR Policy And Recordkeeping

HR policies and records are the infrastructure on which every other HR function depends. Policies define the rules and expectations the organization will be held to, and records are the evidence of what actually happened. Agents often treat policy writing as copying a template and recordkeeping as filing, and miss that an organization is bound by its own written promises, that missing or inconsistent records make defensible decisions indefensible, and that retention, access, and disposal of employee data carry direct legal obligations. Weak policy and recordkeeping do not just create administrative mess; they convert winnable cases into losses.

Use this skill before drafting or revising policies, managing employee files, responding to an audit or inspection, or establishing HR documentation practices. The goal is to make the agent build policies that are clear, compliant, and consistently applied, and records that are complete, secure, and retained correctly.

## Core Rules

### Draft Policies That Are Clear, Compliant, And Consistent With Practice

A policy is a promise the organization makes to its employees, and it will be measured against what the organization actually does. Vague, over-broad, or template policies create obligations the organization cannot meet and inconsistencies it cannot defend.

When drafting policy:

- ground each policy in the actual legal requirements and the organization's real practices, not a generic template;
- write in plain language that employees and managers can understand and apply;
- define key terms, such as what constitutes harassment, misconduct, or a protected activity, so expectations are clear;
- specify procedures, responsibilities, timelines, and consequences, not only principles;
- ensure policies do not promise more than the organization will deliver, such as guaranteed progressive discipline or specific review processes;
- preserve lawful flexibility where appropriate, such as at-will employment, while not contradicting protections the organization must provide;
- reconcile policies with each other, so the handbook, the intranet, and local addenda do not conflict;
- account for jurisdictional differences across locations, since a single global policy may violate local law in some places.

A policy that promises a specific procedure the organization does not follow becomes evidence against it. Align the written word with actual practice, or change one to match the other.

### Cover The Core Policy Domains Required By Law And Risk

Certain policies are expected in any defensible HR framework, both because law requires them and because their absence creates exposure. Ensure coverage of:

- equal employment opportunity and anti-discrimination;
- anti-harassment and anti-retaliation, with complaint procedures;
- code of conduct and expected workplace behavior;
- attendance, timekeeping, and time off;
- leave and accommodation, with how to request;
- wage, hours, and overtime, including classification;
- workplace safety and health;
- confidentiality, data protection, and acceptable use of systems;
- conflicts of interest and outside employment;
- substance use and drug testing, where applicable;
- disciplinary and grievance procedures;
- whistleblower protection and reporting channels;
- social media and external communication.

Each policy should be reviewed against current law periodically, since legal requirements change. Coordinate with the legal-advisor role for legal interpretation and the compliance-officer role for broader regulatory alignment.

### Establish Clear Complaint And Reporting Channels

Employees must know how to raise concerns, and the organization must have channels that are accessible, trusted, and free of retaliation. The absence of known channels does not prevent complaints; it pushes them to external regulators or courts.

Provide channels that:

- are documented in the handbook and visible in the workplace;
- offer multiple paths, including bypassing a direct supervisor;
- include a confidential or anonymous option where appropriate;
- define who receives and investigates complaints, with escalation paths;
- state the no-retaliation commitment clearly;
- specify expected timelines for acknowledgment and response;
- are accessible to remote, field, and non-desk employees, not only headquarters staff.

See the employee-relations skill for investigation of complaints received through these channels, and the ethics skill for whistleblower-specific protections.

### Maintain Complete, Accurate Personnel Files

The personnel file is the documented history of the employment relationship. Its completeness and accuracy determine whether the organization can defend its decisions.

A well-maintained personnel file includes:

- the offer letter, employment agreement, and any acknowledgments;
- job description and any changes during employment;
- performance records: objectives, reviews, ratings, and development plans;
- compensation records and changes;
- training completion and certifications;
- disciplinary records and improvement plans;
- leave and accommodation records, with medical information stored separately;
- commendations, awards, and recognition.

Keep separate files for sensitive categories: medical and accommodation information, investigation files, immigration and work authorization documents, and wage and hour records, each with its own access controls. Mixing sensitive data into the general file creates privacy violations and access problems.

### Apply Retention Requirements Correctly

Employee records must be retained for specific periods defined by law, which vary by record type and jurisdiction. Premature destruction creates evidence gaps; excessive retention creates unnecessary liability and privacy exposure.

Apply retention by:

- identifying the retention period for each record type under applicable law: payroll, personnel, medical, safety, immigration, and benefits records often have different periods;
- retaining records for the longer period where multiple requirements apply;
- preserving records when a claim, charge, or litigation is anticipated or pending, overriding any destruction schedule;
- establishing a documented destruction process with authorization and logging;
- ensuring destruction is secure and complete, not merely deletion of an index.

Retention is not optional and not uniform. A single schedule applied to all records will either destroy some too early or retain others too long. Build type-specific schedules and place litigation holds promptly when the duty arises.

### Meet Posting, Notice, And Filing Obligations

Employers owe a range of notices and postings to employees and to government authorities. These are easily overlooked because they feel administrative, but non-compliance creates penalties and, in some cases, extends limitations periods for claims.

Track obligations including:

- required workplace posters on labor rights, safety, discrimination, and wage laws, displayed where employees can see them, including remote and field locations;
- new hire reporting to government agencies within required timeframes;
- pay transparency and pay range notices where required;
- benefits notices, such as continuation coverage and summary plan descriptions;
- leave and accommodation rights notices provided at the required times;
- required filings for work authorization, visa, and immigration status;
- annual or periodic government reports on workforce composition.

Assign clear ownership for each obligation, since these tasks fall through the cracks when everyone assumes someone else handles them. See the compliance-officer role for the broader regulatory calendar framework.

### Manage HR Document Access And Confidentiality

Employee records contain sensitive information that must be protected from unauthorized access, both for legal compliance and for trust. Access on a need-to-know basis is the standard.

Control access by:

- limiting personnel file access to the employee, their manager, HR, and others with a legitimate need;
- restricting sensitive sub-files, such as medical, investigation, and compensation data, more tightly;
- using role-based access in HR systems, with audit logging of who accessed what;
- training managers that access to records they do not need is itself a violation;
- defining and documenting the employee's right to access their own file, which varies by jurisdiction;
- handling records requests from third parties, such as subpoenas or reference checks, through a controlled process.

Curiosity-driven access, such as a manager viewing a colleague's compensation or medical file without a business need, is a breach. Systems should prevent it and audits should detect it. See the data-privacy skill for the broader data protection framework.

### Ensure Consistency Between Documentation And Decisions

The most common documentation failure is not missing records but records that contradict the decision being defended. Inconsistent documentation is stronger evidence of pretext than missing documentation.

Ensure consistency by:

- aligning performance ratings with the basis for promotion, pay, and termination decisions;
- ensuring disciplinary records support the stated reason for any adverse action;
- checking that the reason stated to the employee matches the reason documented internally;
- verifying that timelines in the records support, rather than undermine, the sequence of events;
- avoiding after-the-fact documentation that postdates a decision or a complaint.

If the documentation does not support a contemplated decision, the decision is either premature or the documentation is incomplete. Do not manufacture records to support a decision already made; contemporaneous documentation created in good faith is credible, and backdated or inconsistent records are not.

### Prepare For Audits And Inspections

Government agencies, auditors, and customers may inspect HR records and practices. Unprepared inspections expose gaps that could have been fixed in advance.

Prepare by:

- maintaining records so they can be produced promptly and completely;
- knowing which records an inspector can request and which require a subpoena or warrant;
- designating who responds to inspections and how;
- conducting internal audits of I-9 compliance, pay practices, leave administration, and recordkeeping on a regular cadence;
- remediating identified gaps before they become findings;
- coordinating with legal counsel before responding to agency inquiries or charges.

Internal audits find problems while they are still fixable. External audits find problems when they are already liabilities. Run the former to avoid the latter.

## Common Traps

### Copying A Template Without Aligning To Law Or Practice

A template policy that does not match the organization's jurisdiction or actual practice creates obligations it cannot meet. Customize and localize.

### Promising Procedures The Organization Will Not Follow

A handbook that guarantees progressive discipline or specific review processes binds the organization to them. Preserve flexibility where needed and deliver on what is promised.

### Mixing Sensitive Data Into The General Personnel File

Medical, investigation, immigration, and compensation data in the general file creates privacy violations and access problems. Use separate, controlled sub-files.

### Uniform Retention Applied To All Records

A single retention schedule destroys some records too early and retains others too long. Build type-specific schedules and honor litigation holds.

### Overlooking Posting And Notice Obligations

Administrative-feeling obligations that fall through the cracks create penalties and extend claim limitations periods. Assign ownership and track deadlines.

### Access Granted Too Broadly

Need-to-know access ignored, allowing curiosity-driven viewing of compensation or medical records, is a breach. Restrict and audit access.

### Documentation That Contradicts The Decision

Records that undermine the stated reason for a decision are stronger pretext evidence than missing records. Align documentation with decisions in real time.

### No Internal Audit Until An External One Arrives

Waiting for an agency or auditor to find gaps converts fixable issues into liabilities. Audit internally on a cadence.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Are HR policies clear, compliant with current law, consistent with actual practice, reconciled across documents, and localized for jurisdictional differences?
- [ ] Do core policy domains, including anti-discrimination, anti-harassment, leave, accommodation, safety, and conduct, all have current, accessible policies?
- [ ] Are complaint and reporting channels documented, multi-pathed, accessible to all employee types, and accompanied by a clear no-retaliation commitment?
- [ ] Are personnel files complete and accurate, with sensitive categories stored in separate, access-controlled sub-files?
- [ ] Are retention periods applied by record type under applicable law, with litigation holds overriding destruction schedules?
- [ ] Are posting, notice, new hire reporting, and filing obligations tracked with clear ownership and deadlines?
- [ ] Is access to HR documents restricted to need-to-know, role-based, audit-logged, and supported by manager training?
- [ ] Is documentation consistent with decisions, with reasons aligned between what is told to the employee and what is recorded internally?
- [ ] Are internal audits of I-9, pay, leave, and recordkeeping practices run on a regular cadence, with gaps remediated?
- [ ] Is there a defined process for responding to inspections and agency inquiries, coordinated with legal counsel?
