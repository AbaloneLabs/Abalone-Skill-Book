# HR Recordkeeping And Document Retention

HR recordkeeping is governed by a web of retention mandates that specify what must be kept, for how long, and in what form. The two failure modes are opposites: under-retention, which destroys evidence the organization needs to defend itself and violates retention laws; and over-retention, which preserves damaging evidence and creates privacy risk. The agent must understand that retention is not optional housekeeping but a legal obligation, that different records have different retention periods, and that records, once created, are discoverable in litigation and can be used against the organization as easily as for it. A disciplined recordkeeping system retains what is required, destroys what is not, and protects sensitive information throughout.

Use this skill when establishing or managing HR recordkeeping. The goal is to make the agent build a compliant, defensible recordkeeping system that retains what is needed and destroys what is not.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Retention Mandates That Apply

Retention requirements come from multiple laws, each specifying particular records and periods. Identify all applicable mandates.

- Identify federal retention requirements: payroll records, basic employee information, contracts, and collective bargaining agreements, typically for specified periods.
- Identify state retention requirements, which may be longer than federal and may cover additional records such as personnel files, pay records, and safety records.
- Identify industry-specific requirements, such as those for government contractors or regulated industries.
- Apply the longest applicable retention period when mandates overlap.
- Document the retention schedule, specifying each record type, its retention period, and the legal basis.

### Retain Personnel Records According To The Schedule

Personnel records include a range of documents with different retention periods. Retain each according to its schedule.

- Personnel files, including performance reviews, disciplinary records, and employment history, for the period required by the applicable state law, often several years after termination.
- Payroll records, including time records, pay rates, and deductions, for the period required by wage-and-hour law.
- Medical records, including accommodation documentation and medical leave records, separately from the personnel file, in confidential medical files, for the required period.
- I-9 employment verification forms, for the shorter of three years after hire or one year after termination, retained separately or separately flagged.
- Benefits records, including plan documents and enrollment records, for the period required by benefits law.

### Maintain Confidentiality And Access Controls

Employee records contain sensitive information that must be protected from unauthorized access. Different records have different access rules.

- Restrict access to personnel files to the employee, their manager, HR, and others with a legitimate business need.
- Restrict access to medical files to those with a specific need, never the employee's direct supervisor.
- Restrict access to pay and benefits data to those who administer them.
- Use role-based access controls in HR systems, with audit trails of who accessed what.
- Protect records from unauthorized external access through physical security for paper and cybersecurity for digital records.

### Handle Employee Access And Inspection Rights

Employees have rights to access their own records in many jurisdictions. Understand and accommodate these rights.

- Understand which records employees have the right to inspect, which varies by jurisdiction and may include personnel files, pay records, and performance documents.
- Provide access within the timeframe required by law, typically a few days.
- Understand the rules on copying: whether the employee may copy records and whether the employer may charge.
- Accommodate inspection without retaliation or interference.

### Manage Records In Litigation And Legal Hold

When litigation is foreseeable or filed, the normal retention rules are suspended for relevant records. A legal hold preserves records that would otherwise be destroyed.

- Issue a litigation hold when litigation is reasonably foreseeable, directing custodians to preserve relevant records.
- Suspend the routine destruction of relevant records, including email, personnel files, and other documents.
- Identify all custodians and record locations that may contain relevant records.
- Document the hold, the custodians, and the records preserved.
- Release the hold when the matter concludes, at which point normal retention resumes.

### Destroy Records On Schedule, Not Before And Not Indefinitely

Destruction is as important as retention. Records kept beyond their retention period create liability and privacy risk; records destroyed before their period expires violate retention law and can be deemed destruction of evidence.

- Destroy records on a regular schedule, according to the retention schedule, once the retention period expires.
- Ensure destruction is complete and irreversible, for both paper and digital records.
- Suspend destruction when a legal hold is in place.
- Document destruction, including what was destroyed and when, to demonstrate compliance.
- Do not retain records indefinitely "just in case," as this preserves discoverable liability and increases privacy risk.

### Manage Records Across Systems And Formats

Records may exist in paper, digital, email, and system databases. Manage retention across all formats.

- Apply the retention schedule to all formats, not just formal files.
- Include email, instant messages, and system data in the retention analysis, as these are records and are discoverable.
- Ensure that digital records are retained in a readable format throughout the retention period, migrating formats as technology changes.
- Consolidate records where possible to simplify management, but do not consolidate records with different retention periods in ways that prevent individual destruction.

### Prepare For Audits And Inspections

Regulators, auditors, and, in litigation, opposing counsel may inspect records. Be prepared to produce records accurately and completely.

- Maintain records in an organized, retrievable manner, so that requests can be fulfilled promptly.
- Be able to demonstrate compliance with retention requirements, through the retention schedule and destruction logs.
- Designate responsibility for record production in audits and litigation.
- Conduct periodic self-audits of recordkeeping to identify and correct gaps.

## Common Traps

### Under-Retention

Destroying records before the retention period expires, violating law and destroying defense evidence. Retain according to schedule.

### Indefinite Retention

Keeping records indefinitely, preserving discoverable liability and increasing privacy risk. Destroy on schedule.

### Commingling Records With Different Retention

Combining records with different retention periods in ways that prevent individual destruction, forcing all-or-nothing retention. Separate records by retention period.

### Improper Access Controls

Allowing unauthorized access to personnel or medical records, creating privacy violations. Apply role-based controls.

### Missing Legal Holds

Failing to suspend destruction when litigation is foreseeable, resulting in destruction of evidence. Issue and track legal holds.

### Ignoring Email And System Data

Treating only formal files as records, missing discoverable email and system data. Include all formats in retention.

### No Destruction Documentation

Destroying records without documenting what and when, unable to demonstrate compliance. Document destruction.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Are all applicable retention mandates identified, with the longest period applied where they overlap?
- [ ] Is a retention schedule documented, specifying each record type, period, and legal basis?
- [ ] Are personnel, payroll, medical, I-9, and benefits records retained according to their specific schedules?
- [ ] Are access controls role-based, with medical records separated and supervisor access restricted?
- [ ] Are employee inspection rights accommodated within required timeframes and without retaliation?
- [ ] Are legal holds issued when litigation is foreseeable, suspending destruction of relevant records?
- [ ] Are records destroyed on schedule, completely and with documentation, across all formats?
- [ ] Is the recordkeeping system prepared for audits and inspections, with periodic self-audits?
