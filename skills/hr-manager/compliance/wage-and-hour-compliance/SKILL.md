# Wage And Hour Compliance

Wage and hour compliance is one of the highest-risk areas in HR because errors are typically systemic, affecting many employees and generating collective or class action liability. A single misclassification, applying an exemption incorrectly, can create back-pay exposure across an entire category of employees. The danger is that wage and hour errors are often invisible, embedded in job descriptions, timekeeping practices, and pay structures that have been in place for years, until an audit, a complaint, or a lawsuit reveals them. The agent must understand that wage and hour compliance requires analyzing actual duties and practices, not job titles, that exemptions are narrowly construed, and that the cost of getting it wrong is multiplied across the workforce.

Use this skill when classifying employees, managing timekeeping, or auditing wage and hour compliance. The goal is to make the agent classify correctly, manage time and pay lawfully, and prevent systemic liability.

## Core Rules

### Classify Exempt Vs Non-Exempt Based On Actual Duties, Not Title

The exempt vs non-exempt classification determines eligibility for overtime. Exemptions are based on the actual duties the employee performs, not on job title, salary level alone, or the employer's preference.

- Assess each exemption, such as executive, administrative, professional, computer, or outside sales, against its specific duties test, which require the employee to perform certain types of work.
- The duties tests focus on the primary duty, the work that is the employee's main or most important activity, not on occasional tasks.
- Job titles do not determine exemption; an employee with the title "manager" who does not actually direct the work of two or more employees is not exempt under the executive test.
- Apply both the salary basis test, paying a predetermined salary not subject to reduction based on work quantity, and the salary level test, meeting the minimum threshold, in addition to the duties test.
- Document the classification analysis for each position, based on actual duties, not aspiration.

### Manage Timekeeping Accurately For Non-Exempt Employees

Non-exempt employees must track all hours worked, and the employer must pay for all hours worked, including overtime. Timekeeping errors generate back-pay liability.

- Require non-exempt employees to record all time worked, including time worked outside scheduled hours, such as early start, late end, or work from home.
- Do not permit or require off-the-clock work, including pre-shift preparation, post-shift cleanup, or work during unpaid breaks.
- Round time entries neutrally, if rounding is used, so that it averages out and does not systematically underpay employees.
- Review time records for patterns suggesting missed punches, under-recorded time, or off-the-clock work.
- Pay overtime at the correct rate, including the regular rate that includes non-discretionary bonuses and shift differentials, not just the base rate.

### Handle Meal And Rest Breaks According To Law

Meal and rest break requirements vary by jurisdiction. In some states, employers must provide and ensure that breaks are taken; in others, they must offer but not police them. Non-compliance generates premium pay or penalty liability.

- Identify the break requirements in each jurisdiction, including duration, frequency, and whether the break must be duty-free.
- Ensure that breaks are duty-free, with the employee relieved of all duties; working through a break makes it compensable.
- Do not deduct time for breaks that are not taken or during which the employee works.
- Manage breaks to comply with the jurisdiction's standard, whether the employer must ensure breaks are taken or merely make them available.
- Track break compliance through time records.

### Comply With Minimum Wage Requirements

Minimum wage requirements vary by jurisdiction and may be higher than the federal rate. Apply the highest applicable rate.

- Identify the minimum wage in each jurisdiction where employees work, including city rates that exceed state rates.
- Apply the highest applicable rate to each employee, based on work location.
- Account for tip credits, where applicable, ensuring that tips plus direct wages meet or exceed minimum wage.
- Adjust for minimum wage increases promptly when they take effect.

### Ensure Pay Statement And Notice Compliance

Many jurisdictions require specific information on pay statements and at hire, including pay rate, pay period, deductions, and employer information. Non-compliance generates per-pay-statement penalties.

- Include all required information on pay statements: gross and net wages, hours worked, pay rate, pay period, all deductions, and employer information.
- Provide the required wage notices at hire, including pay rate, pay basis, regular pay day, and employer information.
- Update notices when required information changes.
- Audit pay statements periodically for compliance with all applicable requirements.

### Manage Travel Time And Training Time

Travel and training time have specific rules about compensability. Errors in treating these as non-compensable generate liability.

- Compensate travel time that is part of the workday, including travel between job sites during the day, but not ordinary home-to-work commute.
- Compensate overnight travel during work hours, including on non-workdays, according to the applicable rules.
- Compensate training time that is required by the employer, during regular work hours, or directly related to the job, unless all four exceptions apply: outside regular hours, voluntary, not job-related, and no other work performed.
- Do not assume travel or training is automatically non-compensable; analyze each situation.

### Handle Deductions Lawfully

Deductions from pay are regulated, and improper deductions generate liability and can destroy exempt status.

- Make only deductions authorized by law, such as taxes and garnishments, or authorized in writing by the employee.
- Do not deduct for business expenses in a way that reduces pay below minimum wage or that violates state expense reimbursement laws.
- Do not make deductions from exempt employees' salary for partial-day absences or for variations in work quantity, which can destroy the salary basis and the exemption.
- Understand the rules on deductions for full-day absences for personal reasons, sickness, and disciplinary suspensions, which are limited.

### Conduct Periodic Wage And Hour Audits

Wage and hour compliance drifts over time as job duties change, practices evolve, and laws update. Periodic audits catch and correct errors before they generate liability.

- Audit classifications periodically, reassessing exempt positions against the duties tests as jobs change.
- Audit timekeeping practices for off-the-clock work, rounding errors, and missed breaks.
- Audit pay statements and wage notices for compliance.
- Audit travel and training time practices.
- Use audit findings to correct errors, including back-pay calculations where underpayment is found, with legal guidance.

## Common Traps

### Classification By Title

Treating job titles or salary as determinative of exemption, without analyzing actual duties. Analyze duties.

### Off-The-Clock Work

Permitting or requiring work before clock-in, after clock-out, or during unpaid breaks. Pay for all hours worked.

### Incorrect Overtime Rate

Calculating overtime on base rate alone, excluding non-discretionary bonuses and differentials from the regular rate. Use the correct regular rate.

### Break Violations

Deducting for breaks not taken or worked through, or failing to ensure breaks where required. Manage breaks lawfully.

### Stale Classifications

Failing to reassess exempt classifications as job duties change over time. Audit periodically.

### Improper Deductions

Making deductions that destroy exempt status or that violate wage law. Limit deductions to those authorized.

### Pay Statement Non-Compliance

Omitting required information from pay statements, generating per-statement penalties. Audit pay statements.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Are exempt classifications based on actual duties analysis, documented for each position, with both salary basis and level met?
- [ ] Do non-exempt employees record all time worked, with off-the-clock work prevented and rounding neutral?
- [ ] Are meal and rest breaks provided and managed according to each jurisdiction's requirements, duty-free?
- [ ] Is the highest applicable minimum wage paid in each jurisdiction, with tip credits managed correctly?
- [ ] Do pay statements and wage notices include all required information, audited for compliance?
- [ ] Are travel time and training time compensated according to the applicable rules?
- [ ] Are deductions limited to those authorized by law or the employee, without destroying exempt status?
- [ ] Are periodic wage and hour audits conducted, with errors corrected including back-pay where needed?
