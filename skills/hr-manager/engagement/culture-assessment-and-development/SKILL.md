# Culture Assessment And Development

Culture is the shared set of behaviors, norms, and assumptions that shape how people act in the organization. It is not the values on the wall or the words in the handbook; it is what employees actually do, particularly when no one is watching. The agent must understand that culture is created by what is rewarded, tolerated, and punished, that assessing culture requires looking at behaviors and systems, not just statements, and that changing culture is among the hardest organizational work, requiring consistency between words, behaviors, and systems over sustained time. Culture work that consists of posters, values, and events, without addressing the underlying behaviors and systems, produces cynicism, not change.

Use this skill when assessing, developing, or changing organizational culture. The goal is to make the agent diagnose culture accurately, align it with strategy, and drive genuine change.

## Core Rules

### Assess Culture Through Behaviors And Systems, Not Statements

Culture is revealed by what happens, not by what is said. Assess culture through behaviors and systems.

- Observe what is rewarded: who gets promoted, recognized, and retained, as this reveals what the culture truly values.
- Observe what is tolerated: what behaviors go unaddressed, as tolerance signals acceptance.
- Observe what is punished: what behaviors draw consequences, as this signals what the culture prohibits.
- Examine the systems: how performance is evaluated, how decisions are made, how resources are allocated, and how conflicts are resolved, as systems shape behavior.
- Compare the stated values to the actual behaviors, identifying the gaps that reveal the real culture.

### Diagnose Cultural Strengths And Toxicities

Culture is not uniformly good or bad. Diagnose both the strengths to preserve and the toxicities to address.

- Identify cultural strengths: the behaviors and norms that support the strategy, engage employees, and drive performance.
- Identify cultural toxicities: the behaviors and norms that undermine the strategy, disengage employees, or create risk, such as fear, blame, exclusion, or short-termism.
- Assess how deeply embedded each element is, as deeply embedded elements are harder to change.
- Assess whether the toxicities are localized, in specific teams or leaders, or systemic, across the organization.
- Use multiple data sources: observation, surveys, interviews, exit interviews, and turnover data.

### Align Culture With Strategy And Values

Culture should support the organization's strategy and reflect its values. Misalignment between culture and strategy creates friction that undermines performance.

- Articulate the strategy and the values that support it, clearly and specifically.
- Identify the cultural elements that support the strategy and those that undermine it.
- Strengthen the elements that support the strategy, through reinforcement, rewards, and systems.
- Address the elements that undermine the strategy, through change in behaviors, systems, and leadership.
- Recognize that a culture that served a past strategy may not serve the current one, and that strategic change requires cultural change.

### Recognize That Culture Is Created By Leadership Behavior

Leadership behavior is the most powerful shaper of culture. What leaders do, particularly what senior leaders do, signals what the organization truly values, regardless of what is said.

- Assess whether leadership behavior aligns with the stated culture, as gaps between leadership behavior and stated values destroy credibility.
- Hold leaders accountable for modeling the desired culture, through evaluation, feedback, and consequences.
- Address leaders whose behavior undermines the desired culture, as tolerating misaligned leadership signals that the culture statements are not serious.
- Recognize that changing culture requires changing leadership behavior, which is difficult and slow.

### Manage Culture During Growth, Merger, And Transformation

Cultural transitions, during rapid growth, merger, or strategic transformation, are high-risk periods. The existing culture may not serve the new context, and cultural clash can derail the transition.

- During growth, recognize that culture dilutes as new employees join, and that intentional onboarding and integration are needed to preserve cultural strengths.
- During merger or acquisition, assess cultural compatibility early, and plan integration to address cultural differences, not just operational integration.
- During transformation, recognize that the existing culture will resist change, and that changing culture requires changing systems, leadership behavior, and reinforcement.
- Communicate the cultural change explicitly, with specific behaviors expected, not just abstract values.

### Drive Culture Change Through Systems, Not Just Statements

Culture change that relies on statements, posters, and events, without changing systems, fails. Drive change through the systems that shape behavior.

- Align performance evaluation with the desired culture, evaluating and rewarding behaviors, not just results.
- Align promotion and succession with the desired culture, advancing those who model it.
- Align recognition with the desired culture, recognizing the behaviors that reflect it.
- Align decision-making processes with the desired culture, changing how decisions are made, not just what is decided.
- Align hiring with the desired culture, selecting for cultural fit with the desired, not the existing, culture.

### Sustain Culture Work Over Time

Culture change is slow, often taking years, and regresses quickly if attention wavers. Sustain the work over time.

- Set realistic expectations for the pace of change, measured in years, not months.
- Maintain consistent focus on the cultural priorities, not shifting to new initiatives each quarter.
- Measure cultural indicators over time, through surveys, behaviors, and outcomes, and adjust the approach.
- Reinforce the change through ongoing communication, leadership modeling, and system alignment.
- Recognize and address regression, as old behaviors reassert themselves under pressure.

### Avoid Culture Work That Produces Cynicism

Culture work that is perceived as inauthentic, values posters that do not match behaviors, or events that do not address real issues, produces cynicism and disengagement. Avoid this.

- Do not launch culture initiatives that are not backed by system and behavior change.
- Do not use culture as a euphemism for avoiding difficult conversations about behaviors or performance.
- Do not blame culture for problems that are actually caused by strategy, structure, or leadership decisions.
- Address the gap between stated and actual culture directly, rather than pretending it does not exist.

## Common Traps

### Assessing Statements Instead Of Behaviors

Reading a values poster or a survey of stated beliefs and treating it as the culture is comforting but misleading, because the gap between espoused and actual culture is often where the most consequential dynamics live. An organization can score well on "we value collaboration" while its bonus system rewards individual heroics and its meetings are dominated by the loudest voice. Assess culture through observable behavior — who gets promoted, what is tolerated, how decisions are actually made, what happens to dissent — because those are the signals employees use to decide what is real.

### Values Posters Without System Change

Launching a culture initiative with refreshed values, posters, and town halls without altering the systems that shape behavior produces a specific kind of damage: it widens the gap between what the organization says and what employees experience, and that gap is itself a driver of cynicism. Employees do not resent a flawed culture as much as they resent being told a flattering story about it. Drive culture change through the systems that govern daily decisions — hiring criteria, performance evaluation, promotion, compensation, meeting norms, and consequence application — because behavior follows incentives and structures, not declarations.

### Tolerating Misaligned Leadership

When a senior leader visibly violates the stated culture and faces no consequence, the organization receives a louder message than any town hall can send: the values are optional for the powerful. This single act of tolerance undoes years of culture work because employees correctly read it as the true statement of what is rewarded. The harm compounds when the misaligned leader is a high performer, because it teaches that results buy an exemption from cultural expectations, which then cascades through the management chain as each layer learns the real rules.

### Blaming Culture For Structural Problems

Attributing a performance, coordination, or execution problem to "culture" when the actual cause is a strategy that is unclear, a structure that creates conflicting incentives, or a leadership decision that under-resources the work, leads to years of culture interventions that cannot fix the issue. The culture work absorbs energy and budget while the structural cause continues to generate the same symptoms, and the organization concludes that culture change is futile. Diagnose the true driver before prescribing culture work — if the problem disappears with a reorg, a clearer goal, or a budget change, it was never culture.

### Unrealistic Change Timelines

Culture change operates on the timescale of turnover and habit formation — typically two to five years for a meaningful shift in a large organization — yet sponsors often expect visible results within a quarter or two. When the expected transformation does not appear, the initiative is declared failed and replaced with a new one, which itself is abandoned on the same timeline, producing a graveyard of culture programs that teaches the workforce that none of them are serious. Set multi-year expectations, measure leading indicators of behavior shift rather than lagging sentiment scores, and protect the initiative through leadership turnover and budget pressure.

### Culture As A Euphemism

Using "culture fit" or "culture issue" as a polite substitute for a difficult conversation about a specific behavior, a performance gap, or a decision someone dislikes, degrades the concept into a vague, unfalsifiable label that can be deployed to exclude or dismiss without accountability. It lets managers avoid naming the actual problem and gives the accused no clear path to improve, because "not a culture fit" is unactionable. Name the specific behavior or decision at issue directly, and reserve culture language for genuine shared-norm questions.

### Initiative Fatigue

Launching a new culture or values initiative each quarter, often driven by a new leader wanting to make a mark, prevents any single change from taking hold because culture requires repetition and stability to embed. Employees learn to wait out each wave, offering surface compliance while their actual behavior stays unchanged, and the organization accumulates a reputation for chasing fads. Maintain a consistent, multi-year focus on a small number of cultural priorities, and measure progress against those rather than relaunching.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is culture assessed through behaviors, rewards, tolerance, and systems, not just statements and values?
- [ ] Are cultural strengths and toxicities diagnosed, with embeddedness and localization assessed?
- [ ] Is culture aligned with strategy and values, with supportive elements strengthened and undermining elements addressed?
- [ ] Is leadership behavior assessed for alignment with the desired culture, with leaders held accountable?
- [ ] Are cultural transitions during growth, merger, and transformation managed with intentional integration?
- [ ] Is culture change driven through systems, evaluation, promotion, recognition, decision-making, and hiring?
- [ ] Is culture work sustained over time, with realistic expectations and regression addressed?
- [ ] Is cynicism avoided through authentic, system-backed culture work rather than posters and events?
