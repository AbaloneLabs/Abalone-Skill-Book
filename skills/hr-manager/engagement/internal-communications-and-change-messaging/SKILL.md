# Internal Communications And Change Messaging

Internal communications shape how employees understand the organization, its direction, and their place in it. The most common failure is treating communication as announcement, a one-way broadcast of decisions already made, rather than as engagement, a two-way process that builds understanding and commitment. The agent must understand that communication is most critical, and most difficult, during change and crisis, that the gap between what leadership says and what employees hear is often wide, and that communication that outruns the organization's ability to deliver destroys credibility. The goal is to make internal communications build understanding, trust, and alignment, particularly when the message is difficult.

Use this skill when designing internal communications or managing change messaging. The goal is to make the agent communicate effectively, reach all employees, and maintain credibility through honesty and follow-through.

## Core Rules

### Treat Communication As Engagement, Not Announcement

One-way announcements inform but do not engage. Effective communication is a two-way process that builds understanding, addresses concerns, and creates commitment.

- Provide context and rationale for decisions, not just the decisions themselves, so employees understand the why.
- Create channels for questions and feedback, and respond to them, rather than broadcasting into a void.
- Acknowledge concerns and difficult emotions, particularly during change, rather than dismissing them.
- Repeat key messages through multiple channels and over time, as one announcement is never enough.
- Measure whether the message was understood, not just whether it was sent.

### Communicate Change With A Clear Structure

Change communication requires a structured approach that addresses the questions employees will have. Anticipate and answer those questions.

- Communicate the what, the why, the impact on employees, the timeline, and what employees need to do.
- Address the question that is always asked first: how does this affect me, including job security, pay, role, and schedule.
- Communicate as early as possible, while there is genuine information to share, rather than waiting until everything is finalized, which allows rumor to fill the gap.
- Be honest about what is not yet decided, rather than filling gaps with false certainty.
- Sequence communication so that those most affected hear first, before broader announcements.

### Reach All Segments Of The Workforce

Communication that reaches headquarters but not remote workers, desk-based but not deskless employees, or native speakers but not others, fails a significant portion of the workforce.

- Use multiple channels: email, intranet, mobile apps, video, print, manager cascade, and town halls.
- Consider who is reached by each channel and who is missed, and fill the gaps.
- Translate communications for non-native speakers, where the workforce requires it.
- Provide access for deskless employees through kiosks, mobile, or printed materials.
- Do not assume that sending an email means the message was received; verify reach.

### Equip Managers As Communication Channels

Managers are the primary communication channel for their teams. Employees trust and attend to their manager more than to corporate communications. Equip managers to communicate effectively.

- Brief managers before broader announcements, so they are prepared to answer questions, not surprised by the news.
- Provide managers with talking points, FAQs, and answers to likely questions.
- Train managers on communicating change, including how to acknowledge concerns and what they can and cannot promise.
- Hold managers accountable for cascading communication, while recognizing that some messages are best delivered centrally.
- Provide a feedback loop from managers to leadership, so that employee concerns and questions are heard.

### Handle Crisis And Sensitive Communication With Care

Crises, such as layoffs, safety incidents, executive misconduct, or public controversies, require careful communication that maintains trust and provides direction.

- Communicate promptly, even when information is incomplete, to establish the organization as the source of truth and prevent rumor.
- Be honest about what is known and what is not, and commit to updating as information develops.
- Acknowledge the impact on employees, with empathy, rather than minimizing it.
- Provide direction on what employees should do, including where to direct questions or concerns.
- Coordinate messaging across all channels and speakers, to ensure consistency.
- After the crisis, conduct a retrospective on the communication, to improve for the future.

### Maintain Credibility Through Honesty And Follow-Through

Credibility is the foundation of effective communication, and it is built through honesty and follow-through, not through spin or optimism.

- Do not make promises that cannot be kept, or communicate intentions as commitments.
- Acknowledge bad news directly, rather than spinning it as positive, as employees detect spin and lose trust.
- Follow through on commitments made in communication, and acknowledge when follow-through is delayed or changed.
- Admit mistakes, rather than pretending they did not happen.
- Recognize that credibility, once lost, is difficult to rebuild, and protect it through consistent honesty.

### Tailor Tone And Channel To The Message

The tone and channel of communication should match the message. A mismatched tone or channel undermines the message.

- Use serious, direct tone for serious matters, such as layoffs or crises; do not bury difficult news in cheerful corporate language.
- Use appropriate channels: sensitive matters may require in-person or video communication, not email; routine matters may be fine in writing.
- Match the seniority of the messenger to the significance of the message, with major changes communicated by senior leaders.
- Consider timing: avoid communicating difficult news on Friday afternoons or before holidays, when support is unavailable.

### Measure Communication Effectiveness

Communication that is not measured cannot be improved. Measure whether employees are receiving, understanding, and acting on communications.

- Track reach: open rates, attendance, and distribution.
- Survey employees on awareness and understanding of key messages.
- Monitor feedback channels for questions and concerns that indicate gaps.
- Adjust the strategy based on the data, focusing on what works.

## Common Traps

### Announcement-Only Communication

Broadcasting decisions without context, feedback, or engagement. Treat communication as engagement.

### Waiting For Full Certainty

Delaying communication until everything is decided, allowing rumor to fill the gap. Communicate early with what is known.

### Channel Gaps

Reaching some segments but missing others, particularly deskless or remote employees. Use multiple channels.

### Unprepared Managers

Surprising managers with announcements, leaving them unable to answer questions. Brief managers first.

### Spin On Bad News

Presenting difficult news as positive, destroying credibility. Be honest.

### Outrunning Delivery

Communicating intentions or changes that the organization cannot deliver, creating broken promises. Match communication to reality.

### Mismatched Tone

Using cheerful corporate language for serious matters, or email for sensitive news. Match tone and channel to the message.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is communication treated as engagement, with context, feedback channels, and repetition, not just announcement?
- [ ] Is change communicated with a clear structure, addressing impact, timeline, and what employees need to do?
- [ ] Do communications reach all segments, through multiple channels, languages, and access methods?
- [ ] Are managers equipped and briefed to cascade communication and answer questions?
- [ ] Is crisis communication prompt, honest, empathetic, and coordinated, with a retrospective afterward?
- [ ] Is credibility maintained through honesty, follow-through, and acknowledgment of mistakes?
- [ ] Are tone and channel matched to the message, with appropriate seniority of messengers?
- [ ] Is communication effectiveness measured through reach, understanding, and feedback, and adjusted accordingly?
