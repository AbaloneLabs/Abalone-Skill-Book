# Conflict Of Interest Management

Conflicts of interest are inevitable in any complex organization. Employees have outside relationships, financial interests, side activities, and personal connections that can intersect with their work. The failure is not having a conflict; it is failing to disclose and manage it transparently. The agent must understand that effective conflict of interest management rests on a culture of disclosure, not a culture of prohibition, that undisclosed conflicts are far more dangerous than disclosed ones, and that the goal is to identify conflicts early, assess their severity, and put appropriate management in place. A rigid prohibition-only approach drives conflicts underground; a disclosure-based approach brings them into the open where they can be managed.

Use this skill when identifying, disclosing, managing, or preventing conflicts of interest. The goal is to make the agent build a disclosure-based system that identifies and manages conflicts appropriately.

## Core Rules

### Build A Culture Of Disclosure, Not Prohibition

Conflicts cannot be eliminated; they can be disclosed and managed. A disclosure culture is more effective than a prohibition culture.

- Communicate that having a conflict is not misconduct; failing to disclose it is.
- Make disclosure easy, routine, and non-punitive, so employees bring conflicts forward rather than hiding them.
- Respond to disclosures with management solutions, not automatic punishment, unless the conflict involves concealment or self-dealing.
- Model disclosure at the leadership level, normalizing the practice.

### Require Regular And Event-Driven Disclosure

Disclosure should be ongoing, not one-time. Conflicts arise and change over time.

- Require annual conflict of interest disclosures from all employees, with more detailed disclosures for those in sensitive roles, such as procurement, finance, or leadership.
- Require event-driven disclosure when a new conflict arises, such as a new outside job, a financial interest, or a family relationship with a candidate or vendor.
- Include disclosure obligations in onboarding, so new employees understand the expectation from day one.
- Make disclosure forms clear and specific, covering common conflict types: outside employment, financial interests, family relationships, vendor relationships, and gifts.

### Identify The Common Conflict Types

Effective management requires recognizing the range of conflicts that arise. Train employees and leaders to identify them.

- Outside employment and side businesses that compete with or conflict with the employer's interests.
- Financial interests in competitors, vendors, customers, or partners that could influence decisions.
- Family and personal relationships that affect hiring, supervision, evaluation, or contracting decisions, including nepotism.
- Gifts, meals, and entertainment from vendors or customers that could create undue influence.
- Self-dealing, using one's position for personal gain, such as steering contracts to a business one owns.
- Post-employment conflicts, such as negotiating future employment with a vendor or competitor while overseeing decisions affecting them.

### Assess Severity And Apply Proportionate Management

Not all conflicts require the same response. Assess severity and apply management proportionate to the risk.

- Assess the risk: does the conflict create a significant risk of biased decision-making, financial harm, or reputational damage?
- Apply proportionate management: minor conflicts may require only disclosure and monitoring; significant conflicts may require recusal, reassignment, or divestiture.
- Document the assessment and the management decision, so the rationale is clear.
- Reassess periodically, as circumstances change.

### Implement Recusal And Separation For Significant Conflicts

For significant conflicts, recusal or separation of duties is often the appropriate management.

- Require recusal from decisions where the conflict exists, such as a manager not evaluating or approving pay for a family member.
- Implement separation of duties, such that the person with the conflict does not control the decision, with oversight or dual approval.
- Document recusals, so the separation is clear and auditable.
- In cases of severe conflict, such as a direct reporting relationship between family members, reassignment may be necessary.

### Manage Hiring And Supervision Conflicts Carefully

Nepotism and personal relationships in hiring and supervision are common and sensitive. Manage them proactively.

- Require disclosure of family and personal relationships with candidates during hiring, and remove the related person from the hiring decision.
- Avoid direct supervision of family members or romantic partners; reassign reporting lines where such relationships exist.
- Address workplace romantic relationships through disclosure and management, recognizing that some are inevitable but that supervision conflicts must be managed.
- Apply rules consistently, regardless of the individuals' roles or seniority.

### Manage Vendor And Gift Conflicts

Vendor relationships and gifts are a frequent source of conflict. Establish clear rules.

- Define gift thresholds and rules, including what must be declined, disclosed, or approved.
- Prohibit gifts that could influence, or appear to influence, business decisions, particularly from vendors seeking or holding contracts.
- Require disclosure of financial interests in vendors, customers, or competitors, and manage through recusal or divestiture where significant.
- Apply rules consistently, regardless of the employee's level or the vendor's importance.

### Address Conflicts Involving Leadership And Sensitive Roles

Leaders and those in sensitive roles, such as procurement, finance, and compliance, face higher conflict risks and warrant stricter management.

- Apply stricter disclosure and management requirements to leadership and sensitive roles.
- Review leadership business interests, outside board roles, and relationships proactively.
- Address conflicts involving senior leaders firmly, as tolerance at the top signals tolerance throughout.
- Consider the appearance of conflict, not just actual conflict, particularly for leadership, as appearances affect trust.

## Common Traps

### Prohibition Without A Disclosure Mechanism

Banning all conflicts outright, without providing a way for employees to disclose the conflicts they inevitably have, does not eliminate conflicts — it drives them underground, where they remain unmanaged and undiscovered until they cause harm. The trap is that a prohibition-only policy feels rigorous and clean, but it creates a system in which every conflict is a violation, so employees conceal rather than disclose, and the organization loses visibility of the very risks it was trying to control. Build a disclosure-based system where having a conflict is expected and manageable and concealment is the misconduct, because a disclosed conflict can be managed while an undisclosed one operates without oversight.

### One-Time Disclosure That Misses Evolving Conflicts

Requiring conflict disclosure only at hire captures the conflicts that exist on day one but misses the conflicts that arise throughout employment — a new outside job, a financial interest acquired through inheritance, a family member hired by a vendor, a romantic relationship that develops between colleagues. The trap is that the initial disclosure feels sufficient because it was comprehensive at the time, but conflicts are dynamic, and a static disclosure process is stale within months. Require annual disclosure refreshers and event-driven disclosure whenever a new conflict arises, and build the expectation that disclosure is ongoing, because a conflict that arose three years ago and was never re-disclosed is effectively undisclosed.

### Inconsistent Application Based On Role Or Performance

Applying conflict rules strictly to lower-level employees while tolerating or not investigating conflicts involving leaders or high performers creates a two-tier system that destroys the credibility of the entire conflict management framework. The trap is that investigating a leader's conflict is politically difficult and investigating a high performer's conflict risks losing talent, so these conflicts are handled informally or not at all, while the same conflict in a junior employee is treated as a violation. Apply conflict rules consistently regardless of role, seniority, or performance, and apply stricter standards to leadership and sensitive roles precisely because their conflicts carry greater risk, because a conflict system that exempts the powerful is a system that protects the wrong people.

### Dismissing The Appearance Of Conflict

Focusing only on actual conflicts — where decision-making is demonstrably biased — while dismissing concerns about the appearance of conflict ignores that the appearance alone erodes trust and creates the perception of favoritism, even when no actual bias occurred. The trap is that the appearance standard feels unfair to the individual whose decisions were genuinely unbiased, so the organization declines to act, but the workforce sees the unmanaged appearance and draws its own conclusions. Address the appearance of conflict with the same seriousness as actual conflict, particularly for leadership and public-facing roles, because trust is shaped by perception, and a conflict that appears improper damages trust whether or not it is actually improper.

### Over-Managing Minor Conflicts While Missing Major Ones

Applying heavy-handed management — formal investigation, recusal demands, disciplinary action — to minor conflicts (a junior employee receiving a branded pen from a vendor) while missing or tolerating significant conflicts (a leader steering contracts to a vendor they have a financial interest in) inverts the risk framework and wastes resources on trivia while real exposure goes unaddressed. The trap is that minor conflicts are easy to identify and manage, so they fill the case load, while significant conflicts are complex and politically sensitive, so they are deferred. Assess severity before assigning management, apply proportionate responses (monitoring for minor, recusal or divestiture for significant), and ensure that the most serious conflicts receive the most attention, because a conflict system that is busy with trivia is not managing risk, it is performing compliance theater.

### Informal Management Without Documentation

Handling conflicts through conversations and verbal agreements — "just recuse yourself from that decision" — without documenting the conflict, the assessment, or the management decision leaves no auditable record, so when the conflict later surfaces in an audit, investigation, or dispute, there is no evidence that it was identified and managed. The trap is that informal management is faster and less confrontational, but it creates a record gap that makes the organization look like it ignored the conflict. Document every conflict disclosure, the severity assessment, the management decision, and the recusal or separation arrangement, because in a conflict of interest, the documentation that the conflict was identified and managed is the organization's primary defense.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is a culture of disclosure built, where having a conflict is expected and concealment is the misconduct?
- [ ] Is disclosure required regularly, such as annually, and event-driven, with detailed forms for sensitive roles?
- [ ] Are common conflict types, including outside employment, financial interests, nepotism, gifts, and self-dealing, identifiable by employees and leaders?
- [ ] Is severity assessed and management applied proportionately, from monitoring to recusal to reassignment?
- [ ] Are recusal and separation of duties implemented and documented for significant conflicts?
- [ ] Are hiring and supervision conflicts, including nepotism and romantic relationships, managed through disclosure and reassignment?
- [ ] Are vendor and gift conflicts managed through clear thresholds, disclosure, and consistent application?
- [ ] Are leadership and sensitive-role conflicts managed more strictly, addressing appearances as well as actual conflicts?
