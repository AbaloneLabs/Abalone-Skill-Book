# Disciplinary Action And Progressive Discipline

Discipline is one of the most consequential actions an organization takes, affecting the employee's livelihood, the team's perception of fairness, and the organization's legal exposure. The agent must understand that effective discipline is proportionate, predictable, documented, and focused on correction rather than punishment, that progressive discipline provides a fair framework for most situations but is not a rigid sequence that must be followed in every case, and that inconsistent or undocumented discipline creates legal liability and destroys trust in the organization's fairness. Discipline is not just about the individual case; it is a signal to the entire workforce about how the organization handles its people.

Use this skill when designing or applying disciplinary action or progressive discipline. The goal is to make the agent apply discipline that is fair, consistent, documented, and focused on appropriate outcomes.

## Core Rules

### Use Progressive Discipline As A Framework, Not A Straitjacket

Progressive discipline, escalating steps from verbal warning to written warning to final warning to termination, provides a fair framework for most situations, but it is not mandatory in every case.

- Use progressive discipline for performance issues and most misconduct, where correction is the goal and the employee can improve.
- Recognize that serious misconduct, such as theft, violence, or severe harassment, may warrant immediate termination without prior steps.
- Document the rationale for departing from progressive steps, when departure is justified, so the decision is defensible.
- Avoid both rigid adherence that prevents appropriate action and erratic departure that appears arbitrary.

### Establish Clear Expectations And Give Notice

Discipline is fair only when expectations were clear and the employee had notice of the problem and opportunity to correct.

- Ensure expectations, through policies, job descriptions, and performance standards, were clear before discipline.
- Give the employee notice of the specific issue, with enough detail to understand and respond.
- Provide an opportunity to improve, with clear expectations and a reasonable timeframe, except in cases of serious misconduct.
- Avoid disciplining for expectations that were never communicated or standards that were not previously applied.

### Be Specific About The Conduct Or Performance Issue

Vague discipline cannot guide improvement and cannot be defended. Be specific.

- Identify the specific conduct or performance issue, with dates, examples, and impact, not general characterizations such as "bad attitude."
- Distinguish performance issues, capability or effort gaps, from misconduct, behavioral or policy violations, as they may warrant different approaches.
- Connect the issue to the specific policy, expectation, or standard violated.
- Avoid mixing unrelated issues into a disciplinary action, which obscures the focus and appears retaliatory.

### Apply Discipline Consistently

Consistency is the core of fair discipline. Inconsistent discipline, for similar issues handled differently, creates liability and destroys trust.

- Apply similar responses to similar conduct or performance issues, across employees, roles, and departments.
- Avoid factors that should not influence discipline, such as the employee's protected characteristics, their past complaints, or their popularity.
- Be aware of patterns: if discipline falls disproportionately on certain groups, investigate whether bias or systemic issues are at play.
- Document the rationale for the level of discipline chosen, referencing how similar cases were handled.

### Document Thoroughly

Discipline that is not documented did not happen, from a legal and management perspective. Documentation is essential.

- Document the issue, the investigation or review, the employee's response, the decision, and the expectations going forward.
- Use clear, factual language; avoid characterizations, opinions, or emotional language.
- Document the conversation with the employee, including their response, and provide a written summary to the employee.
- Retain documentation in the appropriate file, according to the retention schedule.
- See the HR recordkeeping skill for detailed guidance on documentation retention.

### Provide Due Process And Opportunity To Respond

Fair discipline includes giving the employee an opportunity to tell their side before action is taken.

- Present the concern to the employee and listen to their response before finalizing discipline.
- Consider the employee's explanation; it may change the understanding of the issue or the appropriate response.
- Avoid predetermined outcomes where the conversation is a formality; be genuinely open to the employee's perspective.
- For serious matters, conduct a proper investigation before discipline. See the workplace misconduct investigation skill for detailed guidance.

### Focus On Correction And Set Clear Improvement Expectations

For performance and correctable misconduct, the goal of discipline is correction. Set clear expectations for improvement.

- Define specifically what must change, in observable, measurable terms.
- Set a reasonable timeframe for improvement and define how progress will be evaluated.
- Provide support, such as training, coaching, or resources, where the issue is capability rather than willfulness.
- Define the consequences of failure to improve, so the employee understands the stakes.
- Follow up on the expectations, rather than issuing discipline and moving on without checking progress.

### Consider Mitigating And Aggravating Factors

The level of discipline should consider the circumstances, not just the conduct. Mitigating and aggravating factors affect proportionality.

- Mitigating factors: first offense, unclear expectations, provocation, the employee's overall record, or willingness to correct.
- Aggravating factors: repeat behavior, pattern of misconduct, harm caused, leadership position, or willfulness.
- Document the factors considered, so the proportionality of the response is clear.
- Apply factors consistently, not as a mechanism for favoritism or bias.

## Common Traps

### Undocumented Discipline

Issuing verbal warnings or actions without documentation, leaving no record and creating disputes. Document thoroughly.

### Inconsistent Application

Handling similar issues differently across employees, creating liability and perceptions of bias. Apply consistently.

### Vague Or Character-Based Discipline

Disciplining for "attitude" without specifics, providing no guidance and no defense. Be specific about conduct and impact.

### Predetermined Outcomes

Treating the employee's response opportunity as a formality with a predetermined result, undermining fairness. Be genuinely open to response.

### Discipline Without Expectation Of Improvement

Issuing discipline without defining what must change or following up, ensuring recurrence. Set clear expectations and follow up.

### Ignoring Protected Activity

Disciplining shortly after an employee engaged in protected activity, such as complaining, creating retaliation claims. Avoid timing that appears retaliatory and document the independent basis.

### Rigid Or Erratic Departure From Progressive Steps

Either following progressive steps rigidly when immediate action is warranted, or departing erratically without rationale. Use judgment with documented rationale.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is progressive discipline used as a framework for correctable issues, with documented rationale for any departure?
- [ ] Were expectations clear and communicated before discipline, with notice and opportunity to improve?
- [ ] Is the discipline specific about the conduct or performance issue, with dates, examples, and connection to the standard violated?
- [ ] Is discipline applied consistently across employees, roles, and departments, with documented rationale for the level chosen?
- [ ] Is discipline documented thoroughly, with factual language, employee response, and written summary provided?
- [ ] Is the employee given due process and opportunity to respond before action is finalized?
- [ ] Are improvement expectations clear, measurable, time-bound, and supported, with follow-up planned?
- [ ] Are mitigating and aggravating factors considered and documented, applied consistently without bias?
