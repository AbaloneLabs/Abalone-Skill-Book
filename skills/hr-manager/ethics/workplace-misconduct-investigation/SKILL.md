# Workplace Misconduct Investigation

Misconduct investigations, into fraud, theft, harassment, safety violations, policy breaches, or ethical violations, carry higher stakes than ordinary employee relations investigations. They may involve legal exposure, regulatory notification, law enforcement coordination, and significant consequences for the accused and the organization. The agent must understand that misconduct investigations require careful planning, evidence preservation, confidentiality, coordination with legal and security, and protection of the rights of all parties, and that mishandling, through premature conclusions, evidence spoliation, or breach of confidentiality, can create liability exceeding the original misconduct. See also the workplace investigations skill in employee-relations for foundational investigation process; this skill addresses the additional considerations for serious misconduct.

Use this skill when investigating allegations of serious workplace misconduct. The goal is to make the agent conduct investigations that are thorough, fair, legally sound, and protective of all parties and the organization.

## Core Rules

### Assess Severity And Plan Before Acting

Serious misconduct allegations require assessment and planning before any action. Reacting impulsively compromises the investigation.

- Assess the severity and potential impact: legal exposure, regulatory implications, safety risk, financial harm, and reputational risk.
- Determine the investigation scope, team, and approach before beginning, based on the assessment.
- Identify whether legal counsel should be engaged, whether the investigation should be privileged, and whether external investigators are needed.
- Identify preservation requirements for evidence, such as email, documents, and systems, before they can be altered.
- Determine whether interim measures, such as suspension, are needed to protect the investigation, victims, or the organization.

### Engage Legal Counsel Early

Misconduct investigations often have legal implications. Engage counsel early to protect the organization and ensure proper process.

- Engage legal counsel for allegations involving potential legal exposure, regulatory issues, or significant consequences.
- Consider conducting the investigation under legal privilege, particularly for sensitive or high-stakes matters, to protect findings from discovery.
- Follow counsel's guidance on process, documentation, and communication.
- Coordinate with counsel on any regulatory or law enforcement notification obligations.
- Do not let the absence of counsel excuse poor process; when counsel is not engaged, apply rigorous process regardless.

### Preserve Evidence Promptly And Properly

Evidence can be altered or destroyed quickly. Preservation must be immediate and proper.

- Issue a litigation hold or evidence preservation notice to relevant employees, requiring retention of documents, emails, and data.
- Preserve electronic evidence, such as email, system logs, and access records, through IT, before they are overwritten.
- Preserve physical evidence, such as documents, devices, or materials, securely.
- Document the chain of custody for evidence, to maintain its integrity.
- Avoid spoliation, the destruction or alteration of evidence, which can create separate liability.

### Maintain Confidentiality Appropriately

Confidentiality protects the investigation, the parties, and the organization, but it must be balanced against the need for transparency and the rights of the accused.

- Share information strictly on a need-to-know basis, with investigators, decision-makers, and counsel.
- Instruct participants, including witnesses and the accused, to maintain confidentiality, to protect the investigation integrity.
- Do not use confidentiality to conceal the outcome from those with a legitimate need to know, such as the reporter or affected parties.
- Recognize that absolute confidentiality is often impossible; manage information flow rather than promising secrecy.
- Be cautious with public or organizational communications, which can affect legal positions and reputations.

### Protect The Rights Of The Accused

The accused has rights, regardless of the allegations. Protecting those rights is both fair and legally prudent.

- Inform the accused of the allegations in sufficient detail to respond, consistent with investigation integrity.
- Provide the accused an opportunity to respond, present evidence, and identify witnesses.
- Do not predetermine the outcome before the investigation is complete; maintain the presumption of innocence until findings are reached.
- Apply consistent process regardless of the accused's role or seniority.
- Avoid treating the investigation as a formality if the outcome is predetermined, which undermines credibility and creates liability.

### Protect Witnesses And Reporters From Retaliation

Witnesses and reporters are vulnerable to retaliation. Protect them actively.

- Apply anti-retaliation protections to all participants, as outlined in the ethics reporting skill.
- Monitor the treatment of witnesses and reporters during and after the investigation.
- Address any retaliation firmly, as a separate misconduct matter.

### Coordinate With Security, IT, And Other Functions

Misconduct investigations often require coordination across functions. Engage the right expertise.

- Engage IT for electronic evidence preservation and analysis.
- Engage security for physical evidence, threats, or safety concerns.
- Engage finance or audit for financial misconduct, such as fraud or theft.
- Coordinate communications, so that messaging is consistent and does not compromise the investigation.

### Document Thoroughly And Reach Findings Based On Evidence

The investigation must be documented thoroughly, and findings must be based on evidence, not assumption.

- Document the investigation plan, interviews, evidence, analysis, and findings.
- Apply the appropriate standard of proof, typically a preponderance of the evidence for workplace matters, not the criminal standard.
- Distinguish substantiated findings, supported by evidence, from unsubstantiated allegations, where evidence is insufficient.
- Base conclusions on the evidence collected, not on assumptions about character or past behavior.
- Prepare a written report, subject to counsel review where applicable.

### Take Appropriate Action And Close The Loop

Investigation findings must lead to appropriate action, communicated to relevant parties.

- Apply disciplinary or corrective action proportionate to the substantiated findings, consistently with how similar cases have been handled.
- Address systemic issues identified, through policy change, training, or process improvement.
- Communicate outcomes to the reporter, within confidentiality limits, and to the accused.
- Document the resolution and retain records according to the retention schedule.

## Common Traps

### Acting Before Planning

Reacting to allegations impulsively — confronting the accused before a plan is developed, interviewing witnesses in a disorganized sequence, making preliminary findings before evidence is collected — compromises the investigation's integrity by creating leading statements, tipping off the accused, and producing testimony that is shaped by the investigator's initial reaction rather than by the evidence. The trap is that serious allegations create urgency, and the urgency feels like diligence, but acting without a plan destroys the very evidence the investigation needs to collect. Assess the severity, develop an investigation plan — scope, witness sequence, evidence to collect, legal involvement, interim measures — before any interviews or confrontations, because a rushed investigation produces findings that cannot be defended and evidence that has been contaminated by the rush.

### Evidence Spoliation Through Delayed Preservation

Failing to issue a litigation hold and preserve relevant evidence — emails, messages, documents, system logs, security footage — immediately upon learning of the allegation allows evidence to be altered, deleted, or overwritten through routine retention cycles, and the loss of evidence creates separate liability for spoliation that is independent of the underlying misconduct. The trap is that preservation feels premature before the investigation has determined whether the allegation is substantiated, but the duty to preserve attaches when litigation is reasonably anticipated, which is at the time of the allegation, not the conclusion. Issue preservation notices and engage IT to preserve all relevant systems immediately upon receiving a serious allegation, before the first interview, because evidence that is lost cannot be recovered and the spoliation claim may be more damaging than the original allegation.

### Predetermined Conclusions Disguised As Investigation

Conducting an investigation where the conclusion has already been determined — by leadership, by prior knowledge of the accused, or by the investigator's initial assumption — produces an investigation that goes through the motions of fairness while delivering a foregone result, and the sham is discoverable through the investigation record (leading questions, selective evidence, ignored exculpatory information). The trap is that the investigator may genuinely believe the conclusion is obvious, but presenting the investigation as open-minded when it is not invalidates the process and the outcome. Maintain an genuinely open, evidence-based process, follow the evidence wherever it leads, document exculpatory as well as inculpatory findings, and if the evidence does not support the initial assumption, say so, because an investigation that cannot acquit is not an investigation, it is a prosecution, and prosecutions conducted under the guise of investigation create liability.

### Breach Of Confidentiality Beyond Need-To-Know

Sharing investigation information — the allegation, the identities of the reporter and witnesses, the evidence, the findings — with employees who do not have a legitimate need to know compromises the investigation by tipping off witnesses, enabling coordination of stories, exposing the reporter and witnesses to retaliation, and damaging the reputations of parties who may ultimately be cleared. The trap is that investigation information spreads through the natural human impulse to discuss dramatic events, and each person who is told "in confidence" tells another, until the confidentiality is hollow. Restrict investigation information to those with a legitimate need to know — the investigators, legal, decision-makers — instruct every person involved in the confidentiality obligation, and recognize that breach of confidentiality is itself actionable misconduct, because an investigation that becomes known cannot gather candid testimony and cannot protect its participants.

### Denying The Accused Notice And Opportunity To Respond

Confronting the accused with findings or discipline without first providing notice of the specific allegations and a genuine opportunity to respond denies the accused the due process that workplace fairness requires and that legal standards increasingly demand, and it forfeits the exculpatory or mitigating information that the accused could provide if given the chance. The trap is that the evidence may appear overwhelming, so the accused's account seems unnecessary, but the accused often holds information — context, evidence, alternative explanations — that changes the analysis. Inform the accused of the specific allegations in advance of the decision, provide the evidence to be considered, and allow a genuine opportunity to respond before findings are finalized, because a conclusion reached without the accused's input is a conclusion reached without complete information, and it cannot withstand challenge.

### Retaliation Against Witnesses And Reporters

Allowing retaliation — overt or subtle — against employees who report misconduct or participate as witnesses destroys the investigation system's credibility and creates independent liability, because retaliation protections extend to participants in the investigation process, not just to the original reporter. The trap is that retaliation after an investigation is often subtle (exclusion, changed assignments, social ostracism) and is committed by peers rather than by formal decision-makers, so it is difficult to detect and easy to dismiss. Monitor the treatment of reporters and witnesses during and after the investigation, train managers on what constitutes retaliation, provide reporters and witnesses with a safe channel to report retaliatory treatment, and act swiftly against retaliation, because one act of retaliation against a witness teaches every potential witness that participation is dangerous.

### Inconsistent Action Based On Role Or Seniority

Handling similar misconduct differently depending on the accused's role, seniority, or perceived value to the organization — terminating a junior employee for conduct that draws only a warning for a senior leader — undermines the investigation system's credibility and creates direct evidence of disparate treatment. The trap is that the senior leader's context is more complex, the business cost of discipline is higher, and the political dynamics are more sensitive, so the resolution differs, but the difference is perceived as a double standard. Apply consistent standards of evidence and proportionate action regardless of the accused's position, document the rationale for the level of action, and recognize that investigating a senior leader requires at least the same rigor as investigating anyone else, because an investigation system that protects the powerful is a system that investigates only the weak.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is severity assessed and a plan developed before any action, including scope, team, legal engagement, and interim measures?
- [ ] Is legal counsel engaged early, with privilege considered for sensitive matters?
- [ ] Is evidence preserved promptly through holds, IT engagement, and chain-of-custody documentation?
- [ ] Is confidentiality maintained on a need-to-know basis, balanced against transparency and rights?
- [ ] Is the accused informed of allegations and given opportunity to respond, with process consistent regardless of role?
- [ ] Are witnesses and reporters protected from retaliation, with monitoring during and after the investigation?
- [ ] Are security, IT, finance, and other functions coordinated as needed for expertise?
- [ ] Are findings based on evidence with appropriate standard of proof, documented in a written report?
- [ ] Is appropriate action taken proportionate to findings, with systemic issues addressed and outcomes communicated?
