# International Assignment Data and Cross-Border Privacy

International assignments create a privacy challenge that domestic HR does not face: employee data must move across borders, and each jurisdiction imposes its own rules on that data. An employee on assignment from Germany to the United States has data protected by the EU's GDPR, U.S. privacy laws, and potentially the host country's regime — and the transfer of that data between countries is itself regulated. Mishandling cross-border data creates legal liability, assignment delays, and employee privacy harm. The discipline is to map the data flows for each assignment, to establish lawful transfer mechanisms, to apply the highest applicable protection standard, and to coordinate data handling across the home, host, and any intermediate jurisdictions.

## Core Rules

### Map the Data Flows for Each Assignment

Before an assignment begins, map the employee data that will flow across borders: what data (identity, compensation, performance, health, immigration, tax), from where (home country HR systems), to where (host country HR, payroll, tax, immigration providers), through what intermediaries (global mobility providers, tax equalization vendors, immigration counsel), and for what purposes. This map reveals the jurisdictions involved and the transfer points where cross-border rules apply. Without this map, cross-border data handling is opaque and compliance is left to chance. Update the map as the assignment evolves (new vendors, new purposes, repatriation), because data flows change over the assignment lifecycle.

### Establish Lawful Cross-Border Transfer Mechanisms

Cross-border data transfers require a lawful transfer mechanism under the relevant regimes. Under the GDPR, transfers from the EU to most non-EU countries require an adequacy decision (the destination country is deemed adequate), standard contractual clauses (binding data protection terms with the recipient), binding corporate rules (for intra-group transfers), or explicit consent (narrow and difficult to rely on for employment). Other regimes have their own mechanisms. Establish the appropriate mechanism for each transfer before the data flows, document it, and maintain it (mechanisms like SCCs may require updates as law evolves). Transfers without a lawful mechanism violate the source regime and create liability. Consult privacy counsel for complex transfer patterns, because the mechanisms are technical and jurisdiction-specific.

### Apply the Highest Applicable Protection Standard

When an employee's data is subject to multiple privacy regimes (e.g., an EU citizen on assignment in the U.S.), apply the highest applicable protection standard rather than the lowest common denominator. The GDPR's protections — data minimization, purpose limitation, employee rights to access and erasure, breach notification — generally exceed those of many other regimes, and applying them globally for cross-border assignments simplifies compliance and protects the employee. This approach also future-proofs against regulatory evolution: if a jurisdiction tightens its rules, the organization is already meeting a higher standard. Avoid the failure of applying only the host country's (weaker) standard to an employee whose home country protections are stronger, which violates the home regime and under-protects the employee.

### Coordinate Data Handling Across Home, Host, and Intermediaries

Cross-border assignments involve multiple parties handling employee data: home country HR, host country HR, payroll providers in both countries, tax equalization vendors, immigration counsel, global mobility providers, and relocation services. Each party's data handling must be governed — contracts with data protection terms, defined purposes and retention, security requirements, and breach notification obligations. Coordinate so that each party handles only the data it needs, for defined purposes, with appropriate protections, and that the overall data flow is coherent rather than a tangle of ungoverned transfers. The complexity is real but manageable with a data processing inventory and consistent contractual terms across vendors.

### Respect Employee Data Rights Across Jurisdictions

Employees retain their data subject rights — access, rectification, erasure, portability, objection — regardless of where their data is processed or where they are assigned. An EU employee on assignment in Asia can exercise GDPR rights against the data held in either location. Build the capacity to honor these rights across the assignment data landscape: a process for the employee to request access or correction, the ability to compile the response from dispersed systems, and timelines that meet the applicable regime's requirements (GDPR allows one month). Failure to honor employee data rights is a violation and erodes the trust that supports assignment success.

### Manage Data at Assignment End and Repatriation

Assignment end — repatriation, localization, or departure — triggers data handling decisions: what data returns to the home country, what is retained in the host country (and for how long), what is deleted, and how the transfer back is lawfully executed. Plan this data handling as part of repatriation planning, not as an afterthought. Host country retention obligations (e.g., tax records that must be retained for a period) may require retaining some data in the host country even after repatriation. Coordinate with privacy counsel to ensure the end-of-assignment data handling complies with all applicable regimes. Data left ungoverned at assignment end creates retention violations and security risk.

### Prepare for Cross-Border Data Breaches

A data breach involving cross-border assignment data may trigger notification obligations in multiple jurisdictions, each with its own timeline and requirements. The GDPR requires notification to the supervisory authority within 72 hours and to affected individuals without undue delay; other regimes have different triggers and timelines. Prepare a cross-border breach response plan that identifies the potentially applicable regimes, the notification triggers and timelines for each, and the internal process for assessing and reporting. A breach response that misses a jurisdiction's notification deadline creates liability on top of the breach itself. Cross-border breach response is complex; plan it before you need it.

## Common Traps

### Data Flows Unmapped

Without a data flow map, cross-border handling is opaque and compliance is left to chance. Map the flows for each assignment and update as they evolve.

### Transfers Without Lawful Mechanism

Cross-border transfers without an adequate mechanism violate the source regime. Establish and maintain the appropriate mechanism before data flows.

### Applying the Lowest Common Denominator Standard

Applying only the host country's weaker standard to an employee with stronger home protections violates the home regime. Apply the highest applicable standard.

### Intermediaries' Data Handling Ungoverned

Vendors and intermediaries handling assignment data without contractual data protection terms create ungoverned transfers. Govern each party with consistent terms.

### Employee Data Rights Unhonored Across Borders

Failure to honor access, rectification, or erasure rights across dispersed systems violates the applicable regime. Build cross-jurisdiction rights-handling capacity.

### End-of-Assignment Data Left Ungoverned

Data at repatriation or departure without planned handling creates retention violations and security risk. Plan end-of-assignment data handling as part of repatriation.

### Cross-Border Breach Response Unplanned

A breach triggering multi-jurisdiction notification without a plan leads to missed deadlines and liability. Prepare a cross-border breach response plan before it is needed.

## Self-Check

- Have I mapped the data flows for each assignment — what data, from where, to where, through whom, for what purposes — and kept it updated?
- Have I established a lawful transfer mechanism (adequacy, SCCs, BCRs) for each cross-border transfer, documented and maintained?
- Am I applying the highest applicable protection standard rather than the lowest common denominator across jurisdictions?
- Have I governed each intermediary's data handling with contractual data protection terms, defined purposes, and security requirements?
- Can I honor employee data rights — access, rectification, erasure, portability — across the dispersed assignment data landscape within required timelines?
- Have I planned end-of-assignment data handling — what returns, what is retained, what is deleted, lawful transfer back — as part of repatriation?
- Have I prepared a cross-border breach response plan identifying applicable regimes, triggers, timelines, and the internal process?
- Are assumptions, uncertainties, and confidence levels stated explicitly rather than buried in a confident-sounding conclusion?
