# Sustainability and ESG Reporting for People Metrics

Environmental, Social, and Governance (ESG) reporting has expanded from a niche investor concern to a mainstream expectation, and the "Social" pillar increasingly demands workforce disclosures — diversity statistics, pay equity, training hours, safety incidents, turnover, and human rights practices. These disclosures are now scrutinized by investors, regulators, customers, prospective employees, and activists, and they carry legal risk if they misrepresent the organization's actual performance. HR's role is to provide accurate, well-defined, audit-ready people data for these disclosures, to understand the frameworks and standards that govern them, and to navigate the tension between transparency (which stakeholders demand) and the temptation to present metrics in the most favorable light (which undermines credibility when reality diverges).

## Core Rules

### Understand the Reporting Frameworks and Their Requirements

ESG reporting is governed by an evolving landscape of frameworks and standards — including GRI (Global Reporting Initiative), SASB (now part of ISSB), TCFD (for climate, with workforce implications), the EU's CSRD and ESRS, and various SEC proposals. Each framework specifies what workforce data must be disclosed, how it should be defined, and what level of assurance is required. Understand which frameworks apply to your organization based on geography, listing status, customer requirements, and industry. Track the evolving requirements, as the regulatory landscape is shifting rapidly toward mandatory, audited disclosure. Engage with the finance, legal, and sustainability functions that own ESG reporting overall, and ensure HR understands its specific obligations within the broader reporting process.

### Define Workforce Metrics With the Same Rigor as Financial Metrics

ESG-disclosed workforce metrics must be defined with the precision and consistency of financial metrics, because they are increasingly subject to audit and because they will be compared year over year and against peers. Document the definition of each disclosed metric: the population in scope, the measurement methodology, the data source, the consolidation approach (for global organizations, how entities are included), the reporting period, and any restatements of prior periods. Apply the definitions consistently across reporting periods, and document and disclose any methodology changes with restated comparatives. A metric defined loosely for internal use is inadequate for external disclosure; a metric that changes definition silently undermines comparability and credibility.

### Ensure Data Quality and Audit Readiness

ESG-reported workforce data is increasingly subject to external assurance (audit), and the data must withstand that scrutiny. Establish data governance for ESG metrics: documented definitions, controlled data sources, reconciliation to source systems, audit trails of data compilation and approval, and evidence of internal review. Conduct pre-assurance readiness assessments to identify gaps before external auditors find them. Treat ESG data with the same control discipline as financial data — because in many jurisdictions, it is becoming subject to the same legal liability for misrepresentation. Data quality problems discovered during external assurance damage credibility and may trigger regulatory consequences.

### Report Honestly, Including Challenges and Shortfalls

The temptation in ESG reporting is to present only favorable metrics and to omit or obscure unfavorable ones. This is short-sighted and increasingly risky. Stakeholders — particularly investors and regulators — value honesty about challenges and progress, and they punish discovery of greenwashing or selective disclosure. Report the metrics that frameworks require, including those that reflect poorly on current performance, alongside the actions being taken to improve. A company that reports a pay equity gap transparently and describes its remediation plan builds more credibility than one that obscures the gap and is later exposed. Honest reporting that includes challenges is more sustainable than polished reporting that reality will contradict.

### Navigate the Tension Between Transparency and Risk

Workforce transparency serves stakeholders but also creates risk: competitors gain insight into your workforce composition, activists may use disclosures to pressure the organization, and unfavorable metrics may attract negative attention. Navigate this tension deliberately rather than defaulting to minimal disclosure. Understand what is required by framework and regulation (non-negotiable), what is expected by key stakeholders (customers, investors, employees), and what is voluntary. For voluntary disclosures, weigh the credibility and engagement benefits of transparency against the risks, and make informed decisions rather than reflexive minimization. The trend across jurisdictions is toward more mandatory disclosure, so building disclosure capability now prepares the organization for the trajectory.

### Coordinate Across Functions to Ensure Consistency

Workforce data in ESG reports must be consistent with data reported elsewhere — in annual reports, in regulatory filings, in customer questionnaires, and in employer branding. Inconsistencies (different headcount numbers in the annual report and the sustainability report, different diversity definitions across disclosures) undermine credibility and create legal risk. Establish a single source of truth for each workforce metric, coordinate disclosure across functions through a governance process, and reconcile metrics before publication. The HR function owns the people data, but the sustainability, finance, legal, and communications functions all participate in disclosure, and coordination is essential.

### Prepare for Investor and Stakeholder Engagement on Workforce Topics

Investors increasingly engage directly with companies on workforce topics — asking about pay equity, diversity, labor practices, training investment, and human capital management. HR leaders may be called upon to participate in these engagements, providing context beyond the reported metrics. Prepare for this engagement: understand the metrics, their context and trajectory, the actions being taken, and the honest answers to difficult questions. Train HR leaders in investor engagement, which differs from internal HR communication in its expectations for precision, evidence, and strategic framing. Unprepared engagement damages credibility; prepared engagement builds investor confidence.

### Connect Workforce Disclosures to Strategy and Action

ESG reporting that presents metrics without connecting them to strategy and action reads as compliance rather than commitment. Connect each material workforce disclosure to the organization's strategy (why this metric matters to the business), the current performance (honestly reported), and the actions being taken (targets, initiatives, progress). This narrative demonstrates that the organization understands the metric, owns its performance, and is committed to improvement. Metrics without narrative invite the question "so what?"; metrics with strategy and action narrative demonstrate management competence and commitment.

## Common Traps

### Treating ESG Workforce Reporting as a Marketing Exercise

ESG reports that present favorable metrics while obscuring unfavorable ones risk greenwashing exposure and regulatory consequences. Report honestly, including challenges, alongside actions being taken.

### Loose Metric Definitions Inconsistent Across Periods

Metrics defined loosely or changed silently undermine comparability and credibility. Document definitions with financial-grade precision and disclose methodology changes with restated comparatives.

### Data Quality Inadequate for External Assurance

ESG data increasingly subject to audit must withstand scrutiny. Establish data governance, reconciliation, audit trails, and pre-assurance readiness assessments.

### Inconsistencies Across Disclosures

Different headcount or diversity numbers in annual reports, sustainability reports, and regulatory filings undermine credibility. Establish a single source of truth and coordinate across functions.

### Minimal Disclosure as Default

Defaulting to minimal disclosure misses the credibility and engagement benefits of transparency and leaves the organization unprepared for the trend toward mandatory disclosure. Make informed decisions about voluntary disclosure.

### Unprepared Investor Engagement

HR leaders called into investor engagement without preparation damage credibility. Understand metrics, context, trajectory, and actions, and train for the different expectations of investor communication.

### Metrics Without Strategy and Action Narrative

Metrics reported without connection to strategy, performance context, and improvement actions read as compliance. Connect each disclosure to why it matters and what is being done.

### Ignoring the Evolving Regulatory Landscape

ESG reporting requirements are expanding rapidly. Track evolving frameworks and regulations, and build disclosure capability ahead of mandates rather than scrambling at compliance deadlines.

## Self-Check

- Do I understand which ESG frameworks apply to my organization and what workforce disclosures each requires?
- Are my disclosed workforce metrics defined with financial-grade precision, documented, and applied consistently across periods with disclosed methodology changes?
- Is my ESG workforce data governed for quality and audit readiness, with reconciliation, audit trails, and pre-assurance assessments?
- Am I reporting honestly including challenges and shortfalls, alongside actions being taken, rather than only favorable metrics?
- Am I making informed decisions about voluntary disclosure, weighing transparency benefits against risks, rather than defaulting to minimal disclosure?
- Is workforce data consistent across all disclosures (annual report, sustainability report, regulatory filings, customer questionnaires)?
- Are HR leaders prepared for investor engagement on workforce topics, with precision, evidence, and strategic framing?
- Are workforce disclosures connected to strategy, performance context, and improvement actions, not just reported as standalone metrics?
