# Workplace Bullying And Incivility

Workplace bullying and incivility are often dismissed as personality issues or tolerated because the perpetrator is high-performing. But persistent bullying drives turnover, destroys psychological safety, suppresses information flow, and can escalate into conduct that creates legal liability. Unlike harassment based on a protected characteristic, bullying is not always illegal, but it is always damaging. The agent must understand that tolerating bullying because it is not unlawful is a strategic error, and that high performers who bully impose costs on the organization that often exceed their individual contribution. The challenge is to define bullying clearly, distinguish it from legitimate performance management, and intervene effectively before it escalates or causes irreversible damage.

Use this skill when addressing bullying, incivility, or abrasive behavior in the workplace. The goal is to make the agent identify and intervene on bullying effectively, while distinguishing it from tough but legitimate management.

## Core Rules

### Define Bullying Clearly And Distinguish It From Legitimate Management

Bullying is repeated, health-harming mistreatment of one or more persons, through verbal abuse, conduct that is threatening, humiliating, or intimidating, or interference that prevents work from getting done. It is distinct from legitimate management.

- Bullying is targeted, repeated, and driven by a power imbalance, whether formal or informal.
- Legitimate performance management, including direct feedback, accountability, and consequences for poor performance, is not bullying, even when it is uncomfortable.
- The distinction lies in the conduct, not the discomfort: feedback that is specific, factual, and aimed at improvement is management; personal attacks, humiliation, and intimidation are bullying.
- A single severe incident, such as a threatening outburst, can constitute bullying even without repetition.

### Identify The Patterns And Costs Of Bullying

Bullying is often invisible to leadership because it occurs in interactions that are not observed, and because targets are reluctant to report for fear of disbelief or retaliation. Identify bullying through its patterns and costs.

- Patterns: targeted criticism, exclusion from meetings or information, sabotage of work, public humiliation, taking credit for others' work, unrealistic demands, and shifting expectations designed to ensure failure.
- Costs: turnover in the target and in bystanders who witness the bullying, declining engagement and psychological safety, suppressed information and ideas, increased absenteeism and stress claims, and reputational damage.
- A high-performing manager whose team has chronic turnover, low engagement, and reluctance to speak up is likely a bully, regardless of their results.

### Do Not Tolerate Bullying Because The Perpetrator Is High-Performing

A common failure is tolerating bullying because the perpetrator is a top salesperson, technical expert, or rainmaker. This tradeoff is usually a net loss for the organization.

- Calculate the full cost: turnover of targets and bystanders, lost productivity, legal risk, and the suppression of contribution from the bullied team.
- Recognize that the perpetrator's performance is often dependent on the team they are abusing, and that the team's contribution is being suppressed.
- High performance does not license misconduct. The standard of conduct applies to all employees, regardless of contribution.
- Document the costs and the interventions, so that the decision to tolerate or address bullying is based on evidence, not on the perpetrator's reputation.

### Intervene Directly And Early

Bullying does not resolve itself. Without intervention, it escalates and causes increasing damage. Intervene as soon as a pattern is identified.

- Address the conduct directly with the perpetrator, citing specific behaviors and their impact, not vague concerns about style.
- Set clear expectations for changed behavior and the consequences of continuation.
- Do not send the target to training, conflict resolution, or resilience coaching; the problem is the perpetrator's conduct, not the target's response.
- Do not transfer the target to escape the bully; this punishes the victim and leaves the bully in place to harm others.
- Monitor the perpetrator's behavior after the intervention and enforce consequences if the bullying continues.

### Investigate When Bullying Crosses Into Misconduct

Bullying can coexist with, or escalate into, conduct that violates policy or law, such as harassment based on a protected characteristic, threats of violence, or retaliation. When bullying appears to cross these lines, investigate.

- Assess whether the bullying is targeted at members of a protected class, which may indicate discriminatory harassment.
- Assess whether the bullying includes threats, physical intimidation, or destruction of property, which may constitute violence or safety violations.
- Assess whether the target has engaged in protected activity, which may indicate retaliation.
- When in doubt, investigate; bullying that is also harassment or retaliation carries legal liability that informal intervention cannot resolve.

### Support The Target And Rebuild Psychological Safety

Targets of bullying often suffer significant harm: stress, anxiety, diminished confidence, and damaged performance. Supporting the target and rebuilding the team's psychological safety is part of addressing bullying.

- Acknowledge the harm done to the target, without minimizing it or blaming them.
- Provide access to support resources, such as an employee assistance program.
- Restore the target's working conditions, including assignments, reputation, and relationships that were damaged.
- Rebuild the team's psychological safety by demonstrating that bullying is addressed and that speaking up is safe and effective.

### Address The Systemic Conditions That Enable Bullying

Bullying often thrives in conditions that enable it: lack of clear conduct standards, leadership that tolerates or models bullying, performance systems that reward individual results without regard for conduct, and absence of reporting channels. Address these conditions to prevent recurrence.

- Establish and enforce clear conduct standards, distinct from performance standards.
- Hold leaders accountable for the conduct of their teams, not only the results.
- Include conduct and people-management in performance evaluation and rewards.
- Provide channels for reporting bullying that are safe from retaliation.
- Assess whether leadership itself models bullying behavior, which licenses it throughout the organization.

## Common Traps

### The High Performer Who Is Exempt From Standards

The bullying complaints arrive about the top salesperson — the one who generates 40% of revenue, who senior leadership considers untouchable. HR investigates, substantiates the behavior, and then faces the real decision: discipline the rainmaker and risk the revenue, or tolerate the behavior and lose the team. The organization chooses to "coach" the high performer, the behavior moderates temporarily, and the team continues to bleed talent. The trap is that exempting high performers from behavioral standards teaches the entire organization that performance buys permission to abuse, which attracts more high-performing bullies, drives out the collaborators who will not tolerate the environment, and converts a people problem into a structural one. The revenue the high performer generates is offset by the turnover, the lost productivity of the intimidated team, the management time consumed by the dysfunction, and the legal exposure — but those costs are diffuse and invisible while the revenue is concentrated and visible. The decision to discipline a high performer is hard, but the decision not to is a decision to build the organization around the bully.

### Bullying Dismissed As Tough Management

The manager yells, publicly humiliates, sets impossible deadlines, and changes expectations without notice. HR receives complaints and interprets the behavior as "demanding" or "old-school" management — a style issue, not a conduct issue. The trap is that the line between demanding management and bullying is crossed when the behavior is intended to intimidate, humiliate, or control rather than to drive performance, and the effect on the team (fear, silence, turnover, illness) is the evidence. Dismissing bullying as management style legitimizes the behavior, because the organization has effectively said this is acceptable, and the manager escalates with confidence. The distinction is not subjective: persistent, targeted, humiliating conduct that a reasonable person would find hostile is bullying regardless of the manager's intent, because the impact on the team — not the manager's self-perception — is the standard, and an organization that cannot distinguish demanding from degrading has lost the ability to protect its people.

### The Pattern Fragmented Across Reporters

Three employees in the same department resign within six months. Each exit interview cites "better opportunity." No pattern is detected. What the exit interviews did not capture — because no one asked the right questions, or because the employees did not feel safe being honest — is that all three reported to a manager whose behavior was the actual cause. The trap is that bullying is often invisible in the data systems that should detect it, because victims attribute departure to neutral reasons (protecting references, avoiding conflict, survey fatigue), and the organization's metrics treat each departure as independent. Bullying detection requires looking at patterns — turnover clustering by manager, engagement scores by team, exit interview themes by department, grievance frequency by leader — because the individual data points are designed to be unremarkable, and the pattern is visible only when the data is aggregated across the victims the bully has produced.

### Addressing The Bully But Not The Damage

The bully is terminated. The investigation is closed. But the team they leave behind has been damaged: trust is destroyed, the remaining employees have learned that the organization tolerated the behavior for years, high performers have already left, and the survivors are traumatized, cynical, and skeptical that the termination represents real change rather than damage control. The trap is that removing the bully addresses the cause but not the consequence, and the team's recovery requires active intervention — acknowledgment of what happened, support for those affected, rebuilding of norms, and visible reinforcement that the behavior will not recur. Treating the termination as the resolution leaves the damage untreated, and the team that was bullied continues to dysfunction even after the bully is gone, because the conditions the bully created (fear, silence, distrust) persist in the culture they shaped.

### The Witness Who Stays Silent

A team member witnesses the bullying but says nothing — not in the investigation, not in the survey, not to HR. The trap is that silence is not consent; it is self-protection, because the witness has correctly calculated that speaking up invites retaliation from the bully (if the investigation does not result in consequences) and from the organization (if the witness is perceived as a troublemaker). A culture where witnesses stay silent is a culture where bullying thrives, because the bully operates without corroboration and the victim stands alone. Building the conditions for witnesses to speak — strong anti-retaliation protections, anonymous reporting channels, visible consequences for prior bullies, leadership that models speaking up — is the only path to breaking the silence, because an investigation that cannot secure witness participation is an investigation that cannot substantiate, and a bully who cannot be substantiated is a bully who continues.

### The One-Off Training Fix

The bullying complaint is substantiated. The response: the bully is sent to an anger management workshop or a respectful workplace training. The certificate is filed, the case is closed, and the behavior resumes within weeks, because the training addressed awareness but not the structural conditions that enabled the behavior (no consequences, performance rewarded over conduct, a team that cannot report safely, a manager whose own boss tolerates the behavior). The trap is that training is the easiest response and the least effective, because bullying is sustained by a system that rewards or tolerates it, and awareness training does not change the system. The intervention must address the conditions — accountability, consequences, reporting safety, leadership modeling — because training without systemic change is a checkbox that allows the organization to claim it acted while the behavior continues.

### Incivility Treated As Too Small To Address

The behaviors are small: eye-rolls in meetings, emails copied to the boss to create pressure, interruptions, exclusion from the lunch group, sarcastic comments disguised as jokes. No single incident meets the threshold for a formal complaint, so HR does nothing. The trap is that incivility is the precursor to bullying — it is the testing ground where the aggressor learns what they can get away with — and untreated incivility escalates, because the absence of response is permission. By the time the behavior crosses the threshold into actionable bullying, the pattern is entrenched, the team is damaged, and the organization faces a much harder intervention. Addressing incivility early — through norms, feedback, and low-consequence interventions — prevents escalation, because the cost of addressing a rude comment today is a conversation, while the cost of addressing a bullying pattern tomorrow is an investigation, a termination, and a damaged team.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is bullying defined clearly, and distinguished from legitimate performance management and tough feedback?
- [ ] Are the patterns and costs of bullying identified, including turnover, engagement, and suppressed contribution?
- [ ] Is bullying addressed regardless of the perpetrator's performance, with the full cost calculated?
- [ ] Is intervention direct and early, with specific behaviors cited and consequences set?
- [ ] Is the target supported and the team's psychological safety rebuilt?
- [ ] Is bullying investigated when it crosses into harassment, threats, violence, or retaliation?
- [ ] Are systemic conditions, including leadership behavior and performance systems, addressed to prevent recurrence?
- [ ] Is the perpetrator monitored and are consequences enforced if bullying continues?
