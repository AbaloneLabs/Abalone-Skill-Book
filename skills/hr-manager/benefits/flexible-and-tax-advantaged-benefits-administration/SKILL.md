# Flexible and Tax-Advantaged Benefits Administration

Tax-advantaged benefit programs — flexible spending accounts (FSAs), health savings accounts (HSAs), dependent care FSAs, health reimbursement arrangements (HRAs), commuter benefits, and Section 125 cafeteria plans — let employees pay for eligible expenses with pre-tax dollars, reducing both employee tax burden and employer payroll taxes. These programs are popular and valuable, but they carry specific regulatory requirements, administrative complexity, and design decisions that determine their effectiveness. The discipline is to design the programs to maximize employee value within regulatory constraints, to administer elections and claims correctly, to communicate the programs so employees use them effectively, and to ensure the tax compliance that preserves the programs' advantages.

## Core Rules

### Design the Cafeteria Plan and Component Programs Deliberately

Tax-advantaged benefits operate within a Section 125 cafeteria plan (for FSAs and premium conversion) or under specific statutory authority (HSAs under Section 22, commuter benefits under Section 132). Design the overall plan and each component deliberately: which programs to offer (medical FSA, dependent care FSA, HSA-compatible plan, HRA, commuter), the contribution limits consistent with IRS annual limits, the FSA carryover or grace period rules, the HRA design (integrated with the health plan or excepted), and the eligibility and election rules. Each design choice carries regulatory and employee-value implications. Document the plan in a written plan document (required for cafeteria plans and HSAs/HRAs), and provide a summary plan description to employees. Ad hoc programs without written plans fail compliance.

### Administer Elections Within Regulatory Rules

Elections for tax-advantaged programs are governed by specific rules. Section 125 elections are generally irrevocable mid-year except for permitted change events (status change, cost or coverage change, HIPAA special enrollment, FMLA, Medicare/TRICARE, and the annual open enrollment). FSA elections are subject to the use-or-lose rule (with carryover or grace period as elected). HSA contributions can be changed mid-year more flexibly. Administer elections strictly within these rules: process permitted changes correctly, reject impermissible mid-year changes, and document the basis for each change. The most common administrative failures involve allowing election changes that do not qualify as permitted change events, which jeopardizes the plan's tax-qualified status. Train HR and payroll staff on the election rules, because errors here are both common and consequential.

### Process Claims Correctly and Timely

FSA, HRA, and dependent care claims must be processed correctly and timely for the programs to deliver value and maintain compliance. Substantiation rules require that claims be for eligible expenses (defined per program), supported by documentation (receipts, itemized statements), and incurred within the coverage period (for FSAs) or plan year. Process claims within the regulatory timeframe (claims must be paid or denied within a reasonable period, and denied claims trigger specific notice requirements). Coordinate with the third-party administrator (TPA) on claims processing, and audit the TPA's performance for accuracy and timeliness. Claims processing failures — denials without proper notice, payment of ineligible expenses, delayed processing — create employee frustration and compliance risk.

### Communicate Programs So Employees Use Them Effectively

Tax-advantaged programs are only valuable if employees understand and use them, and these programs are notoriously confusing (the difference between FSA and HSA, the use-or-lose rule, eligible expenses, contribution limits). Communicate clearly and repeatedly: explain each program in plain language at onboarding and open enrollment, provide decision-support tools (contribution calculators, eligible expense lookups), and offer year-round education (because expenses arise year-round). Highlight the tax savings concretely (a contribution of $2,000 to a medical FSA saves approximately $500-600 in taxes for a typical employee). Under-communicated programs have low participation, meaning employees leave tax savings unclaimed and the organization leaves payroll tax savings unclaimed. Communication is the lever that converts program design into program value.

### Manage HSA-Specific Considerations

HSAs differ from FSAs in important ways: they are individually owned (not use-or-lose), they require enrollment in a high-deductible health plan (HDHP), they have a triple tax advantage (contributions, growth, and withdrawals for eligible expenses all tax-free), and they carry contribution limits that include both employer and employee contributions combined. Manage HSA-specific considerations: ensure the linked health plan is HSA-eligible (no disqualifying coverage like a general-purpose HRA or non-HDHP plan), track contribution limits across all sources (employer and employee, including another employer if the employee has a spouse with family coverage), and communicate the HSA's long-term savings potential (unlike FSAs, HSAs can be invested and carried into retirement). HSA errors — disqualifying coverage, excess contributions — create tax consequences for the employee.

### Ensure Tax Reporting and Compliance

Tax-advantaged programs carry reporting obligations: FSA and HSA contributions on the W-2 (HSA contributions in Box 12 with code W), HSA reporting on Form 5498-SA (by the custodian) and Form 8889 (by the employee), cafeteria plan compliance maintained through the written plan and nondiscrimination testing. Ensure payroll and tax reporting reflect the programs correctly, coordinate with the TPA and HSA custodian on their reporting, and conduct annual nondiscrimination testing for the cafeteria plan (to ensure the plan does not favor highly compensated employees). Tax reporting errors create employee tax problems and organizational compliance liability. Engage tax counsel or a qualified benefits consultant for the testing and reporting complexity.

### Coordinate Annual Open Enrollment Carefully

Annual open enrollment is the peak period for tax-advantaged program administration, when employees make or change their elections for the coming year. Coordinate carefully: prepare clear communication and decision-support materials, ensure the enrollment system reflects current contribution limits and program design, process elections accurately and confirm them to employees, and handle the transition of FSA carryover and HSA continuing balances. Open enrollment errors — incorrect limits in the system, elections not processed, carryover not calculated — create year-long problems. Treat open enrollment as a project with a timeline, defined owners, testing, and confirmation, not as routine administration.

## Common Traps

### Programs Without Written Plan Documents

Ad hoc tax-advantaged programs without written plans fail compliance. Document the cafeteria plan and each component in written plan documents.

### Impermissible Mid-Year Election Changes

Allowing election changes that do not qualify as permitted change events jeopardizes tax-qualified status. Train staff on the rules and document each change's basis.

### Claims Processing Errors and Delays

Denials without notice, ineligible expense payment, and delays create frustration and risk. Process within regulatory timeframes and audit TPA performance.

### Programs Under-Communicated, Low Participation

Confusing programs with poor communication have low participation, leaving tax savings unclaimed. Communicate in plain language with decision-support tools.

### HSA Disqualifying Coverage or Excess Contributions

Non-HDHP coverage or general-purpose HRAs disqualify HSA eligibility; excess contributions create tax consequences. Ensure HDHP enrollment and track limits across sources.

### Tax Reporting Errors

Incorrect W-2 coding or missed nondiscrimination testing creates employee tax problems and liability. Coordinate reporting and conduct annual testing.

### Open Enrollment Treated as Routine

Open enrollment errors create year-long problems. Treat it as a project with timeline, owners, testing, and confirmation.

## Self-Check

- Have I designed the cafeteria plan and each component (FSA, HSA, HRA, dependent care, commuter) deliberately, documented in written plans?
- Am I administering elections strictly within Section 125 change-event rules, documenting the basis for each permitted change?
- Am I processing claims correctly and timely with proper substantiation, coordinating with and auditing the TPA?
- Am I communicating programs in plain language with decision-support tools to drive participation and capture tax savings?
- Am I managing HSA-specific considerations — HDHP eligibility, no disqualifying coverage, contribution limits across sources, long-term savings communication?
- Am I ensuring tax reporting (W-2 coding, custodian reporting) and annual cafeteria plan nondiscrimination testing?
- Am I coordinating annual open enrollment as a project with timeline, owners, system testing, and election confirmation?
