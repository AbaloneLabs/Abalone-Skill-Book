# Benefits Compliance and Regulatory Administration

Employee benefits — health, welfare, retirement — operate within a dense regulatory framework that imposes obligations on plan design, administration, reporting, and employee communication. Non-compliance creates financial penalties, legal liability, plan disqualification (for retirement plans), and employee harm (loss of coverage, surprise tax bills). The complexity is compounded because benefits regulation spans multiple agencies and regimes — in the U.S., ERISA, ACA, HIPAA, COBRA, IRS, and DOL, each with its own requirements — and because the obligations interact (a HIPAA violation in benefits administration may also be an ERISA breach). The discipline is to maintain a compliance calendar of all obligations, to administer plans strictly within regulatory requirements, to coordinate with carriers and third-party administrators on shared obligations, and to prepare for audits and regulatory inquiries.

## Core Rules

### Maintain a Comprehensive Compliance Calendar

Benefits compliance is driven by deadlines — plan filings (Form 5500, ACA 1094/1095, Medicare Part D), employee notices (annual CHIP, Medicare Part D, COBRA general and election notices), nondiscrimination testing, and plan restatements and amendments. Maintain a comprehensive compliance calendar that captures every obligation, its deadline, its owner, and its status. Review the calendar monthly to ensure upcoming obligations are being prepared. The most common compliance failures are missed deadlines — a filing not submitted, a notice not distributed — and a calendar with ownership and tracking prevents these. Assign clear ownership for each obligation, because compliance tasks without owners are not completed.

### Administer Health Plan Regulatory Requirements Rigorously

Health and welfare plans carry numerous regulatory requirements: ACA compliance (employer shared responsibility, reporting, minimum essential coverage), HIPAA (privacy of health information, special enrollment rights, nondiscrimination), COBRA (continuation coverage election rights, notices, administration), mental health parity, and the Women's Health and Cancer Rights Act, among others. Administer each rigorously: ensure plan design meets the requirements, that administration (enrollment, special enrollment, COBRA elections) follows the rules, and that documentation supports compliance. Engage benefits counsel for plan document review and for complex compliance questions, because the requirements are technical and the penalties for error are significant. Health plan compliance failures create employee harm (loss of coverage), financial penalties, and litigation risk.

### Handle Retirement Plan Compliance and Fiduciary Obligations

Retirement plans (401(k), pension) carry their own compliance framework — ERISA fiduciary obligations, IRS qualification requirements, DOL reporting, and nondiscrimination testing. Administer the plan within these requirements: ensure the plan document is current (restated on the IRS cycle), that annual testing (ADP/ACP, coverage) is performed and passed or corrected, that Form 5500 is filed timely, that participant disclosures are distributed, and that fiduciary obligations (prudence, diversification, reasonable fees) are met. Retirement plan compliance failures can lead to plan disqualification (catastrophic tax consequences for participants and the employer) or DOL enforcement. Engage retirement plan counsel and the plan's third-party administrator for compliance support, and treat fiduciary obligations with the seriousness they carry — fiduciaries can be personally liable for breaches.

### Coordinate With Carriers and TPAs on Shared Obligations

Benefits administration is shared among the employer, insurance carriers, and third-party administrators (TPAs), and compliance obligations are often shared or split among them. Clarify who is responsible for each obligation — who files the Form 5500, who distributes COBRA notices, who performs nondiscrimination testing, who handles HIPAA privacy — and confirm that responsibility in service agreements. Audit the TPA's and carrier's compliance performance periodically, because the employer often remains liable even when a vendor fails to perform. The most common shared-obligation failures involve assuming the vendor is handling something they are not, or the vendor handling it incorrectly. Clear allocation, contractual confirmation, and periodic auditing prevent these failures.

### Manage Employee Benefits Communication and Disclosure Obligations

Benefits regulation requires numerous communications and disclosures to employees — SPDs (summary plan descriptions), SBCs (summary of benefits and coverage), annual notices (CHIP, Medicare Part D, wellness program), COBRA notices, and retirement plan disclosures (fee disclosures, investment notices). Each has content, timing, and distribution requirements. Manage these as a program: maintain a schedule of required disclosures, ensure content is accurate and compliant (often with benefits counsel review), distribute through compliant methods (electronic distribution has specific requirements), and document distribution for audit defense. Communication failures — an SPD not updated, a notice not distributed — are common compliance violations and create employee confusion and harm.

### Prepare for Benefits Audits and Regulatory Inquiries

Benefits plans are subject to audit by regulators (DOL, IRS) and by the organization's own auditors, and to inquiries from employees and their counsel. Prepare for these by maintaining organized, accessible compliance documentation: plan documents and amendments, filings and confirmations, communication distribution records, testing results, and fiduciary process documentation. Conduct internal compliance audits periodically to identify and correct issues before a regulator does. When a regulatory audit or inquiry arises, engage benefits counsel immediately, organize the responsive documentation, and respond thoroughly and accurately. Organizations that maintain compliance documentation and audit themselves are far better positioned in regulatory inquiries than those that scramble to reconstruct records.

### Navigate Leave, Disability, and Benefits Interactions

Benefits administration intersects with leave and disability administration in complex ways: health coverage during FMLA and other leave, COBRA triggers when leave ends or hours reduce, disability benefit coordination with workers' compensation and leave, and retirement plan loan and contribution rules during leave. Navigate these interactions deliberately, coordinating the benefits, leave, and disability processes so that coverage is maintained appropriately, elections and notices are triggered correctly, and the employee is not harmed by process gaps. The intersection of leave and benefits is a common source of errors — coverage lapsing during protected leave, COBRA not offered when required — that create employee harm and compliance liability.

## Common Traps

### Missing Filing Deadlines

Benefits compliance is governed by hard, non-negotiable deadlines — Form 5500, ACA 1094/1095, nondiscrimination testing, SAR distribution, SPD updates — and missing them triggers automatic penalties, excise taxes, or audit exposure that compound the longer they go unaddressed. The trap is that these deadlines are annual and cyclical, so they feel routine, but each year's filings are independently penalized, and a missed deadline in one year cannot be cured by on-time filing in the next. Maintain a compliance calendar with owners and lead times, because the penalty for missing a benefits deadline is not a reprimand but a per-day or per-participant fine that accumulates silently until an audit surfaces it.

### Incomplete SPDs And Wrap Documents

Summary Plan Descriptions are the legal document employees and regulators rely on to understand the plan's terms, and an SPD that is missing required elements — claims procedures, COBRA rights, amendment procedures, ERISA plan name and number — is treated as a compliance failure even if the plan itself is well-administered. The wrap-document trap is the inverse: when a single wrap SPD is used to bundle multiple benefits, the individual plan documents must be consistent with the wrap, or the wrap provides no protection. Review SPDs and wrap documents against the current DOL model language and the plan's actual operations, because an SPD that does not match how the plan actually works is worse than no document, as it creates a misrepresentation the plan sponsor must defend.

### Nondiscrimination Testing Treated As A Year-End Formality

Section 105(h) nondiscrimination testing for self-insured medical plans, and the similar tests for dependent care and cafeteria plans, must pass on both eligibility and benefits dimensions, and failing the test requires corrective action that may include returning contributions or restructuring the plan. The trap is treating testing as a paperwork step performed after the plan year ends, when the results are discovered too late to correct within the year and the failure carries excise tax exposure. Run nondiscrimination testing mid-year, while there is still time to correct eligibility or benefit design before the plan year closes, because a test result that arrives after the correction window is a finding of liability, not a management tool.

### COBRA Administration Gaps

COBRA is a process with multiple time-sensitive steps — employer notice of qualifying event, election notice, election period, premium grace period, and termination notice — and a failure at any step shifts the cost of the former employee's coverage onto the employer. The trap is that COBRA failures are rarely discovered by HR; they surface when a terminated employee incurs a medical claim and discovers they were never offered continuation coverage, at which point the employer is liable for the claim. Audit COBRA administration — notices sent, timelines met, premiums tracked — on a regular schedule, because COBRA liability is retroactive and can extend to substantial medical claims that the employer must pay out of pocket.

### Confusing Plan Design Changes With Plan Document Amendments

Changing a copay, adding a benefit, or modifying eligibility is a plan design change that must be reflected in a formal plan document amendment and communicated through an updated SPD or SMM; treating it as an administrative adjustment that does not require documentation creates a gap between the governing document and the actual plan. The trap is that in an audit or a dispute, the plan document controls, and if the document does not reflect the change, the plan must be administered according to the document — meaning the change is reversed retroactively or the plan is non-compliant. Amend the plan document whenever the design changes, and update the SPD within the required timeframe, because the plan document is the legal instrument, and a design change that is not documented does not legally exist.

### Retirement Plan Fiduciary Obligations Underestimated

ERISA fiduciary obligations for retirement plans — the duty of prudence, diversification, monitoring of investment options, and fee reasonableness — are personal liabilities for the individuals serving as fiduciaries, and they are enforced by the DOL and through participant litigation. The trap is treating the retirement plan as a vendor-administered program that runs itself, when in fact the fiduciary obligation to monitor the recordkeeper, review fees, and evaluate the investment menu is ongoing and non-delegable. Document fiduciary processes — investment committee minutes, fee benchmarking, vendor reviews — and engage ERISA counsel, because a fiduciary breach claim reaches the personal assets of the fiduciaries, not just the plan or the company.

### Shared Obligations Assumed Handled By Vendors

Benefits administration involves carriers, TPAs, and administrators who handle day-to-day operations, but the legal compliance obligations — notices, disclosures, testing, filings — remain with the employer-plan-sponsor, and a vendor's failure to perform does not shift the liability. The trap is the assumption that because a vendor is performing a function, the obligation is met, when the contract may limit the vendor's responsibility or the vendor may simply fail without the employer knowing. Clarify in the administrative services agreement exactly which party owns each obligation, confirm the vendor's performance through periodic audits and reporting, and never assume a function is handled simply because it is outsourced, because the regulator and the participant hold the employer responsible regardless of what the vendor did or did not do.

## Self-Check

- Have I maintained a comprehensive compliance calendar capturing every filing, notice, test, and plan action, with deadlines, owners, and status?
- Am I administering health plan requirements — ACA, HIPAA, COBRA, parity — rigorously, with benefits counsel engagement for technical questions?
- Am I meeting retirement plan compliance and fiduciary obligations — current plan document, testing, Form 5500, disclosures, prudence — with counsel and TPA support?
- Have I clarified and confirmed shared obligations with carriers and TPAs, and am I auditing their compliance performance?
- Am I managing disclosure obligations — SPDs, SBCs, notices — with accurate content, compliant distribution, and documentation?
- Am I maintaining organized compliance documentation and conducting internal audits to prepare for regulatory inquiries?
- Am I navigating leave, disability, and benefits interactions deliberately to maintain coverage and trigger elections correctly?
- Does the output stay within the agent's scope, deferring final authority, licensed judgment, or specialist sign-off to the qualified person where the question exceeds the agent's competence?
