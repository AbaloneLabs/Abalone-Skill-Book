# Retirement Plan Design And Administration

Retirement plans are a critical retention tool and a significant fiduciary responsibility. Under ERISA, those who manage plan assets and administration are fiduciaries, personally liable for acting prudently and solely in participants' interest. The agent must understand that retirement plan administration is not a set-it-and-forget-it benefit but an ongoing fiduciary obligation, that investment selection and vendor fees are subject to scrutiny, and that compliance failures, in nondiscrimination testing, reporting, or fiduciary process, create liability for the organization and its fiduciaries. The value of the plan as a retention tool depends on its design, employer contribution, and employee understanding.

Use this skill when designing, selecting, or administering retirement plans. The goal is to make the agent fulfill fiduciary obligations, comply with requirements, and maximize the plan's value to participants.

## Core Rules

### Understand And Fulfill Fiduciary Obligations

Retirement plan fiduciaries are held to the highest standard under ERISA: the duty of loyalty and the duty of prudence. Understand and fulfill these obligations.

- The duty of loyalty requires acting solely in the interest of participants and beneficiaries, for the exclusive purpose of providing benefits.
- The duty of prudence requires acting with the care, skill, and diligence that a prudent person would use, following a deliberative process.
- Document the fiduciary process: the decisions made, the analysis behind them, and the rationale, as the process, not just the outcome, is what is scrutinized.
- Identify who is a fiduciary, based on functional authority over plan assets or administration, not just title.
- Consider establishing a fiduciary committee, with defined charter, membership, and meeting cadence.

### Design The Plan To Serve Workforce And Organizational Goals

Plan design determines the plan's value as a retention tool and its cost to the organization. Design based on goals, not on default templates.

- Choose the plan type, such as 401(k), 403(b), or defined benefit, based on organizational goals and workforce needs.
- Set the employer contribution strategy: match, nonelective contribution, profit-sharing, or a combination, balancing retention value against cost.
- Set vesting schedules for employer contributions, balancing retention incentives against employee equity, recognizing that long vesting can feel punitive.
- Set eligibility and entry dates, balancing administrative simplicity against early participation.
- Review the design periodically, as goals and workforce needs change.

### Select And Monitor Plan Investments Prudently

Investment selection is a core fiduciary function. The process must be prudent and documented, and investments must be monitored ongoing.

- Establish an investment policy statement that defines the investment selection and monitoring process.
- Select a diversified investment menu, offering a range of options across asset classes and risk levels, including target-date funds for simplicity.
- Evaluate investments on performance, fees, and risk, using appropriate benchmarks, not just on brand recognition.
- Monitor investments regularly, replacing underperforming options through a documented process.
- Consider offering professionally managed options, such as target-date funds or managed accounts, for participants who do not want to self-direct.

### Manage Plan Fees And Revenue Sharing

Plan fees, paid by participants or the employer, are a fiduciary concern. Excessive fees violate the duty of prudence and generate liability.

- Understand all fees: investment management fees, administrative fees, recordkeeping fees, and any revenue sharing.
- Benchmark fees against the market, recognizing that fees vary by plan size and service level.
- Determine which fees are paid by the plan, by the employer, or through revenue sharing, and ensure the allocation is reasonable.
- Negotiate fees, particularly as the plan grows, and document the process.
- Disclose fees to participants as required, transparently and in understandable terms.

### Conduct Nondiscrimination Testing And Corrective Actions

Retirement plans must not discriminate in favor of highly compensated employees. Annual testing ensures compliance, and failures require correction.

- Conduct annual nondiscrimination testing, including the actual deferral percentage and actual contribution percentage tests for 401(k) plans.
- Use safe harbor designs, such as safe harbor matching or nonelective contributions, to avoid testing, where cost-effective.
- If testing fails, take corrective action promptly, such as making additional contributions or refunding excess contributions, following the correction methods.
- Document the testing and any corrections.

### File Required Reports And Disclosures

Retirement plans have significant reporting and disclosure obligations under ERISA and the tax code.

- File the annual Form 5500, with required schedules and audit, for plans that meet the filing threshold.
- Provide required participant disclosures: summary plan description, summaries of material modifications, annual funding notice, and fee disclosures.
- Provide quarterly benefit statements to participants.
- File any required regulatory notices.
- Track deadlines and ensure timely filing, as late filing generates penalties.

### Select And Monitor Plan Vendors

Plan vendors, including recordkeepers, third-party administrators, trustees, and investment advisors, provide critical services. Select and monitor them prudently.

- Select vendors through a competitive process, evaluating services, fees, technology, and service quality.
- Establish service level expectations and monitor performance against them.
- Review vendor contracts and fees periodically, renegotiating as the plan grows.
- Ensure that any fiduciary advisors are acknowledged fiduciaries, with the appropriate standard of care.
- Document the selection and monitoring process.

### Communicate The Plan To Drive Participation And Understanding

A retirement plan only serves its purpose if employees participate and make informed decisions. Communication drives participation and understanding.

- Communicate the plan at onboarding, with clear enrollment instructions.
- Communicate the employer contribution clearly, as it is a significant benefit that drives participation.
- Provide education on investing, risk, and retirement planning, in formats employees can use.
- Use automatic enrollment and automatic escalation, where appropriate, to increase participation and savings rates, while allowing opt-out.
- Use total rewards statements to show the value of the employer contribution.

### Handle Plan Termination Or Merger Carefully

Plan terminations and mergers are complex events with significant fiduciary and compliance obligations. Handle them with care and counsel.

- Plan terminations require a formal process, including participant notices, asset distribution, and final reporting.
- Plan mergers require careful mapping of accounts, vesting, and loans, to protect participant rights.
- Engage legal counsel and the recordkeeper early in the process.
- Communicate clearly with participants about what to expect and what they need to do.

## Common Traps

### No Documented Fiduciary Process

Making investment and vendor decisions without documented analysis, unable to demonstrate prudence. Document the process.

### Excessive Or Unexamined Fees

Allowing high fees to persist without benchmarking or negotiation, violating the duty of prudence. Benchmark and manage fees.

### Investment Selection By Brand

Choosing investments based on recognition rather than analysis of performance, fees, and risk. Analyze investments.

### Testing Failures Without Correction

Failing nondiscrimination testing and not taking timely corrective action. Test and correct promptly.

### Late Or Inaccurate Reporting

Missing Form 5500 or disclosure deadlines, generating penalties. Track and meet deadlines.

### Passive Vendor Management

Selecting vendors without competitive review or performance monitoring. Manage vendors actively.

### Low Participation Despite Employer Cost

Offering a generous employer contribution that many employees do not use, due to poor communication or enrollment barriers. Communicate and simplify enrollment.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Are fiduciary obligations understood and fulfilled, with a documented prudent process and identified fiduciaries?
- [ ] Is the plan design aligned with workforce and organizational goals, with contribution strategy and vesting set deliberately?
- [ ] Are investments selected and monitored prudently, with an investment policy statement and documented process?
- [ ] Are plan fees benchmarked, negotiated, and disclosed transparently?
- [ ] Is nondiscrimination testing conducted annually, with prompt correction of failures?
- [ ] Are required reports and disclosures filed timely and accurately?
- [ ] Are plan vendors selected competitively and monitored for performance and fees?
- [ ] Is the plan communicated to drive participation and understanding, with automatic features where appropriate?
