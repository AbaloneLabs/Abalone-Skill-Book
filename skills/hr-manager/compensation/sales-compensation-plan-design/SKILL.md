# Sales Compensation Plan Design

Sales compensation is one of the most leveraged pay designs in an organization, because it directly shapes what salespeople sell, how they sell, and which customers and products they prioritize. A well-designed plan aligns sales behavior with business strategy and rewards revenue and margin growth. A poorly designed plan pays for the wrong outcomes, incentivizes gaming, creates channel conflict, or rewards activity that does not produce profitable business. Agents often treat sales comp as a commission rate and miss that the interaction of base pay, quota, threshold, accelerator, and cap creates a behavior-shaping system that, misaligned, actively undermines revenue and profitability.

Use this skill before designing or revising a sales compensation plan, setting quotas, or evaluating whether sales incentives drive the right behavior. The goal is to make the agent design a plan that aligns with business strategy, is understandable to the sales force, resists gaming, and drives profitable, sustainable revenue.

## Core Rules

### Start From The Business Strategy And Revenue Goals

A sales compensation plan must serve the business strategy. If the strategy emphasizes new customer acquisition, the plan should reward it. If it emphasizes margin over volume, the plan should reflect that. A plan designed without reference to strategy will optimize for whatever it measures, whether or not that serves the business.

Start from strategy by:

- identifying what the business needs from the sales force, whether new logos, expansion of existing accounts, specific product mix, margin, or retention;
- ensuring that the plan's primary measures reflect the strategic priorities;
- recognizing that what is measured and rewarded is what will be pursued, and aligning measures accordingly;
- reviewing whether the plan inadvertently rewards behavior that contradicts strategy, such as rewarding volume when margin is the priority;
- involving sales leadership, finance, and strategy in the design, so that the plan reflects shared understanding of goals.

A plan that pays commission on revenue regardless of profitability will produce unprofitable revenue. A plan that pays equally for new and existing business will not drive acquisition. The plan is a strategy execution tool.

### Balance Base Salary And Variable Pay Appropriately

The mix of base salary and variable pay shapes the risk profile of the sales role and the type of selling it encourages. Too much base reduces hunger and accountability; too much variable creates excessive risk that may drive turnover or aggressive behavior.

Balance by:

- setting the base-to-variable mix based on the sales model, with transactional or high-volume roles typically having higher variable portions, and consultative or enterprise roles having higher base portions;
- considering the sales cycle length, with longer cycles requiring more base to sustain the salesperson through the cycle;
- ensuring that the variable portion is large enough to motivate but not so large that it creates financial instability for the salesperson;
- being consistent across similar roles, so that the risk profile is comparable;
- reviewing the mix when the sales model changes, such as a shift from hunting to farming.

The mix signals what the organization expects. A high-variable plan says "produce or leave." A high-base plan says "we value relationship building and longer cycles." The mix should match the role's reality.

### Set Quotas That Are Achievable, Credible, And Differentiated

Quotas are the targets against which sales performance and payout are measured. Quotas that are too high demoralize and drive disengagement; quotas that are too low overpay and underperform. Credible quotas are essential to a plan that is trusted.

Set quotas by:

- basing quotas on rigorous analysis of territory potential, historical performance, and market opportunity, not on top-down revenue goals divided by headcount;
- ensuring that quotas are achievable with strong effort, with a meaningful portion of the sales force expected to reach them;
- differentiating quotas by territory potential, so that salespeople in higher-potential territories have proportionally higher quotas;
- building a credible quota-setting process that salespeople trust, with transparency about methodology;
- reviewing and adjusting quotas when territory or market conditions change significantly, not just at annual planning.

Quota setting is the most contentious part of sales compensation. Quotas perceived as arbitrary or unachievable destroy motivation and trust, regardless of how well the rest of the plan is designed.

### Design Payout Curves With Thresholds, Accelerators, And Caps Deliberately

The payout curve, how commission or incentive changes as performance rises above or falls below quota, shapes behavior throughout the performance range. Thresholds, accelerators, and caps are tools that must be used deliberately.

Design payout curves by:

- setting a threshold, a minimum performance level before payout begins, to avoid paying for baseline or low-value activity, while being careful not to set it so high that it discourages effort;
- using accelerators above quota to reward overperformance and motivate top performers to keep selling;
- considering whether to use decelerators below quota to increase accountability, while being aware of the motivational impact;
- deciding whether to cap payouts, recognizing that caps discourage top performers but protect against runaway costs;
- ensuring that the payout curve produces the intended total cost at expected performance levels, through modeling.

A plan with no threshold pays for poor performance. A plan with no accelerator gives top performers no reason to exceed quota. A plan with a cap tells top performers to stop selling once they hit it. Each element must be intentional.

### Define What Gets Paid Clearly And Avoid Gaming

The definition of what constitutes a payable event, the credit rules, determines what behavior gets rewarded. Vague or exploitable rules invite gaming, where salespeople optimize for payout rather than genuine sales.

Define clearly by:

- specifying exactly what triggers payment, whether it is booking, shipment, invoice, or cash collection, and being consistent;
- defining credit attribution rules for team selling, overlay roles, and channel partners, to avoid double-credit or disputes;
- closing loopholes that allow gaming, such as splitting deals to hit thresholds, holding deals to manipulate timing, or rebooking to maximize payout;
- monitoring for gaming behavior and addressing it, as a plan that can be gamed will be;
- ensuring that the rules are documented and communicated, so that expectations are clear.

A plan that pays on bookings may reward deals that never convert to revenue. A plan that allows deal splitting incentivizes artificial activity. The credit rules define what the organization is actually buying.

### Keep The Plan Understandable

A sales compensation plan that salespeople cannot understand cannot motivate them. If the calculation is opaque, salespeople will not trust it, will not know what they need to do to earn, and will focus on arguing about payout rather than selling.

Keep understandable by:

- using a small number of measures, ideally one primary measure with perhaps one or two secondary;
- avoiding complex formulas, multipliers, or look-up tables that require a calculator to understand;
- ensuring that a salesperson can estimate their payout from their performance without needing the compensation department;
- providing clear plan documents and tools, such as calculators or dashboards, that show performance and projected payout;
- training salespeople and managers on the plan at launch, and providing ongoing support for questions.

Complexity does not equal sophistication. The most effective sales plans are often the simplest, because salespeople understand exactly what they need to do and can focus on doing it.

### Plan For Administration And Data Integrity

A sales compensation plan is only as good as the data and systems that support it. If crediting is inaccurate, payments are late, or disputes are unresolved, the plan loses credibility regardless of its design.

Plan for administration by:

- ensuring that the CRM and compensation systems can capture the data needed for crediting and calculation;
- defining the data flow from order to credit to payment, and ensuring integrity at each step;
- establishing a process for dispute resolution, so that crediting questions are resolved quickly and fairly;
- ensuring timely payment, as delayed commissions undermine trust and motivation;
- allocating resources to compensation administration, recognizing that it is a specialized and important function.

A well-designed plan that is poorly administered produces the same demotivation as a poorly designed plan. Administration is part of the design.

### Evaluate And Refresh The Plan Regularly

Sales compensation plans should be reviewed annually and refreshed as business strategy, market conditions, or sales models change. A plan that worked last year may not fit this year's priorities.

Evaluate by:

- assessing whether the plan is driving the intended behaviors and results;
- analyzing payout distribution, looking for signs of dysfunction such as most salespeople below threshold or a few dominating payout;
- gathering feedback from sales leadership and the sales force on what is and is not working;
- reviewing the plan's cost against the revenue it generated, ensuring that the plan is affordable;
- making changes deliberately and communicating them well in advance, as mid-year surprises destroy trust.

A plan that is never evaluated may continue to pay for outdated priorities. A plan that changes too frequently creates instability. Annual review with deliberate, well-communicated changes is the right balance.

## Common Traps

### Strategy Misalignment

Paying on measures that do not reflect business strategy, such as rewarding revenue when margin is the priority. Start from strategy.

### Wrong Pay Mix

Setting a mix that does not match the sales model, creating either complacency or excessive risk. Balance base and variable for the role.

### Arbitrary Quotas

Setting quotas by dividing a revenue goal by headcount, producing unachievable or uncredible targets. Set quotas based on territory potential.

### Unintentional Payout Curve

Using thresholds, accelerators, or caps without modeling their behavioral and cost impact. Design the payout curve deliberately.

### Exploitable Credit Rules

Leaving loopholes that allow deal splitting, timing manipulation, or double-crediting. Define credit rules clearly and monitor for gaming.

### Overly Complex Plan

Creating a plan with so many measures and formulas that salespeople cannot understand it. Keep it simple and transparent.

### Poor Administration

Designing a plan without ensuring that systems and processes can support crediting and payment. Plan for administration and data integrity.

### Static Plan

Never evaluating or refreshing the plan, so it continues to pay for outdated priorities. Evaluate and refresh regularly.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Does the plan's primary measure reflect the business strategy, with sales leadership, finance, and strategy aligned on goals?
- [ ] Is the base-to-variable pay mix appropriate for the sales model, sales cycle, and role type?
- [ ] Are quotas based on territory potential and historical data, achievable with strong effort, and trusted by the sales force?
- [ ] Is the payout curve, including threshold, accelerator, and cap, designed deliberately with cost modeling?
- [ ] Are credit rules clear, consistent, and resistant to gaming, with monitoring for exploitative behavior?
- [ ] Is the plan understandable to salespeople, with a small number of measures and transparent calculation?
- [ ] Are systems, data flows, and dispute resolution processes in place to administer the plan accurately and timely?
- [ ] Is the plan evaluated annually and refreshed based on behavior, results, cost, and feedback, with changes communicated in advance?
