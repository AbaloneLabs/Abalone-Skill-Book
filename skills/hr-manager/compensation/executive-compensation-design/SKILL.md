# Executive Compensation Design

Executive compensation is structurally different from broader employee pay. It involves larger amounts, more variable components, longer time horizons, public scrutiny, and governance oversight. Executive pay packages are designed to attract and retain leaders, align their interests with shareholders and strategy, and reward performance that creates sustainable value. But poorly designed executive pay can reward short-term gains at the expense of long-term health, create pay disparities that damage organizational cohesion, or expose the organization to reputational and governance risk. Agents often treat executive compensation as a scaled-up version of regular pay and miss that the complexity of incentives, equity, performance gates, and governance creates distinct risks that require specialized design.

Use this skill before designing executive pay packages, setting incentive metrics, or evaluating executive compensation governance. The goal is to make the agent design executive pay that aligns with strategy and sustainable value creation, is governed appropriately, and balances competitiveness with proportionality.

## Core Rules

### Align Executive Pay With Long-Term Strategy And Value Creation

Executive compensation should align the leader's interests with the long-term health of the organization, not just short-term results. A package that overweights short-term incentives encourages decisions that boost near-term metrics at the expense of sustainable value.

Align by:

- balancing short-term incentives, typically annual cash bonuses, with long-term incentives, typically equity or performance shares vesting over multiple years;
- ensuring that long-term incentives constitute a meaningful portion of total compensation, so that the leader's wealth is tied to sustained organizational performance;
- designing performance metrics that reflect genuine value creation, not just easily manipulated accounting measures;
- using multi-year performance periods for long-term incentives, so that payouts reflect sustained results, not a single year;
- considering clawback or forfeiture provisions for outcomes that unravel after the incentive was paid.

Short-term incentives reward execution of the annual plan. Long-term incentives reward building durable value. The balance between them signals what the organization wants its leaders to prioritize.

### Design Performance Metrics That Resist Manipulation

The metrics tied to executive incentives will receive intense focus, and executives will optimize for them. If the metrics can be gamed, or if they reward the wrong outcomes, the organization will get precisely what it incentivized, regardless of whether it is good.

Design metrics by:

- choosing metrics that reflect genuine performance, such as revenue growth, profitability, return on capital, or total shareholder return;
- using a balanced set of metrics, so that optimizing one does not sacrifice others, such as pairing growth with margin;
- being wary of metrics that can be manipulated through accounting choices, timing, or short-term decisions;
- setting targets that are challenging but achievable, based on rigorous analysis and benchmarking, not aspiration;
- ensuring that the performance period is long enough to reflect genuine results, not noise or market cycles;
- building in gates or modifiers, such that poor performance on fundamental metrics, like compliance or safety, can reduce or eliminate incentive payout.

A metric that rewards revenue growth without regard to profitability will produce unprofitable growth. A metric that rewards short-term earnings without regard to investment will produce declining competitiveness. Metric design is strategy execution.

### Structure Equity And Ownership To Align Interests

Equity compensation aligns executive interests with shareholders by making the leader an owner. But the structure of equity, the type, the vesting, and the holding requirements, determine whether the alignment is genuine or superficial.

Structure equity by:

- choosing equity instruments, such as restricted stock, performance shares, or options, that fit the organization's situation and goals;
- using vesting schedules that require sustained performance or tenure, typically multi-year, so that the leader cannot cash out and walk away;
- setting performance conditions on a portion of equity, so that vesting depends on achieving defined results, not just remaining employed;
- imposing stock ownership or holding requirements, so that the leader retains a meaningful stake during and after their tenure;
- being aware of dilution and shareholder impact, and managing equity grants accordingly.

Options reward only upside, which can encourage excessive risk-taking. Restricted stock rewards sustained value. Performance shares reward specific outcomes. The choice should reflect what the organization wants to encourage.

### Benchmark Against The Right Comparator Group

Executive pay benchmarks are more complex than broad employee benchmarks, because executive roles vary more across organizations and because the market for executive talent is smaller and more visible. The wrong comparator group produces benchmarks that are too high or too low.

Benchmark by:

- selecting a comparator group of similarly sized organizations in the same industry, that compete for the same executive talent;
- recognizing that the CEO and top executives are often benchmarked against a broader or different group than the one used for the organization's general pay structure;
- using multiple data sources, including specialized executive compensation surveys and proxy analysis of public comparators;
- interpreting data carefully, recognizing that reported pay figures include realized gains that vary with stock performance;
- using benchmarking as one input, not the sole determinant, and adjusting for the organization's specific situation.

Benchmarking executive pay to a group of larger or more complex organizations produces a ratchet upward, as the organization chases comparators it has outgrown. The comparator group must fit the organization.

### Ensure Governance And Oversight

Executive compensation is subject to governance oversight, typically by a board compensation committee, and increasingly to shareholder scrutiny. The process must be rigorous, transparent, and defensible.

Ensure governance by:

- having an independent board committee oversee executive compensation, with access to independent compensation advisors;
- ensuring that the committee understands the pay structure, metrics, and potential risks, and is not merely rubber-stamping management proposals;
- conducting independent benchmarking and analysis, not relying solely on data or recommendations provided by the executives whose pay is being set;
- documenting the rationale for pay decisions, including how they align with strategy and performance;
- being responsive to shareholder feedback and say-on-pay votes, and willing to adjust structures that attract significant opposition.

Governance is not bureaucracy. It is the mechanism by which the organization ensures that executive pay serves the organization's interests, not just the executive's. Weak governance enables pay structures that benefit the executive at the expense of the organization.

### Balance Competitiveness With Proportionality

Executive pay must be competitive enough to attract and retain talent, but pay that is dramatically out of proportion to the rest of the organization or to societal expectations creates risk. Internal pay equity and external perception both matter.

Balance by:

- ensuring that executive pay is justified by the scope, complexity, and performance expectations of the role;
- being aware of the ratio of executive pay to median employee pay, which is increasingly disclosed and scrutinized;
- considering the impact of executive pay decisions on organizational culture and employee perception;
- avoiding pay structures that are so complex or opaque that they cannot be explained or defended;
- recognizing that reputational risk from perceived excessive pay can outweigh the retention benefit.

Competitiveness is necessary but not sufficient. An executive pay package that is competitive but perceived as excessive may retain the executive while damaging the organization's culture, reputation, and ability to attract other talent.

### Design Severance And Change-Of-Control Provisions Carefully

Severance and change-of-control provisions in executive agreements define what the executive receives upon departure or acquisition. These provisions are negotiated at hiring and have significant financial and reputational implications.

Design by:

- ensuring that severance provisions are reasonable and consistent with market practice, not excessive;
- avoiding provisions that reward failure, such as large severance after poor performance, which damages credibility;
- structuring change-of-control provisions to protect the executive's interests in an acquisition, without creating incentives to pursue a transaction that is not in shareholders' interests;
- being transparent about these provisions, as they become public in filings or upon departure;
- reviewing and updating provisions as market practice and governance expectations evolve.

Golden parachutes that reward executives for transactions that destroy shareholder value, or severance that rewards failed performance, attract criticism and undermine the credibility of the compensation program.

### Review And Adjust Based On Performance And Feedback

Executive compensation should be reviewed regularly to ensure it remains aligned with strategy, competitive, and effective. Static structures that are not adjusted may become misaligned as the organization and market evolve.

Review by:

- assessing whether the incentive metrics continue to reflect the right strategic priorities;
- evaluating whether the structure is producing the intended performance alignment;
- gathering shareholder and board feedback, and being willing to make changes;
- benchmarking regularly against the comparator group, and adjusting for market movement;
- being willing to simplify structures that have become overly complex.

Executive compensation that is designed once and never reviewed may drift from its purpose. Regular review keeps it aligned and defensible.

## Common Traps

### Short-Term Focus

Overweighting annual incentives, encouraging decisions that sacrifice long-term value. Balance short and long-term incentives.

### Manipulable Metrics

Using metrics that can be gamed through accounting or timing, producing the rewarded behavior but not genuine value. Design resistant metrics.

### Superficial Equity Alignment

Granting equity without vesting, performance, or holding requirements, so that alignment is temporary. Structure equity for genuine alignment.

### Wrong Comparator Group

Benchmarking against organizations that are larger or different, producing a ratchet upward. Choose the right comparators.

### Weak Governance

Allowing executives to influence their own pay setting, or a board committee to rubber-stamp proposals. Ensure independent oversight.

### Disproportionate Pay

Setting executive pay so far above the rest of the organization that it damages culture and reputation. Balance competitiveness with proportionality.

### Rewarding Failure

Severance or change-of-control provisions that pay large sums regardless of performance. Design provisions carefully.

### Static Structure

Designing executive pay once and not reviewing, so it drifts from strategy and market. Review and adjust regularly.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Does the executive pay package balance short-term and long-term incentives to align with sustainable value creation?
- [ ] Are performance metrics designed to resist manipulation, balanced across dimensions, and gated by fundamental performance?
- [ ] Is equity structured with vesting, performance conditions, and holding requirements that create genuine ownership alignment?
- [ ] Is benchmarking conducted against an appropriate comparator group, using multiple sources and interpreted carefully?
- [ ] Is executive compensation overseen by an independent board committee with independent advisors and documented rationale?
- [ ] Is pay competitive but proportional, with awareness of internal equity ratios and reputational impact?
- [ ] Are severance and change-of-control provisions reasonable, transparent, and not rewarding failure?
- [ ] Is the executive compensation structure reviewed regularly and adjusted based on performance, feedback, and market movement?
