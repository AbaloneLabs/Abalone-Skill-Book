# Pay Equity Analysis And Correction

Pay equity analysis examines whether the organization pays men and women, or people of different races or ethnicities, differently for substantially similar work, after accounting for legitimate factors. Unexplained disparities may indicate discrimination, which is unlawful, harmful, and damaging to reputation and trust. But an analysis that is poorly designed, that controls for the wrong factors, or that uses inappropriate statistical methods can both hide real disparities and create false alarms. Agents often treat pay equity as a simple comparison of averages and miss that without controlling for legitimate factors, raw gaps are misleading, and without rigorous methodology, the analysis is indefensible.

Use this skill before conducting a pay equity analysis, interpreting results, or designing remediation. The goal is to make the agent conduct an analysis that is methodologically sound, controls for legitimate factors, identifies genuinely unexplained disparities, and supports remediation that is fair and defensible.

## Core Rules

### Define The Analysis Scope And Comparison Groups Clearly

A pay equity analysis must define what is being compared. Vague definitions of "similar work" or inconsistent grouping of jobs produce analysis that is either too broad, capturing real differences, or too narrow, missing patterns.

Define scope by:

- identifying the legally relevant comparison, typically substantially similar work, which considers skill, effort, responsibility, and working conditions;
- grouping jobs into comparison groups that reflect similar work, using job evaluation levels or detailed job families;
- deciding the demographic dimensions to analyze, typically gender and race or ethnicity, and any others relevant to the jurisdiction;
- defining the pay measure, whether base salary, total cash, or total compensation, consistently;
- documenting the scope and definitions, so the analysis is reproducible and defensible.

The scope determines what the analysis can and cannot tell you. A scope that lumps dissimilar jobs together will show gaps that reflect real job differences, not discrimination.

### Control For Legitimate Pay Factors

Raw pay gaps, the difference in average pay between groups, are misleading because they do not account for legitimate factors that affect pay. An analysis that reports raw gaps without controls creates false alarms and undermines credibility.

Control for legitimate factors by:

- identifying the factors that legitimately affect pay, such as job level, experience, tenure, performance rating, education, location, and specialization;
- using regression analysis or similar methods to control for these factors simultaneously;
- ensuring that the factors used are genuinely legitimate and not themselves influenced by bias, such as "negotiation" or "prior salary," which may carry forward historical discrimination;
- being cautious with factors that may be proxies for protected characteristics, such as certain measures of experience;
- documenting the model and the factors included, with rationale for each.

The choice of control variables is critical. Including a variable that is itself discriminatory, like prior salary from a previous employer who discriminated, will "explain away" a real disparity. Excluding legitimate variables will produce a false positive. The model must be thoughtfully designed.

### Use Appropriate Statistical Methods

Pay equity analysis is a statistical exercise. The methods must be appropriate for the data and the question, and the results must be interpreted with understanding of statistical significance and practical significance.

Use appropriate methods by:

- using multiple regression analysis, which allows simultaneous control for multiple factors;
- ensuring the sample size is adequate for the number of factors, as too few observations produce unreliable results;
- examining both statistical significance, whether the disparity is unlikely to be due to chance, and practical significance, the actual size of the disparity;
- looking at individual-level outliers, not just group averages, as a few large disparities can skew results;
- being transparent about the methodology, assumptions, and limitations;
- recognizing that statistical methods have limits, and that they inform but do not replace judgment.

A p-value without context, or a regression with too few data points, produces conclusions that appear scientific but are not reliable. Methodological rigor is essential.

### Investigate Unexplained Disparities Before Concluding Discrimination

A statistically significant unexplained disparity does not automatically prove discrimination. It indicates that the legitimate factors in the model do not fully explain the pay difference, and further investigation is needed.

Investigate by:

- examining whether additional legitimate factors, not in the model, may explain the disparity;
- looking at where the disparities concentrate, by department, level, or hiring cohort, which may reveal patterns;
- reviewing hiring and pay-setting practices for potential bias;
- examining whether performance ratings or promotion decisions, used as controls, may themselves be biased;
- considering whether starting pay differences, which compound over time, are driving the gap;
- being honest about what the analysis can and cannot conclude.

An unexplained gap is a signal to investigate, not a verdict. The investigation may find additional legitimate explanations, or it may confirm that the gap is likely due to bias. Both outcomes require action, but the actions differ.

### Design Remediation That Addresses Root Causes

If the analysis identifies unexplained disparities that cannot be explained by legitimate factors, remediation is needed. Remediation should address both the immediate pay gaps and the root causes that produced them.

Design remediation by:

- making pay adjustments to close unexplained gaps, bringing affected employees to the pay they would have received absent the disparity;
- ensuring that adjustments are based on the analysis, not on ad-hoc decisions, so that remediation is consistent and defensible;
- addressing the root causes, such as biased hiring or pay-setting practices, to prevent recurrence;
- reviewing and correcting performance evaluation, promotion, and compensation processes that may contribute to gaps;
- monitoring pay equity after remediation, to confirm that gaps close and do not reappear.

Remediation that only adjusts pay without addressing causes treats the symptom, not the disease. Gaps will reappear if the practices that created them continue.

### Protect The Analysis With Appropriate Privilege

Pay equity analysis may reveal disparities that could be used in litigation against the organization. Conducting the analysis under appropriate legal privilege protects the organization's interests while allowing it to identify and address issues.

Protect by:

- working with legal counsel to determine the appropriate privilege strategy, such as attorney-client privilege or work product doctrine;
- conducting the analysis under counsel's direction, where appropriate, to maintain privilege;
- controlling distribution of the results, sharing only with those who need to know for remediation;
- being aware that privilege protections vary by jurisdiction, and that some jurisdictions may require disclosure;
- not using privilege as a shield to avoid addressing identified problems, which is both unethical and risky.

Privilege is a tool for managing legal risk while doing the right thing. It is not a license to ignore findings. Organizations that use privilege to bury pay equity problems face greater risk when the problems inevitably surface.

### Conduct Analysis Proactively, Not Reactively

Waiting until a complaint, audit, or lawsuit forces a pay equity analysis means discovering problems under pressure, with limited time and options. Proactive analysis allows the organization to identify and address issues on its own terms.

Conduct proactively by:

- establishing a regular cadence for pay equity analysis, at least annually or biennially;
- building pay equity monitoring into ongoing compensation processes, so that disparities are caught as they emerge;
- addressing issues as they are found, rather than allowing them to accumulate;
- communicating to leadership about pay equity status, so that it remains a priority;
- being prepared to act on findings, as analysis without action is worse than useless.

Proactive analysis demonstrates good faith and allows remediation before issues escalate. It also positions the organization well if pay practices are ever questioned externally.

### Communicate Findings And Actions Appropriately

Pay equity is a sensitive topic. How findings and actions are communicated affects employee trust, external reputation, and legal position. Communication must be honest, appropriate, and strategic.

Communicate by:

- being transparent with employees about the organization's commitment to pay equity and the steps being taken;
- communicating remediation in a way that is honest about what was found and what is being done, without creating unnecessary alarm;
- avoiding statements that could be interpreted as admissions of liability, while being genuine about commitment to fairness;
- coordinating communication with legal counsel, to ensure messaging is appropriate;
- recognizing that employees will judge the organization by actions, not just statements.

Silence on pay equity breeds suspicion. Overstatement breeds skepticism. Honest, measured communication, backed by genuine action, builds trust.

## Common Traps

### Analysis Stopped At The Aggregate

Running an analysis only at the company-wide or department-wide level can mask inequities that exist within specific roles, levels, or job families. An organization can show no statistically significant pay gap overall while harboring substantial gaps within particular segments — a specific department, a particular job family, a certain demographic group in a certain role. The trap is that the aggregate "clean bill of health" feels reassuring and definitive, so the organization declares victory and stops looking, leaving the real inequities untouched in the segments it never examined. A credible pay equity analysis must segment the data finely enough that segment-level gaps become visible, because the aggregate is where inequities hide.

### Confusing Explained And Unexplained Gaps

When a regression shows that much of the raw pay gap is "explained" by factors like tenure, experience, or location, organizations sometimes conclude there is no equity problem. The trap is that "explained" does not mean "justified." If women have less tenure because they took parental leave the system penalizes, or if certain groups are concentrated in lower-paid roles because of biased hiring or promotion, the "explained" gap is itself a symptom of inequity upstream of pay. Treating the explained portion as automatically fair, without examining whether the explanatory variables are themselves fair, lets structural inequity pass as neutral statistics. The unexplained residual is the clearest red flag, but the explained variables deserve scrutiny too.

### Correcting Individuals But Not The System and disruptive Remediation

Adjusting the pay of individuals identified in the analysis, without examining and fixing the practices that created the inequity, guarantees the problem will recur with the next hire, the next promotion, the next merit cycle. The trap is that individual corrections feel like solving the problem (the flagged employees are now paid fairly), so the organization moves on, but the unaddressed root causes — a biased starting-pay practice, an unstructured promotion process, a merit budget that compounds historical gaps — generate new inequities immediately. Pay equity is not a one-time fix but an ongoing discipline; without addressing the systemic drivers, the analysis becomes a recurring cleanup exercise rather than a path to sustained equity.

Raising pay for underpaid employees is the correct response, but doing so in a way that creates internal compression, perceived unfairness, or budget shock undermines the goal. A large one-time adjustment that vaults a corrected employee above longer-tenured peers breeds resentment; an adjustment funded by denying merit to others breeds conflict; a sudden correction without explanation breeds confusion. The trap is that the remediation focuses on the equity gap and ignores the relative-pay context into which the correction lands. Phased adjustments, transparent communication of the principle (fair pay for the work), and budgeting that anticipates equity corrections as a recurring cost, are what prevent remediation from creating new problems while solving the old one.

### Analysis Never Repeated and methodology That Cannot Withstand Scrutiny

Conducting a pay equity analysis once, correcting the identified gaps, and then never repeating the analysis allows new inequities to accumulate undetected — through hiring decisions, merit increases, promotions, and organizational changes that reintroduce the very gaps that were corrected. The trap is that the one-time analysis feels like a completed project, so the organization treats pay equity as solved, when it is actually a dynamic state that drifts as the workforce and pay decisions evolve. Pay equity requires recurring analysis — at minimum annually, and after major events like reorganizations, acquisitions, or large hiring waves — because equity is not a destination but a discipline that must be maintained against the constant pressure of new decisions.

An analysis conducted with flawed methodology — cherry-picked comparison groups, omitted relevant variables, inadequate sample sizes treated as definitive, or statistical significance ignored — produces conclusions that collapse under legal, regulatory, or employee challenge. The trap is that the flawed methodology produces a reassuring result (no gap found), so no one questions it, but when the analysis is later scrutinized (in a lawsuit, an audit, an employee demand), the weaknesses destroy the organization's credibility and can be used as evidence of bad faith. A defensible analysis uses sound methodology, documents its assumptions and limitations, involves appropriate expertise, and is prepared to withstand external scrutiny rather than just to produce a comfortable internal conclusion.

### Confidentiality Treated As Secrecy and remediation Without Root-Cause Investigation

Protecting the confidentiality of the analysis — who was examined, what was found at the individual level — is legally and ethically necessary, but treating confidentiality as an excuse for total silence about the existence and results of the program breeds exactly the suspicion it is meant to avoid. Employees who know inequities exist but hear nothing about whether they are being addressed conclude the organization is ignoring the problem or hiding the results. The trap is that confidentiality (protecting individual data) and secrecy (refusing to acknowledge the program) are conflated, so the organization says nothing, and the silence is read as indifference or cover-up. Communicating the existence, scope, and aggregate findings of the program, while protecting individual confidentiality, is what builds the trust that the analysis was meant to support.

Applying pay adjustments to close identified gaps without investigating why those gaps existed — which hiring decision, which promotion pattern, which merit practice, which policy created them — leaves the organization unable to prevent recurrence and unable to demonstrate good faith. The trap is that the adjustment feels like the solution, so the investigation is skipped, but without root-cause understanding the organization cannot distinguish a one-time anomaly from a systemic practice, cannot fix the underlying process, and cannot assure regulators or plaintiffs that the problem is truly resolved. Root-cause investigation is what converts a pay correction from a temporary fix into a permanent improvement, and what demonstrates the proactive good faith that limits legal exposure.

### Biased Control Variables and inadequate Sample Size Treated As Definitive

Using control factors that are themselves influenced by discrimination — prior salary (which imports historical bias), tenure (which can reflect unequal promotion opportunity), or title (which can mask unequal scope) — produces an analysis that controls away the very inequity it is meant to detect. The trap is that the variables look neutral and objective (salary is a number, tenure is a date), so their inclusion feels rigorous, but if the explanatory variable is itself a product of the discrimination being investigated, the regression reports "no gap" precisely because the gap has been laundered through the control. Careful selection of controls, examining each one for whether it is a legitimate productivity factor or a conduit for bias, is what prevents the analysis from becoming a tool that hides the problem rather than revealing it.

Running a regression on too few observations — a small department, a rare job family, a handful of employees in a protected class — produces results that are statistically unreliable, yet an organization may treat the "no significant gap" finding as proof of equity when it is actually proof of nothing. The trap is that statistical insignificance in a small sample means the analysis lacked the power to detect a real gap, not that no gap exists; the smaller the sample, the larger the gap that can hide undetected. Small-sample segments require either pooling across comparable roles, qualitative review, or explicit acknowledgment that the sample was too small to draw a conclusion — never the confident declaration that equity was confirmed.

### Discrimination Inferred From The Unexplained Gap Alone and reactive-Only Analysis Under Pressure

Treating an unexplained pay disparity as proof of discrimination, without further investigation into its causes, overreaches what the statistics can support and can lead to misguided accusations, panicked over-corrections, or legal vulnerability. The trap is that the unexplained gap is the most visible red flag, so it feels like the answer, but an unexplained gap is a signal to investigate, not a conclusion in itself — it may reflect an omitted legitimate variable, a data error, a clustering effect, or actual discrimination. The correct response to an unexplained gap is targeted investigation: what specific decisions, practices, or individuals drove the disparity? Only that investigation can distinguish a remediable inequity from a statistical artifact.

Waiting for a complaint, a lawsuit, or a regulatory audit before conducting pay equity analysis means the organization discovers its inequities under adversarial pressure, when correction is harder, good faith is harder to demonstrate, and the findings may be discoverable. The trap is that proactive analysis feels risky (what if we find something?), so the organization avoids looking until forced, but the avoidance itself is the liability: an organization that never examined its pay practices cannot claim good faith, and an organization that examines only under compulsion appears to be hiding what it knew. Proactive, recurring analysis is both more effective (problems are found and fixed early) and more defensible (it demonstrates the good faith that limits legal exposure) than analysis conducted only reactively.

### Analysis Without Privilege Protection and analysis Conducted But Never Acted Upon

Conducting pay equity analysis without appropriate legal privilege — without attorney direction, without the protections of the work-product doctrine — exposes the findings to discovery in litigation, meaning the organization's own analysis can be used against it. The trap is that the analysis is conducted as an HR project (it is about people and pay, so HR owns it), without recognizing that the findings are legally sensitive and that the methodology, the raw results, and the deliberations all need privilege protection to be safe. Conducting the analysis under appropriate privilege, controlling distribution, and separating the privileged findings from operational remediation is what allows the organization to learn from the analysis without turning it into a litigation liability.

Conducting a pay equity analysis, finding disparities, and then not acting on the findings is worse than not analyzing at all, because it demonstrates knowledge of the problem without addressing it — which can be used as evidence of intentional discrimination in subsequent litigation. The trap is that the analysis is treated as the deliverable (the report is produced, the presentation is made), and the hard part — the remediation, the budget, the difficult conversations — is deferred until it is forgotten, leaving the organization with documented knowledge of inequities it chose not to fix. An analysis must have a committed action plan, with owners, timelines, and budget, or the organization has not managed its risk; it has created new risk by documenting a problem it then ignored.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is the analysis scope defined clearly, with comparison groups based on substantially similar work and documented definitions?
- [ ] Does the analysis control for legitimate pay factors using an appropriate model, with careful selection of variables to avoid biased controls?
- [ ] Are statistical methods appropriate for the data and sample size, with both statistical and practical significance examined?
- [ ] Are unexplained disparities investigated further before concluding discrimination, with root causes explored?
- [ ] Does remediation address both immediate pay gaps and the underlying practices that caused them?
- [ ] Is the analysis conducted with appropriate legal privilege, with distribution controlled and findings acted upon?
- [ ] Is pay equity analysis conducted proactively on a regular cadence, with monitoring built into compensation processes?
- [ ] Are findings and actions communicated honestly and appropriately, balancing transparency with legal sensitivity?
