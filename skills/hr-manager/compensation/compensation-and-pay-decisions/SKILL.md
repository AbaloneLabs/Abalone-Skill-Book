# Compensation And Pay Decisions

Compensation is the most visible and most scrutinized signal of how an organization values its people. Every pay decision is simultaneously a market decision, a budget decision, a retention decision, an equity decision, and a legal decision. Agents often treat pay as a number to negotiate and miss that the structure behind the number determines whether pay is internally fair, externally competitive, legally defensible, and sustainable as the organization grows. Pay inequity, once embedded, is expensive to fix and damaging to trust.

Use this skill before setting an individual's pay, designing or revising a salary structure, making raise or promotion decisions, responding to a pay complaint, or conducting a pay equity review. The goal is to make the agent ground pay decisions in data and consistent criteria, control for bias, and document the rationale so the decision is defensible and explainable.

## Core Rules

### Build A Salary Structure Before Setting Individual Pay

Ad hoc pay decisions, made person by person, inevitably create internal inequity that is discovered later and is expensive to resolve. A structure creates the framework within which individual decisions are consistent.

Define the structure:

- job families and levels that group roles of similar scope, impact, and complexity;
- pay bands or ranges for each level, with a minimum, midpoint, and maximum;
- the relationship between levels, so progression is logical and predictable;
- how the structure maps to the market, using benchmark data for comparable roles;
- the philosophy that positions pay relative to market, such as at median, leading, or lagging, and why;
- how geography, if used, adjusts pay, and the basis for that adjustment;
- review cadence for the structure, since market data ages.

Without a structure, every pay conversation is a negotiation from scratch, and the loudest or most mobile employee wins. The structure is what makes pay a system rather than a series of transactions.

### Benchmark Against Credible, Matched Market Data

Pay decisions based on intuition, last year's number, or a single online source will drift from the market and create retention or cost problems. Use credible data.

Ensure benchmarking:

- uses reputable salary surveys or data providers with adequate sample size;
- matches roles accurately to benchmark jobs, since a title match is not a job match;
- accounts for industry, company size, and geography relevant to the labor market;
- reflects total compensation, not only base, where variable pay or equity is significant;
- is refreshed regularly, since market conditions shift, especially in volatile skill markets;
- is supplemented with offer data, decline data, and recruiter intelligence where survey data is thin.

Market data is a reference, not a verdict. Use it to inform the range, then apply judgment about the specific role, the individual, and the organization's positioning. Document the data source and date so decisions can be revisited.

### Define And Apply Consistent Criteria For Individual Pay

Within the structure, individual pay decisions still need consistent criteria or they will vary by manager bias and negotiation skill.

Apply criteria such as:

- the employee's level and the band midpoint for that level;
- the employee's experience, skills, and performance relative to the role;
- where the employee sits in the band, since paying at the maximum leaves no room and paying at the minimum may under-reward;
- internal comparators at the same level doing similar work;
- market pressure for the specific role, especially scarce-skill roles;
- the organization's pay philosophy and budget constraints.

State the criteria, apply them to every decision, and document the reasoning. When two people in the same role are paid differently, the reason should be explicit and defensible: difference in level, performance, experience, or market timing, not difference in negotiation aggressiveness or manager favoritism.

### Conduct Pay Equity Analysis Proactively

Pay equity is not only a legal obligation; it is a trust and retention issue. Inequity discovered by employees, regulators, or plaintiffs is far more damaging than inequity found and fixed internally.

In a pay equity analysis:

- group employees by similarly situated roles, considering job content and level;
- control for legitimate factors: experience, tenure, performance, location, seniority, specialization;
- compare pay across protected characteristics, primarily gender, race, and ethnicity;
- identify unexplained gaps that remain after legitimate factors are accounted for;
- assess both base pay and total compensation, including bonus and equity;
- review starting pay, since inequity often originates at hire and compounds through percentage-based raises;
- involve legal counsel to protect the analysis under privilege where appropriate;
- develop a remediation plan for unexplained gaps, which may include targeted adjustments.

Pay equity is not a one-time project. Hiring, raises, promotions, and departures constantly reshape the distribution, so the analysis must be repeated on a cadence. See the diversity skill for the broader equity framework.

### Set Starting Pay Carefully To Avoid Embedding Inequity

The single most common source of pay inequity is starting pay based on salary history or negotiation. Because raises are often percentage-based, a gap at hire widens over time.

Control starting pay by:

- basing offers on the role, the band, and market data, not on the candidate's previous salary, which is itself a carrier of prior inequity;
- complying with salary history bans where applicable;
- applying consistent offer criteria across candidates for the same role;
- resisting the urge to pay above band to close a hard search, since it creates internal compression and inequity;
- documenting the basis for any offer above midpoint.

Starting pay decisions are high-leverage because they set the trajectory. A small inequity at hire becomes a large one over a career. Coordinate with the hiring skill for the offer process.

### Manage Pay Transparency Obligations

Pay transparency law is expanding rapidly. Many jurisdictions now require pay ranges in postings, on request, or after interviews, and prohibit retaliation for pay discussions.

Comply by:

- including pay ranges in job postings where required by law;
- having a defined range ready to share with candidates and employees where required;
- allowing employees to discuss pay where legally protected, and not retaliating;
- ensuring managers do not prohibit or discourage pay conversations in violation of law;
- maintaining consistent ranges between what is posted, what is offered, and what is communicated internally.

Inconsistency between posted ranges and actual pay undermines both compliance and credibility. Pay transparency also increases the importance of internal equity, because employees can more easily compare.

### Design Variable Pay To Drive The Right Behavior

Bonus, commission, and incentive plans are powerful and dangerous. They drive precisely the behavior they reward, including behavior the organization did not intend.

When designing variable pay:

- tie incentives to outcomes the individual can genuinely influence;
- balance short-term and long-term measures to avoid sacrificing the future for the present;
- include both individual and team or company components where collaboration matters;
- set targets that are challenging but achievable, and define how they are set to avoid gaming;
- consider caps, floors, and clawback provisions for recovery of overpayments or misconduct;
- assess whether the incentive creates risk-taking, corner-cutting, or customer harm, especially in sales, finance, safety, and compliance roles;
- review the plan's actual effect regularly and adjust.

A plan that rewards revenue without quality, speed without safety, or volume without compliance will produce exactly those distortions. Variable pay is a design problem, not just a math problem.

### Connect Pay To Performance Through A Clear, Fair Mechanism

If pay is connected to performance, the connection must be transparent and based on a credible performance process. Opaque or inconsistent merit decisions erode trust.

Ensure:

- the merit increase matrix or guideline is documented and applied consistently;
- the performance rating driving the increase comes from a calibrated, documented process;
- employees understand how performance translates to pay, so the outcome is not a surprise;
- high performers are differentiated meaningfully, or the system rewards tenure over contribution;
- budget constraints are managed centrally, not by quietly under-increasing people the manager thinks will not notice.

Coordinate with the performance skill. If the performance ratings are unreliable, the pay decisions built on them are unreliable.

### Document The Rationale For Pay Decisions

Pay decisions are challenged through pay equity claims, discrimination charges, and employee complaints. The organization must be able to explain not only what it decided but why.

Document:

- the structure, bands, and market data underlying the framework;
- the criteria applied to the individual decision;
- the comparator analysis showing consistency with similar roles;
- the performance input and its source;
- any market-based adjustment and its justification;
- any equity remediation adjustment and its basis.

Documentation created at the time of the decision is credible. Documentation reconstructed after a complaint looks like justification. Create it contemporaneously.

## Common Traps

### Pay Based On Negotiation Rather Than Criteria

The candidate who negotiates harder gets more, regardless of role or value. This embeds inequity, since negotiation behavior correlates with factors unrelated to performance and often with protected characteristics.

### Basing Offers On Salary History

Previous salary carries forward prior inequity and is prohibited in many jurisdictions. Base pay on the role and market, not the past.

### Pay Compression And Inversion

When new hires are paid at or above tenured employees due to market movement, or when managers earn close to their reports, the structure creates resentment and retention risk. Monitor and correct compression.

### Ignoring Total Compensation

Comparing only base pay hides inequity or market gaps in bonus, equity, and benefits. Assess the full package, especially for senior and sales roles.

### Ad Hoc Raises For The Squeaky Wheel

Giving raises to those who complain or threaten to leave, while others doing equal work stay quiet, punishes loyalty and rewards threat. Manage pay proactively through the structure.

### Bonus Plans That Reward The Wrong Thing

Incentives tied only to volume, revenue, or speed, without quality, safety, or compliance guardrails, produce predictable distortions and risk.

### No Pay Equity Review Until Forced

Waiting for a complaint, audit, or lawsuit to examine pay equity guarantees the finding will be public and damaging. Review proactively and remediate quietly.

### Posting Ranges That Do Not Match Reality

Posting a wide or aspirational range that does not reflect actual pay creates legal risk and destroys candidate trust when the offer lands below expectations.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is there a defined salary structure with levels, bands, market positioning, and a review cadence?
- [ ] Are pay decisions benchmarked against credible, matched, current market data covering total compensation?
- [ ] Are consistent criteria applied to individual pay decisions, with the rationale documented?
- [ ] Is pay equity analyzed proactively across protected groups, controlling for legitimate factors, with legal involvement and a remediation plan?
- [ ] Is starting pay based on role and market, not salary history or negotiation, with consistent offer criteria?
- [ ] Are pay transparency obligations met, with consistent ranges across postings, offers, and internal communication?
- [ ] Does variable pay design drive intended behavior without creating quality, safety, compliance, or risk-taking distortions?
- [ ] Is the connection between pay and performance transparent, consistent, and based on a credible performance process?
- [ ] Is the rationale for pay decisions documented contemporaneously, including structure, criteria, comparators, and performance input?
- [ ] Are compression, inversion, and market drift monitored and corrected?
