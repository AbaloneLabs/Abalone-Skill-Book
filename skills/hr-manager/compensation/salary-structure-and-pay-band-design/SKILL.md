# Salary Structure And Pay Band Design

A salary structure is the architecture of pay within an organization. It defines the range of pay for each job level, providing a framework for setting new-hire pay, guiding increases, and maintaining both internal equity and external competitiveness. A poorly designed structure produces pay inequities that fester until discovered in an audit, makes every hiring and increase decision an ad-hoc negotiation, and fails to keep pace with the market. Agents often treat pay band design as a spreadsheet exercise and miss that the structure's logic must be defensible, that bands that are too wide or too narrow each create distinct problems, and that a structure that cannot adapt to different geographies, functions, or career paths becomes a constraint rather than a tool.

Use this skill before designing or revising a salary structure, pricing jobs into bands, or evaluating whether a structure is equitable and competitive. The goal is to make the agent design a structure that is internally equitable, externally competitive, administratively workable, and adaptable to the organization's realities.

## Core Rules

### Ground The Structure In Job Evaluation And Internal Equity

A salary structure must reflect the relative value of jobs within the organization. Without a defensible internal ranking, the structure's logic is arbitrary, and pay differences between roles appear unjustified, breeding resentment and potential discrimination claims.

Ground in internal equity by:

- conducting a job evaluation process that assesses the relative value of roles based on consistent criteria, such as skills required, responsibilities, effort, and working conditions;
- using a consistent methodology across all roles, so that the ranking is defensible and not influenced by the visibility or power of specific functions;
- grouping jobs of similar evaluated value into the same pay grade or band;
- documenting the evaluation methodology and results, so that the logic can be explained and defended;
- reviewing the evaluation when jobs change significantly, to keep the ranking current.

Internal equity does not mean everyone is paid the same. It means that pay differences are explainable by differences in job value, and that the explanation is consistent and fair.

### Anchor To Market Data For External Competitiveness

A structure that is internally equitable but uncompetitive with the market will fail to attract or retain talent. The structure's pay levels must be informed by reliable market data for comparable roles.

Anchor to market by:

- obtaining compensation survey data for benchmark jobs, from reputable sources that match the organization's industry, size, and geography;
- matching the organization's jobs to survey benchmark jobs accurately, based on job content, not just title;
- selecting a competitive target, such as the median or a specific percentile, based on the organization's compensation strategy;
- interpolating market data for non-benchmark jobs, using the job evaluation ranking to place them appropriately;
- refreshing market data regularly, at least annually, to keep pace with market movement.

Market data quality matters. A survey that does not match the organization's comparators, or a job match that is inaccurate, produces a structure that looks data-driven but is actually wrong.

### Define Band Width, Midpoint, And Range Spread Deliberately

The width of each pay band, the midpoint, and the range spread, the distance from minimum to maximum, are design choices that affect how the structure works in practice. Too narrow, and the structure cannot accommodate legitimate pay differences within a level; too wide, and the band loses meaning, allowing large pay differences for similar work.

Define deliberately by:

- setting band width that accommodates the range of experience and performance within a level, typically 20% to 50% range spread for most professional roles;
- using wider bands for roles with broader scope or where market pay varies more;
- setting the midpoint at the competitive target, reflecting the market rate for a fully proficient performer;
- defining the minimum as the rate for someone still developing in the role, and the maximum as the rate for an exceptional, long-tenured performer;
- ensuring consistency in band design across grades, while allowing appropriate variation for different types of roles.

Band design is a balance. Narrow bands provide tight control but may be too rigid; wide bands provide flexibility but may obscure inequities. The design should serve the organization's compensation philosophy.

### Decide On Grade Structure Versus Broad Banding

Organizations choose between traditional grade structures, with many narrow grades, and broad banding, with fewer wide bands. Each has implications for how pay is managed and how careers progress.

Decide by considering:

- traditional grades provide more precise control and clearer progression, suited to organizations with well-defined career ladders;
- broad banding provides flexibility and emphasizes career growth within a band, suited to flatter organizations or those emphasizing skill development over level progression;
- the organization's culture, career model, and administrative capacity, as each structure requires different management approaches;
- the trade-off between precision and flexibility, and which matters more for the organization;
- avoiding frequent structure changes, which create disruption and confusion.

The choice is not permanent, but it should be deliberate and aligned with how the organization manages careers and pay.

### Handle Geographic Differentials Explicitly

Pay for the same role often varies by location, reflecting differences in cost of labor and cost of living. A structure that ignores geography either overpays in low-cost areas or underpays in high-cost areas, both of which create problems.

Handle geography by:

- deciding whether to differentiate by cost of labor, which reflects what the market pays, or cost of living, which reflects what it costs to live, recognizing they differ;
- defining geographic zones or tiers, and applying different pay levels for each;
- being transparent about the geographic differential methodology, so that employees understand why pay differs by location;
- considering the implications for remote work, where the employee's location may differ from the office;
- reviewing differentials regularly, as costs and markets change.

Geographic differentials are increasingly contentious with remote work. A clear, consistent methodology prevents ad-hoc decisions that appear arbitrary or discriminatory.

### Plan For How The Structure Accommodates Different Career Paths

Modern careers are not always linear, and a structure that assumes a single upward ladder may not fit roles that grow horizontally, through expertise rather than management. The structure should accommodate dual career paths, where technical experts can progress in pay and status without becoming managers.

Accommodate paths by:

- ensuring that technical or individual contributor roles can reach pay levels comparable to management roles of similar value;
- creating dual ladders where appropriate, so that progression does not require a management track;
- ensuring that lateral moves, which develop breadth, are not penalized by the structure;
- reviewing whether the structure inadvertently forces people toward management to advance, which may not suit their strengths or the organization's needs.

A structure that only rewards management progression will push capable individual contributors into roles they do not want or cannot perform, losing their technical contribution and gaining a poor manager.

### Communicate The Structure And Its Logic

A salary structure that employees do not understand creates suspicion and perceived unfairness. Even a well-designed structure will breed resentment if its logic is opaque.

Communicate by:

- explaining the structure's design, including how jobs are evaluated, how market data is used, and how bands are set;
- being transparent about where an employee's pay sits within their range, and what drives movement within it;
- providing managers with the information and language to explain pay decisions in the context of the structure;
- publishing the pay range for each grade or band, so that the framework is visible;
- being honest about what the structure can and cannot do, and not overpromising precision it does not have.

Transparency does not require publishing every individual's pay. It requires that the framework, its logic, and the employee's position within it are understandable.

### Review And Adjust The Structure Periodically

A salary structure is not permanent. Markets move, jobs evolve, and the organization changes. A structure that is not reviewed and adjusted becomes outdated, producing pay that drifts from market and internal equity.

Review by:

- refreshing market data annually and assessing whether the structure's pay levels remain competitive;
- reviewing job evaluations when roles change significantly, to ensure they remain in the appropriate band;
- examining whether the structure is producing the intended outcomes, or creating problems such as compression or pay inequities;
- making structural adjustments deliberately, with analysis and communication, rather than ad-hoc tweaks;
- planning for the cost of structural adjustments, and budgeting accordingly.

A living structure, reviewed and adjusted, maintains its integrity and usefulness. A static one becomes an artifact that no longer reflects reality.

## Common Traps

### Arbitrary Job Ranking

Placing jobs into bands based on title, history, or influence rather than consistent evaluation criteria, producing indefensible pay differences. Ground in job evaluation.

### Stale Or Mismatched Market Data

Using outdated survey data or inaccurate job matches, producing a structure that looks data-driven but is wrong. Anchor to current, accurate market data.

### Bands Too Narrow Or Too Wide

Narrow bands that cannot accommodate legitimate pay variation, or wide bands that obscure inequities. Design band width deliberately.

### Ignoring Geography

Applying one pay level regardless of location, overpaying or underpaying by geography. Handle differentials explicitly.

### Single-Track Structure

Designing a structure that only rewards management progression, forcing technical experts into management. Accommodate dual career paths.

### Opaque Structure

Keeping the structure and its logic hidden, breeding suspicion and perceived unfairness. Communicate the logic.

### Never Reviewed

Creating the structure once and never updating it, so it drifts from market and equity. Review and adjust periodically.

### Compression From Ad-Hoc Adjustments

Making individual pay adjustments outside the structure, creating compression that undermines the framework. Manage pay through the structure.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is the salary structure grounded in a consistent, documented job evaluation methodology that assesses relative job value?
- [ ] Are pay levels anchored to current, accurate market data from reputable sources, with a defined competitive target?
- [ ] Are band width, midpoint, and range spread designed deliberately to accommodate legitimate variation within levels?
- [ ] Has the organization made a deliberate choice between grade structure and broad banding, aligned with its career model?
- [ ] Are geographic differentials handled explicitly, with a clear and communicated methodology?
- [ ] Does the structure accommodate different career paths, including dual tracks for technical and management progression?
- [ ] Is the structure's design and logic communicated to employees, with transparency about how pay is determined?
- [ ] Is the structure reviewed and adjusted periodically to remain competitive, equitable, and relevant?
