# HR Service Delivery and Shared Services

How employees experience HR is determined less by HR's policies and more by HR's service delivery — what happens when an employee has a question, needs a transaction processed, or faces a problem they cannot resolve alone. A well-designed service delivery model routes each employee need to the right resource efficiently, resolves it accurately and consistently, and frees HR business partners to focus on strategic work rather than answering repetitive questions. A poorly designed model routes employees in circles, produces inconsistent answers depending on who they reach, and consumes scarce HRBP capacity on transactional work. The discipline is to design the service delivery architecture deliberately, to measure its effectiveness from the employee's perspective, and to continuously improve it based on data rather than on HR's internal convenience.

## Core Rules

### Design the Tiered Service Model Around Employee Needs

The standard HR service delivery architecture uses tiers: Tier 1 (front-line service center or self-service for high-volume, routine inquiries and transactions), Tier 2 (specialized centers of expertise for complex questions requiring deep knowledge in compensation, benefits, employee relations, etc.), and Tier 3 (HR business partners for strategic, contextual, relationship-based support to leaders and teams). Design the tiering around the nature of employee needs, not around HR's org chart. High-volume, well-defined needs (pay stub questions, benefits enrollment, PTO balance, address changes) belong in Tier 1 or self-service. Complex but bounded needs (leave administration, accommodation requests, specific policy interpretation) belong in Tier 2. Strategic, ambiguous, relationship-dependent needs (organizational design, leadership coaching, complex employee relations) belong in Tier 3. Misrouting — Tier 3 HRBPs answering Tier 1 questions, or Tier 1 attempting Tier 2 complexity — wastes capacity and degrades service.

### Maximize Self-Service for Routine Transactions

Most HR transactions do not require human intervention if the self-service tools are well-designed. Invest in self-service capabilities for the highest-volume transactions: personal data updates, pay and benefits information access, PTO requests, benefits enrollment, paystub and tax form access, and routine inquiries. The goal is not to eliminate human contact but to reserve it for situations that genuinely require it — complex cases, sensitive issues, and strategic support. Measure self-service adoption and continuously improve the user experience. Poor self-service (clunky interfaces, hidden information, broken workflows) drives employees to call or email, defeating the efficiency purpose and frustrating employees. Self-service is only effective if it is genuinely easier than contacting a person.

### Build Case Management for Consistency and Continuity

When employees contact HR with a question or problem, they should enter a case management system that captures the inquiry, routes it to the appropriate resource, tracks it to resolution, and builds a knowledge base. Case management enables consistency (every employee gets the same accurate answer to the same question), continuity (the employee does not have to repeat their situation to multiple people), metrics (volume, resolution time, satisfaction by inquiry type), and continuous improvement (identifying recurring issues that indicate a systemic problem or a self-service gap). Without case management, HR service is person-dependent — the answer depends on who you reach — and quality is unmeasurable. Implement case management as the backbone of the service center.

### Develop and Govern a Knowledge Base

A governed knowledge base — accurate, current, accessible answers to the questions employees actually ask — is the foundation of consistent HR service. The knowledge base enables Tier 1 to answer accurately without escalation, enables self-service by surfacing answers directly to employees, and enables consistency across the HR organization. Assign knowledge owners for each content domain (compensation, benefits, leave, policies, etc.) who are accountable for accuracy and currency. Establish a process for updating content when policies change, and for retiring outdated content. Review and refresh the knowledge base on a regular cadence. A stale or inaccurate knowledge base is worse than none, because it produces confidently wrong answers.

### Measure Service Effectiveness From the Employee Perspective

HR service metrics often measure HR's activity (call volume, handle time, cases closed) rather than the employee's experience (was the issue resolved, was it resolved accurately, was it resolved on first contact, was the employee satisfied). Track both operational efficiency metrics and effectiveness metrics: first-contact resolution rate, accuracy (did the answer turn out to be correct), employee satisfaction (CSAT after resolution), and escalation rate (how often does a case bounce between tiers). Survey employees after case resolution, not just annually. The operational metrics tell HR how busy it is; the effectiveness metrics tell HR how well it is serving employees. Both matter, but effectiveness is the purpose.

### Route Sensitive Inquiries to Appropriate Resources With Confidentiality

Not all HR inquiries belong in a general service center. Sensitive inquiries — harassment complaints, discrimination concerns, mental health crises, accommodation requests, whistleblower reports — must route to specialized resources (employee relations, EAP, ethics hotline) with appropriate confidentiality protections. Design the routing so that an employee who contacts the general service center with a sensitive issue is redirected discreetly and supportively, not turned away or mishandled. Train Tier 1 staff to recognize sensitive issues and to route them appropriately without probing or recording unnecessary detail in the general case system. The confidentiality of sensitive inquiries is both a legal obligation and a trust requirement.

### Continuously Improve Based on Pattern Analysis

The cases flowing through the service center are a rich source of insight about where HR processes, policies, and communications are creating confusion or friction. Analyze case patterns: which inquiry types generate the most volume, which have the lowest first-contact resolution, which generate the most escalations, and which recur from the same populations. Each pattern points to a systemic issue — a policy that is unclear, a process that is broken, a self-service gap, a communication failure. Feed these insights back to the centers of expertise and policy owners for systemic fixes. The service center is not just a response function; it is a diagnostic function that identifies where the broader HR system is failing employees.

### Align the HRBP Role With Strategic, Not Transactional, Work

A common failure of HR service delivery is the HRBP who spends most of their time on transactional inquiries and routine cases, leaving no capacity for the strategic work (organizational design, leadership development, talent strategy, complex employee relations) that justifies the role. The tiered model exists precisely to absorb transactional work so that HRBPs can focus strategically. If HRBPs are doing transactional work, either the tiered model is not functioning (self-service and service center are not effective) or the HRBP is not redirecting work appropriately. Measure how HRBP time is allocated and address the structural reasons for transactional overload. An HRBP who is a high-cost generalist answering routine questions is a misallocation of expensive capability.

## Common Traps

### Self-Service That Is Harder Than Calling

Self-service tools that are clunky, incomplete, or hard to find drive employees to contact HR directly, defeating the efficiency purpose. Invest in the self-service user experience until it is genuinely easier than contacting a person.

### Case Management Without Knowledge Governance

A case management system without a governed, current knowledge base produces inconsistent, person-dependent answers. Assign knowledge owners and establish update processes.

### Effectiveness Metrics Measuring Activity, Not Outcome

Measuring call volume and handle time without measuring resolution, accuracy, and satisfaction optimizes for HR's efficiency at the expense of the employee's experience. Track effectiveness metrics alongside operational ones.

### Sensitive Inquiries Handled in the General Service Center

Harassment, discrimination, mental health, and whistleblower inquiries routed through general Tier 1 risk confidentiality breaches and mishandling. Design routing to specialized resources with confidentiality protections.

### Pattern Data Collected but Not Acted Upon

Case patterns that reveal systemic issues — unclear policies, broken processes, communication gaps — are wasted if they are not fed back for systemic fixes. Treat the service center as a diagnostic function, not just a response function.

### HRBPs Consumed by Transactional Work

When HRBPs spend their time on routine inquiries, strategic work goes undone and the expensive HRBP capability is misallocated. Ensure the tiered model absorbs transactional work and measure HRBP time allocation.

### Tier Misrouting That Wastes Capacity

Routing Tier 1 volume to Tier 3 HRBPs, or Tier 2 complexity to Tier 1, wastes capacity and degrades service. Design routing rules around the nature of the need and monitor for misrouting.

## Self-Check

- Is my tiered service model designed around the nature of employee needs, with high-volume routine work in Tier 1/self-service and strategic work in Tier 3?
- Have I invested in self-service that is genuinely easier than contacting a person, for the highest-volume transactions?
- Does my case management system capture, route, track, and measure every inquiry, with a governed knowledge base for consistent answers?
- Are knowledge owners assigned and accountable for accuracy and currency, with a regular refresh cadence?
- Am I measuring effectiveness (first-contact resolution, accuracy, satisfaction, escalation) alongside operational efficiency?
- Are sensitive inquiries routed to specialized resources with confidentiality protections, with Tier 1 trained to recognize and redirect?
- Am I analyzing case patterns to identify systemic issues and feeding insights back for process, policy, and communication fixes?
- Are HRBPs focused on strategic work, with transactional work absorbed by the tiered model, and is HRBP time allocation measured?
