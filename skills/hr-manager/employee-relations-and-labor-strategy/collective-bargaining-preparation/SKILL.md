# Collective Bargaining Preparation

Collective bargaining preparation is the disciplined work that precedes sitting across the table from a union. The quality of preparation determines the outcome far more than table tactics do. Bargaining is constrained by law — the duty to bargain in good faith, the distinction between mandatory and permissive subjects, information obligations, and the limits on unilateral action — and it is constrained by economics, by the state of the bargaining relationship, and by what the workforce and the public will accept. An organization that arrives at the table with positions but without costing, without a sequenced plan, without an understanding of the union's constraints, and without a contingency for impasse will be outmaneuvered by a union that did prepare, or will make concessions it cannot afford, or will commit an unfair labor practice that hands the union leverage. Preparation is also where the organization decides what success looks like: a durable agreement that the workforce ratifies and that the organization can live with for the contract term.

Use this skill in the months before a contract expiration, when assembling proposals, costing the package, planning the team and timeline, or building a contingency for a possible strike or impasse. The goal is to make the agent prepare thoroughly, bargain lawfully, and enter the round with a coherent strategy rather than a list of demands.

## Core Rules

### Start Preparation Early And Against A Timeline

Bargaining preparation that begins weeks before expiration is already late. The substantive work — costing, proposal development, relationship assessment, and contingency planning — requires months, and starting late forces the organization to react to the union's framing rather than execute its own.

- Build a backward timeline from the expiration date that includes data gathering, proposal drafting, internal alignment, bargaining team training, and contingency preparation, with slack for unexpected developments.
- Begin information sharing and relationship groundwork well before the table, because the state of the relationship established between contracts shapes how the round opens.
- Identify the must-haves, the tradeables, and the items the organization can concede early to build momentum. Arriving with a flat list of demands produces deadlock.
- Plan the public and workforce communication cadence, because bargaining happens in front of the unit, not only across the table.

### Cost The Entire Economic Package Before The First Proposal

The single most damaging error in bargaining is to advance proposals whose aggregate cost the organization has not calculated. Seemingly modest line items compound, and a package built proposal by proposal can quietly exceed what the organization can afford.

- Build a total-cost model that captures wages, benefits, premium pay, leave accruals, differentials, and any new monetary items, modeled across the unit and across the full contract term.
- Model the cost of the union's likely opening positions as well, so the organization understands the gap and where movement is most expensive.
- Distinguish fixed from variable costs and identify which items scale with hours, headcount, or utilization.
- Tie the cost ceiling to a defensible economic rationale — affordability, competitiveness, sustainability — not to an arbitrary number, because a number without a rationale collapses under scrutiny.

### Classify Issues By Legal Subject And Sequence Accordingly

Labor law distinguishes mandatory subjects of bargaining (wages, hours, and other terms and conditions of employment), permissive subjects (which may be bargained but need not be), and prohibited subjects. Misclassification creates legal exposure and tactical confusion.

- Classify each proposed issue before the table, with legal review, because arguing a permissive subject as mandatory or vice versa undermines credibility and may constitute bad faith.
- Sequence the agenda to build momentum on less contentious issues first, reserving the hardest economic items for when the relationship and the pattern of agreement are established.
- Be prepared to bargain over mandatory subjects in good faith; refusal to bargain a mandatory subject is an unfair labor practice regardless of the organization's preference.
- Decide in advance which permissive subjects the organization is willing to discuss and which it will decline, so the response at the table is consistent and principled.

### Assemble And Train The Bargaining Team Deliberately

The bargaining team is the organization's instrument at the table, and its composition, training, and discipline determine whether the strategy survives contact with the union.

- Include the right mix: a lead negotiator with authority and skill, subject-matter experts who can speak credibly to operations and economics, labor relations specialists, and legal counsel as appropriate.
- Train the team on table discipline: who speaks, how concessions are signaled, how to avoid unintended commitments, and how to maintain a unified posture even under provocation.
- Define the team's authority clearly — what it can agree to at the table and what requires escalation — so that the team is neither paralyzed nor empowered to exceed its mandate.
- Brief the team on the union's likely strategy, personalities, and pressure tactics, so that table surprises are minimized.

### Fulfill Information Obligations Proactively

The organization has a legal obligation to provide the union with information reasonably necessary for bargaining, and the handling of information requests is both a legal requirement and a relationship signal.

- Establish a process for receiving, evaluating, and responding to information requests promptly and in good faith.
- Provide information that is relevant and available; document the basis for any request that is declined as not reasonably necessary or unduly burdensome.
- Anticipate the union's data needs and provide core information proactively, which builds credibility and reduces the volume of formal requests.
- Treat information sharing as a continuous obligation throughout bargaining, not a one-time exchange.

### Build A Contingency Plan For Impasse And Work Stoppage

Bargaining can reach impasse, and impasse can produce a strike or a lockout. An organization without a contingency plan is vulnerable to pressure that a prepared organization can absorb.

- Assess operational continuity options: what functions are critical, what can be suspended, what contingency staffing is feasible and lawful, and what customer and supplier communication is required.
- Understand the legal conditions for implementing a last best offer at impasse, and the significant legal risk of declaring impasse prematurely; premature impasse is an unfair labor practice.
- Plan for strike contingencies within the bounds of labor law, recognizing what the organization may and may not do regarding replacement workers, benefits, and communications.
- Define the decision rights and escalation path for a work stoppage scenario, so that decisions under pressure are made by the right people with the right authority.

### Maintain Workforce And Public Communication Within Legal Limits

The unit ratifies the contract, and the workforce's perception of the organization's posture shapes ratification and the post-contract relationship. Communication must be lawful, factual, and non-coercive.

- Communicate the organization's positions and rationale to the workforce through lawful channels, recognizing that communications during bargaining are constrained by the same rules that govern organizing periods.
- Avoid communications that could be read as threats, interrogation, or promises tied to bargaining outcomes.
- Ensure that what is communicated to the workforce is consistent with what is said at the table; inconsistency is detected and destroys credibility.
- Plan for external communication in the event of a public dispute, because labor disputes attract media and stakeholder attention.

## Common Traps

### The Uncosted Package

The bargaining team advances individual proposals — a wage increase here, a new differential there, an enhanced leave — each of which looks affordable in isolation, but no one has modeled the aggregate cost across the unit and the contract term. The trap is that the cumulative cost quietly exceeds the organization's economic ceiling, and the team discovers this only when finance reviews the tentative agreement, at which point the organization must either renege (destroying credibility and risking an unfair labor practice) or absorb a cost it cannot afford. The line-item perspective feels disciplined because each proposal is justified, but bargaining is a package, and a package built without a total-cost model is a package whose price is discovered after the deal is struck. A defensible total-cost ceiling, modeled before the first proposal, is what prevents the organization from negotiating itself into an unsustainable agreement.

### Premature Impasse As A Tactic

Frustrated by slow progress, the organization declares impasse and implements its last best offer to force movement. The trap is that impasse is a high legal threshold — genuine deadlock after good-faith bargaining — and declaring impasse prematurely is itself an unfair labor practice that hands the union leverage, overturns the implemented terms, and exposes the organization to remedies and reputational damage. Impasse feels like a clean escape from a difficult table, but it is a legal determination made after the fact by a body that examines whether the parties genuinely exhausted good-faith bargaining, and a premature declaration reads as bad faith regardless of the organization's subjective frustration. Impasse should be approached only with legal counsel and only when the factual record genuinely supports it, because the consequences of getting it wrong are worse than continuing to bargain.

### Table Indiscipline And Unintended Commitments

A team member, frustrated or eager to be helpful, agrees to a side point or signals flexibility the lead negotiator did not intend, and the union treats the statement as a commitment. The trap is that bargaining creates a record, and statements made at the table can constitute offers or agreements that bind the organization, so undisciplined commentary — even well-intentioned — alters the organization's position without authorization. A team that has not been trained on who speaks and how flexibility is signaled will, under the pressure and provocation of the table, make commitments the organization did not authorize and cannot easily retract. Clear speaking roles, a disciplined process for signaling movement, and pre-aligned authority are what prevent the team from inadvertently giving away leverage that the strategy was designed to preserve.

### Withholding Information To Gain Leverage

The organization declines or delays the union's information requests, reasoning that less information weakens the union's bargaining position. The trap is that the duty to provide information necessary for bargaining is a legal obligation, and refusal or delay is a basis for an unfair labor practice charge that shifts leverage to the union, draws regulatory scrutiny, and damages the relationship for the next round. Information that feels like leverage when withheld becomes liability when the charge is filed, because the obligation does not depend on whether disclosure is strategically convenient. Proactive, good-faith information sharing builds credibility and reduces the formal-request volume, while withholding produces the legal exposure and relationship damage that make the round harder, not easier.

### Bargaining To Win Rather Than To Reach A Durable Agreement

The team approaches the round as a contest to be won — extracting maximum concessions, minimizing movement, treating every union proposal as a battle. The trap is that a contract extracted through raw leverage, with no genuine agreement on the substance, produces a workforce that ratifies reluctantly, a union that returns to the table hostile at the next expiration, and a grievance volume that spikes under a contract the unit resents. The relationship cost of a maximally extractive round is paid over the entire contract term and compounds across rounds, so a victory at the table can produce a loss in operations that exceeds whatever was saved. Durable agreements that both sides can live with — not maximal extraction — are the objective, because the bargaining relationship is recurring and the cost of hostility is borne by the organization every day between contracts.

### No Strike Contingency, Therefore Vulnerable

The organization enters bargaining assuming a strike will not happen, prepares no continuity plan, and is then pressured into concessions by the credible threat of a stoppage it cannot absorb. The trap is that the absence of a contingency is itself a bargaining weakness that a prepared union can detect and exploit, because the organization's behavior at the table reveals that it cannot withstand a stoppage, which invites the very pressure the organization hoped to avoid. A credible, lawful continuity plan does not require the organization to want a strike; it requires the organization to be able to withstand the threat of one, so that bargaining decisions are made on the merits rather than under duress. Contingency preparation is what allows the organization to bargain from a position of economic realism rather than fear.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Does the preparation timeline work backward from expiration with adequate slack, and did substantive work begin months rather than weeks before the round?
- [ ] Has the entire economic package — wages, benefits, premiums, leave, new items — been costed across the unit and the full contract term, with a defensible cost ceiling?
- [ ] Has each proposed issue been classified as mandatory, permissive, or prohibited, with legal review, and sequenced to build momentum?
- [ ] Is the bargaining team assembled with the right roles, trained on table discipline and authority limits, and briefed on union strategy?
- [ ] Is there a process for receiving and responding to information requests promptly and in good faith, with core information provided proactively?
- [ ] Is there a lawful, realistic contingency plan for impasse and possible work stoppage, with decision rights and escalation paths defined?
- [ ] Are workforce and public communications planned, lawful, factual, non-coercive, and consistent with what is said at the table?
- [ ] Is the objective framed as a durable agreement the unit ratifies and the organization sustains, rather than maximal extraction at the table?
