# Job Description Authoring and Maintenance

Job descriptions are simultaneously the most mundane and most consequential HR documents. They define what the organization is hiring for, anchor compensation decisions, establish the essential functions for ADA accommodation analysis, inform performance expectations, and serve as evidence in employment disputes. Yet they are routinely neglected — written hastily for a posting, never updated as the role evolves, and discovered to be inaccurate only when a dispute, audit, or accommodation request exposes the gap. The discipline is to treat job descriptions as living documents with clear standards, to distinguish essential from marginal functions deliberately, and to maintain them through a governance process rather than letting them drift into irrelevance.

## Core Rules

### Write Job Descriptions for Multiple Audiences and Purposes

A job description serves hiring (what are we recruiting for?), compensation (what is the job's value?), performance (what are the expectations?), compliance (what are the essential functions for accommodation?), and legal defense (what did the role actually require?). Each purpose demands specific content. For hiring, the description must attract qualified candidates with clear requirements and an engaging summary. For compensation, it must capture scope, complexity, and impact for job evaluation. For ADA compliance, it must distinguish essential from marginal functions with the basis for that distinction. For legal defense, it must accurately reflect the role's actual requirements. Write with all audiences in mind, and recognize that a description optimized only for the job posting (marketing copy) will fail the other purposes.

### Define Essential Functions Deliberately and Defensibly

The distinction between essential and marginal functions is the foundation of ADA accommodation analysis: an employee must be able to perform essential functions, with or without accommodation, but marginal functions can be reassigned. Define essential functions based on the ADA's factors: whether the position exists to perform that function, the time spent on it, the consequences of not requiring the incumbent to perform it, the work experience of past incumbents, and the nature of the work. Document the rationale — a list of essential functions without the basis for the distinction is weak in an accommodation dispute. Avoid the trap of labeling everything essential (which makes accommodation impossible and invites challenge) or nothing essential (which undermines the accommodation analysis). Be precise and honest.

### Distinguish Minimum Qualifications From Preferred Qualifications

Minimum qualifications are the floor — what the candidate must have to be considered. Preferred qualifications are differentiators — what would make a candidate stronger but is not required. Conflating the two inflates minimum requirements (excluding qualified candidates, often with disparate impact on protected groups) or deflates them (producing unqualified hires). Define minimum qualifications as genuinely necessary for the role, and audit them regularly for unnecessary requirements that serve as barriers. A degree requirement for a role that does not require the knowledge a degree provides is a common example of an unnecessary barrier. Be especially attentive to how minimum qualifications affect diversity — requirements that correlate with protected characteristics can create adverse impact even when unintentional.

### Write in Clear, Specific, Active Language

Job descriptions should be written in clear, specific, active language that describes what the role does and what outcomes it produces. Avoid vague phrases ("handles various duties," "other responsibilities as assigned" used as a catch-all rather than a legitimate flexibility clause) that make the description meaningless. Use action verbs tied to outcomes ("develops and maintains the customer database" rather than "responsible for database"). Specify the scope concretely (team size, budget, geographic reach) rather than abstractly. Write at a reading level accessible to the candidate population. A job description that no one can interpret consistently cannot guide hiring, compensation, or performance.

### Maintain a Current, Governed Job Description Library

Job descriptions drift out of accuracy as roles evolve, responsibilities shift, and technology changes. Establish a job description library as the single source of truth, with version control and a governance process for updates. Require managers to review and confirm the accuracy of their team's job descriptions at least annually — ideally during the performance cycle, when role expectations are already under discussion. Build a process for updating descriptions when roles change significantly (reorganizations, scope changes, technology implementations). A job description library that is not maintained is a liability; the descriptions exist but are wrong, which is worse than having no descriptions because they create false confidence.

### Align the Job Description With the Actual Role, Not the Ideal or the Incumbent

Job descriptions sometimes describe the ideal role the manager wishes existed, or the role shaped around the specific incumbent, rather than the role as it actually needs to function. An idealized description sets expectations no one can meet; an incumbent-shaped description becomes inaccurate when the incumbent changes. Describe the role as it needs to function to deliver its outcomes, validated against how the role actually operates. When the role and the description diverge, either update the description to match reality or change the role to match the description — do not let the gap persist unexamined.

### Coordinate Job Descriptions With Leveling and Compensation

The job description, the level assignment, and the compensation band must be consistent. A description that conveys senior scope paired with a junior level (or vice versa) creates internal inconsistency that surfaces in pay equity analysis, promotion decisions, and employee perception. When a job description is updated, evaluate whether the level and compensation band need corresponding updates. When a level changes, update the description to reflect the level's scope. Treat the three — description, level, compensation — as an integrated set that must move together.

## Common Traps

### Job Descriptions Written Only for the Posting

A posting-optimized description is marketing copy that fails compensation, performance, and compliance purposes. Write a comprehensive job description and derive the posting from it, rather than writing the posting and calling it the job description.

### Everything Labeled Essential

Labeling all functions essential makes accommodation impossible and is indefensible in a dispute. Define essential functions deliberately based on the ADA factors, and be honest about which functions are marginal.

### Minimum Qualifications That Create Unnecessary Barriers

Degree requirements, years-of-experience minimums, and specific certifications that are not genuinely necessary exclude qualified candidates and often produce adverse impact. Audit minimum qualifications regularly for necessity and barrier effect.

### Descriptions That Drift Untouched for Years

A job description that was accurate at hiring but has not been updated as the role evolved is wrong, and wrong is worse than absent. Maintain the library through annual review and change-triggered updates.

### Descriptions Shaped Around the Incumbent

A description built around a specific person becomes inaccurate when they leave. Describe the role as it needs to function, validated against reality, not as the current incumbent has shaped it.

### Vague Language That Cannot Guide Decisions

"Various duties," "as assigned," and abstract scope descriptions cannot guide hiring, compensation, or performance. Write specific, outcome-oriented descriptions that two readers interpret consistently.

### Job Description, Level, and Compensation Out of Sync

When these three are maintained independently, they drift apart and produce internal inconsistencies. Treat them as an integrated set and update them together.

## Self-Check

- Does my job description serve all audiences — hiring, compensation, performance, ADA compliance, and legal defense?
- Have I defined essential functions deliberately based on ADA factors, with documented rationale, rather than labeling everything essential?
- Have I distinguished minimum from preferred qualifications, and audited minimums for unnecessary barriers and adverse impact?
- Is the description written in clear, specific, active, outcome-oriented language that readers interpret consistently?
- Do I maintain a governed, version-controlled job description library with annual review and change-triggered updates?
- Does the description reflect the role as it actually needs to function, not the ideal or the incumbent?
- Are the job description, level assignment, and compensation band consistent and updated together?
- Am I deriving job postings from comprehensive descriptions, rather than treating postings as the descriptions?
