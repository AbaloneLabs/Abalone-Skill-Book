# Job Evaluation and Leveling Framework

A job leveling framework is the hidden architecture that makes compensation, career progression, and organizational design coherent — or incoherent. Without a consistent framework, every hiring manager negotiates a level based on their immediate need, every promotion is an ad hoc argument, and pay inequities proliferate because there is no shared definition of what a "senior" or "director" role actually entails. The discipline is to build a framework that evaluates jobs on their inherent requirements (scope, complexity, impact, knowledge) rather than on the person currently in the role or the salary needed to hire, and to apply it consistently so that levels are comparable across functions. A leveling framework that is applied selectively or overridden by political pressure is worse than none, because it creates the illusion of consistency while reproducing arbitrariness.

## Core Rules

### Evaluate the Job, Not the Person

The foundational principle of job evaluation is that the level reflects the requirements of the role, not the qualifications, performance, or seniority of the individual currently occupying it. A junior employee performing well in a mid-level role does not make the role senior; a senior employee in a junior role does not make the role senior. This distinction is critical for pay equity (paying differently for the same role based on the incumbent is a source of bias) and for organizational clarity (levels must mean the same thing regardless of who holds them). Evaluate the role as defined — its scope, complexity, impact, and knowledge requirements — and place the person into the level that fits their assignment, addressing any person-level gap through development or role adjustment.

### Define Leveling Criteria That Are Observable and Comparable

A leveling framework must define, for each level, what distinguishes it from adjacent levels in terms that are observable and comparable across functions. Effective criteria typically include: scope (team size, budget, geographic or functional breadth), complexity (nature of problems solved, ambiguity, judgment required), impact (influence radius — team, function, business unit, enterprise), knowledge (depth and breadth of expertise, whether the role requires recognized authority), and autonomy (level of supervision, decision-making authority). Write level descriptors that specify these criteria concretely for each level, so that two different evaluators assessing the same job reach the same conclusion. Vague descriptors ("works independently," "handles complex issues") produce inconsistent evaluations.

### Build a Framework That Spans the Whole Organization

A leveling framework that covers only some functions creates seams where jobs cannot be compared — engineering levels that do not map to sales levels, or corporate levels that do not map to operations levels. Build a single framework that spans the entire organization, with function-specific career paths that ladder into the common level structure. This does not mean every function has the same number of levels or the same criteria weights, but it does mean that a Level 5 in engineering and a Level 5 in sales represent comparable organizational value, scope, and impact. Cross-functional comparability is what enables equitable compensation, internal mobility, and consistent organizational design.

### Govern Leveling Decisions Through a Calibration Process

Individual managers and HRBPs should not unilaterally assign levels. Establish a job evaluation governance process — a job evaluation committee or calibration board — that reviews and approves level assignments for new roles, reorganizations, and significant role changes. This process ensures consistency, surfaces level inflation (the tendency for levels to creep upward over time), and maintains the integrity of the framework. Document the evaluation rationale for each role so that future decisions can reference precedent and so that audits can verify consistency. Make the governance process efficient enough that it does not bottleneck hiring, but rigorous enough that it prevents arbitrary leveling.

### Resist Level Inflation Actively

Level inflation — the gradual upward creep of levels for the same work — is the most common failure of leveling frameworks. It occurs because each individual leveling decision seems reasonable (this candidate is strong, this role is important) but the cumulative effect degrades the framework's meaning. A "director" title that applies to individual contributors managing no one and influencing only their own work devalues the title for everyone. Govern level assignments against the framework, track the distribution of levels over time, and flag and investigate concentrations or trends that suggest inflation. Be willing to hold the line on level integrity even when it costs a hire or creates friction with a hiring manager.

### Align Levels With Compensation Structure

The leveling framework and the compensation structure must be tightly integrated: each level maps to a salary band or range, and the level determines the compensation parameters. This integration ensures internal equity (same level, same pay opportunity regardless of function, adjusted for market differences) and enables consistent compensation administration (new hire offers, merit increases, promotion increases). When leveling and compensation are misaligned — when levels exist without corresponding bands, or when bands exist without clear level definitions — the result is ad hoc compensation decisions that produce inequities. Design the two together, and maintain the alignment as either evolves.

### Document and Communicate the Framework Transparently

A leveling framework that exists only in HR's files cannot guide decisions or build employee understanding of career progression. Document the framework — level descriptors, criteria, career paths — and make it accessible to managers and employees. Communicate how levels work, what each level means, and how progression occurs. Transparency about the framework (not necessarily about individual level assignments) builds trust, enables employees to understand their career options, and reduces the resentment that opaque leveling creates. Employees who understand the framework may disagree with a specific decision, but they can engage with the logic rather than experiencing leveling as arbitrary.

### Plan for Maintenance and Evolution

A leveling framework is not a one-time project; it requires ongoing maintenance. New roles emerge that need evaluation, existing roles evolve and may require re-leveling, and the framework itself may need adjustment as the organization grows or changes strategy. Assign ownership for framework maintenance, establish a regular review cadence (annual or biennial), and build a process for ad hoc role evaluation as jobs are created or significantly changed. A framework that is not maintained drifts into irrelevance, with levels assigned by precedent and analogy rather than by the criteria that once made it coherent.

## Common Traps

### Evaluating the Person Instead of the Job

Leveling decisions that consider the incumbent's qualifications, performance, or desired salary produce inequitable and inconsistent results. Evaluate the role as defined; address person-level considerations through development, performance management, or compensation within the level — not by changing the level.

### Vague Level Descriptors

Descriptors like "works independently" or "handles complex problems" mean different things to different evaluators and produce inconsistent level assignments. Write specific, observable criteria for each level that two evaluators would apply similarly.

### Level Inflation Through Accumulated Exceptions

Each individual level upgrade seems reasonable; the cumulative effect degrades the framework. Govern levels against the criteria, track distributions, and hold the line on integrity. A title or level that has been inflated cannot be deflated without pain, so prevention is essential.

### Framework That Covers Only Some Functions

Partial coverage creates seams where jobs cannot be compared and where inequities flourish at the boundaries. Build a single framework spanning the organization, with function-specific paths that ladder into the common structure.

### Leveling and Compensation Designed Separately

When levels and salary bands are designed or maintained independently, they drift apart, producing levels without bands or bands without level definitions. Design and maintain them as an integrated architecture.

### Opaque Framework That Employees Cannot Understand

A framework that employees cannot see or comprehend cannot guide career decisions and breeds resentment. Document and communicate the framework transparently, even while keeping individual evaluations confidential.

### Framework Built Once and Never Maintained

Jobs evolve, new roles emerge, and the organization changes. A framework without ongoing maintenance drifts into irrelevance. Assign ownership and establish a review cadence.

## Self-Check

- Am I evaluating the requirements of the role, not the qualifications or performance of the incumbent?
- Are my level descriptors specific, observable, and comparable across functions — so two evaluators reach the same conclusion?
- Does my framework span the entire organization, with cross-functional comparability at each level?
- Is there a governance process (committee, calibration board) that reviews and approves level assignments to ensure consistency?
- Am I actively monitoring and resisting level inflation, tracking level distributions over time?
- Are levels tightly integrated with compensation bands, designed and maintained together?
- Is the framework documented and communicated transparently so employees understand career progression?
- Is there assigned ownership and a review cadence for ongoing framework maintenance?
