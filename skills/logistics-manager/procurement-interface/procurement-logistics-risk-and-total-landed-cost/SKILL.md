# Procurement Logistics Risk And Total Landed Cost

Procurement price is not the same as delivered cost or supply reliability. Supplier choices create logistics effects through freight, duties, packaging, lead time, minimums, inventory buffers, quality holds, expediting, and disruption risk. Agents often compare unit price and miss the costs that appear later in transportation, warehousing, and service. This skill helps logistics contribute to procurement decisions before contracts lock in bad assumptions.

## Core Rules

### Compare total landed cost, not only unit cost

Include product price, freight, duties, tariffs, brokerage, insurance, port fees, drayage, storage, packaging, handling, minimum order quantities, inventory carrying cost, quality inspection, returns, and expedite risk. Separate recurring and one-time costs.

A cheaper supplier can be more expensive after transport, working capital, and service failures are included.

### Evaluate logistics risk by source

Assess supplier location, port dependency, carrier market, lead-time variability, customs complexity, weather, labor risk, geopolitical exposure, capacity, packaging quality, and responsiveness. Risk varies by supplier and lane.

Do not treat logistics risk as generic country risk. The actual route, product, and supplier behavior matter.

### Include lead time in inventory impact

Longer or more variable lead times require safety stock, earlier commitments, larger orders, and more working capital. Procurement savings may be offset by inventory exposure and obsolescence.

Lead time is not only a planning input. It is a financial and service tradeoff.

### Check packaging and handling assumptions

Supplier packaging affects cube, damage, palletization, container utilization, automation, store readiness, sustainability, and returns. Confirm packaging before quoting freight and landed cost.

Poor packaging can turn a good price into damage claims, rework, and high freight cost.

### Clarify Incoterms and responsibility

Incoterms or freight terms determine who books freight, pays costs, controls routing, handles export/import, bears risk, and owns documentation. Procurement should understand the control and cost implications.

Buying delivered terms may reduce workload but hide freight cost and visibility. Buying ex works may require strong logistics capability.

### Review logistics clauses before contract signature

Contract terms should support routing, packaging, data, documents, service levels, liability, chargebacks, audit rights, and change notification. Logistics should review clauses that affect execution before the award is final.

### Account for supplier compliance burden

New suppliers may need routing guide adherence, ASN, labeling, EDI, export documents, security programs, sustainability data, hazmat documentation, and audit records. Noncompliance creates chargebacks and operational work.

Supplier capability should be part of procurement qualification, not discovered after first shipment.

### Model disruption and recovery costs

Consider alternate lanes, expedite options, dual sourcing, buffer stock, supplier recovery time, and customer impact if the lane fails. Include the cost and feasibility of recovery, not just the probability of disruption.

Some low-cost sources are acceptable only with a clear resilience plan.

### Use post-award performance feedback

Track actual landed cost, lead time, damage, shortages, compliance failures, expedite use, and inventory impact. Feed results into supplier scorecards and future sourcing decisions.

Procurement decisions should learn from logistics execution, not reset with each bid event.

### Build logistics metrics into supplier scorecards

Supplier scorecards should include on-time ready date, ASN accuracy, packaging compliance, damage rate, documentation accuracy, carrier compliance, lead-time reliability, expedite frequency, and responsiveness to exceptions. Weight the metrics according to product and lane criticality.

When supplier scorecards measure only price, quality, and fill rate, logistics failures remain invisible until they become customer or inventory problems. Scorecards should make execution cost visible to sourcing teams.

## Common Traps

- Awarding business on unit price while freight, duty, inventory, and expedite costs rise.
- Using generic risk assumptions instead of supplier-lane-product analysis.
- Ignoring lead-time variability and its effect on safety stock and working capital.
- Quoting freight before packaging, palletization, and cube are known.
- Accepting Incoterms without understanding control, risk, documentation, and visibility tradeoffs.
- Signing sourcing contracts before logistics clauses for routing, packaging, documents, and liability are reviewed.
- Qualifying suppliers without checking ASN, labeling, routing, EDI, and export documentation capability.
- Assuming disruption recovery is cheap or available when a low-cost lane fails.
- Failing to compare actual landed cost to sourcing assumptions after award.
- Keeping supplier scorecards focused on price while logistics failures create hidden cost and risk.
- Leaving logistics out of procurement decisions until the contract is signed.

## Self-Check

- Does total landed cost include price, freight, duties, brokerage, insurance, port, drayage, storage, packaging, handling, inventory, returns, and expedite risk?
- Are logistics risks assessed by supplier, lane, product, port, customs, capacity, weather, labor, and responsiveness?
- Are lead time and variability translated into inventory, working capital, ordering, and service impact?
- Are packaging, cube, palletization, damage, automation, sustainability, and return impacts known before costing?
- Are Incoterms and freight terms understood for cost, risk, control, documentation, and visibility?
- Are logistics clauses for routing, packaging, data, documents, service, liability, chargebacks, and changes reviewed before signature?
- Is supplier capability checked for ASN, labeling, routing guide, EDI, export, hazmat, security, and audit records?
- Are disruption recovery options, dual sourcing, buffers, expediting, and customer impact modeled?
- Is actual post-award logistics performance fed into supplier scorecards and sourcing decisions?
- Do supplier scorecards include ready-date, ASN, packaging, damage, document, routing, lead-time, expedite, and response metrics?
- Can procurement see the tradeoff between purchase price and logistics execution risk?
