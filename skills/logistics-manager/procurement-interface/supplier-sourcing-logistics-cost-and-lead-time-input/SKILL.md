# Supplier Sourcing Logistics Cost And Lead Time Input

Supplier sourcing decisions shape logistics before the first shipment is booked. Supplier geography, production cadence, pack size, minimum order quantity, shipping terms, customs documents, and responsiveness drive cost and service. Agents often enter sourcing after supplier selection and then inherit freight, inventory, and lead-time problems. This skill helps logistics provide sourcing input early enough to affect decisions.

## Core Rules

### Join sourcing before RFQ assumptions lock

Logistics should review supplier location, shipping point, Incoterms, order frequency, packaging, palletization, lead time, minimums, export documents, and target service before RFQs are finalized. Early input prevents bids that cannot be compared fairly.

If logistics is asked only after award, the sourcing decision may already have hidden cost and risk.

### Provide comparable freight scenarios

Estimate freight by mode, lane, shipment size, frequency, consolidation, port, drayage, parcel, LTL, truckload, air, ocean, and expedite. Use comparable assumptions across suppliers.

Supplier bids are often incomparable because one assumes full containers, another assumes air freight, and another hides freight in delivered price.

### Translate lead time into planning impact

Assess production lead time, supplier release cadence, pickup frequency, transit, customs, receiving, quality inspection, and variability. Explain how lead time affects safety stock, service, cash, and obsolescence.

Lead time should not be a single optimistic number. Variability is often more important than average.

### Evaluate logistics capability of suppliers

Check whether suppliers can produce accurate ASNs, labels, packing lists, export documents, pallet quality, routing guide compliance, appointment discipline, and shipment visibility. Ask for examples or test shipments where risk is high.

A supplier with good product capability may still be weak operationally. Logistics readiness is part of supplier qualification.

### Include packaging and MOQ effects

Minimum order quantities, case packs, pallet quantities, container utilization, and packaging choices affect inventory, warehouse labor, freight, damage, and customer handling. Include these effects in sourcing comparison.

Low unit price tied to large minimums can create working capital and obsolescence exposure.

### Model resilience options

Compare single source, dual source, nearshore, offshore, regional stocking, supplier-managed inventory, and buffer strategies. Include recovery options if a supplier, port, border, or mode fails.

Resilience is not free, but neither is disruption. Make the tradeoff explicit.

### Feed operational history into sourcing

Use existing supplier performance: on-time ready date, ASN accuracy, damage, shortage, documentation errors, customs holds, expedite use, quality holds, and responsiveness. Include logistics performance in scorecards.

Past logistics behavior is evidence. It should influence future awards.

### Refresh assumptions during long sourcing cycles

If sourcing runs for months, update freight rates, fuel, port conditions, duties, exchange rates, supplier lead times, packaging, and carrier capacity before final award. Old assumptions can make the selected option look better than it is.

Market conditions change faster than some bid calendars. Revalidate the logistics model before commitment.

### Document assumptions and owners

Record freight rates, fuel assumptions, duties, lead times, MOQ, pack size, service level, inventory assumptions, and who owns updates. Sourcing models should be auditable when actuals differ.

Without documented assumptions, teams argue later about whether sourcing savings were real.

### Run sensitivity analysis on key assumptions

Test what happens if freight rates rise, fuel changes, port dwell increases, lead time slips, MOQ grows, duty changes, demand falls, or air expedite is needed. Identify which supplier options are fragile under plausible changes.

Sourcing decisions should not depend on one perfect scenario. Sensitivity analysis exposes when a low-cost source only wins because every logistics assumption is favorable.

## Common Traps

- Inviting logistics after supplier award rather than before RFQ design.
- Comparing supplier prices that use different freight, mode, pack, and order assumptions.
- Treating lead time as a single value and ignoring variability and inspection time.
- Selecting suppliers without testing ASN, labels, documents, packaging, and visibility capability.
- Ignoring MOQ, case pack, pallet, container, and packaging effects on inventory and freight.
- Choosing lowest source without resilience or recovery options.
- Leaving supplier logistics performance out of scorecards and sourcing decisions.
- Letting old freight, fuel, duty, lead-time, port, packaging, and capacity assumptions survive to final award.
- Forgetting customs, duties, brokerage, and import document complexity in cost comparisons.
- Failing to document assumptions for later variance and savings review.
- Selecting a supplier based on one scenario without testing rate, fuel, lead-time, MOQ, duty, and expedite sensitivity.

## Self-Check

- Did logistics review sourcing assumptions before RFQ and award?
- Are freight scenarios comparable by mode, lane, shipment size, frequency, consolidation, port, and expedite exposure?
- Are production, release, pickup, transit, customs, receiving, inspection, and variability included in lead time?
- Is supplier logistics capability tested for ASN, labels, packing, export documents, routing, appointments, and visibility?
- Are MOQ, case pack, pallet, container, packaging, damage, warehouse labor, and inventory effects included?
- Are resilience options such as dual source, regional stock, nearshore, buffer, and alternate mode evaluated?
- Is supplier history for ready date, damage, shortages, documents, customs, expedite, quality, and responsiveness included?
- Are freight, fuel, port, duty, exchange, lead-time, packaging, and capacity assumptions refreshed before award?
- Are freight, fuel, duty, lead time, MOQ, service, inventory, and ownership assumptions documented?
- Has sensitivity analysis tested freight, fuel, port dwell, lead time, MOQ, duty, demand, and expedite scenarios?
- Can procurement compare suppliers by real logistics cost, risk, and lead-time impact?
