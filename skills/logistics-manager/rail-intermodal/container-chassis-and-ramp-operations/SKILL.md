# Container Chassis And Ramp Operations

Intermodal execution depends on equipment and terminal rules as much as rail linehaul. A container may arrive but still be unavailable because the ramp is congested, a chassis is missing, a hold is unresolved, or appointment capacity is gone. Agents often track train arrival and miss the ground operation that determines when freight can actually move. This skill helps manage containers, chassis, and ramps as an integrated flow.

## Core Rules

### Track true availability, not only arrival

Container availability may depend on train unload, terminal status, customs hold, line hold, freight payment, demurrage, appointment, chassis, and dray carrier capacity. Confirm when the unit is actually pickup-ready.

Rail arrival is only one milestone. A grounded container can still be operationally unavailable.

### Manage chassis supply and compatibility

Check chassis pool, private chassis, genset needs, tire condition, roadability, size compatibility, tri-axle needs, and reservation rules. Chassis shortages can delay pickup even when containers are free.

Chassis planning should be done before last free day, not after the driver reaches the ramp.

### Understand ramp rules and appointment systems

Each ramp may have rules for hours, appointment slots, flip fees, storage, driver credentials, hazmat, overweight moves, reefer access, empty returns, and grounded versus wheeled operations. Build these rules into dispatch.

Terminal rules are operational constraints. Treating them as driver knowledge creates inconsistent execution.

### Control demurrage and per diem exposure

Monitor last free day, free time, holds, pickup attempts, return requirements, empty release, and billing rules. Separate charges caused by terminal delay, customer delay, carrier delay, customs, or internal planning.

Demurrage and per diem are signals of flow breakdown. They need ownership, not just payment review.

### Coordinate drayage capacity early

Dray drivers, appointments, chassis, yard storage, lift capacity, and customer receiving windows must align. Confirm whether drayage can support peak train arrivals and customer deadlines.

Intermodal savings disappear quickly when drayage becomes the bottleneck or requires premium recovery.

### Plan empty return and reuse

Empty containers and chassis need return locations, cutoffs, steamship line rules, rail rules, reuse permissions, street turns, and clean condition. Return failures create per diem and customer-yard congestion.

The move is not complete when the loaded box is delivered. Empty flow closes the cycle.

### Maintain container and seal integrity

Check container condition, seal numbers, damage, leaks, odor, contamination, temperature if reefer, and placards. Record exceptions before pickup or delivery.

Container condition issues can become cargo claims, safety risks, or customer disputes. Evidence matters.

### Align customer receiving and yard capacity

Container pickup is only useful when the consignee, warehouse, or offsite yard can receive the box. Confirm receiving hours, live unload versus drop, yard space, lift equipment, labor, security, detention rules, and whether the container can remain on chassis. If the customer cannot receive, decide whether to hold at ramp, move to a yard, reschedule drayage, or recover by truck.

This decision should compare storage, demurrage, per diem, chassis dwell, labor, and service risk. Moving a container out of the ramp too early can simply transfer congestion to a customer yard or a third-party lot.

### Preserve custody, billing, and dispute evidence and escalate holds and exceptions fast

Keep a timeline of availability, appointments, pickup attempts, gate transactions, holds, releases, seal checks, delivery, unload, empty notice, and return. Capture screenshots or EDI status where billing disputes are likely. Link cost responsibility to facts rather than assumptions.

Evidence is especially important when free time is consumed by terminal congestion, rail delay, customs hold, or customer delay. Without a clean timeline, demurrage and per diem become arguments after the charge has already accrued.

Customs, line, exam, freight, documentation, damage, hazmat, and ramp holds need owners and next steps. Track who can release the hold and what information is needed.

Unresolved holds consume free time and create cost even while teams wait for someone else to act.

## Common Traps

- Treating rail arrival as container availability.
- Sending drivers to ramps without chassis, appointment, credentials, or release confirmation.
- Ignoring terminal-specific rules for flips, empty returns, hazmat, reefers, and overweight moves.
- Letting demurrage and per diem accrue without cause coding and ownership.
- Waiting until last free day to secure drayage and chassis.
- Forgetting empty return rules, locations, and cutoffs; failing to inspect container condition, seals, leaks, odor, damage, and placards
- Pulling containers from the ramp without confirming customer yard capacity, unload labor, or chassis dwell limits; losing appointment, gate, hold, and pickup-attempt evidence needed to dispute avoidable accessorials
- Allowing holds to age because release owner and required action are unclear; treating ramp delays as unavoidable rather than manageable flow constraints

## Self-Check

- Is container availability confirmed beyond train arrival, including unload, holds, payment, appointment, chassis, and drayage?
- Are chassis pool, private chassis, size, genset, tri-axle, roadability, and reservation needs planned?
- Are ramp hours, appointments, flip rules, credentials, hazmat, reefer, overweight, and empty return rules known?
- Are demurrage, free time, per diem, last free day, holds, attempts, and charge ownership monitored?
- Is drayage capacity aligned with train arrival, customer receiving, appointment slots, and chassis availability?
- Are empty returns, street turns, reuse, return locations, cutoffs, and condition rules controlled?
- Are container condition, seals, placards, leaks, odor, contamination, and reefer status inspected and recorded?
- Can the customer or interim yard receive the container without creating chassis dwell, detention, security, or congestion risk?
- Is there a documented timeline for availability, holds, appointments, pickup attempts, delivery, unload, and empty return?
- Are customs, line, exam, freight, documentation, damage, and hazmat holds owned and escalated?; can the container flow complete without uncontrolled ground cost and terminal surprises?
