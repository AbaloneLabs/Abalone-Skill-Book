# Seasonal Assortment Launch And Allocation Flow

Fashion logistics is tied to calendars, seasons, drops, campaigns, weather, and selling windows. A product delivered late can miss its full-price window; a launch allocated poorly can create stockouts in one channel and markdowns in another. Agents often treat seasonal assortment logistics as standard replenishment and miss the timing, allocation, channel, and markdown consequences. This skill helps coordinate launch flow around sell-through value, not just delivery completion.

## Core Rules

### Anchor logistics to the selling calendar

Identify the launch date, campaign date, floor set, online release, wholesale appointment, influencer drop, seasonal weather window, and markdown schedule. Work backward to import, DC processing, allocation, store delivery, site readiness, and contingency dates.

The real deadline is not always purchase-order due date. It is the moment product must be visible, shoppable, and in the right channel.

### Prioritize by commercial impact

When capacity, inventory, or time is constrained, prioritize hero products, advertised items, high-margin styles, key sizes, launch markets, strategic accounts, and weather-sensitive items. Define priority before shortages occur.

Do not allocate scarce logistics capacity evenly if commercial risk is uneven. Equal treatment may produce worse business outcomes.

### Coordinate allocation across channels

Fashion products may split across stores, e-commerce, wholesale, marketplace, outlets, pop-ups, and international markets. Confirm channel rules, inventory reservations, launch embargoes, pack requirements, and transfer options.

Channel conflict happens when one channel ships early, consumes limited stock, or exposes a product before a coordinated launch. Allocation must respect both demand and brand strategy.

### Manage inbound variability and customs risk

Seasonal launches often depend on overseas production, consolidations, ocean cutoffs, air freight decisions, customs documents, quality holds, and supplier readiness. Monitor milestone risk early enough to choose air upgrades, partial shipments, or reallocation.

Do not wait for missed vessel dates to make launch decisions. Late discovery forces expensive and poorly targeted recovery.

### Prepare distribution capacity for launch waves

Launches create spikes in receiving, quality inspection, ticketing, value-added services, allocation, picking, packing, outbound loading, and returns setup. Confirm labor, space, systems, labels, pack materials, and carrier capacity.

A product that arrives at the DC can still miss launch if processing capacity is not reserved.

### Protect size curves and regional fit

Allocation should consider size curves, climate, store profile, local demand, regional fashion preference, historical sell-through, and replenishment lead time. Sending the same pack to every location may be simple but commercially weak.

Misallocated sizes and climate-inappropriate product create markdowns even when total inventory is sufficient.

### Plan launch contingencies

Define what happens if product is short, late, failed quality inspection, missing labels, or split across shipments. Options may include partial launch, channel delay, market prioritization, substitute product, air freight, rework, or campaign change.

Contingency decisions need commercial, logistics, brand, and finance alignment. Logistics alone should not decide to break a launch promise.

### Communicate launch readiness across functions

Merchandising, marketing, stores, e-commerce, customer service, wholesale accounts, and finance need a shared view of what is available, at risk, delayed, or being reallocated. Publish launch readiness with assumptions, dates, shortages, and decision owners.

Silence creates bad promises. Teams may advertise unavailable product, staff stores incorrectly, or answer customers with outdated launch information.

### Track sell-through feedback

After launch, monitor sell-through, stockouts, size breaks, transfers, returns, markdowns, customer complaints, and channel performance. Feed learning into replenishment, future buys, allocation rules, and supplier evaluation.

Seasonal flow does not end at first delivery. The response window determines how much value is recovered.

## Common Traps

- Treating PO due date as the launch deadline while ignoring floor set, campaign, and online release timing.
- Prioritizing logistics evenly instead of by commercial impact and launch risk.
- Letting one channel consume stock or launch early without brand and allocation control.
- Discovering production, customs, quality, or vessel delays too late to choose recovery options.
- Assuming DC receipt means launch readiness without value-added and outbound capacity.
- Sending uniform size packs to regions with different climates, store profiles, and size curves.
- Making air freight decisions without margin, campaign, and markdown tradeoff analysis.
- Launching partial product without customer, store, and marketing alignment.
- Keeping launch risk inside logistics while marketing, stores, e-commerce, and service teams make promises.
- Failing to use sell-through and returns data to improve allocation and future seasons.

## Self-Check

- Are launch, campaign, floor set, online release, wholesale, weather, and markdown dates mapped?
- Are logistics priorities tied to hero items, margin, advertised styles, key markets, strategic accounts, and weather risk?
- Are channel reservations, embargoes, pack rules, transfer options, and launch sequencing controlled?
- Are production, consolidation, ocean, air, customs, quality, and supplier milestones monitored early?
- Is DC capacity reserved for receiving, inspection, ticketing, VAS, allocation, picking, packing, and outbound loading?
- Do allocations respect size curves, climate, store profile, local demand, and replenishment lead time?
- Are short, late, quality-held, missing-label, and split-shipment contingencies defined with commercial approval?
- Are merchandising, marketing, stores, e-commerce, service, wholesale, and finance aligned on launch readiness and risk?
- Are air freight and recovery costs evaluated against margin, campaign exposure, and markdown risk?
- Is sell-through, stockout, size-break, return, transfer, and markdown feedback used after launch?
