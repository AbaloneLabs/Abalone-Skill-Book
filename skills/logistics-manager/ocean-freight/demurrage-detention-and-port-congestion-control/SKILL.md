# Demurrage Detention And Port Congestion Control

Demurrage and detention are not just nuisance fees. They are signals that ocean freight handoffs, documents, customs release, drayage capacity, terminal access, chassis availability, appointment control, or empty return planning have failed. Agents often treat these charges as unavoidable port costs or purely accounting disputes. Strong logistics work prevents the clock from starting unnoticed, controls handoffs before containers arrive, and preserves evidence when charges are wrong. This skill helps the agent reason about ocean container free time and congestion as an operating risk.

## Core Rules

### Separate Demurrage, Detention, Storage, And Related Charges

Terms vary by contract, carrier, terminal, and country, but the operational distinction matters. Demurrage generally relates to containers sitting at the terminal after free time. Detention generally relates to keeping carrier equipment outside the terminal after pickup. Terminal storage, chassis fees, per diem, yard storage, rail storage, trucker waiting, and port congestion surcharges may be separate. If the agent mixes these terms, it may assign the wrong owner or fix the wrong process.

Start by identifying where the container physically was, who controlled it, when free time started and ended, and which tariff or contract governed the charge. A demurrage dispute may require terminal availability evidence; a detention dispute may require empty return appointment or equipment acceptance evidence. Do not use a generic "port fee" label.

### Build A Clock View For Every Container

Each container has a timeline: vessel arrival, discharge, terminal availability, customs entry filed, customs release, holds cleared, freight release, delivery order issued, pickup appointment, chassis availability, gate out, delivery, unloading, empty return appointment, gate in, and final equipment receipt. Charges arise when one of these clocks runs past free time.

Create or request a milestone view before recommending action. Knowing only ETA is insufficient. A container can arrive on time but remain unavailable because of discharge delay, customs hold, exam, freight release issue, unpaid charges, terminal closure, or appointment shortage. The timeline should show which party could act at each moment.

### Confirm Free Time And Last Free Day Early

Free time depends on carrier contract, service contract, terminal rules, container type, port, merchant haulage versus carrier haulage, rail move, holiday treatment, and negotiated exceptions. Last free day should be calculated and communicated before arrival, then updated when availability changes.

Do not assume standard free time. Confirm import demurrage free time, detention free time, combined free time if applicable, weekend and holiday treatment, refrigerated container rules, special equipment rules, and whether extensions are possible. For high-volume importers, free-time terms can be a major commercial lever, not an afterthought.

### Coordinate Documents Before Vessel Arrival

Many demurrage problems begin with late or wrong documents: commercial invoice, packing list, bill of lading, arrival notice, delivery order, customs entry, broker data, ISF or security filing issues, certificates, release holds, freight payment, or original bill surrender. A container that cannot be released because paperwork is incomplete will consume free time.

Plan document readiness before the vessel arrives. Identify who owns broker clearance, freight release, payment, customs exams, government agency holds, and delivery order issuance. For regulated goods, agriculture, food, chemicals, pharma, or controlled goods, allow extra time for agency review and exams.

### Treat Drayage Capacity As A Constraint, Not A Commodity

Even with customs release, containers may sit because truckers lack appointments, chassis, drivers, TWIC or port credentials, yard capacity, or empty return options. During congestion, appointment slots can disappear quickly. A plan that waits until last free day to arrange drayage is fragile.

Pre-book drayage when possible and communicate container availability, last free day, weight, hazardous status, reefer needs, delivery appointment, unload capacity, and empty return location. For heavy or overweight containers, confirm permits, chassis type, route constraints, and scale procedures. Do not assume any trucker can handle any box.

### Manage Empty Return Before Delivery

Detention often results from failing to return the empty container. Empty return may be constrained by terminal rules, carrier instructions, appointment systems, depot availability, rejected equipment, chassis shortages, and split return locations. Delivery to the consignee is not the end of the container cycle.

Plan unload timing, live unload versus drop, consignee free time, empty pickup, empty return appointment, depot hours, and rejected-empty escalation. If the consignee needs multiple days to unload, negotiate or price detention risk before pickup. For drop programs, track container location and dwell daily.

### Preserve Evidence For Disputes And Waivers

Charges may be wrong when containers were unavailable, terminals were closed, appointments were unavailable, carriers changed return locations, holds were carrier-caused, or system errors prevented action. Evidence matters: terminal screenshots, availability records, appointment attempts, trucker emails, gate receipts, customs release timestamps, carrier advisories, screenshots of no return locations, and weather or strike notices.

Capture evidence while it exists. Port systems may not keep easy historical views. A dispute filed without timestamps and proof is weak. Distinguish commercial waiver requests from factual disputes; both need documentation.

### Assign Ownership Across Broker, Forwarder, Drayage, Warehouse, And Customer

Ocean freight handoffs cross many parties. A forwarder may see vessel milestones, a broker sees customs, a drayage carrier sees appointments, a warehouse controls unload time, finance controls payment, and a customer controls receiving hours. If no one owns the end-to-end clock, fees appear as surprises.

Define owner for milestone monitoring, exception escalation, payment holds, delivery appointment, unload readiness, empty return, and charge review. For recurring freight, build scorecards by lane, terminal, carrier, forwarder, drayage provider, customer, and cause code.

## Common Traps

### Calling Every Port Charge Demurrage

Different charges have different clocks, owners, and dispute evidence. Mislabeling prevents root cause control.

### Waiting For Vessel Arrival To Start Clearance Work

Late documents, freight release, and customs issues can consume free time before anyone can pick up the box.

### Assuming Released Means Available

Customs release, freight release, terminal availability, appointment access, and chassis availability are separate gates.

### Planning Delivery But Not Empty Return

Detention risk continues until the empty container is accepted back by the right location.

### Ignoring Weekend And Holiday Treatment

Free-time calculations can change materially depending on how nonworking days are counted.

### Blaming Drayage Without Checking Port Access

Truckers may be blocked by appointments, chassis, terminal closures, or carrier return restrictions.

### Disputing Charges Without Evidence

Screenshots, timestamps, gate receipts, and release records are needed before system history disappears.

### Letting Fees Stay In Accounting

Recurring demurrage and detention should drive operational corrective action, not just invoice coding.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Are demurrage, detention, storage, chassis, per diem, waiting, and congestion charges separated correctly?
- [ ] Is there a milestone clock from vessel arrival through empty return?
- [ ] Are free time, last free day, weekend treatment, equipment type, and contract terms verified?
- [ ] Are customs, freight release, payment, documents, exams, and agency holds assigned before arrival?
- [ ] Is drayage capacity booked with appointment, chassis, weight, reefer, hazmat, and delivery constraints known?
- [ ] Is empty return planned before delivery, with return location, appointment, depot hours, and rejection handling?
- [ ] Is evidence captured for unavailable containers, no appointments, terminal closure, return-location changes, and system errors?
- [ ] Are broker, forwarder, drayage, warehouse, finance, customer, and consignee responsibilities clear?
- [ ] Are recurring charges coded by root cause and owner rather than treated as unavoidable fees?
- [ ] Would the container's charge exposure be visible before the clock expires?
