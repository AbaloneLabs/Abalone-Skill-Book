# Fine Art Crating Condition Report And Courier Control

Fine art logistics is custody, conservation, and risk management as much as transportation. A work can be financially irreplaceable, culturally sensitive, physically fragile, or contractually restricted. Agents often plan the move like high-value freight and miss the conservation controls: condition reporting, crating method, handling authority, courier role, climate exposure, and documentation discipline. This skill helps protect object integrity and accountability.

## Core Rules

### Start from the object's conservation needs

Identify material, medium, age, fragility, prior damage, mounting method, frame condition, surface sensitivity, vibration tolerance, temperature and humidity tolerance, light exposure, orientation limits, pest risk, and handling restrictions. Use curator, registrar, conservator, lender, or collection-management records.

Do not infer handling needs from the object's appearance or value alone. A small work on paper may be more vulnerable than a large sculpture.

### Define the condition-report protocol

Condition reports should document the object's state before packing, at handoffs, on arrival, before installation, after deinstallation, and at return where required. Include photos, diagrams, existing damage, packing notes, signatures, dates, lighting conditions, and who is authorized to assess condition.

Condition reporting is evidence and conservation control. Without it, disputes over damage become subjective and trust erodes.

### Specify crate and packing design

Crates may require custom fit, shock absorption, vapor barriers, archival materials, climate buffering, travel frames, cavity support, glazing protection, orientation markings, tamper indicators, and reusable design. Packing materials must be compatible with the object.

Do not let generic crating decisions be made by cost alone. The crate must match object risk, travel mode, handling points, climate, and loan requirements.

### Control handling authority

Decide who may touch, move, pack, unpack, inspect, photograph, install, or sign for the object. Art handlers, couriers, conservators, registrars, riggers, customs brokers, and security staff have different roles.

Unauthorized helpfulness is a risk. Well-meaning facility staff should not move crates, open packages, or adjust objects unless approved.

### Define courier role and decision rights

Couriers may supervise packing, accompany shipment, observe loading, monitor climate, approve unpacking, witness installation, or halt handling if conditions are unacceptable. Clarify authority, itinerary, communications, per diem, emergency contacts, and documentation duties.

A courier is not a passenger. If their approval is required, schedules and access must allow them to perform the role.

### Protect custody and security

Track seals, crate IDs, vehicle IDs, driver IDs, handoff signatures, route confidentiality, secure storage, alarmed areas, and access logs. For high-value works, minimize unnecessary location disclosure and unobserved dwell.

Chain of custody should be documented enough to support insurance, loan terms, and institutional accountability.

### Control photography and object data

Condition photos, crate photos, object dimensions, valuation notes, lender names, and handling instructions may be sensitive. Define who may photograph, where images are stored, how files are named, and whether photos can be shared with lenders, insurers, carriers, or internal teams.

Documentation should be useful without becoming uncontrolled security or rights exposure. Avoid posting or forwarding object images casually during logistics work.

### Plan for inspection and unpacking conditions

Unpacking may require acclimatization, clean space, table padding, tools, lighting, stable temperature and humidity, pest control, and enough trained staff. Do not open crates immediately after climate stress if acclimatization is required.

Arrival does not mean ready to unpack. The receiving environment must meet conservation and lender requirements.

### Prepare damage or discrepancy response

If damage, moisture, shock, seal discrepancy, crate breach, or condition change is found, stop normal handling, document evidence, notify required parties, preserve packing materials, and follow conservator or insurer direction.

Do not repair, clean, reframe, or discard packing before the responsible owner decides. Evidence may matter as much as the immediate fix.

## Common Traps

- Treating fine art as ordinary high-value cargo rather than conservation-sensitive property.
- Using generic crates without object-specific vibration, climate, orientation, and material requirements.
- Relying on vague condition notes instead of structured reports with photos and signatures.
- Letting unauthorized staff open crates, move objects, or adjust packing.
- Booking a courier without giving them access, time, or authority to supervise.
- Failing to track seals, custody, driver identity, secure storage, and route confidentiality.
- Taking or sharing condition, crate, value, and route photos without access controls.
- Opening objects before receiving conditions, acclimatization, and trained handlers are ready.
- Ignoring old damage until it becomes a dispute after transit.
- Repairing or discarding evidence after damage before lender, insurer, or conservator review.

## Self-Check

- Are conservation needs identified for material, medium, fragility, vibration, climate, orientation, and handling?
- Is the condition-report protocol defined for packing, handoff, arrival, installation, deinstallation, and return?
- Does crate and packing design match object risk, mode, climate, handling points, and loan requirements?
- Are authorized handlers, inspectors, photographers, signers, couriers, conservators, and riggers defined?
- Is the courier's authority, itinerary, access, documentation, and emergency path clear?
- Are seals, crate IDs, vehicle IDs, custody signatures, secure storage, and access logs controlled?
- Are condition photos, crate photos, valuation notes, lender details, and object data stored and shared appropriately?
- Are unpacking conditions ready for acclimatization, clean space, tools, lighting, padding, climate, and trained staff?
- Is there a stop-work process for damage, moisture, shock, seal discrepancy, crate breach, or condition change?
- Can final records support conservation, insurance, lender, and institutional accountability?
