# Warehouse Site Selection Lease And Labor Market Fit

Warehouse site selection is a logistics network decision, not just a real estate search. Rent matters, but so do labor availability, transportation access, customer service geography, zoning, utilities, yard capacity, lease flexibility, expansion options, incentives, and startup risk. Agents often compare sites by cost per square foot and distance while missing the constraints that determine operating performance for years.

## Core Rules

### Start with network purpose

Clarify whether the facility supports e-commerce fulfillment, store replenishment, cross-dock, returns, spare parts, cold chain, import distribution, manufacturing support, final mile, or overflow. The purpose determines location, building, labor, and carrier requirements.

Do not let an attractive building define the strategy. The site should serve the network design and customer promise.

### Evaluate transportation access realistically

Assess distance to customers, stores, ports, rail ramps, parcel hubs, LTL terminals, airports, suppliers, highways, border crossings, and congested corridors. Include drayage cost, carrier capacity, delivery windows, tolls, winter routes, and local truck restrictions.

Mileage is not enough. Transit reliability and carrier depth often matter more than map distance.

### Test labor market fit

Review labor availability, wage rates, unemployment, competing employers, shift preferences, public transit, commute times, language needs, union environment, seasonal labor, turnover risk, and supervisor talent. Include the skill level required for automation, maintenance, quality, and systems.

A cheap building in a weak labor market becomes expensive quickly. Labor fit is a site constraint.

### Compare total occupancy and operating cost

Include rent, taxes, common-area charges, utilities, insurance, security, maintenance, incentives, buildout, racking, MHE, permits, yard improvements, network transportation cost, labor cost, and startup cost. Separate one-time and recurring costs.

Lowest rent can lose if transportation, labor, utilities, or retrofit costs are high.

### Review lease flexibility and risk

Check term, renewal options, expansion rights, termination rights, assignment, sublease, landlord work, maintenance obligations, roof, HVAC, docks, yard rights, signage, parking, operating restrictions, and restoration obligations.

Lease details can limit operations and future network changes. Involve legal and operations before commitments.

### Validate building and zoning fit

Confirm clear height, dock doors, trailer parking, fire protection, floor load, column spacing, office space, utilities, zoning, permitted use, hazardous storage, food or pharma requirements, and neighborhood restrictions.

Do not assume industrial zoning covers the actual process. Truck traffic, hours, refrigeration, chemicals, and waste may be restricted.

### Consider resilience and growth

Evaluate flood, wildfire, storm, seismic, power reliability, water availability, labor growth, nearby development, road expansion, carrier market changes, and ability to add automation or more space. A site should work beyond the first year.

Network assets are hard to move. Resilience and optionality have economic value.

### Weigh incentives and community constraints carefully

Tax incentives, grants, abatements, job commitments, zoning relief, and infrastructure support can improve a business case, but they may impose hiring targets, wage commitments, reporting, clawbacks, traffic conditions, and public scrutiny. Understand obligations before counting the benefit.

Community acceptance matters for truck traffic, noise, hours, parking, and future expansion. A subsidized site can become expensive if commitments are unrealistic.

### Plan startup and transition feasibility

Assess permit lead time, landlord work, utility upgrades, racking installation, hiring ramp, carrier onboarding, system configuration, and inventory move timing. A good site that cannot be ready when needed may not be good enough.

Site selection should include launch feasibility, not only steady-state attractiveness.

## Common Traps

- Choosing a site by rent and square footage while ignoring network purpose.
- Comparing mileage without carrier capacity, congestion, delivery windows, and drayage reality.
- Underestimating labor scarcity, turnover, commute, shift fit, and supervisor availability.
- Omitting transportation, utilities, buildout, security, incentives, and startup costs from site comparison.
- Signing leases that restrict yard use, hours, expansion, sublease, or required operations.
- Assuming zoning and neighborhood rules allow the planned process.
- Ignoring flood, fire, storm, power, water, and climate resilience; counting incentives without understanding hiring commitments, reporting, clawbacks, traffic limits, and public obligations
- Selecting a site that cannot be permitted, built out, staffed, and launched in time; failing to involve operations, transportation, HR, finance, legal, IT, and safety in evaluation

## Self-Check

- Is the facility's network purpose and customer promise clear?
- Are customer, supplier, port, rail, parcel, LTL, highway, border, airport, toll, and congestion factors evaluated?
- Does the labor market support wages, shifts, skills, commute, transit, seasonality, supervisors, and turnover targets?
- Does total cost include occupancy, taxes, utilities, security, maintenance, buildout, MHE, transportation, labor, and startup?
- Are lease terms reviewed for term, renewal, expansion, yard, parking, maintenance, operating limits, and exit options?
- Do building, zoning, fire, utilities, docks, trailer parking, clear height, floor, office, and permitted use fit the operation?
- Are resilience and growth risks assessed for weather, power, water, roads, labor, development, and automation?
- Are incentives, abatements, grants, job commitments, clawbacks, traffic obligations, and community constraints understood?
- Is startup feasibility realistic for permits, landlord work, utilities, racking, hiring, carriers, systems, and inventory move?
- Are operations, transportation, HR, finance, legal, IT, safety, and real estate aligned on the decision?
