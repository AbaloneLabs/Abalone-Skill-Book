# Facility Expansion Relocation And Startup Readiness

Opening, expanding, or relocating a logistics facility is a high-risk transition. The risk is not only construction completion; it is whether inventory, systems, labor, carriers, customer promises, equipment, and processes are ready to run together. Agents often treat relocation as a move date and miss cutover sequencing, parallel operations, inventory accuracy, training, and startup stabilization. This skill helps manage facility change as an operational launch.

## Core Rules

### Define the transition strategy

Choose whether the site will cut over all at once, phase by customer, SKU, channel, region, process, or volume level, or run parallel operations for a period. Define success criteria, rollback options, and decision gates.

Do not let construction completion dictate go-live if systems, labor, carriers, and inventory are not ready. Building readiness and operating readiness are different.

### Map inventory movement and cutover

Plan what inventory moves, when, in what sequence, with what location mapping, count process, hold rules, and reconciliation. Decide how open orders, returns, quarantined stock, slow movers, and obsolete stock are handled.

Inventory is the hardest part of many relocations. If identity and location accuracy are lost during the move, the new site starts unstable.

### Prepare systems and master data

WMS, TMS, ERP, slotting, labor, automation, printers, scanners, scales, EDI, customer integrations, carrier labels, and reporting must reflect the new facility. Test transactions end to end before real volume arrives.

A site can be physically ready but digitally unable to ship. System readiness should be tested with actual workflows and exceptions.

### Align labor, training, and supervision

Recruiting, transfers, temporary labor, supervisors, trainers, safety teams, maintenance, and administrative support need a ramp plan. Train on standard work, systems, equipment, safety, escalation, and quality expectations.

Startup labor is rarely as productive as steady-state labor. Build learning curves into capacity promises.

### Coordinate carriers and appointment changes

Inbound suppliers, parcel carriers, LTL, truckload, drayage, private fleet, customer pickups, and shuttle lanes need updated addresses, route guides, appointment systems, pickup windows, labels, and contacts. Confirm that partners can serve the new site.

Carriers and suppliers should not discover the new facility through failed deliveries. Communication must be explicit and tested.

### Protect customer service during transition

Define service buffers, order cutoffs, blackout periods, priority customers, communication templates, and escalation. Decide what promises can be made during ramp-up and what must be temporarily limited.

Relocation success is measured by customer impact, not only whether boxes moved.

### Stage equipment, supplies, and maintenance

Confirm MHE, chargers, batteries, racking, packing stations, printers, labels, pallets, dunnage, spare parts, tools, cleaning, waste, fuel, and maintenance support. A missing consumable can stop a startup line.

Startup checklists should include ordinary supplies, not only capital equipment.

### Run operational readiness testing

Use mock receiving, putaway, replenishment, picking, packing, shipping, returns, cycle counts, carrier pickup, exception handling, safety drills, and outage scenarios. Include supervisors and frontline users, not only project staff.

Readiness testing should expose failure while time remains to fix it.

### Monitor stabilization deliberately and assign cross-functional startup governance

After go-live, track service, backlog, inventory accuracy, labor productivity, safety, system errors, carrier misses, quality issues, and customer escalations daily. Keep extra support until trends stabilize.

The move is not complete on first ship day. Stabilization is part of the project.

Facility startups need a command structure across operations, IT, inventory, transportation, HR, safety, maintenance, customer service, finance, and vendors. Define daily decision forums, issue severity, owners, escalation, and change control during ramp.

Without governance, every team solves its own piece while customer orders, inventory accuracy, and carrier execution drift apart.

## Common Traps

- Treating construction completion as operating readiness.
- Moving inventory without location mapping, count discipline, and reconciliation.
- Forgetting open orders, returns, quarantined stock, obsolete stock, and slow movers.
- Testing systems with simple transactions while real exceptions fail.
- Assuming new or transferred labor will perform at steady-state productivity immediately.
- Failing to update suppliers, carriers, labels, routing guides, and appointment systems; promising normal service during a transition with no buffers or communication plan
- Missing basic startup supplies such as labels, batteries, pallets, tools, and spare parts; skipping mock operations and safety drills before go-live
- Removing project support before stabilization metrics are healthy; running startup without a cross-functional issue forum, severity rules, owners, and change control

## Self-Check

- Is the transition strategy defined by phase, cutover, parallel run, rollback, success criteria, and gates?
- Is inventory movement sequenced with counts, location mapping, holds, open orders, returns, quarantine, and obsolete stock?
- Are WMS, TMS, ERP, labels, EDI, scanners, printers, reports, and customer integrations tested end to end?
- Are labor hiring, transfers, temporary staffing, supervision, training, safety, maintenance, and learning curves planned?
- Are suppliers, carriers, route guides, appointment systems, addresses, labels, contacts, and pickup windows updated?
- Are customer service buffers, cutoffs, blackout periods, priority rules, and communication templates ready?
- Are MHE, chargers, batteries, racking, stations, labels, pallets, supplies, tools, waste, and maintenance staged?
- Has operational readiness testing covered receiving, putaway, picking, shipping, returns, counts, pickups, exceptions, and outages?
- Are stabilization metrics and support plans in place after go-live?
- Is startup governance assigned across operations, IT, inventory, transport, HR, safety, maintenance, service, finance, and vendors?
