# Parcel Claims Surcharges And Delivery Exceptions

Parcel exception management is high-volume risk control. A single lost package may be small, but patterns in delivered-not-received claims, damage, address corrections, surcharges, and late deliveries can quietly erode margin and customer trust. Agents often handle each parcel issue as customer service noise, missing the operational patterns behind claims and surcharges. This skill helps the agent manage parcel exceptions with evidence, policy, and root-cause feedback.

## Core Rules

### Classify Exceptions By Action Needed

Parcel exceptions include no scan, delayed in transit, exception scan, address correction, delivery attempted, held at access point, returned to sender, delivered-not-received, damaged, lost, misdelivered, refused, weather delay, customs issue, and carrier service failure. Each has different evidence and action.

Do not treat all exceptions as claims. Some need customer instructions, address correction, carrier trace, reship decision, refund, pickup from access point, damage inspection, or invoice audit. Classification prevents over-refunding and under-responding.

### Use Evidence Before Deciding Recovery

Evidence may include tracking events, first scan, last scan, delivery photo, GPS, signature, package weight, label data, address validation, customer communication, carrier trace, proof of value, pick/pack record, CCTV, and prior claim history. Gather enough to make a fair decision based on order value, customer relationship, policy, and fraud risk.

For low-value goods, immediate reship may be more efficient than lengthy investigation. For high-value, repeated claims, regulated products, or suspicious patterns, require stronger evidence. Define thresholds so customer service does not improvise differently each time.

### Manage Delivered-Not-Received Carefully

Delivered-not-received claims can reflect theft, misdelivery, carrier photo error, wrong address, customer confusion, building mailroom delay, fraud, or actual carrier failure. Ask practical questions without blaming: delivery location, neighbors, mailroom, household members, access point, and whether photo/signature matches. Use carrier investigation where value or pattern warrants it.

Avoid binary assumptions. A carrier "delivered" scan is evidence, not always proof. A customer claim is also evidence, not always proof. Policy should balance customer trust, product value, fraud signals, and delivery proof quality.

### Audit Surcharges And Invoice Adjustments

Parcel surcharges include address correction, residential, delivery area, additional handling, oversized, dimensional weight, fuel, peak, return, Saturday, signature, declared value, and international fees. Audit invoices against expected charges. Determine whether the charge is valid, data-caused, packaging-caused, or disputable.

Valid surcharges can still require operational action. Address corrections may signal bad checkout validation. DIM charges may signal packaging mismatch. Additional handling may signal carton size or weight issues. Dispute invalid charges, but fix valid recurring causes.

### Control Claims Filing And Deadlines

Carrier claim rules differ by service, value, damage, loss, declared value, and insurance. Track filing deadlines, required documents, photos, packaging retention, proof of value, repair or replacement cost, and appeal windows. If customers discard packaging, damage claims may weaken.

Decide who owns claim filing and expected recovery. Customer service may make the customer whole while logistics pursues the claim. Finance should not assume full recovery if liability limits, exclusions, or missing evidence reduce reimbursement.

### Feed Exceptions Back Into Parcel Strategy

Track exceptions by carrier, service, lane, package type, SKU, customer segment, warehouse, address type, and season. Use patterns to change packaging, carrier mix, address validation, delivery instructions, signature rules, fraud controls, or customer promise. Parcel exceptions are data for network design.

If a carrier's low rate produces high lost-package or support cost, include those costs in carrier evaluation. If surcharges spike after a product launch, review dimensions and cartonization. If returns rise after failed delivery, review communication and access options.

### Keep Customer Policy And Fraud Controls Aligned

Parcel policies should be simple enough for customer service to explain and strong enough to prevent abuse. Define when to reship immediately, when to require carrier investigation, when to request photos or a police report, when to use signature on future orders, and when to deny a claim. Apply these rules consistently, but leave room for account-level judgment when the evidence and customer relationship justify it.

Fraud controls should not punish ordinary customers for carrier mistakes, porch theft, or building delivery confusion. At the same time, repeated delivered-not-received claims, address changes, reship requests to new locations, high-value goods, and chargeback threats deserve review. The right balance should be visible in policy rather than improvised by each agent.

## Common Traps

- Treating every parcel exception as an isolated support ticket.
- Reshipping high-value goods without checking delivery evidence or claim history.
- Denying delivered-not-received claims solely because tracking says delivered.
- Accepting all carrier surcharges as unavoidable.
- Disputing valid surcharges without fixing address, package, or service data.
- Missing carrier claim deadlines or packaging retention requirements.
- Confusing customer reimbursement with carrier claim recovery.
- Failing to track exception patterns by carrier, lane, product, and address type.
- Letting fraud controls become so harsh they damage legitimate customer recovery.

## Self-Check

- Is the parcel exception classified by action needed?
- Is evidence gathered proportionate to value, customer impact, and fraud risk?
- Are delivered-not-received claims handled with both customer trust and proof quality in mind?
- Are thresholds defined for immediate reship, deeper investigation, refund, denial, and carrier trace?
- Are surcharges audited by type and root cause?
- Are valid recurring surcharges converted into address, packaging, service, or data fixes?
- Are carrier claim deadlines, documents, photos, packaging, proof of value, and appeals tracked?
- Are customer credits and expected carrier recovery tracked separately?
- Are exception patterns reviewed by carrier, service, lane, SKU, warehouse, and customer segment?
- Do findings feed back into carrier selection, packaging, promise design, and fraud controls?
- Are customer claim policies and fraud controls clear enough to apply consistently without blocking legitimate recovery?
