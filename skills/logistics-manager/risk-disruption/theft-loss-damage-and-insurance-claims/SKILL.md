# Theft Loss Damage And Insurance Claims

Freight loss and damage management is both an immediate recovery task and a prevention system. The organization must protect the customer, preserve evidence, understand liability limits, file claims correctly, and fix the root cause that allowed the loss. Agents often jump to "file a claim" without checking delivery records, packaging evidence, inspection deadlines, carrier liability, insurance terms, chain of custody, or customer commitments. This skill helps the agent handle claims with operational discipline rather than paperwork alone.

## Core Rules

### Stabilize The Customer And The Goods First

When theft, loss, or damage is discovered, decide what must happen operationally: replacement shipment, repair, credit hold, quarantine, salvage, inspection, customer notification, safety review, or police report. If goods may be unsafe, contaminated, temperature-excursed, tampered with, or counterfeit-substituted, do not treat the issue as ordinary damage. Protect people and product integrity first.

Clarify whether the priority is customer recovery, inventory recovery, claim recovery, or investigation. Often all matter, but timing differs. A replacement may need to ship before liability is resolved. A damaged product may need to be held for inspection even if the customer wants it removed. A theft may require immediate reporting to carrier security or law enforcement.

### Preserve Evidence And Timeline

Claims depend on evidence. Capture photos of exterior packaging, interior packaging, product damage, seals, labels, pallet condition, trailer condition where possible, temperature records, delivery receipts, exception notes, driver comments, warehouse scans, weight records, and communications. Preserve packaging until the carrier or insurer inspection window is clear. Concealed damage often has strict reporting deadlines.

Build a timeline: tender, pickup, scans, transfers, terminal dwell, delivery appointment, proof of delivery, exception notation, discovery time, report time, and mitigation action. The timeline helps identify where custody changed and whether reporting deadlines were met. It also separates carrier failure from shipper packaging failure, warehouse error, customer receiving issue, or fraud.

### Understand Liability Before Promising Recovery

Carrier liability is not always equal to product value. Parcel, LTL, truckload, ocean, air, courier, warehouse, and 3PL contracts may limit liability by weight, declared value, released value, tariff, service guide, Carmack-style rules, international convention, warehouse receipt, or contractual cap. Insurance may have deductibles, exclusions, conditions, and notice requirements.

Do not promise full reimbursement until coverage is reviewed. Check whether value was declared, insurance was purchased, packaging met requirements, prohibited goods were shipped, concealed damage was reported on time, delivery receipt was signed clean, and claim documents are complete. If the commercial team chooses to make the customer whole beyond recoverable liability, track that as a service decision, not expected claim recovery.

### Match Claim Path To Mode And Contract

Each mode and provider has different claim requirements. Parcel may require online claim submission, photos, invoice proof, and packaging retention. LTL may require delivery receipt notation, inspection, freight bill, invoice, and repair estimates. Ocean or air may require notice within convention deadlines. Warehousing claims may depend on inventory records and service agreement terms. Cargo insurance may require immediate notice and surveyor involvement.

Use the contract and provider rules. Do not rely on generic claim advice. Identify claim owner, deadline, required documents, claim amount basis, salvage rights, inspection procedure, and appeal path. For high-value claims, involve risk management, legal, insurance broker, or claims specialist early.

### Investigate Root Cause

Claims are lagging indicators. Determine whether the root cause was inadequate packaging, pallet overhang, poor blocking and bracing, carrier handling, theft-prone lane, fictitious pickup, seal control failure, misload, wrong label, address error, missed temperature control, warehouse damage, customer unloading, or unclear delivery instructions. The prevention action depends on cause.

Use patterns. Repeated damage by SKU may indicate packaging design issue. Repeated loss by lane may indicate carrier or security problem. Repeated concealed damage may indicate receiving inspection weakness. Repeated denied claims may indicate poor documentation or unrealistic liability assumptions. A claim paid without process correction is not a complete resolution.

### Control Communication And Financial Impact

Coordinate customer service, sales, finance, inventory, warehouse, transportation, and insurance. Decide whether to reship, credit, repair, discount, or wait for inspection. Reserve inventory correctly. Track expected recovery separately from customer credit. Claims can distort margin if service credits, replacements, freight costs, and claim recoveries are not connected.

Communicate status with care. Customers need next steps, evidence requests, replacement timing, and inspection instructions. Carriers and insurers need facts, not accusations. Internal leaders need exposure, likely recovery, and prevention action. Keep emotional language out of formal claim records.

## Common Traps

- Filing a claim before preserving packaging, photos, delivery notes, scan history, and timeline.
- Promising full reimbursement without reviewing carrier liability, declared value, insurance, deadlines, and exclusions.
- Letting a customer discard packaging before inspection.
- Treating clean proof of delivery as the whole story when concealed damage deadlines still matter.
- Assuming every damage event is a carrier failure when packaging, loading, or customer receiving may be involved.
- Missing claim deadlines because ownership is split among customer service, warehouse, transportation, and finance.
- Ignoring theft indicators such as seal mismatch, fictitious pickup, unusual routing, or suspicious carrier assignment.
- Booking replacement freight without connecting cost, inventory, credit, and expected recovery; closing the case when money is recovered but root cause remains

## Self-Check

- Are customer recovery, product safety, quarantine, replacement, repair, and investigation needs separated?
- Is evidence preserved: packaging, product photos, labels, seals, scans, delivery receipts, temperature records, and communications?
- Is there a clear custody and event timeline from tender to discovery?
- Are carrier liability, declared value, insurance coverage, deductibles, exclusions, and notice deadlines reviewed before promising reimbursement?
- Is the claim path matched to the mode, provider contract, and required documents?
- Is there a named claim owner and deadline tracker?
- Are high-value, theft, regulated, or disputed claims escalated to risk, legal, insurance, or claims specialists?
- Is root cause investigated across packaging, loading, carrier, lane, security, labeling, warehouse, and customer receiving?
- Are customer credits, replacement cost, freight cost, inventory impact, and expected claim recovery tracked separately?
- Is a prevention action defined before the issue is considered complete?
