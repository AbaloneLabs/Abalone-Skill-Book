# War Pandemic Strike And Embargo Response

Large-scale disruptions change the rules of logistics. War, pandemics, strikes, embargoes, sanctions, civil unrest, border closures, and quarantine measures can make familiar lanes illegal, unsafe, unavailable, or ethically unacceptable. The agent should not treat these events as ordinary carrier delays. Response requires compliance review, worker safety, customer communication, inventory allocation, alternate sourcing, and explicit decisions about what should not move.

## Core Rules

### Separate operational disruption from legal restriction

Identify whether the problem is capacity, safety, cost, delay, closure, sanctions, embargo, export control, import restriction, quarantine, or prohibited party risk. Legal restrictions require compliance review before movement. A lane may be physically possible but illegal or reputationally unacceptable.

Use current official sources, legal, trade compliance, and risk teams for sanctions, embargoes, export controls, customs changes, and public health rules. Do not rely on memory or carrier rumors. Document the source and date of critical rule assumptions.

### Protect people before freight

Drivers, warehouse staff, port workers, brokers, suppliers, and recipients may face health, violence, detention, protest, or unsafe working conditions. Do not pressure workers or carriers into unsafe areas to meet service promises. Assess route security, curfews, checkpoints, quarantine, PPE, vaccination or testing rules where applicable, and evacuation options.

If labor strikes or civil unrest are involved, avoid actions that escalate conflict or create safety risk. Contingency plans should respect legal labor boundaries and worker safety.

### Reprioritize inventory and commitments

Large disruptions create scarcity. Allocate inventory by life safety, contractual obligation, customer criticality, regulatory requirement, margin, and strategic relationships. Freeze nonessential expedites if they consume capacity needed for critical goods. Sales promises may need to change.

Communicate constraints early. Customers prefer honest revised commitments to repeated optimistic delays. State what is known, what is uncertain, what alternatives exist, and when the next update will come.

### Build alternate routes with compliance and feasibility checks

Alternative routing may involve different ports, airports, rail ramps, border crossings, modes, carriers, warehouses, or countries. Each alternative brings customs, sanctions, permits, insurance, security, transit time, cost, product handling, and documentation issues. Do not reroute blindly.

Check insurance coverage, force majeure notices, contract terms, demurrage exposure, detention, storage, and customer approvals. For temperature-sensitive, hazardous, regulated, or high-value goods, verify whether the alternate route can maintain required controls.

### Capture decisions for after-action review

Crisis decisions are made quickly and later questioned. Document assumptions, approvals, compliance checks, customer communications, carrier notices, inventory allocations, and declined moves. This supports audits, claims, regulatory defense, and learning.

After the disruption, review supplier concentration, lane dependency, safety stock, nearshoring, dual sourcing, contract clauses, carrier diversification, and playbooks. A crisis should improve resilience rather than simply fade from memory.

### Create compliance holds and release criteria

For sanctions, embargoes, export controls, public health restrictions, or border closures, establish a hold process. Shipments, orders, vendors, customers, countries, ports, and carriers that may be affected should be paused until reviewed by the right function. Define who can release the hold and what evidence is required. Avoid informal workarounds such as changing consignee names, routing through third countries, or using unvetted brokers to bypass restrictions.

### Manage workforce and supplier communication

Large disruptions create rumors and anxiety. Drivers, warehouse teams, customer service, suppliers, and customers need consistent messages about safety, restrictions, expected delays, and escalation paths. If information is uncertain, say what is uncertain. Silence encourages unofficial promises and unsafe improvisation.

### Watch for fraud and opportunistic suppliers

Crisis markets attract fake capacity, inflated prices, counterfeit PPE or medical products, fraudulent brokers, and sanctioned or high-risk intermediaries. Vet new suppliers and carriers before use, even when pressure is high. Emergency procurement still needs controls.

## Common Traps

- Treating sanctions, embargoes, and public health restrictions as ordinary delays.
- Moving freight because a carrier offers a route without compliance verification.
- Pressuring workers or drivers into unsafe areas.
- Continuing normal customer promises after capacity or legal conditions have changed.
- Rerouting through countries or modes that create new customs, insurance, or handling risk.
- Ignoring labor relations and legal boundaries during strikes.
- Failing to document fast decisions, making audits and claims difficult.
- Returning to the old network without fixing the dependency that caused the crisis.
- Bypassing sanctions, embargo, or export control concerns through informal rerouting or renamed parties.
- Letting rumor-driven communication push workers, suppliers, or customers into unsafe or noncompliant actions.
- Using emergency suppliers or brokers without fraud, quality, sanctions, and capacity checks.

## Self-Check

- Have I identified whether the disruption is operational, legal, safety, capacity, sanctions, embargo, quarantine, or public health related?
- Did legal, trade compliance, risk, or official sources verify restrictions before any movement?
- Are worker, driver, supplier, and recipient safety risks assessed before pushing freight through disrupted areas?
- Have inventory and capacity been reprioritized by life safety, regulation, contract, customer criticality, and strategic need?
- Are customers receiving clear updates about known facts, uncertainties, alternatives, and next update timing?
- Do alternate routes account for customs, sanctions, permits, insurance, security, product handling, time, cost, and documentation?
- Are assumptions, approvals, compliance checks, allocations, declined moves, and communications documented?
- Will the event trigger resilience review of sourcing, inventory, contracts, carriers, routes, and playbooks?
- Are compliance holds, release criteria, approvers, and evidence requirements defined for affected shipments or parties?
- Have crisis communications and emergency supplier vetting controls been established before workarounds spread?
