# Cargo Theft Fraud And Fictitious Pickup Prevention

Cargo theft increasingly blends physical theft with identity fraud, data leakage, and weak pickup controls. A shipment can disappear because a fake carrier arrives with plausible paperwork, a load is double-brokered, a driver is diverted, a facility releases freight to the wrong person, or sensitive load details are shared too broadly. Agents often treat theft as a security guard problem while missing broker verification, appointment discipline, system access, and chain-of-custody controls. This skill helps the agent design prevention around both people and process.

## Core Rules

### Identify Theft Attractiveness And Exposure

Assess product value, resaleability, scarcity, brand visibility, theft history, black-market demand, load size, packaging visibility, route, dwell time, weekend exposure, parking, driver stops, and information exposure. Electronics, apparel, alcohol, pharmaceuticals, food, cosmetics, metals, tires, batteries, and branded goods may attract theft, but any constrained or easily resold product can be targeted.

Risk changes by lane and time. High-theft corridors, holiday weekends, port congestion, unsecured yards, drop trailers, public load boards, last-minute spot bookings, and unfamiliar carriers increase exposure. Do not apply the same controls to every shipment; focus on loads where theft probability or consequence is high.

### Verify Carrier And Driver Identity

Carrier identity should be verified before pickup, especially for spot freight, brokered loads, high-value goods, new carriers, changed pickup details, or urgent requests. Check authority, insurance, safety status, contact history, email domain, phone numbers, bank details, dispatch contacts, driver name, tractor and trailer number, license plate, and appointment confirmation. Use known contact channels rather than numbers supplied in a suspicious email.

At pickup, match driver ID, carrier name, tractor, trailer, appointment, pickup number, and load details. Require escalation for substitutions, late changes, handwritten documents, mismatched equipment, or pressure to release quickly. Warehouse staff should know that refusing release is acceptable when identity does not match.

### Control Load Information

Theft risk rises when shipment details spread. Limit access to commodity, value, pickup time, route, customer, appointment, and delivery location. Public load boards, broad email chains, unsecured spreadsheets, and over-detailed driver instructions can expose sensitive freight. Use need-to-know communication.

Be careful with last-minute changes. Fraudsters may request new pickup numbers, change delivery address, alter appointment time, or claim dispatch substitutions. Verify through trusted channels. Shipment diversion should require approval from named transportation and customer owners.

### Use Chain Of Custody And Physical Controls

For high-risk shipments, use seal control, photo evidence, trailer inspection, license plate capture, driver sign-in, GPS tracking, geofencing, no-stop instructions, team drivers, secure parking, trailer locks, yard checks, and controlled handoff. Define who applies and records seals, who may break them, and what happens if a seal is missing or mismatched.

Physical controls should be practical. A seal is not meaningful if numbers are not recorded and checked. GPS is not useful if alerts are ignored. Secure parking instructions fail if drivers lack routes and approved locations. Chain of custody should be operational, not ceremonial.

### Watch For Fraud Signals

Common warning signs include new carrier on a high-value load, urgent pressure, rate far below market, email domain mismatch, phone number mismatch, recent authority changes, insurance anomalies, dispatcher avoiding calls, changed banking, changed pickup driver, mismatched trailer, refusal to provide location updates, request to change delivery, or pickup outside normal process.

Do not rely on one signal. Fraud prevention is layered. Train logistics, warehouse, customer service, and finance teams to escalate suspicious patterns. A fictitious pickup can start with a sales email, broker call, altered document, or facility gate interaction.

### Respond Quickly If Theft Is Suspected

If theft or diversion is suspected, act immediately: contact carrier through verified channels, alert broker, notify security, track GPS, contact customer, preserve documents, notify law enforcement where appropriate, inform insurance, and freeze further releases to the suspect carrier. Time matters. Delayed escalation reduces recovery probability.

Preserve evidence: emails, call logs, rate confirmations, bills of lading, gate logs, video, driver ID, license plates, seal numbers, tracking, and system changes. Avoid accusing parties without facts in external communications; coordinate with security, legal, insurance, and leadership.

## Common Traps

- Treating cargo theft as only a warehouse gate issue rather than a carrier identity and information-control issue.
- Releasing freight because the driver has a pickup number, without matching carrier and appointment details.
- Using public load boards or broad emails for high-value loads without controls.
- Accepting last-minute carrier, driver, trailer, delivery address, or bank changes without trusted-channel verification.
- Recording seal numbers but not checking them at delivery.
- Buying GPS tracking but not assigning alert monitoring and escalation.
- Assuming known brokers eliminate double-brokering or identity fraud risk.
- Letting warehouse staff feel pressured to release freight when documentation mismatches; waiting too long to escalate suspected diversion

## Self-Check

- Are theft attractiveness, lane risk, dwell time, holiday exposure, and information exposure assessed?
- Are high-risk loads identified for enhanced controls?
- Is carrier identity verified through trusted channels before pickup?
- Are driver, tractor, trailer, plate, appointment, pickup number, and carrier matched at the gate?
- Are load details limited to need-to-know recipients?
- Are delivery changes, carrier substitutions, pickup time changes, and bank changes verified and approved?
- Are seals, photos, GPS, geofencing, secure parking, and no-stop rules used where risk justifies them?
- Are fraud signals known to transportation, warehouse, customer service, and finance teams?
- Is there a rapid response path for suspected theft, diversion, or fictitious pickup?
- Are evidence, insurance, law enforcement, customer, legal, and leadership notifications handled through defined owners?
