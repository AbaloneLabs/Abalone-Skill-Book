# New Product Launch And End Of Life Logistics

Product lifecycle changes stress logistics because demand, supply, packaging, master data, inventory policy, and customer promises are all changing at once. Agents often focus on launch date or disposal of obsolete stock while missing readiness gates, channel allocation, substitution rules, reverse logistics, and phase-in/phase-out risk. This skill helps plan logistics around launch and end-of-life transitions without creating service gaps or stranded inventory.

## Core Rules

### Build launch readiness before the first order

Confirm item master data, dimensions, weight, barcode, GTIN, tariff code, hazmat status, temperature needs, packaging, labeling, lot/serial rules, quality release, warehouse slotting, carrier compatibility, EDI setup, customer item setup, and system orderability.

A product is not launch-ready just because production has stock. If logistics master data or customer setup is incomplete, orders will fail at receiving, shipping, customs, or invoicing.

### Align allocation with launch strategy

Launch inventory may need priority by channel, region, customer tier, market, sales commitment, sample program, service contract, or regulatory approval. Decide allocation rules before demand arrives.

Without allocation logic, early orders can consume inventory needed for launch events, strategic accounts, or markets with firm commitments.

### Plan ramp-up capacity and variability

New products often have uncertain demand, supplier yield issues, packaging changes, quality holds, and incomplete handling knowledge. Build buffers for inbound variability, warehouse training, carrier pickup, return flow, and customer receiving.

Launch logistics should expect surprises. Capacity plans built on perfect forecast and perfect production will break quickly.

### Control phase-in and substitution rules

When a new SKU replaces an old one, define effective dates, order cutover, substitution permission, customer approval, labeling differences, compatibility, regulatory differences, pricing, warranty, and inventory depletion rules.

Substitution that seems operationally convenient may violate customer contracts, compliance, product fit, or marketing promises.

### Manage end-of-life inventory deliberately

For products being discontinued, forecast remaining demand, service obligations, spare parts, warranty support, returns, customer notifications, last-buy windows, and disposition. Decide whether to run down, transfer, discount, remanufacture, donate, recycle, or destroy.

End-of-life inventory can become obsolete cost, but cutting too aggressively can break contractual service or aftermarket commitments.

### Protect packaging and labeling changes

Lifecycle transitions often include artwork, regulatory label, language, barcode, country-of-origin, carton, pallet, or packaging-size changes. Confirm which version each customer and market can receive.

Mixed old/new packaging can confuse receiving, compliance, shelf presentation, and traceability if version control is weak.

### Coordinate reverse logistics and recalls

Launch defects, packaging errors, customer returns, or EOL pullbacks may require returns, rework, quarantine, field action, recall, or destruction. Define return authorization, disposition, quality inspection, financial credit, and inventory quarantine.

Lifecycle events often create unusual reverse flows that normal returns processes cannot absorb without planning.

### Manage customer and regulatory communication

Some launches and discontinuations require notices to customers, distributors, regulators, customs brokers, service teams, or field technicians. Confirm who can announce dates, substitutions, last buys, label changes, and restricted shipments.

Poor communication can create orders for unavailable products, receipt of nonapproved versions, customs delays, or customers rejecting valid substitute items.

### Run post-launch and post-EOL reviews

Compare planned versus actual demand, fill rate, launch date adherence, freight cost, expedites, damage, returns, inventory leftover, customer issues, and master-data defects. Use findings to improve future lifecycle gates.

The review should cover what logistics learned before the issue becomes institutional memory only in individual teams.

## Common Traps

- Calling a product launch-ready before item master, label, barcode, customer setup, warehouse slot, and carrier data are complete.
- Allowing first orders to consume scarce launch inventory without channel, customer, or market allocation rules.
- Planning ramp-up with no buffer for quality holds, supplier instability, packaging rework, training, or carrier constraints.
- Substituting new and old SKUs without checking customer approval, compatibility, regulatory status, pricing, and warranty.
- Running down old inventory so far that service parts, warranty, or contractual support cannot be met.
- Treating packaging version changes as cosmetic rather than receiving, compliance, traceability, and customer-acceptance risks.
- Forgetting return, rework, quarantine, and disposition capacity during launch defects or EOL pullbacks.
- Communicating launch, substitution, last-buy, or label-change dates inconsistently across customers, distributors, brokers, and service teams.
- Measuring launch logistics only by first ship date instead of fill rate, expedites, returns, and customer experience.
- Leaving obsolete inventory decisions until carrying cost and markdown pressure are already high.

## Self-Check

- Are master data, dimensions, barcode, GTIN, tariff, hazmat, temperature, packaging, labels, lot/serial, quality, slotting, carrier, EDI, and customer setup complete?
- Are launch allocation rules defined by channel, region, customer tier, market, event, service commitment, and regulatory readiness?
- Does ramp-up planning include demand uncertainty, supply yield, quality holds, packaging change, warehouse training, and carrier capacity?
- Are phase-in, phase-out, effective dates, order cutover, substitution, approval, pricing, warranty, and compatibility rules explicit?
- Is end-of-life inventory planned for remaining demand, service obligations, spare parts, warranty, returns, last buys, and disposition?
- Are packaging, labeling, barcode, language, regulatory, origin, carton, and pallet version changes controlled by market and customer?
- Are reverse logistics, rework, quarantine, recall, credit, inspection, and destruction paths ready for launch or EOL exceptions?
- Are customer, distributor, broker, service, and regulatory communications aligned on dates, substitutions, last buys, and version restrictions?
- Does the review compare planned and actual service, inventory, freight cost, expedites, damage, returns, and data defects?
- Can the lifecycle transition happen without hidden service gaps or stranded obsolete inventory?
