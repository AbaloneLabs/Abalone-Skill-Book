# Theft Prevention Custody And Secure Routing

Cargo theft prevention is an operational design problem. High-value freight becomes vulnerable through predictable pickups, public commodity clues, unattended stops, weak carrier vetting, terminal dwell, identity fraud, compromised routing, and poor receiving controls. The agent should treat custody as a chain that must be protected from order release through final delivery. A shipment is only as secure as its weakest handoff.

## Core Rules

### Assess theft attractiveness and information exposure

Start with commodity, value density, resale ease, brand visibility, destination, route, timing, public load boards, carrier access, packaging markings, and whether shipment details are widely shared. Electronics, alcohol, pharmaceuticals, luxury goods, apparel, cosmetics, tools, and consumer goods can attract organized theft. Prototypes or confidential goods may create competitive or reputational risk even if resale value is unclear.

Limit information to those who need it. Avoid unnecessary commodity detail in casual emails, public postings, labels, or unsecured documents. Use neutral packaging where appropriate while still meeting legal and handling requirements. Security by obscurity is not enough, but excessive disclosure increases risk.

### Vet carriers and drivers before tender

Carrier identity fraud and double brokering can defeat every later control. Verify carrier authority, insurance, safety status, contact authenticity, operating history, equipment, driver identity process, tracking capability, and whether subcontracting is allowed. For high-risk freight, prohibit unauthorized rebrokering and confirm driver details before pickup.

At pickup, verify tractor, trailer, driver ID, license plate, carrier name, pickup reference, seal process, and appointment. Train shipping docks not to release high-value freight to unverified drivers, even under time pressure. A fake pickup can look operationally normal until the freight is gone.

### Design secure routing and stop rules

High-risk routes should minimize stops, unattended dwell, weekend layovers, high-theft corridors, unsecured yards, and predictable patterns. Consider direct transit, team drivers, GPS tracking, geofencing, lock systems, seal controls, secure parking, route check-ins, and no-stop rules for the first miles after pickup where theft risk is often high.

If a stop is unavoidable, define approved locations and maximum dwell. If the shipment must hold over a weekend or holiday, use a secured facility rather than a random yard. Dispatch should know when route deviation or tracking silence requires escalation.

Plan pickup timing to avoid predictable vulnerability. Late Friday tenders, holiday departures, and end-of-shift pickups can push loaded equipment into long unattended dwell, even when the linehaul plan looks direct.

### Maintain chain-of-custody evidence

Custody evidence includes pickup authorization, driver verification, seal number, photos, trailer number, GPS start, check-in logs, terminal in/out records, handoff signatures, delivery appointment, recipient ID, seal verification, and proof of delivery. For sensitive shipments, record exceptions immediately and preserve tracking history.

Seals are control points, not guarantees. A seal number is useful only if recorded at origin, checked at handoffs, verified at destination, and investigated when mismatched or broken. Decide who has authority to break seals and how that action is documented.

### Prepare theft and tamper response

If tracking stops, route deviates, seal breaks, driver cannot be reached, or delivery is disputed, respond quickly. Notify carrier security, broker, shipper, consignee, insurer, and law enforcement according to severity. Preserve communications, tracking, pickup records, driver details, photos, and seal logs. Do not wait for routine carrier tracing if the indicators suggest theft.

Customer communication should be factual and controlled. Avoid public disclosure that could compromise recovery or investigation. Internally, separate operational recovery from claims, insurance, and security investigation.

After any suspected theft attempt, review whether the exposure came from carrier selection, leaked load information, weak dock verification, route planning, or receiver readiness. The lesson should update future tender rules, not remain only in an incident file.

### Respect Scope and Escalation Boundaries

Know where the agent's authority and competence end. When the question requires a license, a specialist's judgment, a final approval, or expertise the agent does not hold, the correct action is to escalate rather than to produce a confident answer that overreaches. Scope discipline protects the recipient from harm caused by an unqualified conclusion and protects the agent from liability. State explicitly when the output is advisory and must be confirmed by the qualified person.

## Common Traps

- Posting high-value freight broadly with enough detail for thieves to target it.
- Assuming a carrier's MC number or low rate proves legitimacy. Identity and authorization must be verified.
- Allowing unauthorized rebrokering, which destroys visibility and custody accountability.
- Releasing freight to a driver whose identity, tractor, trailer, or pickup reference is not verified.
- Letting a loaded trailer sit unattended after pickup, during weekends, or near high-theft areas.
- Recording seals at origin but failing to verify them at delivery.
- Treating GPS as useful without geofence alerts, escalation rules, and someone monitoring.
- Waiting too long to escalate tracking silence or route deviation.

## Self-Check

- Have I assessed commodity attractiveness, value density, resale risk, confidentiality, route, timing, and information exposure?
- Are shipment details limited to necessary parties while still meeting legal, safety, and handling requirements?
- Has the carrier been vetted for identity, authority, insurance, operating history, tracking, subcontracting limits, and high-value capability?
- Are driver, tractor, trailer, pickup reference, seal, and appointment verification required before release?
- Does the route reduce stops, dwell, weekend exposure, unsecured yards, high-theft corridors, and predictable patterns?
- Are GPS, geofencing, check-ins, secure parking, team drivers, lock, and no-stop rules used where risk justifies them?
- Is chain-of-custody evidence captured from pickup through delivery, including seal verification and authorized seal breaks?
- Are theft, tamper, route deviation, tracking silence, and disputed delivery escalation steps defined with contacts and evidence preservation?
