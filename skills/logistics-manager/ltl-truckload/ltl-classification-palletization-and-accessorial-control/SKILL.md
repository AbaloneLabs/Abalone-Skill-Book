# LTL Classification Palletization And Accessorial Control

LTL shipments are sensitive to details that agents can easily gloss over: freight class, density, dimensions, pallet quality, accessorial services, bill of lading accuracy, and consignee constraints. A shipment can look planned and still produce reclass fees, reweighs, damage, delivery delays, or customer disputes. This skill helps the agent treat LTL preparation as a controlled billing and handling process rather than a generic freight booking.

## Core Rules

### Get Classification And Density Right Before Tender

LTL price and handling are strongly affected by freight class, density, NMFC item where applicable, dimensions, weight, packaging, liability, stowability, handling, and density-based rules. Incorrect class can lead to reclass fees, billing disputes, customer chargebacks, and poor carrier selection. If the agent does not have authoritative classification data, it should state what must be verified.

Measure actual packaged dimensions and weight, including pallet. Do not rely on catalog item dimensions if dunnage, pallet overhang, mixed cartons, or shrink wrap changes the cube. If a carrier or 3PL uses dimensionalizers, sloppy estimates will be caught later. Keep classification records consistent with item master and rating systems.

### Palletize For LTL Handling, Not Just Warehouse Convenience

LTL freight moves through terminals and may be handled multiple times. Pallets should be stable, cube-efficient, forkable, wrapped, labeled, and appropriate for the freight. Overhang, weak pallets, loose cartons, top-heavy stacks, mixed fragile and heavy items, poor wrap, or no corner protection increase damage and rework.

Consider pallet dimensions, height, weight, stackability, banding, edge protection, slip sheets, labels on multiple sides, and whether freight can be safely stacked by the carrier. If freight is non-stackable, understand the cost and carrier acceptance implications. Do not label freight "do not stack" as a substitute for proper packaging or rating.

### Make Accessorial Needs Explicit

Accessorial charges often come from missing or late information: liftgate, residential, inside delivery, limited access, construction site, school, government facility, military base, airport, trade show, notification, appointment, sort and segregate, protect from freeze, hazardous materials, oversized freight, or detention. These services may be legitimate, but surprise charges indicate weak planning.

Ask what the pickup and delivery locations actually require. A business name does not guarantee a dock. A customer may need an appointment even if the address looks commercial. Limited-access and residential definitions vary by carrier. Capture accessorials at quote and tender so service and cost expectations match.

### Keep The Bill Of Lading Clean And Complete

The bill of lading is the shipment's contract and operating document. It should contain shipper, consignee, third-party bill-to, purchase order or reference numbers, piece count, pallet count, weight, class, description, hazmat information, special instructions, declared value if used, appointment notes, and carrier/pro number once assigned. Errors can cause billing disputes, misdelivery, claims problems, or service failure.

Avoid vague descriptions such as "misc goods" when class, liability, or handling matters. Align BOL, labels, ASN, packing list, purchase order, and TMS tender. If the shipment is prepaid, collect, or third-party billed, make that explicit.

### Control Pickup And Delivery Appointments

LTL shipments can miss service because appointment requirements are discovered late. Pickup windows, dock hours, holiday closures, limited staffing, liftgate needs, and consignee delivery appointments should be known before tender. Some carriers will not deliver until the consignee confirms, which adds time.

For time-sensitive shipments, confirm whether the quoted transit is standard, guaranteed, expedited, or appointment-dependent. A standard transit estimate may not include appointment delay. Communicate realistic delivery expectations to customer service and consignees.

### Watch Reweigh, Reclass, And Inspection Patterns

Carrier inspections can reveal inaccurate weights, dimensions, class, packaging, or accessorials. A single correction may be normal; repeated corrections indicate master data, scale, measuring, packaging, or training problems. Reweigh and reclass disputes require evidence: photos, dimensions, certified weights, product specs, BOL, and prior classification support.

Track corrections by SKU, ship site, carrier, customer, and packaging type. Fix upstream data rather than fighting the same dispute repeatedly. If a carrier's correction seems wrong, dispute with documentation, not assumption.

### Match Carrier To Freight And Lane

LTL carriers differ in network, claims performance, service days, regional strength, appointment handling, residential capability, guaranteed service, technology, and pricing. The cheapest carrier may be wrong for fragile, high-value, time-sensitive, or appointment-heavy freight. A premium carrier may be unnecessary for low-risk replenishment.

Use lane performance, damage history, accessorial competence, customer requirements, and service reliability in carrier selection. If the consignee has routing-guide requirements, follow them or obtain approval for deviations.

### Prepare For Claims And Delivery Exceptions

LTL claims require clear piece counts, delivery notation, damage photos, packaging evidence, and timely filing. Concealed damage is harder to prove. Shortage claims depend on accurate count and seal or handling evidence where relevant. If the consignee signs clean, recovery can be weakened.

Tell consignees how to inspect, notate damage, retain packaging, and report exceptions. For high-value or fragile shipments, proactive receiving instructions and photo requirements may prevent claim failure.

## Common Traps

### Estimating Dimensions From The Product Instead Of The Pallet

Freight bills on packaged dimensions and weight, including pallet and overhang.

### Treating "Do Not Stack" As Free Protection

Non-stackability affects cost and carrier handling. It should reflect real packaging constraints.

### Forgetting Destination Accessorials

Liftgate, residential, limited access, appointment, and inside delivery often appear only after delivery if not captured.

### Using Vague BOL Descriptions

Weak descriptions create class, liability, handling, and dispute problems.

### Assuming Standard Transit Means Delivery Appointment Date

Appointment scheduling can add days beyond standard terminal-to-terminal transit.

### Fighting Reclasses Without Evidence

Disputes need measurements, photos, weights, item support, and consistent master data.

### Choosing Lowest Cost Carrier For Fragile Freight

Claims, damage, and customer dissatisfaction can exceed rate savings.

### Failing To Instruct Consignees On Damage Notation

Clean delivery signatures make visible and concealed damage claims harder.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Are freight class, density, NMFC support where applicable, dimensions, and weight verified before tender?
- [ ] Are packaged dimensions measured including pallet, wrap, dunnage, and overhang?
- [ ] Is palletization stable, forkable, wrapped, labeled, stackable where intended, and appropriate for LTL handling?
- [ ] Are pickup and delivery accessorials identified before quote and tender?
- [ ] Is the BOL complete, consistent with labels/ASN/TMS, and clear about billing and special instructions?
- [ ] Are appointment requirements and realistic transit expectations communicated?
- [ ] Are reweigh, reclass, and inspection patterns tracked and corrected upstream?
- [ ] Is carrier choice based on lane, freight risk, claims, service, and consignee requirements, not price alone?
- [ ] Are receiving instructions and claim evidence requirements clear for high-risk shipments?
- [ ] Would the shipment survive both carrier billing audit and terminal handling without avoidable exceptions?
