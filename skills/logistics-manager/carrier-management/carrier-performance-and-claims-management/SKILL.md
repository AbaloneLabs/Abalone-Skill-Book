# Carrier Performance And Claims Management

Carrier management is not complete when a load is delivered. Performance must be measured, claims must be documented, and service failures must feed back into carrier selection and operations. Agents often track on-time delivery but miss tender acceptance, pickup reliability, dwell, communication quality, damage, accessorial behavior, proof of delivery, and claims recovery. Strong carrier management distinguishes isolated exceptions from patterns and preserves evidence while it is still available.

Use this skill when reviewing carrier performance, handling freight claims, or improving transportation accountability.

## Core Rules

### Define Performance Beyond On-Time Delivery

On-time delivery matters, but it is not the only measure. Track on-time pickup, on-time delivery, tender acceptance, falloffs, tracking compliance, communication, claims ratio, damage severity, billing accuracy, accessorial frequency, dwell, appointment compliance, driver professionalism, and recovery quality.

Choose metrics by lane and service. A final-mile carrier may need customer satisfaction and appointment adherence. A truckload carrier may need tender acceptance and dwell. A cold-chain carrier may need temperature compliance. Performance should reflect what the carrier is hired to protect.

### Establish Clear Data Sources

Carrier scorecards fail when parties dispute the data. Define sources for tender, acceptance, pickup, delivery, appointment, GPS, ELD, proof of delivery, temperature, claim, and invoice data. Decide how late, missing, or corrected data is handled.

Use consistent definitions. "On time" may mean arrival, check-in, docked, unloaded, or signed POD. Align definitions before using metrics for penalties or business awards.

### Preserve Claims Evidence Immediately

Freight claims depend on evidence: bill of lading, proof of delivery with notation, photos, delivery receipt, invoice, packing list, inspection report, temperature logs, seal records, weight, salvage value, repair estimate, and communication timeline. If damage or shortage is discovered, document before product is moved, repaired, discarded, or reshipped.

Train receiving teams to note exceptions clearly on delivery documents. A clean POD can weaken a claim even when damage is real. Evidence discipline is part of carrier management.

### Separate Claim Filing From Customer Recovery

The customer may need replacement, credit, or expedited recovery before the carrier claim is resolved. Do not wait for claim payment to fix the customer issue if service obligations require action. But preserve evidence and cost records so recovery can be pursued.

Track claim amount, customer impact, replacement cost, salvage, carrier response, insurer involvement, and internal cost. Claims management should connect customer service, finance, warehouse, and transportation.

### Analyze Root Cause Of Loss And Damage

Damage may be caused by carrier handling, packaging, loading, blocking and bracing, pallet quality, temperature exposure, mode choice, routing, warehouse loading, or customer unloading. Blaming the carrier without root cause can miss packaging or loading fixes. Excusing all damage as packaging can miss poor carrier handling.

Review patterns by carrier, lane, product, packaging, facility, mode, and season. Use findings to improve packaging, carrier selection, loading standards, claims terms, or customer receiving instructions.

### Manage Accessorials And Disputes

Detention, layover, redelivery, reconsignment, liftgate, residential, limited access, storage, lumper, chassis, demurrage, and fuel charges can be legitimate or preventable. Track accessorial causes and approval rules.

Dispute charges with evidence when appropriate, but also fix root causes. Frequent detention may indicate facility scheduling problems; frequent redelivery may indicate customer appointment issues; frequent reweighs may indicate master data errors.

### Use Corrective Action Before Carrier Replacement

Poor carrier performance may require corrective action: lane review, facility dwell reduction, better forecasts, clearer appointment rules, packaging changes, driver instructions, rate adjustment, or escalation to carrier leadership. Replacement may be necessary, but switching carriers without fixing root cause can repeat the problem.

Define corrective actions, owner, date, and expected metric improvement. If performance does not improve, use the evidence to adjust routing guide allocation or remove the carrier.

### Connect Performance To Procurement And Routing

Scorecards should influence carrier awards, routing guide position, volume allocation, rate negotiations, and backup planning. If low-performing carriers keep receiving volume because they are cheap or familiar, the scorecard has no force.

Use performance by lane and service, not only carrier average. A carrier may be strong in one region and weak in another. Allocate intelligently.

## Common Traps

### Measuring Only Delivery Date

Pickup failure, tender rejection, poor communication, damage, and billing errors may harm the operation even if delivery appears on time.

### Using Ambiguous On-Time Definitions

Arrival, check-in, docked, unloaded, and POD time can differ. Define the metric.

### Discovering Damage Without Noting The POD

Failure to note visible damage or shortage can weaken claims. Train receiving teams.

### Throwing Away Evidence

Packaging, photos, seals, temperature logs, and damaged product may be needed for claims.

### Treating Claims As Pure Carrier Fault

Packaging, loading, mode choice, and warehouse practices may contribute. Analyze root cause.

### Paying Accessorials Without Cause Review

Accessorials can reveal process problems. Code and review them.

### Letting Cheap Carriers Keep Failing

Low rates are not savings if claims, penalties, and service failures are high.

### Failing To Close Claims Financially

Claims need status, recovery, write-off, customer credit, and accounting treatment. Do not leave them open indefinitely.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Are carrier metrics defined beyond on-time delivery, including pickup, tender, tracking, claims, communication, dwell, billing, and accessorials?
- [ ] Are data sources and on-time definitions agreed?
- [ ] Is claims evidence preserved immediately with POD notation, photos, BOL, invoices, temperature logs, seals, and inspection records?
- [ ] Is customer recovery handled separately from carrier claim collection?
- [ ] Are damage and loss root causes analyzed by carrier, lane, product, packaging, loading, mode, and facility?
- [ ] Are accessorials coded, approved, disputed when appropriate, and reviewed for root cause?
- [ ] Are corrective actions assigned before or alongside carrier replacement?
- [ ] Does performance affect routing guide position, awards, and volume allocation?
- [ ] Are claims tracked through recovery, denial, write-off, or accounting close?
- [ ] Could the company prove both the service failure and the cost impact?
