# Carrier Scorecards QBRs And Corrective Actions

Carrier scorecards and business reviews should improve transportation performance, not merely display charts. A scorecard without agreed definitions, root-cause discussion, and corrective actions becomes theater. Agents often produce metric summaries that do not change carrier behavior, internal processes, or routing decisions. Strong carrier governance uses evidence to decide where the carrier must improve, where the shipper must improve, and whether volume, rates, or contract terms should change.

Use this skill when creating scorecards, preparing QBRs, reviewing carrier performance, or managing corrective actions.

## Core Rules

### Build Scorecards Around Service Commitments

Metrics should reflect what the carrier is responsible for and what the business needs. Include tender acceptance, on-time pickup, on-time delivery, appointment adherence, tracking compliance, claims, damage ratio, billing accuracy, accessorials, communication, safety, compliance, and recovery performance as relevant.

Avoid generic scorecards that treat all carriers the same. Parcel, LTL, truckload, drayage, intermodal, cold chain, final mile, and air cargo require different metrics. A scorecard should match mode, lane, customer promise, and cargo risk.

### Agree On Definitions Before Reviewing Results

A QBR will fail if the shipper and carrier argue about definitions. Define numerator, denominator, exclusions, time zone, appointment basis, data source, and event timestamp. Decide how force majeure, shipper-caused delay, customer-caused delay, rejected tenders, and missing data are treated.

When definitions are immature, start by improving data quality. Do not use disputed metrics for penalties or awards until definitions are credible.

### Separate Carrier-Caused And Shipper-Caused Issues

Carriers can fail, but shipper behavior also affects performance: late tendering, inaccurate weights, slow loading, appointment changes, poor packaging, wrong contact information, or facility dwell. A good review separates causes fairly.

Use reason codes and exception notes. If a carrier misses pickup because freight was not ready, the corrective action may belong internally. If a carrier rejects loads because rates are below market, procurement may need review. Fair root cause builds trust and improves results.

### Focus QBRs On Decisions

A quarterly business review should not be a slideshow of old metrics. It should answer: What changed? What is failing? What root causes matter? What actions are required? Who owns them? When will improvement be visible? Should volume allocation, routing guide position, rates, or contract terms change?

Limit discussion to material lanes, customers, costs, risks, and trends. Use detailed data as support, not as the meeting's purpose. End with decisions and action owners.

### Create Corrective Actions With Specific Owners

Corrective actions should be concrete: add drop trailers, change appointment windows, retrain drivers, update routing guide, improve EDI events, fix packaging, adjust rates, change tender lead time, add backup carrier, reduce dwell, improve claims documentation, or remove a weak lane from the carrier.

Each action needs owner, due date, expected metric impact, and follow-up cadence. Vague actions such as "improve communication" should be translated into specific behaviors, such as proactive exception alerts within 30 minutes of missed pickup.

### Use Scorecards To Allocate Freight

If performance evidence does not affect freight allocation, carriers will discount it. Strong governance links scorecards to routing guide position, bid awards, lane expansion, probation, corrective action plans, or removal. Rate matters, but poor service has cost.

Use lane-level performance. A carrier may deserve more volume in strong lanes and less in weak lanes. Avoid punishing or rewarding the carrier based only on broad averages.

### Include Cost And Claims In The Review

Service failures often create hidden cost: premium freight, customer penalties, detention, claims, rework, chargebacks, lost sales, and customer service time. Include cost impact where possible. This helps justify paying more for reliable service or changing carriers.

Also review accessorial patterns and claims aging. A carrier with low rates but high damage or billing disputes may be more expensive than it appears.

### Keep Internal Stakeholders In The Loop

Transportation performance affects sales, customer service, warehouse, procurement, finance, and planning. QBR findings should not stay inside transportation. Internal teams need to know if customer promises, facility behavior, packaging, or ordering patterns contribute to carrier failures.

Share decisions and action items with affected teams. Carrier improvement often requires shipper improvement.

## Common Traps

### Producing Scorecards No One Uses

Metrics that do not drive decisions waste effort. Tie scorecards to actions and allocation.

### Arguing Over Data Every Quarter

If definitions and sources are unresolved, fix the data governance. Repeated metric disputes block improvement.

### Blaming Carrier For Shipper-Caused Failures

Late tenders, long dwell, bad data, and poor packaging may be internal issues. Root cause honestly.

### Averaging Away Lane Problems

A carrier average can hide bad performance on critical lanes. Review lane and customer detail.

### Ending QBRs Without Owners

Discussion without action owners and dates does not improve service.

### Ignoring Accessorial And Claims Cost

Rates do not show total carrier cost. Include damage, detention, disputes, and recovery.

### Letting Sales Or Habit Override Evidence

Carrier allocation should reflect performance and business need, not only relationships.

### Reviewing Too Infrequently For Severe Problems

Quarterly cadence may be too slow for active failures. Use weekly corrective-action reviews when needed.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Are scorecard metrics matched to mode, lane, cargo, customer promise, and carrier responsibilities?
- [ ] Are metric definitions, sources, timestamps, exclusions, and reason codes agreed?
- [ ] Are carrier-caused, shipper-caused, customer-caused, and external issues separated?
- [ ] Does the QBR focus on material trends, decisions, and corrective actions rather than only reporting?
- [ ] Are corrective actions specific, owned, dated, and tied to expected metric improvement?
- [ ] Does performance affect routing guide position, bid awards, probation, or volume allocation?
- [ ] Are cost impacts, accessorials, claims, and billing issues included?
- [ ] Are lane-level and customer-level patterns reviewed instead of only averages?
- [ ] Are internal stakeholders informed where their processes affect carrier performance?
- [ ] Is there a follow-up cadence to verify improvement actually occurred?
