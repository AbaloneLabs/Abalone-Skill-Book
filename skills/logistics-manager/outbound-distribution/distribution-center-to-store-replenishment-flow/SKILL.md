# Distribution Center To Store Replenishment Flow

Store replenishment is not simply sending inventory from a DC. It must match store demand, delivery windows, backroom space, labor, planograms, promotions, and transport cadence. Agents often optimize DC outbound volume while missing the receiving and shelf-readiness constraints at stores. This skill helps plan replenishment as an end-to-end store service flow.

## Core Rules

### Tie replenishment to store operating reality

Consider store hours, receiving windows, backroom capacity, labor availability, fixture resets, promotions, seasonal sets, mall or street access, and customer traffic. A delivery that fits DC productivity may overwhelm a store.

Do not evaluate replenishment only by DC ship date. The store must be able to receive, process, and sell the goods.

### Balance frequency and shipment size

More frequent deliveries reduce backroom pressure and stockouts but increase transport cost and receiving workload. Larger drops improve truck utilization but can create congestion, shrink, and shelf delay.

Choose cadence by store volume, distance, labor, route density, product type, and service promise. One cadence rarely fits every store.

### Protect promotion and launch timing

Promotional, seasonal, and planogram goods should arrive early enough for setup but not so early they clutter backrooms or leak before street date. Coordinate allocation, pack labeling, embargoes, and store instructions.

Promotion freight has a different timing profile than everyday replenishment. Treat it separately when needed.

### Confirm shelf-readiness assumptions

Replenishment should account for whether goods arrive floor-ready, priced, tagged, security-labeled, sorted by department, or needing store prep. Store labor and timing change significantly when goods require extra handling.

### Use store-friendly packing and labeling

Cartons, totes, roll cages, pallets, and mixed-SKU packs should be labeled and built for store unloading, sorting, and shelf stocking. Consider department, aisle, temperature, high-value handling, and fragile goods.

A truck that is efficient to load can be inefficient or unsafe for stores to unload. Store labor should be part of pack design.

### Monitor store receiving performance

Track missed deliveries, late arrivals, unloading time, shortages, damages, overages, backroom congestion, refused loads, and claims by store. Use store feedback to fix routes, packing, appointment windows, and allocation.

Store delivery exceptions often reveal upstream DC or planning issues. Do not treat them as local complaints only.

### Coordinate reverse and return flow

Store routes may bring back returns, salvage, recalled goods, empty totes, pallets, hangers, fixtures, and transfer stock. Plan backhaul capacity, segregation, documentation, and destination.

A full outbound truck may leave no room for returns. Reverse flow should be designed with the route.

### Manage allocation and fairness

When inventory is short, define allocation rules by demand, priority stores, promotions, service level, geography, and strategic accounts. Explain shortages and backorder expectations to field teams.

Unclear allocation drives store-level workarounds, over-ordering, and loss of trust in replenishment.

### Give stores an exception path

Stores need a simple way to report missing totes, wrong departments, damaged pallets, late routes, excess inventory, backroom overload, and urgent stockouts. Define who reviews store issues, what evidence is needed, and how quickly corrections happen.

Store feedback should not disappear into informal calls. A structured path helps distinguish one-off receiving issues from systemic routing, allocation, or packing defects.

### Review delivery cost with store impact

Cost per case, truck utilization, route miles, and drop cost should be reviewed alongside on-shelf availability, store labor, shrink, and backroom congestion. The cheapest route plan may not be the best replenishment plan.

Store service outcomes should be part of outbound distribution metrics.

## Common Traps

- Optimizing DC shipping while ignoring store receiving windows and backroom capacity.
- Using one delivery cadence for stores with different volume, distance, labor, and demand.
- Sending promotional goods too early or too late for setup and street-date control.
- Assuming goods are shelf-ready when stores must still price, tag, sort, secure, or prep them.
- Building loads for DC efficiency that make store unloading and stocking inefficient.
- Treating store complaints as anecdotal instead of operational exception data.
- Forgetting totes, pallets, hangers, recalls, salvage, and transfer returns on backhaul.
- Allocating scarce goods informally without demand and promotion rules.
- Letting stores handle shortages, wrong loads, and backroom overload through informal escalation only.
- Measuring only transport cost while ignoring on-shelf availability and store labor.
- Failing to update store instructions when assortment or delivery patterns change.

## Self-Check

- Are store hours, receiving windows, labor, backroom, access, traffic, promotions, and resets considered?
- Is delivery cadence chosen by store volume, route density, distance, labor, product, and service promise?
- Are promotional, seasonal, planogram, and embargoed goods handled with appropriate timing and labeling?
- Are shelf-readiness, pricing, tagging, security labels, department sorting, and store prep needs known?
- Are cartons, totes, pallets, cages, and labels designed for store unloading, sorting, and shelf stocking?
- Are delivery exceptions, unloading time, shortages, damages, refusals, and backroom congestion tracked by store?
- Are returns, recalls, salvage, transfers, empty totes, pallets, hangers, and fixtures planned on backhaul?
- Are allocation rules clear when inventory is short or prioritized?
- Do stores have an exception path for missing, damaged, wrong, late, excess, and urgent replenishment issues?
- Are cost metrics balanced with on-shelf availability, store labor, shrink, and guest impact?
- Can stores receive and sell what the DC ships without hidden workload or clutter?
