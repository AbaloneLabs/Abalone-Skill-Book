# Spot Market And Capacity Procurement

Spot freight is a pressure valve, not a substitute for transportation strategy. It can protect service when routing guide carriers reject loads, demand spikes, lanes are new, or disruption breaks planned capacity. It can also become uncontrolled cost, weak compliance, poor tracking, and fraud exposure if used casually. Agents often treat spot buying as "find a truck" and miss carrier vetting, price reasonableness, service risk, approval rules, and root cause. Strong spot procurement secures capacity quickly while preserving control and learning why spot was needed.

Use this skill when buying spot capacity, using brokers, covering rejected tenders, or handling emergency freight.

## Core Rules

### Classify Why Spot Is Needed

Before buying capacity, identify the reason: routing guide rejection, late order, forecast miss, carrier failure, new lane, customer expedite, supplier delay, weather, project freight, equipment need, or internal planning issue. The reason affects approval, urgency, cost accountability, and root-cause action.

Spot freight should have reason codes. If the same reason repeats, it is no longer an exception; it is a planning, procurement, or operations problem.

### Match Urgency To Service Need

Not every uncovered load requires the most expensive option. Determine delivery requirement, customer consequence, product criticality, appointment window, penalty, margin, and recovery alternatives. A load due tomorrow to a strategic customer may justify premium capacity; low-urgency replenishment may wait.

Use a decision framework that compares cost, service, risk, and recoverability. Avoid panic buying when a short delay is acceptable.

### Preserve Carrier Vetting Under Time Pressure

Spot markets can expose the company to cargo theft, double brokering, uninsured carriers, unsafe operators, and service failures. Vet authority, insurance, cargo coverage, safety, identity, equipment, tracking, and broker legitimacy. Use approved broker or carrier lists where possible.

For high-value, hazmat, food, pharma, or theft-prone freight, spot vetting must be stricter. Speed does not justify bypassing non-negotiable compliance controls.

### Capture Market Price Evidence

Spot rates vary with season, capacity, weather, fuel, lane balance, and lead time. Capture quotes, load boards, broker responses, routing guide rejections, and market context. This supports cost approval and later rate review.

Do not treat every high spot rate as carrier gouging. But do challenge rates that are unsupported by market conditions, vague service, or weak carrier quality.

### Define Approval And Cost Ownership

Spot freight often costs more than contracted routing. Define who can approve spot spend and who owns the cost: transportation, sales, customer service, procurement, warehouse, supplier, or customer. If the cause is internal error, cost ownership should reveal the process problem.

For emergency shipments, approval thresholds may be simplified but still documented. After the fact, reconcile cost and reason.

### Control Communication And Tracking

Spot loads need strong communication because carrier relationships may be unfamiliar. Confirm pickup, driver, equipment, tracking method, appointment, documents, and escalation contacts. For brokers, clarify whether they provide proactive updates and who handles exceptions.

Track the load through delivery and proof of delivery. A covered load is not complete when a broker says a truck is booked.

### Protect Routing Guide Discipline

Spot buying should not undermine contracted carriers without reason. If primary carriers fail, record rejection and use routing guide fallbacks before open spot where appropriate. If spot consistently outperforms contracted rates or service, revisit the procurement strategy.

Avoid allowing individual teams to bypass routing guides for convenience. Spot should be visible and governed.

### Feed Spot Data Back Into Planning

Analyze spot usage by lane, customer, reason, cost premium, lead time, mode, and carrier. Patterns may show under-contracted capacity, unrealistic customer promises, poor forecast, late warehouse release, rate misalignment, or supplier delays.

Use the analysis to adjust routing guides, contracts, customer pricing, order cutoffs, supplier requirements, and capacity planning. Spot spend should generate learning.

## Common Traps

### Treating Spot As Normal Execution

Frequent spot use indicates routing guide, forecast, capacity, or operational issues. Investigate.

### Skipping Carrier Vetting

Urgency increases fraud and theft risk. Non-negotiable vetting still applies.

### Buying Premium Capacity For Low-Impact Loads

Match urgency to customer and business impact. Not every uncovered load deserves high cost.

### Failing To Record Rejections

Without rejection evidence, spot cost cannot be explained or used to improve contracts.

### Letting Brokers Own All Visibility

The shipper still needs tracking, POD, and exception control. Broker updates must be reliable.

### Ignoring Cost Ownership

If spot premiums are absorbed generically, root causes remain hidden.

### Using Unknown Carriers For High-Risk Freight

High-value, regulated, and theft-prone freight require stricter controls.

### Not Reviewing Spot Trends

Spot data is evidence for procurement and planning. Use it.
