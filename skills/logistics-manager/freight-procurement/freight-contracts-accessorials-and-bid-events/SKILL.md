# Freight Contracts Accessorials And Bid Events

Freight bid events and contracts shape transportation behavior for months or years. A bid that focuses only on rates can produce contracts full of hidden accessorials, weak service commitments, short validity, unclear fuel terms, and ambiguous liability. Agents often run bid comparisons as spreadsheets and miss whether the bid package reflects operational reality or whether contract terms will be enforceable during disruption. Strong freight procurement treats the bid as a controlled sourcing process and the contract as an operating agreement.

Use this skill when planning transportation bids, reviewing carrier contracts, or comparing rate proposals.

## Core Rules

### Build A Bid Package From Accurate Operational Data

Carriers price what the bid shows. If lane volumes, weights, equipment, appointment rules, accessorial history, seasonality, pickup windows, or customer constraints are wrong, bids will be inaccurate and later renegotiated through service failures or extra charges.

Prepare lane-level data: origin, destination, mode, volume, shipment count, weight, cube, frequency, equipment, service requirements, pickup and delivery windows, historical accessorials, claims, dwell, and forecast changes. Mark uncertain assumptions. Better data produces better carrier commitments.

### Normalize Bid Responses Before Comparing

Carrier proposals may differ in fuel basis, minimum charges, accessorials, rate validity, service days, equipment assumptions, volume commitments, payment terms, and exclusions. Normalize before ranking.

Create comparison fields for linehaul, fuel, accessorial schedule, transit, tender acceptance commitment, tracking, claims terms, insurance, technology, and exception support. A carrier that appears cheap may be assuming drop trailers are available or excluding common fees.

### Review Accessorials As Core Economics

Accessorials are not minor add-ons. Detention, layover, liftgate, residential, limited access, inside delivery, reconsignment, redelivery, storage, lumper, sorting, pallet exchange, truck ordered not used, stop-off, chassis, demurrage, and oversized fees can dominate cost on certain lanes.

Negotiate definitions, free time, trigger events, documentation, approval requirements, dispute process, and rates. Track which accessorials are controllable by the shipper or customer. Contract clarity reduces invoice disputes.

### Define Fuel And Rate Validity Clearly

Fuel surcharge programs vary by index, peg, mileage, update frequency, and calculation. Rate validity may be annual, seasonal, or subject to market reopeners. Ambiguous fuel or validity terms create surprises.

Document the fuel program, effective dates, rate expiration, general rate increase treatment, market adjustment triggers, and notice requirements. If volumes are volatile, define how both parties revisit rates without destabilizing service.

### Include Service And Data Obligations

Contracts should include operating expectations: tender response time, pickup and delivery windows, tracking events, EDI/API requirements, appointment compliance, proof of delivery, claims response, escalation, safety, cargo security, and performance reviews. If data is required for customer service, billing, or compliance, make it part of the obligation.

Without service terms, rate agreements become hard to enforce when performance degrades.

### Align Liability, Insurance, And Claims Terms

Cargo liability, limitations, exclusions, claims deadlines, salvage, concealed damage, temperature excursions, high-value freight, and insurance requirements should match freight risk. Standard terms may be insufficient for high-value, fragile, regulated, or temperature-sensitive goods.

Route legal review where needed. Procurement should flag operational implications: what loss is covered, what evidence is required, and whether coverage matches shipment value.

### Govern Bid Ethics And Decision Criteria

Bid events should be fair and structured. Define award criteria before responses arrive: cost, service, capacity, diversity, sustainability, technology, risk, incumbency, and strategic fit. Avoid shifting criteria to justify a preferred carrier without evidence.

Keep communication controlled. Carriers should receive the same material information. Document decisions and exceptions. This protects supplier trust and internal credibility.

### Plan Implementation After Award

A bid is not complete when awards are announced. Update routing guides, systems, EDI, rate tables, carrier contacts, insurance, facility instructions, customer notifications, and exception procedures. Pilot lanes where risk is high.

Track early performance after go-live. Many procurement savings fail during implementation because operations is not ready.

## Common Traps

### Running Bids With Bad Data

Inaccurate volumes, weights, dwell, or accessorial history produce unreliable rates and weak commitments.

### Comparing Only Linehaul

Fuel, accessorials, minimums, transit, claims, and acceptance can change the winner.

### Treating Accessorials As Unavoidable

Some accessorials are negotiated; some are process problems. Define and control them.

### Leaving Fuel Terms Ambiguous

Fuel programs can swing cost. Specify index, peg, update cadence, and calculation.

### Forgetting Service Obligations

A rate without tracking, tender, POD, and escalation commitments may not support operations.

### Accepting Standard Liability For High-Risk Freight

High-value or sensitive goods may need special coverage or terms.

### Awarding Without Implementation Planning

Systems, routing guides, contacts, and facilities must be ready before new carriers go live.

### Letting Politics Override Criteria

Undocumented award logic weakens procurement discipline and supplier trust.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is the bid package based on accurate lane, volume, weight, equipment, timing, accessorial, dwell, and forecast data?
- [ ] Are responses normalized for fuel, minimums, accessorials, transit, validity, equipment, and exclusions?
- [ ] Are accessorial definitions, free time, rates, approval, documentation, and dispute process clear?
- [ ] Are fuel surcharge and rate validity terms explicit?
- [ ] Are tender, tracking, POD, claims, escalation, safety, security, and data obligations included?
- [ ] Are liability, insurance, claim deadlines, and exclusions matched to freight risk?
- [ ] Are award criteria defined and documented before final selection?
- [ ] Is carrier communication fair and controlled during the bid?
- [ ] Is implementation planned for routing guides, systems, rates, contacts, and facilities?
- [ ] Are early post-award performance and savings tracked against the bid promise?
