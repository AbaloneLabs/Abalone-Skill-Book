# Fresh Food Shelf Life And Rotation Control

Fresh food logistics is a race against time and quality degradation. The goal is not simply moving product fast; it is allocating remaining shelf life to the right customer, preserving condition, and preventing unsafe or unsellable inventory from entering the channel. Agents often mention FEFO but miss receiving age, customer minimum shelf life, ripeness, temperature history, inspection, shrink, and the tradeoff between service and waste. This skill helps manage freshness as a measurable operating constraint.

## Core Rules

### Manage Remaining Shelf Life, Not Just Expiration Date

Fresh products lose value every day. A product can be legal to ship but commercially unacceptable if it lacks enough remaining life for the customer. Retailers, foodservice customers, and internal policies may require minimum days remaining at receipt. Different channels may accept different remaining shelf life.

Track production date, harvest date, pack date, use-by date, expiration date, and customer minimums. Allocate older product to channels that can use it quickly and protect strategic customers from short-dated surprises. Do not ship product simply because it is not expired.

### Use FEFO With Real-World Exceptions

First-expired-first-out is a strong default, but exceptions may be needed for quality, customer requirements, ripeness, lot holds, promotions, export lead times, or route timing. FEFO should not override a quality hold or a customer minimum shelf-life rule.

Define who can override FEFO and why. Document exceptions. If staff frequently override FEFO, investigate slotting, receiving, forecasting, or system rules.

### Inspect Quality At Receiving And Before Shipment

Fresh food condition can change during inbound transit, storage, and staging. Inspection may include temperature, appearance, odor, damage, ripeness, packaging integrity, date code, pest signs, moisture, and count. Product that passes receiving can still deteriorate before shipping.

Set inspection standards by product category. Produce, seafood, meat, dairy, bakery, and prepared foods have different signals. Train staff to escalate borderline product rather than hiding it in outbound loads.

### Align Temperature And Humidity To Product Needs

Fresh categories need different conditions. Some produce is chilled; some is sensitive to chill injury. Some products produce or are sensitive to ethylene. Meat, seafood, dairy, prepared foods, and frozen products have stricter temperature needs. Mixed storage can damage quality even if nothing is visibly wrong at first.

Confirm storage zones, airflow, humidity, ethylene sensitivity, condensation risk, door-open time, and staging exposure. Do not assume all refrigerated product belongs in the same zone.

### Plan Allocation Around Demand And Shrink

Fresh inventory decisions balance service level and waste. Overbuying improves fill rate but increases shrink. Underbuying reduces waste but creates stockouts. Promotions, weather, holidays, store traffic, and supplier variability can change demand quickly.

Use demand signals, customer priority, remaining shelf life, inbound age, and substitution rules. Track shrink by cause: overforecast, short shelf life, temperature abuse, damage, late inbound, poor rotation, or rejection. Shrink should drive root cause, not only write-off.

### Control Staging And Route Time

Fresh product can lose condition during staging, loading, delivery, and store receiving. Door-open time, unrefrigerated staging, wrong route sequence, missed appointments, driver delays, and store refusal can turn good inventory into waste.

Minimize out-of-zone dwell. Build routes that respect temperature and shelf-life priority. Use insulated equipment, refrigerated vehicles, temperature checks, and receiving appointments where needed. Staging time should be visible and limited.

### Manage Short-Dated Product Deliberately

Short-dated product can be discounted, diverted, donated if safe and allowed, used in prepared foods, sold through outlet channels, or disposed. These decisions require food safety, brand, customer, and financial review. Do not let short-dated product move invisibly into normal orders.

Define short-date thresholds and approved channels. Communicate clearly to customers if they accept short-dated product. Record credits, donations, and disposals.

### Use Customer Rejections As Quality Data

Store and customer rejections may result from true quality issues, receiving standards, temperature, shelf life, packaging, delivery timing, or documentation. They are valuable data when coded well.

Capture reason, photos, temperature, lot, date code, route, driver, and customer. Review patterns by supplier, product, lane, store, and warehouse. Fix root causes rather than treating rejections as random.

## Common Traps

### Shipping Product Because It Is Not Expired

Remaining customer-usable life matters more than legal expiration alone.

### Applying FEFO Blindly

Quality holds, customer minimums, ripeness, and export lead times can override simple FEFO.

### Treating Refrigerated As One Category

Products differ in temperature, humidity, ethylene, and chill sensitivity.

### Letting Fresh Product Sit In Staging

Out-of-zone dwell can destroy quality quickly.

### Hiding Short-Dated Product In Normal Orders

Short-date disposition needs customer agreement and policy control.

### Coding Shrink As Generic Waste

Shrink causes reveal forecasting, supplier, handling, and rotation problems.

### Ignoring Store Receiving Practices

Delays or inconsistent receiving can create rejection and condition problems.

### Missing Photos And Temperature On Rejections

Without evidence, root cause and claims are weak.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Are production/harvest/pack/use-by/expiration dates and customer minimum shelf life tracked?
- [ ] Does FEFO include controlled exceptions for quality, customer, ripeness, holds, and transit needs?
- [ ] Are receiving and pre-shipment inspections category-specific and documented?
- [ ] Are temperature, humidity, airflow, ethylene, condensation, and staging exposure appropriate by product?
- [ ] Are allocation decisions based on demand, customer priority, remaining life, and shrink risk?
- [ ] Are staging dwell and route timing controlled for freshness?
- [ ] Are short-dated products handled through approved channels with customer agreement where needed?
- [ ] Are donation, discount, prepared-food use, disposal, and credit decisions documented?
- [ ] Are customer rejections coded with photos, temperature, lot, date, route, and reason?
- [ ] Would the plan reduce both stockouts and avoidable food waste?
