# Finished Vehicle Yard Transport And Dealer Delivery

Finished vehicle logistics moves high-value units that are visible to customers and dealers. The product is both cargo and a serialized asset with condition, title, accessories, software, battery, keys, and delivery timing requirements. Agents often plan capacity and overlook yard control, damage, vehicle release, accessorization, battery state, dealer appointments, and proof of delivery. This skill helps manage finished vehicle flow from plant or port to dealer handoff.

## Core Rules

### Maintain vehicle identity and release status

Track VIN, model, trim, color, options, destination, hold status, quality status, customs status, financing or title status, campaign holds, and dealer allocation. Do not move or deliver vehicles that are not released for shipment or sale.

Finished vehicles can be physically available but commercially or legally blocked. Release status must be as important as location.

### Control yard location and dwell

Vehicle yards need accurate slotting, scans, keys, battery management, traffic flow, damage zones, inspection lanes, rail loading, truck loading, and security. Dwell should be monitored by VIN, hold reason, destination, and aging.

Lost vehicles, dead batteries, blocked rows, and stale holds create cost and dealer frustration. Yard accuracy is core inventory control.

### Manage damage prevention and inspection

Vehicles can be damaged by loading, rail, hail, salt, debris, vandalism, door dings, wrong tie-downs, low clearance, improper jump starts, or careless yard movement. Define inspection points, photo standards, damage codes, claims process, and repair routing.

Damage discovered at dealer delivery becomes a dispute unless condition was documented at each custody change.

### Match mode and equipment to vehicle profile

Rail, car-hauler, enclosed transport, driveaway, ocean RoRo, and port shuttle each have different cost, capacity, lead time, damage, and visibility profiles. Vehicle size, ground clearance, battery type, high-value status, and distance affect mode fit.

Do not route specialty, oversized, low-clearance, prototype, or high-value vehicles through standard moves without checking equipment suitability.

### Coordinate processing, accessories, and software readiness

Finished vehicles may require port processing, pre-delivery inspection, accessories, software updates, charging, fuel, washing, title documents, emissions paperwork, or campaign repairs before dealer delivery. Sequence these tasks before loading.

Shipping a vehicle before readiness can push work to dealers, delay sale, or create customer dissatisfaction.

### Protect EV and battery requirements

Electric and hybrid vehicles require state-of-charge monitoring, charging capacity, battery safety, jump-start rules, temperature considerations, and specialized handling after long dwell. Yard plans should avoid leaving EVs inaccessible when charging is needed.

Battery failure can turn a logistics delay into a service or warranty issue. Treat battery condition as part of vehicle readiness.

### Align dealer appointments and communication

Dealers need reliable ETAs, delivery appointments, unload space, after-hours rules, contact names, damage reporting, title status, and expected VIN lists. Update dealers when weather, rail delays, holds, or capacity constraints change delivery timing.

Dealer delivery is the customer-facing end of the network. Poor communication creates avoidable escalation even when transport is performing reasonably.

### Control proof of delivery and custody

Capture VIN, time, location, driver, dealer representative, condition, damage notation, keys, documents, photos where required, and exceptions. For missing accessories, low fuel or charge, or damage, record facts immediately.

Proof of delivery must support payment, claims, dealer dispute resolution, and customer service.

### Review flow constraints and aging

Monitor yard aging, railcar availability, truck capacity, port congestion, inspection backlog, campaign holds, accessory bottlenecks, and dealer constraints. Use aging reports to separate transportation problems from release or processing problems.

Finished vehicle dwell is expensive and visible. Root cause should drive action, not generic pressure to "ship more."

## Common Traps

- Moving vehicles based only on physical location while ignoring release, title, quality, customs, or campaign holds.
- Losing VIN location accuracy in large yards and relying on manual searches.
- Treating damage inspection as a dealer problem instead of a custody-control process.
- Using standard transport for low-clearance, oversized, prototype, EV, or high-value vehicles without suitability review.
- Shipping before processing, accessories, software, title, emissions, fuel, charge, or washing is complete.
- Ignoring EV state of charge and charging access during yard dwell.
- Giving dealers vague ETAs without VIN-level status and appointment coordination.
- Capturing proof of delivery without condition, keys, documents, and exception details; blaming transportation for dwell caused by holds, processing, or dealer readiness

## Self-Check

- Are VIN identity, destination, dealer allocation, release status, quality hold, customs, title, and campaign status accurate?
- Is yard slotting, scanning, key control, battery management, traffic flow, and dwell aging controlled?
- Are inspection points, photo standards, damage codes, repair routing, and claims processes defined?
- Is transport mode and equipment appropriate for vehicle size, clearance, value, distance, and EV or specialty needs?
- Are processing, accessories, software, charging, fuel, wash, title, emissions, and campaign tasks complete before shipment?
- Are EV state-of-charge, charging access, battery safety, and long-dwell requirements monitored?
- Are dealer appointments, VIN lists, ETAs, unload rules, after-hours contacts, and delay updates communicated?
- Does proof of delivery capture VIN, time, dealer, condition, keys, documents, photos, and exceptions?
- Are yard aging and flow constraints separated by transport, release, processing, capacity, and dealer causes?
