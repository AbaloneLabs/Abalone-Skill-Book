# Pharma GDP Temperature And Chain Of Custody

Pharmaceutical logistics under good distribution practice is not merely keeping shipments cold. It requires evidence that products stayed within approved conditions, were handled by qualified parties, remained traceable, and were released or quarantined through authorized quality processes. Agents often recommend insulated packaging or refrigerated trucks without considering lane qualification, packout duration, calibrated loggers, excursion review, tamper evidence, and GDP records. This skill helps plan pharma distribution with defensible quality control.

## Core Rules

### Start With Approved Product Conditions

Each product has approved storage and transport conditions: refrigerated, frozen, controlled room temperature, protect from light, do not freeze, humidity limits, shock sensitivity, or controlled substance security. These conditions may differ by market, label, quality agreement, and product lifecycle stage.

Do not infer requirements from product category. Use approved quality documents, product label, stability data, SOPs, or quality team direction. If conditions are unknown, hold planning decisions until qualified information is available.

### Use Qualified Lanes, Packaging, And Providers

Pharma lanes should be designed around expected ambient profiles, transit duration, handling points, customs, weekends, holidays, and provider capabilities. Packaging may need qualification for season, lane, duration, payload, and orientation. Carriers, 3PLs, and depots should be qualified for GDP expectations.

Confirm lane qualification, packaging qualification, courier training, temperature-controlled facilities, contingency plans, and quality agreements. A generic cold pack or reefer truck is not proof of GDP suitability.

### Monitor Temperature With Usable Evidence

Temperature monitoring may include calibrated data loggers, real-time monitors, trailer records, facility records, and receiving checks. Evidence must be tied to shipment ID, product, lot, time, route, and release decision. Logger placement, calibration, start/stop process, and data retrieval matter.

Define who places loggers, who checks them, what thresholds trigger alerts, who reviews excursions, and where records are stored. Do not treat a green indicator as sufficient if SOP requires full data review.

### Protect Chain Of Custody And Tamper Evidence

Pharma shipments may require seals, restricted access, signature capture, ID checks, controlled-substance procedures, secure storage, GPS, or two-person handling. Chain of custody should show who controlled the product at each handoff.

Match controls to product risk: controlled substances, high-value biologics, specialty medicines, clinical product, samples, and recalled goods may need stronger custody. Carriers and warehouses must know seal and exception rules.

### Handle Temperature Excursions Through Quality Review

Temperature excursions are quality events. Operations should not decide release based only on urgency or visual inspection. Excursion evaluation may require stability data, duration, maximum/minimum, product history, cumulative exposure, and quality approval.

Quarantine affected product physically and systemically until authorized release. Preserve logger data and route history. Communicate with customers carefully, avoiding unsupported statements that product is safe or unsafe before review.

### Plan Customs And Border Time Into Temperature Control

International pharma shipments can be delayed by customs, inspections, missing documents, import permits, broker issues, or airport storage. Temperature protection must cover these risks. Customs holds can also break custody or expose product to uncontrolled areas.

Pre-alert brokers, confirm permits, use correct product descriptions, and choose routes with qualified temperature storage. Build contingency for dry ice replenishment or active container power where needed.

### Maintain GDP Records And Training Evidence

GDP compliance depends on SOPs, training, quality agreements, deviation records, calibration, cleaning, maintenance, temperature maps, shipment records, and corrective actions. A successful delivery without records is weak evidence.

Ensure teams know which records to keep and for how long according to policy and law. Avoid scattered records. Use controlled systems and audit trails where available.

### Coordinate Customer Receiving And Release

The receiving site must know how to inspect seals, retrieve loggers, check temperature, quarantine product, report excursions, and provide delivery confirmation. If customers mishandle product after delivery, disputes and patient risk can follow.

Provide receiving instructions and escalation contacts. For specialty pharmacy, hospital, clinic, or distributor delivery, align appointment, storage, and staff availability. Do not deliver temperature-sensitive product to an unattended dock unless procedure supports it.

## Common Traps

### Assuming Cold Chain Means Refrigerated Only

Some medicines must not freeze; others need controlled room temperature or light protection.

### Using Unqualified Packaging For A New Lane

Season, duration, payload, and route can invalidate assumptions.

### Treating Logger Alerts As Operational Annoyances

Excursions require quality review and documented disposition.

### Forgetting Customs Dwell

Border delays can exceed packout or expose product to uncontrolled areas.

### Delivering Without Receiving Readiness

Temperature-sensitive product needs trained receiving and storage at destination.

### Breaking Seal Rules For Convenience

Seal exceptions must be documented and escalated.

### Keeping GDP Evidence In Emails

Records need controlled retention and auditability.

### Letting Cost Override Product Integrity

Cheaper service is not acceptable if it cannot meet approved conditions.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Are approved product storage and transport conditions verified from qualified sources?
- [ ] Are lane, packaging, season, duration, payload, and provider capabilities qualified for GDP needs?
- [ ] Are calibrated loggers or monitoring records tied to shipment, lot, route, and release decision?
- [ ] Are chain-of-custody, seals, signatures, secure storage, and access controls appropriate to product risk?
- [ ] Are excursions quarantined and reviewed by authorized quality personnel before release?
- [ ] Are customs, broker, permit, border storage, and replenishment risks included in temperature planning?
- [ ] Are GDP records, SOPs, training, quality agreements, calibration, and deviations retained correctly?
- [ ] Are customer receiving instructions, logger retrieval, seal checks, and storage readiness confirmed?
- [ ] Are current regulatory or quality requirements escalated rather than guessed?
- [ ] Would the shipment record support audit, deviation review, or patient safety investigation?
