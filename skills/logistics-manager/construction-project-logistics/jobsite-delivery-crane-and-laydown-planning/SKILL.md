# Jobsite Delivery Crane And Laydown Planning

Construction logistics is constrained by site geometry, trade sequencing, safety, weather, permits, and the build schedule. Material that arrives on time can still fail if the gate is blocked, the crane is unavailable, the laydown area is full, or the trade is not ready. Agents often treat jobsite delivery as a carrier appointment and miss the choreography between delivery, lifting, staging, and installation. This skill helps plan materials around build flow rather than shipment completion.

## Core Rules

### Tie deliveries to workface readiness

Confirm which trade needs the material, where it will be installed, what predecessor work must be complete, and whether drawings, approvals, inspections, and labor are ready. Delivery should support installation, not create site clutter.

Do not ship early just because material is available. Early delivery can damage materials, consume laydown, block access, and shift storage risk to the project.

### Map site access and traffic control

Identify gates, truck routes, road restrictions, turning radius, staging lanes, flagging needs, pedestrian separation, school or neighborhood limits, delivery curfews, and emergency access. Construction sites change as the project progresses, so access plans must be updated.

A route that worked last month may be blocked by excavation, scaffolding, concrete pours, or utility work. Use current site conditions.

### Reserve crane, hoist, and lift resources

Large or elevated materials may require tower crane, mobile crane, forklift, telehandler, hoist, elevator, or rigging crew. Confirm lift plan, capacity, reach, ground bearing, rigging, weather limits, pick sequence, signal person, and exclusion zone.

A truck should not arrive before lift resources and safety controls are ready. Waiting equipment is costly and unsafe improvisation is worse.

### Plan laydown and staging by sequence

Laydown space should be sized and assigned by material type, trade, installation sequence, weather sensitivity, security, and handling equipment. Mark zones, access lanes, fire lanes, and no-stack areas. Decide what can be staged floor-by-floor and what must remain offsite.

Uncontrolled laydown turns into a secondary warehouse with poor inventory accuracy and high damage risk.

### Protect material condition and accountability

Materials may be damaged by weather, mud, dust, theft, impact, moisture, UV exposure, or poor stacking. Confirm packaging, covers, dunnage, cribbing, secure storage, receiving inspection, photos, and custody transfer.

Once material is dropped onsite, responsibility can become unclear. Document condition and handoff immediately.

### Coordinate trade and subcontractor deliveries

Multiple trades may compete for gates, cranes, hoists, docks, elevators, and laydown. Use a delivery calendar that includes concrete pours, steel, MEP racks, facade panels, elevators, interiors, waste removal, and public road closures.

The logistics plan should reduce trade conflict, not merely record requested deliveries.

### Control just-in-time versus buffer tradeoffs

Just-in-time delivery reduces site congestion but increases schedule risk if suppliers, permits, weather, or traffic fail. Buffer inventory protects work continuity but consumes scarce space. Choose deliberately by criticality, lead time, damage risk, and site capacity.

Do not apply one policy to every material. Structural steel, tile, switchgear, drywall, and glass have different risk profiles.

### Prepare for missed windows

Define what happens if a truck is late, crane time is lost, weather stops lifting, inspection fails, or the trade is not ready. Options include offsite storage, reschedule, demurrage approval, alternate gate, short-term staging, or return to vendor.

Missed windows should have a decision owner before the driver calls from the gate.

### Communicate daily site logistics

Publish delivery windows, gate assignments, lift times, laydown changes, restricted zones, and safety reminders to suppliers, carriers, subcontractors, superintendent, safety, and security. Keep one source of truth for the day.

Construction sites run on current information. Old instructions create blocked roads, idle crews, and safety incidents.

## Common Traps

- Delivering material because it is ready, not because the workface is ready.
- Using outdated site access routes after the site layout has changed.
- Scheduling trucks without crane, hoist, rigging, flagging, or exclusion-zone readiness.
- Treating laydown space as infinite or first-come-first-served.
- Allowing materials to sit exposed to weather, theft, impact, mud, or UV damage.
- Failing to document condition and custody at jobsite receipt.
- Letting subcontractors compete for gates, cranes, and hoists without a shared calendar; overusing just-in-time without realistic supplier, permit, traffic, and weather risk
- Having no plan for missed lift windows, late trucks, or unready trades; communicating site changes through scattered calls instead of a daily logistics plan

## Self-Check

- Is each delivery tied to workface readiness, trade labor, approvals, inspections, and installation sequence?
- Are gates, truck routes, turning radius, staging lanes, curfews, flagging, pedestrians, and emergency access current?
- Are crane, hoist, forklift, rigging, capacity, ground bearing, weather limits, and exclusion zones ready?
- Is laydown assigned by trade, material, sequence, handling, security, weather, and access needs?
- Are material protection, receiving inspection, photos, dunnage, covers, secure storage, and custody handoff defined?
- Are subcontractor deliveries coordinated against concrete pours, steel, MEP, facade, interiors, waste, and road closures?
- Are just-in-time and buffer decisions based on criticality, lead time, damage risk, and site capacity?
- Is there an approved response for late trucks, lost crane time, weather holds, failed inspections, and unready trades?
- Is the daily logistics plan shared with suppliers, carriers, subcontractors, safety, security, and site leadership?
