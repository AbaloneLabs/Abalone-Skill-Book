# Air Cargo Service Level And Capacity Selection

Air cargo is often chosen under pressure, but speed is not a single product. Express parcel, deferred air freight, airport-to-airport cargo, door-to-door forwarding, passenger belly capacity, freighter service, charter, onboard courier, and consolidated air freight differ in cutoff times, recovery options, handling, customs flow, cost, and reliability. Agents often say "ship by air" without specifying the service needed to protect the business outcome. This skill helps the agent choose air cargo service level and capacity with the full chain in view.

## Core Rules

### Define The Business Deadline, Not Just Desired Speed

Air freight should be tied to a business need: line-down prevention, product launch, medical delivery, trade show, customer promise, inventory recovery, warranty replacement, sample approval, or contract penalty avoidance. A shipment may need arrival by airport, delivery to consignee, inventory release, installation, or customer handoff. These are different deadlines.

Ask what must happen by when and what failure costs. If cargo needs to be on a production line at 8 a.m., airport arrival at midnight is not enough without customs, transfer, and receiving capability. If the deadline is flexible, deferred air or consolidation may be more appropriate than premium service.

### Compare Service Levels Across The Whole Route

Air cargo service includes origin pickup, export handling, screening, tender cutoff, flight, transshipment, import customs, terminal recovery, destination delivery, and proof of delivery. The fastest flight is not the fastest door-to-door route if airport handling, customs, or local delivery is weak.

Compare door-to-door express, airport-to-airport, airport-to-door, door-to-airport, deferred air, priority air, next-flight-out, onboard courier, and charter by total elapsed time, cutoff feasibility, documentation, tracking, customs, and recovery options. Avoid choosing by flight duration alone.

### Check Capacity, Dimensions, And Aircraft Constraints

Air capacity is constrained by weight, dimensions, chargeable weight, aircraft type, door size, ULD compatibility, pallet build, passenger belly restrictions, freighter availability, hazardous goods, temperature needs, lithium batteries, live animals, perishables, and security screening. Oversized cargo may not fit passenger aircraft even if weight is acceptable.

Measure actual packed dimensions and weight. Confirm piece count, longest dimension, stackability, special handling, center of gravity if relevant, and whether cargo can be screened. Air freight quotes based on estimates can fail at tender or produce major cost changes.

### Understand Chargeable Weight And Cost Escalation

Air freight usually bills by chargeable weight, based on actual or dimensional weight. Volumetric cargo can be expensive even when physically light. Minimum charges, fuel, security, terminal handling, pickup, delivery, customs, storage, airline fees, and priority surcharges can change the real cost.

Estimate landed air cost before promising a shipment. For recurring air use, identify whether packaging or consolidation can reduce chargeable weight. For emergency shipments, still capture cost approval authority and business justification.

### Match Cargo Risk To Handling Model

Air freight can reduce transit time but still involves handling at warehouses, airports, screening facilities, aircraft loading, transshipment hubs, and delivery terminals. Fragile, high-value, temperature-sensitive, regulated, perishable, or theft-prone cargo needs handling controls. Passenger belly and consolidated freight may add more touches; direct freighter or onboard courier may reduce some risks.

Define packaging, tamper seals, shock or temperature monitors, chain of custody, insurance, priority handling, and escalation contacts where needed. Do not assume air automatically means safer.

### Coordinate Customs And Documentation Before Tender

Air freight moves quickly, so paperwork errors become immediate delays. Commercial invoices, packing lists, airwaybill data, export declarations, licenses, certificates, HS classification, country of origin, valuation, consignee tax IDs, power of attorney, and broker instructions must be ready. Dangerous goods, lithium batteries, medical products, food, chemicals, and controlled goods require extra care.

Pre-alert the broker and consignee before departure. For international air, customs readiness may be more important than flight selection. A shipment that flies overnight and waits three days for import data is not expedited.

### Account For Airport And Local Delivery Constraints

Air cargo terminals have recovery hours, storage rules, security processes, truck wait times, and documentation requirements. Destination delivery may depend on local courier capacity, appointment windows, bonded transfer, cold-chain vehicles, liftgate, or consignee receiving hours. Airport-to-airport service can leave the hardest part unsolved.

Plan who recovers cargo, when it is available, what documents are needed, how storage is avoided, and how delivery reaches the final point. If the consignee cannot receive after hours, premium air may not create real value.

### Use Air Freight As A Signal For Root Cause

Emergency air shipments often indicate forecasting, supplier reliability, production planning, quality holds, inventory policy, customs delays, or customer promise problems. Air may be necessary, but recurring air freight should trigger root cause review.

Track air shipments by reason code, business owner, cost, margin impact, lane, supplier, SKU, and avoidability. This prevents "expedite" from becoming a hidden operating model.

## Common Traps

### Treating Airport Arrival As Final Delivery

Cargo still needs recovery, customs, local transport, receiving, and possibly inventory release.

### Choosing The Fastest Flight Without Cutoff Review

Tender, screening, export, and delivery cutoffs may make a nominally fast service impossible.

### Underestimating Dimensional Weight

Bulky lightweight cargo can cost far more than expected.

### Ignoring Passenger Belly Limits

Dimensions, hazmat, batteries, perishables, and security rules may require freighter or special service.

### Assuming Air Means Low Damage Risk

Air cargo still has screening, terminal handling, transfers, and delivery touches.

### Flying Before Customs Data Is Ready

The shipment may arrive quickly and then sit under hold.

### Forgetting Destination Recovery Hours

Airport terminal availability and consignee receiving hours can erase speed benefits.

### Normalizing Expedites

Repeated air freight should expose planning or supply problems, not disappear into freight spend.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is the service tied to the actual business deadline and failure cost?
- [ ] Are pickup, cutoff, screening, flight, transfer, customs, terminal recovery, and final delivery compared?
- [ ] Are dimensions, weight, chargeable weight, piece count, aircraft fit, and ULD or door constraints verified?
- [ ] Are full costs including fuel, security, terminal, pickup, delivery, customs, storage, and priority fees considered?
- [ ] Is the handling model appropriate for fragile, high-value, temperature-sensitive, regulated, or theft-prone cargo?
- [ ] Are export and import documents, broker data, consignee IDs, licenses, and certificates ready before tender?
- [ ] Are destination airport recovery, storage, local delivery, and receiving hours planned?
- [ ] Are cost approval, business owner, and reason code captured for emergency shipments?
- [ ] Does the plan distinguish airport arrival, delivery, and usable inventory availability?
- [ ] Would recurring use of air freight trigger root cause review?
