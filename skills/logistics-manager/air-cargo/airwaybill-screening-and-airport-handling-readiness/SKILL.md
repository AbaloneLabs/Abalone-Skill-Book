# Airwaybill Screening And Airport Handling Readiness

Air cargo can move quickly only when the handoff is clean. The air waybill, security screening, cargo labels, export documents, dangerous goods data, terminal acceptance, and airport handling instructions must align before cargo reaches the airline or forwarder. Agents often focus on booking a flight and miss why cargo is rejected, delayed, reworked, or stored at the terminal. This skill helps the agent prepare air freight so it can be accepted and handled without avoidable delay.

## Core Rules

### Treat The Air Waybill As Both Contract And Operating Data

The air waybill carries shipper, consignee, routing, pieces, weight, dimensions, commodity description, chargeable weight, special handling, declared value if used, payment terms, and handling instructions. Inaccurate AWB data can cause acceptance problems, customs delays, billing disputes, misrouting, or delivery failure.

Check AWB data against commercial invoice, packing list, export filing, booking confirmation, labels, and broker instructions. Avoid vague commodity descriptions when screening, customs, or airline acceptance depends on specificity. If house and master airwaybills are used, ensure references align.

### Prepare For Security Screening Before Tender

Air cargo security screening can include x-ray, physical inspection, canine, explosive trace detection, known shipper procedures, secure chain of custody, and regulated agent requirements. Dense, oversized, foil-wrapped, crated, liquid, powder, machinery, or mixed cargo may be hard to screen. Unscreeenable cargo can miss flights or require alternate handling.

Ask whether cargo is known shipper eligible, screenable in its packed state, and tendered with enough time. Packaging choices can affect screening. If a crate cannot be opened or x-rayed, coordinate screening method before arrival at the terminal. Do not assume security screening is automatic.

### Confirm Airline Acceptance Requirements

Airlines and handlers can reject cargo for wrong labels, bad packaging, missing documents, unsafe condition, leaking, odor, incorrect dangerous goods paperwork, battery issues, weight discrepancies, dimensional mismatch, late tender, or embargoed commodity. Passenger aircraft have additional restrictions compared with freighters.

Before tender, confirm commodity restrictions, cutoff time, piece limits, weight limits, dimension limits, dangerous goods acceptance, temperature handling, live animal or perishables rules, and special cargo booking approval. For regulated or unusual cargo, get acceptance confirmation in writing where practical.

### Label And Mark Cargo For Airport Flow

Air cargo labels and marks should support identification, routing, handling, and customs. Labels need to survive handling and match AWB, booking, piece count, and consignee information. Special handling labels may include orientation, temperature, fragile, keep dry, stack limits, dangerous goods, lithium battery marks, or security seals.

Place labels where handlers can see them. For multi-piece shipments, each piece needs clear identification. If cargo is consolidated, palletized, or ULD-built, piece-level identity should not disappear. Poor labeling can lead to missed pieces or misrouting.

### Handle Dangerous Goods And Batteries Conservatively

Dangerous goods, lithium batteries, chemicals, aerosols, dry ice, biological substances, magnetized materials, and regulated equipment require classification, packaging, marks, labels, documentation, trained personnel, and carrier acceptance. Mistakes can create safety risk and rejected cargo. Rules are detailed and change over time.

Do not guess dangerous goods status. If there is any possibility, require qualified DG review and current regulations. Consumer electronics, samples, medical kits, cosmetics, and repair parts may still contain regulated items. A casual "non-haz" statement is not enough when batteries or chemicals are present.

### Protect Temperature, Time, And Special Handling Instructions

Perishables, pharma, biologics, seafood, flowers, art, live animals, and other special cargo need clear handling plans: temperature range, packaging validation, dry ice or gel packs, reefer storage, priority loading, tarmac exposure limits, monitoring devices, and consignee readiness. Airport terminals may have limited cold room capacity or cutoffs.

Confirm special handling codes, temperature facilities, transfer exposure, and recovery process. A shipment can fly fast and still fail if it sits on a hot dock or misses cold storage at origin or destination.

### Build Airport Handoff Time Into The Plan

Tendering cargo at the airport is not instantaneous. There may be truck queues, document checks, security, screening, breakdown, weight verification, acceptance, and handler processing. Late tender can miss cutoff even if the truck arrives before flight time.

Plan origin pickup with enough margin for traffic, airport congestion, screening, and rework. For high-value or critical shipments, monitor tender acceptance, not just pickup. Confirm cargo is accepted and manifested before assuming it will fly.

### Pre-Alert Destination Parties

The destination broker, consignee, forwarder, and recovery agent need AWB, invoice, packing list, arrival details, special handling notes, permits, and delivery instructions before arrival. Air cargo arrives quickly; missing pre-alerts create storage and customs delays.

Send complete pre-alerts with AWB numbers, pieces, weight, flight, ETA, documents, contact, temperature or DG requirements, and delivery priority. Confirm who will recover cargo and what hours they operate.

## Common Traps

### Treating AWB Creation As Clerical

AWB errors affect airline acceptance, customs, billing, and delivery.

### Discovering Screening Problems At The Airport

Dense, oversized, crated, liquid, or mixed cargo may require planned screening method.

### Assuming Cargo Can Fly On Any Aircraft

Passenger, freighter, dimension, dangerous goods, and commodity restrictions matter.

### Using Vague Commodity Descriptions

Screening and customs may require precise descriptions and supporting documents.

### Missing Piece-Level Labels

Multi-piece shipments can split or misroute if labels do not survive handling.

### Guessing Battery Or Dangerous Goods Status

DG mistakes create safety, legal, and acceptance risk. Qualified review is required.

### Ignoring Terminal Tender Time

Arrival at airport is not the same as accepted by airline before cutoff.

### Failing To Pre-Alert Destination

Fast air transit is wasted when broker or consignee is not ready.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Does AWB data match invoice, packing list, booking, labels, export filing, and broker instructions?
- [ ] Is cargo screenable as packed, with known shipper or security requirements addressed?
- [ ] Are airline acceptance rules for commodity, dimensions, weight, cutoff, aircraft type, and special handling confirmed?
- [ ] Are all pieces labeled and marked for routing, handling, special instructions, and regulatory requirements?
- [ ] Are dangerous goods, lithium batteries, dry ice, chemicals, and regulated items reviewed by qualified sources?
- [ ] Are temperature, time-critical, live, perishable, pharma, or high-value handling needs clearly coded and planned?
- [ ] Is airport tender time enough for queues, screening, acceptance, and rework before cutoff?
- [ ] Is cargo acceptance and manifest status tracked after pickup?
- [ ] Are destination broker, recovery agent, and consignee pre-alerted with complete documents and instructions?
- [ ] Would the shipment be accepted by the handler without preventable rework or rejection?
