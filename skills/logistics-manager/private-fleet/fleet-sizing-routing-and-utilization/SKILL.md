# Fleet Sizing Routing And Utilization

Private fleet decisions trade control, service, cost, flexibility, and asset risk. Too little fleet capacity creates service failures and expensive backup carriers; too much creates idle capital and fixed cost. Agents often calculate fleet size from average volume and miss peak demand, route density, driver constraints, equipment mix, maintenance downtime, and customer delivery windows. This skill helps size and route fleets around real operating variability.

## Core Rules

### Define why the fleet exists

Clarify whether the private fleet exists for service control, specialized handling, brand experience, cost advantage, driver expertise, route density, customer access, emergency response, or lack of carrier market capacity. The purpose guides sizing and outsourcing decisions.

Do not keep a private fleet only because it has always existed. Its value should be explicit.

### Size to demand variability, not average demand

Analyze daily, weekly, seasonal, and peak volume by route, region, equipment type, customer, and service window. Include promotions, weather, holidays, production swings, and returns. Decide what peak percentile the fleet should cover.

Average demand is a weak sizing basis. Peaks determine whether the fleet needs overflow capacity, rentals, or carrier backup.

### Match equipment mix to freight profile

Vehicle types should match weight, cube, temperature, liftgate, route length, road access, delivery density, parking, driver license, and customer unloading. A fleet of the wrong equipment creates low utilization and service constraints.

Equipment mix matters as much as asset count. The wrong truck in the right location may still be unusable.

### Design routes for density and service

Routes should balance stop density, drive time, dwell time, customer windows, delivery sequence, driver hours, backhaul, vehicle capacity, and return-to-base timing. Route density drives fleet economics.

Do not chase utilization by adding stops that break service windows or driver feasibility. Utilization without service is false efficiency.

### Include maintenance and spare capacity

Fleet sizing must include preventive maintenance, breakdowns, inspections, repairs, seasonal prep, and spare vehicles. The right spare ratio depends on asset reliability, maintenance support, and service criticality.

If every vehicle is needed every day, the plan has no resilience. Downtime is inevitable.

### Decide owned fleet versus outsourced support

Use private fleet for lanes where control, density, service, or specialization justifies fixed cost. Use carriers, brokers, rentals, or dedicated providers for overflow, distant lanes, irregular demand, or low-density markets.

The decision should be lane-specific, not ideological. Hybrid models often work best.

### Review route economics by lane and stop

Analyze cost and service by lane, route, stop type, customer, backhaul, dwell, empty miles, and equipment. Some routes look efficient in aggregate while specific stops consume disproportionate time or require policy changes.

Route economics should inform pricing, delivery frequency, minimum order rules, customer conversations, and whether private fleet remains the right option.

### Measure utilization correctly

Track miles, hours, stops, cube, weight, route completion, driver time, empty miles, backhaul, idle time, revenue or service supported, and cost per relevant unit. Avoid using a single utilization metric.

High mileage can hide empty running; high asset utilization can hide driver overtime and poor service.

### Review network changes regularly

Customer locations, demand, service windows, facility nodes, fuel cost, labor market, regulations, and carrier markets change. Revisit fleet size and routes when network assumptions shift.

Fleet size should evolve with the network. Fixed assets should not force outdated routing.

## Common Traps

- Sizing fleet to average volume instead of peak variability and service windows.
- Keeping a private fleet without a clear value proposition versus outsourced options.
- Buying vehicles that do not match freight cube, weight, temperature, access, or unloading needs.
- Optimizing utilization while violating delivery windows, driver hours, or customer requirements.
- Forgetting maintenance downtime and spare ratio in asset counts.
- Using private fleet for low-density irregular lanes where carriers are better suited.
- Hiding unprofitable routes or stops inside average fleet utilization metrics.
- Measuring utilization by miles alone while ignoring empty miles, dwell, stops, and cube.
- Letting owned assets drive route decisions after the network changes.
- Ignoring backhaul potential and return-to-base timing.

## Self-Check

- Is the private fleet purpose clear for service, cost, control, specialization, brand, density, or market capacity?
- Is fleet size based on demand variability, peak percentile, seasonality, customer windows, and route-level volume?
- Does equipment mix fit weight, cube, temperature, liftgate, access, distance, parking, license, and unloading needs?
- Are routes designed for density, service windows, drive time, dwell, capacity, driver hours, backhaul, and return timing?
- Are maintenance, inspections, breakdowns, repairs, seasonal prep, and spare ratio included?
- Are owned fleet, carrier, broker, rental, dedicated, and overflow options chosen by lane and demand pattern?
- Are route and stop economics reviewed by cost, dwell, empty miles, backhaul, customer, and service outcome?
- Are utilization metrics balanced across miles, hours, stops, cube, weight, empty miles, idle, and service outcome?
- Are route and fleet assumptions reviewed when customers, nodes, demand, labor, regulations, fuel, or carrier markets change?
- Can the fleet absorb normal variability without excessive idle assets or chronic overflow?
