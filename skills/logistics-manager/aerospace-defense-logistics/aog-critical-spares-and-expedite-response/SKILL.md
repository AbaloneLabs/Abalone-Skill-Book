# AOG Critical Spares And Expedite Response

AOG and critical aerospace spares logistics is a race against grounded aircraft, mission deadlines, passenger disruption, contract penalties, and safety constraints. But speed cannot override documentation, export controls, part identity, quality release, or installation readiness. The agent should coordinate urgency through a disciplined control process: confirm the true need, verify the part and paperwork, choose a recoverable route, maintain custody, and communicate realistic ETAs.

## Core Rules

### Confirm the operational need and deadline

Start with aircraft tail or platform, location, part number, serial need, alternate part numbers, effectivity, required condition, quantity, maintenance window, latest acceptable delivery, installation crew readiness, and whether the aircraft is truly AOG or a lower-priority expedite. Misclassifying priority wastes scarce capacity and can deprive another aircraft or mission of a critical part.

Ask what happens if the part arrives at each possible time. A next-flight-out shipment may be unnecessary if maintenance cannot install until morning, customs is closed, or the technician lacks required tooling. The logistics deadline should match the operational recovery plan.

### Verify part and paperwork before dispatch

AOG pressure creates wrong-part risk. Confirm part number, dash number, revision, serial, interchangeability, certificate, airworthiness release, shelf life, hazardous status, battery status, export control, and customer approval for alternate or repaired parts. A fast wrong part is worse than a slower correct one.

Do not ship a part if required documents are missing unless authorized quality and customer decision makers accept the consequence. If documents travel separately, track them with the same urgency as the part.

### Choose expedite mode by recoverability

Options include next-flight-out, onboard courier, hand-carry, charter, dedicated truck, air cargo, counter-to-counter, service parts network, cannibalization transfer, or local purchase. Choose based on cutoff times, customs, security screening, dimensions, dangerous goods, weather, airline reliability, destination airport hours, and last-mile transfer.

The fastest advertised option may not be most reliable. A direct drive may beat a flight with late cutoff and customs risk. A hand-carry may fail if export paperwork or airline security rejects the item. Build a primary and backup route for high-consequence moves.

### Maintain control tower communication

AOG response needs one source of truth for order status, quality release, pickup, flight, customs, arrival, handoff, and delivery. Provide timestamps, timezone, carrier references, flight numbers, courier contacts, customs broker, consignee contact, and next update time. Avoid vague "in transit" updates.

Communicate risk early: missing documents, customs hold, offload, missed connection, weather, courier delay, wrong address, or receiver unavailability. Maintenance teams need time to adjust labor, gates, passengers, or mission plans.

### Prepare customs and receiving before arrival

International AOG shipments often fail at customs, broker handoff, airport cargo release, security, or after-hours delivery. Confirm commodity description, value, HTS or tariff data where needed, export license, import permits, broker readiness, tax or duty handling, temporary import or repair return status, and recipient authority.

At destination, confirm who can receive, inspect, sign, transport airside or landside, and deliver to maintenance. If the part needs cold chain, ESD, FOD control, or secure custody, ensure the last mile can maintain it.

### Control hand-carry and onboard courier risk

Hand-carry can be powerful but fragile. Confirm the courier's passport, visa, security clearance if needed, airline acceptance, baggage screening, export documents, customs declaration, lithium battery or dangerous goods rules, and whether the item can remain in cabin custody. A courier with the part but no authority to cross border control does not solve AOG.

If the item is too sensitive for ordinary baggage handling, specify custody rules: never checked, no hotel storage, no unattended vehicle, tamper seal checks, and direct handoff to authorized receiver. If the item must be checked or cargo-screened, decide whether hand-carry still adds value.

### Review the expedite after closure

After the aircraft or mission recovers, review whether the expedite was preventable through stocking, forecasting, repair turnaround, supplier readiness, or documentation quality. AOG patterns should improve spares strategy, not only create heroic shipments.

## Common Traps

- Treating every urgent request as true AOG priority.
- Shipping before confirming effectivity, revision, serial, certificates, shelf life, and quality release.
- Choosing the fastest-looking mode without customs, cutoff, dimensions, dangerous goods, or airport hour checks.
- Sending status updates without timestamps, timezone, flight, courier, and next action.
- Forgetting that documents can ground the part even when the physical item arrives.
- Assuming customs or cargo terminals operate when the flight lands.
- Delivering to a receiver who lacks authority or access to move the part to maintenance; letting urgency bypass export controls or dangerous goods rules
- Assigning a hand-carry courier without verifying visas, customs, cabin custody, screening, and dangerous goods restrictions; treating AOG recovery as complete without reviewing why the expedite was needed

## Self-Check

- Have I confirmed aircraft or platform, location, operational deadline, required condition, installation readiness, and true priority?
- Are part number, revision, serial, effectivity, interchangeability, certificate, shelf life, hazmat, battery, and export status verified?
- Is quality release complete, or has an authorized decision maker accepted documented risk?
- Did I compare next-flight-out, hand-carry, charter, dedicated truck, air cargo, and local alternatives by recoverability?
- Are cutoff, customs, security, dimensions, dangerous goods, weather, airport hours, and last-mile constraints considered?
- Is there one control tower status with timestamps, timezone, references, flight, courier, broker, receiver, and next update?
- Are customs broker, import/export documents, duties, temporary import or repair return, and receiving authority ready?
- Does the last mile preserve custody, ESD, FOD, temperature, security, and delivery-to-maintenance requirements?
- If hand-carry is used, are courier documents, airline acceptance, customs, cabin custody, and direct handoff controlled?
- Will the expedite be reviewed for stocking, repair, documentation, supplier, and planning improvements?
