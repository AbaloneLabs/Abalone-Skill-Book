# Secure Storage Custody And Quality Release

Aerospace and defense storage is part of the product's compliance and airworthiness chain. A component can lose usable status while sitting in a warehouse if custody, environment, shelf life, access, documentation, or quality release is not controlled. The agent should treat storage and release decisions as regulated operations, not generic inventory handling. Secure storage must protect the part, records, program restrictions, and evidence of who touched what.

## Core Rules

### Define storage status and release authority

Every controlled part should have a clear status: received pending inspection, quarantine, serviceable, unserviceable, repairable, scrap, customer-owned, government-furnished, bonded, export-controlled, life-limited, shelf-life controlled, or quality released. Do not mix statuses physically or in systems.

Define who can release a part to stock, repair, scrap, shipment, customer return, or installation. Warehouse staff should not infer release from urgency or a salesperson request. Quality, compliance, program, or customer authority may be required.

### Preserve custody and access control

Secure areas should control who can enter, pick, inspect, pack, photograph, or move items. Use badges, logs, CCTV where appropriate, locked cages, tamper seals, visitor escort, and system permissions. For defense or export-controlled items, restrict foreign-person access and unauthorized technical data exposure.

Chain of custody should be recorded for high-value, classified, serialized, customer-owned, or regulated parts. If custody is broken, stop and investigate before release. A missing seal or unexplained movement is not an administrative nuisance.

### Manage environment and shelf life

Parts may require temperature, humidity, ESD, FOD, dust, vibration, light, corrosion, battery, lubricant, or packaging controls. Shelf-life items need expiration, cure date, recertification, extension, and first-expire-first-out logic. Life-limited parts need remaining life records.

Monitor conditions and keep records where required. If environmental control fails, quarantine affected parts until quality review. Do not assume parts are acceptable because packaging appears intact.

### Protect documentation and system integrity

Storage records should connect physical location, serial, lot, condition, certificate, purchase order, customer ownership, export status, inspection status, and shelf life. Avoid manual workarounds that break traceability. If labels are replaced, ensure old and new identifiers remain linked.

Quality release requires the correct evidence. Receiving inspection, certificate review, nonconformance disposition, repair station paperwork, customer approval, and export screening may all be prerequisites. The system should prevent shipment before release.

### Control repair, returns, and scrap paths

Unserviceable, repairable, warranty, exchange core, and scrap parts need segregated flows. Scrap or rejected parts must not re-enter serviceable stock. Mutilation, disposal, customer return, or vendor return may require documentation and approval.

Core returns and repair loops should track due dates, ownership, condition, serials, and documents. Losing a core can create financial penalties; returning the wrong core can create compliance and customer trust issues.

### Prepare storage for audit and customer inspection

Aerospace and defense customers may audit storage, quality release, segregation, shelf-life control, export control, and custody records. The warehouse should be able to show status, location, access logs, environmental records, training, nonconformance actions, and release evidence without emergency cleanup. Audit readiness should reflect daily operations.

If a customer or government contract requires specific storage, marking, cybersecurity, visitor, or record-retention controls, translate those clauses into warehouse procedures. Contract requirements that remain in procurement files will not protect the part on the shelf.

### Control mixed ownership and program segregation

Customer-owned, government-furnished, consigned, rotable, exchange, warranty, and company-owned inventory may sit in the same facility but require different rights and reporting. Segregate physically or logically so one customer's part is not used for another program without authorization. Program restrictions can matter as much as physical part identity.

### Train handlers on stop conditions

Warehouse personnel should know when to stop: broken seal, missing certificate, wrong serial, expired shelf life, environmental excursion, foreign object contamination, unauthorized visitor, damaged packaging, or system mismatch. Stop conditions should be rewarded, not punished as delays.

## Common Traps

- Treating warehouse receipt as quality release.
- Mixing serviceable, unserviceable, quarantine, repair, and scrap inventory.
- Allowing unauthorized access to export-controlled parts or technical data.
- Ignoring shelf life, environmental excursions, ESD, FOD, humidity, and packaging integrity.
- Moving serialized parts manually without updating custody and system location.
- Shipping because a customer is urgent before release evidence is complete.
- Letting scrap or rejected material remain recognizable and re-enterable.
- Losing repair core documents, ownership records, or return deadlines.
- Discovering contract storage or audit requirements only when a customer visit is scheduled.
- Mixing customer-owned, government-furnished, exchange, and company stock without program controls.
- Training handlers to move fast but not to stop when release evidence or custody is wrong.

## Self-Check

- Is every part status clear in both physical storage and system records?
- Are release authorities defined for stock, shipment, repair, scrap, customer return, and installation?
- Are secure access, visitor escort, CCTV, cages, seals, logs, and system permissions appropriate to part risk?
- Are foreign-person access, export-controlled data, customer-owned material, and program restrictions controlled?
- Are temperature, humidity, ESD, FOD, shelf life, life limit, corrosion, dust, and packaging conditions monitored?
- Does environmental excursion or broken custody trigger quarantine and quality review?
- Do records link serial, lot, part, revision, condition, certificate, ownership, export status, location, and release status?
- Are repair, core return, warranty, unserviceable, rejected, and scrap flows segregated and documented?
- Can the facility show audit evidence for access, environment, training, release, nonconformance, and custody without special cleanup?
- Are customer-owned, government-furnished, consigned, exchange, warranty, and company-owned materials segregated by rights and program rules?
- Do handlers have explicit stop conditions for seal, certificate, serial, shelf-life, environment, visitor, packaging, and system mismatches?
