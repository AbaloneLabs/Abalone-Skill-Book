# Traceability ITAR And Controlled Part Logistics

Aerospace and defense logistics is not ordinary high-value shipping. The part, paperwork, technical data, country, consignee, end use, storage condition, and chain of custody can determine whether the movement is legal, airworthy, payable, or usable. The agent should protect traceability and export-control integrity before optimizing speed or cost. A part that arrives quickly but lacks the correct certificates, release documents, serial history, or export authorization may be unusable and may create serious compliance exposure.

## Core Rules

### Identify the control status before movement

Determine whether the item, technical data, tooling, software, repair documentation, or defense article is subject to ITAR, EAR, sanctions, embargoes, end-use controls, military classification, customer restrictions, or program security rules. Do not infer status from product description alone. Use export compliance, legal, trade, engineering, or program security sources.

Control status affects who may see data, who may handle the shipment, where it may transit, which countries are prohibited, what license or exemption applies, and what documents must accompany the part. A routing that is efficient commercially may be unacceptable because it transits a restricted country or exposes controlled data to unauthorized parties.

### Protect part identity and traceability

Aerospace parts often require serial number, lot number, part number, revision, condition code, shelf life, maintenance history, life-limited part status, repair station documentation, certificate of conformity, airworthiness release, material certs, and customer purchase order references. Verify identity before shipping and at receipt. Similar part numbers or revisions can create critical errors.

Do not separate the part from required documentation. If originals are needed, control them. If electronic records are used, ensure access, integrity, and recipient acceptance. Missing paperwork can ground aircraft, fail receiving inspection, block payment, or force quarantine.

### Control technical data exposure

Shipping documents, labels, drawings, packing photos, repair records, and emails may contain controlled technical data or sensitive program information. Use approved systems and limit recipients. Avoid placing sensitive details on external labels or unsecured messages when not needed for transport.

If a freight forwarder, broker, carrier, warehouse, or repair vendor will access controlled data or defense articles, confirm they are approved and authorized. Subcontracting can create hidden exposure. The logistics plan should state whether rebrokering, uncontrolled storage, or foreign-person access is prohibited.

### Match packaging and handling to airworthiness risk

Controlled parts may need ESD protection, FOD control, humidity protection, shock control, temperature control, tamper seals, clean packaging, shelf-life labeling, or hazardous material documentation. Packaging should preserve both physical condition and quality status. A part damaged by poor handling may not be recoverable by insurance alone.

Receiving should include condition, seal, documentation, serial, temperature or shock indicators, and quarantine rules. If a discrepancy appears, stop normal flow and involve quality, compliance, and the customer or program authority.

### Document decisions and escalations

For regulated moves, keep evidence of classification, license or exemption, screening, approved parties, routing, documents, custody, release, and receipt. If a shipment is urgent, document who approved any deviation. Speed pressure is not a defense for export or quality failures.

When uncertain, stop and escalate. The cost of asking export compliance or quality is smaller than the cost of unauthorized export, rejected part, or airworthiness failure.

### Refresh screening when facts change

Export and control review is not one-time if the destination, consignee, end user, end use, carrier, broker, route, repair location, or program changes. A shipment released for one customer and country may not be valid after reroute or drop-ship instruction. Re-screen parties and routing when facts change, especially during urgent AOG or defense support.

Denied party, sanctions, embargo, and end-use screening should be documented with date, source, and reviewer where required. If systems are down or a manual process is used, record the temporary control and reconcile later.

## Common Traps

- Treating controlled aerospace parts like generic high-value freight.
- Shipping before confirming ITAR, EAR, sanctions, end-use, or license requirements.
- Using efficient routings that transit restricted countries or expose controlled goods to unauthorized parties.
- Separating parts from certificates, serial records, airworthiness releases, or repair documentation.
- Putting sensitive technical data on labels, emails, or broker instructions unnecessarily.
- Allowing forwarders or carriers to subcontract without control of data, custody, and country exposure.
- Ignoring ESD, FOD, shelf life, shock, humidity, or clean packaging requirements.
- Letting urgency override export compliance, quality release, or discrepancy quarantine.
- Treating export screening as permanent even after routing, consignee, end use, or repair location changes.
- Failing to document manual screening or temporary controls used during urgent moves.

## Self-Check

- Have I confirmed control status through export compliance, legal, trade, engineering, quality, or program authority?
- Are ITAR, EAR, sanctions, embargo, end-use, license, exemption, country, routing, and party restrictions addressed?
- Have part number, revision, serial, lot, condition, shelf life, life-limited status, and required certificates been verified?
- Are documents kept with the part or available through an accepted controlled system?
- Is controlled technical data protected from unauthorized labels, emails, systems, brokers, carriers, warehouses, and foreign-person access?
- Are forwarders, carriers, brokers, repair vendors, and warehouses approved for controlled goods and data?
- Does packaging preserve airworthiness, ESD, FOD, humidity, shock, temperature, tamper, and clean handling requirements?
- Are classification, screening, license, routing, custody, release, receipt, discrepancies, and deviations documented?
- Does any change in consignee, route, broker, carrier, end user, end use, repair site, or program trigger re-screening?
- Are screening date, source, reviewer, and manual workarounds recorded where required?
