# Incoterms Duties And Import Export Risk

Incoterms and duty responsibility shape cost, control, liability, service promise, and compliance exposure. They are not decorative three-letter codes on an invoice. Agents often confuse who pays freight with who owns customs risk, or assume that a customer-friendly promise such as delivered-duty-paid can be offered without importer capability in the destination country. This skill helps the agent evaluate trade terms as an operating model, not a label, before making cross-border commitments.

## Core Rules

### Separate Freight Payment, Risk Transfer, And Customs Responsibility

Clarify three questions independently: who pays for each freight leg, where risk transfers if goods are lost or damaged, and who handles export and import formalities. Incoterms help define these responsibilities, but internal systems, customer contracts, quotes, carrier billing, and invoices often use shorthand that causes confusion. Do not assume prepaid freight means seller bears all risk or duties. Do not assume collect freight means the buyer handles import compliance.

Map the shipment journey from origin facility to final delivery. Include export packing, origin pickup, export clearance, terminal handling, main carriage, insurance, import clearance, duties and taxes, destination handling, storage, final mile, returns, and exception costs. A strong answer identifies which party controls and pays for each step, and whether that party is actually capable of performing it.

### Check Whether The Chosen Term Is Operationally Possible

Some terms look attractive but are impractical in a given lane. Delivered-duty-paid service may require the seller to act as importer of record, register for tax, maintain local compliance records, pay duties, handle local product requirements, and accept post-entry audit risk. Ex works may seem easy for the seller but can create export-control and pickup problems if the buyer's carrier cannot meet origin requirements. Port-based terms may not fit containerized door-to-door movements.

Consider mode and geography. Air, ocean, parcel, courier, truck, rail, and intermodal movements differ in how charges, handoffs, customs filing, insurance, and delivery control appear. Terms designed around named ports or terminals should not be casually applied to door delivery without understanding the consequences. If an Incoterm is chosen mainly because "we always use it," treat that as a prompt for review.

### Make Landed Cost Visible Before Commitment

Duty, tax, brokerage, disbursement fees, storage, demurrage, detention, inspection, documentation, destination delivery, and accessorials can exceed the visible freight quote. Before quoting or promising international delivery, estimate landed cost and identify who absorbs variance. If the customer expects a fixed delivered price, decide how exchange rates, classification changes, tax changes, inspections, or customer-caused delays will be handled.

Do not hide landed-cost uncertainty. For regulated goods, new countries, new classifications, or unfamiliar customers, use ranges and assumptions. If finance, sales, or customer service is promising a total price, logistics should expose the assumptions behind it. A cheap sale can become unprofitable if duties, clearance failures, storage charges, or return costs were not priced.

### Assign Importer And Exporter Capability

Importer and exporter roles are legal and operational obligations, not just names on documents. Confirm registration numbers, tax IDs, powers of attorney, broker relationships, product admissibility, recordkeeping obligations, and authority to make declarations. If a distributor, end customer, affiliate, marketplace, or event organizer is expected to import, verify that they know and accept the role.

Watch for indirect risk. A company may avoid being importer on paper but still create regulatory exposure by controlling classification, selecting broker instructions, providing product claims, or selling into a restricted market. Conversely, a logistics team may be asked to arrange DDP shipments into countries where the company has no legal entity or tax setup. Escalate these cases rather than solving them with creative paperwork.

### Align Terms With Customer Experience And Exception Handling

The best term depends partly on service expectations. DAP or DDP may improve customer experience by reducing border surprises, but they require stronger internal control. FCA or FOB may reduce seller responsibility, but customers may face delays, surprise charges, or poor visibility. The chosen term should match the relationship, shipment value, customer sophistication, and tolerance for customs intervention.

Define exception handling. If customs reclassifies goods, duties increase, the importer refuses charges, tax ID is invalid, paperwork is wrong, or goods are returned, who pays and who decides? If the answer is not known before shipment, the dispute will arise under time pressure while storage charges accumulate.

### Keep Commercial Language Consistent

Incoterms should appear consistently in quotes, sales contracts, purchase orders, invoices, broker instructions, booking notes, and customer communications. Include the named place and version where appropriate. A term without a named place is incomplete; "DAP" alone does not say where responsibility extends. Avoid mixing outdated terms, internal abbreviations, or conflicting language that brokers and customers may interpret differently.

## Common Traps

- Treating Incoterms as freight billing codes instead of risk and responsibility rules.
- Offering DDP because it sounds customer-friendly while lacking importer registration, tax setup, broker authority, or product compliance support.
- Using EXW to avoid work while creating export filing, pickup, or restricted-party problems.
- Failing to name the place. The difference between a port, terminal, warehouse, and final door can decide major costs.
- Quoting international sales without estimating duty, tax, brokerage, destination handling, and inspection risk.
- Assuming the broker or forwarder owns compliance truth. They usually file based on shipper or importer data.
- Letting sales, finance, logistics, and customer service use different terms for the same shipment.
- Ignoring returns. Import terms for the outbound sale may not solve duty recovery, re-export, repair, or customer return complexity.
- Using one default term for every country, customer, mode, and product type.

## Self-Check

- Are freight payment, risk transfer, export responsibility, import responsibility, and duty or tax payment separated clearly?
- Is the named place complete and consistent across commercial and shipping documents?
- Is the chosen Incoterm operationally feasible for the mode, lane, customer, and legal entities involved?
- Has importer/exporter capability been verified rather than assumed?
- Are duty, tax, brokerage, storage, inspection, destination handling, and return exposure visible in the cost model?
- Does the customer promise match the actual term and exception handling plan?
- Are DDP, DAP, EXW, FOB, FCA, or other terms used only where their practical consequences are understood?
- Is there a defined owner for customs reclassification, duty variance, tax ID failure, rejected charges, or return decisions?
- Do sales contracts, purchase orders, invoices, broker instructions, and customer messages use consistent language?
- Has uncertainty been escalated to trade compliance, legal, finance, or tax where the logistics team cannot safely decide alone?
