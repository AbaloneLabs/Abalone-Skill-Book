# Sanctions Restricted Goods And Screening

Sanctions and restricted-goods controls can block a shipment even when every operational step is ready. The risk is not limited to obvious weapons or embargoed destinations; dual-use items, replacement parts, software, technical data, chemicals, electronics, aerospace parts, medical goods, and high-risk intermediaries can create compliance obligations. Agents often treat screening as a checkbox or assume the carrier will catch problems. This skill helps the agent recognize sanctions and restricted-goods risk, require current qualified screening, and avoid overconfident shipping advice.

## Core Rules

### Treat Screening As A Current Compliance Requirement

Sanctions lists, embargoes, export controls, licensing requirements, and restricted-party rules change frequently. The correct answer depends on date, jurisdiction, parties, ownership, product, origin, destination, end use, and route. The agent should not rely on memory for exact sanctions or licensing rules.

When shipping risk exists, require current screening through approved compliance tools, government sources, legal counsel, export compliance, customs broker, or trade compliance team. State uncertainty clearly. Do not tell a user a shipment is allowed unless authoritative review supports it.

### Screen All Relevant Parties, Not Only The Consignee

Restricted-party risk can involve shipper, consignee, buyer, seller, payer, notify party, end user, broker, forwarder, carrier, bank, owner, parent company, intermediate consignee, and beneficial owner. A shipment routed through a third party may still be prohibited if the true end user or owner is restricted.

Collect full party details: legal names, addresses, countries, registration numbers where relevant, aliases, and ownership relationships. Watch for mismatches, vague trading companies, freight forwarders in high-risk hubs, or refusal to identify end user.

### Classify The Goods Before Judging Risk

Product classification drives restricted-goods and export-control decisions. HS codes, ECCN or equivalent export classification, military/dual-use status, technology content, encryption, chemicals, biological materials, batteries, aerospace use, nuclear use, and controlled components may matter. Replacement parts and samples can still be controlled.

Do not infer control status from product name alone. Request classification from product engineering, regulatory, trade compliance, or supplier declarations. If classification is unknown, hold the shipment or escalate rather than shipping on assumption.

### Evaluate End Use And End User

Even goods that are not obviously restricted can become prohibited or licensable based on end use or end user: military, surveillance, nuclear, missile, chemical/biological, oil and gas, aerospace, law enforcement, sanctioned industry, or human rights concerns. Red flags include unusual routing, vague end use, refusal to provide documents, inconsistent quantities, or customers with no clear need for the product.

Ask what the goods will be used for and by whom. For high-risk destinations or industries, require end-use statements, licenses, or compliance review. Do not ignore red flags because the commercial team wants to ship.

### Include Route, Transshipment, And Financial Flow

Sanctions and export controls may be affected by countries of origin, export, re-export, transshipment, aircraft or vessel route, port calls, banks, currencies, and payment parties. A shipment may not be acceptable because of a transit country, vessel ownership, airline restrictions, or bank compliance.

Coordinate with forwarder, carrier, broker, finance, and trade compliance. Screen vessels, airlines, ports, and banks where required by policy or risk. Avoid route changes that introduce sanctioned or restricted exposure without review.

### Stop Shipment On Red Flags Until Resolved

Red flags should create a hold, not a note. Examples include party-name matches, high-risk destination, inconsistent documents, suspected diversion, controlled goods, military end use, restricted industry, denied end-user information, unusual payment, or customer pressure to misdescribe goods.

Define who can release a hold and what evidence is required. Operational urgency should not override compliance. If a carrier or forwarder asks for a document change that changes commodity, value, origin, or consignee, escalate.

### Document Screening And Release Decisions

Compliance decisions must be auditable. Records should include screened parties, date, tool/source, product classification, end-use documentation, license determination, approvals, holds, release rationale, and shipment identifiers. A later investigation needs evidence that screening was performed before shipment.

Store records in controlled systems with retention aligned to policy and law. Do not rely on verbal assurance that someone "checked it."

### Train Operations To Recognize Escalation Triggers

Warehouse, customer service, transportation, and export staff may see red flags first: changed addresses, relabel requests, unusual routing, split shipments, vague descriptions, pressure to ship quickly, or mismatched documents. They need simple escalation paths, not detailed legal expertise.

Build SOPs and stop-ship authority. Encourage escalation without penalty. A culture that punishes delays can create compliance violations.

## Common Traps

### Screening Only The Ship-To Name

Buyers, end users, banks, owners, brokers, forwarders, and intermediate parties may create risk.

### Assuming Ordinary Parts Are Uncontrolled

Dual-use, aerospace, electronics, encryption, chemicals, and replacement parts can be restricted.

### Treating Past Approval As Current Approval

Sanctions, parties, ownership, routes, and product classifications can change.

### Ignoring End Use

A harmless-seeming product may be restricted for military, surveillance, nuclear, or sanctioned-industry use.

### Letting Route Changes Bypass Review

Transshipment, vessel, airline, or port changes can introduce risk.

### Changing Descriptions To Ease Shipping

Misdescription can create customs, sanctions, safety, and fraud exposure.

### Treating Carrier Acceptance As Compliance Clearance

Carrier movement does not prove legal permission to export or deliver.

### Failing To Keep Evidence

Screening and license decisions need records, not memories.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Are sanctions, restricted-party, export-control, and licensing claims based on current approved screening or escalated?
- [ ] Are shipper, consignee, buyer, seller, payer, notify party, broker, forwarder, bank, end user, and beneficial owner considered?
- [ ] Are product HS/export classifications, dual-use status, controlled technology, and regulated components verified?
- [ ] Are end use, end user, industry, and diversion red flags assessed?
- [ ] Are origin, destination, transshipment, route, carrier, vessel/airline, port, and financial flows reviewed where relevant?
- [ ] Is shipment held when red flags appear until authorized compliance release?
- [ ] Are screening date, tool/source, parties, classification, license decision, approvals, and rationale documented?
- [ ] Are operations staff given clear stop-ship and escalation triggers?
- [ ] Are document changes that alter value, origin, commodity, or parties escalated?
- [ ] Would the shipment record show defensible compliance review before movement?
