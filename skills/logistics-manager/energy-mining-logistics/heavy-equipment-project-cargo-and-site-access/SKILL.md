# Heavy Equipment Project Cargo And Site Access

Energy and mining logistics often moves equipment that is oversized, mission-critical, expensive, and difficult to recover once misrouted. A move can fail because of route clearance, bridge limits, port capability, weather, site roads, permits, escort availability, or unloading readiness. Agents often think in terms of carrier booking and miss the engineering and site-access constraints that determine whether the cargo can physically and safely arrive.

## Core Rules

### Verify cargo and lifting data

Confirm dimensions, weight, center of gravity, lifting points, tie-down points, disassembly state, shipping drawings, preservation requirements, and whether measurements include skids, crates, or accessories. Validate final as-built data before permits and equipment are locked.

Heavy equipment changes during fabrication or maintenance. Old drawings can invalidate route permits, crane plans, and transport equipment selection.

### Survey the route to the actual work site

Include ports, railheads, staging yards, public roads, private roads, bridges, grades, switchbacks, culverts, tunnels, power lines, gates, haul roads, and the final pad or laydown area. Remote access may be more restrictive than public roads.

The final miles may be the hardest part of the move. A route that reaches the mine entrance or energy facility gate is not enough if internal roads cannot take the load.

### Match transport equipment to terrain and cargo

Specialized trailers, multi-axle units, lowboys, SPMTs, barges, railcars, modular trailers, and heavy-haul tractors must match cargo weight, axle loads, grades, turning radius, road conditions, and unloading method. Include mobilization and demobilization time.

Do not select equipment based only on highway feasibility. Mine roads, wind farms, drilling pads, and remote energy sites create different handling realities.

### Coordinate site readiness and unloading

Confirm laydown space, crane pad, ground bearing, rigging crew, lifting plan, exclusion zones, permits, lighting, shift coverage, weather limits, and installation sequence. Decide whether equipment arrives just in time or stages at a secure intermediate yard.

Heavy equipment arriving before the site is ready creates extreme standby cost and safety pressure.

### Manage permit and stakeholder dependencies

Moves may require road authority permits, police escorts, utility lifts, indigenous or community consultation, landowner access, environmental restrictions, security clearance, and site owner approval. Lead times may be longer than transport time.

Stakeholder dependencies should be tracked like critical-path tasks. A missing road-use approval can stop the entire move.

### Protect equipment preservation

Equipment may need corrosion protection, weather covers, vibration limits, desiccants, battery isolation, fluid handling, preservation records, or periodic inspection during storage and transit. Long ocean or remote staging can create condition risk.

Project cargo can degrade while waiting. Preservation should be assigned, inspected, and documented.

### Prepare recovery and contingency plans

Plan for road washouts, snow, flooding, wildfire, permit delays, mechanical failure, escort cancellation, crane downtime, and community disruptions. Identify holding locations, repair support, re-permit process, communication owners, and cost authority.

Heavy equipment cannot always wait safely on the shoulder. Contingency locations and decision rights must be preplanned.

### Document custody, condition, and handoff

Capture photos, inspection reports, seals, tie-down checks, route milestones, equipment condition, handoff signatures, and exception notes. Notify engineering, project controls, construction, and site operations of changes.

Documentation supports claims, warranty, project schedule, and safe commissioning. It is not administrative decoration.

### Connect delivery to commissioning readiness

Heavy equipment often needs preservation release, assembly, vendor supervision, calibration, power, fluids, inspection, spare parts, and commissioning documentation after delivery. Confirm who owns the transition from logistics handoff to installation and startup.

If logistics declares success at delivery while commissioning lacks parts, vendor access, or preservation records, the project can still lose critical time.

## Common Traps

- Using preliminary equipment dimensions after fabrication, disassembly, or accessory changes.
- Surveying only public roads and ignoring private roads, haul roads, gates, pads, and laydown.
- Selecting trailers without considering grades, switchbacks, road condition, axle load, and unloading method.
- Shipping before crane pad, rigging, ground bearing, permits, and site labor are ready.
- Underestimating stakeholder, community, landowner, road authority, and environmental approvals.
- Leaving equipment exposed during long staging without preservation inspection.
- Having no safe holding location when weather, permit, escort, or mechanical failures occur; failing to capture condition and tie-down evidence at each handoff
- Ending logistics handoff before assembly, preservation release, vendor support, and commissioning needs are ready; treating project cargo as transport-only rather than transport plus installation readiness

## Self-Check

- Are final cargo dimensions, weight, center of gravity, lift points, tie-downs, preservation, and as-built data verified?
- Has the full route to the actual pad, laydown, or installation point been surveyed?
- Is transport equipment selected for cargo, axle load, terrain, grade, turning radius, road condition, and unloading?
- Are laydown, crane pad, rigging, ground bearing, permits, lighting, weather limits, and site labor ready?
- Are road, police, utility, landowner, community, environmental, security, and site approvals tracked?
- Are preservation, covers, corrosion protection, desiccants, fluid handling, and inspections assigned?
- Are weather, road, permit, escort, mechanical, crane, and community disruption contingencies prepared?
- Are photos, condition reports, tie-down checks, milestones, handoffs, and exceptions documented?
- Are assembly, preservation release, vendor supervision, calibration, spare parts, and commissioning handoffs assigned?
- Can the move proceed without forcing unsafe unloading, unauthorized access, or unplanned standby?
