# Freight Audit Payment And Accrual Control

Freight payment is a control process, not just invoice processing. Logistics decisions create financial obligations through rates, accessorials, fuel, detention, parcel surcharges, spot buys, and service failures. Agents often focus on moving freight and leave finance with unclear accruals, weak invoice evidence, and preventable overpayment. This skill helps align carrier payment with operational facts, contracts, and accounting timing.

## Core Rules

### Define the payment basis

Confirm whether freight is paid by contract rate, spot quote, tariff, parcel agreement, broker invoice, customer chargeback, prepaid-and-add, collect terms, or third-party billing. Identify the source of truth for rate, fuel, minimums, and accessorials.

An invoice cannot be audited without knowing which commercial agreement applies. Ambiguity creates overpayment and disputes.

### Match invoice to shipment evidence

Validate carrier, shipment ID, lane, weight, cube, class, service level, delivery date, proof of delivery, tender acceptance, quoted rate, and accessorial evidence. Require documentation for detention, layover, reconsignment, liftgate, residential, storage, lumper, and redelivery charges.

Do not approve charges only because they are common. Accessorials should be tied to operational events and contract rules.

### Control duplicate and exception payments

Freight invoices can duplicate through corrected bills, broker/carrier double billing, split shipments, parcel adjustments, rebills, and manual uploads. Use controls for invoice number, shipment ID, amount, carrier, date, and duplicate patterns.

Exception payments should be reviewed with enough context to avoid paying the same move twice or paying both disputed and corrected charges.

### Build accurate accruals

At period end, estimate freight cost for shipped-not-invoiced, received-not-invoiced, in-transit shipments, parcel adjustments, accessorials, and pending disputes. Use shipment activity, tender rates, historical accessorial factors, and known spot quotes.

Accruals should reflect operational reality. Missing freight accruals make logistics cost look better until invoices arrive later.

### Separate operational approval from accounting posting

Logistics may validate that a charge happened; finance posts the cost and handles accounting classification. Define who approves rate disputes, who codes cost centers, who approves accruals, and who releases payment.

Segregation of duties prevents both slow payment and uncontrolled spend. Avoid one person owning every step.

### Manage disputes with evidence and timing

Disputes should capture reason, amount, contract clause, supporting documents, carrier response, owner, and aging. Decide when to short pay, hold, pay under protest, or escalate commercially.

Late disputes lose leverage. Weak evidence damages carrier relationships and payment credibility.

### Govern rate and master-data changes

Freight audit depends on accurate carrier master data, fuel tables, rate effective dates, lane definitions, accessorial rules, customer billing codes, GL accounts, and cost centers. Define who can change rates, how changes are approved, when they become effective, and how historical invoices are protected.

Many payment errors are not invoice errors. They come from outdated tariffs, duplicate carrier records, wrong fuel indexes, expired spot quotes, or cost-center mapping that no one owns. Master-data changes should be tested before they affect payment runs.

### Reconcile freight cost to operations

Compare paid freight to shipments, lanes, customers, products, modes, accessorial trends, carrier performance, and budget. Identify missing invoices, unusual charges, and cost leakage.

Freight audit data should feed operational improvement, not only payment approval.

### Maintain audit trail and retention

Keep contracts, rate confirmations, tenders, BOLs, PODs, delivery scans, accessorial evidence, dispute notes, approvals, accrual calculations, and payment records. Retention should match finance, tax, customer, and carrier dispute needs.

If the cost is challenged, the organization should be able to reconstruct why it was paid or accrued.

## Common Traps

- Approving invoices without identifying the governing contract, quote, tariff, or billing terms.
- Paying accessorials without operational evidence and contract eligibility.
- Missing duplicate invoices from rebills, corrected bills, broker-carrier overlap, and parcel adjustments.
- Under-accruing shipped-not-invoiced freight and accessorial exposure at period end.
- Letting one person validate, code, approve, accrue, and release payments without control.
- Allowing disputes to age without evidence, owner, and commercial decision.
- Letting outdated rates, duplicate carrier records, wrong fuel tables, and cost-center mappings drive payments.
- Treating freight audit as a finance task disconnected from operations.
- Reconciling cost only after payment, when recovery is harder.
- Keeping poor records for BOL, POD, quotes, tenders, contracts, approvals, and accruals.

## Self-Check

- Is the payment basis clear for contract, spot, tariff, broker, parcel, collect, prepaid, or third-party billing?
- Are invoices matched to shipment, tender, lane, weight, class, service, POD, quote, and accessorial evidence?
- Are duplicates, corrected bills, rebills, parcel adjustments, and broker-carrier overlap controlled?
- Are shipped-not-invoiced, received-not-invoiced, in-transit, accessorial, parcel, and dispute accruals estimated?
- Are operational approval, cost coding, accrual approval, dispute approval, and payment release separated?
- Are disputes tracked by reason, amount, clause, evidence, owner, carrier response, and aging?
- Are rate tables, fuel indexes, effective dates, carrier records, GL codes, and cost centers governed and tested?
- Is paid freight reconciled to shipments, modes, lanes, customers, products, accessorials, performance, and budget?
- Are records retained for contracts, quotes, tenders, BOLs, PODs, approvals, accruals, disputes, and payments?
- Can finance and logistics explain both the operational event and the accounting treatment?
