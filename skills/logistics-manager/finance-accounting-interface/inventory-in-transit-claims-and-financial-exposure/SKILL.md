# Inventory In Transit Claims And Financial Exposure

In-transit inventory sits between operational control and financial reporting. Ownership, risk of loss, insurance, claims, customer billing, and accounting recognition can diverge from physical location. Agents often track shipment status but miss the financial exposure created by late, lost, damaged, stolen, or disputed freight. This skill helps logistics teams preserve evidence and align with finance when inventory is not yet settled.

## Core Rules

### Clarify ownership and risk transfer

Determine when title and risk transfer under Incoterms, customer contract, supplier agreement, collect/prepaid terms, consignment, drop ship, or internal transfer rules. The party that physically controls freight may not be the party that owns the risk.

Do not assume in-transit goods belong to whoever ordered the truck. Financial exposure depends on contract terms.

### Track in-transit status by financial significance

Prioritize visibility for high-value, high-risk, customer-committed, perishable, regulated, or revenue-critical inventory. Track departure, custody changes, estimated arrival, delay, hold, damage, theft, and delivery acceptance.

A delayed low-value shipment and a delayed revenue-critical shipment need different escalation. Use financial exposure to set monitoring intensity.

### Preserve claim evidence immediately

For loss, damage, shortage, theft, temperature excursion, seal discrepancy, or delay, capture BOL, POD, photos, seal numbers, inspection reports, temperature data, packaging condition, driver notes, carrier notices, and receiver exceptions. Note timing requirements for claims.

Claims are often won or lost in the first hours after exception discovery. Missing evidence turns recoverable loss into write-off.

### Coordinate with insurance and risk owners

Logistics claims may involve carrier liability, cargo insurance, customer claims, supplier claims, broker liability, warehouse legal liability, or self-insured retention. Notify risk management, finance, legal, or insurers according to thresholds.

Carrier liability may not cover full value, consequential loss, temperature failure, theft, or force majeure. Know the coverage assumptions before promising recovery.

### Align financial reserves and write-offs

Finance may need reserves for lost inventory, claim recoveries, damaged goods, customer credits, chargebacks, insurance receivables, or write-offs. Provide timely estimates, claim status, and recovery probability.

A claim filed is not the same as cash recovered. Accounting should reflect uncertainty.

### Protect customer and supplier communication

When shipment exceptions affect revenue or service, coordinate messaging about replacement, credit, delay, inspection, or claim. Avoid admitting liability casually or promising recovery before facts and contract responsibility are known.

Communication should preserve relationships without undermining legal or insurance positions.

### Manage salvage, rework, and disposition

Damaged or delayed goods may be salvageable, reworkable, returnable, quarantined, donated, scrapped, or sold through secondary channels. Define who can decide and how value is recorded.

Do not dispose of goods before insurer, carrier, customer, or quality owner requirements are satisfied. Disposition can affect claim recovery.

### Reconcile claim lifecycle

Track claim filed date, required documents, carrier acknowledgement, insurer status, customer credit, supplier recovery, reserve, settlement, denial, appeal, and closure. Keep logistics and finance aligned until the financial exposure is closed.

Open claims should not disappear from operational dashboards once replacement freight ships.

### Review aging exposure and unresolved custody

Create aging views for in-transit inventory, unresolved delivery exceptions, pending claims, denied claims under appeal, damaged goods awaiting disposition, and insurance or carrier receivables. Assign owners and escalation thresholds for aged exposure.

Old in-transit balances can hide lost inventory, duplicate replacement shipments, unclaimed recoveries, or accounting entries that no longer match physical reality. Aging review forces logistics, finance, and customer teams to close the loop instead of letting exposure drift.

## Common Traps

- Assuming physical possession determines ownership and risk of loss.
- Monitoring all in-transit inventory equally instead of by financial and service exposure.
- Discovering claim evidence is missing after the carrier claim window closes.
- Promising full recovery when carrier liability or insurance coverage is limited.
- Filing claims without telling finance about reserves, write-offs, and uncertain recovery.
- Admitting liability or promising credits before contract and evidence review.
- Disposing of damaged goods before claim, insurance, quality, or customer requirements are met.
- Treating replacement shipment as closure while claim recovery remains unresolved.
- Allowing aged in-transit balances, unresolved custody, and claim receivables to remain unowned.
- Failing to reconcile customer credits, supplier recovery, insurance receivable, and carrier settlement.

## Self-Check

- Are title and risk transfer defined by Incoterms, contract, collect/prepaid terms, consignment, or internal rules?
- Is in-transit visibility prioritized by value, risk, revenue, customer commitment, regulation, and perishability?
- Are BOL, POD, photos, seals, inspection notes, temperature data, packaging, driver notes, and receiver exceptions preserved?
- Are carrier liability, insurance, broker, warehouse, supplier, customer, and self-insured exposure owners notified?
- Are reserves, write-offs, claim receivables, customer credits, chargebacks, and recovery probability shared with finance?
- Are customer and supplier communications coordinated to preserve facts, relationships, and legal positions?
- Are salvage, rework, quarantine, return, donation, scrap, and secondary-sale decisions approved and valued?
- Is the claim lifecycle tracked through filing, documents, acknowledgement, settlement, denial, appeal, and closure?
- Are aged in-transit balances, unresolved exceptions, pending claims, damaged goods, and receivables reviewed with owners?
- Can the organization reconcile physical exception, financial exposure, and recovery outcome?
