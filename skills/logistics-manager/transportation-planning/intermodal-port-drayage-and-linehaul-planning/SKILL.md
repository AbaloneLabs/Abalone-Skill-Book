# Intermodal Port Drayage And Linehaul Planning

Intermodal, port drayage, and long-haul linehaul planning depend on handoffs. Containers, trailers, documents, appointments, chassis, rail cutoffs, vessel arrival, customs release, yard capacity, driver availability, and inland delivery windows must line up. Agents often compare these modes by rate and transit time while missing dwell, demurrage, detention, free time, chassis shortages, port congestion, rail ramp delays, transload capacity, and documentation readiness. A cheap move can become expensive when a container sits, a pickup appointment is missed, or a linehaul leg lacks capacity.

Use this skill when planning container moves, port-to-door flows, rail or intermodal shipments, drayage, transload, long-haul truckload, or multi-leg freight involving ramps, terminals, ports, or handoffs.

## Core Rules

### Map Every Handoff And Responsibility

Multi-leg freight requires clear ownership at each handoff: steamship line, terminal, customs broker, freight forwarder, drayage carrier, chassis provider, transload warehouse, rail carrier, linehaul carrier, destination warehouse, and consignee. Each party may control only part of the timeline.

Create a handoff map with required documents, status triggers, appointment ownership, equipment responsibility, free-time clocks, and escalation contacts. If no one owns a handoff, the shipment can sit while each party assumes another is acting. This is especially risky around customs release, terminal availability, rail ramp pickup, and empty return.

### Track Free Time, Demurrage, Detention, And Per Diem

Port and intermodal costs can escalate through time-based charges. Demurrage, detention, storage, per diem, chassis rental, pre-pull, yard storage, and rail ramp fees depend on free-time rules and equipment return. These rules vary by carrier, terminal, contract, port, and situation.

For each container or trailer, know when free time starts, when it ends, what must happen to stop the clock, who pays, and what documentation proves completion. Build a last-free-day tracker and prioritize moves accordingly. Do not wait until invoices arrive to discover exposure.

### Confirm Documentation And Release Before Dispatch

Drayage and intermodal moves require documentation accuracy: bill of lading, delivery order, customs release, freight release, terminal release, container number, seal, weight, hazardous documents, temperature settings, pickup number, appointment confirmation, and consignee instructions. Missing or incorrect documents can cause failed pickup, storage, driver detention, or customs issues.

Before dispatch, confirm that the cargo is actually available and legally releasable. Vessel arrival does not mean container availability. Rail arrival does not mean pickup readiness. Dispatching without release wastes driver capacity and may create fees.

### Plan Equipment And Appointment Constraints

Equipment can be the constraint: chassis, gensets, refrigerated plugs, specialized trailers, hazmat-certified drivers, overweight permits, tri-axle chassis, container lifts, yard space, or drop capacity. Appointments at ports, terminals, rail ramps, warehouses, and receivers may be scarce and time-specific.

Secure appointments and equipment early for high-risk lanes. Include driver hours, gate hours, queue time, weigh stations, road restrictions, and receiving windows. A plan that ignores equipment and appointment scarcity is not executable.

### Decide When To Transload, Pre-Pull, Or Direct Deliver

Containers may move direct to destination, be pre-pulled to avoid demurrage, transloaded to domestic trailers, stored temporarily, or routed by rail. Each option has tradeoffs. Direct delivery reduces handling but requires receiver capacity and appointment timing. Pre-pull protects free time but adds yard cost and may start detention. Transload can improve inland economics or speed but adds handling, damage risk, and coordination.

Choose based on free-time exposure, destination capacity, product urgency, inventory strategy, labor availability, trailer capacity, and customer needs. Do not transload by habit; do it when the value exceeds handling and coordination cost.

### Build Buffers Around Unreliable Milestones

Ports, ramps, vessels, rail schedules, and long-haul linehaul are exposed to weather, labor disruption, congestion, customs exams, equipment shortages, and appointment scarcity. Published transit times are not guarantees. Build buffers where downstream operations depend on arrival.

For critical freight, define alternate ports, expedited drayage, team drivers, air conversion, alternate rail ramps, or safety stock. For less urgent freight, communicate realistic arrival windows and avoid overpromising delivery dates based only on vessel ETA or rail schedule.

### Manage Weight, Compliance, And Safety

Container and linehaul moves must comply with weight limits, axle rules, hazmat regulations, food safety, temperature requirements, seal control, security, and route restrictions. Overweight containers may need permits, transload, or special equipment. Hazmat and temperature-controlled freight require documentation and qualified carriers.

Confirm weights and special requirements before booking. A container that cannot legally move on standard chassis or roads will create delays and cost. Safety and compliance should not be solved at the gate.

### Keep Stakeholders Updated With Operational Status

Multi-leg freight creates many status points: vessel arrival, discharge, customs release, terminal availability, appointment booked, container out-gated, yard arrival, delivery, empty return, rail departure, rail arrival, pickup, linehaul departure, and final delivery. Stakeholders need the status that affects their decisions, not vague "in transit" updates.

Use milestone visibility and exception alerts. Customer service, warehouse, procurement, inventory planning, and finance may all need different updates. Clear status reduces panic, duplicate calls, and poor downstream scheduling.

## Common Traps

### Treating ETA As Availability

Vessel, rail, or truck arrival does not mean freight is available for pickup or delivery. Release, unloading, customs, appointments, and equipment still matter.

### Missing The Last Free Day

Demurrage, detention, storage, and per diem can accumulate quickly. Track last free day and empty return deadlines proactively.

### Dispatching Without Release

Sending a driver before cargo, documents, or appointments are ready wastes capacity and can create detention or dry-run charges.

### Ignoring Chassis And Equipment Shortages

Rates and appointments are meaningless if the needed chassis, genset, trailer, or permitted equipment is unavailable.

### Overpromising Intermodal Transit

Intermodal can be cost-effective but may face ramp delays and schedule variability. Promise windows with realistic buffers.

### Transloading Without Counting Handling Risk

Transload can solve cost or timing problems but adds labor, damage, miscount, security, and documentation risk. Use it deliberately.

### Forgetting Empty Return

The move is not financially closed when freight is delivered. Empty return timing can affect detention and per diem.

### Weak Handoff Ownership

When broker, forwarder, dray carrier, terminal, warehouse, and consignee all assume someone else is acting, freight stalls. Assign ownership at each step.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Are all handoffs, owners, documents, release triggers, appointments, and escalation contacts mapped?
- [ ] Are free time, demurrage, detention, storage, per diem, chassis, and empty return deadlines tracked?
- [ ] Is cargo release confirmed before dispatch, including customs, freight, terminal, and delivery order status?
- [ ] Are appointments, gate hours, receiving windows, driver hours, and equipment constraints planned?
- [ ] Is the choice between direct delivery, pre-pull, transload, rail, and linehaul justified by cost, timing, and risk?
- [ ] Are buffers and alternatives defined for port, rail, weather, labor, customs, and equipment disruption?
- [ ] Are weight, hazmat, temperature, seal, route, and safety requirements checked before booking?
- [ ] Are operational milestones visible to warehouse, customer service, inventory, procurement, and finance stakeholders?
- [ ] Is empty return or equipment release included in closeout?
- [ ] Could the plan still work if one handoff is delayed or an appointment is missed?
