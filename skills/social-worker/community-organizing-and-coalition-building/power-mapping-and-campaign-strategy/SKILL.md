# Power Mapping And Campaign Strategy

Power mapping and campaign strategy are the disciplines by which a social work organizer turns a grievance into a winnable campaign, and the central judgment problem is that passion for a just cause does not, by itself, produce change, because change is won by mobilizing and applying leverage against the specific decision-maker who has the authority to grant the demand. The organizer must resist the pull to act on the issue that feels most urgent or most morally clear and instead act on the issue that is winnable given the actual distribution of power, the organization's real capacity, and the political moment. Selecting a target is not selecting an enemy; it is identifying the person or body with the authority to deliver the demand, and a campaign aimed at the wrong target wastes energy no matter how energized the base. Tactics must flow from a strategy that links each action to a theory of how pressure accumulates on the target, not from a desire to express outrage or demonstrate commitment. Agents err by confusing activity with progress, by choosing targets who symbolize the problem but lack authority, by escalating tactics faster than the base can sustain, and by treating negotiation as betrayal rather than as the moment the campaign was building toward.

## Core Rules

### Define a Specific, Winnable Demand Before Choosing a Target

A campaign begins with a demand that is specific enough that its grant or denial can be clearly recognized, and winnable enough that success is plausible within the organization's capacity and the political context. Translate broad values, housing justice, educational equity, into a concrete demand, a specific policy change, budget allocation, or administrative action, that a defined decision-maker has the authority to deliver. Test the demand against whether it is measurable, whether it materially improves lives, whether it builds the organization's power for future fights, and whether it can plausibly be won. A demand that is too broad cannot be granted and cannot be won, and a campaign without a winnable demand becomes an expression of grievance that exhausts the base without producing change. Define the win before naming the target.

### Map Power to Identify Who Can Actually Grant the Demand

Power mapping is the systematic analysis of who holds the authority to grant the demand, who influences that decision-maker, and where the organization's leverage lies, and it must be done with factual accuracy rather than assumption. Identify the primary target, the person or body with formal authority to decide, and where that target is inaccessible, identify the secondary target, someone more reachable who has influence over the primary. Research the target's interests, constituencies, vulnerabilities, relationships, public commitments, and past decisions, because leverage is found in the gap between what the target wants and what your campaign can affect. Map the stakeholders, allies, opponents, and persuadables around the target, and assess each for their power, their relationship to the target, and their likely behavior. A campaign aimed at a target who cannot grant the demand is structurally unwinnable.

### Match Tactics to a Theory of Change That Pressures the Target

Every tactic should be chosen because it applies specific pressure on the target through a plausible theory of how it moves them, not because it expresses the campaign's feelings or demonstrates its commitment. A tactic works when it activates a lever the target cares about, threatening their reputation, their relationships, their constituency support, their funding, their political future, or their ability to govern, and each tactic should be traceable to that lever. Escalate tactics in a deliberate sequence that builds pressure while preserving the base's energy and the organization's credibility, and sequence actions so that each one creates the conditions for the next. Tactics disconnected from a theory of change produce activity without progress and burn out participants who cannot see how their effort leads to the win.

### Assess Organizational Capacity Honestly Before Committing

A campaign's viability depends on the organization's real capacity, its leaders, active members, staff time, money, relationships, credibility, and the depth of the base, and honest assessment prevents overreach that demoralizes the organization. Assess how many people can be turned out, how much staff and leader time the campaign will consume, what relationships can be leveraged, what funding is available, and what else the organization is carrying. Choose a campaign scope that stretches but does not exceed capacity, and build in the leadership development and base-building that will grow capacity over the course of the campaign. A campaign chosen without capacity assessment collapses mid-way, leaving the organization weaker and the base disillusioned. Winning builds power; losing, especially from overreach, destroys it.

### Build and Track the Base as the Source of Power

The organization's power is its organized people, and a campaign must build and mobilize the base rather than rely on staff or a few charismatic leaders. Set numeric goals for turnout, one-to-one relational meetings, new leaders identified and developed, and commitments secured, and track them as rigorously as the policy goal. Use the campaign to deepen relationships, develop new leaders through real responsibility, and expand the base so that the organization emerges stronger regardless of the immediate outcome. A campaign that wins a policy but exhausts and shrinks the base is a strategic loss, because the organization's long-term power is its people. Power is built between campaigns and spent during them, and the campaign must replenish what it spends.

### Choose the Right Moment and Read the Political Opportunity

Campaigns succeed or fail partly on timing, and reading the political moment, a scandal, a budget cycle, an election, a media window, a crisis that shifts public attention, determines whether the same demand is winnable now or must wait. Assess whether the moment amplifies or dampens the campaign's leverage, whether allies are available or distracted, whether the target is vulnerable or fortified, and whether public attention can be engaged or is elsewhere. Seize openings that expand the winnable and retreat or hold when the moment is unfavorable, conserving capacity for when opportunity returns. Timing is not everything, but a strong campaign launched into a closed moment will fail where a slightly weaker campaign launched into an open moment will win.

### Plan for Negotiation as the Campaign's Culmination

The purpose of a campaign is to win the demand, and winning almost always involves negotiation with the target, which is the moment the campaign was building toward, not a departure from it. Prepare for negotiation by knowing your bottom line, your ideal outcome, the trade-offs you will and will not accept, and the evidence and relationships you bring to the table. Enter negotiation with the power the campaign has built and the credibility to deliver your side's agreement, and recognize that a negotiated win that materially improves lives and builds power is a victory, not a sellout. Treat negotiation as a strategic phase that requires the same preparation and discipline as the public campaign, and debrief the outcome with the base to consolidate the learning and the power.

## Common Traps

### Choosing a Target Who Symbolizes the Problem but Lacks Authority

The organizer targets a prominent figure who embodies the grievance, a CEO, a governor, a national body, because they are the face of the issue, but who lacks the specific authority to grant the demand. The mechanism is that the symbolic target channels the base's anger. The false signal is energized, angry mobilization. The harm is that even a successful pressure campaign cannot produce the demand, because the target cannot grant it, and the effort dissipates without a win. The target must have the authority to deliver.

### Confusing Activity With Progress

The campaign holds rallies, publishes reports, and generates media, and the organizer reads the volume of activity as evidence of momentum. The mechanism is that visible action feels like forward motion. The false signal is busy calendars and press coverage. The harm is that activity without a theory of change does not accumulate pressure on the target, the base burns out, and the demand goes unmet. Each tactic must be linked to how it moves the target.

### Escalating Tactics Faster Than the Base Can Sustain

The organizer, eager to demonstrate strength, escalates to high-risk tactics early, before the base is deep enough or prepared enough to sustain them. The mechanism is that escalation feels like commitment and urgency. The false signal is dramatic action. The harm is that the base cannot sustain the escalation, turnout collapses, the organization is exposed as weaker than its tactics implied, and credibility with the target and allies is damaged. Escalation must be sequenced to match real capacity.

### Running a Campaign Without a Winnable Demand

The campaign is organized around a broad value or a general grievance rather than a specific, grantable demand, and there is no clear point at which it can be won. The mechanism is that broad demands feel more ambitious and more righteous. The false signal is a compelling moral framing. The harm is that the campaign cannot be won because no decision-maker can grant an abstraction, the base fights without the experience of winning, and the organization's power erodes. The demand must be specific and winnable.

### Ignoring Capacity and Overreaching

The organizer chooses a campaign scope that exceeds the organization's people, money, and relationships, assuming that commitment will close the gap. The mechanism is that ambition and urgency feel like the right stance. The false signal is bold goals. The harm is that the campaign collapses mid-way from exhaustion and resource depletion, the base is demoralized by a visible loss, and the organization is weaker for years. Capacity assessment must discipline ambition.

### Treating Negotiation as Betrayal

The organizer or base comes to see any negotiation with the target as selling out, so that the campaign cannot move from pressure to win. The mechanism is that purity of opposition feels more principled than compromise. The false signal is uncompromising militancy. The harm is that the campaign cannot conclude, the demand goes unmet, and the energy eventually dissipates without a win. Negotiation is the purpose of the campaign and must be prepared for.

### Winning the Policy but Shrinking the Base

The campaign secures the demand but exhausts its leaders, depletes its resources, and fails to develop new leaders or expand membership, so the organization is weaker after the win. The mechanism is that the policy goal crowds out the power-building goal. The false signal is a celebrated policy victory. The harm is that the organization has less capacity for the next fight, and the win is fragile without the organized people to defend it. The campaign must build power as it pursues the demand.

## Self-Check

- Did I define a specific, winnable demand that a defined decision-maker has the authority to grant?
- Did I map power accurately to identify the primary target who can grant the demand, or a secondary target who influences them?
- Did I choose tactics based on a theory of how each one applies leverage to the target, not on the desire to express outrage?
- Did I assess organizational capacity honestly before committing to the campaign's scope?
- Did I set and track base-building and leadership development goals alongside the policy goal?
- Did I read the political moment to determine whether the demand is winnable now, and conserve capacity when it is not?
- Did I prepare for negotiation, knowing my bottom line and trade-offs, and treat a negotiated material win as a victory?
- Did I ensure the campaign builds the organization's power and base, so it is stronger for the next fight?
