# Risk and Safety Screening

Risk screening is the structured attempt to detect danger — to the client, from the client, or within the client's environment — early enough to act. It is the single highest-stakes routine task in social work, because the conditions being screened for (suicide, homicide, abuse, neglect, intimate partner violence) are the ones most likely to end in death or serious harm, and because a missed or mishandled screen is the most common precursor to tragedy. Agents miss risk because the cues are often subtle, because clients minimize or conceal, because workers fear that asking about suicide will "put the idea" in someone's head, and because a negative screen can create false reassurance. This skill covers how to screen directly, how to interpret screens, and how to escalate appropriately — with the overriding rule that screening is worthless without a defined response pathway for every possible result.

## Core Rules

### Screen Every Relevant Client Directly and Routinely

Risk screening is not reserved for clients who "look" at risk; it is routine for every client in any setting where risk is possible, which is essentially all social work settings. Ask directly and in plain language: about suicidal thoughts and plans, about thoughts of harming others, about current or recent abuse and violence, about exposure to danger in the home, and about substance use that creates risk. Direct questions do not increase risk; the evidence is clear that asking about suicide does not plant the idea and instead relieves the client's isolation. Normalize the questions as standard practice so the client does not feel singled out. Screen at intake and re-screen when circumstances change — a loss, a relationship breakdown, a discharge, a deterioration — because risk is dynamic, not a fixed trait.

### Use Validated Tools and Structure the Inquiry

Unstructured risk impressions are subject to bias, hard to defend, and less accurate than validated instruments. Use the structured tools appropriate to the risk domain: validated suicide risk assessment frameworks, danger assessments for intimate partner violence, neglect and abuse screens for children and older adults, and substance use screens. The tool structures the inquiry, ensures key factors are covered (ideation, plan, means, intent, prior attempts, protective factors), and produces a documented basis for the decision. But treat the tool as a support for clinical judgment, not a replacement: a low score on a tool never overrides a worker's concern, and a high score demands action regardless of how the client presents. Document the tool used, the result, and your interpretation.

### Assess the Components of Risk, Not Just a Global Level

A useful risk assessment breaks risk into its components: ideation (is the person thinking about it), plan (have they worked out how), means (do they have access to the method), intent (how committed are they to acting), and history (prior attempts are among the strongest predictors). Also assess precipitants (what has changed or is about to change), protective factors (what is holding them back — relationships, responsibilities, beliefs, fear), and warning signs (behavioral changes, giving away possessions, saying goodbye). The interaction of these components determines the level of risk and the response, not any single factor. A person with ideation, a specific plan, access to means, and a recent precipitating loss is at high risk even if they minimize; a person with passive thoughts, no plan, and strong protectors is at lower risk but still requires follow-up.

### Distinguish Acute From Chronic Risk

Risk is not a single state; it fluctuates. Acute risk is the elevated danger in a defined window — the days after a breakup, a discharge, a loss, or a decompensation — and demands immediate, intensive response. Chronic risk is the longer-term elevation that comes from enduring factors (chronic mental illness, ongoing violence, persistent isolation) and requires sustained management rather than crisis response. Confusing the two leads to errors in both directions: treating chronic risk as acute burns out workers and over-restricts clients, while treating acute risk as chronic under-responds and leaves the client exposed during the dangerous window. Name which kind of risk you are assessing and match the response to the timeframe.

### Define a Response Pathway for Every Screen Outcome Before You Screen

The cardinal rule of screening is that you must know what you will do for every possible result before you ask the question. If the screen is positive for suicide with plan and means, what is the immediate action — who do you call, where does the client go, how is means restriction arranged? If the screen is positive for ongoing violence, what is the safety plan and where can the client go tonight? If the screen is positive for child or elder abuse, what is the reporting threshold and timeline? Never screen into a vacuum. If your setting lacks a defined pathway for a given outcome, escalate to a supervisor and build the pathway before relying on the screen. A positive screen with no response plan is worse than no screen, because it creates an illusion of safety management while leaving the client unprotected.

### Act on Concern Even When the Screen Is Ambiguous

Screens and tools produce ambiguous results more often than not, and clients in genuine danger frequently minimize, deny, or lack insight. If your clinical concern is elevated — based on presentation, history, collateral, or instinct — act on the concern even if the formal screen is negative or equivocal. Concern is data. This may mean a higher level of monitoring, a safety plan, a follow-up within a tight window, consultation with a supervisor, or arranging collateral information. Document the basis for your concern and the action taken. The cost of over-responding to ambiguous risk is usually recoverable; the cost of under-responding to real risk is often not.

### Restrict Means and Build Safety Plans as Concrete Actions

Risk reduction requires concrete action, not reassurance. For suicide risk, means restriction — reducing access to the method the client has identified or that is statistically most lethal (firearms, medications) — is one of the most effective interventions, far more than "no-suicide contracts" which have no evidence base. For violence risk, safety planning includes identifying safe destinations, code words, packed bags, and the lethality-reducing steps the client can take. Write the safety plan down with the client, in their words, with specific phone numbers and steps, and confirm they can use it. A safety plan the client cannot recall or access is useless.

### Document the Screen, the Reasoning, and the Response

Every risk screen must be documented with the tool, the result, the worker's formulation, the action taken, and the follow-up arranged — including when the screen is negative. The documentation is the evidence, in any later review or legal proceeding, that risk was considered and addressed; its absence is treated as its failure. Document the client's exact words for key disclosures. Document consultations and supervisor involvement. Document the safety plan and that it was given to the client. Treat the risk record as a living document that is updated as risk changes.

## Common Traps

### Fear of Asking About Suicide

Workers sometimes avoid direct suicide questions out of fear that asking will introduce the idea or worsen the client's distress. The trap mechanism is that the avoidance feels protective and compassionate, so the worker rationalizes it as sensitivity. The false signal is a calm interview that did not upset the client. The harm is that suicidal ideation goes undetected in exactly the clients most at risk, because the people most likely to act are often those most able to mask, and the worker has removed the one intervention — direct inquiry — most likely to surface it. The evidence is unambiguous that asking does not increase risk; not asking does.

### False Reassurance From a Negative Screen

A client denies suicidal thoughts on a screen, or scores low on a tool, and the worker treats the case as de-risked. The trap is that the negative result is salient and relieving, so it dominates the worker's memory of the encounter, crowding out the contextual factors (recent loss, access to means, prior attempt) that actually carry the risk. The false signal is a clean screen. The harm is that high-risk clients are discharged or deprioritized on the strength of a denial, when denial in a high-risk context is itself a warning. A screen is one input among many; it never cancels clinical concern.

### Screening Without a Response Pathway

A worker administers a screen because the protocol requires it, but has not thought through what a positive result would trigger. The trap is that the act of screening feels like risk management, so the worker believes the risk is being handled when in fact nothing is in place to act on a positive result. The false signal is a completed screen in the chart. The harm is that a positive screen leads to delay, confusion, or inaction during the exact window when speed matters, and the client is left unprotected by a system that appeared to be monitoring them.

### Relying on No-Suicide Contracts Instead of Means Restriction

No-suicide contracts (asking the client to promise not to harm themselves) have no evidence of effectiveness and can create false reassurance for both worker and client. The trap is that obtaining a signature feels like a concrete intervention and is easy to document, so it substitutes for the harder, more effective work of means restriction and safety planning. The false signal is a signed contract in the file. The harm is that the actual lethal means remains accessible and the worker has a false sense of security; clients who have signed such contracts have gone on to die by suicide. Means restriction and collaborative safety planning are the evidence-based substitutes.

### Confusing Acute and Chronic Risk

A client with chronic, enduring risk factors is treated as perpetually in crisis, receiving intensive interventions that exhaust resources and restrict autonomy without clear benefit; or an acutely elevated client is treated as chronically risky and given routine follow-up that is far too slow for the dangerous window. The trap is that "high risk" is treated as a label rather than a timeframe. The false signal is a risk level assigned without a temporal frame. The harm is mismatched intensity — either burnout and over-restriction, or dangerous under-response during the peak danger period. Naming acute versus chronic risk forces the right timeframe onto the response.

### Under-Responding to Ambiguous Concern

A worker has a gut sense that something is wrong but the screen is equivocal and the client minimizes, so the worker talks themselves out of concern to avoid overreacting. The trap is that ambiguity feels like permission to defer, and deferring feels prudent. The false signal is the absence of a clear positive. The harm is that the worker's accumulated clinical concern — often the most accurate signal available — is overridden by the ambiguity, and the client is left with inadequate protection during a genuinely dangerous period. Acting on concern through monitoring, consultation, and tighter follow-up is the appropriate response to ambiguity.

## Self-Check

- Did I screen every relevant client directly and in plain language for suicide, harm to others, abuse, violence, and substance-related risk, and re-screen when circumstances changed?
- Did I use a validated tool appropriate to the domain and document the tool, result, and my interpretation?
- Did I assess the components of risk — ideation, plan, means, intent, history, precipitants, and protectors — rather than assign a global level from a single cue?
- Did I distinguish acute from chronic risk and match the response intensity to the timeframe?
- Before screening, did I confirm a defined response pathway for every possible outcome, including the reporting pathway for abuse disclosures?
- Did I act on clinical concern even when the formal screen was ambiguous or negative, documenting the basis for concern and the action taken?
- Did I build a concrete, written safety plan with the client including means restriction for suicide risk and specific steps for violence risk, rather than relying on a no-suicide contract?
- Does my documentation capture the screen, the client's words, my formulation, the action, the consultation, and the follow-up — even when the result was negative?
