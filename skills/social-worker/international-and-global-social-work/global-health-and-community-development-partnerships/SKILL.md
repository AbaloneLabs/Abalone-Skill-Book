# Global Health And Community Development Partnerships

Global health social work and international community development partnerships carry power that is easy to misuse and harm that is easy to hide, because the worker arrives with resources, credentials, and frameworks from a higher-income context into a setting where the local system has its own history, structures, and expertise. The judgment problem is that the worker must contribute to health and development goals that the community itself has defined, while resisting the patterns of neo-colonial practice that import foreign solutions, displace local capacity, extract data and credit, and leave dependency or disruption behind. Agents err by treating the partnership as a delivery of expertise rather than a relationship, by measuring success in activities and outputs rather than in durable local capacity, by ignoring the power asymmetry that distorts every conversation, and by leaving when funding ends without having built anything that survives.

## Core Rules

### Let the Community Define the Problem and the Solution

The starting point of ethical global health and development work is that the community defines its own priorities, its own understanding of the problem, and its own vision of a good outcome. The worker's role is to support, resource, and accompany that agenda, not to import a pre-formed program. Begin with community assessment that listens before it proposes, that surfaces existing assets and leadership, and that distinguishes what the community wants from what an outsider assumes they need. Where the worker's framework and the community's framing diverge, the community's framing prevails unless there is a protection issue that requires raising it. Programs designed without community definition of the problem will be irrelevant, resented, or abandoned.

### Build Local Capacity Rather Than Deliver Services

The measure of sustainable global health and development work is whether local capacity to address the problem has grown, not whether services were delivered during the project. Prioritize train-the-trainer models, supervision and mentoring of local professionals, strengthening of local systems and organizations, and transfer of skills and resources to local control. Design every intervention with an exit or transition plan from the outset, identifying what local actors will carry forward and what support they will need. A program that delivers services through expatriate labor and collapses when the expatriates leave has built dependency, not capacity. Capacity building is slower and less photogenic than direct service, but it is the only durable contribution.

### Make Power Asymmetry Explicit and Compensate For It

Every partnership between a higher-resourced and a lower-resourced setting carries power asymmetry: the higher-resourced partner controls funding, sets agendas, holds credentials, and shapes what counts as knowledge. Make this asymmetry explicit with partners rather than pretending the relationship is equal, and design governance, decision-making, and budget structures that compensate for it, such as local leadership of the steering group, transparent budgets, and local authority over spending. Recognize that communities may agree to proposals they do not endorse because refusing risks funding or relationship, and create genuine space for dissent. Power unacknowledged reproduces itself in every decision; power named and structured against can be redistributed.

### Practice Bidirectional Learning, Not Unidirectional Expertise

Ethical partnership treats the engagement as bidirectional: the worker brings specific skills and resources, and learns from the community's knowledge, resilience, structures, and innovations. Approach the setting as a learner, document what the community does well, and resist the framing that expertise flows only from the higher-resourced partner. Local practitioners often manage complex problems with few resources in ways that are instructive, and dismissing their approaches as inferior reflects the worker's bias, not their error. Credit local knowledge in publications, presentations, and reports, and refuse to extract data or stories for academic or career advancement without genuine partnership and benefit to the community.

### Ensure Sustainability and Plan the Exit From the Start

Every project should be designed with its own ending in view, asking what will continue after external funding and personnel withdraw, who will carry it, and what resources they will need. Build sustainability into the design: local training, local ownership, integration into existing systems, and realistic assessment of what local resources can sustain. Avoid creating services or structures that require ongoing external support the community cannot provide, and avoid distorting local labor markets by paying above local rates or poaching skilled staff from local systems. Discuss the exit timeline honestly with partners from the beginning, and resist the pressure to expand scope to match funding when the community cannot sustain the expansion.

### Protect Data Sovereignty and Community Control of Information

Data collected in communities, including health data, stories, photographs, and program evaluations, belongs to the community and should be governed by community consent and control. Obtain informed consent for collection and for each use, including publication and secondary analysis, and recognize that consent to receive services is not consent to research or to use of one's story. Store and share data according to agreements that respect community sovereignty, return results to the community in accessible form, and do not publish identifiable information that could endanger or stigmatize participants. Photographs of children and of suffering require particular care and should generally be avoided or governed by strict consent. Data extraction without benefit to the community is a neo-colonial pattern.

### Attend to Equity Within the Community, Not Only Between Partners

Communities are not homogeneous, and partnerships can reinforce local inequities by working through elites, by reaching those easiest to reach, or by directing resources to those already advantaged. Assess who within the community is included and excluded, whose voices are heard in community representation, and whether the program reaches the most marginalized, including women, ethnic or caste minorities, people with disabilities, and displaced people. Equity within the community is part of ethical practice, and a program that serves the local powerful while claiming to serve the community reproduces harm.

## Common Traps

### Importing a Pre-Packaged Program

The worker arrives with a proven program from another context and implements it without adaptation to local conditions, structures, and priorities. The mechanism is that the program has evidence and momentum. The false signal is a well-run intervention. The harm is that the program is irrelevant or disruptive, local capacity is bypassed, and resources are wasted on what the community did not want. Programs must be co-designed, not imported.

### Measuring Success in Activities and Outputs

The worker and funder measure success in people trained, clinics held, items distributed, or visits completed, rather than in durable changes in local capacity, health outcomes, or community control. The mechanism is that outputs are countable and impressive. The false signal is a strong report. The harm is that activity is mistaken for impact, sustainability is never assessed, and the program ends having delivered nothing lasting. Outcomes and capacity indicators must accompany output counts.

### Short-Term Trips That Deliver and Leave

Short-term medical or service trips deliver concentrated care or labor and depart, leaving no follow-up, no capacity, and sometimes harm from interventions that cannot be maintained. The mechanism is that the trip feels impactful and the participants feel fulfilled. The false signal is grateful recipients and good photographs. The harm is that chronic conditions go unmanaged after the trip, acute interventions lack follow-up, local providers are undermined, and the trip becomes a service to the visitors rather than the community. Short-term engagement must be embedded in long-term partnership and local capacity.

### Extracting Data and Stories Without Reciprocity

The worker collects data, stories, or photographs for research, publications, or fundraising, treating the community as a source of material for the worker's advancement. The mechanism is that documentation feels like rigor or advocacy. The false signal is published outputs and raised funds. The harm is that the community's suffering is commodified, participants receive no benefit, and the worker advances on extracted material. Data and stories require partnership, consent, and reciprocal benefit.

### Paying Above Local Rates and Distorting Systems

The worker, intending to be generous, pays local staff or participants above local rates, or recruits skilled staff away from local health and social systems. The mechanism is that better pay feels fair. The false signal is a motivated team. The harm is that local labor markets are distorted, local systems lose staff, and the program becomes unsustainable at local rates. Compensation must be calibrated to local context and sustainability.

### Ignoring Power Asymmetry and Calling It Partnership

The worker describes the relationship as a partnership while the higher-resourced partner controls funding, agenda, and decisions, and local partners accommodate to retain support. The mechanism is that the partnership language feels respectful. The false signal is harmonious collaboration. The harm is that local voice is performative, decisions reflect the funder's priorities, and the asymmetry reproduces itself. Power must be named and structurally addressed.

### Treating Local Knowledge as Inferior

The worker dismisses local healing practices, community structures, or practitioner approaches as unscientific or backward, and imposes external frameworks. The mechanism is that the worker's training feels authoritative. The false signal is professional standards. The harm is that effective local practices are undermined, community trust is lost, and the intervention fails to fit the context. Local knowledge must be engaged as a starting point, not dismissed.

## Self-Check

- Did I let the community define the problem and the solution, rather than importing a pre-formed program?
- Did I prioritize building local capacity, with a realistic exit and transition plan designed from the start?
- Did I make power asymmetry explicit and structure governance, budget, and decision-making to compensate for it?
- Did I approach the engagement as bidirectional learning, crediting local knowledge and refusing data extraction without reciprocity?
- Did I design for sustainability, calibrating compensation and scope to what local systems can carry forward?
- Did I protect data sovereignty, obtaining consent for each use and returning results to the community?
- Did I attend to equity within the community, ensuring the program reaches the most marginalized rather than local elites?
- Did I resist measuring success in activities and outputs, and instead assess durable capacity and outcomes?
