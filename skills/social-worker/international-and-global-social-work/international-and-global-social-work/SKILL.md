# International And Global Social Work

International and global social work crosses borders of nation, culture, language, legal system, and economic context, whether through work with refugees and migrants, transnational practice with diaspora communities, development and humanitarian work abroad, or advocacy on global issues. The judgment problem is that the worker brings frameworks, methods, and assumptions formed in one context into another where they may not fit, where they may impose, and where they may reproduce the very power imbalances the profession opposes. Agents err by treating Western or dominant-context practice models as universal, by importing solutions without understanding the local system, by conflating helping with the worker's own cultural values, and by underestimating how legal status, language, and migration history shape what is possible and what is safe.

## Core Rules

### Understand the Local Context Before Importing Practice

Every social work method is shaped by the context it was developed in, and methods that work in one setting may be inappropriate, ineffective, or harmful in another. Before importing a practice, understand the local context: the culture, the family and community structures, the economic conditions, the political and legal system, the history (including colonial history and conflict), the religious and spiritual frameworks, and the existing helping traditions. Learn what local people and local professionals already do, and build on local strengths rather than replacing them. Practice that ignores local context is at best irrelevant and at worst a form of cultural imposition.

### Center Local Voices and Local Leadership

The people most affected by a problem and most knowledgeable about its context are the local people and local professionals, and effective global practice centers their voices, priorities, and leadership. Do not assume the worker, as an outsider or as trained in a dominant tradition, knows best. Listen to local people about what they need and want, support local leadership and capacity, and position external expertise as a resource in service of local priorities rather than the reverse. The most sustainable interventions build local capacity rather than creating dependency on external actors.

### Navigate Language and Communication Honestly

Language is not a neutral medium; it carries culture, meaning, and power, and working across language requires skilled interpretation and cultural mediation, not literal translation. Use trained interpreters, brief them on the purpose and content of the interaction, speak directly to the person rather than to the interpreter, and check understanding rather than assuming it. Recognize that concepts (mental illness, family, trauma, consent) do not map cleanly across languages and cultures, and that what is sayable and meaningful differs. Do not rely on family members or untrained bystanders to interpret sensitive material, and do not mistake shared language for shared meaning.

### Recognize the Power Dynamics of Cross-Border Practice

Global social work is shaped by power imbalances: between the worker's country or organization and the local setting, between dominant and marginalized traditions, between funders and recipients, and between the worker and the people served. Recognize these dynamics, name them where appropriate, and work to mitigate them rather than reproduce them. Be wary of the savior dynamic, in which the worker's need to help drives interventions that serve the worker's identity more than the community's needs. Accountability to the people served, not to the worker's self-image or the funder's framework, must guide the work.

### Understand How Legal Status and Migration History Shape Everything

For refugees, migrants, and displaced people, legal status (asylum seeker, refugee, undocumented, temporary, permanent) and migration history shape access to services, safety, rights, family unity, and future, and these are often the central facts of the case. Understand the relevant legal frameworks (refugee and asylum law, immigration law, the frameworks of the host country and country of origin), the person's status and history, and how these constrain and enable what is possible. Recognize that migration is often the result of persecution, conflict, poverty, or climate, and that the person carries that history. Do not treat legal status as a side issue; it is often the issue.

### Adapt Assessment and Intervention to the Cultural and Contextual Reality

Assessment tools, diagnostic frameworks, and intervention models developed in one context may not be valid in another, and using them uncritically produces distortion and harm. Consider whether a tool has been validated or adapted for the population and context, interpret presentation through the cultural lens (what looks like a symptom in one context may be a normative expression in another), and adapt the intervention to fit the cultural, familial, and economic reality. Use cultural consultation and local expertise to inform interpretation, and document the adaptations made and their rationale.

### Attend to the Ethical Obligations Specific to Global Practice

Global practice carries ethical obligations beyond the domestic: to practice within competence across cultural difference, to avoid harm through imposition, to respect local sovereignty and self-determination, to address the global inequities that produce the problems, and to represent the work honestly rather than in ways that exoticize or victimize the people served. Recognize the limits of what an outsider can and should do, and refer or collaborate with local professionals where their competence exceeds yours. Do not take on work you are not competent to do across the cultural and contextual gap, and do not let urgency override the ethical obligation to practice carefully.

## Common Traps

### Treating Dominant-Context Models as Universal

The worker applies practice models developed in a dominant context as if they were universal, without adapting them to the local context. The mechanism is that the models are the worker's training and feel correct. The false signal is evidence-based practice. The harm is that the intervention is irrelevant or harmful, local strengths are overridden, and the worker reproduces cultural imposition under the banner of professional expertise. Models must be contextually validated and adapted.

### The Savior Dynamic

The worker, motivated by care, centers their own need to help and rescues in ways that serve the worker's identity more than the community's needs. The mechanism is that helping feels good and moral. The false signal is compassionate engagement. The harm is that the community's agency and capacity are undermined, dependency is created, and the intervention serves the worker rather than the served. Accountability must run to the community, not the worker's self-image.

### Conflating Shared Language With Shared Meaning

The worker and the person share a language or use an interpreter and assumes the concepts and meanings align, missing the cultural differences that produce misunderstanding. The mechanism is that translation feels like communication. The false signal is a smooth interaction. The harm is that assessment and intervention are built on misunderstood information, consent is not real, and the person's actual needs and meanings are missed. Meaning, not just words, must be checked.

### Using Untrained Interpreters for Sensitive Material

The worker uses a family member, child, or bystander to interpret sensitive material to save time or resources. The mechanism is that someone present speaks both languages. The false signal is a practical workaround. The harm is that the interpretation is inaccurate, the sensitive material is exposed to someone who should not hear it, the person cannot speak freely, and the child or family member is burdened by the role. Trained interpreters are required for sensitive material.

### Ignoring Legal Status as the Central Fact

The worker focuses on psychosocial needs and treats legal status as a side issue, missing that status is often the determinant of safety, services, and future. The mechanism is that legal status feels like someone else's domain. The false signal is a focus on the person's wellbeing. The harm is that the central constraint is not addressed, services are planned that the person cannot access, and the worker misses the lever that would actually help. Legal status must be understood as central.

### Exoticizing or Victimizing the People Served

The worker represents or engages the people served in ways that exoticize their culture or reduce them to victimhood, for fundraising, awareness, or narrative purposes. The mechanism is that simplified narratives are compelling. The false signal is advocacy and awareness. The harm is that the people's dignity and complexity are stripped, stereotypes are reinforced, and the power imbalance is deepened. Representation must be honest and dignifying.

## Self-Check

- Did I understand the local context and build on local strengths before importing practice?
- Did I center local voices and local leadership, positioning external expertise in service of local priorities?
- Did I use trained interpreters and check meaning, not just words, across language and culture?
- Did I recognize and mitigate the power dynamics of cross-border practice, avoiding the savior dynamic?
- Did I understand the person's legal status and migration history as central, not peripheral?
- Did I adapt assessment tools and interventions to the cultural and contextual reality?
- Did I practice within my competence across the cultural gap, referring or collaborating where local expertise exceeds mine?
- Did I represent the work honestly and with dignity, avoiding exoticization or victimization?
