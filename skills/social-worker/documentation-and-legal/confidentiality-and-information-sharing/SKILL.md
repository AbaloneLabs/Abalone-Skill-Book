# Confidentiality And Information-Sharing

Confidentiality is one of the foundations of the social work relationship — the promise that what the client shares will be protected — and it is also one of the most frequently misapplied principles, in both directions. Workers over-apply confidentiality, withholding information that could and should be shared to protect the client or others, hiding behind "I can't tell you" when the law permits or requires sharing, and fragmenting care. Workers under-apply confidentiality, sharing information casually, for convenience, without consent or legal basis, or in ways that expose the client to harm. Both errors damage trust and endanger clients. Agents err by treating confidentiality as a blanket rule rather than a set of specific obligations with defined exceptions, by failing to explore what the client would consent to share, by sharing without a proper basis, and by not documenting the basis for their sharing or withholding decisions. This skill covers how to navigate confidentiality and information-sharing — with the overriding rule that confidentiality is the client's right, not the worker's, and the worker's task is to apply the specific legal and ethical framework, document the basis, and err on the side of what protects the client and the vulnerable.

## Core Rules

### Understand Confidentiality as the Client's Right With Defined Exceptions

Confidentiality is the client's right: the information they share belongs to them, and the worker holds it in trust, to be protected and shared only with the client's consent or on a defined legal basis. The exceptions that permit or require sharing without consent are specific and limited: mandatory reporting of abuse or neglect; warnings to protect against serious, foreseeable harm to identifiable others (the duty to warn/protect); sharing for the client's own care in emergencies; sharing between professionals for the client's coordinated care (within limits); and disclosures compelled by law (court orders, subpoenas, with protections). Know your jurisdiction's specific framework, because the details vary. Do not treat confidentiality as a blanket "I can't share anything" (which over-applies it and harms clients) or as a general discretion to share as judged useful (which under-applies it and violates rights). Document the framework applied to each sharing decision.

### Seek the Client's Consent First, and Explore What They Will Agree To

The default basis for sharing is the client's informed consent, and the worker should seek it first, explore what the client is willing to share and with whom, and document the consent specifically. Many sharing situations that feel like confidentiality dilemmas are resolved by simply asking the client what they consent to, and clients often consent to more than the worker assumes. Where the client consents, document what was shared, with whom, and the scope of the consent. Where the client refuses consent, the refusal itself is information that must be respected unless an exception applies. Explore the refusal: the client may consent to sharing some information but not all, or to sharing with some parties but not others, and a nuanced consent often serves the client's care better than a blanket refusal. Document the consent or refusal and the exploration.

### Apply the Exceptions That Permit or Require Sharing Without Consent

Where consent is refused or cannot be sought, apply the specific exceptions that permit or require sharing without consent. Mandatory reporting of suspected abuse or neglect overrides confidentiality when the threshold is met, and the report is made notwithstanding the client's wishes. The duty to warn or protect permits or requires disclosure when there is a serious, foreseeable threat to an identifiable victim. Emergencies permit sharing of information necessary for the client's immediate care. Coordinated care among treating professionals permits limited sharing on a need-to-know basis (within the framework the jurisdiction sets). Know each exception, its threshold, its scope (what may be shared, with whom), and its documentation requirements. Document the exception applied and the basis.

### Share on a Need-to-Know Basis, With the Minimum Necessary

When information is shared, whether with consent or under an exception, share only what is necessary for the purpose, with only those who need it, and no more. The "minimum necessary" principle limits both the content and the recipients: share the specific information needed for the specific purpose, not the whole record; share with the specific people who need it, not broadly. Over-sharing — sharing the whole record when a summary would do, or sharing with a broad team when one person needs the information — violates the client's confidentiality even when the basis for sharing exists. Document what was shared, with whom, and why it was the minimum necessary.

### Manage Confidentiality Across Agencies and Teams

Multi-agency work — common in child protection, mental health, and complex cases — requires careful management of confidentiality across the agencies involved. Each agency may have its own consent and information-sharing framework, and the client's consent to share with one agency may not extend to another. Establish at the outset what information will be shared among the agencies, on what basis, and with the client's documented consent or the applicable exception. Use information-sharing agreements where appropriate. Be cautious about sharing information that originates from a third party (a family member, another agency) that has its own confidentiality protections. Document the multi-agency information-sharing arrangements.

### Respond to Subpoenas and Court Orders Appropriately

Subpoenas and court orders compel the production of records or testimony, and they must be responded to appropriately, not ignored and not automatically complied with. Distinguish a subpoena (which requires a response but may be challenged or limited) from a court order (which generally must be complied with). On receiving a subpoena, notify the client (whose records are at issue), seek legal counsel, assert applicable privileges (especially therapist-client privilege, where it applies), produce only what is compelled and no more, and seek protective orders where the request is overbroad. Do not produce records in response to a subpoena without legal review and without notifying the client. Document the response to any legal demand for records.

### Protect Confidentiality in All Communications

Confidentiality is protected or breached in everyday communications, not only in formal disclosures: casual conversations in elevators or hallways, messages to colleagues, voicemails left on shared phones, emails to incorrect recipients, and discussions in open-plan offices. Protect confidentiality in all communications: verify the recipient before sharing, use secure channels, leave only necessary information on voicemails, send records to verified addresses, and conduct sensitive discussions in private. A confidentiality breach through carelessness is still a breach. Document the safeguards in routine communications.

### Address the Client's Right to Access Their Record

In many jurisdictions, clients have the right to access their own records, with defined exceptions (information that would endanger the client or others, third-party information). Know the client's access rights and the process for requests, and respond appropriately. Do not reflexively deny access; provide what the client is entitled to, with the specified protections for excepted information. The client's access to their record is part of their autonomy and their right to understand their own care. Document the access requests and the responses.

## Common Traps

### Over-Applying Confidentiality to Withhold Necessary Sharing

The worker applies confidentiality as a blanket rule, withholding information that could and should be shared (with consent or under an exception) to protect the client or coordinate care, hiding behind "I can't tell you." The trap mechanism is that strict confidentiality feels ethically safe. The false signal is principled practice. The harm is that care is fragmented, risks are not communicated, and the client is endangered by the withholding. Confidentiality has defined exceptions that must be applied.

### Under-Applying Confidentiality to Share Without Basis

The worker shares client information casually, for convenience, without consent or a legal basis, violating the client's right to confidentiality. The trap is that the sharing feels useful or routine. The false signal is coordinated practice. The harm is that the client's trust is betrayed, their information is exposed, and they may be harmed by the disclosure. Sharing requires consent or a defined exception.

### Failing to Explore What the Client Would Consent To

The worker treats the client's initial refusal or the absence of consent as the end of the matter, missing that the client might consent to some sharing with exploration. The trap is that the refusal feels definitive. The false signal is respect for the client's wishes. The harm is that the client's care is compromised by under-sharing that the client would have consented to with discussion. Consent should be explored, not assumed refused.

### Sharing More Than the Minimum Necessary

The worker, having a basis to share, shares more than necessary — the whole record rather than a summary, with a broad team rather than the specific person — violating the client's confidentiality beyond what the basis justifies. The trap is that thorough sharing feels complete. The false signal is comprehensive coordination. The harm is that the client's information is over-exposed, and confidentiality is breached despite a basis for some sharing. The minimum necessary must be shared.

### Casual Communications That Breach Confidentiality

The worker breaches confidentiality through careless everyday communications — hallway conversations, misdirected emails, voicemails on shared phones — rather than formal disclosures. The trap is that the communication feels routine. The false signal is efficient work. The harm is that the client's information is exposed to people who should not have it, and trust is damaged. Confidentiality must be protected in all communications.

### Automatic Compliance With Subpoenas

The worker receives a subpoena and automatically produces the client's records, without legal review, without notifying the client, and without asserting applicable privileges. The trap is that the subpoena feels authoritative. The false signal is legal compliance. The harm is that the client's privileged records are disclosed without the protections that legal review and client notification would have provided. Subpoenas require an appropriate response, not automatic compliance.

### Denying the Client Access to Their Record

The worker reflexively denies the client's request to access their own record, treating the record as the agency's property rather than the client's. The trap is that the record feels like the worker's professional document. The false signal is protecting the record. The harm is that the client's right to their own information is violated, and their autonomy is undermined. The client's access rights must be respected.

## Self-Check

- Did I understand and apply confidentiality as the client's right with specific, defined exceptions, rather than a blanket rule or a general discretion?
- Did I seek the client's consent first, exploring what they would agree to share rather than assuming refusal?
- Did I apply the exceptions — mandatory reporting, duty to warn, emergencies, coordinated care — at their specific thresholds and scopes?
- Did I share on a need-to-know basis, with the minimum necessary content and recipients?
- Did I manage confidentiality across agencies with clear arrangements and documented consent or exceptions?
- Did I respond to subpoenas and court orders appropriately, with legal review, client notification, and privilege assertions?
- Did I protect confidentiality in all routine communications, not only formal disclosures?
- Did I respect the client's right to access their record, with the specified protections for excepted information?
- Did I document the basis for every significant sharing or withholding decision?
