# Recordkeeping And Case Note Documentation

The case record is the durable, legal, and clinical account of the social work provided, and it outlives the worker's memory, the client's engagement, and often the worker's tenure. The judgment problem is that the record must serve multiple masters (clinical continuity, accountability, legal protection, billing, audit) that pull in different directions, must be written under time pressure that rewards brevity at the expense of completeness, and must be honest without being self-incriminating or client-damaging. Agents err by documenting too little to be useful or defensible, by documenting opinion as fact, by recording sensitive material without considering who may later read it, and by treating documentation as an afterthought rather than as a core clinical and legal act.

## Core Rules

### Document Concurrently and Accurately

Documentation should be done as close to the contact as possible, while the details are accurate, because memory degrades quickly and late notes are both less accurate and less defensible. Record what happened, what was observed, what was said, what was assessed, what was planned, and what was done, with enough specificity to be useful and credible. Distinguish what you observed from what was reported, use the client's own words for sensitive or clinically important statements, and record dates, times, and participants. Concurrent, accurate documentation is the foundation of a defensible and useful record.

### Write to the Multiple Audiences of the Record

The record will be read by future providers (for continuity), supervisors (for oversight), auditors and funders (for compliance), the client (who may request access), and attorneys and the court (in legal proceedings), and it must serve these audiences without being written for any one of them. Write professionally and objectively, avoid pejorative or speculative language, record clinically relevant detail, and anticipate that anything written may be read aloud in a proceeding or shown to the client. The record that is useful to a future provider and defensible in court is the standard; the record written only for billing or only for self-protection fails the other audiences.

### Distinguish Fact, Observation, Client Report, and Professional Judgment

In the record, distinguish what was directly observed, what the client or others reported, what was assessed, and what was planned or recommended. Use clear language: "Client stated..." (report), "Worker observed..." (observation), "Assessment indicates..." (judgment), "Plan..." (action). This distinction allows the reader to weigh the information by its source and protects against the conflation of report with fact that misleads future providers and is vulnerable in legal proceedings. Each entry should make clear what is known from what is inferred.

### Document the Clinical Reasoning, Not Only the Actions

A record that lists only actions (met with client, referred, closed) is a log, not a clinical record, and it fails to capture the reasoning that makes the care understandable and defensible. Document the clinical reasoning: why this assessment, why this plan, why this referral, what alternatives were considered, what the client wanted, what the risks and protective factors were, and what the basis for the decision was. The reasoning is what a future provider needs for continuity and what a reviewer needs to evaluate the quality of the care. Actions without reasoning are indefensible in retrospect.

### Document Consent, Refusal, and the Client's Perspective

The record should capture the client's informed participation: what they consented to, what they refused, what their goals and concerns were, and where their view differed from the worker's recommendation. Document the consent discussion for sensitive services or information sharing, the client's refusal of services or recommendations and the reasoning given, and the client's own words where they capture the perspective. This documents the client's autonomy and the worker's respect for it, and it protects against later claims that the client was not informed or did not agree.

### Protect Confidentiality and Sensitive Information in the Record

The record must contain the clinically and legally necessary information, but it must not contain information that is irrelevant, gratuitously sensitive, or harmful if disclosed, because the record may be accessed by people the client did not anticipate. Record what is necessary for the care and the accountability, use the client's own words for sensitive material only where clinically important, and avoid recording third-party information beyond what is needed. Understand who can access the record under what authority, and apply the principle of recording what you would be comfortable having the client read.

### Correct Errors Transparently

Errors in the record must be corrected transparently, not concealed, because the record's integrity is its credibility. Follow the agency's procedure for corrections (strike-through, initial, date, or the electronic record's audit trail), do not delete or overwrite in ways that hide the original, and document the correction and its reason. Concealed corrections, when discovered, destroy the record's credibility and the worker's, while transparent corrections preserve it. Never alter a record in anticipation of or during legal proceedings.

### Maintain the Record as a Legal and Ethical Obligation

Recordkeeping is not optional or administrative; it is a core legal and ethical obligation, and failure to maintain an adequate record is itself a form of negligence and unprofessional conduct. Understand the retention requirements, the security requirements, the client's right of access, and the worker's duty to maintain and to produce the record. Allocate the time for documentation as part of the work, not as an optional add-on, and do not let caseload pressure produce a record that fails the client and the worker.

## Common Traps

### Documenting Too Little to Be Useful or Defensible

The worker, under time pressure, documents the bare minimum (met with client, doing well), and the record is neither useful for continuity nor defensible in review. The mechanism is that brevity feels efficient. The false signal is a manageable workload. The harm is that future providers lack the information for continuity, the worker cannot defend the care if challenged, and the record fails its clinical and legal functions. Adequate documentation is non-negotiable.

### Documenting Opinion as Fact

The worker records inferences and judgments as if they were observed facts, blurring the distinction and misleading the reader. The mechanism is that the worker's interpretation feels like observation. The false signal is a confident record. The harm is that the reader cannot weigh the information by its source, the record is vulnerable in legal proceedings, and the client may be misrepresented by an inference recorded as fact. Fact and judgment must be distinguished.

### Recording Sensitive Material Without Considering Future Readers

The worker records sensitive material (trauma details, third-party identities, speculative diagnoses) beyond what the care requires, and the record becomes a source of harm when accessed. The mechanism is that thoroughness feels clinically responsible. The false signal is a comprehensive record. The harm is that the client's privacy is violated, third parties are exposed, and the record contains material that can damage the client in other proceedings. Only necessary sensitive material should be recorded.

### Late or Reconstructed Documentation

The worker documents long after the contact, reconstructing from memory, and the record is inaccurate and less defensible. The mechanism is that documentation is deferred under workload pressure. The false signal is a completed record. The harm is that details are wrong or missing, the timing undermines credibility, and the record cannot be relied upon. Documentation must be concurrent.

### Actions Without Reasoning

The worker records what was done but not why, producing a log that cannot support continuity or defense. The mechanism is that actions are easier to record than reasoning. The false signal is a clean, action-oriented record. The harm is that future providers cannot understand the care, and the worker cannot defend the decisions if challenged. The reasoning must be documented.

### Failing to Document Consent, Refusal, and the Client's View

The worker does not record the client's consent, refusal, goals, or differing view, and the record appears to show unilateral decisions. The mechanism is that these seem implicit. The false signal is a focused record. The harm is that the client's autonomy is not documented, later claims of non-consent are harder to rebut, and the record misrepresents the collaborative nature of the work. Consent and perspective must be documented.

### Concealed Corrections

The worker alters or deletes an entry to correct an error, concealing the original, and the integrity of the record is destroyed. The mechanism is that the correction feels cleaner without the original. The false signal is an accurate record. The harm is that the concealment, when discovered, destroys the record's credibility and the worker's, and may constitute tampering. Corrections must be transparent.

## Self-Check

- Did I document concurrently and accurately, close to the contact, with sufficient specificity?
- Did I write to the multiple audiences of the record, professionally and objectively?
- Did I distinguish fact, observation, client report, and professional judgment in each entry?
- Did I document the clinical reasoning behind the assessment and plan, not only the actions?
- Did I record consent, refusal, and the client's perspective and own words where relevant?
- Did I protect confidentiality by recording only necessary sensitive information?
- Did I correct errors transparently, following procedure and never concealing the original?
- Did I treat documentation as a core legal and ethical obligation, allocating the time it requires?
