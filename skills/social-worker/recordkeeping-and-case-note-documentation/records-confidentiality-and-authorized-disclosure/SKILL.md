# Records Confidentiality And Authorized Disclosure

Records confidentiality and authorized disclosure are governed by a layering of federal and state laws, professional ethics, and client consent, and the worker must navigate them precisely because every disclosure is a potential breach and every refusal a potential denial of a client's rights. The judgment problem is that the worker must release what is authorized and necessary, withhold what is protected, redact what falls in between, and do so under legal frameworks (HIPAA, 42 CFR Part 2 for substance use records, state mental health statutes, privileged communication laws) that interact in complex and sometimes stricter-than-expected ways. Agents err by treating HIPAA as a single permission rather than a floor with stricter overlays, by releasing on the strength of a subpoena that lacks authority, by failing to recognize the special protections on substance use and psychotherapy notes, and by releasing whole records when redaction of a portion would have honored both the request and the client's privacy. A confidentiality breach cannot be undone, and an unjustified refusal can deny a client access to their own information or to services that depend on it.

## Core Rules

### Identify the Governing Frameworks Before Acting on Any Request

Before responding to any records request, identify every framework that governs the record, because a single record may be subject to HIPAA, to 42 CFR Part 2 (if it contains substance use diagnosis or treatment information), to state mental health or HIV confidentiality laws, to privileged communication statutes (such as therapist-patient or social worker-client privilege), and to the agency's own policies. The strictest applicable rule controls, and applying only HIPAA when a stricter overlay applies produces a breach. Determine the record type, the information categories it contains, the jurisdictions involved, and the special protections that attach, and consult counsel or compliance when frameworks conflict or are unclear. The first step is always to map the legal terrain.

### Confirm the Authority for the Disclosure Before Releasing Anything

A disclosure is authorized only when there is a valid basis: a client-signed release of information that meets the requirements of every applicable framework, a court order of sufficient specificity, or a permitted exception (such as treatment, payment, operations, or certain mandated reports). Examine the authority carefully: a release must be specific as to what is released, to whom, for what purpose, and for what time period, and a deficient release authorizes nothing. A subpoena, in most jurisdictions, is not by itself authority to release protected records; it requires a court order or client authorization, and releasing on a subpoena alone may be a breach. Confirm the authority in writing before releasing, and when authority is ambiguous or conflicting, seek guidance rather than acting on assumption.

### Recognize and Apply the Heightened Protections for Sensitive Categories

Certain categories of information carry protections stricter than HIPAA and must be handled accordingly. Substance use disorder treatment records governed by 42 CFR Part 2 require a specific, compliant consent for most disclosures, restrict redisclosure, and apply even within a general medical record that contains substance use information. Psychotherapy notes (the therapist's private process notes) require separate authorization and are excluded from much of HIPAA's permitted disclosure. HIV/AIDS status, mental health treatment, genetic information, and reproductive health information may carry state-specific protections. Do not assume that a general release authorizes disclosure of these categories; apply the heightened standard, and when in doubt, segregate and protect. Treating sensitive categories as ordinary records is a breach.

### Release Only What Is Authorized and Necessary, and Redact the Rest

Even when disclosure is authorized, release only the information that falls within the scope of the authorization and is necessary for the stated purpose, and redact what does not. A request for records related to a specific incident or period does not authorize release of the entire record; a release for coordination of care does not authorize disclosure of unrelated sensitive content. Review the records against the request, identify what falls within scope, and redact what does not, documenting the basis for redactions. Over-release is a breach even when some release was authorized, and it is one of the most common and preventable failures. The discipline is to release the minimum necessary, not the maximum convenient.

### Handle Psychotherapy Notes, Process Notes, and Privileged Content Separately

Psychotherapy notes, when they meet the legal definition, are segregated from the rest of the record and require separate, specific authorization for release, with limited exceptions. Process notes, supervisor consultation notes, and other materials that may be privileged or protected must be identified and handled according to their status, not lumped into a general production. Privileged communications (such as those protected by therapist-client or social worker-client privilege in the jurisdiction) cannot be disclosed without a waiver or an applicable exception. Identify these materials early, maintain them separately if policy requires, and do not produce them on a general release. Conflating them with the general record exposes privileged content improperly.

### Document the Request, the Authority, the Scope, and the Disclosure

Every records request and the response to it must be documented, so that the agency can demonstrate what was requested, what authority supported the release, what scope was applied, what was released, what was redacted or withheld, and on what basis. Maintain the release or order, the log of disclosures, the redaction decisions, and the correspondence, because the documentation is the defense if the disclosure is later challenged. A disclosure that cannot be reconstructed from documentation is indefensible, and a disclosure that is reconstructed from memory is unreliable. Build the documentation as the request is handled, not afterward.

### Attend to Redisclosure, Minimum Necessary, and the Client's Right to Access

Two obligations often overlooked are the prohibition on unauthorized redisclosure and the client's own right of access. Recipients of protected information, especially under 42 CFR Part 2, may not redisclose without separate authorization, and the agency should mark disclosures accordingly. The minimum necessary standard requires that uses and disclosures for purposes other than treatment be limited to the minimum needed. And clients generally have the right to access and copy their own records, with specific exceptions, and that right must be honored promptly and without improper barriers. Confusing the obligations owed to outside requesters with the rights owed to the client produces failures in both directions.

## Common Traps

### Treating HIPAA as the Only or Controlling Framework

The worker applies HIPAA and releases accordingly, missing a stricter overlay such as 42 CFR Part 2 or state mental health law. The mechanism is that HIPAA feels comprehensive. The false signal is compliance with a known law. The harm is that protected information is released in breach of the stricter framework, the client's heightened privacy is violated, and the agency faces legal exposure. All applicable frameworks must be identified and the strictest applied.

### Releasing on a Subpoena Without Independent Authority

The worker releases records on receipt of a subpoena, assuming the subpoena is authority. The mechanism is that a subpoena looks official and demands response. The false signal is responsiveness to legal process. The harm is that, in most cases, a subpoena alone does not authorize release of protected records, the disclosure is a breach, and the client's information is disclosed without proper authority. A court order or client authorization is typically required.

### General Release Treated as Authorizing Sensitive Categories

The worker treats a general release as authorizing disclosure of substance use, mental health, HIV, or psychotherapy note content, when those categories require specific authorization. The mechanism is that the release looks broad. The false signal is that consent covers the record. The harm is that highly protected information is released without the specific authorization required, a breach occurs, and the client's most sensitive information is exposed. Sensitive categories require specific authorization.

### Over-Release of Whole Records

The worker produces the entire record when the request is for a specific part, because review and redaction take effort. The mechanism is that whole-record production feels thorough. The false signal is a complete response. The harm is that information beyond the scope of authorization is disclosed, the breach is broader than necessary, and the client's privacy is violated in areas unrelated to the request. Only what is authorized and necessary should be released.

### Failing to Segregate Psychotherapy or Privileged Notes

The worker includes psychotherapy notes, process notes, or privileged materials in a general production, not recognizing their separate status. The mechanism is that all notes look like part of the record. The false signal is complete production. The harm is that separately protected content is disclosed without the required authorization, privilege is waived, and the worker and agency are exposed. These materials must be identified and handled separately.

### Undocumented or Reconstructed Disclosures

The worker handles a request informally, releases without documenting the authority and scope, and later cannot reconstruct what was released or why. The mechanism is that the request felt routine. The false signal is a satisfied requester. The harm is that the disclosure cannot be defended if challenged, the agency cannot account for its disclosures, and the client has no protection against an unjustified release. Every disclosure must be documented as it happens.

## Self-Check

- Did I identify every governing framework (HIPAA, 42 CFR Part 2, state law, privilege) and apply the strictest?
- Did I confirm the specific authority for the disclosure (compliant release, court order, permitted exception) before releasing?
- Did I recognize and apply the heightened protections for substance use, mental health, HIV, psychotherapy notes, and other sensitive categories?
- Did I release only what was authorized and necessary, redacting the rest and documenting the basis?
- Did I segregate psychotherapy notes, process notes, and privileged content from general production?
- Did I document the request, authority, scope, disclosure, and redactions so the action can be defended?
- Did I address redisclosure restrictions and the minimum necessary standard for non-treatment disclosures?
- Did I honor the client's own right to access their records, with only the specific exceptions allowed?
