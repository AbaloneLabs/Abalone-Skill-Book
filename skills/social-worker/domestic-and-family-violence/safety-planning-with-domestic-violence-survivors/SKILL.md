# Safety Planning With Domestic Violence Survivors

Safety planning is the core, practical intervention of domestic violence social work: a collaboratively developed plan that reduces the survivor's danger in their specific circumstances, whether they are staying, leaving, or have already left. It is not a generic checklist handed to the survivor; it is a detailed, individualized strategy built from the survivor's expert knowledge of their own situation and the worker's knowledge of risk and resources. Agents err by treating safety planning as a formality, by imposing a generic plan that does not fit the survivor's reality, by planning only for the moment of leaving (ignoring that danger persists and often peaks after leaving), and by failing to account for the survivor's constraints (children, finances, immigration, disability) that make "just leave" impossible or dangerous. This skill covers how to build safety plans that actually work in real circumstances — with the overriding rule that the survivor is the expert on their own situation and their decisions about staying or leaving must be respected, because coercive control has already stripped them of choice and the worker must not replicate that by imposing decisions.

## Core Rules

### Build the Plan Collaboratively With the Survivor as the Expert

The survivor knows the abuser's patterns, triggers, surveillance, and the household's constraints far better than the worker ever will, and an effective safety plan is built from this expertise. Engage the survivor as the primary source: what has worked before, what the abuser does when angry, where the dangers are, what the survivor needs to stay safe. The worker contributes knowledge of risk factors, resources, and strategies, but the plan must fit the survivor's reality, not an abstract ideal. A plan the survivor did not help build, or that does not fit their circumstances, will not be used, and an unused plan protects no one. Document the collaborative process and the survivor's input.

### Plan for the Three Phases: Staying, Leaving, and After

Safety planning must address all three phases the survivor may be in, because each has distinct dangers. For a survivor who is staying, the plan focuses on surviving the current situation: identifying the safest rooms, escape routes, code words with children or neighbors, where to keep documents and money, and how to call for help safely. For a survivor who is planning to leave, the plan focuses on the departure: gathering documents, money, and essentials in advance; identifying where to go; timing the departure for when the abuser is absent; and the supports to activate. For a survivor who has left, the plan focuses on the ongoing danger: securing the new residence, managing contact (especially around children), protecting against stalking, and recognizing that the period after leaving is often the most dangerous. A plan that addresses only one phase misses the dangers of the others. Document the phase-specific plans.

### Address the Practical and Logistical Barriers to Safety

Safety is not only a matter of will; it depends on practical resources that the survivor may lack. Address the barriers: money (the survivor may have no independent finances due to financial abuse), housing (where will they go — shelter, family, and the limits of each), transportation, documents (identification, immigration papers, financial records often controlled by the abuser), children (schools, custody, the abuser's use of children), immigration status (which the abuser may have used as a threat), and disability or health needs. A safety plan that ignores these barriers is an abstraction; the plan must include concrete steps to address them. Connect the survivor to resources that fill the gaps. Document the barriers and the resources.

### Plan for the Children's Safety Specifically

Children in homes with domestic violence are at risk directly and indirectly, and their safety must be planned for specifically, not assumed within the adult plan. Plan for where children go during an incident, code words or signals to signal them to leave or hide, how to teach them to call emergency services (age-appropriately, without making them responsible for the adult's safety), and how to protect them from being used by the abuser. Where children are at risk of direct harm, mandatory reporting obligations may apply. Consider the impact of the plan on custody and the abuser's potential use of children to locate or control the survivor after leaving. Document the children's safety plan.

### Build in Technology and Digital Safety

Abusers frequently use technology to surveil, stalk, and control survivors — phone tracking, shared accounts, location sharing, spyware, social media monitoring — and a safety plan that ignores digital safety leaves the survivor exposed. Assess the digital surveillance risks: who has access to the survivor's phone and accounts, is location sharing on, could spyware be installed, are the survivor's movements trackable. Build digital safety into the plan: use a safe phone or device the abuser cannot access for sensitive communication, change passwords from a safe device, check for and remove tracking, be cautious about social media, and consider the metadata in photos. Provide resources on technology safety. Document the digital safety assessment and plan.

### Plan for the Most Dangerous Periods Specifically

Certain periods are the most dangerous for a survivor and require specific, heightened planning: the period around leaving; the period around legal actions (filing for protective orders, custody, divorce, criminal charges); pregnancy and the postpartum period; and any period of escalation or destabilization in the abuser. For each, build a heightened plan: where will the survivor be, who will know, what is the escape route, what is the backup. Recognize that protective orders, while valuable, can also trigger escalation, and plan for the period around their service. Document the heightened plans for high-risk periods.

### Connect the Survivor to Ongoing Specialist Support

A safety plan is not a one-time document; it requires ongoing support, updating, and the resources of specialist domestic violence services. Connect the survivor to a domestic violence advocate or service for ongoing work, to shelter or housing services if needed, to legal services for protective orders and custody, to counseling and peer support, and to financial and practical resources. The survivor's isolation is part of the abuse, and connection to supports is part of the safety. Make warm referrals, not just phone numbers. Document the referrals and the connections made.

### Respect the Survivor's Decisions, Including the Decision to Stay

The survivor may choose to stay, to return after leaving, or to delay leaving, for reasons that are real and that the worker may not fully see (financial dependence, children, immigration, love, hope, fear, lack of alternatives). Respect these decisions, even when the worker judges the situation dangerous, because the survivor is the expert on their own survival and coercive control has already stripped them of agency. The worker's role is to reduce harm and keep the door open, not to coerce the survivor into the worker's preferred decision (which would replicate the abuser's control). Continue engagement, continue safety planning for the current circumstances, and maintain the relationship for when the survivor is ready. Document the respect for the survivor's decisions and the ongoing engagement.

## Common Traps

### The Generic Checklist Plan

The worker hands the survivor a generic safety plan checklist, which does not fit the survivor's specific situation, abuser, or constraints, and which the survivor does not use. The trap mechanism is that the checklist feels like a complete intervention. The false signal is a documented safety plan. The harm is that the plan does not work in the survivor's reality and provides no actual protection. Individualized, collaborative planning is required.

### Planning Only for the Moment of Leaving

The worker focuses the safety plan on the departure and neglects the period after leaving, when danger often peaks. The trap is that leaving feels like the goal and the solution. The false signal is a departure plan. The harm is that the survivor leaves and is then at highest risk without a plan for the aftermath. Planning must address staying, leaving, and after.

### "Just Leave" Without Addressing Barriers

The worker advises or expects the survivor to leave, without addressing the financial, housing, immigration, childcare, and disability barriers that make leaving impossible or dangerous. The trap is that leaving is the obvious safety measure. The false signal is clear advice. The harm is that the survivor, unable to leave without resources, is left feeling they have failed, and the real work of addressing barriers is not done. The barriers must be assessed and addressed.

### Ignoring Digital Surveillance

The worker builds a safety plan without addressing the abuser's use of technology to surveil and stalk, so the survivor's communications, movements, and plans are visible to the abuser. The trap is that digital surveillance is invisible and easy to overlook. The false signal is a plan that looks complete. The harm is that the abuser discovers the plan and the danger escalates. Digital safety must be assessed and built in.

### The Worker Imposing the Decision to Leave

The worker, judging the situation dangerous, pressures the survivor to leave, replicating the coercive control the survivor is experiencing and overriding their agency. The trap is that leaving is clearly safer and the worker wants to act. The false signal is decisive, protective practice. The harm is that the survivor's autonomy is violated, the relationship may be damaged, and the survivor may disengage from the only support they have. The survivor's decisions must be respected.

### Children's Safety Assumed Within the Adult Plan

The worker includes children in the adult safety plan without planning specifically for their risks, code words, and the abuser's potential use of them. The trap is that children seem covered if the adult plan is covered. The false signal is a family plan. The harm is that children's specific risks are unaddressed, and they may be directly harmed or used by the abuser. Children's safety must be planned for specifically.

### The One-Time Plan Never Updated

The worker develops a safety plan at one point and never revisits it, so it becomes stale as circumstances, risk, and the abuser's behavior change. The trap is that the plan is done and feels complete. The false signal is a documented plan. The harm is that the plan no longer fits the current danger and provides no protection. Safety plans must be living documents, updated at every contact and change.

## Self-Check

- Did I build the safety plan collaboratively, treating the survivor as the expert on their own situation?
- Did I plan for all three phases — staying, leaving, and the period after leaving (often the most dangerous)?
- Did I assess and address the practical barriers — money, housing, documents, children, immigration, disability — that determine whether the plan is usable?
- Did I plan specifically for the children's safety, including code words, escape, and the abuser's potential use of them?
- Did I assess and address digital and technology safety — phone tracking, shared accounts, spyware, social media?
- Did I build heightened plans for the most dangerous periods — around leaving, legal actions, pregnancy, escalation?
- Did I connect the survivor to ongoing specialist domestic violence support with warm referrals?
- Did I respect the survivor's decisions, including the decision to stay or return, and maintain engagement without coercing them into my preferred decision?
- Did I treat the safety plan as a living document to be updated at every contact and change?
