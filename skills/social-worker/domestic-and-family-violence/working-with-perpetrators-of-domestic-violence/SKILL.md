# Working With Perpetrators Of Domestic Violence

Working with those who use violence is a distinct and high-stakes domain of domestic violence social work, and it requires a fundamentally different posture from working with survivors. The perpetrator is the source of the danger, and any contact, assessment, or intervention must be designed first to avoid increasing the danger to the victim and to hold the perpetrator accountable for their behavior. Agents err by treating the perpetrator as a co-victim or as a partner in a mutual conflict, by conducting couple or family work that endangers the survivor, by being manipulated by the perpetrator's presentation (which is often charming, reasonable, and focused on shifting blame), and by failing to recognize that the perpetrator's account is a tactic of control, not a neutral history. This skill covers how to engage perpetrators safely, maintain accountability, and coordinate with the survivor's safety — with the overriding rule that the victim's safety is the paramount consideration in every decision about the perpetrator, and that couple work is generally contraindicated where domestic violence is present.

## Core Rules

### Make Victim Safety the Paramount Consideration in All Perpetrator Work

Every decision about perpetrator work — whether to engage, what to assess, what to refer, what to document — must be made first through the lens of victim safety. Before any perpetrator engagement, assess the risk that the engagement itself poses to the victim: will the perpetrator learn of the victim's contact with services and retaliate? Will the perpetrator use the engagement to locate, surveil, or control the victim? Will the perpetrator use the worker's involvement to shift blame or undermine the victim's credibility? Where victim safety cannot be reasonably assured, do not proceed with perpetrator engagement, or proceed only with explicit safety planning for the victim. Coordinate with the survivor's worker (with appropriate consent and information-sharing protocols) so that perpetrator engagement does not catch the survivor off guard. Document the victim safety assessment in every perpetrator decision.

### Hold the Perpetrator Accountable for Their Behavior

The foundational stance in perpetrator work is that the perpetrator alone is responsible for their violence, and the work must reinforce this accountability rather than diffuse it. Do not accept explanations that shift blame to the victim, to substance use, to stress, to childhood victimization, or to circumstances; these may be context, but they are not causes, and accepting them as causes reinforces the perpetrator's minimization and denial. Name the behavior as violence and control. Hold the perpetrator to the expectation of non-violence and to the consequences of their behavior (legal, relational, parental). Accountability, delivered consistently and without hostility, is itself an intervention. Document the accountability stance and the perpetrator's responses.

### Do Not Conduct Couple or Family Work Where Domestic Violence Is Present

Couple counseling, family therapy, mediation, and joint sessions are generally contraindicated where domestic violence is present, because they endanger the survivor (who cannot speak freely in the perpetrator's presence and may face retaliation for what they say), reinforce the perpetrator's control (by treating the violence as a mutual problem), and obscure the accountability that is the core of the work. Exceptions are rare and require the violence to be past, the perpetrator to have taken full responsibility, the survivor to genuinely want the work and to be safe to participate, and a therapist specifically trained in domestic violence. Default to separate work with the survivor and the perpetrator, by different workers where possible. Document the decision and its rationale whenever joint work is considered.

### Recognize and Resist the Perpetrator's Manipulation of the Worker

Perpetrators of domestic violence are often skilled at presenting as reasonable, charming, aggrieved, or reformed, and at manipulating professionals to shift blame to the victim, to gain information about the victim, or to use the professional's involvement to their advantage. Be alert to common tactics: presenting as the "real" victim; minimizing the violence or framing it as mutual; using the worker's empathy and the worker's desire to see change; seeking information about the victim's location or activities; using the engagement to demonstrate "change" to courts or child protection without genuine change. Maintain a critical, evidence-based stance: verify claims against the record and the victim's account, do not accept the perpetrator's framing uncritically, and do not share information about the victim. Document the perpetrator's presentation and the worker's critical assessment.

### Refer to Evidence-Based Perpetrator Intervention Programs

Refer perpetrators to evidence-based perpetrator intervention programs (often called batterer intervention programs), which are designed to challenge the patterns of control, build accountability, and reduce reoffending, over a sustained period (commonly 26-52 weeks). Distinguish these from anger management, couples counseling, or generic counseling, which do not address the pattern of coercive control and can be counterproductive by implying the violence is about anger or mutual dynamics. Know the local programs, their approaches, and their effectiveness. Recognize that program completion does not guarantee change, and that the most dangerous perpetrators may be those who complete a program and use the "reformed" status to regain access. Document the referral and the program details.

### Assess and Manage the Perpetrator's Ongoing Risk

Perpetrators of domestic violence pose ongoing risk to their victims, to new partners, and sometimes to children, and this risk must be assessed and managed throughout the engagement. Use structured risk assessment tools designed for perpetrators (e.g., SARA, B-SAFER), and re-assess at changes in circumstances (new relationship, separation, legal proceedings, relapse). High-risk perpetrators require heightened management: information-sharing with the survivor's services, attention to protective orders, coordination with law enforcement and probation where involved, and vigilance for escalation. Recognize that risk is not static and that the period around separation, new relationships, or legal consequences is highest risk. Document the risk assessments and the management plan.

### Coordinate With the Justice and Child Protection Systems

Perpetrator work often intersects with the justice system (criminal charges, probation, protective orders) and child protection (the perpetrator's impact on children, custody and access), and coordination with these systems is part of risk management. Understand the legal context and the perpetrator's obligations, share information appropriately (within the limits of confidentiality and the victim's safety), and advocate for responses that protect the victim and hold the perpetrator accountable. Recognize that these systems can both protect and endanger the victim, and navigate them with the victim's safety central. Document the system coordination.

### Recognize the Limits of Perpetrator Engagement and Do Not Over-Promise

Perpetrator engagement has modest evidence of effectiveness; some perpetrators change, many do not, and the worker cannot predict or guarantee change. Do not over-promise to the victim, to the courts, or to child protection that the perpetrator will change, and do not base the victim's safety plan on the assumption of the perpetrator's change. The victim's safety must rest on the victim's own safety planning and the system's protections, not on the perpetrator's promised reform. Be honest with all parties about the limits. Document the realistic framing of the engagement's potential.

## Common Traps

### Treating the Violence as a Mutual or Relationship Problem

The worker engages the perpetrator and the survivor as parties to a mutual conflict, using couple or family approaches that imply shared responsibility for the violence. The trap mechanism is that relationship problems are real and the mutual framing feels balanced. The false signal is fair, even-handed practice. The harm is that the survivor is endangered (cannot speak freely, faces retaliation), the perpetrator's accountability is diffused, and the violence is reinforced as a shared dynamic. The violence is the perpetrator's responsibility; couple work is contraindicated.

### Being Manipulated by the Perpetrator's Presentation

The worker is taken in by the perpetrator's charm, reasonableness, or claimed victimhood, and accepts the perpetrator's framing of the violence as mutual, provoked, or overstated. The trap is that the perpetrator presents well and the worker wants to believe in change. The false signal is a productive engagement. The harm is that the worker becomes an instrument of the perpetrator's control, the victim's credibility is undermined, and the victim's safety is compromised. A critical, evidence-based stance is essential.

### Engaging the Perpetrator Without Victim Safety Planning

The worker engages the perpetrator without first assessing and planning for the risk that the engagement poses to the victim, and the perpetrator retaliates or uses the engagement to locate or control the victim. The trap is that the engagement feels like the intervention. The false signal is active perpetrator work. The harm is that the intervention endangers the very person it is meant to protect. Victim safety must be assessed and planned before any perpetrator engagement.

### Referring to Anger Management or Generic Counseling

The worker refers the perpetrator to anger management, couples counseling, or generic therapy, which do not address the pattern of coercive control and imply the violence is about anger or mutual dynamics. The trap is that these services are more available and feel like a referral. The false signal is a service connection. The harm is that the underlying pattern is unaddressed, the framing reinforces the perpetrator's minimization, and the victim remains at risk. Evidence-based perpetrator intervention programs are the standard.

### Over-Promising Change to the Victim or Courts

The worker, invested in the perpetrator's engagement, conveys to the victim, courts, or child protection that the perpetrator is changing, leading the victim to reduce their safety measures or the courts to allow contact. The trap is that engagement looks like progress. The false signal is an optimistic assessment. The harm is that the victim's safety is compromised by false confidence, and the perpetrator, who has not genuinely changed, regains access and reoffends. The limits of perpetrator engagement must be honestly conveyed.

### Seeking or Sharing Information About the Victim

The worker, in engaging the perpetrator, shares information about the victim's location, activities, or service engagement, or seeks information from the perpetrator that compromises the victim, and the perpetrator uses it to locate, surveil, or retaliate. The trap is that information-sharing feels collaborative. The false signal is coordinated practice. The harm is that the victim is exposed and endangered by the very worker meant to protect them. Information about the victim must be rigorously protected.

## Self-Check

- Did I make the victim's safety the paramount consideration in every decision about perpetrator engagement?
- Did I hold the perpetrator accountable for their behavior, refusing to accept blame-shifting to the victim, substances, stress, or circumstances?
- Did I avoid couple, family, or joint work, recognizing it as contraindicated where domestic violence is present?
- Did I maintain a critical, evidence-based stance, recognizing and resisting the perpetrator's manipulation of the worker?
- Did I refer the perpetrator to an evidence-based perpetrator intervention program, not anger management or generic counseling?
- Did I assess and manage the perpetrator's ongoing risk, using structured tools and re-assessing at changes?
- Did I coordinate with the justice and child protection systems to protect the victim and hold the perpetrator accountable?
- Did I honestly convey the limits of perpetrator engagement and avoid over-promising change to the victim, courts, or child protection?
- Did I rigorously protect information about the victim in all perpetrator engagement?
