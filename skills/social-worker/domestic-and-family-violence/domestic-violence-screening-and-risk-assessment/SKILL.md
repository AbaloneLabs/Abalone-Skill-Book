# Domestic Violence Screening And Risk Assessment

Domestic and family violence is common, often hidden, frequently escalating, and potentially lethal, and the social worker is often the first or only professional positioned to identify it. The stakes are extreme: the period around a victim's decision to leave, or around a disclosure, is the most dangerous time, and the most dangerous abuser is the one whose control is being threatened. Agents err by not screening at all (assuming the client would disclose), by screening in a way that endangers the victim (in front of the abuser, or in ways the abuser can discover), by treating a single "no" as definitive, by mistaking the absence of physical violence for the absence of danger, and by under-weighting the risk factors that predict lethal escalation. This skill covers how to screen safely, assess risk rigorously, and interpret the findings — with the overriding rule that the victim's perception of their own danger is itself a primary risk factor and must be centered, and that errors toward over-protecting are far less costly than errors toward under-protecting in a domain where the harm is death.

## Core Rules

### Screen Universally, Routinely, and Privately

Screen every client for domestic violence routinely, not only those who present with obvious signs, because violence cuts across every demographic and most victims do not disclose spontaneously. Frame screening as standard ("I ask all my clients about safety in relationships because it is so common and so important"), which normalizes the question and reduces the shame of answering. Screen privately — never in the presence of a partner, family member, or anyone who could be the abuser or report to the abuser — and verify privacy at the start (is anyone else in the room, on the phone, able to overhear). If privacy cannot be assured, do not screen for violence at that time; use the contact to build rapport and arrange a private follow-up. Document that screening was offered and, if not completed, why.

### Use Validated Screening Tools and Structured Risk Assessment

Use validated screening tools (e.g., HITS, HARK, the Woman Abuse Screening Tool) for initial identification, and follow a positive screen with a structured risk assessment (e.g., the Danger Assessment, ODARA) that examines the factors empirically associated with severe and lethal violence. Structured tools are more accurate than unstructured judgment, which is vulnerable to bias and to missing factors the worker does not think to ask about. Administer the tool thoroughly and document each factor. A structured assessment does not replace clinical judgment but structures and informs it. Document the tool used and the results.

### Assess the Specific Risk Factors for Lethal Escalation

Know and explicitly assess the risk factors that predict lethal or near-lethal violence, because their presence elevates risk dramatically. Key factors include: a recent or planned separation (the most dangerous time); the abuser's threats to kill (a strong predictor, often dismissed); access to firearms (a major lethality multiplier); escalation in frequency or severity of violence; strangulation history (a major predictor of future lethality — non-fatal strangulation dramatically raises the risk of later homicide); stalking and controlling behaviors; substance use by the abuser, especially with the above; pregnancy, especially first pregnancy; and the victim's sense of fear for their life. Assess each factor explicitly and document its presence or absence. The accumulation of factors matters more than any single one.

### Center the Victim's Own Assessment of Their Danger

The victim's perception of their own risk — their sense of how dangerous the abuser is, whether they will be killed, what will trigger violence — is itself one of the strongest predictors of future violence, because the victim knows the abuser and the patterns intimately. Always ask the victim how dangerous they believe the abuser to be, and weight their answer heavily. A victim who says they fear for their life is at high risk, regardless of other factors; a victim who minimizes may still be at risk (minimization is common for many reasons) but their fear, when present, is a critical signal. Do not override the victim's assessment with the worker's own judgment. Document the victim's self-assessed danger.

### Understand the Dynamics of Coercive Control, Not Just Physical Violence

Domestic violence is fundamentally a pattern of coercive control — power and control exercised through intimidation, isolation, financial control, emotional abuse, threats, using children, minimizing and denying, and physical and sexual violence — and physical violence is often only one tactic within this pattern. Assess the full pattern of control, not only physical incidents, because a relationship with severe coercive control but no recent physical violence can be extremely dangerous (the control is the danger), and a focus on physical incidents alone misses the lived reality. The absence of recent physical violence does not mean safety; it may mean the control is currently effective without it. Document the pattern of coercive control.

### Assess Risk to Children and Other Vulnerable Household Members

Domestic violence does not affect only the direct victim; children in the home are at risk of direct abuse, of witnessing harm (itself damaging and, in many jurisdictions, a form of maltreatment), and of being used as tools of control. Assess the presence and risk to children and any other vulnerable household members (elderly, disabled), and recognize that the presence of children elevates the victim's danger (the abuser may use them) and the complexity of leaving. Where children are at risk, mandatory reporting obligations may apply (see the relevant skill). Document the assessment of risk to children and others.

### Interpret the Assessment Conservatively and Plan Accordingly

Interpret the risk assessment conservatively: where the evidence is ambiguous or factors accumulate, plan for the higher level of risk. The cost of over-estimating risk (more intensive safety planning, more support) is far lower than the cost of under-estimating it (injury or death). Share the assessment with the victim (they have the right to know their assessed risk and it informs their decisions), and develop the safety plan collaboratively based on the assessment (see the safety planning skill). Where risk is high, connect the victim to domestic violence specialist services, shelter, and legal options urgently. Document the interpretation, the sharing with the victim, and the plan.

### Recognize the Limits of Risk Assessment and Maintain Vigilance

Risk assessment is a snapshot, not a guarantee; situations change rapidly in domestic violence, and a low-risk assessment can become high-risk with a separation, a pregnancy, an escalation, or an abuser's destabilization. Re-assess at every contact and at every change in circumstances, and maintain vigilance throughout the engagement. Do not treat a single assessment as settling the question. Document the re-assessments and the changes.

## Common Traps

### Not Screening at All, or Screening Once and Treating "No" as Permanent

The worker does not screen, assuming the client would disclose, or screens once, receives a "no," and never screens again, missing violence that emerges later or that the client was not ready to disclose initially. The trap mechanism is that screening feels intrusive and a "no" feels definitive. The false signal is a completed intake. The harm is that violence goes undetected, escalates, and the worker misses the chance to intervene. Routine, repeated screening is the standard.

### Screening Without Privacy, Endangering the Victim

The worker screens for violence in the presence of the partner, family member, or children, or in a way the abuser can discover (visible notes, messages, follow-up calls to a shared number), and the screening itself triggers violence. The trap is that the worker proceeds with screening without verifying privacy. The false signal is thorough assessment. The harm is that the screening, meant to protect, endangers the victim, sometimes fatally. Privacy must be verified before any violence screening.

### Dismissing the Abuser's Threats to Kill

The worker hears that the abuser has threatened to kill the victim and dismisses it as hyperbole or manipulation common in relationships, when threats to kill are among the strongest predictors of homicide. The trap is that threats are common in conflict and often not carried out, so they feel like bluster. The false signal is clinical calm. The harm is that a major lethality predictor is ignored, and the threat is fulfilled. Threats to kill must be weighted as a serious risk factor.

### Missing Strangulation History

The worker does not ask about non-fatal strangulation, or hears of it and does not recognize its significance, when a history of strangulation is one of the strongest predictors of future homicide. The trap is that strangulation may leave no visible marks and the victim may not name it as serious. The false signal is no visible injury. The harm is that a critical lethality predictor is missed, and the victim is later strangled fatally. Strangulation history must be explicitly assessed and weighted.

### Over-Relying on Unstructured Judgment

The worker assesses risk by impression, without a structured tool, and misses factors they did not think to ask about or that bias leads them to underweight. The trap is that experienced workers trust their judgment. The false signal is confident assessment. The harm is that the assessment is less accurate than a structured tool would have been, and risk is misjudged. Validated tools must structure the assessment.

### The Worker's Assessment Overriding the Victim's Fear

The worker, using the structured factors, concludes the risk is moderate, while the victim states they fear for their life, and the worker's assessment prevails, down-weighting the victim's fear. The trap is that the tool produces a number and the worker trusts it over the victim's report. The false signal is objective assessment. The harm is that the victim's fear — a top predictor — is overridden, and the danger is under-estimated. The victim's self-assessed danger must be centered.

### Equating No Recent Physical Violence With Safety

The worker learns there has been no recent physical violence and concludes the situation is safer, when the absence may reflect effective coercive control rather than reduced danger. The trap is that physical incidents are the visible measure. The false signal is a calm period. The harm is that the coercive control, which is the danger, continues unaddressed, and the risk is under-estimated. The full pattern of control must be assessed.

## Self-Check

- Did I screen the client for domestic violence routinely, privately, and framed as standard practice?
- Did I use a validated screening tool and follow a positive screen with a structured risk assessment?
- Did I explicitly assess the lethality risk factors — separation, threats to kill, firearms, strangulation, escalation, stalking, controlling behaviors, substance use, pregnancy?
- Did I ask for and center the victim's own assessment of their danger, weighting their fear as a primary factor?
- Did I assess the full pattern of coercive control, not only physical incidents?
- Did I assess the risk to children and other vulnerable household members, and consider mandatory reporting obligations?
- Did I interpret the assessment conservatively, planning for the higher level of risk where evidence is ambiguous?
- Did I share the assessment with the victim, plan collaboratively, and connect to specialist services where risk is high?
- Did I recognize the limits of a single assessment and commit to re-assessing at every contact and change?
