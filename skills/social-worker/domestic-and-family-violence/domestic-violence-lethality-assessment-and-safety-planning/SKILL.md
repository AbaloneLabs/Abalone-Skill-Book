# Domestic Violence Lethality Assessment and Safety Planning

Domestic violence homicide is among the most predictable of homicides, in the sense that the risk factors that precede it are known, assessable, and often present for weeks or months before the lethal event — and yet it remains among the most under-assessed risks in practice, because the factors are easy to ask about badly, easy to dismiss, and easy to defer to the survivor's own sense of risk, which the abuse itself has systematically distorted. The worker's job is to assess lethality risk using a structured tool, to weigh the survivor's perception alongside the objective factors (not in place of them), to build a safety plan that is concrete and tailored to the survivor's actual circumstances, and to recognize that the highest-risk period — separation, pregnancy, escalation — demands heightened, coordinated intervention rather than routine referral. This skill covers the judgment used to assess lethality and to plan for safety in high-risk domestic violence cases, with the overriding principles that the survivor's risk perception is data but not the whole picture, that certain factors sharply elevate lethality risk, and that the period around leaving is the most dangerous.

## Core Rules

### Use a Structured Lethality Assessment Tool, Not Unguided Judgment

Unguided clinical judgment of domestic violence lethality is unreliable, because the factors that elevate risk (access to firearms, strangulation history, escalation, threats to kill, separation) are weighted inconsistently and the worker's affective response to the survivor distorts the assessment. Use a validated structured tool — the Danger Assessment, the Lethality Assessment Program protocol, or an equivalent — to ensure the key factors are asked about consistently and weighted appropriately. The tool does not replace judgment, but it structures it and surfaces the factors the worker might otherwise avoid or underweight. Document the assessment, the factors present, and the resulting risk level.

### Screen Specifically for the Highest-Lethality Factors

Certain factors sharply elevate the risk of lethal violence, and the assessment must screen for them specifically and directly: a history of non-fatal strangulation (one of the strongest predictors of later homicide), access to firearms, threats to kill the survivor or the children, escalation in frequency or severity, forced sex, substance use by the perpetrator, separation or the survivor's attempt to leave, pregnancy, and the perpetrator's jealousy, control, and suicide threats or attempts. Ask about each directly, because survivors often do not volunteer them and because each one materially changes the risk picture. Strangulation history in particular must be asked about explicitly and its medical and legal implications addressed.

### Weight the Survivor's Risk Perception Alongside, Not Instead Of, the Objective Factors

The survivor's own sense of their risk is important data — survivors often perceive risk accurately, and their fear is itself a factor — but it is not the whole picture, because the abuse systematically distorts risk perception (normalization, minimization, the perpetrator's manipulation), and survivors may under-rate their risk for reasons of survival, hope, or psychological protection. Weight the survivor's perception alongside the objective factors from the structured assessment, and where they diverge — the survivor feels safe but the factors are severe — treat the case as high-risk and plan accordingly. Do not defer entirely to the survivor's reassurance when the objective factors indicate danger.

### Recognize and Plan for the Highest-Risk Periods

Lethality risk is not uniform over time; it spikes during specific periods: when the survivor is leaving or has just left, during pregnancy, during escalation, after intervention (arrest, protective order, disclosure), and around custody or separation proceedings. The period around separation is consistently the most dangerous. Identify whether the survivor is in or approaching a high-risk period, and intensify the safety planning, the coordination, and the surveillance during it. A survivor who is safe today may be at extreme risk next month when she files for divorce, and the plan must anticipate that.

### Build a Concrete, Personalized Safety Plan Tailored to the Survivor's Circumstances

A safety plan is not a generic checklist; it is a concrete, personalized set of strategies for the survivor's specific living situation, relationship, resources, and children. It addresses safety in the home (where to go, how to escape, where to keep documents and money), safety in public and at work (changing routes, informing trusted people, workplace security), safety around technology (phone, location tracking, social media, digital evidence), the children's safety and their own age-appropriate plan, the plan for leaving (where to go, what to take, who to call), and the post-separation safety measures (protective orders, changed locks, school notification). Build it with the survivor, in their circumstances, and revisit it as circumstances change.

### Address Firearms and Strangulation Specifically and Urgently

Firearms and strangulation are the two factors most tightly linked to lethal outcome, and they require specific, urgent action. Where the perpetrator has access to firearms, the safety plan and any legal interventions (protective orders, law enforcement involvement) must address firearm removal or restriction, because firearms in the home multiply the homicide risk. Where there is a strangulation history, address its medical implications (delayed injury, stroke risk, internal injury), its evidentiary value (it is a serious crime and a strong predictor), and the survivor's safety going forward. These two factors cannot be folded into a general plan; they demand focused attention.

### Coordinate Across Systems for High-Risk Cases

High-risk cases require coordinated response across systems — advocacy, law enforcement, the courts (protective orders, prosecution), housing, and sometimes child welfare — because no single system can provide the layered protection a high-risk survivor needs. Use a coordinated high-risk or fatality review team where one exists, share information appropriately across systems, and ensure the survivor has an advocate who stays with them across the systems. A high-risk survivor handed a list of referrals and sent to navigate them alone is a survivor whose risk is unmanaged. Coordination is the intervention for high risk.

## Common Traps

### Unguided Judgment Replacing Structured Assessment

The worker assesses lethality by impression, asking about some factors and not others, and weighting them inconsistently, producing an unreliable risk picture. The mechanism is that unguided assessment feels like clinical skill and the tool feels bureaucratic. The false signal is a thoughtful, individualized assessment. The harm is that the strongest predictors (strangulation, firearms, escalation) are missed or underweighted, and the survivor is treated as lower-risk than they are. A structured tool must anchor the assessment.

### Strangulation History Not Asked About

The worker does not ask directly about non-fatal strangulation, and the survivor does not volunteer it, so one of the strongest predictors of later homicide goes undetected. The mechanism is that strangulation is uncomfortable to ask about and survivors often minimize it. The false signal is a completed assessment. The harm is that the case is under-rated, the medical and legal implications go unaddressed, and the survivor remains at extreme risk without the intensified response the factor demands. Strangulation must be asked about explicitly.

### Survivor's Reassurance Accepted as the Risk Picture

The survivor says she is not afraid and does not think he will really hurt her, and the worker accepts this as the risk picture, closing the assessment. The mechanism is that the survivor's reassurance is relieving and feels like respecting her autonomy. The false signal is a survivor who "feels safe." The harm is that the abuse has distorted the survivor's risk perception, the objective factors are severe, and the case is treated as low-risk when it is high-risk. The survivor's perception is data, but it must be weighted alongside the objective factors.

### The Separation Period Treated as Routine

The survivor is planning to leave, and the worker treats this as a positive step without intensifying the safety planning for the highest-risk period. The mechanism is that leaving looks like progress and the danger of the transition is under-appreciated. The false signal is a survivor taking action. The harm is that the survivor enters the most dangerous period without the heightened protection it requires, and the risk of lethal violence spikes unmanaged. Separation demands intensified, coordinated safety planning.

### Generic Safety Plan Untailored to Circumstances

The worker hands the survivor a printed safety plan checklist that does not account for her home layout, her children, her technology exposure, or her resources. The mechanism is that a generic plan is quick to provide and a tailored plan takes time and collaboration. The false signal is a plan provided. The harm is that the plan does not fit the survivor's life and is not usable in the moment, so it fails when needed. The plan must be built with the survivor in their circumstances.

### Firearms in the Home Not Addressed

The assessment notes the perpetrator's violence but does not address firearm access, and the safety plan and legal interventions proceed without it. The mechanism is that firearms are politically and practically uncomfortable to address, and the worker may not see it as their role. The false signal is a plan focused on the violence. The harm is that the factor most tightly linked to lethal outcome goes unaddressed, and the survivor remains at multiplied risk. Firearm access must be assessed and addressed specifically.

### High-Risk Case Handed Off to Referrals Without Coordination

The worker identifies a high-risk case and provides referrals to advocacy, law enforcement, and the courts, but no one coordinates across the systems or stays with the survivor. The mechanism is that referrals feel like action and coordination is hard. The false signal is a survivor "connected to services." The harm is that the survivor must navigate fragmented systems alone during the highest-risk period, gaps and conflicts go unmanaged, and the layered protection she needs is never assembled. High-risk cases require coordinated, advocate-led response.

## Self-Check

- Did I use a validated structured lethality assessment tool, documenting the factors present and the resulting risk level, rather than relying on unguided judgment?
- Did I screen specifically for the highest-lethality factors — strangulation history, firearm access, threats to kill, escalation, forced sex, substance use, separation, pregnancy, perpetrator suicidality?
- Did I weight the survivor's risk perception alongside the objective factors, treating severe objective factors as high-risk even if the survivor feels safe?
- Did I identify whether the survivor is in or approaching a highest-risk period (separation, pregnancy, escalation, post-intervention), and intensify the response accordingly?
- Is the safety plan concrete and personalized — home escape, public and work safety, technology and evidence, children's plan, leaving plan, post-separation measures — built with the survivor in their circumstances?
- Did I address firearm access and strangulation history specifically and urgently, including legal, medical, and evidentiary implications?
- For high-risk cases, did I coordinate across advocacy, law enforcement, courts, and housing, with an advocate who stays with the survivor, rather than handing off referrals?
