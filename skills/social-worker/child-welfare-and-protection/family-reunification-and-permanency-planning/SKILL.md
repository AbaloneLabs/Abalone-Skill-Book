# Family Reunification and Permanency Planning

Permanency planning is the process by which a child in foster care moves toward a lasting family — ideally back to the parents through reunification, and otherwise to adoption, guardianship, or another planned permanent living arrangement — and it is governed by a tension that is easy to mishandle in either direction. Move too slowly toward reunification, and a child drifts in temporary care, accumulating losses and aging toward emancipation without a family; abandon reunification too quickly, and a family that could have been preserved is severed, often along lines shaped by poverty, bias, or inadequate services rather than by the child's best interest. The worker's job is to build a reunification plan that is specific, time-limited, and genuinely achievable, to measure parental progress honestly against it, to practice concurrent planning so that no time is lost if reunification fails, and to make the permanency decision on evidence rather than on optimism, fatigue, or pressure. This skill covers the judgment used to plan for and decide permanency, with the overriding principle that a child's need for a permanent family is time-bound and cannot wait indefinitely.

## Core Rules

### Build a Specific, Time-Limited, and Achievable Reunification Plan

A reunification plan is not a list of services; it is a specific, measurable set of conditions that the parent must meet, within the statutory timeframe, for the child to return safely. Specify the behaviors and capacities that must change (not merely "attend therapy," but what the therapy must address and what observable change is expected), the services that will support the change, the visitation progression that builds the parent-child relationship, and the milestones and deadlines. Ensure the plan is genuinely achievable — that the services exist, are accessible, and address the actual issues — because a plan whose requirements are unavailable or unrealistic sets the family up for failure and the child up for drift. Review the plan with the parent so the expectations are understood, and document it.

### Measure Parental Progress Honestly Against Observable Criteria

Progress must be assessed against observable, documented criteria — not against impressions of effort, attendance, or likability. Did the parent complete the substance use treatment and demonstrate sustained sobriety, or only attend? Did the parenting capacity improve in ways observable in supervised visits and at home, or only in the clinic? Did the mental health or domestic violence issues that brought the child into care actually resolve, or only go unmentioned? Honest measurement serves both directions: it identifies genuine progress that justifies reunification, and it documents the absence of progress that justifies a shift to an alternative permanency goal. Optimism that is not evidence-based keeps children waiting; pessimism that is not evidence-based severs families prematurely.

### Practice Concurrent Planning So No Time Is Lost

Concurrent planning means pursuing reunification actively while simultaneously preparing an alternative permanency path (often adoption or kinship guardianship), so that if reunification fails the child does not start over at zero. Concurrent planning is not ambivalence or dual loyalty; it is the acknowledgment that the statutory clock runs regardless, and that a child should not bear the cost of a late pivot. Identify the concurrent plan, take the steps that support it (placement with a family willing to adopt, early identification of kin, honest communication with the child and caregivers), and revisit it at every review. Concurrent planning done well reduces the time a child spends in limbo.

### Apply the Reasonable Efforts Standard and Document It

The law requires that reasonable efforts be made to prevent removal and to achieve reunification, and the worker must both make and document those efforts. Reasonable efforts means the services, referrals, visitation, and supports that a prudent system would provide to give the family a genuine chance — not a token referral or a service that does not exist in the community. Document each effort, the parent's response, and the outcome, because the record is what the court reviews and what protects the family's rights and the worker's decisions. Failure to make or document reasonable efforts can invalidate a termination proceeding and prolong a child's uncertainty.

### Shift the Permanency Goal on Evidence, Not on Optimism or Fatigue

The decision to shift from reunification to an alternative permanency goal (adoption, guardianship, APPLA) must be evidence-based and timely. The shift is justified when the parent has not made substantial progress within the statutory timeframe despite reasonable efforts, when the conditions that brought the child into care persist, or when the child's developmental need for permanency cannot wait longer. Resist both the optimism that keeps extending deadlines past the point of realism and the fatigue or bias that shifts the goal prematurely. The child's age, attachment, and time in care weight the decision heavily, because the window for a successful permanent placement narrows as children age.

### Engage the Child Age-Appropriately in Permanency Decisions

Children, including young children, have perspectives on their family, their placement, and their future that must be sought and weighed age-appropriately. Engage the child in a developmentally appropriate way about their wishes, their relationships, and their understanding of the plan, and document their input. The child's voice is not the sole determinant, but it is a required input, and permanency decisions made without it risk placing a child where they do not want to be or severing relationships the child experiences as primary. For older youth, the plan must reflect their goals and their emerging autonomy.

### Navigate Termination of Parental Rights With Procedural and Ethical Care

Termination of parental rights (TPR) is the most consequential legal action in child welfare, severing the legal relationship permanently, and it must be pursued on solid ground: documented reasonable efforts, clear statutory grounds, the child's best interest, and a viable permanency plan. Pursue TPR when the evidence supports it and the statutory clock requires it, but do not pursue it as a default or a shortcut. Ensure the procedural rights of the parents are protected (notice, representation, opportunity to be heard), and that the post-TPR permanency plan is realistic. TPR without a viable adoptive plan leaves a child legally parentless, which is its own harm.

## Common Traps

### Open-Ended Reunification Without Time-Limited Milestones

The plan lists services but no deadlines or measurable outcomes, and the case drifts as months pass without a decision. The mechanism is that open-ended plans feel supportive and avoid the confrontation of a deadline, while the statutory clock runs regardless. The false signal is a family "working their plan." The harm is that the child accumulates time in temporary care, attachment relationships form and break, and the window for a stable permanent placement narrows, all while the family believes it is progressing. The plan must have time-limited, measurable milestones tied to the statutory timeframe.

### Progress Measured by Attendance Rather Than Behavioral Change

The parent attends every service, and attendance is treated as progress, when the underlying conditions (substance use, domestic violence, mental health, parenting capacity) have not actually changed. The mechanism is that attendance is easy to document and feels like engagement, while behavioral change is harder to assess and slower to appear. The false signal is a compliant parent. The harm is that reunification proceeds, or the goal is extended, on the basis of participation rather than safety, and the child returns to unchanged conditions or waits past the realistic window. Progress must be measured against observable behavioral change.

### Concurrent Planning Treated as Disloyal or Abandoned

The worker avoids concurrent planning because it feels disloyal to the reunification goal or because it is administratively heavy, and if reunification fails the child starts over with no alternative in motion. The mechanism is that concurrent planning creates emotional and procedural tension, and single-track planning feels cleaner. The false signal is a focused reunification effort. The harm is that the child loses months or years to a late pivot, aging further past the window for a stable placement. Concurrent planning is a standard of practice, not an expression of doubt, and it protects the child's timeline.

### Reasonable Efforts Not Made or Not Documented

The worker refers to services that do not exist, or makes efforts but does not document them, and the court later finds reasonable efforts were not made, invalidating progress toward TPR. The mechanism is that documentation feels bureaucratic and the efforts feel sufficient in the moment. The false signal is a family "referred to services." The harm is that the legal foundation of the permanency decision collapses, prolonging the child's uncertainty and potentially requiring a restart of the timeline. Reasonable efforts must be both genuine and documented.

### Goal Shift Driven by Fatigue or Bias Rather Than Evidence

The worker, fatigued by a difficult case or influenced by bias, shifts the goal to adoption before the evidence and the statutory timeframe justify it, severing a family that could have reunified. The mechanism is that difficult cases erode optimism, and bias (by class, race, substance use history) shapes the perception of progress. The false signal is a decisive move toward permanency. The harm is the premature severance of a family and the placement of a child for adoption when reunification was achievable. The goal shift must rest on documented evidence and statutory grounds, not on the worker's fatigue or assumptions.

### TPR Pursued Without a Viable Permanent Plan

TPR is filed and granted, but no realistic adoptive or guardianship plan exists, and the child becomes legally parentless in long-term foster care. The mechanism is that TPR feels like resolution, while the post-TPR placement work is deferred. The false signal is a permanency decision made. The harm is that the child loses the legal family without gaining a permanent one, the worst of both outcomes. TPR should be pursued with a viable concurrent or subsequent permanency plan in place, so that legal severance leads to legal permanence.

## Self-Check

- Is the reunification plan specific, time-limited, and genuinely achievable, with measurable behavioral outcomes tied to the statutory timeframe?
- Am I measuring parental progress against observable behavioral change rather than attendance, impressions, or likability?
- Am I practicing concurrent planning so that an alternative permanency path is in motion if reunification fails, rather than running a single track?
- Have I both made and documented reasonable efforts, with services that actually exist and address the issues?
- Is any shift in the permanency goal based on documented evidence and statutory grounds, not on optimism, fatigue, or bias?
- Did I engage the child age-appropriately about their wishes, relationships, and understanding of the plan, and document their input?
- If pursuing termination of parental rights, is it on solid statutory and evidentiary ground, with a viable permanent plan in place so the child is not left legally parentless?
