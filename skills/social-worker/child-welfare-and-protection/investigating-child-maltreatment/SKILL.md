# Investigating Child Maltreatment

Child maltreatment investigation is the structured process of determining whether abuse or neglect occurred, who is responsible, what the ongoing risk is, and what intervention is required. It differs from assessment and service in that its purpose is fact-finding within a legal framework, and its findings can lead to removal, criminal proceedings, and the termination of parental rights. Agents err because investigation requires a posture (neutral, evidence-gathering) that conflicts with the helping posture of much social work, because children's disclosures are often delayed, partial, or recanted, and because the consequences of both false substantiation and false clearance are severe. This skill covers how to investigate thoroughly and neutrally, how to gather and weigh evidence including from children, and how to reach a defensible determination — with the overriding rule that the investigator's role is to establish facts that will withstand legal scrutiny, not to advocate for a predetermined outcome.

## Core Rules

### Adopt a Neutral, Evidence-Gathering Posture

Investigation is not advocacy. Approach the case with genuine neutrality about what occurred, gathering evidence that could support or refute the allegation, rather than building a case for a conclusion you have already reached. Confirmation bias — anchoring on the allegation and seeking only confirming evidence — is the central threat to investigation validity, and it is reinforced by the emotional gravity of abuse allegations. Actively seek alternative explanations for the evidence: an injury may have an accidental cause, a child's statement may reflect coaching or confusion, a caregiver's explanation may be true. Document the alternative explanations considered and why they were accepted or rejected. A determination that survives challenge is one that has already considered and addressed the counterarguments.

### Gather Information From All Relevant Sources

A thorough investigation draws on multiple sources: interviews with the child, each caregiver, other household members, siblings, collateral witnesses (teachers, doctors, relatives), and records (medical, school, prior reports, law enforcement). Triangulate: when independent sources converge, confidence rises; when they conflict, the conflict itself is data that must be explained. Do not rely on a single source, however compelling. Records are especially valuable because they are contemporaneous and less subject to retrospective editing — medical records of injuries, school records of attendance and behavior, prior CPS history. Document each source consulted, what they provided, and the dates. A determination based on one interview is far weaker than one built from triangulated sources.

### Interview the Child Using Forensically Sound Methods

The child's interview is often the central evidence, and its validity depends on how it is conducted. Use developmentally appropriate, open-ended, non-leading questions; leading questions can contaminate the child's memory and render the disclosure inadmissible. Begin with broad, open invitations ("Tell me about...") and follow the child's lead, using specific questions only to clarify. Avoid suggestive language, repeated questioning of the same content (which can produce false confirmation), and questions that imply the expected answer. Where a formal forensic interview protocol exists (such as a Children's Advocacy Center model), use it or refer to a trained forensic interviewer. Document the interview in detail, using the child's words. Recognize that children's disclosures are often delayed, partial, and incremental, and that the absence of a complete disclosure does not mean nothing happened.

### Weigh Disclosure, Recantation, and Denial Carefully

Children's statements about abuse are rarely straightforward: disclosures are often delayed, partial, told to a non-professional first, retracted under pressure, or recanted later. Recantation is common and does not reliably indicate that the original disclosure was false — it often reflects pressure from the perpetrator, family loyalty, fear of consequences, or the distress of the investigation itself. Weigh the totality: the original disclosure, the context, the consistency across tellings, the presence of corroborating evidence, and the dynamics that might explain a recantation. Do not treat recantation as proof of falsity, nor treat an uncorroborated disclosure as proof of truth; weigh the evidence as a whole. Document the reasoning. The same care applies to caregiver denial, which is the expected response whether the abuse occurred or not and has limited evidentiary value on its own.

### Assess Physical Evidence and Medical Findings

Where there is physical evidence — injuries, neglect signs, medical conditions — obtain and document medical evaluation by a qualified provider, ideally one with child abuse expertise. Distinguish findings that are diagnostic of abuse from those that are ambiguous or consistent with accidental causes; not all injuries are abuse, and over-interpretation of ambiguous medical findings has caused serious injustices. Document the medical findings precisely and the provider's interpretation. Consider the history offered for the injury: does it fit the injury's age, type, and severity? Is the explanation consistent across tellings? Inconsistencies between the injury and the explanation are significant evidence, but they require careful, expert-informed interpretation, not lay judgment.

### Determine Substantiation Against the Legal Standard

The determination (substantiated, indicated, unfounded, or the jurisdiction's equivalent) must be made against the specific legal standard, which varies by jurisdiction but generally requires some level of evidence that the maltreatment occurred — often a preponderance of the evidence or "credible evidence." Apply the standard as defined, not a personal threshold. The determination must be based on the gathered evidence and the reasoning that connects the evidence to the conclusion, documented explicitly. Identify the specific type of maltreatment substantiated (physical abuse, sexual abuse, neglect, emotional abuse) and the specific person responsible, as these findings have distinct legal consequences. A determination that does not state the standard applied and the evidence supporting it is legally vulnerable.

### Separate the Investigation From Service Provision

Investigation and ongoing service provision involve different postures and can conflict: the investigator must be neutral and evidence-gathering, while the ongoing worker builds a helping alliance. Where possible, separate these roles so that the family's disclosures to a service worker are not compromised by the investigative posture, and so that the investigator's neutrality is not eroded by a helping relationship. Where the roles are combined (common in some systems), be transparent with the family about the dual role and its limits. Document which role you are in for each interaction. Do not use a service relationship to gather investigative evidence through the back door.

### Document the Investigation to Withstand Legal Scrutiny

The investigation record is the evidence in any subsequent legal proceeding — dependency, criminal, or civil. Document every contact, interview, observation, record reviewed, and decision, with dates and sources. Use direct quotation for key statements. Document the alternative explanations considered and why they were rejected. Document the standard applied and the specific evidence supporting the determination. Document consultation with supervisors and experts. Anticipate that the record will be read by opposing parties, reviewed in court, and scrutinized years later. A determination without a thorough, contemporaneous record is a determination that may not survive challenge.

## Common Traps

### Confirmation Bias Anchored on the Allegation

The worker receives an allegation and begins building a case to confirm it, attending to evidence that supports the allegation and discounting or not seeking evidence that refutes it. The trap mechanism is that the allegation provides a salient starting hypothesis, and human reasoning naturally seeks confirmation, so the investigation narrows around the initial framing. The false signal is a coherent case that "fits." The harm is that exculpatory evidence is missed, innocent families are subjected to unjust findings and interventions, and the determination collapses under legal challenge. Active search for alternative explanations is the defense.

### Leading Questions That Contaminate the Child's Account

The worker, wanting to confirm the abuse, asks leading or suggestive questions ("Did Daddy touch you there?") or repeats questions until the child gives the expected answer. The trap is that leading questions feel like they are helping the child disclose, and the resulting answers feel like confirmation. The false signal is a child who "disclosed." The harm is that the child's actual memory may be contaminated, the disclosure becomes inadmissible, a genuine case is weakened, and in the worst case a false disclosure is manufactured. Forensically sound, open-ended interviewing is the only defensible method.

### Treating Recantation as Proof of Falsity

A child discloses abuse, then retracts under family pressure or investigation distress, and the worker treats the retraction as evidence the original disclosure was false, clearing the case. The trap is that the retraction is recent and salient, and it resolves the uncomfortable implication of the original disclosure, so it dominates. The false signal is a child who now says it didn't happen. The harm is that genuine abuse goes unsubstantiated, the child remains at risk, and the perpetrator is emboldened by the system's retreat. Recantation must be weighed in context, not treated as dispositive.

### Over-Reliance on a Single Source

The investigation rests on one interview — the child's, or one caregiver's — without triangulation, because that source is compelling or because other sources are hard to access. The trap is that a single coherent account feels sufficient, especially under time pressure. The false signal is a clear, consistent statement. The harm is that the determination rests on evidence that may be unreliable, uncorroborated, or contested, and collapses when challenged or when contradictory evidence emerges. Triangulation across sources is the standard.

### Lay Interpretation of Ambiguous Medical Findings

The worker, without medical expertise, interprets an injury or medical finding as diagnostic of abuse when it is ambiguous or consistent with accidental causes. The trap is that the finding seems to fit the allegation, and lay interpretation feels straightforward. The false signal is a confident-sounding medical note. The harm is that innocent families are wrongly substantiated, with devastating consequences, on the basis of misread evidence; conversely, dismissing genuinely diagnostic findings leaves children at risk. Medical findings require expert interpretation, documented from a qualified provider.

### Investigating Through the Service Relationship

The worker, also serving as the ongoing caseworker, uses the helping relationship to gather evidence the family would not have disclosed in an investigative context. The trap is that this yields rich information efficiently. The false signal is thorough case knowledge. The harm is that the family's trust is exploited, the dual role undermines both the investigation's neutrality and the service relationship's integrity, and evidence obtained this way may be challenged. Role separation, or at minimum transparency about the dual role, is required.

## Self-Check

- Did I approach the investigation with genuine neutrality, actively seeking alternative explanations and documenting why each was accepted or rejected?
- Did I gather information from all relevant sources — child, each caregiver, household members, collaterals, and records — and triangulate rather than rely on a single source?
- Did I interview the child using forensically sound, open-ended, non-leading methods, and document the interview in the child's words?
- Did I weigh disclosure, recantation, and denial in context, rather than treating recantation as proof of falsity or denial as proof of innocence?
- Did I obtain expert medical evaluation for physical evidence and rely on qualified interpretation rather than lay judgment of ambiguous findings?
- Did I make the determination against the specific legal standard, identifying the type of maltreatment and responsible person, with the supporting evidence documented?
- Did I separate the investigative role from service provision, or transparently manage the dual role, rather than investigating through the service relationship?
- Does my documentation capture every contact, source, observation, alternative considered, standard applied, and the reasoning connecting evidence to conclusion — sufficient to withstand legal scrutiny?
