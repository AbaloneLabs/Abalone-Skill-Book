# Solution-Focused And Narrative Approaches

Solution-focused and narrative approaches are strengths-based, constructionist therapies that shift the clinical focus from the analysis and resolution of problems to the co-construction of solutions, preferred futures, and alternative identity stories. The judgment problem is that these approaches require the worker to resist the pull of problem-analysis that defines most clinical work, to trust the client's expertise on their own life, and to use language as the primary tool of change, all while recognizing that the approaches are not appropriate for every situation and that their brevity and optimism can be misused to avoid necessary attention to risk, trauma, and structural conditions. Agents err by applying the techniques mechanically without the underlying philosophy, by using solution-focused optimism to bypass real suffering or danger, by externalizing problems in ways that absolve responsibility, and by treating these approaches as universally applicable rather than as one set of tools among several.

## Core Rules

### Understand the Philosophy, Not Only the Techniques

Solution-focused and narrative approaches rest on constructionist assumptions: that problems are maintained by the stories and patterns people construct around them, that change follows from constructing new stories and noticing exceptions, and that the client is the expert on their own life. The techniques (the miracle question, exception-finding, scaling, externalization, unique outcomes) are expressions of this philosophy, and using them without the philosophy reduces them to gimmicks. Practice from the understanding that the client's language and framing shape their experience, that solutions need not be logically tied to problems, and that the worker's role is to co-construct rather than to diagnose and prescribe. The philosophy is what makes the techniques coherent and effective.

### Co-Construct Goals and Preferred Futures With the Client

In solution-focused work, the goals are the client's, and they are constructed collaboratively through questions that help the client articulate what they want rather than what they do not want. Use the miracle question (if the problem were solved overnight, what would be different?), goal questions, and scaling to help the client define a concrete, behavioral, attainable preferred future. Do not impose the worker's goals or the goals the system prefers, and do not accept a goal stated only as the absence of the problem; help the client articulate the presence of what they want. The co-constructed goal guides the work and belongs to the client.

### Find and Amplify Exceptions

Exceptions, times when the problem is absent or less intense, are the raw material of solution-focused change, because they reveal that the client already has some of what they need and that change is possible. Elicit exceptions through questions (when is the problem not happening, when is it less, what is different about those times), explore them in detail (what the client did, what others did, what made it possible), and amplify them by connecting them to the client's goals and strengths. Exceptions are not dismissed as flukes; they are treated as evidence of the client's resources and as the foundation for building more of the preferred pattern. The focus on what works, rather than what is wrong, is the engine of the approach.

### Externalize the Problem to Separate It From the Person

In narrative work, externalization is the linguistic move of naming the problem as separate from the person, so that the person and the problem are not the same thing, and the person can relate to the problem rather than be defined by it. Externalize by giving the problem a name (the anxiety, the critic, the temper), speaking of it as an entity the person interacts with, and exploring the person's relationship to it (when does it show up, what does it tell you, how do you resist it). Externalization reduces shame and blame, opens new possibilities for action, and preserves the person's agency. Externalize the problem, never the responsibility for harm the person has caused to others, which must remain accountable.

### Re-Author Identity Stories Around Strengths and Values

Narrative therapy holds that people live by the stories they tell about themselves, and that problem-saturated stories (I am broken, I am a failure) constrain the person, while alternative stories (I am someone who has survived, who cares, who persists) open possibility. Elicit the person's values, hopes, and commitments, identify moments (unique outcomes) that contradict the problem-saturated story, and help the person re-author their identity story around these. The re-authored story is not a denial of suffering; it is a fuller account that includes the strengths and values the problem-saturated story excluded. The new story, told and retold, becomes the ground for new action.

### Use Language as the Primary Tool of Change

Both approaches use language as the primary tool: the questions asked, the words chosen, the reflections and summaries offered, all shape the client's construction of their situation and their possibilities. Choose language that opens rather than closes, that constructs the client as agent rather than victim, that names strengths and possibilities, and that avoids the pathologizing and definitive language that fixes the client in the problem. Attend to the client's language and use it, reflecting and amplifying their own words for change. The worker's language is never neutral; it constructs the reality of the session, and skillful use of it is the practice.

### Recognize the Limits and Contraindications

These approaches are powerful but not universal, and the worker must recognize when they are insufficient or inappropriate. Solution-focused optimism that bypasses active risk (suicide, abuse, danger) is dangerous; narrative re-authoring that reframes harm the person has caused without accountability is evasive; brief, strengths-based work that ignores structural conditions (poverty, racism, lack of resources) can blame the person for problems the system creates. Use these approaches where they fit, integrate them with other approaches where needed, attend to risk and structural reality, and do not let the optimism of the approaches override the necessity of assessment, safety, and justice.

## Common Traps

### Techniques Without the Philosophy

The worker uses the miracle question, scaling, or externalization as techniques without the underlying constructionist philosophy, and they become gimmicks. The mechanism is that techniques are easier to learn than philosophy. The false signal is skilled practice. The harm is that the techniques feel mechanical and manipulative, the client experiences them as inauthentic, and the approach is discredited. The philosophy must be practiced.

### Solution-Focused Optimism Bypassing Risk

The worker, oriented to solutions and strengths, bypasses active risk (suicide, abuse, danger) that requires direct assessment and intervention. The mechanism is that the approach focuses on the positive. The false signal is strengths-based practice. The harm is that a real danger goes unaddressed, the client is harmed, and the approach is misused to avoid difficult assessment. Risk must be assessed regardless of the approach.

### Externalizing Away Responsibility for Harm

The worker externalizes a problem the person has caused to others (violence, abuse) in a way that absolves them of responsibility. The mechanism is that externalization reduces shame. The false signal is narrative practice. The harm is that the person is relieved of accountability for harm they have caused, the victims are not protected, and the approach is misused. Responsibility for harm must remain accountable.

### Imposing the Worker's Goals as the Client's

The worker, under system pressure or personal conviction, steers the co-constructed goals toward what the worker or the system wants, and the goals are no longer the client's. The mechanism is that the worker knows what would be best. The false signal is collaborative goal-setting. The harm is that the client's agency is overridden, the goals do not belong to them, and the work loses its foundation. Goals must be the client's.

### Treating the Approaches as Universal

The worker applies these approaches to every client and every problem, regardless of fit, and forces the work into a frame that does not serve the situation. The mechanism is that the approaches feel broadly applicable. The false signal is consistent practice. The harm is that clients whose needs require other approaches (structured assessment, trauma processing, concrete resources) are poorly served, and the worker's rigidity limits the care. The approaches must be matched to the situation.

### Ignoring Structural Conditions

The worker focuses on the client's strengths, stories, and solutions while ignoring the structural conditions (poverty, racism, lack of resources) that produce the problem, and the work individualizes a systemic issue. The mechanism is that the approaches focus on the individual. The false signal is empowering practice. The harm is that the client is helped to cope with a problem the system creates, the system is not challenged, and the approach is complicit with the oppression. Structural conditions must be addressed.

### Denying Suffering Through Reframing

The worker, through narrative re-authoring or solution-focused reframing, denies or minimizes the client's real suffering, and the client feels unseen. The mechanism is that reframing feels helpful. The false signal is strengths-based work. The harm is that the client's pain is invalidated, trust erodes, and the reframe is experienced as dismissive. Suffering must be acknowledged before it is reframed.

## Self-Check

- Did I practice from the constructionist philosophy, not only the techniques?
- Did I co-construct the goals and preferred future with the client, ensuring they were the client's?
- Did I find and amplify exceptions as evidence of the client's resources and possibility?
- Did I externalize the problem to separate it from the person, while preserving accountability for harm caused?
- Did I re-author the identity story around the client's strengths, values, and unique outcomes?
- Did I use language deliberately as the primary tool, opening rather than closing possibility?
- Did I recognize the limits and contraindications, assessing risk and not bypassing it?
- Did I address structural conditions rather than individualizing systemic problems, and acknowledge suffering before reframing it?
