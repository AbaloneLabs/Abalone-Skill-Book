# Externalization And Reauthoring In Narrative Practice

Narrative therapy rests on the premise that people make meaning of their lives through stories, that many of the stories people carry are problem-saturated and shaped by dominant cultural discourses that locate the problem inside the person, and that the therapeutic task is to externalize the problem, uncover the unique outcomes that contradict it, and help the client reauthor a thicker, preferred story grounded in their own values and lived experience. The judgment problem is that the techniques of externalization and reauthoring are subtle and easily reduced to linguistic relabeling that sounds narrative but leaves the problem-saturated story intact, and that the worker must hold a genuinely non-pathologizing, collaborative stance while still engaging with real suffering and risk. Agents err by using externalization as a verbal trick rather than a relational practice, by hunting for unique outcomes before establishing the problem story, by thickening alternative stories the worker prefers rather than the ones the client's life supports, and by deploying therapeutic documents as gimmicks rather than as tools that consolidate an emerging identity.

## Core Rules

### Externalize the Problem as Separate From the Person's Identity

Externalization is the foundational move of narrative practice, and it consists of naming and personifying the problem as an entity separate from the person, so that the person and the problem can stand in a different relationship rather than being fused. Use language that externalizes: not "you are anxious," but "how is Anxiety operating in your life right now"; not "you are a failure," but "what does The Critic tell you, and when does it speak loudest." Develop the externalized problem with the client's own language and imagery, name it collaboratively, and map its influence across the person's life, relationships, and identity. The function is not mere wordplay; it is to create space between the person and the problem so that the person can act upon it rather than being defined by it, and so that their identity is not reduced to the problem's story.

### Elicit and Map the Problem-Saturated Story Before Seeking Alternatives

Before reauthoring can occur, the dominant, problem-saturated story must be heard, understood, and mapped in detail, because the alternative story can only emerge in relation to the problem story it contradicts. Listen for the problem-saturated narrative, the beliefs about the self, the world, and the future that the problem has authored, and map its influence: how it speaks, when it is loudest, what it tells the person about who they are, what effects it has on their relationships and actions, and what sustains it. Honor the suffering in this story rather than rushing past it, because clients need their experience validated before they can consider alternatives. Skipping the problem story to jump to strengths feels dismissive and produces alternative stories that have no grounding.

### Identify Unique Outcomes That Contradict the Dominant Story

Unique outcomes, sometimes called sparkling moments, are the lived experiences that contradict the problem-saturated story, the times the person resisted, defied, survived, or acted against the problem, and they are the seeds from which an alternative story grows. Search for them deliberately: when was the problem's voice weaker, when did the person act against its instructions, when did they make a choice the problem would not have allowed, what did they do that surprised them. These moments are often dismissed by the client as trivial, accidental, or too small to matter, and the worker's task is to recognize their significance and draw them out. A unique outcome is not invented; it is uncovered, and it carries the client's own agency and values, which is what makes it a foundation for reauthoring rather than an imposed solution.

### Reauthor by Thickening the Alternative Story Across Time and Relationships

Reauthoring is the process of building a new, preferred story around the unique outcomes, thickening it by exploring its history, its implications, and its connections to the person's values, relationships, and identity. Take a unique outcome and thicken it: what does this moment say about what the person values, who they are when the problem is not in charge, what other times in their life it connects to, who in their life would recognize this version of them, what future it opens. The alternative story must be grounded in the client's actual lived experience and their own preferred values, not in the worker's agenda, and it must be thickened across multiple domains, past, present, relationships, identity, and future, so that it becomes a robust narrative the person can inhabit rather than a single encouraging moment.

### Ground Reauthoring in the Client's Own Values and Cultural Context

The preferred story that emerges through reauthoring must be grounded in the client's own values, cultural context, and chosen communities, not in dominant cultural discourses or the worker's assumptions about what a good life looks like. Elicit the values that underlie the unique outcomes, connect them to the cultural traditions, relationships, and commitments the client holds meaningful, and resist imposing mainstream or professional ideals of health, success, or normalcy. Narrative practice is explicitly attentive to the power of dominant discourses, including those the worker may carry, to colonize a person's story, and reauthoring that ignores the client's cultural ground produces an alternative story that is not authentically theirs. The client is the primary author; the worker is a collaborator.

### Use Therapeutic Documents to Consolidate and Sustain the New Story

Therapeutic documents, letters, certificates, declarations, or recorded statements that capture and affirm the emerging alternative story, are tools that consolidate reauthoring and extend it beyond the session. Write a letter to the client that summarizes the unique outcomes, the values they reveal, and the alternative story taking shape, using the client's own language; or co-create a document that marks a milestone in the person's relationship to the problem. These documents are read and reread, shared with supportive others, and become part of the person's narrative environment, reinforcing the preferred story against the problem's return. Use them purposefully, grounded in the actual work, not as formulaic exercises, because a document that does not capture the client's real emerging story will ring false and be discarded.

### Hold a Collaborative, Non-Pathologizing, Decentered Stance

Narrative practice requires the worker to occupy a decentered but influential position, in which the client is the expert on their own life and the worker is a collaborator who asks questions and facilitates the client's authoring, rather than an authority who diagnoses and prescribes. Hold this stance consistently: use questions rather than interpretations, follow the client's lead on language and direction, avoid pathologizing labels and deficit framing, and stay aware of the power dynamics, including those of culture, class, and profession, that shape the conversation. A worker who slips into the expert role, however benevolently, undermines the reauthoring process, because the alternative story must be authored by the client to be owned and sustained. The stance is the method.

## Common Traps

### Externalization as Verbal Trick Without Relational Practice

The worker relabels the problem using externalizing language, calling depression "The Black Dog" or anxiety "The Worry," but does not develop the externalization relationally or map its influence, so the technique produces new vocabulary without changing the person's fused relationship to the problem. The mechanism is that the relabeling sounds narrative. The false signal is sophisticated language. The harm is that the problem-saturated story remains intact beneath the new words, and the client and worker mistake linguistic change for therapeutic change. Externalization must be a relational practice, not a relabeling.

### Jumping to Strengths Before the Problem Story Is Heard

The worker, eager to reauthor, rushes past the problem-saturated story to identify strengths and unique outcomes, leaving the client feeling that their suffering was not heard and that the alternative story is imposed rather than earned. The mechanism is that strengths work feels positive and efficient. The false signal is an uplifting session. The harm is that the client feels dismissed, the alternative story has no grounding in contrast to the problem, and the reauthoring does not hold. The problem story must be mapped first.

### Imposing the Worker's Preferred Alternative Story

The worker, finding a unique outcome, thickens it toward an alternative story that reflects the worker's values or professional ideals, such as independence, productivity, or emotional regulation, rather than the client's own values and lived experience, producing a preferred story that is not authentically the client's. The mechanism is that the worker believes they know what a good outcome looks like. The false signal is a coherent, sensible reauthoring. The harm is that the story is not owned, it does not sustain, and the client's own values are overridden. The alternative story must be the client's own.

### Treating Unique Outcomes as Trivial or Accidental

The worker or the client dismisses a unique outcome as too small, accidental, or unrepresentative to matter, and the seed of the alternative story is lost before it can be thickened. The mechanism is that unique outcomes feel insignificant against the weight of the problem-saturated story. The false signal is that the moment does not count. The harm is that the client's unrecognized agency remains invisible, and the foundation for reauthoring is discarded. Unique outcomes must be recognized and drawn out as significant.

### Therapeutic Documents as Gimmicks

The worker produces a therapeutic letter or certificate as a formulaic exercise, without grounding it in the actual unique outcomes and emerging story of the work, and the document feels generic and performative to the client. The mechanism is that documents are easy to generate and look like tangible products. The false signal is a concrete takeaway. The harm is that the client experiences the document as inauthentic, it is not used or internalized, and the tool is discredited. Documents must capture the client's real emerging story.

### Colonizing the Story With Dominant Discourse

The worker, unaware of the dominant cultural and professional discourses they carry, shapes the reauthoring toward mainstream ideals of health, success, or normalcy that do not reflect the client's cultural context or chosen values, and the alternative story becomes a vehicle for assimilation rather than authentic self-authoring. The mechanism is that dominant discourse feels neutral and correct. The false signal is a culturally competent outcome. The harm is that the client's own cultural ground is overridden, the story is not theirs, and narrative practice becomes another form of colonization. The client's cultural context must ground the reauthoring.

### Slipping Into the Expert Role Under Pressure

The worker, under pressure to produce outcomes or faced with a client who seems stuck, abandons the decentered, collaborative stance and begins interpreting, diagnosing, or prescribing, undermining the client's authorship and the reauthoring process. The mechanism is that the expert role feels efficient and responsible. The false signal is decisive intervention. The harm is that the client is disempowered, the alternative story is no longer theirs, and the method is violated. The collaborative stance must be held even under pressure.

## Self-Check

- Did I externalize the problem as separate from the person's identity, developing it relationally and mapping its influence, not merely relabeling it?
- Did I elicit and map the problem-saturated story in detail, honoring the suffering, before seeking alternatives?
- Did I search deliberately for unique outcomes that contradict the dominant story, recognizing their significance even when the client dismissed them?
- Did I reauthor by thickening the alternative story across time, relationships, identity, and future, grounded in the client's actual lived experience?
- Did I ground the reauthoring in the client's own values and cultural context, resisting dominant discourse and professional assumptions?
- Did I use therapeutic documents purposefully to consolidate the emerging story, grounded in the actual work, not as formulaic exercises?
- Did I hold a collaborative, non-pathologizing, decentered stance, using questions rather than interpretations and following the client's lead?
- Did I avoid imposing my preferred alternative story and avoid slipping into the expert role under pressure?
