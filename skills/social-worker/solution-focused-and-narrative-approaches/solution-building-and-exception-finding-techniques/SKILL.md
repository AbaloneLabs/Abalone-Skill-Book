# Solution-Building And Exception-Finding Techniques

Solution-focused brief therapy is a coherent approach that builds change by attending to what the client wants instead of what is wrong, to the times the problem is absent, and to the resources the client already possesses, and it requires disciplined restraint from the problem-analysis habits that dominate most helping conversations. The judgment problem is that workers trained in deficit and pathology models reflexively investigate the problem's history and causes, which solution-focused work deliberately sets aside, and that the techniques, though simple in form, are precise in function and easily reduced to cheerful gimmicks that please the worker but do not build change. Agents err by deploying the techniques as isolated tricks rather than as an integrated stance, by using the miracle question to gather goals the worker finds reasonable rather than the client's own, by forcing a solution-focused frame onto situations that require risk assessment or crisis stabilization, and by mistaking the client's polite compliance for genuine co-construction of a preferred future.

## Core Rules

### Adopt the Solution-Focused Stance Before Deploying Any Technique

The techniques of solution-focused work, the miracle question, exception-finding, scaling, and coping questions, are expressions of a coherent stance, and they fail when used as isolated tools stripped from that stance. The stance holds that the client is the expert on their own life, that change is constant and the worker's task is to notice it, that the worker does not need to know the cause of a problem to help solve it, and that small changes can initiate larger ones. Adopt this stance fully before using any technique: listen for resources and strengths as carefully as for deficits, assume the client has competencies they have not yet recognized, and resist the pull to diagnose and explain. A technique used without the stance produces shallow, performative sessions that the client experiences as the worker not taking their suffering seriously.

### Use the Miracle Question to Elicit the Client's Own Preferred Future

The miracle question asks the client to describe, in concrete behavioral detail, what life would look like if the problem were resolved overnight, and its function is to surface the client's own goals and desired outcomes, not the worker's professionally reasonable ones. Pose the question carefully, allow silence, and then pursue the specifics: what would be the first small sign, what would the client notice, what would others notice, what would be different about the client's actions. The detail matters, because vague aspirations, such as being happy, do not guide change, while concrete behaviors, such as getting out of bed and making breakfast, do. Resist the temptation to edit the client's miracle toward what the worker thinks they should want, because the client's own picture is what mobilizes their motivation.

### Find and Amplify Exceptions to the Problem

Exceptions are the times when the problem is absent, weaker, or handled differently, and they are the empirical evidence that the client's preferred future is already partially present and achievable. Search systematically for exceptions: when is the problem not happening, when is it less severe, what is different about those times, what is the client doing differently, and what would it take to make those times more frequent or more sustained. Amplify the exceptions by exploring them in detail, validating the client's agency in creating them, and connecting them to the miracle picture. Exceptions are not accidents; they are the client's existing solutions in partial form, and the worker's attention to them makes them visible and repeatable.

### Use Scaling Questions to Make Change Measurable and Actionable

Scaling questions translate vague experience into a concrete, trackable metric that makes change visible and next steps specific. Ask the client to place themselves on a scale, typically from one to ten, where ten is the miracle picture and one is the worst, to identify where they are now, what puts them there rather than lower, what one point higher would look like behaviorally, and what small action might move them up. The scale is the client's own, anchored in their own definitions, and the value of the question lies in the conversation it generates about the specifics of movement, not in the number itself. Scaling makes progress concrete, surfaces what is already working, and identifies a manageable next step.

### Use Coping Questions to Honor Survival and Surface Resilience

Coping questions ask how the client has managed to endure or keep going despite significant difficulty, and their function is to honor the client's survival, surface unrecognized resilience, and build a foundation of competence on which change can be built. Ask, genuinely and without flattery, how they have kept going, what has stopped things from being worse, what strengths they have drawn on, and what that says about them. Coping questions are especially valuable when the client feels hopeless or when the situation is genuinely dire and the miracle feels unreachable, because they locate agency even in endurance. They are not consolation; they are a precise method for making existing resilience available for the work ahead.

### Build on the Client's Strengths and Resources, Not Worker-Imposed Ones

Solution-focused work builds change on the strengths, resources, relationships, past successes, and values the client actually possesses, not on the ones the worker believes they should possess. Elicit the client's resources through careful questioning: what has worked before, who supports them, what do they value, what are they good at, what would people who know them well say are their strengths. Connect these resources to the preferred future and the exceptions, so the change plan is built from the client's own materials. Imposing externally derived strengths or solutions, however well-intentioned, produces plans the client does not own and will not sustain, and it violates the stance that the client is the expert on their own life.

### Know When Solution-Focused Work Is Not the Right Approach

Solution-focused brief therapy is powerful but not universal, and competent practice requires recognizing when it is contraindicated and a problem-focused or risk-oriented approach is required. Do not deploy solution-focused techniques when active risk assessment is needed, such as suicidality, danger to a child, or acute crisis, when the client needs concrete resources or safety before any therapeutic conversation, when the client's cognitive state prevents the future-oriented reflection the techniques require, or when the client explicitly needs their suffering and its causes to be understood before they can move forward. In these situations, conduct the necessary assessment, ensure safety, and return to solution-focused work only when it is appropriate. Technique loyalty must never override clinical necessity.

## Common Traps

### Techniques Used Without the Underlying Stance

The worker deploys the miracle question, scaling, or exception-finding as isolated techniques while continuing to think in deficit and pathology terms, producing sessions that feel scripted and inauthentic and that the client experiences as the worker not engaging with their actual suffering. The mechanism is that the techniques are easy to learn and the stance is hard to adopt. The false signal is a session that looks solution-focused on the surface. The harm is that the work is shallow, the client feels unheard, and the approach is discredited in the client's eyes. The stance must precede the technique.

### Editing the Miracle Toward the Worker's Goals

The worker, hearing the client's miracle picture, subtly redirects it toward professionally reasonable goals, such as sobriety, employment, or compliance, rather than honoring and exploring the client's own desired future, and the resulting plan serves the worker's values rather than the client's motivation. The mechanism is that the worker believes they know what the client should want. The false signal is a sensible, appropriate goal. The harm is that the client's motivation is lost, the plan is not owned, and the change does not sustain. The miracle must remain the client's own.

### Exceptions Treated as Accidents Rather Than Evidence

The worker notices an exception but treats it as a lucky accident or a trivial detail rather than investigating it as evidence of the client's existing competence and partial solution, and the opportunity to build on it is lost. The mechanism is that exceptions feel small compared to the problem. The false signal is that the exception does not matter. The harm is that the client's unrecognized strengths remain invisible, and the foundation for change is missed. Exceptions must be amplified as evidence of existing solutions.

### Scaling Reduced to a Number Without the Conversation

The worker asks for a scale number, records it, and moves on, without generating the conversation about what puts the client at that number, what one point higher looks like, and what small action might create movement, which is where the actual change-building occurs. The mechanism is that the number feels like data. The false signal is measurable progress tracking. The harm is that the technique produces a metric but no change, and the session becomes a checklist. The conversation, not the number, is the intervention.

### Forcing Solution-Focus Onto Situations Requiring Risk Assessment

The worker, committed to the solution-focused approach, applies its techniques to a situation that requires active risk assessment, crisis stabilization, or concrete safety, and the client's danger goes unassessed because the worker was looking for strengths and exceptions instead of risk. The mechanism is that the approach feels positive and empowering. The false signal is client engagement. The harm is that risk is missed, safety is not ensured, and the client may be harmed. Risk and crisis override technique loyalty.

### Mistaking Polite Compliance for Co-Construction

The worker interprets the client's polite, agreeable responses to the miracle and scaling questions as genuine engagement and co-construction of a preferred future, when the client is in fact performing compliance to please the worker or end the session, and the resulting plan has no real motivational anchor. The mechanism is that agreeable responses feel like progress. The false signal is a smooth, cooperative session. The harm is that the plan is not owned, no change occurs, and both parties are puzzled by the failure. Genuine co-construction must be distinguished from compliance.

### Imposing Externally Derived Strengths and Solutions

The worker identifies strengths and solutions from their own professional framework, such as the client's apparent resilience or the obvious utility of a particular coping strategy, and builds the plan on these rather than on the resources the client actually recognizes and values, producing a plan the client does not own. The mechanism is that professional strengths feel objective. The false signal is a well-grounded assessment. The harm is that the plan is imposed rather than co-constructed, the client's agency is overridden, and the change does not sustain. Strengths must be the client's own.

## Self-Check

- Did I adopt the solution-focused stance fully before using any technique, listening for resources as carefully as for deficits?
- Did I use the miracle question to elicit the client's own preferred future in concrete behavioral detail, without editing it toward my goals?
- Did I search systematically for exceptions and amplify them as evidence of the client's existing solutions?
- Did I use scaling to generate the conversation about specific movement, not merely to record a number?
- Did I use coping questions to honor survival and surface unrecognized resilience, especially when hope was low?
- Did I build the change plan on the client's own strengths and resources, not on strengths I imposed?
- Did I recognize when risk assessment, crisis stabilization, or concrete safety was required and set aside the solution-focused approach accordingly?
- Did I distinguish genuine co-construction of a preferred future from polite client compliance?
