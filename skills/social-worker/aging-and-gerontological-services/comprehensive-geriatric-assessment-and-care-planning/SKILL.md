# Comprehensive Geriatric Assessment and Care Planning

Comprehensive geriatric assessment (CGA) is a multi-dimensional, interdisciplinary evaluation of an older adult designed to capture the full picture of their health, function, cognition, social situation, environment, and resources — and to translate that picture into a coordinated, person-centered care plan. The judgment problem is that older adults present with multiple interacting conditions, atypical presentations, and accumulated losses that no single-domain assessment can capture, and that the most consequential problems (cognitive decline, falls, polypharmacy, caregiver burnout, self-neglect, financial exploitation) are invisible to a problem-focused visit. Agents err by assessing only the presenting complaint, by using a single tool as a diagnosis, by writing care plans that reflect the system's services rather than the person's goals, by treating assessment as a one-time event, and by failing to coordinate the multiple providers an older adult inevitably accumulates. This skill covers how to conduct a CGA across its core domains, how to select and interpret standardized instruments, how to build a person-centered care plan, and how to coordinate and reassess over time — with the overriding rule that the assessment exists to serve the older adult's own goals and that any sign of abuse, neglect, exploitation, or acute incapacity triggers escalation rather than routine planning.

## Core Rules

### Assess All Six Domains Rather Than Only the Presenting Complaint

A comprehensive geriatric assessment covers, at minimum, six domains: physical health (medical conditions, polypharmacy, pain, nutrition, sensory impairment, sleep), cognitive and mental health (cognition, mood, anxiety, substance use), functional status (activities of daily living and instrumental activities), psychosocial status (social network, roles, loneliness, bereavement, meaning), environmental status (home safety, fall hazards, access, neighborhood), and financial and legal status (income adequacy, benefits, insurance, advance directives, exposure to exploitation). The presenting complaint is often the least important problem: a fall brings an older adult to attention, but the fall may reflect polypharmacy, untreated depression, visual impairment, and a caregiver who just stopped coming. Assess every domain even when the referral names only one, because the domains interact and the unassessed domain is frequently where the risk lives. Document each domain with concrete findings, not impressions, and flag the interactions between them. If any domain reveals suspected abuse, neglect, financial exploitation, or self-neglect, escalate to Adult Protective Services per your jurisdiction's mandatory reporting law before continuing routine planning.

### Use Standardized Instruments Correctly and Interpret Them in Context

Standardized tools give structure and comparability, but they are screens, not diagnoses. Use validated instruments appropriate to the domain and population: the Mini-Mental State Examination (MMSE) or Montreal Cognitive Assessment (MoCA) for cognition, the Katz ADL and Lawton IADL scales for functional status, the Geriatric Depression Scale (GDS) for mood, a validated fall risk assessment (such as the Timed Up and Go or a multifactorial fall risk tool) for falls, and a validated screening for caregiver burden where a caregiver is involved. Administer each tool in conditions that give the older adult a fair chance — corrected hearing and vision, adequate time, their preferred language, freedom from acute pain or delirium — because a low score obtained under poor conditions is meaningless and can mislabel a capable adult as impaired. Interpret every score in context: a low MoCA in a person with limited formal education, fatigue, or recent bereavement is not dementia; a normal MMSE does not rule out depression or early cognitive change the MMSE is too coarse to detect. Document the tool, the score, the conditions of administration, and your contextual interpretation, and refer for formal neuropsychological or clinical evaluation when the findings are consequential or ambiguous.

### Center the Care Plan on the Person's Own Goals and Priorities

A care plan that lists available services but does not reflect what the older adult actually wants will not be followed and will not work. Elicit the person's goals in their own words — where they want to live, what they want to be able to do, what they fear, what they are willing and unwilling to accept — and build the plan around those goals. Older adults frequently have goals that differ from what families or providers assume: remaining at home despite risk, avoiding hospitalization, preserving a specific relationship, protecting a pet, managing pain over extending life. Where goals seem to conflict with safety, do not override them; explore the values underneath, present the real tradeoffs in plain language, and document the person's informed choices. Person-centered planning also means the older adult directs the plan to the extent they are able, with family and providers in supporting roles. Where capacity is in question for a consequential decision, arrange a formal capacity assessment by the appropriately qualified professional rather than inferring incapacity from the plan disagreement, and use supported decision-making and the least restrictive mechanisms before any substitute arrangement.

### Match Interventions to the Specific Assessed Needs and Prioritize

Translate assessment findings into specific, prioritized interventions matched to the actual gaps, not to a generic service menu. If polypharmacy is driving falls, the intervention is a medication review with the prescriber, not a generic "fall prevention" referral; if the kitchen is the hazard, the intervention is a home safety modification, not a pamphlet. Prioritize because older adults and caregivers cannot act on twenty recommendations at once: identify the two or three highest-impact, highest-feasibility changes and sequence the rest. Specify for each intervention who is responsible, by when, and how completion will be verified, because a recommendation without an owner and a date is a wish. Match interventions to the person's resources, culture, and what they have agreed to, because a plan the person cannot afford, does not understand, or has refused is not a plan. Document the prioritized interventions, the responsible parties, the target dates, and the person's consent.

### Coordinate Care Across the Multiple Providers an Older Adult Accumulates

Older adults typically have a primary care provider, one or more specialists, a pharmacist, possibly home care, therapy services, and family caregivers — and the fragmentation between them is itself a major source of harm: duplicate or interacting medications, contradictory plans, missed appointments, and no one holding the whole picture. Identify who owns the whole picture and take or assign that role. Obtain consent for cross-provider communication, share the minimum necessary, and convene the team for complex cases. Reconcile medications across prescribers, because polypharmacy and adverse drug events are among the leading preventable causes of decline and hospitalization in older adults. Manage every handoff explicitly and verify receipt, especially at hospital discharge where medication errors and missed follow-up are common. Document the provider map, the shared care plan, and the coordination activities, and advocate for the older adult when systems fail them.

### Build Reassessment Cycles Proportional to Risk and Instability

A geriatric assessment is a snapshot of a dynamic situation: cognition fluctuates, function declines, caregivers burn out, new conditions emerge, and exploitation can appear at any time. Build reassessment into the plan on a cycle proportional to risk and instability — frequent reassessment for cognitively impaired, socially isolated, medically unstable, or recently discharged older adults; longer intervals for stable, well-supported ones. Define the triggers that prompt reassessment outside the cycle: a hospitalization, a fall, a caregiver change, a new diagnosis, a sudden change in mood or function, or any report of suspected exploitation. Treat a change in function as a signal to investigate, not as expected aging, because decline attributed to "getting older" is frequently a treatable condition, abuse, or medication effect. Document the reassessment schedule, the triggers, and the findings at each cycle, and revise the care plan at each reassessment rather than letting it go stale.

### Screen for and Escalate Abuse, Neglect, Exploitation, and Capacity Concerns

Every geriatric assessment must actively screen for elder abuse, neglect (including self-neglect), and financial exploitation, because these are common, frequently hidden, and frequently missed when the worker focuses only on health and function. Watch for unexplained injuries, poor hygiene inconsistent with the person's means, sudden financial changes, a controlling caregiver who answers for the adult, isolation, and a mismatch between the person's condition and the care supposedly provided. Know your jurisdiction's mandatory reporting law: in most jurisdictions, social workers are mandated reporters of suspected elder abuse and neglect, and the standard is reasonable suspicion, not certainty — report and let APS investigate. Where acute incapacity, immediate danger, or a medical emergency is present, escalate immediately to emergency services and the appropriate clinical and legal authorities rather than folding the concern into a routine care plan. Document the indicators observed, the report made, to whom, and when, and continue to support the older adult through the investigation.

## Common Traps

### Assessing Only the Presenting Complaint and Missing the Interacting Problems

The worker focuses on the referral reason — a fall, a hospitalization, a family concern — and never assesses the other domains, so the cognitive impairment, untreated depression, polypharmacy, or financial exploitation driving the presentation goes undetected. The trap mechanism is that the presenting complaint feels like the whole task and the other domains feel like scope creep. The false signal is a focused, efficient assessment. The harm is that the root causes remain unaddressed, the presenting problem recurs, and the hidden risks (exploitation, self-neglect, suicide) continue under the cover of the treated symptom. A comprehensive geriatric assessment must cover all six domains precisely because the unassessed domain is where the danger often lives.

### Treating a Screening Tool Score as a Diagnosis

The worker administers the MMSE or MoCA, obtains a low score, and records "dementia" — or obtains a normal score and rules out cognitive impairment — without formal evaluation, contextual interpretation, or attention to the conditions of testing. The trap mechanism is that the number feels objective and diagnostic. The false signal is a test score in the chart. The harm is that a person is mislabeled with a progressive brain disease on the basis of a screen obtained under poor conditions (uncorrected hearing loss, fatigue, limited education), or that early impairment is missed because the screen was too coarse. Screens inform; they do not diagnose. Refer for formal evaluation when findings are consequential.

### Writing a Care Plan That Reflects Available Services, Not the Person's Goals

The worker fills the care plan with the services the agency offers or the family prefers, without eliciting what the older adult actually wants, producing a plan that is internally coherent but belongs to someone other than the client. The trap mechanism is that available services are concrete and easy to write down, while the person's goals require elicitation and may be inconvenient. The false signal is a complete, detailed care plan. The harm is that the plan is not followed, the person experiences the plan as imposed control rather than support, and the unmet goals that actually matter to them remain unaddressed. The plan must begin with the person's goals in their own words.

### Attributing Decline to Aging Instead of Investigating

The older adult's function or mood declines and the worker records "expected age-related decline" rather than investigating, missing that the decline is a treatable condition (depression, infection, medication effect, vitamin deficiency), abuse, neglect, or exploitation. The trap mechanism is that aging provides a ready, non-alarming explanation that requires no further work. The false signal is a plausible narrative of normal aging. The harm is that reversible or abusive causes of decline go untreated, and the older adult suffers or is exploited under the label of "getting older." Decline warrants investigation, not assumption.

### Failing to Reconcile Medications Across Prescribers

The older adult sees several prescribers who do not communicate, accumulates medications that interact, duplicate, or impose an impossible adherence burden, and the worker never reconciles the list. The trap mechanism is that medication management feels like a clinical task outside the social worker's role. The false signal is a documented medication list. The harm is that polypharmacy and adverse drug events are among the leading preventable causes of falls, delirium, hospitalization, and death in older adults, and the unreconciled list is a standing hazard. Reconciling medications with the prescriber and pharmacist is a core safety function of geriatric care coordination.

### Letting the Care Plan Go Stale Without Reassessment

The worker completes a thorough assessment and care plan, files it, and never reassesses, so the plan no longer reflects the older adult's current cognition, function, caregivers, or risks. The trap mechanism is that the completed plan feels like finished work. The false signal is a current care plan in the record. The harm is that the older adult deteriorates or is exploited while the plan describes a person who no longer exists, and interventions continue or lapse inappropriately. A geriatric care plan is a living document that must be reassessed on a risk-proportional cycle and revised whenever circumstances change.

### Missing Abuse or Exploitation Because the Presentation Looks Like Decline

The older adult is losing weight, missing appointments, showing new confusion, or has unexplained financial changes, and the worker attributes it to dementia or self-neglect rather than screening for abuse, neglect, or financial exploitation by a caregiver or new associate. The trap mechanism is that decline and exploitation can present identically, and the decline narrative requires no report. The false signal is a coherent medical explanation. The harm is that exploitation and neglect continue undetected, the older adult is harmed, and the mandated report that would have triggered protection is never made. Active screening and mandated reporting on reasonable suspicion are the defense.

## Self-Check

- Did I assess all six domains — physical, cognitive, functional, psychosocial, environmental, and financial/legal — rather than only the presenting complaint, and document the interactions between them?
- Did I use validated, population-appropriate instruments (MMSE/MoCA, Katz ADL, Lawton IADL, GDS, fall risk, caregiver burden) administered under fair conditions, interpreting each score in context rather than treating it as a diagnosis?
- Did I center the care plan on the older adult's own goals and priorities in their own words, and where goals conflicted with safety, document the informed choices rather than overriding them?
- Did I match interventions to the specific assessed gaps, prioritize the highest-impact changes, and assign a responsible party and date to each, rather than listing generic services?
- Did I coordinate care across providers — identifying who owns the whole picture, reconciling medications, managing handoffs with verified receipt — rather than leaving the older adult to navigate fragmentation alone?
- Did I build a reassessment cycle proportional to risk and instability, with defined triggers (hospitalization, fall, caregiver change, functional change) that prompt reassessment outside the cycle?
- Did I actively screen for elder abuse, neglect, self-neglect, and financial exploitation, and where I had reasonable suspicion, make the mandated report to APS and document it?
- Where capacity was in question for a consequential decision, did I arrange formal capacity assessment by an appropriately qualified professional rather than inferring incapacity from disagreement with the plan?
