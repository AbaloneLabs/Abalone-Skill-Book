# Advance Care Planning and Guardianship

Advance care planning and guardianship are the two ends of the spectrum of decision-making support for older adults: at one end, the capacitated adult directs their future care through advance directives while they still can; at the other, a court appoints a substitute decision-maker when the adult can no longer decide and left no guidance. The judgment problem is that both are high-stakes legal and clinical processes where errors strip rights, impose unwanted treatment, or leave a vulnerable adult unprotected, and where agents routinely confuse capacity with competence, defer to family pressure over the adult's own wishes, pursue over-broad guardianship when narrower mechanisms would do, or treat advance directives as paperwork rather than as the expression of a person's values. This skill covers how to facilitate advance care planning, how to distinguish and coordinate the different instruments (advance directives, POLST/MOLST, durable power of attorney), how to coordinate capacity assessment, how to pursue guardianship or conservatorship only when necessary and in the least restrictive form, and how to honor supported decision-making — with the overriding rule that capacity is presumed, that the adult's own voice and prior wishes govern, and that any substitute arrangement must be the least restrictive that meets the need.

## Core Rules

### Initiate Advance Care Planning Early, Repeatedly, and as a Conversation

Advance care planning is not a one-time form completed at admission; it is an ongoing conversation about what matters to the person, what they would and would not want as illness progresses, and who they trust to decide for them if they cannot. Initiate it while the adult has capacity, not in a crisis after capacity is lost, because the value of advance directives depends entirely on completing them while the person can still express genuine wishes. Revisit the conversation at milestones — a new diagnosis, a hospitalization, a functional decline, a bereavement — because goals and preferences change as circumstances change. Frame the conversation around the person's values and fears (what makes life worth living, what would be worse than death, what tradeoffs they accept) rather than jumping to specific interventions, because values are more durable than checklists and guide the proxy when the specific situation was not anticipated. Document the conversation, not only the resulting form, because the proxy and the providers need to understand the reasoning, not just the boxes checked. Where the person wants no life-sustaining treatment, ensure the directives are explicit and accessible where decisions will actually be made.

### Distinguish and Use Each Instrument for Its Specific Purpose

The instruments of advance care planning are not interchangeable, and each has a specific scope and legal weight. A living will (advance directive) states the person's wishes about life-sustaining treatment in future incapacity; a healthcare proxy (healthcare power of attorney) appoints a person to make healthcare decisions when the adult cannot; a durable power of attorney for finances appoints a person to manage financial affairs and remains effective after incapacity; a POLST or MOLST (Physician/Medical Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment) is a medical order, signed by a clinician, that directs actual treatment in an emergency and across settings, and it carries more immediate weight than an advance directive at the bedside. Know your jurisdiction's specific forms, witnessing and notarization requirements, activation triggers (some financial powers of attorney are immediate, others spring into effect on incapacity), and scope limits. Match the instrument to the goal: an advance directive expresses wishes but does not itself direct emergency responders, which is the role of the POLST; a financial power of attorney does not authorize healthcare decisions, which is the role of the proxy. Ensure completed instruments are accessible — given to the proxy, filed with the medical record, and, where applicable, registered in a state registry — because a directive that cannot be found in a crisis has no effect. Document which instruments exist, their scope, and their location.

### Distinguish Capacity From Competence and Coordinate Formal Assessment

Capacity and competence are often used interchangeably but are distinct: capacity is a clinical judgment about a person's ability to make a specific decision at a specific time, assessed functionally; competence is a legal status determined by a court. A person may lack capacity for a specific decision (managing substantial finances, consenting to major treatment) while retaining it for others, and capacity fluctuates with delirium, medication, time of day, and acute illness. For consequential decisions, do not rely on impression — coordinate formal capacity assessment by the appropriately qualified professional (physician, psychologist, or specialist clinician, depending on the decision and jurisdiction), assess at a time and in conditions favorable to the person's capacity, and reassess when the impairing state may be reversible (delirium, depression, medication effects). Know your jurisdiction's legal standard for the specific decision type, because the threshold for making a will differs from consenting to treatment or managing finances. The social worker's role is typically to identify the capacity question, gather the information, arrange the formal assessment, and act on its findings — not to make the final determination alone for high-stakes decisions. Document the decision assessed, the standard, the assessor, the findings, and the conditions.

### Exhaust Least Restrictive Alternatives Before Pursuing Guardianship

Guardianship (of the person) and conservatorship (of the estate) are the most restrictive forms of substitute decision-making: they strip the adult of fundamental rights and transfer them to a court-appointed decision-maker, and they should be pursued only when less restrictive alternatives cannot meet the need. Before seeking guardianship, exhaust and document the alternatives: supported decision-making, in which the adult retains the right to decide but is assisted by trusted supporters; existing advance directives and powers of attorney that already designate a decision-maker; representative payee arrangements for benefits; limited or tailored financial management; and community supports that reduce the decisions the adult must make alone. When guardianship is necessary, pursue the least restrictive form: limited guardianship scoped to the specific decisions the adult cannot make (finances only, healthcare only, placement only) rather than plenary guardianship that removes all rights. The petition must demonstrate with specific evidence why lesser alternatives are insufficient, what specific decisions the adult cannot make, and why the proposed guardian is appropriate. Honor the adult's prior expressed wishes and preferences in any guardianship arrangement, because substitute decisions should approximate what the adult would have wanted. Document the alternatives considered, the evidence of incapacity, the scope sought, and the legal process followed.

### Honor the Adult's Prior Wishes and Use Supported Decision-Making

The default in substitute decision-making is the adult's own prior expressed wishes, not what the family, the provider, or the guardian thinks is best. Elicit and document the adult's preferences while they have capacity, through advance care planning conversations, and honor those preferences even when the adult can no longer express them, because a person's right to self-determination extends through incapacity via their prior voice. Where the adult retains some capacity or it fluctuates, use supported decision-making — a formally or informally arranged network of trusted supporters who help the adult understand, weigh, and communicate decisions — as the least restrictive alternative that preserves the adult's legal rights. Supported decision-making contrasts with guardianship by keeping the right to decide with the adult, with support, rather than transferring the right to another. Match the support to the adult's specific needs (a financial supporter, a healthcare supporter, a daily-decisions supporter) and document the arrangement. Where prior wishes are unknown and cannot be inferred, use the best-interest standard informed by the adult's known values, relationships, and what a reasonable person in their situation would want — never by the convenience of the institution or the preferences of the family alone.

### Evaluate the Proposed Guardian and Build Safeguards Against Misuse

Guardianship transfers enormous power over a vulnerable adult, and guardians can exploit, neglect, or impose unwanted decisions as readily as anyone else, so the selection and oversight of the guardian is itself a safety function. Evaluate the proposed guardian's relationship to the adult, their motives, their capacity to perform the role, any conflicts of interest (a family member who stands to inherit, a caregiver with a history of conflict), and any history of undue influence over the adult. Prefer a guardian the adult nominated while capacitated, then a trusted family member or friend, then a professional or public guardian — recognizing that family is not always the safest choice and that professional guardians provide oversight but lack the personal knowledge of the adult's values. Build in safeguards: court oversight and required reporting, scoping the guardianship narrowly, requiring the guardian to consult the adult and honor prior wishes, and ensuring the adult retains maximum rights consistent with their capacity. Where a guardian is suspected of abuse, neglect, or exploitation, the worker's obligation to report and protect applies to the guardian as to anyone else. Document the guardian evaluation, the safeguards, and any concerns.

### Coordinate With the Legal, Medical, and Family Systems

Advance care planning and guardianship sit at the intersection of legal, medical, and family systems, and coordination across them determines whether the adult's wishes are actually honored. Ensure the medical team knows the directives and the proxy, the POLST travels with the patient across settings, the family understands the plan and their roles, and the legal instruments are valid and accessible. Where guardianship proceedings are underway, coordinate the capacity assessment evidence, the petition, the proposed guardian, and the adult's representation (the adult is entitled to legal representation and, in many jurisdictions, to an independent visitor or guardian ad litem). Manage the family conflict that often surrounds these decisions — disputes over the directive's meaning, over who should be proxy or guardian, over end-of-life choices — by focusing on the adult's documented wishes, mediating where possible, and escalating to the court where the conflict cannot be resolved and the adult is at risk. Document the coordination across systems and the resolution of conflicts.

## Common Traps

### Confusing Capacity With Competence and Treating Either as Global

The worker treats a single capacity concern as a global legal finding of incompetence, or infers incapacity from a diagnosis or an unwise decision, and applies the conclusion across all decisions and permanently. The trap mechanism is that a global label is simpler than decision-specific, time-specific functional assessment. The false signal is a capacity note in the chart. The harm is that the adult is stripped of rights in domains where they retain capacity, subjected to substitute decision-making they do not need, and the finding may be hard to reverse. Capacity is a clinical, decision-specific, fluctuating judgment; competence is a legal status; both must be specific and reassessed.

### Completing Advance Directives as Paperwork Without the Conversation

The worker has the adult sign advance directive forms at admission without any conversation about values, fears, and specific wishes, producing documents that technically exist but do not reflect considered preferences. The trap mechanism is that the completed form feels like compliance with the requirement. The false signal is a directive on file. The harm is that the proxy and providers have no understanding of the adult's actual wishes, the directive is ignored or contested in a crisis, and the planning that could have guided care fails precisely when it is needed. The conversation, documented, is the substance; the form is its record.

### Pursuing Plenary Guardianship When a Limited Mechanism Would Suffice

The worker, seeking to protect comprehensively, pursues plenary guardianship that removes all rights when a limited guardianship, a representative payee, a durable power of attorney, or supported decision-making would address the specific incapacity. The trap mechanism is that comprehensive guardianship feels thoroughly protective. The false signal is a fully protected adult. The harm is that the adult loses rights and self-determination far beyond what their incapacity requires — a significant harm in itself, and one that is difficult to reverse. The least restrictive mechanism that addresses the specific, demonstrated incapacity is required, and the alternatives must be documented as insufficient.

### Deferring to Family Pressure Over the Adult's Own Wishes

The worker, facing an insistent family, allows the family's preferences for treatment, placement, or decision-making to override the adult's documented prior wishes or current capacitated choices. The trap mechanism is that family unanimity feels like consensus and disagreement with family feels like conflict. The false signal is a family in agreement. The harm is that the adult's voice and rights are displaced by the family's, unwanted treatment or placement is imposed, and the right to self-determination is violated. The adult's own wishes — current where capacity permits, prior where it does not — govern, and the worker's role is to center them even against family pressure.

### Treating a Financial Power of Attorney as Authorizing Healthcare Decisions

The worker or family assumes that a durable financial power of attorney empowers the agent to make healthcare decisions, or vice versa, conflating the two instruments. The trap mechanism is that the instruments sound similar and the distinction is a legal technicality. The false signal is a power of attorney on file. The harm is that decisions are made by a person without legal authority, the decisions are invalid, treatment or placement proceeds without proper consent, and the resulting care may be challenged or reversed. Each instrument has a specific scope, and the worker must know which authorizes which decisions under the jurisdiction's law.

### Assuming Family Is Always the Safest Guardian

The worker defaults to appointing a family member as guardian on the assumption that family is trustworthy and knows the adult best, without evaluating the family member's motives, capacity, conflicts of interest, or history of undue influence. The trap mechanism is that family preference feels natural and respectful. The false signal is a willing family member. The harm is that a family member with financial interest, a history of conflict, or exploitative motives is given legal power over a vulnerable adult, and the abuse that follows is shielded by the guardian's authority. The proposed guardian must be evaluated, and family is not always the safest choice.

### Letting Advance Directives and POLST Become Inaccessible or Outdated

The worker completes directives and a POLST but does not ensure they are accessible where decisions are made (the medical record, the registry, with the proxy, in the home) or revisited as circumstances change, so the documents cannot guide the care they were meant to guide. The trap mechanism is that the completed form feels like the finished task. The false signal is a directive on file somewhere. The harm is that emergency responders cannot find the POLST, the hospital does not have the directive, the proxy does not know their role, and the adult receives unwanted treatment or is denied wanted treatment because the planning never reached the point of decision. Accessibility and periodic review are part of the planning.

## Self-Check

- Did I initiate advance care planning as an ongoing values-based conversation while the adult has capacity, documenting the reasoning and revisiting it at milestones, rather than completing a form once?
- Did I distinguish and correctly use each instrument — living will, healthcare proxy, durable financial power of attorney, POLST/MOLST — matching scope to goal and meeting the jurisdiction's witnessing, notarization, and activation requirements?
- Did I distinguish capacity (clinical, decision-specific, fluctuating) from competence (legal), coordinate formal assessment by an appropriately qualified professional for consequential decisions, and reassess when the impairing state may be reversible?
- Did I exhaust and document least restrictive alternatives — supported decision-making, existing directives and powers of attorney, representative payee, limited guardianship — before pursuing plenary guardianship or conservatorship?
- Did I honor the adult's prior expressed wishes as the default for substitute decisions, using supported decision-making where the adult retains or fluctuates in capacity, and the best-interest standard informed by known values only where wishes are unknown?
- Did I evaluate the proposed guardian's relationship, motives, capacity, and conflicts of interest, prefer the adult's nominee, scope the guardianship narrowly, and build in oversight safeguards?
- Did I coordinate across the legal, medical, and family systems — ensuring directives reach the point of decision, the POLST travels with the patient, and family conflict is resolved around the adult's documented wishes?
- Did I ensure completed instruments are accessible (medical record, registry, proxy, home) and revisited as circumstances change, so the planning actually guides care rather than sitting unread?
