# Diversion and Alternatives to Incarceration

Diversion is the practice of redirecting justice-involved clients away from incarceration and toward community-based interventions that address the underlying causes of their justice involvement — mental illness, substance use, trauma, poverty, homelessness, cognitive impairment. Diversion can occur at multiple points: pre-arrest (law enforcement diversion to treatment or services), pre-filing (prosecutor diversion), pretrial (release with services in lieu of detention), and post-conviction (alternative sentencing, problem-solving courts, community supervision with treatment). The judgment problem is that diversion is not simply "better than jail" — it carries its own risks (surveillance, net-widening, conditions that set clients up to fail), requires the client's informed participation, and must be matched to the client's needs and the legal situation. The worker must understand the legal landscape, assess the client honestly, and build plans that are both legally viable and clinically sound.

## Core Rules

### Intervene at the Earliest Possible Point in the Justice Process

The earlier the intervention, the more options exist and the less damage the justice involvement inflicts. Pre-arrest diversion (co-response teams, crisis intervention, deflection to services) avoids the arrest entirely. Pre-filing diversion avoids the charge and the record. Pretrial diversion avoids detention and may result in dismissal. Each successive point narrows the options and deepens the consequences — a conviction, even a diverted one, creates collateral consequences that persist. Advocate for and facilitate intervention at the earliest point the client's situation allows, because the difference between pre-arrest and post-conviction diversion is the difference between avoiding the justice trajectory and merely softening it.

### Understand the Legal Landscape and the Decision-Makers

Diversion operates within a legal system with specific actors, criteria, and processes. Know the local landscape: what diversion programs exist, at what points, with what eligibility criteria, what the referral process is, who the decision-makers are (law enforcement, prosecutors, judges, supervision officers), and what the outcomes are (dismissal, reduced charges, alternative sentencing). Know the client's legal status (pre-arrest, charged, pretrial, convicted, sentenced, supervised) and what options are available at that status. Coordinate with the defense attorney, because diversion decisions are legal decisions and the client's defense strategy may depend on or conflict with diversion. Do not practice law, but know enough to advocate effectively and to avoid giving legal advice.

### Assess the Client for Diversion Appropriateness, Not Just Eligibility

Eligibility (does the client meet the program's criteria) is necessary but not sufficient. Appropriateness (is the diversion the right intervention for this client) requires clinical judgment. Assess whether the client has needs the diversion addresses (mental illness, substance use, trauma, homelessness), whether the client can meet the conditions (treatment attendance, supervision check-ins, sobriety, housing stability), whether the client's support network can sustain the plan, and whether the client understands and consents to the diversion (including its conditions, its duration, and the consequences of failure). A diversion that the client cannot complete is worse than no diversion, because failure triggers the consequence the diversion was meant to avoid, often with enhanced penalties.

### Build a Plan That Addresses the Underlying Cause, Not Just the Legal Condition

Diversion fails when it addresses the legal condition (the charge, the supervision requirement) without addressing the underlying cause (the mental illness, the substance use, the homelessness, the trauma). The client who is diverted to supervision without treatment for the condition that drove the offense will reoffend. Build a plan that connects the client to the services that address the cause: behavioral health treatment, substance use treatment (including medication-assisted treatment where indicated), housing, income supports, trauma-informed care, peer support, family intervention. Coordinate with the service providers, ensure the plan is feasible (the services exist, are accessible, and the client can get to them), and monitor the client's engagement.

### Navigate the Tension Between Support and Surveillance

Diversion programs often blend support (treatment, services, case management) with surveillance (drug testing, monitoring, check-ins, condition enforcement). The worker must navigate this tension honestly with the client. The support elements are genuinely helpful; the surveillance elements carry risks (detection of relapse or noncompliance that triggers sanctions, net-widening into the justice system, the psychological burden of monitoring). Be transparent with the client about what the program requires, what the consequences of noncompliance are, and what the worker's role is (advocate, monitor, both). Do not minimize the surveillance, and do not let the support be overwhelmed by it. Advocate for the least restrictive conditions that meet the legal requirements, because excessive conditions set the client up to fail.

### Plan for the Transition Out of the Diversion Program

Diversion programs are time-limited, and the client must transition to sustainable supports when the program ends. The trap is treating successful program completion as the finish line, when the underlying conditions (mental illness, addiction, housing instability) persist and the supports (treatment, supervision, case management) are ending. Build a transition plan from the beginning: connect the client to mainstream benefits and services that will outlast the program, cultivate natural supports (family, peers, community), and plan for the predictable challenges of the post-program period. The measure of diversion success is not program completion but sustained stability after the program ends.

## Common Traps

### Treating Diversion as Unambiguously Better Than Incarceration

Diversion is generally preferable to incarceration, but it is not without costs. The trap is pushing diversion without assessing whether the client can meet the conditions, whether the surveillance burden is tolerable, and whether the program addresses the client's actual needs. The harm is that the client enters a diversion they cannot complete, fails, and faces enhanced consequences — sometimes worse than the original disposition would have been. The mechanism is that "diversion" sounds unambiguously good, masking the risks of net-widening, condition overload, and surveillance. The corrective is to assess appropriateness as rigorously as eligibility, to be honest with the client about the demands, and to advocate for conditions the client can realistically meet.

### Failing to Coordinate With the Defense Attorney

Diversion decisions are legal decisions with legal consequences, and the defense attorney is the client's legal advocate. The trap is the social worker pursuing diversion without coordinating with the defense, creating a plan that conflicts with the legal strategy, that the prosecutor or judge rejects, or that the client does not understand. The harm is wasted effort, a plan that fails, and potential prejudice to the client's legal position. The mechanism is that the social worker's clinical frame and the attorney's legal frame may diverge, and without coordination they work at cross-purposes. The corrective is to involve the defense attorney from the beginning, to align the diversion plan with the legal strategy, and to let the attorney advise the client on legal consequences.

### Building a Plan With Conditions the Client Cannot Meet

Diversion plans are often built around what the program requires rather than what the client can realistically sustain. The trap is a plan with multiple weekly appointments, frequent drug testing, mandatory employment, and housing conditions, assembled for a client with no transportation, unstable housing, active addiction, and cognitive limitations. The harm is that the client fails the conditions, the diversion is revoked, and the client faces the consequence the diversion was meant to avoid. The mechanism is that program requirements are standardized while client capacity is individual. The corrective is to assess the client's capacity honestly, to advocate for individualized conditions, to build the supports (transportation, housing, treatment) that make compliance possible, and to decline diversion when the conditions are unmeetable.

### Addressing the Legal Condition Without the Underlying Cause

The diversion that addresses the charge (community service, supervision, a fine) without addressing the cause (addiction, mental illness, homelessness) produces a clean legal outcome and a clinical failure. The trap is that the legal system measures compliance with conditions while the clinical reality (the untreated condition) persists, and the client reoffends because the cause was never addressed. The harm is recidivism that the diversion was meant to prevent. The mechanism is that legal conditions are concrete and measurable while underlying-cause treatment is complex and long-term. The corrective is to insist on treatment and services as core components of the diversion plan, not as optional add-ons, and to measure success by the client's stability, not by condition compliance alone.

### Losing the Client at Program Completion

The client who successfully completes diversion may believe the work is done, and the system may close the case, but the underlying conditions persist and the program supports are ending. The trap is treating completion as the finish line and losing the client to recurrence. The harm is that the client, now without supports, returns to the conditions that produced the justice involvement. The mechanism is that program completion is a measurable milestone while sustained stability requires ongoing engagement. The corrective is to build the transition plan from the beginning, to connect the client to supports that outlast the program, and to follow up after completion to verify sustained stability.

## Self-Check

- Did I intervene at the earliest possible point in the justice process, maximizing the client's options?
- Do I understand the local diversion landscape — programs, criteria, decision-makers, processes?
- Did I assess the client for diversion appropriateness (can they meet the conditions, does it address their needs), not just eligibility?
- Did I coordinate with the defense attorney, aligning the diversion plan with the legal strategy?
- Did I build a plan that addresses the underlying cause (mental illness, substance use, housing, trauma), not just the legal condition?
- Did I navigate the support-surveillance tension honestly with the client, being transparent about conditions and consequences?
- Did I advocate for the least restrictive conditions the client can realistically meet?
- Did I build a transition plan for when the program ends, connecting the client to sustainable supports?
- Did I measure success by sustained stability after the program, not by program completion alone?
