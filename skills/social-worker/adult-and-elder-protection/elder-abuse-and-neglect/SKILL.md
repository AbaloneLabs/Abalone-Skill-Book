# Elder Abuse and Neglect

Elder abuse — physical, emotional, sexual, neglect, or financial exploitation of an older adult — is vastly underdetected and underreported, because victims are often isolated, dependent on the very people harming them, and unable or unwilling to name the harm. Unlike child protection, adult protection operates under a fundamental tension: the older adult is an adult with the right to make their own decisions, including decisions the worker judges harmful, unless capacity is impaired. Agents err by treating elder abuse like child abuse (overriding autonomy) or by deferring to the family's framing (missing the abuse), by mistaking the absence of disclosure for the absence of abuse, and by underestimating how completely a perpetrator can control an older adult's presentation. This skill covers how to detect and assess elder abuse, how to weigh capacity and consent, and how to intervene in ways that protect while respecting autonomy — with the overriding rule that an older adult with capacity has the right to refuse services, and that the worker's role is to ensure the decision is genuinely the adult's own, made free of coercion.

## Core Rules

### Recognize the Forms and Their Often-Subtle Signs

Elder abuse takes several forms, each with distinct indicators, and the signs are often attributed to aging or frailty rather than recognized as abuse. Physical abuse presents as injuries (bruises in unusual patterns or locations, fractures, burns), delays in seeking care, inconsistent explanations, and caregiver refusal to leave the older adult alone with the worker. Neglect presents as untreated medical conditions, poor hygiene, malnutrition, dehydration, unsafe living conditions, medication non-adherence, and pressure injuries — and may be active (intentional) or passive (caregiver overwhelmed or lacking capacity). Financial exploitation presents as sudden changes in finances, unpaid bills despite adequate resources, new "friends" or love interests with financial access, changes to wills or titles, missing possessions, and the older adult's confusion about their own finances. Emotional abuse is harder to detect: the caregiver belittling, threatening, isolating, or controlling the older adult in the worker's presence, or the older adult's fearfulness, withdrawal, or deference to the caregiver. Recognize that the presence of a caregiver who answers for the older adult, refuses to allow private interview, or monitors the interaction is itself a warning sign.

### Interview the Older Adult Privately and With Patience

The single most important assessment practice is to interview the older adult alone, out of the presence of caregivers and family. A perpetrator's presence fundamentally distorts what the older adult can say, and many disclosures emerge only when the older adult senses they are safe to speak. Insist on private interview; if the caregiver refuses, treat the refusal itself as significant data. Interview with patience: allow time for the older adult to process questions, accommodate sensory and cognitive limitations (hearing, vision, processing speed), use clear plain language, and avoid rushing. Do not interpret slowness or confusion as incapacity; it may reflect the conditions of the interview or untreated health issues. Build rapport before sensitive questions, and watch for disclosure cues — hesitation, euphemism, looking toward the caregiver, affect shifts. Document the older adult's own words.

### Assess Capacity as Decision-Specific, Not Global

The central legal and ethical question in adult protection is whether the older adult has the capacity to make the relevant decisions — to remain in the home, to manage finances, to accept or refuse services, to leave a relationship. Capacity is not global; a person may have capacity for some decisions and not others, and capacity fluctuates (with delirium, time of day, medication effects). Capacity for a specific decision requires the ability to understand the relevant information, appreciate the situation and its consequences, reason with the information, and communicate a choice. Assess capacity specifically for the decision at hand, using the appropriate standard, and obtain formal capacity assessment by a qualified professional where the decision is consequential (large financial decisions, placement, medical treatment). Document the basis for the capacity determination. Never assume incapacity from age, diagnosis, or a decision you disagree with.

### Distinguish Self-Neglect From Abuse, and Assess Capacity in Both

Much of adult protection work involves self-neglect — an older adult living in conditions that endanger their health or safety, often due to cognitive impairment, depression, physical limitation, or a combination. Self-neglect is not abuse by another, but it is a serious risk, and it raises the same capacity question: does the adult understand and accept the risk? A capacitated adult has the right to live in conditions the worker finds unacceptable, however distressing; an incapacitated adult requires protective intervention. The assessment must determine capacity and, if impaired, identify the least restrictive intervention that addresses the risk. Do not collapse self-neglect into abuse, but do not dismiss it; self-neglect carries significant mortality and warrants serious response when capacity is impaired.

### Investigate Financial Exploitation Specifically

Financial exploitation is the most common and most invisible form of elder abuse, often perpetrated by family members with access and trust, and it can strip an older adult of a lifetime of savings before it is detected. Investigate specifically: review bank statements for unusual withdrawals or transfers, changes to powers of attorney or account ownership, unpaid bills, new acquaintances with financial involvement, sudden changes to estate documents, and the older adult's own account of their finances. Where exploitation is suspected, involve financial institutions, law enforcement, or financial abuse specialist teams, and consider freezing or protecting assets to prevent further loss. The older adult may minimize or deny the exploitation out of loyalty, shame, or fear, so corroborate with records and collateral. Document the financial picture precisely, because financial exploitation cases often become legal or criminal matters.

### Evaluate the Caregiver Dynamic and Risk Factors

Most elder abuse is perpetrated by family caregivers, and the dynamic often involves caregiver stress, dependency, substance use, mental illness, or a longstanding pattern of family dysfunction — though none of these excuses the abuse, and some abuse is pure exploitation without caregiver burden. Assess the caregiver's role, capacity, stresses, and relationship to the older adult. Identify risk factors for escalation: the caregiver's financial dependence on the older adult, isolation of the older adult from others, substance use, history of violence, and the older adult's increasing dependency. A caregiver who is the sole source of care, housing, or financial management holds concentrated power that heightens risk. Do not assume that a family caregiver is protective by default; assess them as you would any other person in a position of trust.

### Intervene at the Least Restrictive Level That Addresses the Risk

Adult protection intervention must be the least restrictive that addresses the identified risk, because the older adult's autonomy is a legal right and a wellbeing factor. The continuum runs from education and support, to increased services (home care, meals, financial management assistance), to monitoring, to formal protective arrangements (representative payee, guardianship conservatorship in narrow scope), to, only when necessary and authorized, removal from the home or comprehensive guardianship. Each step up the restrictiveness ladder requires stronger justification and, usually, legal process and capacity findings. A capacitated adult who refuses intervention must be allowed to refuse, with the worker's role limited to offering help, reducing barriers, and monitoring — and documenting the refusal and the capacity basis. Over-riding a capacitated adult is itself a harm and may be illegal.

### Build Safety Plans That Account for Dependency and Coercion

An elder safety plan must account for the reality that the older adult may depend on the abuser for care, housing, finances, or emotional connection, and that leaving or confronting the abuser may be impossible or unwanted. The plan may involve introducing alternative caregivers, reducing the abuser's access to finances, increasing monitoring and contact, involving other family or community supports, or, where the older adult chooses and capacity allows, relocation. The plan must be feasible given the older adult's dependencies and preferences, and it must be monitored because elder abuse tends to recur and escalate. Provide the older adult with information about options and resources, including the right to change their mind. Document the plan, the older adult's informed choices, and the monitoring arrangement.

## Common Traps

### Deferring to the Caregiver's Account and Framing

The caregiver is present, coherent, and helpful, and the worker accepts their account of the older adult's condition and needs without private interview of the older adult. The trap mechanism is that the caregiver's account is accessible and resolves the concern efficiently, and challenging it feels intrusive. The false signal is a coherent, cooperative caregiver. The harm is that the older adult's actual experience — fear, abuse, exploitation — is never heard, and the assessment is built on the account of the person most motivated to conceal. Private interview of the older adult is non-negotiable; refusal to allow it is itself a warning sign.

### Treating Capacity as Global or Inferring It From Diagnosis

The worker treats the older adult as "incapacitated" based on a dementia diagnosis or a confusing moment, overriding autonomy across all decisions, or conversely assumes capacity globally based on composed presentation. The trap is that capacity feels like a fixed trait rather than a decision-specific, fluctuating assessment. The false signal is a diagnostic label or a single observation. The harm is that capacitated adults are stripped of their rights and self-determination, or that incapacitated adults are left unprotected under the assumption of autonomy. Capacity must be assessed specifically for the consequential decision, by appropriate means.

### Overriding Autonomy "For Their Own Good"

The worker, distressed by the older adult's living conditions or choices, arranges placement or services against the older adult's wishes, treating the refusal as evidence of incapacity. The trap is that the worker's assessment of risk feels obviously correct and intervention feels protective. The false signal is a safer-seeming situation. The harm is that a capacitated adult's legal rights and self-determination are violated — a harm in itself — and the intervention may be reversed or resisted, undermining trust and protection. A capacitated adult has the right to make decisions the worker judges unwise; the worker's role is to ensure the decision is genuinely their own and informed.

### Missing Financial Exploitation Because It Is Not "Abuse-Shaped"

The worker screens for physical abuse and neglect but does not investigate finances, because financial exploitation does not present with injuries and is not what "abuse" evokes. The trap is that the visible (injuries, hygiene) dominates attention while the financial drain proceeds invisibly. The false signal is an older adult who appears physically cared for. The harm is that an older adult is quietly stripped of their assets, often by a trusted family member, with consequences for their long-term care and security. Financial exploitation requires specific, proactive inquiry.

### Confusing Self-Neglect With Abuse or Dismissing It

The worker either treats all squalor and self-neglect as abuse requiring intervention against the adult's wishes, or dismisses self-neglect as a lifestyle choice not warranting response. The trap is that self-neglect does not fit the abuse frame cleanly, so it provokes either over-reaction or inaction. The false signal is a clear categorization. The harm is either autonomy violation or unaddressed mortality risk. Self-neglect requires capacity assessment and, where capacity is impaired, the least restrictive intervention; where capacity is intact, the adult's choice stands, with support offered.

### Accepting Recantation or Minimization as Closure

The older adult, after initial concern or disclosure, minimizes or recants under family pressure or investigation distress, and the worker closes the case. The trap is that the recantation resolves the uncomfortable implication and allows case closure. The false signal is an older adult who now says everything is fine. The harm is that abuse and exploitation continue, often escalating once the system withdraws. Recantation in adult protection, as in child protection, must be weighed in context, with attention to the dependency and coercion dynamics that produce it.

## Self-Check

- Did I recognize the forms of elder abuse — physical, emotional, sexual, neglect, financial exploitation — and their specific indicators, rather than attributing signs to aging?
- Did I interview the older adult privately and patiently, accommodating sensory and cognitive limitations, and treating caregiver refusal of private interview as a warning sign?
- Did I assess capacity specifically for the consequential decision at hand, recognizing it as decision-specific and fluctuating, rather than treating it as global or inferring it from diagnosis?
- Did I distinguish self-neglect from abuse and assess capacity in both, intervening where capacity is impaired and respecting choice where it is intact?
- Did I investigate financial exploitation specifically, reviewing records and involving specialists, rather than screening only for physical abuse?
- Did I assess the caregiver dynamic and risk factors, including financial dependence, isolation, substance use, and concentrated power, rather than assuming family caregivers are protective?
- Is my intervention the least restrictive that addresses the risk, with stronger justification and legal process for each step up, and did I respect a capacitated adult's right to refuse?
- Does my safety plan account for the older adult's dependencies and coercion dynamics, is it feasible and monitored, and does it document the older adult's informed choices?
