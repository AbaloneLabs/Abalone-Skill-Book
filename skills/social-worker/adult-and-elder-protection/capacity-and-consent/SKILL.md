# Capacity and Consent

Capacity — the ability to make a specific decision — is the legal and ethical hinge on which adult protection, healthcare decision-making, and self-determination turn. A capacitated adult can refuse life-saving treatment, remain in an abusive relationship, or give away their money, and the worker must respect that; an incapacitated adult requires protection and substituted decision-making through proper legal process. Agents err because capacity is treated as a fixed global trait rather than a decision-specific and fluctuating assessment, because incapacity is inferred from a diagnosis or an unwise decision rather than assessed, and because consent is assumed from cooperation without examining whether it was free and informed. This skill covers how to assess capacity rigorously, how to evaluate the genuineness of consent including under undue influence, and how to act on the findings — with the overriding rule that capacity is presumed in adults and must not be overturned on the basis of a decision the worker disagrees with.

## Core Rules

### Presume Capacity and Assess Specifically to Rebut the Presumption

Every adult is presumed to have capacity until demonstrated otherwise through proper assessment. The presumption matters because the default — autonomy — is a legal right, and overturning it requires evidence, not impression. When capacity is in question for a consequential decision, assess it specifically for that decision, using the appropriate legal standard. Do not treat capacity as global: a person may have capacity to make a will but not to manage daily finances, capacity to consent to a routine service but not to a major medical procedure, capacity in the morning but not during evening confusion. Document the specific decision assessed, the standard applied, the findings on each component, and the basis for the conclusion. A capacity determination that does not name the specific decision and the standard is not a determination.

### Apply the Four-Component Functional Test

Capacity is generally assessed functionally — by the person's actual decision-making ability — rather than by the outcome of the decision or the diagnosis. The functional test examines four components: understanding (the person can comprehend the relevant information — the situation, the options, the risks and benefits), appreciation (the person recognizes that the information applies to them and their situation, not abstractly), reasoning (the person can weigh the options and arrive at a choice through a process that is not severely disrupted), and expressing a choice (the person can communicate a decision, by any means). Assess each component, not just the final choice. A person who arrives at a choice the worker disagrees with, but through intact understanding, appreciation, and reasoning, has capacity. A person who arrives at a "right" choice but cannot explain why or shows no understanding may not.

### Recognize That Capacity Fluctuates and Assess at the Right Time

Capacity is not static; it fluctuates with delirium, fatigue, time of day, medication effects, pain, infection, mood, and the context of the assessment. A person assessed as incapacitated during an acute delirium may have full capacity once the delirium resolves; a person assessed in the evening may have capacity in the morning; a person overwhelmed in a crisis may have capacity when calm. Assess at the time and in the conditions most favorable to the person's capacity, and re-assess when circumstances change. For consequential decisions, do not rely on a single assessment during an acute state. Document the conditions of the assessment and any factors that may have affected it. The reversibility of many capacity-impairing states (delirium, medication effects, untreated depression) means incapacity findings should be revisited, not treated as permanent.

### Obtain Formal Assessment for Consequential Decisions

For low-stakes decisions (consent to a routine service), a social worker's functional assessment may suffice. For consequential decisions — major medical treatment, placement against the person's wishes, management of substantial assets, end-of-life decisions, changes to legal documents — obtain formal assessment by the appropriately qualified professional (physician, psychologist, specialist clinician, depending on jurisdiction and decision type), because the consequences of error and the legal weight of the determination require specialist rigor. Know your jurisdiction's requirements for who can assess capacity for which decisions and what standard applies. A social worker's role is often to identify the capacity question, gather the information, arrange the formal assessment, and act on its findings — not to make the final determination alone for high-stakes decisions.

### Evaluate Consent for Freedom From Undue Influence

Consent is only valid if it is free, and one of the most common threats to free consent in adult protection is undue influence — the substitution of one person's will for another's through coercion, manipulation, isolation, or exploitation of dependency. Assess whether the person making the decision is genuinely their own: Are they isolated from other influences? Does a caregiver or family member answer for them or control access to information? Has the decision changed suddenly or in a pattern that benefits someone else? Is the person afraid to disagree with someone? Does the "choice" align consistently with another's interests rather than their own? Undue influence can coexist with apparent capacity, because the person technically understands and "chooses" but the choice is not their own. Document the indicators of undue influence and, where present, the capacity assessment must account for it and the intervention must address the coercive dynamic, not just the surface decision.

### Distinguish an Unwise Decision From an Incapacitated One

People with full capacity make decisions that are unwise, risky, contrary to their interests, and contrary to what the worker would choose — and they have the right to. The right to make "bad" decisions is the core of autonomy, and a worker's disagreement with a decision is never, by itself, evidence of incapacity. The error to avoid is equating a decision the worker judges harmful with incapacity, then overriding autonomy "for their own good." The test is the process of deciding, not the outcome. A capacitated adult who refuses treatment, stays with an abusive partner, or gives money to a manipulative relative must be allowed to do so, with the worker's role limited to ensuring the decision is informed, free of coercion, and revisable. Document that the decision was the person's own, made with capacity, and the supports and information offered.

### Use the Least Restrictive Substitute Decision-Making When Capacity Is Impaired

When capacity is impaired for a consequential decision, substitute decision-making may be required, and it must use the least restrictive mechanism available. The hierarchy typically runs: supported decision-making (the person retains the right but is assisted), then the person's prior wishes and advance directives, then a legally authorized substitute decision-maker (healthcare proxy, power of attorney, guardian), then court-appointed guardianship or conservatorship as the most restrictive. Use existing mechanisms before creating new ones, honor the person's prior expressed wishes, scope any guardianship to the specific decisions the person cannot make (rather than plenary guardianship that strips all rights), and ensure all legal processes are properly followed. Substitute decision-making should approximate what the person would have wanted, not what the decision-maker prefers. Document the legal authority for any substituted decision.

### Revisit Capacity and Consent Over Time

Capacity and the conditions affecting consent change. A person assessed as incapacitated may regain capacity; a person free of undue influence may come under it; a person who refused services may accept them later in different conditions. Build in reassessment, especially after changes in health, medication, relationships, or circumstances. A capacity or consent determination is a snapshot, not a permanent label. Document reassessments and the basis for any change in the intervention. Treating a past incapacity finding as permanent, or a past consent as irrevocable, fails the person whose situation has changed.

### Document the Decision, the Standard, and the Basis

Every capacity determination and consent evaluation must be documented with the specific decision assessed, the standard applied, the findings on each component, the conditions and timing of the assessment, who performed it, the indicators of undue influence considered, and the conclusion and its basis. The documentation is the legal record and the protection against challenge. A conclusion without the reasoning and the components is indefensible. This documentation obligation runs through every step above: the presumption of capacity you started from, the specific decision that triggered assessment, the formal assessment you arranged, the undue influence you examined, and the least restrictive mechanism you selected.

## Common Traps

### Inferring Incapacity From an Unwise Decision

The worker disagrees with the client's decision — refusing treatment, staying in a risky situation, giving away money — and treats the disagreement as evidence the client lacks capacity. The trap mechanism is that the worker's assessment of risk feels obviously correct, so a contrary choice feels irrational, and irrationality feels like incapacity. The false signal is a decision that is clearly harmful. The harm is that a capacitated adult's autonomy is overridden, a legal right is violated, and the intervention may be reversed or resisted, undermining protection. The test is the process of deciding, not the outcome; people with capacity make harmful decisions, and have the right to.

### Treating Capacity as Global

The worker assesses capacity once, or infers it from a diagnosis, and applies the finding across all decisions. The trap is that a global label is simpler than decision-specific assessment. The false signal is a single capacity determination in the chart. The harm is that the person is stripped of rights in domains where they retain capacity, or left unprotected in domains where they lack it. Capacity is decision-specific and must be assessed for each consequential decision.

### Equating Cooperation With Valid Consent

The client agrees to a service, a placement, or a relationship, and the worker treats the agreement as valid consent without examining whether it was free and informed. The trap is that cooperation resolves the situation and feels like the client's choice. The false signal is a client who says yes. The harm is that consent obtained under coercion, isolation, or without relevant information is not consent, and acting on it may perpetuate the very exploitation or harm the worker is meant to address. Consent must be free, informed, and the person's own, examined for undue influence.

### Assessing Capacity During an Acute, Reversible State

The worker assesses capacity during delirium, acute intoxication, severe pain, or a medication effect, and treats the resulting incapacity as the person's baseline. The trap is that the acute presentation is salient and the assessment feels timely. The false signal is a clear incapacity finding. The harm is that a person whose capacity would return once the acute state resolves is subjected to substitute decision-making and rights restriction based on a transient state. Reversible causes must be addressed and capacity reassessed.

### Missing Undue Influence Because the Person "Chooses"

The person technically understands and "chooses," so the worker treats the choice as capacitated and valid, missing that the choice is being driven by a coercive other. The trap is that the surface decision looks autonomous. The false signal is a person who articulates a choice. The harm is that exploitation and coercion continue under the guise of consent, because the worker did not examine whether the choice was genuinely the person's own. Undue influence can coexist with apparent capacity and must be specifically assessed.

### Over-Broad Guardianship When Narrower Mechanisms Suffice

The worker, seeking to protect, pursues plenary guardianship that strips all rights when a narrower mechanism (representative payee, limited guardianship for finances only, supported decision-making) would address the specific incapacity. The trap is that comprehensive guardianship feels thoroughly protective. The false signal is a protected person. The harm is that the person loses rights and self-determination far beyond what their incapacity requires, a significant harm in itself, and one that is hard to reverse. The least restrictive mechanism that addresses the specific incapacity is required.

## Self-Check

- Did I presume capacity and assess specifically to rebut the presumption, naming the specific decision and the standard applied?
- Did I apply the four-component functional test — understanding, appreciation, reasoning, expressing a choice — rather than judging capacity by the outcome of the decision?
- Did I account for fluctuation and assess at the time and conditions most favorable to the person's capacity, with reassessment when circumstances change?
- For consequential decisions, did I obtain formal assessment by an appropriately qualified professional, and do I know my jurisdiction's requirements?
- Did I evaluate consent for undue influence — isolation, control, alignment with another's interests, fear — recognizing it can coexist with apparent capacity?
- Did I distinguish an unwise decision from an incapacitated one, respecting a capacitated adult's right to choose harm, and documenting that the choice was informed and free?
- Where capacity was impaired, did I use the least restrictive substitute mechanism, honoring prior wishes, scoping any guardianship narrowly, and following legal process?
- Does my documentation capture the decision, standard, components, conditions, undue-influence analysis, conclusion, and basis — sufficient to withstand legal challenge?
