# Court Report Writing And Affidavit Drafting

Court reports and affidavits are written evidence that can remove children, restrict liberty, determine custody, or commit a person to treatment, and the central judgment problem is that the register of clinical narrative, interpretive, holistic, fluent, often assuming rather than demonstrating its basis, is the opposite of the register legal documents require, which is precise, sourced, balanced, and explicit about the distinction between what is known and what is inferred. The writer must produce documents that serve the court's need for honest, complete, well-grounded information while resisting the pull of any party who wants the document shaped toward a preferred outcome, including the writer's own agency or clinical opinion. Every statement in a court document must be traceable to its source and labeled by its type, fact, assessment, or opinion, because conflating them misleads the court about the weight and basis of the evidence and hands opposing counsel the means to discredit the whole document. Conclusions must be earned by showing the evidence and reasoning that support them, not merely asserted, because a report that is all conclusion and no basis will be challenged and may be disregarded. Agents err by writing advocacy disguised as assessment, by using conclusory or pejorative language, by omitting unfavorable evidence, and by treating the document as a clinical narrative rather than as evidence that will be scrutinized line by line.

## Core Rules

### Treat Every Court Document as Evidence That Will Be Scrutinized

A court report, affidavit, or sworn statement is evidence, and it will be read by parties who have an interest in challenging it, by a judge who must weigh it, and by a record that preserves it indefinitely. Treat every such document as something that will be scrutinized for its factual basis, its reasoning, its balance, its language, and its internal consistency, and produce it with that scrutiny in mind. Write with the awareness that the document stands on its own, that any sentence may be quoted against you or your client, and that its quality reflects on your credibility in this case and across your career. A casual or sloppy court document damages the case, the client, and the professional reputation. The standard is not what suffices in clinical documentation but what withstands adversarial review.

### Distinguish Fact, Assessment, and Opinion Explicitly Throughout

In every court document, distinguish clearly and consistently between fact, what was directly observed, reliably documented, or reported by a identified source; assessment, the professional interpretation of the facts; and opinion, the professional judgment or recommendation. Use language and structure that signal the distinction: attribute facts to their sources ("The mother stated...", "The record indicates..."), label assessment as interpretation ("The assessment indicated..."), and present opinion as professional judgment ("In my professional opinion...", "It is my recommendation that..."). Each type of statement carries different weight and requires a different basis, and the court relies on the distinction to evaluate the evidence. State the basis for each statement, and where the basis is thin or secondhand, acknowledge it, because a court that discovers an unlabeled inference presented as fact loses trust in the entire document.

### Base Every Conclusion on Evidence and Reasoning, Not Assertion

Every conclusion in a court document, that a child is at risk, that a parent lacks capacity, that reunification is appropriate, that a client requires commitment, must be supported by the specific evidence and the reasoning that connects the evidence to the conclusion. Present the facts, cite their sources, show the inferential steps, and arrive at the conclusion, so the court can follow and independently evaluate the reasoning. A conclusion asserted without its basis is a vulnerable conclusion, and a report that consists of conclusions without their foundation will be challenged and may be disregarded entirely. Where the evidence is mixed or the conclusion is uncertain, present the complexity honestly rather than forcing a false clarity, because the court is better served by a candid assessment of uncertainty than by an overconfident assertion. Show the work.

### Write in a Balanced, Precise, and Neutral Register Free of Advocacy

Court reports and affidavits should present the evidence, the assessment derived from it, and the reasoning that connects them, in a way that is balanced and honest, including evidence that complicates or contradicts the preferred view. State the facts and their sources, present the evidence that supports and that cuts against your conclusions, provide the professional reasoning, and state the conclusions and recommendations with their basis and their limits. The language should be precise, neutral, and professional, describing behavior and observations in terms the evidence supports, rather than characterizing people or situations with pejorative or loaded terms that smuggle in judgment the evidence has not earned. Replace conclusory labels, "non-compliant," "manipulative," "dangerous," with specific behavioral descriptions tied to observed evidence, and reserve evaluative terms for the assessment and opinion sections where they are explicitly labeled. Avoid conclusory language that asserts without demonstrating, and avoid advocacy disguised as assessment, which is the most common and most damaging failure mode. A balanced report that acknowledges its weaknesses is more credible and more useful to the court than a one-sided brief, because the court trusts the writer who demonstrates command of the complications.

### Attribute Every Fact to a Source and Date It

Every factual statement in a court document should be traceable to a specific source and, where relevant, a specific date, so the court and the parties can verify and weigh it. Attribute observations to yourself with the date, statements to the person who made them, records to the document they come from, and reports from other professionals to their authors, so that no fact floats free of its origin. Unattributed facts appear to be the writer's own observation or knowledge when they may be secondhand, unverified, or misremembered, and this ambiguity is a primary target of cross-examination. Build the habit of sourcing as you write, and review the draft specifically to confirm that every factual claim carries its attribution and date.

### Present Unfavorable Evidence Honestly Rather Than Omitting It

The duty of honesty to the court requires that unfavorable or complicating evidence be presented, not omitted, because a court document that presents only the evidence supporting the preferred conclusion is advocacy disguised as assessment, and its omission will be discovered. Identify the evidence that complicates or contradicts your conclusions, present it fairly, address its significance in your reasoning, and explain why your conclusion holds or must be modified in light of it. A report that acknowledges and grapples with the unfavorable evidence is more credible than one that pretends it does not exist, because the court recognizes that honest assessment encounters complication. Omission, exposed, destroys credibility more thoroughly than the unfavorable evidence itself would have.

### Handle Confidentiality and Records Requests According to Authority

Court documents and the records that accompany them are governed by legal and ethical rules of confidentiality, consent, and authority, and the writer must handle them accordingly. Confirm the authority for any records release, consent, court order, or subpoena, release only what is authorized and necessary, redact or withhold what is protected, and seek guidance on ambiguous requests rather than defaulting to compliance. Recognize that exhibits, attachments, and supporting documents carry the same confidentiality obligations as the report itself, and that over-disclosure can breach privacy as seriously as the report's text. When in doubt about the scope of authority, consult counsel or supervision before releasing.

## Common Traps

### Advocacy Disguised as Assessment

The writer produces a document that advocates for a preferred outcome, shading the evidence and omitting the unfavorable, while presenting it as professional assessment. The mechanism is that the writer believes in the outcome. The false signal is a clear, confident recommendation. The harm is that the court is misled, the writer's credibility is destroyed when the advocacy is exposed, and the integrity of the proceeding is compromised. The duty of honesty overrides the preferred outcome.

### Conclusions Without the Underlying Basis

The document asserts conclusions without presenting the evidence and reasoning that support them, leaving the court unable to evaluate the basis. The mechanism is that the conclusions feel well-founded to the writer. The false signal is a decisive recommendation. The harm is that the court cannot follow the reasoning, the conclusion is vulnerable to challenge, and the writer appears to assert rather than demonstrate. Conclusions must show their work.

### Conflating Fact, Assessment, and Opinion

The writer presents opinion as fact, or assessment as direct observation, blurring the distinctions the court uses to weigh evidence. The mechanism is that the distinctions feel technical in fluent practice. The false signal is seamless, confident narrative. The harm is that the court is misled about the weight and basis of the evidence, and the document is vulnerable to line-by-line challenge on cross-examination. The distinctions must be explicit throughout.

### Pejorative or Conclusory Language Substituting for Evidence

The writer uses loaded terms, "non-compliant," "manipulative," "deadbeat," that assert judgment the evidence has not earned, in place of specific behavioral description. The mechanism is that the terms feel accurate to the writer's impression. The false signal is vivid, persuasive characterization. The harm is that the language signals bias, invites challenge, and substitutes labels for the evidence and reasoning the court needs. Precise behavioral description must replace pejorative characterization.

### Omitting Unfavorable Evidence

The writer leaves out evidence that complicates or contradicts the preferred conclusion, presenting only the supportive material. The mechanism is that the unfavorable evidence feels like a distraction from the right answer. The false signal is a clean, coherent report. The harm is that the omission is discovered, the writer's credibility and the document's reliability collapse, and the court is misled. Unfavorable evidence must be presented and addressed.

### Overstating Certainty Beyond the Basis

The writer uses language that implies more certainty than the evidence supports, "the child will be harmed," "the parent is definitely incapable," exposing the document to challenge. The mechanism is that strong language feels persuasive. The false signal is authoritative assessment. The harm is that the overstatement is exposed, the writer appears to exaggerate, and the legitimate evidence is discounted by association with the overreach. Language must match the basis and acknowledge uncertainty.

### Unattributed Facts Presented as Observation

The writer states facts without sources, so they appear to be direct observation or established knowledge when they are secondhand, reported, or unverified. The mechanism is that unsourced facts read smoothly in narrative. The false signal is a well-written report. The harm is that the ambiguity is exploited on cross-examination, the basis of the facts is unclear, and the document's reliability is questioned. Every fact needs its source.

## Self-Check

- Did I treat the document as evidence that will be scrutinized, writing to withstand adversarial review?
- Did I distinguish fact, assessment, and opinion explicitly and consistently throughout?
- Did I base every conclusion on the specific evidence and reasoning, showing the work?
- Did I write in a balanced, evidence-based register, free of advocacy and pejorative language?
- Did I attribute every fact to a specific source and date it where relevant?
- Did I present unfavorable or complicating evidence honestly rather than omitting it?
- Did I use precise, neutral language, replacing pejorative terms with behavioral description?
- Did I handle confidentiality and any records release according to the governing authority?
