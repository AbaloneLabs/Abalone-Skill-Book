# Mass Casualty And Disaster Psychological First Aid

Psychological first aid in mass casualty and disaster contexts is the structured, evidence-informed provision of immediate, practical, and compassionate support to people who have just been exposed to a traumatic event, in settings that are often chaotic, unsafe, and emotionally overwhelming. The judgment problem is that the worker arrives into a scene where normal rules are suspended, where dozens or hundreds of people need help at once, where information is incomplete or wrong, and where the temptation to do something dramatic, to push survivors to talk, to dispense reassurance, must be resisted in favor of the quieter, more disciplined work of reducing arousal, restoring practical functioning, and connecting people to what they need. Agents err by importing clinical therapy into an acute field setting, by pressing for trauma narratives before survivors are ready, by triaging on who is loudest rather than who is most vulnerable, and by neglecting their own and their team's sustainability in the rush to respond. This skill governs the acute, hands-on provision of psychological first aid, distinct from the longer arc of disaster recovery case management.

## Core Rules

### Triage by Acuity, Vulnerability, and Capacity, Not by Visibility

In a mass casualty or disaster scene, the people who demand attention are often those with the resources and capacity to seek it, while the most acute and most vulnerable, people in shock, people with disabilities, older adults, separated children, people with limited language, people who have frozen or dissociated, may be silent or overlooked. Conduct active, systematic triage: scan the setting for the quiet and immobile as well as the agitated, use a rapid mental health triage approach to sort by acuity (who is a danger to self or others, who is unable to function, who is acutely dissociated, who is in physiological crisis), and prioritize the most acute and the most vulnerable first. Recognize that the visible crowd is not the whole affected population, and that equity in the response requires actively finding those who cannot reach you. Document triage decisions briefly, because the volume of need exceeds memory.

### Establish Safety and Reduce Physiological Arousal Before Anything Else

Survivors in the acute aftermath are often in a state of physiological hyperarousal or dissociation that makes everything else, information processing, decision-making, disclosure, impossible, and the first task is to help the nervous system settle. Establish physical safety (move them away from the scene, the bodies, the noise), offer the basics of comfort (water, a blanket, a place to sit, something to orient to), use grounding and orientation, speak in a calm, slow, simple register, and allow silence. Do not press for a trauma narrative, do not ask "what happened to you," and do not interpret silence as a need to be drawn out, because pushing a survivor to process before they are regulated can retraumatize them. Arousal reduction is not a preliminary step before the real work; it is most of the real work in the acute phase.

### Provide Practical Assistance and Accurate Information

In the acute phase, survivors need practical help, finding a loved one, reaching shelter, getting medical care, locating belongings, contacting family, navigating the immediate next steps, far more than they need psychological processing, and accurate information is itself a stabilizing intervention. Offer concrete help with the immediate practical tasks, connect survivors to the services and information that exist, and be honest about what you do not know rather than offering false reassurance to soothe. Rumor and misinformation spread rapidly in disasters and cause additional harm, so verify information before sharing it, correct misinformation gently, and direct people to official information sources. Practical assistance and honest information restore a measure of agency and predictability that the disaster stripped away.

### Support Natural Coping and Connection, Do Not Replace It

Most survivors recover from disaster exposure through their own coping and their own social connections, and the role of psychological first aid is to support and restore those natural mechanisms, not to substitute professional intervention for them. Encourage connection to family, friends, faith community, and existing supports; help survivors contact and reunite with loved ones; reinforce the coping they are already doing; and normalize the range of acute reactions (numbness, agitation, tears, anger, dissociation) as expected responses to an abnormal event. Avoid implying that survivors need clinical treatment to recover, avoid pathologizing normal acute distress, and reserve referral to higher-level care for those whose reactions indicate risk or failure of natural recovery. The default expectation is recovery, supported by connection, not disorder requiring treatment.

### Handle Death Notification and Family Assistance With Care

Death notification and family assistance in mass casualty events are among the most consequential tasks a disaster responder undertakes, and they must be done with preparation, accuracy, compassion, and respect. Confirm identity before any notification, deliver notification in a private, supported setting with a colleague present, use clear and unambiguous language, allow time for the reaction without rushing, and offer practical next steps and connection to ongoing support. Anticipate the range of reactions (including anger, collapse, denial) and do not take them personally, do not minimize with false comfort, and do not leave the family alone immediately after. Coordinate with the family assistance center, medical examiner, and law enforcement so that families receive consistent information and are not re-traumatized by repeated or conflicting notifications.

### Work Within the Incident Command and Coordination Structure

Disaster response operates under an incident command or coordination structure that assigns roles, authority, and communication channels, and psychological first aid is delivered within that structure, not as independent clinical practice. Know your role and your span of authority, report through the established chain, share information about needs and resources, and avoid freelancing that duplicates or conflicts with other responders' work. The psychosocial and vulnerability perspective that social work brings is often missing from logistics- and security-oriented response, so contribute it clearly, persistently, and through the structure so that it shapes decisions. Working outside the structure, however well-intentioned, fragments the response and can endanger survivors and responders.

### Monitor and Protect Your Own and Your Team's Capacity

Acute disaster response is physically and psychologically depleting, and responders who push past their limits make worse decisions, miss cues, and may cause harm, so sustainability is an operational requirement, not an optional virtue. Build in rotation, rest, hydration, and breaks; monitor yourself and your teammates for signs of acute stress, dissociation, or collapse; speak up when you or a teammate need to step back; and use peer support and supervision during and after the shift. Do not frame working beyond healthy limits as heroism, because the depleted responder is a liability to survivors and to the team, and the model of unsustainable practice spreads through the team. Protecting capacity is part of providing competent care.

## Common Traps

### Deploying Trauma Therapy in the Acute Phase

The worker, trained in trauma treatment, deploys techniques that require the survivor to process or recount the trauma while still in the acute, dysregulated phase. The mechanism is that trauma expertise feels relevant and urgent. The false signal is skilled, timely intervention. The harm is that the survivor is retraumatized or overwhelmed when they most need regulation and practical support, and the intervention is unsupported by evidence for the acute phase. Acute-phase support is practical and regulating, not therapeutic.

### Pressing Survivors to Recount the Trauma

The worker believes that talking through the trauma is healing and presses survivors to narrate what happened before they are ready or willing. The mechanism is that disclosure feels like the path to relief. The false signal is compassionate, attentive listening. The harm is that premature disclosure can intensify distress and embed the trauma narrative, and survivors may withdraw or feel violated. Disclosure follows the survivor's lead and readiness, not the worker's agenda.

### Triage by Visibility and Volume

The worker responds to whoever is loudest, most demanding, or most visible, and the quiet, frozen, or hidden survivors, often the most acute or vulnerable, go unattended. The mechanism is that visible distress feels like the priority. The false signal is responsive, efficient triage. The harm is that the most vulnerable are missed, inequity is reproduced, and preventable deterioration occurs. Triage must be active and systematic, not reactive to volume.

### False Reassurance to Soothe Rather Than Honest Information

The worker, wanting to comfort, offers reassurance that everything will be fine, that loved ones are surely safe, or that recovery is certain, when the worker does not actually know. The mechanism is that reassurance feels kind and lowers visible distress. The false signal is compassionate, calming care. The harm is that trust is destroyed when the reassurance proves false, the survivor is re-traumatized by the gap, and misinformation spreads. Honest acknowledgment of uncertainty is more respectful and more stabilizing.

### Pathologizing Normal Acute Distress

The worker interprets the normal range of acute reactions (numbness, agitation, dissociation, anger, intrusive imagery in the first hours and days) as signs of disorder, and refers survivors for clinical treatment they do not need. The mechanism is that strong reactions look like pathology. The false signal is vigilant, clinically informed response. The harm is that survivors are medicalized, natural recovery is disrupted, and limited clinical resources are diverted from those who truly need them. Most acute distress is expected and self-resolving.

### Freelancing Outside the Coordination Structure

The worker, seeing unmet needs, acts independently of the incident command structure, creating parallel services, conflicting information, or duplicated effort. The mechanism is that visible need feels urgent and the structure feels slow. The false signal is dedicated, responsive practice. The harm is that the response is fragmented, survivors receive conflicting information, and other responders' work is undermined. Response must be coordinated through the structure.

### Neglecting Self and Team Sustainability

The worker sustains the response beyond healthy limits, framed as commitment, and develops acute stress, dissociation, or impaired judgment that harms survivors. The mechanism is that duty and adrenaline override self-monitoring. The false signal is heroic dedication. The harm is that the responder is harmed, decisions deteriorate, and an unsustainable model is set for the team. Sustainability is an operational requirement of competent care.

## Self-Check

- Did I triage by acuity and vulnerability, actively finding the quiet and hidden survivors rather than reacting to volume?
- Did I establish safety and reduce physiological arousal before pressing for any disclosure or processing?
- Did I provide practical assistance and accurate, verified information rather than false reassurance?
- Did I support natural coping and connection, reserving clinical referral for those whose reactions indicate risk or failed recovery?
- Did I handle death notification and family assistance with preparation, accuracy, compassion, and without leaving families alone?
- Did I work within the incident command structure, contributing the psychosocial perspective through the established channels?
- Did I monitor and protect my own and my team's capacity, treating rest and rotation as operational requirements?
- Did I avoid deploying trauma therapy and premature disclosure in the acute phase, where evidence does not support them?
