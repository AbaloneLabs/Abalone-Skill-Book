# Refugee Resettlement and Basic Needs Stabilization

Refugee and asylum-seeking clients arrive carrying the cumulative weight of pre-migration trauma, dangerous transit, and the disorienting shock of arrival in a country whose systems, language, and social codes are unfamiliar. The social worker's first task is not clinical — it is stabilization. A family that has no safe housing, no food security, no enrolled children, and no access to healthcare cannot engage meaningfully in any longer-term plan, no matter how well designed. The judgment problem is that the urgency of basic needs can crowd out the assessment of safety and legal status, and that well-intentioned rapid referrals can accidentally trigger immigration consequences or re-traumatize clients who do not yet understand what they are being asked to consent to.

## Core Rules

### Establish Legal Status Before Any Service Referral

Before coordinating services, determine — with the client or their legal representative — what immigration status they hold or are pursuing, because status determines eligibility for nearly every benefit program, the right to work, access to public healthcare, and the risk of interacting with government agencies. A pending asylum claim, refugee resettlement status, undocumented presence, Temporary Protected Status, a pending U-visa, and lawful permanent residency each carry different eligibility maps and different exposure. Do not assume eligibility from nationality or from the word "refugee" alone. Where status is uncertain, connect the client to immigration legal services before initiating benefits applications that could require disclosure of presence or work history, and document what you know versus what remains unverified.

### Sequence Stabilization by Survival Hierarchy

Address needs in the order that failure is most immediately dangerous: immediate safety from violence or trafficking, emergency shelter, food, urgent medical and mental health care, and then enrollment of children in school. Resist the pressure to front-load employment authorization or long-term planning while a family is still food-insecure or sleeping in a car. Within each tier, identify the fastest legitimate pathway rather than the most comprehensive one — a family that can eat tonight and sleep indoors is in a fundamentally better position to engage with case planning than one who has been waiting two weeks for the "right" housing program.

### Use Trauma-Informed and Interpretation-Appropriate Communication

Assume trauma exposure in every refugee or asylum-seeking client until assessment indicates otherwise, even when the client presents as calm and composed. Forced migration routinely involves exposure to violence, loss, persecution, sexual violence, torture, family separation, and dangerous transit including exploitation. Use trauma-informed communication: explain what you are going to ask and why before asking it, give the client control over pacing, allow silence, and never push for a trauma narrative during intake. Use professionally trained interpreters — never family members, never children, never untrained bilingual staff — and brief the interpreter on trauma-informed practice before the session. Confirm the client's preferred language and dialect, because a client who speaks "Arabic" may need a specific dialect interpreter, and a client who appears to speak English may be functionally limited under stress.

### Coordinate With Resettlement Agencies and Avoid Duplicate or Conflicting Enrollment

Refugees admitted through formal resettlement typically have a designated voluntary agency that is responsible for core services during the initial period, and enrolling a client in parallel services can create duplicate benefits, conflicting case plans, or benefit overpayments that the client later must repay. Before initiating services, identify whether the client has an assigned resettlement agency, a case manager, or an existing benefits case, and coordinate rather than duplicate. Document the agency, case manager name, and case number. Where the client has fallen out of contact with their resettlement agency, reconnecting them is often faster and more comprehensive than building services from scratch.

### Protect Confidentiality Against Immigration Enforcement Exposure

Refugee and immigrant clients may fear that any contact with social services will lead to immigration enforcement. Understand the confidentiality protections that apply to your agency and your records, including any limits on information sharing with immigration enforcement, and explain these protections to the client in their language. Do not collect or record information about immigration status, country of origin details, or transit history beyond what is necessary for service provision, because any record can become discoverable. When a client discloses undocumented family members, do not record identifying information in the case file unless it is necessary for service. Be aware of which localities and agencies have policies limiting cooperation with immigration enforcement, and do not reassure clients with blanket promises you cannot guarantee.

### Address the Children's Educational and Developmental Needs Promptly

Refugee children experience disruption in education, language, peer relationships, and developmental routine, and delayed school enrollment compounds trauma and developmental harm. Federal protections generally require immediate enrollment of school-age children regardless of immigration status and regardless of missing documents, and social workers should advocate for enrollment rather than wait for complete paperwork. Coordinate with school social workers, English language learner programs, and culturally responsive school staff. Be alert to bullying, social isolation, and the particular burden placed on children who serve as family interpreters — a role that can both accelerate language acquisition and expose children to adult content they are not developmentally equipped to process.

### Screen for Trafficking, Exploitation, and Ongoing Coercion

Refugees and asylum seekers are at elevated risk for labor trafficking, sex trafficking, and financial exploitation, including by sponsors, employers, landlords, and even extended family. During intake and ongoing contact, screen indirectly for indicators: a third party who controls the client's documents, who speaks for the client, who receives the client's wages, who restricts the client's movement, or who is present and refuses to leave the interview. Where trafficking is suspected, do not confront the suspected trafficker, do not remove the client from the situation without a safety plan, and connect immediately to trafficking-specific services and legal advocacy, because trafficking victims may be eligible for specialized immigration relief that has strict timelines.

## Common Traps

### Treating the Resettlement Timeline as the Client's Timeline

Resettlement programs operate on fixed timelines — ninety days of core services, a certain number of months of medical assistance, deadlines for employment placement. The trap is internalizing these program timelines as if they reflect the client's actual readiness. A client who is still experiencing flashbacks, who has not yet grieved family separation, or who is caring for a traumatized child cannot be pushed through a self-sufficiency timeline designed for an untraumatized adult. When workers optimize for program-completion metrics rather than client stabilization, clients disengage, services end prematurely, and the family becomes invisible to the system until crisis re-surfaces them. The mechanism of harm is that program timelines are administrative constructs, while trauma recovery follows its own pace, and forcing the former onto the latter produces neither self-sufficiency nor stability.

### Assuming English Proficiency From Apparent Comprehension

A client who nods, smiles, and responds to simple questions may appear to understand English, but under stress, fatigue, and the cognitive load of navigating an unfamiliar system, functional comprehension often collapses. The trap is proceeding on the basis of apparent comprehension — having the client sign forms, agree to referrals, or attend appointments — without verifying genuine understanding, which results in missed appointments, misunderstood instructions, signed documents the client did not comprehend, and ultimately disengagement. The harm is compounded because the client often cannot articulate their confusion without losing face or appearing uncooperative, so the misunderstanding remains invisible until a consequence materializes. The corrective is to use teach-back in the client's language, to use trained interpreters even when the client appears to manage in English, and to treat apparent comprehension as a hypothesis to be tested rather than a fact.

### Front-Loading Employment Services Before Trauma Stabilization

Resettlement programs are funded on a self-sufficiency model, and the pressure to move clients toward employment is intense and often begins within weeks of arrival. The trap is pushing a client who is experiencing acute trauma symptoms, untreated medical conditions, or overwhelming family caregiving demands into job search and job placement, where they underperform, lose the job, and enter a cycle of failed placements that damages both their confidence and their case record. The deeper harm is that premature employment can re-traumatize clients placed in exploitative or unsafe work, can interfere with asylum claim preparation that requires the client's focused attention, and can mask untreated mental health needs that will eventually surface as crisis. The mechanism is that employment is treated as the goal rather than as an outcome of stabilization, inverting the actual sequence that produces durable self-sufficiency.

### Recording Immigration Status Details That Create Future Risk

The case management instinct is to document thoroughly, and the trap is applying that instinct to immigration status information — recording the client's entry date, method of entry, prior deportations, family members' status, or transit route details — without evaluating whether each piece of information is necessary for the service being provided. Because case files can be subpoenaed, shared with oversight agencies, or inadvertently disclosed, every recorded detail about immigration status is a potential future liability for the client. The harm is that information collected to help a client can later be used against them in immigration proceedings, custody disputes, or benefits fraud investigations. The corrective is to record only what is necessary for eligibility and service provision, to use general rather than specific language where possible, and to consult agency counsel before recording sensitive status details.

### Conflating Culture With Trauma or Pathology

When working across cultures, there is a risk of misreading culturally normative behavior as pathology, or conversely, of attributing trauma symptoms to culture and thereby failing to assess them. A client who avoids eye contact may be expressing cultural respect, trauma-related avoidance, or both. A client who describes physical symptoms rather than emotional ones may be expressing distress in a culturally shaped idiom, not somatizing pathology. The trap is collapsing these possibilities into a single interpretation — pathologizing culturally normative behavior, or normalizing trauma symptoms as "cultural" — and the harm is that both errors produce inappropriate intervention: unnecessary psychiatric referral in the first case, and failure to treat real suffering in the second. The corrective is to seek cultural consultation, to ask rather than assume, and to hold multiple hypotheses simultaneously.

## Self-Check

- Did I establish or verify the client's immigration status, or connect them to immigration legal services, before initiating benefits applications that could require status disclosure?
- Did I sequence stabilization by survival hierarchy — safety, shelter, food, urgent medical and mental health care, children's school enrollment — rather than by program timelines or self-sufficiency metrics?
- Did I use professionally trained interpreters in the client's preferred language and dialect, and did I verify comprehension through teach-back rather than apparent understanding?
- Did I identify whether the client has an assigned resettlement agency or existing case, and coordinate rather than duplicate services?
- Did I screen indirectly for trafficking and exploitation — controlling third parties, document control, wage control, movement restriction — and connect to trafficking-specific services where indicated?
- Did I explain the confidentiality protections that apply to my records, and did I limit recording of immigration status details to what is necessary for service?
- Did I treat apparent English comprehension as a hypothesis to be tested rather than a fact, particularly for forms, consent, and appointments?
- Did I seek cultural consultation rather than conflating culturally normative behavior with trauma or pathology?
