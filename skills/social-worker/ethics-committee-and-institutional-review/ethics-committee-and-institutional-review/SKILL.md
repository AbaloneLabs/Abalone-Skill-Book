# Ethics Committee And Institutional Review

Ethics committees and institutional review boards are the structures through which organizations and professions deliberate on hard cases, review research and practice for ethical soundness, and develop policy on recurring ethical issues. The judgment problem is that these bodies must apply ethical principles and regulatory requirements to complex, particular situations, must deliberate carefully without becoming a rubber stamp or an obstruction, and must balance the protection of participants and clients against the value of the work and the autonomy of the professionals involved. Agents err by treating ethics review as a procedural hurdle rather than substantive deliberation, by applying rules rigidly without engaging the particular situation, by deferring to the authority or prestige of the applicant, and by failing to recognize the power dynamics and conflicts of interest that compromise the review.

## Core Rules

### Engage in Substantive Ethical Deliberation, Not Procedural Compliance

Ethics review is not the completion of a form or the verification that boxes are checked; it is substantive deliberation on whether the proposed research or practice is ethically sound, given the principles (beneficence, non-maleficence, autonomy, justice, fidelity), the regulatory requirements, and the particular situation. Engage the substance: what are the risks and benefits, who bears them, is the consent real, are the vulnerable protected, is the design necessary to answer the question, are the conflicts of interest managed. A review that confirms procedural compliance without engaging these questions fails the ethical function, however complete its paperwork.

### Apply Principles to the Particular Situation

Ethical principles are general, and their application requires careful attention to the particular situation: the population, the setting, the intervention, the risks, the benefits, the alternatives, and the context. Avoid both the rigid application of rules without engagement with the situation and the relativistic abandonment of principles to the situation. Reason from the principles to a conclusion that fits the case, explain the reasoning, and recognize where genuine ethical uncertainty requires humility, further information, or conditions. The value of the committee is in this particularized reasoning, which neither a rulebook nor an individual can provide alone.

### Scrutinize Risk, Benefit, and Their Distribution

The core of research and practice ethics is the assessment of risks and benefits, and crucially, of who bears the risks and who receives the benefits. Assess the nature, magnitude, and probability of the risks, the nature and likelihood of the benefits, the relationship between them, and whether those who bear the risk are also those who benefit or whether the benefits flow to others (researchers, institutions, future populations). Particular scrutiny is required where the risks fall on vulnerable populations who cannot fully protect their own interests, and where the benefits flow primarily to parties other than the participants. An unfavorable risk-benefit distribution, however well-intentioned the work, is an ethical problem.

### Verify That Consent Is Real, Not Merely Documented

Consent is the process by which a person understands and voluntarily agrees to participate, and the signed form is only the documentation of that process, not the process itself. Scrutinize whether the consent is real: is the information understandable to this population, is the person capable of understanding, is there coercion or undue influence, is there adequate time, is the person aware they can refuse or withdraw without penalty, and is the consent ongoing rather than one-time. Particular attention is required where the population is vulnerable (cognitive impairment, incarceration, hierarchy, dependency), and where the power asymmetry between researcher and participant is large. Documented consent that is not real consent is not ethical.

### Identify and Manage Conflicts of Interest

Conflicts of interest, where the reviewer or the applicant has a stake in the outcome that could compromise independent judgment, are pervasive and must be identified and managed. Reviewers must disclose and recuse from decisions where they have a personal, financial, or institutional interest; applicants must disclose their interests and funding; and the committee must weigh whether the interests compromise the design, the consent, or the risk-benefit balance. Do not assume that good intentions neutralize conflicts, and do not let the prestige or authority of the applicant override the scrutiny the conflict requires. Conflicts that are disclosed and managed can be tolerated; conflicts that are concealed or unmanaged corrupt the review.

### Protect Vulnerable Populations With Heightened Scrutiny

Vulnerable populations (children, prisoners, people with cognitive impairments, pregnant people, economically or socially disadvantaged, students and subordinates) require heightened ethical scrutiny because their capacity to protect their own interests, to refuse, or to access the benefits of the work may be limited. Apply additional protections: surrogate consent with the person's assent where appropriate, limits on risk, attention to coercion and undue influence, and assurance that the research cannot be done with a less vulnerable population. Do not treat vulnerable populations as convenient subjects for research that could be done elsewhere, and do not exclude them from research that could benefit them out of overprotection that denies them access. The balance is delicate and requires careful reasoning.

### Maintain Independence From the Applicant and the Institution

The ethics committee's value depends on its independence from the applicants whose work it reviews and from the institution whose interests it may constrain. Maintain that independence: do not allow the applicant to shape the review, do not defer to institutional pressure to approve work that serves the institution's interests, and do not let the desire to be collegial compromise the scrutiny. Seek outside consultation where the committee lacks expertise or where the internal relationships compromise independence. A committee that is not independent is a rubber stamp, regardless of its formal authority.

### Document the Deliberation and Its Reasoning

The committee's decision must be documented with its reasoning, not only its conclusion, because the reasoning is what makes the decision reviewable, educates future deliberation, and establishes the ethical basis of the work. Record the questions considered, the information reviewed, the reasoning from principles to conclusion, the conditions or modifications required, and the basis for the decision. A decision documented only as approved or denied fails the functions of accountability, learning, and precedent that the record serves.

## Common Traps

### Procedural Compliance Without Substantive Deliberation

The committee confirms that the application is complete and the forms are signed, and approves it, without engaging the substantive ethical questions. The mechanism is that procedural completion feels like review. The false signal is a thorough process. The harm is that ethically problematic research or practice is approved, participants or clients are harmed, and the committee fails its protective function. Substantive deliberation is required.

### Deferring to the Authority or Prestige of the Applicant

The committee defers to a senior researcher, a prestigious institution, or an influential clinician, and does not scrutinize the work as it would a less prominent applicant. The mechanism is that authority feels like assurance. The false signal is respect for expertise. The harm is that the work of prominent applicants is held to a lower standard, ethical problems are missed, and the committee's independence is compromised. Scrutiny must be uniform.

### Rigid Rule Application Without Engagement With the Situation

The committee applies rules rigidly, without engaging the particular situation, and reaches decisions that are ethically obtuse or obstructive. The mechanism is that rules feel objective and safe. The false signal is rigorous review. The harm is that ethically sound work is obstructed, the particular ethical features of the case are missed, and the committee becomes an obstacle rather than a resource. Principles must be applied to the situation.

### Documented Consent Treated as Real Consent

The committee accepts a signed consent form as evidence of real consent, without scrutinizing whether the person understood, was capable, or was free of coercion. The mechanism is that documentation feels like evidence. The false signal is compliant consent procedures. The harm is that participants are enrolled without real consent, the ethical requirement is unmet, and the harm is concealed by the paperwork. Real consent must be verified.

### Concealed or Unmanaged Conflicts of Interest

The committee member or applicant with a conflict does not disclose or recuse, and the review is compromised by the undisclosed interest. The mechanism is that the interest feels manageable or the disclosure feels embarrassing. The false signal is a unanimous decision. The harm is that the decision is biased, the committee's integrity is compromised, and the conflict, when exposed, discredits the review. Conflicts must be disclosed and managed.

### Overprotecting or Underprotecting Vulnerable Populations

The committee either excludes vulnerable populations from beneficial research out of overprotection, or subjects them to risky research out of convenience, missing the careful balance required. The mechanism is that protection and access feel opposed. The false signal is either vigilance or inclusion. The harm is that vulnerable people are either denied the benefits of research or exposed to its risks without adequate protection. The balance must be reasoned.

### Decisions Documented Without Reasoning

The committee records its decision as approved or denied without the reasoning, and the decision is unreviewable and uneducative. The mechanism is that the conclusion feels sufficient. The false signal is a clear decision. The harm is that the ethical basis is opaque, future deliberation cannot learn from it, and the decision cannot be effectively reviewed or appealed. The reasoning must be documented.

## Self-Check

- Did I engage in substantive ethical deliberation, not merely procedural compliance?
- Did I apply the principles to the particular situation, avoiding both rigidity and relativism?
- Did I scrutinize the risks, benefits, and their distribution across the affected parties?
- Did I verify that consent is real (understanding, capacity, voluntariness), not merely documented?
- Did I identify and manage conflicts of interest, disclosing and recusing as required?
- Did I apply heightened scrutiny to vulnerable populations, balancing protection and access?
- Did I maintain independence from the applicant and the institution throughout the review?
- Did I document the deliberation and its reasoning, not only the conclusion?
