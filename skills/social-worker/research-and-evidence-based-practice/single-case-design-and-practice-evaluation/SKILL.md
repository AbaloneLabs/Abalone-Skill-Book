# Single-Case Design And Practice Evaluation

Single-case experimental designs allow a social worker to evaluate whether a specific intervention is working for a specific client, using the client as their own control, and they are among the most practical tools for accountability in real practice. Unlike group research, which averages across people and may say nothing about the individual in front of you, single-case design tracks one person's behavior across phases and asks whether change coincides with the introduction, withdrawal, or reintroduction of the intervention. The recurring judgment problem is that the method is easy to do badly and hard to do well: a few points on a graph can be made to tell any story, an unstable baseline can manufacture or mask an effect, and the clinician who is also the data collector is subject to powerful biases toward seeing improvement. Agents err by starting an intervention before establishing a stable baseline, by graphing without pre-specifying what would count as change, by abandoning the design the moment the data become inconvenient, and by overclaiming from an N of one what can only be inferred about a single person. Done rigorously, however, single-case design is one of the most honest forms of practice evaluation available, because it forces the worker to test, in real time, whether the help they are offering is actually helping. This skill covers AB and ABAB withdrawal designs, multiple-baseline designs, baseline stability, goal attainment scaling, routine progress monitoring, the use of session-by-session data to adjust intervention, and the limitations and ethics of N equals one, with the overriding rule that single-case design exists to make practice accountable to evidence, not to manufacture evidence for a preferred conclusion.

## Core Rules

### Establish A Stable Baseline Before Introducing The Intervention

The baseline phase is the foundation of any single-case design, because it defines the client's typical functioning against which change is judged, and a baseline that is unstable, trending, or too short undermines every later inference. Collect enough data points, typically a minimum of three to five, to reveal both the level and the variability of the behavior, and continue until the data show a stable pattern rather than a clear upward or downward trend that would confound the intervention effect. If the baseline is trending in the direction of the hoped-for change, any later improvement may simply be the continuation of that trend, not the effect of the intervention, and the design cannot distinguish the two. Do not begin the intervention simply because the session arrived or because the baseline data are uncomfortable; wait for stability, or document explicitly that stability was not achieved and therefore the inference is weaker. A disciplined baseline is what separates an experiment from a clinical impression.

### Match The Design To The Question And The Risk Of Withdrawal

The two most useful families of design in practice are the withdrawal or reversal designs (AB, ABA, ABAB) and the multiple-baseline design, and each answers a different question under different constraints. In an ABAB design, A is the baseline and B is the intervention phase, and the reintroduction and withdrawal of the intervention tests whether change reliably follows the intervention; the strength is that a replicated effect across phases is hard to attribute to coincidence, but the ethical cost is that withdrawing an effective intervention, even temporarily, may cause harm, and ABAB is therefore inappropriate for dangerous behaviors, skills that should not be unlearned, or any case where withdrawal is unethical. The multiple-baseline design introduces the intervention at different times across different behaviors, settings, or individuals, and demonstrates effectiveness when change follows the introduction in each case without requiring withdrawal, making it the ethically safer choice when the behavior is harmful or the intervention should not be reversed. Choose the design before collecting data, based on the question, the behavior, and the ethical constraints on withdrawal.

### Pre-Specify What Will Count As Change Before You See The Data

The most defensible single-case evaluation states, in advance, what pattern of data would count as a meaningful change: the magnitude of shift in level, the change in trend, the reduction in variability, the number of data points required, and the criterion for success. Pre-specification protects against the natural temptation to see improvement in ambiguous data after the fact, and it forces the clinician to commit to a standard before self-interest can shape the interpretation. Define the target behavior operationally, choose the measurement method, set the criterion, and write it down before the intervention phase begins. Without pre-specification, a graph becomes a Rorschach test in which the clinician reads whatever they hoped to find, and the evaluation provides no genuine accountability.

### Measure The Target Behavior Operationally And Consistently

Single-case design depends on data that are precise, repeatable, and free of drift, which requires an operational definition of the target behavior so that any observer could identify the same instance. Define whether you are measuring frequency, duration, latency, intensity, or percentage of intervals, and use the same measure across all phases, because changing the instrument between phases manufactures apparent change. Use repeated measurement, ideally at every session, because infrequent measurement produces a fragile graph and cannot capture variability. Where feasible, use independent observers or records to reduce the clinician's own bias, because the worker who delivers the intervention and scores the outcome is positioned to see what they wish to see. Graph the data continuously so that the pattern is visible in real time, not only at the end.

### Use Goal Attainment Scaling When Outcomes Are Individualized And Heterogeneous

Goal attainment scaling (GAS) is a method for evaluating progress toward individually set goals where standardized outcome measures do not fit, as is common in social work where clients' goals are diverse and personal. For each goal, define a continuum of outcomes from the least favorable expected outcome through the expected level to the most favorable, score the attained level, and aggregate the scaled scores. GAS captures change that matters to the specific client and that standardized instruments miss, and it builds client participation into the definition of success. The tradeoff is that GAS scores are idiosyncratic and not comparable across clients, and the scaling is subject to rater bias, so it complements rather than replaces standardized measurement. Use GAS when the goal is genuinely individualized, and standardize the scaling procedure to keep it defensible.

### Monitor Progress Session By Session And Use The Data To Adjust

The purpose of single-case evaluation is not to produce a study but to improve the intervention, and that requires that the data be reviewed continuously and used to adjust the plan. At each session, or at a defined interval, graph the new data point, compare it to the baseline and to the pre-specified criterion, and decide: if the client is improving toward criterion, continue; if improvement has stalled, intensify or modify the intervention; if the client is deteriorating, reassess the formulation and the intervention. Treat the plan as a hypothesis and the data as the test, and adjust the hypothesis when the data contradict it. A plan that is unchanged across many sessions of stagnant data is a plan being followed rotely rather than evaluated, and the single-case data exist precisely to prevent that.

### Respect The Limits And Ethics Of N Equals One

Single-case design generates evidence about one client and cannot, on its own, support claims about the intervention in general, and the clinician must hold this boundary honestly. Do not generalize a single-case result to other clients, to the model, or to the population, and do not present an N of one as if it were group research. Be transparent with the client about the purpose of the measurement, obtain consent for any design that involves withdrawal, and never use a withdrawal phase when doing so would risk harm, even in the service of a cleaner design. The ethics of single-case design require that the client's welfare takes precedence over the rigor of the experiment, and that measurement itself must not become burdensome or intrusive.

## Common Traps

### Intervening Before The Baseline Is Stable

The clinician, eager to help or pressed for time, introduces the intervention after only one or two baseline points or while the baseline is still trending, so that any later change cannot be attributed to the intervention rather than to the pre-existing trend. The mechanism is that waiting for stability feels like withholding help, and a short baseline feels efficient. The false signal is a graph that appears to show improvement after the intervention. The harm is that the evaluation is uninterpretable, an ineffective intervention may be credited with success, and the client continues a treatment that is not working. The defense is to wait for a stable baseline or to choose a multiple-baseline design when waiting is not feasible.

### Seeing Improvement In Ambiguous Data

The clinician reads a graph of a few variable data points and concludes the intervention is working, because the desire to see improvement shapes the interpretation of ambiguous data. The mechanism is confirmation bias, reinforced by the clinician's investment in the outcome and the absence of a pre-specified criterion. The false signal is a graph that looks roughly higher in the intervention phase. The harm is that a non-effect is treated as success, the intervention is not adjusted, and the client's stagnation continues under the illusion of progress. The defense is to pre-specify the criterion for change before the intervention and to judge the data against it.

### Abandoning The Design When The Data Are Inconvenient

The clinician maintains the design while the data are favorable but stops measuring, changes the measure, or reframes the outcome when the data show no effect or deterioration, so that the evaluation only ever confirms the intervention. The mechanism is that negative data threaten the clinician's confidence and the relationship, and stopping feels kinder. The false signal is a discontinued graph or a shifted outcome. The harm is that the evaluation loses all accountability value, a failing intervention is not revised, and the client is denied the benefit of honest monitoring. The defense is to commit to the design in advance and to treat negative data as information for adjustment, not as failure to be hidden.

### Withdrawal Used Unethically To Produce A Cleaner Design

The clinician uses an ABAB withdrawal design for a behavior or skill where withdrawing the intervention is harmful, such as self-injury, safety behaviors, or hard-won gains, prioritizing design rigor over the client's welfare. The mechanism is that ABAB produces a more convincing demonstration and the ethical cost is underweighted. The false signal is an elegant, replicated effect. The harm is that the client loses a functional gain or returns to a dangerous state in the service of the experiment, violating the principle that welfare precedes rigor. The defense is to choose a multiple-baseline design whenever withdrawal is ethically untenable.

### Measuring Infrequently And Calling It A Design

The clinician collects a data point every few sessions or only at review points, producing a graph too sparse to capture level, trend, or variability, and then draws conclusions as if the design were sound. The mechanism is that frequent measurement is burdensome and infrequent measurement feels sufficient. The false signal is a graph with a few points and a line. The harm is that the evaluation cannot detect real change or real stagnation, and decisions are made on fragments rather than data. The defense is to measure at every session and to graph continuously.

### Overclaiming From An N Of One

The clinician, having demonstrated an effect with one client, generalizes the finding to the intervention, to the population, or to other clients, exceeding what a single case can support. The mechanism is that a vivid, well-documented single case feels persuasive and the boundary of inference is easy to overlook. The false signal is a clear, replicated within-subject effect. The harm is that an ungeneralizable result drives practice decisions for other clients, the limits of the evidence are obscured, and the field accumulates overclaim. The defense is to state explicitly that the result applies to this client and to seek converging group evidence for broader claims.

### GAS Scaled Subjectively Without A Standard Procedure

The clinician uses goal attainment scaling but defines the levels loosely, scores them impressionistically, or changes the scaling after seeing the outcome, so that the GAS scores reflect clinician optimism rather than client change. The mechanism is that GAS is flexible and the scaling invites subjective judgment. The false signal is favorable GAS scores. The harm is that idiosyncratic, biased scores masquerade as evaluation, and the client's true progress is obscured. The defense is to define and fix the scaling before measurement and to follow a standardized procedure.

## Self-Check

- Did I establish a stable baseline with enough data points to reveal level and variability, and did I avoid intervening while the baseline was trending?
- Did I choose a design, AB, ABAB, or multiple-baseline, that matches the question and the ethical constraints on withdrawal?
- Did I pre-specify, before the intervention, what magnitude and pattern of change would count as a meaningful effect?
- Did I define the target behavior operationally, use the same measure across phases, measure at every session, and graph the data continuously?
- Did I use goal attainment scaling only for genuinely individualized goals, with the scaling fixed in advance and a standardized procedure?
- Did I review the session-by-session data at each contact and adjust the intervention, continuing, intensifying, or revising, rather than following the plan rotely?
- Did I respect the limits of N equals one, obtain consent for any withdrawal, and ensure that the client's welfare took precedence over design rigor?
- Did I avoid seeing improvement in ambiguous data, abandoning the design when data were inconvenient, and overclaiming from a single case?
