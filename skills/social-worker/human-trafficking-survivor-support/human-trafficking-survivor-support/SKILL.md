# Human Trafficking Survivor Support

Human trafficking is the exploitation of a person through force, fraud, or coercion for labor or commercial sex, and the survivors who reach social services are often profoundly controlled, traumatized, and isolated, with legal, immigration, medical, and safety needs that are entangled with the exploitation. The judgment problem is that the worker must build trust with someone whose exploitation has often involved a person or system that presented as a helper, must gather information that the survivor may not recognize or be able to name as trafficking, and must coordinate complex services across systems while the trafficker may still exert control. Agents err by treating trafficking as a one-time event rather than an ongoing relationship of control, by pressing for disclosure before safety and trust are established, by focusing on criminal justice outcomes at the expense of the survivor's priorities, and by offering services that the survivor cannot safely access while still being controlled.

## Core Rules

### Recognize Trafficking as Ongoing Control, Not a Past Event

Trafficking is not primarily a discrete incident; it is a sustained relationship in which a trafficker exercises control through violence, threats, debt bondage, manipulation, isolation, and dependency, and that control often continues after the survivor has left the immediate situation. Assess for ongoing control: Is the trafficker still in contact? Does the survivor owe a debt? Are family members threatened? Is the survivor dependent on the trafficker for housing, immigration status, or documents? Does the survivor feel they must return? Recognize that leaving is not the same as being free, and that the control dynamics shape what services can reach the survivor and what the survivor can safely do. Plan around the ongoing control, not as if it has ended.

### Build Trust Before Pressing for Disclosure

Survivors of trafficking have often been harmed by people who presented as helpers, may have had bad experiences with law enforcement or prior services, and may not trust the worker enough to disclose the exploitation, and they may not even frame their experience as trafficking. Do not press for a full disclosure or a statement before safety and trust are established. Begin with basic needs and respect, explain confidentiality and its limits honestly, let the survivor set the pace, and recognize that disclosure may unfold over many contacts. A survivor who is pressed too soon may disappear back into the trafficking situation. The relationship is the intervention that makes everything else possible.

### Assess With Trafficking-Specific Indicators, Not Only General Trauma

Trafficking has specific indicators that general trauma or victimization screening may miss: controlled movement or documents, debt to an employer or facilitator, isolation from supports, working or living conditions the person cannot leave, third parties who speak for the person, signs of physical or sexual violence, dependency on the trafficker for basic needs, and inconsistent or scripted stories. Use a trafficking-informed assessment that probes these indicators sensitively, corroborate with observation and collateral where possible, and recognize that the survivor's minimization or loyalty to the trafficker is itself an indicator of the control dynamic. Do not require the survivor to use the word trafficking or to self-identify as a victim to access services.

### Prioritize Safety and Survivor Autonomy Over Criminal Justice Goals

The criminal justice system's goals (prosecution, evidence, witness cooperation) and the survivor's goals (safety, stability, healing, family) may diverge, and the worker's primary duty is to the survivor, not to the prosecution. Provide services regardless of whether the survivor cooperates with law enforcement, support the survivor's informed decisions about reporting and cooperation, and do not condition services on participation in the criminal case. Where the survivor chooses to engage with law enforcement, prepare and accompany them, and advocate for their rights and needs within the process. Recognize that the threat of retaliation from the trafficker is real and that the survivor's caution about reporting is often well-founded.

### Coordinate Across the Entangled Systems

Trafficking survivors' needs span immigration, criminal justice, civil legal, medical, mental health, housing, income, and family systems, and these are interdependent: immigration relief may depend on cooperation with law enforcement, housing may depend on immigration status, mental health may depend on safety, and so on. Coordinate across these systems, understand the dependencies, sequence the interventions so that one opens the door to the next, and assign clear ownership of each domain so that nothing falls through the gaps. Use a comprehensive case plan that addresses the entangled needs, and maintain continuity as the survivor moves across systems and providers.

### Address Immigration Status as a Central, Not Peripheral, Concern

For many trafficking survivors, immigration status is the lever the trafficker used and the barrier to safety and services, and addressing it is central. Understand the immigration relief available to trafficking survivors (such as continued presence, T nonimmigrant status, U nonimmigrant status, asylum), the eligibility criteria, the timelines, and the relationship to law enforcement cooperation. Connect the survivor to immigration legal services early, because immigration relief has deadlines and dependencies, and do not let the worker's unfamiliarity with immigration law delay what only an immigration attorney can do. Recognize that the fear of deportation is a primary tool of control and that credible immigration relief is often the precondition for the survivor's safety and cooperation.

### Plan for Long-Term Recovery, Not Only Emergency Exit

Emergency exit from trafficking is the beginning, not the end, of the work, and the period immediately after exit is one of high risk for re-exploitation, return, and crisis. Plan for the long term: safe and stable housing, income and employment that does not re-expose the survivor to exploitation, mental health and substance use treatment, restoration of relationships, legal resolution of status and records, and the slow rebuilding of autonomy and identity. Recognize that recovery is non-linear, that returns to the trafficker or to exploitation are part of many survivors' trajectories, and that the worker's consistency across relapse and return is itself protective.

## Common Traps

### Pressing for Disclosure Before Safety and Trust

The worker, oriented to identification and reporting, presses the survivor for a trafficking disclosure before trust is established, and the survivor disappears. The mechanism is that identification feels like the first step. The false signal is thorough assessment. The harm is that the survivor, not yet safe enough to disclose, retreats back into the trafficking situation, and the window closes. Disclosure must follow relationship, not precede it.

### Treating Trafficking as a Past Event Rather Than Ongoing Control

The worker assumes that because the survivor has left the immediate situation, the trafficking is over, and plans services as if the survivor is free. The mechanism is that exit looks like resolution. The false signal is a survivor who has left. The harm is that the ongoing control (debt, threats, dependency, contact) is not addressed, the survivor remains trapped, and the services fail because they do not reach the actual dynamic. Control must be assessed as ongoing.

### Conditioning Services on Criminal Justice Cooperation

The worker or the system makes access to services contingent on the survivor's cooperation with law enforcement, conflating the survivor's needs with the prosecution's goals. The mechanism is that cooperation serves the system's interests. The false signal is a coordinated response. The harm is that survivors who cannot or will not cooperate are denied help, the worker becomes an extension of the prosecution, and survivors learn that help comes with conditions they cannot meet. Services must be survivor-centered, not prosecution-conditioned.

### Focusing on Sex Trafficking and Missing Labor Trafficking

The worker screens for sex trafficking and misses labor trafficking, which is less visible and often occurs in domestic, agricultural, service, or construction settings. The mechanism is that sex trafficking dominates the public frame. The false signal is awareness of trafficking. The harm is that labor trafficking survivors go unidentified and unserved, and the worker's narrow frame leaves a whole category of exploitation unaddressed. Both forms must be assessed.

### Letting Immigration Unfamiliarity Delay Relief

The worker does not understand the immigration options and defers or delays the referral to immigration legal services, missing deadlines and dependencies. The mechanism is that immigration law is specialized and intimidating. The false signal is caution about overstepping. The harm is that immigration relief with strict timelines is missed, the survivor remains deportable, and the trafficker's primary tool of control remains in place. Immigration legal referral must be made early.

### Assuming the Survivor Self-Identifies as a Victim

The worker waits for the survivor to name their experience as trafficking or to self-identify as a victim, and does not recognize the indicators when the survivor does not use that frame. The mechanism is that victimhood seems self-evident to an outside observer. The false signal is respect for the survivor's self-definition. The harm is that survivors who are loyal to, dependent on, or minimizing of the trafficker go unidentified and unserved. Indicators, not self-labeling, must drive identification.

## Self-Check

- Did I assess for ongoing control, rather than treating the trafficking as a past event?
- Did I build trust and address basic needs before pressing for disclosure?
- Did I use trafficking-specific indicators rather than relying on general trauma screening?
- Did I prioritize the survivor's safety and autonomy over criminal justice goals, and provide services regardless of cooperation?
- Did I coordinate across the entangled systems and sequence interventions to open doors?
- Did I address immigration status as a central concern and refer to immigration legal services early?
- Did I plan for long-term recovery, anticipating non-linear progress and possible returns?
- Did I assess for both labor and sex trafficking, and not require the survivor to self-identify as a victim?
