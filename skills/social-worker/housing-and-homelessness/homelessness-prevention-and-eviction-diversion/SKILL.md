# Homelessness Prevention and Eviction Diversion

Prevention and diversion work upstream of homelessness, intervening with housed clients who are at imminent risk of losing their housing. The logic is straightforward and evidence-based: it is far less expensive, less traumatic, and more effective to keep a person housed than to re-house them after they have become homeless. The judgment problem is that the window for prevention is narrow, the causes of housing instability are multiple and interconnected (income loss, medical crisis, family change, landlord conflict, domestic violence, mental health or substance use crisis), and the interventions must be both immediate (to stop the immediate threat) and structural (to address the underlying cause) or the client will return to crisis. The worker must assess quickly, act decisively, and avoid the trap of providing emergency assistance that delays rather than prevents homelessness.

## Core Rules

### Act Within the Narrow Window Before the Crisis Becomes Irreversible

Prevention has a time horizon measured in days or weeks. Once an eviction filing occurs, once a judgment is entered, once a writ of possession issues, the options narrow dramatically and the client's trajectory toward homelessness accelerates. Intervene as early as possible — at the first missed rent payment, at the first pay-or-quit notice, at the first utility disconnection notice — when the most and best options are available. Understand the local eviction timeline: how many days between the notice and the filing, between the filing and the hearing, between the judgment and the writ. Each stage closes doors. Prioritize cases by imminence and act within the window, because a case that could have been prevented at the notice stage may be unpreventable at the writ stage.

### Identify the Immediate Threat and the Underlying Cause

Housing crises have an immediate trigger (the missed rent, the eviction notice, the utility shutoff) and an underlying cause (job loss, medical bills, family change, mental health or substance use crisis, domestic violence, exploitative housing arrangement). Both must be addressed. Stopping the immediate threat without addressing the cause produces a temporary reprieve followed by recurrence — the client pays the back rent with emergency assistance and is back in crisis the next month because the income loss has not been resolved. Assess the full picture: what triggered the crisis, what the client's income and expenses are, what debts and obligations exist, what health and behavioral health factors are involved, what family and social supports exist, what the client's housing goals are. Intervene on both levels: resolve the immediate threat and connect the client to the supports that address the cause.

### Use the Full Range of Prevention Resources

Prevention resources typically include emergency financial assistance (back rent, security deposits, utility payments), legal assistance (eviction defense, negotiation with landlords), mediation (landlord-tenant conflict resolution), case management (connection to benefits, employment, behavioral health), and housing stabilization services (budgeting, tenant education, landlord communication). Know what is available locally, what the eligibility criteria are, what the application process is, and what the timelines are. Use the full range, not just the financial assistance, because many crises are resolvable through mediation, legal intervention, or case management without requiring emergency funds — and financial assistance alone, without the other supports, often fails.

### Negotiate With Landlords and Creditors to Preserve the Tenancy

The most effective prevention often involves negotiation rather than payment. A landlord who has filed for eviction may be willing to dismiss in exchange for a payment plan, a mediation agreement, or the assurance of ongoing case management. A utility company may restore service under a payment arrangement or medical protection. A creditor may accept reduced payments during a hardship. The worker can broker these negotiations, bringing credibility (the agency's involvement signals that the client is connected to supports), resources (emergency assistance to fund the agreement), and structure (a written agreement that both parties can rely on). Preserve the tenancy whenever possible, because the client's current housing is almost always preferable to the alternatives, and because an eviction on the record damages the client's housing prospects for years.

### Assess Whether Prevention Is Appropriate or Whether the Housing Itself Is the Problem

Not every housing crisis should be prevented. Some housing situations are themselves the problem: units that are unaffordable at any sustainable income level, units that are uninhabitable, units where the client is being exploited or abused, units that are inappropriate for the client's needs. In these cases, the right intervention may be relocation rather than preservation — helping the client exit the housing to a more stable situation rather than spending resources to preserve a tenancy that will fail again. Assess honestly: is this housing sustainable at the client's realistic income, is the landlord willing to work with the client, is the unit safe and appropriate, is the client safer here or elsewhere. Do not default to preservation when relocation is the better answer.

### Connect the Client to Ongoing Stabilization, Not Just Emergency Resolution

Emergency resolution — paying the back rent, stopping the eviction — stabilizes the immediate crisis but does not prevent recurrence. Connect the client to ongoing stabilization: benefits enrollment (if eligible), employment services, behavioral health care, budgeting and financial counseling, tenant education, ongoing case management, and a plan for the next month and the months after. The client who receives emergency assistance and no follow-up is likely to return to crisis. Build a stabilization plan that addresses the underlying cause, that connects the client to supports that will outlast the immediate intervention, and that includes a follow-up contact to verify that the client remains stable.

## Common Traps

### Providing Emergency Funds Without Addressing the Underlying Cause

The most common prevention failure is paying the back rent (or utility, or deposit) without addressing why the client fell behind. The trap is that the immediate crisis is resolved, the outcome is recorded as a success, and the client is back in crisis the next month because the income loss, the medical debt, the behavioral health condition, or the family change has not been addressed. The harm is that emergency resources are consumed on temporary reprieves that do not prevent homelessness, and that the client's trajectory is unchanged. The mechanism is that financial assistance is concrete, measurable, and completable while underlying-cause intervention is complex, long-term, and hard to measure. The corrective is to require case management alongside financial assistance, to address the cause as a condition of the intervention, and to measure stability over months rather than only the immediate outcome.

### Acting Too Late in the Eviction Timeline

Prevention options narrow as the eviction progresses. The trap is that the client seeks help at the writ stage — days from physical removal — when the options are limited to emergency relocation rather than preservation of the tenancy. The harm is that cases that could have been prevented at the notice stage become homelessness because the client and the system acted too late. The mechanism is that clients often delay seeking help due to shame, denial, or hope that the situation will resolve, and that the system does not reach them until the crisis is advanced. The corrective is to make prevention services visible and accessible, to educate the community about early intervention, and to prioritize cases by imminence so that the narrowest-window cases receive attention first.

### Assuming the Current Housing Should Always Be Preserved

Prevention work is oriented toward preserving tenancy, and this orientation can become a default that prevents honest assessment. The trap is spending resources to preserve a tenancy that is fundamentally unsustainable — a unit that costs 80% of the client's income, a unit with a landlord who will not cooperate, a unit that is unsafe or uninhabitable, a unit where the client is being abused. The harm is that resources are wasted on a losing effort, and the client eventually loses the housing anyway, now with depleted resources and an eviction on the record. The mechanism is that preservation feels like success and relocation feels like failure, when the honest assessment may be the opposite. The corrective is to assess sustainability honestly and to recommend relocation when the current housing cannot be sustained.

### Over-Promising to Landlords and Then Failing to Deliver

Negotiation with landlords depends on credibility, and the worker who over-promises — "the client will pay on time every month, the case manager will be available 24/7, there will be no further problems" — and then fails to deliver destroys the relationship and the client's tenancy. The trap is making promises to secure the immediate agreement that cannot be sustained over the tenancy. The harm is that the landlord's trust is broken, the client loses the housing, and the landlord refuses to work with the agency or with voucher holders in the future. The mechanism is that the immediate crisis creates pressure to say whatever secures the agreement. The corrective is to promise only what can be delivered, to be honest about the client's challenges and the supports available, and to build landlord relationships on realistic expectations.

### Losing the Client After the Immediate Crisis Resolves

The client whose immediate crisis is resolved may disengage from the stabilization plan, assuming the problem is solved. The trap is that the worker also treats the case as closed, and the client returns to crisis because the underlying cause was never addressed. The harm is recurrence of homelessness that could have been prevented. The mechanism is that crisis resolution feels like completion while stabilization is ongoing and requires sustained engagement. The corrective is to build follow-up into the intervention, to maintain contact through the stabilization period, and to measure outcomes over months rather than at the point of crisis resolution.

## Self-Check

- Did I act within the narrow window before the crisis became irreversible, prioritizing by imminence?
- Did I identify both the immediate threat and the underlying cause, and intervene on both?
- Did I use the full range of prevention resources — financial, legal, mediation, case management — not just emergency funds?
- Did I negotiate with landlords and creditors to preserve the tenancy wherever appropriate?
- Did I honestly assess whether the current housing is sustainable, or whether relocation is the better answer?
- Did I connect the client to ongoing stabilization that addresses the underlying cause, not just emergency resolution?
- Did I build follow-up into the intervention to verify ongoing stability?
- Did I promise landlords and creditors only what I can deliver, protecting the relationship for future clients?
