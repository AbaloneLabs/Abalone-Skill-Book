# Early Childhood Transition Planning

Early childhood transitions are the points at which a child moves between service systems, providers, and settings, and the central judgment problem is that the very children who need continuity most are the ones most likely to lose it at the boundary, because eligibility rules, funding streams, philosophies, and timelines differ across systems and a gap in services can erase developmental gains. The transition from Part C early intervention to Part B preschool special education at age three is the most consequential and most failure-prone, because a child who was eligible under Part C may not be eligible under Part B, the timeline is fixed by the child's birthday regardless of family readiness, and the family must suddenly navigate a new system with new language, new rights, and new decision-making structures. Beyond the system transition, children move between childcare settings, home and center-based programs, and providers, and each shift carries the risk that hard-won relationships, routines, and strategies are lost. The worker must plan early, prepare families to participate as decision-makers, document what must transfer, and advocate for continuity without overpromising outcomes the new system may not deliver. Agents err by treating transition as an administrative handoff rather than a developmental and family event, by starting too late, by failing to explain eligibility differences, and by allowing the child to fall through gaps created by misaligned timelines.

## Core Rules

### Begin Transition Planning Early Enough to Be Proactive, Not Reactive

Transition planning must begin months before the actual move, not in the final weeks, because effective transition requires assessments, conferences, eligibility determinations, family preparation, and coordination among multiple parties that all take time. For the Part C to Part B transition, notification to the family, referral to the local education agency, and the transition conference must occur within mandated early windows, typically at least ninety days before the child's third birthday, and practical planning should begin even earlier. Starting late compresses the process, forces rushed decisions, increases the risk of a service gap at the birthday, and leaves families feeling swept along rather than prepared. Build the transition timeline backward from the child's birthday or move date, identify every step and responsible party, and begin before the pressure forces it. A transition managed in crisis is a transition that fails the family.

### Prepare Families to Participate as Informed Decision-Makers

The family is the constant across the transition while providers and settings change, and their informed participation is the single best predictor of continuity and appropriate services. Explain in plain language what will change, what the new system's eligibility criteria and service model are, what rights the family has, what decisions they will be asked to make, and what role they play in meetings. Prepare the family for the transition conference and any eligibility or IEP meetings, rehearse their questions and concerns, and ensure they understand that consent and participation are theirs to give or withhold. A family that walks into a transition meeting not understanding the system cannot advocate for their child and is likely to accept whatever is offered or disengage. Capacity building for the transition is capacity building that lasts beyond it.

### Make Eligibility Differences Explicit and Honest

The eligibility criteria under Part C and Part B are different, and a child who qualified for early intervention may not qualify for preschool special education, a reality the family must understand well before the birthday arrives. Explain the difference between the broader developmental and risk-based eligibility of Part C and the categorical disability and educational-need criteria of Part B, and be honest that eligibility is not guaranteed and that the level or type of service may change. Do not reassure the family with vague promises that services will continue, because overpromising sets up betrayal and erodes trust when the determination goes differently. Prepare the family for the range of possible outcomes, including continued services, reduced services, a different setting, or ineligibility, and plan for each. Honesty about uncertainty is more respectful than false reassurance.

### Ensure Continuity of Services and Prevent Gaps at the Boundary

The central harm of a poorly managed transition is a gap in services at the age boundary, where early intervention ends on the birthday and preschool services have not yet begun or been determined, leaving the child without support during a critical developmental period. Coordinate timelines so that eligibility determination, IEP development, and service initiation are scheduled to follow the birthday without a gap wherever possible, and where a gap is unavoidable, identify interim supports, community resources, or strategies the family can use. Document the child's current services, goals, effective strategies, and provider contacts so that the receiving team has what it needs to continue rather than restart. Track each transitioning case to confirm that services actually begin on the other side, and follow up when they do not. A transition is not complete when the referral is made; it is complete when the child is served.

### Transfer Meaningful Information, Not Just Paperwork

A transition packet that contains forms, dates, and eligibility categories but not the living knowledge of what works for this child is a packet that forces the receiving team to start from scratch. Transfer the information that matters for continuity: the child's current developmental profile, the goals being pursued, the strategies and routines that have been effective, the accommodations that help, the providers and relationships involved, and the family's priorities, concerns, and strengths. Obtain consent for the transfer of records and share what is authorized and relevant. Write the information in a form that is usable by the receiving team, concise, concrete, and oriented to action. The goal is not to discharge a documentation requirement but to give the next provider what they need to serve the child well from day one.

### Address the Emotional and Relational Dimensions of Transition

For the child and family, transition means the loss of trusted providers, familiar routines, and a known setting, often alongside anxiety about the unknown, and these losses are real regardless of how well the logistics are managed. Acknowledge the ending, help the family and child say goodbye to providers and settings that mattered, prepare the child for the new environment through visits, photos, and conversation, and recognize that a family's grief or anxiety about the transition is not resistance but a response to loss. Connect the family to the new providers and setting before the transition so that relationships begin to form, and provide support through the early weeks of the new placement. A transition that handles the paperwork but ignores the relationships leaves the family emotionally unsupported precisely when support matters most.

### Coordinate Across All the Systems and Providers Involved

A child's transition may involve early intervention, the school system, childcare, health providers, and specialized therapists, each with its own timeline, requirements, and culture, and coordination among them is the worker's responsibility to facilitate. Identify every party, clarify roles and responsibilities, convene or contribute to transition meetings, and ensure that information flows among them rather than siloing within each. Misalignment among systems, a childcare that does not know the IEP, a therapist whose schedule does not match the new setting, a school that has not received the records, is a primary cause of transition failure. The worker often serves as the connective tissue that no single system provides on its own.

## Common Traps

### Treating Transition as an Administrative Handoff

The worker completes the referral, sends the records, and considers the transition done, treating it as paperwork rather than a developmental and family event. The mechanism is that the administrative steps feel like the whole task. The false signal is a completed file. The harm is that the family is unprepared, the child's needs are not communicated in usable form, and services may never actually begin on the other side. Transition is a process of continuity, not a discharge of documentation.

### Starting Too Late and Rushing the Timeline

The worker delays planning until the birthday is imminent, then compresses assessments, conferences, and family preparation into a few weeks. The mechanism is that the birthday feels far off until it is not. The false signal is that there is still time. The harm is rushed decisions, missed deadlines, a likely service gap, and a family overwhelmed by a process they cannot meaningfully participate in. The timeline must be built backward and started early.

### Overpromising Continued Eligibility or Services

The worker reassures the family that services will continue after the transition, implying or stating that eligibility is assured, when Part B criteria differ and the outcome is uncertain. The mechanism is that reassurance feels kind in the moment. The false signal is a relieved family. The harm is that the family is blindsided and trust collapses if eligibility is denied or services reduced, and the family was not prepared to advocate or plan alternatives. Honesty about uncertainty is the kinder course.

### Failing to Explain the New System's Language and Rights

The family enters the new system not understanding the terminology, the meetings, their parental rights, or their role, and is unable to participate meaningfully. The mechanism is that the terms are familiar to the worker and go unexplained. The false signal is a family who nods in meetings. The harm is that the family cannot give informed consent, cannot advocate, and accepts or rejects plans they do not understand. Preparation must translate the system for the family.

### Allowing a Service Gap at the Birthday

Early intervention ends on the third birthday and preschool services have not started, leaving the child unsupported for weeks or months. The mechanism is that the two systems' timelines are not aligned and no one owns the gap. The false signal is that the referral has been made. The harm is lost developmental ground, family stress, and sometimes the family disengaging entirely. The gap must be anticipated and actively closed or mitigated.

### Transferring Forms Without Transferring Usable Knowledge

The transition packet contains eligibility paperwork and dates but not the strategies, routines, goals, and relationships that would let the receiving team continue effectively. The mechanism is that forms satisfy the documentation requirement. The false signal is a complete packet. The harm is that the new team starts from scratch, effective strategies are abandoned, and continuity is lost despite the paperwork. Transfer the knowledge that enables continuity.

### Ignoring the Emotional Loss of Providers and Setting

The worker manages logistics but does not acknowledge that the family and child are losing trusted relationships and a familiar environment. The mechanism is that emotions seem secondary to the system tasks. The false signal is a family who seems fine. The harm is that grief and anxiety go unsupported, the new placement feels cold by comparison, and the family's adjustment suffers. Transition includes endings that deserve acknowledgment.

## Self-Check

- Did I begin transition planning early enough, building the timeline backward from the birthday or move date?
- Did I prepare the family to participate as informed decision-makers who understand the new system and their rights?
- Did I make the eligibility differences between systems explicit and honest, without overpromising outcomes?
- Did I coordinate timelines to prevent a service gap at the age boundary, and identify interim supports if a gap is unavoidable?
- Did I transfer meaningful, usable knowledge about the child, not just forms and paperwork?
- Did I acknowledge and support the emotional and relational losses the family and child experience in transition?
- Did I coordinate across all involved systems and providers so that information flowed and roles were clear?
- Did I follow up to confirm that services actually began on the other side of the transition?
