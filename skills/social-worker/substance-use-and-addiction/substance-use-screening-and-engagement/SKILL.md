# Substance Use Screening And Engagement

The first engagement with a client who may have a substance use disorder is consequential: it determines whether the client stays in services, how they are treated thereafter, and whether the underlying issues are surfaced or buried under defensiveness. Substance use is heavily stigmatized, and clients have often experienced judgment, coercion, and being labeled "addict" in ways that make them guarded, minimize their use, or disengage entirely. Agents err by treating screening as a checkbox rather than an engagement, by approaching the client with assumptions about what they "should" do (usually abstinence immediately), by mistaking the client's stage of change and pushing action they are not ready for, and by missing that substance use is often a solution to a problem (trauma, pain, mental illness, deprivation) before it becomes the problem. This skill covers how to screen for substance use accurately, engage a client who may not be ready, and match the intervention to the stage of change — with the overriding rule that the goal of screening and engagement is to keep the client in a helping relationship and build their readiness, not to extract an immediate commitment to abstinence that they cannot sustain.

## Core Rules

### Screen Universally, Nonjudgmentally, and With Validated Tools

Screen for substance use universally, because it is common, often hidden, and consequential across every social work domain — child welfare, mental health, medical, housing, domestic violence. Use validated screening tools appropriate to the setting (e.g., AUDIT for alcohol, DAST for drugs, single-item screening questions for quick use), administered in a matter-of-fact, nonjudgmental way that normalizes the questions. Frame screening as routine ("I ask all my clients about substance use because it affects so many areas"), not as suspicion. A positive screen is not a diagnosis; it is a signal to explore further with a more detailed assessment. Avoid relying on clinical impression alone, which misses many cases, especially high-functioning users. Document the screening tool, the results, and the follow-up assessment.

### Explore the Function, Pattern, and Impact of the Use

When substance use is identified, explore it specifically and respectfully to understand its role in the client's life, because this drives everything that follows. Assess the substances used, the pattern and frequency, the route of administration, the duration of use, the function the substance serves (what problem it solves — coping, social, performance, escape), the impact on the client's life (health, relationships, work, legal, finances, parenting), the client's own view of the use, and their past attempts to change. Understand the use in context: trauma history, mental health, pain, social environment, deprivation. A substance use assessment that captures only quantity and frequency, missing the function and context, will produce a plan that targets the symptom while leaving the driver intact. Document the functional assessment.

### Assess the Stage of Change and Match the Intervention

Clients move through stages of change — pre-contemplation, contemplation, preparation, action, maintenance — and the effective intervention matches the stage. A client in pre-contemplation (not recognizing a problem) needs information and a relationship, not an action plan; a client in contemplation needs help weighing the decision; a client in preparation needs help planning; a client in action needs support and skills; a client in maintenance needs relapse prevention. Mismatched interventions fail: pushing action on a pre-contemplative client produces resistance and disengagement; providing information to a client ready for action wastes their readiness. Assess the stage from the client's own words and behavior, and match the intervention. Document the stage assessment and the matched approach.

### Use Motivational Approaches to Build Readiness

Motivational interviewing and related approaches are the evidence-based method for building readiness to change in clients who are ambivalent or not yet ready. The core stance is to elicit the client's own reasons for change (rather than imposing the worker's reasons), to explore and resolve ambivalence (rather than arguing against the use), to roll with resistance (rather than confronting it), and to support the client's self-efficacy (their belief that they can change). Avoid the "righting reflex" — the urge to point out the problem and prescribe the solution — which reliably increases resistance. Open questions, affirmations, reflective listening, and summaries are the tools. The goal is to have the client voice the arguments for change, because change talk predicts change. Document the motivational work and the client's change talk.

### Meet the Client Where They Are: Harm Reduction First

Harm reduction is the principle of meeting the client where they are and reducing the harms of their use, even if abstinence is not their current goal. Harm reduction is not the opposite of recovery; it is often the path to it, keeping the client alive, engaged, and reducing harm while readiness develops. Provide harm reduction information and resources (safer use practices, overdose prevention including naloxone, needle exchange, safe consumption environments where available), and do not make engagement contingent on the client committing to abstinence. A client who is not ready for abstinence but who accepts harm reduction is still engaged, still reducing risk, and still moving toward the possibility of change. Withholding harm reduction to pressure abstinence costs lives. Document the harm reduction provided.

### Address the Underlying Drivers, Not Just the Use

Substance use is often a solution to a problem before it becomes the problem, and the drivers — trauma, mental illness, chronic pain, social isolation, poverty, homelessness, grief, oppression — must be addressed for sustained change. A treatment plan that targets the substance use without addressing the driver produces short-term change that relapses when the driver reasserts itself. Assess the drivers, integrate them into the plan, and coordinate the services that address them (trauma therapy, mental health treatment, pain management, housing, social connection). This is especially true for trauma, which is the most common driver and which, unaddressed, drives recurrence. Document the driver assessment and the integrated plan.

### Assess for Withdrawal Risk and the Need for Medical Management

Withdrawal from some substances — notably alcohol, benzodiazepines, and sometimes opioids — can be medically dangerous, even fatal, and abrupt cessation without medical management is a serious risk. Assess the substances, the quantity, the duration, and the pattern, and refer for medical detoxification assessment where withdrawal risk exists. Do not advise a client to simply stop; advise medical assessment for a safe withdrawal plan. Other substances (cannabis, stimulants) have less dangerous withdrawal but still produce distress that can derail change. Know the withdrawal profiles of common substances and the local medical detoxification resources. Document the withdrawal risk assessment and the referral.

### Maintain Engagement Through Relapse

Relapse is a common part of the recovery process, not a failure of it, and how the worker responds to relapse determines whether the client continues or gives up. Respond to relapse without moralizing or punishment: assess what happened and what can be learned, adjust the plan, intensify support, and reaffirm the client's capacity to continue. A client who relapses and is met with judgment or discharge is a client lost; a client who relapses and is met with renewed engagement is a client who may yet recover. Do not close a case for relapse if the need persists. Document the relapse, the response, and the adjusted plan.

## Common Traps

### Screening as a Checkbox Rather Than an Engagement

The worker administers a screening tool, records the score, and moves on, missing that the screening is the first engagement and the opportunity to build the relationship and surface the issue. The trap mechanism is that screening feels complete once the form is filled. The false signal is a completed screen. The harm is that the use is recorded but not explored, the client is not engaged, and the opportunity to build readiness is lost. Screening must be the start of an engagement, not the end of a task.

### Pushing Abstinence on a Client Not Ready

The worker, recognizing the harm of the use, pushes the client toward immediate abstinence and an action plan, when the client is in pre-contemplation or contemplation and not ready, producing resistance and disengagement. The trap is that abstinence is the worker's clear goal and pushing it feels like good practice. The false signal is a clear treatment goal. The harm is that the client, pressured beyond their readiness, disengages, and the use continues unaddressed. The intervention must match the stage of change.

### The Righting Reflex That Increases Resistance

The worker, seeing the harm, argues the case for change, points out the problems, and prescribes the solution, triggering the client's defensiveness and reducing their openness. The trap is that the worker's points are correct and the urge to make them is strong. The false signal is effective psychoeducation. The harm is that arguing the case for change reliably increases the client's arguments against it, and resistance hardens. Eliciting the client's own reasons for change is more effective than imposing the worker's.

### Harm Reduction Withheld to Pressure Abstinence

The worker withholds harm reduction resources (naloxone, safer use information) on the grounds that providing them enables the use, when in fact harm reduction keeps the client alive and engaged. The trap is that withholding feels like refusing to condone the use. The false signal is principled practice. The harm is that the client, denied harm reduction, continues to use unsafely, and some die of preventable overdose. Harm reduction is not the opposite of recovery; it is often the path to it.

### Targeting the Symptom, Missing the Driver

The worker builds a plan focused on stopping the substance use without addressing the trauma, mental illness, pain, or deprivation that drives it, so the change is short-lived. The trap is that the use is the visible problem and stopping it is the apparent goal. The false signal is a focused treatment plan. The harm is that the driver, unaddressed, reasserts itself and relapse follows. The drivers must be assessed and integrated into the plan.

### Relapse Met With Judgment or Discharge

The client relapses, and the worker responds with moralizing, reduced engagement, or case closure, treating relapse as a failure rather than a common part of recovery. The trap is that relapse feels like the plan failed and the client did not try. The false signal is accountability. The harm is that the client, met with judgment at a vulnerable moment, gives up, and the recovery that was still possible is lost. Relapse must be met with renewed engagement and plan adjustment.

### Missing Withdrawal Danger

The worker advises or expects the client to stop using, without assessing for withdrawal risk from alcohol or benzodiazepines, and the client undergoes dangerous or fatal withdrawal. The trap is that stopping substance use seems like the obvious goal. The false signal is a clear abstinence recommendation. The harm is that unmanaged withdrawal from alcohol or benzodiazepines can cause seizures, delirium, and death. Withdrawal risk must be assessed and medically managed.

## Self-Check

- Did I screen for substance use universally and nonjudgmentally using validated tools, framed as routine rather than suspicious?
- Did I explore the function, pattern, and impact of the use in context, not just the quantity and frequency?
- Did I assess the client's stage of change and match the intervention to the stage, rather than pushing action on a client not ready?
- Did I use motivational approaches to elicit the client's own reasons for change, rather than imposing the worker's reasons through the righting reflex?
- Did I provide harm reduction resources and information, keeping the client engaged and alive regardless of their readiness for abstinence?
- Did I assess and integrate the underlying drivers — trauma, mental illness, pain, deprivation — into the plan, rather than targeting only the use?
- Did I assess for withdrawal risk, especially from alcohol and benzodiazepines, and refer for medical management where needed?
- Did I anticipate and plan for relapse as a common part of recovery, and commit to meeting it with renewed engagement rather than judgment or discharge?
