# Competency Restoration And Criminal Justice Liaison

Competency restoration is the process by which a defendant who has been found incompetent to stand trial receives treatment and education aimed at restoring the ability to understand the proceedings and assist counsel, and the social worker in this space sits at a pressured intersection of mental health treatment, criminal prosecution, and constitutional rights. The judgment problem is that the worker must serve a client whose liberty is restricted by a court order, whose treatment is legally mandated, and whose every statement may be recorded and used in proceedings, while also maintaining the therapeutic relationship that makes restoration possible. Agents err by collapsing the forensic and therapeutic roles, by promising confidentiality that the setting cannot deliver, by allowing restoration goals to drift toward case resolution or plea convenience, and by failing to recognize that a justice-involved client's rights to refuse treatment, to counsel, and to due process operate differently than in voluntary care.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Forensic From Therapeutic Role Explicitly And Continuously

Competency restoration is a forensic process, not ordinary treatment, and the worker's role is defined by a court order, a legal standard, and an obligation to report to the court. Make this distinction explicit to the client at the outset and reinforce it whenever the setting blurs it, because the client's understanding of the worker's role is itself part of the competency question. Explain that the purpose of the work is to help the court determine whether the client can stand trial, that progress is reported to the court, and that the worker is not functioning as the client's therapist in the confidential clinical sense. When the forensic and therapeutic functions must coexist, name them, document the boundaries, and escalate the conflict to supervision when the roles pull in opposite directions.

### Protect The Rights Of Justice-Involved Clients As A Core Function

Justice-involved clients retain constitutional and statutory rights, including the right to counsel, the right to due process, the right to refuse certain treatments, the right to the least restrictive means of restoration, and protections against self-incrimination. Treat protection of these rights as a core social work function, not an obstacle to treatment, and know the specific rights and procedures that govern the jurisdiction. Ensure the client has access to counsel, that any waivers are knowing and voluntary, that treatment is within the legally authorized scope, and that confinement is no longer than the law permits. When a right appears to be compromised, raise it through the proper channel rather than assuming the system will self-correct.

### Deliver Restoration Services Within The Legal Standard And Court Order

Competency is a legal standard, not a clinical judgment, and restoration services must be delivered within the framework the court has ordered and the evaluator will apply. Understand the specific competency standard in the jurisdiction, the elements the court has identified as impaired, and the scope of the restoration order, and tailor the intervention to those elements. Do not expand the work into areas the court did not authorize, do not substitute clinical improvement for legal competency, and do not opine on competency unless that is your assigned forensic role. When the client's clinical needs exceed the restoration scope, address them through appropriate referral and documentation rather than by expanding the forensic mandate.

### Liaise With Criminal Justice Stakeholders With Clarity And Boundaries

The worker liaises with prosecutors, defense counsel, judges, probation and parole officers, and corrections staff, and each relationship has a different function and a different boundary. Clarify the worker's role and the limits of what can be shared with each stakeholder, confirm the legal authority for any information exchange, and route requests through the proper channel. Recognize that defense counsel's interests and the prosecution's interests differ, that the worker's reports may be used by either side, and that informal communication with one party can compromise the worker's neutrality and the client's rights. Document all material contacts and the basis for any disclosure.

### Manage The Limits Of Confidentiality In A Forensic Setting

Confidentiality in a forensic setting is limited by the court order, the reporting obligations, the presence of third parties, and the legal status of the client's statements, and the worker must understand and communicate these limits. At the outset, explain what will be reported, to whom, and for what purpose, and avoid creating an expectation of confidentiality the setting cannot honor. Distinguish what is protected from what is not, and recognize that statements about the offense, about dangerousness, and about competency-relevant functioning may all be reportable. When in doubt about whether information is protected, seek guidance before disclosing rather than assuming.

### Conduct Forensic Psychosocial Assessment With Validity And Balance

Forensic psychosocial assessment in this context must be valid, balanced, and grounded in observed and documented behavior, not in assumptions about diagnosis or criminality. Use reliable sources, corroborate self-report where possible, distinguish historical fact from current functioning, and avoid the confirmation bias that treats a criminal charge as evidence of a clinical condition. Present the assessment in a way that separates observation from inference, acknowledges uncertainty, and resists pressure from any party to shape the findings toward a preferred legal outcome. The assessment is only as credible as its method and its honesty.

### Plan For Transition And Continuity Beyond Restoration

Competency restoration ends, and the client's clinical needs often continue, so plan for transition and continuity from the beginning. Identify the services the client will need after restoration (community mental health, substance treatment, housing, benefits), begin the linkage early, and coordinate with receiving providers so that treatment is not interrupted. Recognize that restoration without continuity increases the risk of decompensation, re-arrest, and repeat incompetency findings, and that discharge planning is part of the forensic social work role, not an afterthought. Document the plan and the referrals.

## Common Traps

### Collapsing Forensic And Therapeutic Roles

The worker treats the restoration assignment as ordinary therapy, offers the confidentiality of a therapeutic relationship, and is then surprised when statements are reported to the court or used in proceedings. The mechanism is that therapeutic instincts feel right and the setting may resemble a clinical one. The false signal is a warm, trusting relationship. The harm is that the client is misled about the role, self-incriminating statements are disclosed, the therapeutic alliance is shattered when the limits emerge, and the worker's credibility is damaged. The forensic role must be explicit and continuous.

### Promising Confidentiality The Setting Cannot Deliver

The worker, wishing to build trust, assures the client that what is shared is confidential, when the court order, the reporting obligations, or the presence of monitors make that assurance false. The mechanism is that confidentiality feels like the foundation of engagement. The false signal is a client who opens up. The harm is a breach of trust, an ethical violation, and potentially compromised legal proceedings when the limits are revealed. Confidentiality must be described accurately at the outset.

### Allowing Restoration Goals To Drift Toward Case Resolution

The worker allows the restoration goals to drift toward what is convenient for case resolution, such as moving toward a plea or toward prolonged treatment, rather than toward the legal standard of competency. The mechanism is that system pressures reward certain outcomes. The false signal is a smoothly progressing case. The harm is that the client's right to a competency determination based on the legal standard is compromised, and the worker becomes an agent of case management rather than of restoration. Goals must remain tied to the legal standard.

### Failing To Recognize And Protect Client Rights

The worker, focused on treatment, overlooks that the client has the right to counsel, to refuse certain treatment, to the least restrictive setting, and to release when the legal basis for confinement ends. The mechanism is that the clinical frame crowds out the legal frame. The false signal is compliant treatment. The harm is that rights are eroded, confinement extends beyond its legal basis, and the worker becomes complicit in rights violations. Rights must be monitored as a core function.

### Opining On Competency Outside The Assigned Role

The worker, asked for a view, offers an opinion on whether the client is competent, when the competency determination belongs to the evaluator or the court and the worker's role is to provide services and observations. The mechanism is that the worker has relevant information and the question feels answerable. The false signal is helpful input. The harm is that the worker exceeds scope, the opinion may conflict with the formal evaluation, and the worker's neutrality is compromised. Opinions on competency belong to the assigned evaluator.

### Informal Communication Compromising Neutrality And Client Rights

The worker has a casual conversation with a prosecutor or probation officer about the client's progress, outside the authorized channel, and the information is used in ways that harm the client. The mechanism is that informal exchange feels efficient and collaborative. The false signal is good interagency relations. The harm is that unauthorized disclosure occurs, the client's rights are compromised, and the worker's neutrality is questioned. Communication must be channeled and documented.

### Treating The Charge As Evidence Of Illness

The worker assumes that because the client was charged with an offense, the client must have a mental illness that explains it, and shapes the assessment accordingly. The mechanism is that the charge provides a narrative. The false signal is a coherent clinical picture. The harm is confirmation bias, an invalid assessment, and a restoration plan built on a false premise. The charge is not clinical evidence and must not be treated as such.

## Self-Check

- Did I distinguish my forensic role from a therapeutic role explicitly and continuously with the client?
- Did I protect the client's rights to counsel, due process, refusal of treatment, and the least restrictive setting as a core function?
- Did I deliver restoration services within the legal competency standard and the scope of the court order?
- Did I liaise with criminal justice stakeholders with clear role definition, documented contacts, and proper authority for disclosure?
- Did I accurately describe the limits of confidentiality at the outset and avoid promising confidentiality the setting cannot deliver?
- Did I conduct forensic psychosocial assessment that is valid, balanced, grounded in observed behavior, and free of confirmation bias?
- Did I keep restoration goals tied to the legal standard rather than to case resolution or convenience?
- Did I plan for transition and continuity beyond restoration, with early linkage to community services?
