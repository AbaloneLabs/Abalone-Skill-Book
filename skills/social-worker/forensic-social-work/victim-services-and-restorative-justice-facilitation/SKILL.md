# Victim Services And Restorative Justice Facilitation

Victim services and restorative justice facilitation require the worker to hold two realities at once: the victim's experience of harm and the justice system's need for participation, evidence, and resolution, which do not always align. The judgment problem is that the worker must center the victim's safety, autonomy, and self-determined recovery while also navigating a system that may pressure the victim to forgive, to participate, to accept an outcome, or to serve the offender's rehabilitation narrative, and while facilitating encounters where the power imbalance between victim and offender is real and dangerous if mishandled. Agents err by treating restorative justice as inherently healing, by allowing the offender's remorse or the system's efficiency to override the victim's pace, by failing to assess coercion and safety before an encounter, and by confusing the worker's role as victim advocate with a neutral facilitator role that the process demands.

## Core Rules

### Center Victim Autonomy, Safety, And Self-Determination Throughout

The victim's autonomy, safety, and right to self-determination are the foundation of all victim services and restorative justice work, and they must be centered from first contact through any encounter and afterward. This means the victim chooses whether to participate, at what pace, with what boundaries, and may withdraw at any time without penalty to their rights or services. Assess safety continuously, recognize that pressure to participate can come from the system, from family, from culture, and from the worker's own desire for a good outcome, and actively protect the victim's right to decline. Self-determination includes the right to define what repair means, which may not match what the system or the offender offers.

### Explain Victim Rights, Restitution, And Process Realistically

Victims enter a system whose procedures, timelines, rights, and possible outcomes are opaque, and the worker's job is to explain them realistically, without inflating hope or minimizing risk. Explain the victim's statutory rights (notification, participation, input, protection, restitution), the realistic likelihood and timeline of each, and the points at which the victim's wishes may and may not control the outcome. Distinguish what the criminal process can deliver from what restorative justice can deliver, and be honest about the limits of both, including that restitution is often unpaid, that outcomes are uncertain, and that participation does not guarantee a particular result. Realistic explanation builds trust; inflated promises destroy it.

### Assess Readiness And Safety Before Any Restorative Encounter

Restorative justice encounters are not appropriate for all victims, all offenders, or all cases, and readiness and safety must be assessed before any encounter is scheduled. Assess the victim's emotional readiness, the stability of their recovery, the absence of coercion, the offender's acceptance of responsibility, the power dynamics, the risk of re-traumatization, and any ongoing threat. Use structured preparation, not a single conversation, and be prepared to conclude that an encounter is not appropriate even when both parties nominally agree. The decision to proceed must be evidence-based and safety-grounded, not driven by program targets or a belief that encounter is always beneficial.

### Maintain Role Clarity Between Advocate And Facilitator

Victim advocacy and restorative justice facilitation place different demands on the worker, and conflating them compromises both. The advocate centers and champions the victim's interests; the facilitator holds a neutral process in which both victim and offender participate. When the worker serves as both, the conflict must be named and managed, and in many cases the roles should be separated across different people. If serving both, be explicit with the victim about when you are advocating and when you are facilitating, document the boundaries, and recognize that the victim's trust in your advocacy can be exploited if you then appear neutral toward the offender in a facilitation role.

### Prepare Both Parties Thoroughly For Encounter

Restorative encounters require thorough preparation of both the victim and the offender, not merely scheduling and good intentions. Prepare the victim for the emotional impact of seeing the offender, for the possibility of apology or its absence, for the offender's narrative, and for the range of outcomes; prepare the offender to take responsibility, to listen, and to engage without minimizing or re-injuring. Conduct separate preparation sessions, screen for readiness, establish ground rules, and rehearse the process so that neither party is surprised. Inadequate preparation is a primary cause of harm in restorative encounters.

### Balance Victim And Offender Needs Without Sacrificing Either

Restorative justice seeks to address the needs of both victim and offender, and the worker must balance them without sacrificing the victim's recovery to the offender's rehabilitation or the offender's accountability to the victim's desired outcome. Recognize that the offender's need for reintegration and the victim's need for repair are both legitimate, that they may conflict, and that the process must hold both rather than collapsing one into the other. Do not allow the process to become a vehicle for the offender's redemption narrative at the victim's expense, and do not allow the victim's pain to become the instrument of the offender's punishment. Balance is an active practice, not a default.

### Provide Continuous Support Before, During, And After The Process

Support does not end at the encounter or the case disposition; it must be continuous before, during, and after. Before, prepare and stabilize; during, attend and monitor for distress; after, debrief, assess for re-traumatization, connect to ongoing services, and check in over time as delayed reactions emerge. Restorative encounters and justice participation can surface trauma long after the event, and the absence of immediate distress is not evidence of resolution. Build follow-up into the service design rather than treating it as optional.

## Common Traps

### Treating Restorative Justice As Inherently Healing

The worker assumes that a restorative encounter is healing by definition and proceeds without adequate assessment, preparation, or attention to the victim's actual needs. The mechanism is the ideology that restoration is therapeutic. The false signal is both parties agreeing to meet. The harm is re-traumatization, a process that serves the offender's narrative, and a victim who feels used by the system. Restoration is a process, not a guarantee, and must be assessed and prepared.

### Allowing Offender Remorse Or System Efficiency To Override Victim Pace

The worker, seeing offender remorse or system pressure for resolution, moves the victim toward encounter or acceptance faster than the victim's recovery supports. The mechanism is that visible remorse and efficient resolution feel like success. The false signal is a progressing case. The harm is that the victim's autonomy is compromised, participation becomes coerced, and the outcome reflects the system's needs rather than the victim's. The victim's pace must govern.

### Failing To Assess Coercion And Safety Before Encounter

The worker proceeds to encounter based on nominal agreement without assessing whether the victim is being coerced by family, culture, the offender, or the system, or whether ongoing safety risks exist. The mechanism is that agreement looks like readiness. The false signal is consent. The harm is that a coerced or unsafe encounter re-injures the victim and may expose them to ongoing harm. Coercion and safety must be assessed, not assumed.

### Confusing Advocate And Facilitator Roles

The worker serves as both advocate and facilitator without naming or managing the conflict, and the victim's trust in advocacy is compromised when the worker appears neutral toward the offender. The mechanism is that one worker handling both feels efficient. The false signal is continuity of relationship. The harm is role confusion, compromised advocacy, and a facilitation that the victim cannot trust. The roles must be clarified or separated.

### Inflating Restitution And Outcome Expectations

The worker tells the victim that restitution will be paid or that a particular outcome will result, when both are uncertain, and the victim's trust collapses when reality differs. The mechanism is that hope feels supportive. The false signal is a reassured victim. The harm is betrayal of trust, compounded harm, and disengagement from services. Expectations must be realistic.

### Rushing Encounter Preparation

The worker, pressed for time or results, abbreviates the preparation of victim and offender and moves to encounter before either is ready. The mechanism is that preparation feels like delay. The false signal is efficiency. The harm is an encounter that re-injures, fails, or produces an outcome neither party can sustain. Preparation is the work, not the prelude.

### Abandoning Support After Disposition Or Encounter

The worker provides intense support through the case and the encounter, then reduces contact once the process concludes, assuming resolution. The mechanism is that the case ending feels like the work ending. The false signal is the victim appearing stable. The harm is that delayed trauma reactions go unsupported, the victim feels abandoned, and recovery stalls. Support must continue after the process.

## Self-Check

- Did I center the victim's autonomy, safety, and self-determination throughout, including the right to decline or withdraw?
- Did I explain victim rights, restitution, and process realistically, without inflating expectations?
- Did I assess readiness and safety, including coercion, before any restorative encounter?
- Did I maintain clarity between my advocate and facilitator roles, naming and managing the conflict or separating the roles?
- Did I prepare both the victim and the offender thoroughly through separate, structured sessions?
- Did I balance victim and offender needs without sacrificing the victim's recovery to the offender's narrative?
- Did I provide continuous support before, during, and after the process, with follow-up for delayed reactions?
- Did I avoid treating restorative justice as inherently healing and instead ground each step in assessment and evidence?
