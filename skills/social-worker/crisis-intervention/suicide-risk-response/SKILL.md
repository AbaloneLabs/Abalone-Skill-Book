# Suicide Risk Response

Suicide risk response is the highest-acuity, highest-stakes task in social work: the decisions made in the hours around a suicidal crisis can determine whether a person lives or dies. The work requires both rapid, structured assessment and concrete, immediate action, often under emotional pressure and with incomplete information. Agents err because the cues are subtle and often concealed, because the impulse to reassure ("they'll be fine") competes with the need to act decisively, because workers fear that asking directly will worsen risk, and because the gap between a positive assessment and an effective intervention is where most failures occur. This skill covers how to assess suicide risk in real time, how to build a workable safety plan, how to arrange means restriction, and how to determine and execute the level of care — with the overriding rule that the standard is not "did I assess" but "did I act to reduce the danger," and that an assessment without a corresponding intervention is a failure.

## Core Rules

### Ask Directly and Specifically About Suicidal Thoughts and Plans

The first and most important step is to ask, directly and in plain language, whether the person is thinking about suicide, and if so, to explore the specifics. Use the word: "Are you thinking about killing yourself?" Euphemism and indirect probing ("Are you thinking about hurting yourself?") are less effective and can be misinterpreted. If the person confirms ideation, assess the components: frequency and intensity of the thoughts, whether there is a specific plan, what the plan is, whether they have access to the means (firearms, medications, other lethal methods), whether they have a timeframe, and their intent — how close they feel to acting. Prior attempts are among the strongest predictors and must be asked about specifically. Direct questions do not increase risk; they relieve isolation and surface the danger so it can be addressed. Document the person's exact words for the key disclosures.

### Assess the Full Risk Picture: Precipitants, Protectors, and Warning Signs

Beyond the ideation components, build the full risk picture. Precipitants: what has changed or is about to change — a loss, a relationship breakdown, a financial or legal crisis, a discharge, a humiliation, an anniversary of a loss. Precipitants elevate acute risk and define the dangerous window. Protectors: what is holding the person back — relationships, responsibilities, religious or moral objections, fear of death, hope for the future. Protectors are real and matter, but they are not static; a protector that held yesterday may not hold today, and over-reliance on a single protector (a child, a pet, a belief) without verifying its current strength is dangerous. Warning signs: behavioral changes — giving away possessions, saying goodbye, acquiring means, withdrawing, sudden calm after agitation (which can indicate a decision has been made). Integrate all three into an explicit risk formulation, not a global impression.

### Determine the Level of Care Required and Execute It

The assessment must drive a level-of-care decision, and the decision must be executed, not merely recommended. The continuum runs from outpatient care with a safety plan and close follow-up, to intensive outpatient or partial hospitalization, to voluntary inpatient psychiatric admission, to involuntary hospitalization when the person is an imminent danger to themselves and refuses voluntary care. The threshold for each level is defined by the risk: passive ideation with strong protectors and no plan may be managed outpatient with tight follow-up; active ideation with a plan, access to means, and a recent precipitant generally requires a higher level of care, often inpatient. Imminent danger (a stated intent to act soon, with means available) requires immediate action — do not let the person leave unobserved; arrange evaluation, and if they refuse and the danger is imminent, initiate the legal process for involuntary evaluation per your jurisdiction's standard. Know your jurisdiction's criteria and process before you need them. The gap between deciding a higher level of care is needed and actually arranging it is where deaths occur.

### Build a Collaborative Safety Plan, Not a No-Suicide Contract

A safety plan is a concrete, written, collaborative document the person helps build and can actually use when the thoughts intensify. It is not a no-suicide contract (asking the person to promise not to act), which has no evidence of effectiveness and can create false reassurance. A good safety plan includes: the person's personal warning signs (how they know the thoughts are getting worse), internal coping strategies they can use alone, people and social settings that provide distraction or support, people they can ask for help (with names and numbers), professionals and crisis lines (with numbers, including the local crisis line and 988 if applicable), and a specific means-restriction plan. Build it with the person, in their words, confirm they will have access to it (a copy on their phone, in their wallet), and rehearse how they would use it. A safety plan the person cannot recall or access when distressed is useless.

### Restrict Access to Lethal Means Concretely

Means restriction — reducing the person's access to the method they have identified or that is statistically most lethal — is one of the most effective suicide prevention interventions, because most suicidal crises are time-limited and method-specific, and adding friction to the lethal method often allows the crisis to pass. For firearms, this may mean temporary transfer to a trusted person off-site, secure storage with a trusted holder of the key or combination, or use of legal means (extreme risk protection orders, voluntary storage programs) where available. For medications, this may mean a trusted person holding and dispensing the supply, or limiting the quantity available. Work with the person collaboratively where possible, and involve family or supports with the person's consent; where the person refuses and the danger is imminent, the legal intervention pathway applies. Means restriction must be specific and verified, not a general agreement to "be safe." Document the means identified, the restriction arranged, and who holds the means.

### Arrange Concrete, Time-Defined Follow-Up

The period immediately after a suicidal crisis — the days and weeks after discharge or after the acute intervention — is the highest-risk window, and the person must not be left without a defined next contact. Arrange a specific follow-up appointment within a tight timeframe (often within 24-72 hours for high-risk discharges), confirm the person knows when and where it is, and verify transportation and access. Provide the crisis line number and the after-hours pathway. Where possible, make a bridging contact (a call or check-in) in the interval. A discharge with a "follow up with outpatient" instruction and no specific, confirmed appointment is a known failure point. Document the follow-up arrangement, the timeframe, and the confirmation.

### Involve and Brief Natural Supports Where Appropriate

Isolation is a major risk factor, and natural supports — family, friends, partners — are often the most important protective resource and the first to notice warning signs. Where the person consents (and where it does not increase danger), involve natural supports: brief them on the warning signs to watch for, the safety plan, the means restriction arrangement, and the crisis resources. This is not breaching confidentiality when done with consent; it is mobilizing protection. Where the person refuses to involve supports and the risk is high, weigh the confidentiality obligation against the safety imperative, consult, and know your jurisdiction's exceptions for imminent danger. Document the consent, the supports involved, and what they were told.

### Document the Assessment, the Intervention, and the Follow-Up

Every suicide risk encounter must be documented contemporaneously: the assessment (ideation, plan, means, intent, history, precipitants, protectors, warning signs, and the formulation), the level-of-care decision and its rationale, the safety plan and that it was provided to the person, the means restriction arranged and verified, the follow-up arranged and confirmed, the consultations sought, and the involvement of natural supports. The documentation is the evidence in any later review that risk was assessed and acted upon; its absence is treated as its failure. Use the person's exact words for key disclosures. Document even when the risk was judged low, because the absence of a recorded assessment is the worst possible record.

## Common Traps

### Reassurance Over Action

The worker assesses suicidal ideation, feels the danger is being managed by the conversation, and relies on the person's stated intent to stay safe without arranging concrete intervention. The trap mechanism is that the conversation provides relief to both worker and client, and the relief feels like resolution. The false signal is a calmer client who agrees to be safe. The harm is that the suicidal state is typically fluctuating, the calm may be transient or may indicate a decision has been made, and the person leaves the encounter with the danger unaddressed and the means still accessible. The standard is whether danger was reduced through concrete action, not whether the person feels better.

### The No-Suicide Contract as a Substitute for Means Restriction

The worker obtains a promise or signature that the person will not harm themselves and treats it as sufficient intervention. The trap is that the contract feels concrete and is easy to document. The false signal is a signed agreement. The harm is that the actual lethal means remains accessible and the worker has false reassurance; people who have signed such contracts have gone on to die by suicide. Means restriction and a collaborative safety plan are the evidence-based substitutes.

### Failing to Verify Means Restriction

The worker agrees with the person that they will "stay away from" the means or that a family member will hold it, but does not verify the arrangement actually happened. The trap is that the agreement feels like action. The false signal is a documented means-restriction plan. The harm is that the means remains accessible and the person is treated as protected when they are not. Means restriction must be specific and verified — who holds the firearm, where is it, is it off-site; who dispenses the medication, in what quantity.

### The Discharge Without a Confirmed Follow-Up

The person is stabilized, a higher level of care is judged unnecessary, and they are discharged with an instruction to follow up outpatient, but no specific appointment is made or confirmed. The trap is that the immediate crisis has passed and the discharge feels complete. The false signal is a person leaving with a referral. The harm is that the highest-risk window — the days after the crisis — passes without contact, and the person who seemed stable deteriorates without anyone noticing. A specific, confirmed appointment within a tight timeframe, plus a crisis pathway, is required.

### Over-Relying on a Single Protector

The risk assessment identifies a strong protector (a child, a partner, a pet, a religious belief) and the worker calibrates the response downward on its strength. The trap is that the protector is salient and feels decisive. The false signal is a stated reason to live. The harm is that protectors are not static; they can collapse (the relationship ends, the belief wavers, the child is removed), and a single protector without verification of its current and enduring strength can leave the person far less protected than the assessment suggested. Multiple, verified protectors and concrete means restriction are more reliable than a single cited reason.

### Avoiding Involuntary Intervention When It Is Indicated

The person is in imminent danger but refuses voluntary admission, and the worker hesitates to initiate involuntary evaluation, fearing it will damage the relationship or violate autonomy. The trap is that involuntary intervention feels harsh and the worker hopes voluntary options will suffice. The false signal is preserving the alliance. The harm is that a person in imminent danger is left unprotected during the peak risk window, with potentially fatal consequences. When the legal threshold for imminent danger is met, the obligation to act overrides the preference to preserve the relationship; the alliance can be rebuilt, a death cannot.

## Self-Check

- Did I ask directly and specifically about suicidal ideation, plan, means, intent, timeframe, and prior attempts, using the word and documenting the person's words?
- Did I build the full risk picture — precipitants, protectors (verified, not single), and warning signs — and integrate them into an explicit formulation?
- Did the assessment drive a level-of-care decision that I executed, not merely recommended, including initiating involuntary evaluation where the imminent-danger threshold was met?
- Did I build a collaborative, written safety plan in the person's words, confirmed they can access it, rather than relying on a no-suicide contract?
- Did I arrange specific, verified means restriction — who holds the means, where, and how it is confirmed — rather than a general agreement to be safe?
- Did I arrange a specific, confirmed follow-up within a tight timeframe plus a crisis pathway, rather than a generic referral?
- Did I involve and brief natural supports with consent where appropriate, recognizing isolation as a risk factor and supports as protection?
- Does my contemporaneous documentation capture the assessment, formulation, level-of-care decision and rationale, safety plan, means restriction, follow-up, consultations, and support involvement — sufficient to show risk was assessed and acted upon?
