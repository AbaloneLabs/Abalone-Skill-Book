# Safety Planning With Clients

A safety plan is a personalized, written, collaborative tool that helps a client navigate a future crisis — by recognizing their own warning signs, using internal coping, reaching out to supports, and accessing professional help before the danger peaks. It is distinct from a one-time safety assessment (a snapshot of current risk) and from a no-suicide contract (an ineffective promise). A good safety plan is built with the client, in their language, tailored to their specific patterns, and physically accessible when crisis hits. Agents err by treating safety planning as a form to complete rather than a tool to co-design, by producing plans too generic to use, by failing to arrange the means restriction and supports the plan relies on, and by never reviewing or revising the plan. This skill covers how to build a safety plan that will actually be used, how to verify its feasibility, and how to integrate it into ongoing care — with the overriding rule that a safety plan is only as good as its usability in the moment of crisis, and a plan the client cannot recall, access, or execute is not a plan.

## Core Rules

### Build the Plan Collaboratively, Not as a Form Imposed on the Client

A safety plan works only if the client owns it, and ownership comes from co-authorship. Build the plan with the client, eliciting their own words, their own warning signs, their own coping strategies, and their own support people — not inserting generic content the worker chooses. The client knows their crisis patterns better than anyone; the worker's role is to structure the elicitation and ensure the plan is complete and usable. A plan handed to the client pre-filled, or completed by the worker with the client passively nodding, will not be used in crisis because the client does not recognize it as their own. Spend the time to make it genuinely collaborative; this is the intervention, not paperwork. Confirm the client's understanding and agreement with each element.

### Start With the Client's Personal Warning Signs

The plan should begin with how this specific client knows a crisis is building — their personal early warning signs, before the crisis peaks. Elicit these concretely: What do you notice in your body? In your thoughts? In your behavior? What have you noticed in the past before things got bad? Common warning signs include sleep disruption, withdrawing from people, increased substance use, racing thoughts, a specific kind of irritability, neglecting self-care, rumination, or a feeling the client can name. The earlier and more specific the warning signs, the more time the plan buys. Generic warning signs ("feeling down") are less useful than the client's own ("when I stop answering my sister's calls and start sleeping all day"). Document the client's own descriptions.

### Identify Internal Coping Strategies the Client Has Actually Used or Will Try

Internal coping — things the client can do alone to reduce the arousal or redirect the thoughts — is the first line of the plan, because it requires no one else and can be deployed at the earliest warning sign. Elicit strategies the client has used before that helped, even partially: exercise, music, specific breathing or grounding techniques, going outside, a distracting activity, a pet, a creative practice. Be realistic: a strategy the client has never tried and does not believe in will not be used in crisis. If the client has few strategies, teach and rehearse one or two simple ones (grounding, breathing) during the planning, so they are familiar. Avoid loading the plan with a long menu; a few strategies the client will actually use are better than many they will not.

### Identify People and Settings for Distraction and Support

Social connection reduces isolation and arousal, and the plan should identify specific people and social settings the client can turn to — for distraction (being around others without necessarily disclosing the crisis) and for direct support (people they can tell they are struggling). Elicit specific names and numbers, with the client's consent to contact them, and confirm those people are willing and appropriate. Distinguish social settings (a coffee shop, a community center, a place of worship, a meeting) from specific individuals. Verify contact information. A plan that names "family" or "friends" generically is not actionable in crisis; a plan that names "Maria, 555-1234, she knows to expect my calls" is.

### Identify Professionals and Crisis Lines, With Numbers

The plan must include professional and crisis resources, with current numbers: the client's therapist or psychiatrist (if any), the local crisis line, the national crisis line (988 in the US, and equivalents elsewhere), and emergency services. Include the client's clinician's after-hours pathway. Confirm the client knows when to use each (the clinician for non-urgent, the crisis line for urgent but not emergency, emergency services for imminent danger). Write the numbers into the plan, because a client in crisis may not be able to find them. For clients without a clinician, the crisis line and a follow-up appointment are the professional layer.

### Arrange Specific Means Restriction as Part of the Plan

For suicide and self-harm risk, the plan must include concrete means restriction — what lethal means the client has identified or has access to, and the specific arrangement to reduce access during the risk period. This is often the most important element. For firearms: who will hold them off-site, or how will they be secured with another person holding access. For medications: who will hold and dispense them, in what quantity. For other means the client has identified: the specific arrangement. Means restriction must be specific and verified — not "I'll keep them safe" but "my brother David will store the firearm at his house starting tonight, I've called him and he's agreed." Build this collaboratively with the client and the support person where possible; where the client refuses and the risk is imminent, the legal pathway applies. Document the means, the arrangement, and the verification.

### Make the Plan Physically Accessible and Rehearse Its Use

A safety plan that lives in the chart and not with the client is useless in crisis. Give the client a copy — on paper, on their phone, saved as a note or photo — and confirm they will have it with them or know where it is. Rehearse with the client how they would use it: "When you notice [warning sign], what will you do first? If that doesn't help, what next? When would you call the crisis line?" Walking through the sequence surfaces gaps (a strategy the client doubts, a number they cannot find, a step they would skip) and strengthens the likelihood of use. A rehearsed plan is recalled and used; an unrehearsed plan is forgotten. Confirm the client's copy is accessible and that they have rehearsed the sequence.

### Review and Revise the Plan as Circumstances Change

A safety plan is a living document. Review it at follow-up contacts: did the client use it, did it work, what needs to change? Update warning signs as new patterns emerge, update supports as relationships change, update coping as new strategies are learned, and update means restriction as access changes. A plan built once and never reviewed becomes stale and unrepresentative of the client's current situation and network. Re-review the plan especially after any crisis event, any change in risk, any change in the client's supports or living situation, and any change in treatment. Document the reviews and revisions.

## Common Traps

### The Generic, Form-Driven Plan

The worker completes a safety plan template with generic content — "warning signs: feeling sad; coping: deep breathing; supports: family" — that does not reflect the client's actual patterns, strategies, or network. The trap mechanism is that the template provides structure and the completed form satisfies the documentation requirement, so it feels like a plan. The false signal is a signed safety plan in the chart. The harm is that the plan is unrecognizable to the client and unusable in crisis, because the warning signs are not theirs, the coping is untried, and the supports are unnamed. A plan must be personal and specific, co-built with the client.

### The Plan That Lives in the Chart, Not With the Client

The plan is documented in the record but the client leaves without a copy they can access. The trap is that the documentation feels complete. The false signal is a plan on file. The harm is that in crisis the client cannot recall the steps, the numbers, or the arrangements, and the plan that existed on paper provides no protection. The client must have a copy, in an accessible form, and the use must be rehearsed.

### Means Restriction as a General Agreement Rather Than a Verified Arrangement

The plan states the client will "limit access to means" or "stay away from firearms," but no specific, verified arrangement is made or confirmed. The trap is that the statement feels like action. The false signal is a means-restriction entry in the plan. The harm is that the means remains accessible and the client is treated as protected when they are not. Means restriction requires specifics — who, what, where, verified — and is often the single most protective element of the plan.

### Unverified or Unwilling Supports

The plan names support people whose willingness, availability, or contact information has not been confirmed. The trap is that naming a support feels sufficient. The false signal is a plan with names and numbers. The harm is that in crisis the client reaches out to someone who does not answer, does not know they are a support, or is not appropriate, and the plan fails at the moment it is needed. Supports must be confirmed, with consent, and the client must know they can be called.

### The Plan Built Once and Never Reviewed

The plan is created during one encounter and treated as complete, never revisited as the client's situation, network, and risk change. The trap is that the plan exists and feels enduring. The false signal is a plan on file. The harm is that the plan becomes stale — supports change, warning signs evolve, means access shifts, coping strategies prove unhelpful — and the plan the client would reach for no longer reflects their reality. Regular review and revision keep the plan alive and usable.

### Overloading the Plan With Strategies the Client Will Not Use

The worker, wanting to be thorough, lists many coping strategies and supports, including ones the client has not tried or does not believe in. The trap is that a long list feels comprehensive. The false signal is a thorough-looking plan. The harm is that in crisis the client faces a menu they cannot navigate, defaults to none of it, and the plan's bulk undermines its usability. A few strategies and supports the client will actually use, rehearsed, are more effective than a comprehensive list.

## Self-Check

- Did I build the plan collaboratively with the client, eliciting their own words, warning signs, coping, and supports, rather than imposing a generic template?
- Did I start with the client's personal, specific warning signs that buy time before the crisis peaks?
- Did I identify internal coping strategies the client has used or will realistically try, teaching and rehearsing new ones rather than listing untried techniques?
- Did I identify specific, named people and social settings for distraction and support, with verified numbers and confirmed willingness?
- Did I include professionals and crisis lines with current numbers, and confirm the client knows when to use each?
- Did I arrange specific, verified means restriction — who, what, where, confirmed — rather than a general agreement to limit access?
- Did I give the client an accessible copy and rehearse the sequence of use, surfacing and fixing gaps?
- Did I build in review and revision at follow-up and after changes in risk, supports, or circumstances, keeping the plan a living document?
