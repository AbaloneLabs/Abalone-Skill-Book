# Disaster Response and Mass Casualty Social Work

Disaster and mass-casualty social work is fundamentally different from routine practice, because the scale, the chaos, and the simultaneity of need overwhelm the systems that normally deliver care, and the worker must operate without the infrastructure, the information, and the time that ordinary casework assumes. In a disaster, clients arrive in waves rather than appointments, needs are basic and urgent (shelter, food, safety, medication, missing family) rather than reflective, the formal systems are damaged or saturated, and the worker's own safety and stability may be compromised. The recurring failure is to import routine practice into a disaster — lengthy assessments, individualized plans, slow referrals — when the situation demands triage, psychological first aid, practical stabilization, and activation of the informal and mutual-aid networks that survive when formal systems do not. This skill covers the judgment used to deliver social work in disaster and mass-casualty settings, with the overriding principles that basic needs and safety precede psychological processing, that the worker is also a survivor, and that the response must build on existing community capacity rather than displace it.

## Core Rules

### Triage by Need and Risk, Not by Arrival or Acquaintance

In a disaster, the volume of need exceeds the capacity to respond, and the worker must triage: medical emergencies and immediate safety threats first, then those whose basic needs (shelter, medication, supervision of children and dependent adults) are unmet, then those whose psychological distress is significant but stable. Triage is not a judgment of worth; it is the allocation of scarce attention to where it prevents the most harm. Use a consistent triage framework, reassess as conditions change, and resist the pull toward the most visible or sympathetic cases when others are more urgent. Document triage decisions at the level the situation allows.

### Stabilize Basic Needs Before Psychological Processing

People in acute disaster cannot benefit from psychological processing while they are cold, hungry, unsafe, separated from their children, or without their medications. The first social work intervention is practical: shelter, food, water, warmth, safety, medication access, reunification with family, and connection to immediate resources. Psychological first aid — providing comfort, reducing physiological arousal, orienting, connecting to supports — follows and accompanies practical stabilization, but it does not precede it. Do not push survivors to narrate trauma before they are safe and their basic needs are met; premature processing can worsen distress. The hierarchy is needs first, then safety, then support, then — much later — processing.

### Provide Psychological First Aid, Not Routine Therapy

Psychological first aid is the evidence-informed approach for survivors in the immediate aftermath: establishing contact, ensuring safety and comfort, stabilizing those who are overwhelmed, information-gathering about needs, practical assistance, connection to social supports, coping information, and linkage to ongoing services. It is not therapy, not a formal mental health assessment, and not a debriefing that requires the survivor to recount the trauma. Avoid single-session debriefing approaches that press for emotional processing, which lack evidence and may cause harm. The goal of PFA is to reduce arousal, restore a sense of safety, and connect the survivor to the supports that will carry them forward.

### Prioritize Family Reunification and the Protection of Vulnerable Populations

Disasters separate families — children from parents, dependent adults from caregivers, people from their support networks — and reunification is both a safety and a psychological priority. Establish and use reunification systems, document separated family members, and attend especially to unaccompanied minors, people with disabilities, older adults, people with limited language access, and people whose vulnerabilities are amplified by displacement. These populations face elevated risks of exploitation, neglect, medical deterioration, and trafficking in the chaos of displacement. Active identification and protection of vulnerable groups is a core disaster social work function, not an afterthought.

### Activate and Coordinate With Existing Community and Mutual-Aid Networks

The formal systems (agencies, clinics, government programs) are often damaged or saturated in a disaster, but the community's informal networks — faith communities, cultural organizations, neighborhood groups, mutual aid — usually survive and are the first and most sustained responders. Map and connect with these networks, distribute resources through them, and avoid displacing or duplicating them. Imposing an external response that ignores existing capacity fragments the recovery and breeds dependency; a response that activates and supports local networks builds resilience. Cultural and linguistic matching through community partners is often more effective than external provision.

### Operate Within Incident Command and Coordinate Across Agencies

Disaster response is governed by incident command and emergency management structures, and the social worker must operate within them — understanding the chain of command, the coordination mechanisms, the information-sharing protocols, and the boundaries of role and authority. Coordinate with other agencies (health, public safety, emergency management, NGOs) to avoid duplication and gaps, share information appropriately, and participate in the multi-agency case coordination that complex cases require. Operating outside the structure, however well-intentioned, creates confusion, duplication, and risk. Know the structure, find your place in it, and work the system.

### Monitor and Protect the Responder, Including Yourself

Disaster responders — including social workers — are themselves exposed to trauma, loss, exhaustion, and sometimes personal impact from the same disaster, and the cumulative toll produces burnout, secondary trauma, and impaired judgment. Monitor your own and your colleagues' functioning, enforce rest and rotation, use peer support and supervision, and recognize when a responder needs to step back. A depleted responder makes worse decisions and may harm those they intend to help. Self-care and team-care in disaster response are operational requirements, not luxuries, and leadership must enforce them because individuals under pressure will not.

### Plan the Transition From Response to Recovery

The acute response phase ends, but recovery is long, and the transition from emergency services to ongoing recovery is a predictable failure point where survivors lose the supports that carried them through the acute phase. Plan the transition: connect survivors to ongoing case management, benefits, housing, and mental health services before the emergency services stand down, and ensure the community networks activated during response are sustained into recovery. A response that ends without a recovery handoff leaves survivors to navigate a fragmented system alone, exactly when their resilience is most depleted.

## Common Traps

### Routine Assessment Imported Into a Disaster

The worker attempts a full biopsychosocial assessment on a survivor in a shelter, consuming time and attention that dozens of others need, and producing a document that no one will use. The mechanism is that routine practice is the worker's trained default, and the disaster context does not automatically switch it off. The false signal is thorough, professional work. The harm is that the worker's scarce capacity is spent on one person while others go untriaged, and the assessment itself may overwhelm a survivor whose basic needs are unmet. Disaster demands triage and PFA, not routine assessment.

### Psychological Processing Pushed Before Basic Needs Are Met

The worker encourages a survivor to recount the trauma while the survivor is still cold, separated from family, or without medication, on the assumption that processing helps. The mechanism is that the therapeutic frame is applied where practical stabilization is needed. The false signal is compassionate, attentive care. The harm is that processing in an unsafe, unmet-needs state can worsen distress and re-traumatize, while the practical needs that would actually stabilize the survivor go unaddressed. Basic needs and safety precede processing.

### Vulnerable Populations Overlooked in the Chaos

Unaccompanied minors, people with disabilities, older adults, and limited-language survivors fall through the general response because they cannot advocate in the chaos, and their elevated risks (exploitation, medical decline, trafficking) materialize. The mechanism is that the general response serves the visible and the vocal, and the vulnerable are quieter and harder to reach. The false signal is a response serving "everyone." The harm is that the people most at risk receive the least protection. Vulnerable populations must be actively identified and served, not left to access the general response.

### External Response Displacing Existing Community Capacity

Outside responders arrive, set up parallel services, and ignore or displace the community networks already responding, fragmenting the effort and breeding dependency. The mechanism is that external responders bring resources and structures and assume the local capacity is absent or inadequate. The false signal is a robust external operation. The harm is that local resilience is undermined, the response becomes less culturally and practically attuned, and the community is less capable after the responders leave. The response must map, activate, and support existing community capacity.

### Operating Outside Incident Command

A well-intentioned worker acts independently, distributing resources or making decisions outside the coordination structure, creating duplication, gaps, and confusion. The mechanism is that the worker sees a need and acts, and the coordination structure feels like a constraint. The false signal is responsive, decisive action. The harm is duplicated effort, conflicting information, unmet needs in areas everyone assumed someone else covered, and risk to the worker and survivors. Disaster response requires operating within the command structure, even when it feels slow.

### Responder Self-Care Treated as Optional

The worker pushes through exhaustion and exposure without rest, rotation, or support, on the rationale that the need is too great to stop. The mechanism is that the scale of need feels to demand total commitment, and self-care feels selfish. The false signal is dedication. The harm is impaired judgment, secondary trauma, burnout, and decisions that harm survivors, and the loss of a responder the operation needs for the longer recovery. Self and team care are operational requirements enforced by leadership, not personal indulgences.

### Acute Response Ending Without a Recovery Handoff

The emergency services stand down, the external responders leave, and the survivors lose the supports that carried them through, with no transition to ongoing care. The mechanism is that the response is funded and structured for the acute phase, and the recovery phase is someone else's problem. The false signal is a completed response. The harm is that survivors, most depleted at the transition, are left to navigate a fragmented recovery system alone, and the gains of the response are lost. The transition to recovery must be planned and executed before the response ends.

## Self-Check

- Am I triaging by need and risk using a consistent framework, rather than responding to the most visible or sympathetic cases?
- Am I stabilizing basic needs (shelter, food, safety, medication, reunification) before any psychological processing?
- Am I providing psychological first aid — comfort, orientation, practical assistance, connection to supports — rather than routine therapy or trauma debriefing?
- Am I actively identifying and protecting vulnerable populations (unaccompanied minors, people with disabilities, older adults, limited-language survivors) rather than relying on the general response to reach them?
- Am I mapping, activating, and supporting existing community and mutual-aid networks rather than displacing them with external services?
- Am I operating within the incident command and coordination structure, sharing information and avoiding duplication and gaps?
- Am I monitoring and enforcing responder rest, rotation, and support — for myself and colleagues — as an operational requirement?
- Have I planned the transition from acute response to ongoing recovery, connecting survivors to sustained services before the emergency response stands down?
