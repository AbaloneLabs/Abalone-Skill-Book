# Care Coordination and Collaboration

Few social work clients are served by a single provider; most are involved with multiple systems — healthcare, behavioral health, child welfare, schools, housing, benefits, justice, substance use treatment — and the coordination of these services is itself a major determinant of the client's outcomes. When coordination works, the services reinforce each other and the client experiences coherent care; when it fails, services duplicate, contradict, gap, or conflict, and the client — often the least equipped to navigate the fragmentation — falls through. Agents err because each system has its own frame and language, because information does not flow, because no one owns the whole picture, and because coordination is invisible work that no single system rewards or funds. This skill covers how to coordinate care across systems, how to convene and sustain a team, how to manage handoffs and conflict, and how to ensure continuity — with the overriding rule that coordination is a clinical function, not an administrative afterthought, and that the absence of someone owning the whole picture is itself a harm to the client.

## Core Rules

### Identify Who Owns the Whole Picture and Take or Assign That Role

In any multi-system case, someone must hold the whole picture — all the providers, all the plans, the medications, the appointments, the goals, the risks — or the client will be served in fragments that no one integrates. Identify early whether that role is yours (often the case for the primary case manager), belongs to another designated coordinator, or is unassigned. If it is unassigned and the case is complex, take it or advocate for its assignment, because the absence of a coordinator is the most common cause of fragmentation. Holding the whole picture means maintaining a current list of all providers and contacts, the services each is providing, the medications and treatments, the appointments, and the goals, and sharing this picture across the team with the client's consent. Document the coordinator role and the provider map.

### Obtain Consent for Communication and Share the Minimum Necessary

Multi-system coordination requires sharing client information across providers, and this requires the client's informed consent (except where a specific legal exception applies). Obtain written consent specifying who can share what with whom, explain to the client why coordination helps them, and honor the limits the client sets. When sharing, apply the minimum-necessary principle: share what each provider needs to coordinate effectively, not the client's entire history. Be especially careful with highly sensitive information (substance use treatment records, HIV status, sexual orientation, immigration status) which has special legal protections in many jurisdictions. Re-confirm consent periodically, as providers and circumstances change. Document the consent and the information shared, and what was withheld and why.

### Convene the Team and Establish Regular Communication

For complex cases, convene the team — all the key providers and, where appropriate, the client and natural supports — to align on goals, plans, and roles. A team meeting (in person, by phone, or by video) establishes shared understanding, surfaces conflicts and gaps, assigns responsibilities, and creates accountability. Establish a regular communication rhythm proportionate to the case's complexity and risk: frequent contact and meetings for high-acuity, high-fragmentation cases; less for stable, well-coordinated ones. Use a shared care plan that all providers can reference. Identify a single point of contact for urgent issues. A team that never meets operates as separate providers who happen to share a client; a team that meets operates as a coordinated system. Document the team composition, the meetings, and the shared plan.

### Manage Handoffs Explicitly and Verify Receipt

Handoffs — between shifts, between providers, between agencies, at discharge — are the points where information is most often lost and clients most often fall through. Manage every handoff explicitly: what information is being transferred, to whom, by when, and how it will be confirmed received. Use structured handoff communication (a written summary, a warm handoff call, a shared record) rather than relying on the receiving party to find the information. For high-risk handoffs (a discharge, a transfer to a new agency), verify receipt — confirm the receiving provider has the information and has accepted the case — rather than assuming the referral or transfer constituted care. The gap between sending and receiving is where clients are lost. Document the handoff and the verification of receipt.

### Reconcile Plans and Medications Across Providers

When multiple providers each have a plan, the plans may conflict — one provider's goal undermines another's, two providers prescribe without knowing the other's medications, services duplicate or gap. Reconcile: ensure the providers know each other's plans, that the plans are aligned or at least not contradictory, and that medications are reviewed together for interactions and adherence burden. A client on medications from three prescribers who do not communicate is at risk of interactions, duplication, and non-adherence; reconciling the medication list across providers is a safety function. Bring conflicts to the team and resolve them; do not let contradictory plans proceed in parallel. Document the reconciliation and the resolution of conflicts.

### Resolve Inter-Agency Conflict Through the Client's Interest

Agencies and providers disagree — about diagnosis, about the plan, about who is responsible for what, about boundaries of scope. Left unresolved, these conflicts paralyze the case and leave the client underserved. Address conflict directly: name it, convene the parties, focus on the client's interest rather than agency turf, and seek a resolution that serves the client. Escalate within agencies when a frontline conflict cannot be resolved. Do not let professional discomfort with conflict leave the client in a gap. When agencies point to each other as responsible and no one acts, the client is unserved; the coordinator's role is to force clarity about who is doing what. Document the conflict, the resolution, and the responsibilities assigned.

### Advocate for the Client Within and Across Systems

The client often cannot navigate, advocate, or push back against the systems serving them — they may lack the capacity, the language, the knowledge, or the standing. Coordination includes advocacy: pushing for an appointment that is delayed, challenging a denial, ensuring the client's voice is heard in decisions about them, insisting on services the client is entitled to, and protecting the client from system failures (a discharge too early, a service withdrawn, a gap opening). Advocacy is not optional refinement; it is often the difference between the plan working and the plan failing. Know the client's rights and entitlements, and use formal processes (appeals, grievances, complaints) when informal advocacy fails. Document the advocacy and its outcomes.

### Maintain Continuity Through Worker and System Changes

Workers change jobs, agencies reorganize, providers leave — and at each transition, the client's continuity is at risk if the coordination knowledge lives only in a departing worker's head. Build continuity into the system: maintain the provider map and shared care plan in the record, document the coordination decisions and rationale, and ensure a handoff that transfers the coordination knowledge, not just the case file. When you are the one leaving, brief your successor thoroughly on the team, the plans, the history, and the current issues. When a key provider leaves, identify the replacement and re-establish the relationship. Continuity is a property of the system, not the individual, and it must be built deliberately. Document the continuity arrangements.

## Common Traps

### No One Owning the Whole Picture

Each provider serves their piece, and no one integrates, so the client is served in fragments that may conflict or gap. The trap mechanism is that each provider is doing their job, and the fragmentation is invisible because no one is looking at the whole. The false signal is multiple active services. The harm is that services contradict or duplicate, the client is overwhelmed or underserved, and the gaps between services are where crises and failures occur. Identifying and assigning the coordinator role is the defense.

### The Unverified Referral or Handoff

A provider refers or transfers the client and treats the act as completed care, without verifying the receiving party accepted the case and received the information. The trap is that the referral feels like action. The false signal is a referral in the chart. The harm is that the receiving party never got the information, never accepted the case, or had no capacity, and the client is unserved during the gap — often a high-risk gap. Verifying receipt and acceptance is part of the handoff.

### Information Not Flowing Across Systems

Providers do not share information (due to consent gaps, system incompatibility, or habit), so each operates with a partial picture and the plans conflict. The trap is that each provider's piece is coherent. The false signal is each provider feeling informed. The harm is that medications interact, plans contradict, and no one sees the whole — the client bears the consequences. Obtaining consent and establishing information flow, with minimum-necessary sharing, is the defense.

### Parallel Plans That Contradict

Two providers each pursue a plan that undermines the other (one pushing abstinence while another prescribes, one requiring attendance that conflicts with another's required attendance), and the contradiction goes unaddressed because the providers do not coordinate. The trap is that each plan is internally coherent. The false signal is active treatment. The harm is that the client is set up to fail by contradictory demands, drops out, and is blamed for non-compliance when the real failure was the lack of reconciliation. Reconciling plans across providers is a coordination function.

### Turf Conflict Paralyzing the Case

Agencies disagree about scope or responsibility, point to each other, and no one acts, leaving the client in a gap while the conflict persists. The trap is that each agency's position feels justified. The false signal is that the case is "being discussed." The harm is that the client is unserved during the dispute, sometimes for months, and the underlying need worsens. Naming the conflict, focusing on the client's interest, and forcing clarity about responsibility is the coordinator's role.

### Continuity Lost When a Worker Leaves

A coordinator leaves and the coordination knowledge — the relationships, the history, the team dynamics — leaves with them, so the next worker starts from scratch and the client experiences a rupture. The trap is that the case file is handed off. The false signal is a transferred case. The harm is that relationships and knowledge are lost, the team fragments, and the client's care is disrupted during the rebuild. Building continuity into the record and the system, and briefing successors thoroughly, is the defense.

## Self-Check

- Did I identify who owns the whole picture — all providers, plans, medications, appointments — and take or assign that role for complex cases?
- Did I obtain informed consent for cross-provider communication, share the minimum necessary, and protect specially-protected information?
- Did I convene the team and establish a communication rhythm proportionate to complexity and risk, with a shared care plan and a single urgent contact?
- Did I manage every handoff explicitly and verify the receiving party received the information and accepted the case, rather than assuming the referral constituted care?
- Did I reconcile plans and medications across providers to prevent contradiction, interaction, and duplication?
- Did I address inter-agency conflict directly, focusing on the client's interest and forcing clarity about responsibility, rather than letting it paralyze the case?
- Did I advocate for the client within and across systems — pushing for services, challenging denials, ensuring their voice — using formal processes when informal advocacy failed?
- Did I build continuity into the record and system, and brief successors thoroughly, so worker and provider changes do not rupture the client's care?
