# Care Coordination and Service Integration

Clients with complex needs — serious mental illness, chronic medical conditions, substance use, housing instability, involvement with multiple systems — almost always have more providers than coordination, and the consequence is a service experience that is fragmented, duplicated, and gapped in ways that harm the client. Each provider sees their piece, no one sees the whole, referrals are made and not confirmed, medications are prescribed by clinicians who do not communicate, appointments are scheduled in conflict, and the client — often the least equipped to navigate — is left to integrate a system that was designed to be integrated for them. The social worker's role in care coordination is to hold the whole picture, to make the providers function as a team rather than a collection, to close the loops that referrals assume closed, and to build a coherent plan from disconnected services. This skill covers the judgment used to coordinate and integrate services for complex clients, with the overriding principle that a referral made is not care delivered, and that the client's outcomes depend on the connections between services, not the services themselves.

## Core Rules

### Build and Maintain a Complete Map of Providers and Services

Coordination begins with a complete inventory of who is in the client's life: every provider, every agency, every medication, every appointment, every benefit, and every informal support. Build and maintain this map actively, because it changes and because providers are often unaware of each other. The map is the foundation for identifying duplication (two therapists, overlapping medications, conflicting case managers), gaps (a medical condition with no provider, a benefit unaccessed), and conflicts (appointments that collide, providers working at cross-purposes). A client whose map is incomplete is a client whose coordination is built on ignorance.

### Establish Communication Loops and Close Every Referral

A referral is not complete until the receiving service confirms engagement and the referring party receives feedback. Establish the communication loops explicitly: who will contact whom, by when, what the feedback mechanism is, and how the worker will confirm the client actually connected. Track open referrals and follow up on those that go silent, because a significant fraction of referrals never materialize into care, and the client's deterioration is often the first sign. Closing the loop is the unglamorous core of coordination; without it, the system appears to be serving the client while actually losing them between the cracks.

### Convene the Team Around the Client, With the Client

For complex clients, convene the providers as a team — virtually or in person — with the client at the center, to align goals, share information (with consent), resolve conflicts, and assign accountability. A team that meets periodically, even briefly, catches the emerging problems that no individual provider sees: a medication change that conflicts with another, a service ending that no one planned for, a goal that two providers are pursuing differently. Include the client so the plan reflects their goals and they understand it; a plan made about the client rather than with them fails. Document the team's agreements and the accountable parties.

### Reconcile Medications and Treatments Across Prescribers

Clients with multiple prescribers (psychiatry, primary care, specialists, pain management) are at high risk of medication problems: duplication, interaction, competing side effects, and contradictory regimens. Coordinate with the prescribers and the pharmacy to reconcile the complete medication list, identify problems, and ensure each prescriber is aware of the others' prescriptions. The social worker does not prescribe, but is often the only person who sees all the prescribers and can flag the need for reconciliation. Medication reconciliation is a recurring, not one-time, task, because regimens change.

### Integrate Goals Across Systems So They Reinforce Rather Than Conflict

Different systems pursue different goals — the court wants compliance, the clinic wants sobriety, the housing program wants rent payment, the medical provider wants adherence — and these can conflict in the client's daily life, forcing choices that set back one goal to advance another. Work across systems to integrate the goals where possible, sequence them sensibly, and resolve conflicts explicitly rather than leaving the client to navigate contradictory demands. An integrated plan acknowledges the tensions and sequences the work; a fragmented plan issues competing ultimatums and blames the client when they cannot meet all of them.

### Assign Clear Accountability for Each Action

Coordination fails when everyone assumes someone else is handling a task. For each action in the plan, assign a specific accountable person — not an agency, a person — with a deadline and a feedback mechanism. Confirm the assignment is accepted, not just assigned, and follow up. Diffuse accountability is the structural cause of most dropped balls in complex care; explicit, accepted, tracked accountability is the structural fix. Document the assignments and review them at each team contact.

### Address the Practical Barriers That Defeat Good Plans

A perfect plan that the client cannot implement is worthless, and complex clients face practical barriers — transportation, phone access, literacy, cognitive impairment, competing survival demands, mistrust of systems — that defeat plans designed without them. Build the plan around the client's real capacity and circumstances: arrange transportation, use reminder calls, simplify the schedule, address the basic needs that consume the client's bandwidth, and accept that a less elegant plan the client can follow beats an optimal plan they cannot. Coordination that ignores practical reality produces a beautiful plan and a failing client.

## Common Traps

### The Referral Treated as Completed Care

A referral is made and the task is marked complete, with no confirmation that the client connected to the service. The mechanism is that the referral feels like action and the follow-up feels like extra work. The false signal is a service "arranged." The harm is that a large fraction of referrals never result in care, the client's need goes unmet, and the system believes it has served them while they deteriorate. Every referral must be tracked and confirmed closed.

### Each Provider Working Their Piece With No Whole-Picture Holder

Each provider competently addresses their domain, but no one holds the whole picture, and the interactions between the pieces go unmanaged. The mechanism is that providers are trained and incentivized for their domain, and whole-picture coordination is no one's explicit job. The false signal is a client "in care with multiple providers." The harm is duplication, conflict, and gaps that no individual provider detects, and a client whose combined care is less than the sum of its parts. Someone must hold the whole map and convene the team.

### Diffuse Accountability Where Everyone Assumes Someone Else Acts

A task is discussed in a meeting, no specific person is assigned, and it falls through the cracks because everyone assumed someone else would do it. The mechanism is that group discussion feels like decision and the assignment step is skipped. The false signal is a plan agreed upon. The harm is that the agreed action never happens, the client's need persists, and the team is surprised at the next meeting. Every action needs a specific, accepted, tracked owner.

### Competing Goals Issued as Simultaneous Ultimatums

The court, the clinic, the housing program, and the medical provider each issue their requirements as non-negotiable, and the client cannot meet all of them simultaneously, so they fail at all. The mechanism is that each system optimizes its own goal without awareness of the others. The false signal is clear expectations from each system. The harm is that the client is set up to fail and then blamed for non-compliance, when the real problem is uncoordinated, conflicting demands. Goals must be integrated and sequenced across systems.

### The Plan Designed Without the Client's Practical Barriers

The plan assumes transportation, phone access, time, and cognitive bandwidth the client does not have, and it fails in implementation. The mechanism is that the plan is designed from the provider's vantage, where these resources are assumed. The false signal is a well-constructed plan. The harm is that the client cannot follow it, is labeled non-compliant, and the underlying need persists. The plan must be built around the client's real capacity and practical barriers.

### Medication Reconciliation Omitted Across Prescribers

Multiple prescribers write for one client with no awareness of each other, and the resulting regimen is duplicative, interacting, or contradictory. The mechanism is that prescribers operate in separate systems and no one reconciles the full list. The false signal is a client "on medications." The harm is adverse drug events, therapeutic failure, and side-effect burden that the client experiences as worsening illness. The worker, who sees all the prescribers, must flag the need for reconciliation.

### The Team That Meets Without the Client

Providers convene and plan without the client present, producing a plan the client does not understand, did not shape, and will not follow. The mechanism is that meeting without the client is logistically easier and feels efficient. The false signal is a coordinated plan. The harm is a plan built on assumptions about the client's goals and capacity that the client may not share, leading to non-engagement. The client must be at the center of the team, shaping the plan.

## Self-Check

- Do I maintain a complete, current map of all providers, services, medications, benefits, and informal supports in the client's life?
- Is every referral tracked and confirmed closed, with feedback from the receiving service, rather than treated as completed care?
- Do I convene the providers as a team with the client at the center, to align goals, share information, and resolve conflicts?
- Have I flagged the need for medication reconciliation across prescribers, and is the complete medication list current?
- Are goals across systems integrated and sequenced, rather than issued as competing simultaneous ultimatums?
- Does every action in the plan have a specific, accepted, tracked owner with a deadline, rather than diffuse accountability?
- Is the plan built around the client's real practical barriers (transportation, phone, cognition, survival demands), and does it follow the client's goals with their input?
