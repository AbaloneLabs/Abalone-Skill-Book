# Service Planning and Goal Setting

Service planning is the process of translating a client's needs, strengths, and circumstances into a concrete, prioritized, actionable plan that the client and the team will follow. A good plan focuses effort, sequences interventions sensibly, builds on the client's goals and capacities, and provides a basis for measuring progress. A bad plan — vague, worker-imposed, overloaded, or mis-sequenced — generates paperwork without change, frustrates the client, and obscures accountability. Agents err because the form invites generic goals, because workers substitute their own priorities for the client's, because plans try to address everything at once, and because goals are written in ways that can never be measured or achieved. This skill covers how to build a plan that is genuinely collaborative, prioritized, measurable, and sequenced — with the overriding rule that a plan the client did not help build will not be implemented by the client, and that the plan is a tool for change, not a document for compliance.

## Core Rules

### Start With the Client's Goals and Build the Plan Around Them

The foundation of an effective plan is what the client wants to achieve, in their own words and priorities, not what the worker thinks they should achieve. Elicit the client's goals early and concretely: what would be different in your life, what do you most want to work on, what would success look like to you? The client's goals may differ from the worker's assessment of need, and reconciling the two is part of the work — not by overriding the client, but by finding the overlap, exploring what underlies the client's goals, and educating about options the client may not have considered. Where the worker's mandate imposes requirements (a court order, a safety requirement), be transparent about what is non-negotiable and build the negotiable parts around the client's aims. A plan built on the client's goals is implemented; a plan imposed on the client is resisted or ignored. Document the client's goals in their own words.

### Prioritize Ruthlessly: Not Everything Can Be Addressed at Once

Clients typically present with multiple, interlocking needs, and the temptation is to address them all simultaneously in the plan. This overloads the client, dilutes effort, and ensures that nothing gets enough attention to change. Prioritize: identify the one to three goals that matter most right now, considering urgency (what is most dangerous or time-limited), leverage (what change would most reduce other problems), the client's readiness (what they are most motivated and able to work on), and sequencing (what must be stable before other work can proceed — e.g., housing and safety before deeper clinical work). Name what is being deferred and why, and revisit priorities over time. A plan with three focused goals is more effective than one with ten scattered ones. Document the prioritization rationale.

### Make Goals SMART: Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-Bound

Vague goals ("improve mental health," "be a better parent," "address housing") cannot be measured, cannot be achieved in any verifiable sense, and permit drift. Translate each goal into SMART form: Specific (what exactly will be different), Measurable (how will we and the client know it happened — what observable indicator), Achievable (realistic given the client's circumstances and resources), Relevant (connected to the client's goals and the assessed needs), and Time-bound (by when). "Attend weekly therapy sessions for three months and report a reduction in panic attacks from daily to fewer than two per week" is a SMART goal; "feel better" is not. Write goals in behavioral, observable terms so that progress can be assessed honestly rather than impressionistically. Document the SMART formulation for each goal.

### Break Goals Into Objectives and Action Steps

A goal is the destination; objectives are the milestones; action steps are the specific moves. Break each goal into the objectives that mark progress toward it, and each objective into the concrete action steps — who does what, when, with what resource. Action steps must be specific enough that anyone reading the plan knows what is happening: "Client will attend the substance use intake at [agency] on [date]; worker will arrange transportation and confirm attendance" rather than "client will seek treatment." Assign responsibility for each step (client, worker, other provider) and a timeframe. Vague action steps ("work on housing") produce no action; specific ones (worker submits housing application by Friday, client gathers documents by Wednesday) produce movement. Document the objectives and action steps with responsible parties and timeframes.

### Sequence Interventions According to Dependency and Readiness

Order matters. Some interventions depend on others (you cannot keep a therapy appointment if you have no transportation or are homeless; you cannot address trauma before the immediate safety and stabilization are in place; you cannot parent effectively while in active addiction). Sequence the plan to respect these dependencies: stabilize safety and basic needs first, then address the conditions that undermine functioning, then the deeper work. Also sequence by readiness: a client in pre-contemplation about substance use will not engage with treatment, but may engage with harm reduction or with addressing a different need they identify. Pushing the right intervention at the wrong time fails; meeting the client where they are and building toward the next step succeeds. Document the sequencing rationale and adjust as readiness and circumstances change.

### Build on Strengths and Existing Capacities

A plan built only on deficits frames the client as a bundle of problems and ignores the capacities that will make change possible. Identify the client's strengths — coping skills, relationships, past successes, values, competencies — and build the plan to mobilize them. A client who has maintained employment through hardship has organizational capacity that can be applied to other goals; a client with a strong faith community has a support resource; a client who has left a violent relationship has already demonstrated enormous courage and problem-solving. Strengths are not decorative; they are the scaffolding of the plan. Document strengths as concretely as needs, and reference them in the action steps.

### Ensure the Plan Is Feasible: Services Must Exist and Be Accessible

A plan that refers the client to services that do not exist, have year-long waiting lists, are unaffordable, or are inaccessible (no transportation, no childcare, language barriers) is a plan that will fail. Before writing a service into the plan, verify it exists, accepts the client, has capacity, and is accessible; if it does not, either find an alternative, build the access supports (transportation, interpretation, advocacy for a slot), or adjust the goal. Infeasible plans are a leading cause of "non-compliance" that is actually system failure. The worker's role includes advocating to remove access barriers and, where services are genuinely unavailable, naming the gap and escalating it rather than writing a plan that cannot be executed. Document feasibility verification and the access supports arranged.

### Review, Measure, and Revise the Plan Regularly

A plan is a living document, reviewed at every meaningful contact and revised as circumstances, progress, and priorities change. At each review, measure progress against the specific objectives and action steps — what was done, what was achieved, what was not and why. Honest measurement, including of stagnation or regression, is essential; a plan that is never honestly measured drifts and provides cover for inaction. Revise the plan based on what is learned: adjust goals that prove unrealistic, intensify interventions that are not working, build on what is succeeding, and reprioritize as needs change. Document the reviews, the measurements, and the revisions, with the client's input. A plan that has not been revised in months is almost certainly stale.

## Common Traps

### The Worker-Imposed Plan

The worker, believing they know best, writes the plan according to their assessment of the client's needs, with the client's involvement limited to signing. The trap mechanism is that the worker's expertise produces a clinically sound plan, and the client's signature makes it appear agreed. The false signal is a signed, well-formulated plan. The harm is that the client does not experience the plan as their own, does not implement it between contacts, and the plan fails — not because it was wrong, but because it was not theirs. Plans built with the client, on the client's goals, are implemented; plans imposed on the client are not.

### The Overloaded Plan

The plan attempts to address every identified need simultaneously, producing a document with eight goals and forty action steps. The trap is that comprehensiveness feels thorough and responsible. The false signal is a complete-looking plan. The harm is that effort is diluted across too many fronts, the client is overwhelmed, nothing gets enough focus to change, and the plan becomes unmanageable to track. Ruthless prioritization of the few goals that matter most now is more effective than comprehensive coverage.

### Vague Goals That Cannot Be Measured

The plan lists goals like "improve coping" or "stabilize housing" without specifying what would constitute achievement. The trap is that vague goals feel achievable and avoid the difficulty of defining success. The false signal is a plan full of goals. The harm is that progress cannot be measured honestly, drift is invisible, and the plan provides cover for inaction because no one can tell whether anything is changing. SMART, behavioral, measurable goals are the defense.

### The Infeasible Referral

The plan refers the client to a service that does not exist, is full, or is inaccessible, and treats the referral as the intervention. The trap is that the referral feels like action and satisfies the plan requirement. The false signal is a service listed in the plan. The harm is that the client cannot access the service, the need goes unmet, and the client is often blamed for "non-compliance" when the real failure was the system's. Feasibility must be verified, and access barriers removed or the goal adjusted.

### Mis-Sequencing the Right Intervention at the Wrong Time

The plan prescribes an intervention the client is not ready for (treatment for a client in pre-contemplation, trauma work before stabilization, parenting classes during active addiction). The trap is that the intervention is clinically correct for the problem. The false signal is an evidence-based service in the plan. The harm is that the client does not engage, drops out, or is labeled resistant, and the underlying need goes unaddressed because the sequencing was wrong. Meeting the client's stage of readiness and building toward the next step is more effective than forcing the "right" intervention prematurely.

### The Plan That Is Never Reviewed or Measured

The plan is written, filed, and never revisited, so no one knows whether it is working. The trap is that the plan's existence feels like ongoing management. The false signal is a plan on file. The harm is that progress is not measured, drift and regression go unnoticed, and the plan becomes a stale document rather than a living tool. Regular review, honest measurement, and revision keep the plan effective.

## Self-Check

- Did I start with the client's own goals and build the plan around them, being transparent about any non-negotiable mandate requirements?
- Did I prioritize ruthlessly to a few focused goals, naming what is deferred and why, rather than attempting to address everything at once?
- Is each goal SMART — specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound — in behavioral, observable terms?
- Did I break each goal into objectives and specific action steps with responsible parties and timeframes, rather than vague intentions?
- Did I sequence interventions according to dependency and the client's readiness, rather than forcing the right intervention at the wrong time?
- Did I build the plan on the client's strengths and existing capacities, documenting them as concretely as the needs?
- Did I verify that every service in the plan exists, has capacity, and is accessible, arranging access supports or adjusting goals where it is not?
- Did I build in regular review, honest measurement against objectives, and revision, with the client's input, so the plan stays a living document?
