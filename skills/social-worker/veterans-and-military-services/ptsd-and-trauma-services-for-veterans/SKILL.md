# PTSD and Trauma Services for Veterans

Post-traumatic stress disorder in veterans is one of the most consequential conditions a social worker will encounter: it drives depression, substance use, relationship breakdown, employment loss, homelessness, and suicide, and it is frequently under-recognized, under-treated, or mistreated. The judgment problem is that PTSD in this population does not look like the civilian textbook image — veterans present with anger and emotional numbing as often as with fear and flashbacks, with stoic denial of symptoms, with self-medication that masks the underlying trauma, and with comorbid traumatic brain injury and chronic pain that complicate the picture. A worker who relies on a single screening score or a brief clinical impression will both over-diagnose normal combat stress responses and miss severe, chronic PTSD. The work requires validated assessment, knowledge of which treatments actually work (and which popular approaches do not), the ability to navigate a VA mental health system that is large and sometimes slow, and the recognition that PTSD is a family and social disease, not an individual one. Because PTSD substantially elevates suicide risk, every encounter must include a direct suicide screen, and any positive finding must trigger the crisis pathway described below. The standard is not that the veteran was screened or referred, but that the veteran was connected to an evidence-based treatment and supported to remain in it.

## Core Rules

### Assess With Validated Instruments and a Full Clinical Picture, Not a Score Alone

Use the PCL-5 (Posttraumatic Stress Disorder Checklist for DSM-5) as the standard screening instrument, and interpret it correctly: it requires a known index trauma to be meaningful, a cutoff score (commonly 31-33) suggests probable PTSD but is not a diagnosis, and symptom-cluster scores map onto the DSM-5 criteria of intrusion, avoidance, negative alterations in cognition and mood, and arousal and reactivity. Never treat a single PCL-5 score as a diagnosis or as the basis for a treatment plan; it is a screen that triggers a structured clinical assessment. Build the full clinical picture: the index trauma(s) — combat, military sexual trauma, training accidents, non-military traumas that predate or follow service — the current symptom profile, the duration and course, the functional impact on work, relationships, sleep, and safety, and the comorbidities that so often accompany PTSD in veterans. Distinguish PTSD from normal post-deployment readjustment, from acute stress disorder (within the first month), from grief and moral injury, and from TBI, because these have different treatments. Document the index trauma, the screen results, the clinical formulation, and the differential, because this record supports both treatment and any future disability claim.

### Know the Evidence-Based Treatments and Route Veterans to Them

The single most important clinical decision is connecting the veteran to a trauma-focused, evidence-based psychotherapy, because these treatments work and many veterans never receive them. The VA and Department of Defense endorse three first-line therapies: Cognitive Processing Therapy (CPT), a 12-session structured therapy that targets the stuck points (maladaptive beliefs about self, others, and the world) that maintain PTSD; Prolonged Exposure (PE), which uses imaginal and in-vivo exposure to process the trauma and reduce avoidance; and Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing (EMDR), which uses bilateral stimulation during trauma processing. These are the treatments with the strongest evidence, and the VA trains its clinicians in them and makes them widely available. Know the basics of each so you can explain them to a veteran who is frightened of "talking about it," can normalize the temporary worsening that often occurs early in treatment, and can match the veteran to a modality and clinician. Be honest that these therapies are hard work and that dropout is common, and build the support (scheduling, transportation, symptom management, encouragement) that helps the veteran complete a full course. Pharmacotherapy (particularly SSRIs and SNRIs) is an evidence-based adjunct, not a substitute for trauma therapy. Be aware of and cautious about widely marketed but less-supported approaches, and never let a veteran abandon an evidence-based treatment for an unproven one without a candid discussion.

### Navigate VA Mental Health Access and Eligibility Concretely

VA mental health care is the primary pathway for most veterans with PTSD, and navigating it is a core competency. Know that VA facilities are required to provide same-day access to mental health services, that PTSD treatment is available through VA medical centers, community-based outpatient clinics, and specialized PTSD programs (residential and intensive outpatient), and that Vet Centers offer readjustment counseling for combat veterans and their families outside the formal VA medical system. Understand eligibility: many veterans assume they are ineligible when they are not, and enrollment depends on service history, characterization of discharge, and priority group — but MST-related care and certain combat-related care are available even to veterans who would otherwise be barred. Know the community-care pathway (referral to a community provider when VA cannot meet access or specialty standards) and how to initiate it. Recognize the access barriers that defeat veterans: long waits in some regions, distance, stigma, distrust, and the complexity of a large bureaucracy — and counter them with warm handoffs, appointment confirmation, transportation support, and follow-up. For veterans who will not use the VA, maintain knowledge of community mental health, nonprofit veteran organizations, and sliding-scale providers.

### Address Comorbid Substance Use as a Rule, Not an Exception

Substance use disorders co-occur with veteran PTSD at very high rates, because alcohol and drugs are the most accessible form of self-medication for intrusion, hyperarousal, and sleep disturbance — and untreated substance use undermines trauma therapy, elevates suicide risk, and drives the legal, employment, and family consequences that destabilize the veteran. Screen routinely for alcohol and drug use (the AUDIT-C and DAST are standard), and do not treat PTSD and substance use as separate, sequential problems to be addressed one at a time; integrated treatment that addresses both concurrently is the evidence-based standard. Be alert to veteran-specific patterns: heavy drinking normalized within military and veteran peer culture, opioid use beginning with prescribed pain medication, and the interaction of substance use with firearms that amplifies suicide lethality. Refer to integrated dual-diagnosis treatment where available, and never discharge a veteran from PTSD treatment for substance use without an active plan to address both. Recognize that abstinence is not always the immediate or only goal, and that harm reduction and engagement are often the necessary first steps.

### Treat PTSD as a Family and Relationship Disease

PTSD does not stay inside the veteran; it reshapes intimate relationships, parenting, friendships, and employment. Partners of veterans with PTSD often develop their own secondary stress, depression, and burnout, and children in the home can develop behavioral and emotional problems. The symptoms that define PTSD — emotional numbing and withdrawal, hypervigilance and irritability, avoidance of crowds and intimacy, sleep disturbance and nightmares, and explosive anger — directly attack the intimacy, safety, and predictability that sustain families. Assess the relationship and family impact directly, involve partners and family with the veteran's consent, and connect them to VA family therapy, caregiver support programs, and community family services. Recognize the risk of intimate partner violence, which is elevated in this population, and have a clear pathway for safety assessment and intervention. Normalize the family's struggle as a predictable consequence of the illness rather than a personal failing, and build the family's capacity to support the veteran's treatment rather than undermine it.

### Build Peer Support as a Protective Layer

Peer support — from other veterans who have lived experience of PTSD and recovery — is one of the most powerful and underused resources in veteran mental health, because it addresses the isolation, the loss of unit cohesion, and the civilian-civilian communication gap that clinical care alone cannot. Peer support takes many forms: VA peer specialists (trained veterans employed in treatment teams), peer-led support groups, and veteran service organizations and nonprofits that offer community and purpose. For many veterans, the first willingness to engage comes from a peer, not a clinician. Connect veterans to peer resources early and repeatedly, and recognize that peer support is complementary to, not a substitute for, evidence-based clinical treatment. Be thoughtful about group composition and facilitation, because poorly run groups can reinforce avoidance, substance use, or hopelessness, and because MST survivors and combat veterans may have different needs that not every group meets.

### Screen for and Act on Suicide Risk at Every Encounter

Veterans with PTSD die by suicide at elevated rates, and the risk is amplified by comorbid depression, substance use, TBI, chronic pain, access to firearms, and the isolation that PTSD produces. Every PTSD encounter must include a direct suicide screen — ideation, plan, means (especially firearms), intent, timeframe, and prior attempts — and any positive finding must trigger the full risk response described in the crisis pathway below. Recognize that PTSD symptoms themselves can be warning signs: increasing isolation, giving away possessions, a sudden calm after agitation, and statements about being a burden. Act concretely on any risk: means restriction with verified firearm storage, a collaborative safety plan, a level-of-care decision that is executed (not merely recommended), and confirmed follow-up within a tight window. Do not let a calm, stoic veteran lower your threshold for action.

## Common Traps

### Diagnosing PTSD From a Single PCL-5 Score

The worker administers the PCL-5, sees a score above cutoff, records "PTSD," and builds a treatment plan on the number alone. The trap mechanism is that the score feels objective and efficient, so the structured clinical assessment is skipped. The false signal is a quantified, documented result. The harm is twofold: some veterans are labeled with PTSD when their symptoms reflect normal readjustment, grief, TBI, or a different condition, and receive the wrong treatment; others with real PTSD but a lower score (common in stoic under-reporters) are missed. The corrective is to use the PCL-5 as a screen that triggers a full clinical assessment, never as a standalone diagnosis.

### Letting the Veteran Avoid Trauma-Focused Therapy Because It Is Uncomfortable

The veteran is frightened of revisiting the trauma (a core PTSD symptom is avoidance), reports that talking about it "makes it worse," and asks for medication or supportive counseling instead, and the worker accommodates to preserve the alliance. The trap mechanism is that avoidance feels protective to the veteran and accommodating it feels kind to the worker. The false signal is a comfortable therapeutic relationship. The harm is that the veteran never receives the evidence-based treatment that would resolve the PTSD, symptoms persist or worsen, and years pass in supportive but ineffective care. The corrective is to normalize the discomfort, explain that temporary worsening is expected and resolves, build the support to complete treatment, and route to CPT, PE, or EMDR rather than indefinite supportive counseling.

### Treating Substance Use as a Prerequisite to PTSD Treatment

The worker identifies comorbid substance use and insists the veteran achieve sobriety before beginning trauma therapy, effectively gatekeeping the evidence-based treatment behind a condition the substance use is being used to manage. The trap mechanism is a sequential-treatment logic that sounds responsible but contradicts the evidence. The false signal is a clear, staged treatment plan. The harm is that the veteran never stabilizes the substance use (because the underlying PTSD is untreated), never reaches the gate, and never receives trauma therapy; the result is chronicity and elevated risk. The corrective is integrated, concurrent treatment of both conditions.

### Assuming VA Ineligibility and Never Checking

The veteran says "I'm not eligible for the VA" or "I have a bad discharge," and the worker accepts the statement and routes to overstretched community resources. The trap mechanism is that the worker trusts the veteran's (often mistaken) belief and avoids the bureaucracy. The false signal is a confident-sounding eligibility statement. The harm is that a veteran entitled to VA PTSD care — including MST and combat-related care available regardless of some discharges — never receives it. The corrective is to verify eligibility directly through VA enrollment, to know the exceptions, and to never accept an unverified eligibility assumption.

### Confusing PTSD With TBI or Moral Injury

The worker attributes all symptoms to PTSD when some reflect traumatic brain injury (concentration problems, irritability, headaches, dizziness from blast exposure) or moral injury (shame, loss of meaning, spiritual crisis from actions or failures during service), which have different treatments. The trap mechanism is that PTSD is the familiar diagnosis and the overlapping symptoms make it easy to stop there. The false signal is a coherent PTSD formulation. The harm is that TBI goes without neurological evaluation and rehabilitation, and moral injury goes without the meaning-making and often spiritual or community-based work it requires, while the PTSD treatment alone yields incomplete recovery. The corrective is to screen for and differentiate TBI and moral injury and to refer accordingly.

### Providing a Referral Instead of a Confirmed Connection

The worker gives the veteran a VA phone number or a referral and considers the navigation complete. The trap mechanism is that the referral feels like action and is easy to document. The false signal is a documented referral in the chart. The harm is that a symptomatic, avoidant, often depressed veteran does not make the call, the appointment is never made, and the treatment never begins; the window of willingness closes. The corrective is a warm handoff with the veteran present, appointment confirmation, and follow-up to verify the connection occurred.

## Self-Check

- Did I use the PCL-5 as a screen that triggered a full structured clinical assessment, including the index trauma, symptom clusters, functional impact, course, and differential — rather than diagnosing from a score alone?
- Did I route the veteran to an evidence-based trauma therapy (CPT, PE, or EMDR), explain what to expect including temporary worsening, and build the support to complete a full course?
- Did I screen for and plan concurrent, integrated treatment of comorbid substance use, rather than gating trauma therapy behind sobriety?
- Did I differentiate PTSD from TBI and moral injury and refer for neurological evaluation and meaning-making support where indicated?
- Did I navigate VA mental health with verified eligibility, a warm handoff, appointment confirmation, and follow-up — using Vet Centers and community care where appropriate — rather than a phone number?
- Did I assess the family and relationship impact, involve supports with consent, screen for intimate partner violence, and connect the family to caregiver and family services?
- Did I connect the veteran to peer support as a complementary layer, with attention to group fit for MST survivors and combat veterans?
- Did I screen directly for suicide risk at this encounter and, on any positive finding, execute the crisis pathway — means restriction (especially verified firearm storage), safety planning, executed level-of-care decision, and confirmed follow-up — plus 988 then 1 (Veterans Crisis Line), text 838255, or 911 for imminent danger?

## Crisis Escalation Reference

PTSD substantially elevates suicide risk, and any positive suicide screen requires immediate, concrete action consistent with the suicide-risk-response standard. Use these resources directly and document the action taken.

- **Veterans Crisis Line**: Dial **988**, then press **1**; or text **838255**; or chat at veteranscrisisline.net. Available 24/7, confidential, staffed by responders trained in military and veteran culture, and available to veterans not enrolled in VA care.
- **988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline**: Dial **988** for any person in suicidal crisis or emotional distress.
- **Emergency services**: Dial **911** (or local emergency number) for imminent danger, a weapon present, or a medical emergency. Do not leave the veteran unobserved while arranging transport.
- **Same-day VA mental health**: Connect the veteran to the local VA or a VA social worker for urgent same-day evaluation; VA facilities are required to provide same-day mental health access.
- **Means restriction**: Because firearms are the leading method of veteran suicide and are common in veteran homes, arrange concrete, verified firearm restriction (off-site temporary transfer, secure storage with a separate key/combination holder, or an extreme risk protection order where available) — not a general agreement to "be safe." Also restrict medication access where relevant.
- **Follow-up**: Arrange a specific, confirmed contact within 24-72 hours and provide the crisis line pathway, because the post-crisis window and the early phase of trauma therapy are both high-risk periods.
