# Wildfire And Extreme Heat Design

Wildfire and extreme heat are climate hazards that demand opposite but related envelope strategies: wildfire design hardens the building against ember, radiant, and flame exposure from outside, while extreme heat design manages the building's thermal balance so it remains habitable and efficient under sustained high temperatures. Both are growing in severity and geographic range, and both reward decisions made at the massing, orientation, and material stage that are expensive to change later. Agents often treat wildfire as a roofing and siding specification and extreme heat as an air conditioning load, and miss that both require integrated design — defensible space, ignition-resistant assemblies, ventilation that does not admit embers, and passive cooling that reduces dependence on mechanical systems that may fail during a heat event. The architect owns this integration because the building's resilience is a design problem before it is a specification problem. The goal is a building that resists ignition during a wildfire and remains safe and habitable during extreme heat, including when power is lost.

## Core Rules

### Determine The Wildfire Exposure And Applicable Code First

Begin by determining whether the site is in a wildland-urban interface fire area designated by the jurisdiction, which triggers the International Wildland-Urban Interface Code or its local equivalent and prescribes ignition-resistant construction based on exposure severity. Even where no code applies, assess the actual wildfire risk from vegetation, slope, wind patterns, and fire history, because code maps lag behind changing conditions. The wildfire exposure — ember, radiant heat, or direct flame — determines which assemblies and details must be hardened, and the severity level drives the construction class required. Document the exposure determination and its source, because the building's wildfire resilience must be defensible to insurers and future owners.

### Design Defensible Space As A Site And Landscape Integration

Defensible space — the managed area around the building where vegetation is controlled to slow fire spread — is the first line of wildfire defense, and it is a site design decision that couples with the landscape, the architecture, and the owner's maintenance capacity. Design the immediate zone around the building as non-combustible, with hardscape, low ground cover, and separation between plants; extend the managed zones outward per the code or best practice; and avoid placing combustible features like decks, fences, and outbuildings where they can carry fire to the building. Coordinate the landscape design with the architectural envelope, because a hardened building surrounded by unmanaged vegetation is not resilient. Plan for the owner's ability to maintain the defensible space over time, because a design that requires intensive upkeep will degrade.

### Harden The Envelope Against Ember, Radiant, And Flame Exposure

Embers are the primary cause of wildfire ignition, traveling far ahead of the flame front and entering buildings through vents, gaps, and openings, so ember-resistant ventilation is the single most important envelope detail. Specify ember-resistant vents or Vulcan Vents that resist ember entry, seal gaps around the roof, walls, and foundations, and design the roof-to-wall and wall-to-foundation intersections to resist ember accumulation. For radiant and flame exposure, specify ignition-resistant or non-combustible exterior materials — roofing, siding, soffits, decking, and windows — with ratings appropriate to the exposure severity. The envelope hardening must be comprehensive, because a single vulnerable assembly — a combustible deck, an unsealed vent, a single-pane window — can defeat an otherwise hardened building.

### Address Roof, Eave, And Opening Vulnerabilities Specifically

The roof is the most vulnerable surface because it accumulates embers and debris, so specify a non-combustible or Class A roof assembly, design the roof-to-wall intersections to prevent debris accumulation, and screen gutters or eliminate them where possible. Eaves and soffits are vulnerable to ember ignition and heat trapping, so specify enclosed, ignition-resistant eaves rather than open framing. Windows fail under radiant heat and are a direct path for embers into the interior, so specify tempered or multi-pane glass and consider shutters for severe exposures. Each of these vulnerabilities has a specific mitigation, and they must each be addressed rather than relying on a generic ignition-resistant specification.

### Design For Extreme Heat Through Passive And Passive-First Strategies

Extreme heat resilience begins with reducing the building's cooling load through passive strategies: orient the building to minimize east and west glazing, shade glazing with overhangs, fins, or landscape sized for the solar angles, and specify a high-performance envelope with insulation and thermal breaks that keep heat out. Design natural ventilation paths for periods when outdoor conditions allow, and use thermal mass strategically to absorb heat during the day and release it at night. Cool roofs and high-albedo surfaces reduce the heat the building absorbs, and they compound with shading and insulation. Passive strategies reduce dependence on mechanical cooling, which matters because extreme heat often coincides with power stress or outages.

### Provide Safe Habitability During Power Loss And Peak Heat

A building that depends entirely on mechanical cooling becomes uninhabitable when the power fails, and power failures are increasingly common during extreme heat events. Design for passive survivability — the ability to maintain safe indoor temperatures without mechanical cooling — through the envelope, thermal mass, and ventilation strategies above. Where critical, provide resilient power through solar with battery storage, generators, or microgrid connection, sized to maintain essential loads including cooling for vulnerable occupants. Identify the cooling refuge spaces within the building where occupants can gather during sustained heat, and ensure these spaces have the envelope, ventilation, and backup power to remain safe. The architect must design for the failure mode, not only the normal condition.

### Mitigate The Urban Heat Island At The Site And Building Scale

At the site scale, maximize tree canopy and permeable surfaces, because vegetation and evaporation cool the air, and minimize dark, impervious surfaces that absorb and re-radiate heat. At the building scale, use cool roofs, green roofs where structurally feasible, and high-albedo paving to reduce the heat the project contributes to its surroundings. Coordinate the landscape and hardscape with the building's cooling strategy, because a shaded, vegetated site reduces the building's cooling load and a cool roof reduces the heat island the building creates. The urban heat island is a cumulative effect, and each project either mitigates or worsens it through design choices the architect controls.

## Common Traps

### Treating Wildfire As A Siding And Roofing Specification Only

The team specifies non-combustible siding and a Class A roof and concludes the building is wildfire-resilient, while leaving standard vents that admit embers, a combustible deck attached to the building, and unmanaged vegetation at the foundation. The mechanism is that materials are the visible wildfire feature and ember and gap pathways are invisible, and the false signal is that the building looks hardened. The harm is that embers enter through the vents or the deck carries fire to the siding, and the building ignites despite its specified materials. Wildfire resilience requires comprehensive hardening of every assembly and pathway, with ember-resistant vents, sealed gaps, and defensible space, not just material ratings.

### Ignoring The Wildfire Code Because The Site Is Not On The Map

The site is just outside the designated wildland-urban interface area, so the team applies no wildfire measures, when the actual vegetation, slope, and fire history indicate real risk that the map has not yet captured. The mechanism is that code maps lag behind changing conditions and teams rely on the map as the risk assessment, and the false signal is that no code means no risk. The harm is that the building is built to standard construction in a high-risk location, ignites in the next fire, and the owner suffers a loss that design could have prevented. Wildfire risk must be assessed from site conditions regardless of the map, and ignition-resistant measures applied where the actual risk warrants.

### Designing Glazing And Orientation Without Heat In Mind

The team orients the building for views or street presence, glazes the west facade heavily for the sunset, and then sizes the mechanical cooling to handle the resulting load, only to find the building is expensive to cool and uninhabitable when the power fails during a heat event. The mechanism is that orientation and glazing are driven by market and aesthetic preferences and cooling is treated as a mechanical problem, and the false signal is that the HVAC will handle it. The harm is that the building is energy-intensive, expensive to operate, and unsafe during outages, with a cooling load that passive design could have eliminated. Orientation, glazing, and shading must be designed for thermal performance first, with mechanical cooling as a supplement, not a substitute.

### Relying On Mechanical Cooling Without Passive Survivability

The building is well-insulated and air-conditioned, and the team assumes it is heat-resilient, but when the power fails during a multi-day heat event the interior becomes unsafe because there is no passive cooling path. The mechanism is that mechanical cooling feels sufficient under normal conditions and passive survivability feels like an extra, and the false signal is that the building cools well today. The harm is that occupants — especially vulnerable ones — face unsafe indoor temperatures during the exact events the design should have addressed. The design must provide passive survivability through envelope, mass, and ventilation, with backup power for critical cooling where occupants cannot self-evacuate.

### Planting For Defensible Space At Installation But Ignoring Maintenance

The landscape is installed with proper defensible space, photographed for the permit, and then grows unchecked, so within a few years the vegetation carries fire to the building that the original design prevented. The mechanism is that defensible space requires ongoing maintenance and the design assumes the installation condition persists, and the false signal is that the landscape was designed correctly. The harm is that the building's wildfire resilience degrades over time and the owner is unaware until a fire exposes the gap. Defensible space must be designed for the owner's realistic maintenance capacity, with low-maintenance plant choices and a documented maintenance plan handed to the owner.

### Treating Cool Roofs As A Checkbox Without Heat Island Strategy

The team specifies a cool roof to earn a sustainability point and stops there, while the site is dominated by dark paving and the building contributes substantially to the urban heat island. The mechanism is that cool roofs are an easy single measure and a comprehensive heat island strategy feels like landscape scope, and the false signal is that the cool roof checks the resilience box. The harm is that the project's net heat contribution remains high, the microclimate around the building is hotter than necessary, and the cooling load the cool roof reduced is offset by the heated surroundings. Cool roofs must be part of an integrated heat island strategy that includes canopy, permeability, and high-albedo surfaces across the site.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has the wildfire exposure — ember, radiant, flame — and severity been determined from the code map and the actual site conditions, with the determination documented?
- [ ] Is defensible space designed as an integrated site and landscape strategy, with non-combustible immediate zones, managed outer zones, and a realistic maintenance plan?
- [ ] Are ember-resistant vents, sealed gaps, and ignition-resistant or non-combustible roofing, siding, eaves, decking, and windows specified comprehensively, with no vulnerable assembly left unaddressed?
- [ ] Is the building oriented and shaded to minimize east and west glazing and solar heat gain, with the cooling load reduced passively before mechanical sizing?
- [ ] Does the design provide passive survivability — safe indoor temperatures without mechanical cooling — through envelope, thermal mass, and natural ventilation?
- [ ] Is resilient power provided for critical cooling loads, and are cooling refuge spaces identified with the envelope and backup capacity to remain safe during outages?
- [ ] Does the project mitigate the urban heat island through canopy, permeable surfaces, cool roofs, and high-albedo paving as an integrated strategy?
- [ ] Has wildfire resilience been verified at the assembly and pathway level — every vent, gap, deck, and opening — rather than only at the material specification level?
