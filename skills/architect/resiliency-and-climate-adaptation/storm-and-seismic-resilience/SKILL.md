# Storm And Seismic Resilience

Storm and seismic resilience is the practice of designing a building to withstand extreme wind and ground motion events and, increasingly, to remain functional or quickly recoverable after them. The building code establishes life-safety minimums, but life safety is not the same as recoverability: a building that protects its occupants but must be demolished after a major event has failed an owner who needed continuity of operations. Agents often treat wind and seismic design as structural engineering scope and miss that the architectural plan — the massing, the openings, the diaphragm continuity, the nonstructural component layout — determines what the structural system can achieve, and that the resilience performance objective must be set as a design decision before engineering begins. The architect owns the integration of resilience into the building's conception, because the structural engineer can only optimize within the architectural framework the architect provides. The goal is a building whose performance in an extreme event matches the owner's actual need for safety, recovery, and continuity.

## Core Rules

### Set The Performance Objective Beyond Code Minimum

Begin by establishing, with the owner, what the building must do after a design event: protect life only, be repairable and re-occupiable within weeks, or remain fully operational throughout and after. The code delivers the first; the second and third require performance-based design and additional investment. The performance objective is a design decision driven by the building's function — a hospital must remain operational, a school should be re-occupiable quickly, a warehouse may accept life safety alone — and it must be set before the structural strategy is chosen. Document the objective and the design event, because the resilience of the building is measured against this target, not against a generic code compliance claim.

### Integrate The Seismic Force-Resisting System With The Architectural Plan

The seismic force-resisting system — shear walls, braced frames, moment frames, base isolation — must be coordinated with the architectural plan from the earliest studies, because its location and continuity drive the program, the leasable area, and the architectural expression. Shear walls demand continuous vertical alignment that constrains the plan; braced frames occupy bays that affect program; base isolation requires a horizontal separation plane that affects the foundation, the entry, and the utilities. The architect must not design a plan and then ask the engineer to fit the seismic system into it, because the result is either a compromised plan or a compromised seismic system. Coordinate the seismic strategy and the plan together, with the structural engineer, from the start.

### Maintain Diaphragm Continuity And Load Path Integrity

The lateral load path is only as strong as its weakest link, and the architectural plan controls many of those links: floor diaphragms must be continuous, openings in diaphragms must be limited and detailed, and the connection from the diaphragm to the vertical seismic system must be direct. Large floor openings for atria, stairs, and duct shafts interrupt the diaphragm and require engineering attention, and the architect must coordinate their size and location with the structural strategy. Vertical continuity of the seismic system — no discontinuous shear walls, no offsets in the lateral system — is an architectural decision, because the program drives whether walls can continue to the foundation. The architect must understand enough of the load path to protect its continuity through the design.

### Design For Wind And Hurricane Forces At The Envelope

Wind design is not only structural; it is an envelope and opening design problem. Roof shape, parapet configuration, and overhang geometry determine uplift forces and where wind-driven rain will penetrate. Openings — windows, doors, vents — must resist wind pressure and impact from wind-borne debris in hurricane and tornado zones, with impact-rated glazing or shutters specified per the code and the exposure. The continuous load path for wind — from roof to walls to foundation — must be maintained through connections that the architect details and the engineer verifies, including hold-downs, straps, and roof-to-wall connections. The envelope and the structure must be designed together for wind, because a structurally sound building with a failed envelope is uninhabitable.

### Address Tornado And Extreme Wind Shelters Where Required

In regions with tornado risk, the code may require storm shelters in schools, critical facilities, and large assembly occupancies, and these shelters must meet the extreme wind criteria of ICC 500, which are far more stringent than standard wind design. Storm shelters require dedicated structural systems, hardened envelope, protected openings, and independent ventilation and emergency systems, and they must be located and sized for the occupant load they serve. Even where not required, the architect should consider a refuge area for buildings housing vulnerable occupants. Identify the shelter requirement early, because it affects the plan, the structure, and the cost, and a shelter added late cannot achieve the required performance.

### Secure And Detail Nonstructural Components For Life Safety And Functionality

Nonstructural components — ceilings, partitions, mechanical equipment, light fixtures, piping, exterior cladding — cause the majority of earthquake injuries and most of the post-event functional loss, and their restraint and detailing is coordinated by the architect. Heavy equipment must be anchored, suspended ceilings and lights must be braced, tall partitions must be laterally supported, and cladding must accommodate drift without falling. For operational performance objectives, nonstructural systems must remain functional, which requires flexible connections to equipment, drift-compatible cladding systems, and braced sprinkler and piping. The architect must specify and coordinate nonstructural restraint because the structural engineer designs the skeleton but the architect owns the building the occupants actually experience.

### Plan For Post-Event Occupancy And Functional Recovery

Resilience includes what happens after the event: how the building will be inspected, repaired, and re-occupied. Design with redundant egress that survives partial damage, with accessible refuge areas for occupants who cannot evacuate, and with shutoffs, drains, and access for rapid inspection of the structural and mechanical systems. For operational facilities, plan for backup power, water, and communications that survive the event, and for the rapid restoration of the systems most critical to function. The architect must design for the recovery phase, because a building that survives the event but cannot be re-occupied has not met the resilience objective.

## Common Traps

### Confusing Code Life Safety With Operational Resilience

The team designs to code and claims the building is resilient, when the code's life-safety objective means the building may be badly damaged and require demolition after a design event, which fails an owner who needed continuity. The mechanism is that code compliance feels like the resilience standard and performance-based design feels like an upgrade, and the false signal is that the building meets code and so is safe. The harm is that the owner suffers a total loss they did not anticipate, because no one explained that life safety and recoverability are different objectives. The performance objective must be set explicitly with the owner, and where recoverability or operability is required, performance-based design must be pursued beyond code minimums.

### Designing The Plan And Forcing The Seismic System To Fit

The architect develops a plan with discontinuous walls, offset cores, and large floor openings, then hands it to the structural engineer to fit a seismic system, producing either a compromised plan redesign or a seismic system with discontinuities that concentrate damage. The mechanism is that seismic systems are seen as engineering scope and the plan is seen as architecture, and the false signal is that the disciplines can work in sequence. The harm is that the seismic performance suffers, the structure becomes expensive or impossible, and the plan must be reworked late. The seismic strategy and the plan must be developed together from the start, with the structural engineer engaged in schematic design.

### Interrupting The Diaphragm With Uncoordinated Openings

Large atria, dramatic stair openings, and extensive duct shafts interrupt the floor diaphragm, and the team designs them for architectural effect without coordinating their size, location, or detailing with the lateral system, weakening the load path. The mechanism is that openings are architectural features and diaphragm continuity is invisible, and the false signal is that the floor plate is large enough to handle it. The harm is that the diaphragm is compromised, the lateral system cannot deliver forces as intended, and the structural engineer must add collectors, drag struts, or reinforcing that disrupt the architecture. Diaphragm openings must be coordinated with the lateral strategy, sized conservatively, and detailed for force transfer.

### Specifying Openings And Cladding Without Wind And Debris Considerations

The team specifies large glazing assemblies for views and daylight in a hurricane zone without impact rating, or designs roof overhangs and parapets that amplify wind uplift, and the envelope fails in the first major storm. The mechanism is that openings and envelope forms are driven by aesthetics and wind performance is treated as structural scope, and the false signal is that the glazing and roof look standard and so are acceptable. The harm is that the envelope breaches, wind-driven rain destroys the interior, and the building is uninhabitable despite structural survival. Openings, glazing, and roof forms must be designed for the wind and debris exposure, with impact-rated assemblies and shutters where required.

### Ignoring Nonstructural Restraint As A Detail Item

The structural skeleton is well designed, but the ceilings, lights, piping, and equipment are not braced, so the building suffers injuries and functional loss in a moderate earthquake from falling and swinging components that the structure itself survived. The mechanism is that nonstructural restraint is invisible in the design and feels like contractor scope, and the false signal is that the structure passed review. The harm is that occupants are injured by falling components, sprinkler breaks flood the building, and operational facilities cannot function despite structural survival. Nonstructural restraint must be specified and coordinated by the architect, with details and specifications that the contractor can execute.

### Forgetting The Recovery Phase In The Design

The building is designed to survive the event but with no thought to inspection, repair, or re-occupancy, so after the event the owner cannot determine whether the building is safe, cannot access critical systems, and faces months of uncertainty. The mechanism is that the event itself is the design focus and recovery is treated as someone else's problem, and the false signal is that survival equals resilience. The harm is that the building sits unusable for far longer than necessary, the owner's continuity is lost, and the resilience investment yields no operational benefit. The design must include access for inspection, shutoffs, redundant systems, and a recovery plan coordinated with the owner's operations.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has the performance objective — life safety, re-occupancy, or full operability — been set explicitly with the owner, with the design event documented?
- [ ] Is the seismic force-resisting system integrated with the architectural plan from schematic design, with vertical continuity and coordinated core and wall locations?
- [ ] Are floor diaphragm openings — atria, stairs, shafts — coordinated with the lateral system, sized conservatively, and detailed for force transfer?
- [ ] Does the envelope design — roof form, parapets, overhangs, glazing — respond to wind uplift and debris impact, with impact-rated assemblies or shutters where required?
- [ ] Have storm shelters or refuge areas been provided where required by code or warranted by occupant vulnerability, designed to ICC 500 or equivalent?
- [ ] Are nonstructural components — ceilings, lights, equipment, piping, cladding — specified and detailed for restraint and drift compatibility, coordinated by the architect?
- [ ] Does the design support post-event inspection, repair, and re-occupancy, with redundant egress, refuge areas, shutoffs, and access to critical systems?
- [ ] For operational facilities, are backup power, water, and communications sized to maintain function through and after the design event?
