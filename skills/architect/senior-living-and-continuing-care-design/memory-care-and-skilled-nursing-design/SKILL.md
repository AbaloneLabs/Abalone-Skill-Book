# Memory Care and Skilled Nursing Design

Memory care and skilled nursing are the highest-acuity environments in a senior living continuum, serving residents with advanced dementia (memory care) or complex medical and personal care needs (skilled nursing), and the design must serve three demanding masters simultaneously: the resident whose cognition or health is severely impaired, the staff who deliver intensive care, and the regulatory and safety regime that governs these settings. The judgment problem is that these environments are deeply prone to institutionalization, because the care, the safety, and the infection control pressures all push toward a clinical, secure, and controlled setting, yet the evidence is clear that a homelike, understandable, and dignified environment improves the resident's function, reduces the agitation and the behaviors, and supports the staff. A memory care unit with a dead-end corridor produces agitation and exit-seeking; a skilled nursing room too small for a hoist transfer injures the staff; an infection-control failure in a shared room outbreaks the whole unit. This skill covers the decisions that determine whether a memory care or skilled nursing unit works for the high-acuity resident, including the secure household model, the resident room for care and transfer, the staff workflow and the care delivery, the infection control, and the non-institutional homelike character.

## Core Rules

### Organize the Unit as Small Households or Neighborhoods, Not as a Large Ward

Memory care and skilled nursing units must be organized as small households or neighborhoods (typically 12 to 16 residents per household), not as large wards, because the large ward overwhelms the dementia resident with the stimulation and the navigation, and it degrades the care by spreading the staff over too many residents. The designer must divide the unit into the households with their own living, dining, and kitchen area, must size the household for the small group (12 to 16 residents), and must verify the household supports the staff assignment and the resident familiarity. The tradeoff is that the small household requires more support space per resident, but it reduces the agitation, the behaviors, and the staff burden; the resolution is to treat the household as the care model, not as an area penalty.

### Design the Memory Care Circulation as a Continuous Loop, With No Dead-Ends

The memory care circulation must be a continuous loop (a circular or a figure-eight path), with no dead-end corridors, because the dementia resident has a strong drive to walk (the wandering) and the dead-end is a source of the agitation, the frustration, and the exit-seeking, while the loop allows the safe, continuous walking that calms the resident. The designer must design the household circulation as a loop that returns to the common area, must eliminate the dead-end corridors and the cul-de-sacs, and must disguise the exit doors (the mural, the camouflaged door, the door at the staff zone) to reduce the exit-seeking without compromising the egress. The trap is a dead-end corridor that traps the agitated resident and triggers the behavior.

### Size the Skilled Nursing Room for the Hoist Transfer, the Caregiving, and the Infection Control

The skilled nursing resident room must be sized for the hoist transfer (the ceiling lift, the space to maneuver the lift around the bed and to the bathroom), the multi-person caregiving (the space for two or more staff), and the infection control (the single-occupancy or the spatial separation, the handwashing, the soiled utility), because the skilled nursing care requires the equipment and the staff that a standard room cannot accommodate. The designer must provide the ceiling lift track and the clear floor area for the hoist, must size the room for the two-staff caregiving and the family visit, must provide the single-occupancy rooms or the adequate separation for the infection control, and must locate the handwashing and the soiled utility for the staff. The trap is a room sized for the bed only, which injures the staff and fails the infection control.

### Plan the Staff Workflow and the Care Delivery for Efficiency and Observation

The staff workflow (the medication, the documentation, the care rounding, the supply access) must be planned for the efficiency and the observation, because the staff are the scarcest resource and the care quality depends on the staff's ability to see the residents, deliver the care, and document without excessive walking. The designer must locate the staff station or the decentralized charting for the observation of the residents, must provide the medication and the supply storage distributed to the point of care, and must verify the staff walking distance is minimized. The trap is a central nurse station that removes the staff from the residents and produces the long walking and the poor observation.

### Balance the Infection Control With the Homelike Character, Not at Its Expense

The infection control (the handwashing, the soiled utility, the isolation, the cleanable surfaces) is essential in the skilled nursing and the memory care, but it must be balanced with the homelike character, because the pure infection-control design produces a clinical, institutional environment that degrades the resident well-being and the staff satisfaction. The designer must provide the infection-control infrastructure (the handwashing at the point of care, the soiled utility, the isolation capacity) using the residential-scaled and the cleanable materials, must avoid the clinical details (the hospital handrails, the sterile colors) where the residential alternatives meet the standard, and must verify the infection control does not dominate the residential character. The tradeoff is that the cleanable materials may be less warm, but the residential-scaled detailing preserves the character; the resolution is the integrated design.

## Common Traps

### A Dead-End Corridor That Traps the Agitated Dementia Resident

The memory care household has a dead-end corridor (a bedroom wing, a bathroom spur) because the layout was efficient. The mechanism of the trap is that the dementia resident has a strong drive to walk, and when they reach a dead-end, they cannot figure out how to turn around, so they become agitated, frustrated, and may exhibit the behavior (the yelling, the striking) or the exit-seeking at the dead-end, and the staff must intervene, which escalates the situation and consumes the staff time. The false signal is the layout efficiency, which suggests a good plan but masks the circulation failure. The harm is resident agitation, behaviors, and staff burden. The defense is to design the circulation as a continuous loop with no dead-ends, and to disguise the exit doors.

### A Skilled Nursing Room Too Small for the Hoist Transfer

The skilled nursing room is sized for the bed and the basic circulation, like a small bedroom. The mechanism of the trap is that the skilled nursing care requires the hoist transfer (the ceiling lift or the floor lift) to move the resident from the bed to the bathroom and the chair, and a room sized for the bed only has no space to maneuver the lift around the bed and to the bathroom, so the staff are forced to do the manual transfer, which injures the staff backs (the leading injury in skilled nursing) and risks dropping the resident, and the care quality and the staff retention suffer. The false signal is the room area, which suggests a bedroom but masks the care space failure. The harm is staff injury, resident fall risk, and poor care. The defense is to size the room for the hoist transfer, provide the ceiling lift track, and verify the clear floor area for the lift maneuver.

### A Central Nurse Station That Removes the Staff From the Residents

The skilled nursing unit has a central nurse station where all the staff congregate and document. The mechanism of the trap is that the central nurse station removes the staff from the residents, so the staff cannot observe the residents (the fall, the call, the distress) from the station, and the staff must walk back and forth between the station and the residents, which wastes the time, delays the response, and produces the long walking that fatigues the staff and degrades the care, and the residents feel unobserved and unsupported. The false signal is the staff efficiency, which suggests a centralized workflow but masks the observation and the walking failure. The harm is poor observation, delayed response, staff fatigue, and degraded care. The defense is to decentralize the charting and the medication to the point of care, and to locate the staff for the resident observation.

### An Infection-Control Design That Produces a Clinical, Institutional Environment

The skilled nursing unit is designed with the pure infection-control approach (the hospital handrails, the sterile colors, the clinical materials, the exposed conduit) to maximize the cleanability. The mechanism of the trap is that the clinical, institutional environment degrades the resident's well-being (the agitation, the depression, the slower recovery) and the staff satisfaction (the burnout, the turnover), because the evidence shows that the homelike environment improves the function and the outcomes, and the pure infection-control design sacrifices the homelike character for the cleanability, producing a setting that is clean but harmful. The false signal is the cleanability, which suggests safety but masks the well-being failure. The harm is degraded resident well-being, staff burnout, and poorer outcomes. The defense is to balance the infection control with the homelike character, using the residential-scaled and cleanable materials and the integrated details.

### A Large Ward Layout That Overwhelms the Dementia Resident

The memory care or the skilled nursing unit is organized as a large ward (30 or 40 residents) with a central living and dining area, to gain the staffing efficiency. The mechanism of the trap is that the large ward overwhelms the dementia resident with the stimulation (the noise, the people, the navigation) and the staff are spread over too many residents, so the resident becomes agitated and disoriented in the large group, the staff cannot build the familiarity and the relationships, and the care quality and the behaviors deteriorate, which increases the staff burden rather than reducing it. The false signal is the staffing efficiency, which suggests a lean operation but masks the care and the behavior failure. The harm is resident agitation, behaviors, and degraded care. The defense is to organize the unit as small households of 12 to 16 residents with their own living and dining.

## Self-Check

- Did I organize the unit as small households or neighborhoods of 12 to 16 residents, each with its own living, dining, and kitchen area?
- Did I design the memory care circulation as a continuous loop with no dead-end corridors or cul-de-sacs, and did I disguise the exit doors to reduce the exit-seeking?
- Did I size the skilled nursing room for the hoist transfer (the ceiling lift track and the clear floor area), the two-staff caregiving, and the family visit?
- Did I provide the single-occupancy rooms or the adequate separation for the infection control, with the handwashing and the soiled utility at the point of care?
- Did I decentralize the charting and the medication to the point of care, locate the staff for the resident observation, and minimize the staff walking?
- Did I balance the infection control with the homelike character, using the residential-scaled and cleanable materials and the integrated details?
- Did I verify no dead-end corridor exists in the memory care household, and that the loop returns the resident to the common area?
