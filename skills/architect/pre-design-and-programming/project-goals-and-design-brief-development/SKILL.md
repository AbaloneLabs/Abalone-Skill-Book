# Project Goals And Design Brief Development

The design brief is the document that converts a client's scattered aspirations, operational needs, and constraints into a coherent intent that design can serve. It is not a wish list and not a program; it is the statement of what success means, against which every later design decision is tested. Agents often treat brief-writing as recording what the client says, but the value lies in what the client has not yet said, the unstated assumptions, the conflicting priorities among stakeholders, and the success criteria that no one has articulated. A brief that captures only the spoken requests produces a building that meets the letter of every demand and fails the spirit of the project, because the organizing intent was never defined.

Use this skill when developing a design brief, defining project goals, or reconciling stakeholder vision. The goal is a brief that is specific enough to guide design, stable enough to anchor decisions, and honest enough to surface the tradeoffs the project must resolve.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Vision, Goals, Objectives, And Success Criteria

These four layers are often collapsed into a single list of aspirations, which makes the brief untestable. Each layer does different work, and conflating them destroys the brief's ability to guide decisions.

Define each layer distinctly:

- vision: the overarching ambition, qualitative and aspirational, the change the project intends to create;
- goals: the broad priorities the project must advance, such as connectivity, resilience, or identity, stated as directions rather than metrics;
- objectives: specific, testable outcomes tied to each goal, such as a target energy performance or a desired adjacencies pattern;
- success criteria: the measurable or observable conditions that determine whether the objective is met, agreed before design begins so that success is not redefined after the fact.

Without success criteria, every design review becomes a re-litigation of intent, because no one can say whether the design is succeeding.

### Capture Stakeholder Aspiration Through Structured Elicitation, Not Transcription

Clients rarely state their real needs cleanly on first asking. The brief-writer's job is to elicit, not transcribe, because the spoken request often masks the underlying need, and the loudest stakeholder often does not represent the organization's true priorities.

Elicit by:

- conducting individual interviews with each stakeholder group, capturing their priorities, concerns, and definitions of success before group dynamics suppress dissent;
- using structured workshops to surface and resolve conflicts between stakeholders, rather than allowing conflicts to remain hidden until design;
- probing the "why" behind each stated requirement, because a request for more space often masks a need for different relationships, not more area;
- documenting dissent explicitly, so that a decision to override a stakeholder priority is recorded as a choice, not an oversight;
- distinguishing the client's decision-makers from its advisors and end-users, and weighting input accordingly.

### Make The Brief Specific Enough To Guide And Constrain

A brief composed of generic aspirations, to be welcoming, flexible, sustainable, and timeless, provides no guidance because it cannot distinguish one design response from another. Specificity is what makes the brief useful.

Build specificity by:

- defining what each aspiration means operationally in this project, not in the abstract;
- stating the priorities among competing values when they conflict, because a brief that ranks everything equally ranks nothing;
- articulating the non-negotiables, the constraints that cannot be traded, separately from the desires;
- connecting each qualitative goal to the quantitative program and performance targets, so that aspiration and measurement reinforce each other;
- providing examples or precedents that illustrate the intended quality, recognizing that words like "warm" or "civic" mean different things to different people.

### Reconcile Competing Priorities Into A Coherent Hierarchy

Every project contains competing priorities: cost versus quality, speed versus completeness, flexibility versus specificity, openness versus security. A brief that lists all of these as goals guarantees conflict during design, because every decision will favor one and violate another. The brief must establish a hierarchy.

Reconcile by:

- forcing the client to rank priorities explicitly, even when the ranking is uncomfortable;
- documenting the tradeoffs the client has accepted, so that later pressure to reverse them can be tested against the recorded decision;
- identifying the pairs of values most likely to conflict in design, and pre-deciding which prevails in the typical case;
- recognizing that some priorities are contextual, prevailing in some situations and yielding in others, and defining those contexts.

### Anchor The Brief In Organizational Strategy And Operational Reality

A building serves an organization that has a strategy, a culture, and an operational model. A brief that ignores these produces architecture that is internally coherent but organizationally irrelevant. The brief must connect design intent to how the organization actually works and intends to evolve.

Anchor by:

- understanding the organization's strategic plan, growth trajectory, and the role this facility plays in it;
- documenting the operational model the building must support, including work patterns, service delivery, and public interface;
- capturing cultural values that the building must express or support, recognizing that architecture communicates identity;
- testing whether the brief assumes the organization will change in ways it has not committed to, and flagging those assumptions.

### Manage Brief Evolution Without Losing Stability

Briefs evolve as understanding deepens, but a brief that shifts with every conversation cannot anchor decisions. The discipline is to distinguish genuine refinement from scope drift, and to manage changes deliberately.

Manage evolution by:

- versioning the brief and recording what changed, why, and who approved it;
- distinguishing brief refinements that reflect better understanding from changes that reflect new demands, which carry cost and schedule consequences;
- establishing a point at which the brief is baselined for each design phase, so that phase deliverables are measured against a defined intent;
- requiring formal approval of the brief by the client's decision-makers, because an unapproved brief has no authority to constrain design.

## Common Traps

### The Generic Aspiration Brief

When the brief consists of universally desirable qualities, to be inspiring, sustainable, flexible, and timeless, it provides no basis for distinguishing one design response from another, because no project aspires to be uninspiring, unsustainable, rigid, or dated. The trap is that such a brief feels safe and complete, offending no stakeholder, while actually being empty of guidance. Every design decision then becomes a negotiation, because the brief offers no hierarchy to resolve conflicts, and the project drifts toward the preferences of whoever holds the most influence at each review. The harm is a building that satisfies no coherent intent because none was ever established; the corrective is to force specificity and ranking, accepting that a useful brief will name priorities and therefore disappoint someone.

### Capturing The Loudest Voice As The Client's Intent

In stakeholder engagement, the most senior, most articulate, or most persistent participant often dominates, and the brief records their priorities as the project's. The trap is invisible because the input was gathered through a legitimate process, yet it may not represent the organization's actual needs or the end-users' reality. When the building is completed and the dominant stakeholder moves on, the misalignment surfaces in operational friction, underused spaces, and spaces that fail the people they were meant to serve. Structured elicitation that separates decision-makers from advisors and end-users, documents dissent, and probes the need behind the stated request is what prevents a brief that reflects one voice mistaken for the organization's intent.

### Success Criteria Defined After The Design Is Fixed

When success criteria are established only at the end of design or during construction, they inevitably describe what the design already delivers, because no one wants to declare failure. The trap is that the criteria appear to validate the project while actually having been reverse-engineered from it, providing no genuine accountability. The harm is that the organization cannot learn whether its investment achieved its intent, because the intent was never measured against an independent standard. Success criteria must be defined before design begins, tied to the goals and objectives, and measurable, so that post-occupancy evaluation can honestly assess performance and feed lessons into the next project.

### The Brief That Ignores Operational Reality

A brief that articulates a beautiful vision but never connects to how the organization actually operates produces architecture that the client cannot inhabit as intended. Work patterns assumed in the brief may not match how staff actually collaborate; service delivery models may require adjacencies the brief never specified; the public interface may demand a configuration the design does not provide. The trap is that the operational disconnect is invisible during design, when everyone is focused on the building's qualities, and surfaces only at occupancy, when the staff cannot do their work. The corrective is to ground the brief in documented operational reality, to involve the people who run the organization, and to test design responses against operational scenarios before they are fixed.

### Unmanaged Brief Drift

As a project progresses, every new stakeholder, every site discovery, and every budget pressure generates pressure to add to or change the brief. When these changes accumulate without discipline, the brief loses coherence, contradictions multiply, and the design team cannot tell which intent governs. The trap is that each individual change feels reasonable, so no one notices the accumulation until the project is over budget, behind schedule, and internally inconsistent. The harm is a project that satisfies no one because it tried to satisfy everyone, with scope and cost growing silently. Version control, baselining at each phase, formal approval of changes, and a clear distinction between refinement and new demand are what keep the brief stable enough to anchor decisions.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Does the brief distinguish vision, goals, objectives, and measurable success criteria as separate layers?
- [ ] Were success criteria defined before design began, tied to goals, and independently measurable rather than reverse-engineered from the design?
- [ ] Was stakeholder input gathered through structured elicitation that separated decision-makers, advisors, and end-users, with dissent documented?
- [ ] Does the brief establish an explicit hierarchy of priorities to resolve the tradeoffs most likely to arise in design?
- [ ] Are qualitative aspirations defined operationally for this project, with precedents or examples illustrating the intended quality?
- [ ] Is the brief anchored in the organization's documented strategy, operational model, and cultural values?
- [ ] Are non-negotiable constraints distinguished from desires, and are the tradeoffs the client accepted recorded?
- [ ] Is the brief version-controlled, baselined per phase, and formally approved by the client's decision-makers?
- [ ] Are brief changes distinguished as refinements versus new demands, with cost and schedule consequences identified for the latter?
- [ ] Does the brief flag assumptions about how the organization will change, rather than silently embedding them?
