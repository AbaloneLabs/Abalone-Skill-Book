# Feasibility And Site Analysis

Feasibility and site analysis is the work of determining, before design commits, whether a site can actually support the project the client intends. It is where optimism meets reality: zoning envelopes, environmental constraints, geotechnical conditions, utility capacity, access, and title burdens each have the power to render a site uneconomic or a program undeliverable. Agents often treat site analysis as a visual exercise, producing attractive context diagrams, while the constraints that actually determine feasibility, the legal, subsurface, and infrastructural ones, remain unverified. A feasibility conclusion built on unconfirmed assumptions is not analysis; it is a gamble presented as professional advice, and the losses land on the client in the form of a site that cannot be built as promised.

Use this skill when evaluating a site, conducting due diligence, or assessing whether a program fits. The goal is a feasibility judgment grounded in verified constraints, with risks quantified and unknowns explicitly flagged rather than papered over.

## Core Rules

### Establish The Zoning And Regulatory Envelope First

The single most consequential site constraint is what the law permits to be built. Before any design testing, the buildable envelope must be defined by the applicable zoning, overlay districts, and land-use regulations, because every later feasibility test happens inside or outside that envelope.

Establish the envelope by:

- confirming the zoning district, permitted uses, and whether the intended use is permitted by right, conditionally, or prohibited;
- quantifying the dimensional controls: setbacks, height limits, floor area ratio, lot coverage, and density;
- identifying overlay districts and additional authorities: historic, coastal, floodplain, airport, environmental, and design review;
- determining whether variances, special permits, or rezoning are required, and the realistic probability and timeline of obtaining them;
- checking subdivision, lot merger, and easement constraints that affect the developable area.

Treat the zoning conclusion as a deliverable that must be defensible. If the conclusion depends on an interpretation, state the interpretation, who would make it, and the risk if they decide differently.

### Verify Subsurface And Environmental Conditions Before Committing

What is underground determines whether the building can stand, what it will cost to found, and whether contamination or groundwater will derail the project. These conditions are invisible from the surface and cannot be assumed; they must be investigated.

Verify by:

- commissioning a geotechnical investigation with borings or test pits sufficient for the building type and site variability, and reading the report for bearing capacity, settlement, groundwater, and expansive or unstable soils;
- coordinating a Phase I Environmental Site Assessment, and a Phase II if the Phase I identifies recognized environmental conditions;
- identifying high water table, wetlands, floodplain, karst, or seismic site class implications;
- translating subsurface findings into cost and schedule consequences, because poor soils mean expensive foundations and contaminated material means costly disposal or remediation.

The architect's role is not to perform these investigations but to ensure they are commissioned, to read them critically, and to defer to geotechnical and environmental specialists on their interpretation.

### Confirm Utility Capacity, Access, And Infrastructure

A site that cannot be served by water, sewer, power, gas, telecommunications, and fire protection access is not feasible regardless of its zoning or area. Utility and access constraints are routinely underestimated because the mains appear to be nearby.

Confirm by:

- requesting will-serve letters or capacity confirmations from each utility provider, not assuming capacity from the presence of adjacent mains;
- verifying water flow and pressure for fire suppression, which can require upsized mains or a tank and pump;
- confirming sanitary sewer capacity and invert elevations, recognizing that gravity flow may require a pump station;
- assessing electrical capacity and the cost and timeline of a substation upgrade if the load exceeds available capacity;
- evaluating vehicular access, sight lines, turning movements, and whether the access permits required from the highway authority are obtainable.

### Assess Contextual, Solar, And Environmental Fit

Beyond legal and infrastructural feasibility, the site's physical context shapes what kind of building will succeed. Orientation, topography, views, microclimate, neighboring uses, and noise each constrain or enable design moves that affect long-term performance and occupant experience.

Assess by:

- documenting solar orientation, shading from adjacent structures and topography, and prevailing winds, because these drive energy, daylighting, and outdoor comfort;
- analyzing topography and drainage, identifying where cut and fill, retaining walls, or stormwater management will be required;
- evaluating neighboring uses for compatibility, nuisance (noise, odor, vibration), and the privacy and security implications of adjacencies;
- identifying view corridors, landmark relationships, and the site's role in the public realm.

### Coordinate Survey And Title Information

The site's legal description, boundaries, easements, encumbrances, and existing conditions must be established by a professional survey and title commitment. Designing against an assumed property line or an unverified easement produces a building that encroaches or cannot be permitted.

Coordinate by:

- commissioning an ALTA or boundary survey that establishes property lines, easements, encroachments, and existing conditions;
- reviewing the title commitment for restrictive covenants, deed restrictions, and liens that limit use or transfer;
- confirming the legal access, because landlocked or access-restricted parcels fail feasibility regardless of their area;
- identifying any riparian, littoral, or mineral rights issues relevant to the jurisdiction.

### Distinguish Verified Facts From Assumptions And Flag Unknowns

A feasibility study's value lies in its honesty about what is known, what is assumed, and what remains unknown. Conflating these categories converts a risk assessment into a sales document.

Maintain discipline by:

- separating each finding into verified (documented), assumed (reasonable but unconfirmed), and unknown (requires investigation);
- assigning a cost and schedule consequence to each material assumption and unknown;
- recommending the next investigations required to convert unknowns into facts before the client commits capital;
- stating explicitly what could make the site infeasible that has not yet been checked.

## Common Traps

### The Attractive Context Diagram Mistaken For Analysis

When the deliverable is a set of polished diagrams showing site photos, sun paths, and neighborhood context, the feasibility conclusion acquires an unearned aura of rigor. The trap is that the visually compelling work feels complete while the constraints that actually determine feasibility, zoning entitlements, subsurface conditions, and utility capacity, remain unverified. The client proceeds on the strength of the presentation, commits to the site, and discovers only during design development that the floor area ratio caps the program at sixty percent of what was assumed, or that the soils require piling that breaks the budget. The harm is a capital commitment made against analysis that looked thorough and was in fact superficial; the corrective is to treat the regulatory and infrastructural verification as the primary deliverable and the contextual diagrams as supporting material.

### Assuming Capacity From Adjacent Infrastructure

The presence of a water main, sewer line, or electrical service at the property line creates a strong but false signal that the site can be served. In reality, the main may be at capacity, the sewer invert may be too shallow for gravity drainage, the electrical service may require a substation upgrade with a multi-year timeline, or the fire flow may be insufficient without upsized infrastructure paid for by the project. The trap is invisible until the utility provider's will-serve letter or capacity study arrives, by which point the client may have closed on the land. The harm is a site that requires unbudgeted infrastructure costs or a delayed schedule that destroys the project's economics. Utility capacity must be confirmed in writing from the provider, never inferred from the existence of adjacent mains.

### Zoning Interpretation Presented As Settled Law

When a feasibility conclusion depends on a favorable interpretation of an ambiguous zoning provision, whether a use is permitted, how a height limit is measured, or whether a lot merger is allowed, presenting that interpretation as a settled fact conceals the real risk. The trap is that the interpretation feels reasonable to the architect, so it is reported as a finding rather than a question pending the authority's ruling. If the municipality interprets the provision differently, the entire project envelope can collapse, and the client has made decisions on a false basis. Ambiguous interpretations must be identified as such, with the ruling authority named, the alternative interpretation stated, and the consequence of an adverse ruling quantified, so the client understands the exposure before committing.

### Ignoring Subsurface Risk Until Design Development

Geotechnical and environmental conditions are the feasibility factors most often deferred, because they require spending money on investigation before the project is certain to proceed. The trap is that the deferral feels prudent, conserving cash during due diligence, while it actually transfers a large, late-discovered risk onto the design phase. When poor bearing soils, contamination, or groundwater are discovered at design development, the foundations must be redesigned, the budget must absorb the cost, and the schedule extends, often after the client has committed irrevocably. The corrective is to commission sufficient geotechnical and environmental investigation during feasibility to characterize the risk, treating the investigation cost as insurance against a far larger downstream loss, and deferring to the specialists on what the findings mean.

### Easement And Title Discoveries Late In Design

Easements for utilities, access, drainage, or neighboring properties, and restrictive covenants running with the land, can render portions of the site unbuildable or prohibit the intended use entirely. The trap is that these burdens do not appear on a visual site visit and are often not reviewed until permitting, because the team assumes the title is clean. When an easement is discovered bisecting the building footprint, or a covenant prohibits the use, the design must be reworked or the project abandoned at a stage where the sunk cost is substantial. A current survey and title review must be completed and read during feasibility, with every easement and restriction plotted on the site plan, so the buildable area reflects the legal reality rather than the apparent one.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has the buildable envelope been established from verified zoning, including setbacks, height, FAR, coverage, and all overlay districts?
- [ ] Are required variances, special permits, or rezoning identified, with the ruling authority, timeline, and probability of success stated?
- [ ] Has a geotechnical investigation been commissioned and read, with foundation, groundwater, and soil implications translated into cost and schedule?
- [ ] Has a Phase I (and Phase II if needed) Environmental Site Assessment been completed, with remediation cost implications identified?
- [ ] Are utility capacities confirmed in writing by each provider, including fire flow, sewer capacity and invert, and electrical capacity?
- [ ] Has vehicular access, including sight lines and highway authority permits, been confirmed as obtainable?
- [ ] Is there a current survey and title commitment, with all easements, covenants, and encumbrances plotted on the site plan?
- [ ] Are solar orientation, topography, drainage, and neighboring-use impacts documented for their design and performance consequences?
- [ ] Does the feasibility report separate verified facts from assumptions and unknowns, with cost and schedule consequences assigned to each?
- [ ] Are the next investigations required to resolve material unknowns explicitly recommended before the client commits capital?
