# Post-Occupancy Evaluation And Precedent Research

Precedent research and post-occupancy evaluation (POE) are how architecture learns from itself. Precedent study examines existing buildings to understand what worked and what failed; POE systematically measures how a completed building performs for its occupants. Together they convert architecture from a discipline that repeats its mistakes into one that compounds its knowledge. Agents often treat precedent research as image collection, gathering photographs of admired buildings, and treat POE as an optional nicety performed after handover if budget allows. Both habits waste the cheapest source of design intelligence available: the accumulated experience of buildings already in use. A design that ignores what similar buildings have already discovered is condemned to rediscover their failures at full cost.

Use this skill when researching precedents, evaluating a completed building, or benchmarking against similar projects. The goal is to extract transferable lessons, distinguish what is specific to a precedent from what generalizes, and feed evidence into design rather than impression.

## Core Rules

### Define The Research Question Before Selecting Precedents

Precedent study without a question becomes browsing. The agent collects admired buildings, extracts whatever is visually striking, and presents the images as inspiration. Useful precedent research begins from a design problem and asks which existing buildings have addressed a comparable problem, so that the lessons are relevant rather than decorative.

Define the question by:

- stating the specific design challenge the research must inform, such as daylighting a deep plan, organizing a secure public lobby, or resolving a complex section;
- identifying the variables that make a precedent comparable: building type, scale, climate, user group, regulatory context, and budget class;
- distinguishing research for spatial organization from research for detail, material, or technical performance, because each requires different precedents;
- setting criteria for what counts as a useful precedent before the search begins, to prevent selection bias toward the photogenic.

### Study Failure As Rigorously As Success

Published architecture is biased toward success; buildings are photographed at their best, shortly after completion, before use reveals their flaws. A precedent study that reads only the published record inherits a distorted picture in which every admired building appears flawless. The most valuable lessons often lie in what did not work.

Study failure by:

- seeking post-occupancy data, facility manager interviews, and user feedback that reveal operational problems the photography hides;
- visiting precedents in use when possible, observing patterns of wear, adaptation, avoidance, and repair that signal design failures;
- researching buildings of the same type that were not published, because the unpublished ones often failed in instructive ways;
- documenting the gap between the design intent stated in the project narrative and the performance reported by occupants.

### Structure Post-Occupancy Evaluation Around Measurable And Perceptual Data

A rigorous POE combines objective measurement of building performance with structured occupant perception. Either alone is incomplete: measured data without occupant input misses whether the building serves its users, and occupant surveys without measurement cannot distinguish a real performance problem from a perceptual one.

Structure POE by:

- measuring environmental performance: energy use intensity, water consumption, daylight autonomy achieved, thermal comfort, and acoustic conditions, against the design targets;
- surveying occupants on thermal, visual, and acoustic comfort, on the suitability of spaces for their tasks, and on the building's effect on their work or wellbeing;
- conducting walkthroughs and focus groups to capture qualitative insight the surveys miss, including workarounds occupants have invented;
- comparing performance against the design intent and benchmarks, identifying where the building over- or under-delivered and why;
- timing the evaluation to capture both early-occupancy adjustment and stabilized operation, typically six to twelve months after move-in, with a later follow-up.

### Distinguish Transferable Lessons From Context-Specific Ones

A precedent's success may depend on conditions that do not apply to the new project: a different climate, code, budget, user culture, or site. Transferring a solution without understanding why it worked in its original context produces a copy that fails in the new one. The discipline is to identify the principle behind the solution and assess whether the principle transfers.

Distinguish by:

- analyzing the causal chain: what problem did the precedent solve, by what mechanism, and under what conditions;
- identifying which conditions in the precedent match the new project and which differ;
- separating the underlying principle, which may transfer, from its specific expression, which may not;
- testing the transferred lesson against the new project's constraints before adopting it;
- documenting the reasoning so that the design team and client understand why a precedent informed a decision.

### Benchmark Quantitatively Against Comparable Projects

Benchmarking positions the project's targets and performance relative to its peers. Without benchmarks, targets are either generic or aspirational, unconnected to what similar buildings actually achieve. Benchmarking converts targets from wishes into informed commitments.

Benchmark by:

- gathering performance data from comparable completed projects, normalized for climate, size, occupancy, and operating hours;
- using the benchmarks to set realistic targets and to test whether the project's aspirations are achievable;
- recognizing the limitations of benchmarks: they describe what has been done, not what is possible, and a project aiming to advance practice should exceed them deliberately;
- distinguishing median, leading, and best-in-class benchmarks, and choosing the appropriate reference for each target.

### Feed Lessons Forward Into Design, Not Just Into Reports

Research and POE have value only if they change design decisions. Too often the evaluation is produced, filed, and ignored, because the link from finding to design action was never made. The closing discipline is to translate each significant lesson into a specific design implication.

Feed forward by:

- summarizing research and POE findings as design implications, not just observations;
- connecting each lesson to a specific decision in the current project where it applies;
- revisiting the lessons at design reviews to test whether the design is honoring them;
- building an accessible knowledge base of lessons across projects, so that institutional memory survives staff turnover.

## Common Traps

### Image Collection Mistaken For Precedent Research

When precedent study consists of assembling photographs of admired buildings, the research inherits the publication bias of architectural media, which showcases buildings at their most photogenic, shortly after completion, unoccupied and untested. The trap is that the images feel like rigorous research, because they are organized and annotated, yet they contain no information about how the buildings perform, what they cost to operate, or whether their occupants are satisfied. The designer then copies forms whose underlying problems are invisible, reproducing failures the precedent already demonstrated. The corrective is to treat images as a starting point and to pursue the operational and post-occupancy record that reveals how the building actually works, studying failure as seriously as the published success.

### The Post-Occupancy Evaluation That Is Never Done

POE is the most consistently deferred activity in architectural practice, treated as a luxury for when budget and schedule allow, which they never do. The trap is that the deferral feels prudent in the moment, because the project is over and the next one is pressing, while it actually guarantees that the firm repeats its mistakes indefinitely. Without feedback, every project is designed against assumptions rather than evidence, and the gap between design intent and operational reality is never measured, so it is never closed. The harm is institutional: a practice that never evaluates cannot learn, and its later projects are no more informed than its earlier ones. POE must be scoped into the project from the outset, budgeted as a non-negotiable phase, and treated as the source of the firm's competitive intelligence.

### Transferring Solutions Without Understanding Causality

When a designer admires a feature in a precedent and replicates it in a new project without understanding why it worked in the original, the copy frequently fails, because the feature depended on conditions absent in the new context. A brise-soleil that performs in one climate may be pointless in another; an atrium that organizes one building type may fragment another; a material detail that succeeds at one scale may fail at another. The trap is that the transfer feels justified by the precedent's prestige, so no one questions whether the underlying principle applies, until the copied element underperforms or creates new problems. The corrective is to trace the causal chain from problem to solution in the precedent, assess which conditions transfer, and adapt the principle rather than copy the form.

### Survivorship Bias In Benchmark Selection

When benchmarks are drawn only from successful, published projects, they describe the performance of buildings that worked well enough to be publicized, not the full population of similar buildings. The trap is that the resulting targets appear data-driven while actually being biased upward, because the failures that would lower the benchmark are invisible, never having been documented or shared. A project that sets its targets against this inflated benchmark may conclude it is underperforming when it is actually typical, or may over-invest in performance that the building type rarely achieves. The corrective is to seek benchmark data from broad surveys and operational databases that include ordinary and underperforming buildings, not only the celebrated ones, and to interpret the targets against the full distribution.

### The POE Report That Changes Nothing

When a post-occupancy evaluation is commissioned, produced, and filed without being connected to design decisions, the exercise consumes resources and produces no improvement. The trap is that the report's existence satisfies the procedural requirement to evaluate, so the team believes learning has occurred, while the findings have had zero effect on practice. The harm is that the same problems recur in the next project because the lessons were never translated into design action, and the investment in evaluation is wasted. The corrective is to require that every significant POE finding be stated as a design implication tied to a decision type, to revisit the lessons at reviews of subsequent projects, and to maintain a living knowledge base that survives the individuals who produced it.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Did the precedent research begin from a defined design question and selection criteria, rather than from image browsing?
- [ ] Were precedents studied for operational performance and failure, not only for their published photographic success?
- [ ] Does the POE combine measured environmental performance with structured occupant perception, timed for stabilized occupancy?
- [ ] For each transferred lesson, is the causal chain in the precedent documented, and is the transferability to the new project assessed?
- [ ] Are benchmarks drawn from broad populations including ordinary and underperforming buildings, not only celebrated ones?
- [ ] Are POE and research findings translated into specific design implications connected to decisions in the current project?
- [ ] Are lessons revisited at design reviews to test whether the design honors them?
- [ ] Is there an accessible, maintained knowledge base that preserves lessons across projects and staff turnover?
- [ ] Is POE scoped and budgeted from project outset as a non-negotiable phase rather than deferred?
- [ ] Does the research distinguish what is context-specific in a precedent from the underlying principle that may generalize?
