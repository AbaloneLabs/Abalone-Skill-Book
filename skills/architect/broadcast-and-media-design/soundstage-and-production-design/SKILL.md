# Soundstage and Production Design

A soundstage is a large, clear-span room built to film television, film, or video, and it is defined by the combination of a clear volume for set construction and camera movement, a lighting and rigging grid overhead, an acoustic isolation that allows sync-sound recording, and a support infrastructure (scene shop, props, wardrobe, post-production) that serves the production. The judgment problem is that a soundstage looks like a big empty box, so designers tend to size the box and underweight the clear height for the rigging, the acoustic isolation for the sound, the power for the lighting, and the support spaces that the production depends on. A stage whose clear height cannot fly the set and the lighting forces the production to cut the design; a stage whose isolation picks up the exterior noise ruins the sync sound; a stage whose support spaces cannot build, store, and dress the production cannot host the show. This skill covers the decisions that determine whether the soundstage works for production, including clear span and height, the lighting and rigging grid, acoustic isolation, the power and the infrastructure, and the support spaces.

## Core Rules

### Provide the Clear Span and the Clear Height for Set Construction, Camera, and Rigging

A soundstage must be a clear span (no interior columns) across the full stage area, and the clear height must accommodate the set construction, the camera movement (the crane, the dolly, the tracking), and the rigging (the lighting grid, the set flying, the effects) simultaneously, because these three uses occupy the same volume. The clear span is achieved with a long-span structural system (steel trusses, space frames), and the clear height (commonly 8 to 15 meters or more for a major stage) must be measured from the stage floor to the bottom of the rigging grid, with the grid itself and the structure above adding to the total building height. The designer must verify the clear span for the largest set and the camera move, must verify the clear height for the set, the camera, and the rigging simultaneously, and must ensure the structure, the grid, and the lighting do not intrude into the working volume. The trap is sizing the clear height for the set and the camera and discovering the rigging grid cannot fly the lighting or the set.

### Design the Lighting Grid and the Catwalks for the Production Load and Access

The lighting grid (the overhead structure that supports the lighting fixtures, the rigging motors, and the set flying) and the catwalks (the access walkways that reach every lighting position and every motor) must be designed for the production load and for safe, efficient access. The grid must carry the concentrated point loads from the rigging motors and the lighting (commonly hundreds of point loads, each significant), must be laid out on a regular module that matches the production's rigging plot, and must provide the motor power and the control distribution at every position. The catwalks must reach every lighting position and every motor without a ladder (because a fixture that cannot be reached cannot be focused and a motor that cannot be reached cannot be flown), must be sized for the crew and the equipment, and must be coordinated with the rigging so that the grid and the access do not conflict. The trap is a grid that carries the load but cannot be accessed, or catwalks that miss the critical positions.

### Isolate the Stage Acoustically for Sync-Sound Recording

A soundstage used for sync-sound recording (where the audio is captured with the picture) must be isolated from the exterior noise (the traffic, the aircraft, the adjacent machinery) and the structure-borne vibration (the footfall, the rail, the HVAC) to a threshold that allows a clean recording, because the microphone captures every sound that leaks in. The designer must define the noise criterion for the stage (commonly NC 20 to NC 25 for a sync-sound stage), must isolate the envelope (the walls, the roof, the doors) to resist the exterior noise, must float the stage floor or the structure to resist the vibration, must silence the HVAC (the air handler, the ductwork, the airflow), and must coordinate the stage doors (the sound lock, the gasketed doors) so that the entry does not leak the exterior noise. The trap is a stage that looks isolated but picks up the exterior noise or the HVAC rumble, which ruins the sync sound and forces the production to re-record (ADR) at additional cost.

### Size the Power and the Infrastructure for the Lighting and the Production Load

A soundstage's power load is dominated by the lighting (which may be tens or hundreds of kilowatts for a large set), and the electrical infrastructure must be sized for that load, with the distribution and the control the production requires. The designer must compute the lighting load (from the fixture count and the wattage, including the shift to LED which reduces but does not eliminate the load), must provide the power distribution to the stage (the feeder, the dimmer or the power racks, the circuits to the grid and the floor), must provide the control (the DMX or the network distribution to every fixture), and must coordinate the data and the communication infrastructure (the camera, the sound, the video village, the intercom). The trap is sizing the power for a normal building and discovering the lighting load trips the feeder on the first day of the shoot.

### Plan the Support Spaces (Scene Shop, Props, Wardrobe, Post) for the Production Sequence

A soundstage is supported by a sequence of spaces (the scene shop that builds the set, the props and the wardrobe that dress it, the loading dock that receives the materials, the post-production that finishes the product), and these spaces must be planned for the production sequence and the connection to the stage. The scene shop must connect to the stage through a route that a built set can traverse (oversized doors, swept corners), the props and the wardrobe must be accessible from the stage during the shoot, the loading dock must receive the trucks and the materials, and the post-production (the editing, the sound, the color) must be connected to the stage's data and the workflow. The designer must plan the support spaces for the production sequence, must verify the connections (the shop-to-stage route, the dock, the data), and must size the spaces for the production scale. The trap is a soundstage with the right volume and no support, which cannot build or dress the set.

## Common Traps

### A Clear Height Sized for the Set and the Camera That Cannot Fly the Rigging

A soundstage is sized with a clear height adequate for the set and the camera, because those were the visible uses, and the rigging grid is placed at the top of the volume. The mechanism of the trap is that the rigging (the lighting, the set flying, the effects) occupies the volume above the set and the camera, and a clear height sized only for the set and the camera leaves no room to fly the lighting above the set or to fly the set itself, so the production must cut the lighting design or the set design to fit the height, or must build a lower set that compromises the shot. The false signal is the clear height figure, which suggests a large stage but masks the shortfall for the rigging. The harm is compromised production, cut designs, and a stage that cannot host the shows it was built for. The defense is to verify the clear height for the set, the camera, and the rigging simultaneously, measure to the bottom of the grid, and provide the total building height the grid and the structure require.

### A Lighting Grid That Carries the Load but Cannot Be Accessed

A lighting grid is designed with the structural capacity for the rigging motors and the lighting, and the catwalks are added where the structure allowed. The mechanism of the trap is that a grid that carries the load but cannot be accessed (because the catwalks miss the positions, or the motors are out of reach) forces the crew to use ladders or lifts to focus the lighting and to fly the rigging, which is slow, dangerous, and disruptive to the shoot, so the production takes longer and costs more, and the stage is avoided by the crews who know the access is poor. The false signal is the structural capacity, which suggests a functional grid but masks the access failure. The harm is slow, costly production, safety risk, and a stage with a poor reputation. The defense is to design the catwalks to reach every lighting position and every motor without a ladder, size them for the crew and the equipment, and coordinate them with the rigging plot.

### A Stage That Looks Isolated but Picks Up the Exterior Noise or the HVAC Rumble

A soundstage is built with massive walls and a heavy roof, and the isolation is assumed to be adequate for sync sound. The mechanism of the trap is that sync-sound recording requires a very low noise criterion (NC 20 to NC 25), and a stage with massive walls still picks up the structure-borne vibration (the rail, the footfall, the road) and the HVAC rumble (the air handler, the airflow) that the massive envelope does not address, so the recording is contaminated by the low-frequency rumble and the vibration that the microphone captures, and the production must re-record the dialogue (ADR) in a studio at additional cost and with a loss of performance quality. The false signal is the massive envelope, which suggests isolation but masks the structure-borne and the HVAC paths. The harm is contaminated recordings, ADR costs, and a stage that cannot deliver sync sound. The defense is to float the stage floor or the structure, silence the HVAC to the stage criterion, and verify the noise floor at commissioning.

### Power Sized for a Normal Building That Trips on the First Day of the Shoot

A soundstage is provided with the electrical service sized for a building of its area, and the lighting distribution is added during fit-out. The mechanism of the trap is that a soundstage's lighting load (tens or hundreds of kilowatts for a large set) far exceeds a normal building load per area, and a service sized for a normal building is dramatically undersized for the stage, so the feeder trips the moment the full lighting load is energized on the first day of the shoot, the production is delayed, and the stage requires an expensive service upgrade or a rented generator to operate. The false signal is the building service, which suggests adequate power but masks the shortfall against the lighting load. The harm is production delays, costly upgrades, and a stage that cannot operate at full load. The defense is to compute the lighting load from the fixture count and wattage, size the service and the distribution for that load, and provide the control and data infrastructure.

### A Soundstage With the Right Volume but No Support Spaces

A soundstage is built with the right clear span and height, and the support spaces (scene shop, props, wardrobe, post-production) are minimized or omitted to save cost. The mechanism of the trap is that a soundstage is only the room where the filming happens, and the production depends on the support spaces that build, dress, and finish the show, so a stage with the right volume but no support cannot build the set (no scene shop or a shop that cannot reach the stage), cannot dress the production (no props or wardrobe access), and cannot finish the product (no post-production connection), and the production must rent the support elsewhere or build it in temporary space, which is costly and slow. The false signal is the stage volume, which suggests a production facility but masks the absence of the support. The harm is costly, slow production and a stage that cannot host a complete show. The defense is to plan the support spaces for the production sequence, verify the connections (shop-to-stage, dock, data), and size them for the production scale.

## Self-Check

- Did I verify the clear span for the largest set and camera move, and the clear height for the set, the camera, and the rigging simultaneously, measured to the bottom of the grid?
- Did I design the lighting grid to carry the concentrated point loads from the rigging motors and the lighting, laid out on a module that matches the production rigging plot, with motor power and control at every position?
- Did I design the catwalks to reach every lighting position and every motor without a ladder, size them for the crew and the equipment, and coordinate them with the rigging plot?
- Did I define the noise criterion for the stage (NC 20 to NC 25 for sync sound), isolate the envelope, float the floor or the structure, silence the HVAC, and coordinate the sound lock and the gasketed stage doors?
- Did I compute the lighting load from the fixture count and wattage, size the electrical service and the distribution for that load, and provide the control (DMX or network) and the data and communication infrastructure?
- Did I plan the support spaces (scene shop, props, wardrobe, loading dock, post-production) for the production sequence, verify the connections (shop-to-stage route, dock, data), and size them for the production scale?
- Did I verify the shop-to-stage route can traverse a built set (oversized doors, swept corners)?
- Did I verify the noise floor at commissioning, with the HVAC running and the exterior at its typical level, before the stage is released for sync-sound production?
