# Product Substitution and Equals Evaluation

A substitution request is a contractor's proposal to use a different product than the one specified, and the designer's evaluation of that request is a high-stakes judgment that must protect the design intent while treating the contractor fairly. The judgment problem is that a substitution request looks like a simple product comparison (does the proposed product match the specified one), so designers tend to compare the spec sheets and approve or reject, missing that the basis of design product was selected for reasons that may not appear on a spec sheet (coordination with adjacent systems, warranty, maintenance, aesthetics, the documented design intent). A substitution that matches on paper but is heavier, or louder, or uses a different finish, or requires a different rough-in can cascade into conflicts with the structure, the acoustics, the architecture, and the plumbing. This skill covers the decisions that determine whether substitutions are evaluated rigorously and fairly, including the basis of design, the prescriptive versus performance decision, the "or equal" evaluation, and the documentation that protects the designer and the owner.

## Core Rules

### Document the Basis of Design for Every Specified Product

The basis of design is the specific product on which the specification and the design coordination are based, and it must be documented with the reasons for its selection, not just the product name. The basis of design documentation should record the product, the model, the key characteristics (size, weight, power, finish, performance), and the design reasons for the choice (coordination with the structure, the acoustics, the adjacent systems, the warranty, the maintenance, the aesthetics). This documentation is the standard against which any substitution is evaluated, because it tells the reviewer not just what was specified but why, so a proposed substitute can be judged against the actual design intent rather than against a spec sheet. The basis of design must be established during design (not invented during construction), must be coordinated with the other disciplines, and must be recorded in the project file.

The trap is specifying a product without recording the design reasons, so a substitution can only be compared on spec-sheet characteristics, missing the coordination and the intent that drove the original choice. The defense is to document the basis of design with the design reasons during design, to coordinate it across disciplines, and to use it as the evaluation standard for substitutions.

### Choose Prescriptive Versus Performance Specifications Deliberately

The choice between a prescriptive specification (naming the product and the installation) and a performance specification (defining the required result and allowing any product that meets it) is a risk allocation decision, and it must be made deliberately for each scope. A prescriptive specification with a basis of design gives the designer control over the result and makes substitutions a defined exception, but it limits bid competition and requires a defensible substitution process. A performance specification gives the contractor flexibility and bid competition, but it transfers the performance risk to the designer, who must verify that any proposed product meets the performance criteria and must accept any product that does. The choice depends on the scope: design-driven scopes (visible finishes, coordinated systems) favor prescriptive with a basis of design; commodity scopes (standardized materials) favor performance with clear criteria.

The tradeoff is design control (prescriptive) versus bid competition (performance). The resolution is to specify prescriptively with a basis of design for design-driven scopes, to specify by performance with clear criteria for commodity scopes, and to never mix the two in one section without verifying consistency.

### Define What "Or Equal" Means With Documented Criteria

"Or equal" is the most common and the most dangerous phrase in a specification, because it invites substitution without defining the standard for equality. A specification that lists a basis of design and adds "or equal" is open-ended unless the equality criteria are documented, because the contractor will propose the cheapest product that arguably matches the spec sheet, and the designer has no documented basis to reject it. The defense is to define "or equal" with a documented set of criteria: the product characteristics that must be matched (size, capacity, performance, material, finish, warranty), the coordination requirements (weight, power, rough-in, acoustic), and the submittal requirements (the contractor must demonstrate compliance with the criteria). With documented criteria, "or equal" becomes a defined and defensible standard; without it, it is an open invitation to substitution disputes.

The false signal is that "or equal" is a common phrase that contractors understand, which suggests a workable standard but defines nothing measurable. The defense is to define the equality criteria in the specification, to require the contractor to demonstrate compliance, and to evaluate substitutions against the documented criteria.

### Evaluate Substitutions Against the Design Intent, Not Just the Spec Sheet

A substitution request must be evaluated against the basis of design and the design intent, not just against the spec-sheet characteristics of the two products. The evaluation must compare the proposed product to the basis of design on the documented criteria (performance, size, weight, power, finish, warranty) and on the coordination requirements (does the substitute fit the structure, the rough-in, the adjacent systems, the acoustic design), and it must consider the design reasons that drove the original choice. A substitute that matches on the spec sheet but is heavier (requiring structural reinforcement), or louder (failing the acoustic design), or uses a different finish (failing the architecture) must be rejected or accepted only with a documented change to the affected systems. The evaluation must be timely (per the substitution time limits in the general conditions) and must be documented with the decision and the reasons.

The trap is comparing the two products on the spec sheet and approving the substitute because it matches on paper, missing the coordination and the design intent that the substitution disrupts. The defense is to evaluate the substitution against the basis of design and the design intent, to check the coordination requirements, and to document the decision and the reasons.

### Enforce the Substitution Time Limits and Procedures

The general conditions establish the procedures and the time limits for substitution requests, typically allowing substitutions before bid (through a named substitution process) or after bid only with the designer's approval for cause. The designer must enforce these limits, because a substitution requested after the contracts are signed (when the contractor has the work) is a much higher-risk proposal than one requested before bid (when the products can be compared on a level field). A post-bid substitution is often a request to use a cheaper product the contractor has already bought or can buy cheaply, and the designer must evaluate it against the same criteria as a pre-bid substitution, with the added scrutiny that the contractor's motivation is cost, not value. The procedures (the form, the documentation, the response time) must be followed, and the decisions must be documented.

The false signal is that a substitution is a substitution whenever it is requested, which suggests a uniform process but ignores that post-bid substitutions carry higher risk and require more scrutiny. The defense is to enforce the substitution time limits, to evaluate post-bid substitutions with added scrutiny, and to follow the procedures and the documentation requirements.

### Document Substitution Decisions to Protect the Design and Limit Liability

Every substitution decision (approval, conditional approval, rejection) must be documented with the decision, the reasons, and any conditions, because the documentation is the designer's protection against later claims that the substitution caused a defect. An approval should record the criteria met and any conditions (for example, the contractor must verify the rough-in, or the owner must accept a different finish); a rejection should record the criteria not met and the design intent at risk; a conditional approval should record the conditions and the contractor's acceptance. The documentation must be issued formally (through the substitution review process, not by informal email) and must be filed in the project record. The documentation also protects the owner, who is the party that bears the cost of a substitution that fails.

The trap is approving or rejecting a substitution informally, without documented reasons, which leaves the designer exposed to a later claim that the decision was arbitrary or that it caused a defect. The defense is to document every substitution decision with the reasons and the conditions, to issue it formally, and to file it in the project record.

## Common Traps

### Approving a Substitute Because It Matches on the Spec Sheet

A substitution request is evaluated by comparing the proposed product's spec sheet to the basis of design's spec sheet, and the substitute matches on the listed characteristics, so it is approved. The mechanism of the trap is that the basis of design was selected for reasons that may not appear on the spec sheet (coordination with the structure, the acoustics, the adjacent systems, the warranty, the aesthetics), and a substitute that matches on paper may fail on the unlisted criteria, cascading into conflicts with the other systems. The false signal is the matching spec sheet, which suggests equivalence but ignores the coordination and the design intent. The harm is a substitute that disrupts the structure, the acoustics, the architecture, or the maintenance, requiring expensive corrections. The defense is to evaluate the substitution against the basis of design and the design intent, to check the coordination requirements, and to document the decision.

### "Or Equal" Without Defined Equality Criteria

A specification lists a basis of design and adds "or equal," with no documented equality criteria, and the contractor proposes the cheapest product that arguably matches the spec sheet. The mechanism of the trap is that "or equal" without criteria is open-ended, and the designer has no documented basis to reject a proposed substitute, because the standard for equality is undefined, so the substitution becomes a negotiation rather than an evaluation. The false signal is that "or equal" is a common, understood phrase, which suggests a workable standard but defines nothing measurable. The harm is substitution disputes, cheaper products that do not match the design intent, and designer exposure to claims of arbitrary rejection. The defense is to define the equality criteria in the specification, to require the contractor to demonstrate compliance, and to evaluate substitutions against the documented criteria.

### A Basis of Design Without Documented Design Reasons

A product is specified as the basis of design during design, but the design reasons for the choice are not recorded, so when a substitution is proposed the evaluation can only compare spec sheets. The mechanism of the trap is that the basis of design is the standard for substitution evaluation, and without the design reasons the evaluation cannot consider the coordination and the intent that drove the choice, so the substitute is judged only on the spec-sheet characteristics that may not capture the design intent. The false signal is that the basis of design is named, which suggests a clear standard but omits the reasons that make the standard meaningful. The harm is substitutions that match on paper but fail on the unlisted design reasons, with disruption to the building. The defense is to document the basis of design with the design reasons during design, to coordinate it across disciplines, and to use it as the evaluation standard.

### Accepting a Post-Bid Substitution Without Added Scrutiny

A substitution is requested after the contracts are signed, when the contractor has the work, and it is evaluated with the same process as a pre-bid substitution, without added scrutiny. The mechanism of the trap is that a post-bid substitution is often motivated by the contractor's cost (a cheaper product already bought or available), not by value, and the contractor's incentive is to minimize cost, which may not align with the design intent, so a post-bid substitution carries higher risk and requires more scrutiny than a pre-bid one. The false signal is that a substitution is a substitution whenever requested, which suggests a uniform process but ignores the higher risk of post-bid substitutions. The harm is a cheaper substitute that fails the design intent, with the cost borne by the owner. The defense is to enforce the substitution time limits, to evaluate post-bid substitutions with added scrutiny, and to follow the procedures.

### Mixing Prescriptive and Performance Requirements in One Section

A section specifies a product prescriptively (a basis of design) and then adds a performance requirement (a rating or a load), and a substitution is proposed that meets the performance but not the prescriptive basis. The mechanism of the trap is that prescriptive and performance requirements create two paths to compliance, and a contractor may propose a substitute that meets the performance but not the basis of design (or vice versa), forcing the designer to either accept a different product or to defend a conflict. The false signal is that both requirements are stated, which looks thorough but creates a conflict the contractor can exploit. The harm is substitution disputes, change orders, and a product that may not match the design intent. The defense is to choose one approach per scope, to verify consistency, and to avoid mixing the two in one section.

### An Informal Substitution Approval With No Documented Reasons

A substitution is approved by informal email, without documented reasons or conditions, and later the substitute causes a defect, and the owner claims the approval was arbitrary or caused the defect. The mechanism of the trap is that the substitution decision is a high-stakes judgment that must be defensible, and an informal approval without documented reasons leaves the designer exposed to a claim that the decision was arbitrary or that it caused the defect, with no record to defend against. The false signal is that the approval was communicated, which suggests a complete decision but omits the reasons and the conditions. The harm is designer liability for the defect, with no documentation to defend the decision. The defense is to document every substitution decision with the reasons and the conditions, to issue it formally, and to file it in the project record.

## Self-Check

- Did I document the basis of design for every specified product with the design reasons (coordination, performance, warranty, aesthetics) during design, and use it as the evaluation standard for substitutions?
- Did I choose prescriptive (basis of design) versus performance specifications deliberately for each scope, and avoid mixing the two in one section without verifying consistency?
- Did I define "or equal" with documented equality criteria (performance, size, weight, power, finish, warranty, coordination), require the contractor to demonstrate compliance, and evaluate substitutions against the criteria?
- Did I evaluate each substitution against the basis of design and the design intent, check the coordination requirements (structure, rough-in, acoustics, adjacent systems), and consider the design reasons that drove the original choice?
- Did I enforce the substitution time limits in the general conditions, evaluate post-bid substitutions with added scrutiny, and follow the substitution procedures and documentation requirements?
- Did I document every substitution decision (approval, conditional approval, rejection) with the reasons and any conditions, issue it formally, and file it in the project record?
- Did I verify that an approved substitution does not disrupt the structure, the acoustics, the architecture, the plumbing, or the maintenance, or accept it only with a documented change to the affected systems?
- Did I protect the owner's interest by evaluating substitutions for value (not just cost) and by ensuring the owner bears no unintended cost from a substitution that fails?
