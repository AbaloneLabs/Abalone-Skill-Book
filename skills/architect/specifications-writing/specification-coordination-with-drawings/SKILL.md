# Specification Coordination With Drawings

The drawings and the specifications together form the contract documents, and they are judged by whether they describe one coherent building or two documents that disagree. The judgment problem is that drawings and specifications are produced by different people, on different schedules, and with different tools, so the assumption that they will naturally agree is the source of most construction conflicts. A drawing that shows a 4-inch slab where the spec calls for 6 inches, a wall section that shows a fire rating the spec does not require, a detail that shows a product the spec does not list: each of these is a conflict that triggers the order of precedence, generates a request for information, and often becomes a change order. This skill covers the decisions that determine whether the drawings and the specifications are coordinated, including the complementary relationship, conflict resolution, the order of precedence, and the pre-bid coordination review.

## Core Rules

### Treat Drawings and Specifications as Complementary, Not Redundant

The contract documents establish that the drawings and the specifications are complementary: the drawings show quantity, location, and configuration (what and where), and the specifications define quality, product, and installation (what kind and how). What is required by one is required by both, and a requirement stated in one place does not need to be restated in the other, but it must not be contradicted. The drafter must understand this division of labor: the drawings should not try to specify the product (that is the spec's job), and the spec should not try to dimension the work (that is the drawings' job). The most common coordination error is restating a requirement in both documents with slightly different wording, which creates a conflict where none existed.

The tradeoff is redundancy (restating in both, which seems safe but creates conflicts) versus complementarity (stating once in the right place, which requires discipline). The resolution is to state each requirement once in the appropriate document, to cross-reference the other document where helpful, and to avoid restating requirements with different wording.

### Resolve Conflicts Before Bid, Not During Construction

Conflicts between the drawings and the specifications are inevitable in a complex set of documents, and the goal of coordination is to find and resolve them before the documents go to bid, because a conflict found in coordination costs only time while a conflict found in construction costs money and schedule. The coordination review must systematically compare the drawings and the specifications for each scope: do the products named in the spec match the products shown in the details, do the dimensions and ratings shown in the drawings match the spec requirements, do the notes on the drawings match the spec sections they reference, and do the general notes conflict with the general requirements. Each conflict must be resolved by a decision (which document is correct) and a correction (editing the wrong document), documented so the design intent is clear.

The trap is relying on the contractor to find conflicts through requests for information, which shifts the cost of coordination to construction and rewards the contractor who finds the conflict with a change order. The defense is to perform a systematic coordination review before bid, to resolve every conflict by decision and correction, and to document the design intent.

### Establish and Respect the Order of Precedence

The agreement establishes the order of precedence, which is the ranking of the contract documents that governs in a conflict (for example, the agreement, then the addenda, then the specifications, then the drawings, or some other defined order). The order of precedence exists to resolve conflicts that survive coordination, but it is not a substitute for coordination, because a conflict resolved by precedence may award the work to the version the designer did not intend. The drafter must know the order of precedence in the project agreement, must avoid creating conflicts that trigger it, and must understand that the specifications typically govern over the drawings (because the specifications are more specific), which means the specifications must be accurate and complete.

The false signal is that the order of precedence exists to resolve conflicts, which suggests a safety net but masks the fact that precedence resolves conflicts in a defined order that may not match the design intent. The defense is to know the order of precedence, to avoid creating conflicts, and to treat the specifications as the governing document for product and quality.

### Cross-Reference, Do Not Restate, Across Documents

Cross-referencing is the discipline that keeps the documents complementary without redundancy. Each specification section should cross-reference the related drawings (in Part 1, Related Work or by note) and the related sections, and each drawing detail should reference the governing specification section (by note). Cross-references must be accurate (the referenced section or drawing must exist and must govern the work) and must be bidirectional where it matters (the spec references the drawings, the drawings reference the spec). Restating a requirement in both documents, with different wording, is the opposite of cross-referencing and is the source of most conflicts, because the two wordings drift apart over revisions.

The trap is restating a requirement in both the drawings and the spec to "be safe," which creates two versions that drift apart over revisions and become a conflict. The defense is to state each requirement once, to cross-reference the other document, and to keep the cross-references accurate through revisions.

### Coordinate General Notes, Keynotes, and Specification Sections

General notes on the drawings (the block of notes that applies to the whole sheet or the whole set) and keynotes (the numbered notes that point to specific elements) are a common source of conflict with the specifications, because they are often written early, edited late, and not reconciled with the spec. General notes that restate spec requirements (fire ratings, acoustic requirements, material standards) must match the spec exactly or they create a conflict, and keynotes that reference a spec section must reference the correct section and must not add requirements the section does not contain. The coordination review must include a check of the general notes and keynotes against the specifications, with conflicts resolved by editing whichever document is wrong.

The false signal is that the notes are on the drawings and therefore govern the work shown, which suggests coordination but ignores that the notes must match the spec. The defense is to treat general notes and keynotes as cross-references to the spec (not as restatements), to verify they match the spec, and to reconcile them during the coordination review.

### Manage Revisions to Keep the Documents Coordinated

Coordination is not a one-time event; it is a discipline that must be maintained through every revision, because a change to the drawings that is not carried to the spec (or vice versa) creates a conflict. The revision process must include a check of the coordinated documents: when a drawing detail is revised, the related spec section must be checked and revised if needed; when a spec section is revised, the related drawings must be checked. The revision must be documented (in the revision log, in the spec revision log) so the coordination is traceable, and the addenda and the change orders during construction must be checked for the same coordination, because a change during construction that is not coordinated becomes a new conflict.

The trap is revising one document (the drawings or the spec) and not carrying the change to the other, which creates a conflict that surfaces in construction. The defense is to check the coordinated documents with every revision, to document the coordination in the revision logs, and to apply the same discipline to addenda and change orders.

## Common Traps

### Restating a Requirement in Both Documents With Different Wording

A requirement (a fire rating, a material standard, a dimension) is stated in both the drawings and the specification, with slightly different wording, to "be safe," and over revisions the two wordings drift apart. The mechanism of the trap is that the documents are complementary, and restating a requirement in both creates two versions that can diverge, so a revision to one that is not carried to the other produces a conflict where there was none. The false signal is that the requirement is stated in both places, which seems thorough but creates the divergence risk. The harm is a conflict that triggers the order of precedence or a change order, with the result possibly not matching the design intent. The defense is to state each requirement once in the appropriate document, to cross-reference the other, and to keep the cross-references accurate.

### A Drawing Detail That Shows a Product the Spec Does Not List

A wall section or a detail shows a specific product (a membrane, a sealant, a panel) that the specification does not list or that the specification lists differently, and the conflict is not caught until the submittal. The mechanism of the trap is that the drawings and the spec must agree on the product, and a detail that shows a product the spec does not list creates a conflict the contractor can exploit by proposing either product. The false signal is that the detail is drawn and the spec is written, which suggests completeness but masks the product mismatch. The harm is a submittal for the wrong product, a rejection, and a change order or delay. The defense is to verify the products shown in the details match the products listed in the spec, and to resolve mismatches during the coordination review.

### Relying on the Order of Precedence Instead of Coordination

The team knows the order of precedence will resolve any conflict, so the coordination review is skipped or rushed, and conflicts are left for the contractor to find. The mechanism of the trap is that the order of precedence resolves conflicts in a defined order (often specifications over drawings), which may award the work to the version the designer did not intend, and it rewards the contractor who finds the conflict with a change order. The false signal is that the order of precedence is a safety net, which suggests the conflicts are manageable but masks the cost and the risk of an unintended result. The harm is change orders, schedule delays, and a building that may not match the design intent. The defense is to perform the coordination review before bid, to resolve every conflict by decision and correction, and to treat precedence as a last resort.

### General Notes That Conflict With the Specifications

The general notes on the drawings state a fire rating, an acoustic requirement, or a material standard that differs from the specification, and the notes were written early and not reconciled with the spec. The mechanism of the trap is that general notes are binding contract documents, and a note that conflicts with the spec creates a conflict that triggers the order of precedence, with the note possibly governing over the spec or vice versa depending on the order. The false signal is that the notes are on the drawings and look authoritative, which suggests coordination but masks the conflict. The harm is a dispute over which requirement governs, a change order, and a result that may not match the design intent. The defense is to treat general notes as cross-references to the spec, to verify they match the spec, and to reconcile them during the coordination review.

### A Revision to One Document Not Carried to the Other

A change is made to the drawings (a revised dimension, a changed product, a new detail) during a revision cycle, and the related specification section is not checked or revised, creating a conflict. The mechanism of the trap is that coordination must be maintained through revisions, and a change to one document that is not carried to the other produces a conflict that surfaces in construction, often as a submittal for the wrong product or a request for information. The false signal is that the revision is issued and logged, which suggests completeness but masks the uncoordinated spec. The harm is a conflict, a change order, and a delay. The defense is to check the coordinated documents with every revision, to document the coordination in the revision logs, and to apply the same discipline to addenda.

### A Keynote That References the Wrong Specification Section

A keynote on a drawing points to a specification section number that is wrong (a section that does not exist, or that governs a different scope), and the contractor follows the keynote to the wrong section. The mechanism of the trap is that keynotes are the link between the drawings and the spec, and a wrong keynote sends the contractor to the wrong requirements, producing a submittal or an installation that does not match the design intent for that element. The false signal is that the keynote has a section number, which looks like a correct reference but points to the wrong section. The harm is the wrong product or installation, a rejection, and a change order. The defense is to verify every keynote references the correct governing section, to check the keynotes during the coordination review, and to keep the keynotes accurate through revisions.

## Self-Check

- Did I treat the drawings and specifications as complementary (drawings show what and where, specifications tell what kind and how), and state each requirement once in the appropriate document?
- Did I perform a systematic coordination review before bid, comparing the drawings and the specifications for each scope, and resolve every conflict by decision and correction?
- Did I know the order of precedence in the project agreement, avoid creating conflicts that trigger it, and treat the specifications as the governing document for product and quality?
- Did I cross-reference (not restate) across documents, with accurate bidirectional references between the spec sections and the drawings, and avoid restating requirements with different wording?
- Did I check the general notes and keynotes against the specifications, treat them as cross-references rather than restatements, and reconcile any conflicts during the coordination review?
- Did I check the coordinated documents with every revision, document the coordination in the revision logs, and apply the same discipline to addenda and change orders during construction?
- Did I verify the products shown in the details match the products listed in the spec, and resolve any mismatches before bid?
- Did I verify every keynote references the correct governing specification section, and keep the keynotes accurate through revisions?
