# MasterFormat Specification Authoring

A specification is a legally binding instruction that tells the contractor exactly what to furnish and how to install it, and it is judged by whether a competent contractor reading it cold can build the intended result without guessing. The judgment problem is that a specification looks like a reference document that lists products and standards, so drafters tend to copy from a master, swap in a product name, and ship it, missing that every ambiguous term, every un-cited standard, every open-ended "as required," and every conflicting requirement becomes a claim, a change order, or a defect. A specification that says "provide high-quality paint" is unenforceable; one that cites "ASTM" without the number points to nothing; one that mixes prescriptive and performance requirements invites the contractor to choose the cheaper path. This skill covers the decisions that determine whether the specification is clear, enforceable, and coordinated, including MasterFormat organization, the 3-part structure, standards referencing, ambiguity avoidance, and the closed specification versus open "or equal" decision.

## Core Rules

### Organize the Project Manual per MasterFormat Divisions and Sections

MasterFormat is the standard organization for construction specifications, with 50 divisions (00 Procurement, 01 General Requirements, 02 through 49 technical divisions) and each division broken into 6-digit section numbers (for example, 09 21 16 for Gypsum Board Assemblies). The project manual must be organized so that each section covers one definable scope of work, the section numbers follow MasterFormat, and the divisions do not overlap or leave gaps. A common error is to split one scope across multiple sections (for example, gypsum board in one section and gypsum board finishing in another) or to combine unrelated scopes in one section, both of which create coordination gaps and conflicts. The division of scope must be consistent with the drawings, so that a detail on the drawings references one specification section, not several.

The tradeoff is granularity (more sections, clearer scope, but more coordination) versus consolidation (fewer sections, less coordination, but blurred scope). The resolution is to follow the MasterFormat logic (one section per definable scope), to avoid creating custom sections where a MasterFormat section exists, and to coordinate the section list with the drawings and the bid schedule.

### Draft Each Section in the 3-Part Format (General, Products, Execution)

Each specification section follows the 3-part format: Part 1 General (related sections, references, submittals, quality assurance, delivery, handling, warranty), Part 2 Products (materials, manufacture, fabrication, the basis of design and acceptable manufacturers), and Part 3 Execution (inspection, preparation, installation, field quality control, cleaning, protection). The 3-part format is the discipline that forces the drafter to separate what is being furnished (Part 2) from how it is installed (Part 3), and to put the administrative and quality requirements in Part 1. Each article within a part follows a standard order, and the drafter should use a master (such as a guide specification or office master) as the scaffold, editing rather than writing from scratch, so the structure and the cross-references stay consistent.

The trap is writing a free-form narrative that mixes products and installation, which blurs the contractor's obligations and makes the section hard to enforce. The defense is to follow the 3-part format strictly, to keep products in Part 2 and installation in Part 3, and to use a master as the scaffold so the structure is consistent across sections.

### Reference Standards by Full Citation and Current Edition

Specifications routinely reference industry standards (ASTM, ANSI, UL, NFPA, AISC, ACI) to incorporate the technical requirements by reference rather than restating them, and these references must be complete and current. A proper reference cites the standard's designation (for example, ASTM E84), the title (Standard Test Method for Surface Burning Characteristics of Building Materials), and the edition year, and the project manual should include a list of reference standards with full titles and editions in each section's Part 1. The edition year matters because standards are revised, and a reference without a year points to whatever edition is current at the time of a dispute, which may not be the edition the design was based on. The references must also be consistent across sections (the same standard cited the same way everywhere) and must not conflict (two sections citing different editions of the same standard).

The false signal is a reference that names the standard (for example, "ASTM E84"), which looks complete but omits the title and the year, leaving the reference ambiguous. The defense is to cite every standard by designation, title, and year, to maintain a reference list in each section, and to verify the references are current and consistent across the project manual.

### Write Imperative, Closed-Ended, and Enforceable Language

A specification is an instruction, and its language must be imperative (directing the contractor to do something), closed-ended (defining a specific result, not an open range), and enforceable (testable against the work). Imperative language uses the imperative mood ("Install," "Provide," "Submit") or the streamlined form ("Provide paint.") rather than the passive or the suggestive ("Paint should be provided."). Closed-ended language defines a measurable result (a paint with a specific gloss, a coating thickness, a fire rating) rather than an open quality ("high-quality paint"). Enforceable language ties the requirement to a test, a standard, or a submittal, so that compliance can be verified. The drafter must avoid words that create ambiguity ("as required," "as needed," "to the satisfaction of the architect" without criteria) and must avoid words that shift responsibility ("by others" without naming the party).

The trap is writing descriptive language that reads well but is unenforceable, which gives the contractor room to interpret and the owner no basis to reject. The defense is to write in the imperative mood, to define measurable results, to tie requirements to tests and standards, and to eliminate ambiguous phrases.

### Choose Closed (Basis of Design) Versus Open (Or Equal) Specifications Deliberately

A specification may be closed (naming a basis of design and a limited list of acceptable manufacturers, with substitutions only by approval) or open (naming a performance requirement and allowing any product that meets it). The choice is a risk decision: a closed specification gives the designer control over the result but requires a documented basis of design and a defensible substitution process; an open specification gives the contractor flexibility but transfers the performance risk to the designer, who must verify that any proposed product meets the performance. For most architectural scopes, a closed specification with a basis of design and a small list of pre-approved alternates is the safer choice, because it preserves the design intent and the coordination. For commodity items (drywall, concrete, rebar), an open performance specification is appropriate because the products are standardized.

The tradeoff is design control (closed) versus bid competition (open). The resolution is to specify closed with a basis of design for design-driven scopes, open with clear performance criteria for commodity scopes, and to never use "or equal" without defining what "equal" means (a documented set of criteria the substitute must meet).

### Coordinate the Specification With the Drawings and the General Requirements

The specification does not stand alone; it is part of a coordinated set that includes the drawings (which show quantity, location, and configuration), the general requirements (Division 01, which governs administration, submittals, and quality), and the agreement (which establishes the order of precedence). Each specification section must cross-reference the related drawings (in Part 1, Related Work) and the related sections, and must not repeat or conflict with the general requirements. The drawings and the specifications are complementary: the drawings show what and where, the specifications tell what kind and how, and a requirement in one should not contradict the other. The order of precedence (which governs in a conflict) is set in the agreement, and the drafter must know that order and avoid creating conflicts that trigger it.

The trap is writing the specification as a standalone document that repeats or conflicts with the drawings and the general requirements, which creates the very conflicts the order of precedence exists to resolve. The defense is to cross-reference the drawings and related sections, to avoid repeating Division 01 requirements, and to coordinate the specification with the drawings so they are complementary, not conflicting.

## Common Traps

### Citing a Standard Without the Title or the Edition Year

A specification references "ASTM E84" for surface burning characteristics, without the title or the year, and the reference looks complete to a casual reader. The mechanism of the trap is that standards are revised, and a reference without the year points to whatever edition is current at the time of a dispute, which may differ from the edition the design was based on, and a reference without the title can be misidentified if the designation is ambiguous or re-used. The false signal is that the standard is named, which looks like a complete citation but omits the title and the year that make it unambiguous. The harm is a specification that points to a moving target, with the contractor able to comply with a different edition than the designer intended, and disputes over which edition governs. The defense is to cite every standard by designation, title, and year, to maintain a reference list, and to verify the references are current and consistent.

### Open-Ended Language Like "As Required" or "High Quality"

A specification uses phrases like "as required," "as needed," "to the satisfaction of the architect," or "high-quality" to describe a result, and the language reads smoothly but defines nothing measurable. The mechanism of the trap is that these phrases are unenforceable, because they define no specific result and no test, so the contractor can interpret them broadly and the owner has no basis to reject the work, turning every quality question into a dispute. The false signal is that the language sounds professional and complete, which suggests a requirement but defines no standard. The harm is a specification that cannot be enforced, with quality disputes, change orders, and defective work that the owner cannot reject. The defense is to write imperative, closed-ended language tied to a measurable result, a test, or a standard, and to eliminate ambiguous phrases.

### Mixing Prescriptive and Performance Requirements in One Section

A section specifies the product prescriptively (a specific manufacturer and model) and then adds a performance requirement (a fire rating or a load), and the two are not verified to be consistent. The mechanism of the trap is that prescriptive and performance requirements create two paths to compliance, and a contractor may propose a substitute that meets the performance but not the prescriptive basis of design (or vice versa), forcing the designer to either accept a different product or to defend a conflict. The false signal is that both requirements are stated, which looks thorough but creates a conflict the contractor can exploit. The harm is substitution disputes, change orders, and a product that may not match the design intent. The defense is to choose one approach (prescriptive with basis of design, or performance with clear criteria) per scope, to verify consistency, and to avoid mixing the two in one section.

### "By Others" Without Naming the Responsible Party

A specification assigns work "by others" or "by the contractor" without naming the specific party or the specific section, leaving the responsibility ambiguous. The mechanism of the trap is that "by others" without a reference shifts the work to an unnamed party, and when the work is not done, every party can point to another, leaving the owner with a gap and no responsible party to enforce against. The false signal is that the work is assigned, which suggests a complete requirement but leaves the responsible party unnamed. The harm is scope gaps, disputes over who furnishes or installs the work, and change orders to fill the gap. The defense is to name the responsible party or to reference the specific section that governs the work, and to avoid "by others" without a cross-reference.

### Repeating Division 01 Requirements in a Technical Section

A technical section repeats the submittal, substitution, or quality assurance requirements that are already in Division 01 (General Requirements), sometimes with different wording or criteria. The mechanism of the trap is that Division 01 governs project-wide administration, and repeating its requirements in a technical section creates two sets of rules that may conflict, so the contractor can follow the more lenient version and the designer loses the consistent administration. The false signal is that the requirement is stated in the technical section, which looks thorough but duplicates and potentially conflicts with Division 01. The harm is inconsistent administration, conflicting requirements, and disputes over which version governs. The defense is to reference Division 01 in each technical section (rather than repeating it), to state only the section-specific requirements, and to verify the technical sections do not conflict with Division 01.

### A Specification That Conflicts With the Drawings

A specification section states a requirement (a material, a dimension, a rating) that conflicts with what the drawings show, and the conflict is not caught until construction. The mechanism of the trap is that the drawings and the specifications are complementary and both are binding, and a conflict between them triggers the order of precedence in the agreement, which may award the conflict to the version the designer did not intend, or it generates a request for information and a change order. The false signal is that both documents are complete, which suggests coordination but masks the conflict. The harm is change orders, schedule delays, and a result that may not match the design intent. The defense is to cross-reference the drawings in each section, to coordinate the specification with the drawings during a specification review, and to resolve conflicts before bid.

## Self-Check

- Did I organize the project manual per MasterFormat, with each section covering one definable scope, section numbers following the standard, and no overlapping or combined scopes?
- Did I draft each section in the 3-part format (Part 1 General, Part 2 Products, Part 3 Execution), keeping products and installation separate and using a master as the scaffold?
- Did I cite every referenced standard by designation, title, and edition year, maintain a reference list in each section, and verify the references are current and consistent across the project manual?
- Did I write imperative, closed-ended, enforceable language tied to measurable results, tests, or standards, and eliminate ambiguous phrases like "as required," "as needed," or "high quality"?
- Did I choose closed (basis of design) versus open (performance) specifications deliberately for each scope, and define what "or equal" means with documented criteria where used?
- Did I cross-reference the related drawings and related sections in each Part 1, avoid repeating Division 01 requirements, and verify the technical sections do not conflict with the general requirements?
- Did I coordinate the specification with the drawings so they are complementary (drawings show what and where, specifications tell what kind and how), and resolve conflicts before bid?
- Did I review the project manual for consistency (the same standard cited the same way, the same product named the same way) and for completeness (no scope gaps, no unnamed "by others")?
