# Project Delivery Method Selection

The delivery method is the contractual and procedural backbone of a building project. It decides who holds design responsibility, who holds construction risk, how cost certainty arrives, how change is resolved, and how early the builder's input shapes the design. Owners often select a delivery method by default or by lowest-fee instinct, then discover too late that the method they chose cannot meet the schedule they promised, cannot deliver the cost certainty they assumed, or cannot support the collaborative quality goals they advertised. The architect's job at this decision point is not to pick a method for the owner, but to make the tradeoffs explicit, surface the constraints that should drive the choice, and protect the design and professional interests that each method treats differently.

Use this skill before an owner commits to a delivery method, when comparing approaches during pre-design or early schematic work, when writing a request for proposals, or when the team is being assembled. The goal is a deliberate choice grounded in project priorities, not a default.

## Core Rules

### Establish The Owner's Priority Ranking Before Comparing Methods

Delivery methods optimize for different things. No method is universally best; each trades schedule certainty, cost certainty, design quality, and risk allocation differently. Before naming a method, force the owner to rank what matters most.

Establish priorities by:

- asking the owner to rank, explicitly and in writing, the relative importance of cost certainty, schedule speed, design quality and customization, and risk transfer to the builder;
- identifying hard schedule drivers, such as a grant deadline, an academic-year opening, a lease expiration, or financing milestones that cannot move;
- identifying budget drivers, such as fixed funding caps, value-engineering tolerance, and whether the owner can absorb contingency or needs a guaranteed maximum price;
- clarifying the owner's tolerance for ambiguity and change during construction, since some methods absorb change more gracefully than others;
- surfacing unstated assumptions, such as "design-build is always faster" or "low bid always saves money," and testing them against the specific project.

A method chosen without a priority ranking is chosen by inertia. The ranking is the decision's foundation.

### Match Method Characteristics To Project Realities

Each delivery method has structural behaviors that should be weighed against the project's facts, not its hopes.

Match by considering:

- design-bid-build separates design and construction, gives the owner a clear low-bid price, but loses builder input during design, invites lowest-bidder quality risk, and makes design errors surface as change orders during construction;
- construction-manager-at-risk brings the builder on during design for preconstruction input and pricing, then converts to a guaranteed maximum price, balancing early collaboration with cost certainty, but requires a capable CM and a collaborative owner;
- design-build collapses design and construction under one contract, accelerating schedule and giving single-point accountability, but reduces the owner's independent design advocate and can subordinate design quality to builder profit if the owner is not sophisticated;
- integrated project delivery ties owner, designer, and builder into a single multi-party agreement with shared risk and reward, maximizing collaboration and early trade input, but demands high trust, sophisticated participants, and a willingness to share decision authority.

The right method fits the project's complexity, the owner's sophistication, the schedule pressure, and the quality bar. A method that fits a repeat warehouse will not fit a one-of-a-kind cultural building.

### Address Schedule Implications Honestly

Schedule is one of the most common reasons owners choose a method, and one of the most commonly misjudged. Overlapping design and construction can save calendar time, but the overlap shifts risk and demands faster, more decisive coordination.

Address schedule by:

- distinguishing design-bid-build's sequential phases, which are slower but more predictable in quality control, from fast-tracked methods that overlap phases and compress the timeline;
- recognizing that design-build and CM-at-risk can start construction before documents are complete, which accelerates the calendar but requires a team that can make decisions without complete information;
- accounting for the time required to assemble the team, negotiate the contract, and reach a guaranteed maximum price, which can offset apparent schedule gains;
- testing whether the compressed schedule is real or whether it merely front-loads risk and rework that surfaces later as cost or quality problems;
- sequencing long-lead procurement, which often governs the critical path more than the delivery method itself.

A schedule promise that ignores these dynamics will break, and the architect will be blamed for the gap between promise and reality.

### Clarify The Architect's Role And Contract Under Each Method

The delivery method changes what the architect is hired to do, who the architect's client is during construction, and where the architect's liability sits. These are not administrative details; they define the engagement.

Clarify the role by:

- confirming that under design-bid-build the architect typically serves as the owner's independent representative during construction administration, observing and certifying, which preserves design advocacy but concentrates liability;
- recognizing that under design-build the architect may be employed by the builder rather than the owner, shifting the architect's primary loyalty and changing how design decisions are weighed against cost;
- understanding that under CM-at-risk the architect remains the owner's design advocate but coordinates with a CM who holds the construction contract, requiring clear division of site responsibilities;
- acknowledging that under integrated project delivery the architect shares decision authority and risk with the owner and builder, which is collaborative but dilutes unilateral design control;
- ensuring the owner-architect agreement matches the delivery method, because a mismatch between the contract structure and the actual delivery flow creates gaps in responsibility.

The architect must not let the delivery method silently redefine the scope of services or the standard of care.

### Protect Design Quality Regardless Of Method

Some delivery methods exert structural pressure on design quality, rewarding speed and cost reduction over design refinement. The architect must anticipate this pressure and build defenses for the design intent into the process.

Protect quality by:

- defining the design intent, performance criteria, and quality benchmarks in the procurement documents, so that bidders and builders price to a standard, not to a minimum;
- insisting on builder qualifications and selection criteria that value relevant experience and craft, not only price, particularly under design-build and CM-at-risk;
- embedding review points and mockups into the schedule where the design can be verified against execution before mass production;
- documenting design decisions and their rationale so that value engineering later cannot strip intent without an explicit tradeoff being recorded;
- reserving the architect's role in submittal review and site observation proportionate to the project's complexity, rather than allowing the delivery method to reduce oversight.

A delivery method that quietly degrades quality does so because no one protected the quality benchmarks at the outset.

### Document The Decision And Its Assumptions

A delivery method choice made on assumptions that are never written down cannot be defended when those assumptions prove wrong. Owners, funders, and future team members need to see why the method was chosen and what it requires to succeed.

Document by:

- recording the priority ranking, the method comparison, and the rationale for the selected method in a decision memo the owner signs;
- listing the conditions required for the method to perform as intended, such as a capable CM, a collaborative owner, early trade involvement, or a defined GMP process;
- flagging the risks the method does not eliminate, such as design risk under design-build or schedule risk under fast-tracking, so they are managed, not assumed away;
- revisiting the decision if project conditions change materially before construction, because a method chosen for one set of facts may not fit a changed project.

## Common Traps

### Choosing By Familiarity Instead Of Fit

An owner, and sometimes the architect, defaults to the delivery method used on the last project because it is familiar, the contracts are on file, and the team knows the rhythm. The trap is that familiarity masquerades as sound judgment: the method feels safe because it is known, so no one tests whether it fits this project's schedule pressure, cost constraints, or quality ambitions. When the project's actual drivers diverge from the last project's, the familiar method produces friction it was never designed to absorb, surfacing as cost overruns, schedule slips, or design compromises that get blamed on the team rather than on the method mismatch. The harm is a project that underperforms not because anyone did poor work, but because the structural choice was never examined against the project's real needs.

### Assuming Design-Build Is Always Faster And Cheaper

Design-build is frequently sold as inherently faster and cheaper because it overlaps phases and offers single-point accountability. The trap is that the speed comes from overlapping design and construction, which front-loads decision-making and shifts risk to the owner if the design is not sufficiently resolved before construction starts; the cost savings often come from the builder controlling design decisions the owner cannot see, not from efficiency. Owners who assume design-build guarantees both speed and savings discover that the compressed schedule forces rushed decisions, that the single-point accountability means there is no independent advocate when design quality erodes, and that the "savings" were achieved by value-engineering the design intent away. A sophisticated owner can thrive in design-build; an unsophisticated one often gets a faster, cheaper building that is not the building they wanted.

### Ignoring How The Method Redefines The Architect's Standard Of Care

Each delivery method shifts the architect's role and exposure, but teams often carry over contract language and liability assumptions from a different method. The trap mechanism is that under design-bid-build the architect is the owner's independent construction observer with concentrated site liability, while under design-build the architect works for the builder and owes design duties through the builder to the owner, changing who can sue whom and for what. When this goes unrecognized, the architect either accepts liability that the method's structure does not warrant or, conversely, fails to carry the liability that it does, and the owner loses the independent design voice they assumed they had. The harm materializes in a claim, when the contract structure and the actual working relationship do not align and no one can locate the boundary of responsibility.

### Promising Cost Certainty The Method Cannot Deliver

Owners crave a guaranteed maximum price early, and some delivery methods are chosen specifically to provide it. The trap is that a GMP issued before the design is sufficiently developed is not a guarantee, it is a guess dressed as certainty, and the gap is recovered through scope reduction, value engineering, or change orders that defeat the certainty the owner paid for. The false signal is the number itself, which looks firm on paper, concealing that it was built on incomplete documents and optimistic assumptions. The harm is that the owner makes financial commitments based on a false floor, then faces either a degraded building or a budget breach, and the architect is caught between the design intent and a cost ceiling that was never real.

### Letting Schedule Overlap Hide Quality Risk

Fast-tracking, common in CM-at-risk and design-build, overlaps design and construction so that foundations are poured before the envelope is detailed. The trap is that the calendar savings are real and visible, while the quality risk, design decisions made under time pressure with incomplete information, is invisible until the building fails to perform. The false signal is the on-time milestone chart, which rewards speed without measuring the deferred decisions accumulating underneath. The harm is rework, change orders, and performance failures that surface late, often costing more than the time saved, and eroding the design quality the overlap was supposed to serve.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has the owner explicitly ranked cost certainty, schedule, design quality, and risk transfer before a method was named?
- [ ] Has the chosen method been matched against the project's actual complexity, owner sophistication, and schedule drivers rather than familiarity?
- [ ] Are schedule claims for overlapping phases tested against the decision speed and team capability they require?
- [ ] Is the architect's role, client relationship, and liability under the selected method clearly understood and reflected in the contract?
- [ ] Are design intent, performance criteria, and quality benchmarks embedded in procurement so bidders price to a standard?
- [ ] Is the decision, its assumptions, and the conditions required for success documented in a memo the owner has signed?
- [ ] If project conditions changed after the decision, has the delivery method been revisited rather than assumed still correct?
- Does the output stay within the agent's scope, deferring final authority, licensed judgment, or specialist sign-off to the qualified person where the question exceeds the agent's competence?
