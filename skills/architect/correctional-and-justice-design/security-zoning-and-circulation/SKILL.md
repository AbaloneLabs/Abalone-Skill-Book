# Security Zoning and Circulation

Security zoning is the discipline of organizing a building and its site into layers of controlled access, so that the people, the assets, and the operations are protected by a sequence of security boundaries rather than by a single front door. The judgment problem is that security is often treated as a system of doors and cameras added to a plan, when in fact the most effective security is the spatial zoning: the separation of public, restricted, and secure zones; the layering of perimeter, building, and internal boundaries; and the routing of circulation so that the threats (the in-custody person, the armed attacker, the unauthorized visitor) are contained by the geometry before they reach the target. A building with good spatial zoning can be secured by a small staff; a building with poor zoning cannot be secured by any number of doors and cameras. This skill covers the decisions that determine whether the security zoning works, including layered security, zone separation, sally port and search point design, blast and forced entry protection, and the balance of security with daily operations.

## Core Rules

### Layer Security From the Perimeter to the Asset, Not at a Single Front Door

Effective security is layered, with concentric boundaries from the site perimeter (the standoff, the vehicle barrier), to the building envelope (the entrance, the lobby, the screening), to the internal zones (the restricted floors, the secure rooms, the asset), so that a threat must defeat multiple boundaries to reach the target. Each layer must provide detection (the threat is identified), delay (the threat is slowed), and response (the security force can react), and the layers must be designed so that the delay time at each exceeds the response time of the security force. The designer must define the assets and the threats with the security consultant, must establish the layers from the perimeter to the asset, and must verify that the cumulative delay exceeds the response time. The trap is concentrating security at a single front door, which, once breached, leaves the asset unprotected.

### Separate Public, Restricted, and Secure Zones With Independent Circulation

The building must be zoned into public (open access), restricted (credential access), and secure (highest control) zones, and the circulation of each zone must be independent, so that the public cannot reach the restricted or secure zone, and the restricted staff do not cross the secure zone. The separation must hold at every floor, at every core, and at every service area, because a single connection (a shared restroom, a shared corridor, a shared elevator) compromises the zone. The designer must map the three zones on every floor, must verify the circulation of each is independent, and must provide the controlled access points (credential readers, mantraps, sally ports) only where the operations plan requires a connection. The trap is a plan where the zones are nominally separated but connected by a shared service core or a back-of-house corridor, which allows zone-jumping and defeats the zoning.

### Design Sally Ports and Search Points for the Threat and the Throughput

A sally port (a two-door vestibule where a vehicle or a person is contained and searched between zones) or a search point (a screening station) must be designed for the specific threat it addresses and the throughput the operations require. A vehicle sally port must be sized for the vehicle, must have the two doors interlocked so they cannot open simultaneously, must provide the search and the inspection the threat requires, and must drain and ventilate for a contained vehicle. A person screening point must process the peak arrival flow without queuing that backs into the approach, must provide the search technology (magnetometer, x-ray), and must provide the secondary search and the holding for the person who fails screening. The designer must define the threat and the throughput with the security consultant, must size and lay out the sally port or search point for both, and must verify the interlocks, the search, and the holding are integrated.

### Coordinate Blast Resistance, Forced Entry, and Ballistic Protection With the Architecture

For facilities facing blast, forced entry, or ballistic threats (embassies, government buildings, police stations, critical infrastructure), the protective measures must be coordinated with the architecture from the start, because they affect the structure, the envelope, the glazing, and the layout. Blast resistance requires standoff distance (the perimeter and the site), a structure designed for the blast load, and glazing designed to resist the pressure and to reduce laceration from flying glass; forced entry and ballistic resistance require the envelope, the doors, and the glazing rated for the threat, with no weak points at the frames or the joints. The designer must define the threat with the security consultant, must establish the standoff and the structural and envelope requirements, and must integrate the protective measures into the architecture rather than adding them later. The trap is designing the building and applying the protective measures as an overlay, which produces weak points and a compromised design.

### Balance Security With Daily Operations, Dignity, and Life Safety

Security measures must be balanced with the daily operations of the building (the staff must be able to work), the dignity of the users (the public should not feel they are entering a fortress), and the life safety (the security must not trap the occupants in a fire or an emergency). The designer must verify that the security zoning does not unduly burden the daily operations (a credential check at every door slows the work), that the entrance and the lobby project the appropriate character (a courthouse is dignified, not militarized), and that the security measures (the locked doors, the sally ports, the access control) are coordinated with the egress so that the occupants can escape in an emergency. The tradeoff is that more security slows operations and can conflict with egress; the resolution is to design the security and the egress as a single system, with fail-safe releases on the access control that allow egress in an emergency.

## Common Traps

### Security Concentrated at a Single Front Door With No Layered Defense

A building is secured with a screening station at the front entrance and access control on the internal doors, but no perimeter layer and no standoff, because the security was treated as an entrance system. The mechanism of the trap is that a single front door, once breached (by a vehicle, an armed attacker, or an insider), leaves the assets unprotected, because there is no perimeter detection, no standoff to reduce a blast, and no internal layer to delay the threat while the security force responds. The false signal is the entrance screening, which suggests security but masks the absence of the layered defense. The harm is a single point of failure that allows a threat to reach the asset. The defense is to layer security from the perimeter to the asset, with detection, delay, and response at each layer, and to verify the cumulative delay exceeds the response time.

### Zones Nominally Separated but Connected by a Shared Service Core

A building is zoned into public, restricted, and secure areas on the plan, but the zones share a service core (a restroom, a corridor, an elevator, a mechanical room) for efficiency. The mechanism of the trap is that a shared service core allows a person in the public zone to enter the restricted or secure zone through the back of the core, bypassing the access control, because the zone boundary is not continuous at the core, and this zone-jumping defeats the zoning and allows an unauthorized person to reach the asset. The false signal is the zone map, which suggests separation but masks the core connection. The harm is unauthorized access, security incidents, and a zoning system that does not actually control access. The defense is to map the three zones on every floor, verify the circulation of each is independent, and eliminate shared cores that connect the zones.

### A Sally Port or Search Point Sized for the Vehicle or the Person but Not the Throughput

A vehicle sally port is sized for the largest vehicle, or a screening point is sized for the magnetometer, but neither is sized for the peak arrival flow. The mechanism of the trap is that a sally port or a screening point must process the peak throughput within the time the operations allow, and a point sized for the vehicle or the technology but not the flow produces a queue that backs into the approach, delays the operations, and tempts the staff to bypass the procedure (propping a door, waving through the search) to relieve the queue, which defeats the security. The false signal is the sally port or the screening station on the plan, which suggests a security point but masks the throughput failure. The harm is operational delays, procedural bypass, and a security point that is circumvented under pressure. The defense is to size the sally port and the search point for the peak throughput, provide the queue space, and verify the procedure is not bypassed under load.

### Blast and Forced Entry Measures Applied as an Overlay to a Fixed Design

A building is designed for its architecture, and the blast resistance, the forced entry rating, and the ballistic protection are applied as an overlay during construction documents, when the security consultant is engaged. The mechanism of the trap is that the protective measures affect the structure, the standoff, the envelope, and the glazing, and applying them after the design is fixed produces weak points (the structure is under-designed for the blast, the glazing frame fails before the glass, the entrance has no standoff) and a compromised architecture that must be retrofitted at high cost. The false signal is the applied protection, which suggests a secured building but masks the weak points. The harm is a building that does not actually resist the threat, and expensive retrofits. The defense is to define the threat early, establish the standoff and the structural and envelope requirements, and integrate the protective measures into the architecture from the start.

### Security Measures That Conflict With Egress and Trap Occupants in an Emergency

A building is secured with locked doors, access control, and sally ports, and the security is effective against the threat, but the egress is not coordinated with the security. The mechanism of the trap is that security measures (locked doors, interlocked sally ports, access-controlled exits) can prevent egress in a fire or an emergency, trapping the occupants, because the security that delays a threat also delays the escape, unless the access control is fail-safe and coordinated with the fire alarm to release in an emergency. The false signal is the effective security, which suggests protection but masks the egress conflict. The harm is occupants trapped in an emergency, which is a life safety failure and a liability. The defense is to design the security and the egress as a single system, with fail-safe releases on the access control that allow egress when the fire alarm activates, and to verify the egress paths are not blocked by security measures.

## Self-Check

- Did I define the assets and the threats with the security consultant, establish the layers from the perimeter to the asset, and verify the cumulative delay at each layer exceeds the response time?
- Did I map the public, restricted, and secure zones on every floor, verify the circulation of each is independent, and eliminate shared service cores that connect the zones?
- Did I size the sally ports and search points for both the threat (the vehicle, the person) and the peak throughput, provide the queue space, and verify the procedure is not bypassed under load?
- Did I define the blast, forced entry, and ballistic threats early, establish the standoff and the structural and envelope requirements, and integrate the protective measures into the architecture from the start?
- Did I verify the glazing, the frames, and the doors are rated for the threat, with no weak points at the joints?
- Did I balance the security with the daily operations, the dignity of the users, and the life safety, so the building is secure but functional and humane?
- Did I design the security and the egress as a single system, with fail-safe releases on the access control that allow egress in an emergency, and verify the egress paths are not blocked?
- Did I coordinate the entrance and the lobby to project the appropriate character (dignified for a courthouse, secure for a police station) rather than a generic fortress?
