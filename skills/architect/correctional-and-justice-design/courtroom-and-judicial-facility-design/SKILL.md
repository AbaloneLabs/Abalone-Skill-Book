# Courtroom and Judicial Facility Design

A courthouse is a building whose core is the courtroom, and whose architecture must simultaneously project the dignity of the law, protect the safety of the participants, and run the daily proceedings efficiently. The judgment problem is that a courtroom looks like a single formal room, so designers tend to shape the well and underweight the three-zone circulation (public, restricted, secure), the prisoner transport, the jury and witness support, and the technology that modern litigation requires. A courtroom where the prisoner in custody crosses the public lobby creates a security incident; a courtroom where the jury cannot deliberate privately or the witness cannot testify safely compromises the proceeding; a courtroom where the evidence technology was added after construction is wired across the historic millwork. This skill covers the decisions that determine whether the courtroom and the courthouse function, including the courtroom well and spatial relationships, three-zone circulation, prisoner transport and the secure path, jury and witness support, and technology integration.

## Core Rules

### Design the Courtroom Well for the Spatial Relationships the Proceeding Requires

The courtroom well must organize the judge, the jury, the witness, the parties, the counsel, the court staff, and the defendant in spatial relationships that serve the proceeding and the dignity of the court. The judge must be elevated and central, with sightlines to every participant; the jury must see the witness, the evidence, and the judge; the witness must testify from a position visible to the judge, the jury, and counsel, with a secure path to the stand; the parties and counsel must have work surfaces, sightlines to the judge and the witness, and access to the evidence technology; and the defendant (in custody or not) must be positioned per the proceeding type. The designer must lay out the well for the specific court type (criminal, civil, family, appellate), must verify the sightlines among all participants, and must size the well for the participant count the proceeding type requires. The trap is laying out a generic well that does not match the court type and discovering the proceeding cannot run as designed.

### Separate Public, Restricted, and Secure Circulation Into Three Independent Zones

A courthouse must separate three circulation zones that must never cross: the public zone (the lobby, the corridors, the spectator seating), the restricted zone (the judges, the staff, the jury, the counsel, accessed by credential), and the secure zone (the in-custody defendants, accessed only through the prisoner transport path). The three zones must be completely independent, with the public never entering the restricted or secure zone, the restricted staff never crossing the secure path, and the in-custody defendant reaching the courtroom dock through a secure route from the sally port that does not cross the public or the restricted zone. The designer must map the three zones, must verify they do not cross at any point, and must provide the controlled connections (a security door, a sally, a vestibule) only where the operations plan requires them. The trap is a compact plan where the zones touch or cross, which produces a security breach and a safety incident.

### Design the Prisoner Transport Path From Sally Port to Dock as a Secure Sequence

The in-custody defendant must travel from the sally port (where the transport vehicle enters) to the courtroom dock (where they sit during the proceeding) through a fully secure path that does not cross the public or the restricted zone, and that path must be designed as a sequence of secure holding and movement spaces. The path includes the sally port, the central holding, the interview booths (where counsel meets the client), the secure elevator, the courtroom holding cell, and the dock, and every segment must be secure, observable, and ligature-resistant. The designer must lay out the secure path from the sally port to the dock, must verify it does not cross the other zones, must provide the holding cells and the interview booths at the required count, and must detail the holding cells for ligature resistance and observation. The trap is routing the secure path through a shared corridor or elevator, which produces a crossing and a security incident.

### Provide Jury Deliberation, Witness Waiting, and Support Spaces That Protect the Proceeding

The jury must have a private deliberation room adjacent to the courtroom (with a restroom and a secure path that does not cross the public), the witness must have a secure waiting room (separate from the parties and the public, especially in cases of intimidation risk), and the court must have the support spaces (the judge's chambers, the clerk's office, the evidence storage, the court reporter's position) that the proceeding requires. The designer must locate the jury deliberation room adjacent to the courtroom with a direct and private path, must provide the witness waiting room in a secure and separate location, and must size and locate the support spaces for the court type and the docket. The trap is treating the jury room and the witness room as generic support spaces and discovering the proceeding is compromised by a lack of privacy or security.

### Integrate Evidence Presentation, Recording, and Technology Into the Well From the Start

Modern litigation uses evidence presentation (displays, document cameras, video), real-time recording or transcription, remote testimony, and assistive listening, and this technology must be integrated into the courtroom well and the infrastructure from the start, not added after construction. The designer must coordinate the technology (the displays at the judge, the witness, the jury, and counsel; the evidence camera; the recording system; the remote testimony link; the assistive listening) with the well layout, must provide the power, data, and conduit in the well and the floor, and must verify the technology does not compromise the dignity or the sightlines of the court. The trap is designing the well for a traditional proceeding and adding the technology later, which produces exposed conduit, blocked sightlines, and compromised millwork.

## Common Traps

### A Generic Well Layout That Does Not Match the Court Type

A courtroom is laid out with a standard well that approximates the participant positions, without reference to the specific court type it will serve. The mechanism of the trap is that criminal, civil, family, and appellate courts have different participant configurations (a criminal court needs a dock and a secure path; a family court needs a less adversarial layout; an appellate court has no witness or jury), and a generic well that does not match the court type produces a proceeding that cannot run as designed, with participants in the wrong positions, sightlines that fail, and operational workarounds. The false signal is the formal courtroom, which suggests a functioning court but masks the mismatch with the actual proceeding. The harm is disrupted proceedings, operational friction, and a courtroom that does not serve its purpose. The defense is to lay out the well for the specific court type, verify the participant positions and sightlines, and size the well for the participant count.

### Public, Restricted, and Secure Zones That Cross in a Compact Plan

A courthouse is planned for compactness, and the public lobby, the staff corridor, and the prisoner transport path share a core or cross at a vestibule. The mechanism of the trap is that the three circulation zones must be completely independent for security and safety, and a crossing allows the public to enter the restricted zone, the staff to encounter the in-custody defendant, or the defendant to access the public, any of which is a security breach and a safety incident that can shut down the courthouse and generate liability. The false signal is the compact plan, which suggests efficiency but masks the zone crossing. The harm is security incidents, lockdowns, and liability. The defense is to map the three zones, verify they do not cross at any point, and provide controlled connections only where the operations plan requires.

### The Prisoner Transport Path Routed Through a Shared Corridor or Elevator

The secure path from the sally port to the courtroom dock is routed through a corridor or an elevator that also serves the restricted or public zone, because the building core was shared for efficiency. The mechanism of the trap is that the in-custody defendant is the highest-risk person in the courthouse, and a secure path that shares a corridor or an elevator with the restricted or public zone produces an encounter (the defendant with the victim's family, the defendant with the public) that is a security incident and a safety risk, and that can compromise the proceeding and the verdict. The false signal is the shared core, which suggests efficiency but masks the security failure. The harm is security incidents, intimidation, escapes, and liability. The defense is to lay out a fully secure path from sally port to dock, provide dedicated secure elevators, and verify the path crosses no other zone.

### Jury and Witness Spaces Treated as Generic Support, Compromising Privacy and Security

The jury deliberation room and the witness waiting room are sized and located as generic support spaces, near the public corridor or the staff area. The mechanism of the trap is that the jury must deliberate privately and reach the courtroom without crossing the public (to avoid influence and intimidation), and the witness (especially in cases with intimidation risk) must wait securely and separately from the parties and the public, and treating these as generic spaces produces a jury that is approached in the corridor, a witness who is intimidated, and a proceeding that is compromised. The false signal is the support space provision, which suggests adequacy but masks the privacy and security failure. The harm is compromised proceedings, mistrials, and safety risk to jurors and witnesses. The defense is to locate the jury deliberation room adjacent to the courtroom with a private path, and the witness waiting room in a secure and separate location.

### Evidence and Recording Technology Added After the Courtroom Is Built

The courtroom is designed and built for a traditional proceeding, and the evidence presentation, recording, and remote testimony technology is added during fit-out or after occupancy. The mechanism of the trap is that modern courtroom technology requires power, data, conduit, and display positions integrated into the well and the floor, and adding it after construction produces exposed conduit, cut millwork, blocked sightlines, and a courtroom whose dignity and function are compromised by the retrofit. The false signal is the completed courtroom, which suggests a ready space but masks the technology shortfall. The harm is operational friction, compromised dignity, and expensive retrofits. The defense is to coordinate the evidence, recording, and remote testimony technology with the well layout during design, provide the infrastructure in the well and floor, and verify the technology does not compromise sightlines or dignity.
