# Detention and Housing Unit Design

A detention housing unit is the place where confined people sleep, eat, and spend most of their day, and its design directly governs the safety of the staff, the safety of the confined population, the suicide risk, and the conditions that courts and oversight bodies measure against constitutional and human rights standards. The judgment problem is that a housing unit looks like a repetitive cell block, so designers tend to lay out the cells and the dayroom and underweight the supervision model, the classification logic, the ligature and anti-suicide detailing, and the operational sequence that determine whether the unit is safe and humane. A unit designed for indirect supervision that is staffed for direct supervision produces blind spots and assaults; a cell with a ligature point produces a suicide; a dayroom that cannot be observed from the control station produces violence that the staff cannot see. This skill covers the decisions that determine whether the housing unit is safe, secure, and humane, including the supervision model, cell and dayroom design, classification and separation, ligature resistance and anti-suicide design, and the balance of security with conditions.

## Core Rules

### Commit to a Supervision Model and Design the Unit Geometry to Match It

The supervision model (direct supervision, where staff are stationed inside the dayroom with the population; indirect supervision, where staff observe from a control station through glazing; or hybrid) must be chosen before the unit is laid out, because the geometry of the unit must serve the model. A direct supervision unit requires a dayroom and a staff position that together allow the staff member to see the entire dayroom, the cells, and the access points from within the space, with no blind spots; an indirect supervision unit requires a control station with sightlines through secure glazing to every part of the dayroom and the cell fronts. The designer must commit to the model with the operator, must verify that the unit geometry provides the sightlines the model requires from every staff position, and must eliminate blind spots (reentrant corners, recessed cell entries, shielded alcoves) that the model cannot supervise. The trap is designing a unit for one model and staffing it for another, which produces the blind spots and the unsupervised spaces where incidents occur.

### Size Cells and Dayrooms to the Classification Standard and the Rated Capacity

Cell size, dayroom area, and the unit capacity must meet the applicable standard (the American Correctional Association, a state jail standard, or a constitutional minimum established by case law), and they must be designed for the classification (the security level and the population type) the unit will house. A cell rated for one confined person that is double-bunked exceeds the standard and degrades conditions; a dayroom sized for the rated capacity that is overflowed produces crowding that drives violence. The designer must verify the cell area, the dayroom area per person, and the unit capacity against the applicable standard, must design for the classification (maximum, medium, minimum; male, female; adult, juvenile; special needs), and must resist the pressure to rate the unit above the standard to absorb population pressure. The trap is sizing to the minimum and accepting overflow, which produces conditions that fail the standard and that courts and oversight bodies cite.

### Detail Every Fixture and Surface for Ligature Resistance and Anti-Suicide Safety

Suicide is a leading cause of death in detention, and the cell and the dayroom must be detailed so that there is no ligature point a confined person can use. Every fixture (the bunk, the toilet, the sink, the light, the vent, the door hardware) must be ligature-resistant, with no gaps, hooks, or projections that can support a ligature; the cell must be designed so that a person cannot hang from any point; and the materials must resist being fashioned into a weapon or a ligature. The designer must specify anti-ligature fixtures from manufacturers who test them, must detail the cell (the ceiling, the light, the vent, the door, the bunk) to eliminate ligature points, and must verify the design against the institution's suicide prevention protocol. The trap is specifying a standard fixture that has a hidden ligature point, which produces a suicide risk that is invisible until a death occurs.

### Separate the Population by Classification to Prevent Victimization and Violence

The housing unit must separate the population by classification (security level, gender, age, special needs, and vulnerability), because mixing classifications produces victimization (the strong prey on the weak), violence, and liability. The designer must understand the facility's classification system, must provide the units and the separation (by unit, by wing, by floor) that the classification requires, and must provide the special housing (protective custody, medical, mental health, disciplinary) that the population needs. The separation must extend to the circulation (a maximum-security population must not cross a minimum-security population in a corridor) and to the program and service access (each classification must reach visitation, medical, recreation, and programs without a conflicting crossing). The trap is providing the right number of beds in the wrong configuration, so the classification cannot be maintained and populations are mixed.

### Balance Security, Safety, and Humane Conditions of Confinement

A detention housing unit must be secure (it must confine), safe (it must protect staff and population from harm), and humane (it must provide conditions that meet constitutional and human rights standards), and these three goals must be balanced in every design decision. Security drives toward hard materials, controlled openings, and minimal amenity; safety drives toward observation, ligature resistance, and classification separation; humane conditions drive toward natural light, air quality, adequate space, and connection to programs and visitation. The designer must meet all three, must not let security dominate to the exclusion of humane conditions (which courts cite as cruel and unusual), and must verify the unit provides natural light, outdoor recreation, adequate ventilation, and program access that the standards require. The tradeoff is that humane design costs more and may feel less punitive, but the failure to provide humane conditions produces litigation, oversight intervention, and worse outcomes for the population and the staff.

## Common Traps

### A Unit Designed for Indirect Supervision Staffed for Direct, Producing Blind Spots

A housing unit is designed with a control station and glazing for indirect supervision, but the facility is staffed for direct supervision (a staff member in the dayroom), because the staffing model changed after design or because the operator assumed the two were interchangeable. The mechanism of the trap is that an indirect-supervision geometry has blind spots from the dayroom floor (reentrant corners, recessed cell entries) that the control station sees but a floor staff member may not, so when the unit is staffed for direct supervision, those blind spots are unsupervised and incidents occur in them. The false signal is the supervision designation, which suggests a supervised unit but masks the mismatch between the geometry and the actual staffing. The harm is assaults, suicides, and violence in the unsupervised spaces. The defense is to commit to the supervision model before design, design the geometry to match it, and verify sightlines from every staff position the model uses.

### Cells and Dayrooms Sized to the Minimum and Overflowed Beyond the Rated Capacity

A unit is designed to the minimum cell and dayroom area and the rated capacity, and the standard is met on paper, but population pressure leads to double-bunking and dayroom overflow. The mechanism of the trap is that detention populations fluctuate and often exceed the design capacity, and a unit sized to the minimum cannot absorb the overflow without degrading the conditions below the standard, producing crowding that drives violence, mental health decline, and conditions that courts and oversight bodies cite as unconstitutional. The false signal is the standard compliance at the rated capacity, which suggests adequacy but masks the failure under overflow. The harm is crowding, violence, litigation, and oversight intervention. The defense is to size to the standard, resist rating above the standard, and design the unit and the facility with the flexibility to absorb population fluctuation without overflow.

### A Standard Fixture With a Hidden Ligature Point That Becomes a Suicide Risk

A cell is specified with standard detention-grade fixtures (a bunk, a toilet, a light), and the fixtures look robust and secure. The mechanism of the trap is that a fixture that looks secure may have a hidden ligature point (a gap between the bunk and the wall, a projection on the light, a hook on the vent) that a person in crisis can use, and the ligature point is invisible until a suicide occurs, at which point the design is implicated in the death. The false signal is the robust fixture appearance, which suggests security but masks the ligature risk. The harm is a suicide, which is the worst outcome in detention, and litigation and oversight scrutiny that follows. The defense is to specify anti-ligature fixtures from manufacturers who test them, detail every surface to eliminate ligature points, and verify the design against the suicide prevention protocol.

### Populations Mixed Because the Bed Count Is Right but the Configuration Is Wrong

A facility provides the total bed count the population requires, but the beds are in the wrong unit configuration (too many in one classification, too few in another), so the classification cannot be maintained. The mechanism of the trap is that detention populations must be separated by classification to prevent victimization and violence, and a facility with the right total beds but the wrong configuration is forced to mix classifications, placing vulnerable people with predatory people, or mixing security levels, which produces assaults, extortion, and liability. The false signal is the total bed count, which suggests adequate capacity but masks the configuration failure. The harm is victimization, violence, and litigation. The defense is to design the unit configuration to the facility's classification system, provide the special housing the population needs, and verify the circulation and service access do not force classification crossings.

### Security Hardened to the Exclusion of Humane Conditions, Inviting Litigation

A housing unit is designed with maximum security hardening (hard materials, minimal openings, no natural light, minimal amenity) because security was the dominant driver, and the humane conditions (natural light, ventilation, outdoor recreation, program access) are treated as optional. The mechanism of the trap is that constitutional and human rights standards require humane conditions of confinement, and a unit hardened to the exclusion of those conditions is vulnerable to litigation, court oversight, and findings of cruel and unusual punishment, which can force expensive remediation and operational control of the facility by the courts. The false signal is the secure design, which suggests a safe facility but masks the conditions failure. The harm is litigation, court oversight, remediation cost, and worse outcomes for population and staff. The defense is to balance security, safety, and humane conditions in every decision, and to verify natural light, ventilation, recreation, and program access meet the standards.

## Self-Check

- Did I commit to a supervision model with the operator before design, and verify the unit geometry provides the required sightlines from every staff position with no blind spots?
- Did I verify the cell area, dayroom area per person, and unit capacity against the applicable standard and the classification, and resist rating above the standard to absorb population pressure?
- Did I specify anti-ligature fixtures from tested manufacturers, detail every surface (ceiling, light, vent, door, bunk) to eliminate ligature points, and verify the design against the suicide prevention protocol?
- Did I design the unit configuration to the facility's classification system, provide the special housing (protective custody, medical, mental health, disciplinary) the population needs, and verify the bed configuration matches the classification mix?
- Did I verify the circulation and the service access (visitation, medical, recreation, programs) do not force classification crossings between populations?
- Did I balance security, safety, and humane conditions, and verify the unit provides natural light, outdoor recreation, adequate ventilation, and program access that the standards require?
- Did I design the unit and the facility with flexibility to absorb population fluctuation without overflow below the standard?
- Did I coordinate the housing unit design with the operator's actual staffing model, so the geometry and the staff positions match the way the unit will be run?
