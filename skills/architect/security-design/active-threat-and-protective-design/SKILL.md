# Active Threat And Protective Design

Active threat and protective design addresses the most severe security risks — active shooters, hostile vehicle attacks, blasts, and forced entry — through a combination of standoff, barriers, hardened assemblies, and response planning that protects occupants while the building continues to function as a normal environment every other day. It is the most demanding security discipline because the measures must be effective under rare, extreme events without degrading the daily experience or violating life safety, and because the threat tactics evolve faster than codes. Agents often treat protective design as a specification of blast-resistant glazing or ballistic walls and miss that the most important measures — standoff distance, vehicle barrier planning, and the integration of protective assemblies with the plan and the structure — are architectural decisions made at the earliest design. The architect owns this integration because no security consultant controls the plan, the structure, or the daily function. The goal is a building that resists and survives an active threat while remaining a humane, usable environment in normal conditions.

## Core Rules

### Define The Design Basis Threat And The Performance Objective

Protective design begins with a design basis threat — the specific adversary, tactic, weapon, and delivery method the design must resist — and a performance objective — what the building must do under that threat, from preventing entry to protecting occupants to remaining functional. The design basis threat is developed through a risk assessment with the owner, the security consultant, and where relevant public safety, and it must reflect the actual threat environment, not a generic standard. The performance objective — prevent, protect, or survive — drives the level of protection and the investment, and it must be set explicitly because it determines the design. Document the design basis threat and the performance objective, because the protective measures will be evaluated against whether they resist the documented threat and meet the documented objective.

### Maximize Standoff Distance As The Primary Protective Measure

Standoff distance — the separation between a threat, particularly a vehicle-borne weapon or blast, and the building — is the single most effective protective measure, because blast effects diminish dramatically with distance and because distance provides time for detection and response. Maximize standoff at the site planning stage by setting the building back from the perimeter, by controlling vehicle approach through site geometry and barriers, and by designing the landscape and the site features to enforce the standoff. Standoff is free protection that no assembly can replace, and a building crowded to the curb cannot be protected by hardening alone. The architect must fight for standoff at the site planning stage, because once the building is sited the opportunity is lost.

### Plan Vehicle Barriers And Hostile Vehicle Mitigation

Hostile vehicle attacks — whether weaponized or accidental — are mitigated through vehicle barriers that stop a vehicle at the standoff line, and the barrier planning is an architectural and site design challenge. Select barrier types — bollards, planters, walls, reinforced landscape — rated to stop the design vehicle at the design speed, and lay them out to enforce the standoff without blocking legitimate access, emergency response, or life safety. Coordinate the barrier layout with the site circulation, the loading and service access, and the perimeter security, because barriers that block operations will be removed or bypassed. Integrate the barriers into the design so they contribute to the public realm rather than announcing the threat, because hostile-vehicle mitigation can be both effective and welcoming.

### Specify Ballistic, Forced-Entry, And Blast-Resistant Assemblies

Where the design basis threat includes weapons, forced entry, or blast, the assemblies at the threatened perimeter — glazing, walls, doors, and louvers — must be specified to resist the specific threat, with tested assemblies and documented performance. Ballistic-resistant glazing and walls are rated against specific ammunition, forced-entry assemblies are rated against specific tools and time, and blast-resistant assemblies are designed for specific peak pressure and impulse, and the ratings must match the design basis threat. Coordinate the assemblies with the structure, because blast loads transfer to the structure that must resist them, and with the daily function, because protective assemblies are heavier, thicker, and more constraining than standard construction. The architect must specify the assemblies with the consultant and integrate them into the design, not delegate the specification.

### Integrate Lockdown, Shelter-In-Place, And Response

Active threat response depends on the building's ability to support lockdown, shelter-in-place, and evacuation, and the design must enable each. Design rooms and zones that can be secured — lockable doors, hardened assemblies, communication — to support lockdown; design the envelope, the HVAC, and the filtration to support shelter-in-place against environmental threats; and design the egress and the communication to support evacuation and response. Coordinate the response capabilities with the building's emergency plan, the security operations, and first responder access, because the building is part of the response system. The architect must design for the response, not only for resistance, because the outcome of an active threat depends on how the building supports the people in it.

### Reconcile Protective Design With Life Safety And Accessibility

Protective measures must not compromise life safety: barriers must not block egress, hardened doors must permit evacuation, access control must fail safe, and the protective envelope must not trap occupants. This reconciliation is the most difficult coordination in protective design, because the measures that resist a threat often conflict with the measures that protect against fire and emergency, and the conflicts must be resolved assembly by assembly and path by path. Ensure that egress routes are not blocked by vehicle barriers, that hardened doors can be opened from the inside, and that the protective design accommodates the full range of occupants including those with disabilities. The architect must integrate protective design and life safety so that they support rather than conflict.

### Balance Protection With Daily Function And Aesthetic

A building that is protective but unusable, ugly, or alienating has failed, because the protective measures must operate within a building that functions every day. Integrate protective assemblies so they are indistinguishable from standard construction where possible, use the landscape and the architecture to deliver protection rather than fortress hardware, and engage the users to understand how the measures affect their daily experience. The most successful protective design is invisible to the daily user and effective under the threat, and achieving that balance is the architect's responsibility. The building must be both protected and a good building, and the architect must not sacrifice either goal.

## Common Traps

### Specifying Protective Assemblies Without A Design Basis Threat

The team specifies blast-resistant glazing and ballistic walls based on a generic standard or a consultant default, without defining the specific threat the assemblies must resist, so the measures are either inadequate for the actual risk or wasteful for a risk that does not apply. The mechanism is that protective assemblies feel like the solution and the threat analysis feels like overhead, and the false signal is that the building has protective measures. The harm is that the measures do not match the threat — they fail when needed or waste investment — and the protection claim is false. The design basis threat and performance objective must be defined and documented before assemblies are specified.

### Sacrificing Standoff For Site Efficiency

The building is sited at the curb to maximize site efficiency or street presence, the standoff is lost, and the team attempts to compensate with hardened assemblies that cannot replace the protection distance would have provided. The mechanism is that site efficiency is the visible driver and standoff feels like wasted land, and the false signal is that the building fits the site. The harm is that the building is far more vulnerable to blast and vehicle attack, the hardening costs escalate, and the protection is inferior to what standoff would have delivered for free. Standoff must be protected at site planning, even at the cost of site efficiency, because it is the primary protective measure.

### Installing Vehicle Barriers That Block Operations Or Egress

The bollards and barriers are laid out to enforce the standoff but block loading docks, fire lanes, accessible routes, or egress paths, so they are removed, bypassed, or never installed, defeating the protection. The mechanism is that barriers are designed for threat stopping and the operational and life safety coordination is overlooked, and the false signal is that the barrier line is established. The harm is that the barriers compromise operations or safety, they are removed under pressure, and the standoff is lost. Vehicle barriers must be coordinated with circulation, loading, emergency access, and egress, with crash gates and removable bollards where legitimate access is required.

### Hardening The Threat Perimeter But Neglecting The Response

The assemblies resist the threat, but the building cannot support lockdown, shelter-in-place, or evacuation, so the occupants are protected from the initial threat but cannot respond, and the outcome is poor. The mechanism is that resistance is the visible protection and response is treated as operational, and the false signal is that the building is hardened and so it is protected. The harm is that the occupants cannot lock down, shelter, or evacuate effectively, and the protection does not translate to safety. The design must support the full response — lockdown, shelter, evacuation, communication, first responder access — not only the resistance.

### Allowing Protective Measures To Compromise Life Safety

The hardened doors lock to resist forced entry but cannot be opened for egress, the vehicle barriers block the fire lane, or the blast-resistant envelope traps occupants, so the protective measures create life safety hazards. The mechanism is that protective and life safety systems are designed separately and not reconciled, and the false signal is that the building is protected. The harm is that the building violates egress code, fails inspection, or, worst case, endangers occupants in a non-security emergency. Protective measures must be reconciled with life safety assembly by assembly, with egress always possible and barriers never blocking evacuation.

### Degrading The Building Into A Fortress That Fails As A Place

The protective measures are imposed as visible fortress hardware — heavy barriers, dark glazing, threatening signage — producing a building that is protected but hostile, alienating the users and degrading the public realm. The mechanism is that protection is prioritized over daily experience and integration is not pursued, and the false signal is that the building is secure. The harm is that the building is avoided, the users feel unsafe in a building designed to protect them, and the place fails despite its protection. Protective measures must be integrated into the design so they are effective under threat and invisible or contributory in daily use, and the building must remain a good place.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is the protective design grounded in a documented design basis threat and performance objective developed through a risk assessment with the owner and security consultant?
- [ ] Has standoff distance been maximized at site planning, with the building set back and vehicle approach controlled, even at the cost of site efficiency?
- [ ] Are vehicle barriers rated for the design vehicle and speed, laid out to enforce the standoff without blocking legitimate access, emergency response, or egress?
- [ ] Are ballistic, forced-entry, and blast-resistant assemblies specified to match the design basis threat, with tested ratings and coordination with the structure?
- [ ] Does the design support lockdown, shelter-in-place, evacuation, communication, and first responder access as an integrated response system?
- [ ] Are protective measures reconciled with life safety assembly by assembly, so that egress is always possible and barriers never block evacuation?
- [ ] Is the protective design integrated so it is effective under threat and invisible or contributory in daily use, avoiding a fortress aesthetic?
- [ ] Have the users been engaged to ensure the protective measures support rather than degrade the daily experience of the building?
