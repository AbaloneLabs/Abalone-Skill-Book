# Energy Modeling And Passive Design Strategies

Energy modeling and passive design are the tools by which a building's environmental performance is predicted, optimized, and verified, and they are most powerful when used early, iteratively, and in service of design decisions rather than as a compliance afterthought. The judgment problem is that energy modeling is easy to use badly: run once at the end to demonstrate compliance, with inputs that do not reflect the actual design, producing a report that documents a performance the building will not achieve; or run with a complexity that obscures the design decisions the model should inform. The skilled architect uses energy modeling early and iteratively to inform the design decisions that determine performance (orientation, massing, envelope, glazing, shading, systems), implements the passive strategies that reduce the loads before the active systems are sized, and balances the energy performance against the first cost and the occupant comfort. The recurring failures are the model run too late, the model with unrealistic inputs, the passive strategies ignored in favor of the active systems, the optimization for the energy cost without the comfort, and the model that does not match the as-built building. The architect's role is to use the modeling to inform the design, to implement the passive strategies, and to balance the performance, the cost, and the comfort.

## Core Rules

### Use Energy Modeling Early And Iteratively To Inform Design

Energy modeling is most valuable when used early, at the concept and the schematic phases, to inform the decisions that determine performance (the orientation, the massing, the envelope, the glazing), and iteratively, at each phase, to refine the design. Use the modeling early and iteratively, not once at the end.

- Use the early-concept modeling (the simple models, the shoebox models, the parametric studies) to inform the orientation, the massing, the envelope, and the glazing, because these decisions determine the performance and are hard to change later.
- Use the design-development modeling (the detailed models) to refine the envelope, the shading, the systems, and the controls, because these decisions refine the performance.
- Use the compliance modeling (the code-compliance models, the certification models) to document the performance, because the documentation is required for the code and the certification.
- Run the model iteratively, at each phase, and use the results to inform the next decisions, because the modeling is a design tool, not a documentation tool.

### Implement The Passive Strategies Before The Active Systems

The passive strategies (the orientation, the shading, the thermal mass, the natural ventilation, the daylighting) reduce the loads before the active systems (the heating, the cooling, the lighting) are sized, and the reduction produces a building that requires less active system capacity and less energy. Implement the passive strategies first, and size the active systems for the reduced loads.

- Implement the orientation: the long axis east-west for the daylight and the solar control, because the orientation is the first passive decision and the most consequential.
- Implement the shading: the overhangs, the fins, the louvers, and the external shading that block the summer sun and admit the winter sun, because the shading reduces the cooling load and the glare.
- Implement the thermal mass: the concrete, the masonry, and the heavy materials that absorb the heat during the day and release it at night, because the thermal mass reduces the temperature swing and the cooling load.
- Implement the natural ventilation: the operable windows, the stack ventilation, and the cross-ventilation that cool the building with the outside air, because the natural ventilation reduces the cooling load and the energy.
- Implement the daylighting: the sidelighting, the toplighting, and the light shelves that daylight the space, because the daylighting reduces the lighting load and the energy.
- Size the active systems (the heating, the cooling, the lighting) for the reduced loads, because the passive strategies reduce the loads and the active systems should not be oversized for the unreduced loads.

### Optimize The Envelope Performance

The envelope (the insulation, the glazing, the air sealing, the thermal bridging) is the primary determinant of the building's heating and cooling loads, and the envelope's performance should be optimized, not just code-compliant. Optimize the envelope, and document the basis.

- Optimize the insulation: the R-value of the walls, the roof, and the foundation, and the continuity of the insulation (the thermal bridging), because the insulation determines the heating and cooling loads.
- Optimize the glazing: the U-value, the solar heat gain coefficient, the visible transmittance, and the glazing area, because the glazing is the largest load and the largest daylight source, and the optimization balances the loads and the daylight.
- Optimize the air sealing: the air barrier's continuity and the leakage rate, because the air leakage carries the moisture and the heat, and the sealing reduces both.
- Optimize the thermal bridging: the continuous insulation, the thermal breaks, and the detail at the structural connections, because the thermal bridging short-circuits the insulation and increases the loads.

### Balance The Energy Performance Against The First Cost And The Comfort

The energy performance is one objective among three — the performance, the first cost, and the occupant comfort — and the three are often in tension. Balance them deliberately, and document the trade-offs.

- Balance the performance against the first cost: the higher-performance envelope and the systems cost more, and the cost should be weighed against the energy savings, because the optimization is for the life-cycle cost, not the energy cost alone.
- Balance the performance against the comfort: the higher-performance envelope and the passive strategies affect the comfort (the daylight and the glare, the thermal mass and the temperature swing, the natural ventilation and the drafts), and the comfort should be weighed against the energy.
- Use the parametric analysis (the studies of the options, with the performance, the cost, and the comfort for each) to inform the balance, because the parametric analysis reveals the trade-offs.
- Document the trade-offs and the rationale, because the balance is a design judgment that should be explicit, not implicit.

### Use Realistic Inputs In The Model

The model is only as good as its inputs, and the inputs should reflect the actual design, the actual use, and the actual operations. Use realistic inputs, and document the basis.

- Use the actual design inputs: the actual envelope, the actual glazing, the actual systems, and the actual controls, because the model should reflect the design, not an idealized version of it.
- Use the actual use inputs: the actual occupancy, the actual schedule, the actual plug loads, and the actual lighting, because the model should reflect the use, not a standard profile.
- Use the actual operations inputs: the actual utility rates, the actual climate, and the actual system efficiencies, because the model should reflect the operations, not an assumption.
- Calibrate the model to the actual building, when the actual data is available, because the calibration confirms the model's accuracy and the building's performance.

### Coordinate The Model With The Design And The Construction

The model should be coordinated with the design (the model reflects the design) and the construction (the building reflects the model), and the coordination should be maintained through the project. Coordinate the model, and confirm the coordination.

- Coordinate the model with the design: the model's inputs reflect the design decisions, and the design decisions are informed by the model, because the model and the design are a feedback loop.
- Coordinate the model with the construction: the building is built to the design the model reflects, and the model is updated when the design changes, because the model should reflect the as-built building.
- Confirm the coordination at each phase, and update the model when the design changes, because the model that does not reflect the design is a model that misleads.
- Use the model to verify the as-built performance, when the building is operating, because the verification confirms the design and the model.

## Common Traps

### The Model Run Too Late

When the energy model is run once at the end of the design — to demonstrate the code compliance or the certification — the decisions that determine the performance (the orientation, the massing, the envelope, the glazing) have already been made, and the model documents a performance the design cannot improve, because the model was not used to inform the decisions. The mechanism is that the model reads as a compliance tool, so the late run feels appropriate, while the decisions the model should have informed were made without it, and the performance is fixed by the design the model documents. The harm is that the building's performance is lower than it could have been, the opportunities for the passive strategies and the envelope optimization are lost, and the model is a compliance document rather than a design tool, while the architect believed the performance was addressed. The defense is to use the energy modeling early and iteratively, to run the simple models at the concept and the schematic phases to inform the orientation, the massing, the envelope, and the glazing, to use the detailed models at the design development to refine the design, and to recognize that the model is a design tool, not a compliance tool, because the performance is determined by the early decisions and the model must inform them.

### The Model With Unrealistic Inputs

When the energy model is run with inputs that do not reflect the actual design, the actual use, or the actual operations — the standard occupancy, the idealized schedule, the optimistic system efficiencies — the model predicts a performance the building will not achieve, and the prediction misleads the design and the owner. The mechanism is that the standard inputs read as the model's defaults, so the model feels grounded, while the actual design, use, and operations differ, and the model predicts a performance that is not achievable. The harm is that the building, operating with the actual conditions, uses more energy than the model predicted, the owner's expectation is not met, and the certification or the code compliance may be in jeopardy, while the architect believed the performance was predicted. The defense is to use realistic inputs in the model, to reflect the actual design, use, and operations, to calibrate the model to the actual building when the data is available, to document the inputs and the basis, and to recognize that the model is only as good as its inputs, because a model with unrealistic inputs is a model that misleads.

### The Passive Strategies Ignored In Favor Of The Active Systems

When the passive strategies (the orientation, the shading, the thermal mass, the natural ventilation, the daylighting) are ignored in favor of the active systems (the heating, the cooling, the lighting), the active systems are sized for the unreduced loads, and the building uses more energy and requires more system capacity than the passive strategies would have allowed. The mechanism is that the active systems read as the solution, so the design feels complete, while the passive strategies, which would have reduced the loads, are not implemented, and the active systems are oversized for the loads the passive strategies would have reduced. The harm is that the building uses more energy, the systems are larger and more expensive, and the building is less resilient (dependent on the active systems), while the architect believed the systems were designed. The defense is to implement the passive strategies before the active systems, to reduce the loads with the orientation, the shading, the thermal mass, the natural ventilation, and the daylighting, to size the active systems for the reduced loads, and to recognize that the passive strategies reduce the loads and the active systems should serve the reduced loads, because the active systems without the passive strategies are oversized and inefficient.

### The Optimization For The Energy Cost Without The Comfort

When the energy optimization is driven by the energy cost alone, without regard for the occupant comfort, the design may achieve the energy target at the expense of the comfort — the glare from the unshaded glazing, the drafts from the natural ventilation, the temperature swing from the thermal mass — and the occupants suffer the discomfort for the energy savings. The mechanism is that the energy cost reads as the metric, so the optimization feels objective, while the comfort, which is also a performance objective, is not in the optimization, and the design achieves the energy at the comfort's expense. The harm is that the occupants are uncomfortable, the complaints and the overrides (the blinds closed, the windows shut, the thermostats adjusted) defeat the energy strategies, and the building's actual performance departs from the modeled, while the architect believed the energy was optimized. The defense is to balance the energy performance against the occupant comfort, to include the comfort in the optimization (the glare, the temperature, the drafts, the daylight), to use the parametric analysis to reveal the trade-offs, to document the balance, and to recognize that the energy and the comfort are both performance objectives, because an energy optimization that sacrifices the comfort is an optimization that the occupants will defeat.

### The Model That Does Not Match The As-Built Building

When the energy model is not updated to reflect the design changes and the as-built conditions, the model documents a performance the building does not achieve, and the mismatch misleads the owner, the certification, and the operations. The mechanism is that the model reads as the design's representation, so it feels current, while the design changes (the value-engineered envelope, the substituted systems, the modified controls) are not reflected in the model, and the model predicts a performance the as-built building will not achieve. The harm is that the building, operating with the as-built conditions, uses more energy than the model predicted, the owner's expectation is not met, the certification may be in jeopardy, and the operations cannot use the model for the optimization, while the architect believed the model was current. The defense is to coordinate the model with the design and the construction, to update the model when the design changes, to confirm the coordination at each phase, to use the model to verify the as-built performance, and to recognize that the model must reflect the as-built building, because a model that does not match the building is a model that misleads everyone who relies on it.

### The Envelope Optimized For The Code, Not For The Life-Cycle Cost

When the envelope is optimized for the code compliance (the minimum R-value, the maximum U-value) rather than for the life-cycle cost (the optimal insulation, glazing, and air sealing that minimizes the life-cycle cost), the envelope meets the code but uses more energy and costs more over its life than the optimal envelope, because the code minimum is not the economic optimum. The mechanism is that the code-compliant envelope reads as the requirement, so the optimization feels complete, while the code minimum is not the life-cycle cost optimum, and the envelope uses more energy and costs more over its life. The harm is that the building uses more energy and costs more over its life, the opportunity for the life-cycle cost optimization is lost, and the owner pays more for the energy than the additional insulation would have cost, while the architect believed the envelope was optimized. The defense is to optimize the envelope for the life-cycle cost, to use the parametric analysis to find the optimum (the insulation, glazing, and air sealing that minimize the life-cycle cost), to weigh the first cost against the energy savings, and to recognize that the code minimum is a floor, not an optimum, because the envelope optimized for the code is an envelope that costs more over its life.
