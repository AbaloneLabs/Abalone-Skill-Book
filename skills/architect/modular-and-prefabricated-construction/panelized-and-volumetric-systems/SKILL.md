# Panelized and Volumetric Systems

Prefabrication spans a spectrum from the panelized (the flat wall, floor, and roof panels shipped and assembled on site) to the volumetric (the three-dimensional room modules fully finished in the factory) and the hybrid systems that combine them, and the choice of the system drives the structural design, the envelope, the transport, the site labor, and the design flexibility. The judgment problem is that the system is often chosen for the trend or the single advantage (the speed, the cost) without weighing the tradeoffs, when in fact each system has a distinct profile of the factory content, the site labor, the transport efficiency, the joint complexity, and the design flexibility, and the wrong choice for the project produces the cost overrun, the delay, or the design compromise. A volumetric system chosen for a complex form produces 70 percent site work; a panelized system chosen for a repetitive hotel loses the speed advantage. This skill covers the decisions that determine whether the prefab system fits the project, including the system selection, the structural and the envelope resolution, the joint and the erection, and the factory-site balance.

## Core Rules

### Select the System Based on the Project's Form, Repetition, Transport, and Site Constraints

The system (the panelized, the volumetric, the hybrid) must be selected based on the project's form (the simple or the complex), the repetition (the high or the low), the transport (the access, the distance), and the site constraints (the crane, the staging, the labor), because each system fits a different project profile, and the mismatch produces the failure. The designer must evaluate the project profile against the system strengths (the volumetric for the high-repetition, the simple-form, the transportable; the panelized for the complex-form, the low-repetition, the transport-constrained; the hybrid for the mixed), and must select the system that fits. The tradeoff is that the volumetric gains the speed but loses the flexibility, and the panelized gains the flexibility but loses the speed; the resolution is the informed choice.

### Resolve the Structure for the System, Not as a Conventional Structure Adapted

The structure must be resolved for the chosen system, not as a conventional structure adapted to the prefab, because the panelized and the volumetric systems have the different structural logic (the load-bearing panels, the module frame, the podium), and the conventional structure adapted wastes the prefab advantage and creates the conflict. The designer must design the structure for the system (the load-bearing panels for the panelized, the steel or the timber frame for the volumetric, the transfer structure for the hybrid), must verify the load path and the lateral system, and must coordinate the structure with the factory production. The trap is a conventional steel or concrete frame with the prefab panels infilled, which wastes the prefab.

### Resolve the Envelope and the Thermal Bridge for the System and the Joint

The envelope and the thermal bridge must be resolved for the system and the joint, because the prefab envelope (the panelized wall, the module wall) and the joint between the panels or the modules are the thermal bridge risk, and the unmanaged bridge produces the energy loss, the condensation, and the mold. The designer must detail the envelope for the continuous insulation across the joint, must design the thermal break at the structural connection (the bracket, the pad), and must verify the envelope meets the energy code and the performance. The trap is the structural connection that bridges the insulation and the condensation.

### Design the Joint and the Erection Sequence for the Panel or the Module

The joint between the panels or the modules and the erection sequence must be designed for the structural connection, the weatherproofing, the fire, and the tolerance, and the sequence must allow the safe and the efficient assembly, because the joint is the defect risk and the sequence is the schedule risk. The designer must detail the panel or the module joint (the structural connection, the seal, the firestop), must plan the erection sequence (the order, the temporary support, the crane time), and must verify the joint is accessible for the connection and the inspection. The trap is a joint that cannot be accessed or a sequence that requires the impossible lift.

### Balance the Factory Fabrication Level With the Site Labor and the Transport

The factory fabrication level (the percentage of the work done in the factory) must be balanced with the site labor and the transport, because the higher factory content reduces the site labor and the time but increases the transport cost and the module size, and the lower factory content reduces the transport but increases the site labor, and the balance determines the cost and the schedule. The designer must evaluate the factory content for the project (the volumetric at 70 to 80 percent, the panelized at 30 to 50 percent, the hybrid in between), must balance it with the site labor cost and the transport cost, and must verify the balance optimizes the cost and the schedule. The tradeoff is the factory content versus the transport; the resolution is the project-specific balance.

## Common Traps

### A Volumetric System Chosen for a Complex Form That Becomes 70 Percent Site Work

The volumetric system is chosen for its speed and quality, but the project has a complex form (the setback, the angle, the custom). The mechanism of the trap is that the volumetric system excels at the high-repetition, the simple-form project, and a complex form cannot be fully modularized, so the complex parts are built on site, and the site work grows to 50 or 70 percent of the project, which negates the speed and the quality advantage of the volumetric system, and the project pays the volumetric premium without the volumetric benefit, and the cost and the schedule suffer. The false signal is the volumetric trend, which suggests modern construction but masks the form mismatch. The harm is lost speed, lost quality, and a wasted premium. The defense is to select the system based on the project profile, and to use the panelized or the hybrid for the complex form.

### A Panelized System Chosen for a Repetitive Hotel That Loses the Speed

The panelized system is chosen for its flexibility and its low transport cost, but the project is a repetitive hotel with 200 identical rooms. The mechanism of the trap is that the panelized system ships the flat panels and assembles them on site, which requires the significant site labor and the time, and a repetitive project (the hotel, the dormitory) is the ideal candidate for the volumetric system that finishes the rooms in the factory, so the panelized choice loses the speed and the factory-quality advantage that the volumetric would have provided, and the project takes longer and costs more in the site labor. The false signal is the panel flexibility, which suggests adaptability but masks the missed speed. The harm is lost speed, more site labor, and higher cost. The defense is to select the volumetric system for the high-repetition project.

### A Conventional Structure With the Prefab Panels Infilled That Wastes the Prefab

The structure is a conventional steel or concrete frame, and the prefab panels are infilled between the frames. The mechanism of the trap is that the prefab panels can be the load-bearing structure (the cross-laminated timber, the precast, the structural insulated panel), and using them only as the infill wastes the structural capacity and requires the double structure (the frame and the panel), which increases the cost, the depth, and the complexity, and negates the prefab efficiency, so the project pays for the prefab and the conventional frame. The false signal is the structural familiarity, which suggests a safe design but masks the waste. The harm is double structure, higher cost, and lost efficiency. The defense is to resolve the structure for the system, using the load-bearing panels.

### A Thermal Bridge at the Structural Connection That Causes the Condensation and the Mold

The structural connection between the panels or the modules (the steel bracket, the angle) penetrates the insulation. The mechanism of the trap is that the steel is a high-conductivity material, and a steel connection that penetrates the insulation is a thermal bridge that conducts the heat out and the cold in, and at the bridge the interior surface is cold, and the moisture condenses on the cold surface, and the condensation feeds the mold, which is a health hazard and a building defect, and the bridge also wastes the energy. The false signal is the structural connection, which suggests a normal detail but masks the bridge. The harm is energy loss, condensation, mold, and the health risk. The defense is to detail the continuous insulation, design the thermal break at the connection, and verify the envelope performance.

### A Joint That Cannot Be Accessed or a Sequence That Requires the Impossible Lift

The joint between the panels or the modules is detailed without the access, or the erection sequence requires a lift that the crane cannot do. The mechanism of the trap is that the joint must be connected, sealed, and inspected on site, and a joint with no access (the hidden connection, the tight gap) cannot be properly connected or inspected, and the defect goes undetected, and the erection sequence that requires the impossible lift (the module over the occupied space, the panel at the extreme radius) cannot be executed, and the installation stalls, so the project suffers the defect and the delay. The false signal is the joint or the sequence on paper, which suggests a buildable design but masks the access or the lift failure. The harm is defects, delays, and stalled installation. The defense is to design the joint for the access and the sequence for the feasible lift.

## Self-Check

- Did I evaluate the project profile (the form, the repetition, the transport, the site) against the system strengths, and select the system (panelized, volumetric, hybrid) that fits?
- Did I resolve the structure for the system (the load-bearing panels, the module frame, the transfer structure), and avoid the conventional frame with the prefab infill?
- Did I detail the envelope for the continuous insulation across the joint, design the thermal break at the structural connection, and verify the energy code and the performance?
- Did I design the joint (the structural connection, the weatherproofing, the fire, the tolerance) for the access and the inspection, and plan the erection sequence for the feasible lift?
- Did I evaluate the factory fabrication level (the volumetric at 70 to 80 percent, the panelized at 30 to 50 percent), and balance it with the site labor and the transport cost?
- Did I verify the system choice matches the project (the volumetric for the high-repetition, the panelized for the complex-form)?
- Did I verify no thermal bridge exists at the structural connection?
