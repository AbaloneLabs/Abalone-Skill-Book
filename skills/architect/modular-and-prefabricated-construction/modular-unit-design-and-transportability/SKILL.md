# Modular Unit Design and Transportability

Volumetric modular construction builds three-dimensional room-sized modules in a factory and ships them to the site for assembly, and the design is governed not only by the architectural and the structural logic but by the physical constraints of the road transport, the crane, and the factory production line. The judgment problem is that a modular building is often designed as a conventional building and then "modularized" by drawing lines on the plan, when in fact the module size, the seam location, the joint, and the production sequence must drive the design from the start, because the transport limits (the width, the height, the length, the weight) and the crane limits determine what can be built and shipped, and a design that ignores them produces modules that cannot be legally transported or safely lifted. A module 4.5 meters wide cannot travel without an escort; a module too heavy for the site crane stalls the installation; a seam through the middle of a bathroom leaks. This skill covers the decisions that determine whether a modular design is buildable and shippable, including the module sizing and the grid, the seam and the joint, the transport and the crane, and the factory-to-site coordination.

## Core Rules

### Size the Modules Within the Road Transport Limits From the First Design Move

The module dimensions must be within the road transport limits (the width, the height, the length, the weight) from the first design move, because the oversized module requires the special permit, the escort, the utility clearance, or is simply illegal, and the redesign late in the project is costly and disruptive. The designer must know the transport limits for the route (the typical maximum width 3.5 to 4.2 meters, the height 4.0 to 4.5 meters including the trailer, the length 12 to 16 meters, the weight 20 to 30 tonnes), must size the modules within the limits, and must verify the route from the factory to the site (the bridge clearance, the turn radius, the road width). The tradeoff is that the smaller module means more seams and more joints, but the oversized module cannot ship; the resolution is to design the module to the limit.

### Plan the Module Grid and the Seams to Align With the Room and the Structure

The module grid (the pattern of the module boundaries) and the seams (the joints between the modules) must be planned to align with the room boundaries and the structural walls, because the seam is the structural joint and the waterproofing challenge, and a seam through the middle of a room or a bathroom produces the leak, the crack, and the misalignment. The designer must lay out the module grid so the seams fall at the walls and the corridors, must avoid the seam through the wet area (the bathroom, the kitchen), and must verify the structural load path transfers through the module joints. The trap is a seam through the bathroom or the middle of the room.

### Design the Module Joint for the Structure, the Fire, the Weather, and the Tolerance

The module-to-module joint must be designed for the structural load transfer, the fire separation, the weatherproofing, the acoustic separation, and the construction tolerance, because the joint is the weakest point of the modular building and the failure at the joint (the leak, the crack, the fire spread, the sound flanking) is the leading defect. The designer must design the structural connection (the bolted or the welded joint, the load transfer), the fire separation (the rated joint, the firestop), the weatherproofing (the overlapping membrane, the sealant, the cap), and the tolerance (the shim, the adjustment), and must verify the joint meets the code and the performance. The tradeoff is that the robust joint costs more, but the weak joint fails; the resolution is the tested, detailed joint.

### Plan the Crane Access, the Lift, and the Site Logistics for the Module Installation

The crane access, the lift, and the site logistics must be planned for the module installation, because the modules are large and heavy, and the crane (the size, the reach, the position) and the site (the access, the staging, the set-down) must accommodate the lift, and a site with no crane access or no set-down area stalls the installation. The designer must verify the crane can reach every module position (the radius, the capacity at the radius), must plan the module delivery sequence (the just-in-time or the staging), and must verify the site access for the truck and the crane. The trap is a site where the crane cannot reach the far corner or the set-down is too small.

### Coordinate the Factory Production and the Site Work on a Just-in-Time Basis

The factory production and the site work (the foundation, the core, the connection) must be coordinated on a just-in-time basis, because the modular construction depends on the precise sequencing of the factory output and the site readiness, and a mismatch (the factory ahead of the site, or the site ahead of the factory) stalls one or the other and wastes the money. The designer must coordinate the production schedule with the site schedule, must verify the site is ready (the foundation, the utilities, the crane) when the modules arrive, and must verify the module design is frozen before the production (the late change is very costly). The trap is the late design change after the production has started.

## Common Traps

### An Oversized Module That Cannot Be Legally Transported Without a Permit

The module is sized at 4.5 meters wide or 5 meters high to fit the room. The mechanism of the trap is that the road transport limit (the width, the height) is strict, and an oversized module requires the special permit, the escort, the utility clearance, or is illegal, and the permit may be denied or may restrict the travel to the night or the specific route, which delays the delivery, inflates the cost, and may make the module unshippable, and the redesign late in the project is costly. The false signal is the room fit, which suggests a good module but masks the transport failure. The harm is permit delays, cost inflation, and potential unshippability. The defense is to size the modules within the transport limits from the first design move, and to verify the route.

### A Seam Through the Bathroom or the Middle of the Room

The module grid is drawn to maximize the module size, and the seam falls through the bathroom or the middle of the living room. The mechanism of the trap is that the seam is the structural joint and the waterproofing challenge, and a seam through the wet area (the bathroom, the kitchen) is the leading source of the leak, because the waterproofing membrane must join at the seam and the joint is vulnerable to the movement and the defect, and a seam through the middle of the room produces the visible joint, the crack, and the floor misalignment, which are the leading resident complaints. The false signal is the module size, which suggests efficiency but masks the seam failure. The harm is leaks, cracks, misalignment, and resident complaints. The defense is to plan the module grid so the seams fall at the walls and the corridors, and to avoid the seam through the wet area.

### A Weak Joint That Leaks, Cracks, or Fails the Fire or the Acoustic Rating

The module joint is detailed minimally to save the cost and the time. The mechanism of the trap is that the joint is the weakest point of the modular building, and a minimally detailed joint (no robust waterproofing, no firestop, no acoustic seal, no tolerance adjustment) fails at the first movement or the first weather event, producing the leak, the crack, the fire spread, or the sound flanking, and the defect is expensive to repair because the joint is between the modules and the access is difficult, so the building has the chronic defect and the warranty claims. The false signal is the joint simplicity, which suggests efficiency but masks the failure risk. The harm is leaks, cracks, fire spread, sound flanking, and warranty claims. The defense is to design the joint for the structure, the fire, the weather, the acoustic, and the tolerance, and to use the tested detail.

### A Site Where the Crane Cannot Reach or the Set-Down Is Too Small

The site is planned for the conventional construction, with no consideration of the crane and the module staging. The mechanism of the trap is that the modular installation requires a large crane (to lift the 20-tonne module) and a set-down area (to stage the modules before the lift), and a site with no crane access (the narrow street, the overhead wires) or no set-down area (the tight site, the full plot) cannot accommodate the installation, so the crane must be larger (more expensive) or the modules must be delivered just-in-time (risky), and the installation is stalled or more expensive. The false signal is the site plan, which suggests a normal site but masks the crane and staging failure. The harm is installation delays, higher crane cost, and stalled work. The defense is to plan the crane access, the reach, and the set-down area for the module installation.

### A Late Design Change After the Factory Production Has Started

The design is changed (the window moved, the wall relocated) after the factory production has started. The mechanism of the trap is that the modular production is a factory line, and a change after the start requires the line stoppage, the rework, the scrap, and the re-sequencing, which is extremely costly and disruptive (far more than the same change in the conventional construction), and the change may delay the whole production and the site schedule, and the factory may refuse or charge the premium, so the project suffers the cost and the delay. The false signal is the change feasibility, which suggests a normal change but masks the factory-line disruption. The harm is high cost, delay, and factory conflict. The defense is to freeze the module design before the production and to manage the change rigorously.

## Self-Check

- Did I size the modules within the road transport limits (the width, the height, the length, the weight) from the first design move, and verify the route from the factory to the site?
- Did I plan the module grid so the seams fall at the walls and the corridors, and avoid the seam through the wet area (the bathroom, the kitchen)?
- Did I design the module joint for the structural load transfer, the fire separation, the weatherproofing, the acoustic separation, and the tolerance, and verify it meets the code?
- Did I verify the crane can reach every module position (the radius and the capacity), plan the module delivery sequence, and verify the site access for the truck and the crane?
- Did I coordinate the factory production and the site work on a just-in-time basis, and verify the site is ready when the modules arrive?
- Did I freeze the module design before the production, and verify no late change is made after the start?
- Did I verify the set-down area is sized for the module staging?
