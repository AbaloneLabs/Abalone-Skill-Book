# Fume Hood and Ventilation Strategy

A chemical laboratory is a building whose ventilation system is its primary safety device, and whose fume hoods are the last barrier between the researcher and the hazardous chemical. The judgment problem is that lab ventilation looks like a high air change rate on a mechanical schedule, so designers tend to size the air and place the hoods and underweight the interaction between the hood airflow, the room air distribution, the makeup air, and the energy load that the high air change rate imposes. A hood placed at the door or under a supply diffuser does not contain; a lab whose makeup air does not match the exhaust cannot maintain the hood face velocity; a lab whose air change rate is set for safety with no energy strategy consumes far more energy than necessary. This skill covers the decisions that determine whether the lab ventilation protects the researcher and the budget, including fume hood layout and face velocity, supply and exhaust air sizing, makeup air and room pressurization, chemical storage and emergency stations, and the energy strategy.

## Core Rules

### Place Fume Hoods for Containment and for the Researcher's Work Pattern

A fume hood contains the chemical vapor by drawing air through the face opening at a velocity that overcomes the turbulence and the cross-drafts, and the hood's location determines whether that velocity can be maintained. A hood must be located away from doors (the opening disrupts the face velocity), away from supply diffusers (the jet disrupts the airflow into the hood), and away from high-traffic aisles (the passing body creates turbulence), and the hood must be positioned so the researcher can work at it without blocking the aisle or the egress. The designer must place the hoods along the perimeter wall, away from doors and diffusers, must verify the face velocity (commonly 80 to 100 feet per minute at the working sash height) is maintained at every point, and must coordinate the hood layout with the bench layout and the researcher's work pattern. The trap is placing the hood for plan efficiency at the door or under a diffuser, which disrupts the containment.

### Size the Supply and Exhaust Air for the Hood Load and the Room Heat Load

The lab ventilation must serve two loads simultaneously: the hood exhaust (the air drawn through the hoods) and the room air change rate (the minimum ventilation for safety and for heat removal), and the supply and exhaust must be sized for the larger of the two at the peak. When the hoods are operating, the exhaust is the sum of the hood flows, and the supply must match it (minus the pressurization offset) to maintain the room pressure; when the hoods are closed (with a variable air volume system), the exhaust drops to the minimum air change rate, and the supply must drop to match. The designer must compute the peak hood exhaust, the minimum air change rate (commonly 6 to 12 air changes per hour for a chemical lab, per the standard), and the heat load (the equipment and the people), and must size the supply and exhaust for the governing load, with the cooling sized for the peak heat load at the peak airflow. The trap is sizing the air for the air change rate and underweighting the hood exhaust, which starves the hoods when they are all open.

### Coordinate Makeup Air and Room Pressurization With the Exhaust

The lab must maintain a pressure relationship to the corridor (commonly negative for a chemical lab, so vapors do not escape), and the makeup air (the supply) must be coordinated with the exhaust to maintain that relationship across the range of hood operation. With a constant volume hood system, the exhaust and the supply are fixed, and the offset (supply slightly less than exhaust) maintains the negative pressure; with a variable air volume system, the exhaust and the supply track the hood sash position, and the controls must maintain the offset and the minimum air change rate across the range. The designer must coordinate the supply and exhaust controls to maintain the pressure offset, must provide the makeup air tempered and conditioned to match the room, and must verify the room pressure at every operating condition. The trap is a control sequence that holds the pressure at design but fails at part load, releasing vapors into the corridor.

### Plan Chemical Storage and Emergency Eyewash and Shower Stations for the Hazards

A chemical lab must store its chemicals safely (in a vented flammable storage cabinet, an acid storage cabinet, and a general storage, segregated by compatibility), and must provide the emergency eyewash and the safety shower at accessible locations for the hazards present. The chemical storage must be located for the delivery and the daily use, must be vented to the lab exhaust (not to the room), and must be sized for the inventory the lab will hold. The eyewash and the safety shower must be located within 10 seconds (roughly 55 feet) of every point of chemical hazard, must be on the path of egress (so the injured person does not cross the hazard to reach it), must provide the flow and the duration the standard requires, and must drain to prevent flooding. The designer must locate the storage and the emergency stations for the specific hazards, verify the distance and the path, and coordinate the drainage. The trap is placing the eyewash and shower to satisfy the code and discovering they are not reachable from the actual hazard or they flood the lab.

### Balance the Air Change Requirement Against Energy Use With a Control Strategy

A chemical lab's high air change rate (the air is 100 percent outside air, heated, cooled, humidified, and exhausted in one pass) makes the lab one of the most energy-intensive room types in a building, and the design must balance the safety requirement (the air change rate) against the energy cost. The strategies include a variable air volume system that reduces the airflow when the hoods are closed (down to the minimum air change rate), a sash position sensor that rewards the researcher for closing the sash, a heat recovery system on the exhaust, and an occupied versus unoccupied air change rate (reduced when the lab is empty, with the full rate on a trigger such as a sensor or a timer). The designer must define the air change requirement with the safety officer, must implement the control strategy that reduces the airflow without compromising safety, and must verify the energy saving does not reduce the containment. The trap is a constant volume system at the full air change rate, which is safe but consumes far more energy than a controlled system.

## Common Traps

### A Fume Hood at the Door or Under a Supply Diffuser That Cannot Contain

A fume hood is placed at the lab door or directly under a supply diffuser, because the layout was efficient and the hood fit the wall. The mechanism of the trap is that a hood's containment depends on a steady, undisturbed airflow into the face, and a door that opens or a supply diffuser that blows creates a cross-draft or a turbulence that overcomes the face velocity and pushes the vapor out of the hood into the room, so a hood that is certified on the bench fails in the field because of its location. The false signal is the certified hood, which suggests containment but masks the room-induced disruption. The harm is a chemical exposure, a health incident, and a lab that does not protect its users. The defense is to place the hood away from doors, diffusers, and traffic, verify the face velocity is maintained with the door and the diffuser operating, and coordinate the room air distribution with the hood.

### Supply and Exhaust Sized for the Air Change Rate That Starves the Hoods

The lab air is sized for the minimum air change rate, and the hoods are added to the exhaust, but the supply is not increased to match the peak hood exhaust. The mechanism of the trap is that when all the hoods are open, the exhaust exceeds the supply, and the room goes deeply negative, drawing air from the corridor (through the door undercut and the gaps) and starving the hoods of the makeup air they need, which drops the face velocity below the containment threshold and releases the vapor, or which reverses the airflow in adjacent rooms through the shared envelope. The false signal is the air change rate compliance, which suggests adequate ventilation but masks the shortfall at the peak hood load. The harm is a loss of containment, a chemical release, and a building pressure disruption. The defense is to size the supply and exhaust for the larger of the hood load and the air change rate, and to verify the face velocity at the peak hood operation.

### A Variable Air Volume Control Sequence That Loses the Pressure Offset at Part Load

A lab uses a variable air volume system to save energy, and the controls track the hood sash position, reducing the exhaust and the supply as the sashes close. The mechanism of the trap is that the pressure offset (supply slightly less than exhaust for negative pressure) must be maintained across the full range of operation, and a control sequence that does not maintain the offset at part load (because the minimum air change rate overrides it, or because the sensors drift) allows the room to go positive at low hood operation, pushing the chemical vapor into the corridor through the door and the gaps. The false signal is the VAV system, which suggests efficient, controlled ventilation but masks the pressure failure at part load. The harm is a vapor release into the corridor and an exposure. The defense is to coordinate the supply and exhaust controls to maintain the offset across the range, verify the room pressure at every operating condition, and alarm the pressure.

### Eyewash and Shower Placed to Satisfy the Code but Not Reachable From the Hazard

The eyewash and the safety shower are placed in the lab to satisfy the code requirement, at a location that fit the plumbing. The mechanism of the trap is that the standard requires the eyewash and shower within 10 seconds (roughly 55 feet) of every point of chemical hazard and on the path of egress, and a unit placed without reference to the actual hazard locations or the egress path may be too far from the hazard or may require the injured person to cross the hazard to reach it, so the code-compliant unit does not protect the actual user in the actual emergency. The false signal is the eyewash on the plumbing plan, which suggests compliance but masks the reach and path failure. The harm is a delayed or unavailable emergency response, a worsened injury, and liability. The defense is to map the chemical hazard locations, place the eyewash and shower within the distance and on the egress path, and coordinate the drainage to prevent flooding.

### A Constant Volume System at the Full Air Change Rate That Wastes Energy

A lab is designed with a constant volume system at the full air change rate, because it is simple and safe, and the energy cost is treated as the price of safety. The mechanism of the trap is that the full air change rate is required for safety only when the lab is occupied and the hoods are in use, and a constant volume system heats, cools, and exhausts 100 percent outside air at the full rate 24 hours a day, including the unoccupied hours when the rate is unnecessary, so the lab consumes far more energy than a controlled system and burdens the institution's operating budget and its carbon target. The false signal is the safety compliance, which suggests a correct design but masks the energy waste. The harm is high operating cost, high carbon emissions, and a lab that is harder to justify or to sustain. The defense is to implement a variable air volume system with a sash sensor and an occupied versus unoccupied air change rate, recover heat from the exhaust, and verify the control strategy does not compromise safety.

## Self-Check

- Did I place the fume hoods away from doors, supply diffusers, and high-traffic aisles, and verify the face velocity is maintained at the working sash height with the room operating?
- Did I size the supply and exhaust for the larger of the peak hood exhaust and the minimum air change rate, and verify the face velocity at the peak hood operation?
- Did I coordinate the makeup air and the room pressurization with the exhaust, maintain the pressure offset (negative for a chemical lab) across the full range of operation, and alarm the pressure?
- Did I provide the chemical storage in vented, segregated cabinets (flammable, acid, general) sized for the inventory, and vent the storage to the lab exhaust?
- Did I map the chemical hazard locations, place the eyewash and safety shower within 10 seconds and on the egress path, and coordinate the drainage to prevent flooding?
- Did I implement a control strategy (variable air volume, sash sensor, occupied versus unoccupied air change rate, heat recovery) that reduces the airflow and the energy without compromising safety?
- Did I verify the room pressure at every operating condition, including the part-load and the unoccupied conditions?
- Did I coordinate the lab air distribution (diffuser types and locations) with the hood layout to avoid cross-drafts and turbulence at the hood face?
