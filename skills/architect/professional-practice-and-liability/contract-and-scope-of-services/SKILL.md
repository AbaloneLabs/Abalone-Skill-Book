# Contract And Scope Of Services

The owner-architect agreement is the document that defines what the architect is hired to do, what the architect is not hired to do, how the work will be paid for, and where the architect's liability begins and ends. It is not a formality to be signed and filed; it is the primary instrument of professional risk management and the reference to which every later dispute returns. Architects often accept contract terms drafted by the owner or its counsel without careful review, assume that the standard AIA agreement covers the project's specific conditions, or proceed on scope that was discussed but never written, with the result that the actual engagement bears little resemblance to the signed document. When a problem arises, a cost overrun, a delay, a construction defect, a demand for uncompensated services, the contract determines who bears the loss, and a contract that does not match the work exposes the architect to liability, unpaid effort, and disputes that the document was meant to prevent. The discipline here is to treat the contract as a living description of the engagement, to make scope explicit, and to manage deviations as written amendments rather than silent drift.

Use this skill when establishing or revising scope of services, reviewing an owner-architect agreement, pricing or documenting additional services, or managing scope changes during a project. For contract negotiation and legal interpretation, defer to legal counsel; the architect coordinates the scope definition but does not practice law.

## Core Rules

### Define Basic Services By Phase, Deliverable, And Effort

Vague scope is the root of most fee and liability disputes. "Design the building" is not a scope; a scope specifies what is produced at each phase, to what level of detail, and with what coordination and review.

Define by:

- listing the phases included, schematic design, design development, construction documents, bidding or negotiation, and construction administration, and what each phase produces;
- specifying the deliverables at each phase, drawings, specifications, models, reports, and their anticipated content and level of development;
- defining the extent of service within each phase, such as the number of design alternatives in schematic design, the number of owner review cycles, and the coordination depth with consultants;
- clarifying what is explicitly excluded, because exclusions are as important as inclusions in preventing scope creep and liability for work not intended;
- tying the scope to the fee, so that the effort the fee supports matches the services the scope describes.

### Distinguish Basic From Additional Services Explicitly

Many services that owners expect, additional design cycles, extensive construction administration, response to owner-directed changes, sustainability certification support, are not basic services under standard agreements. The line between basic and additional must be explicit, or the architect absorbs unpaid work.

Distinguish by:

- defining basic services as the standard scope that the basic fee covers, using the AIA or equivalent framework as the reference;
- listing the common additional services the project is likely to need, such as revisions after a defined review cycle, services triggered by owner changes, expanded construction administration, feasibility studies, programming beyond the contracted scope, and specialty consulting;
- establishing the mechanism for authorizing and pricing additional services, so that when the need arises, the process is defined rather than improvised;
- documenting additional services in writing as they occur, with scope, fee, and owner authorization, before performing the work;
- recognizing that services performed without authorization may be uncompensable, and services authorized verbally may be disputed, so written authorization is essential.

### Match The Agreement To The Project's Specific Conditions

Standard agreements are starting points, not complete contracts. Every project has specific conditions, delivery method, owner structure, regulatory context, site conditions, that the standard form does not address, and these gaps become disputes.

Match by:

- confirming that the agreement reflects the actual delivery method, because the architect's role and responsibilities differ under design-bid-build, CM-at-risk, and design-build, and a mismatch creates liability gaps;
- addressing the specific project risks, such as renovation with unknown existing conditions, fast-track delivery, phased permitting, or work in occupied buildings, with scope and fee that reflect the added effort;
- clarifying the owner's responsibilities, such as providing survey, geotechnical, and existing-conditions information, making timely decisions, and securing financing, because owner failures to perform are a primary cause of delay and the contract must allocate that risk;
- addressing the consultant structure, whether the architect engages consultants as subconsultants or the owner contracts them directly, because the structure affects coordination authority and liability;
- supplementing the standard form with project-specific conditions rather than relying on the form to anticipate what is unique.

### Define Deliverables, Reviews, And Decision Points

The contract should establish the rhythm of the engagement: what the architect delivers, when, how the owner reviews, and how decisions are recorded. Ambiguity here produces delays the architect absorbs.

Define by:

- specifying the number and timing of owner review cycles at each phase, because reviews beyond the contracted number are additional services;
- establishing the duration of owner review periods and the consequence of delay, because an approval that gates a phase must have a defined clock;
- defining the form of deliverables, digital versus printed, model versus drawing, and the level of detail, because format disputes consume fee;
- clarifying that construction documents are not construction instructions, and that the architect does not direct the contractor's means and methods, because conflating these expands liability;
- establishing the construction administration scope precisely, the number of site visits, the documentation, and the limits of the architect's site role, because CA scope is frequently expanded informally.

### Manage Scope Changes As Written Amendments

Projects change, and scope changes with them. The risk is not change itself but undocumented change, where the architect performs work outside the contract without authorization or compensation.

Manage by:

- identifying scope changes as they arise, distinguishing owner-directed changes, code or regulatory changes, and changes driven by conditions, and addressing each through the appropriate mechanism;
- documenting changes through written amendment or additional-services authorization before performing the work, not after, because retroactive authorization is often refused;
- pricing changes based on their actual effort and impact, including ripple effects on schedule and other work, not merely the direct task;
- recognizing the pattern of incremental scope creep, many small additions that individually seem minor but cumulatively exceed the fee, and aggregating them into a scope conversation;
- maintaining a current scope baseline, so that what is contracted is always clear and deviations are visible.

### Allocate Risk Fairly And Defensibly

The contract allocates risk between owner and architect, and the allocation should reflect what each party can control. Standard agreements allocate based on this principle; deviations that load risk onto the architect for matters outside the architect's control are both unfair and dangerous.

Allocate by:

- resisting indemnification clauses that exceed the architect's standard of care or insurance coverage, because an indemnity broader than insurance is an uninsured promise;
- confirming that the architect's liability is tied to the standard of care, ordinary professional competence, not to warranties or guarantees of outcome, which are uninsurable;
- resisting clauses that make the architect responsible for site safety, contractor means and methods, or construction cost guarantees, which are outside the architect's control;
- ensuring dispute resolution, limitation of liability, and insurance provisions are reviewed, because these determine exposure if a claim arises;
- involving legal counsel and insurance review for non-standard or owner-drafted terms, because the architect is not qualified to assess legal risk alone.

## Common Traps

### The Unsigned Or Outdated Scope

The team proceeds on a scope discussed in a proposal or a prior project's agreement, while the signed contract contains a different or vaguer scope, and the gap is discovered only when a dispute arises. The trap mechanism is that the working understanding and the legal document diverge silently, because no one reconciles them as the project evolves. The false signal is the signed contract in the file, which appears to govern while actually describing an engagement that no longer matches the work. The harm is that the architect performs services the contract does not cover, cannot be paid for them, and may be liable for outcomes outside the documented scope, because the reference document does not describe the actual engagement.

### Scope Creep Absorbed Silently

The owner requests additional reviews, extra alternatives, expanded coordination, or services beyond the contract, and the architect performs them to keep the project moving, without authorization or fee. The trap is that each addition feels minor and urgent, so no one aggregates them, while they accumulate into substantial unpaid work that erodes the fee and the margin. The false signal is the smooth project progression, which appears collaborative while concealing the scope drift. The harm is a project where the architect has delivered far more than contracted for the original fee, where the team is overworked and undercompensated, and where the owner has no reason to formalize the additions because they are being provided for free, until the fee is exhausted and the remaining work is uncompensated.

### Standard Form Applied Without Project-Specific Supplement

The team signs the standard AIA agreement unchanged, assuming it covers the project, when the project has specific conditions, delivery method, risks, or owner responsibilities the standard form does not address. The trap is that the standard form feels comprehensive because it is professionally drafted, while leaving gaps that the project's specifics will expose. The false signal is the completeness of the form, which appears sufficient while omitting the project's actual conditions. The harm is disputes that the contract cannot resolve because the relevant condition was never addressed, liability for risks the standard form did not contemplate, and an engagement governed by a document that does not describe it, because the specific supplements that would have made it fit were never written.

### Liability Allocated Beyond Standard Of Care

The owner's counsel inserts warranties, guarantees, or indemnities that exceed the architect's standard of care or insurance coverage, and the architect signs without recognizing the exposure. The trap mechanism is that these clauses look like standard legal language but convert professional liability, which is insurable, into warranty liability, which is not, exposing personal and firm assets. The false signal is the professional presentation of the contract, which appears routine while encoding uninsurable promises. The harm is that if a problem arises, the architect faces liability beyond what insurance covers, the firm's assets are exposed, and the clause that created the exposure was accepted without legal or insurance review, because it looked standard.

### Construction Administration Scope Expanded Informally

The owner or contractor relies on the architect for site services beyond the contracted CA scope, additional visits, extensive documentation, direction to subcontractors, and the architect provides them to support the project without formalizing the expansion. The trap is that CA work is relationship-driven and hard to refuse in the moment, so it expands informally while the contract remains unchanged. The false signal is the engaged construction administration, which appears diligent while exceeding the scope. The harm is unpaid effort, liability for site matters outside the contracted role, and an expectation set that future projects will provide the same expanded service for the standard fee, because the boundary was never enforced.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is basic services defined by phase, deliverable, and effort, with explicit exclusions, and tied to the fee?
- [ ] Are additional services distinguished, with a defined authorization and pricing mechanism, and documented in writing when performed?
- [ ] Does the agreement match the project's delivery method, specific risks, owner responsibilities, and consultant structure?
- [ ] Are deliverables, review cycles, review durations, and construction administration scope defined precisely?
- [ ] Are scope changes managed as written amendments or additional-services authorizations before work is performed?
- [ ] Is risk allocated to the standard of care, with indemnities, warranties, and uninsurable clauses reviewed by counsel and insurance?
- [ ] Is the signed contract reconciled with the working scope, rather than allowed to drift from the actual engagement?
