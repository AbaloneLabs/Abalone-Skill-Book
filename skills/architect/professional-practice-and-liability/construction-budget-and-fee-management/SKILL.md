# Construction Budget And Fee Management

The architect's fee is the resource that funds the design work, and its relationship to the scope determines whether the project can be done well, done adequately, or done poorly. A fee that is too low for the scope forces the team to compress the work, defer coordination, under-document, or absorb the loss, and the consequences appear as design quality erosion, document deficiencies, construction-phase claims, and staff burnout. A fee that is well-matched to the scope, and managed against it through the project, allows the work to be done at the standard of care with sustainable effort. Architects often focus on winning the work, accepting the fee the market or the owner will bear, and under-manage the fee through the project, discovering only at the end that the effort exceeded the compensation and the project was subsidized by the firm. The discipline here is to establish a fee that reflects the scope, to track effort against it, to recognize and address scope-fee imbalance when it arises, and to treat fee management as integral to both project quality and firm sustainability.

Use this skill when establishing or negotiating a fee, managing fee profitability, tracking effort against budget, pricing additional services, or addressing a scope-fee imbalance. For the construction cost budget, which is distinct from the architect's fee, see the cost control skill; this skill addresses the architect's compensation and its management.

## Core Rules

### Base The Fee On The Actual Scope, Not On A Formula Or A Wish

Fees are often set by percentage of construction cost, by comparison to past projects, or by what the owner will pay, without analysis of what the actual scope requires. A fee set without scope analysis is a guess, and guesses are usually low.

Base the fee by:

- analyzing the actual services required, by phase, including the coordination depth, the documentation level, the construction administration extent, and the specific project complexities;
- recognizing that percentage-of-construction-cost fees assume a typical relationship between cost and effort, which breaks down for atypical projects: a simple high-cost building may require less effort than its cost suggests, while a complex low-cost building may require more;
- accounting for the project's specific risk and complexity factors, renovation, fast-track, unusual systems, demanding approvals, extensive coordination, which increase effort beyond the baseline;
- building in contingency for the inevitable scope additions and revisions, because a fee with no contingency is consumed by the first change;
- being honest with the owner and the firm about what the scope costs to perform at the standard of care, because a fee below that cost produces a deficient project or a subsidized firm.

### Match Fee Type To Project Certainty And Risk

Different fee types, fixed lump sum, percentage, hourly with a cap, incentivize different behaviors and carry different risk. The fee type should match the project's certainty.

Match by:

- using fixed or lump-sum fees where the scope is well-defined and predictable, because they provide cost certainty to the owner and require disciplined scope management by the architect;
- using percentage fees where the scope scales with construction cost and the relationship is reliable, while recognizing the atypical-project breakdown;
- using hourly or time-and-materials fees where the scope is uncertain or evolving, such as early feasibility or existing-building assessment, because they compensate for undefined effort;
- recognizing that hourly fees without a not-to-exceed cap create owner anxiety and that capped hourly fees transfer scope-uncertainty risk to the architect;
- avoiding fee types that misalign risk, such as a fixed fee for an uncertain scope that forces the architect to absorb the uncertainty.

### Track Effort Against Fee Continuously

A fee unmanaged is a fee overspent. Continuous tracking of effort against the fee reveals problems while they can be addressed, rather than at project end when the loss is realized.

Track by:

- establishing a fee budget by phase and by discipline, so that effort can be compared to allocation, not only to the total;
- recording effort contemporaneously, through timesheets or equivalent, because reconstructed effort data is unreliable;
- reviewing effort against fee at regular intervals, and identifying phases or tasks where effort is exceeding allocation before it consumes the whole;
- diagnosing the cause of overruns, whether scope creep, underestimation, coordination failure, or client-driven revision, because the cause determines the response;
- acting on overruns when they are small, through scope conversation, additional-services billing, or process adjustment, rather than absorbing them until the fee is gone.

### Identify And Address Scope-Fee Imbalance

Scope-fee imbalance, where the scope exceeds the fee, is the most common threat to both project quality and firm profitability. It must be identified and addressed, not absorbed.

Address by:

- recognizing the signs of imbalance, effort exceeding allocation, scope additions without fee adjustment, compressed phases, and team stress, and treating them as management signals, not as endurance tests;
- distinguishing imbalance caused by underestimation, the fee was set too low, from imbalance caused by scope growth, the work expanded beyond the contract, because the responses differ;
- for scope growth, invoking the additional-services process, documenting the addition and seeking authorization and fee before performing the work;
- for underestimation, having an honest conversation with the owner about the gap, seeking a fee adjustment or a scope reduction, because absorbing the gap produces a deficient project;
- recognizing that chronic imbalance signals a firm-level problem in fee setting or scope management that must be corrected, not endured project after project.

### Price Additional Services Fairly And Promptly

Additional services are work beyond basic services that deserves additional compensation. Underpricing or delaying additional-services billing erodes the fee and trains the owner to expect unpaid work.

Price by:

- identifying additional services as they arise, and documenting them with scope, rationale, and fee before performing the work;
- pricing additional services based on their actual effort, not discounted to preserve the relationship, because discounting converts additional work into absorbed work;
- billing additional services promptly, because delayed billing accumulates and becomes harder to collect, and the owner's memory of the value fades;
- maintaining the discipline of written authorization, because additional services performed without authorization may be uncompensable, regardless of their value;
- recognizing that consistent additional-services management protects the basic fee and signals to the owner that scope has value.

### Balance Profitability With Project Quality And Staff Sustainability

A fee managed only for profitability produces a project under-resourced and a team overworked; a fee managed only for project quality produces a firm that subsidizes its work and cannot sustain itself. Both must be balanced.

Balance by:

- recognizing that a fee must cover the cost of performing the work at the standard of care, plus a sustainable margin, and that a fee below cost produces a deficient project regardless of the team's effort;
- recognizing that staff sustainability is a constraint, not a variable, because burnout and turnover impose costs, in quality, in rework, and in recruitment, that exceed the fee saved;
- recognizing that project quality and firm reputation are long-term assets, and that a project done poorly for a low fee damages assets worth more than the fee;
- using fee management to support quality, by ensuring the resources exist for coordination, documentation, and construction administration, rather than treating fee pressure as a reason to cut the work;
- being willing to have difficult conversations with the owner about scope and fee, because avoiding them transfers the cost to the team and the project.

## Common Traps

### The Fee Set To Win Rather Than To Perform

The fee is set at what the owner will accept or what wins the competition, below what the scope requires, on the assumption that the firm will absorb the difference or make it up later. The trap mechanism is that winning feels like success, so the fee gap is not confronted, while it guarantees that the project will be under-resourced from the start. The false signal is the awarded project, which appears to validate the fee while embedding a shortfall. The harm is a project done at the standard of care only through unpaid overtime and deferred coordination, document deficiencies that surface as construction claims, a team that bears the cost of the fee gap, and a firm that subsidizes work it cannot sustain, because the fee was set to win rather than to perform.

### Percentage Fee Applied To An Atypical Project

The fee is set as a percentage of construction cost on a project where the cost-to-effort relationship is atypical, such as a simple but expensive building or a complex but modest one. The trap is that the percentage appears objective, derived from a standard method, while producing a fee that is wildly mismatched to the effort. The false signal is the methodological fee, which appears rigorous while encoding a mismatch. The harm is a fee far above or below the actual effort, either overcharging the owner in a way that damages the relationship when discovered, or underfunding the work in a way that produces a deficient project, because the percentage was applied without analyzing whether the typical relationship held.

### Scope Growth Absorbed Without Fee Adjustment

The owner adds scope through the project, additional reviews, extra alternatives, expanded coordination, new requirements, and the architect performs the additions without additional-services billing, to keep the relationship smooth. The trap is that each addition feels minor and relational, so no one formalizes it, while the cumulative additions consume the fee. The false signal is the collaborative relationship, which appears healthy while concealing the scope drift. The harm is a fee exhausted before the work is complete, a project whose later phases are under-resourced precisely when documentation rigor matters most, and an owner who has no incentive to formalize additions because they are provided for free, until the fee is gone and the remaining work is uncompensated.

### Effort Tracked Only At Project End

Effort is recorded but not reviewed until the project closes, so that overruns are discovered only when the fee is already spent. The trap is that the tracking system exists but is not used for management, so the data accumulates without informing decisions. The false signal is the existence of timesheets, which appears to demonstrate control while never being acted upon. The harm is overruns that compound invisibly, phases that consume disproportionate effort without correction, and a project that arrives at completion with a realized loss that could have been managed if the data had been reviewed when intervention was possible, because the tracking was archival rather than active.

### Profitability Pursued At The Expense Of Quality

Facing a tight fee, the firm compresses the work, reduces coordination, under-documents, and cuts construction administration, to protect the margin. The trap is that the margin is preserved in the short term while the project quality erodes, producing deficiencies that generate claims, rework, and reputational damage worth more than the margin saved. The false signal is the profitable project on the financial report, which appears successful while embedding future cost. The harm is construction-phase claims for document deficiencies, a damaged reputation that costs future work, and a project that met its fee by failing its standard of care, converting a margin problem into a liability problem that costs the firm far more than the fee preserved.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is the fee based on analysis of the actual scope, complexity, and risk, rather than on a formula or what the owner will bear?
- [ ] Does the fee type match the project's certainty, with risk allocated to the party that can manage it?
- [ ] Is effort tracked against the fee budget continuously by phase and discipline, with overruns acted on when small?
- [ ] Is scope-fee imbalance identified and addressed, distinguishing underestimation from scope growth?
- [ ] Are additional services identified, priced at actual effort, authorized in writing, and billed promptly?
- [ ] Is the fee managed to balance profitability with project quality and staff sustainability?
- [ ] Is the firm willing to have scope-and-fee conversations with the owner rather than absorbing imbalance silently?
