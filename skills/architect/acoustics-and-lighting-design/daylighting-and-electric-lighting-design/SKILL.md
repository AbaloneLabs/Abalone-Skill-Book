# Daylighting and Electric Lighting Design

Lighting design shapes whether a building works for its occupants — whether they can see to perform their tasks, whether they are comfortable, whether the space supports their circadian rhythms and psychological wellbeing, and whether the building uses energy responsibly. It is also one of the most technically demanding disciplines in architecture, sitting at the intersection of human physiology (vision, circadian regulation), physics (photometry, optics), engineering (electrical distribution, controls), and regulation (energy codes, standards for specific occupancies). The architect who treats lighting as a selection of fixtures after the design is complete will produce a building that is visually uncomfortable, energy-inefficient, and physiologically undermining.

The judgment problem is that lighting operates on multiple axes that often conflict: task visibility requires sufficient illuminance, but high illuminance from the wrong direction causes glare; daylighting is free and physiologically beneficial but variable and difficult to control; energy codes push toward lower connected lighting power but occupants demand more light; the aesthetic of a luminous space may conflict with the visual comfort of the people in it. The architect must integrate these axes rather than optimize one at the expense of the others, and the integration must begin with the architectural design of the building, not with the fixture schedule.

## Core Rules

### Design Daylighting as Architecture, Not as a Fixture Substitution

Daylighting is determined by the building form, orientation, glazing, and interior finishes — decisions made in schematic design — not by skylights added late as a sustainability feature. Orient the building to maximize useful daylight (typically north and south facades, which are easier to control than east and west), design the depth and section of occupied spaces so that daylight penetrates to the needed depth (the rule of thumb is daylight reaches 2 to 2.5 times the window head height for sidelighting), and use interior finishes with appropriate reflectance (light surfaces bounce daylight deeper) to distribute the light.

Control the daylight from the beginning. Uncontrolled direct sun through a window produces glare, overheating, and UV damage to materials, and the occupants will cover the window with blinds that eliminate the daylight benefit entirely. Design shading — overhangs, fins, louvers, exterior screens — sized and oriented for the solar geometry of the facade, and specify interior shading (blinds, shades) that allows the occupant to manage glare without eliminating daylight. The building that provides beautiful daylight in the rendering and covered windows in occupancy has failed; design for the sun that will actually hit the building.

### Establish Light Levels and Quality Based on Task and Occupant Needs

Lighting design begins with understanding what the occupants need to do in each space and at each time of day, and establishing the appropriate illuminance (measured in lux or footcandles), uniformity, color rendering, and directionality for those tasks. Use established standards (IES Lighting Handbook, EN 12464-1, or occupancy-specific standards) as the basis, and adjust for the age of the expected occupants (older adults need more light and are more sensitive to glare), the duration and criticality of the task, and the consequences of visual error.

Quality matters as least as much as quantity. The Color Rendering Index (CRI) or the more accurate TM-30 metrics determine whether colors appear natural; high-CRI light is essential in healthcare (for assessing skin tone and clinical signs), retail (for product appearance), and any space where color discrimination matters. The correlated color temperature (CCT) affects the perceived warmth or coolness of the space and the circadian effect; warmer light (2700-3000K) is restful and appropriate for residential and hospitality, cooler light (4000-5000K) supports alertness and is appropriate for task and daytime commercial use. Specify both the quantity and the quality of light, not just the lux level.

### Control Glare as a Primary Design Constraint, Not an Afterthought

Glare — discomfort or disability from excessive contrast or brightness in the visual field — is the most common lighting failure and the most common reason occupants reject daylighting and override lighting controls. Design for glare control from both daylight (direct sun, bright sky visible from the task position) and electric light (exposed lamps, high-brightness fixtures in the field of view). Use the Unified Glare Rating (UGR) for interior electric lighting and daylight glare metrics for daylighting, and design to keep glare below the threshold for the task and duration.

The trap is that glare is directional and task-dependent, not a property of the fixture alone. A fixture that produces no glare for a person facing one direction may produce severe glare for a person facing another, and a window that is comfortable at 10 AM may be blinding at 2 PM. Design for the worst case — the sun position, the time of day, the task orientation — and provide the occupant with the means to adjust. Daylight glare is controlled with shading; electric glare is controlled with fixture selection (lensed, indirect, or baffle fixtures rather than exposed lamps) and placement (avoid placing bright sources in the occupant's field of view).

### Integrate Daylight and Electric Lighting with Controls

The energy benefit of daylighting is realized only when electric lighting is reduced in response to available daylight, and this requires lighting controls — photosensors that measure daylight and dim or switch electric lighting in response. Design the controls strategy with the daylighting strategy, so that the zones of electric lighting correspond to the zones of daylight availability (parallel to the windows, not perpendicular), and specify controls that respond smoothly to changing daylight rather than abruptly switching fixtures on and off.

Occupancy and vacancy sensors further reduce energy use by turning lighting off in unoccupied spaces, but they must be specified and placed correctly to avoid the common failure modes: sensors that do not detect stationary occupants (who are then plunged into darkness), sensors that are too sensitive and cycle lighting annoyingly, or sensors that are overridden by occupants who tape them into a fixed state. Specify the right sensor technology (PIR, ultrasonic, or dual-technology depending on the space), place it to cover the occupied area including stationary occupants, and provide manual override so the occupant retains control.

### Design for Circadian and Psychological Wellbeing

Light affects human physiology beyond vision — it regulates the circadian system, influences mood and alertness, and shapes the psychological experience of space. The circadian system is most sensitive to short-wavelength (blue-enriched) light at high intensity, which is why daylight and cool electric light support daytime alertness and warm, dim light supports evening rest. In buildings where occupants spend their entire day — workplaces, healthcare facilities, schools, and especially senior living — the lighting should support circadian regulation through high daytime illuminance with cool color temperature, and warmer, lower illuminance in the evening.

Healthcare lighting deserves particular attention. Patients who are already physiologically stressed benefit from daylight exposure and from lighting that supports their circadian rhythms; continuous bright light at night disrupts sleep, which impairs healing. Design patient rooms with distinct daytime and nighttime lighting, with very low-level, warm nighttime lighting that allows staff to navigate without disrupting the patient's sleep. In senior living, where circadian disruption and vision impairment are common, provide high daytime illuminance with good glare control and uniform low-level nighttime lighting for safe navigation.

### Coordinate with Lighting Consultants and Electrical Engineers

Engage lighting designers on projects with demanding lighting requirements — performance venues, museums, retail, healthcare, and any project pursuing LEED or WELL certification. The lighting designer develops the lighting concept, selects fixtures, calculates light levels, and specifies controls, and their work integrates with the architect's design and the electrical engineer's power and distribution design. The architect's role is to coordinate — to ensure that the lighting design supports the architectural concept, that fixtures are integrated into the architecture rather than applied to it, and that the electrical design accommodates the lighting loads and control requirements.

Coordinate with the electrical engineer early on the lighting power density, which is constrained by energy codes (ASHRAE 90.1, IECC, or local equivalents) that set maximum watts per unit area. The lighting design must achieve the required light levels within the power allowance, which pushes toward efficient sources (LED) and careful fixture selection. The architect who specifies inefficient fixtures and then asks the engineer to make the energy code work has created a conflict that results in either code non-compliance or compromised lighting.

### Address Emergency, Life Safety, and Special Use Lighting

Beyond the functional and aesthetic lighting, buildings require emergency lighting (to illuminate egress paths during power failure), exit sign lighting, and lighting for special uses (security, audiovisual presentation, cleaning, maintenance). These systems have code requirements for illuminance, duration (emergency lighting must operate for a minimum duration on battery or generator power), and placement, and they must be integrated into the lighting design rather than added as an afterthought.

Design the emergency lighting to provide the required illuminance on the egress path — not just at the fixtures, but at the floor, measured at the required points — and specify a system (battery, generator, or self-contained fixtures) appropriate to the building's life safety infrastructure. Consider night lighting in residential and healthcare uses, security lighting in parking and perimeter areas, and the lighting needed for maintenance access to building systems. The building that is beautifully lit for occupancy but unsafe during evacuation or impossible to maintain has failed a fundamental requirement.

## Common Traps

### Treating Lighting as Fixture Selection After the Design Is Complete

The architectural design is developed through schematic and design development without lighting consideration, and fixtures are selected during construction documents to provide the required light levels. The trap is that the quality of the lighting — the daylight availability, the glare control, the integration of light with the architectural form — is determined by the building design, not by the fixtures, and by the time fixtures are selected, the opportunities for integrated lighting design have passed. The building will be illuminated but not well-lit, and the difference is visible to every occupant.

### Designing Daylight Without Shading Controls

The rendering shows beautiful daylight streaming through full-height glazing, and the building is constructed with the glazing but without exterior shading or appropriate interior shading. The trap is that uncontrolled daylight produces glare and overheating, and the occupants — who cannot work in the glare or tolerate the heat — cover the glazing with blinds that eliminate the daylight entirely, leaving the building darker and more dependent on electric light than if the glazing had been smaller. The daylight strategy that does not include shading is a strategy for covered windows, not for daylight.

### Optimizing Illuminance and Ignoring Glare

The lighting calculation shows the required lux levels are achieved, and the design is considered complete, without analysis of glare. The trap is that high illuminance from the wrong direction — exposed lamps, bright windows in the field of view, high-brightness fixtures directly overhead — produces discomfort and disability that the lux level does not capture, and the occupants experience the space as uncomfortable even though it meets the illuminance standard. Lighting quality includes glare control, color rendering, and directionality, and a design that optimizes only illuminance has optimized the easiest metric at the expense of the experience.

### Selecting LEDs Without Attention to Color Quality

The LED fixtures are selected for efficacy and lumen output, without attention to color rendering, and the space is lit with poor-CRI light that makes skin tones gray, colors dull, and clinical assessment unreliable. The trap is that LED technology varies enormously in color quality, and the cheapest high-efficacy LEDs often have poor color rendering, and the energy savings are achieved at the cost of visual quality. In healthcare, retail, and any space where color matters, specify high-CRI or high-TM-30 LEDs and verify the specification, because the substitution of cheaper, lower-quality LEDs during construction is common and the difference is immediately visible.

### Designing Lighting Controls That Occupants Override

The photosensors and occupancy sensors are specified and installed, but the controls cycle lighting annoyingly, fail to detect stationary occupants, or respond too slowly to changing conditions, and the occupants override them to a fixed state. The trap is that lighting controls that are not designed for occupant acceptance are worse than no controls, because the overridden system provides neither energy savings nor occupant satisfaction. Specify the right sensor technology for the space, tune the controls after installation, provide manual override, and educate the occupants — a controls system that works with the occupant will save energy; one that fights the occupant will be defeated.

### Neglecting Nighttime and Circadian Lighting in 24-Hour Occupancies

The healthcare facility or senior living residence is designed with uniform bright lighting for staff visibility, and the lighting is the same at 3 AM as at 3 PM. The trap is that continuous bright light disrupts patient sleep, impairs healing, and exacerbates the delirium common in hospitalized older adults, and the staff-friendly lighting is patient-harmful lighting. Design 24-hour occupancies with distinct day and night lighting, with high circadian-stimulus light during the day and warm, low-level light at night that allows staff navigation without circadian disruption for the patients.

### Ignoring Energy Code Constraints Until Construction Documents

The lighting design specifies fixtures and light levels without regard to the energy code's lighting power density limits, and during construction documents it is discovered that the design exceeds the allowance. The trap is that the late discovery forces a redesign — fewer fixtures, lower-wattage sources, or reduced light levels — that compromises the lighting concept, and the redesign is done under pressure rather than with design intent. Integrate the energy code constraints from schematic design, and track the lighting power density as the design develops, so that the final design achieves both the lighting quality and the code compliance.

## Self-Check

- Have I designed the daylighting strategy as architecture — orientation, form, glazing, shading, interior finishes — from schematic design, not as skylights added late?
- Have I established light levels, color quality (CRI/TM-30), and color temperature based on the specific tasks and occupants, using standards as the basis?
- Have I designed for glare control — from both daylight and electric light — as a primary constraint, using shading and appropriate fixture selection and placement?
- Does my electric lighting zoning correspond to daylight availability, and are controls specified to integrate daylight and electric light and to respond to occupancy?
- For 24-hour occupancies — healthcare, senior living — have I designed distinct day and night lighting to support circadian regulation and sleep?
- Have I coordinated the lighting power density with energy code limits from the beginning, tracking it through design development?
- Have I engaged a lighting designer for demanding applications, and coordinated their work with the electrical engineer's power and control design?
- Have I designed and specified emergency, egress, and life safety lighting to meet code, integrated into the overall lighting design?
