# Structural System Selection And Lateral Design

The structural system is the skeleton that makes the architecture stand, and its selection shapes the building's spans, its floor-to-floor heights, its materiality, its openness, and its cost, long before any calculation is run. The architect does not size beams or calculate loads — that is the structural engineer's licensed work — but the architect selects and coordinates the system, because the system is an architectural decision with structural consequences, not a structural decision imposed on the architecture. Agents often treat structure as a constraint to be received and miss that the system choice is a design lever, that the lateral system in particular determines where solid walls and braced bays must live in the plan, and that a structural concept decided without architectural input produces a building the architecture must accommodate rather than one it shapes. The architect owns the integration; the engineer owns the calculation. The goal is a structural concept, selected jointly and coordinated through the grid and the lateral layout, that serves the architecture and that the engineer can confidently design.

## Core Rules

### Select The Gravity System Against Spans, Loads, And Architecture

The gravity system — steel framing, concrete framing, mass timber, masonry bearing, precast — is chosen against the required spans, the live loads, the floor-to-floor constraints, the fire-rating requirements, and the architectural intent. Long spans and column-free space favor steel or post-tensioned concrete; short spans and repetitive plans favor precast or masonry; exposed structure and sustainability goals may favor mass timber, subject to code and fire constraints. Understand the implications of each system on floor depth, because deeper structure raises the floor-to-floor and the building's height and cost, and on the column grid, because a system that cannot span far forces columns into the plan where the architecture does not want them. Select the system in dialogue with the structural engineer, who confirms feasibility and cost, but lead with the architectural requirements the system must serve.

### Plan The Structural Grid As An Architectural Decision

The structural grid — the column locations and the bay spacing — is one of the most consequential architectural decisions, because it orders the plan, determines the column-free spans, and constrains every subsequent layout. Establish the grid early, aligned to the program's module — the parking bay, the office lease span, the residential unit bay — because a grid that does not serve the program forces columns into rooms and drives inefficiency. Coordinate the grid across floors, because a column that moves between floors creates a transfer, and transfers are expensive and consume space. Keep the grid regular where possible, because irregular grids complicate the structure and the construction, but introduce deliberate variation where the architecture demands it and the structure can absorb it. The grid is the architect's, validated by the engineer.

### Locate The Lateral System Deliberately In The Plan

The lateral force-resisting system — moment frames, braced frames, shear walls — resists wind and seismic loads, and its elements are rigid and must be placed where the architecture can accept them. Shear walls are solid and continuous, and they must run from foundation to roof in the same location, which constrains the plan on every floor; braced frames occupy bays that cannot be opened; moment frames permit openness but require deeper beams and special joints. Locate the lateral elements early in design, distributed symmetrically to avoid torsion, aligned to avoid blocking corridors and doors, and continuous through the building's height, because a lateral system added late forces walls into places the architecture has already designed around. The lateral layout is a collaboration: the architect proposes locations that serve the plan, the engineer confirms they work structurally, and the two iterate until the system is both architecturally and structurally sound.

### Account For Seismic Versus Wind Governing Conditions

The governing lateral load — wind or seismic — depends on the location, the height, the building's mass, and its stiffness, and the governing condition shapes the structural and architectural response. In seismic regions, the building's weight and its irregularity drive the loads, so a heavy, irregular building pays a seismic penalty, and the code imposes configuration limits on setbacks, soft stories, and plan irregularities that the architect must respect. In wind regions, the building's height and exposure drive the loads, and the lateral system must resist overturning and drift. Understand which condition governs, because it changes the constraints: seismic design penalizes mass and irregularity and rewards symmetry and continuity, while wind design penalizes height and exposure. The architect must know the governing condition to design a plan and massing the lateral system can serve.

### Respect Structural Irregularity Limits In Seismic Design

The seismic code defines plan and vertical irregularities — torsional irregularity, re-entrant corners, discontinuous shear walls, soft and weak stories, mass and stiffness discontinuities — and irregular buildings trigger design penalties, additional analysis, and in some cases are not permitted. The architect must understand these limits, because an irregular massing or plan — a setback, a discontinuous wall, a soft ground story for retail — can create an irregularity that complicates or prohibits the design. Design the massing and the lateral layout to avoid irregularities where possible, because a regular building is simpler, cheaper, and safer in seismic events, and where an irregularity is unavoidable, engage the engineer early to confirm it can be permitted and to scope the additional analysis. Treating seismic configuration limits as a design constraint, not an afterthought, is what prevents late structural-driven redesign.

### Coordinate Floor-To-Floor, Structure Depth, And Building Height

The structural system's depth — the distance from floor slab to floor slab that the structure consumes — drives the floor-to-floor height, which drives the total building height, which drives the cost and the zoning and code compliance. A steel system with composite decking may be shallow, a concrete system with dropped beams deeper, a long-span system deepest of all, and the choice ripples into the curtain wall area, the envelope cost, and the building's compliance with height limits. Coordinate the structure depth with the mechanical system's needs, because ducts and structure compete for the same ceiling space, and a shallow structure with deep ducts may not save floor-to-floor. The floor-to-floor is an integration decision the architect owns, informed by the structural and mechanical depths, because it determines the building's fundamental vertical economics.

### Define The Boundary Between Architectural And Structural Responsibility

The structural engineer designs the primary gravity and lateral elements, but many conditions sit at the boundary — cladding attachments, slab edges, openings, penetrations, toppings, and architectural elements that carry load — and unclear responsibility produces gaps. Define who designs each condition: the engineer sizes the slab and the beams, the architect coordinates the openings and the penetrations, and the engineer reviews the effect of each on the structure. Coordinate penetrations for mechanical, plumbing, and electrical systems, because a duct or pipe through a beam or a shear wall is a structural condition that must be reviewed and detailed, not a free edit. Establish a coordination protocol so that every condition at the boundary is assigned, reviewed, and documented, because uncoordinated penetrations and attachments are a leading source of field problems.

## Common Traps

### Selecting A System For Aesthetics Without Confirming Feasibility

The architect selects mass timber for its exposed warmth or a long-span steel system for its openness, without confirming that the system can meet the fire-rating, the span, the vibration, or the cost constraints, and the design advances on an assumption the structure cannot support. The mechanism is that aesthetic and programmatic preferences drive the selection and feasibility is assumed, and the false signal is that the system "exists" and so must be usable. The harm is that late discovery forces a system change that ripples through the floor-to-floor, the fire ratings, the finishes, and the cost, often degrading the very architecture that drove the choice. Every structural system selection must be confirmed with the engineer against the project's actual loads, spans, ratings, and budget before the design commits to it.

### The Lateral System Added After The Plan Is Fixed

The team designs the floor plan without locating the lateral elements, then asks the engineer to fit shear walls or braced frames into the completed plan, and the elements land in corridors, doorways, or unit interiors. The mechanism is that the lateral system is treated as an engineering overlay rather than a plan-making element, and the false signal is that the plan is complete. The harm is that the lateral elements disrupt the architecture, force redesign of the units or the corridors, and may create an irregular configuration that triggers seismic penalties. The lateral system must be located as part of the plan's development, with the architect and engineer iterating on placement, symmetry, and continuity, because a lateral system that fits the plan and works structurally is the product of joint design, not of late insertion.

### Ignoring Seismic Configuration Irregularities

The architect designs a setback tower, a soft ground story for a lobby, or a plan with a large re-entrant corner, and the configuration constitutes a seismic irregularity that triggers design penalties, additional analysis, or prohibition. The mechanism is that seismic irregularities are defined in the code and are not intuitive to designers focused on form, and the false signal is that the massing "works" visually and programmatically. The harm is that the engineer must either undertake expensive additional analysis, redesign the lateral system to compensate, or in extreme cases inform the team the configuration is not permitted, forcing a massing change late in design. The architect must know the irregularity categories and design within them, engaging the engineer early where the architecture pushes the limits, because seismic configuration is a design constraint, not a review finding.

### Floor-To-Floor Set Without Coordinating Structure And Mechanical Depth

The architect sets the floor-to-floor based on ceiling height and program, without accounting for the structural depth or the mechanical duct depth, and the assembled building cannot fit the systems in the available space. The mechanism is that floor-to-floor is set early on programmatic grounds and the systems are assumed to fit, and the false signal is that the dimension is established. The harm is that the team must either deepen the floor-to-floor, raising the building's height and cost and potentially breaching zoning, or compress the ceiling, degrading the architecture. The floor-to-floor must be set through a section study that stacks the structure, the ducts, the ceiling, and the floor finish, coordinated with the structural and mechanical engineers, because the vertical dimension is an integration problem, not a programmatic one.

### Columns And Load-Bearing Walls In Conflict With The Program

The structural grid places columns in the middle of rooms, bearing walls where openings are needed, or transfer columns where the program assumed continuity, because the grid was set without the program or the program changed without the grid. The mechanism is that the grid and the program evolve on separate tracks and are never reconciled, and the false signal is that each is internally consistent. The harm is columns in living rooms, bearings walls blocking doorways, and transfers that consume space and budget, all discovered late when the cost of change is high. The grid must be set to serve the program, reconciled at each milestone as the program evolves, and the bearing walls must be located where the architecture can accept them, because the structure and the program are one design.

### Uncoordinated Penetrations Through Structural Elements

The mechanical and plumbing design runs ducts and pipes through beams, slabs, and shear walls without structural review, weakening the elements or requiring costly field fixes. The mechanism is that the disciplines lay out their systems independently and the penetrations are not coordinated, and the false signal is that each system fits in its allotted space. The harm is weakened structure, field conflicts that delay construction, and change orders to revise either the structure or the systems, with the cost borne by the owner. Every penetration through a structural element must be reviewed by the structural engineer, detailed on the drawings, and coordinated in a clash-detection pass, because uncoordinated penetrations are a structural risk and a construction dispute.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has the gravity system been selected against the project's spans, loads, floor-to-floor, fire-rating, and architectural intent, with feasibility confirmed by the structural engineer?
- [ ] Is the structural grid planned to serve the program's module, coordinated across floors to avoid transfers, and as regular as the architecture permits?
- [ ] Has the lateral system been located in the plan during design, distributed symmetrically, continuous through the height, and coordinated with corridors and openings?
- [ ] Is the governing lateral condition — wind or seismic — understood, and do the massing and plan respect the seismic configuration limits where seismic governs?
- [ ] Has the floor-to-floor been set through a coordinated section study that stacks structure, mechanical ducts, ceiling, and finishes?
- [ ] Are columns and load-bearing walls reconciled with the program at each milestone, with no columns in rooms or bearing walls blocking openings?
- [ ] Has the boundary between architectural and structural responsibility been defined, with a protocol for reviewing every penetration, attachment, and opening?
- [ ] Has the structural engineer been engaged early and iteratively, with the architect leading integration and the engineer owning calculation and sizing?
