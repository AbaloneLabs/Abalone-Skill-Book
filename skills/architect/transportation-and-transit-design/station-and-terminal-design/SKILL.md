# Station and Terminal Design

A transit station is a building whose primary function is to move very large numbers of people through a sequence of decision points, fare gates, platforms, and vertical circulation, and whose failure is measured not in aesthetics but in crowd congestion, missed connections, and safety incidents during the peak. The judgment problem is that a station looks like a large waiting room on a plan, so designers tend to shape it for architectural drama and underweight the passenger flow analysis that determines whether the concourse clears at the peak or chokes at the fare array. A platform that is too narrow for the peak load becomes a crush hazard; a vertical circulation core with too few escalators becomes a bottleneck that delays the connection the station exists to provide; a concourse sized for the average hour cannot clear the peak. This skill covers the decisions that determine whether the station moves people, including passenger demand analysis, platform and concourse geometry, vertical circulation, fare array and gate line, and the integration of multiple transit modes.

## Core Rules

### Size Every Element From the Peak Passenger Demand, Not the Average Hour

A transit station must be sized for the peak design hour, which is the busiest fifteen-to-thirty-minute window of the busiest day the system is expected to handle within its design life, and every element (platform, concourse, fare array, vertical circulation, corridor) must be verified against that peak. The peak demand is expressed in passengers per minute or passengers per hour in the peak direction, and it drives the width of platforms, the number of fare gates, the number and width of stairs and escalators, and the area of waiting spaces. The designer must obtain the ridership forecast from the transit authority, must apply a peak factor to convert the daily or hourly figure to the design minute, and must verify that every element clears that load without exceeding the crowd density the safety standard allows (commonly roughly 2 to 3 passengers per square meter for comfort, higher for emergency analysis). The tradeoff is that peak sizing produces a station that feels oversized in the off-peak, but a station sized for the average hour fails catastrophically at the peak and cannot be cheaply expanded.

### Design Platforms for Boarding, Alighting, and Dwell, Not Just for Standing

A platform must hold the waiting passengers, the alighting passengers, and the circulating passengers simultaneously during the peak, and it must be wide enough that the crowd at the platform edge does not obstruct the circulation path behind it. Platform width must account for the queuing zone at the edge (where passengers wait to board), the alighting zone (where passengers exit the train), and the circulation zone (where passengers move to and from the vertical circulation), and the designer must verify that the sum of these zones does not exceed the crowd density threshold. Platform length must match the train length with a margin for stopping precision, and the platform must be straight or follow a geometry the train can serve without excessive gap. The designer must also consider dwell time (the time the train spends at the platform with doors open), because a platform that slows boarding and alighting increases dwell, which reduces the line capacity and cascades into delays across the system.

### Provide Vertical Circulation Capacity That Exceeds the Peak One-Way Load

Vertical circulation (stairs, escalators, elevators) connects the concourse to the platform, and it must be sized so that the peak load can clear the platform or fill it within the headway between trains, otherwise passengers miss connections or accumulate on the platform. Escalator capacity is roughly 6,000 to 9,000 passengers per hour per unit depending on speed and width, stair capacity is lower, and elevator capacity is far lower and serves primarily accessibility and freight. The designer must provide redundant vertical circulation (at least two independent paths) so that a single escalator failure does not strand passengers, must size the total capacity to clear the peak one-way load within the train headway, and must locate the vertical circulation to distribute passengers along the platform rather than concentrating them at one end. The trap is providing the code-minimum number of stairs and treating escalators as convenience rather than as the primary capacity element they are.

### Lay Out the Fare Array and Gate Line for the Peak Bidirectional Flow

The fare array (the row of fare gates, turnstiles, or validators) is the choke point where passengers pass between the unpaid and paid areas, and it must process the peak bidirectional flow without queuing that backs into the concourse. The number of gates must be computed from the peak entry and exit demand and the processing rate per gate (which depends on the fare technology, the gate width, and the share of passengers with strollers, bicycles, or luggage), and the gate line must be laid out so that entry and exit flows do not cross. The unpaid concourse must hold the queue that forms during the peak without blocking the approach from the street, and the paid area must hold the queue that forms at the vertical circulation without blocking the gate line. The designer must model the queue at the gate line during the peak and verify it does not exceed the available concourse depth.

### Integrate Multiple Transit Modes With Explicit Transfer Paths and Connection Times

An intermodal station (rail to bus, rail to rail, ferry to rail) exists to enable transfers, and the transfer path between modes must be direct, legible, and short enough that the connection time is competitive with the headways of the connecting services. The designer must map every transfer pair (rail to bus, bus to rail, rail to ferry), must measure the walk time for each path, and must verify that the connection time (walk plus wait) is within the threshold the operations plan defines as acceptable. Transfer paths must be legible, with clear sightlines and wayfinding, and must not require the passenger to exit the paid area and re-enter where a same-system transfer is intended. The tradeoff is that a compact intermodal layout improves transfer time but may concentrate circulation, while a spread layout reduces crowding but lengthens the connection; the resolution is to prioritize the highest-volume transfer pairs with the shortest, most direct paths.

## Common Traps

### Sizing the Station for Average Ridership and Failing the Peak Design Hour

A station is sized for the average daily ridership divided across the operating hours, and the platform, concourse, and vertical circulation pass a nominal load check. The mechanism of the trap is that transit demand is sharply peaked, with a large share of the daily load concentrated in a one-to-two-hour window, and a station sized for the average hour is overwhelmed at the peak, producing platform crowding that exceeds the safety density, missed trains, and a cascade of delays. The false signal is the daily ridership figure, which suggests adequate capacity but masks the peak concentration. The harm is crowd safety incidents, service delays, and a station that must be retrofitted or expanded at far greater cost. The defense is to size every element from the peak design minute, apply the peak factor, and verify crowd density against the safety standard.

### A Platform Width Sized for Standing That Ignores the Boarding, Alighting, and Circulation Zones

A platform is dimensioned to hold the waiting passengers at a comfortable density, and the width passes a simple area calculation. The mechanism of the trap is that a platform must simultaneously hold waiting passengers at the edge, alighting passengers exiting the train, and circulating passengers moving to and from the vertical circulation, and a width sized only for standing crowd density is too narrow once the three zones are overlaid, producing a platform edge crowd that blocks circulation and creates a fall risk at the platform edge. The false signal is the platform area calculation, which suggests adequate space but ignores the zone conflict. The harm is platform crowding, safety risk at the edge, and dwell delays that reduce line capacity. The defense is to allocate the platform width across the queuing, alighting, and circulation zones and verify the combined density at the peak.

### Vertical Circulation at the Code Minimum That Becomes a Bottleneck at the Peak

A station provides the code-minimum number of stairs and one escalator per direction, and the egress check passes. The mechanism of the trap is that the code minimum addresses emergency egress, not the operational peak flow, and a single escalator per direction becomes a bottleneck that cannot clear the platform within the train headway, causing passengers to miss connections and accumulate on the platform beyond the safe density. The false signal is the egress compliance, which proves emergency capacity but not operational flow. The harm is missed connections, platform crowding, and a station that underperforms its ridership potential. The defense is to size vertical circulation from the peak one-way load and the train headway, provide redundant paths, and distribute the circulation along the platform.

### A Fare Gate Line That Processes Average Flow but Queues at the Bidirectional Peak

The fare array is sized for the average entry rate, and the gate line passes a simple throughput calculation. The mechanism of the trap is that the peak is bidirectional and concentrated, with large entry and exit flows occurring simultaneously, and a gate line sized for average flow produces queues at the gates that back into the concourse, block the approach from the street, and delay passengers past their train. The false signal is the gate throughput figure, which suggests processing capacity but ignores the bidirectional peak and the queue depth. The harm is missed trains, concourse congestion, and passenger frustration. The defense is to model the bidirectional peak queue at the gate line, size the gate count and the unpaid and paid concourse depth to hold it, and separate entry and exit flows so they do not cross.

### Transfer Paths Between Modes That Are Long, Indirect, or Require Re-Entry

An intermodal station places the bus terminal, the rail station, and the ferry dock at separate entries, and the transfer requires the passenger to exit, cross a street, and re-enter. The mechanism of the trap is that a long or indirect transfer path adds walk time and wayfinding burden that exceeds the acceptable connection threshold, making the transfer uncompetitive and driving passengers to single-mode car trips, which defeats the purpose of the intermodal investment. The false signal is the co-location of modes on the site, which suggests integration but masks the poor connection. The harm is low transfer ridership, underused connecting services, and a facility that does not deliver the mode-shift it was funded to achieve. The defense is to map every transfer pair, measure the walk time, keep the highest-volume paths short and direct, and avoid requiring re-entry for same-system transfers.

## Self-Check

- Did I obtain the peak design hour ridership forecast, apply the peak factor to derive the design minute, and verify that every element (platform, concourse, fare array, vertical circulation, corridor) clears that load within the crowd density standard?
- Did I allocate the platform width across the queuing zone at the edge, the alighting zone, and the circulation zone, and verify the combined density at the peak does not exceed the safety threshold?
- Did I size vertical circulation from the peak one-way load and the train headway, provide at least two redundant paths, and distribute the circulation along the platform to avoid end concentration?
- Did I model the bidirectional peak queue at the fare gate line, size the gate count to process it, and verify the unpaid and paid concourse depth holds the queue without blocking the street approach?
- Did I map every transfer pair between modes, measure the walk time for each path, and verify the connection time is within the acceptable threshold for the operations plan?
- Did I keep the highest-volume transfer paths short, direct, and legible, and avoid requiring passengers to exit and re-enter for same-system transfers?
- Did I verify platform length matches the train length with stopping margin, and that the platform geometry does not create an excessive train-to-platform gap?
- Did I confirm that dwell time at the platform is not increased by boarding and alighting congestion, since increased dwell reduces line capacity and cascades into system delays?
