# Submittal And Shop Drawing Review

Submittal review is the construction-administration task at which the design intent meets the contractor's means and methods, and it is the task at which most construction-phase design errors are caught or missed. The judgment problem is that a submittal is the contractor's detailed proposal for how the work will be executed, and the architect's review is for conformance with the contract documents — not for the contractor's means and methods, not for dimensions the contractor is responsible for, and not for a re-performance of the design. The skilled architect reviews the submittal against the contract documents, focuses on the design intent and the specified requirements, limits the review to the architect's responsibility, stamps the submittal with the correct action, and documents the review. The recurring failures are the perfunctory "approved" stamp, the review that exceeds the architect's responsibility (and assumes liability for the contractor's work), the submittal that conflicts with other trades, the delegated design not checked, and the review that takes too long and delays the work. The architect's role is to review for conformance with the design intent, to limit the review to the architect's responsibility, and to document the review.

## Core Rules

### Review For Conformance With The Contract Documents

The submittal review is for conformance with the contract documents — the design intent, the specified requirements, the detailed assemblies — and the review confirms that the contractor's proposed products and assemblies meet the documents. Review for conformance, not for the contractor's means and methods, and not for a re-performance of the design.

- Review the submittal against the contract documents: the drawings, the specifications, the general requirements, and the addenda, and confirm the submittal conforms to the design intent and the specified requirements.
- Confirm the products, the materials, the assemblies, the dimensions (the design dimensions, not the contractor's installation dimensions), the performance, and the quality, because the submittal must conform to the documents.
- Confirm the deviations: identify where the submittal deviates from the documents, and flag the deviations for resolution, because a deviation is a change that must be documented.
- Do not review for the contractor's means and methods, the safety, the installation techniques, or the construction sequencing, because these are the contractor's responsibility, not the architect's.

### Limit The Review To The Architect's Responsibility

The architect's review is limited by the contract, and exceeding the limit assumes liability the architect does not carry. Limit the review to the design intent and the specified requirements, and do not assume responsibility for the contractor's work.

- Do not check the contractor's installation dimensions, the fabrication dimensions, or the field dimensions, because these are the contractor's responsibility, and checking them assumes the liability for them.
- Do not review the contractor's means and methods, the safety, the erection procedures, or the construction sequencing, because these are the contractor's responsibility.
- Do not review for code compliance of the contractor's means and methods, because the code compliance of the means and methods is the contractor's responsibility.
- State the limit of the review in the submittal stamp, and document that the review is for conformance with the design concept and the information given, not for the dimensions, the fabrication, or the installation.

### Stamp The Submittal With The Correct Action

The submittal stamp is the architect's action on the submittal, and the action must be correct and unambiguous. Use the correct action — approved, approved as noted, revise and resubmit, or rejected — and document the basis.

- "Approved" or "Furnish as Submitted": the submittal conforms to the contract documents, and the contractor may proceed. Use this action only when the submittal fully conforms, because an unqualified approval assumes the submittal is correct.
- "Approved as Noted" or "Furnish as Submitted Subject to Corrections Noted": the submittal conforms with the noted corrections, and the contractor may proceed with the corrections. Use this action when the corrections are minor and do not change the submittal's character, and document the corrections.
- "Revise and Resubmit" or "Submit Specified Item": the submittal does not conform, and the contractor must revise and resubmit. Use this action when the submittal has significant deviations that require revision, and document the required revisions.
- "Rejected" or "See Remarks": the submittal is rejected, and the contractor must submit a new submittal. Use this action when the submittal is fundamentally non-conforming, and document the basis.
- Document the basis for the action in the remarks, because the remarks are the record of the review.

### Coordinate Submittals Across Trades

The submittals of different trades interact — the structural steel and the mechanical ductwork, the curtain wall and the structural frame, the electrical and the lighting — and the conflicts between trades are the most common source of construction-phase problems. Coordinate the submittals across trades, and identify the conflicts.

- Review the submittals for cross-trade conflicts: the structural steel and the mechanical, the curtain wall and the structure, the electrical and the lighting, the plumbing and the structure, because the conflicts are at the interfaces.
- Identify the conflicts, and require the contractor to resolve them, because the conflicts are the contractor's coordination responsibility.
- Do not assume that the submittals are coordinated because they are submitted separately, because the separate submission does not confirm the coordination.
- Use the coordination to inform the review, and flag the conflicts for resolution.

### Check The Delegated Design

The delegated design — the steel connections, the trusses, the precast, the engineering by the contractor's engineer — is a part of the work the contractor designs, and the architect's review confirms that the delegated design conforms to the design intent and the performance requirements, not that the engineering is correct. Check the delegated design for conformance, and document the limit.

- Confirm that the delegated design conforms to the design intent: the loads, the performance, the geometry, and the connections, because the delegated design must serve the design.
- Confirm that the delegated design is sealed by a licensed engineer, because the engineering is the responsibility of the contractor's engineer, not the architect.
- Do not check the engineering calculations, because the engineering is the contractor's engineer's responsibility, and checking it assumes the liability.
- Document the limit of the review of the delegated design, and confirm that the review is for conformance with the design intent, not for the engineering.

### Manage The Submittal Log And The Schedule

The submittal log tracks the submittals, their status, and their review, and the schedule of the submittals affects the construction schedule. Manage the log, review the submittals within the contract time, and flag the delays.

- Maintain the submittal log: the submittals, their status (submitted, in review, approved, revise and resubmit), and the dates, because the log is the record of the submittal process.
- Review the submittals within the contract time (commonly 14 days), because a delayed review delays the construction, and the delay may be the architect's responsibility.
- Flag the submittals that are critical to the schedule, and prioritize them, because the critical submittals affect the construction sequence.
- Flag the submittals that are late (submitted after the schedule), and document the impact, because a late submittal may be the contractor's responsibility for a delay.

### Document The Review

The submittal review is documented by the stamp, the remarks, and the log, and the documentation is the record of the architect's review. Document the review, and maintain the record.

- Document the stamp: the action, the date, the reviewer, and the remarks, because the stamp is the architect's action.
- Document the remarks: the specific corrections, deviations, and required revisions, because the remarks are the basis of the action.
- Maintain the log: the submittals, the status, the dates, and the actions, because the log is the record of the process.
- Maintain the file: the submittals, the stamps, the remarks, and the correspondence, because the file is the evidence of the review.

## Common Traps

### The Perfunctory "Approved" Stamp

When the submittal is stamped "approved" without a thorough review — a quick scan, a reliance on the contractor's representation, a desire to avoid delay — the approval assumes the submittal conforms, and the non-conformities are missed, to be discovered in the field when they are expensive to fix. The mechanism is that the approval satisfies the process, so the review feels complete, while the submittal was not thoroughly reviewed, and the non-conformities (the wrong product, the wrong dimension, the wrong assembly) propagate to the field. The harm is that the building is built to a non-conforming submittal, the corrections are expensive and delayed, and the liability for the missed non-conformities falls on the architect who approved, while the architect believed the review was done. The defense is to review the submittal thoroughly against the contract documents, to confirm the products, the dimensions, the assemblies, and the quality, to use the correct action (approved, approved as noted, revise and resubmit, rejected) based on the review, to document the basis in the remarks, and to recognize that an unqualified approval assumes the submittal is correct and assumes the liability for it, because a perfunctory approval is not a review but an assumption.

### The Review That Exceeds The Architect's Responsibility

When the architect reviews the contractor's installation dimensions, the fabrication dimensions, or the means and methods, the review exceeds the architect's responsibility, and the architect assumes the liability for the contractor's work, because the review of the contractor's responsibility is an assumption of it. The mechanism is that the thorough review reads as diligent practice, so it feels responsible, while the review exceeds the contract, and the architect assumes the liability for the dimensions, the fabrication, and the means and methods that are the contractor's responsibility. The harm is that, when the contractor's dimensions or methods fail, the liability falls on the architect who reviewed them, and the architect's effort to be thorough produces the liability the contract was meant to limit, while the architect believed the review was diligent. The defense is to limit the review to the design intent and the specified requirements, to state the limit in the submittal stamp, to not check the contractor's installation or fabrication dimensions, to not review the means and methods, and to recognize that the thorough review of the contractor's responsibility is an assumption of it, because the contract limits the architect's responsibility and exceeding the limit defeats the limit.

### The Submittal That Conflicts With Other Trades

When the submittals are reviewed in isolation — the structural steel without the mechanical, the curtain wall without the structure — the cross-trade conflicts are not identified, and the conflicts are discovered in the field, where they are expensive to fix, because the coordination was not done in the review. The mechanism is that each submittal reads as conforming to its own documents, so the review feels complete, while the conflicts between the trades (the duct and the beam, the mullion and the column) are not identified, and the field discovers them. The harm is that the building, with uncoordinated trades, requires rework in the field, the corrections are expensive and delayed, and the conflicts generate disputes over responsibility, while the architect believed the submittals were reviewed. The defense is to review the submittals for cross-trade conflicts, to identify the conflicts at the interfaces, to require the contractor to resolve them (the coordination is the contractor's responsibility), to use the coordination to inform the review, and to recognize that the submittals interact and a review in isolation misses the conflicts, because the conflicts are at the interfaces and the interfaces are where the coordination is tested.

### The Delegated Design Not Checked For Conformance

When the delegated design — the steel connections, the trusses, the precast — is stamped "approved" without confirming that it conforms to the design intent and the performance requirements, the delegated design may not serve the design, and the non-conformance is discovered in the field or in a failure. The mechanism is that the delegated design reads as the contractor's engineer's responsibility, so the review feels unnecessary, while the architect's review is for conformance with the design intent and the performance requirements, and the non-conformance is missed. The harm is that the building, with a delegated design that does not conform, requires rework or fails to perform, and the liability for the non-conformance is disputed, while the architect believed the delegated design was the contractor's responsibility. The defense is to check the delegated design for conformance with the design intent and the performance requirements, to confirm the loads, the geometry, and the connections, to confirm the delegated design is sealed by a licensed engineer, to not check the engineering calculations (the engineer's responsibility), and to document the limit of the review, because the delegated design must serve the design and the conformance is the architect's review.

### The Review That Takes Too Long

When the submittal review takes longer than the contract time, the construction is delayed, and the delay may be the architect's responsibility, because the contract requires the review within the time, and a delayed review is a breach. The mechanism is that the review reads as thorough, so the delay feels justified, while the contract requires the review within the time, and the delay delays the construction. The harm is that the construction is delayed, the delay may be the architect's responsibility, and the project suffers the cost and the schedule impact, while the architect believed the review was thorough. The defense is to review the submittals within the contract time, to prioritize the critical submittals, to flag the submittals that require more time and communicate the status, to maintain the submittal log, and to recognize that the review time is a contract obligation and a delay is a breach, because the construction depends on the review and a delayed review delays the construction.

### The Submittal Treated As A Change Order Opportunity

When the submittal reveals a deviation from the contract documents and the deviation is treated as a change order opportunity (by the contractor, seeking additional cost) or as a design error (by the architect, seeking to correct it), the deviation is not resolved on its merits, and the resolution is driven by the financial interest rather than the contract. The mechanism is that the deviation reads as a change, so the financial interest is engaged, while the deviation may be a contractor's substitution (resolved by the substitution provisions), a design error (resolved by the architect), or an ambiguity (resolved by the clarification), and the resolution depends on the cause. The harm is that the deviation is not resolved on its merits, the change order is disputed, and the project suffers the cost and the delay, while the parties pursue their financial interests. The defense is to identify the cause of the deviation (contractor's substitution, design error, ambiguity), to resolve it by the appropriate provision (substitution, correction, clarification), to document the cause and the resolution, and to recognize that the deviation is a contract issue to be resolved on its merits, not a financial opportunity to be pursued.
