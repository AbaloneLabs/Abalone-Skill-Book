# RFI Response And Design Clarification

RFI response is the construction-administration task at which the contract documents are interpreted, clarified, and occasionally corrected, and it is the task at which scope creep, design changes, and liability are most easily introduced through careless language. The judgment problem is that an RFI is the contractor's request for clarification of the documents, and the architect's response should interpret the documents as written, not improve them, not change them, and not add scope — but the pressure to resolve the construction issue, the desire to be helpful, and the ambiguity of the documents often lead the architect to issue a response that changes the design, adds scope, or assumes liability the documents did not carry. The skilled architect responds to the RFI by interpreting the contract documents, limits the response to the clarification requested, identifies when the RFI reveals a genuine ambiguity or error (and handles it through the correct channel — clarification, change order, or supplemental instruction), and documents the response. The recurring failures are the response that changes the design, the response that adds scope, the response that assumes liability, the response that takes too long, and the RFI used as a change-order vehicle. The architect's role is to interpret the documents, to clarify the design intent, and to document the response without changing the scope.

## Core Rules

### Respond By Interpreting The Contract Documents

The RFI response is an interpretation of the contract documents, and the interpretation should be grounded in the documents as written. Respond by interpreting the documents, and identify the specific drawings, specifications, and details that answer the question.

- Respond by interpreting the contract documents: identify the specific drawings, specifications, details, and notes that answer the RFI, and quote or reference them, because the response should be grounded in the documents.
- Do not invent a new requirement: if the documents do not address the question, say so, and identify the appropriate channel (a clarification, a supplemental instruction, or a change) for the resolution.
- Do not improve the design: the RFI is not an opportunity to redesign, and the response should interpret the documents as written, not as the architect wishes they were written.
- Confirm the interpretation against the documents, and ensure the response is consistent with the design intent and the other documents, because an inconsistent interpretation creates new conflicts.

### Limit The Response To The Clarification Requested

The RFI response should address the specific question asked, and not expand to related issues, additional requirements, or scope additions. Limit the response to the clarification requested, and document the limit.

- Address the specific question asked, and do not expand to related issues, additional requirements, or scope additions, because an expanded response adds scope and liability.
- Do not use the RFI response to add requirements that are not in the documents, because the response interprets the documents and does not create them.
- If the RFI reveals a related issue, flag it for separate resolution (a separate clarification or a change), and do not resolve it in the response, because the resolution of the related issue may be a change.
- Document the limit of the response, and confirm that the response addresses the question and does not expand the scope.

### Identify When The RFI Reveals An Ambiguity Or Error

Not every RFI is a request for clarification of an unambiguous document; some RFIs reveal a genuine ambiguity or error in the documents, and the response must identify this and handle it through the correct channel. Identify the ambiguity or error, and handle it correctly.

- Identify when the RFI reveals an ambiguity (the documents are unclear or conflicting) or an error (the documents are wrong), and do not paper over it with an interpretation, because the ambiguity or error requires a resolution, not an interpretation.
- Handle the ambiguity by a clarification: issue a clarification that resolves the ambiguity, consistent with the design intent, and document it.
- Handle the error by a correction: if the documents are wrong, issue a correction (by supplemental instruction if minor, by change order if the correction changes the scope or the cost), and document it.
- Do not treat an ambiguity or an error as the contractor's problem, because the documents are the architect's responsibility, and the resolution is the architect's task.

### Prevent Scope Creep Through The Response

Scope creep is the expansion of the work through the RFI responses, and it is the most common source of construction-phase cost increases that are attributed to the architect. Prevent the scope creep by limiting the response to the documents, and by identifying the changes that require a change order.

- Prevent the scope creep by limiting the response to the interpretation of the documents, and by not adding requirements, details, or features that are not in the documents.
- Identify the changes that require a change order: if the RFI response would change the scope, the cost, or the time, it is a change, and it should be handled by a change order, not by the RFI response.
- Do not use the RFI response to approve a substitution, a value-engineering change, or a scope change, because these are changes that require the change-order process.
- Document the limit of the response, and confirm that the response does not change the scope, the cost, or the time.

### Use The Correct Channel For The Resolution

The RFI response is one of several channels for the construction-phase communication, and the correct channel depends on the nature of the resolution. Use the correct channel — the RFI response, the clarification, the supplemental instruction, or the change order — based on the nature.

- Use the RFI response for the interpretation of the documents, when the documents answer the question and the response is a clarification of the answer.
- Use the clarification (or the supplemental instruction) for the resolution of an ambiguity or a minor correction, when the documents are unclear or wrong and the resolution does not change the scope or the cost.
- Use the change order for the resolution that changes the scope, the cost, or the time, when the resolution is a change to the contract.
- Do not use the RFI response for a change, because the change requires the change-order process and the agreement of the parties.

### Document The Response And Maintain The Log

The RFI response is documented by the response, the drawings or details that accompany it, and the log, and the documentation is the record of the clarification. Document the response, and maintain the log.

- Document the response: the interpretation, the specific documents referenced, and the clarification, because the response is the record of the clarification.
- Document the drawings or details that accompany the response, when the clarification requires a sketch or a detail, because the drawing is part of the clarification.
- Maintain the RFI log: the RFIs, the dates, the responses, and the status, because the log is the record of the process.
- Maintain the file: the RFIs, the responses, the clarifications, and the related correspondence, because the file is the evidence of the clarifications.

### Respond Within The Contract Time

The RFI response is subject to the contract time, and a delayed response delays the construction. Respond within the contract time, and flag the RFIs that require more time.

- Respond within the contract time (commonly 10 to 14 days), because a delayed response delays the construction, and the delay may be the architect's responsibility.
- Flag the RFIs that require more time (the complex clarifications, the changes that require the client's input), and communicate the status, because the communication manages the expectation.
- Prioritize the RFIs that are critical to the construction sequence, and respond to them first, because the critical RFIs affect the schedule.
- Flag the RFIs that are late (submitted after the submittal schedule), and document the impact, because a late RFI may be the contractor's responsibility for a delay.

## Common Traps

### The Response That Changes The Design

When the RFI response changes the design — adds a detail, modifies an assembly, specifies a product not in the documents — the response exceeds the interpretation and becomes a design change, and the change adds scope, cost, and liability that the documents did not carry. The mechanism is that the change reads as a helpful clarification, so the response feels constructive, while the response changes the design, and the change adds scope and cost that the contractor may claim as a change order, and the liability for the change falls on the architect. The harm is that the project's cost increases, the change is disputed, and the liability for the change falls on the architect who issued it, while the architect believed the response was a clarification. The defense is to respond by interpreting the contract documents, to not invent a new requirement, to not improve the design, to identify the specific documents that answer the question, and to recognize that a response that changes the design is a change, not a clarification, because the RFI response interprets the documents and does not create them.

### The Response That Adds Scope

When the RFI response adds scope — a new requirement, a higher quality, an additional feature — the response expands the work, and the expansion adds cost that the owner did not agree to and the contractor may claim. The mechanism is that the added scope reads as a clarification of the intent, so the response feels appropriate, while the response adds a requirement that is not in the documents, and the addition is a scope change that requires a change order. The harm is that the project's cost increases, the scope change is disputed, and the liability for the added scope falls on the architect who added it, while the architect believed the response was a clarification. The defense is to limit the response to the clarification requested, to not add requirements, details, or features that are not in the documents, to identify the changes that require a change order, to use the correct channel for the resolution, and to recognize that a response that adds scope is a change, not a clarification, because the scope is defined by the documents and the response does not expand it.

### The Response That Assumes Liability

When the RFI response assumes liability the documents did not carry — by taking responsibility for the contractor's dimensions, by approving the contractor's means and methods, by warranting a performance — the response expands the architect's liability beyond the contract, and the expansion is not covered by the insurance or the fee. The mechanism is that the response reads as a thorough clarification, so it feels responsible, while the response assumes the liability for the contractor's work, and the assumption is not covered by the contract. The harm is that, when the contractor's work fails, the liability falls on the architect who assumed it, and the architect's effort to be thorough produces the liability the contract was meant to limit, while the architect believed the response was responsible. The defense is to limit the response to the interpretation of the documents, to not take responsibility for the contractor's dimensions, means, or methods, to state the limit of the response, and to recognize that the assumption of liability is not a clarification but an expansion of the contract, because the contract limits the liability and the response does not expand it.

### The Response That Takes Too Long

When the RFI response takes longer than the contract time, the construction is delayed, and the delay may be the architect's responsibility, because the contract requires the response within the time, and a delayed response is a breach. The mechanism is that the response reads as thorough, so the delay feels justified, while the contract requires the response within the time, and the delay delays the construction. The harm is that the construction is delayed, the delay may be the architect's responsibility, and the project suffers the cost and the schedule impact, while the architect believed the response was thorough. The defense is to respond within the contract time, to prioritize the critical RFIs, to flag the RFIs that require more time and communicate the status, to maintain the RFI log, and to recognize that the response time is a contract obligation and a delay is a breach, because the construction depends on the response and a delayed response delays the construction.

### The RFI Used As A Change-Order Vehicle

When the contractor uses the RFI to propose a change — a substitution, a value-engineering change, a scope reduction — and the architect responds to the RFI as if it were a clarification, the change is processed as a clarification rather than a change order, and the change-order process (with its cost and time adjustments) is bypassed. The mechanism is that the RFI reads as a request for clarification, so the response feels appropriate, while the RFI is a proposal for a change, and the response, if it approves the change, processes it as a clarification without the change-order process. The harm is that the change is made without the cost and time adjustments, the owner pays or saves without the agreement, and the change is disputed, while the architect believed the response was a clarification. The defense is to identify the RFIs that are proposals for changes (substitutions, value-engineering, scope reductions), to route them to the change-order process, to not approve them in the RFI response, to document the routing, and to recognize that an RFI that proposes a change is a change request, not a clarification, because the change requires the change-order process and the agreement of the parties.

### The Ambiguity Or Error Papered Over

When the RFI reveals a genuine ambiguity or error in the documents, and the architect responds with an interpretation that papers over the ambiguity or the error, the resolution is not grounded in the documents (because they are ambiguous or wrong), and the response assumes a design intent the documents do not support. The mechanism is that the interpretation reads as a clarification, so the response feels grounded, while the documents are ambiguous or wrong, and the interpretation invents a resolution that the documents do not support. The harm is that the resolution is not grounded in the documents, the contractor may claim the resolution as a change, and the liability for the resolution falls on the architect who issued it, while the architect believed the response was a clarification. The defense is to identify when the RFI reveals an ambiguity or an error, to handle it by the correct channel (a clarification for the ambiguity, a correction for the error), to not paper over it with an interpretation, and to recognize that an ambiguity or an error requires a resolution, not an interpretation, because the documents are the architect's responsibility and the resolution is the architect's task.
