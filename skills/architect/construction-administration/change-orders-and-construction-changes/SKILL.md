# Change Orders And Construction Changes

Changes during construction are inevitable, but how they are processed determines whether the project stays controlled or descends into disputes. A change order is a formal, agreed amendment to the contract that adjusts scope, price, or time. A construction change directive is an instruction to proceed with a change before the cost and time are fully agreed, used when the work cannot wait. Claims are assertions by the contractor of entitlement to additional cost or time that the owner or architect has not yet acknowledged. Each of these instruments carries contractual and financial weight, and mishandling them produces cost overruns, schedule slippage, and adversarial relationships that can poison the rest of the job. Agents often treat changes as isolated transactions and miss that every undocumented or casually approved change becomes precedent and evidence in any later dispute.

Use this skill when processing change orders, evaluating construction change directives, handling contractor claims, or assessing the cost and schedule impact of construction changes. The goal is to maintain rigorous documentation, distinguish legitimate entitlement from inflated or unfounded claims, protect the owner's budget and schedule, and preserve the contractual framework.

## Core Rules

### Establish The Root Cause And Responsibility Before Agreeing To A Change

Before any dollar or day is added, determine why the change is needed and who bears responsibility. A change required because the contract documents contain an error or omission is the design team's exposure, typically addressed through the owner. A change required because the contractor failed to coordinate, mis-measured, or damaged work is the contractor's responsibility. A change required because of an owner request or an unforeseen site condition is a different category again. Failing to establish cause first leads to the owner paying for contractor errors or the design team absorbing responsibility that is not theirs.

Establish cause by:

- requesting the contractor's written justification, including the specific contract document reference and the reason the change is required;
- distinguishing among design error or omission, owner-directed change, unforeseen condition, and contractor means and methods;
- documenting the determination of responsibility before negotiating price or time;
- consulting the owner and, where design liability may be involved, the appropriate consultant or insurer.

### Distinguish A Change Order From A Construction Change Directive

These two instruments serve different purposes and carry different risks. A change order represents agreement among owner, contractor, and architect on the scope, price, and schedule adjustment before the work proceeds. A construction change directive orders the work to proceed when agreement cannot be reached in time, with cost and time resolved later. Misusing the directive to avoid negotiation, or using a change order when urgency demands a directive, creates procedural and financial confusion.

Distinguish by:

- using a change order when scope, price, and time can be agreed before the work proceeds;
- reserving the construction change directive for genuinely time-critical changes where delay would cause greater harm, such as weather exposure or life-safety conditions;
- understanding that a directive does not guarantee the owner will accept the contractor's later pricing, and that disputes over directive costs are common;
- documenting the basis for the directive clearly so later pricing can be evaluated against the actual scope performed.

### Evaluate Cost Proposals Against A Defensible Basis

Contractor pricing for changes is frequently inflated, loaded with overhead, or unsupported by actual costs. The architect's role is not to accept the first number but to evaluate it against a defensible basis, whether that is unit prices from the contract, quotes from subcontractors, time and material records, or an independent estimate. Unchallenged pricing accumulates into significant overruns.

Evaluate by:

- checking whether the contract contains unit prices or allowances that govern the pricing of the changed work;
- requiring breakdowns of labor, material, equipment, overhead, and profit, and scrutinizing each component;
- comparing proposed costs to independent estimates or recent similar work;
- challenging lump-sum pricing that cannot be substantiated, and requesting supporting documentation;
- recognizing that overhead and profit on changes are often double-counted or applied at rates exceeding the contract basis.

### Evaluate Schedule And Time Impact Rigorously

Time is money on a construction project, and schedule extensions or claims for delay are as consequential as direct cost. The contractor may assert that a change disrupted the critical path and request a schedule extension, liquid damages relief, or extended overhead recovery. Accepting a schedule claim without analysis grants time and money that may not be owed.

Evaluate by:

- requiring the contractor to demonstrate, through an updated critical path method schedule, how the change actually affects the critical path;
- distinguishing between changes on the critical path, which affect project completion, and changes with float, which may not;
- recognizing concurrent delay, where owner and contractor delays overlap, which can complicate or negate entitlement;
- documenting time extensions granted and their basis, because undocumented verbal extensions are routinely disputed;
- deferring schedule analysis to a qualified schedule or delay consultant for complex or high-value claims.

### Document Everything Contemporaneously

The strength of any party's position in a change or claim dispute rests on contemporaneous documentation: records created at the time events occurred, before positions hardened. Recollections reconstructed after the fact carry little weight. The architect's discipline in maintaining daily records, correspondence, and logs is what makes the difference between a defensible position and an unsupported assertion.

Document by:

- maintaining daily observation notes that record conditions, weather, work in progress, and any issues or directions;
- requiring the contractor to provide notice of changes or claims within the contractual notice period, and noting when notice is late;
- preserving all correspondence, RFI responses, submittal actions, photographs, and meeting minutes that bear on the change;
- recording the rationale for accepting, rejecting, or negotiating each change, in writing, at the time.

### Recognize And Escalate Claims Appropriately

A claim is a demand for additional compensation or time that has not been resolved through normal change processing. Claims can escalate into mediation, arbitration, or litigation. The architect must recognize when a dispute has crossed from a change into a claim, follow the contractual claims procedure, and avoid taking actions that prejudice the owner's position. The architect should also recognize when a claim implicates the design team's own liability and notify the appropriate parties.

Escalate by:

- following the contract's claims resolution procedure, including required notices and timeframes;
- not unilaterally denying or admitting claims without owner involvement and, where warranted, legal counsel;
- recognizing when a claim alleges design error and ensuring the owner's and the design team's interests are separately protected;
- recommending qualified counsel or a construction claims consultant for high-value or legally complex disputes.

## Common Traps

### The Casual Verbal Change

On site, an architect or owner verbally directs a small change, add a receptacle here, shift a wall there, and everyone assumes it is too minor to document or that it will be captured later. The trap is that undocumented verbal changes accumulate invisibly, and when the contractor submits a bill or a claim months later, there is no record of what was actually directed, what the agreed price was, or whether the change was even authorized. The false signal is that the change seemed trivial and the parties trusted each other, so paperwork felt unnecessary. The harm is that the owner faces charges for work of uncertain origin and scope, the architect cannot prove the limits of what was directed, and the project budget erodes through a thousand untracked adjustments. Directing that every change, however small, be documented in writing before proceeding is what prevents this drift.

### Paying For Contractor Errors

When the contractor encounters a problem caused by their own mis-coordination, mis-measurement, or damaged work, they may frame the necessary correction as a change request, presenting it as though it flows from the design. The trap is that the architect, under pressure to keep the job moving and avoid conflict, processes the change without investigating whether the cause is actually a contractor responsibility. The false signal is that the request looks plausible and resolving it quickly serves the schedule. The harm is that the owner pays for the contractor's mistakes, the budget inflates, and a precedent is set that any problem can be converted into a billable change. Establishing root cause and responsibility before agreeing to any price or time is what prevents the owner from subsidizing the contractor's errors.

### The Inflated Change Pricing

Contractor pricing for changes routinely includes overhead and profit loaded on top of already-loaded rates, unsupported labor hours, or premium material costs that do not reflect actual procurement. The trap is that the numbers appear formal and itemized, so they feel authoritative, and the architect under time pressure stamps them through. The false signal is that a detailed breakdown equals a fair breakdown, when the components themselves may be inflated. The harm is that cumulative overpricing can add many percentage points to the change budget, and once accepted, the pricing is difficult to unwind. Evaluating every cost proposal against a defensible basis, unit prices, independent estimates, or substantiated actuals, is what keeps change pricing honest.

### The Undocumented Schedule Extension

The contractor asserts that a change has delayed the project and requests a schedule extension, and the architect grants it verbally or by informal note without analyzing the critical path or checking for concurrent delay. The trap is that granting the extension feels cooperative and avoids a confrontation, and the project is still finishing on time so no harm seems to occur. The harm materializes when the project ultimately finishes late and the contractor points to the granted extensions as proof that delays were excusable, escaping liquid damages and recovering extended overhead costs that were never truly owed. Requiring critical path analysis and documenting the basis for any time extension is what prevents schedule claims from succeeding retroactively.

### The Late Notice Claim

The contract requires the contractor to give notice of a change or claim within a specific period, often 7 to 21 days, but the contractor submits a claim months after the underlying event, often bundled into a large end-of-job claim. The trap is that the architect, focused on substance rather than procedure, evaluates the claim on its merits without enforcing the notice requirement, forfeiting the owner's strongest procedural defense. The false signal is that fairness suggests considering the claim regardless of timing. The harm is that late claims are far harder to investigate because conditions and memories have degraded, and waiving the notice requirement weakens the owner's position on every subsequent claim. Enforcing contractual notice requirements, while documenting the enforcement, is what preserves the procedural protections the contract provides.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has the root cause and responsibility for each change been established and documented before agreeing to any price or time adjustment?
- [ ] Is each change processed through the correct instrument, change order when agreement is reached, construction change directive only when urgency demands?
- [ ] Has each cost proposal been evaluated against a defensible basis such as contract unit prices, independent estimates, or substantiated actuals, with overhead and profit scrutinized?
- [ ] Have schedule and time impact claims been evaluated against the critical path, with concurrent delay considered and the basis for any extension documented?
- [ ] Is there contemporaneous documentation, daily notes, correspondence, notices, and photographs, supporting the position taken on each change?
- [ ] Have claims been recognized, processed through the contractual procedure, and escalated to counsel or a claims consultant when high-value or design-liability-implicating?
