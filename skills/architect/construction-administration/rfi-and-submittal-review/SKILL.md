# RFI And Submittal Review

During construction administration, RFIs and submittals are the primary channels through which the design intent is tested against the realities of fabrication, procurement, and field assembly. An RFI is a formal request for clarification or interpretation of the contract documents; a submittal is the contractor's detailed proposal of what they intend to furnish or install. Both carry legal weight: a delayed, vague, or overreaching response can trigger schedule and cost claims, while an approval that exceeds the architect's scope of review can transfer liability for means, methods, and safety onto the design team. Agents often treat these documents as paperwork to clear quickly and miss that each response is a contractual act that must be timely, scoped, and defensible.

Use this skill when reviewing RFIs, processing submittals and shop drawings, or responding to contractor clarifications. The goal is to answer within contractual time limits, stay within the proper scope of review, preserve the design intent, and avoid inadvertently taking on liability for construction means, methods, sequencing, or safety.

## Core Rules

### Respond Within The Contractual Time Limit And Track Every Item

RFIs and submittals are schedule-critical. A late response is not merely an administrative lapse; it becomes the basis for a delay or disruption claim, because the contractor can assert that the architect's tardiness prevented timely procurement or installation. The contract typically specifies a response period, often 10 or 14 days, and the clock starts when the item is received in proper form.

Manage time by:

- logging every RFI and submittal on receipt with a unique number, date received, required response date, and responsible reviewer;
- screening immediately for items that affect the critical path or long-lead procurement, and expediting those;
- returning incomplete or improperly formatted items promptly rather than letting the clock run, with a clear statement of what is needed;
- never letting a deadline pass without a response or a written, agreed extension;
- documenting the date of each response, because in a dispute the record of timeliness matters more than the content.

### Define And Hold The Scope Of Your Review

The architect's review of submittals is for general conformance with the design concept and the information given in the contract documents. It is not a review of construction means, methods, techniques, sequences, procedures, or safety precautions, which remain the contractor's responsibility. This boundary is the single most important protection against liability, and it must be reflected in both the substance and the wording of every response.

Hold scope by:

- reviewing for conformance with the design intent, dimensions, materials, performance criteria, and coordination with other trades shown in the documents;
- explicitly disclaiming review of fabrication methods, erection sequences, field measurements, safety, and jobsite procedures;
- avoiding language that implies you have checked everything, such as "approved" without qualification, when your review was limited;
- using contractually defined action codes, such as "reviewed," "furnish as submitted," "revise and resubmit," or "rejected," each with precise meaning;
- remembering that an architect's review does not relieve the contractor from compliance with the contract documents or from errors in their own submittals.

### Distinguish Design Clarification From Design Change

Many RFIs ask for clarification, but some actually request a change to the design. Conflating the two is dangerous: a clarification that is really a change may trigger a cost or schedule adjustment that the owner is entitled to evaluate and approve, and silently answering a change request as if it were a clarification can expose the architect to a claim that they altered the work without authorization.

Distinguish by:

- determining whether the documents are genuinely ambiguous or contradictory, in which case clarification is appropriate;
- recognizing when the contractor's question reveals a condition that requires a design revision, and routing it through the change order process rather than answering informally;
- identifying when an RFI is actually a veiled claim or a request for a substitution, and handling it under the correct procedure;
- coordinating with the owner before responding to anything that has cost, schedule, or scope implications.

### Coordinate Consultant Input Before Responding

Many RFIs and submittals require input from structural, mechanical, electrical, civil, or fire protection engineers. Responding without that input, or responding before the consultant has reviewed, risks issuing a conflicting answer and creates exposure if the consultant later disagrees. The architect is typically the coordinator, not the author of every technical answer.

Coordinate by:

- routing discipline-specific items to the appropriate consultant with enough lead time for their review;
- consolidating consultant responses so the contractor receives a single, coherent answer rather than conflicting ones;
- never improvising a structural, life-safety, or MEP answer that belongs to an engineer;
- documenting when a consultant's input shaped the response, to preserve the chain of responsibility.

### Preserve The Design Intent And Reject Silent Substitutions

Submittals frequently contain substitutions: a manufacturer or model different from the specified basis of design, often offered for cost or availability reasons. Some substitutions are equivalent and acceptable; others quietly degrade performance, durability, maintainability, or aesthetics. The architect must evaluate substitutions against the specified performance criteria, not merely against the appearance of similarity.

Preserve intent by:

- comparing proposed substitutions against the salient characteristics named in the specifications, not just the category of product;
- rejecting substitutions that were not submitted through the formal substitution procedures and within the allowed time window;
- considering long-term implications such as maintenance, warranty, parts availability, and energy performance, not only first cost;
- documenting the basis for accepting or rejecting a substitution so the decision is defensible.

### Document Responses To Be Defensible Later

Every RFI response and submittal action is a potential exhibit in a dispute. Responses that are vague, undocumented, or verbally communicated and never confirmed in writing are difficult to defend. The discipline of clear, complete, written responses is what protects the architect, owner, and contractor alike.

Document by:

- writing responses that are specific, cite the relevant contract document, and state exactly what is required or approved;
- confirming any verbal direction in writing the same day, because verbal direction that is not confirmed is routinely disputed;
- retaining the complete record, including logs, correspondence, consultant input, and the final action;
- avoiding speculative or gratuitous commentary that could be misread as admitting an error or assuming a responsibility.

## Common Traps

### The Overreaching Approval

When an architect stamps a submittal "approved" without qualifying the scope of review, the approval can be read as a warranty that the item is correct in every respect, including fabrication details, field measurements, and safety of installation that were never actually checked. The trap is seductive because an unqualified approval feels collaborative and moves the project forward, and nothing goes wrong while the item installs correctly. But when a fabricated component fails to fit, leaks, or causes an accident, the contractor and owner point to the architect's blanket approval as proof that the design team assumed responsibility for the very means and methods the contract assigns to the contractor. The damage is a liability transfer that the architect never intended, often discovered only in litigation. Qualifying every action with the contractually defined review scope is what prevents an administrative act from becoming an unintended warranty.

### The Silent Design Change Disguised As Clarification

A contractor submits an RFI that appears to ask for clarification, but the only sensible answer requires altering the design, such as relocating a structural element or changing a fire-rated assembly. The architect, wanting to be helpful and keep the job moving, answers with the necessary change without routing it through the change order process or notifying the owner of the cost or schedule impact. The trap is that the response feels like problem-solving, and the project continues smoothly until the owner receives an invoice for work the architect effectively authorized without approval. The harm is twofold: the owner is surprised by a cost they never approved, and the architect is exposed to a claim for directing changes beyond their authority. Routing any answer that alters scope, cost, or schedule through the proper change process is what separates a legitimate clarification from an unauthorized directive.

### The Verbal Direction Never Confirmed

On a fast-moving site, the architect gives direction verbally, a detail here, a tolerance there, trusting that the paperwork will follow or that the relationship is good enough that it does not matter. The trap is that verbal direction is routinely misremembered, selectively recalled, or denied entirely once a cost or defect dispute arises, and without a contemporaneous written confirmation there is no reliable record of what was actually said. The false signal is that the project is proceeding fine and the parties are cooperative, so documentation feels unnecessary — until it becomes essential, at which point it is too late. The harm is that the architect cannot prove the direction given, cannot prove its scope, and may be held responsible for an outcome they never actually directed. Confirming every verbal instruction in writing the same day is the only reliable defense.

### The Substitution That Looks Equivalent

A submittal proposes a product that appears to match the specified category, a similar window system, a comparable membrane, an analogous fixture, and the architect approves it based on surface similarity without checking the salient performance characteristics. The trap is that substitutions are often engineered to look equivalent on a cut sheet while differing in the attributes that matter: air infiltration, thermal performance, durability, warranty, or maintenance requirements. The false signal is that the product is from a reputable manufacturer and the contractor assures equivalence, which suppresses the scrutiny the item deserves. The harm emerges months or years later when the substituted system underperforms, leaks, or fails prematurely, and the owner holds the architect responsible for approving a product that did not meet the specification's intent. Evaluating substitutions against the specified performance criteria, not against the appearance of similarity, is what prevents silent quality erosion.

### The Consultant Input Bypassed

Under schedule pressure, the architect answers an RFI that touches on structural capacity, fire protection, or mechanical coordination without routing it to the responsible engineer, reasoning that the answer seems obvious or that there is no time to wait. The trap is that the architect has now authored a technical answer outside their expertise, and if that answer conflicts with the engineer's later judgment or with code requirements, the architect bears the consequences. The false signal is that the answer appears reasonable and the project moves forward, so the risk feels hypothetical. The harm is a coordination conflict, a code violation, or a defect that traces directly to an answer the architect was not qualified to give. Routing discipline-specific questions to the appropriate consultant, even under pressure, is what preserves the proper division of responsibility.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is every RFI and submittal logged with a number, date received, required response date, and reviewer, and is no deadline allowed to pass without a response or agreed extension?
- [ ] Does each response and submittal action use contractually defined action codes and explicitly limit the scope of review to conformance with the design concept, disclaiming means, methods, sequencing, and safety?
- [ ] Have any items that actually require a design change been routed through the change order process with owner notification, rather than answered informally as clarifications?
- [ ] Have discipline-specific items been routed to the appropriate consultant with adequate lead time, and have consultant responses been consolidated into a single coherent answer?
- [ ] Have substitutions been evaluated against the specified salient performance characteristics, and have out-of-window substitutions been rejected or handled through the formal procedure?
- [ ] Has every verbal direction been confirmed in writing the same day, and does the record retain the logs, correspondence, and consultant input supporting each response?
- Is the reasoning documented clearly enough that another practitioner could review the basis and reproduce the conclusion?
- Are assumptions, uncertainties, and confidence levels stated explicitly rather than buried in a confident-sounding conclusion?
