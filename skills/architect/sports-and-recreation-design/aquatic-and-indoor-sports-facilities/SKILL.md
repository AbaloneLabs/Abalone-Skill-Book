# Aquatic and Indoor Sports Facilities

An aquatic center or an indoor sports hall is a building whose interior environment is extreme: a natatorium holds a large body of heated water whose evaporation loads the air with moisture and chlorine, and an indoor sports hall requires a long clear span and a tall volume for the sports it hosts. The judgment problem is that these facilities look like large rooms with a pool or a set of courts, so designers tend to shape them for the plan and underweight the environmental and structural systems that determine whether the building survives. A natatorium whose air handling cannot control humidity condenses water on every cold surface, corrodes the structure, and grows mold within years; an indoor sports hall whose structure intrudes on the court or whose clear height is too low for the sport cannot host the play it was built for. This skill covers the decisions that determine whether the facility functions and endures, including natatorium environmental control, pool deck and water quality, clear span and volume for sport, lighting and environmental separation, and spectator and athlete flow.

## Core Rules

### Design the Natatorium Environment as an Integrated Water, Air, and Envelope System

A natatorium is governed by the relationship between the pool water (heated, chemically treated, evaporating), the air (which must absorb and exhaust the moisture), and the envelope (which must not condense). The pool water evaporates continuously, loading the air with moisture and with chloramine byproducts that produce the characteristic pool smell and irritate lungs, and the air handling system must remove that moisture, supply tempered replacement air, and distribute it across the pool surface and the deck without creating drafts on the swimmers. The envelope (walls, roof, glazing, skylights) must be kept above the dew point of the pool air at every point, which requires thermal performance and thermal breaks that far exceed normal construction, because any cold surface condenses water, drips, corrodes, and grows mold. The designer must size the dehumidification system for the evaporation load, must design the air distribution to sweep the pool surface and the breathing zone, must detail the envelope to stay above the dew point, and must coordinate all three as a single system. The trap is treating the natatorium as a normal room with a pool and a dehumidifier, which fails within years.

### Control Water Quality and Pool Deck Drainage to Protect Swimmers and the Structure

Pool water quality is maintained by the filtration and chemical treatment system, and the pool deck must drain to prevent standing water, slip, and the migration of pool water into the structure. The pool must be designed with the turnover rate, the circulation pattern (surface skimming, floor returns), and the chemical control (chlorine, pH, and for some pools UV or ozone) that the bather load requires, and the pool tank must be waterproofed as a continuous system that does not leak into the ground or the structure. The pool deck must slope to deck drains that capture the splash and the wash-down water, must be finished with a slip-resistant surface that is still cleanable, and must be detailed so that the water does not migrate behind the pool gutter or into the wall base. The designer must coordinate the pool hydraulic design with the pool builder, must detail the deck drainage and the waterproofing, and must verify the water quality system meets the health code for the bather load.

### Provide the Clear Span and the Clear Height the Sport Requires

An indoor sports hall (basketball, volleyball, tennis, indoor soccer) requires a clear span across the full court area (no interior columns) and a clear height above the court that allows the sport to be played without the ball or the player striking the structure. The clear span is achieved with a long-span structural system (steel trusses, glulam, precast, or a tensile structure), and the clear height must be set by the sport's governing body standard (commonly 7 to 12 meters for court sports, more for indoor track or field events). The designer must verify the court layout (including run-off space around the court, the official dimensions, and the multiple-court configurations the facility needs), must provide the clear span and the clear height for every configuration, and must ensure the structure, the lighting, and the HVAC do not intrude into the playing volume. The trap is sizing the clear height for the primary sport and discovering it cannot host the secondary sport or the official competition the facility was meant to serve.

### Separate the Pool and Sport Environments When They Share a Building

When a natatorium and a dry sports hall share a building, the two environments must be separated by an air and vapor barrier, because the warm humid pool air will migrate into the dry sports hall and condense on its colder surfaces, corroding the structure and growing mold. The designer must provide a physical and environmental separation (a wall, a vestibule, a pressure differential) between the pool and the dry areas, must design the pool air handling to keep the pool zone at a slight negative pressure so moisture does not migrate out, and must verify the envelope of the dry areas stays above the dew point even with incidental humidity migration. The trap is placing the pool and the gym in the same building envelope without separation, which corrupts the dry environment within years.

### Plan Spectator, Athlete, and Equipment Flow for Competition and Training

An aquatic or indoor sports facility must separate the athlete flow (locker room to pool deck or court), the spectator flow (entry to seating), and the equipment flow (pool equipment, sports equipment, maintenance) so that they do not conflict during competition and training. Spectator seating must be sized for the competition crowd, must have sightlines to the pool or court, and must be accessed without crossing the athlete or the deck zone. Locker rooms must serve the peak bather load, must connect directly to the pool deck or the court, and must be sized for the team and the public mix. The designer must map the three flows, verify they do not conflict at the peak, and provide the accessible routes from the locker rooms to the pool and court that the code requires.

## Common Traps

### A Natatorium With a Dehumidifier but No Integrated Air, Water, and Envelope Strategy

A natatorium is designed with a pool and a dehumidification unit, and the air distribution, the envelope thermal performance, and the pool chemistry are treated as standard. The mechanism of the trap is that a dehumidifier sized for the evaporation load cannot control condensation if the air distribution does not sweep the pool surface and the breathing zone, or if the envelope has thermal bridges that drop below the dew point, so water condenses on the structure, the glazing, and the skylights, corroding the building and growing mold within a few years. The false signal is the dehumidifier on the equipment schedule, which suggests environmental control but masks the absence of the integrated air, envelope, and water strategy. The harm is structural corrosion, mold, premature envelope failure, and a facility that requires expensive remediation. The defense is to design the natatorium as an integrated water, air, and envelope system, distribute air to sweep the pool and the deck, and detail the envelope to stay above the dew point.

### Glazing or Skylights That Drop Below the Dew Point and Rain Condensation

A natatorium is designed with large windows or skylights for daylight and drama, specified with standard glazing. The mechanism of the trap is that the pool air dew point is high (often above 20 degrees Celsius), and standard double glazing or aluminum frames without thermal breaks drop below that dew point on a cold day, condensing water that runs down the glass, pools on the sills, corrodes the frames, and drips onto the deck and the swimmers, creating a slip hazard and a maintenance burden. The false signal is the daylight design, which suggests a bright pool but masks the condensation failure. The harm is continuous condensation, frame corrosion, slip hazards, and the eventual covering or removal of the glazing. The defense is to specify high-performance glazing and thermally broken frames that stay above the dew point, verify the interior surface temperature against the pool dew point, and provide condensation drainage at sills.

### A Clear Height Sized for the Primary Sport That Cannot Host Competition or Secondary Sports

An indoor sports hall is sized with a clear height adequate for recreational basketball, because that is the primary use, and the roof structure is set accordingly. The mechanism of the trap is that official competition for several sports (volleyball, badminton, indoor tennis) and secondary uses (indoor soccer, indoor track) require a greater clear height, and a hall sized for recreational play cannot host the official competition or the secondary sports, limiting the facility's programming and revenue and frustrating the users who expected a multi-sport venue. The false signal is the clear height for the primary sport, which suggests a functional hall but masks the shortfall against the full sport standard. The harm is limited programming, lost revenue, and user frustration. The defense is to set the clear height by the most demanding sport the facility will host, verify the structure and the lighting do not intrude, and protect the height during value engineering.

### A Pool and a Dry Gym in the Same Envelope Without Environmental Separation

A community facility combines a natatorium and a gymnasium under one roof, separated by a standard partition wall, to share lobby and locker room space. The mechanism of the trap is that the warm humid pool air migrates through the partition and the shared envelope into the dry gym, condensing on the colder gym surfaces, corroding the gym structure and equipment, and growing mold, because the two environments were not separated by an air and vapor barrier and a pressure differential. The false signal is the shared building, which suggests efficiency but masks the environmental conflict. The harm is corrosion and mold in the gym, premature failure of the gym envelope, and a facility that degrades faster than its peers. The defense is to separate the pool and the dry zones with an air and vapor barrier, keep the pool zone at negative pressure, and verify the dry zone envelope stays above the dew point.

### Spectator, Athlete, and Equipment Flows That Cross on the Pool Deck or Court

A facility is planned with the spectator entry, the locker room, and the equipment storage all accessing the pool deck or the court from the same side, because the plan was compact. The mechanism of the trap is that during competition the spectator crowd, the athletes, and the equipment handlers all converge on the same deck or court edge, producing congestion that disrupts the competition, creates a safety hazard on the wet deck, and degrades the experience for athletes and spectators. The false signal is the compact plan, which suggests efficiency but masks the flow conflict. The harm is disrupted competition, safety incidents, and a facility that cannot host clean events. The defense is to map the spectator, athlete, and equipment flows, separate their access to the deck or court, and verify they do not conflict at the peak.

## Self-Check

- Did I size the dehumidification for the evaporation load, design the air distribution to sweep the pool surface and the breathing zone, and detail the envelope to stay above the dew point at every point?
- Did I specify high-performance glazing and thermally broken frames for any windows or skylights, verify the interior surface temperature against the pool dew point, and provide condensation drainage?
- Did I coordinate the pool hydraulic design (turnover, circulation, chemical control) with the pool builder, detail the deck drainage and waterproofing, and verify the water quality meets the health code for the bather load?
- Did I set the clear span and clear height by the most demanding sport or configuration the facility will host, verify the structure, lighting, and HVAC do not intrude into the playing volume, and protect the height during value engineering?
- Did I separate the pool environment and any dry sports areas with an air and vapor barrier, keep the pool zone at negative pressure, and verify the dry zone envelope stays above the dew point?
- Did I map the spectator, athlete, and equipment flows, separate their access to the pool deck or court, and verify they do not conflict during competition?
- Did I size spectator seating for the competition crowd with sightlines to the pool or court, and access it without crossing the athlete or deck zone?
- Did I size locker rooms for the peak bather load, connect them directly to the pool deck or court, and provide the accessible routes the code requires?
