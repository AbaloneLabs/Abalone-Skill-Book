# User Group And Occupant Consultation

The people who will use a building know things the architect and the owner do not. They know how the current space fails, where the work actually happens, what the daily friction is, and what would make the difference between a building that supports the work and one that obstructs it. User consultation is the process of gathering that knowledge and translating it into design requirements. Architects often conduct consultation as a courtesy, a workshop to attend and a survey to distribute, then design from the program and their own assumptions, because user input is messy, contradictory, and often framed as wishes rather than needs. The result is a building that meets the documented program but misses the operational reality, where staff circumvent the design because it does not fit how the work actually flows. Effective consultation is not data collection; it is the disciplined translation of operational knowledge into design intelligence.

Use this skill when planning user consultation, conducting workshops or interviews, synthesizing user input into requirements, or using post-occupancy evaluation to inform design. The goal is a design grounded in how the building will actually be used.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Right Users And The Right Input

Not all users have the same knowledge, and not all input is equally useful. A consultation that convenes whoever is available gathers noise alongside signal.

Identify by:

- mapping the user populations, frontline staff, supervisors, support functions, visitors, patients, students, residents, and recognizing that each experiences the building differently;
- prioritizing users whose daily work the building must support, because their knowledge is the most operationally consequential;
- including users with accessibility needs, neurodivergent users, and users with disabilities, whose experience of the building is often most affected by design decisions and most often overlooked;
- including users from different shifts, roles, and tenures, because the night shift and the new hire see what the day shift and the veteran take for granted;
- recognizing that management's account of how work happens often differs from frontline staff's account, and seeking both.

### Distinguish Needs From Wants And Solutions

Users frequently express needs as solutions: "we need a bigger room," "we need more storage," "we need a wall here." Designing to the stated solution misses the underlying need, which may have a better design response.

Distinguish by:

- asking why, repeatedly, to surface the operational problem beneath the proposed solution, because a request for a bigger room may reflect a need for a different workflow, not more area;
- observing the work in its current setting, because users often cannot articulate what they do, and observation reveals the friction they have stopped noticing;
- separating functional needs, what the space must enable, from preferences, what users would like, and from habits, what users do because the current space forces it;
- recognizing that users often ask for what they know, replicating the current space, rather than imagining what could be better;
- translating input into performance requirements, such as "support concurrent small-group work without acoustic bleed," rather than literal specifications.

### Reconcile Contradictions Through Operational Understanding

User input is inherently contradictory. One department wants openness, another wants enclosure; one wants centralization, another wants autonomy. These are not errors to average out; they are different legitimate needs that the design must reconcile.

Reconcile by:

- mapping the contradictions to their sources, because conflicting requests often reflect different operational realities that can both be served if the underlying needs are separated;
- using the operational model, how the work flows, who interacts with whom, what is shared and what is private, as the arbiter of competing requests;
- recognizing when a contradiction reflects a genuine organizational tension that the owner, not the architect, must resolve, and escalating it rather than choosing silently;
- testing proposed resolutions with the users, because a solution that addresses the underlying need often satisfies parties whose surface requests conflicted;
- documenting the tradeoffs when not all needs can be met, so the choices are explicit and defensible.

### Sequence Consultation To The Design Phases

Consultation has different value at different phases. Early consultation shapes program and relationships; mid-phase consultation tests specific design responses; late consultation risks being window dressing because decisions are already made.

Sequence by:

- conducting operational discovery and programming consultation before schematic design, when input can shape the fundamental organization;
- bringing users back at schematic and design development to test specific proposals, because users respond more usefully to concrete options than to abstract questions;
- avoiding major consultation after design development, when changes are expensive and input can only frustrate, unless a specific operational question remains open;
- closing the loop with users who contributed, reporting back how their input shaped the design, because consultation that vanishes breeds cynicism and disengagement.

### Use Post-Occupancy Evaluation As Design Intelligence

The most valuable user input often comes from buildings already in use, whether the current project's predecessor or the firm's past work. Post-occupancy evaluation closes the feedback loop that turns experience into expertise.

Use POE by:

- evaluating the building the project replaces, if applicable, because its failures and successes are direct evidence of what the new design must do;
- gathering feedback on the firm's completed projects, because the gap between design intent and operational reality is where the firm learns;
- structuring POE to capture both measured performance, energy, space utilization, and experienced performance, comfort, satisfaction, workflow support;
- feeding POE findings into the firm's standards and the current project's requirements, so lessons are applied, not just collected;
- recognizing that without POE the firm repeats its mistakes, because no one returns to learn whether the design worked.

### Translate User Input Into Requirements The Design Can Use

Consultation produces narratives, preferences, and observations. The design needs requirements: specific, testable, and integrated into the program and the design criteria.

Translate by:

- synthesizing workshop and interview input into a documented set of functional requirements, tied to the operational model;
- expressing requirements as performance criteria where possible, so the design has latitude to respond rather than being locked to a literal request;
- prioritizing requirements, distinguishing those that are essential to the work from those that are beneficial, so tradeoffs can be made intelligently;
- connecting each requirement to its source, the user group and operational need it serves, so the rationale is traceable when the requirement is later questioned or value-engineered.

## Common Traps

### Consultation As Ceremony

The team holds a workshop, distributes a survey, records the input, and proceeds to design from the program and the architect's assumptions, having checked the consultation box. The trap mechanism is that the consultation satisfies the appearance of participation while the input never reaches the design decisions, and users recognize token consultation immediately. The false signal is the completed engagement report, which documents that input was gathered while concealing that it was not used. The harm is a building that meets the documented program but fails the operational reality, users who feel their time was wasted and whose cynicism makes future consultation harder, and a design that missed intelligence it could have had.

### Designing To Stated Solutions Instead Of Underlying Needs

Users request bigger rooms, more storage, specific layouts, and the architect designs to the requests without investigating the operational need beneath them. The trap is that the literal solution often fails to solve the real problem, because the user asked for what they know rather than for what they need, and the building inherits the current space's limitations in new form. The false signal is the satisfied user at the design presentation, who sees their request reflected, while the underlying friction remains unaddressed. The harm is a building that replicates operational problems at great expense, where staff work around the design because it solved the wrong problem, and the investment in consultation produced a more elaborate version of the old failure.

### Averaging Contradictory Input

Faced with conflicting user requests, the architect synthesizes a compromise that splits the difference, satisfying no one and serving the work poorly. The trap mechanism is that contradiction feels like an error to resolve rather than a signal to interpret, so the team averages instead of investigating the different operational needs the contradiction reveals. The false signal is the resolved requirements document, which appears to reconcile input while actually erasing the legitimate needs on both sides. The harm is a design that serves neither operational reality, where both user groups work around compromises that fit neither, and where the consultation, had it been interpreted rather than averaged, could have revealed a solution that served both.

### Consulting Only The Available Or The Senior

The consultation reaches the people who are easy to convene, often management or daytime staff, and misses the frontline, the night shift, the part-time, and the users with the most operational knowledge. The trap is that available voices are treated as representative, so the input is skewed toward those least affected by daily friction. The false signal is the comprehensive-seeming input, which appears thorough while sampling the wrong population. The harm is a design optimized for management's account of the work rather than the work itself, where the building looks right on paper and fails in operation, and the users whose knowledge would have prevented the failure were never in the room.

### No Feedback Loop

Users contribute input and never learn whether or how it shaped the design, so the consultation feels extractive and the relationship is one-directional. The trap is that closing the loop feels optional, a courtesy, when it is actually what makes consultation cumulative: users who see their input matter contribute more honestly next time, and users who see it ignored disengage. The false signal is the completed consultation, which appears self-contained while actually severing the relationship that ongoing engagement requires. The harm is that post-occupancy evaluation becomes impossible, because users will not share honest feedback with a team that did not respond to their earlier input, and the firm loses the learning loop that turns projects into expertise.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Does the consultation reach the right user populations, including frontline staff, accessibility-needs users, and different shifts and roles?
- [ ] Is input investigated for underlying operational needs rather than designed to as literal solutions?
- [ ] Are contradictions interpreted against the operational model rather than averaged into compromises?
- [ ] Is consultation sequenced to phases, with operational discovery before schematic design and option testing during design development?
- [ ] Is post-occupancy evaluation used as design intelligence, feeding lessons into the current project and firm standards?
- [ ] Is user input translated into prioritized, traceable performance requirements the design can act on?
- [ ] Is the loop closed with users, reporting how their input shaped the design?
- Is the reasoning documented clearly enough that another practitioner could review the basis and reproduce the conclusion?
