# Client Presentation And Design Approval

Design presentations are decision events, not status reports. Each presentation asks the client to commit, to approve a direction, to authorize the next phase, or to accept a tradeoff. Architects often treat presentations as showcases of the design, focusing on imagery and narrative, while under-managing the decision the client is actually being asked to make. The result is a presentation that wins admiration but not approval: the client is impressed, asks for more options, defers the decision, or approves something they do not fully understand, and the project either stalls or proceeds on a false consensus that unravels later. A presentation that secures a genuine, informed approval is one engineered for decision, not for display.

Use this skill when preparing any client presentation that seeks a decision, when structuring a phase approval gate, when managing client feedback that is vague or conflicting, or when a previous approval is being revisited. The goal is an informed, documented decision that holds.

## Core Rules

### Frame The Presentation Around The Decision Required

Before designing the presentation, define the decision the client must make. A presentation without a clear ask produces commentary, not commitment.

Frame around the decision by:

- stating, at the outset and in the invitation, what approval is being sought, such as schematic design approval, budget acceptance, or selection of a cladding strategy;
- structuring the presentation to build toward that decision, so that each section contributes the information the client needs to decide;
- distinguishing between decisions that are final at this gate and decisions that remain open, so the client knows what they are committing to and what they are not;
- confirming the decision authority in the room, because presenting to people who cannot approve wastes the meeting and defers the decision;
- ending with an explicit request for the decision, not an open invitation for further thoughts.

### Match The Presentation Depth To The Phase And The Decision

Over-presenting early, with fully rendered images that imply completion, forecloses the client's legitimate input. Under-presenting late, with diagrams that leave the design ambiguous, asks the client to approve what they cannot evaluate.

Match depth by:

- presenting concepts and relationships in schematic design, with enough clarity to convey the organizing ideas but enough openness to invite direction;
- presenting resolved systems, materials, and dimensions in design development, with specificity sufficient for the client to evaluate cost and performance implications;
- presenting comprehensive, coordinated information at construction document milestones, where the decision is to proceed to construction;
- calibrating rendering realism to the design's actual resolution, because a photorealistic image of an unresolved design misleads the client into believing decisions are made that are not;
- explaining what is fixed and what is still under study at each presentation, so the client reads the materials correctly.

### Make Tradeoffs And Constraints Visible

Clients approve more confidently when they understand the constraints that shaped the design and the tradeoffs it embodies. Hiding constraints to present a clean solution produces approvals that collapse when the constraints surface later.

Make tradeoffs visible by:

- explaining the competing demands the design reconciles, such as program area against budget, daylight against energy code, or site coverage against stormwater;
- presenting alternatives where a real choice exists, with the implications of each, rather than offering a single solution as inevitable;
- being explicit about what the design does not do, the program elements deferred, the qualities sacrificed for cost, the risks accepted;
- connecting the design to the project's stated priorities, so the client can see how the decision serves what they said mattered;
- quantifying where possible, because square footage, cost per square foot, and energy metrics make tradeoffs concrete where adjectives make them abstract.

### Manage Client Feedback Structurally

Client feedback is rarely clean. It arrives as preferences, reactions, second-hand opinions, and contradictions, often from multiple voices. Unstructured feedback produces a direction no one intended.

Manage feedback by:

- capturing feedback in writing during or immediately after the meeting, attributed to who said what, before it blurs;
- distinguishing between feedback that is direction, the client wants the entry reconfigured, and feedback that is reaction, the client is uneasy but cannot say why;
- identifying the underlying concern behind stated preferences, because a request for a bigger lobby may reflect a concern about arrival sequence that has other solutions;
- reconciling conflicting feedback from different client representatives by surfacing the conflict and asking the client to resolve it, rather than silently choosing one voice;
- summarizing the feedback and the resulting direction back to the client for confirmation, so the direction is agreed before work proceeds.

### Document Approvals Explicitly

An approval that is not documented is an approval that can be retracted without consequence. Verbal approvals in meetings dissolve into disagreement about what was agreed.

Document by:

- issuing a written summary of the presentation, the decisions made, and any conditions attached, promptly after the meeting;
- obtaining explicit sign-off on the deliverable and the decision, through a signature, an email confirmation, or a formal transmittal, scaled to the gate's significance;
- recording any scope changes, schedule impacts, or fee implications that follow from the client's direction, and securing agreement before acting;
- treating a client request for changes after approval as a scope event, documenting it and pricing it rather than absorbing it silently;
- maintaining a decision log across the project, so that the sequence of approvals and their conditions is traceable.

### Prepare For The Approval To Hold Under Pressure

Approvals are tested by cost, by time, by second thoughts, and by stakeholders not in the room. An approval that survives is one built on understanding, not on momentum.

Prepare by:

- ensuring the client understands the cost implications of what they approved, so the approval is not undone by the next estimate;
- surfacing the risks and tradeoffs at approval time, so they cannot be raised later as surprises that justify reversal;
- identifying who else needs to support the decision, a board, a funder, a user group, and sequencing their buy-in;
- recognizing when a client is approving to keep the schedule moving rather than from genuine conviction, and slowing down to build real consensus.

## Common Traps

### Presenting To Impress Rather Than To Decide

The architect invests in beautiful imagery and a compelling narrative, the client is visibly impressed, and the meeting ends with admiration but no decision. The trap mechanism is that the presentation is optimized for the architect's craft and ego rather than for the client's decision, so it generates reaction instead of commitment. The false signal is the positive meeting, the compliments, the engaged discussion, which feels like success but produces no approval and no direction. The harm is a project that stalls or proceeds on ambiguity, with the architect interpreting enthusiasm as consent and the client later reversing or revising because they never actually decided, and the relationship strained by a gap between what each party thought happened.

### Over-Resolved Early Presentations

A schematic design presentation built from photorealistic renderings implies the design is finished, foreclosing the client's legitimate role in shaping direction at the phase where direction is meant to be set. The trap is that the realism signals resolution the design does not have, so the client either accepts an image they cannot evaluate or pushes back on surface details because the substantive decisions feel already made. The false signal is the polished deliverable, which appears to demonstrate progress while actually premature-committing decisions that should still be open. The harm is that the client's input is displaced to late phases where changes are expensive, the design develops without the direction it needed, and the architect has trained the client to react to images rather than to engage with ideas.

### Hidden Constraints That Surface As Reversals

The architect presents a solution without explaining the constraints that shaped it, the budget pressure, the code limit, the structural compromise, and the client approves. The trap is that when the constraint surfaces later, through a cost estimate or a code review, the client experiences it as a surprise and a betrayal, because they approved a design they understood to be unconstrained. The false signal is the clean approval, which conceals that it was granted without the information needed to make it informed. The harm is a reversal that costs time and trust, because the client reasonably asks why they were not told, and the design must be reworked under worse conditions than if the constraint had been on the table from the start.

### Unmanaged Conflicting Feedback

Multiple client representatives give contradictory direction, the architect picks a path, and later is told they went the wrong way. The trap mechanism is that conflicting feedback feels resolvable by choosing, so the architect synthesizes without surfacing the conflict, but the synthesis satisfies no one and violates someone's direction. The false signal is the apparent agreement in the meeting, where no one objected because the conflict was not made explicit. The harm is rework driven by the dissenting voice that was never reconciled, eroded trust because the client feels unheard, and a design that lurches as different voices assert themselves in sequence, because the architect failed to force the client to speak with one voice.

### The Undocumented Approval

The client nods in the meeting, says "looks good, proceed," and the architect advances the design, only to find weeks later that the client has a different recollection or has changed their mind. The trap is that verbal approval feels sufficient in the moment, so no one documents it, and the approval evaporates when conditions change. The false signal is the warm meeting and the clear next steps, which create confidence that obscures the absence of a record. The harm is that the architect cannot defend the work already done, may absorb the cost of the reversal, and loses leverage in a scope dispute, all because the approval was never captured in a form that could be referenced.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Does the presentation state the specific decision being sought, and is the decision authority present?
- [ ] Is the presentation depth calibrated to the phase, avoiding over-resolution early and under-resolution late?
- [ ] Are the constraints and tradeoffs that shaped the design made visible to the client?
- [ ] Is client feedback captured in writing, with underlying concerns identified and conflicts surfaced for resolution?
- [ ] Is the approval documented in writing with any conditions, scope changes, or fee implications recorded?
- [ ] Are cost and risk implications understood by the client so the approval can hold under pressure?
- [ ] Is a decision log maintained so the sequence of approvals and their conditions is traceable?
- Are assumptions, uncertainties, and confidence levels stated explicitly rather than buried in a confident-sounding conclusion?
