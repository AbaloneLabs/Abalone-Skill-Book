# Entitlement Strategy And Schedule

Entitlements are the discretionary approvals — zoning relief, site plan review, special permits, design review, historic approval, environmental review, subdivision, and the building permit — that a project must obtain before construction, and the strategy and schedule for obtaining them determine whether the project proceeds on time, on budget, and at all. A complex project may require a dozen approvals from a half-dozen boards, each with its own criteria, its own timeline, its own sequencing dependencies, and its own risk of denial or delay. Agents often treat entitlements as a linear checklist, file the applications in the order they think of them, and discover too late that an approval they need was a prerequisite for another, that a board's calendar slipped the schedule by months, or that a denial reset the whole path. The architect's role is to define the entitlement strategy, to sequence the approvals, and to align the schedule with the design and the financing, because the entitlement path is the project's critical path. The goal is a strategy whose sequence is correct, whose schedule is realistic, whose risks are identified and mitigated, and whose approvals are obtained in the right order at the right time.

## Core Rules

### Map Every Required Approval And Its Dependencies

The first step in entitlement strategy is to map every approval the project requires — every board, every permit, every review — and the dependencies among them, because the sequence is determined by which approvals are prerequisites for which others. A site plan approval may require a zoning variance first; a building permit may require a design review and an environmental review; a subdivision may require a road approval and a utility approval. Research each approval's requirements, its prerequisites, its timeline, and its decision criteria, and build a dependency map that shows the critical path. The architect must map the approvals and the dependencies, because a missed dependency is a schedule slip, and a wrong sequence is a wasted application.

### Sequence The Approvals Along The Critical Path

Once the approvals and their dependencies are mapped, sequence them along the critical path, so that each approval is filed when its prerequisites are met and in time for the next approval to proceed. Identify the longest sequence — the chain of approvals that takes the most time — and manage it as the critical path, because a slip on the critical path slips the whole project. Look for opportunities to parallelize: approvals that are independent can be pursued concurrently, and some boards allow a concept review before the full application. The architect must sequence the approvals along the critical path, because the sequence determines the schedule, and the schedule determines the project's viability.

### Align The Entitlement Schedule With The Design Milestones

The entitlement schedule must be aligned with the design milestones, because each approval requires a specific level of design — a concept for a zoning hearing, a site plan for a site plan review, construction documents for a building permit — and the design must be ready when the application is filed. Build the design schedule backward from the entitlement deadlines, so that the design is at the right level at the right time, and build the entitlement schedule backward from the construction start, so that the approvals are in hand when construction must begin. The architect must align the entitlement schedule with the design milestones, because a design that is not ready delays the application, and an application that is filed too early is incomplete.

### Align The Entitlement Schedule With The Financing Milestones

The financing — the acquisition loan, the construction loan, the equity commitments — is tied to the entitlements, because lenders and investors require specific approvals before they release funds. The acquisition loan may require zoning confirmation; the construction loan may require the building permit; the equity may require the discretionary approvals. Align the entitlement schedule with the financing milestones, so that the approvals are obtained in time for the financing, and communicate the entitlement schedule to the lender and the investor, because their confidence depends on it. The architect must support the alignment of the entitlement and the financing schedules, because a slip in the entitlements can trigger a financing default.

### Identify The Risk Approvals And Mitigate Them

Not all approvals carry the same risk: a building permit is largely ministerial if the code is met, but a zoning variance, a special permit, a design review, or a historic approval is discretionary and can be denied. Identify the risk approvals — the discretionary ones, the ones with neighborhood opposition, the ones with subjective criteria — and develop a mitigation strategy for each. The mitigation may be early outreach, a stronger design response, a alternative approval path, or a fallback design that does not require the relief. The architect must identify the risk approvals and mitigate them, because a denial of a risk approval can reset the entire entitlement path.

### Build Float And Contingency Into The Schedule

The entitlement schedule must include float and contingency, because boards meet on fixed calendars, hearings are deferred, staff reviews take longer than estimated, and neighbors appeal. Build float into the critical path — time between approvals that absorbs the slippage — and build contingency into the overall schedule — a fallback if an approval is denied or delayed. Do not present a schedule with no float as realistic, because it is not, and a slip with no float becomes a crisis. The architect must build float and contingency into the schedule, because the entitlement process is uncertain, and a schedule without float is a schedule that breaks.

### Coordinate Across Jurisdictions And Agencies

A project may require approvals from multiple jurisdictions — the city, the county, the state, a special district — and multiple agencies — planning, building, fire, health, public works, environmental — and these must be coordinated. Each jurisdiction and agency has its own requirements, its own timeline, and its own concerns, and a conflict between them — a city approval that a state agency overrides, a fire requirement that conflicts with a planning condition — can stall the project. Map the jurisdictions and the agencies, coordinate the requirements, and resolve the conflicts early. The architect must coordinate across the jurisdictions and the agencies, because a project with conflicting approvals cannot proceed.

## Common Traps

### Treating Entitlements As A Linear Checklist

The team treats the entitlements as a checklist to be filed in order, without mapping the dependencies, and discovers that an approval they need was a prerequisite for another, so the sequence is wrong and months are lost. The mechanism is that the dependencies are not researched, and the false signal is that the approvals are independent. The harm is a schedule slip of months, a financing risk, and a project that may not proceed. The approvals must be mapped with their dependencies, and sequenced along the critical path, before any applications are filed.

### Filing Applications Before The Design Is Ready

The team files an application to save time, but the design is not at the level the application requires, and the application is incomplete, deferred, or denied, costing more time than was saved. The mechanism is that the design milestone is not aligned with the application, and the false signal is that an early filing saves time. The harm is a deferral, a denial, or a refile that slips the schedule. The design must be at the right level for each application, with the design schedule built backward from the entitlement deadlines.

### Underestimating The Discretionary Approvals

The team underestimates the risk and the time of the discretionary approvals — the variances, the special permits, the design reviews — and the schedule assumes they will be approved on the first hearing, and a deferral or a denial slips the project by months. The mechanism is that the discretionary approvals are treated like the ministerial ones, and the false signal is that the project meets the criteria. The harm is a schedule slip, a financing risk, and a project reset. The discretionary approvals must be identified as risks, mitigated with outreach and design response, and given float in the schedule.

### No Float In The Schedule

The schedule is built with every approval back-to-back and no float, and a single deferral — a board that continues a hearing, a staff review that takes an extra week — slips the entire schedule and triggers a financing or construction crisis. The mechanism is that the schedule is optimized for the best case, and the false signal is that the schedule is aggressive and realistic. The harm is a crisis when the inevitable slippage occurs. The schedule must include float on the critical path and contingency for the overall path, because the entitlement process is uncertain.

### Ignoring The Multi-Jurisdiction Conflicts

The team obtains the city approvals but discovers that a state agency or a special district has a conflicting requirement, and the project is stalled between the jurisdictions, requiring redesign or a new approval. The mechanism is that the jurisdictions are treated as independent, and the false signal is that the city approval is sufficient. The harm is redesign, new applications, and schedule loss. The jurisdictions and the agencies must be mapped, their requirements coordinated, and the conflicts resolved before the applications are filed.

### Misaligning The Entitlements And The Financing

The entitlement schedule and the financing milestones are not aligned, and a lender or an investor requires an approval that is not yet obtained, so the financing falls through or is delayed, and the project stalls. The mechanism is that the financing is treated as independent of the entitlements, and the false signal is that the approvals will come in time. The harm is a financing default, a stalled project, and a potential loss of the site. The entitlement schedule must be aligned with the financing milestones, with the approvals obtained in time for the funds.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has every required approval been mapped, with the prerequisites and the dependencies identified, and the critical path sequenced?
- [ ] Is the entitlement schedule aligned with the design milestones, so that the design is at the right level for each application?
- [ ] Is the entitlement schedule aligned with the financing milestones, so that the approvals are obtained in time for the lender and the investor?
- [ ] Have the risk approvals — the discretionary ones with opposition or subjective criteria — been identified, with a mitigation strategy for each?
- [ ] Does the schedule include float on the critical path and contingency for the overall path, to absorb the deferrals and the denials?
- [ ] Have the multi-jurisdiction and multi-agency requirements been mapped and coordinated, with the conflicts resolved before the applications are filed?
- [ ] Is the entitlement strategy communicated to the owner, the lender, and the investor, with the risks and the contingencies transparent?
- [ ] Is the entitlement path treated as the project's critical path, with the sequence, the schedule, and the risks managed as actively as the design?
