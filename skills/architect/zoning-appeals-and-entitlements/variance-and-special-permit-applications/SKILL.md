# Variance And Special Permit Applications

Zoning codes are written for the general case, and every project site carries specific conditions — an odd shape, a steep slope, a wetland, a nonconforming structure, a use that the code did not anticipate — that the code does not address fairly. Variances and special permits are the regulatory mechanisms that grant relief, but they are not entitlements; they are discretionary approvals that require the applicant to meet specific legal findings, to present evidence to a board, and to satisfy neighbors and officials who may oppose the relief. Agents often treat the application as a paperwork exercise, assemble generic hardship arguments, and lose the hearing because the findings were not met, the evidence was thin, or the neighborhood opposition was underestimated. The architect's role is to build the case — the findings, the evidence, the design response, and the community context — because the approval depends on the quality of the argument, not the correctness of the form. The goal is an application whose findings are demonstrably met, whose evidence is specific and credible, and whose design response earns the board's confidence.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Exact Relief Required And The Exact Mechanism

The first step is to identify the exact relief the project requires — the specific dimensional standard (setback, height, lot coverage, parking), the specific use issue (a use not permitted by right, a conditional use, an expansion of a nonconformity) — and the exact mechanism the code provides for that relief. A variance requires a hardship specific to the site; a special permit requires a set of use-specific criteria; a conditional use requires findings about impact; and each mechanism has different findings, different procedures, and different chances of success. Read the code's relief provisions carefully, because applying for the wrong mechanism wastes time and may prejudice the board. The architect must identify the exact relief and the exact mechanism before preparing the application, because the mechanism determines the entire case.

### Build The Findings Of Fact As The Backbone Of The Application

Variances and special permits are granted or denied based on findings of fact — the specific, legally-defined criteria that the board must find to be met — and these findings are the backbone of the application. Identify each finding the code requires (for a variance: the hardship is due to the site's characteristics, not the owner; the hardship is substantial; the relief is the minimum; the relief is not detrimental to the neighborhood; for a special permit: the use meets the specific criteria, the site is suitable, the impact is mitigated), and build the evidence and the argument for each finding. Address every finding explicitly, because a board cannot grant relief without finding each criterion met, and an unaddressed finding is a basis for denial. The architect must structure the application around the findings, because the findings are the legal test.

### Assemble Site-Specific Hardship Evidence

For a variance, the hardship must be specific to the site — the shape, the slope, the wetland, the nonconformity — and not to the owner's circumstances or the project's economics. Assemble the site-specific evidence: the survey showing the odd shape, the topography showing the slope, the wetland delineation, the nonconformity's history, and the comparable lots in the neighborhood that show the hardship. Avoid arguing economic hardship, because most codes do not allow it as a basis for a variance, and avoid arguing that the relief would make the project more profitable, because the board will reject it. The architect must assemble the site-specific hardship evidence, because a generic hardship argument fails the legal test.

### Develop The Design Response That Mitigates The Relief

The board's decision is influenced by the design response to the relief, because the board wants to see that the project, with the relief, fits the neighborhood and mitigates its impact. Develop the design response — the massing that steps down at the setback relief, the landscape that screens the parking relief, the materials and the facade that respect the context, the access and the circulation that minimize the impact — and present it as part of the application. The design response should make the relief feel reasonable and the project feel appropriate, because the board approves projects, not abstract dimensional standards. The architect must develop the design response as part of the case, because the design earns the board's confidence.

### Coordinate With The Code Official Before The Hearing

The code official (the building inspector, the zoning officer, the planning director) is often the first reviewer of the application and a key witness at the hearing, and coordinating with them before the hearing can clarify the requirements, the findings, and the likely opposition. Meet with the code official early, present the project and the relief, and ask for their read on the findings and the issues. The code official cannot promise approval, but they can identify the weaknesses in the case and the concerns the board will have, which allows the application to be strengthened. The architect must coordinate with the code official, because the official's testimony at the hearing carries weight and their concerns are the board's concerns.

### Anticipate And Address The Neighborhood Opposition

Neighborhood opposition is the most common reason that variances and special permits are denied, and the application must anticipate and address it. Identify the likely opponents — the abutters, the neighborhood association, the civic groups — and their likely concerns — traffic, parking, density, scale, character — and address them in the application and the design response. Meet with the abutters before the hearing, present the project, and listen to their concerns, because a hearing where the abutters are surprised and angry is a hearing that the board denies. The architect must anticipate the opposition and address it, because the board is sensitive to the neighborhood, and unaddressed opposition is a basis for denial.

### Prepare The Hearing Presentation And The Evidence

The hearing is the moment of decision, and the presentation and the evidence must be prepared to make the case clearly and credibly. Prepare a presentation that walks the board through the project, the relief, the findings, the evidence, and the design response, with drawings, photographs, and data that support each point. Prepare the witnesses — the architect, the owner, the engineers, the neighborhood supporters — and rehearse the testimony and the cross-examination. Anticipate the board's questions and the opponents' arguments, and prepare the responses. The architect must prepare the hearing presentation and the evidence, because a well-prepared hearing wins and an unprepared hearing loses.

## Common Traps

### Applying For The Wrong Mechanism

The team applies for a variance when a special permit is available, or vice versa, and the application fails because the wrong findings were addressed or the wrong procedure was followed. The mechanism is that the code's relief provisions are not read carefully, and the false signal is that any relief application is the same. The harm is wasted time, a denied application, and a prejudiced board. The exact mechanism must be identified before the application is prepared, with the code's relief provisions read carefully and the right findings and procedure followed.

### Generic Hardship Arguments

The application argues hardship in generic terms — the lot is small, the project is difficult, the economics do not work — without the site-specific evidence that the law requires, and the board denies the variance because the hardship is not tied to the site. The mechanism is that the legal standard for hardship is not understood, and the false signal is that any difficulty is a hardship. The harm is a denied variance and the loss of the project. The hardship must be site-specific, with survey, topography, wetland, and comparables evidence, and economic hardship must be avoided unless the code allows it.

### Ignoring The Neighborhood

The application is prepared without engaging the abutters or the neighborhood association, and the hearing is dominated by surprised and angry neighbors whose opposition sways the board to deny. The mechanism is that the neighborhood is treated as an obstacle rather than a stakeholder, and the false signal is that the legal findings are enough. The harm is a denial driven by opposition that could have been addressed. The abutters and the neighborhood must be engaged before the hearing, their concerns heard and addressed, and their support cultivated, because the board is sensitive to the neighborhood.

### Unaddressed Findings

The application addresses some of the required findings but not all, and the board denies because an unaddressed finding cannot be found, regardless of the strength of the others. The mechanism is that the findings are not all identified, and the false signal is that the strong findings carry the weak. The harm is a denial on a technical basis that could have been addressed. Every required finding must be identified and addressed explicitly, with evidence and argument, because the board must find each criterion met.

### Over-Reliance On The Code Official's Informal Approval

The team relies on an informal nod from the code official and does not prepare the hearing, and the board denies despite the official's support, because the board's decision is independent and the official's view is only one input. The mechanism is that the official's informality is treated as a guarantee, and the false signal is that the official controls the board. The harm is a denial that the official could not prevent. The hearing must be prepared regardless of the official's view, with the findings, the evidence, the design response, and the witnesses ready.

### Presenting The Design Without The Findings

The presentation focuses on the architecture and the design quality but does not address the findings of fact, and the board denies because the legal test was not met, regardless of the design's merit. The mechanism is that the design is treated as the case, and the false signal is that a good design earns the relief. The harm is a denial on the findings despite a strong design. The findings must be the backbone of the presentation, with the design response supporting the findings, because the board decides on the legal test.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has the exact relief required — the specific dimensional standard or use issue — and the exact mechanism (variance, special permit, conditional use) been identified from the code?
- [ ] Are the required findings of fact identified, and is each finding addressed explicitly with evidence and argument?
- [ ] Is the hardship evidence site-specific — survey, topography, wetland, nonconformity, comparables — and not based on the owner's circumstances or the project's economics?
- [ ] Does the design response mitigate the relief, with massing, landscape, materials, and circulation that fit the neighborhood and earn the board's confidence?
- [ ] Has the code official been consulted before the hearing, with the project and the relief presented and their concerns addressed?
- [ ] Have the abutters and the neighborhood association been engaged before the hearing, with their concerns heard, addressed, and their support cultivated?
- [ ] Is the hearing presentation prepared, with the project, the relief, the findings, the evidence, the design response, the witnesses, and the anticipated questions and opposition?
- [ ] Are the findings of fact the backbone of the application and the presentation, with the design response supporting the findings rather than replacing them?
