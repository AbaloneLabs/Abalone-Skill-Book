# Designation And Treatment Standards

Work on historic properties is governed by a framework of standards and treatment approaches that exist to preserve the cultural significance of irreplaceable fabric. In the United States, the Secretary of the Interior's Standards for the Treatment of Historic Properties provide the overarching guidance, with four distinct treatments — preservation, rehabilitation, restoration, and reconstruction — each with its own philosophy and rules. Choosing the wrong treatment, or applying the standards mechanically without understanding the property's significance, produces work that destroys the very character the standards exist to protect. Agents often treat the Standards as a checklist applied to obtain approvals, missing that they encode a philosophy about authenticity, reversibility, and the hierarchy of historic fabric that must guide every design decision. The architect's role is to determine the property's significance and designation, select the appropriate treatment, apply the Standards in judgment, and defer specialized preservation expertise to historians, preservation architects, and reviewing authorities.

Use this skill before planning work on a historic or potentially historic property, choosing a treatment approach, or applying the Secretary's Standards. The goal is treatment that preserves significance, complies with the standards, and is defensible to reviewing authorities.

## Core Rules

### Establish Significance And Designation Before Designing

Treatment follows significance. Before any design decision, the property's historic significance, period of significance, character-defining features, and designation status must be established, because they determine what must be preserved and which standards apply.

Establish by:

- determining whether the property is listed, eligible, or a contributor to a historic district, at national, state, or local levels, each of which carries different rules and review;
- commissioning or reviewing a determination of eligibility or historic resource report to identify significance, integrity, and character-defining features;
- identifying the period of significance and the historic use, which anchor what the property means and which features matter;
- distinguishing individual landmarks (where every feature may matter) from district contributors (where the contribution to the streetscape matters most);
- recognizing that local designation often carries the strictest review and that state and national listing may carry no review absent incentives or permits.

### Select The Treatment Approach Deliberately

The Secretary's Standards define four treatments, each appropriate to different goals. Selecting the treatment is a judgment that should reflect the property's significance, the owner's needs, and the reviewing authority's expectations.

Select among:

- **Preservation**, which maintains and stabilizes the property in its current form, retaining all historic fabric and replacing only what is deteriorated beyond repair, suited to properties whose existing state is itself the significance;
- **Rehabilitation**, which allows contemporary use through repair, alteration, and addition while preserving character-defining features, the most common treatment for adaptive reuse and the basis for historic tax credits;
- **Restoration**, which returns the property to a specific period by removing later features and recreating missing ones, suited to properties whose significance lies in a particular moment;
- **Reconstruction**, which recreates a non-surviving property for interpretive purposes, used rarely and typically in memorial or interpretive contexts.

The treatments are not interchangeable; each carries distinct rules about what may be removed, altered, or recreated.

### Apply The Standards As Judgment, Not Checklist

The Standards are principles, not a recipe. Applying them requires judgment about which features are character-defining, how much change is acceptable, whether alterations are reversible, and whether new work is compatible or differentiated. Mechanical application produces both over-cautious work that fails the owner's needs and over-aggressive work that erodes significance.

Apply by:

- identifying the character-defining features — the materials, features, spaces, and relationships that convey significance — and prioritizing their retention;
- following the core principles: minimize loss of historic fabric; repair rather than replace; replace in kind only what is beyond repair; make new work compatible but, where appropriate, differentiated so history is legible;
- avoiding destructive investigation and irreversible alterations where less-invasive options exist;
- treating the building as a whole, including its site and setting, because significance often extends beyond the walls;
- documenting the treatment rationale, because reviewing authorities and future stewards need to understand why decisions were made.

### Respect The Hierarchy Of Historic Fabric

Not all fabric is equally significant. The Standards distinguish the historic period fabric, which conveys significance, from later alterations, which may or may not have acquired significance themselves. Treatment must respect this hierarchy.

Respect by:

- preserving the highest-significance fabric — primary facades, significant interior spaces, original materials — with the greatest care;
- recognizing that later additions may have acquired significance in their own right (a notable mid-century addition to a 19th-century building) and warrant preservation;
- allowing more change to non-character-defining features and secondary spaces, where owner needs can be met without eroding significance;
- avoiding the assumption that "old" equals "significant" or that "new" equals "non-contributing"; the determination requires analysis;
- documenting and archiving removed fabric where removal is necessary, preserving the record even when the material cannot be kept.

### Coordinate With Reviewing Authorities And Specialists

Historic work is subject to review by the relevant authority — a historic preservation commission, the State Historic Preservation Office, or a federal agency — depending on designation and triggers. The architect coordinates the design with these authorities and defers specialized expertise.

Coordinate by:

- identifying the reviewing authority early and understanding its standards, process, and required submissions;
- engaging a preservation specialist — a historic architect, historian, or preservation consultant — for complex or highly significant properties;
- presenting design decisions in terms of the Standards, with clear rationale tied to significance and treatment;
- recognizing that review is iterative and that early consultation prevents late redesign;
- deferring specialized tasks — historic structure reports, materials analysis, conditions assessment — to the appropriate specialists.

### Understand The Triggers For Standards Application

The Standards apply in different ways depending on the project. Understanding which triggers apply determines whether the Standards are advisory, required, or tied to incentives.

Understand triggers by:

- local landmark or district designation, which typically mandates Standards-consistent review for any work;
- federal involvement — federal funds, permits, or property — which triggers Section 106 review against the Standards;
- historic tax credits, which require Standards-compliant rehabilitation certified by the reviewing authority;
- state and local incentives, each with their own standards and review;
- recognizing that even absent formal triggers, the Standards represent best practice for any historic property.

## Common Traps

### Choosing The Treatment To Suit The Design

Selecting a treatment — usually rehabilitation, because it permits the most change — to justify a predetermined design, rather than choosing the treatment based on the property's significance and the owner's goals, inverts the logic of the Standards. The trap is that the chosen treatment appears in the documents and the design proceeds, so the work feels standards-compliant, while in reality the treatment was reverse-engineered to permit the desired intervention, and the significance that should have driven the choice was never genuinely assessed. The harm is loss of character-defining fabric, review denial, and the erosion of the very significance the Standards protect. Determining significance first, then selecting the treatment that fits it, is what keeps the Standards as a guide rather than a rubber stamp.

### Mechanical Application Without Judgment

Applying the Standards as a literal checklist — "repair rather than replace," "compatible but differentiated" — without understanding the property's specific significance and character-defining features, produces both timid work that fails the owner and destructive work that erodes significance. The trap is that the checklist is followed and the documents cite the Standards, so the work appears compliant, while in reality the judgment about which features matter, how much change is acceptable, and whether new work is truly compatible was never made. The harm is review challenges, lost fabric, and outcomes that satisfy neither preservation nor use. Applying the Standards as judgment — grounded in a real understanding of significance and character — is what makes them effective rather than procedural.

### Assuming Old Equals Significant

Treating all existing fabric as equally historic and significant, regardless of when it dates from or whether it conveys significance, over-protects non-contributing fabric and under-protects the features that actually matter. The trap is that the building is old, so every element is treated as sacrosanct, which feels like rigorous preservation, while in reality later alterations, non-original materials, and non-character-defining features consume protection that should focus on the genuinely significant fabric. The harm is owner frustration, inflated cost, and missed opportunities to meet use needs through changes that would not have harmed significance. Analyzing which fabric is character-defining and which is not, and applying protection proportionally, is what makes preservation effective rather than indiscriminate.

### Ignoring Local Review Authority And Standards

Proceeding on the assumption that national or state listing is the governing framework, when a local landmark or district designation imposes stricter review and different standards, leads to designs that fail local review even where they meet federal guidance. The trap is that the Standards have been applied and the design feels compliant, so the work appears sound, while in reality the local commission — often the binding authority — has its own guidelines, deadlines, and criteria that have not been addressed. The harm is permit denial, redesign, and schedule loss. Identifying the binding reviewing authority early — often the local commission — and coordinating with its specific standards and process is what prevents late and costly conflicts.

### Destroying Fabric Through Investigation Or Construction

Allowing destructive investigation, demolition, or careless construction to remove or damage historic fabric before significance is assessed and treatment is agreed, destroys the very material the project exists to preserve. The trap is that investigation and early demolition feel like normal project steps, so they proceed without preservation review, while in reality the fabric removed may have been character-defining, and its loss is irreversible. The harm is the permanent destruction of significance, review violations, and in tax-credit projects the loss of certification. Conducting non-destructive investigation, sequencing demolition after treatment agreement, and requiring preservation-aware construction practices are what protect fabric through the process.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has the property's significance, period of significance, character-defining features, and designation status (national, state, local) been established before design?
- [ ] Is the treatment approach — preservation, rehabilitation, restoration, or reconstruction — chosen based on significance and owner goals, not reverse-engineered to suit a design?
- [ ] Are character-defining features identified and prioritized for retention, with repair preferred over replacement and replacement in kind only beyond repair?
- [ ] Is new work compatible with the historic fabric and, where appropriate, differentiated so history remains legible?
- [ ] Is the hierarchy of fabric respected — highest care for the most significant fabric, more flexibility for non-character-defining features and secondary spaces?
- [ ] Have later additions been assessed for acquired significance before removal?
- [ ] Is the binding reviewing authority identified early, with its standards, process, and submissions understood and coordinated?
- [ ] Has a preservation specialist been engaged for complex or highly significant properties?
- [ ] Are the triggers for Standards application — local designation, federal involvement, tax credits, incentives — understood, and is the project compliant with each that applies?
- [ ] Is investigation non-destructive, demolition sequenced after treatment agreement, and construction preservation-aware, to avoid destroying fabric before significance is assessed?
