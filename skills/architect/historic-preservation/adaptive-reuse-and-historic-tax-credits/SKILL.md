# Adaptive Reuse And Historic Tax Credits

Adaptive reuse converts a historic building to a new use while preserving the character that makes it significant. It is one of the most sustainable forms of development — reusing embodied carbon, preserving urban fabric, and giving new life to buildings that might otherwise be lost — but it is also one of the most constrained, because the new use, contemporary codes, and the financial structure must all be reconciled with the preservation of significant fabric. Historic rehabilitation tax credits (federal and many state programs) can transform the financial feasibility of a project, but they condition the credits on a certified rehabilitation that complies with the Secretary of the Interior's Standards, reviewed at multiple stages. Agents often treat tax credits as a financing detail handled by the developer, missing that the Standards compliance that earns the credits governs the design from massing to detail, and that decisions made casually early in design can forfeit millions in credits. The architect's role is to design a reuse that meets the new use and code while preserving significance, to integrate Standards compliance into every decision, and to coordinate the certification process with the preservation consultant, developer, and reviewing authority.

Use this skill before planning an adaptive reuse, pursuing tax credits, or making design decisions on a historic building whose feasibility depends on incentives. The goal is a project that is viable, code-compliant, preservation-appropriate, and certifiable.

## Core Rules

### Confirm Feasibility And Credit Eligibility Early

Adaptive reuse and tax credits depend on conditions that must be confirmed before design is committed: the property's historic status, the credit program's requirements, the new use's compatibility with the building, and the financial structure. Discovering ineligibility late can collapse a project.

Confirm by:

- verifying that the property is a certified historic structure — listed on the National Register individually or as a contributor to a registered district — which is required for federal credits;
- confirming the state credit program's requirements, which vary and may apply to non-certified structures in some jurisdictions;
- assessing whether the proposed new use is compatible with the building's form, floor plates, heights, and significance, because some uses require changes that the Standards will not permit;
- engaging a historic tax credit consultant or developer experienced with credits early, to structure the application and confirm feasibility;
- recognizing that credits are earned only through a certified rehabilitation reviewed against the Standards, and that certification can be denied.

### Design The Reuse To Preserve Character-Defining Features

Adaptive reuse under the Standards requires that the new use preserve the character-defining features of the building. The use must be chosen and designed to fit the building, not the building forced to fit the use, because forcing destroys the significance the credits exist to reward.

Design by:

- identifying character-defining features — facades, significant interior spaces, materials, plan, setting — early and prioritizing their retention;
- selecting a use whose program, floor plate, and servicing needs can be met within the building's existing form, minimizing destructive alteration;
- locating new functions, services, and partitions in non-character-defining areas, preserving significant spaces intact;
- designing new construction within and against the historic fabric to be compatible but differentiated, so the building's history remains legible;
- treating significant interiors with the same care as exteriors, because interior features often carry significance that credits require preserving.

### Meet Contemporary Codes Through Preservation-Compatible Means

Adaptive reuse must meet contemporary building, accessibility, fire, and energy codes, which often conflict with historic fabric. Resolving these conflicts through preservation-compatible means — rather than destructive compliance — is a central challenge of reuse.

Meet codes by:

- using the International Existing Building Code or the applicable rehabilitation code, which provides graduated compliance based on the scope of work rather than full new-building requirements;
- pursuing code alternatives, variances, and equivalencies that achieve safety without destroying character-defining features, documented through the authority having jurisdiction;
- designing accessibility that provides access without removing significant features, recognizing that preservation law and accessibility law must be reconciled;
- addressing fire and life safety through compartmentation, detection, and suppression that respect the fabric, rather than demolition and reconstruction;
- coordinating with code officials early, because historic code compliance relies on negotiated, project-specific solutions.

### Manage The Certified Rehabilitation Process

Federal (and most state) tax credits require a three-part certification: Part 1 evaluates the property's historic status, Part 2 describes the proposed rehabilitation, and Part 3 confirms the completed work. Each stage is reviewed by the State Historic Preservation Office and the National Park Service, and denial at any stage can forfeit the credits.

Manage by:

- submitting Part 1 (historic status) early, before design is committed, to confirm eligibility;
- submitting Part 2 (proposed work) during design, with detailed descriptions and drawings of every treatment, so that the reviewing authority's feedback can shape the design before construction;
- documenting the completed work thoroughly for Part 3, with photographs and records that demonstrate compliance with the approved Part 2;
- recognizing that deviations from the approved Part 2 during construction can jeopardize certification, and that changes must be reviewed;
- coordinating the certification schedule with the project's financing and construction milestones, because credits are claimed based on certification.

### Integrate Standards Compliance Into Every Decision

For a tax-credit project, the Secretary's Standards are not a review hurdle; they are the design framework. Every decision — from massing and addition to window replacement, MEP routing, and interior partition — must be made with Standards compliance in mind, because cumulative small decisions can deny certification.

Integrate by:

- engaging a preservation architect or consultant to review the design continuously, not only at submission;
- documenting the Standards rationale for each significant decision, because the reviewing authority evaluates the reasoning;
- avoiding the most common denial triggers: demolishing character-defining features, replacing historic windows wholesale, adding incompatible massing, gutting significant interiors;
- treating the building's site and setting as part of the certified work, because significance often extends beyond the walls;
- recognizing that the Standards apply to the entire certified rehabilitation, including work that might seem minor in isolation.

### Coordinate The Team And The Triggers

A tax-credit adaptive reuse involves many parties — owner, developer, architect, preservation consultant, structural and MEP engineers, SHPO, NPS, code officials, and often a historic tax credit investor — whose actions must be coordinated. Miscommunication among them jeopardizes both the design and the credits.

Coordinate by:

- establishing clear roles: who leads certification, who designs, who reviews for Standards, who manages construction compliance;
- communicating Standards constraints to the entire design and construction team, because a contractor's default methods can destroy compliance;
- coordinating the certification submissions with the design and construction schedule, so that reviews do not delay work or work outpace review;
- documenting construction thoroughly — photographs, shop drawings, material submittals — because Part 3 certification depends on demonstrating what was built;
- recognizing that the credits' financial value justifies the coordination effort, and that a single non-compliant decision can forfeit the benefit.

## Common Traps

### Credits Assumed Without Confirming Eligibility

Proceeding on the assumption that the building qualifies for historic tax credits, because it is old or locally landmarked, without confirming certified historic structure status, can collapse a project's financial feasibility when certification is denied. The trap is that the building is clearly historic and the credits appear in the pro forma, so the project feels bankable, while in reality the property may not be listed on the National Register, may not be a contributor to a registered district, or may have lost the integrity required for eligibility. The harm is a financing gap discovered too late, project collapse, or redesign under financial pressure. Confirming certified historic structure status through Part 1 submission before design is committed is what establishes eligibility; assuming it is a gamble with the project's foundation.

### Forcing The Use Onto The Building

Selecting a use whose program, floor plates, or servicing require destructive alteration of the historic fabric, because the use is most profitable or familiar, destroys the very significance the project should preserve and forfeits credits. The trap is that the use is viable and the building is large, so the project appears feasible, while in reality the Standards will not permit the alterations the use demands, and the design either fails certification or destroys the building's character to comply with nothing. The harm is denied credits, lost fabric, and a project that achieves neither preservation nor viability. Choosing and designing a use that fits the building's form and significance, minimizing destructive alteration, is what makes adaptive reuse both feasible and certifiable.

### Code Compliance That Destroys Character-Defining Features

Applying new-building code requirements rigidly to a historic building — widening corridors, replacing stairs, demolishing partitions for fire separation, gutting for accessibility — destroys character-defining features and forfeits credits, when the existing building code and negotiated alternatives could achieve safety without destruction. The trap is that the code requirements are real and the compliance feels responsible, so the destruction appears unavoidable, while in reality the rehabilitation code offers graduated compliance and the authority can accept equivalencies that preserve the fabric. The harm is lost significance, denied credits, and a diminished building. Using the existing building code, pursuing alternatives and equivalencies, and coordinating with code officials early are what meet safety without sacrificing the features that matter.

### Part 2 Submitted After Design Is Fixed

Submitting the Part 2 description of proposed work after the design is complete and committed, treating certification as a paperwork step rather than a design review, forfeits the opportunity to shape the design and risks denial of decisions that cannot be undone. The trap is that the design is complete and the Part 2 describes it accurately, so the submission feels timely, while in reality the reviewing authority's feedback — which might have improved compliance — arrives too late to change anything, and non-compliant elements are already in construction documents. The harm is redesign under pressure, denied certification, or forfeited credits. Submitting Part 2 during design, with the authority's feedback integrated before construction documents, is what makes certification a design tool rather than a gamble.

### Construction Deviating From The Approved Work

Allowing construction to deviate from the Part 2-approved work — substituting materials, altering treatments, demolishing features that were to be retained — because the field conditions or contractor preferences drove changes, jeopardizes Part 3 certification and the credits. The trap is that each change feels minor and field-driven, so no one flags it, while in reality the cumulative deviations may no longer meet the Standards, and Part 3 certification — based on what was actually built — is denied. The harm is forfeited credits, financial loss, and sometimes remediation. Communicating Standards constraints to the construction team, reviewing all changes against the approved work, and documenting construction thoroughly for Part 3 are what protect the credits through to certification.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has the property's certified historic structure status been confirmed through Part 1 submission before design is committed?
- [ ] Are the applicable credit programs — federal and state — confirmed, with their requirements and a consultant engaged to structure the application?
- [ ] Is the new use chosen and designed to fit the building's form, floor plates, and significance, minimizing destructive alteration?
- [ ] Are character-defining features — exterior and significant interiors — identified and preserved, with new work compatible but differentiated?
- [ ] Is code compliance pursued through the existing building code, alternatives, and equivalencies that preserve character-defining features, coordinated with the authority having jurisdiction?
- [ ] Is accessibility provided without removing significant features, reconciling preservation and accessibility requirements?
- [ ] Has Part 2 (proposed work) been submitted during design, with the reviewing authority's feedback integrated before construction documents?
- [ ] Is a preservation architect or consultant reviewing the design continuously for Standards compliance, with rationale documented for significant decisions?
- [ ] Are Standards constraints communicated to the construction team, with all field changes reviewed against the approved Part 2?
- [ ] Is construction documented thoroughly — photographs, shop drawings, submittals — to support Part 3 certification of the completed work?
