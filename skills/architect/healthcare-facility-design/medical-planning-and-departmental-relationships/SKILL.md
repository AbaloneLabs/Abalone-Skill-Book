# Medical Planning And Departmental Relationships

Medical planning is the architectural discipline of arranging the departments, circulation systems, and support functions of a healthcare facility so that clinical workflow, patient experience, staff efficiency, and logistics all function together. It is the part of healthcare design where a wrong adjacency compounds into decades of operational cost and clinical risk, because the building's plan literally shapes how care is delivered. Agents often treat medical planning as a room-list exercise and miss that the relationships between departments — who must be next to whom, which flows must be separated, how the building will expand — are the design, and that the room sizes follow from those relationships. The architect owns medical planning because it is the integration of clinical knowledge, code, and design into a coherent plan. The goal is a plan that supports current care models and can adapt to future ones, with adjacencies and separations that are defensible to the clinical staff who must work in the building.

## Core Rules

### Build The Program From Clinical Workflow, Not From A Room List

Begin by understanding how care is actually delivered: the patient journey from arrival through diagnosis, treatment, and discharge; the staff workflows among assessment, procedure, and documentation; and the material flows of supplies, linens, waste, and medications. The program must be derived from these workflows, with each department and room sized and located to support the activities it contains, rather than from a generic room list applied to a floor area target. Engage clinical stakeholders early to validate the workflow assumptions, because clinicians know the friction points that planning must resolve. Document the workflow basis of the program, because the plan will be reviewed against it by clinicians and by regulators.

### Establish The Departmental Adjacency Diagram Before The Plan

Before any floor plan, develop a departmental adjacency diagram that records which departments must be adjacent, which must be near, which must be separated, and which require specific relationships to shared resources like surgery, imaging, and the laboratory. The adjacency diagram is the design logic of the building: emergency adjacent to surgery and imaging, surgery adjacent to sterile processing and intensive care, maternity separated from general traffic, the laboratory near the emergency department and the surgical suite. Test the diagram against the workflow analysis and the site constraints, and only then translate it into a floor plan. A plan developed without an adjacency diagram has no defensible logic and will be revised under clinical review.

### Separate The Distinct Circulation Systems

Healthcare buildings carry multiple circulation systems that must be separated for safety, privacy, and efficiency: public circulation for patients and visitors, staff circulation, patient transport, clean supply distribution, soiled and waste removal, and in many cases cadaver and prisoner movement. Design separate or visually separated paths for these flows, with dedicated clean and soiled corridors where required, and locate elevators and vertical cores to serve each system without crossing them. The separation is not only operational; it is a code and infection control requirement in many departments, particularly surgery and sterile processing. Map the circulation systems on the plan and verify they do not conflict, because crossed flows are the most common planning defect.

### Coordinate The Surgery, Imaging, And Critical Care Triad

The surgical suite, the imaging department, and the intensive care unit form a triad whose relationships drive the plan of any acute hospital, because emergency patients flow among them and because they share expensive equipment and specialized staff. Surgery must be adjacent to sterile processing, the laboratory, and the ICU; imaging must serve both the emergency department and surgery, with some modalities shared; the ICU must be near surgery and the emergency department. The plan must resolve these relationships simultaneously, and the structural, mechanical, and radiation shielding requirements of these departments compound the challenge. Treat the triad as the structural core of the plan, and arrange the rest of the departments around it.

### Plan The Logistics And Materials Management Spine

A hospital consumes and produces enormous quantities of materials — supplies, linens, food, medications, clean and soiled items, waste of multiple streams — and the logistics systems that move them are as important as the clinical circulation. Plan a logistics spine or core that connects receiving, central sterile, the pharmacy, materials management, and food service to the departments they serve, with dedicated elevators, carts, and routes that do not cross clinical or public flows. Size the receiving, storage, and waste handling areas for the volumes the facility will actually process, because undersized logistics cripple operations. The logistics spine is often invisible in presentations but is the backbone of a functional hospital, and the architect must design it deliberately.

### Design For Phased Growth And Change

Healthcare facilities change constantly: departments grow, technologies shift, care models evolve, and the building must accommodate this change without shutting down. Plan for horizontal and vertical expansion from the outset, with departments arranged so that growth can occur at the perimeter, with shell space reserved, and with the mechanical and electrical systems sized and routed to serve future load. Avoid locking critical departments into configurations that cannot expand, and design the structural grid, the floor-to-floor, and the interstitial or mechanical distribution to support future renovation. The architect must design for the building's second and third lives, because a hospital that cannot adapt becomes obsolete within a generation.

### Integrate The Patient And Family Experience Into The Plan

Clinical efficiency is necessary but not sufficient; the plan must also support the experience of patients and families who are often frightened, disoriented, and in pain. Design clear arrival and wayfinding sequences, provide waiting and family spaces near care areas, locate patient rooms for privacy and dignity, and bring daylight and views into the spaces where people spend time. Balance the operational logic of the plan with the human experience, because a plan that optimizes staff efficiency at the expense of patient dignity fails the building's purpose. The architect is the advocate for the patient and family in the planning process, because the clinical staff will optimize for their own workflow.

## Common Traps

### Planning From A Room List Without Workflow Validation

The team takes a program of room names and areas, arranges them on a site, and produces a plan that looks complete but does not reflect how care is delivered, so clinicians immediately identify adjacencies that do not work and flows that conflict. The mechanism is that a room list feels like a program and workflow analysis feels like extra discovery, and the false signal is that the rooms are all present and sized. The harm is that the plan is reworked under clinical review, the schedule slips, and the team loses credibility with the clinical stakeholders. The program must be built from validated clinical workflow, with the workflow basis documented, before the plan is developed.

### Crossing Clean And Soiled, Or Public And Service, Circulation

The plan routes the clean supply corridor across the soiled return path, or the public visitor corridor through the service core, creating infection control risks and operational conflicts that the code and the clinicians will not accept. The mechanism is that circulation is drawn as connecting lines and the distinct systems are not differentiated, and the false signal is that the plan circulates and so it works. The harm is that the plan must be reorganized to separate the flows, the floor plates change, and the structural and mechanical systems follow. The circulation systems must be mapped as distinct networks on the plan and verified for non-conflict, with dedicated clean and soiled routes where required.

### Misplacing The Surgery-Imaging-ICU Triad

The plan places the surgical suite on one side of the building, imaging on another, and the ICU on a third floor, forcing emergency patients to travel long distances among them and complicating the shared staffing and equipment that should link them. The mechanism is that each department is planned for its own internal logic without resolving the triad relationships, and the false signal is that each department is well planned. The harm is that emergency flow is slow, shared resources are inefficient, and the plan must be reorganized to bring the triad together. The surgery-imaging-ICU triad must be resolved as the structural core of the plan before the surrounding departments are arranged.

### Undersizing The Logistics And Materials Management Spine

The team focuses on the clinical departments and treats receiving, storage, central sterile, pharmacy, and waste handling as support space to be minimized, so the hospital opens with logistics that cannot handle the volumes and operations are crippled from day one. The mechanism is that logistics space does not appear in the clinical program and feels like overhead, and the false signal is that the clinical departments are fully planned. The harm is that supplies are stored in corridors, waste accumulates, and the operations team must improvise workarounds that compromise safety and efficiency. The logistics spine and the receiving, storage, and waste areas must be sized for actual volumes and integrated into the plan as a first-order system.

### Locking The Building Into A Configuration That Cannot Grow

The plan fills the site, places critical departments where they cannot expand, and routes the mechanical systems through the only available growth zones, so when the hospital needs to grow it must either demolish functional space or build on a new site. The mechanism is that the immediate program drives the plan and future growth feels speculative, and the false signal is that the current program is fully accommodated. The harm is that the building becomes obsolete within a generation, the owner faces expensive expansion or replacement, and the design has not served its life cycle. The plan must reserve growth zones, size systems for future load, and arrange departments so expansion can occur without disrupting operations.

### Optimizing Staff Efficiency At The Expense Of Patient Dignity

The plan arranges patient rooms and support spaces to minimize staff travel, producing double-loaded corridors with rooms facing noisy core areas, no daylight in patient spaces, and waiting areas remote from care. The mechanism is that staff efficiency is measurable and patient experience is qualitative, and the false signal is that the plan is efficient and therefore good. The harm is that the building is operationally efficient but experientially poor, patient satisfaction suffers, and the architect has failed the advocacy role. The plan must balance operational logic with patient and family dignity, daylight, privacy, and clear wayfinding.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is the program derived from validated clinical workflow — patient journeys, staff workflows, and material flows — rather than from a generic room list, with the workflow basis documented?
- [ ] Has a departmental adjacency diagram been developed and tested against workflow and site constraints before the floor plan was produced?
- [ ] Are the distinct circulation systems — public, staff, patient transport, clean supply, soiled and waste — mapped as separate networks and verified for non-conflict?
- [ ] Is the surgery-imaging-ICU triad resolved as the structural core of the plan, with shared resources and emergency flows coordinated?
- [ ] Is the logistics and materials management spine — receiving, central sterile, pharmacy, materials, food, waste — sized for actual volumes and integrated into the plan?
- [ ] Does the plan reserve horizontal and vertical growth zones, with mechanical and electrical systems sized and routed for future load?
- [ ] Does the plan balance staff efficiency with patient and family dignity, daylight, privacy, and clear wayfinding?
