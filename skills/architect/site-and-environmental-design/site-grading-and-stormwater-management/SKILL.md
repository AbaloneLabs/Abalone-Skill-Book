# Site Grading And Stormwater Management

Site grading and stormwater management shape how a site performs, how it is experienced, and how it discharges water onto neighbors and into municipal systems. Grading determines accessibility, the relationship of the building to the ground, the readability of outdoor space, and the constructability of the work. Stormwater management is increasingly regulated, with municipalities imposing volume, rate, and water-quality controls that can dictate site layout, planting, and even building placement. Agents often treat grading as a technical detail to be resolved by the civil engineer late in design, missing that the grading concept must be established during site planning because it drives floor elevations, accessible routes, retaining walls, and the usable area of the site. The architect's role is to set the grading and stormwater strategy in concept, coordinate it with building and landscape design, and defer the engineering calculations to a licensed civil engineer.

Use this skill before establishing site grading concepts, locating stormwater features, setting floor elevations, or coordinating drainage with consultants. The goal is an integrated site design that is accessible, buildable, compliant, and that defers engineering to the right professional.

## Core Rules

### Establish The Grading Concept Early In Site Planning

Grading is not a late-stage refinement; it is a site-planning decision that constrains building placement, entry locations, and the usable ground plane. A grading concept established during schematic design prevents costly rework when civil engineering begins.

Establish the concept by:

- identifying existing contours, high points, and drainage patterns from an accurate topographic survey before massing or placing the building;
- setting finished floor elevations that balance cut and fill, minimize retaining walls, and establish accessible entries;
- designing positive drainage away from the building at a minimum slope, typically two percent for paved surfaces and less for lawns, to prevent ponding and infiltration at foundations;
- conceptualizing how water moves across the site, from where it falls to where it is collected, detained, or discharged;
- coordinating floor elevations with adjacent street grades, utility connections, and accessible routes so that the building meets the ground logically.

The concept does not need to be engineered, but it must be internally consistent. A floor elevation set without regard to existing grade produces ramps, retaining walls, or drainage problems that could have been avoided.

### Coordinate Floor Elevations With Accessibility And Entries

Grading and accessibility are inseparable. The slope of walks, the rise of ramps, the number of curb cuts, and the landing at entries are all grading decisions. A site that "works" on plan can fail accessibility review if the grades between street, parking, and entry exceed allowable running or cross slopes.

Coordinate by:

- designing accessible routes from public way and parking to entries at slopes meeting code, typically one-to-twenty running slope for walks and steeper only where ramps with landings are provided;
- verifying that door landings meet maximum slopes and that threshold heights are within code limits;
- checking cross slope on walks and parking, which is often violated inadvertently;
- planning ramp locations early, because ramps consume length and can dominate a façade or entry sequence if not integrated;
- recognizing that accessible routes are not just code compliance but primary user paths for delivery, maintenance, and many occupants.

Accessibility must be designed, not checked at the end. A grading scheme that forces a long ramp at the front door is a site-planning failure, not a code problem.

### Integrate Stormwater Management Into The Site Design

Modern stormwater regulations require controlling the rate, volume, and quality of runoff, often through green infrastructure: bioretention, permeable paving, green roofs, detention basins, and infiltration galleries. These features occupy real area and have real constraints, so they must be located and sized during site planning, not appended at the end.

Integrate by:

- understanding the local stormwater regulations early, including rate and volume controls, water-quality treatment requirements, and any incentives for green infrastructure;
- reserving area for stormwater features proportional to the impervious area being created, recognizing that bioretention and detention consume meaningful site area;
- locating features where they can function, considering inflow, overflow, and discharge points, and where they add landscape value rather than detract from it;
- coordinating with the landscape architect so that stormwater planting supports both function and aesthetic goals;
- considering maintenance access, because a basin that cannot be mowed or inspected will fail over time.

Stormwater features that are decorative but non-functional, or functional but poorly sited, are common failures of late integration.

### Manage Cut And Fill And Earthwork Balance

Earthwork is a major cost driver. A site that imports or exports large volumes of soil pays for trucking and disposal, while a balanced site, where cut roughly equals fill, is economical. The architect should understand the earthwork implications of grading decisions even though the civil engineer computes the quantities.

Manage earthwork by:

- setting floor elevations and grading concepts that aim for a rough balance of cut and fill, not a precise one, which the civil engineer will refine;
- minimizing retaining walls, which are expensive and can create drainage and aesthetic problems;
- recognizing that steep sites, urban infill, and sites with poor soils may require structural solutions that change the cost and feasibility of the design;
- coordinating with geotechnical findings, because expansive, organic, or contaminated soils may need removal or treatment that affects earthwork balance;
- deferring quantity calculations and shrink-swell factors to the civil engineer, but understanding their effect on cost.

### Defer Engineering To The Licensed Civil Engineer

The architect sets the concept and coordinates; the civil engineer performs the engineering. Stormwater calculations, pipe sizing, basin hydraulics, retaining wall structural design, and regulatory submissions are the civil engineer's licensed scope. The architect must not represent these as complete without civil engineering, and must build coordination time into the schedule.

Defer by:

- engaging the civil engineer during schematic design, not after, so that concepts are validated before they are committed;
- clearly scoping what is concept and what is engineered, in drawings and in communications;
- routing all regulatory stormwater submissions through the civil engineer of record;
- coordinating utility connections, including water, sewer, and storm, as shared scope where the architect's site plan and the engineer's utility design must align;
- documenting review and coordination, so that discrepancies between disciplines are caught before construction.

The architect who attempts civil engineering produces exposure for the firm and risk for the owner. The line between coordination and engineering is a licensure boundary.

## Common Traps

### Grading Deferred Until Civil Engineering Begins

When the architect hands the site plan to the civil engineer late in design with floor elevations already fixed, the engineer discovers that the grades will not drain, that accessible routes exceed maximum slopes, or that stormwater features cannot fit where they were shown. The trap is that the drawings looked complete — contours were sketched, entries were shown — so no one recognized that the grading concept was never actually validated against the topography. By the time the problem surfaces, the building massing, entry locations, and even the floor plans are committed, and correcting the grading forces expensive redesign that the schedule and budget cannot absorb. A grading concept established and pressure-tested during schematic design, with the civil engineer's early input, is what prevents this late and costly discovery.

### Stormwater Treated As A Landscape Amenity Only

Designing bioretention areas, rain gardens, and detention basins purely for their visual qualities, without accounting for their hydraulic function, produces features that either fail to perform or perform in ways that undermine the design. The trap is that green infrastructure looks soft and natural, so it is drawn as planting and assumed to work, when in fact it requires specific side slopes, ponding depths, overflow structures, and soil media that constrain its form and location. A rain garden placed at a building corner for symmetry may receive too little runoff to function, or too much, flooding the entry it was meant to grace. Coordinating the stormwater function with the landscape design, and sizing features to their actual contributing drainage area, is what makes green infrastructure both beautiful and effective; treating it as decoration guarantees underperformance or failure.

### Ignoring Off-Site Drainage And Discharge

A site design that collects and conveys its own runoff but discharges it onto a neighbor's property, into a street that cannot accept it, or into an undersized municipal system has solved one problem by creating another. The trap is that the on-site drainage appears complete — inlets, pipes, and a basin are all shown — so the design feels finished, while the discharge point, the legal right to convey water there, and the capacity of the receiving system remain unverified. Downstream owners and municipalities have legal recourse against increased runoff, and a project that worsens off-site flooding can face injunctions, redesign, or denial of permits. Verifying the discharge point, its capacity, and the regulatory allowance to use it is a coordination task the architect must initiate and the civil engineer must confirm.

### Floor Elevations Set Without Regard To Existing Grade

Setting finished floor elevations to suit interior ceiling heights or a desired entry sequence, without checking them against the existing topography, produces sites littered with retaining walls, long ramps, and awkward transitions between building and ground. The trap is that the floor elevation feels like an architectural decision — it governs the section, the views, the relationship to the street — so it is set on design logic alone, only for the civil engineer to reveal that the building now sits four feet above or below grade with no graceful way to reach the door. The resulting ramps and walls are expensive, often ugly, and sometimes non-compliant, all because a number was chosen without the ground in mind. Floor elevations must be set in dialogue with the existing grade, balancing architectural intent against constructability and accessibility.

### Assuming Accessibility Can Be Solved With A Ramp

Treating accessible routes as a code checkbox solved by adding a ramp after the grading is set ignores that ramps consume length, require landings, and can dominate an entry or consume usable site area. The trap is that a ramp appears on the accessibility plan, so the route seems provided, when in reality the ramp is so long, steep, or prominently placed that it degrades the entry for everyone and singles out users who need it. Worse, cross slopes on walks and parking, which ramps do not address, are frequently violated and caught only at inspection. Designing the accessible route as the primary path — integrated into the grading concept from the start, with running and cross slopes controlled throughout — is what makes a site genuinely accessible rather than nominally compliant.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Was a grading concept established during schematic design using an accurate topographic survey, before floor elevations and entries were finalized?
- [ ] Do finished floor elevations balance cut and fill, minimize retaining walls, and relate logically to existing grade and adjacent streets?
- [ ] Are accessible routes from public way and parking to all entries designed at code-compliant running and cross slopes, with landings and thresholds verified?
- [ ] Has area been reserved for stormwater management proportional to impervious area, with features located for function, discharge, and maintenance access?
- [ ] Are stormwater features coordinated with the landscape architect so they perform hydraulically and contribute to the site design?
- [ ] Has the local stormwater regulation been understood, including rate, volume, and water-quality requirements and any green-infrastructure incentives?
- [ ] Has the civil engineer been engaged during schematic design to validate the grading and stormwater concept before it is committed?
- [ ] Is the discharge point for site runoff verified for capacity, legal right of conveyance, and regulatory allowance, confirmed by the civil engineer?
- [ ] Are utility connections (water, sewer, storm) coordinated between the architect's site plan and the civil engineer's utility design?
- [ ] Is the distinction between the architect's concept and the civil engineer's licensed engineering scope clearly documented, with regulatory submissions routed through the engineer of record?
