# Detail Development And Joinery

Detail development is the work of resolving how a building's assemblies actually go together: how materials meet, how water is managed, how movement is accommodated, how loads transfer, and how the building is constructed in a sequence the builder can follow. It is where the design becomes buildable or remains an abstraction. Agents often treat detailing as a documentation task, producing standard details that show the intent without resolving the real conditions, when detailing is a design activity whose quality determines whether the building leaks, cracks, deteriorates, and disappoints. The majority of building failures, leaks, callbacks, and litigation concentrate at details, specifically at transitions, penetrations, and interfaces, which is exactly where casual detailing is weakest.

Use this skill when developing details, designing joinery, or resolving material transitions. The goal is details that are constructable, weather-tight, movement-tolerant, maintainable, and faithful to the design intent across the conditions the building will actually experience.

## Core Rules

### Detail For Water Management As The Primary Concern

Water is the agent of most building deterioration, and the majority of water intrusion occurs at details, where assemblies meet and the continuous membranes, flashings, and seals that protect field areas must be terminated, lapped, or penetrated. A detail that does not manage water definitively is a failure waiting to happen, regardless of how it looks.

Detail for water by:

- following the rain screen or drained cavity principle at enclosure details, providing a primary weather barrier, a drainage cavity, and a secondary water and air barrier;
- designing flashings that lap shingle-fashion in the direction of water flow, with end dams where water must be directed, rather than relying on sealant as the primary defense;
- detailing penetrations, such as scupper, vent, and pipe openings, with sleeves, flashings, and seals that accommodate movement rather than cracking;
- coordinating with the building envelope consultant on complex enclosure assemblies, because waterproofing is a specialty where errors are costly and hidden.

### Accommodate Movement Explicitly In Every Assembly

Buildings move: thermally, structurally, from settlement, from seismic action, and from moisture changes in materials. Details that do not accommodate movement crack, leak, and fail, because rigid connections concentrate stress until something gives. Movement accommodation is not optional; it is a requirement of every joint.

Accommodate movement by:

- locating expansion joints where the building's geometry and structure predict stress concentration, and sizing them for the anticipated movement;
- using sealants, gaskets, and flexible membranes at material transitions to absorb differential movement rather than transmitting it;
- designing cladding attachments that allow for thermal and structural movement without buckling or cracking;
- recognizing the differential movement between materials with different coefficients, such as concrete and steel, or masonry and metal, and detailing the joint between them to accept it.

### Resolve Material Transitions With Constructable Geometry

The way two materials meet is a geometric problem as much as a material one. Transitions that cannot be built cleanly, because the dimensions do not align, the joint is inaccessible, or the sequence cannot be executed, become failures regardless of the design intent. Constructability is a design criterion.

Resolve transitions by:

- checking that materials of differing thicknesses can meet at a clean reveal, transition strip, or offset, rather than at an awkward furred junction;
- ensuring that the joint is accessible to the trades who must build it, because a joint that cannot be reached cannot be sealed or fastened properly;
- sequencing the construction in the detail, so that each trade's work does not block the next, and the final assembly can be built as drawn;
- avoiding details that require impossible tolerances, because real construction varies and the detail must absorb the variation.

### Coordinate Detailing Across Disciplines At Every Interface

Details sit at the intersection of architectural, structural, mechanical, electrical, and plumbing work, and the failure to coordinate these disciplines at the detail produces conflicts that are discovered during construction, when they are expensive to resolve. A detail drawn by the architect without the structural and mechanical implications resolved is incomplete.

Coordinate by:

- integrating structural connections, embeds, and bearing conditions into architectural details, in coordination with the structural engineer;
- resolving mechanical, electrical, and plumbing penetrations through enclosure assemblies, with firestopping and waterproofing at each penetration;
- coordinating fire-rated assemblies, ensuring that the rated construction is continuous through every transition and penetration;
- checking that the detail does not conflict with adjacent trades' work, including the routing of conduit, ductwork, and piping.

### Preserve The Design Intent Through The Detailing

A detail that resolves the technical requirements but betrays the design intent produces a building whose execution contradicts its concept. The discipline of detailing is to solve the technical problem in a way that serves the architectural idea, rather than defaulting to a standard detail that ignores it.

Preserve intent by:

- developing the detail from the design concept, so that the way materials meet expresses the organizing idea rather than contradicting it;
- refusing to accept standard details that compromise the intent where a designed alternative is feasible, while being honest about cost;
- testing the detail against the design narrative, asking whether the way it resolves the assembly is consistent with the building's logic;
- documenting the intent behind nonstandard details, so that value engineering and substitutions can be evaluated against it.

### Defer To Specialists On Complex Enclosure And Structural Details

The architect owns the detail's design intent and coordination, but does not own the calculation of waterproofing performance, structural capacity, or fire resistance. Complex enclosure, structural, and fire details require specialist input that must be sought, not assumed.

Defer by:

- engaging the building envelope consultant on enclosure details, especially at complex geometries, transitions, and penetrations;
- coordinating with the structural engineer on load path, connections, and movement at every structural interface;
- consulting the fire protection engineer on rated assemblies, penetrations, and firestopping, because fire ratings are calculated, not assumed;
- treating the specialist's input as authoritative within their domain, while the architect retains responsibility for integration and intent.

## Common Traps

### Sealant As The Primary Waterproofing Strategy

When a detail relies on sealant as the primary defense against water intrusion, rather than on lapped flashings, drained cavities, and a continuous secondary barrier, the detail fails the moment the sealant ages, loses adhesion, or is installed imperfectly. The trap is that sealant produces a detail that looks watertight on the drawing, with a continuous bead closing every joint, so the team believes the water management is resolved, while the sealant is actually a temporary seal with a service life far shorter than the building's. The harm is hidden water intrusion that rots substrates, corrodes components, and breeds mold, discovered only when the damage is extensive and the repair requires dismantling the assembly. Water management must rely on geometry, gravity, and layered defenses, with sealant as a secondary seal, because no sealant can be the primary barrier for the life of a building.

### Movement Ignored Until Cracks Appear

When details are drawn as rigid connections without expansion joints, flexible seals, or accommodation for differential movement, the building's inevitable thermal, structural, and moisture movement concentrates stress at the rigid points until the material cracks, the joint opens, or the assembly fails. The trap is that the rigid detail looks clean and resolved in the drawing, and the movement is invisible until the building experiences its first seasons, so the team believes the detail is sound. The harm is cracking, leaking, and deterioration that appears within the first years of service and recurs even after repair, because the underlying failure to accommodate movement persists. Movement must be accommodated explicitly in every assembly, with joints and flexible elements sized for the anticipated movement, because a building that cannot move breaks.

### The Inaccessible Joint

When a detail locates a critical connection, seal, or fastening where the builder cannot reach it during construction or the maintainer cannot reach it during service, the joint is built poorly and maintained not at all, regardless of how well it is drawn. The trap is that the detail resolves the geometry on paper, with the joint correctly positioned and specified, while the physical reality of access makes the assembly impossible to execute as intended. The harm is a joint that leaks, fails, or deteriorates because it was never properly sealed or fastened, and that cannot be repaired without dismantling adjacent work, converting a maintenance task into a reconstruction. Every critical joint must be accessible to the trades who build it and the staff who maintain it, because a joint that cannot be reached is a joint that fails.

### Uncoordinated Multidisciplinary Interfaces

When an architectural detail is developed without integrating the structural connection, mechanical penetration, electrical routing, and firestopping that pass through the same assembly, the conflicts between disciplines are discovered during construction, when the trades' work collides in the field. The trap is that each discipline's drawings show their own work correctly, so no one detects the conflict in the documents, while the combined reality at the detail is impossible to build as drawn. The harm is change orders, delays, and compromised assemblies, as the conflict is resolved hastily in the field, often by cutting, patching, or omitting elements that compromise the detail's performance. Every interface must be coordinated across disciplines in the detail itself, with structural, mechanical, electrical, plumbing, and fire protection input integrated, because a detail that has not been coordinated is not complete.

### Standard Details That Contradict The Design Intent

When a project defaults to library standard details at conditions where the design concept demands a specific resolution, the building's execution contradicts its intent, producing a structure whose concept is visible in the renderings and absent in the construction. The trap is that the standard detail is familiar, quick to produce, and code-compliant, so the team accepts it as efficient, while it actually undermines the architectural idea the project is built around. The harm is a building that disappoints, whose details read as generic where the concept promised specificity, and whose coherence dissolves at the scale the occupant actually encounters. Details must be developed from the design concept, with standard details used only where they genuinely serve the intent, because a detail that betrays the concept is a failure of design, not merely of documentation.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is movement accommodated explicitly at every assembly, with expansion joints and flexible seals sized for anticipated thermal, structural, and moisture movement?
- [ ] Are material transitions resolved with constructable geometry, accessible joints, and buildable sequencing?
- [ ] Has each detail been coordinated across structural, mechanical, electrical, plumbing, and fire protection disciplines at the interface?
- [ ] Are fire-rated assemblies continuous through every transition and penetration, with firestopping confirmed by the fire protection engineer?
- [ ] Does each detail preserve the design intent, with standard details used only where they serve the concept?
- [ ] Has the building envelope consultant been engaged on complex enclosure details, transitions, and penetrations?
- [ ] Are structural connections, embeds, and load paths integrated into architectural details with the structural engineer?
- [ ] Are critical joints accessible to the trades who build them and the staff who maintain them?
- [ ] Are nonstandard details documented with their intent, so value engineering and substitutions can be evaluated against it?
