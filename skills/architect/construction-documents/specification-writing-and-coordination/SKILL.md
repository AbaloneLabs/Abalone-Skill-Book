# Specification Writing And Coordination

Specifications are the written half of the contract documents, and they carry the legal weight that determines quality, performance, and responsibility in ways the drawings cannot. The judgment problem is that specifications are easy to under-write (relying on drawings and generic notes), easy to over-write (copying guide specifications wholesale without editing to the project), and easy to leave uncoordinated with the drawings — and each failure produces a different kind of harm: the under-written spec leaves quality undefined and the contractor chooses the cheapest compliant option; the over-written spec carries contradictory and inapplicable requirements that generate change orders; and the uncoordinated spec and drawings create conflicts the contractor resolves in its own favor. The skilled architect writes specifications that establish clear, enforceable, project-specific quality and performance requirements, coordinates them precisely with the drawings, uses guide specifications as a starting point rather than a finished product, and resolves the conflicts between drawings and specifications before they reach the field. The recurring failures are the unedited guide spec, the drawing-spec conflict, the ambiguous quality requirement, the proprietary specification that limits competition without justification, and the specification that contradicts the code. The architect's role is to write specifications that define the project's quality unambiguously, to coordinate them with the drawings, and to maintain them as enforceable contract documents.

## Core Rules

### Write Project-Specific Specifications, Not Unedited Guide Specs

Guide specifications (MasterSpec, SpecText, and similar) are starting points, not finished products, and using them unedited imports contradictory, inapplicable, and missing requirements that do not match the project. Edit the guide specification to the project: retain the applicable requirements, delete the inapplicable, add the project-specific, and ensure every requirement serves the project.

- Use the guide specification as a framework, and edit it section by section to the project's actual requirements, retaining what applies and deleting what does not.
- Do not leave "or/equal" clauses, bracketed options, or editorial notes in the issued specification; every requirement must be resolved to a definite, enforceable statement.
- Ensure the specification reflects the actual design: the products specified are the products detailed, the assemblies specified are the assemblies drawn, and the quality specified is the quality intended.
- Review the edited specification against the drawings to confirm consistency, because the guide spec describes assemblies that may not match the project's details.

### Coordinate Specifications With Drawings Precisely

The drawings and the specifications are complementary contract documents, and they must not conflict. The drawings show quantity, location, and general configuration; the specifications establish quality, products, installation, and performance. Coordinate the two so each refers to the other without contradiction, and resolve every conflict before the documents are issued.

- The drawings show what and where; the specifications establish of what quality and how installed. Do not duplicate information across the two in a way that allows conflict, and do not leave a requirement in one that contradicts the other.
- Cross-reference: the drawings reference the specification sections, and the specifications reference the drawing details, so the reader can find the complementary information.
- Review the drawings and the specifications together, section by section, to identify and resolve conflicts before issuance, because a conflict found in bidding or construction is a conflict the contractor will resolve in its own favor.
- When a conflict is found after issuance, resolve it promptly by addendum or clarification, and do not allow it to persist into construction.

### Establish Clear, Enforceable Quality And Performance Requirements

Quality and performance requirements must be specific enough to be enforced: a requirement for "high-quality" or "commercial grade" is unenforceable, because it allows the contractor to choose the cheapest option that can be argued to comply. Establish the quality by reference to standards (ASTM, ANSI, AWI), by performance criteria (structural, acoustic, thermal), and by specific product or manufacturer where appropriate.

- Specify quality by reference to established standards (ASTM, ANSI, UL, AWI, NEMA), and cite the specific standard and edition, because the standard is enforceable and the generic adjective is not.
- Specify performance criteria where the product is defined by performance rather than by description: structural capacity, fire rating, acoustic performance, thermal performance, and the test methods by which performance is verified.
- Specify products by description, by performance, by reference standard, by proprietary specification, or by a combination, and choose the method that best serves the project and the competition.
- Avoid unenforceable adjectives: "heavy-duty," "commercial grade," "architectural grade," and "first quality" are not enforceable, because they do not define a verifiable standard.

### Use Proprietary Specifications Deliberately

A proprietary specification (naming a manufacturer and product) limits competition and may increase cost, and it is appropriate when a specific product is required for the design (a specific aesthetic, a specific performance, a specific compatibility) and cannot be defined by description or performance. Use proprietary specifications deliberately, justify the limitation, and provide a basis-of-design product when using an "or equal" basis.

- Use a proprietary specification when a specific product is required and cannot be defined by description or performance, and document the reason.
- When using "or equal," provide a basis-of-design product (the specific product the design is based on) with clear criteria for what constitutes an equal, so the contractor can propose alternatives and the architect can evaluate them against defined criteria.
- Avoid naming multiple "acceptable manufacturers" without criteria, because the list without criteria allows the contractor to choose the cheapest, which may not meet the intent.
- Confirm that the proprietary specification does not violate the procurement requirements of the project (public projects often restrict proprietary specifications), and document the justification.

### Prepare Division 01 To Establish The Project's Rules

Division 01 (General Requirements) establishes the rules that govern the entire project: the scope, the coordination, the submittals, the quality control, the substitutions, and the administrative procedures. A weak Division 01 leaves the project's administration undefined and the architect's authority unclear, and a strong Division 01 establishes the framework that makes the technical specifications enforceable.

- Establish the scope and the coordination requirements, so the contractor understands the extent of the work and the interfaces.
- Establish the submittal requirements: what is submitted, when, in what format, and how reviewed, so the submittal process is defined and enforceable.
- Establish the quality control requirements: the inspections, the testing, the mockups, and the standards of workmanship, so quality is defined and verifiable.
- Establish the substitution procedures, so the basis-of-design products are protected and substitutions are evaluated against defined criteria, not accepted by default.

### Coordinate Specifications With The Code

The specifications must not contradict the building code, and they must establish the code-required performance explicitly where the drawings do not. A specification that establishes a lower fire rating, a lower structural capacity, or a lower accessibility performance than the code requires is a specification that creates a non-compliant building, regardless of the drawings.

- Confirm that the fire ratings, the structural capacities, the accessibility performance, the energy performance, and the other code-regulated qualities in the specifications meet or exceed the code.
- Cite the code and the referenced standards explicitly where the specification establishes a code-regulated quality, so the requirement is enforceable and the compliance is documented.
- Do not allow the specifications to establish a lower performance than the code, even if the drawings establish the code-required performance, because the conflict allows the contractor to choose the lower.

### Maintain The Specifications Through Bidding And Construction

The specifications are living documents through bidding and construction, and they must be maintained: addenda resolve conflicts and clarify requirements, change orders modify the scope, and the record specifications reflect the as-built conditions. Maintain the specifications as the project evolves, and do not allow the issued documents to diverge from the actual requirements.

- Issue addenda promptly to resolve conflicts and clarify requirements found in bidding, and ensure the addenda are acknowledged in the bids.
- Process substitutions, requests for information, and change orders against the specification requirements, and do not allow the specifications to be eroded by informal agreements.
- Maintain the record specifications to reflect the as-built conditions, because the record documents are the basis for future maintenance, renovation, and liability assessment.

## Common Traps

### The Unedited Guide Spec

When the guide specification (MasterSpec, SpecText) is issued with minimal editing — the bracketed options unresolved, the inapplicable sections retained, the editorial notes still present — the specification imports requirements that do not match the project, contradicts the drawings, and presents the contractor with contradictory and inapplicable requirements that generate change orders. The mechanism is that the guide spec reads as comprehensive, so it feels complete, while the unedited content includes requirements for assemblies not in the project, omits requirements for assemblies that are, and leaves the bracketed options unresolved, so the specification is internally inconsistent and inconsistent with the drawings. The harm is that the contractor bids the inconsistent specification, the conflicts generate change orders during construction, and the quality of the work is determined by the conflicts rather than the intent, while the architect believed the specification was comprehensive. The defense is to edit the guide specification section by section to the project's actual requirements, to resolve every bracketed option and editorial note, to delete the inapplicable and add the project-specific, and to review the edited specification against the drawings, because a guide spec is a starting point, not a finished product, and its unedited use imports more problems than it solves.

### The Drawing-Spec Conflict

When the drawings and the specifications establish different requirements for the same condition — the drawing shows one product, the specification names another; the drawing shows one fire rating, the specification establishes a different one — the conflict allows the contractor to choose the lower-cost option, and the architect's intent is defeated by the conflict the architect created. The mechanism is that the drawings and the specifications are prepared by different team members at different times, so the conflict is easy to introduce, and the conflict reads as a discrepancy to be clarified rather than a failure of coordination, while the contractor, bidding the documents, prices the lower-cost option and builds to it. The harm is that the building is built to the lower requirement, the design intent is defeated, and the conflict generates a change order or a dispute, while the architect believed the documents were consistent. The defense is to coordinate the drawings and the specifications section by section, to review them together for conflicts before issuance, to resolve conflicts by addendum when found in bidding, and to recognize that the drawings and specifications are complementary contract documents that must not conflict, because a conflict is resolved by the contractor in its own favor, not by the architect's intent.

### The Ambiguous Quality Requirement

When the specification establishes quality by unenforceable adjectives — "commercial grade," "heavy-duty," "architectural grade," "first quality" — rather than by reference to standards or performance criteria, the requirement cannot be enforced, and the contractor chooses the cheapest product that can be argued to comply, because the adjective does not define a verifiable standard. The mechanism is that the adjective reads as a quality statement, so the requirement feels established, while the adjective has no defined meaning, and the contractor's cheapest option can be argued to meet it, so the requirement is unenforceable. The harm is that the building receives the cheapest product the contractor can justify, the quality falls below the intent, and the architect cannot enforce a higher standard because the specification did not establish one, while the architect believed the quality was specified. The defense is to specify quality by reference to established standards (ASTM, ANSI, UL, AWI) and by performance criteria (structural, acoustic, thermal) with defined test methods, to cite the specific standard and edition, and to avoid unenforceable adjectives, because a quality requirement is only as good as its enforceability, and an adjective is not a standard.

### The Proprietary Specification Without Justification

When the specification names a single proprietary product without justification — without a basis of design, without criteria for equals, without the documentation required for public procurement — the specification limits competition, may increase cost, and may violate the procurement requirements, because the proprietary limitation is not justified by a design requirement. The mechanism is that the named product reads as the design intent, so the specification feels specific, while the limitation of competition is not justified by a requirement that the specific product be used, and the specification may violate the public procurement requirement for open competition. The harm is that the project pays more for the product, the competition is limited without justification, and the specification may be challenged or rejected under the procurement requirements, while the architect believed the product was specified. The defense is to use proprietary specifications deliberately, to justify the limitation by a design requirement that the specific product be used, to provide a basis-of-design product with clear criteria for equals when using "or equal," and to document the justification for the procurement requirements, because a proprietary specification limits competition and must be justified by a requirement, not by a preference.

### The Specification That Contradicts The Code

When the specification establishes a lower performance than the building code requires — a lower fire rating, a lower structural capacity, a lower accessibility performance — the specification creates a non-compliant building, regardless of what the drawings show, because the conflict between the specification and the code allows the contractor to build to the specification and the building fails to comply. The mechanism is that the specification reads as a quality statement, so it feels compliant, while it establishes a performance below the code, and the conflict between the specification and the code is resolved by the contractor in favor of the lower specification. The harm is that the building fails to comply with the code, the occupancy is delayed or denied, and the liability for the non-compliance falls on the architect, while the architect believed the building was compliant because the drawings showed the code-required performance. The defense is to confirm that the code-regulated qualities in the specifications meet or exceed the code, to cite the code and the referenced standards explicitly, to not allow the specifications to establish a lower performance than the code, and to recognize that a specification that contradicts the code creates a non-compliant building regardless of the drawings, because the specification is a contract document and its requirements are enforced.
