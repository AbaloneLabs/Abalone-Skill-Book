# Bidding And Permit Document Preparation

The transition from design documents to permit and bidding documents is where the architect's work meets the regulatory and commercial machinery of construction. A permit set must satisfy an authority having jurisdiction whose review focuses on life safety, accessibility, structural concept, and energy compliance, and whose comments can delay a project for weeks. A bidding set must allow multiple contractors to price the same scope so that bids are comparable, because ambiguous scope produces wide bid spreads and subsequent claims. Agents often treat these submissions as administrative milestones and miss that the permit set is a legal artifact that defines what may be built, that the bidding documents are a contract whose ambiguities will be construed against the architect, and that the relationships with the authority and the bidders must be managed deliberately. The goal is a package that clears review on the first cycle, that produces tight and comparable bids, and that establishes a clean contractual baseline for construction.

## Core Rules

### Confirm The Authority Having Jurisdiction And Applicable Codes First

Permit requirements are not universal; they are determined by the authority having jurisdiction, which may be a city, county, state, or a special district, and each applies its own adopted code editions and amendments. Before producing a permit set, identify the AHJ, confirm which edition of the IBC, IRC, IECC, and referenced standards is adopted, and obtain the AHJ's submittal checklist, because many jurisdictions require specific sheets, calculations, forms, and review fees that are not obvious. Confirm whether the project triggers additional reviews — health department, environmental, historical, zoning, fire marshal, accessibility — and sequence those reviews, because some must precede the building permit. A permit set prepared to the wrong code edition or missing a required review will be rejected on intake, costing weeks.

### Structure The Permit Set To The Reviewer's Needs

A permit reviewer is not reading the set for design intent; they are checking compliance against a checklist of code provisions. Structure the set so that compliance is demonstrated, not hidden: include a code analysis sheet or life-safety plan showing occupancy classification, construction type, height and area calculations, separated and non-separated occupancies, fire barriers, and means of egress with travel distances and widths called out. Provide the structural concept and the engineer's calculations, the energy compliance path and documentation, and the accessibility analysis. A set that makes the reviewer hunt for compliance information invites comments and delays; a set that presents compliance clearly earns faster approval. Include a cover sheet with project data, scope summary, and a sheet index, because the reviewer's first impression of organization shapes the review.

### Define The Bidding Scope And Bid Package Deliberately

A bidding document is only useful if every bidder prices the same scope, which requires the architect to define scope with precision. Confirm what is in the bid and what is excluded — site work, utilities, owner-furnished items, alternates, allowances, unit prices — and state each explicitly in the instructions to bidders and the bid form. Decide the delivery structure — single prime, multiple primes, construction manager, design-build — because it determines how the bid package is divided and how coordination risk is allocated. Define bid alternates carefully so that each adds or subtracts a discrete scope with a discrete price, because a poorly defined alternate produces prices that cannot be compared. Provide a complete bid set to every bidder at the same time, and use a documented addendum process for all clarifications, because informal answers to one bidder's question unbalance the bidding.

### Manage Addenda As The Only Permitted Communication Channel

Once bidding begins, the only legitimate way to change, clarify, or correct the bid documents is through a written addendum issued to every bidder, and every other form of communication — a phone call, an email to one bidder, a site visit answer — creates exposure. Establish an RFI process during bidding where bidder questions are answered in writing, and consolidate answers into numbered addenda issued to all plan holders with a documented distribution list. Acknowledge all addenda on the bid form so that the architect can confirm every bid includes the same scope changes. Never allow the team to "clarify" a drawing verbally during a pre-bid walkthrough without following up in an addendum, because the bidder who heard the clarification and the bidder who did not will price different scopes.

### Reconcile The Bid Form, Alternates, And Allowances

The bid form is where scope becomes price, and its structure determines whether bids are comparable. List base bid scope, then each alternate as a discrete additive or deductive item with a clear scope description, then allowances for undefined work with the basis on which they will be reconciled. Confirm that unit prices are requested for anticipated changes — concrete cubic yards, demolition square footage, rock excavation — so that subsequent change orders are priced against an agreed rate rather than negotiated under duress. Verify that the bid form requires acknowledgment of all addenda, a contract duration, a list of subcontractors, and the required certifications, because a bid missing these may be non-responsive and a low bid that is non-responsive is worse than no bid at all.

### Plan For Plan Review Comments And Resubmission

Most permit sets receive comments on the first review, and the project schedule must assume at least one resubmittal cycle. Establish a process for receiving, logging, and responding to comments: each comment gets a tracking number, a responsible party, and a documented response that either revises the drawings or argues the basis for no change. Respond to every comment in writing, even those the architect intends to dispute, because an unanswered comment is treated as unresolved and blocks approval. Distinguish between comments that require design changes, comments that require documentation, and comments that reflect a reviewer's misreading that can be resolved with explanation. Treat the plan reviewer as a collaborator whose approval you need, not an adversary, because a contested review takes longer than a cooperative one.

### Archive The Permitted And Bid Baselines

Once the permit is issued and the bid is awarded, freeze those document sets as contractual baselines, because every subsequent change is measured against them. Archive the stamped, approved permit set separately from the construction set, and archive the bid set with all addenda incorporated as the basis of the contract. A change made after permit approval but not documented as a revision becomes a construction dispute; a change made after bid award without a change order becomes a claim. The baseline sets are the reference points for every modification, and their integrity must be protected through version control, restricted access, and a clear revision protocol.

## Common Traps

### Submitting To The Wrong Code Edition Or AHJ

The team prepares the permit set to the current published IBC, only to discover the jurisdiction has not yet adopted that edition, or has adopted it with local amendments that change the requirements, or that a separate fire district applies a more restrictive overlay code. The mechanism is that code adoption is local and lagging, and teams assume the newest published code governs, and the false signal is that the set was prepared to "the code" and so seems compliant. The harm is that the set is rejected at intake or receives extensive comments requiring redesign, delaying the project and eroding the owner's confidence. The AHJ, the adopted code edition, and all local amendments must be confirmed in writing before the permit set is produced, not after it is submitted.

### Ambiguous Scope Producing Wide Bid Spreads

The bid documents leave a scope — perimeter caulking, testing, cleanup, permits — undefined or ambiguous, and each bidder prices it differently based on their own assumption, producing bids that vary by far more than the cost of the work. The mechanism is that scope gaps are invisible until pricing reveals them, and the false signal is that the documents look complete. The harm is that the owner cannot tell whether the low bid is cheap because of efficiency or because of an omitted scope assumption, the apparent low bidder may pursue change orders to recover the gap, and the owner is forced to either accept a risky bid or rebid, losing time. Every scope item must be explicitly assigned to the contractor or excluded, and allowances and alternates must be defined with enough precision that bidders price the same thing.

### The Undocumented Pre-Bid Answer

During a pre-bid walkthrough or in a phone call, a bidder asks a question and the architect answers verbally, intending to follow up with an addendum but never doing so, or issuing the addendum only to the bidder who asked. The mechanism is that bidding is fast and informal communication feels efficient, and the false signal is that the answer was given and so the issue is resolved. The harm is that the bidders who did not receive the written answer price a different scope, the bids are not comparable, and the losing bidders have grounds to protest the award, potentially forcing a rebid. The iron rule of bidding is that every clarification must be issued as a numbered addendum to every documented plan holder, with no exceptions for verbal exchanges.

### Hiding Compliance Rather Than Demonstrating It

The team produces a permit set that is design-complete but does not include a code analysis sheet, a life-safety plan, or explicit egress and fire-rating documentation, assuming the reviewer will infer compliance from the construction documents. The mechanism is that design teams focus on constructability and treat code documentation as overhead, and the false signal is that the set is complete because it shows the building. The harm is that the reviewer cannot verify compliance efficiently, issues extensive comments asking for the missing analysis, and the permit cycle extends by weeks while the team produces documentation that should have been in the original submittal. A permit set must demonstrate compliance affirmatively on dedicated sheets, because the reviewer's job is to verify, not to reverse-engineer.

### Treating The First Review As The Last

The schedule assumes one permit review cycle, so when comments arrive the team is unprepared, the responses are rushed, and the resubmittal introduces new errors that generate a second round of comments. The mechanism is that optimism bias treats the first submission as likely to pass, and the false signal is that the set was carefully prepared. The harm is that each unanticipated review cycle adds weeks, the owner's financing and lease commitments slip, and the team's credibility with the owner erodes. The schedule should assume at least one resubmittal, the comment response process should be established before comments arrive, and responses should be deliberate rather than reactive, because a hasty resubmittal often generates more comments than it resolves.

### Baseline Documents Not Frozen After Award

After permit approval and contract award, the team continues to edit the construction documents in place rather than issuing tracked revisions against the frozen baseline, so that the permitted set and the construction set drift apart with no record of what changed. The mechanism is that version discipline feels bureaucratic and the team wants to keep improving the documents, and the false signal is that the changes are improvements. The harm is that there is no clean reference for change orders, a field discrepancy cannot be traced to a decision, and in a dispute the team cannot prove what was permitted or bid. The permitted and bid sets must be archived as immutable baselines, and all subsequent changes must be issued as numbered, tracked revisions against those baselines.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has the authority having jurisdiction, the adopted code edition, local amendments, and all required secondary reviews been confirmed in writing before the permit set was produced?
- [ ] Does the permit set include a code analysis sheet, life-safety plan, and explicit documentation of occupancy, construction type, egress, and fire ratings that affirmatively demonstrate compliance?
- [ ] Does the bidding package define base scope, exclusions, alternates, allowances, and unit prices with enough precision that every bidder prices the same work?
- [ ] Is there a documented addendum process, with all bidder questions answered in numbered written addenda distributed to every plan holder and acknowledged on the bid form?
- [ ] Has the project schedule assumed at least one permit resubmittal cycle, with a comment-response process established before comments arrive?
- [ ] Are the approved permit set and the awarded bid set, with all addenda incorporated, archived as immutable contractual baselines?
- [ ] Has the bid form been structured to require addenda acknowledgment, contract duration, subcontractor list, and required certifications so that non-responsive bids can be identified?
- [ ] Have all communications during bidding been routed through the formal RFI and addendum channel, with no undocumented verbal answers?
