# Specification Writing And Product Selection

Specifications are the written half of the contract documents, and they carry equal legal weight to the drawings even though they are read far less carefully. Where a drawing answers "where and how big," a specification answers "of what quality, by what standard, installed how, and accepted on what basis." Agents often treat specifications as boilerplate to be edited from a master and miss that a careless spec edit can lock the project into an unavailable product, void a warranty, or contradict a drawing in a way that the contractor will exploit. The architect's judgment in specification writing is about defining quality precisely enough to be enforced, loosely enough to allow competitive pricing, and consistently enough that the drawings and the project manual never disagree. The goal is a project manual that a contractor can bid, a supplier can satisfy, and an inspector can enforce without ambiguity.

## Core Rules

### Choose The Right Specification Method For Each Condition

CSI recognizes four methods — proprietary, basis-of-design with alternatives, performance, and descriptive — and the choice is a risk allocation decision, not a stylistic one. A proprietary closed specification names a single product and permits no substitution, which guarantees the result but eliminates competition and can invite a bid protest; use it only when a product is genuinely unique or owner-mandated. A basis-of-design specification names a product as the benchmark and lists approved equals, which is the most common method because it balances control with competition, but it requires the architect to define "equal" with real criteria, not a vague phrase. A performance specification states the required results and leaves the means to the contractor, which shifts risk and is appropriate for systems where the architect wants innovation but must then define testable acceptance criteria. Descriptive specifications describe the required characteristics without naming products, which is precise but verbose. Match the method to the risk and the need for competition.

### Master The CSI MasterFormat Division Structure

Specifications are organized by CSI MasterFormat, with Division 01 governing general requirements that apply to the entire project and Divisions 02 through 49 governing specific work results. Division 01 is where procedural requirements live — substitution procedures, submittal procedures, quality control, testing, project meetings, and closeout — and it must be written first and carefully because it governs every other section. A common error is burying product-specific requirements in Division 01, where they belong in a Division 02-49 section, or vice versa. Each Section follows the three-part format — Part 1 General, Part 2 Products, Part 3 Execution — and the discipline of this structure is what makes a spec enforceable: administrative requirements in Part 1, material and product requirements in Part 2, and installation and field quality requirements in Part 3.

### Establish A Defensible Basis-Of-Design

When using a basis-of-design approach, the named product is the standard against which substitutions are judged, so it must be selected with rigor. Verify that the named product is currently manufactured, available in the project's geography, compatible with adjacent systems, and compliant with the applicable codes and standards. Document the salient characteristics — performance, material, finish, warranty, dimensional tolerances — that make the product appropriate, because these are the criteria by which a proposed equal will be evaluated. A basis-of-design that is out of production, or whose salient characteristics are undefined, hands the substitution decision to the contractor and erodes the architect's control over quality. Review basis-of-design selections against the project's performance requirements and against the drawings, which must show the basis product's dimensions and installation.

### Define Substitution Rules And Timing Explicitly

Substitutions are where specifications most often break down. Specify when substitutions will be considered — typically before bid or during a defined post-bid window — and define the information a substitution request must include: product data, performance test results, samples, installed cost comparison, and confirmation of compatibility. Require that substitutions demonstrate equality on the salient characteristics defined in the basis-of-design, not merely that they fit the opening. Establish that the architect's approval of a substitution does not relieve the contractor of responsibility for coordination, fit, or performance, because contractors routinely propose a cheaper product and then claim the architect approved the resulting problems. A clear, written substitution procedure in Division 01, reinforced in each section, is what prevents substitution disputes from consuming construction.

### Coordinate Specifications With Drawings And Codes

The project manual and the drawings are one contract, and any conflict between them will be construed against the drafter. Every product noted on a drawing must appear in a specification section, and every specification reference to a detail must point to an existing detail. Confirm that specified assemblies meet the code-required ratings, that specified materials are permitted by the applicable codes, and that specified accessibility dimensions and features match the drawings. Pay particular attention to fire-rated assemblies, where a specified assembly number must correspond to a listed design that the drawings actually depict. A specification that calls for a one-hour wall but names an assembly that is only rated for three-quarters of an hour, or that the drawings do not match, is a defect that the building official will catch and the contractor may price.

### Use Referenced Standards Correctly

Specifications routinely incorporate standards by reference — ASTM, ANSI, UL, NFPA, ACI, AISC — and the date and edition of each referenced standard matters. Cite the specific edition where the standard is subject to revision, and confirm that the cited edition is the one adopted by the applicable code, because a newer edition may contain provisions the code has not adopted. Do not reference a standard you have not read; a vague reference to "ASTM standards" is unenforceable, and a reference to a standard whose requirements you do not know can bind the project to obligations you did not intend. Where a standard conflicts with a specification provision, state explicitly which governs, because the default rule in most contracts gives the specific requirement precedence over the referenced general standard, but only if the specification says so.

### Address Sustainability, Health, And Regulatory Requirements

Modern specifications carry obligations that go beyond constructability: volatile organic compound limits, recycled content, regionally sourced materials, low-emitting finishes, Forest Stewardship Council certification, and red-list chemicals. Incorporate these requirements explicitly in the relevant sections, with the certifications and documentation required to prove compliance, because a credit that depends on a material attribute fails if the specification is silent. Coordinate sustainability requirements with the rating system strategy so that the documentation the contractor must submit is specified, not assumed. Be aware that some product categories are subject to regulatory restrictions — asbestos, lead, formaldehyde, certain flame retardants — and that specifying a prohibited substance is both a code violation and a liability.

## Common Traps

### The Out-Of-Production Basis-Of-Design

An architect names a product as the basis-of-design copied from a project completed years earlier, without verifying current availability, and the product has been discontinued or reformulated in the interim. The mechanism is that master specifications are reused without re-verification and product literature is trusted past its shelf life, and the false signal is that the specification reads cleanly and looks authoritative. The harm is that bidders either price a product they cannot obtain, inflating the bid with risk, or propose substitutions that the architect must evaluate under time pressure, often accepting inferior equals to avoid delay. Every basis-of-design must be verified as currently manufactured and available before the specification is issued, and re-verified if the project sits dormant between phases.

### Vague "Or Equal" Language

The specification names a product and adds "or equal" without defining the salient characteristics on which equality will be judged, treating the phrase as a magic incantation rather than a contractual standard. The mechanism is that defining equality requires real engineering judgment and the specifier takes a shortcut, and the false signal is that "or equal" appears in every master spec and so seems sufficient. The harm is that the contractor proposes the cheapest product that fits the description, the architect has no documented basis to reject it, and the installed product fails to perform, with the dispute turning on an undefined term the architect cannot defend. Equality must be defined by enumerated, testable characteristics — performance, material, finish, warranty, dimensional tolerance — stated in the specification itself.

### Contradictions Between Drawings And Specifications

The drawings call for a hollow metal door in one assembly and the specifications require a wood door in the same opening, or the drawings show a two-hour wall where the specification names a one-hour assembly, because the two documents were edited by different hands and never reconciled. The mechanism is that drawings and specifications are produced and revised on different schedules and the coordination pass is skipped, and the false signal is that each document is internally consistent, so a review of either alone passes. The harm is that the conflict is construed against the architect under the standard contract rule, the contractor builds the cheaper interpretation, and the correction becomes a change order paid by the owner. A pre-issue reconciliation pass that cross-checks every product and assembly between drawings and specifications is the only reliable defense.

### Over-Specifying And Locking Out Competition

The architect specifies a single manufacturer with no substitutions permitted for a commodity product that has many equivalents, either to match an owner preference or out of caution, and the closed specification eliminates competitive pricing. The mechanism is that closed specs feel safer because they guarantee a known result, and the false signal is that the owner approved the product, so the restriction seems justified. The harm is that the bid reflects a sole-source premium, the owner overpays, and if the specified product becomes unavailable during construction the project has no fallback. Reserve closed proprietary specifications for genuinely unique products or owner mandates, and use basis-of-design with defined equals for everything else, because competition is what protects the owner's budget.

### Unread Referenced Standards

The specification references an ASTM or UL standard by number without the specifier having read it, on the assumption that the standard says what its title implies. The mechanism is that standards are numerous and technical and reading each one is time-consuming, so specifiers rely on titles and summaries, and the false signal is that a cited standard looks rigorous. The harm is that the standard contains provisions — test methods, acceptance criteria, limitations — that conflict with the design intent or add cost the team did not anticipate, or that the cited edition has been superseded by one the code does not adopt. Every referenced standard must be read, its edition confirmed against the adopted code, and any conflict with the specification's explicit provisions resolved in writing before issue.

### Burying Product Requirements In Division 01

A specifier places product-specific requirements — a particular coating system, a specific membrane — in Division 01 general requirements, either because a master spec was edited carelessly or because the requirement seemed project-wide. The mechanism is that Division 01 is the catch-all and editors drop content there for convenience, and the false signal is that the requirement appears somewhere in the project manual and so seems covered. The harm is that the contractor's sub bids are organized by Division 02-49 work results, so a requirement hidden in Division 01 is missed by the subcontractor responsible for that work, and the obligation goes unmet. Product requirements belong in the work-results section that governs the relevant trade, and Division 01 must contain only procedural and project-wide requirements.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has a deliberate specification method — proprietary, basis-of-design, performance, or descriptive — been chosen for each section based on risk and competition needs?
- [ ] Does every basis-of-design product remain currently manufactured, available, code-compliant, and coordinated with the drawings?
- [ ] Are substitution procedures and timing defined in Division 01, with equality criteria enumerated as testable salient characteristics in each section?
- [ ] Have the drawings and specifications been cross-checked so that every noted product, assembly, and rating is consistent across both documents?
- [ ] Has every referenced standard been read, its edition confirmed against the adopted code, and any conflict with explicit provisions resolved?
- [ ] Are sustainability, health, and regulatory requirements — VOC limits, recycled content, prohibited substances — incorporated with the documentation needed to prove compliance?
- [ ] Are product-specific requirements placed in the correct Division 02-49 sections rather than buried in Division 01?
- [ ] Does each section follow the three-part format with administrative requirements in Part 1, products in Part 2, and execution in Part 3?
