# Detail Development And Assembly Design

Detail development is where design intent meets physical reality, and it is the phase at which a building's long-term performance, durability, constructability, and liability exposure are largely determined. The judgment problem is that details are easy to draw and hard to make work: a wall section that reads clearly in section can conceal incompatible materials, unsequenced trades, thermal bridges, unmanaged water, and unbuildable geometry, and the failures do not appear until construction (when they are expensive) or years later (when they are litigated). The skilled architect develops details from the physics outward — water, air, heat, and vapor first, then materials, then geometry — sequences the assembly so each trade can do its work, coordinates the transitions where most failures occur, and documents the detail with enough specificity that the builder builds what was intended. The recurring failures are the aesthetic detail that ignores physics, the unsequenced assembly, the unresolved transition, the detail that assumes a perfect builder, and the detail that cannot accommodate movement. The architect's role is to design assemblies that are buildable, durable, and code-compliant, and to document them with the specificity that makes the intent unambiguous.

## Core Rules

### Design From The Physics Outward: Water, Air, Heat, Vapor

A building assembly must manage water, air, heat, and vapor in that order of priority, because water does the most damage, air carries the most moisture, heat drives the energy performance, and vapor must be managed to prevent condensation. Design the detail by addressing each in turn, and ensure the layers are compatible and continuous.

- Water: every assembly must shed water at the exterior, provide a drainage plane (water-resistant barrier or building paper) that directs water down and out, and provide a path for water that gets past the first line of defense to drain. Anticipate that water will get in; design for it to get out.
- Air: the air barrier must be continuous across the entire enclosure, sealed at every penetration and transition, because air leakage carries moisture into the assembly where it condenses and rots. Identify the air control layer in every detail and show its continuity.
- Heat: the thermal insulation must be continuous (ideally outboard of the structure to avoid thermal bridging), and the detail must show how the insulation is maintained at transitions where it is often interrupted (shelf angles, lintels, slab edges, parapets).
- Vapor: the vapor control layer must be matched to the climate and the assembly — vapor open or vapor closed, and positioned to prevent condensation on cold surfaces — and the detail must show its position relative to the insulation and the air barrier.

### Sequence The Assembly For Constructibility

An assembly must be buildable in a sequence that allows each trade to do its work, and the detail must show the layers in the order they are installed. A detail that requires one trade to work over another's finished work, or that requires access that does not exist at the time of installation, is a detail that will be built differently than drawn.

- Lay out the layers in the order of installation, from the structure outward, and confirm that each layer can be installed after the previous one without damaging it.
- Identify the trade responsible for each layer, and confirm that the sequence does not require one trade to wait for another in a way that disrupts the schedule.
- Provide the access and the clearance each trade needs: the cavity for the mason, the access for the insulator, the clearance for the window installer, and the room for the sealant joint.
- Show the fasteners, the clips, and the connections, and confirm they are installable with the access available at the time of installation.

### Resolve The Transitions, Where Most Failures Occur

Most enclosure failures occur at transitions: window-to-wall, roof-to-wall, wall-to-foundation, dissimilar-material joints, and movement joints. The detail that works in the field of the wall fails at the transition, and the skilled detailer gives the transitions more attention than the field.

- Detail every transition: window head, jamb, and sill; roof-to-wall and parapet; wall-to-foundation; expansion joints; and the joints between dissimilar materials (steel-to-masonry, concrete-to-wood).
- At each transition, confirm the continuity of the water, air, thermal, and vapor control layers, because transitions are where continuity is lost.
- Show the sealant joints, the flashing, the laps, and the overlaps that make the transition watertight, and confirm they are buildable.
- Test the transition mentally against wind-driven rain: where does the water go, and does it get out?

### Detail For Movement

Every building moves — thermal expansion and contraction, structural deflection, seismic movement, material shrinkage — and the detail must accommodate the movement or it will fail. Identify the movement at each detail and provide the joint, the slip connection, or the flexible material that accommodates it.

- Identify the expected movement at each detail: thermal expansion of the cladding, deflection of the structure, shrinkage of the wood frame, seismic movement.
- Provide the movement joint, the expansion joint, or the control joint that accommodates the movement, sized for the expected displacement.
- Use slip connections, flexible membranes, and elastic sealants at the joints that must move, and avoid rigid connections across movement joints.
- Confirm that the movement accommodation does not break the continuity of the water, air, thermal, or vapor control layers.

### Coordinate Materials For Compatibility And Durability

Materials in contact must be compatible (galvanic corrosion, chemical reaction, staining) and durable in their position (moisture, UV, temperature), and the detail must specify materials that perform in the assembly. A detail that pairs incompatible materials creates a failure that appears years later.

- Check galvanic compatibility: dissimilar metals in contact in the presence of moisture corrode, and the detail must isolate them (coatings, gaskets, or compatible fasteners).
- Check chemical compatibility: sealants, membranes, and finishes must be compatible with the substrates, and incompatible pairings (sealant on stone that stains, membrane on a substrate it attacks) must be avoided.
- Check durability in position: materials exposed to moisture, UV, or high temperature must be rated for the exposure, and materials in concealed cavities must be durable without maintenance.
- Confirm the warranty and the manufacturer's requirements for each material, and detail in accordance with them, because a detail that does not meet the manufacturer's requirements voids the warranty.

### Document With Specificity That Makes Intent Unambiguous

A detail is a communication to the builder, and its clarity determines whether the builder builds what was intended. Document the detail with enough specificity — materials, dimensions, fasteners, sealants, laps — that the intent is unambiguous, and reference the specifications for the material requirements.

- Show the materials, the dimensions, the fasteners, the sealants, the laps, and the overlaps, with enough specificity that the builder does not have to guess.
- Reference the specifications for the material requirements, and do not contradict the specifications in the detail.
- Use notes to clarify the intent where the geometry is ambiguous, and use section bubbles and references to connect the detail to its context.
- Show the detail at a scale that reveals the information (large scale for the assembly, context scale for the location), and do not overload a single detail with more information than it can show.

### Coordinate Across Disciplines

A wall section is not solely an architectural document; it integrates structure, mechanical, electrical, and plumbing, and the detail must reflect the coordination. A detail that shows the architectural layers without the structural, mechanical, and electrical elements that penetrate or occupy the assembly is a detail that will conflict in the field.

- Overlay the structural, mechanical, electrical, and plumbing elements on the architectural detail, and resolve the conflicts.
- Coordinate the penetrations: the duct, the conduit, the pipe, and the structural element must pass through the assembly without breaking the control layers, and the detail must show how.
- Coordinate the structural depth, the mechanical clearance, and the architectural finish, and confirm they fit in the assembly.

## Common Traps

### The Aesthetic Detail That Ignores Physics

When the detail is developed to achieve a visual effect — a crisp reveal, a thin edge, a flush condition — without first resolving the water, air, heat, and vapor management, the assembly looks right and performs wrong, because the physics that determine durability and performance were subordinated to the appearance. The mechanism is that the visual intent reads as the design, so the detail feels complete, while the water, air, thermal, and vapor control layers are discontinuous or absent at the critical locations, and the assembly fails at the first wind-driven rain or the first condensation season. The harm is that the building leaks, rots, or condenses within years of completion, the repair requires disassembly of the enclosure, and the liability falls on the architect who detailed for appearance over physics, while the detail looked correct in the documents. The defense is to design from the physics outward, to resolve the water, air, heat, and vapor management before the geometry, to identify each control layer in every detail and confirm its continuity, and to recognize that a detail that ignores physics is a detail that will fail regardless of how it looks, because the building does not care about the appearance, it cares about the physics.

### The Unsequenced Assembly

When the detail shows the layers of the assembly without regard for the sequence of installation, the builder encounters a condition in which one trade must work over another's finished work, or must access a location that is already closed in, and the assembly is built differently than drawn because the drawn sequence is unbuildable. The mechanism is that the layers read as a complete assembly, so the detail feels buildable, while the sequence in which they must be installed is not feasible, and the builder, faced with an unbuildable sequence, improvises a sequence that departs from the intent. The harm is that the assembly is built differently than detailed, the control layers are compromised by the improvised sequence, and the performance departs from the design, while the architect believes the detail was built as drawn. The defense is to sequence the assembly for constructibility, to lay out the layers in the order of installation, to confirm that each trade can do its work without damaging the previous trade's work, to provide the access and clearance each trade needs, and to recognize that a detail that cannot be built in sequence is a detail that will be built differently than drawn, because the builder cannot defy the sequence.

### The Unresolved Transition

When the detail is developed for the field of the wall and the transitions — window-to-wall, roof-to-wall, parapet, foundation — are left to the builder or to a generic note, the transitions, where most failures occur, are the least detailed conditions, and the assembly fails at exactly the locations that were not resolved. The mechanism is that the field detail reads as representative, so the assembly feels resolved, while the transitions, which require distinct detailing, are undocumented, and the builder, lacking detail, improvises a transition that breaks the continuity of the control layers. The harm is that the building leaks at the windows, the parapets, and the roof-to-wall transitions, the failures concentrate at the undocumented conditions, and the repair requires disassembly at the most difficult locations, while the architect believed the transitions were covered by the field detail. The defense is to detail every transition explicitly, to confirm the continuity of the water, air, thermal, and vapor control layers at each transition, to show the sealant joints, the flashing, the laps, and the overlaps that make the transition watertight, and to recognize that the transitions require more attention than the field, because the field rarely fails and the transitions routinely do.

### The Detail That Assumes A Perfect Builder

When the detail requires a level of precision, access, or workmanship that is not achievable in normal construction — a sealant joint in a location with no access, a flashing lap with no tolerance, a membrane installation requiring perfect alignment in a concealed cavity — the detail assumes a perfect builder, and normal construction cannot achieve it, so the assembly is built with the imperfections the detail did not accommodate. The mechanism is that the detail reads as buildable in principle, so it feels complete, while it requires a precision or access that normal construction does not provide, and the imperfections that are normal in construction break the control layers the detail assumed would be perfect. The harm is that the assembly, built with normal imperfections, fails because the detail did not tolerate them, and the failure is attributed to workmanship when the root cause is a detail that demanded perfection. The defense is to detail for the construction that will actually be built, to provide tolerance, access, and redundancy (a second line of defense for water), to avoid details that require precision in inaccessible locations, and to recognize that a detail must work with the imperfections of normal construction, because a detail that assumes a perfect builder is a detail that will fail with a normal one.

### The Detail That Cannot Accommodate Movement

When the detail does not provide for the movement the building will undergo — thermal expansion, structural deflection, material shrinkage, seismic displacement — the movement occurs anyway, and the rigid detail cracks, leaks, or separates, because the movement the detail ignored is imposed on the assembly regardless. The mechanism is that the detail reads as a stable assembly, so it feels complete, while the building moves, and the rigid connections across movement joints crack the finishes, break the sealants, and open the control layers. The harm is that the building cracks, leaks, and separates at the movement joints the detail did not accommodate, the failures appear within the first cycles of movement, and the repair requires reworking the joints, while the architect believed the assembly was stable. The defense is to identify the movement at each detail, to provide the movement joint, expansion joint, or control joint sized for the expected displacement, to use slip connections, flexible membranes, and elastic sealants at the joints that must move, and to recognize that every building moves and the detail must accommodate it or fail, because the movement is imposed regardless of the detail.

### The Incompatible Materials In Contact

When the detail pairs materials that are incompatible in contact — dissimilar metals that corrode galvanically, a sealant that stains a stone, a membrane that attacks a substrate — the incompatibility produces a failure that appears months or years after construction, and the failure is attributed to the material when the root cause is the pairing. The mechanism is that each material reads as appropriate in its position, so the pairing feels sound, while the contact between them produces a chemical or galvanic reaction that degrades one or both, and the failure appears after the warranty period. The harm is that the materials corrode, stain, or degrade in contact, the repair requires replacement of the affected materials, and the failure is diffuse and recurring, while the architect believed the materials were appropriate. The defense is to check galvanic compatibility (isolate dissimilar metals), chemical compatibility (confirm sealants, membranes, and finishes are compatible with substrates), and durability in position (confirm materials are rated for the exposure), and to detail in accordance with the manufacturer's requirements, because a detail that pairs incompatible materials is a detail that will fail at the contact regardless of the individual materials.

- [ ] Did I sequence the assembly for constructibility, confirming that each layer can be installed after the previous one without damage, and that each trade has the access and clearance it needs?
- [ ] Did I detail every transition — window head, jamb, sill; roof-to-wall and parapet; wall-to-foundation; expansion joints; dissimilar-material joints — and confirm the continuity of the control layers at each?
- [ ] Did I identify the expected movement at each detail (thermal, structural, shrinkage, seismic) and provide the movement joint, slip connection, or flexible material sized for the displacement?
- [ ] Did I check galvanic compatibility, chemical compatibility, and durability in position for all materials in contact, and detail in accordance with the manufacturer's requirements?
- [ ] Did I coordinate the structural, mechanical, electrical, and plumbing elements on the detail, and resolve the penetrations without breaking the control layers?
- [ ] Did I document the detail with enough specificity — materials, dimensions, fasteners, sealants, laps — that the intent is unambiguous, and reference the specifications without contradicting them?
