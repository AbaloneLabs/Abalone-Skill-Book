# Space-Efficient Unit Planning

A tiny or micro-housing unit (often 15 to 30 square meters) is an exercise in extreme spatial efficiency, where every fraction of a meter and every furniture decision determines whether the unit is habitable or claustrophobic, and the design must fit the sleep, the cook, the eat, the bathe, the work, and the store into a footprint smaller than a conventional bedroom. The judgment problem is that a micro-unit is not a conventional unit scaled down, because the overlap of the functions (the bed that is also the sofa, the counter that is also the desk, the table that folds away) creates conflicts that a conventional layout avoids, and the designer who plans it as a small apartment produces a unit where no function works well and the resident cannot live comfortably. A bed placed so the resident cannot stand at the kitchen counter; a door swing that blocks the only circulation; a window that lights only the bed and leaves the kitchen dark. This skill covers the decisions that determine whether a micro-unit is genuinely habitable, including the zone overlap and the furniture, the wet wall and the services, the circulation and the door swings, and the psychological comfort of the small space.

## Core Rules

### Plan the Unit Around the Overlapping Functions and the Multi-Function Furniture

The micro-unit must be planned around the overlap of the functions (the sleep, the sit, the eat, the work, the cook) and the multi-function furniture (the Murphy bed, the fold-down table, the sofa bed, the convertible counter), because the footprint cannot fit a dedicated piece for each function, and the overlap is the only way to serve all the functions. The designer must map the daily cycle of the functions (the morning, the day, the evening) and the furniture transformations, must verify each function has its zone and its clearances when in use, and must verify the transformations do not conflict (the bed down does not block the bathroom door). The tradeoff is that the multi-function furniture is more expensive and more complex, but it is the only way to fit the functions; the resolution is to invest in the quality transformation hardware.

### Cluster the Wet Functions Around a Single Wet Wall to Save Space and Cost

The wet functions (the kitchen sink, the bathroom, the shower, the laundry) must be clustered around a single wet wall (the plumbing wall), because the wet wall is the most expensive and the most space-consuming element, and clustering the wet functions around one wall saves the plumbing runs, the wall thickness, and the floor area. The designer must locate the kitchen and the bathroom back-to-back on the single wet wall, must minimize the plumbing horizontal runs, and must verify the wet wall does not consume the prime frontage or the window wall. The trap is the separated kitchen and bathroom that require two wet walls and consume the area.

### Resolve the Circulation and the Door Swings to Preserve the Usable Area

The circulation and the door swings are critical in the micro-unit, because a door swing consumes a full circle of floor area (often a full square meter) and a poorly planned circulation path bisects the usable area, and in a 20-square-meter unit every fraction matters. The designer must use the pocket doors or the sliding doors to eliminate the door swing, must plan the circulation as a single, clear path that does not bisect the furniture zones, and must verify every door (the entry, the bathroom, the closet) opens without blocking the function. The trap is a conventional hinged door whose swing consumes the only usable corner.

### Preserve the Daylight, the Outlook, and the Air Quality for the Psychological Comfort

The daylight, the outlook, and the air quality are disproportionately important in the micro-unit, because the small space is psychologically demanding and the resident's comfort depends on the connection to the outside, the natural light, and the fresh air, and a dark, airless micro-unit is uninhabitable regardless of the layout efficiency. The designer must preserve the window for the living zone (not the closet or the bathroom), must provide the outlook from the primary seated position, and must provide the operable window or the mechanical ventilation for the fresh air. The trap is a layout that puts the closet or the bathroom at the window and leaves the living zone dark.

### Provide the Minimum Clearances and the Functional Dimensions, Even at the Micro Scale

The minimum clearances (the kitchen counter frontage, the bathroom turning, the bed access, the desk depth) and the functional dimensions must be provided even at the micro scale, because the function fails if the clearance is below the minimum, and the resident cannot use the kitchen counter of 400mm depth or the bathroom with no turning. The designer must verify the kitchen counter frontage and depth, the bathroom clear floor, the bed access on at least one side, and the desk or the table depth, and must not sacrifice the function for the area. The tradeoff is that the minimum clearance consumes area, but the function is non-negotiable; the resolution is to find the area elsewhere.

## Common Traps

### A Bed Placed So the Resident Cannot Use the Kitchen Counter

The bed is placed against the wall near the kitchen, and the kitchen counter is on the adjacent wall. The mechanism of the trap is that the bed, when in use or when extended (a Murphy bed), projects into the kitchen counter zone, so the resident cannot stand at the counter or access the counter when the bed is down, and the two functions conflict in time, so the resident must make the bed every time they cook, which is a daily friction that makes the unit unworkable and drives the resident to eat takeout on the bed. The false signal is the area efficiency, which suggests both functions fit but masks the conflict. The harm is functional conflict, daily friction, and an unworkable unit. The defense is to map the furniture transformations and verify each function has its clearance when in use.

### A Door Swing That Consumes the Only Usable Corner

The entry door or the bathroom door is a conventional hinged door, and its swing occupies the only usable corner of the unit. The mechanism of the trap is that a hinged door requires a full swing circle (often a square meter), and in a micro-unit the only usable corner may be the place where the desk or the storage could go, so the door swing consumes the corner and the function (the desk, the storage) has nowhere to go, and the unit loses a critical function to the door swing. The false signal is the conventional door, which suggests a normal detail but masks the area loss. The harm is lost function, lost storage, and a less usable unit. The defense is to use the pocket doors or the sliding doors, and to plan the circulation as a single clear path.

### A Layout That Puts the Closet or Bathroom at the Window

The layout places the closet or the bathroom at the window wall to free the interior for the bed. The mechanism of the trap is that the window is the only source of the daylight, the outlook, and the fresh air, and placing the closet or the bathroom (the functions that do not need the window) at the window blocks the daylight and the outlook from the living zone, and the resident sits and sleeps in a dark, airless interior with no connection to the outside, which is psychologically oppressive and uninhabitable regardless of the layout efficiency. The false signal is the interior freed for the bed, which suggests a good layout but masks the daylight failure. The harm is a dark, airless, oppressive unit. The defense is to preserve the window for the living zone, provide the outlook from the primary position, and locate the closet and the bathroom at the interior wall.

### Separated Wet Functions That Require Two Wet Walls and Consume the Area

The kitchen is on one wall and the bathroom is on the opposite wall, each with its own plumbing. The mechanism of the trap is that the wet wall (the plumbing wall) is the most expensive and the space-consuming element, and separating the kitchen and the bathroom requires two wet walls, which doubles the plumbing runs, the wall thickness, and the floor area consumed by the plumbing, and the lost area is critical in a micro-unit, so the unit is less efficient and more expensive for no functional benefit. The false signal is the layout separation, which suggests a clear kitchen and bathroom but masks the duplication. The harm is lost area, higher cost, and a less efficient unit. The defense is to cluster the wet functions around a single wet wall.

### A Function Sacrificed for the Area, Leaving the Unit Unworkable

The kitchen counter is reduced to 400mm depth or the bathroom is reduced to no turning to save the area. The mechanism of the trap is that the function fails below a minimum clearance (the counter too shallow for the cooktop, the bathroom too small for the turning), and the resident cannot use the function as intended, so the kitchen becomes a coffee station and the bathroom becomes unusable, and the unit that looked efficient on paper is unworkable in practice, and the resident's daily life is degraded. The false signal is the area saving, which suggests efficiency but masks the functional failure. The harm is lost function, daily friction, and an unworkable unit. The defense is to verify the minimum clearances and the functional dimensions, and to find the area elsewhere.

## Self-Check

- Did I map the daily cycle of the functions and the furniture transformations, and verify each function has its zone and clearance when in use without conflict?
- Did I cluster the wet functions (the kitchen, the bathroom, the laundry) around a single wet wall, and verify the plumbing runs are minimized?
- Did I use the pocket doors or the sliding doors, plan the circulation as a single clear path, and verify every door opens without blocking a function?
- Did I preserve the window for the living zone, provide the outlook from the primary seated position, and provide the operable window or the mechanical ventilation?
- Did I verify the minimum clearances (the kitchen counter frontage and depth, the bathroom turning, the bed access, the desk depth) are met?
- Did I verify no furniture transformation (the bed down, the table up) blocks another function or a door?
- Did I verify the window is not consumed by the closet or the bathroom?
