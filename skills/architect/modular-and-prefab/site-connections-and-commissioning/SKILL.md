# Site Connections And Commissioning

The site phase of a modular or prefab project is where the industrialized system meets the ground, the core, the enclosure, and the building systems, and where the design's quality is either realized or lost. The factory-built units arrive precise and complete, but they must connect to site-built foundations that are less precise, to cores and circulation that are built by a different crew, to enclosure systems that must seal the joints, and to mechanical, electrical, and plumbing systems that must cross the module boundaries. Agents often treat the site phase as straightforward assembly, but it is in fact the most failure-prone phase, because the interfaces, the tolerances, the sequence, and the commissioning all converge here. The architect's role is to detail the site connections, to coordinate the installation sequence, and to commission the building across the module joints, because the building's performance is defined by how well the units are integrated on site. The goal is a modular building whose site connections are as carefully designed as the factory units, whose installation sequence is coordinated, and whose systems are commissioned as a whole.

## Core Rules

### Detail The Foundation-To-Module Connection For Tolerance And Load

The connection between the site-built foundation and the factory-built modules is the first and most critical interface, because it must transfer the gravity, lateral, and uplift loads while accommodating the tolerance difference between the site work and the factory work. Detail the connection with a defined bearing area, a leveling method (shims, grout, leveling nuts), and a lateral connection that resists the seismic and wind loads. Specify the foundation tolerances — top-of-footing elevation, levelness, location — so that the module sits within the designed shim range, and inspect the foundation against these tolerances before the modules arrive. The architect must coordinate the foundation-to-module connection with the structural engineer and the manufacturer, because a foundation that is out of tolerance forces field corrections that compromise the connection.

### Coordinate The Installation Sequence And The Crane Plan

The installation sequence is critical-path in modular construction, because the crane, the delivery, and the crew must be synchronized, and the sequence determines which units can be installed and in what order. Coordinate the installation sequence with the crane plan — the crane position, the lift radius, the capacity at each lift, and the clearances to the building and the surroundings — and with the delivery sequence, so that the units arrive in the order they are installed. Consider the sequence's effect on the connections: a module installed last may close off access to the joint beside it, so the sequence must allow the connections to be made. The architect must review the installation sequence and the crane plan, because a sequence that traps connections or stalls the crane causes delays and compromises the joints.

### Design The Structural Connections Between Modules For Inspectability

The structural connections between modules — the gravity bearing, the lateral tie, the seismic connection — must be designed not only for the loads but for inspectability, because the code official and the special inspector must verify them. Detail the connections so that they can be accessed, built, and inspected from the available space, with the connection hardware, the welds, or the bolts visible or accessible through designed access panels. A connection that is enclosed behind finishes without access cannot be inspected and cannot be verified, which compromises the code compliance and the structural integrity. The architect must coordinate the connection details with the structural engineer and the inspector, because a connection that cannot be inspected is a connection that cannot be accepted.

### Seal The Enclosure At The Module Joints

The enclosure must be continuous across the module joints, and the joints are the most vulnerable points for moisture, air, and thermal leakage. Design the joint seal as a layered system — the water-shedding surface, the air barrier, the thermal break, and the water-resistant barrier — with each layer continuous across the joint and each layer detailed for the movement and the tolerance. Consider the access for sealing: a joint between two ceiling or floor levels must be sealable from below or above, and the access must be designed in. The architect must detail the joint seal, because the manufacturer seals within the module but the joint between modules is a design responsibility, and a leak at the joint causes moisture damage throughout the building.

### Coordinate The Service Crossings At The Module Boundaries

The mechanical, electrical, plumbing, and fire protection systems must cross the module boundaries, and these crossings must be coordinated so that the systems connect, the fire ratings are maintained, and the access is provided. Design the service crossings — the plumbing stub-outs, the ductwork connections, the electrical conduits, the sprinkler drops — at defined locations, with the mating halves aligned between modules, and with the fire-rated assemblies maintained through the penetrations. Coordinate the crossings with the fire, acoustic, and access requirements, because a service penetration that breaches a fire-rated assembly or an acoustic separation is a performance failure. The architect must coordinate the service crossings across the disciplines, because each discipline tends to design within the module and leave the crossing to the field.

### Commission The Building Systems Across The Module Joints

The building systems in a modular building span the module joints, and the commissioning must verify that the systems operate as a whole, not only within each module. Commission the HVAC, the electrical, the plumbing, and the fire protection across the joints, testing the airflow across the duct connections, the circuit continuity across the electrical splices, the pressure and the flow across the plumbing connections, and the alarm and the suppression across the fire systems. The commissioning must also verify the envelope — the air leakage at the joints, the thermal performance, the moisture — because the joints are the vulnerable points. The architect must coordinate the commissioning across the module joints, because the systems were built in pieces and must operate as a whole.

### Plan The Inspection And Acceptance For A Split-Built Building

A modular building is split between the factory and the site, and the inspection and acceptance must cover both: the factory-built units, inspected at the factory or through certification, and the site-built connections, inspected on site. Plan the inspection program — the factory inspections, the special inspections for the connections, the envelope testing, and the final acceptance — and coordinate the documentation so that the code official has the evidence for both. The architect must coordinate the split inspection, because a project that cannot demonstrate the inspection of both the factory and the site work cannot be permitted or occupied.

## Common Traps

### Treating The Site Phase As Simple Assembly

The team treats the site phase as straightforward assembly of factory-built units, and the interfaces, the tolerances, and the sequence are under-planned, so the installation encounters conflicts, delays, and compromised connections. The mechanism is that the factory work feels like the hard part and the site feels easy, and the false signal is that the units are complete. The harm is installation delays, connection failures, and quality loss at the interfaces. The site phase must be planned with the same rigor as the factory production, with the connections, the tolerances, the sequence, and the commissioning designed and coordinated.

### The Foundation Out Of Tolerance

The foundation is built to conventional tolerances, the modules arrive to factory tolerances, and the two do not match, so the modules must be shimmed beyond the designed range, field-cut, or re-engineered, compromising the connections. The mechanism is that the foundation is treated as conventional site work without the tighter tolerances that modular requires, and the false signal is that the foundation is within normal construction tolerance. The harm is modules that do not fit, connections that cannot be made, and schedule loss while the foundation is corrected. The foundation must be specified and built to the tolerances that the modular system requires, and inspected before the modules arrive.

### Enclosing The Connections Without Access

The structural connections between modules are enclosed behind finishes without access panels, so they cannot be inspected by the code official or the special inspector, and the building cannot be verified or accepted. The mechanism is that the connections are treated as structural details rather than inspection requirements, and the false signal is that the connections are built and so they are fine. The harm is non-compliance, the inability to obtain the occupancy permit, and un-verifiable structural integrity. The connections must be designed with access for inspection, with access panels or exposed locations that allow the inspector to verify the connection.

### Leaving The Joint Seal To The Field

The seal between modules — the air barrier, the water barrier, the thermal break — is left to the contractor to resolve in the field, and the result is inconsistent, incomplete seals that leak air and water. The mechanism is that the joint is treated as a construction detail rather than a design detail, and the false signal is that the manufacturer's unit is sealed. The harm is moisture intrusion, air leakage, energy loss, and long-term damage at the most vulnerable point in the building. The joint seal must be designed and documented, with each layer of the enclosure continuous across the joint and the access for installation provided.

### Commissioning The Modules But Not The Systems

The commissioning tests each module's systems in isolation but does not test the systems across the module joints, so the ductwork, the electrical, and the plumbing connections are not verified, and the systems fail in operation. The mechanism is that the commissioning follows the factory logic — testing each unit — rather than the building logic — testing the systems as a whole, and the false signal is that each module passed its tests. The harm is systems that do not operate across the joints, comfort and safety failures, and re-commissioning after occupancy. The commissioning must verify the systems across the module joints, testing the airflow, the circuits, and the flows as integrated systems.

### Assuming One Inspection Covers The Factory And The Site

The team assumes that the site's code official will inspect the whole building, including the factory-built units, and discovers that the factory is outside the jurisdiction and the units need a separate certification. The mechanism is that the split-built nature of modular is not accounted for in the inspection plan, and the false signal is that a building permit covers the whole building. The harm is approval delays, re-inspection, and in the worst case, units that cannot be legally accepted. The inspection must be planned for both the factory and the site, with the certifications coordinated and the documentation complete for both.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is the foundation-to-module connection detailed for the loads and the tolerances, with the foundation specified and inspected to the tolerances the modular system requires?
- [ ] Is the installation sequence coordinated with the crane plan and the delivery sequence, so that the units are installed in order and the connections remain accessible?
- [ ] Are the structural connections between modules designed for inspectability, with access for the code official and the special inspector to verify each connection?
- [ ] Is the enclosure continuous across the module joints, with the air barrier, water barrier, and thermal break detailed as a layered system and accessible for installation?
- [ ] Are the service crossings at the module boundaries coordinated across the disciplines, with the mating halves aligned and the fire and acoustic ratings maintained through the penetrations?
- [ ] Does the commissioning verify the building systems across the module joints, testing the airflow, circuits, flows, and alarms as integrated systems, not only within each module?
- [ ] Is the inspection and acceptance program planned for both the factory-built units and the site-built connections, with the certifications and documentation coordinated?
- [ ] Has the site phase been planned with the same rigor as the factory production, with the connections, tolerances, sequence, and commissioning designed and coordinated?
