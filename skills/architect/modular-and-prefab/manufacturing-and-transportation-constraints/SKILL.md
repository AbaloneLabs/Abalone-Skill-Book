# Manufacturing And Transportation Constraints

Designing for industrialized construction means designing twice: once for the factory that builds the units and once for the site that assembles them. The factory imposes its own constraints — module dimensions that fit the production line, panel sizes that two workers can handle, finishes that survive shipping, and a sequence that matches the line's rhythm — and the transportation and site impose another set — module dimensions that fit the road, weights that fit the truck and the crane, and a laydown area that fits the delivery sequence. Agents often design the building and then hand it to a manufacturer to "modularize," which produces units that fight the factory, the road, and the crane, and that arrive on site with damage, delays, and cost overruns. The architect's role is to design within the manufacturing and transportation constraints from the start, because a unit that cannot be built, shipped, or lifted efficiently loses every benefit of prefab. The goal is a design whose dimensions, weights, and details respect the factory, the road, and the site, and whose units arrive ready to install.

## Core Rules

### Design To The Module Dimensions That The Road And The Crane Allow

The governing dimensional constraints on a modular or panelized system are the transportation envelope and the crane capacity, and the design must be developed within these from the earliest massing. The transportation envelope — the maximum width, height, and length that can be shipped legally or by permit, given the route's bridges, curves, and clearances — defines the maximum module size. The crane capacity and reach, given the crane's position and the building's footprint, define the maximum module weight. Establish these envelopes early, with a transportation provider and a crane plan, and design the structural grid, the module sizes, and the panel dimensions within them. A design that exceeds the envelope must be split, which adds joints, cost, and vulnerability, so design within the envelope from the start.

### Coordinate The Factory Production Sequence With The Design

The factory produces units in a sequence, on a line or in stations, and the design must support an efficient sequence rather than forcing custom, one-off units that disrupt the line. Group the units by type, minimize the number of unique configurations, and design the variations so that they share the same frame, the same connections, and the same service routes. A factory that builds the same unit repeatedly is fast and high-quality; a factory that builds a different unit every time is slow and error-prone. The architect must understand the factory's production logic — its line, its stations, its jig, its cycle time — and design to it, because the production sequence is as much a design parameter as the structural grid.

### Detail Finishes And Connections To Survive Shipping And Lifting

Factory-built units are subjected to shipping vibration, lifting forces, and weather exposure in transit and during installation, and the finishes, the connections, and the structure must be detailed to survive these without damage. Gypsum board cracks at module joints if the structure flexes; tile and stone debond if the module twists during lifting; windows and doors rack if the opening is not braced. Detail the units with the shipping and lifting forces in mind — robust structures, braced openings, flexible joints, and finishes that tolerate movement — and specify the lifting points and the shipping protection. The architect must coordinate with the manufacturer on the lifting engineering, because a unit that arrives damaged must be repaired on site, which defeats the quality benefit of factory production.

### Resolve The Tolerance Strategy Between Factory And Site Work

Factory-built units are produced to tight tolerances, but the site-built foundations, cores, and connections are built to conventional construction tolerances, and the interface between them must absorb the difference. Define the tolerance strategy — the allowable variation in the factory units, the allowable variation in the site work, and the shim, adjust, and seal strategy at the interface — and detail the connections to accommodate the range. A modular unit placed on a foundation that is out of level must be shimmed, and the shim space must be designed and accessible. The architect must coordinate the tolerance strategy between the factory and the site, because a mismatch produces units that do not fit, connections that cannot be made, and joints that cannot be sealed.

### Plan The Site Logistics: Laydown, Sequence, And Crane

The site logistics for industrialized construction are different from conventional construction, because the units arrive just-in-time or require a laydown area, and the crane is critical-path. Plan the site logistics early — the delivery sequence that matches the installation sequence, the laydown area for units that cannot be installed immediately, the crane position and capacity, and the road access for the delivery trucks. A site that cannot lay down units requires just-in-time delivery, which demands a precise schedule and a reliable supply chain; a site that can lay down units offers flexibility but requires space. The architect must coordinate the site logistics with the contractor and the manufacturer, because a design that cannot be erected efficiently loses the schedule benefit of prefab.

### Design The Joints For Performance And For Access

The joints between factory-built units are the most critical details in an industrialized building, because they carry the structural loads, the fire ratings, the acoustic isolation, the weather seal, and the service crossings. Design each joint for its full performance requirements — the structural connection, the fire-rated assembly, the acoustic break, the weather barrier, and the service penetration — and detail it so that it can be built and inspected from the available access. A joint between two modules that is enclosed by the ceiling and the floor must be buildable from below or above, and the access must be designed in. The architect must own the joint details, because the manufacturer and the contractor each tend to design only their side.

### Specify The Protection And The Acceptance Criteria For Delivered Units

Factory-built units must be protected in transit and on site, and the acceptance criteria — what constitutes a deliverable unit, what damage is repairable, and what requires rejection — must be specified before production begins. Specify the shipping protection, the site protection, and the inspection and acceptance process, with the criteria for accepting, repairing, or rejecting a unit. A unit that arrives with damage must be assessed against the criteria, and the repair must be specified, because ad-hoc field repairs compromise the quality. The architect must define the acceptance criteria, because without them the project descends into disputes over what is acceptable.

## Common Traps

### Designing First And Modularizing Later

The team designs the building conventionally and then asks a manufacturer to modularize it, and the result is units that do not fit the factory, the road, or the crane, with custom modules, split units, and joints that defeat the prefab logic. The mechanism is that prefab is treated as a construction method rather than a design discipline, and the false signal is that any design can be modularized. The harm is the loss of speed, cost, and quality benefits, and a building that pays the constraints of prefab without its advantages. The design must be developed within the manufacturing and transportation constraints from the start, with the manufacturer engaged early.

### Exceeding The Transportation Envelope

The module dimensions exceed the legal or practical shipping limits, and the units must be split, shipped by permit at higher cost, or routed around obstacles, erasing the schedule and cost benefits. The mechanism is that the transportation envelope is not checked during design, and the false signal is that the dimensions are within standard limits. The harm is redesign, permit delays, and cost overruns late in the project. The transportation envelope must be established early with a logistics provider, verified against the route, and enforced as a design constraint.

### Underestimating The Lifting Forces

The modules are designed without engineering the lifting forces, and the units rack, crack, or deform during lifting, arriving on site with damage that must be repaired. The mechanism is that lifting is treated as a rigging problem rather than a structural and detailing problem, and the false signal is that the units are robust enough to lift. The harm is damaged units, field repairs, schedule loss, and in the worst case, structural compromise. The lifting forces must be engineered, the lifting points specified, and the structure and finishes detailed to survive the lift.

### Ignoring The Tolerance Stack-Up

The factory units are produced to tight tolerances, but the site-built work is not held to the same tolerances, and the units do not fit the site conditions, requiring field cuts, shims, and adjustments that compromise the connections. The mechanism is that the factory and the site are treated as the same tolerance environment, and the false signal is that tight factory tolerances solve the fit. The harm is units that do not fit, connections that cannot be made, and joints that cannot be sealed. The tolerance strategy must be defined across the factory and the site, with the interface designed to absorb the variation.

### Leaving The Joints Undesigned

The joints between units are left to the manufacturer and the contractor to resolve in the field, and the result is inconsistent, uncoordinated joints that fail the structural, fire, moisture, and acoustic requirements. The mechanism is that the joints are treated as construction details rather than design details, and the false signal is that the systems will meet at the joint. The harm is performance failures at the most vulnerable detail in the building. The joints must be designed and documented by the design team, with the structural, fire, acoustic, weather, and service requirements resolved.

### Assuming Just-In-Time Delivery Without A Reliable Supply Chain

The site has no laydown area, so the plan relies on just-in-time delivery, but the supply chain is not reliable, and the units arrive out of sequence or late, stalling the crane and the installation. The mechanism is that just-in-time is assumed to work without verifying the supply chain, and the false signal is that the schedule is planned. The harm is idle cranes, delayed installation, and schedule loss. The delivery strategy must match the site's laydown capacity and the supply chain's reliability, with a sequence that the factory and the trucking can sustain.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Are the module and panel dimensions developed within the transportation envelope and the crane capacity, verified with a logistics provider and a crane plan?
- [ ] Does the design support an efficient factory production sequence, with units grouped by type and variations sharing the same frame, connections, and service routes?
- [ ] Are the finishes, connections, and structure detailed to survive the shipping and lifting forces, with lifting points specified and shipping protection required?
- [ ] Is the tolerance strategy defined between the factory and the site, with the interface detailed to absorb the variation through shims, adjusts, and seals?
- [ ] Is the site logistics plan developed, with the delivery sequence, the laydown area, the crane position, and the road access coordinated?
- [ ] Are the joints designed for their full performance — structural, fire, acoustic, weather, and service — and detailed for buildability and inspection from the available access?
- [ ] Are the shipping protection, site protection, and acceptance criteria specified, with the process for accepting, repairing, or rejecting a delivered unit defined?
- [ ] Has the design been developed within the manufacturing and transportation constraints from the start, rather than modularized after conventional design?
