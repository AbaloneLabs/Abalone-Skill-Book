# DISA And Volumetric System Selection

Industrialized construction asks the architect to commit to a manufacturing logic far earlier than conventional construction, because the system choice — volumetric modules, panelized assemblies, or discrete components — determines the structural strategy, the degree of factory finish, the transportation envelope, the connection details, and the amount of design freedom that remains. DISA (Design for Industrialized Structural Assembly) is the discipline of designing structure, enclosure, and service distribution so that they align with repeatable, factory-friendly assemblies. Agents often choose a system based on speed or aesthetics without reconciling it with the site constraints, the structural intent, or the regulatory path, and the result is a hybrid that loses the benefits of industrialization while inheriting all of its constraints. The architect's role is to lead system selection as a design decision, not a procurement decision, because the system defines the architecture. The goal is a system whose degree of prefabrication matches the project's logistics, whose repetition is expressed rather than hidden, and whose connections and tolerances are resolved before manufacturing begins.

## Core Rules

### Select The System Against The Project's Real Constraints

System selection must begin from the project's real constraints, not from a preference for a technology. Volumetric modules maximize factory finish and speed but are constrained by transportation dimensions and weights, and they leave little freedom for irregular geometry. Panelized systems preserve more design freedom and are easier to transport but require more site work and weather-dependent assembly. Component-based prefab (mass timber, precast, cold-formed steel) offers the most design flexibility but the least factory integration. Evaluate the site access, the transportation route, the crane capacity, the structural grid, the program repetition, the regulatory path, and the budget, and choose the system whose logic fits. A system chosen against the constraints becomes a liability; a system chosen with the constraints becomes an advantage.

### Engage The Manufacturer And The Logistics Chain Early

Manufacturers and transportation providers must be engaged at concept design, not at construction documents, because their capabilities, their dimensional standards, and their lead times define what is buildable. A manufacturer's module width, a trucking route's bridge clearances, a port's lift capacity, or a crane's reach can each invalidate a design that was developed without them. Bring at least one manufacturer and one logistics provider into the early design conversation, and document their constraints as design parameters. The architect must lead this engagement, because the manufacturer will optimize for their own production, and without the architect's coordination the design intent is eroded by production convenience. Treat the manufacturer as a design partner with a written scope, not a vendor responding to a bid.

### Define The Structural Strategy And The Load Path Through The Connections

Industrialized systems live or die by their connections, because the modules or panels are only as good as the joints between them. Define the structural strategy — whether modules stack and bear, whether panels are load-bearing, whether a separate frame carries the loads — and resolve the load path through the connections before the design is frozen. Consider gravity, lateral, and uplift paths, and the seismic or wind demands on each joint. The connections must also accommodate the tolerance stack-up between factory-built units, because the cumulative dimensional variation across a building must be absorbed without forcing field cuts. The architect must coordinate with the structural engineer to make the connections buildable, inspectable, and code-compliant, because a connection that cannot be inspected is a connection that cannot be verified.

### Reconcile The Degree Of Prefabrication With The Enclosure And Services

The degree of prefabrication determines how much of the enclosure and the building services are installed in the factory versus on site, and this must be reconciled with the climate, the weathertightness strategy, and the service distribution. A volumetric module with factory-installed finishes and services maximizes speed but creates vulnerable joints between modules that must be sealed on site, often in difficult access. A panelized enclosure with site-installed services preserves access but loses factory integration. Decide the split deliberately, and document the interface details where factory work meets site work, because these interfaces are where moisture, fire, and acoustic failures occur. The architect must own the interface details, because the manufacturer and the contractor each tend to disclaim them.

### Plan The Regulatory Path For An Industrialized System

Industrialized systems challenge conventional code enforcement, because the building is partly built in a factory that is outside the jurisdiction of the site's code official. Plan the regulatory path early, including the approval of the factory's modular units (often through a state modular program, a third-party inspection, or a precast plant certification), the coordination between the factory approval and the site permit, and the inspection of the site-built connections. Identify whether the jurisdiction accepts the factory certifications, whether a special inspector is required for the connections, and whether the fire rating of an assembly differs when built in a factory. The architect must coordinate the dual approval path, because a project stalled between the factory approval and the site permit is a common failure.

### Design For Repetition Without Sacrificing Human Variety

Industrialized construction rewards repetition, because the factory's efficiency comes from producing the same unit many times. But repetition that is imposed without thought produces monotonous, institutional architecture. Design for repetition at the level of the unit, the connection, and the service core, and introduce variety through the arrangement, the facade treatment, the common spaces, and the relationship to the site. The repetition should be the structural and production logic, expressed honestly, not a constraint that is hidden behind applied variety. The architect must use the repetition as a design generator, because the best industrialized architecture celebrates the system rather than disguising it.

### Document The Tolerance And Interface Strategy In The Contract Documents

The contract documents must define the tolerance strategy, the interface details, and the responsibilities for the connection between systems, because industrialized construction fails at the interfaces. Document the dimensional tolerances of the factory-built units, the tolerance stack-up across the building, the shim and adjust strategy at the connections, and the sealant and firestop strategy at the joints. Define who is responsible for each interface — the manufacturer, the site contractor, or a separate scope — because the gaps between scopes are where the failures occur. The architect must specify the interfaces as carefully as the systems, because a modular building is a system of systems whose joints define its performance.

## Common Traps

### Choosing Volumetric For Design Freedom

The team selects a volumetric system for its speed and factory finish, then develops an irregular, site-specific geometry that the modules cannot accommodate, and the result is a compromised design that loses the speed and the quality of industrialization while paying its constraints. The mechanism is that volumetric systems are marketed on their benefits without their constraints, and the false signal is that any design can be modularized. The harm is rework, custom modules that defeat the factory logic, and a building that is neither fast nor well-detailed. Volumetric systems must be selected for projects whose geometry, program, and repetition fit the module logic, and the design must be developed within that logic from the start.

### Ignoring The Transportation Envelope

The design is developed without checking the transportation route, and the modules or panels exceed a bridge clearance, a turning radius, or a weight limit, so the units must be split, re-engineered, or shipped by a longer route that erases the schedule benefit. The mechanism is that transportation is treated as a contractor's logistics problem rather than a design parameter, and the false signal is that the units are within standard dimensions. The harm is redesign, cost increases, and schedule loss late in the project. The transportation envelope — route, clearances, weights, and permits — must be a design constraint documented early and verified with a logistics provider.

### Leaving The Connections To The Contractor

The connections between modules or panels are left to the contractor to resolve in the field, and the result is inconsistent, un-inspectable joints that compromise the structural, fire, and moisture performance of the building. The mechanism is that connections are treated as construction details rather than design decisions, and the false signal is that the manufacturer or the contractor will engineer them. The harm is structural risk, code non-compliance, and moisture and fire failures at the joints. The connections must be designed, detailed, and documented by the design team, with the structural engineer, and inspected as part of the construction.

### Assuming The Factory And The Site Are The Same Jurisdiction

The team assumes that the site's code official will inspect the factory-built units, and discovers late that the factory is in a different state, under a different program, and that the site official will not accept the factory's work without a separate certification. The mechanism is that industrialized construction splits the building across jurisdictions, and the false signal is that a building permit covers the whole building. The harm is approval delays, re-inspection, and in the worst case, units that cannot be legally installed. The regulatory path must be planned early, with the factory approval and the site permit coordinated and the acceptance of certifications confirmed.

### Pursuing Repetition To The Point Of Institutional Monotony

The design maximizes repetition to optimize the factory production, and the result is a monotonous, institutional building that meets the production logic but fails the human and contextual goals. The mechanism is that repetition is optimized as a production metric without a design counterweight, and the false signal is that efficiency equals quality. The harm is a building that is efficient to produce but unpleasant to inhabit, and that undermines the case for industrialized construction. Repetition must be balanced with variety through arrangement, facade, and common spaces, so that the production logic serves the architecture rather than dominating it.

### Forgetting The Services Distribution In The Module Joints

The building services — plumbing, ductwork, electrical, fire sprinkler — must cross the joints between modules, and the design does not provide for these crossings, so the services are field-routed in ways that compromise the fire ratings, the acoustics, and the access. The mechanism is that services are coordinated within the modules but not across them, and the false signal is that each module is complete. The harm is fire-rated assemblies breached by uncoordinated service penetrations, acoustic failures, and maintenance access problems. The service distribution across the module joints must be designed and documented, with the penetrations coordinated with the fire, acoustic, and access requirements.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has the system — volumetric, panelized, or component — been selected against the project's real constraints of site access, transportation, structural grid, program repetition, and budget?
- [ ] Has at least one manufacturer and one logistics provider been engaged at concept design, with their constraints documented as design parameters?
- [ ] Is the structural strategy defined, with the gravity, lateral, and uplift load paths resolved through the connections and the tolerance stack-up accommodated?
- [ ] Is the degree of prefabrication reconciled with the enclosure and service strategy, with the factory-to-site interfaces detailed for moisture, fire, and acoustic performance?
- [ ] Is the regulatory path planned, with the factory approval and the site permit coordinated and the acceptance of certifications confirmed?
- [ ] Does the design use repetition as a structural and production logic while introducing variety through arrangement, facade, and common spaces?
- [ ] Are the tolerances, interfaces, and responsibilities documented in the contract documents, with each connection specified and assigned to a scope?
- [ ] Are the service crossings at the module joints designed and coordinated with the fire, acoustic, and access requirements, rather than left to field routing?
