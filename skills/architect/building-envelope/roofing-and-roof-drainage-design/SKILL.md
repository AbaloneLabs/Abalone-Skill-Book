# Roofing And Roof Drainage Design

The roof is the building's primary shield against the most concentrated form of water: rainfall directed by gravity and driven by wind. Roof failures are among the most common and costly building defects, and they almost always trace to a small set of causes: inadequate drainage that ponds water, flashing failures at penetrations and terminations, membrane incompatibility or poor installation, and wind uplift from inadequate attachment. Roofing design is not selecting a membrane and moving on; it is designing a complete assembly, substrate, insulation, vapor and air control, membrane, attachment, drainage, and flashing, each chosen for the specific conditions and detailed to perform together. Agents often treat roofing as a specification selection and miss that drainage design, flashing detail, and wind uplift resistance are where roofs actually succeed or fail.

Use this skill when designing roofing, planning roof drainage, selecting roof systems, or detailing roof flashing and penetrations. The goal is to select an assembly appropriate to slope, exposure, and use, design positive drainage with adequate redundancy, detail the vulnerable transitions and penetrations, and ensure the assembly resists wind, thermal, and moisture loads for its intended service life.

## Core Rules

### Match The Roof System To The Slope And Use

Roof systems divide fundamentally into low-slope, often called flat, and steep-slope assemblies, and each demands different membranes, substrates, and drainage strategies. Applying a low-slope membrane to a steep roof, or a steep-slope material to a low-slope condition, produces failures. Within low-slope roofing, the membrane types, built-up, modified bitumen, single-ply EPDM, TPO, PVC, and fluid-applied, each have distinct strengths, compatibilities, and climate suitabilities. The selection must consider slope, traffic, exposure, insulation strategy, and the building's life expectancy.

Match by:

- confirming the actual structural slope and designing the roofing system appropriate to it, recognizing that low-slope membranes require positive slope to drain, typically a minimum of 1/4 inch per foot;
- selecting membrane type based on climate, exposure, reflectivity goals, traffic, and compatibility with adjacent materials;
- considering the roof's use, whether it is occupied, vegetated, photovoltaic-equipped, or a terrace, and selecting a system that supports that use;
- avoiding face-sealed or membrane systems on slopes below their minimum, where ponding will occur.

### Design Positive Drainage With Redundancy

Standing water on a roof is the precursor to nearly every low-slope roof failure. Ponding degrades membranes, adds load, freezes and thaws, and finds every imperfection. Drainage design must establish positive slope to drains, provide enough drains to handle the design rainfall, and include secondary, overflow drainage that activates if the primary system is blocked. The absence or inadequacy of overflow drainage is a recurring cause of roof collapse under rain or snow load.

Design drainage by:

- establishing and maintaining positive slope to every drain throughout the roof area, accounting for structural deflection and insulation tapering;
- sizing and locating primary drains for the design rainfall intensity and roof area, coordinating with the plumbing engineer for pipe sizing;
- providing secondary overflow drains or scuppers at a higher elevation, sized to carry the flow if the primary drains are obstructed, as required by code;
- locating scuppers and drains at low points and ensuring the structural design supports the drainage pattern;
- considering parapet walls and adjacent taller construction that may shed water onto the roof, increasing the drainage load.

### Detail Flashing At Every Penetration And Termination

Flashing is where roofs leak. The field of the roof membrane is rarely the failure point; the failures occur at drains, penetrations, parapet caps, expansion joints, and where the roof meets walls and curbs. Each of these conditions requires specific, scaled flashing details that integrate the membrane with the adjacent construction. Generic or omitted flashing details leave the contractor to improvise, and improvised flashing fails.

Detail by:

- providing scaled details at every roof drain, showing the clamping ring, sump pan, and membrane integration;
- detailing every penetration, plumbing vents, HVAC curbs, duct supports, lightning protection, with appropriate flashing boots, pitch pockets, or curb flashing;
- detailing base flashing at parapets and walls, with counter-flashing and a capped, sloped parapet top to prevent water entry at the top of the wall;
- detailing expansion joints and roof-to-wall transitions with continuous, flexible, and lapped flashing;
- avoiding pitch pockets where possible, as they are maintenance-dependent failure points, preferring flashed penetrations.

### Ensure Wind Uplift Resistance Through Proper Attachment

Wind creates uplift forces on roofs that can peel membranes from the substrate, particularly at corners, edges, and perimeters where pressures concentrate. Roof assembly attachment must be designed for the specific building's wind exposure, height, and geographic wind zone. Under-designed attachment leads to membrane blow-off in storms, a catastrophic and dangerous failure. Wind uplift design is an engineering calculation, not a default specification.

Ensure resistance by:

- determining the design wind uplift pressures for the specific building, considering wind speed, exposure category, building height, and roof zone, field, perimeter, and corner;
- specifying the membrane attachment method, fully adhered, mechanically fastened, or ballasted, and the fastener pattern or adhesive to meet the calculated uplift loads;
- increasing attachment at roof perimeters and corners where uplift forces are highest, per the calculated zone pressures;
- coordinating with the structural engineer that the roof deck can accept the attachment loads and that the deck type is compatible with the specified system;
- specifying and enforcing the manufacturer's approved assembly and requiring a wind uplift warranty where appropriate.

### Coordinate Insulation, Vapor, And Air Control Within The Assembly

A roof assembly is a layered system in which the insulation, vapor retarder, air barrier, and membrane must be sequenced correctly for the climate. Insulation placed above or below the deck changes the dew point location and the condensation risk. A vapor retarder in the wrong place traps moisture. The air barrier must be continuous from wall to roof. These layers must be designed as a system, not selected independently.

Coordinate by:

- locating insulation to keep the dew point outside of condensation-prone layers, typically using above-deck insulation in most climates;
- determining whether a vapor retarder is needed and where, based on climate and interior humidity, recognizing that cold-climate roofs with high interior humidity are particularly at risk;
- ensuring the roof air barrier connects continuously to the wall air barrier at the parapet or roof-to-wall transition;
- considering a vapor venting layer or two-stage assembly where drying is needed;
- coordinating with the mechanical engineer on roof-top equipment and the structural engineer on deck type and load.

### Plan For Roof Access, Maintenance, And Life Safety

Roofs require maintenance: drains must be cleared, membranes inspected, flashings maintained, and equipment serviced. A roof that cannot be safely accessed will not be maintained, and unmaintained roofs fail prematurely. Roof access also intersects life safety: fall protection, guardrails, and safe paths to equipment are obligations, not optional amenities.

Plan by:

- providing permanent, code-compliant access to the roof, such as stairs or an access hatch with a ladder designed for the use;
- designing fall protection or guardrails where roof access is required near edges, compliant with applicable safety standards;
- providing walkway pads or protection for the membrane where traffic is expected to service equipment;
- coordinating safe paths to roof-top equipment for mechanical service personnel;
- considering the roof's life-safety role, such as occupant refuge or fire department access, where applicable.

## Common Traps

### Ponding Water From Inadequate Slope Or Drainage

A low-slope roof designed without sufficient positive slope to drains, or one where structural deflection creates low spots, accumulates standing water after rain. The trap is that the roof appears flat and the drains are present, so drainage seems adequate on the drawings, and minor ponding may go unnoticed in dry weather. The harm is that ponding water degrades the membrane through prolonged saturation, ultraviolet concentration, and freeze-thaw cycling, adds dead load that worsens the deflection in a vicious cycle, and finds every seam and imperfection to leak through. The false signal is that the roof holds water only briefly after rain, when in fact persistent ponding indicates a design or construction defect. Designing and verifying positive slope, tapering insulation, and providing adequate drains with overflow redundancy is what prevents the progressive failure that ponding initiates.

### The Missing Or Undersized Overflow Drainage

Primary roof drains can become blocked by debris, ice, or membrane failure, and without secondary overflow drainage, water rises on the roof until it finds a way in or until the structural load causes collapse. The trap is that overflow scuppers and drains are often omitted or undersized because they seem redundant, and they are rarely tested so their absence is invisible. The harm is catastrophic: roof collapses under accumulated rain or snow load have killed people and destroyed buildings, and the absence of overflow drainage is a direct code and life-safety failure. The false signal is that the primary drains have always worked, so overflow seems unnecessary, right up until the moment they are needed and absent. Providing correctly sized and located overflow drainage at the code-required height is a non-negotiable life-safety provision, not an optional redundancy.

### Flashing Failures At Penetrations And Terminations

The roof membrane in the field is reliable; the leaks occur at drains, pipe penetrations, HVAC curbs, parapet caps, and expansion joints, where the membrane must transition to other materials and accommodate movement. The trap is that these conditions are often left to generic notes or field improvisation because they are numerous and varied, so they receive less design attention than the field membrane. The harm is that each un-detailed or poorly flashed penetration is a near-certain future leak, and the leaks are hidden above ceilings until damage is extensive. The false signal is that the membrane is specified and the penetrations will be "flashed per detail," a note that conceals the absence of actual detail. Providing scaled, specific flashing details at every penetration and termination, and requiring conformance, is what makes the roof reliable at its most vulnerable points.

### Wind Uplift Blow-Off From Under-Designed Attachment

A membrane specified without a wind uplift calculation for the specific building's exposure, height, and wind zone may be inadequately attached, particularly at perimeters and corners where uplift forces concentrate. The trap is that the membrane and attachment are specified to a manufacturer's standard, which appears authoritative, without verifying that the standard meets the building's actual loads. The harm is that during a windstorm the membrane peels from the substrate, exposing the building to water intrusion, damaging property, and creating flying debris hazards. The false signal is that the system carries a warranty, which may not cover the actual failure mode or may be voided by installation defects. Performing the wind uplift calculation for the specific building and specifying the attachment to meet it, with enhanced attachment at perimeters and corners, is what prevents catastrophic storm failure.

### The Incompatible Assembly Layers

Selecting insulation, vapor retarder, adhesive, and membrane independently can produce chemically or thermally incompatible layers: an adhesive that solvents the membrane, a polystyrene insulation dissolved by a solvent-based adhesive, or a vapor retarder that traps moisture in an assembly that needs to dry. The trap is that each product is reputable and specified by category, so the assembly appears sound component by component. The harm is that incompatibility manifests as membrane blistering, adhesive failure, insulation degradation, or concealed condensation, failures that are expensive to diagnose and require full roof replacement. The false signal is that each layer meets its individual specification. Specifying a complete, manufacturer-validated assembly, and confirming compatibility across all layers for the specific climate, is what prevents the systemic failures that component-by-component selection invites.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is the roof system matched to the actual structural slope and intended use, with low-slope membranes provided positive slope to drain?
- [ ] Is positive drainage designed with adequately sized and located primary drains, plus correctly sized secondary overflow drainage at the code-required height?
- [ ] Are scaled flashing details provided at every drain, penetration, parapet, curb, and expansion joint, rather than left to generic notes or field improvisation?
- [ ] Has wind uplift resistance been calculated for the specific building and zone, with attachment, enhanced at perimeters and corners, specified to meet the loads?
- [ ] Are insulation, vapor control, air barrier, and membrane coordinated as a compatible, climate-appropriate assembly with the dew point managed?
- [ ] Is safe, code-compliant roof access and fall protection provided, with protected paths for maintenance of drains and equipment?
