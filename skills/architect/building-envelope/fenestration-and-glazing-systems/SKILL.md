# Fenestration And Glazing Systems

Fenestration, windows, curtain walls, storefronts, and skylights, is where the building envelope is most complex and most prone to failure. Glazing systems must simultaneously manage water, air, thermal transfer, solar gain, condensation, acoustic transmission, structural wind load, and often fire rating, all while delivering the architectural vision of light, view, and transparency. They are assembled from many components, frames, glass, seals, gaskets, and setting blocks, each of which must be compatible and correctly installed. Fenestration failures, leaks, fogging, condensation, breakage, and operational problems, are among the most common building defects and the most litigated. Agents often select fenestration by appearance and nominal size and miss that performance criteria, installation detailing, and system type determine whether the glazing performs or fails.

Use this skill when selecting fenestration, designing curtain walls or storefronts, specifying glazing, or detailing window installation. The goal is to select the system type appropriate to the application, specify measurable performance criteria, detail the integration with the wall envelope, and coordinate structural and life-safety requirements.

## Core Rules

### Match The System Type To The Application

Fenestration systems are not interchangeable. Windows, typically punched or ribbon, are factory-assembled units set into wall openings. Storefront systems are field-assembled, typically single-span, ground-floor glazing with moderate performance. Curtain walls are field-assembled, multi-span, high-performance exterior wall systems that span floor to floor or the full building height. Window walls are similar but sit within slab-to-slab openings. Each has distinct structural, water-management, and thermal capabilities, and applying the wrong type produces failures. A curtain wall used where a storefront suffices wastes cost; a storefront used where a curtain wall is required leaks and fails structurally.

Match by:

- selecting curtain wall for multi-span, high-performance, large-scale applications requiring engineered water management and structural performance;
- selecting storefront for single-span, ground-floor applications with moderate exposure and performance needs;
- selecting windows for punched openings where factory-assembled units suit the wall type;
- recognizing that storefront systems have height and span limits and drainage capabilities below curtain wall, and should not be pushed beyond them;
- documenting the system type and its performance basis so procurement matches the design intent.

### Specify Measurable Performance Criteria

Fenestration performance must be specified by measurable criteria, not by manufacturer or model alone. The critical criteria include air infiltration, water penetration resistance, structural performance under wind load, thermal performance, U-factor and solar heat gain coefficient, condensation resistance, and acoustic performance where relevant. Specifying only a product name leaves performance undefined and allows substitution to inferior products. Specifying measurable criteria allows verification and prevents silent quality erosion.

Specify by:

- setting air infiltration limits per recognized standards, such as ASTM E283, appropriate to the building's use and climate;
- setting water penetration resistance per ASTM E331 at a pressure appropriate to the exposure, recognizing this is the most common failure mode;
- setting structural performance per ASTM E330 for the design wind load, with deflection limits appropriate to the glazing type;
- specifying thermal performance, U-factor and SHGC, to meet energy code and comfort requirements, and condensation resistance for humid or cold-climate interiors;
- requiring third-party certification or testing where performance is critical.

### Detail The Integration With The Wall Envelope

A window or curtain wall that is well-designed as a product can still leak if its integration with the surrounding wall is poorly detailed. The interface between the fenestration frame and the wall's water, air, and thermal control layers is where most leaks originate. The architect must detail how the wall's waterproofing and air barrier connect to the window frame, how the sill is flashed and drained, and how thermal continuity is maintained across the connection.

Detail by:

- detailing the head, jamb, and sill of every window to integrate the wall's water control layer with the frame, using flashing that laps over the frame flange or into a receptor;
- providing sill pan flashing under every window to capture and drain any water reaching the rough opening, the single most important detail for leak prevention;
- connecting the wall air barrier continuously to the window frame, sealing the gap between rough opening and frame;
- maintaining thermal continuity by insulating the gap between frame and rough opening and avoiding thermal bridges at the perimeter;
- coordinating the curtain wall or storefront perimeter with the structural support and the wall's control layers.

### Manage Condensation Through Thermal Performance

Condensation on glazing or frames occurs when interior moisture contacts a cold surface. It damages finishes, breeds mold, and pools on sills. Condensation risk increases with high interior humidity, cold exterior temperatures, and thermally inferior frames or glass. Thermal break design in aluminum frames is critical: a non-thermally-broken aluminum frame conducts cold directly to the interior and condenses readily. Condensation is a performance failure that is often overlooked until occupants complain.

Manage by:

- specifying thermally broken frames in all but the mildest climates, recognizing that aluminum conducts heat aggressively without a break;
- selecting glazing with appropriate thermal performance, often insulating glass with a low-e coating and argon fill, to keep the interior glass surface warm;
- evaluating condensation resistance using a metric such as the Condensation Resistance Factor, especially for humid interiors like swimming pools or cold climates;
- detailing to deliver conditioned air across the glazing or to isolate the glazing from humid interior air where condensation is a risk;
- coordinating with the mechanical engineer on interior humidity and air distribution at the perimeter.

### Coordinate Structural Support And Wind Load

Curtain walls and storefronts are structural systems that must resist wind load and transfer it to the building structure. They require engineered anchorage to the floor slabs or structure, designed for the specific wind loads and deflection criteria. Under-designed anchorage or uncoordinated structural support leads to excessive deflection, glass breakage, and water infiltration from frame distortion. The structural design of the glazing system is the responsibility of the system's engineer working with the project structural engineer, and the architect must coordinate the interface.

Coordinate by:

- providing the design wind loads and deflection criteria to the glazing system supplier for their engineering;
- coordinating the location and capacity of anchor points with the structural engineer, ensuring the structure can accept the loads;
- setting deflection limits appropriate to the glazing type, recognizing that flexible frames stress seals and glass;
- requiring shop drawings and engineering calculations for the curtain wall or storefront, and reviewing them for conformance;
- coordinating slab edge conditions and structural tolerances that affect anchorage.

### Address Safety, Fire, And Accessibility Requirements

Fenestration intersects several code requirements that are easy to overlook. Safety glazing is required in hazardous locations, such as doors and near walking surfaces. Fire-rated glazing assemblies are required where the opening is in a fire-rated wall. Fall prevention is required where large glazing is near the floor. Egress through windows may be required in sleeping rooms. Each of these is a life-safety provision that must be specified, not assumed.

Address by:

- specifying safety glazing, typically tempered or laminated, in all code-required hazardous locations;
- specifying fire-rated glazing assemblies, with the correct rating and hose-stream performance, where openings occur in rated walls, and coordinating with the fire protection design;
- providing fall prevention, through glazing design or guards, where large panes are near the floor per code;
- ensuring egress windows in sleeping units meet the required clear opening dimensions, sill height, and operability;
- coordinating these requirements into the specifications rather than leaving them to the contractor's interpretation.

## Common Traps

### Storefront Used Beyond Its Capabilities

A storefront system is selected for a tall, exposed, or multi-floor application because it is less expensive than a curtain wall, and the design proceeds assuming comparable performance. The trap is that storefront systems have lower water penetration resistance, limited height capability, and inferior drainage compared to curtain wall, but they look similar on drawings and in early mockups. The harm is that the system leaks under wind-driven rain, deflects excessively under wind load stressing the seals, and cannot manage the water that penetrates, leading to widespread interior damage and the eventual need for replacement. The false signal is that the storefront passed initial performance or appears to function in mild weather. Selecting curtain wall for demanding applications and respecting storefront's performance and height limits is what prevents using a system beyond its engineered capabilities.

### Performance Specified By Name, Not Criteria

Specifying fenestration by manufacturer and model, or by a generic category, without measurable performance criteria, leaves the actual performance undefined and open to substitution. The trap is that a named product feels specific and protective, so the architect assumes performance is fixed. The harm is that manufacturers change products, substitutions are offered that meet the name but not the performance, and the installed fenestration may have inferior air, water, thermal, or structural performance than intended, with the deficiency discovered only when the building leaks or underperforms. The false signal is that the specification names a product, masking the absence of performance requirements. Specifying measurable criteria, air infiltration, water penetration, structural, thermal, and condensation, is what allows verification and prevents silent degradation through substitution.

### The Unflashed Rough Opening

A window is installed into a rough opening without sill pan flashing or proper integration of the wall's waterproofing with the frame, relying on the window's own seals and a bead of sealant to keep water out. The trap is that the window as a product tested fine, so the installation seems secondary, and sealant around the perimeter appears sufficient. The harm is that water inevitably penetrates behind the frame flange through capillary action, sealant failure, or wind pressure, and with no sill pan to capture and drain it, the water saturates the rough opening, the wall, and the interior finishes, producing hidden rot and mold. The false signal is that the window is installed and sealed, concealing the absence of the redundant drainage that actually protects the opening. Detailing and requiring sill pan flashing and integrated rough opening waterproofing is what prevents the most common window leak failure.

### Condensation From Non-Thermally-Broken Frames

Aluminum frames without a thermal break conduct exterior cold directly to the interior surface, causing condensation whenever interior humidity is moderate and exterior temperatures are low. The trap is that non-thermally-broken frames are less expensive and may be specified by default or for appearance, and they perform acceptably in mild conditions or dry interiors. The harm is that in cold climates or humid interiors, water runs off the frames continuously, damaging finishes, breeding mold at the sill and behind the wall, and creating occupant complaints that are difficult to resolve without frame replacement. The false signal is that the frames are aluminum and appear durable, masking the thermal deficiency. Specifying thermally broken frames and evaluating condensation resistance for the climate and interior humidity is what prevents the chronic moisture problems that non-broken frames cause.

### The Uncoordinated Fire-Rated Opening

A glazing opening occurs in a fire-rated wall, and standard glazing or an inappropriate fire-rated product is specified without coordinating the required rating, the hose-stream test, and the assembly's limitations. The trap is that fire-rated glazing is a specialized field with strict product limitations, and a generic "fire-rated glass" note conceals the need for a rated assembly including the frame. The harm is that an inadequate or non-compliant installation compromises the fire separation, a life-safety failure that may not be discovered until a fire or an inspection, and that can require costly replacement. The false signal is that fire-rated glass was specified, when the assembly, frame, and rating may not meet the wall's requirement. Coordinating fire-rated glazing assemblies with the fire protection design, specifying the correct rating and assembly, and consulting a fire protection specialist for complex conditions is what maintains the integrity of the rated separation.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is the fenestration system type, window, storefront, curtain wall, or window wall, matched to the application's span, exposure, and performance demands?
- [ ] Are measurable performance criteria specified for air infiltration, water penetration, structural wind load, thermal U-factor and SHGC, and condensation resistance?
- [ ] Is every window and opening detailed with sill pan flashing and integration of the wall's water and air control layers with the frame?
- [ ] Are frames thermally broken and condensation resistance evaluated for the climate and interior humidity?
- [ ] Is structural support and anchorage coordinated with the structural engineer, with deflection limits and shop drawing review required?
- [ ] Are safety glazing, fire-rated glazing assemblies, fall prevention, and egress window requirements specified and coordinated with code and fire protection design?
