# Audience Experience and Sightline Design

The audience experience is the product the venue sells, and it is determined by decisions that look like simple dimensioning on a plan but actually govern whether every seat can see the performance, whether the audience can arrive and exit without crowding, and whether the intermission is a pleasure or a queue. The judgment problem is that seating geometry is constrained by economics (more seats mean more revenue), by code (egress and accessibility), and by sightline quality, and these constraints conflict. A designer who maximizes seat count produces seats with obstructed views, cramped rows, and a lobby that cannot hold the intermission crowd; a designer who optimizes only sightlines produces a venue that cannot pay its bills. This skill covers the decisions that determine whether the audience actually enjoys the event, including sightline geometry, seating comfort and egress, balcony and rake design, accessibility, and lobby and intermission circulation.

## Core Rules

### Design Sightlines From the Worst Seat, Not the Best Seat

A sightline is only as good as its worst seat, and the designer must verify that the audience member in the last row of the balcony, at the side of the orchestra, and under the overhang can see the full performance area, not just the center of the stage. Sightlines are governed by the relationship between the eye height of the viewer, the height of the stage and the performer, and the obstruction created by the head of the person in the row ahead, and the floor rake (the slope of the seating) must be set so that each row's sightline clears the head ahead. The designer must establish the performance area (the full stage width and depth, including the conductor's podium and the downstage edge), must compute the sightline from the worst seat using the eye height and head obstruction, and must adjust the rake and the seating offset until every seat sees the full performance area. The tradeoff is that a steeper rake improves sightlines but increases the vertical height of the room and the structural complexity; the resolution is to set the rake from the sightline requirement, not from a nominal angle.

### Set Row Spacing and Seat Width for Real Human Bodies and Real Egress Loads

Row spacing (the distance between seat backs, commonly called row-to-row or seat pitch) and seat width determine both audience comfort and egress capacity, and they must be set for the actual population and the actual code, not for a historical minimum. A row spacing that was acceptable in the twentieth century is cramped for contemporary audiences and for the accessibility equipment some patrons use, and a seat width below roughly 500 to 550 millimeters center-to-center is uncomfortable for an average adult on a two-hour performance. Egress must be computed with the row spacing and the aisle width, because the audience must exit through the row and into the aisle, and a tight row spacing slows egress and reduces the calculated occupant load the aisles can serve. The designer must verify row spacing against both comfort and egress, must provide aisle width that meets the code for the occupant load, and must ensure that the cross aisle and vomitory layouts allow the audience to reach an exit without crossing the full width of the house.

### Shape Balconies and the Rake to Avoid Obstructed Views and Dead Zones

Balconies concentrate seating in a vertical stack, and their geometry must be shaped so that the balcony front does not obstruct the view of the row behind it, the overhang does not cut off the stage from the seats beneath it, and the side balcony seats can see the full stage width. The balcony front elevation must be set by the sightline from the worst balcony seat to the stage, the overhang depth must be limited so that under-balcony seats retain sightlines and acoustic reflection paths, and the balcony side must be pulled back or angled so that extreme side seats are not sold with obstructed views. The designer must check sightlines from the front row, the last row, and the extreme side seats of every balcony, and must mark any seat with an obstructed view as such or remove it from the inventory, because selling obstructed seats damages the venue's reputation.

### Provide Accessible Seating Distributed Throughout the House, Not Only at the Back

Accessible seating must be provided in the count and distribution required by the accessibility code, and it must offer a real choice of locations and price points, not only the back row or the aisle. Accessible seating positions must have companion seats, must provide the turning and transfer space the code requires, and must be distributed across the orchestra and the balconies so that a patron using a wheelchair can sit with their party in a good seat, not in a segregated section. The designer must integrate accessible positions into the seating layout from the start, must verify the dispersion across price zones and viewing quality, and must ensure the accessible route from the entrance to the seats is level, direct, and code-compliant. The trap is adding accessible seats at the back or the aisle as an afterthought, which meets the letter of the code but fails the spirit of inclusion.

### Plan Lobby and Intermission Circulation for Peak Demand, Not Average Occupancy

The lobby, the restrooms, the concessions, and the coat check must serve the audience during the two peak events: the pre-show arrival, when the full audience enters within roughly thirty minutes, and the intermission, when the full audience exits to the lobby simultaneously and seeks restrooms, drinks, and seating. Restroom fixture counts must be computed for the peak intermission demand, with the gender split the actual audience demographics suggest, and the lobby area must be large enough to hold the intermission crowd without forcing people into queues that block circulation. The designer must compute the fixture count from the occupant load and the peak demand model, must size the lobby for the intermission population, and must locate restrooms, concessions, and coat check so that their queues do not block the primary circulation path or the return-to-house doors.

## Common Traps

### Seating Layout Optimized for Seat Count With Obstructed-View Seats Sold at Full Price

A venue is laid out to maximize seat count, and the extreme side seats and the under-balcony corners are included in the inventory because they add revenue, even though they cannot see the full stage. The mechanism of the trap is that obstructed seats generate revenue on paper but produce dissatisfied patrons who post negative reviews, demand refunds, and do not return, so the marginal revenue is offset by reputational damage and customer service costs. The false signal is the seat count, which suggests a productive venue but masks the quality shortfall at the margins. The harm is bad reviews, refund disputes, and a venue reputation for bad seats. The defense is to verify sightlines from every seat, mark or remove obstructed seats, and resist the pressure to sell corners that cannot see the performance.

### Row Spacing at a Historical Minimum That Fails Contemporary Bodies and Egress

A venue uses a row spacing from an older standard, because it allows one more row per section, and the layout passes the egress check on paper. The mechanism of the trap is that historical row spacings were set for smaller bodies and less accessibility equipment, and a contemporary audience in a tight row is uncomfortable, cannot pass a seated patron without standing, and evacuates more slowly than the egress calculation assumes, so the code-compliant row is operationally inadequate. The false signal is the egress approval, which proves code compliance but not comfort or real-world egress. The harm is audience discomfort, accessibility failures, and egress that is slower than designed under real load. The defense is to set row spacing for contemporary bodies and accessibility, verify egress with realistic assumptions, and accept a slightly lower seat count for a venue that patrons want to return to.

### A Balcony Overhang So Deep That Under-Balcony Seats Cannot See or Hear

A balcony is extended to maximize upper-level seats, and the overhang is deep enough that the seats beneath it lose both the sightline to the upper stage and the acoustic reflections from the ceiling. The mechanism of the trap is that a deep overhang cuts off the line of sight to the top of the performance area and the path of the ceiling reflections to the seats below, producing seats that are both visually and acoustically poor, and these seats are often the discount inventory that first-time patrons encounter, shaping their impression of the venue. The false signal is the balcony seat count, which suggests productive inventory but masks the quality collapse beneath it. The harm is discount seats that generate complaints and a venue reputation for a bad balcony. The defense is to limit overhang depth to roughly twice the opening height, verify sightlines and acoustic reflection paths to under-balcony seats, and remove seats that fail both checks.

### Accessible Seating Placed Only at the Back Row or a Single Aisle

Accessible seating is provided in the required count, but all positions are at the back of the orchestra or at a single aisle, because that was the easiest place to add them. The mechanism of the trap is that concentrating accessible seating in one location segregates patrons using wheelchairs, denies them the choice of seat location and price that other patrons enjoy, and may fail the dispersion requirement of the accessibility code, which requires distribution across viewing quality and price zones. The false signal is the accessible seat count, which suggests compliance but masks the failure of dispersion and choice. The harm is a venue that is technically compliant but exclusionary in practice, and potential accessibility complaints. The defense is to distribute accessible positions across the orchestra and balconies, across price zones, with companion seats and the required transfer space, and to verify the dispersion against the code.

### Restroom and Lobby Sized for Average Occupancy, Failing the Intermission Peak

Restrooms and the lobby are sized for a nominal occupant load, and the design passes a standard plumbing fixture calculation, but the intermission crowd exceeds the capacity and produces long queues. The mechanism of the trap is that the intermission is a simultaneous peak in which the full audience seeks restrooms and concessions within a fifteen-minute window, and a fixture count sized for average or steady-state use produces queues that block the lobby, frustrate the audience, and cause late returns to the house that disrupt the performance. The false signal is the plumbing fixture calculation, which suggests code compliance but ignores the peak demand model. The harm is intermission queues, audience frustration, and late returns. The defense is to compute fixture counts from a peak intermission demand model with the actual gender split, size the lobby for the intermission population, and locate queues so they do not block circulation or the return-to-house doors.

## Self-Check

- Did I establish the full performance area (stage width, depth, conductor's podium, downstage edge) and verify the sightline from the worst seat in the orchestra, the balcony, and the extreme sides clears the head of the row ahead?
- Did I set the floor rake from the sightline requirement rather than a nominal angle, and check that the rake does not create a vertical height or structural problem?
- Did I set row spacing and seat width for contemporary bodies and accessibility equipment, verify egress with realistic assumptions, and provide aisle and cross-aisle width for the occupant load?
- Did I limit balcony overhang depth to roughly twice the opening height, verify sightlines and acoustic reflection paths to under-balcony seats, and remove or mark seats that fail either check?
- Did I distribute accessible seating across the orchestra and balconies, across price zones and viewing quality, with companion seats and the required transfer space, and verify the accessible route from entrance to seats?
- Did I compute restroom fixture counts from a peak intermission demand model with the actual audience gender split, and size the lobby to hold the intermission crowd without blocking circulation?
- Did I locate restroom, concession, and coat check queues so they do not block the primary lobby path or the return-to-house doors?
- Did I mark or remove obstructed-view seats rather than selling them at full price, and verify that no seat in the inventory lacks a sightline to the full performance area?
