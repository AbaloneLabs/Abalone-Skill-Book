# Electrical And Lighting System Coordination

Electrical and lighting systems are pervasive in a building, touching nearly every space through power outlets, lighting fixtures, switches, devices, and the low-voltage systems, data, communications, security, audiovisual, and fire alarm, that modern buildings require. The architect is not the electrical system designer, that is the electrical engineer's role, but the architect controls the decisions that enable or constrain the electrical design: electrical room and panel placement, lighting design intent and fixture selection, coordination of devices with architecture and furniture, and integration of the many low-voltage systems that must be accommodated. When the architect treats electrical as a matter of placing outlets and lights, the result is undersized electrical rooms, lighting that fails the design intent, low-voltage systems added late without coordination, and a building where the technology and the architecture conflict. Agents often underestimate the spatial and coordination demands of electrical and low-voltage systems and miss that lighting design and system integration are architectural responsibilities.

Use this skill when coordinating electrical systems, planning lighting, integrating low-voltage systems, or coordinating electrical rooms and devices. The goal is to make architectural decisions that enable sound electrical engineering, allocate adequate space for distribution and equipment, drive a lighting design that serves the architecture and use, and coordinate the many building systems into a coherent whole.

## Core Rules

### Allocate Adequate Space For Electrical Distribution And Equipment

Electrical distribution requires space that is frequently underestimated: main switchgear rooms, panel board closets, transformer and data rooms, and the raceways, conduits, and busways that distribute power. Electrical rooms need clearances around equipment for code and maintenance, and they need to be located to serve their distribution area efficiently. Squeezing or mislocating electrical space produces panels in occupied rooms, inadequate clearance, and distribution that cannot reach its loads efficiently. Space allocation for electrical equipment is an architectural responsibility informed by electrical input.

Allocate space by:

- coordinating with the electrical engineer early to size and locate main service, switchgear, and distribution rooms, providing adequate area and clearance;
- providing panel board closets or wall space distributed through the building to serve each zone without long, congested runs;
- locating electrical rooms to minimize distribution distances and to avoid occupying prime program space inappropriately;
- coordinating the structural loads and slab penetrations for heavy equipment and large conduits;
- providing access for equipment installation and future expansion, including pathways for cable and conduit.

### Drive The Lighting Design To Serve Architecture And Use

Lighting is a primary determinant of how a space is perceived and how well it functions, and it is an architectural concern even when the electrical engineer performs the technical design. The architect must define the lighting intent: illuminance levels, light distribution, color quality, fixture expression, and the relationship of light to the architecture and the activities. Deferring lighting entirely to the engineer, who optimizes for code compliance and uniformity, produces flat, characterless illumination that fails the design. The architect must engage the lighting design as an architectural element.

Drive lighting by:

- defining the lighting design intent for each space type, including illuminance, distribution, color rendering and temperature, and fixture expression;
- coordinating daylight with electric lighting, using controls to integrate the two and reduce energy;
- selecting or guiding fixture selection to support the architecture, considering scale, finish, light distribution, and maintenance;
- coordinating lighting control strategies, dimming, occupancy, daylight harvesting, and scene control, to serve use and energy goals;
- engaging a lighting designer for complex or high-performance projects, and coordinating their work with the electrical engineer.

### Coordinate Low-Voltage And Building Technology Systems

Modern buildings require numerous low-voltage and technology systems: data and communications, security and access control, audiovisual, public address, and fire alarm. Each requires devices, pathways, equipment rooms, and coordination with the architecture. These systems are often procured separately or added late, and without early coordination they produce congested pathways, devices scattered without relation to use, and equipment rooms that conflict with program. The architect must identify these systems early and coordinate their accommodation.

Coordinate by:

- identifying all required low-voltage and technology systems during design, including those procured by the owner or as separate packages;
- allocating equipment rooms and pathway space, such as cable trays and conduits, for these systems in coordination with the electrical engineer;
- coordinating device locations, cameras, access readers, speakers, data outlets, with the architecture, use, and furniture;
- coordinating the fire alarm system design, including device locations and notification, with the fire protection engineer and code requirements;
- recognizing that technology systems evolve, and providing pathway capacity and flexibility for future changes.

### Integrate Lighting And Power Controls With Use And Energy Goals

Energy codes increasingly mandate lighting controls, occupancy and daylight sensors, dimming, and multi-level switching, and these controls must serve both energy performance and occupant use. Poorly coordinated controls produce lights that turn off when occupants are present, sensors that fail to detect, or systems so complex that occupants defeat them. The architect must coordinate control strategies with the use patterns and ensure the controls are intuitive and effective.

Integrate by:

- coordinating lighting control zones with the architectural use and daylight patterns, not just the electrical layout;
- specifying control strategies appropriate to the space, occupancy sensors in intermittent-use spaces, daylight harvesting in daylit areas, and dimming where light levels vary;
- ensuring controls are intuitive and accessible, with overrides and manual controls where users need them;
- coordinating the integration of daylight, electric light, and window shading or blinds for effective daylight harvesting;
- deferring the technical control design to the electrical engineer while ensuring the strategies serve the use.

### Coordinate Emergency, Life Safety, And Code-Required Electrical

Electrical systems carry several life-safety and code requirements: emergency and egress lighting, fire alarm power, exit signs, and the electrical supply to life-safety systems like smoke control and fire pumps. These must be coordinated with the egress and fire protection design and must meet code requirements for reliability, including emergency power where required. The architect must coordinate these systems across disciplines.

Coordinate by:

- coordinating egress and emergency lighting with the means of egress design, ensuring required illumination on the path of egress;
- coordinating fire alarm device locations, notification appliances, and power with the fire protection engineer and code requirements;
- coordinating exit sign locations with egress paths and visibility;
- determining emergency power requirements for life-safety and critical systems, and coordinating generator or alternate source location and capacity with the electrical engineer;
- ensuring code-required electrical systems are on reliable, often dedicated, power and are coordinated with the overall electrical design.

### Coordinate Aesthetic And Functional Integration Of Devices

Electrical devices, switches, outlets, light fixtures, and technology devices, are visible elements that affect the architecture. Uncoordinated placement produces outlets and switches at inconsistent heights, fixtures that conflict with ceiling elements, and devices that clutter walls. The architect must coordinate the placement and expression of electrical elements as part of the design.

Coordinate by:

- establishing consistent device heights, styles, and finishes across the project;
- coordinating outlet and switch locations with furniture, art, and use, not just defaulting to code minimums;
- coordinating fixture locations with ceiling elements, diffusers, sprinklers, and structure for a coherent overhead;
- coordinating the expression of technology devices, cameras, sensors, speakers, with the architecture;
- reviewing reflected ceiling plans and device plans holistically rather than discipline by discipline.

## Common Traps

### The Undersized Or Mislocated Electrical Room

Electrical rooms are squeezed into leftover space or located far from their distribution area to preserve prime program space, and the result is equipment without code-required clearance, panels shoehorned into occupied rooms, and distribution that cannot efficiently reach its loads. The trap is that electrical rooms are unglamorous, so they are treated as residual space, and the consequence is invisible until equipment cannot be installed or maintained. The harm is code violations from inadequate clearance, equipment failure from poor heat dissipation, maintenance difficulty, and costly distribution runs that consume plenum space elsewhere. The false signal is that the room is shown on the plan, masking that it is too small or poorly located. Allocating adequate electrical room space early, informed by the electrical engineer, and respecting code clearances is what prevents the compromises that under-allocation forces.

### Lighting That Fails The Design Intent

Lighting is left to the electrical engineer, who designs to code illuminance and uniformity, and the result is flat, overlit, or characterless illumination that defeats the architectural intent. The trap is that the engineer's design meets the measurable criteria, so it appears correct, while the qualitative and experiential lighting goals are never defined or pursued. The harm is spaces that feel sterile or gloomy despite meeting code, architecture that is not revealed by the light, and occupant dissatisfaction that is difficult to attribute and fix. The false signal is that the lighting calculation passes, masking the absence of a design. Defining the lighting intent architecturally and engaging lighting design, whether by the architect, a lighting designer, or in coordination with the engineer, is what produces lighting that serves the architecture rather than merely meeting the code.

### Low-Voltage Systems Added Without Coordination

Data, security, audiovisual, or other technology systems are added late, often by the owner or a separate vendor, without coordinated pathway, equipment space, or device locations. The trap is that these systems are frequently procured separately and treated as the owner's responsibility, so they fall outside the coordinated design. The harm is congested and inadequate pathways, devices placed without regard to use or architecture, equipment rooms that conflict with program, and field-run cabling that compromises fire ratings and aesthetics. The false signal is that the base building electrical is designed, masking the omission of the systems that make the building functional. Identifying all low-voltage and technology systems during design, allocating space and pathways, and coordinating device locations is what prevents the disruptive and uncoordinated additions that otherwise occur.

### Controls That Occupants Defeat

Lighting and energy controls are specified to meet code but are poorly matched to use patterns, so sensors fail to detect occupants, lights turn off in occupied rooms, or systems are so complex that occupants find workarounds. The trap is that the controls meet the energy code on paper, so they appear successful, while in practice they frustrate users who then disable them, negating the energy savings. The harm is wasted energy from defeated controls, occupant dissatisfaction, and a system that performs worse than a simpler one would have. The false signal is that the controls are installed and commissioned, masking their mismatch with actual use. Coordinating control strategies and zones with use patterns, providing intuitive overrides, and commissioning against real occupancy is what makes controls effective rather than counterproductive.

### The Uncoordinated Ceiling Plane

Light fixtures, air diffusers, sprinklers, speakers, cameras, and other ceiling elements are placed by separate disciplines without holistic coordination, producing a cluttered, incoherent overhead. The trap is that each discipline places its devices to suit its own logic, and absent architectural coordination the result is visual chaos that undermines the design. The harm is a ceiling that looks haphazard regardless of the quality of the individual elements, conflicts between devices, and a design that appears uncoordinated to occupants and clients. The false signal is that each system is designed, masking the absence of integration. Coordinating the reflected ceiling plan holistically, balancing all ceiling elements into a coherent layout, is what produces an overhead that supports rather than detracts from the architecture.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is adequate space allocated and located for main service, switchgear, distribution rooms, and panel closets, with code clearances and access provided?
- [ ] Is the lighting design intent defined architecturally for each space, with daylight integration, fixture selection, and control strategies coordinated?
- [ ] Are all low-voltage and technology systems identified during design, with equipment rooms, pathways, and device locations coordinated?
- [ ] Are lighting and power control strategies matched to use patterns, with intuitive overrides and commissioning against real occupancy?
- [ ] Are emergency, egress, and fire alarm electrical systems coordinated with the egress and fire protection design, with reliable power provided?
- [ ] Is the reflected ceiling plan and device placement coordinated holistically across all disciplines for a coherent architectural integration?
