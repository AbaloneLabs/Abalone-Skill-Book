# HVAC System Selection And Zoning

HVAC system selection and zoning shape occupant comfort, energy performance, first cost, operating cost, maintenance burden, and the architectural form of the building through the space that equipment, ducts, and distribution require. The architect is not the mechanical system designer, that is the mechanical engineer's role, but the architect drives the decisions that constrain and enable the mechanical design: program loads, space allocation for equipment and distribution, zoning aligned with use and occupancy, aesthetic integration of diffusers and terminals, and coordination of structural and architectural impacts. When the architect defers entirely to the engineer without engaging these interfaces, the result is oversized equipment shoehorned into inadequate space, zoning that conflicts with room use, and exposed distribution that compromises the design. Agents often treat HVAC as the engineer's problem and miss that the architect's early decisions about space, zoning, and integration determine whether the mechanical design can succeed.

Use this skill when selecting HVAC systems, planning zoning, coordinating mechanical rooms, or integrating mechanical systems with the architecture. The goal is to make architecturally informed decisions that enable sound mechanical engineering, allocate adequate space and structure for equipment and distribution, align zoning with use, and defer technical sizing and selection to the mechanical engineer.

## Core Rules

### Engage Mechanical Design Early And Define Loads And Use

The single most consequential architectural act for HVAC is defining, early, how each space will be used and what its thermal and ventilation loads will be. Occupancy density, equipment and lighting loads, solar exposure, and hours of use drive the mechanical engineer's load calculations and system selection. Late or vague program definition forces the engineer to assume conservative, oversized loads and prevents right-sizing. The architect must communicate the program clearly and early, and must understand how program choices affect mechanical implications.

Engage early by:

- providing the mechanical engineer with detailed program information: occupancy, equipment, lighting, use schedules, and special requirements, before schematic design freezes;
- identifying spaces with unusual loads, such as server rooms, kitchens, labs, or high-occupancy assembly, that need dedicated treatment;
- understanding that glazing area and orientation, envelope performance, and internal loads drive mechanical size and must be coordinated with the envelope design;
- recognizing that energy code ventilation and efficiency requirements shape system options and must be factored from the start.

### Allocate Adequate Space For Equipment And Distribution

Mechanical equipment and distribution, ducts, pipes, conduits, and terminals, require substantial space that is often underestimated. Central plant equipment, air handlers, boilers, chillers, and cooling towers need dedicated rooms with access for installation and maintenance, clearance around equipment, and structural support. Distribution requires ceiling plenum depth, shafts, chases, and floor-to-floor height that accommodates structure, ducts, and lighting. Squeezing this space produces conflicts, exposed ducts, inadequate maintenance access, and noisy, inefficient systems. Space allocation is an architectural responsibility that must be informed by mechanical input.

Allocate space by:

- coordinating with the mechanical engineer early to size and locate central plant rooms, providing floor area, ceiling height, and structural capacity;
- providing adequate ceiling plenum depth and floor-to-floor height for ductwork, structure, lighting, and other systems, recognizing that compressed plenums force compromises;
- locating vertical distribution shafts strategically to minimize horizontal runs and serve all zones efficiently;
- providing access for equipment installation and replacement, including pathways to the roof or exterior for large components;
- coordinating structural loads of equipment, including vibration isolation, with the structural engineer.

### Align Zoning With Use, Occupancy, And Exposure

Zoning groups spaces served by a single thermostat or control zone. Good zoning groups spaces with similar thermal loads, schedules, and comfort requirements. Bad zoning places spaces with conflicting needs, a south-facing office and a north-facing storage room, or a 24-hour server room and a daytime office, on the same zone, guaranteeing discomfort and energy waste. Zoning is a collaborative decision between architect and engineer, driven by the program.

Align zoning by:

- grouping spaces with similar occupancy schedules, solar exposure, and internal loads into common zones;
- separating spaces with conflicting needs, such as perimeter versus interior, or rooms with special temperature requirements;
- recognizing that perimeter zones with different orientations, north, south, east, west, typically need separate zoning due to differing solar loads;
- coordinating zoning with the architectural layout so zone boundaries align with walls and the system can serve the zones efficiently;
- identifying spaces requiring individual control, such as offices or patient rooms, and selecting systems that provide it.

### Understand System Type Tradeoffs At An Architectural Level

The architect does not select the specific mechanical system, but must understand the architectural implications of system families to make informed space and integration decisions. All-air systems, such as VAV, distribute conditioned air through ducts and require significant plenum space but offer good ventilation control. Hydronic systems distribute heated or cooled water through smaller pipes, reducing plenum demands but requiring terminal units in each space. Packaged and split systems are decentralized, requiring less central plant but distributing equipment throughout the building. Radiant systems embed distribution in floors or ceilings, offering comfort and low plenum demand but slow response. Each has architectural consequences the architect must grasp to coordinate effectively.

Understand tradeoffs by:

- discussing system family options with the mechanical engineer early and understanding each option's space, structural, and integration demands;
- recognizing that decentralized systems, while simpler in distribution, place equipment near occupied space with noise and access consequences;
- understanding that radiant and displacement systems may constrain floor finishes, furniture layout, or ceiling design;
- considering maintenance implications: central plant is concentrated and accessible, decentralized equipment is distributed and harder to service;
- deferring the specific sizing and selection to the mechanical engineer while ensuring the architecture accommodates the chosen family.

### Coordinate Aesthetic And Functional Integration

Mechanical systems are visible: diffusers, grilles, thermostats, units, and equipment all affect the architecture. Uncoordinated integration produces visible ducts, poorly placed diffusers, awkward ceiling layouts, and equipment that compromises the design. The architect must coordinate the placement and expression of mechanical elements as part of the design, not as an afterthought.

Coordinate by:

- coordinating diffuser and grille locations with the ceiling and lighting layout for a coherent overhead plane;
- deciding consciously whether to expose or conceal ductwork and piping, and detailing accordingly;
- coordinating thermostat and control locations for accessibility and aesthetics;
- integrating rooftop and exterior equipment with screening and placement that respects the architecture and code line-of-sight requirements;
- coordinating the acoustic implications of mechanical placement, keeping noisy equipment away from quiet spaces.

### Address Energy Performance And Ventilation Requirements

Energy codes increasingly mandate ventilation rates, equipment efficiency, and overall energy performance that shape mechanical design. The architect's decisions on envelope, glazing, daylighting, and building form significantly affect the mechanical load and the ability to meet energy targets. Treating energy as solely the mechanical engineer's concern leads to oversized systems struggling to compensate for a poor envelope. High-performance buildings require integrated design where envelope and mechanical work together.

Address by:

- coordinating envelope performance, glazing ratio and properties, and shading with the mechanical engineer to reduce loads;
- understanding the applicable energy code's ventilation and efficiency requirements and their implications for system selection;
- considering passive strategies, daylighting, natural ventilation, thermal mass, that reduce mechanical demand, where appropriate to the climate and use;
- coordinating commissioning requirements that energy codes impose on mechanical systems;
- deferring detailed energy modeling to the mechanical engineer or energy consultant, but informing it with sound architectural decisions.

## Common Traps

### Inadequate Space For Equipment And Distribution

The architect underestimates the space required for mechanical equipment, ductwork, and piping, providing tight mechanical rooms, shallow plenums, and inadequate shafts, assuming the engineer will make it fit. The trap is that mechanical systems are spatially demanding, and the under-allocation is invisible until construction documents when conflicts emerge between ducts, structure, lighting, and sprinklers. The harm is compressed plenums force smaller, noisier, less efficient ducts; inadequate mechanical rooms prevent maintenance and equipment access; and the conflicts are resolved in the field through compromises that degrade performance, appearance, or both. The false signal is that the drawings show the systems fitting, when the coordination was never actually resolved. Allocating adequate space early, informed by mechanical input, and coordinating ceiling and shaft space in design, not construction, is what prevents the cascade of compromises that under-allocation forces.

### Zoning That Conflicts With Use

Zoning groups spaces with dissimilar loads, schedules, or comfort needs onto a single control zone, such as combining a sun-baked southwest office with a shaded northeast office, or a 24-hour equipment room with a daytime classroom. The trap is that zoning is often driven by duct layout convenience rather than use, and the conflict is invisible because the thermostat reads an average. The harm is chronic discomfort: one space is always too hot while another is always too cold, occupants run supplemental heaters or open windows, and energy is wasted conditioning against the conflict. The false signal is that the system is operating and producing conditioned air, masking the misallocation. Aligning zones with similar loads, schedules, and exposures, and providing individual control where needs differ, is what prevents the persistent discomfort that bad zoning creates.

### Deferring Entirely To The Engineer

The architect treats mechanical design as entirely the engineer's domain, providing no program detail, no space coordination, and no integration guidance, then is surprised when the installed system does not fit the architecture or serve the spaces well. The trap is that the division of responsibility feels clean, so the architect disengages, but the engineer cannot make sound decisions without architectural input on program, space, and integration. The harm is oversized equipment in inadequate space, diffusers and ducts that conflict with the design, zoning that ignores room use, and a building where the mechanical system fights the architecture. The false signal is that the engineer is producing drawings, so the design seems to be progressing. Engaging the mechanical engineer early, providing detailed program and space coordination, and integrating mechanical elements into the architecture is what produces a building where the systems and the design work together.

### The Decentralized Equipment Placed Near Occupants

To reduce central plant and distribution, decentralized equipment, such as packaged terminal units, split systems, or heat pumps, is distributed throughout the building near or within occupied spaces. The trap is that decentralized systems reduce duct and pipe runs and central plant space, appearing efficient, but they place compressors, fans, and vibration sources near or above occupied rooms. The harm is noise, vibration, and maintenance disruption in the very spaces occupants use, and service access that requires entering occupied rooms. The false signal is that the equipment is small and self-contained, so it seems unobtrusive. Considering the acoustic and access implications of decentralized placement, locating equipment over non-critical spaces, and providing isolation, is what prevents trading central plant efficiency for chronic occupant disturbance.

### The Poor Envelope Shifting Load To Mechanical

The architect designs a glazing-heavy, poorly insulated envelope and assumes the mechanical engineer will size equipment to handle the resulting load. The trap is that the envelope decisions are made for aesthetics without mechanical consequence, and the engineer can indeed size equipment to compensate, so the system "works." The harm is that the oversized equipment costs more to install, more to operate, struggles with part-load efficiency and comfort, and may fail to meet energy code or performance targets, all to compensate for an envelope that could have been designed better. The false signal is that the building conditions adequately, masking the energy and cost penalty. Coordinating envelope performance with the mechanical engineer and treating the envelope as the first line of load management is what prevents saddling the mechanical system with avoidable demand.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has the mechanical engineer been engaged early with detailed program information on occupancy, loads, schedules, and special requirements?
- [ ] Is adequate space allocated and coordinated for central plant rooms, ceiling plenums, shafts, and equipment access, with structural loads coordinated?
- [ ] Are control zones aligned with similar occupancy schedules, solar exposure, and internal loads, with individual control where needs differ?
- [ ] Does the architect understand the architectural implications of the selected system family, including space, structural, acoustic, and maintenance demands?
- [ ] Are diffusers, grilles, thermostats, and equipment locations coordinated with the architecture, with conscious decisions to expose or conceal?
- [ ] Is envelope performance coordinated with mechanical loads to meet energy code and performance targets, with passive strategies considered where appropriate?
