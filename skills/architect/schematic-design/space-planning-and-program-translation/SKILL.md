# Space Planning And Program Translation

Space planning is the translation of an abstract program (a list of spaces with areas and relationships) into a concrete spatial organization, and it is the decision that determines whether the building works for its occupants. The judgment problem is that a program is not a design: the areas, the adjacencies, and the relationships on paper must be resolved into rooms with proportions, circulation, daylight, structure, and code compliance, and a program that is merely "blocked" into the envelope produces a building where the rooms are the wrong shape, the circulation is wasteful, the adjacencies are wrong, and the building does not work. The skilled architect translates the program through a deliberate sequence — adjacencies first, then blocking, then room sizing, then circulation — testing each step against the program, the site, and the code, and documenting the decisions so the translation is traceable. The recurring failures are the program taken at face value without verification, the adjacency diagram that ignores the circulation, the room sized by area without proportion, the department isolated from its support, and the program that does not fit the envelope. The architect's role is to translate the program into a spatial organization that works, to test the translation against the constraints, and to document the basis.

## Core Rules

### Verify The Program Before Translating It

The program is the basis of the space planning, and it must be verified before it is translated. A program that is taken at face value — the areas, the adjacencies, the relationships — may contain errors, omissions, and assumptions that propagate through the design, and the verification is the first task.

- Verify the areas: confirm that each space's area is appropriate for its use, its occupancy, and its equipment, and that the areas sum to the program total, because an error in the areas propagates to the building.
- Verify the adjacencies: confirm that the required relationships (the department to its support, the public to the private, the clean to the dirty) are complete and correct, and that the conflicts (the noisy to the quiet) are identified.
- Verify the assumptions: confirm the occupancy, the staffing, the equipment, the storage, and the future growth the program assumes, because the assumptions drive the areas and the relationships.
- Verify the completeness: confirm that the program includes the support spaces (the circulation, the restrooms, the mechanical, the storage) and the code-required spaces, because a program that omits the support produces a building that does not fit.

### Develop The Adjacencies Before The Blocking

The adjacencies (which spaces must be next to which) are the logic of the space planning, and they must be resolved before the blocking (which spaces go where in the envelope). Develop the adjacency diagram first, test it against the program, and use it to guide the blocking.

- Develop the adjacency diagram: the bubble diagram that shows the required relationships (the department to its support, the public to the private, the clean to the dirty, the noisy to the quiet), and the conflicts to be resolved.
- Identify the primary adjacencies (must be adjacent), the secondary adjacencies (should be adjacent), and the conflicts (must be separated), and prioritize them, because not all adjacencies are equal.
- Test the adjacency diagram against the program and the operations, because the adjacencies must serve the use, and a diagram that looks logical may not serve the operations.
- Use the adjacency diagram to guide the blocking, because the blocking is the spatial resolution of the adjacencies.

### Block The Program Against The Site And The Envelope

The blocking (the placement of the program in the envelope) is the first spatial translation, and it must be tested against the site, the envelope, and the code. Block the program, test the fit, and iterate.

- Block the program in the envelope: place the departments and the major spaces, confirm the areas and the adjacencies, and identify the conflicts.
- Test the fit against the site: the orientation, the views, the access, the street, and the context, because the blocking must respond to the site.
- Test the fit against the envelope: the floor plates, the floor-to-floor heights, the structure, and the core, because the blocking must fit the envelope.
- Test the fit against the code: the occupancy, the egress, the accessibility, and the fire separation, because the blocking must comply with the code.
- Iterate the blocking based on the testing, because the blocking is a hypothesis to be refined.

### Size And Proportion The Rooms For Their Use

A room is not defined by its area alone; it is defined by its proportion, its shape, its daylight, its ceiling height, and its relationship to the circulation, and a room sized by area without proportion is a room that does not serve its use. Size and proportion each room for its use, and document the basis.

- Size the room by its use: the furniture, the equipment, the circulation, the clearances, and the occupancy, because the area must serve the use.
- Proportion the room for its use: the aspect ratio (the length to width), the shape (rectangular, L-shaped, square), and the relationship to the daylight and the views, because the proportion determines the room's usability.
- Confirm the ceiling height for the use: the high ceiling for the assembly, the lower ceiling for the office, the ducted ceiling for the lab, because the height is part of the room.
- Confirm the daylight and the views: the window area, the daylight factor, the view, and the glare control, because the daylight is part of the room's quality.

### Resolve The Circulation As A System

The circulation (the corridors, the lobbies, the stairs, the elevators) is a system that connects the spaces, and it must be resolved as a system, not as the leftover after the rooms are placed. Resolve the circulation, confirm its efficiency, and confirm its compliance with the code.

- Resolve the primary circulation: the main entry, the main lobby, the primary corridors, and the vertical circulation (stairs and elevators), because the primary circulation is the building's spine.
- Resolve the secondary circulation: the departmental corridors, the service circulation, and the emergency egress, because the secondary circulation serves the departments.
- Confirm the circulation efficiency: the ratio of the circulation area to the total area, because an inefficient circulation wastes the envelope and the budget.
- Confirm the code compliance: the egress paths, the travel distances, the exit widths, and the accessibility, because the circulation must comply with the code.

### Resolve The Departmental Relationships

The departments (the program groupings with internal relationships) must be resolved both internally (the spaces within the department) and externally (the department to the building), and the relationships must serve the operations. Resolve the departments, and confirm the relationships.

- Resolve the department internally: the spaces within the department, their adjacencies, their circulation, and their support, because the department must function as a unit.
- Resolve the department externally: the department's relationship to the building (the entry, the public spaces, the support, the other departments), because the department must function in the building.
- Confirm the department's support: the storage, the equipment, the staff spaces, and the restrooms, because the department's support is part of its function.
- Confirm the department's future growth: the expansion, the flexibility, and the change, because the department must adapt over the building's life.

### Document The Translation

The translation from the program to the space plan is a series of decisions, and the decisions should be documented so the translation is traceable. Document the program verification, the adjacency diagram, the blocking, the room sizing, and the circulation, because the documentation supports the design and the client's understanding.

- Document the program verification: the confirmed areas, adjacencies, and assumptions, because the program is the basis and the verification is the first decision.
- Document the adjacency diagram: the required relationships, the conflicts, and the priorities, because the adjacencies are the logic of the space plan.
- Document the blocking: the placement of the departments and the major spaces, the testing against the site and the code, and the iterations, because the blocking is the first spatial translation.
- Document the room sizing: the area, the proportion, the height, and the daylight for each room, because the room sizing is the detailed translation.

## Common Traps

### The Program Taken At Face Value

When the program is taken at face value — the areas, the adjacencies, and the relationships accepted without verification — the errors, omissions, and assumptions in the program propagate through the design, and the building is planned to a program that does not match the actual need. The mechanism is that the program reads as the client's requirement, so it feels authoritative, while the program may contain errors (an area too small for the use), omissions (a missing support space), and assumptions (a staffing level that drives the areas) that are not verified, and the unverified program produces a building that does not fit. The harm is that the building, planned to an unverified program, does not serve the actual use, the rooms are the wrong size, the support is missing, and the building requires rework, while the architect believed the program was authoritative. The defense is to verify the program before translating it, to confirm the areas, the adjacencies, the assumptions, and the completeness, to test the program against the actual use, and to recognize that the program is a hypothesis to be verified, not a requirement to be accepted, because the program is the basis and an error in the basis propagates to the building.

### The Adjacency Diagram That Ignores The Circulation

When the adjacency diagram is developed without regard to the circulation — the corridors, the lobbies, the vertical circulation that connect the spaces — the adjacencies are resolved on paper but not in space, and the blocking produces a plan where the adjacencies are defeated by the circulation that separates them. The mechanism is that the adjacency diagram reads as logical, so the relationships feel resolved, while the circulation that connects the spaces is not part of the diagram, and the blocking introduces circulation that breaks the adjacencies. The harm is that the building, with adjacencies defeated by the circulation, functions poorly, the departments are separated by the corridors, and the operations are compromised, while the architect believed the adjacencies were resolved. The defense is to develop the adjacencies with the circulation in mind, to resolve the circulation as a system that serves the adjacencies, to test the blocking against the adjacencies and the circulation together, and to recognize that the adjacencies and the circulation are a single system, because the circulation is what connects the adjacencies and an adjacency defeated by the circulation is not an adjacency.

### The Room Sized By Area Without Proportion

When the room is sized by its area without regard to its proportion — a long narrow room where a squarer one is needed, a square room where a linear one is needed — the room does not serve its use, because the proportion determines the usability as much as the area. The mechanism is that the area reads as the requirement, so the room feels sized, while the proportion, which determines how the room is used, is not addressed, and the room is the wrong shape for its use. The harm is that the room, with the wrong proportion, does not fit its furniture, its equipment, or its circulation, the use is compromised, and the occupants suffer the poor proportion for the life of the building, while the architect believed the room was sized. The defense is to size and proportion the room for its use, to consider the furniture, the equipment, the circulation, the clearances, and the daylight, to confirm the aspect ratio and the shape, and to recognize that the area is necessary but not sufficient, because the proportion is what makes the room usable and an area without proportion is a number, not a room.

### The Department Isolated From Its Support

When the department is placed without regard to its support — the storage, the equipment, the staff spaces, the restrooms — the department is isolated from the spaces that serve it, and the operations are compromised, because the support is not adjacent. The mechanism is that the department reads as the primary space, so it feels resolved, while the support spaces, which the department depends on, are placed elsewhere, and the department cannot function without its support. The harm is that the department, isolated from its support, functions poorly, the staff must travel for the storage, the equipment, and the restrooms, and the operations are inefficient, while the architect believed the department was placed. The defense is to resolve the department with its support, to place the storage, the equipment, the staff spaces, and the restrooms adjacent to the department, to confirm the department's internal and external relationships, and to recognize that the department is not the primary space alone but the primary space and its support, because a department without its support does not function.

### The Program That Does Not Fit The Envelope

When the program is blocked into the envelope without testing the fit, the program may not fit — the areas exceed the envelope, the floor plates are the wrong shape, the floor-to-floor heights are insufficient — and the building cannot contain the program as planned. The mechanism is that the blocking reads as a placement, so the program feels contained, while the fit has not been tested, and the program exceeds the envelope in area, in shape, or in height. The harm is that the building, with a program that does not fit, must be redesigned (a larger envelope, a smaller program, a different floor-to-floor), the redesign is costly and late, and the project is delayed, while the architect believed the program fit. The defense is to test the program fit against the envelope early and often, to confirm the areas, the floor plates, the floor-to-floor heights, and the core, to iterate the blocking based on the fit, and to recognize that the fit is the first test of the translation, because a program that does not fit the envelope is a program that must be re-planned.

### The Circulation Treated As Leftover

When the circulation is treated as the leftover after the rooms are placed, rather than as a system that connects the spaces, the circulation is wasteful, inefficient, and non-compliant, because it was not designed but resulted from the room placement. The mechanism is that the rooms read as the primary spaces, so the circulation feels secondary, while the circulation, as the leftover, is longer, wider, and more convoluted than it needs to be, and the efficiency and the code compliance suffer. The harm is that the building, with wasteful circulation, wastes the envelope and the budget, the egress may not comply with the code, and the occupants experience a confusing and inefficient plan, while the architect believed the circulation was provided. The defense is to resolve the circulation as a system, to design the primary and secondary circulation, to confirm the efficiency (the ratio of circulation to total area), to confirm the code compliance, and to recognize that the circulation is the building's spine and a system to be designed, not a leftover to be accepted, because the circulation connects the spaces and an undesigned circulation wastes the building.
