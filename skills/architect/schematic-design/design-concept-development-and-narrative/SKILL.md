# Design Concept Development And Narrative

The design concept is the organizing idea that gives a building coherence: the parti, the central proposition from which the building's organization, form, and detail flow. The design narrative is how that idea is articulated to the client, the review body, and the design team. A strong concept is not decoration applied to a building; it is the generative logic that, when followed, produces a building whose parts reinforce one another. Agents often mistake a stylistic preference or a catchy phrase for a concept, and mistake a marketing description for a narrative. The result is a building whose presentation sounds compelling and whose design has no internal logic to hold it together when pressures mount, because the concept was never strong enough to govern decisions.

Use this skill when developing a concept, articulating a parti, or writing a design narrative. The goal is a concept specific enough to generate and constrain design, and a narrative honest enough to be tested against the design it describes.

## Core Rules

### Develop The Concept As A Generative Logic, Not A Label

A concept earns its place by generating the building. If removing the concept changes nothing about the organization, form, or detail, it was a label, not a generative idea. The test of a concept is whether following it produces design decisions that would not otherwise have been made.

Develop as generative logic by:

- stating the concept as a proposition about how the building should work or be experienced, not as a noun or image it should resemble;
- tracing the concept through to its implications for organization, form, material, light, and sequence, so that each is a consequence of the idea;
- testing whether the concept resolves a real design problem, such as reconciling program and site, or it merely accompanies the solution;
- ensuring the concept can generate the mundane decisions, not only the heroic ones, because a concept that governs only the showpiece is not governing the building.

### Make The Parti Simple Enough To Hold The Whole Building

A parti is the compressed expression of the concept, the diagram that captures the organizing move. Its power lies in its simplicity: a parti that can be drawn in a few lines and remembered by every team member can hold a building together through thousands of decisions. A parti so complex it cannot be held in mind cannot govern.

Make it simple by:

- distilling the concept to its essential organizational move, the one decision from which others cascade;
- expressing the parti as a clear diagram, not a paragraph, because diagrams travel intact through a team;
- resisting the urge to bundle multiple competing partis into one project, which produces a building of unresolved fragments;
- recognizing when a project genuinely requires a composite of ideas, and defining the hierarchy among them rather than leaving them equal.

### Connect The Narrative To Verifiable Design Consequences

A design narrative explains why the building is the way it is. Its credibility depends on the connection between the words and the design being demonstrable. A narrative that could describe any building, or that describes a building other than the one presented, has no explanatory power.

Connect by:

- referencing specific features of the design as evidence for each claim in the narrative, so the argument is grounded;
- avoiding generic claims, to create a sense of place, to respond to context, that could attach to any competent building;
- checking that the narrative does not attribute to the concept decisions that were actually driven by budget, code, or precedent;
- being honest about where the design departs from the concept, and why, rather than papering over the gaps.

### Test The Concept Against Constraints Before Committing

A concept that survives only in the absence of pressure is fragile. Concepts must be tested against the constraints that will bear on the design: budget, code, structure, program, schedule, and client preference. A concept that breaks under the first constraint is not a foundation; it is an aspiration that will be abandoned.

Test by:

- pressure-testing the concept against the program's hardest requirements, not its easiest;
- checking the concept against the budget class and construction type, because a concept that requires premium construction to read may be unaffordable;
- assessing whether the concept can absorb the changes the project will inevitably undergo without losing coherence;
- identifying the elements essential to the concept, so that when cuts come, the team knows what must be defended.

### Use The Narrative To Align The Team And The Client

The narrative is not only for presentation; it is the shared understanding that lets a team make consistent decisions and lets the client recognize whether the design is fulfilling the intent they approved. A narrative that lives only in the lead designer's head cannot coordinate a team.

Use the narrative by:

- communicating the concept to the full design and consultant team early, so that engineering and detailing decisions support it;
- returning to the narrative at each review to test whether the design is still faithful to it;
- using the narrative to explain to the client why a design move serves their goals, converting subjective preference into reasoned judgment;
- updating the narrative when the concept genuinely evolves, and recording why, so the team and client track the change.

### Distinguish Concept Coherence From Stylistic Preference

A concept is coherent when its parts reinforce one another; a style is coherent when its elements share a vocabulary. These are different. A building can be stylistically consistent and conceptually empty, or conceptually rich and stylistically varied. Agents often defend a concept when they mean a style, which collapses the discussion into taste.

Distinguish by:

- asking whether the concept's coherence lies in its logic or in its visual consistency;
- being willing to vary the visual vocabulary where the concept demands it, rather than forcing stylistic uniformity;
- recognizing that clients and reviewers respond to logic they can follow, and that taste arguments cannot be won on their own terms.

## Common Traps

### The Catchy Phrase Mistaken For A Concept

When the design is described by a memorable phrase, a metaphor, or an image the building is meant to evoke, and the phrase is treated as the concept, the design often proceeds without a generative logic, because the phrase generates nothing. The trap is that the phrase feels like a concept, it is memorable, it animates the presentation, and it gives the team a shared vocabulary, while actually being detachable from every design decision. When constraints pressure the building, there is no logic to resolve the pressure, because the phrase offers no guidance about what matters. The harm is a building whose parts have no reason to be together, defended by rhetoric that dissolves under scrutiny; the corrective is to demand that the concept generate specific, traceable consequences in the organization and form.

### The Narrative That Describes A Different Building

When the design narrative articulates an intent loftier or more coherent than the design actually delivers, the gap between words and building is often invisible to the presenter, who sees the design through the narrative, and glaring to any critical reviewer. The trap is that the narrative sounds persuasive in the room, so the team believes the design has achieved the intent, while the building presented does not embody the claims being made. The harm is a credibility collapse when the client or reviewer compares the narrative to the design and finds them disconnected, after which no amount of explanation restores trust. The narrative must reference specific features as evidence and must be honest about where the design falls short, because a narrative the design cannot support is worse than no narrative.

### The Concept That Cannot Survive Constraints

When a concept is developed and approved in the freedom of early schematic design, without being tested against budget, code, structure, and the program's hardest requirements, it often collapses the moment those constraints engage. The trap is that the concept flourishes in the presentation, where constraints are abstract, and fails in development, where they are concrete, by which point the team is committed and the client has approved the imagery. The result is either an expensive fight to preserve a concept the building cannot afford, or a quiet abandonment that leaves the design conceptually empty. The corrective is to pressure-test the concept against the real constraints before it is presented as the solution, identifying which elements are essential and ensuring they can survive the pressures the project will apply.

### Stylistic Consistency Mistaken For Conceptual Coherence

When a building's elements share a consistent visual vocabulary, the consistency is often mistaken for a concept, and the design is defended as conceptually rigorous when it is merely stylistically uniform. The trap is that visual consistency is easy to achieve and easy to present, so it satisfies the desire for coherence without doing the harder work of generative logic. The harm is a building that looks resolved and thinks shallowly, whose decisions follow taste rather than reason, and whose coherence dissolves when a reviewer asks why a choice was made. The corrective is to distinguish logic from vocabulary, to demand that the concept explain the organization and not merely the appearance, and to accept stylistic variation where the concept requires it.

### The Concept Held Only By The Lead Designer

When the concept lives in the lead designer's understanding and is never fully communicated to the team, every team member makes decisions against their own interpretation, and the building accumulates inconsistencies that no one notices because each decision seemed reasonable in isolation. The trap is that the lead designer believes the concept is shared, because it was mentioned in meetings, while the team is actually working from fragments. The harm is a building that lacks the coherence the concept was meant to provide, discovered only when the fragments fail to integrate. The corrective is to articulate the concept and narrative explicitly to the full team, including consultants, to return to it at reviews, and to make it the shared standard against which decisions are tested.

- [ ] Does the narrative reference specific features of the design as evidence, avoiding generic claims that could describe any building?
- [ ] Has the concept been pressure-tested against budget, code, structure, and the program's hardest requirements before being committed?
- [ ] Are the elements essential to the concept identified, so the team knows what to defend when cuts come?
- [ ] Has the concept and narrative been communicated to the full design and consultant team, not only the lead designer?
- [ ] Is the narrative returned to at each review to test whether the design remains faithful to it?
- [ ] Has conceptual coherence been distinguished from stylistic consistency, with logic taking precedence over vocabulary?
- [ ] Is the narrative honest about where the design departs from the concept, rather than papering over gaps?
