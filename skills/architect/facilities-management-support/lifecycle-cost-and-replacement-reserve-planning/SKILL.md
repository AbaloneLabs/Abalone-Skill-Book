# Lifecycle Cost and Replacement Reserve Planning

First cost is visible; lifecycle cost is not. Owners routinely make design decisions on capital cost alone and discover, years later, that a cheaper system carried far higher operating, maintenance, and replacement costs. The architect who can frame design choices in terms of whole-life cost helps the owner avoid decisions that look prudent at procurement and become burdensome in operation. Facilities management depends on replacement reserve planning to forecast when major systems will fail and what capital must be set aside to renew them.

This skill covers the judgment needed when preparing lifecycle cost analyses (LCA/LCC), defining replacement reserve studies, and supporting the owner's long-term capital planning. The risk is producing numbers that look authoritative but rest on unsupported assumptions about service life, energy price, maintenance cost, and discount rate, leading the owner to under-reserve or over-invest.

## Core Rules

### Make assumptions explicit and sourced

Every lifecycle cost figure rests on assumptions: service life of each system, annual maintenance cost, energy consumption and price escalation, discount rate, and residual value. State each assumption, cite its source (manufacturer data, industry benchmarks, ASHRAE service life tables, comparable projects), and show sensitivity to the assumptions that most drive the result. A lifecycle cost without transparent assumptions is not analysis; it is a number.

### Align service life assumptions with the building's use and duty

Manufacturer service life data assume ideal conditions. Real service life depends on operating hours, load profile, water quality, ambient conditions, and maintenance quality. Adjust published service life for the actual duty: a chiller running 24/7 in a process facility will not last as long as one in a seasonal office building. Document the adjustment logic so the owner understands why the reserve schedule differs from a generic table.

### Structure the replacement reserve by system and by year

A replacement reserve study must forecast, by system and by year, when each major component will reach end of life and what it will cost to renew. Aggregate these into an annual capital requirement and compare to the owner's reserve contributions. The output is not a single number but a schedule that shows the peaks and valleys of future capital need, allowing the owner to smooth contributions or plan financing.

### Use lifecycle cost to compare alternatives, not to justify a preferred option

LCC is most defensible when used to compare design alternatives on a common basis: same period, same discount rate, same escalation, same boundary of costs included. It is weakest when used to produce a single absolute number for one option, because the absolute value is dominated by assumptions (especially discount rate and energy price) that are uncertain. Frame LCC as comparative, and present ranges or sensitivity, not point estimates.

### Include the costs that owners actually pay, not only the costs that are easy to model

A complete LCC includes capital cost, energy, maintenance, periodic replacement, and end-of-life disposal. Easy models capture capital and energy; the costs that dominate long-term budgets—maintenance, replacement, and disposal—are often omitted or underestimated. Include soft costs of renewal (design, permitting, disruption) because major replacement in an occupied building is more expensive than new construction.

### Coordinate the reserve study with the FM maintenance plan

A replacement reserve assumes a maintenance regime. If the FM team defers maintenance, service life shortens and the reserve is depleted early. Tie the reserve study to an assumed maintenance standard and flag where actual maintenance falls below that standard, so the owner understands the reserve may be inadequate if upkeep is deferred.

## Common Traps

### Using generic service life tables without adjustment

Published service life tables (ASHRAE, RICS) are starting points, not answers. Applying them without adjusting for duty, environment, and maintenance quality produces a reserve schedule that is precise but wrong. The trap is the apparent authority of a published number.

### Hiding the discount rate

The discount rate is the single most influential assumption in long-horizon LCC. A high discount rate makes future costs vanish; a low rate makes them dominate. Presenting a lifecycle cost without disclosing the discount rate, or using a rate chosen to favor a preferred option, is the most common form of analytical bias in this work.

### Omitting maintenance and replacement costs

Models that capture only capital and energy cost flatter high-first-cost, efficient options and penalize simpler systems. Because maintenance and replacement often exceed energy cost over a building's life, omitting them inverts the ranking of alternatives. Always include the full operating and renewal cost boundary.

### Forgetting disruption and soft costs of renewal

Replacing a roof or a chiller in an occupied building carries design, permitting, temporary works, and disruption costs that can exceed the equipment cost. A reserve study that prices only the asset under-reserves for renewal. Include renewal soft costs, especially for occupied and critical facilities.

### Treating the reserve study as a one-time deliverable

Service lives, costs, and conditions change. A reserve study completed at handover and never updated loses accuracy within a few years. Recommend periodic re-baselining as systems age, conditions are inspected, and costs escalate.

## Self-Check

- Are all lifecycle cost assumptions (service life, maintenance, energy, discount rate, escalation, residual) stated, sourced, and shown with sensitivity?
- Have I adjusted published service life data for the actual duty, environment, and maintenance regime of each system?
- Does the replacement reserve study forecast capital need by system and by year, aggregated into an annual requirement?
- Am I using LCC to compare alternatives on a common basis, with ranges or sensitivity, rather than to produce a single authoritative number?
- Does the cost boundary include maintenance, periodic replacement, disposal, and renewal soft costs—not only capital and energy?
- Is the reserve study tied to an assumed maintenance standard, with a flag for where actual maintenance falls short?
- Have I disclosed the discount rate and tested the result against alternative rates?
- Have I recommended periodic re-baselining of the reserve study as conditions change?
