# Post Occupancy And Seasonal Commissioning

Post-occupancy and seasonal commissioning is the phase after the building is occupied where the systems are tested under the seasonal conditions that were not available during construction, the performance is monitored against the Owner's Project Requirements, the occupancy-driven issues are resolved, and the building is transitioned to ongoing commissioning for continuous performance. It is the phase that confirms whether the building actually works for its occupants and its climate, because construction commissioning verifies installation and basic function but cannot verify performance across the seasons or under actual occupancy. Agents often treat commissioning as complete at occupancy and miss that the most important verification — how the building performs for its users across the year — happens after, and that without post-occupancy commissioning the building drifts from its design performance within years. The architect owns the transition from project to performance, because the design intent must be defended into operation. The goal is a building whose performance is verified across the seasons, whose occupancy issues are resolved, and whose ongoing commissioning sustains the performance over its life.

## Core Rules

### Plan For Seasonal And Deferred Testing Before Occupancy

Some commissioning tests cannot be performed during construction because the seasonal conditions are not available — heating system tests in summer, cooling system tests in winter, and envelope and daylight tests under specific solar conditions — and these must be deferred to the appropriate season after occupancy. Plan the seasonal and deferred testing before occupancy, with the specific tests, the conditions required, the schedule, and the responsibilities defined, so that the testing occurs when the conditions arrive and is not forgotten. Document the deferred tests in the commissioning plan and the acceptance documents, with the conditions for their completion, because a deferred test that is not tracked is a test that is never performed. The architect must plan the seasonal testing as part of the commissioning, because the building's year-round performance depends on it.

### Monitor Performance Against The OPR After Occupancy

The building's performance must be monitored against the OPR criteria after occupancy, because construction commissioning verifies capability but not sustained performance under actual use. Establish a performance monitoring program — energy use, environmental conditions, system operation, occupant comfort — that compares the actual performance to the OPR targets, using the building automation system, submetering, and occupant feedback. Review the performance data regularly in the first year, because the patterns that emerge reveal the issues that point testing cannot. The architect must coordinate the performance monitoring with the owner's operations team, because the monitoring is the basis for verifying that the building meets the owner's requirements.

### Resolve Occupancy-Driven Issues Through A Tracked Process

Occupancy reveals issues that construction commissioning cannot — systems that perform differently under actual loads, controls that do not match occupant behavior, environmental conditions that do not meet comfort criteria — and these must be resolved through a tracked process that continues after occupancy. Maintain the issues log from construction into the post-occupancy phase, with the owner's operations team reporting issues, the contractor and the commissioning authority resolving them, and the design team supporting as needed. The post-occupancy issues often require tuning and adjustment rather than major correction, but they must be addressed, because unresolved issues erode performance and occupant satisfaction. The architect must support the post-occupancy issue resolution, because the design intent is defended through it.

### Conduct A Post-Occupancy Evaluation With The Occupants

The occupants are the ultimate judges of the building's performance, and a post-occupancy evaluation — surveys, interviews, and walkthroughs with the users — reveals whether the building meets their needs in ways that the systems data cannot. Conduct a structured post-occupancy evaluation at six to twelve months after occupancy, covering comfort, lighting, acoustics, functionality, and satisfaction, and correlate the feedback with the performance data to identify the root causes of dissatisfaction. Use the evaluation to drive corrective action and to inform future designs, because the lessons from one building should improve the next. The architect must conduct the post-occupancy evaluation, because the building's success is measured by the people in it.

### Transition To Ongoing Commissioning For Continuous Performance

Buildings drift from their commissioned performance over time, through sensor calibration loss, control sequence changes, equipment degradation, and occupancy changes, and ongoing commissioning is the process that sustains performance over the building's life. Transition the building from the project commissioning to an ongoing commissioning program, with the monitoring, the periodic re-commissioning, the issue tracking, and the staff responsibilities defined, and train the owner's operations team to execute it. Ongoing commissioning is the owner's responsibility, but the architect must set it up, because a building handed over without an ongoing commissioning plan drifts from its performance within years. The architect must design the transition to ongoing commissioning as a commissioning deliverable.

### Verify Energy And Environmental Performance Against Targets

The OPR often includes energy and environmental performance targets — energy use intensity, water use, thermal comfort, daylight — and these must be verified against the actual performance over the first year and beyond. Compare the actual energy and water use, the measured environmental conditions, and the occupant comfort to the OPR targets, using the metering and the monitoring established for commissioning. Where the performance falls short, diagnose the causes — system operation, occupancy patterns, envelope issues — and drive corrective action, because the targets were the basis of the design and the owner's expectation. The architect must coordinate the performance verification, because the building's claimed performance must be demonstrated.

### Coordinate Warranty, Training, And Operations Support

The post-occupancy period coincides with the warranty period, and the issues that arise must be routed through the warranty process, the contractor, and the equipment vendors, with the commissioning authority verifying the resolutions. Coordinate the warranty claims with the commissioning issues, so that the same problem is not tracked in two places, and ensure that the owner's operations staff have the training and the documentation to operate the building as designed. Provide operations support in the first year, because the operations staff are learning the building and the design team's support prevents the workarounds that degrade performance. The architect must coordinate the warranty, training, and operations support, because the building's performance depends on the people who operate it.

## Common Traps

### Treating Commissioning As Complete At Occupancy

The team declares commissioning complete when the construction tests pass and the building is occupied, and the seasonal testing, the performance monitoring, and the post-occupancy issues are never addressed, so the building's actual performance is unverified and drifts from the design intent. The mechanism is that occupancy feels like completion and post-occupancy commissioning feels like extra scope, and the false signal is that the commissioning passed. The harm is that the building underperforms, the occupants are dissatisfied, and the commissioning investment is wasted. Commissioning must continue through the post-occupancy phase, with seasonal testing, performance monitoring, and issue resolution planned and executed.

### Forgetting The Deferred Seasonal Tests

The heating tests deferred to winter, or the cooling tests deferred to summer, are documented in the commissioning plan but never performed, because no one tracks them to the right season, and the systems' seasonal performance is unverified. The mechanism is that deferred tests are easy to lose track of and the seasonal conditions arrive without triggering the test, and the false signal is that the tests were planned. The harm is that a heating or cooling system fails in its first season, the occupants are uncomfortable, and the problem is discovered in operation rather than in testing. The deferred seasonal tests must be tracked, scheduled, and performed when the conditions arrive, with the commissioning authority verifying.

### Monitoring Without Acting On The Data

The building automation system collects performance data, but no one reviews it or acts on it, so the data accumulates unused and the performance issues that the data reveals persist. The mechanism is that monitoring feels like verification and acting on data requires operations effort, and the false signal is that the building is monitored and so it is performing. The harm is that the performance degrades, the issues that the data would reveal are missed, and the monitoring investment is wasted. The performance data must be reviewed regularly, with issues identified and acted upon, in the first year and as part of ongoing commissioning.

### Neglecting The Post-Occupancy Evaluation

The building is occupied, the team moves on, and no post-occupancy evaluation is conducted, so the design team never learns whether the building meets the occupants' needs and the same mistakes are repeated in the next project. The mechanism is that the project feels complete and the evaluation feels like extra scope, and the false signal is that the building was delivered. The harm is that the occupants' dissatisfaction is unaddressed, the design team's learning is stunted, and the next building repeats the same errors. A post-occupancy evaluation must be conducted, with the feedback driving corrective action and informing future work.

### Handing Over Without An Ongoing Commissioning Plan

The building is handed over with a commissioning report but no ongoing commissioning plan, so the operations team has no process to sustain the performance, and the building drifts from its commissioned state within a few years. The mechanism is that ongoing commissioning feels like the owner's responsibility and the project commissioning feels complete, and the false signal is that the commissioning was delivered. The harm is that the performance degrades, the energy use rises, and the commissioning investment is lost over time. The transition to ongoing commissioning must be a commissioning deliverable, with the monitoring, the re-commissioning, and the staff responsibilities defined and the operations team trained.

### Ignoring The Performance Targets After Occupancy

The OPR set energy and environmental targets, the design claimed to meet them, but after occupancy no one verifies the actual performance against the targets, so the building's claimed performance is unconfirmed and the owner's expectations may be unmet. The mechanism is that the targets were a design basis and the verification feels like extra scope, and the false signal is that the design met the targets. The harm is that the building underperforms, the owner's expectations are unmet, and the performance gap is never addressed. The actual performance must be verified against the OPR targets, with shortfalls diagnosed and corrected.

- [ ] Has the building been transitioned to an ongoing commissioning program, with monitoring, re-commissioning, issue tracking, and staff responsibilities defined and the operations team trained?
- [ ] Are the actual energy and environmental performance verified against the OPR targets, with shortfalls diagnosed and corrected?
- [ ] Are the warranty claims, the commissioning issues, and the operations support coordinated, with the owner's staff trained and supported in the first year?
