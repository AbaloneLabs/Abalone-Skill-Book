# Preparation And Design Review

Commissioning preparation and design review is the front end of the commissioning process, where the owner's project requirements are documented, the design is reviewed for commissionability and operability, and the commissioning plan, specifications, and test procedures are developed before construction begins. It is the most influential phase of commissioning, because problems caught in design are cheap to fix and problems that reach construction are expensive, and because a project that begins commissioning in construction has already lost the opportunity to design for operability. Agents often treat commissioning as a construction testing program and miss that its greatest value is in design, where the owner's requirements are made explicit, the design is tested against them, and the building is set up to be operable and verifiable. The architect owns the integration of commissioning into design, because the design decisions made here determine whether the building can be commissioned and operated. The goal is a design that is explicitly responsive to documented owner requirements, that is commissionable, and that sets up a construction and acceptance process the team can execute.

## Core Rules

### Document The Owner's Project Requirements As The Commissioning Basis

The Owner's Project Requirements, or OPR, is the document that records what the owner needs the building to do — occupancy, environmental conditions, energy performance, durability, operability, maintainability, and the specific performance criteria the commissioning will verify. Develop the OPR with the owner early, because it is the basis against which the design and the commissioning are evaluated, and a project without a documented OPR has no defensible performance target. The OPR must be specific enough to test against — temperature and humidity ranges, lighting levels, ventilation rates, energy targets, acoustic criteria — and it must reflect the owner's actual operational needs, not generic standards. The architect must drive the OPR development, because the owner often cannot articulate the requirements without facilitation, and the design must respond to them.

### Review The Basis Of Design Against The Owner's Project Requirements

The Basis of Design, or BOD, is the design team's narrative of how the design meets the OPR — the systems selected, the capacities, the assumptions, the codes and standards applied, and the operational strategies. Review the BOD against the OPR to confirm that the design responds to the owner's requirements, that the assumptions are documented, and that the systems selected are capable of meeting the performance criteria. A BOD that does not trace to the OPR, or that contains undocumented assumptions, is a commissioning risk, because the design may not deliver the performance the owner expects. The architect must ensure the BOD is complete, documented, and traceable to the OPR, because it is the bridge between the owner's needs and the design.

### Review The Design For Commissionability And Operability

Commissioning reviews the design not for code compliance but for commissionability — whether the systems can be tested — and operability — whether the building can be operated and maintained as designed. Review the design for access to equipment for testing and maintenance, for the instrumentation and sensors needed to verify performance, for the control sequences that are testable and unambiguous, and for the system configurations that support rather than hinder operation. Identify design features that cannot be commissioned — inaccessible sensors, ambiguous sequences, systems without measurement points — and require redesign, because a system that cannot be tested cannot be verified. The architect must integrate commissionability and operability into the design review, because they are design qualities, not testing logistics.

### Develop The Commissioning Plan And Specifications

The commissioning plan defines the scope, the roles, the process, the deliverables, and the schedule of commissioning across the project, and the commissioning specifications define the contractor's commissioning responsibilities in the construction documents. Develop the commissioning plan early, with the owner, the commissioning authority, and the design team, and develop the specifications to require the contractor participation, the test procedures, the documentation, and the training that commissioning demands. A project with a weak commissioning specification produces a contractor who treats commissioning as optional, and the process fails. The architect must ensure the commissioning plan and specifications are complete and enforceable, because they are the contractual basis of the commissioning process.

### Develop Test Procedures That Verify Performance Against The OPR

The commissioning test procedures — the pre-functional checklists and the functional performance tests — verify that the installed systems perform against the OPR criteria under the full range of operating conditions. Develop the test procedures during design, with the design team and the commissioning authority, so that they test the actual sequences and the actual performance criteria, and so that the contractor knows what will be tested. The test procedures must be specific, with the modes, the conditions, the acceptance criteria, and the documentation defined, because vague procedures produce tests that verify nothing. The architect must coordinate the development of the test procedures, because they translate the OPR into verifiable tests.

### Integrate Commissioning Into The Design Milestones

Commissioning is not a phase added at the end; it is a process integrated into every design milestone, with reviews, deliverables, and updates at each stage. Schedule commissioning reviews at schematic design, design development, and construction documents, with the commissioning authority providing input that the design team incorporates, and update the OPR, the BOD, and the commissioning plan as the design evolves. A project that defers commissioning to construction loses the design-phase value and arrives at construction with uncorrected commissionability and operability problems. The architect must integrate commissioning into the design schedule, with reviews and deliverables at each milestone.

### Plan For Training, Documentation, And Handover

The end of commissioning is the handover of a building the owner can operate, supported by complete documentation and trained staff, and the planning for this handover begins in design. Specify the operations and maintenance documentation the contractor must deliver — system manuals, as-built sequences, control drawings — and the training the owner's staff must receive, with the content, the duration, and the record defined. Plan the systems manual, the commissioning report, and the handover process, because a building handed over without documentation or training cannot be operated as designed. The architect must plan the handover during design, because the documentation and training requirements must be specified before construction.

## Common Traps

### Beginning Commissioning In Construction And Losing The Design Value

The team treats commissioning as a construction testing program and engages the commissioning authority after the design is complete, so the design is never reviewed for commissionability and operability, and the problems that commissioning would have caught in design reach construction where they are expensive to fix. The mechanism is that commissioning feels like testing and design review feels like extra cost, and the false signal is that the design is complete and commissioning will verify it. The harm is that the design contains uncommissionable systems, the construction encounters problems, and the building cannot be verified or operated as intended. Commissioning must begin in design, with the OPR, BOD, and design reviews integrated into the milestones.

### Developing An OPR That Is Too Vague To Test Against

The OPR states that the building will be "energy efficient" and "comfortable," without specific performance criteria, so the commissioning has no verifiable targets and cannot confirm that the building meets the owner's needs. The mechanism is that specific criteria feel constraining and the owner cannot articulate them without facilitation, and the false signal is that the OPR is documented. The harm is that the commissioning verifies nothing specific, performance disputes cannot be resolved, and the owner has no basis to evaluate the building. The OPR must contain specific, testable performance criteria, developed with the owner through facilitation.

### Reviewing The Design For Code Compliance But Not Operability

The design is reviewed for code compliance and constructability, but not for operability, so the building meets code but cannot be operated or maintained — equipment is inaccessible, sensors are unmeasurable, sequences are ambiguous. The mechanism is that code and constructability are the familiar reviews and operability feels like an operations concern, and the false signal is that the design is complete. The harm is that the building is difficult and expensive to operate, maintenance is deferred or improvised, and the performance degrades over time. The design must be reviewed for operability and commissionability, with inaccessible equipment and untestable sequences redesigned.

### Writing A Weak Commissioning Specification

The commissioning specification is vague or optional, so the contractor treats commissioning as a paperwork exercise, the tests are not performed rigorously, and the process fails to verify performance. The mechanism is that commissioning specifications feel like boilerplate and the contractor's role is underemphasized, and the false signal is that the specification includes commissioning. The harm is that the commissioning is superficial, the systems are not verified, and the owner receives a building whose performance is unconfirmed. The commissioning specification must clearly define the contractor's responsibilities, the test procedures, the documentation, and the training, enforceably.

### Developing Test Procedures That Verify Compliance Rather Than Performance

The test procedures check that the systems are installed as specified, but do not test performance under the range of operating conditions, so the commissioning confirms installation but not that the building meets the OPR. The mechanism is that installation checks are easy and performance testing is demanding, and the false signal is that the tests are performed and passed. The harm is that the building passes commissioning but does not perform, the owner discovers the gap in operation, and the commissioning has not delivered its value. The test procedures must verify performance against the OPR criteria, under the full range of conditions, not only installation compliance.

### Neglecting The Handover Documentation And Training

The commissioning focuses on testing, and the handover documentation and training are treated as afterthoughts, so the owner receives a building without the manuals, the as-builts, or the trained staff to operate it. The mechanism is that testing is the visible commissioning activity and handover feels like paperwork, and the false signal is that the commissioning is complete when the tests pass. The harm is that the building cannot be operated as designed, performance degrades, and the commissioning investment is wasted. The handover documentation and training must be specified in design and delivered as commissioning deliverables.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has the Owner's Project Requirements been documented with specific, testable performance criteria, developed with the owner through facilitation?
- [ ] Has the Basis of Design been reviewed against the OPR, with assumptions documented and the design confirmed to respond to the owner's requirements?
- [ ] Has the design been reviewed for commissionability and operability — equipment access, instrumentation, testable sequences, maintainable configurations — with problems redesigned?
- [ ] Are the commissioning plan and specifications complete and enforceable, defining the contractor's responsibilities, test procedures, documentation, and training?
- [ ] Are the test procedures developed during design, specific to the systems and the OPR criteria, testing performance under the full range of conditions?
- [ ] Is commissioning integrated into every design milestone, with reviews, deliverables, and updates at each stage?
- [ ] Are the handover documentation, the systems manual, the commissioning report, and the owner's training specified and planned during design?
- [ ] Has the commissioning authority been engaged early enough to influence the design, not only to test the construction?
