# Construction And Acceptance Testing

Construction and acceptance testing is the phase of commissioning where the installed systems are verified to perform against the Owner's Project Requirements, through submittal review, installation verification, functional performance testing, issue resolution, and integrated system testing before the building is accepted for occupancy. It is the phase where design intent meets installed reality, and where the gaps between specification, installation, and performance are exposed and must be closed. Agents often treat construction commissioning as a witness sign-off and miss that it is an active process of verification, problem-finding, and problem-solving that requires the architect to coordinate the contractor, the commissioning authority, the design team, and the owner against a tight occupancy schedule. The architect owns the integration of commissioning into the construction process, because the design intent and the owner's requirements must be defended through acceptance. The goal is a building whose systems are verified to perform as designed, whose deficiencies are resolved before occupancy, and whose acceptance is based on demonstrated performance rather than schedule pressure.

## Core Rules

### Review Submittals For Commissionability Before Equipment Is Ordered

Commissioning begins in construction with the submittal review, because the equipment and systems the contractor proposes must be commissionable — capable of being tested, instrumented, and verified against the OPR — and must conform to the design intent. Review the submittals with the commissioning authority, focusing on the features that affect commissioning: the sensors and instrumentation, the control sequences, the access for testing, the compatibility with the building automation system, and the performance data needed for verification. Reject submittals that substitute equipment lacking the commissioning features, because equipment ordered without testability cannot be commissioned after installation. The architect must integrate the commissioning submittal review with the design review, because both protect the design intent.

### Verify Installation Against The Construction Documents

Before functional testing, verify that the systems are installed as designed, through pre-functional checklists that confirm the equipment, the piping, the ductwork, the wiring, the controls, and the access match the construction documents. The installation verification catches the defects that functional testing cannot — reversed sensors, missing access, incorrect wiring — and it must be performed rigorously, because functional testing of an incorrectly installed system produces misleading results. Coordinate the installation verification with the contractor and the commissioning authority, and require that deficiencies be corrected before functional testing begins. The architect must require installation verification, because testing an unverified installation wastes effort and obscures the real problems.

### Conduct Functional Performance Testing Against The OPR

Functional performance testing is the core of construction commissioning, where each system is operated under the full range of conditions — occupied and unoccupied, heating and cooling, normal and emergency — and verified to perform against the OPR criteria and the design sequences. Conduct the tests with the contractor operating the systems, the commissioning authority verifying and documenting the performance, and the design team and owner witnessing as appropriate, using the test procedures developed in design. The tests must exercise every operating mode, every failure mode, and every sequence, because a system that works in one mode may fail in another. The architect must ensure the functional testing is comprehensive and performed against the OPR, not merely a demonstration that the systems turn on.

### Resolve Issues And Deficiencies Through A Tracked Process

Commissioning will find issues — installation defects, sequence errors, performance shortfalls — and these must be resolved through a tracked process that records each issue, assigns responsibility, defines the corrective action, and verifies the resolution. Manage the issues log actively, with the contractor, the commissioning authority, and the design team, and require that issues be resolved and re-tested before the affected systems are accepted. Avoid accepting systems with open issues under schedule pressure, because unresolved issues become the owner's problems after occupancy. The architect must manage the issues resolution process, because the design intent and the owner's requirements are defended through it.

### Coordinate Testing, Balancing, And Controls Integration

The testing and balancing of the air and water systems, the integration of the controls, and the functional performance testing are interdependent, and their coordination is essential to commissioning success. Sequence the work so that testing and balancing precedes functional testing, that controls integration is complete before the systems are tested, and that the interactions among systems — a chiller and its pumps, an air handler and its VAV boxes — are tested together. Coordinate the contractors and the commissioning authority so that the testing is performed in the right order, because testing out of sequence produces results that must be redone. The architect must coordinate the sequencing of the testing, balancing, and controls work, because the commissioning depends on it.

### Conduct Integrated System Testing Before Occupancy

Individual system testing verifies each system in isolation, but the building operates as an integrated whole, and integrated system testing verifies that the systems work together under realistic operating conditions. Conduct integrated testing — sometimes called a blackout test, a seasonal test, or a full-building simulation — that exercises the fire and life safety, the HVAC, the lighting, the access control, and the emergency power together, under the scenarios the building will face. The integrated testing exposes the interface problems that individual testing cannot, and it must be performed before occupancy, with the owner participating. The architect must require integrated system testing, because the building's performance depends on the systems working together.

### Manage Acceptance Against Demonstrated Performance, Not Schedule

The acceptance of the building for occupancy must be based on demonstrated performance — the systems verified to meet the OPR, the issues resolved, the integrated testing passed — rather than on the occupancy schedule. Resist the pressure to accept incomplete or underperforming systems, because the owner inherits the problems and the commissioning value is lost. Where schedule requires partial occupancy, define clearly which systems are accepted, which are not, and what conditions must be met for full acceptance, with the commissioning authority concurring. The architect must defend performance-based acceptance, because the owner's interests are served by a building that works, not by one that is accepted on time.

## Common Traps

### Skipping The Submittal Review For Commissionability

The submittals are reviewed for design conformance but not for commissionability, so equipment is ordered without the sensors, instrumentation, or control features needed for testing, and the commissioning cannot verify performance after installation. The mechanism is that commissionability feels like a testing detail and the submittal review focuses on conformance, and the false signal is that the equipment meets the specification. The harm is that the systems cannot be fully commissioned, performance cannot be verified, and the owner receives a building whose operation is unconfirmed. The submittals must be reviewed for commissionability, with the commissioning authority, before equipment is ordered.

### Functional Testing That Demonstrates Rather Than Verifies

The functional tests turn the systems on, confirm that they operate, and are signed off, without exercising the full range of operating modes, failure modes, and sequences, so the testing verifies that the systems run but not that they perform against the OPR. The mechanism is that demonstrations are quick and verifications are demanding, and the false signal is that the tests were performed and passed. The harm is that the systems pass commissioning but fail in operation, the owner discovers the gaps, and the commissioning has not delivered its value. Functional testing must exercise every mode and sequence, verifying performance against the OPR criteria, not merely demonstrating operation.

### Accepting Systems With Open Issues Under Schedule Pressure

The occupancy date approaches, several commissioning issues remain open, and the team accepts the systems with the issues to be resolved later, but later never comes and the owner inherits the problems. The mechanism is that schedule pressure is immediate and the issues feel minor, and the false signal is that the systems are substantially complete. The harm is that the owner operates a building with unresolved deficiencies, performance suffers, and the commissioning investment is wasted. Systems must be accepted based on resolved issues and demonstrated performance, with partial acceptance clearly defined where schedule requires.

### Testing Systems Out Of Sequence

The functional testing is performed before the testing and balancing is complete, or before the controls integration is finished, so the test results are invalid and must be redone after the prerequisite work is complete. The mechanism is that the schedule pressures parallel work and the interdependence is overlooked, and the false signal is that the testing is progressing. The harm is that the testing is wasted, the schedule slips, and the team loses confidence in the commissioning process. The testing, balancing, controls integration, and functional testing must be sequenced correctly, with the commissioning authority coordinating the order.

### Omitting Integrated System Testing

Each system is tested individually and passes, but the systems are never tested together, so the interface problems — a fire alarm that does not release the smoke control, an emergency power transfer that drops the life safety — are not discovered until an actual event. The mechanism is that individual testing feels sufficient and integrated testing is demanding and disruptive, and the false signal is that each system passed its tests. The harm is that the building fails when the systems must work together, the interface problems surface in emergencies, and the commissioning has not verified the integrated performance. Integrated system testing must be conducted before occupancy, exercising the systems together under realistic scenarios.

### Letting The Issues Log Drift Without Resolution

The issues log accumulates open items, no one drives them to resolution, and the log becomes a record of unresolved problems rather than a management tool, so the commissioning closes with deficiencies that the owner inherits. The mechanism is that issue resolution requires coordination and the schedule absorbs attention, and the false signal is that the issues are logged and so they are tracked. The harm is that the deficiencies persist, the building underperforms, and the commissioning has not delivered a verified building. The issues log must be actively managed, with each issue assigned, resolved, and verified before the affected systems are accepted.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Have the submittals been reviewed for commissionability — sensors, instrumentation, control sequences, access, performance data — before equipment was ordered?
- [ ] Has installation been verified against the construction documents through pre-functional checklists, with deficiencies corrected before functional testing?
- [ ] Does functional performance testing exercise every operating mode, failure mode, and sequence, verifying performance against the OPR criteria rather than merely demonstrating operation?
- [ ] Is the issues log actively managed, with each issue assigned, resolved, re-tested, and verified before the affected systems are accepted?
- [ ] Are testing and balancing, controls integration, and functional testing sequenced correctly, with the commissioning authority coordinating the order?
- [ ] Has integrated system testing been conducted before occupancy, exercising the systems together under realistic scenarios including emergency conditions?
- [ ] Is acceptance based on demonstrated performance and resolved issues, with partial acceptance clearly defined where schedule requires?
- [ ] Has the owner participated in the testing and acceptance, so they understand the building's verified performance and any conditions?
