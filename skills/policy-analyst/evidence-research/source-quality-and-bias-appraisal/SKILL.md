# Source Quality And Bias Appraisal

Policy analysis often uses imperfect evidence. Studies can be underpowered, reports can be advocacy-driven, official statistics can be delayed, administrative data can be incomplete, and expert claims can reflect institutional incentives. Agents may cite sources because they are reputable, recent, or convenient without asking whether they answer the question well. This skill helps the analyst appraise source quality and bias before relying on evidence.

## Core Rules

### Assess Fit To The Question First

A high-quality source can be irrelevant. Start by asking whether the source addresses the population, intervention, context, outcome, time period, and decision question. A rigorous study of a national program may not answer a local implementation question. A credible legal memo may not answer fiscal cost.

Do not equate prestige with relevance. Evidence should be judged by both quality and fit.

### Identify Source Type And Purpose

Classify the source: peer-reviewed article, government evaluation, administrative dataset, audit, budget note, regulatory impact analysis, stakeholder submission, advocacy report, industry report, think tank paper, media investigation, expert interview, or international comparison. Then identify why the source was produced.

Purpose matters. A budget office may emphasize fiscal exposure, an advocacy group may highlight harms, an industry association may emphasize compliance costs, and an agency may defend an existing program. Bias does not make a source useless, but it changes how it should be read.

### Review Methodology And Data Provenance

For quantitative evidence, examine sample, comparison group, measurement, missing data, confounding, statistical uncertainty, time horizon, and whether results are causal or descriptive. For qualitative evidence, examine sampling, interview method, coding, triangulation, researcher positionality, and whether dissenting views are included. For administrative data, check collection purpose, definitions, coverage, data linkage, and reporting incentives.

Do not cite a number without understanding how it was produced.

### Check Conflicts And Incentives

Look for funding sources, author affiliations, political commitments, agency incentives, industry interests, litigation posture, election timing, procurement relationships, or advocacy goals. A source can still be useful if conflicts are disclosed and methods are sound, but undisclosed incentives reduce confidence.

Also consider incentives in official data. Agencies may have reasons to undercount wait times, overstate compliance, or define success favorably.

### Distinguish Evidence From Interpretation

Sources often mix findings, interpretation, and recommendations. Separate what the data show from what the author argues should be done. A report may accurately document harm but overstate the effect of its preferred remedy. A study may show average impact but not prove equity or feasibility.

When writing, cite the source for what it supports, not for broader claims it does not prove.

### Look For Replication And Triangulation

Confidence increases when different methods, datasets, jurisdictions, or independent sources point in the same direction. It decreases when evidence depends on a single study, self-reported survey, proprietary model, or stakeholder claim.

Triangulate across evidence types: statistics, evaluations, implementation reports, stakeholder experience, fiscal data, and legal analysis. Disagreement should be explored, not averaged away.

### Rate Uncertainty Transparently

Use confidence language tied to evidence quality: strong, moderate, limited, mixed, emerging, contested, or insufficient. Explain why. Do not present uncertain evidence with definitive language because the policy memo needs a clear recommendation.

Decision makers can act under uncertainty, but they need to know what is known and what is not.

### Treat Lived Experience And Stakeholder Evidence Carefully

Stakeholder and lived-experience evidence can reveal access barriers, administrative burdens, harms, and implementation realities that datasets miss. It is not a random sample unless designed as one. Use it for insight, issue identification, and plausibility checks, while being clear about representativeness.

Do not dismiss lived experience as anecdote when it identifies a real mechanism. Do not overgeneralize one story into population-wide prevalence.

### Check Whether The Evidence Is Current Enough and document The Appraisal So Others Can Recheck It

Policy evidence can age when laws, markets, technology, agency capacity, demographics, or social conditions change. A study may remain methodologically strong but no longer describe current implementation realities. Conversely, new evidence may be timely but weak.

State whether recency affects confidence. Do not discard older evidence automatically, but explain what must still hold for it to remain useful.

Source appraisal is only useful if it is visible. When you rely on a source, record a brief note of its type, purpose, methods, conflicts, and the main weakness that affects how it should be read. This lets a reviewer, a dissenting colleague, or a future analyst see why you weighted the source as you did and challenge it on the merits rather than on impression. It also forces you to be honest: writing down "advocacy report, strong selection bias, but only available data on subgroup harm" is more disciplined than quietly citing it. Appraisal notes should travel with the evidence, not live only in your head, because policy work is reviewed, contested, and inherited. A conclusion backed by sources whose appraisal cannot be traced is hard to defend when challenged.

## Common Traps

- Assuming peer review, government authorship, or recent publication guarantees relevance.
- Citing advocacy or industry reports without noting perspective and incentives.
- Treating model outputs as facts without checking assumptions.
- Using administrative data without understanding definitions and collection incentives.
- Confusing a source's recommendation with evidence for that recommendation.
- Ignoring null, failed, or contradictory evidence; overgeneralizing from pilot programs with unusually strong implementation support
- Treating stakeholder testimony as either worthless anecdote or representative proof; hiding uncertainty to make a recommendation look stronger
- Failing to distinguish evidence quality from policy preference; using high-quality but outdated evidence without checking whether the policy context changed

## Self-Check

- Does each source fit the policy question, population, context, and outcome?
- Is the source type and purpose identified?
- Are methods, data provenance, and limitations understood?
- Are conflicts, funding, affiliations, and institutional incentives considered?
- Are findings separated from interpretation and recommendations?
- Is confidence based on replication, triangulation, and method quality?
- Are official statistics and administrative data checked for definition and collection issues?
- Is lived experience used appropriately without dismissal or overgeneralization?
- Are uncertainty labels explicit and justified?
- Would a skeptical reviewer agree that the evidence supports only the claims made?; is evidence recency assessed against current law, market, technology, and implementation context?
