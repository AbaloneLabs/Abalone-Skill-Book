# Evidence Question And Search Strategy

Policy evidence work starts before reading sources. A vague evidence question produces a pile of studies, reports, anecdotes, and statistics that cannot answer the decision. Agents often search broadly, collect impressive citations, and then retrofit them to a policy recommendation. This skill helps the policy analyst define the evidence question, search boundaries, and evidence types needed for a defensible policy answer.

## Core Rules

### Start From The Decision Question

Translate the policy task into answerable evidence questions. A minister asking whether to expand a subsidy may need evidence on need, target population, cost, take-up, market response, distributional impact, administrative capacity, and alternatives. A regulator considering a ban may need evidence on harm, exposure, enforcement feasibility, substitution, and legal risk.

Do not let the search question become broader than the decision. "What works in education?" is too broad; "what evidence exists on tutoring programs for middle-school math recovery in low-income districts, and what implementation conditions affect results?" can guide a search.

### Separate Descriptive, Causal, Predictive, And Normative Questions

Different evidence answers different questions. Descriptive evidence shows scale and distribution. Causal evidence estimates effects. Predictive evidence supports forecasts. Normative evidence concerns values, rights, fairness, and public priorities. Implementation evidence shows whether a policy can be delivered.

Avoid using descriptive evidence as if it proves causality. A trend after a policy was introduced does not necessarily show the policy caused the change.

### Define Inclusion And Exclusion Criteria

Set scope for geography, time period, population, policy instrument, outcome, study type, language, publication status, and relevance to the jurisdiction. Decide whether to include academic studies, government evaluations, audit reports, administrative data, court decisions, stakeholder submissions, international comparisons, and expert interviews.

Exclusion criteria should be principled, not convenient. If older or foreign studies are excluded, explain why context or time limits reduce relevance.

### Search Beyond Academic Literature

Policy questions often require grey literature: government reports, inspector general audits, legislative fiscal notes, agency evaluations, implementation manuals, consultation responses, regulatory impact analyses, parliamentary committee reports, budget documents, court filings, and civil society reports. These sources may contain practical implementation evidence that academic studies omit.

Grey literature varies in quality. Include it deliberately and appraise it rather than treating it as either authoritative or worthless.

### Capture Search Terms And Paths

Record databases, websites, search strings, keywords, synonyms, date searched, filters, and snowballing paths from references. This lets another analyst understand what was searched and what might have been missed. It also reduces confirmation bias.

Use synonyms and policy-specific language. A workforce policy may be described as labor market activation, employment services, job placement, skills training, apprenticeships, or work requirements depending on source.

### Look For Contrary And Null Evidence

A good search actively looks for evidence that a preferred option may fail, have no effect, create harm, or work only under certain conditions. Search terms should include adverse outcomes, implementation failures, equity impacts, cost overruns, litigation, noncompliance, and unintended consequences.

If all evidence supports the recommendation, ask whether the search strategy filtered out dissenting evidence.

### Match Evidence Depth To Decision Risk

High-cost, high-rights-impact, irreversible, or politically contentious policies require deeper evidence review than low-risk operational changes. Emergency decisions may need rapid evidence synthesis, but the analyst should state limitations and revisit evidence later.

Do not overbuild a systematic review for a minor decision, but do not use a quick scan for a policy with major consequences.

### End With An Evidence Map

Summarize what evidence exists, what is strong, what is weak, what is missing, and what questions remain. The map should distinguish problem evidence, intervention evidence, implementation evidence, fiscal evidence, equity evidence, and stakeholder evidence.

An evidence map prevents a memo from presenting isolated citations as if they answer the full policy question.

## Common Traps

- Searching before clarifying the decision question.
- Treating a broad topic scan as a policy evidence review.
- Using descriptive statistics as proof of policy effectiveness.
- Ignoring grey literature that contains implementation and fiscal evidence.
- Including sources because they are easy to find rather than relevant.
- Excluding contrary evidence without an explicit criterion.
- Failing to record search terms and source paths; overweighting studies from contexts that differ materially from the target jurisdiction
- Using rapid evidence review language without stating its limits; ending with citations instead of an evidence map tied to policy decisions

## Self-Check

- Is the evidence question tied to the actual policy decision?
- Are descriptive, causal, predictive, normative, and implementation questions separated?
- Are inclusion and exclusion criteria explicit?
- Does the search include relevant academic, government, administrative, comparative, and grey literature sources?
- Are search terms, databases, sites, dates, filters, and snowballing paths recorded?
- Has contrary, null, and adverse evidence been searched for?
- Is review depth proportionate to decision risk and timeline?
- Are context limits identified for evidence from other jurisdictions?
- Does the output map evidence strengths, gaps, and uncertainties?
- Would another analyst be able to reproduce or critique the search strategy?
