# Tradeoff Framing And Decision Criteria

Policy decisions rarely optimize every value at once. A policy may be effective but costly, equitable but administratively complex, fast but legally fragile, popular but weak, or efficient but unfair. Agents often try to make a recommendation sound unambiguously best by softening tradeoffs. This skill helps the analyst frame decision criteria and tradeoffs so the decision maker can choose knowingly.

## Core Rules

### Name The Values In Tension

Start by identifying which values or objectives conflict: effectiveness, equity, fiscal cost, speed, privacy, liberty, administrative simplicity, legal defensibility, local flexibility, national consistency, innovation, accountability, participation, or political feasibility. Tradeoffs should be named before the recommendation is defended.

If no tradeoff appears, ask whether the analysis is shallow. Most meaningful policies shift burdens, risks, or authority.

### Define Criteria In Operational Terms

Criteria should be clear enough to compare options. "Feasible" might mean legal authority, staffing, data systems, procurement, political support, or implementation timeline. "Equitable" might mean distribution of benefits, burden, participation, access, non-discrimination, or remedying historical harm. "Cost" might mean fiscal outlay, compliance burden, opportunity cost, or long-term liabilities.

Avoid vague labels that let the analyst move the goalposts during comparison.

### Separate Evidence-Based Judgments From Value Judgments

Evidence may estimate cost, effect size, administrative burden, or distribution. Values determine how much weight those dimensions receive. Be explicit when a recommendation depends on giving priority to equity over speed, fiscal savings over coverage, or liberty over enforcement intensity.

Do not present value choices as if they were purely technical conclusions.

### Use Criteria That Match The Decision Stage

Early-stage policy development may compare strategic direction, evidence needs, and political path. Late-stage decision making may compare precise legal, budget, and implementation details. Emergency decisions may prioritize speed and harm reduction while requiring later review.

Do not demand precision that the stage cannot support, but do not accept vague criteria when the decision is ready for commitment.

### Consider Who Bears Each Tradeoff

Tradeoffs are not abstract. Costs, burdens, risks, and benefits fall on different groups: beneficiaries, taxpayers, regulated firms, local agencies, front-line workers, future generations, marginalized communities, or neighboring jurisdictions. A policy may look efficient overall while imposing concentrated harm.

Describe incidence. Decision makers need to know who pays, who benefits, who waits, and who is exposed if assumptions fail.

### Avoid False Precision In Scoring

Scoring matrices can help structure discussion, but they can also hide subjective weights behind numbers. A five-point score for equity or feasibility may look scientific when it reflects judgment. Use scoring only when criteria and weights are transparent.

Narrative explanation is often more honest than a total score. If using scores, include sensitivity to weights.

### Show Dominant, Close, And Conditional Choices

Sometimes one option clearly dominates. More often, the best choice depends on priorities or assumptions. State when an option is dominant, when options are close, and when a recommendation is conditional on funding, legal authority, implementation capacity, or stakeholder support.

Conditional recommendations are stronger than overconfident ones because they show what must be true.

### Provide A Decision Rationale

After comparing tradeoffs, explain why the recommended option is preferred. Link the rationale to objectives, criteria, evidence, and constraints. Also state the main downside and how it should be mitigated.

A recommendation that hides its downside invites later mistrust. A recommendation that names its downside shows discipline.

### Identify Non-Negotiable Constraints

Some criteria are tradeable; others are hard constraints. Legal authority, constitutional limits, basic rights, fiscal caps, safety thresholds, treaty obligations, or minimum service standards may define what cannot be sacrificed for better performance elsewhere.

Do not average hard constraints into a total score. An option that is illegal, unaffordable, or rights-violating should not win because it scores well on other dimensions.

## Common Traps

- Writing as if one option maximizes every policy value.
- Using vague criteria such as "better," "fair," or "feasible" without definition.
- Treating value judgments as technical facts.
- Ignoring who bears costs and risks.
- Using scoring tables that conceal subjective weights.
- Comparing options by different criteria; treating political feasibility as a reason to ignore policy merit, or ignoring it entirely
- Failing to show how the recommendation changes under different priorities; avoiding the main downside of the recommended option
- Offering tradeoffs without a clear decision rationale; treating hard legal, rights, fiscal, or safety constraints as ordinary weighted criteria

## Self-Check

- Are the values and objectives in tension named?
- Are criteria operational enough to compare options?
- Are evidence judgments separated from value judgments?
- Do criteria match the decision stage and available evidence?
- Is incidence of costs, burdens, benefits, and risks identified?
- Are scoring or rankings transparent about weights and uncertainty?
- Are dominant, close, and conditional choices distinguished?
- Is the recommended option tied to stated criteria?
- Is the main downside and mitigation plan visible?
- Would a decision maker know what value choice they are being asked to make?; are non-negotiable constraints separated from ordinary tradeoffs?
