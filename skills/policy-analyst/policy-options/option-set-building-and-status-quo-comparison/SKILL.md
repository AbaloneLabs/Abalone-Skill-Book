# Option Set Building And Status Quo Comparison

An options paper can manipulate a decision by offering only one serious option, weak straw alternatives, or no baseline comparison. Policy analysts must build option sets that are broad enough for a real decision and disciplined enough to compare. Agents often produce a preferred recommendation with two decorative alternatives. This skill helps the analyst construct options that reveal tradeoffs rather than hide them.

## Core Rules

### Include The Status Quo As A Real Option

The status quo is rarely neutral. It has costs, benefits, risks, distributional effects, political implications, and future trajectory. Include current policy, planned changes, and expected trends as the baseline. A no-new-action option may be unacceptable, but it still anchors comparison.

Do not compare a policy option against an imaginary world where the problem stays fixed. Compare against what is likely to happen without the proposed change.

### Build Options Along Meaningful Dimensions

Options should differ in instrument, scale, target population, intensity, timing, governance, funding, enforcement, or implementation pathway. Three versions of the same idea with minor funding differences may not provide a real choice unless funding scale is the central decision.

Use option architecture deliberately: do nothing, improve existing policy, targeted intervention, broad reform, regulatory option, fiscal option, pilot, or phased approach. The set should match the decision maker's authority and risk tolerance.

### Avoid Straw Options

A weak option included only to make the preferred recommendation look good damages credibility. Each option should be plausible enough that a reasonable decision maker might consider it under some priorities. If an option is included because stakeholders will ask about it, analyze it honestly and explain its limits.

Do not hide value judgments by making disfavored options obviously impractical.

### Define Each Option Consistently

For each option, specify instrument, target group, eligibility, delivery mechanism, cost, timeline, authority, implementing agency, enforcement or administration, expected benefits, major risks, and evaluation approach. Inconsistent option descriptions make comparison unfair.

If information is missing for one option, state it. Do not provide rich detail only for the preferred option.

### Consider Hybrid And Sequenced Options

Some problems require combinations: regulation plus compliance support, subsidy plus supply expansion, pilot plus evaluation, public communication plus enforcement, or phased rollout plus statutory reform. A binary choice can obscure better sequencing.

However, do not create a hybrid that combines every attractive feature without acknowledging complexity and cost. Hybrid options should have coherent logic.

### Use Decision Criteria Before Scoring

Define criteria before comparing: effectiveness, equity, fiscal cost, legal authority, feasibility, speed, political acceptability, administrative burden, stakeholder legitimacy, risk, reversibility, and alignment with objectives. Weighting may be explicit or qualitative, but the criteria should be visible.

Avoid scoring systems that create false precision. A table can aid comparison, but it should not replace judgment.

### Show Sensitivity To Decision Priorities

The best option may depend on what the decision maker prioritizes. If speed matters most, an administrative option may lead. If equity matters most, targeted investment may lead. If legal defensibility matters most, narrower regulation may lead. If fiscal restraint dominates, incremental reform may lead.

Explain how the recommendation changes under different priority assumptions. This makes tradeoffs honest.

### Identify Options That Need Further Development

Some options may be promising but underdeveloped due to data gaps, legal uncertainty, stakeholder concerns, or implementation complexity. Mark them as future work, pilot candidates, or contingent options rather than forcing a premature recommendation.

The option set should support both immediate decisions and longer policy development.

### Keep Options Mutually Understandable

Options do not always need to be mutually exclusive, but the relationship between them should be clear. State whether options are alternatives, complements, stages, fallback positions, or packages. If one option can be combined with another, explain what changes in cost, authority, and implementation burden.

Confusing option relationships can make a decision maker approve a concept without understanding the actual commitment.

## Common Traps

- Offering one real option and two decorative alternatives.
- Omitting the status quo or treating it as costless.
- Comparing options against different assumptions.
- Designing options that exceed the decision maker's authority.
- Using a scoring table that hides subjective weights.
- Ignoring hybrid or phased designs; making every option a variation of the same instrument
- Dismissing politically inconvenient options without analysis; providing rich detail for the preferred option and vague sketches for others
- Treating a pilot as a full solution without saying what it tests; mixing alternatives, complements, and phases without explaining their relationship

## Self-Check

- Is the status quo or baseline included as a real option?
- Do options differ in meaningful design dimensions?
- Are options plausible rather than straw alternatives?
- Are option descriptions consistent across instrument, target, cost, authority, timeline, delivery, and risk?
- Are hybrid or sequenced options considered where useful?
- Are comparison criteria defined before judgment?
- Are tradeoffs shown under different decision priorities?
- Are uncertain or future-development options labeled appropriately?
- Is the preferred option stronger because of analysis, not option-set manipulation?
- Would a decision maker understand the real choice being made?; are option relationships clear: alternative, package, phase, complement, or fallback?
