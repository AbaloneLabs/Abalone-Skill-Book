# Logic Model And Theory Of Change

A theory of change is the policy's causal story. It explains how resources and activities are expected to produce outputs, outcomes, and impacts under specific assumptions. Agents often draw linear logic models that look tidy but ignore behavior, context, implementation failure, and feedback loops. This skill helps the policy analyst build theories of change that are useful for design, monitoring, and evaluation rather than decorative diagrams.

## Core Rules

### Start With The Outcome, Then Work Backward

Define the desired end outcome and intermediate outcomes before listing activities. A training program, grant, regulation, or campaign is not the goal; it is a means. Work backward from the change needed in people, markets, institutions, environments, or systems.

If the pathway cannot explain how activities cause the outcome, the policy design is incomplete.

### Distinguish Inputs, Activities, Outputs, Outcomes, And Impacts

Inputs are resources. Activities are what implementers do. Outputs are immediate deliverables. Outcomes are changes in behavior, access, conditions, compliance, capacity, or wellbeing. Impacts are longer-term public value. Keep these categories separate.

Do not call outputs outcomes. "Number of workshops" does not prove changed practice.

### Make Assumptions Explicit

The causal pathway depends on assumptions: people know about the policy, eligible groups can access it, providers participate, regulated parties respond, funding arrives, enforcement is credible, data systems work, and political support remains. Write assumptions into the theory.

Assumptions are not filler. They are risks to monitor.

### Identify External Conditions

Outcomes may depend on labor markets, housing supply, weather, economic cycles, court decisions, technology, social norms, private actors, or other policies. The theory of change should distinguish what the policy controls from what it only influences.

This prevents unfair attribution and helps interpret results.

### Include Equity Pathways

If the policy aims to reduce disparities, show how the intervention reaches disadvantaged groups differently or removes barriers. A universal activity may not create equitable outcomes unless access, trust, language, disability, cost, geography, and eligibility barriers are addressed.

Equity should not be added only as a final outcome. It should be present in the mechanism.

### Include Potential Negative Pathways

Policies can create displacement, stigma, administrative burden, gaming, exclusion, market distortion, or enforcement harms. Add risk pathways and guardrails to the theory of change. This makes monitoring more realistic.

A theory that shows only positive causal steps is advocacy, not analysis.

### Use The Theory To Select Measures

Each key link should have indicators: inputs, fidelity, outputs, intermediate outcomes, final outcomes, assumptions, and risks. If a link is critical but unmeasured, the evaluation may not explain success or failure.

Measurement should test the theory, not merely count activity.

### Revise The Theory As Evidence Emerges and identify The Weakest Link

Theory of change is not fixed once written. Implementation data, stakeholder feedback, evaluation findings, and context changes may show that assumptions were wrong. Revise the theory and document why.

Learning is part of policy management.

Every theory has a link most likely to fail: awareness, eligibility, provider participation, enforcement credibility, behavior response, funding stability, data sharing, or political support. Name the weakest links and design monitoring around them.

This prevents the model from treating every step as equally likely. A policy often fails at one practical bottleneck rather than across the entire chain.

### Use The Theory To Compare Alternatives and distinguish Necessary From Sufficient Conditions

Different options may rely on different causal pathways. A subsidy depends on affordability response; a regulation depends on compliance and enforcement; a campaign depends on attention and behavior change. Comparing theories can show which option has fewer fragile assumptions.

Do not build a theory only after the preferred option is selected.

A pathway may list every condition that must hold without showing whether those conditions are jointly sufficient to produce the outcome. Necessary conditions explain what is required for the mechanism to operate at all; sufficient conditions explain whether meeting them is enough to generate the intended change. Many policies satisfy their necessary conditions yet still fail because a missing enabling factor, a competing force, or a threshold effect prevents the outcome from emerging.

Map where the chain is merely necessary versus where it is sufficient. If the theory relies on several independent conditions all holding simultaneously, the probability of success compounds downward, and the weakest condition dominates. Make this compounding explicit rather than presenting the pathway as a sequence of equally likely steps. Where sufficiency is uncertain, identify the additional evidence, capacity, or complementary intervention that would close the gap.

## Common Traps

- Starting with activities instead of outcomes.
- Treating outputs as proof of impact.
- Hiding assumptions that determine whether the policy works.
- Ignoring external conditions outside policy control.
- Adding equity as a slogan rather than a causal pathway; omitting negative or unintended pathways
- Creating a diagram too vague to guide measurement; measuring only what agencies already collect
- Treating the theory of change as a grant-document formality; never revising the theory after implementation begins
- Treating all causal links as equally plausible instead of identifying the weakest link; building a theory only for the preferred option rather than comparing alternatives

## Self-Check

- Does the theory work backward from desired outcomes?
- Are inputs, activities, outputs, outcomes, and impacts distinct?
- Are key assumptions explicit?
- Are external conditions and limits of control identified?
- Are equity pathways built into the mechanism?
- Are negative pathways and guardrails included?
- Do indicators test each critical causal link?
- Are evidence gaps tied to monitoring or evaluation needs?; is the theory simple enough to use but detailed enough to test?
- Is there a plan to revise the theory as evidence emerges?; are weakest links identified and used to guide monitoring?
- Are competing options compared by their causal pathways?; are assumptions written in a way that can be tested later?
