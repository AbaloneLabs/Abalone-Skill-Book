# Evaluation Design And Causal Claims

Evaluation determines whether a policy worked, for whom, why, and at what cost. Poor evaluation can overclaim causality, miss implementation failure, or measure the wrong outcome. Agents often compare before-and-after data and call it impact. This skill helps the policy analyst design evaluations and interpret causal claims with appropriate rigor and humility.

## Core Rules

### Define The Evaluation Question

Clarify whether the evaluation asks about implementation, impact, cost-effectiveness, equity, user experience, compliance, mechanisms, or scalability. Different questions require different methods. A process evaluation can explain delivery failure; an impact evaluation estimates effects; a developmental evaluation supports adaptation.

Do not use one evaluation design to answer every question.

### Identify The Counterfactual

Causal claims require a credible answer to what would have happened without the policy. Randomized trials, natural experiments, difference-in-differences, regression discontinuity, matching, synthetic controls, interrupted time series, or contribution analysis can support different levels of causal confidence.

Before-and-after comparisons alone are weak when trends, shocks, selection, or other policies may explain change.

### Match Method To Policy And Ethics

Randomization may be rigorous but not always ethical, legal, political, or operationally feasible. Quasi-experimental methods may fit phased rollout, eligibility thresholds, or geographic variation. Qualitative methods may be essential for mechanisms and equity. Mixed methods often provide the best picture.

Choose the strongest feasible design, not the most impressive label.

### Plan Evaluation Before Implementation

Baseline data, comparison groups, consent, data agreements, randomization, rollout sequencing, and outcome definitions must often be set before launch. Retrofitted evaluations may be possible but weaker.

If evaluation is important, build it into policy design, budget, and governance.

### Measure Implementation Fidelity And Context

Impact estimates are hard to interpret without knowing whether the policy was delivered as intended, who received it, what dose they received, and what context changed. Include process measures, take-up, compliance, service quality, and implementation variation.

A null impact may mean the theory was wrong, implementation failed, or measurement was poor.

### Protect Equity In Evaluation

Assess subgroup effects, access barriers, differential take-up, and harms. But avoid underpowered subgroup claims. Plan sample size and qualitative methods to understand equity where relevant.

Evaluation should not only ask whether the average effect is positive. It should ask who benefited, who did not, and why.

### Avoid Overclaiming

State limitations: selection bias, spillovers, attrition, measurement error, external validity, short follow-up, small sample, implementation variation, and unobserved confounding. Match language to evidence strength.

Do not call an evaluation "proof" when it provides suggestive evidence.

### Use Evaluation Findings For Decisions and address External Validity Before Scaling

Evaluation should inform continuation, scaling, redesign, targeting, funding, enforcement, or termination. Define decision points before results arrive so findings are not ignored when politically inconvenient.

Evaluation without a use plan becomes reporting rather than learning.

An evaluation may estimate effects in one site, provider network, time period, or population. Scaling requires asking whether implementation capacity, participant mix, funding, labor markets, partners, and political conditions are similar enough elsewhere.

Do not assume an effect from a controlled pilot will reproduce under ordinary conditions. External validity should shape scale-up pace and monitoring.

### Protect Evaluation Independence And Ethics and pre-Register The Analysis Plan To Curb Selective Reporting

Evaluation can be distorted by sponsor pressure, selective reporting, weak consent, unfair burden on participants, or withholding beneficial services. Define governance, publication expectations, ethics review where needed, data protection, and conflict management.

Credible evaluation requires trust in both method and independence.

Once results are visible, it is hard to remain impartial: analysts may tune outcome definitions, comparison groups, time windows, or subgroup cuts until something looks significant, and readers cannot tell whether a finding was specified in advance or chosen after seeing the data. Counter this by writing down the evaluation questions, primary outcomes, analysis method, and planned subgroups before results exist, and share that plan with decision makers. Pre-registration does not forbid exploratory analysis, but it separates confirmatory findings from post-hoc patterns, which is exactly the distinction a skeptical reviewer or a future government will probe. An evaluation whose conclusions cannot be tied to a pre-specified plan is more easily dismissed as rationalization.

## Common Traps

- Treating before-and-after change as causal impact.
- Choosing methods for prestige rather than fit.
- Waiting until after implementation to think about evaluation.
- Measuring outcomes without measuring fidelity and take-up.
- Ignoring spillovers and contamination between treatment and comparison groups; making subgroup claims with insufficient data
- Hiding limitations to protect a program; treating a null result as failure without checking implementation
- Ignoring external validity when scaling; producing evaluation findings with no decision pathway
- Scaling from a successful pilot without checking whether context and capacity transfer; letting sponsor pressure or selective reporting weaken evaluation credibility

## Self-Check

- Is the evaluation question specific?
- Is there a credible counterfactual for causal claims?
- Is the method feasible, ethical, and matched to the policy?
- Are baseline, data, comparison, and governance planned before implementation where needed?
- Are fidelity, take-up, dose, and context measured?
- Are equity effects assessed with appropriate power and methods?
- Are limitations and uncertainty stated clearly?
- Are spillovers, attrition, and measurement errors considered?
- Are findings tied to decisions about scale, redesign, funding, or termination?
- Would the causal language survive review by a skeptical evaluator?; are external validity, independence, ethics, and publication expectations addressed?
