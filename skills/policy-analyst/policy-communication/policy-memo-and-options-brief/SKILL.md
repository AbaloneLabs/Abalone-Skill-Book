# Policy Memo And Options Brief

A policy memo is not a research essay. It is a decision document that should make the problem, options, evidence, tradeoffs, recommendation, and implementation implications clear enough for action. Agents often write comprehensive background and then hide the decision. This skill helps the policy analyst produce memos that are concise, balanced, and useful to a decision maker who must choose under constraints.

## Core Rules

### Put The Decision Up Front

State what decision is required, by whom, by when, and why now. If the memo is for information only, say so. If approval, direction, funding, legislation, consultation, or implementation authorization is needed, make the ask explicit.

Do not make the reader infer the decision after pages of context.

### Separate Background From Analysis

Background explains context. Analysis compares options, evidence, risk, cost, equity, legality, and feasibility. Keep background short enough to orient the reader without replacing the decision analysis.

If a fact does not help the decision, move it to an annex or omit it.

### Present Options Symmetrically

For each option, describe what it does, who it affects, cost, authority, benefits, risks, timing, implementation requirements, and stakeholder implications. Avoid giving rich detail only for the preferred option.

A fair options brief lets the reader understand why the recommendation is stronger.

### Make Tradeoffs Visible

A policy memo should not hide the downside of the recommended option. Name tradeoffs around effectiveness, equity, cost, administrative burden, political feasibility, legal risk, rights, and timing. State what the recommendation sacrifices and why that sacrifice is acceptable.

Decision makers need controlled candor, not advocacy disguised as neutrality.

### Use Evidence Proportionately

Include the evidence needed to support the decision, with confidence and limitations. Avoid long literature review inside the main memo. Use citations, footnotes, or annexes where required, but keep the main text focused on implications.

State uncertainty clearly. Do not overclaim because the memo is concise.

### Tailor To The Decision Maker's Authority

Recommendations should match what the recipient can do. A minister, agency head, budget director, regulator, or committee may need different framing and action options. If the decision maker cannot implement an option directly, say what authority or partner is needed.

Memo usefulness depends on institutional fit.

### Include Implementation And Monitoring Implications

Even a short memo should flag who implements, timeline, capacity needs, budget, legal steps, stakeholder engagement, data collection, and early risks. A recommendation without implementation implications is incomplete.

If these details are unknown, state the next analysis needed before commitment.

### Write For Fast, Accurate Reading

Use clear headings, short paragraphs, concrete language, and consistent option labels. Avoid jargon unless the audience expects it. Use tables only when they improve comparison. Do not bury critical caveats in dense prose.

The memo should help a busy reader make a better decision quickly.

### Make Assumptions And Open Questions Visible and preserve A Traceable Decision Record

Policy memos often have to proceed before every fact is settled. State the assumptions that matter, the open questions, and whether they affect the recommendation now or later. Separate assumptions that can be verified before decision from assumptions that must be managed during implementation.

Do not hide uncertainty in order to make the memo feel complete. Decision makers need to know which parts of the advice are firm and which depend on pending evidence, legal review, fiscal scoring, or stakeholder input.

The memo should leave enough record for future readers to know what was recommended, why, under what assumptions, and what was decided. Include version date, decision status, owner, and follow-up where appropriate. If the memo revises earlier advice, explain what changed.

Policy decisions often return months later during implementation, audit, litigation, or public scrutiny. A clear record prevents confusion and protects analytic integrity.

## Common Traps

- Writing a research summary without a decision ask.
- Burying the recommendation.
- Providing extensive background and thin option comparison.
- Making the preferred option look strong by underdeveloping alternatives.
- Hiding the main downside of the recommendation; using evidence citations without explaining policy relevance
- Ignoring authority, budget, and implementation constraints; overloading the memo with technical detail better suited to an annex
- Using vague phrases such as "stakeholders may have concerns" without saying who and why; ending with next steps that do not assign responsibility
- Hiding material assumptions or pending reviews to make advice look more certain; leaving no traceable record of what changed from prior advice

## Self-Check

- Is the decision, decision maker, timing, and ask clear at the start?
- Is background limited to what supports the decision?
- Are options described symmetrically?
- Are tradeoffs and downsides visible?
- Is evidence summarized with confidence and limitations?
- Does the recommendation fit the decision maker's authority?
- Are implementation, budget, legal, stakeholder, and monitoring implications included?
- Is technical detail placed appropriately in annexes or notes?
- Are next steps assigned to owners?
- Could a busy decision maker act after reading the memo?; are assumptions, open questions, version, decision status, and follow-up traceable?
