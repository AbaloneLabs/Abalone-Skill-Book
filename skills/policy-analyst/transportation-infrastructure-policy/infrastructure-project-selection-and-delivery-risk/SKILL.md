# Infrastructure Project Selection And Delivery Risk

Infrastructure decisions commit public money for decades. A project may be politically attractive but low value, under-costed, hard to deliver, or expensive to maintain. Agents often compare ribbon-cutting benefits while missing lifecycle cost, optimism bias, procurement risk, land acquisition, permitting, utility relocation, and governance. This skill helps the policy analyst evaluate infrastructure as a portfolio of public assets, not a list of projects.

## Core Rules

### Define The Problem Before The Project

Start with the service problem: congestion, safety, flood risk, capacity, reliability, access, economic development, emissions, or asset failure. A project should be one possible response, not the assumed solution.

Problem-first analysis prevents project advocacy from substituting for policy judgment.

### Compare Alternatives

Alternatives may include demand management, repair, operations, pricing, smaller upgrades, technology, land-use changes, nature-based solutions, or no-build. Major capital projects should be compared against lower-cost or faster options.

The preferred project should earn selection through evidence.

### Use Lifecycle Cost

Capital cost is only part of the commitment. Operations, maintenance, staffing, energy, renewal, debt service, insurance, and decommissioning can exceed initial cost over time.

Do not approve assets without a plan to operate and maintain them.

### Address Optimism Bias

Large projects often underestimate cost, time, complexity, and risk while overestimating demand and benefits. Reference-class forecasting, independent review, contingencies, and stage gates can reduce bias.

Political urgency should not remove risk realism.

### Evaluate Delivery Capacity

Agency staffing, procurement skills, contractor market, design readiness, utility coordination, right-of-way, permits, community agreements, and governance affect delivery. A funded project can still fail if delivery capacity is absent.

Project selection should consider who can actually build and manage it.

### Prioritize Maintenance And State Of Good Repair

New projects are attractive, but deferred maintenance can create safety risk, service failure, and higher future cost. A portfolio should balance expansion with repair.

Neglecting existing assets is a hidden policy choice.

### Include Distributional Effects

Infrastructure benefits and burdens vary by neighborhood, income, race, disability, business type, and property ownership. Construction disruption, displacement, noise, pollution, access, and land value changes should be analyzed.

Project benefits should not be counted only at aggregate level.

### Design Governance And Accountability

Megaprojects require clear decision rights, reporting, risk registers, change control, public transparency, and escalation. Fragmented governance creates delay and blame diffusion.

Accountability should be designed before problems appear.

### Track Benefits After Completion

Ridership, travel time, safety, reliability, flood reduction, emissions, economic outcomes, and maintenance cost should be measured after delivery. Post-project evaluation improves future selection.

Infrastructure learning requires honest comparison of promised and actual benefits.

### Select Projects Against Transparent Criteria

Infrastructure project selection should compare alternatives using clear criteria: safety, state of repair, access, economic value, climate resilience, emissions, equity, land use, lifecycle cost, deliverability, and fiscal risk. Political visibility or shovel-ready status should not substitute for public value.

The analyst should identify whether the project solves the defined problem or merely expands capacity. Demand forecasts, induced demand, maintenance obligations, right-of-way, utility relocation, environmental review, procurement, and community impact can change the value proposition.

### Manage Delivery Risk Before Commitment

Cost overruns and delays often arise from underestimated scope, weak project governance, inflation, geotechnical conditions, permitting, contractor market limits, change orders, and utility conflicts. The policy should include contingency, independent cost review, risk register, delivery method, accountability, and decision gates.

A project that cannot be maintained after construction may be a liability. Lifecycle funding should be part of selection, not an afterthought.

### Keep Alternatives Alive Long Enough

Project analysis should compare demand management, repair, smaller-scale safety fixes, service changes, and no-build alternatives before a major capital solution becomes inevitable. Alternatives analysis loses value if it occurs after political commitment.

## Common Traps

- Selecting projects for political visibility or readiness without transparent public-value criteria.
- Approving capital projects without lifecycle cost, risk register, contingency, and maintenance plan.

- Starting with a favored project rather than the service problem.
- Ignoring lower-cost operational or demand options.
- Treating capital cost as total cost.
- Accepting optimistic estimates without independent challenge.
- Funding projects that agencies cannot deliver.
- Expanding while existing assets deteriorate.
- Ignoring construction disruption and displacement.
- Hiding risk in vague contingencies.
- Letting fragmented governance obscure accountability.
- Measuring success by groundbreaking or opening day.
- Failing to evaluate actual benefits after completion.

## Self-Check

- Were repair, demand management, operational, smaller-scale, and no-build alternatives compared before commitment?

- Are projects compared against transparent criteria tied to the problem they are meant to solve?
- Are cost, schedule, procurement, maintenance, environmental, and governance risks assessed before commitment?

- Is the underlying service problem clearly defined?
- Are alternatives compared before project selection?
- Are lifecycle operations and maintenance costs included?
- Is optimism bias addressed through independent or reference-class review?
- Is delivery capacity realistic?
- Is state of good repair considered alongside expansion?
- Are distributional benefits and burdens analyzed?
- Are governance, reporting, and change control clear?
- Are post-completion outcomes measured?
- Would the project still rank highly if costs rise and benefits are delayed?
