# Fiscal Impact And Budget Scoring

Fiscal analysis answers a different question from social impact: what does the policy do to public budgets, revenues, obligations, and fiscal risk? A policy with strong social benefits can be unaffordable under a budget cap; a policy that saves one agency money can increase costs elsewhere. Agents often produce a headline cost without separating one-time, recurring, capital, administrative, and transfer costs. This skill helps the policy analyst prepare fiscal estimates that are decision-useful and transparent.

## Core Rules

### Define The Budget Perspective

State whose budget is affected: central government, state, local government, agency, department, public insurer, school district, regulator, public enterprise, or grant recipient. Fiscal impact can shift across levels of government or agencies.

Do not present "government cost" as a single number if the proposal creates unfunded mandates or cross-agency savings. Budget authority and political feasibility depend on where the cost lands.

### Separate Cost Categories

Break out direct program costs, transfers, tax expenditures, capital costs, operating costs, staffing, IT systems, procurement, administration, outreach, enforcement, evaluation, facilities, debt service, and contingency. Separate one-time startup costs from recurring costs.

A policy can look cheap if administrative and system costs are omitted. It can also look expensive if one-time setup is confused with steady-state operations.

### Estimate Take-Up And Caseload Carefully

Many fiscal estimates depend on who participates, qualifies, complies, or claims benefits. Take-up may be lower than eligible population because of awareness, stigma, complexity, documentation, language, or access barriers. It may be higher than expected if incentives attract new participants.

Document caseload assumptions and test high and low scenarios. Avoid assuming perfect targeting or constant participation.

### Include Revenue Effects And Offsets Honestly

Policies may change tax revenue, fees, fines, premiums, federal matching funds, grants, reimbursements, avoided costs, or recoveries. Offsets should be included only when they are plausible, legally available, and budget-recognized. Avoid counting the same savings in multiple places.

Some benefits are real but not cashable. A reduced jail population may not save money unless facilities, staffing, or contracts can actually be reduced.

### Track Timing And Appropriation Rules

Budget decisions depend on fiscal years, obligation authority, cash flow, accrual rules, capital budgets, carry-forward authority, matching requirements, procurement lead time, and grant periods. A policy may need funding before benefits or savings arrive.

Show multi-year profiles rather than a single annualized number. Identify when peak cost occurs and when savings, if any, materialize.

### Account For Administrative Capacity

New policies often require eligibility workers, analysts, inspectors, auditors, call centers, IT changes, data sharing, legal support, procurement, training, and management. Underfunding administration can cause delays, poor take-up, fraud risk, or service failure.

Do not treat administration as overhead to minimize without considering delivery risk.

### Identify Fiscal Risk And Uncertainty

Fiscal estimates should include uncertainty from caseload, price inflation, wage growth, vendor costs, litigation, economic conditions, behavior response, enrollment, utilization, and implementation delay. Entitlements and open-ended subsidies carry different fiscal risk than capped grants.

If the proposal creates uncapped exposure, say so. If a cap limits cost but may ration service, say that too.

### Reconcile Fiscal Cost With Policy Design

Budget scoring should feed design choices: eligibility, benefit level, phase-in, cap, indexation, matching formula, sunset, pilot, administrative funding, or evaluation. Fiscal analysis is not an after-the-fact price tag.

When costs exceed affordability, propose design alternatives rather than only reporting the gap.

### Identify Budget Games And Cost Shifts

Policies can look affordable by delaying costs, shifting them to local governments, relying on temporary funds, underfunding administration, using optimistic savings, or moving obligations off budget. These choices may be politically attractive but operationally risky.

Name cost shifts and timing devices explicitly. A balanced first-year score can still create unfunded future obligations.

## Common Traps

- Reporting a single cost number without categories or assumptions.
- Ignoring costs shifted to local governments, agencies, providers, or beneficiaries.
- Treating eligible population as actual take-up.
- Counting avoided costs that cannot be realized as budget savings.
- Omitting administration, IT, enforcement, outreach, and evaluation costs.
- Confusing startup cost with recurring cost; ignoring fiscal-year timing and appropriation rules
- Hiding open-ended exposure behind average cost estimates; treating budget caps as harmless when they may ration access
- Failing to connect fiscal findings back to policy design; hiding future or local costs through timing, temporary funds, or off-budget obligations

## Self-Check

- Is the budget perspective and affected public entity clear?
- Are one-time, recurring, capital, transfer, administrative, enforcement, and evaluation costs separated?
- Are caseload, take-up, unit cost, and participation assumptions explicit?
- Are revenue effects and offsets plausible, legal, and not double-counted?
- Is the fiscal profile shown over multiple years?
- Are appropriation rules, funding sources, and timing constraints considered?
- Is administrative capacity funded enough to deliver the policy?
- Are fiscal risks and uncertainty scenarios shown?
- Are capped and uncapped exposures distinguished?
- Does the fiscal analysis inform design choices rather than merely price a preferred option?; are cost shifts, temporary funding, and future obligations identified?
