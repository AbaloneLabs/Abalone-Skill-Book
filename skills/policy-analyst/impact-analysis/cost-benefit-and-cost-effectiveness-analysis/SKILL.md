# Cost Benefit And Cost Effectiveness Analysis

Economic appraisal can clarify policy choices, but it can also create false precision. Costs may be visible while benefits are diffuse, delayed, or hard to monetize. Some impacts should not be reduced to money without ethical caution. Agents often cite benefit-cost ratios without checking assumptions, distribution, uncertainty, or whether cost-effectiveness would be more appropriate. This skill helps the policy analyst use economic analysis as disciplined evidence rather than as a mechanical answer.

## Core Rules

### Choose The Right Appraisal Frame

Cost-benefit analysis compares monetized social costs and benefits. Cost-effectiveness analysis compares the cost of achieving a defined outcome. Fiscal analysis focuses on government budgets. Value-for-money assessment may include qualitative criteria. Choose the frame that fits the policy question.

If benefits cannot be credibly monetized, cost-effectiveness or multi-criteria analysis may be more honest than forcing weak dollar values. Explain what the chosen frame includes and excludes.

### Define Perspective And Scope

State whether the analysis takes a societal, government, agency, household, industry, regulated-party, or beneficiary perspective. A policy can save government money while shifting costs to households, or impose costs on firms while creating social benefits.

Scope should include direct, indirect, administrative, compliance, opportunity, transition, enforcement, and maintenance costs where material. Benefits may include avoided harm, productivity, quality of life, environmental improvement, time savings, resilience, or rights protection.

### Build A Credible Baseline

Compare options against the no-new-policy baseline, not against current conditions frozen in time. Include expected trends, existing commitments, demographic change, market adaptation, and already approved reforms. If the baseline is wrong, net benefits will be wrong.

Document baseline assumptions clearly. Decision makers need to know whether benefits come from the policy or from changes likely to happen anyway.

### Avoid Monetization Overreach

Some benefits, such as dignity, rights, trust, cultural value, biodiversity, or democratic participation, may be difficult or inappropriate to monetize fully. Monetization can still be useful for some components, but non-monetized impacts should remain visible.

Do not set unmonetized effects to zero. Present them separately and explain how they affect the recommendation.

### Treat Discounting And Time Horizon Transparently

Policies with long-term benefits are sensitive to discount rates and time horizons. Infrastructure, climate, early childhood, prevention, health, and education policies may look very different under different assumptions. Use required public-sector guidance where applicable and show sensitivity.

Do not hide long-term value or long-term cost by selecting a convenient time horizon.

### Include Distribution And Incidence

A positive net benefit can still harm a vulnerable group or impose concentrated costs. Identify who pays, who benefits, when, and where. Cost-benefit ratios do not replace equity analysis.

If distributional weights are used, explain them. If not used, still show distributional effects separately.

### Use Sensitivity And Scenario Analysis

Test key assumptions: effect size, take-up, unit cost, compliance rate, administrative cost, displacement, discount rate, avoided cost, behavior response, and implementation delay. Identify break-even points where the recommendation changes.

Single-point estimates are rarely enough. A policy whose case depends on optimistic assumptions should be labeled that way.

### Distinguish Real Savings From Transfers

Government savings, household savings, tax revenue, subsidies, fines, and transfers affect budgets and incentives, but they are not always net social benefits. Avoid double counting. A transfer from one group to another may be distributionally important without increasing total social welfare.

Clarify whether the analysis is about economic efficiency, fiscal affordability, or redistribution.

### Account For Implementation Failure Risk

Expected benefits should reflect realistic delivery, not ideal statutory design. Delays, low take-up, weak enforcement, procurement failure, staff shortages, or poor compliance can reduce benefits and increase costs. If the appraisal assumes full implementation, state that assumption and test a lower-performance scenario.

Economic appraisal should value the policy as it is likely to operate, not only as designed.

## Common Traps

- Presenting a benefit-cost ratio without showing assumptions.
- Monetizing what is easy and ignoring what is important.
- Confusing fiscal savings with social benefits.
- Double-counting avoided costs, transfers, or productivity benefits.
- Using a baseline that ignores already planned changes.
- Choosing a time horizon or discount rate that favors the preferred option; reporting net benefits without distributional incidence
- Treating unmonetized rights, dignity, or environmental impacts as zero; failing to test sensitivity to effect size, cost, and take-up
- Using cost-benefit analysis when cost-effectiveness would better fit the objective; assuming full implementation success when delivery risk is material

## Self-Check

- Is the appraisal frame appropriate to the decision question?
- Are perspective, scope, included costs, and included benefits explicit?
- Is the baseline credible and forward-looking?
- Are non-monetized impacts visible rather than implicitly zero?
- Are time horizon and discount rate justified and tested?
- Is distributional incidence shown separately from aggregate net benefit?
- Are sensitivity, scenario, and break-even analyses included?
- Are transfers distinguished from net social benefits?
- Are key assumptions traceable to evidence or clearly labeled?
- Would the recommendation still stand under plausible less favorable assumptions?; are implementation failure, delay, low take-up, and weak compliance tested?
