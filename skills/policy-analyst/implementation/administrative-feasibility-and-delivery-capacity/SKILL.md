# Administrative Feasibility And Delivery Capacity

Policies fail when analysis assumes institutions can do what the statute, memo, or strategy says. Administrative feasibility is not a minor implementation detail; it determines whether benefits reach people, rules are enforced, and public trust survives. Agents often recommend policies based on desirability while ignoring staff, systems, procurement, training, governance, and operational bottlenecks. This skill helps the policy analyst test whether the delivery system can actually carry the policy.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Operating Model

Describe how the policy will work in practice: who applies, who decides, who verifies, who pays, who enforces, who answers questions, who handles appeals, who collects data, who manages vendors, and who owns performance. A policy with no operating model is still a concept.

Do not rely on phrases such as "the agency will implement" without naming units, tasks, and handoffs.

### Assess Staffing And Skills

Consider staff numbers, skills, training, supervision, turnover, unions, workload, regional coverage, language capacity, technical expertise, legal support, procurement staff, data analysts, inspectors, and call-center capacity. A policy can require specialized skills that the agency does not have.

If staffing is insufficient, identify whether the solution is hiring, training, outsourcing, simplifying policy design, phasing, or narrowing scope.

### Test Systems And Data Capacity

New policies may require application portals, case management, payment systems, identity verification, data sharing, reporting dashboards, privacy controls, cybersecurity, analytics, and integration with legacy systems. IT timelines often exceed policy timelines.

Do not treat system changes as automatic. Identify whether temporary manual workarounds are acceptable and what risks they create.

### Review Procurement And Vendor Dependencies

Delivery may depend on contractors, grantees, technology vendors, evaluators, inspectors, payment processors, or service providers. Procurement rules, market capacity, contract management, conflicts, data access, and vendor performance affect feasibility.

If the provider market is thin, a policy may need capacity-building before scale. Procurement lead time should be part of the policy timeline.

### Consider Local And Partner Capacity

National or central policy often relies on local governments, schools, hospitals, nonprofits, courts, employers, or community organizations. These partners may face resource constraints, different priorities, legal limits, or uneven geography.

Do not assume mandates create capacity. If local actors must deliver, assess their incentives and support needs.

### Identify Bottlenecks And Failure Points

Map the user journey and administrative workflow to find likely bottlenecks: eligibility verification, document collection, inspections, approvals, payment, appeals, technical support, language access, enforcement, or data reporting. Ask what happens when volume spikes.

Capacity analysis should include stress points, not only average workload.

### Align Policy Complexity With Capacity

Complex eligibility, exemptions, formulas, reporting, or enforcement may improve targeting but overwhelm delivery. Simpler designs may be less precise but more reliable. The analyst should make this tradeoff explicit.

If administrative capacity is low, policy design should reduce friction or invest in capacity before expecting results.

### Build Capacity Measures Into The Recommendation

Feasibility analysis should lead to staffing estimates, IT plans, training, guidance, budget, procurement timeline, governance, risk controls, and implementation metrics. If capacity gaps remain, recommend pilot, phased rollout, narrower eligibility, or delayed launch.

Do not call an option feasible because it is legally authorized. Feasibility means deliverable.

### Check Exception Handling And Appeals Capacity and test Capacity Under Peak Demand

Even well-designed policies produce edge cases: missing documents, contested eligibility, data mismatches, language needs, disability accommodations, hardship claims, fraud flags, provider disputes, or technology failures. Delivery capacity includes the ability to resolve exceptions quickly and fairly.

If exception handling is under-resourced, the policy may produce backlogs, inequity, litigation, or front-line improvisation. Build appeal routes, escalation authority, service standards, and staffing into feasibility analysis.

Launches, deadlines, media coverage, economic shocks, disasters, or enforcement campaigns can create demand far above average volume. Systems that work under normal load may fail at peak. Estimate surge scenarios and decide whether to stagger rollout, add temporary staff, extend deadlines, or create triage rules.

Average workload is not enough for feasibility.

## Common Traps

- Treating implementation as a separate problem after the recommendation.
- Using "agency will administer" without an operating model.
- Ignoring staffing, training, and supervision needs.
- Assuming legacy IT systems can absorb new rules.
- Missing procurement lead time and vendor market limits; imposing duties on local partners without resources
- Designing complex eligibility that overwhelms staff and users; ignoring volume spikes and appeals workload
- Measuring capacity only by budget, not skills and systems; recommending full launch when pilot or phasing is needed
- Ignoring exception handling, appeals, and accommodation workload; sizing capacity for average demand while launch demand or crisis demand is much higher

## Self-Check

- Is the operating model described in concrete tasks and owners?
- Are staffing, skills, training, workload, and supervision needs assessed?
- Are IT, data, privacy, cybersecurity, and integration requirements identified?
- Are procurement and vendor dependencies realistic?
- Are local and partner capacities assessed rather than assumed?
- Are bottlenecks, handoffs, and volume spikes mapped?
- Is policy complexity matched to administrative capacity?
- Are capacity investments included in budget and timeline?
- Are pilot, phase-in, or simplification options considered if capacity is weak?
- Would implementers recognize the feasibility assessment as operationally realistic?; are exception handling, appeals, accommodations, and surge demand included?
