# Statutory Authority And Policy Constraints

Policy analysis must respect the legal frame. A policy can be evidence-based, popular, and affordable but still unlawful or vulnerable if the decision maker lacks authority, violates rights, ignores procedure, or exceeds delegated powers. Agents often add a brief "legal review needed" note without integrating legal constraints into option design. This skill helps the policy analyst identify legal boundaries early while leaving legal conclusions to qualified counsel when needed.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Source Of Authority

Determine whether authority comes from statute, constitution, regulation, charter, executive order, appropriation, treaty, contract, delegation, agency rule, or common-law power. Name the actor authorized to act and what action is authorized: regulate, spend, tax, inspect, license, collect data, enforce, issue guidance, or enter agreements.

Do not assume a good public purpose creates authority. Public agencies act through powers granted to them.

### Distinguish Mandatory, Permissive, And Prohibited Action

Some laws require action, some permit action, and some prohibit action. A policy may be discretionary within a statutory purpose, mandated by a deadline, or barred by rights, preemption, procurement rules, privacy law, labor law, or fiscal restrictions.

The difference affects recommendation language. "Must," "may," and "cannot" should not be used casually.

### Check Procedural Requirements

Policy adoption may require notice and comment, consultation, impact assessment, environmental review, privacy assessment, legislative vote, public hearing, collective bargaining, tribal consultation, regulatory analysis, budget approval, or publication. Procedure can determine legal validity.

Do not treat procedure as bureaucracy only. Procedures often protect legitimacy, evidence, rights, and affected communities.

### Account For Rights And Equality Obligations

Policies may affect due process, privacy, speech, property, disability accommodation, equal protection, non-discrimination, Indigenous rights, labor rights, migration status, or access to essential services. Rights obligations can shape eligibility, enforcement, data use, appeals, exemptions, and safeguards.

If rights are implicated, escalate to legal experts and design safeguards rather than waiting for late legal veto.

### Consider Jurisdiction And Preemption

Authority may be divided across federal, state, local, tribal, supranational, sectoral, or international institutions. A lower-level policy may be preempted or constrained by higher law. Cross-border activity may trigger additional requirements.

Map which jurisdiction controls the lever, which can coordinate, and which may challenge the policy.

### Evaluate Litigation And Review Risk

Court or tribunal risk may arise from procedural defects, irrationality, discrimination, lack of evidence, arbitrariness, improper purpose, inadequate consultation, takings, proportionality, or exceeding authority. The analyst should not predict legal outcomes beyond competence, but should identify risk factors.

Legal risk is not only whether government wins in court. Litigation can delay implementation, create uncertainty, and change political feasibility.

### Integrate Legal Constraints Into Options

If a preferred option is legally risky, design alternatives: narrower scope, clearer statutory basis, phased rulemaking, improved record, stronger appeals, exemptions, sunset clause, legislative amendment, or intergovernmental agreement. Legal constraints should shape design, not appear as a footnote.

If no lawful path exists under current authority, say so and propose the authority change required.

### Document Legal Assumptions And Referrals and watch Guidance That Functions Like Binding Policy

Policy memos should distinguish analyst assumptions from legal advice. Note where counsel, legislative drafters, privacy officers, procurement counsel, or rights specialists must review. Keep legal uncertainty visible for decision makers.

Do not present uncertain law as settled because the recommendation depends on it.

Agencies may prefer guidance, circulars, manuals, FAQs, or informal letters because they are faster than formal rules. But guidance can create legal risk if staff or regulated parties experience it as mandatory, especially when it changes rights, eligibility, enforcement, or obligations.

Ask whether the instrument actually binds behavior in practice. If so, legal process, consultation, publication, or rulemaking requirements may be triggered.

### Identify Remedies If Authority Is Weak and check Fiscal And Appropriation Authority Early

When authority is uncertain or weak, the analyst should not simply reject the option. Identify possible remedies: seek legislative amendment, use a narrower delegated power, issue nonbinding guidance, partner with another authority, fund voluntary programs, pilot under existing authority, or redesign eligibility.

This helps decision makers understand legal pathways rather than only legal obstacles.

Many policy options fail not on regulatory power but on money rules. Spending authority may be limited by appropriation, earmarking restrictions, debt ceilings, anti-deficiency rules, fiscal-year boundaries, matching requirements, or limits on multi-year commitments. A policy that is otherwise lawful can stall because no funded mandate exists, because the chosen funding source is restricted, or because continued funding depends on a future legislative vote the agency cannot guarantee. Check whether the proposal has actual money behind it, who controls that money, and whether the funding is one-time or recurring. Treating fiscal authority as a separate late-stage budget question is a common reason that legally sound policies never reach implementation, so raise it alongside statutory authority from the start.

## Common Traps

- Assuming policy desirability creates legal authority.
- Treating "legal review needed" as enough analysis.
- Confusing discretionary authority with a mandate.
- Ignoring procedural steps that determine validity.
- Considering rights impacts only after the policy is designed; missing jurisdiction, preemption, or shared authority
- Underestimating litigation delay even if government might ultimately win; presenting guidance as nonbinding when it operates like a rule
- Ignoring appropriation or fiscal law constraints; hiding legal uncertainty to protect a preferred option
- Treating informal guidance as risk-free when it changes practical obligations; stopping at "no authority" without identifying lawful alternative pathways

## Self-Check

- Is the source and holder of authority identified?
- Are mandatory, permissive, and prohibited actions distinguished?
- Are required procedures and approvals mapped?
- Are rights, equality, privacy, due process, and accommodation issues flagged?
- Are jurisdiction, preemption, and shared-authority issues considered?
- Are litigation and review risks identified without overclaiming legal conclusions?
- Are legal constraints integrated into option design?
- Are authority changes identified where current authority is insufficient?
- Are legal assumptions and referrals documented?; would qualified counsel see the policy analysis as legally aware rather than legally conclusive?
- Is informal guidance assessed for whether it functions like a binding rule?; are lawful alternative pathways identified when authority is weak?
