# Digital Service Access And Administrative Burden

Digital services can reduce burden, but they can also exclude people who lack devices, broadband, literacy, language access, disability accommodations, stable identity documents, or trust. Agents often treat online conversion as modernization and miss who is pushed out. This skill helps the policy analyst evaluate digital service design by real completion, accessibility, and administrative burden rather than transaction volume alone.

## Core Rules

### Map The User Journey

Digital service includes awareness, eligibility check, account creation, identity verification, form completion, document upload, payment, status tracking, notices, appeal, renewal, and help. Failure can occur at any step.

Analyze completion and abandonment, not only portal visits.

### Preserve Non-Digital And Assisted Channels

Some users need phone, in-person, paper, community navigator, or assisted digital support. Removing alternatives can reduce access for people with disability, low literacy, limited English, unstable housing, or no broadband.

Digital-first should not become digital-only by default.

### Test Identity Verification Burden

Identity proofing can fail for people with thin credit files, name changes, immigration documents, homelessness, domestic violence safety needs, youth, elderly users, or people without smartphones. Fraud controls must be balanced with access.

A secure system that eligible users cannot pass is a policy failure.

### Include Accessibility And Language

Services should work with screen readers, keyboard navigation, captions, plain language, mobile devices, translation, interpretation, and low-bandwidth connections. Accessibility is not a final visual check.

Design for the people most likely to need the service.

### Reduce Documentation And Repetition

Digital forms often replicate paper burden. Use prefilled data, consented data reuse, plain questions, save-and-return, document alternatives, and status visibility where lawful.

Digitizing complexity is not simplification.

### Protect Privacy And Security

Digital systems collect sensitive data and create new risks: breaches, unauthorized sharing, vendor access, tracking, and secondary use. Data minimization, retention limits, access controls, and audit logs should be built in.

Convenience does not justify uncontrolled data collection.

### Design Help Into The Service

Users need clear error messages, live help, callback, escalation, community support, and appeal routes. Help should be available before users abandon or miss deadlines.

Support is part of service design, not an afterthought.

### Monitor Equity In Completion

Track starts, completions, denials, time to complete, document failures, identity failures, help requests, appeals, and abandonment by demographic and channel where lawful and privacy-protective.

A high digital completion rate can hide who never entered.

### Plan For System Failure

Outages, cyber incidents, vendor downtime, peak demand, and broken integrations can block essential services. Continuity plans should include alternative processing and deadline relief.

Digital dependency requires resilience planning.

### Measure Burden From The User's Full Journey

Digital service policy should map the entire user journey: eligibility discovery, identity proofing, application, document upload, language support, mobile access, assistive technology, status updates, error correction, appeals, renewal, and help when the system fails. A portal can look efficient to the agency while shifting work to people with low bandwidth, unstable housing, limited English, disabilities, low literacy, or shared devices.

The analyst should distinguish digitization from simplification. Moving a confusing paper form online can preserve the same burden while making exclusion less visible. Strong policy removes unnecessary questions, pre-fills data where lawful, supports multiple channels, and tracks where users abandon the process.

### Preserve Human And Offline Backstops

Some users will need phone, in-person, paper, community-based, or assisted digital support. Backstops are not merely courtesy; they protect access, due process, and continuity during outages, fraud locks, device loss, or identity proofing failure. The analysis should identify who funds and staffs those backstops.

Digital service metrics should include successful completion, time to benefit, error correction, appeal success, support wait time, and user burden, not only portal visits or agency processing speed. A service that processes fewer applications faster may be excluding eligible users.

## Common Traps

- Calling a service modern because it is online while the user burden remains or increases.
- Removing offline or human support before proving digital access works for high-need users.

- Treating online availability as access.
- Removing in-person or phone support too quickly.
- Using identity proofing that excludes eligible people.
- Translating content but leaving workflows inaccessible.
- Digitizing long forms without reducing documentation burden.
- Collecting more data because digital storage is easy.
- Hiding help behind chatbots that cannot resolve complex cases.
- Measuring portal traffic instead of successful outcomes.
- Ignoring people who never start online applications.
- Launching without outage and continuity planning.
- Treating vendor design choices as neutral.

## Self-Check

- Does the analysis map the full user journey and identify burden shifted from agency to user?
- Are human, offline, accessibility, language, and outage backstops preserved and funded?

- Is the full digital journey mapped through completion and appeal?
- Are non-digital and assisted channels preserved where needed?
- Are identity verification failures and alternatives addressed?
- Are accessibility, language, mobile, and low-bandwidth needs designed in?
- Is documentation burden reduced rather than digitized?
- Are privacy, security, retention, and secondary-use limits included?
- Is help available at points of failure?
- Are equity patterns in completion and abandonment monitored?
- Is there a continuity plan for outages?
- Would the most burdened eligible user be able to complete the service?
