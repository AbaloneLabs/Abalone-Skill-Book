# Policy Problem Definition And Scope

Policy work fails early when the problem is framed too casually. A request such as "fix housing affordability," "reduce crime," "improve access," or "regulate AI" can hide multiple problems with different causes, affected populations, jurisdictions, and tradeoffs. Agents often jump to policy options before defining the problem precisely enough to test whether an intervention fits. This skill helps the policy analyst turn a broad concern into an analyzable policy problem without prematurely narrowing the answer.

## Core Rules

### Separate The Condition From The Policy Problem

Start by describing the observable condition: who is affected, what is happening, where, when, how often, and with what consequences. Then identify why this condition is a policy problem rather than only a private, market, administrative, or political concern. A high price, service delay, environmental harm, health gap, or safety risk becomes a policy problem when collective action, public authority, public resources, rights, externalities, market failures, equity obligations, or institutional duties are implicated.

Do not assume the existence of a problem from public attention alone. A policy problem needs evidence of harm, unmet need, failure, risk, or opportunity, and a plausible role for public action.

### Define Population, Place, Time, And Unit Of Analysis

Policy problems change when the population or geography changes. "Unemployment" may mean youth unemployment, long-term unemployment, regional job loss, disabled workers, displaced workers, or informal workers. "Housing" may mean renters, first-time buyers, homeless households, older adults, developers, or local governments. State the unit of analysis clearly.

Time matters as well. A temporary spike, chronic trend, seasonal gap, long-term structural issue, or looming future risk needs different evidence and interventions. The analyst should not blend emergency response, structural reform, and prevention unless the scope explicitly covers all three.

### Distinguish Symptoms, Drivers, And Constraints

List the visible symptoms, candidate causes, and constraints separately. For example, overcrowded emergency departments may reflect primary care access, insurance coverage, staffing shortages, discharge delays, behavioral health gaps, aging population, or poor care coordination. If the analyst treats the symptom as the cause, policy options will be shallow.

Use causal language cautiously. Early scoping should identify hypotheses and evidence needs, not declare root causes before analysis. Mark uncertain drivers and avoid building a solution around the easiest-to-measure factor.

### Identify The Baseline And Counterfactual

A problem definition should state what happens if no new policy is adopted. The baseline includes current law, existing programs, market trends, budget commitments, demographic change, enforcement practice, and expected future conditions. The counterfactual prevents analysts from attributing every future improvement or decline to the proposed policy.

If the baseline is unclear, the policy problem may be overstated or understated. A policy that appears urgent under a static baseline may look different if existing reforms are already phasing in.

### Surface Competing Problem Frames

Different actors may define the same issue differently. A labor shortage can be framed as a skills gap, wage problem, immigration issue, childcare issue, regional development problem, working-condition problem, or automation transition. Each frame privileges different options and stakeholders.

The analyst should name competing frames and explain why the chosen frame is used. Ignoring frames makes the analysis look neutral while embedding a hidden political or value judgment.

### Bound The Analysis Without Hiding Spillovers

Scope must be narrow enough to analyze, but not so narrow that it hides important spillovers. A policy on school discipline may affect education outcomes, disability rights, racial disparities, safety, teacher workload, juvenile justice, and family trust. Decide what is in scope, what is out of scope, and what must still be acknowledged.

Boundaries should be justified by mandate, decision timeline, data availability, jurisdiction, materiality, or client need. Do not exclude inconvenient effects merely to simplify the memo.

### Define The Policy Lever Under Consideration

Clarify whether the analysis concerns legislation, regulation, budget allocation, program design, enforcement, tax policy, grants, procurement, public communication, service delivery, intergovernmental coordination, or voluntary guidance. Problem framing should match the available lever.

If the decision maker lacks authority over the main driver, say so. A city may not control national interest rates, but it may control zoning, permitting, local subsidies, taxes, service delivery, or advocacy.

### Write A Problem Statement That Can Guide Options

A strong problem statement identifies the affected population, harm, scale, drivers, jurisdiction, baseline, and why current arrangements are inadequate. It should be specific enough to reject irrelevant options and broad enough to include plausible alternatives.

Weak problem statements are slogans. Strong ones create analytical discipline.

## Common Traps

- Treating a political demand or media headline as a fully defined policy problem.
- Defining the problem as the absence of a preferred solution, such as "lack of subsidies" or "need for a ban."
- Mixing several problems into one statement without explaining how they connect.
- Using national averages when the affected group or geography is more specific.
- Confusing correlation, symptom, and cause during early scoping.
- Ignoring the baseline and assuming current conditions will continue unchanged.
- Hiding value choices inside supposedly neutral framing; scoping the analysis so narrowly that distributional, fiscal, legal, or implementation spillovers disappear
- Accepting the client's preferred frame without testing alternative frames; proceeding to options before the decision maker's authority is clear

## Self-Check

- Is the observable condition separated from the policy problem?
- Are affected population, geography, time horizon, and unit of analysis explicit?
- Are symptoms, hypothesized drivers, and constraints distinguished?
- Is the baseline or no-new-policy scenario stated?
- Are competing frames identified and the chosen frame justified?
- Are scope boundaries explained without hiding material spillovers?
- Is the relevant public authority or policy lever clear?
- Does the problem statement avoid embedding a preferred solution?
- Are uncertainties and evidence gaps visible?
- Would the problem statement help reject options that do not address the actual issue?
