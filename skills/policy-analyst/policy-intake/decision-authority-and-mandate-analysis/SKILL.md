# Decision Authority And Mandate Analysis

Policy recommendations are only useful when they are addressed to an actor who can lawfully and practically act. Analysts often produce strong ideas that fail because the decision maker lacks statutory authority, budget control, regulatory jurisdiction, implementation capacity, or political mandate. This skill helps the policy analyst identify who can decide, what they can decide, and what constraints shape the recommendation before analysis goes too far.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Actual Decision Maker

Start by naming the person, body, or institution that will use the analysis: minister, mayor, agency head, legislature, regulator, budget office, council, board, court-adjacent body, interagency committee, donor, or program manager. Then identify whether the analysis is for decision, advice, negotiation, implementation, oversight, or public communication.

Do not write for an abstract government. A recommendation to "government should" is weaker than a recommendation to a named authority with known powers and constraints.

### Map Formal Authority

Determine what law, regulation, charter, appropriation, executive order, delegation, contract, program rule, or administrative policy authorizes action. Identify whether the decision maker can create rules, spend funds, change eligibility, enforce standards, collect data, procure services, issue guidance, coordinate agencies, or request legislation.

If authority is uncertain, flag it for legal review. A policy option that exceeds authority may still be worth noting as a legislative option, but it should not be presented as immediately implementable.

### Distinguish Authority, Influence, And Responsibility

An institution may be responsible for an outcome without controlling all drivers. A health department may be accountable for public health while housing, labor, education, and transportation agencies affect health determinants. A city may influence emissions while national energy policy matters. Separate direct authority from indirect influence and advocacy.

This distinction prevents analysts from overpromising and helps identify partnerships, intergovernmental coordination, or escalation needs.

### Understand The Mandate And Decision Question

Clarify what has been asked: diagnose a problem, compare options, justify a budget bid, draft regulation, evaluate a program, respond to a crisis, prepare a legislative briefing, or recommend implementation design. A mandate may include scope limits, political commitments, excluded options, fiscal ceilings, legal constraints, stakeholder commitments, or timing.

Ask whether the mandate is analytical, political, legal, or operational. Hidden mandates can shape what recommendation is acceptable.

### Identify Required Approvals And Veto Points

Policy decisions often require cabinet approval, legislative vote, agency clearance, budget sign-off, legal review, procurement review, union consultation, public comment, intergovernmental agreement, regulator approval, or board authorization. These steps affect timing and option design.

A technically strong option may fail if it ignores veto points. Map who can approve, delay, amend, block, litigate, underfund, or undermine implementation.

### Check Budget And Resource Authority

Authority to announce a policy is not the same as authority to fund it. Identify whether funds are appropriated, reprogrammable, grant-funded, fee-funded, donor-funded, or dependent on future budget cycles. Also check staff, data, systems, enforcement, procurement, and delivery capacity.

Recommendations should distinguish no-cost legal changes, administrative reallocations, funded program expansions, and options requiring new appropriations.

### Account For Timing And Procedural Requirements

Policy routes have different timelines. Emergency orders, guidance, rulemaking, legislation, procurement, budget bids, pilots, and negotiated agreements each require different steps. Public comment, consultation, impact assessment, environmental review, privacy review, or collective bargaining can be mandatory.

Do not recommend a route that cannot meet the decision deadline unless the analysis states that tradeoff.

### Shape Recommendations To The Decision Authority

Once authority is mapped, tailor recommendations. If the decision maker can act directly, specify the action. If they need legislation, recommend a legislative path. If authority is shared, recommend coordination. If authority is absent, recommend referral, advocacy, or reframing.

Good policy analysis respects institutional reality without becoming passive. It shows what can be done now and what requires a different mandate.

## Common Traps

- Addressing recommendations to "government" without naming the acting authority.
- Assuming responsibility for an outcome implies control over the main levers.
- Presenting legislative changes as if an agency can implement them alone.
- Ignoring budget authority, staff capacity, data systems, or procurement lead time.
- Missing approval gates, veto points, public comment, or required consultation.
- Treating a political promise as legal authority.
- Failing to distinguish immediate administrative options from longer statutory reforms; designing options around a mandate that was never clarified
- Overlooking shared jurisdiction or intergovernmental constraints; hiding authority uncertainty instead of routing it for legal or governance review

## Self-Check

- Is the actual decision maker or decision body named?
- Is formal authority identified and separated from influence or responsibility?
- Is the mandate and decision question clear?
- Are statutory, regulatory, budget, and administrative constraints visible?
- Are required approvals, veto points, and procedural steps mapped?
- Is resource authority distinguished from announcement authority?
- Are timing constraints matched to the policy route?
- Are options labeled as immediate, delegated, legislative, budget-dependent, or coordination-dependent?
- Are authority uncertainties escalated for legal or governance review?
- Would the recommendation be actionable by the named decision maker?
