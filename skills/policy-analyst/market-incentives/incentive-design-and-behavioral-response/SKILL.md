# Incentive Design And Behavioral Response

Incentives are not magic levers. People and organizations respond to them through constraints, beliefs, habits, risk, trust, liquidity, administrative burden, and strategic behavior. Agents often assume a subsidy increases desired behavior or a penalty deters unwanted behavior without checking whether the target actor can respond, will notice the incentive, or can game it. This skill helps the policy analyst design incentives around realistic behavior and unintended response.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Target Behavior

Define the exact behavior the incentive should change: applying, buying, investing, hiring, reporting, conserving, complying, switching providers, adopting technology, attending appointments, reducing emissions, or serving higher-need users. Vague goals such as "encourage innovation" or "improve quality" must be translated into observable behavior.

If the behavior cannot be described, the incentive cannot be measured or designed.

### Know The Decision Maker

The person who receives the incentive may not be the person who changes behavior. A landlord may receive a subsidy while tenants bear conditions. A provider may receive performance payments while front-line staff do the work. A household may want to respond but lack credit, time, transportation, language access, or trust.

Map who decides, who pays, who benefits, who bears risk, and who administers the transaction.

### Estimate Responsiveness

Incentive size should reflect likely elasticity or behavioral responsiveness. Some behaviors are highly price-sensitive; others are constrained by fixed costs, habits, regulation, capacity, stigma, or lack of alternatives. A small nudge may work for low-friction choices, but not for structural barriers.

Use evidence, pilots, or sensitivity analysis when responsiveness is uncertain.

### Check Timing And Liquidity

Upfront rebates, delayed tax credits, monthly payments, penalties after inspection, and performance bonuses all affect behavior differently. Low-income households, small businesses, and local providers may be unable to wait for reimbursement or finance upfront costs.

An incentive that arrives too late may not change the decision it targets.

### Avoid Perverse And Strategic Responses

Targets may game metrics, relabel activity, avoid hard cases, split transactions, reduce baseline effort, pass costs to consumers, or concentrate on rewarded outputs while neglecting unrewarded ones. Penalties may encourage underreporting. Subsidies may raise prices where supply is constrained.

Design safeguards, audits, complementary rules, and metrics that reduce predictable gaming.

### Decide Whether To Reward Inputs, Outputs, Or Outcomes

Input incentives are easier to administer but may not produce results. Output incentives are more direct but can encourage volume over quality. Outcome incentives align with public goals but may be noisy, delayed, and unfair when actors serve harder populations.

Choose the level of incentive based on measurability, control, risk adjustment, and administrative capacity.

### Include Distributional Effects

Incentives can reward those who would have acted anyway, exclude people without tax liability or cash flow, burden small firms more than large firms, or benefit areas with stronger administrative capacity. Analyze who can take advantage of the incentive and who cannot.

Equity analysis should include take-up barriers, not only formal eligibility.

### Combine Incentives With Enabling Supports

Behavior may require information, technical assistance, financing, workforce, infrastructure, permits, trust, or default changes. A payment alone may be insufficient. Conversely, support without an incentive may not change priorities.

Design the package around the real barrier to behavior change.

### Plan Monitoring And Adjustment and account For Baseline And Additionality and distinguish Marginal From Average Responsiveness

Incentives should be monitored for take-up, cost, targeting, fraud, price effects, quality, equity, and unintended behavior. Include triggers for adjustment if the incentive is too generous, too weak, poorly targeted, or producing harm.

Static incentive design is risky when markets or behavior change quickly.

An incentive should distinguish behavior caused by the policy from behavior that would have happened anyway. This matters for grants, tax credits, performance payments, emissions reductions, hiring subsidies, and investment incentives. Paying for baseline behavior may still be politically acceptable in some equity or stabilization programs, but it should not be claimed as additional impact.

Define baseline, eligibility window, counterfactual evidence, and anti-windfall rules where the public purpose depends on new behavior.

Incentive design often relies on an average elasticity, but the actors who respond at the margin are rarely the average actor. A subsidy may be claimed mostly by households or firms that would have acted anyway, so the average responsiveness looks healthy while the marginal response, the additional behavior actually caused by the policy, is small. Conversely, an incentive may produce weak average effects because most of the target group cannot respond, while still changing behavior decisively for a constrained subgroup that the policy most wanted to reach. Designing to the average can either overstate impact or miss where the incentive genuinely works.

Identify who is marginal, meaning whose decision the incentive is actually meant to change, and estimate responsiveness for that group specifically rather than for the eligible population as a whole. Ask what would move a marginal actor across the decision threshold, and whether the incentive is large enough, timely enough, and accessible enough to do so given their real constraints. Where marginal and average responsiveness differ sharply, the cost-effectiveness of the policy depends on targeting the marginal group and avoiding large windfalls to inframarginal actors who need no inducement. Present marginal and average effects separately so decision makers understand how much behavior the incentive is actually buying per public dollar.

## Common Traps

- Defining the goal but not the specific behavior to change.
- Paying one actor while expecting another actor to respond.
- Assuming responsiveness without evidence or sensitivity testing.
- Using delayed credits or reimbursements for actors with liquidity constraints.
- Rewarding activity that would have happened anyway; creating cliff effects or penalties that discourage marginal improvement
- Ignoring gaming, metric manipulation, or selection against harder cases; rewarding easily measured outputs while harming quality or equity
- Subsidizing demand when supply cannot expand; ignoring pass-through of subsidies or penalties into prices, rents, wages, or fees
- Failing to adjust incentives after early evidence shows weak or harmful response; counting rewarded behavior as policy impact without testing whether it is additional to the baseline

## Self-Check

- Is the target behavior observable and specific?
- Are decision makers, payers, beneficiaries, and risk bearers mapped?
- Is behavioral responsiveness estimated or tested?
- Does incentive timing match the moment of decision?
- Are liquidity, administrative burden, trust, and capacity barriers addressed?
- Are perverse incentives and strategic responses anticipated?
- Is the choice of input, output, or outcome payment justified?
- Are distributional and take-up effects analyzed?
- Are enabling supports included where behavior requires more than money?; are monitoring data and adjustment triggers defined?
- Would the incentive still look sound if actors responded strategically rather than as the policy hopes?; is additionality defined where the policy claims new behavior, savings, investment, or reductions?
