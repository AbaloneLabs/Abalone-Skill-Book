# Industrial Strategy Supply Chain And Cluster Policy

Industrial strategy tries to shape sector capacity, supply chains, technology, and competitiveness. It can address real coordination failures and security risks, but it can also become subsidy capture, protectionism without productivity, or unfocused spending. Agents often endorse strategic sectors without testing market failure, public capability, and accountability. This skill helps the analyst evaluate industrial policy as a coordinated portfolio with clear rationale and exit discipline.

## Core Rules

### Name The Strategic Rationale

Rationales may include national security, supply resilience, learning spillovers, decarbonization, regional development, job quality, export competitiveness, or critical infrastructure. Each rationale implies different tools and metrics.

Do not label an industry strategic merely because it is politically favored.

### Map The Supply Chain

Identify inputs, components, labor skills, logistics, energy, technology, suppliers, customers, chokepoints, and foreign dependencies. A policy targeting final assembly may miss upstream vulnerability.

Supply chain maps should show where public action can change risk.

### Diagnose Market Or Coordination Failure

Industrial policy may address underinvestment in R&D, skills, infrastructure, first-of-kind plants, standards, finance, or network coordination. Without a failure, subsidy may only transfer rents.

The intervention should match the failure.

### Evaluate Public Capability

Government must choose projects, monitor performance, prevent capture, coordinate agencies, and adjust when evidence changes. Weak administrative capacity increases failure risk.

Industrial strategy requires competent governance.

### Set Performance Conditions

Support should include milestones, domestic capacity, job quality, training, emissions, supply commitments, private co-investment, reporting, and clawbacks where appropriate.

Public investment should buy public outcomes.

### Avoid Permanent Protection Without Learning

Temporary support may help industries scale or learn. Permanent protection can reduce competition and innovation. Define review points and exit criteria.

Strategic support should not become entitlement.

### Coordinate Skills, Infrastructure, And Demand

Sector policy may need workforce training, energy capacity, transport, permitting, procurement, standards, and finance. A factory subsidy fails if workers, power, or suppliers are unavailable.

Industrial policy is system design.

### Consider International And Trade Constraints

Subsidies, procurement preferences, export controls, and local content rules may trigger trade disputes, retaliation, or treaty constraints. International coordination may be better than unilateral escalation.

Strategic autonomy still faces external rules.

### Monitor Spillovers And Regional Effects

Track productivity, supplier growth, wages, jobs, emissions, patents, exports, resilience, and regional distribution. Avoid counting private investment that would have happened anyway.

Industrial strategy should demonstrate additional public value.

### Identify The Binding Constraint In The Cluster

Industrial strategy fails when it funds generic activity without identifying the constraint that limits the cluster. The issue may be supplier depth, workforce skills, patient capital, permitting, energy cost, transport, university translation, procurement demand, standards, export access, or anchor-firm coordination. The analyst should state which constraint the policy addresses and why that lever is plausible.

Cluster policy should also distinguish traded-sector competitiveness from local multiplier hopes. A region may benefit from an anchor industry, but public support should be justified by spillovers, resilience, strategic capability, or market failures, not only by branding.

### Manage Concentration And Capture Risk

Industrial policy can be captured by incumbent firms, politically favored sectors, or consultants selling cluster narratives. The analyst should examine governance, eligibility, conflict-of-interest controls, performance metrics, small-supplier access, labor standards, and exit criteria. A strategy should be able to stop funding a failing bet.

Supply-chain resilience analysis should include redundancy, domestic or allied capacity, inventory, standards, cybersecurity, logistics bottlenecks, and environmental constraints. Resilience is not the same as localizing every input.

### Use Stage Gates For Strategic Bets

Industrial strategy often requires uncertainty, but uncertainty should be managed through stage gates. Define what evidence justifies scaling, pausing, or ending support: supplier entry, private co-investment, workforce placement, export growth, resilience gains, or technology readiness.

## Common Traps

- Funding a cluster label without identifying the binding constraint and the policy mechanism.
- Allowing incumbent capture or prestige sectors to define industrial strategy without exit criteria.

- Calling sectors strategic without a clear rationale.
- Supporting final products while ignoring upstream chokepoints.
- Subsidizing firms without identifying market failure.
- Assuming government can pick winners without capability and safeguards.
- Writing weak performance conditions.
- Keeping protection after learning goals fail.
- Funding plants without workforce, power, permits, or suppliers.
- Ignoring trade rules and retaliation.
- Counting gross investment without additionality.
- Allowing incumbent capture of strategy.
- Confusing regional job politics with national supply resilience.

## Self-Check

- Are stage gates defined so public support can scale, pause, or end based on evidence?

- Is the cluster's binding constraint identified and matched to a plausible public lever?
- Are capture risk, governance, supplier access, resilience, and exit criteria built into the strategy?

- Is the strategic rationale explicit?
- Is the supply chain and chokepoint map credible?
- Is the market or coordination failure diagnosed?
- Does government have capability to manage the strategy?
- Are performance conditions enforceable?
- Are review and exit criteria defined?
- Are skills, infrastructure, demand, and finance coordinated?
- Are trade and international effects considered?
- Are spillovers and additionality measured?
- Would the policy build durable capacity rather than subsidize incumbents?
