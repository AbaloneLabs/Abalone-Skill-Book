# Business Attraction Retention And Incentive Risk

Business attraction policy can produce jobs and investment, but it can also create windfalls, interjurisdictional bidding wars, weak job quality, and hidden fiscal costs. Agents often count announced jobs without testing whether the firm would have located there anyway, whether jobs are accessible to residents, and whether public incentives are enforceable. This skill helps the analyst evaluate business attraction and retention deals as public investments with opportunity cost and accountability.

## Core Rules

### Define The Public Objective

Economic development deals may aim to create jobs, retain a major employer, redevelop a site, build a cluster, increase tax base, support distressed areas, or anchor infrastructure. The objective should be explicit because success metrics differ.

Do not treat any corporate investment as automatically worth public subsidy.

### Test But-For Claims

Firms often claim they need incentives to locate, expand, or stay. Analysts should examine competing sites, cost gaps, strategic reasons, workforce availability, infrastructure, customer access, and evidence that the incentive changes the decision.

The hardest question is whether public money is buying something additional.

### Analyze Job Quality And Access

Job count is incomplete. Wages, benefits, stability, hours, career pathways, safety, union neutrality, local hiring, transit access, and credential requirements determine public value.

A deal that creates inaccessible or low-quality jobs may not meet community goals.

### Include Full Fiscal Impact

Calculate incentive cost, tax abatements, infrastructure, schools, services, utilities, public safety, debt, and administrative monitoring. New revenue may be delayed, uncertain, or shifted from other local businesses.

Net fiscal impact should be shown over time.

### Design Enforceable Clawbacks

Performance agreements should define job creation, wage levels, investment, location, retention period, reporting, audit rights, repayment, and remedies. Weak clawbacks create symbolic accountability.

Clawbacks should be practical enough to enforce when a firm misses targets.

### Watch Interjurisdictional Competition

Regions may compete against themselves when nearby jurisdictions bid for the same firm. Incentives can shift jobs across boundaries without creating regional growth.

Regional coordination may produce better outcomes than subsidy escalation.

### Consider Small Business And Existing Employers

Large attraction deals can overshadow existing local firms that need workforce, infrastructure, permitting, or financing support. Incentives may create unfair advantages.

Economic development should not only chase mobile firms.

### Include Community Impacts

Projects can affect housing demand, traffic, displacement, environmental burden, land use, and public services. Community benefit agreements may help but must be enforceable and representative.

Job announcements should not hide local costs.

### Monitor After The Announcement

Track actual jobs, wages, residency, investment, tax revenue, compliance, layoffs, automation, and subsidy payments. Public reporting should continue after press events.

Economic development accountability begins after the deal is signed.

### Test Additionality And Opportunity Cost

Business incentives should answer whether the investment would happen without the public subsidy, at what scale, and in what location. The analyst should examine company financials where available, competing offers, site constraints, labor access, infrastructure needs, and whether the incentive changes the decision or merely rewards an already likely move.

Opportunity cost must be visible. Funds, tax abatements, land, infrastructure, and staff attention used for one attraction deal cannot be used for small business support, workforce training, housing, schools, or basic services. A strong memo compares the incentive to alternative uses, not only to projected jobs.

### Build Accountability Into The Agreement

Job, wage, capital investment, local hiring, environmental, and timeline promises should be measurable and enforceable. Clawbacks, reporting, audit rights, community benefits, sunset dates, and public disclosure rules need to be defined before approval. Otherwise incentive policy becomes announcement management.

The analysis should also test downside scenarios: automation reduces headcount, the company misses wage targets, the industry shifts, or infrastructure costs exceed estimates. Public risk should not be hidden behind best-case multipliers.

### Plan For Public Explanation

Large incentives need a public rationale that can withstand scrutiny after the announcement. The analyst should prepare a clear explanation of why the deal is additional, why the company was chosen, what the public receives, and what happens if promises are missed.

## Common Traps

- Counting all announced jobs as public benefit without testing whether the subsidy changed the company's decision.
- Approving incentives without enforceable job, wage, reporting, clawback, and sunset terms.

- Treating announced jobs as guaranteed outcomes.
- Accepting but-for claims without evidence.
- Counting jobs without wages, benefits, and accessibility.
- Ignoring infrastructure, school, housing, and service costs.
- Writing clawbacks that are politically or legally hard to enforce.
- Subsidizing relocation within the same labor market.
- Neglecting small businesses and existing employers.
- Overlooking displacement and environmental burden.
- Measuring success by investment press releases.
- Keeping incentive agreements confidential without public accountability.
- Ignoring opportunity cost of foregone revenue.

## Self-Check

- Could the incentive rationale and accountability terms be explained publicly without relying on confidential optimism?

- Does the analysis test additionality and compare the incentive with alternative public uses?
- Are job, wage, investment, reporting, clawback, and downside-risk provisions explicit?

- Is the public objective specific?
- Is the but-for case evidenced?
- Are job quality and access analyzed?
- Is full fiscal impact shown over time?
- Are clawbacks enforceable and monitored?
- Is regional competition considered?
- Are existing local businesses included in strategy?
- Are community impacts and benefits credible?
- Are outcomes reported after announcement?
- Would the incentive still be justified if only additional, good-quality jobs counted?
