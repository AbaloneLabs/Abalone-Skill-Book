# Scenario Analysis And Sensitivity Testing

Policy decisions are made under uncertainty. Forecasts can be wrong, take-up can differ, costs can shift, legal challenges can delay implementation, and behavior can change in response to policy. Agents often present one expected case and treat caveats as footnotes. This skill helps the policy analyst test whether a recommendation remains sound under plausible futures and identify which assumptions matter most.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Assumptions That Drive The Decision

List assumptions about demand, take-up, cost, compliance, economic conditions, population, technology, staffing, litigation, funding, stakeholder behavior, and implementation timeline. Then identify which assumptions materially affect the recommendation.

Do not test every minor assumption equally. Focus on those that could change the preferred option or require safeguards.

### Build Plausible Scenarios, Not Fantasies

Scenarios should be plausible and decision-relevant: high take-up, low take-up, recession, inflation, court delay, supply shortage, weak enforcement, strong opposition, technology failure, climate event, provider exit, or rapid demand growth. Include a central case, downside case, and stress case where appropriate.

Avoid unrealistic worst cases that paralyze decision-making or rosy cases that merely validate the proposal.

### Use Sensitivity Testing To Find Breakpoints

Sensitivity testing asks how results change when key variables move. Identify breakpoints: the cost level, effect size, take-up rate, compliance rate, or delay at which the policy no longer meets objectives or affordability limits.

Breakpoints are often more useful than point estimates because they show what must be true.

### Test Distributional And Operational Sensitivity

Aggregate results may be stable while impacts on subgroups, local areas, or implementers are volatile. Test whether vulnerable groups, small entities, rural areas, or front-line agencies face worse outcomes under stress.

A policy that is robust on average may still fail equity or delivery guardrails.

### Include Behavioral Response

People, firms, agencies, and political actors respond to policy. They may comply, avoid, game, substitute, litigate, relocate, increase prices, reduce quality, or change applications. Scenario analysis should include strategic response where plausible.

Do not assume policy targets remain passive.

### Tie Scenarios To Design Choices

Scenario analysis should inform caps, triggers, phase-ins, contingency funds, review points, sunset clauses, data monitoring, staffing reserves, legal safeguards, and communication. If scenarios do not affect design, they may be decorative.

For each major risk scenario, ask what would be done differently before or after launch.

### Communicate Uncertainty Without Paralysis

Decision makers need to know uncertainty, not be overwhelmed by it. Present the few uncertainties that matter most and the actions that manage them. State which risks can be monitored, which can be mitigated, and which must be accepted.

Uncertainty should shape policy posture: full rollout, pilot, staged rollout, delayed decision, or no action.

### Update Scenarios As Evidence Changes and assign Owners For Risk Signals

Scenario analysis should be revisited when new data, market changes, court decisions, implementation signals, or stakeholder behavior emerge. A scenario plan from early design may become stale before launch.

Build review points into the policy process.

Each important scenario should have a signal owner: who watches the data, who interprets it, who can escalate, and who can recommend a change. A risk register without ownership becomes documentation rather than management.

Signals may include caseload growth, unit costs, litigation milestones, compliance rates, provider exits, complaints, media narratives, system outages, or geographic disparities. Define reporting cadence and decision forum.

### Link Scenarios To Contingency Actions and distinguish Risk From Deep Uncertainty

For each material downside scenario, identify the contingency action: release reserve funds, narrow eligibility, extend deadlines, add staff, change guidance, pause enforcement, seek legislative amendment, trigger emergency procurement, or begin public communication. If no action is available, say the risk is accepted.

Scenario analysis should leave leaders knowing what they will do, not only what might happen.

Not all uncertainty can be quantified. Risk describes futures where outcomes are unknown but probabilities can be reasonably estimated from data, history, or models, allowing expected-value reasoning. Deep uncertainty describes futures where the model itself, the relevant variables, or the probability distributions are unknown or contested, so that point estimates and confidence intervals can convey false precision. Treating deep uncertainty as quantifiable risk produces recommendations that look rigorous but rest on hidden, fragile assumptions.

Match the analytical method to the type of uncertainty. Where risks are quantifiable, sensitivity testing and breakpoint analysis are appropriate. Under deep uncertainty, prefer methods that perform well across many plausible futures rather than optimize for one: robust decision making, scenario discovery, adaptive pathways, or minimax reasoning that asks which option avoids catastrophic failure across the widest range. State explicitly which assumptions are uncertain in distribution versus uncertain in form, because the policy posture differs: a quantifiable risk may justify insurance or hedging, while deep uncertainty favors options that preserve optionality, allow reversal, and include explicit review triggers.

## Common Traps

- Presenting only a central forecast.
- Testing assumptions that do not affect the decision while ignoring key drivers.
- Using implausible worst cases or optimistic best cases.
- Ignoring subgroup, local, and operational sensitivity.
- Treating affected actors as passive; producing scenarios that do not change design or monitoring
- Hiding uncertainty to make the recommendation look decisive; treating uncertainty as a reason to avoid all action
- Forgetting to update scenarios after conditions change; confusing sensitivity testing with evidence that the policy will work
- Creating a risk register with no owner, cadence, or decision forum; describing downside scenarios without contingency actions

## Self-Check

- Are decision-driving assumptions identified?
- Are scenarios plausible, decision-relevant, and varied?
- Are breakpoints calculated or qualitatively identified?
- Are subgroup, local, fiscal, and operational sensitivities tested?
- Are behavioral responses considered?
- Do scenarios affect design, safeguards, monitoring, or rollout strategy?
- Is uncertainty communicated clearly without false precision?
- Are risks categorized as avoidable, mitigable, monitorable, or accepted?
- Are scenario review points defined?
- Would the recommendation remain defensible under plausible downside conditions?; does each material scenario have signal ownership and a contingency action?
