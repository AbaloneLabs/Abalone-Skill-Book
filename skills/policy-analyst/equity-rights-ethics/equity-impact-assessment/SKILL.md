# Equity Impact Assessment

Equity analysis asks whether a policy treats different people fairly given different needs, barriers, histories, and risks. It is not a paragraph added after economic analysis. A policy can improve averages while worsening disparities or excluding the people it claims to help. Agents often mention equity without changing policy design. This skill helps the policy analyst assess equity impacts in a way that affects objectives, options, implementation, and monitoring.

## Core Rules

### Define The Equity Concern

State which inequity matters and why: income, race, ethnicity, Indigenous status, disability, gender, age, geography, language, migration status, housing status, caste where relevant, rurality, sector, or other context-specific disadvantage. Tie the concern to evidence, legal obligations, policy objectives, or affected community input.

Do not use "equity" as a vague virtue. Define the disparity, barrier, or unfair burden.

### Identify Mechanisms Of Inequity

Ask how inequity is produced: eligibility rules, cost, geography, digital access, documentation, discrimination, service quality, enforcement, exposure to harm, language access, disability access, time burden, or historical exclusion. The mechanism determines the remedy.

If the mechanism is unknown, do not jump to a symbolic fix. Gather evidence or engage affected groups.

### Analyze Both Benefits And Burdens

Equity assessment should include who receives benefits, who bears costs, who faces compliance burden, who is excluded, who is surveilled, and who carries implementation risk. A policy targeted to disadvantaged groups can still impose stigma, paperwork, or enforcement risk.

Do not assume good intent produces equitable impact.

### Use Targeted Universalism Where Useful

Universal goals can require differentiated strategies. For example, universal access may require language support, rural delivery, disability accommodations, outreach, or additional resources for high-need areas. Equal treatment can preserve unequal outcomes when barriers differ.

Explain when a targeted design is needed to achieve a universal objective.

### Include Affected Groups In Interpretation

Data may show disparity but not explain experience, trust, barriers, or acceptable remedies. Engage affected groups and intermediaries with care, compensation, accessibility, and privacy. Community input should inform design, not merely validate a completed proposal.

Avoid assuming one organization or participant represents an entire group.

### Assess Legal And Rights Interaction

Equity policies may intersect with anti-discrimination law, constitutional rules, disability rights, Indigenous rights, privacy, language access, or equal protection. Some targeted approaches require legal review. Some apparently neutral rules may create disparate impact.

Flag legal review without letting legal uncertainty erase equity analysis.

### Design Mitigation And Accountability

If inequity risks exist, propose mitigation: eligibility changes, outreach, accommodations, funding formula, burden reduction, appeal rights, monitoring, community governance, targeted resources, or enforcement safeguards. Assign owners and indicators.

Equity assessment is incomplete if it only describes disparity.

### Monitor Equity Over Time and separate Within-Group From Between-Group Effects

Equity impacts can change after launch. Track take-up, outcomes, complaints, appeals, enforcement, delays, and burdens by relevant groups where privacy-safe. If data is unavailable, build collection or qualitative monitoring into the policy.

Do not rely on one pre-adoption assessment as proof of equitable implementation.

Equity analysis often compares average outcomes between groups, but a policy can widen disparities within a disadvantaged group while improving its average relative to others. A targeted benefit may reach the better-connected segment of an eligible population while missing the most marginalized within it, so the headline gap narrows even as the worst-off fall further behind. Conversely, a universal policy can reduce between-group inequality while concentrating its costs on a small, less visible subgroup. Aggregating to a single group label can hide these dynamics and produce a reassuring average that masks harm.

Examine distribution within each equity group, not only across groups. Ask who inside the group has access, voice, documentation, digital literacy, proximity to services, or freedom from enforcement risk, and whether the policy's design favors them. Where data permits, disaggregate one layer further: by income, geography, disability, age, or migration status within the group. Where data does not permit, state that the within-group effect is unknown and cannot be assumed benign. A policy that improves a group average by serving its most advantaged members is not equitable merely because the beneficiaries belong to a disadvantaged category.

### Compare Procedural And Substantive Equity and identify Equity Tradeoffs Honestly

Procedural equity concerns who has voice, access, notice, appeal, and respectful treatment. Substantive equity concerns outcomes, burdens, resources, exposure, and opportunity. A policy may consult widely but still produce unequal outcomes; another may improve outcomes while using a process that damages trust.

Assess both. Strong equity analysis should ask whether the process is fair and whether the result is fair.

Equity goals can conflict: targeting one group may exclude another, privacy protection may limit disaggregated monitoring, universal benefits may improve dignity but reduce resources for highest-need groups, and strict anti-fraud rules may reduce access. Name these tradeoffs instead of treating equity as a single direction.

When tradeoffs exist, explain the value choice and mitigation.

## Common Traps

- Adding equity language without changing policy design.
- Treating averages as sufficient evidence of benefit.
- Defining equity groups too broadly to see specific barriers.
- Assuming targeted programs are automatically equitable.
- Ignoring burden, stigma, surveillance, and exclusion; using affected communities only to endorse a preselected policy
- Avoiding targeted design because legal issues feel complicated; failing to assign owners for mitigation
- Collecting disaggregated data without privacy and interpretation safeguards; treating equity assessment as complete at launch
- Treating a participatory process as proof of equitable outcomes; hiding equity tradeoffs among different disadvantaged groups or design values

## Self-Check

- Is the equity concern specific and evidence-based?
- Are mechanisms of inequity identified?
- Are benefits, burdens, exclusions, and risks analyzed?
- Is differentiated design considered where universal goals require it?
- Are affected groups engaged in interpretation and design?
- Are legal and rights issues flagged for review?
- Are mitigation actions specific and owned?
- Are equity indicators included in monitoring?
- Are privacy and small-number risks handled in disaggregated data?
- Would the policy design change if the equity assessment is taken seriously?; are procedural equity, substantive equity, and equity tradeoffs all addressed?
