# Consultation Design And Feedback Integrity

Consultation can improve policy, but it can also become theater, capture, or a biased sample of whoever had time and resources to respond. Agents often summarize consultation by volume of comments or loudest themes without checking who was reached, who was missing, and how feedback should influence the decision. This skill helps the policy analyst design and synthesize consultation so it informs policy honestly.

## Core Rules

### Define The Purpose Of Consultation

Clarify whether consultation seeks problem diagnosis, option generation, impact validation, legal compliance, legitimacy, co-design, implementation planning, or communication. The purpose determines who to engage, how, when, and what questions to ask.

Do not ask people for input if the decision is already fixed unless the process is transparent about what can still change. False openness damages trust.

### Engage Early Enough To Matter

Consultation after the preferred option is fully designed often becomes risk management rather than learning. Engage stakeholders when problem framing, options, implementation details, or safeguards can still change.

If late consultation is required by law or timeline, state what aspects remain open and what constraints are fixed.

### Use Accessible And Proportionate Methods

Methods may include public comment, targeted interviews, community meetings, surveys, focus groups, workshops, advisory committees, deliberative forums, site visits, or co-design. Match methods to audience, literacy, language, disability access, digital access, geography, trust, and topic sensitivity.

Do not rely only on online portals or weekday hearings if affected groups cannot use them. Consultation design should reduce participation barriers.

### Ask Questions That Produce Usable Evidence

Consultation questions should be clear, neutral, and tied to policy decisions. Avoid leading questions that invite support for a preferred option. Ask about tradeoffs, implementation barriers, distributional effects, unintended consequences, and alternatives.

Separate factual claims, lived experience, preferences, and values. Each type of input informs policy differently.

### Track Who Was Heard And Who Was Missing

Consultation is rarely statistically representative unless designed that way. Report participation by stakeholder type, geography, affected group, sector, and other relevant dimensions where appropriate and privacy-safe. Identify gaps and explain how they affect confidence.

Do not imply that the number of responses equals public opinion. Organized campaigns can dominate comment volume.

### Synthesize Feedback Transparently

Summarize major themes, minority views, evidence claims, implementation concerns, and tradeoffs. Distinguish frequency from importance. A rare comment may identify a severe rights risk; a common comment may reflect a mobilized interest.

Explain how feedback changed the policy, why it did not change the policy, or what remains unresolved. Consultation without response is extractive.

### Guard Against Capture And Manipulation

Powerful stakeholders may flood consultations, provide technical claims, sponsor research, mobilize form letters, or seek private access. This input can be relevant but should be interpreted alongside incentives and evidence quality.

Balance openness with analytic discipline. Do not let the best-resourced voices define the entire record.

### Protect Participants And Sensitive Information

Consultation may involve trauma, legal status, health, income, disability, employment, community conflict, or retaliation risk. Use privacy controls, consent, safe facilitation, anonymization, and referral support where needed. Do not publish identifiable comments without appropriate permission.

Safety and dignity are part of consultation quality.

### Separate Consultation Evidence From Consent and plan Feedback Coding Before Reading Submissions

Hearing from stakeholders does not mean they consent to the policy. Affected people may provide useful evidence while still opposing the option. Do not write as if consultation itself creates legitimacy or endorsement.

Report support, opposition, conditions, and unresolved concerns separately. Decision makers should not confuse engagement with agreement.

Define how submissions will be coded, who will code them, how disagreements will be resolved, and how form letters, duplicate campaigns, expert evidence, personal stories, and technical attachments will be treated. Without a coding approach, synthesis can become subjective cherry-picking.

Coding does not remove judgment, but it makes judgment visible and reviewable.

## Common Traps

- Consulting after decisions are effectively closed.
- Treating consultation as a legitimacy checkbox.
- Relying on methods inaccessible to affected groups.
- Asking leading questions that validate a preferred option.
- Equating comment volume with representativeness; ignoring organized campaigns or power imbalances
- Summarizing majority themes while missing severe minority risks; failing to explain how feedback affected the policy
- Publishing sensitive information unnecessarily; treating stakeholder preference as evidence without quality appraisal
- Describing consultation as endorsement when participants only provided input; coding submissions informally after seeing which themes support the preferred option

## Self-Check

- Is the purpose of consultation clear?
- Can consultation still influence problem framing, options, implementation, or safeguards?
- Are methods accessible for affected groups?
- Are questions neutral, decision-relevant, and tradeoff-aware?
- Is participation tracked and missing input identified?
- Are frequency, severity, evidence quality, and stakeholder incentives distinguished?
- Is capture or organized response risk addressed?
- Are sensitive participants and information protected?
- Does the report explain how feedback changed or did not change the policy?; would participants recognize the synthesis as fair even if not all requests were accepted?
- Are evidence, support, opposition, and consent kept distinct?; is the feedback coding and synthesis method defined before conclusions are written?
