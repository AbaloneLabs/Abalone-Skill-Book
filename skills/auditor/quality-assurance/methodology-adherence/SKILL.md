# Methodology Adherence

Audit methodology is the structured set of procedures, decision rules, and documentation requirements that translate standards into consistent practice. Its purpose is to ensure that every engagement meets a baseline of rigor and that conclusions across engagements are comparable and defensible. But methodology is widely misunderstood. Some treat it as a rigid script to follow without thought, completing steps mechanically whether or not they address the engagement's risks. Others treat it as a suggestion to tailor away, skipping steps that feel unnecessary without documenting why. Both failures undermine assurance. Methodology followed in form but not in substance produces a complete-looking file that did not actually address the risks. Methodology departed from without justification produces an idiosyncratic engagement whose conclusions cannot be defended. The discipline is to follow the methodology where it applies, tailor it thoughtfully where the engagement requires, and document the reasoning in either case.

Use this skill when applying methodology, deciding on deviations, reviewing adherence, or evaluating whether methodology is sufficient and followed in substance. The goal is to ensure the methodology serves the audit's objectives rather than becoming either a mechanical checklist or an optional suggestion.

## Core Rules

### Follow Methodology Because It Addresses Risk, Not Because It Is Required

Methodology exists to ensure that significant risks are addressed consistently. When auditors understand why each step exists, they apply it with judgment; when they apply it blindly, they complete steps without connecting them to the engagement's risks.

For each significant methodology requirement, understand:

- what risk or assertion the procedure is designed to address;
- how the procedure's depth and extent relate to assessed risk;
- what conclusion the procedure is meant to support;
- how the documentation requirement enables review and challenge.

Understanding the purpose allows the auditor to apply the procedure where it matters, scale it to risk, and recognize when a procedure is not adding value because the underlying risk is absent. Blind completion, by contrast, fills the file without ensuring the risk is addressed.

### Tailor Methodology To The Engagement's Specific Risks

Methodology is designed for a range of engagements, and no single engagement presents all the risks the methodology anticipates. Effective adherence means tailoring the application to the engagement's specific risk profile, not applying every step uniformly regardless of relevance.

Tailor by:

- mapping the methodology to the engagement's identified risks;
- increasing the depth or extent of procedures where risk is higher;
- reducing or omitting procedures only where the underlying risk is demonstrably absent or negligible;
- documenting the rationale for any tailoring, including the risk assessment that supports it;
- ensuring tailoring does not omit procedures required by standards regardless of risk.

Tailoring is judgment, and judgment must be documented. An undocumented deviation is indistinguishable from a shortcut.

### Document Adherence In Substance, Not Only In Form

A workpaper that records a procedure as performed, with the methodology reference cited, may satisfy a completeness check while revealing nothing about whether the procedure was genuinely effective. Documentation must show what was done, what was found, and how the conclusion follows.

Substantive documentation includes:

- the objective of the procedure and the risk it addresses;
- what was examined, the population and period covered, and the method used;
- the results observed, including exceptions and how they were resolved;
- the conclusion reached and how it links to the evidence;
- any departure from the standard methodology and the reasoning.

A file full of methodology references with no substance behind them provides the appearance of adherence without the assurance. Reviewers should be able to see not only that a step was performed but that it was performed effectively.

### Justify And Document Any Deviation

Deviations from methodology are sometimes appropriate, because the engagement's circumstances differ from the methodology's assumptions. But deviations must be justified, documented, and approved, not made silently.

For any deviation, document:

- what the standard methodology requires;
- why the standard procedure is not appropriate or not necessary for this engagement;
- what alternative procedure was performed, if any, and why it addresses the relevant risk;
- who approved the deviation and the basis for approval;
- the impact on the engagement's conclusions and assurance.

Silent deviations are the most dangerous, because they leave the file appearing compliant while actually omitting relevant work. Make deviations visible so they can be reviewed.

### Recognize The Difference Between Scaling And Omitting

Methodology often allows procedures to be scaled to risk: performed to a greater or lesser extent depending on circumstances. Scaling is not the same as omitting. Confusing the two leads to under-auditing in the name of efficiency.

Clarify the distinction:

- scaling means performing the procedure but adjusting sample size, frequency, depth, or coverage to match risk;
- omitting means not performing the procedure at all;
- scaling down to zero is omission, not scaling, and requires the same justification as any deviation;
- even low risk rarely justifies complete omission of a procedure that addresses a significant assertion.

When in doubt, scale rather than omit, and document the risk basis for the level of work performed.

### Ensure Supervision And Review Verify Substance

Methodology adherence is enforced through supervision and review. If reviewers check only that steps are present and signed, the team learns to complete steps without substance. If reviewers challenge whether procedures were effective, the team learns to perform them effectively.

Effective review verifies:

- that the procedure performed actually addressed the identified risk;
- that the documentation shows what was done and found, not only that it was done;
- that conclusions follow from the evidence, not merely from the procedure's completion;
- that deviations are justified and approved;
- that the depth of work matches the assessed risk.

Review that challenges substance, not just form, is what keeps methodology adherence honest.

### Keep Methodology Current With Standards And Practice

Methodology is not static. Standards evolve, new risks emerge, and lessons from completed engagements reveal where methodology is weak. A methodology that is not updated becomes an artifact that no longer reflects current requirements.

Maintain currency by:

- updating methodology when standards or regulations change;
- incorporating lessons from quality reviews, peer reviews, and findings;
- reviewing methodology periodically for gaps, ambiguities, or outdated procedures;
- soliciting feedback from engagement teams on where methodology is impractical or unclear;
- version-controlling changes and communicating them to practitioners.

Practitioners cannot adhere to methodology that is outdated, ambiguous, or impractical. Keeping it current is a prerequisite for meaningful adherence.

## Common Traps

### Checklist Completion Without Risk Connection

Filling out methodology steps without linking them to the engagement's risks produces a complete file that may not address what matters. Connect each step to risk.

### Silent Deviation

Skipping steps that seem unnecessary without documenting why leaves the file appearing compliant while omitting work. Make deviations visible and justified.

### Confusing Scaling With Omitting

Reducing a procedure to zero and calling it scaling bypasses the justification requirement. Scaling to zero is omission.

### Form Over Substance In Documentation

Citing methodology references without recording what was done, found, and concluded provides the appearance of adherence without the evidence. Document substance.

### Review That Checks Only Completeness

Reviewers who confirm steps are signed but not whether they were effective teach the team to complete rather than perform. Review for substance.

### Rigid Application Without Tailoring

Applying every step regardless of relevance wastes effort and can miss engagement-specific risks that the standard steps do not address. Tailor thoughtfully.

### Outdated Methodology

Following a methodology that no longer reflects current standards or risks provides false assurance that the engagement meets requirements. Keep methodology current.

## Self-Check

- Is each significant methodology requirement understood in terms of the risk or assertion it addresses, so it is applied with judgment rather than blindly?
- Has the methodology been tailored to the engagement's specific risks, with increased depth where risk is higher and documented rationale for any reduction?
- Does the documentation show what was examined, what was found, and how the conclusion follows, rather than only that the step was performed?
- Are all deviations from standard methodology justified, documented, and approved, with the alternative procedure and its risk basis explained?
- Is the distinction between scaling and omitting maintained, with any reduction to zero treated as an omission requiring justification?
- Does supervision and review challenge whether procedures were effective in substance, not merely whether they were completed in form?
- Are conclusions shown to follow from the evidence, with exceptions resolved and reasoning documented?
- Is the methodology itself kept current with standards, regulations, lessons learned, and practitioner feedback, with changes version-controlled and communicated?
- Are ambiguities or impracticalities in methodology surfaced and addressed rather than worked around silently?
- Could an independent reviewer confirm that methodology adherence addressed the engagement's risks in substance, not only in documented form?
