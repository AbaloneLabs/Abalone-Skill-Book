# Engagement Quality Review

An engagement quality review is an independent check on the significant judgments of an audit before the report is released. Its purpose is to provide a second lens on the conclusions that matter most: the risk assessment, the materiality and scoping decisions, the resolution of difficult accounting or control issues, and the opinion or rating ultimately issued. A well-executed EQR catches errors and over-optimism that the engagement team, immersed in its own conclusions, cannot see. A poorly executed EQR becomes a formality: the reviewer signs the file, the team proceeds, and the review adds cost without assurance. The difference lies in whether the reviewer genuinely challenges the significant judgments or merely confirms that the required procedures appear to have been performed.

Use this skill when performing an EQR, selecting engagements for EQR, defining the reviewer's role and authority, or evaluating whether an EQR function is effective. The goal is to ensure the EQR is a substantive challenge to the engagement's riskiest conclusions, not a procedural checkpoint.

## Core Rules

### Apply EQR To Engagements Based On Risk, Not Routine

Not every engagement requires an EQR, and applying it uniformly treats low-risk work the same as high-stakes judgments. EQR delivers the most value where the consequences of a wrong conclusion are greatest and where judgment is most involved.

Target EQR toward engagements involving:

- listed entities or public-interest engagements;
- significant accounting estimates, fair value, or going concern judgments;
- qualified, adverse, or disclaimed opinions, or material control deficiency findings;
- fraud, noncompliance, or integrity concerns;
- first-year engagements or engagements with significant changes in scope;
- high-risk industries, complex group structures, or significant related-party transactions;
- engagements where the team encountered significant resistance from management.

The criteria for requiring EQR should be documented so that selection is consistent and defensible, not left to the engagement team's preference.

### Ensure The Reviewer Is Genuinely Independent Of The Engagement

The value of EQR depends on the reviewer's independence from the engagement conclusions. A reviewer who participated in the engagement, who reports to the engagement partner, or who has a stake in the outcome cannot provide a genuinely independent challenge.

Confirm independence by checking:

- the reviewer did not participate in the engagement or make significant decisions on it;
- the reviewer is not in the engagement partner's reporting line and has authority to require changes;
- the reviewer has no personal or financial interest that could bias the conclusion;
- the reviewer has sufficient experience and seniority to challenge the engagement partner credibly;
- for group audits, the reviewer is independent of the components reviewed.

If independence is impaired, a different reviewer must be assigned. An EQR by a non-independent reviewer provides no assurance beyond the engagement team's own review.

### Focus The Review On Significant Judgments, Not Completeness Of Forms

The most common failure of EQR is to review whether the file contains the required documents, signatures, and checklists rather than whether the significant judgments are well-supported. A file can be complete and still wrong.

Focus the review on:

- the risk assessment and whether it drove the procedures performed;
- materiality and scoping decisions and whether they are defensible;
- the resolution of significant accounting, control, or compliance issues;
- the sufficiency and appropriateness of evidence for significant amounts and disclosures;
- the evaluation of identified misstatements, control deficiencies, or findings;
- the conclusions on estimates, fair value, going concern, and other judgment areas;
- the consistency of the opinion or rating with the evidence documented;
- the resolution of any disagreements within the engagement team.

Procedural completeness matters, but it is the judgment that determines whether the conclusion is right.

### Require The Reviewer's Conclusion To Be Explicit

An EQR that produces only a signature conveys little. The reviewer should record an explicit conclusion on whether the significant judgments are supported and whether the report is appropriate.

The reviewer's documentation should include:

- the significant judgments reviewed and the basis for agreeing with each;
- any disagreements with the engagement team and how they were resolved;
- any additional procedures or evidence required before the reviewer would concur;
- the reviewer's explicit conclusion that the report is appropriate, or a statement that it is not;
- the date of the conclusion and that it preceded report release.

A reviewer who cannot concur must have the authority to prevent or delay report release until the matter is resolved. An EQR without the power to require changes is advisory, not a control.

### Conduct The Review At The Right Time

An EQR performed after the report is released cannot prevent a flawed conclusion from reaching the user. The review must occur before release, at a point when significant judgments are sufficiently developed to be challenged but early enough that changes can still be made.

Time the review by:

- scheduling it before the report is signed or issued;
- ensuring the file is sufficiently complete that the reviewer can assess the significant judgments;
- allowing time for the reviewer to require additional work and for the team to perform it;
- for long or complex engagements, considering interim reviews of significant judgments before the final review;
- confirming the reviewer's conclusion is dated before the report release date.

A retroactive EQR is a post-mortem, not a control.

### Challenge, Do Not Ratify

The reviewer's posture should be one of independent challenge, not confirmation. The engagement team has already reached its conclusions; the reviewer's role is to test whether those conclusions hold under scrutiny.

Challenge effectively by:

- asking the team to explain the basis for significant judgments, not merely pointing to the documentation;
- considering alternative conclusions the team may have dismissed too quickly;
- probing whether contradictory evidence was adequately pursued and resolved;
- assessing whether the team's conclusion represents the best-supported view or merely an acceptable one;
- being willing to require additional evidence or a revised conclusion where support is thin.

A reviewer who agrees with everything has either a perfect engagement team or has not challenged deeply enough. Both are possible; only scrutiny distinguishes them.

### Document And Resolve Disagreements

Disagreements between the reviewer and the engagement team are valuable, not problematic. They surface judgment differences that deserve examination. The process must ensure disagreements are resolved through evidence and reasoning, not through hierarchy or deference.

Handle disagreements by:

- documenting the nature of the disagreement and each party's position;
- requiring additional evidence or consultation to resolve the matter;
- escalating to a higher authority if the reviewer and partner cannot agree;
- ensuring the final conclusion reflects the evidence, not the seniority of the parties;
- recording the resolution and its basis in the file.

Suppressing disagreement to meet a deadline produces a cleaner file and a weaker audit.

## Common Traps

### Rubber-Stamping The File

Signing the EQR after a cursory review of the index adds cost without assurance. The reviewer must engage with the significant judgments, not the table of contents.

### Reviewing Completeness Instead Of Judgment

Confirming that every required form is present is necessary but insufficient. The EQR's value is in challenging conclusions, not checking boxes.

### Non-Independent Reviewer

A reviewer who participated in the engagement or reports to the partner cannot provide independent challenge. Independence is the foundation of EQR credibility.

### EQR Without Authority

A reviewer who can only advise but not require changes is not a control. The reviewer must have the authority to prevent release until concerns are resolved.

### Retroactive Review

Performing the EQR after release defeats its preventive purpose. Schedule it before the report is issued.

### Deference To The Partner

The reviewer agreeing because the partner is senior, rather than because the evidence supports the conclusion, abandons the review's purpose. Evidence, not hierarchy, should resolve disagreements.

### Applying EQR Uniformly To Low-Risk Work

Mandatory EQR on every engagement regardless of risk wastes capacity on routine work and dilutes attention to high-risk judgments. Target EQR by risk.

## Self-Check

- Are engagements selected for EQR based on documented risk criteria targeting high-judgment, high-consequence work, rather than applied uniformly?
- Is the reviewer demonstrably independent of the engagement, with no participation, reporting-line conflict, or personal interest, and sufficient seniority to challenge?
- Does the review focus on significant judgments, risk assessment, evidence sufficiency, and the appropriateness of the opinion, not merely procedural completeness?
- Does the reviewer document an explicit conclusion on each significant judgment and on whether the report is appropriate, with disagreements and resolutions recorded?
- Is the EQR performed and concluded before the report is released, with adequate time for any required additional work?
- Does the reviewer adopt a challenge posture, testing whether conclusions hold under scrutiny rather than ratifying them?
- Are disagreements between reviewer and engagement team documented, resolved through evidence, and escalated if necessary rather than suppressed?
- Does the reviewer have the authority to prevent or delay release until concerns are addressed?
- For group audits, is the reviewer independent of the components reviewed?
- Could an independent observer confirm that the EQR provided substantive challenge to the engagement's riskiest conclusions rather than serving as a procedural checkpoint?
