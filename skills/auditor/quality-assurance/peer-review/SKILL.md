# Peer Review

Peer review is an external evaluation of an audit function's quality by reviewers from outside the function, whether from a peer organization, a professional body, a regulator, or an internal quality group operating independently. Its purpose is to provide an independent perspective on whether the audit function's methodology, execution, and conclusions meet applicable standards, and to identify where they fall short. Peer review differs from engagement quality review in scope: it evaluates the system and a sample of engagements, not a single file. The danger is that peer review becomes a performance to be passed rather than a diagnostic to be learned from. Functions that prepare spotless files for inspection while routine work remains weak, or that contest every finding to protect a rating, extract no value from the process beyond the certificate.

Use this skill when undergoing or conducting a peer review, responding to findings, evaluating a quality system, or using review outcomes to improve. The goal is to treat peer review as a genuine diagnostic of audit quality, not as a compliance hurdle to clear.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Scope And Basis Of The Review

Peer reviews differ in scope, basis, and consequences. A regulatory inspection carries different weight than a voluntary professional peer review, and the standards applied shape what is evaluated. Engaging with the review effectively requires understanding what is being assessed and why.

Clarify at the outset:

- what standards, framework, or methodology is the review benchmarked against?
- is the review of the quality system overall, of selected engagements, or both?
- what is the population from which engagements will be selected?
- what is the consequence of the findings (public report, rating, regulatory action, internal improvement)?
- what access will reviewers have to files, personnel, and systems?

Misalignment on scope leads to disputes later. Confirm the basis in writing and ensure the engagement team understands what is being evaluated.

### Prepare Files That Reflect Routine Quality, Not Inspection Theater

The temptation to polish files for inspection is strong, but it defeats the review's purpose and can itself be a finding. A file that is materially improved only for the review misrepresents the quality of work that supported the original conclusion.

Prepare appropriately by:

- ensuring files are complete and organized as they should be for any engagement, not specially for inspection;
- avoiding backdated or post-hoc documentation that did not exist at report release;
- making minor cleanup that clarifies existing content without altering substance;
- identifying any known issues proactively rather than concealing them;
- refraining from generating new evidence to fill gaps that existed at the time.

If the review discovers that files were materially altered for inspection, the credibility of the entire function suffers. Honest files, even with imperfections, serve improvement better than polished ones that misrepresent routine practice.

### Select A Representative Sample, Not A Favorable One

If the reviewed function participates in selecting engagements for review, the selection must be representative of the work performed, not curated to show only successes. A review of only clean engagements provides no diagnostic value.

Ensure representative selection by:

- allowing the reviewer to select engagements from the full population where possible;
- including higher-risk, higher-judgment, and first-year engagements, not only routine ones;
- including any engagements with known issues or complaints;
- documenting the selection criteria and the population from which selections were drawn;
- refraining from excluding difficult engagements without documented, legitimate reason.

A favorable sample produces a favorable rating and no learning. A representative sample produces a realistic assessment.

### Engage Substantively With Findings

Peer review findings are diagnostic information. The function under review should engage with them as opportunities to understand weakness, not as criticisms to be defended or minimized.

Engage substantively by:

- reading each finding carefully and understanding the underlying concern;
- assessing whether the finding reflects a genuine quality issue or a difference in interpretation;
- where the finding is valid, accepting it and planning corrective action;
- where the finding appears incorrect, providing a reasoned, evidence-based response rather than blanket disagreement;
- looking for patterns across findings that indicate systemic rather than isolated issues;
- distinguishing findings about individual engagements from findings about the quality system.

Contesting every finding to protect a rating signals defensiveness and forfeits the review's improvement value. Accepting every finding without analysis forfeits the review's accuracy.

### Look For Systemic Causes, Not Just File-Level Fixes

A finding that a specific file lacked sufficient evidence may reflect an individual lapse, but a pattern of similar findings across files points to a systemic weakness in the quality system. The most valuable use of peer review is to identify and address systemic causes.

Look for systemic causes by asking:

- do multiple findings reflect the same type of gap (e.g., insufficient documentation of judgment, weak risk assessment, late completion of procedures)?
- does the methodology adequately address the areas where findings recur?
- does supervision, review, or training adequately support quality in those areas?
- are time, staffing, or budget pressures contributing to the lapses?
- does the tone from leadership prioritize quality or throughput?

Corrective action that addresses only the specific file, leaving the system unchanged, guarantees recurrence.

### Use Findings To Drive Sustained Improvement

A peer review's value is realized in the improvement that follows, not in the rating itself. Findings that are acknowledged, briefly addressed, and then forgotten change nothing.

Drive sustained improvement by:

- developing a corrective action plan for each systemic finding, with owners and dates;
- tracking implementation and verifying effectiveness, not just completion;
- incorporating lessons into methodology, templates, training, and supervision;
- monitoring whether the same findings recur in subsequent reviews;
- reporting progress to governance until corrective action is verified effective.

A finding that recurs in the next review indicates the corrective action did not address the cause. Treat recurrence as a signal to dig deeper.

### Maintain Independence And Competence On The Reviewer Side

For those conducting peer review, the same standards of independence, competence, and challenge apply as in any audit. A peer reviewer who is too close to the reviewed function, too junior to challenge, or too invested in a favorable outcome provides no genuine evaluation.

As a reviewer, ensure:

- independence from the reviewed function and its personnel;
- sufficient experience and seniority to assess the engagements reviewed;
- a challenge posture, not a collegial deference that softens findings;
- consistency in applying the review criteria across engagements;
- documentation that supports each finding with specific evidence.

Peer review conducted as a courtesy rather than an evaluation provides false assurance to the reviewed function and its stakeholders.

## Common Traps

### Inspection Theater

Polishing files for review while routine work remains weaker misrepresents quality and can itself become a finding. Prepare honest files.

### Curating A Favorable Sample

Selecting only clean engagements for review produces a good rating and no diagnostic value. Allow representative or reviewer-selected sampling.

### Defending Every Finding

Treating findings as attacks to be repelled forfeits the review's improvement purpose. Engage with each on its merits.

### Accepting Every Finding Without Analysis

Uncritical acceptance of findings, including those based on misinterpretation, distorts the assessment. Provide reasoned responses where appropriate.

### File-Level Fixes For Systemic Problems

Correcting individual files while leaving methodology, supervision, or training unchanged guarantees the same findings recur.

### Forgetting Findings After The Rating

Acknowledging findings to obtain a rating, then dropping corrective action, wastes the review. Track implementation and verify effectiveness.

### Reviewer Deference Or Incompetence

A peer reviewer who is too close, too junior, or too collegial cannot provide genuine evaluation. Independence and competence are prerequisites.

## Self-Check

- Is the scope, basis, standards, and consequence of the peer review clearly understood and documented before the review begins?
- Do files presented for review reflect routine quality rather than inspection-driven polish, with no backdated or post-hoc documentation?
- Is the engagement sample representative of the work performed, including higher-risk and difficult engagements, rather than curated favorably?
- Are findings engaged with substantively, accepting valid ones and providing evidence-based responses where interpretation differs?
- Are systemic patterns across findings identified and addressed at the cause level, not only at the individual file level?
- Does a corrective action plan exist for each systemic finding, with owners, dates, and effectiveness verification rather than mere completion?
- Are lessons incorporated into methodology, templates, training, and supervision to prevent recurrence?
- Is recurrence of the same findings in subsequent reviews treated as a signal to investigate deeper causes?
- For reviewers, are independence, competence, challenge posture, and consistent criteria maintained throughout?
- Could an independent observer confirm that the review drove genuine improvement rather than producing a rating and a closed file?
