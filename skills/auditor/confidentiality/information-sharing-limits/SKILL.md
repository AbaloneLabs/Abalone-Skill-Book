# Information Sharing Limits

Confidentiality obliges the auditor to protect information acquired during the engagement and to share it only as permitted. This sounds simple until the pressures of practice arrive. Governance wants details, regulators request information, other teams within the firm want to reuse findings, journalists or analysts inquire, and the entity's own personnel seek information beyond their need. Each request carries a plausible rationale, and each creates a confidentiality decision. The failure mode is not usually malicious disclosure; it is well-intentioned over-sharing, where the auditor provides more than is permitted, to the wrong party, or without the conditions that should attach. Information sharing limits are the discipline of knowing what can be shared, with whom, under what authority, and with what restrictions, and of declining or narrowing requests that exceed those limits.

Use this skill when deciding what audit information to share, handling third-party requests, communicating with governance and regulators, managing cross-engagement information flow, or recognizing over-sharing. The goal is to share information appropriately for the engagement's legitimate purposes while protecting confidentiality against disclosure that exceeds it.

## Core Rules

### Anchor Sharing In The Engagement's Legitimate Purpose

Information acquired during an audit may be used and shared for the engagement's legitimate purpose: performing the audit, reporting to those entitled to the report, and meeting legal or professional obligations. Sharing beyond that purpose, however well-intentioned, exceeds the basis on which the information was provided.

Anchor sharing by asking:

- is the sharing for a purpose connected to the engagement, or for a separate purpose?
- is the recipient entitled to the information by role, contract, law, or professional duty?
- is the level of detail proportionate to the recipient's need, or is more being shared than necessary?
- are there conditions, such as confidentiality agreements or legal restrictions, that apply?

If the sharing serves a purpose other than the engagement's legitimate one, it likely exceeds the permitted use, and the default is to decline or narrow it.

### Identify Who Is Entitled To What

Different parties are entitled to different levels of audit information, and confusion about entitlements leads to over- or under-sharing. The entity's management, those charged with governance, regulators, and third parties each have different claims, and the auditor must know what each is entitled to receive.

Clarify entitlements:

- management is generally entitled to findings relevant to their responsibilities, but not to information beyond their role or need;
- those charged with governance are entitled to information needed to discharge their oversight, including significant findings and control issues;
- regulators may have legal authority to request information, but the scope of that authority varies and should be understood;
- third parties, including other firms, analysts, or the public, generally have no entitlement unless the entity or law provides it.

Sharing based on the recipient's request rather than their entitlement leads to over-sharing. Determine entitlement first, then respond accordingly.

### Handle Third-Party Requests Deliberately

Requests for audit information from third parties, subpoenas, regulator inquiries, requests from prospective investors or lenders, or inquiries from media, require deliberate handling rather than reflexive compliance or refusal. Each request must be assessed for its legal basis, the auditor's obligations, and the entity's interests.

Handle third-party requests by:

- assessing the legal authority behind the request, such as a subpoena, a regulatory demand, or a voluntary inquiry;
- determining what the auditor is legally permitted or required to disclose;
- consulting the entity and, where appropriate, legal counsel, before responding;
- disclosing only what is required and no more, with appropriate context;
- documenting the request, the assessment, and the response.

Reflexive compliance with any request over-shares; reflexive refusal may breach a legal obligation. Deliberate assessment gets the response right.

### Apply Need-To-Know Within The Engagement Team

Confidentiality limits apply within the engagement team as much as externally. Not every team member needs access to all information, and sharing within the team should follow need-to-know principles. This is especially important for highly sensitive matters such as fraud investigations, executive issues, or personal data.

Apply need-to-know internally by:

- limiting access to sensitive findings to those working on the relevant area;
- controlling discussion of sensitive matters to those who need to know;
- avoiding casual sharing of engagement information in open team settings or with personnel from other engagements;
- recognizing that the firm's other clients and teams are themselves third parties with respect to this engagement's information.

Over-sharing within the team, or across engagements, can breach confidentiality as surely as external disclosure. Need-to-know applies internally.

### Manage Cross-Engagement And Cross-Client Information Flow

Audit firms conduct many engagements, and information from one can be relevant to another. But using or sharing information across engagements creates confidentiality risk, because information acquired for one client's engagement is generally not available for another's purposes without proper basis.

Manage cross-engagement flow by:

- not using one client's confidential information to serve another client;
- obtaining proper authorization before sharing information that could benefit a related or common engagement;
- recognizing that common ownership or related parties do not automatically permit cross-sharing;
- applying information barriers where the firm serves clients whose interests may conflict;
- anonymizing or generalizing lessons learned so that transferable knowledge is shared without client-specific detail.

The convenience of reusing information across engagements does not override the confidentiality owed to each client. Cross-sharing requires a proper basis.

### Recognize Permitted And Required Disclosures

Confidentiality is not absolute. There are circumstances where disclosure is permitted or required: to comply with legal or regulatory obligations, to respond to a properly authorized request, or, in some circumstances, to protect against imminent harm. The auditor must recognize these circumstances and respond appropriately, while understanding their limits.

Recognize permitted and required disclosure by:

- understanding the legal and professional obligations that may require disclosure, such as responding to a regulator or court;
- distinguishing a requirement to disclose from a permission, and a permission from a prohibition;
- disclosing only what the obligation requires, with appropriate context and no more;
- documenting the basis for any required or permitted disclosure;
- recognizing that some disclosures, even when permitted, may have consequences for the engagement or relationship.

Confidentiality yields to genuine legal requirements, but the auditor should not treat convenience or pressure as a requirement. Confirm the basis before disclosing.

### Communicate Findings With Appropriate Precision And Restraint

Even when sharing with entitled parties, how information is communicated matters. Over-detailed sharing, speculative commentary, or characterization beyond the evidence can create misunderstanding, breach confidentiality about third parties, or expose the auditor to liability. Findings should be communicated with precision and restraint.

Communicate carefully by:

- stating findings as supported by evidence, without speculation or characterization beyond the support;
- limiting detail to what the recipient needs to understand the finding and its significance;
- avoiding unnecessary identification of individuals or third parties where not relevant to the finding;
- providing context that prevents misinterpretation, without editorializing;
- distinguishing confirmed findings from preliminary observations or allegations.

Sharing the right information with the right party, but expressed carelessly, can still cause harm. Precision and restraint protect both the entity and the auditor.

## Common Traps

### Well-Intentioned Over-Sharing

Providing more detail than the recipient needs or is entitled to, out of helpfulness, exceeds the permitted use. Share proportionately.

### Sharing Based On Request Rather Than Entitlement

Responding to what is asked rather than what the recipient is entitled to receive leads to over-sharing. Determine entitlement first.

### Reflexive Compliance With Third-Party Requests

Treating any request as something to satisfy, without assessing legal authority, over-discloses. Assess each request deliberately.

### Internal Or Cross-Engagement Casual Sharing

Discussing one engagement's information openly, or reusing it for another client, breaches confidentiality. Apply need-to-know internally.

### Convenience Treated As A Basis For Cross-Sharing

Reusing one client's information to serve another, because it is convenient, overrides confidentiality without proper basis. Obtain authorization.

### Over-Detailed Or Speculative Communication

Sharing findings with excessive detail, speculation, or characterization beyond the evidence creates misunderstanding and exposure. Communicate with precision and restraint.

### Convenience Or Pressure Treated As A Required Disclosure

Treating pressure or convenience as a legal requirement to disclose over-shares. Confirm the genuine basis before disclosing.

## Self-Check

- Is each instance of information sharing anchored in the engagement's legitimate purpose, with detail proportionate to the recipient's need?
- Is the entitlement of each recipient, management, governance, regulator, or third party, determined before sharing, rather than sharing based on request?
- Are third-party requests assessed for legal authority, with consultation and documented response, rather than met with reflexive compliance or refusal?
- Is need-to-know applied within the engagement team, with sensitive matters limited to those who require them?
- Is cross-engagement and cross-client information flow controlled, with no reuse of one client's information for another without proper basis?
- Are permitted and required disclosures recognized, with the basis confirmed and disclosure limited to what the obligation requires?
- Are findings communicated with precision and restraint, supported by evidence, without speculation or unnecessary identification?
- Is the distinction between confirmed findings and preliminary observations maintained in communication?
- Are confidentiality obligations to third parties mentioned in findings respected, not only those of the entity?
- Could an independent reviewer confirm that information was shared appropriately for legitimate purposes and protected against disclosure that exceeded those purposes?
