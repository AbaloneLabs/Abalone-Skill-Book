# Internal Audit And Assurance Engagements

Internal audit occupies a different position from external audit. It sits inside the organization, reports to governance rather than to external users, and serves the entity's own improvement rather than issuing a public opinion. This position gives internal audit broad access and deep familiarity, but it also creates distinctive risks: organizational pressure can shape scope, the auditor's closeness to management can erode objectivity, and the line between assurance and consulting can blur until the internal auditor is effectively doing management's job. A strong internal audit function challenges the organization it serves; a weak one becomes a rubber stamp that lends the appearance of oversight without the substance.

Use this skill when performing internal audit engagements, building a risk-based audit plan, assessing the function's organizational independence, conducting consulting engagements, or deciding how to preserve objectivity while adding value. The goal is to keep internal audit independent enough to be credible and useful enough to matter, without collapsing into management's perspective.

## Core Rules

### Establish And Protect Organizational Independence

Internal audit's credibility depends on its position within the organization. If the chief audit executive reports administratively to management, can be overruled on scope, or can be removed without governance involvement, the function's conclusions cannot be trusted to be free of management influence.

Confirm the structural conditions for independence:

- the chief audit executive reports functionally to the board or audit committee;
- the board approves the internal audit charter, the audit plan, the budget, and the chief audit executive's appointment and removal;
- the chief audit executive has direct and unrestricted access to the board and its chair;
- compensation of internal audit is not controlled by the functions being audited;
- internal audit is free to set scope, perform procedures, and report results without management veto;
- the charter is approved by the board and reviewed periodically.

Where these conditions are absent, the function is impaired regardless of the competence of its staff. Document the independence assessment and raise structural deficiencies with governance.

### Build A Risk-Based Audit Plan Aligned To Strategy

An internal audit plan that is not risk-based becomes a rotating checklist that tests the same areas every year while emerging risks go unaddressed. The plan should direct scarce audit resources to where they add the most assurance and improvement value.

Build the plan by:

- understanding the organization's strategy, objectives, and key risks;
- assessing risk across processes, systems, geographies, and initiatives;
- incorporating input from the board, management, regulators, and prior audit results;
- considering fraud, compliance, technology, cybersecurity, third-party, and ESG risks;
- balancing assurance engagements with consulting requests and mandated reviews;
- accounting for resource and competence constraints;
- leaving capacity for emerging issues and investigations during the year;
- obtaining board approval and explaining what was included and excluded and why.

The plan is not fixed. Reassess it during the year when strategy, risk, or events shift the priorities.

### Distinguish Assurance From Consulting Engagements

Internal audit performs both assurance engagements, which provide an independent conclusion against criteria, and consulting engagements, which provide advice and recommendations without the same conclusion. The two have different objectives, scope, evidence, and reporting, and confusing them creates false expectations.

For assurance engagements:

- define objective, scope, criteria, and the conclusion to be reached;
- apply standards of evidence and documentation appropriate to the assurance level;
- report a conclusion supported by evidence;
- preserve objectivity and independence throughout.

For consulting engagements:

- clarify that the engagement is advisory and does not provide assurance;
- avoid making management decisions or taking on operational responsibility;
- recognize the self-review threat if the area later becomes subject to assurance;
- document the nature and limits of the engagement.

Do not present consulting work as assurance, and do not allow consulting to compromise the independence needed for future assurance in the same area. When consulting and assurance overlap, disclose the threat and consider safeguards or reassignment.

### Preserve Individual Objectivity And Rotation

Even with strong organizational independence, individual objectivity can erode through familiarity, friendship, prior roles, or consulting involvement. Internal auditors who audit their own prior work, their friends, or processes they helped design cannot be fully objective.

Protect individual objectivity by:

- rotating auditors off assignments after a defined period;
- avoiding assignment of auditors to areas where they recently held operational roles;
- disclosing and managing personal relationships with auditees;
- not allowing auditors to make management decisions in areas they later audit;
- reassigning auditors where consulting work creates a self-review threat;
- monitoring for undue familiarity over long engagements.

Objectivity is a state to be maintained, not assumed. Build the practices that sustain it into staffing and assignment decisions.

### Apply Professional Standards Appropriate To Internal Audit

Internal audit is typically conducted under professional standards such as the IIA's International Professional Practices Framework, which sets expectations for the charter, independence, proficiency, due professional care, and quality. Applying external audit standards mechanically, or no standards at all, produces work that does not meet the function's professional obligations.

Apply:

- the mandatory guidance including the definition of internal auditing, the code of ethics, and the standards;
- conformance with requirements for planning, performing, communicating, and monitoring;
- a quality assurance and improvement program with internal and external assessments;
- due professional care appropriate to the engagement's complexity and risk.

Where local or sector-specific internal audit standards apply, comply with those in addition. Document the basis for the standards applied.

### Report To Governance With Unfiltered Conclusions

Internal audit's reporting line to governance is what distinguishes it from management's own review. If internal audit filters, softens, or withholds conclusions to avoid displeasing management, the governance reporting line is defeated.

Report to governance:

- the audit plan and any deviations and why;
- significant findings, risks, and control deficiencies without management-friendly editing;
- the status of remediation of prior findings;
- the function's independence and any impairments;
- the results of the quality assurance program;
- emerging risks identified during the year;
- areas where management restricted scope or access.

The chief audit executive owns the conclusions. Management can provide response and context, but cannot veto the finding or its severity. Escalate attempts to suppress or alter conclusions.

### Coordinate With External Audit And Other Assurance Providers

Internal audit does not operate in isolation. External auditors, risk management, compliance, security, and other assurance functions may overlap in coverage. Coordination prevents gaps and duplication and allows external audit to place appropriate reliance on internal audit work.

Coordinate by:

- sharing the audit plan and understanding external audit's planned scope;
- making internal audit workpapers available where external audit may rely on them;
- assessing internal audit's competence and objectivity as a basis for reliance;
- aligning timing so that work is available when external audit needs it;
- avoiding any arrangement that compromises internal audit's independence;
- maintaining a combined assurance view that maps coverage across providers.

Reliance decisions rest with external audit, which must assess internal audit's work before placing weight on it. Internal audit should not assume its work will be relied upon.

### Maintain Due Professional Care Without Assurance Overreach

Internal audit applies due professional care, meaning reasonable care and competence, not absolute assurance. Engagements should be scoped to the risk and resources, and conclusions should not overstate what the work supports.

Maintain discipline by:

- stating the engagement's objectives, scope, and limitations clearly;
- not implying a level of assurance the procedures do not support;
- acknowledging that internal audit cannot guarantee that all risks are detected;
- distinguishing tested conclusions from observations and improvement suggestions;
- documenting procedures and evidence sufficient to support the conclusion.

Overreach, claiming more than the work supports, undermines credibility when a later event reveals an undetected issue. Underreach, vague conclusions that add no assurance, makes the function dispensable. Aim for conclusions that match the evidence.

## Common Traps

### Reporting Administratively To Management

If the chief audit executive reports to management and can be directed on scope, the function's independence is structurally impaired regardless of competence. Functional reporting to the board is essential.

### Plan As A Rotating Checklist

A plan that tests the same areas each year misses emerging risks and wastes effort on low-risk areas. Rebuild the plan from risk each year.

### Consulting That Compromises Future Assurance

Consulting on a process and later auditing it creates a self-review threat. Either avoid the consulting, reassign the audit, or disclose and safeguard.

### Familiarity Eroding Objectivity

Long assignments, friendships, and prior operational roles quietly erode the independence that makes conclusions credible. Rotate and disclose relationships.

### Filtering Findings For Management

Softening or withholding conclusions to maintain relationships defeats the governance reporting line. The chief audit executive owns the conclusions.

### Applying No Standards Or Wrong Standards

Work performed without reference to internal audit standards, or using external audit standards inappropriately, fails professional obligations and cannot be relied upon by others.

### Assuming External Audit Will Rely On Internal Audit

Reliance is external audit's decision based on its assessment of competence and objectivity. Do not assume it; produce work that withstands that assessment.

### Overstating Assurance

Claiming a process is effective based on limited testing overstates the conclusion. Match the conclusion to the evidence and the engagement type.

## Self-Check

- Does the chief audit executive report functionally to the board or audit committee, with board approval of charter, plan, budget, appointment, and removal?
- Is the audit plan risk-based, aligned to strategy, board-approved, and reassessed during the year as risks change?
- Are assurance and consulting engagements distinguished, with scope, criteria, evidence, and reporting matched to the engagement type?
- Are individual objectivity safeguards, rotation, disclosure of relationships, and avoidance of self-review, in place?
- Is the applicable internal audit professional standard identified and conformance documented, including a quality assurance program?
- Are significant findings, risks, impairments, and remediation status reported to governance without management filtering?
- Is coordination with external audit and other assurance providers managed to avoid gaps and duplication without compromising independence?
- Are conclusions matched to the evidence, without overstating assurance or implying guarantees the procedures do not support?
- Are scope restrictions, access limitations, and management pressure escalated to governance?
- Is the internal audit charter approved by the board and reviewed periodically?
