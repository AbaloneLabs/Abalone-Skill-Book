# Operational And Performance Audit

Operational and performance audits do not ask whether the numbers are right. They ask whether the organization is doing the right things, efficiently, economically, and effectively. This makes them powerful and also hard. The criteria are less prescribed than in financial audit, the evidence is often a mix of quantitative metrics and qualitative observation, and the conclusions can be politically sensitive because they judge whether programs and managers are succeeding. The distinctive risk is that without disciplined criteria and evidence, an operational audit becomes an opinion piece: the auditor finds inefficiency because they expected to, or declares success because the metrics management chose look good. Credibility depends on establishing objective criteria before judging performance.

Use this skill when planning or performing an operational or performance audit, defining economy, efficiency, and effectiveness criteria, selecting performance indicators, evaluating program outcomes, or reporting on operational results. The goal is to produce conclusions grounded in objective criteria and sufficient evidence rather than impressions or management-selected metrics.

## Core Rules

### Define The Three Es Explicitly

Operational and performance audits are built on three interrelated dimensions, and conflating them produces muddled conclusions. Each must be defined and assessed distinctly.

- economy: acquiring resources of the right quality at the right cost, minimizing input cost without sacrificing quality;
- efficiency: obtaining the maximum output from given inputs, or minimizing inputs for a given output, the input-output relationship;
- effectiveness: achieving the intended objectives or outcomes, doing the right things.

A program can be economical but ineffective, efficient at doing the wrong thing, or effective but wasteful. State which dimension the audit addresses, and do not let a good score on one substitute for a poor score on another. A conclusion that a process is efficient says nothing about whether it is effective.

### Establish Objective Criteria Before Judging

Unlike financial audit, operational audit criteria are not always prescribed by a framework. The auditor must identify or construct criteria that are relevant, complete, objective, and accepted. Without this, conclusions are subjective.

Source criteria from:

- the program's enabling legislation, mandate, or charter;
- board-approved objectives, targets, and strategic plans;
- industry benchmarks and comparable organizations;
- historical performance and trend data;
- regulatory or contractual performance requirements;
- service-level agreements and customer expectations;
- professional or technical standards for the activity;
- management's own stated goals and budgets.

Where management has set no objectives, that is itself a finding. Avoid imposing the auditor's personal standards as criteria; ground them in authoritative sources and disclose their origin. Where criteria are contested, present the basis and the alternatives considered.

### Measure Against Outcomes, Not Only Outputs

A frequent failure is to measure outputs, the things produced, and infer effectiveness. Outputs are easy to count but do not prove that the program achieved its purpose. Effectiveness requires evidence about outcomes.

Distinguish:

- inputs: resources consumed, such as money, staff time, materials;
- activities and outputs: what was done and produced, such as inspections conducted, claims processed, training delivered;
- outcomes: the change or result achieved, such as reduced incidents, faster resolution, improved satisfaction, compliance achieved;
- impacts: the broader effect on the problem the program exists to address.

An operational audit that counts outputs without assessing outcomes can certify a busy, expensive process that does not accomplish its goal. Design procedures to reach outcome-level evidence where feasible, and acknowledge where outcome measurement is limited by data or attribution.

### Address Attribution And Causation Honestly

Performance conclusions require showing that the program, not some other factor, caused the observed outcome. Attribution is often difficult because outcomes are influenced by many variables outside the program. Overstating causation undermines credibility.

Assess attribution by:

- comparing to a baseline, control group, or comparable unit where possible;
- considering alternative explanations for the observed outcome;
- using multiple lines of evidence, quantitative and qualitative, to triangulate;
- acknowledging confounding factors such as economic conditions, other initiatives, or external events;
- avoiding before-after designs that assume the program caused any change that followed it.

Where attribution cannot be established firmly, report the outcome evidence and the limitations rather than implying the program caused the result. Honest uncertainty is more credible than false precision.

### Select Performance Indicators Critically

Management often selects the indicators that show the program favorably. The auditor must evaluate whether the indicators measure what matters or whether they have been chosen to flatter. Indicators that are easy to game or that measure activity rather than result provide false comfort.

Evaluate indicators for:

- relevance to the objective, not merely to what is measurable;
- validity, whether the indicator actually reflects the underlying performance;
- reliability and whether the data is collected consistently;
- resistance to gaming and unintended incentives;
- completeness, whether key dimensions are omitted;
- timeliness and whether the data supports timely decisions;
- comparability across periods and units.

Where the existing indicators are weak, recommend better ones, but base current conclusions on the best available evidence, not on indicators that happen to look good.

### Use Mixed Methods And Triangulate

Operational audits rarely rest on a single evidence type. Performance is multifaceted, and strong conclusions combine quantitative data, qualitative input, observation, and comparison. Relying on one source risks missing the picture or being misled by it.

Combine:

- quantitative analysis of cost, volume, time, and outcome data;
- process walkthroughs and observation of operations;
- interviews and surveys of staff, customers, and stakeholders;
- benchmarking against comparable organizations or internal units;
- document review of policies, reports, and management information;
- root cause analysis of problems identified.

Triangulation means that when different evidence types point to the same conclusion, confidence rises, and when they diverge, the discrepancy is investigated rather than ignored.

### Quantify Opportunity And Cost Of Inefficiency

Operational audit findings are more compelling and actionable when the cost of the problem is quantified. Even where exact figures are not possible, estimating the scale of waste, delay, or missed outcome helps governance prioritize.

Quantify where feasible:

- excess cost relative to benchmark or efficient practice;
- cost of rework, errors, delays, or duplication;
- cost of unused capacity or overstaffing;
- value of missed outcomes or benefits foregone;
- cost of risks created by the inefficiency;
- potential savings or improvement from recommended changes.

Where quantification is not feasible, describe the nature and potential scale of the inefficiency and explain why a figure could not be developed. Do not invent precision to make a finding look stronger.

### Report Conclusions Against Criteria With Evidence

The report structure mirrors the audit logic: objective, criteria, condition, cause, effect, and recommendation. The reader should be able to see what the program was supposed to achieve, what was found, why, and what should change.

Structure findings to show:

- the objective or standard, the criteria;
- what was found, the condition, with evidence;
- why the condition exists, the cause;
- the consequence, the effect, quantified where possible;
- the recommendation addressing the cause;
- management response where appropriate.

Avoid conclusory language such as the operation is inefficient without showing compared to what, how measured, and how significant. Operational audit conclusions invite challenge, so the evidentiary basis must be transparent.

### Manage Sensitivity And Fairness

Operational and performance audits judge people and programs, which makes them politically and personally sensitive. The auditor must be fair, give management the chance to respond, and avoid language that assigns blame beyond the evidence. At the same time, sensitivity must not soften supported conclusions.

Maintain fairness by:

- sharing findings with management before finalizing;
- validating factual accuracy and context;
- distinguishing systemic issues from individual performance;
- avoiding blame-laden language where cause is structural;
- including management response and planned action;
- preserving the auditor's conclusion where evidence supports it.

Fairness does not mean avoiding difficult conclusions. It means reaching them through a process that those affected can recognize as even-handed.

## Common Traps

### Conflating Economy, Efficiency, And Effectiveness

A good score on cost or output does not mean the program is effective. Assess each dimension separately and do not substitute one for another.

### Criteria Chosen After Seeing The Results

Selecting criteria after observing performance allows the auditor to pick standards that produce a desired conclusion. Establish criteria before judging.

### Measuring Outputs And Calling It Effectiveness

Outputs prove activity, not results. Reaching outcome-level evidence is harder but necessary to support effectiveness conclusions.

### Overstating Attribution

Assuming the program caused an outcome because the outcome followed it ignores confounding factors. Triangulate and acknowledge alternative explanations.

### Trusting Management-Selected Indicators

Indicators chosen to flatter will flatter. Evaluate whether indicators measure what matters and are resistant to gaming.

### Single-Source Evidence

Relying only on data, only on interviews, or only on documents risks missing or misreading the situation. Triangulate across methods.

### Conclusory Findings Without Criteria

Saying an operation is inefficient without stating the benchmark, the measurement, and the significance is an opinion, not a finding. Anchor every conclusion in criteria and evidence.

### Softening Conclusions To Avoid Conflict

Operational audits judge performance, which is sensitive. Fairness requires a fair process, not weakened conclusions. Preserve supported findings while presenting them even-handedly.

## Self-Check

- Are economy, efficiency, and effectiveness defined and assessed as distinct dimensions rather than collapsed?
- Are criteria established before judging performance, sourced from authoritative references, and disclosed with their origin?
- Does the audit reach outcome-level evidence rather than relying only on outputs and activity counts?
- Is attribution assessed honestly, with alternative explanations considered and limitations acknowledged where causation cannot be firmly established?
- Have performance indicators been evaluated for relevance, validity, reliability, resistance to gaming, and completeness rather than accepted because they look favorable?
- Does the audit use mixed methods and triangulate across quantitative, qualitative, observational, and comparative evidence?
- Is the cost of inefficiency or the value of missed outcomes quantified where feasible, with honest acknowledgment where it is not?
- Does each finding state objective, criteria, condition, cause, effect, and recommendation with transparent evidence?
- Has management had a fair opportunity to respond, with factual accuracy validated and response included, while the auditor's conclusion is preserved?
- Are conclusions free of blame-laden language beyond the evidence, focusing on systemic cause rather than individual fault?
