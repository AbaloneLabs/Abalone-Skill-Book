# Business Combination Impact

A business combination is one of the most disruptive events that can occur during an audit period. It changes the entity's structure, introduces new operations and risks, creates complex purchase accounting, and often brings goodwill and intangibles that become recurring significant risks. Agents often treat an acquisition as an incremental area to test rather than a reshaping event, applying the prior-year approach plus a little extra work on the new subsidiary. That under-response misses integration risk, purchase price allocation errors, and the fraud and override opportunities that acquisitions create around the deal date. A business combination must be assessed as a change event that ripples through scope, risk, materiality, and procedures.

Use this skill when the entity has acquired, disposed of, or merged during the period, and when adjusting the audit for the effects. The goal is to recognize the combination as a reshaping event and to recalibrate scope, risk, and procedures accordingly.

## Core Rules

### Recognize The Combination As A Reshaping Event

A business combination is not an incremental area; it reshapes the entity. Treat it as a change event that affects structure, risk, and approach.

Recognize the effects:

- the reporting entity's structure and boundaries change;
- new operations, locations, and systems are introduced;
- new risks, including those of the acquired entity, enter the group;
- purchase accounting creates new balances, including goodwill and intangibles;
- integration creates transition risk in controls, systems, and processes;
- comparative information and consistency are affected.

Document the combination's effects across the audit, not only in the acquisition workpaper. A combination treated as a side area leaves its ripple effects unaddressed.

### Adjust Scope And Boundaries For The New Structure

The combination changes what is in scope. Update the scope and boundaries to reflect the new entity structure.

Adjust scope by:

- adding the acquired entity, its locations, systems, and accounts to the audit;
- determining the acquisition date and the period of consolidation;
- removing disposed operations and determining their treatment up to the disposal date;
- identifying whether component auditors are needed for the acquired entity;
- reassessing multi-location strategy given the new footprint;
- confirming the engagement scope still matches the reporting entity.

A scope that does not reflect the post-combination entity leaves parts of the reporting entity unaddressed or includes disposed operations that should be excluded.

### Assess Purchase Accounting And Purchase Price Allocation

Purchase accounting is a significant, judgment-heavy area that is a common source of material misstatement, especially in the allocation of the purchase price.

Assess purchase accounting by:

- confirming the purchase consideration, including contingent consideration and debt assumed;
- identifying and valuing the identifiable assets acquired and liabilities assumed;
- evaluating the valuation of intangibles, often requiring a specialist;
- confirming the measurement period and any adjustments to provisional amounts;
- testing the recognition and measurement of goodwill, including what gives rise to it;
- assessing the treatment of pre-acquisition contingencies and tax positions;
- evaluating the completeness of liabilities assumed, including undisclosed or contingent items.

Purchase price allocation errors, especially overstatement of intangibles to reduce goodwill, are common and material. Engage specialists for valuations and challenge the assumptions.

### Recalibrate Risk For The Acquired Entity's Risks

The acquired entity brings its own risks, which now belong to the group. Assess and incorporate them.

Recalibrate risk by:

- understanding the acquired entity's business, industry, and risk profile;
- identifying its significant risks, estimates, and fraud exposures;
- assessing its control environment, which may differ from the acquirer's;
- determining whether its controls can be relied upon or whether a substantive approach is needed;
- incorporating its risks into the group risk assessment and significant risk list.

An acquired entity with weak controls or aggressive accounting imports those risks into the group. Do not assume the acquirer's control environment extends automatically to the acquisition.

### Evaluate Integration Risk And Control Transition

Integration is where controls, systems, and processes are in transition, creating gaps and errors. Assess integration risk specifically.

Assess integration risk by considering:

- whether the acquired entity's controls continue to operate during integration or are disrupted;
- whether the acquirer's controls have been extended to the acquisition and are operating;
- whether systems are being converted or interfaced, creating data integrity risk;
- whether key personnel have departed, losing knowledge and control continuity;
- whether processes are being standardized, creating transition errors;
- whether intercompany transactions between acquirer and acquiree are identified and eliminated.

Integration periods are high-risk for control failure and error. Treat the transition as a risk factor and design procedures to cover it.

### Reassess Materiality And Comparability

A combination can distort benchmarks and comparability, requiring materiality and comparative information to be reassessed.

Reassess:

- whether the benchmark used for materiality is distorted by the combination, such as one-off deal costs or acquired earnings;
- whether comparative figures are restated for a uniting-of-interests or common-control transaction;
- whether pro-forma or supplementary information is required and is accurate;
- whether the combination affects going concern, leverage, or covenant assessment;
- whether segment reporting and disclosure are affected.

Document the materiality reassessment and the treatment of comparative information. A benchmark distorted by the combination can produce a materiality figure that no longer fits.

### Address Fraud And Override Opportunities Around The Deal

Business combinations create fraud and override opportunities, especially around the acquisition date and in purchase accounting.

Address by:

- scrutinizing pre-acquisition reserves and provisions of the acquiree for big-bath bias;
- examining post-acquisition earnings for release of overstated acquired liabilities;
- testing for channel-stuffing or revenue acceleration around the deal date;
- reviewing related-party aspects of the transaction, including the seller's identity;
- assessing whether the transaction's substance matches its form;
- including purchase accounting journal entries in override testing.

Acquisitions are occasions for earnings management through purchase accounting. Apply heightened skepticism to the allocation, the assumptions, and the timing.

## Common Traps

### Treating The Acquisition As An Incremental Area

A combination reshapes the entity. Treating it as a side area plus a little extra work misses its ripple effects on scope, risk, and materiality.

### Accepting Purchase Price Allocation Without Challenge

Purchase accounting is judgment-heavy and a common source of misstatement, especially intangibles and goodwill. Accepting the allocation without challenging assumptions and engaging specialists is a high-risk shortcut.

### Assuming The Acquirer's Controls Extend To The Acquisition

The acquired entity has its own control environment, which may be weaker. Do not assume integration has transferred the acquirer's controls automatically.

### Overlooking Integration Risk

Integration disrupts controls, systems, and personnel. Treating the transition as routine misses the period of highest control failure risk.

### Ignoring The Acquired Entity's Risks

The acquisition imports its own significant risks, estimates, and fraud exposures. These must be incorporated into the group risk assessment.

### Failing To Reassess Materiality And Comparability

A combination can distort benchmarks and comparability. A materiality figure based on a distorted benchmark no longer fits the entity.

### Missing Fraud Opportunities Around The Deal

Acquisitions create earnings management opportunities through purchase accounting and pre- or post-deal timing. Apply heightened skepticism to the allocation and the surrounding transactions.

## Self-Check

- Is the business combination recognized as a reshaping event, with its effects documented across scope, risk, materiality, and procedures?
- Is the scope and entity boundary adjusted to include the acquired entity from the acquisition date and exclude disposed operations from the disposal date?
- Is purchase accounting assessed, including consideration, identifiable assets and liabilities, intangibles, goodwill, measurement period adjustments, and contingent items, with specialists engaged for valuations?
- Are the acquired entity's significant risks, estimates, and fraud exposures incorporated into the group risk assessment?
- Is integration risk assessed, including control disruption, system conversion, personnel departure, and intercompany elimination?
- Are materiality and comparative information reassessed for benchmark distortion and restatement requirements?
- Are fraud and override opportunities around the deal addressed, including pre-acquisition reserves, post-acquisition releases, deal-date revenue, and purchase accounting entries?
- Could the auditor demonstrate, from the documentation, how the combination changed the audit approach rather than merely adding an acquisition workpaper?
