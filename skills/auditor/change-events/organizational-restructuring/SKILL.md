# Organizational Restructuring

Organizational restructuring, whether a reorganization, a cost-cutting program, a divestiture, or a reduction in force, is a change event that affects risk, estimates, going concern, and often the control environment. Agents often treat restructuring as a non-recurring item to test in isolation, missing its broader effects: the provisions and charges it creates are estimate-heavy and prone to bias, the disruption weakens controls and loses key personnel, and the strategic shift can signal or create going concern pressure. A restructuring must be assessed as a change event that ripples through risk assessment, estimates, controls, and viability, not merely as an expense to verify.

Use this skill when the entity has undergone or announced a restructuring during the period, and when adjusting the audit for its effects. The goal is to recognize the restructuring as a multifaceted risk event and to recalibrate procedures across estimates, controls, and going concern.

## Core Rules

### Recognize Restructuring As A Multifaceted Risk Event

Restructuring is not a single expense; it is an event with effects across the financial statements and the control environment.

Recognize the effects:

- restructuring charges and provisions are estimate-heavy and prone to bias;
- asset impairments often accompany restructuring and require testing;
- the control environment is disrupted by reorganization, layoffs, and departures;
- going concern and viability may be affected, positively or negatively;
- segmentation, reporting lines, and processes change, affecting consistency;
- fraud and override opportunities arise around the timing and measurement of charges.

Document the restructuring's effects across the audit. A restructuring treated as an isolated expense misses its ripple effects.

### Assess Restructuring Charges And Provisions For Bias

Restructuring provisions are estimates, and estimates can be biased. Assess the recognition and measurement of restructuring charges deliberately.

Assess charges and provisions by:

- confirming that the criteria for recognizing a liability are met, such as a detailed plan and communication;
- evaluating the components of the charge, including severance, lease termination, and asset write-downs;
- testing the assumptions behind the estimates, such as headcount, timing, and costs;
- assessing whether the charge is overstated to create a reserve for future release (big bath);
- assessing whether the charge is understated to protect current earnings;
- reviewing subsequent utilization of the provision for releases into earnings.

Restructuring charges are a classic earnings management tool. Look back at the provision utilization and challenge both the initial estimate and the releases.

### Test Impairment Triggered By Restructuring

Restructuring often triggers impairment of assets, goodwill, or intangibles, because operations are being scaled back or exited.

Test impairment by:

- identifying assets, cash-generating units, or reporting units affected by the restructuring;
- assessing whether the restructuring constitutes an impairment indicator;
- evaluating management's impairment test, including cash flow projections and discount rates;
- challenging assumptions about future cash flows given the restructuring's effect on the business;
- assessing whether the restructuring changes the recoverability of goodwill;
- considering whether a specialist is needed for valuation.

Restructuring assumptions and impairment assumptions must be consistent. A restructuring that scales back a business but an impairment test that assumes growth is internally contradictory.

### Evaluate The Effect On The Control Environment

Restructuring disrupts the control environment through reorganization, layoffs, and the departure of key personnel.

Evaluate the effect by:

- assessing whether key control personnel departed, losing knowledge and continuity;
- assessing whether reorganized reporting lines have clear control ownership;
- assessing whether reduced staffing leaves insufficient segregation of duties;
- assessing whether morale pressure increases the risk of fraud or error;
- assessing whether controls were redesigned for the new structure and are operating.

A restructured organization may have weakened controls during the transition. Adjust control risk and procedures for the disruption.

### Reassess Going Concern And Viability

Restructuring is often a response to financial difficulty, and it can affect going concern in both directions: a successful restructuring may restore viability, while a failed one may deepen doubt.

Reassess going concern by:

- evaluating the business rationale and expected outcomes of the restructuring;
- assessing management's going concern assessment, including cash flow projections;
- considering whether the restructuring costs and disruptions worsen liquidity;
- assessing whether the restructuring achieves the cost savings or synergies projected;
- evaluating the adequacy of disclosure of going concern and viability matters.

A restructuring announced to address losses is a going concern signal. Do not accept optimistic projections without challenge, and ensure disclosure is adequate.

### Address Fraud And Override Opportunities

Restructuring creates fraud and override opportunities, particularly around the timing and measurement of charges and the utilization of provisions.

Address by:

- scrutinizing the timing of restructuring announcements relative to period end;
- testing for big-bath behavior, where charges are inflated in a bad period;
- testing for provision release into earnings in later periods;
- examining any one-time or unusual entries associated with the restructuring;
- assessing whether the restructuring masks underlying performance deterioration;
- including restructuring-related entries in override testing.

Restructuring is an occasion for earnings management through the size and timing of charges. Apply heightened skepticism to the estimates and the subsequent utilization.

### Adjust Scope And Procedures For The New Structure

Restructuring changes the entity's structure and processes, requiring scope and procedures to be updated.

Adjust by:

- updating the process understanding and walkthroughs for reorganized processes;
- reassessing risk for areas where controls or personnel changed;
- updating the multi-location or component strategy for divested or closed operations;
- ensuring procedures cover both the pre- and post-restructuring periods;
- considering whether the restructuring affects comparability and segment reporting.

If procedures rely on the pre-restructuring structure and controls, they may not fit the post-restructuring reality. Update the understanding and the procedures.

## Common Traps

### Treating Restructuring As An Isolated Expense

Restructuring has effects across estimates, impairment, controls, and going concern. Treating it as a single charge to verify misses its ripple effects.

### Accepting Restructuring Provisions Without Bias Review

Restructuring provisions are estimate-heavy and a classic earnings management tool. Review both the initial estimate and subsequent utilization for bias.

### Ignoring Impairment Triggered By Restructuring

Scaling back or exiting operations often triggers impairment. Failing to test impairment, or accepting inconsistent assumptions, misses a material effect.

### Overlooking Control Environment Disruption

Layoffs and reorganization weaken controls and lose key personnel. A restructured organization may have reduced segregation and continuity.

### Missing The Going Concern Signal

Restructuring is often a response to difficulty. Accepting optimistic projections without challenge, or failing to ensure adequate disclosure, is a going concern failure.

### Missing Fraud Opportunities Around Timing And Measurement

The timing of charges and the utilization of provisions are earnings management opportunities. Apply heightened skepticism to both.

### Relying On Pre-Restructuring Controls And Structure

If procedures assume the old structure and controls, they may not fit the new reality. Update the understanding and procedures for the restructured entity.

## Self-Check

- Is the restructuring recognized as a multifaceted risk event, with effects documented across estimates, impairment, controls, and going concern?
- Are restructuring charges and provisions assessed for recognition criteria, assumptions, and bias, including big-bath overstatement and subsequent release?
- Is impairment triggered by the restructuring tested, with assumptions consistent with the restructuring's effect on the business?
- Is the effect on the control environment evaluated, including departures, reorganized lines, reduced segregation, and redesigned controls?
- Is going concern reassessed, with management's projections challenged and disclosure adequacy confirmed?
- Are fraud and override opportunities around timing and measurement addressed, including entries associated with the restructuring?
- Are scope and procedures adjusted for the new structure, with walkthroughs, risk, and multi-location strategy updated?
- Could the auditor demonstrate, from the documentation, how the restructuring changed the audit approach rather than merely adding a restructuring workpaper?
