# System Conversion Impact

A system conversion is a high-risk event for financial reporting. When an entity changes its financial system, data must be migrated, configurations must be rebuilt, controls must be re-established, and the cut-over from old to new must be clean. Each of these is a point where completeness, accuracy, and continuity can break. Agents often treat a go-live as an IT project that IT will handle, performing only a cursory check that the new system produces reports. That under-response misses migration errors, configuration mistakes, and the period of control disruption around go-live, any of which can create material misstatement. A system conversion must be assessed as a change event that affects data integrity, controls, and procedures throughout the period.

Use this skill when the entity has implemented, converted, or significantly upgraded a financial system during the period, and when adjusting the audit for the effects. The goal is to recognize the conversion as a data and control risk event and to design procedures that cover migration, configuration, and cut-over.

## Core Rules

### Recognize The Conversion As A Data And Control Risk Event

A system conversion is not an IT milestone; it is a financial reporting risk event. Treat it as a change that affects data integrity, controls, and procedures.

Recognize the effects:

- data migrated from the old system may be incomplete, inaccurate, or mis-mapped;
- configurations in the new system may not replicate the old, changing recognition or calculation;
- controls built into the old system may not exist or operate in the new;
- the cut-over period is a time of heightened error and control disruption;
- IT general controls around the new system may be immature;
- reporting and reconciliations may change, affecting controls that depend on them.

Document the conversion's effects across the audit. A conversion treated as an IT matter leaves its financial reporting risks unaddressed.

### Assess Data Migration Completeness And Accuracy

Data migration is the highest-risk part of a conversion. The auditor must confirm that what moved into the new system completely and accurately reflects the old.

Assess migration by:

- reconciling opening balances in the new system to closing balances in the old;
- reconciling record counts and monetary totals for master and transaction data;
- testing the mapping of fields, accounts, and codes between systems;
- identifying items that were excluded, consolidated, or transformed during migration;
- testing that historical detail needed for ongoing processing, such as unamortized balances, was preserved;
- assessing how migration errors were identified and corrected.

Migration errors can persist undetected for periods, affecting balances and calculations that depend on the migrated data. Reconcile and test, do not assume.

### Evaluate Configuration And Calculation Replication

The new system's configurations determine how transactions are recognized, calculated, and classified. Confirm that critical configurations replicate the intended accounting.

Evaluate configuration by:

- identifying critical rules, such as revenue recognition, depreciation, inventory costing, and allocations;
- testing that the configured rule produces the correct result on sample transactions;
- comparing calculations between old and new systems for overlapping periods, such as a parallel run;
- assessing whether the new configuration changed any accounting policy or estimate;
- confirming that thresholds, limits, and approval rules are configured correctly.

A configuration that differs from the old system, intentionally or not, can change recognition and measurement. Test the rules, not just the reports.

### Test Controls During And After Go-Live

Controls do not automatically transfer to a new system. Assess which controls existed in the old system, whether they exist in the new, and whether they operate.

Test controls by:

- mapping old controls to new controls and identifying gaps;
- assessing whether automated controls were rebuilt and configured correctly;
- assessing whether manual controls were updated for the new process and system;
- testing controls both in the period before go-live (old system) and after (new system);
- recognizing that the cut-over period may have reduced or disrupted controls.

A conversion can leave a period with weakened controls. Where controls are disrupted, shift toward substantive procedures for the affected period.

### Assess Parallel Run And Cut-Over Risk

The transition from old to new, whether through a parallel run or a hard cut-over, is a period of elevated risk. Assess how the transition was managed.

Assess cut-over by:

- determining whether a parallel run was performed and whether differences were reconciled;
- identifying the exact cut-over date and how transactions straddling it were handled;
- testing that no transactions were lost, duplicated, or mis-dated at the boundary;
- assessing whether period-end balances were correctly established in the new system;
- evaluating whether reconciliation controls covered the transition.

A hard cut-over with no parallel run and no reconciliation is a high-risk transition. Design procedures specifically around the cut-over date.

### Evaluate IT General Controls Maturity In The New Environment

A new system often has immature IT general controls, especially around access, change management, and operations. Assess ITGCs in the new environment.

Assess ITGCs by:

- reviewing user access provisioning in the new system and whether it follows least-privilege;
- assessing change management for the new system, including emergency changes around go-live;
- evaluating operations controls, such as batch processing, backups, and incident management;
- considering whether the new system relies on a service organization and obtaining its report;
- recognizing that ITGC weaknesses undermine reliance on automated controls.

Reliance on automated controls in the new system requires reliance on its ITGCs. Where ITGCs are immature, treat automated controls as unreliable until proven otherwise.

### Adjust Procedures For The Conversion Period

The conversion must change the procedures, not just the documentation. Expand testing around migration, configuration, and cut-over.

Adjust procedures by:

- expanding substantive testing in the period after go-live, where controls are immature;
- performing detailed reconciliation of pre- and post-conversion balances;
- testing calculations and configurations on sample transactions;
- increasing scrutiny of period-end balances established in the new system;
- extending procedures over any one-time adjustments or clean-up entries around go-live;
- considering whether the conversion affected going concern, such as through implementation costs or disruption.

If procedures are identical to a no-conversion audit, the conversion risk was not addressed. The conversion is a trigger for expanded, targeted procedures.

## Common Traps

### Treating The Conversion As An IT Project

A system conversion is a financial reporting risk event. Treating it as IT's responsibility leaves data integrity and control risks unaddressed.

### Assuming Migration Was Clean

Data migration is the highest-risk part. Reconcile opening balances, record counts, and field mappings; do not assume the migration was accurate.

### Not Testing Configuration Replication

Configurations determine recognition and calculation. A configuration that differs from the old system can change accounting. Test the rules on sample transactions.

### Assuming Controls Transferred Automatically

Controls do not move themselves. Map old controls to new, identify gaps, and recognize that the cut-over period may have weakened controls.

### Overlooking Cut-Over Risk At The Boundary

The transition date is where transactions are lost, duplicated, or mis-dated. Design procedures around the cut-over, especially for hard cut-overs with no parallel run.

### Relying On Automated Controls Without ITGC Support

A new system often has immature ITGCs. Reliance on its automated controls requires testing the ITGCs that protect them.

### Leaving Procedures Unchanged

If the procedures do not change for the conversion, the risk was not addressed. Expand substantive testing, reconciliations, and configuration testing around go-live.

## Self-Check

- Is the system conversion recognized as a data and control risk event, with effects documented across the audit?
- Is data migration completeness and accuracy assessed, including reconciliation of balances, record counts, field mappings, and treatment of excluded or transformed items?
- Is configuration replication evaluated, including testing of critical rules on sample transactions and comparison to old-system calculations?
- Are controls tested both before and after go-live, with gaps between old and new controls identified and the cut-over period recognized as disrupted?
- Is parallel run or cut-over risk assessed, including the exact cut-over date, straddling transactions, and period-end balance establishment?
- Are IT general controls in the new environment assessed, including access, change management, operations, and any service organization reliance?
- Are procedures adjusted for the conversion period, including expanded substantive testing, detailed reconciliations, and scrutiny of go-live adjustments?
- Could the auditor demonstrate, from the documentation, that data integrity and control continuity were verified rather than assumed?
