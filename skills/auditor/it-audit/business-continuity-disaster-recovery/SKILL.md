# Business Continuity And Disaster Recovery

Business continuity and disaster recovery (BC/DR) capabilities determine whether an entity can continue operations and recover its financial systems after a disruption, whether from a cyber incident, natural disaster, hardware failure, or human error. The recurring failure is treating BC/DR as an IT resilience matter outside the financial audit's scope, or accepting the existence of a plan without testing whether it would actually work. A disruption that impairs the entity's ability to prepare financial records, process transactions, or recover data has direct financial statement implications, including effects on going concern, the reliability of records, and the disclosure of contingencies. The auditor must assess whether BC/DR capabilities are adequate to support the entity's ability to prepare reliable financial records and continue operations, and must consider the financial statement implications of any gaps.

Use this skill when auditing business continuity and disaster recovery, when evaluating recovery objectives and testing plans, and when considering the financial statement implications of continuity gaps. The goal is an assessment that addresses whether BC/DR capabilities support financial reporting reliability and continuity of operations.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Financial Statement Implications Of Disruption

A disruption to financial systems can impair the entity's ability to prepare records, process transactions, and recover data. The financial statement implications include effects on the reliability of records, the completeness and accuracy of transactions, going concern, and the disclosure of contingencies for recovery costs and losses.

Understand that:

- a disruption can impair the completeness and accuracy of transaction records, requiring reconstruction or adjustment;
- prolonged disruption can affect the entity's ability to continue as a going concern;
- recovery costs, data loss, and business interruption may create contingent liabilities requiring disclosure;
- the reliability of records recovered from backup or alternative systems must be evaluated.

Understanding the financial dimensions prevents treating BC/DR as outside the audit's scope.

### Evaluate Recovery Time And Recovery Point Objectives

Recovery time objective (RTO) is the target time to restore a system after a disruption; recovery point objective (RPO) is the target maximum data loss, measured in time. These objectives must be appropriate for the system's role in financial reporting and operations.

Evaluate by:

- identifying the RTO and RPO for each significant financial system;
- assessing whether the objectives are appropriate, given the system's role and the entity's tolerance for downtime and data loss;
- comparing the objectives to the entity's actual recovery capabilities, not merely its stated targets;
- identifying systems whose objectives, if unmet, would materially affect financial reporting or operations.

Objectives that are aspirational, or that exceed actual capabilities, provide false assurance about recovery.

### Test The Disaster Recovery Plan

A disaster recovery plan that has not been tested is an intention, not a capability. The auditor must determine whether the plan has been tested, whether the tests were realistic, and whether the results support the stated objectives.

Test by:

- confirming that the disaster recovery plan is documented, current, and accessible to those who would execute it;
- identifying tests performed during the period, including their scope and realism;
- evaluating whether test results support the stated RTO and RPO;
- identifying gaps between tested capabilities and stated objectives;
- considering whether tests included realistic scenarios, such as loss of the primary site or key personnel.

An untested plan, or tests that do not reflect realistic scenarios, does not support confidence in recovery.

### Evaluate Backup And Data Recovery Capabilities

Backups are the foundation of data recovery. Their adequacy, frequency, integrity, and test restoration determine whether data can actually be recovered after a loss.

Evaluate by:

- confirming that backups of significant financial data are performed at a frequency consistent with the RPO;
- testing the integrity of backups, including their completeness and readability;
- confirming that test restorations have been performed and succeeded;
- assessing whether backups are stored securely, with copies offsite or in geographically separated locations;
- identifying single points of failure in the backup process.

Backups that are incomplete, corrupted, or never tested for restoration provide false assurance about recoverability.

### Assess The Risk Of Prolonged Downtime

Prolonged downtime can affect the entity's ability to continue operations and to prepare financial records. The auditor must assess the risk of prolonged downtime and its potential financial statement implications.

Assess by:

- considering the systems whose loss would most impair operations and financial reporting;
- evaluating the entity's ability to operate manually or through alternative systems during downtime;
- assessing the potential duration of downtime given the recovery capabilities;
- considering the effect of prolonged downtime on revenue, costs, going concern, and disclosures.

Prolonged downtime risk, where significant, may warrant going concern evaluation and disclosure consideration.

### Consider The Effect On Record Reliability

Where a disruption has occurred, or where recovery capabilities are weak, the reliability of records prepared from recovered or alternative systems must be evaluated. Recovered data may be incomplete, corrupted, or inconsistent with the original records.

Consider by:

- where a disruption has occurred, determining which systems, periods, or data were affected;
- assessing whether the recovered records are complete, accurate, and consistent with the original;
- where reliability is impaired, designing procedures to reconstruct or independently verify affected records;
- concluding whether the records provide a reliable basis for the audit.

Treating recovered records as reliable without evaluation undermines the foundation of the audit.

### Evaluate Disclosure And Contingency Implications

Where continuity gaps exist or a disruption has occurred, the auditor must evaluate disclosure and contingency implications. Recovery costs, business interruption losses, and data loss may create contingent liabilities requiring disclosure.

Evaluate by:

- identifying the potential exposures, including recovery costs, business interruption, and data loss;
- assessing whether exposures are probable and estimable, requiring accrual, or reasonably possible, requiring disclosure;
- considering whether the entity has appropriately evaluated and disclosed the exposures;
- determining whether the exposures, individually or aggregated, are material to the financial statements.

Failure to evaluate disclosure and contingency implications leaves material liabilities undisclosed.

## Common Traps

### Treating BC/DR As An IT Resilience Matter Outside The Audit

BC/DR has direct financial statement implications. Treating it as outside the audit's scope leaves risks unaddressed.

### Accepting The Plan Without Testing

An untested plan is an intention. Testing, including realistic scenarios, is required to support confidence.

### Treating Aspirational RTO And RPO As Actual Capabilities

Objectives must be compared to actual capabilities. Aspirational targets provide false assurance.

### Assuming Backups Are Reliable Without Test Restoration

Backups must be tested for integrity and restoration. Assuming reliability leaves recoverability unverified.

### Overlooking Prolonged Downtime And Going Concern

Prolonged downtime can affect going concern. Overlooking it leaves a significant risk unaddressed.

### Treating Recovered Records As Reliable Without Evaluation

Recovered records may be incomplete or corrupted. Their reliability must be evaluated.

### Missing Disclosure And Contingency Implications

Continuity gaps and disruptions create exposures. Missing these leaves material liabilities undisclosed.

## Self-Check

- Are the financial statement implications of disruption understood, including effects on record reliability, going concern, and contingencies?
- Are RTO and RPO for significant financial systems evaluated for appropriateness and compared to actual capabilities?
- Has the disaster recovery plan been tested, with realistic scenarios and results that support the stated objectives?
- Are backup and data recovery capabilities evaluated for frequency, integrity, secure storage, and successful test restoration?
- Is the risk of prolonged downtime assessed, with consideration of its effect on operations, financial reporting, and going concern?
- Where a disruption has occurred, is the reliability of recovered records evaluated, with additional procedures where reliability is impaired?
- Are disclosure and contingency implications evaluated, including recovery costs, business interruption, and data loss?
- Could an independent reviewer confirm that BC/DR capabilities are assessed for their support of financial reporting reliability and continuity?
- Is the application free of treating BC/DR as an IT matter, accepting the plan without testing, or treating aspirational objectives as actual capabilities?
- Are prolonged downtime, record reliability, and disclosure implications addressed rather than overlooked?
