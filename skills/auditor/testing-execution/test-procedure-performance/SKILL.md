# Test Procedure Performance

Test procedure performance is where the designed procedure meets the real population. A well-designed procedure can still produce weak or misleading evidence if it is performed carelessly: the wrong items selected, the procedure shortcut, the evidence incompletely captured, or a deviation encountered and glossed over. The discipline is to perform each procedure exactly as designed, to capture complete and contemporaneous evidence, to recognise when execution realities require a documented modification, and to preserve the integrity of the evidence as fieldwork pressures build.

## Core Rules

### Perform the procedure as designed, and document any deviation

The procedure was designed to address a specific assertion with a specific method. Perform it as designed: select the items per the selection method, apply the test steps in order, examine the evidence specified. If execution realities force a deviation (an item cannot be located, a system is unavailable, a sample item is not in the population), document the deviation, its cause, and how it was addressed — do not silently substitute a different item or a different procedure. Silent substitution breaks the link between the designed procedure and the evidence, and undermines the defensibility of the conclusion.

### Select items per the documented selection method

The selection method (random, systematic, targeted, haphazard) was chosen to produce a representative or appropriately targeted sample. Apply it faithfully:

- for random or systematic selection, use the documented method and seed;
- for targeted selection, apply the targeting criteria consistently;
- for haphazard selection, avoid any pattern that introduces bias (avoiding old items, difficult items, or items requiring translation).

Substituting "more convenient" items for selected items, or skipping difficult items, destroys the sample's properties and the conclusion it can support. If a selected item cannot be tested, document it and treat it per the methodology (often as a deviation), rather than replacing it silently.

### Capture complete and contemporaneous evidence

Capture the evidence at the time of performance, not reconstructed later:

- the specific items tested, with identifiers;
- the evidence examined, with source, date, and reference;
- the result for each item;
- exceptions, deviations, or unusual matters, with their evaluation;
- who performed the procedure and when.

Reconstructed evidence — written up days after the procedure from memory — is weaker and more error-prone than contemporaneous evidence. Build the workpaper as the procedure is performed, so the evidence is complete and dated accurately.

### Apply the procedure to each item with the same rigour

Each sampled item deserves the same depth of testing. Avoid the drift toward lighter testing as the sample progresses:

- examine the full evidence specified, not a summary;
- follow up on anomalies within each item;
- do not accept superficial evidence for items that are difficult or time-consuming.

The discipline of uniform rigour is what makes the sample representative. If some items are tested lightly and others deeply, the sample's properties are compromised.

### Recognise and document deviations and exceptions as they arise

A deviation (a control that did not operate, a sampled item that is in error) is evidence, not an inconvenience. When one arises:

- document it specifically — what was the deviation, what was its cause and effect;
- evaluate whether it is isolated or indicative of a broader issue;
- follow the methodology for evaluating deviations (extend the sample, assess the effect on reliance, consider qualitative implications).

Deviations encountered and glossed over, or "resolved" without documentation, distort the sample's results. Capture every deviation honestly; the pattern of deviations is often more informative than the clean items.

### Preserve evidence integrity under fieldwork pressure

Fieldwork pressure — deadlines, unavailable personnel, system outages, late-arriving information — is when evidence quality is most at risk. Resist the pressure to:

- shortcut procedures to meet a deadline;
- accept incomplete evidence because the full evidence is hard to obtain;
- defer documentation to "later," when memory has faded;
- conclude before the evidence is complete.

Where pressure makes the planned procedure infeasible in the time available, document the constraint and either extend the timeline or modify the procedure deliberately with documented rationale, rather than performing a degraded version of the planned procedure and presenting it as complete.

### Modify the procedure deliberately when execution requires it, with documented rationale

Sometimes execution realities legitimately require a modified procedure: a population differs from what was assumed, a system has changed, a risk has shifted based on what was found. Modify deliberately:

- state what changed and why;
- state the modified procedure and how it still addresses the assertion;
- consider whether the modification changes the risk assessment or the sufficiency of evidence;
- obtain review of significant modifications.

Deliberate, documented modification is part of competent fieldwork; silent degradation is not. The distinction is whether the modification is reasoned, reviewed, and recorded.

### Maintain a clear link from performance to conclusion

The workpaper should let a reviewer follow the path from the items selected, through the evidence examined, to the conclusion reached. Each step should be explicit:

- what was the assertion and the procedure?
- what items were selected and how?
- what evidence was examined, and what did it show?
- what deviations or exceptions arose, and how were they evaluated?
- what is the conclusion, and how does it follow from the evidence?

A workpaper where any link is missing or implicit is hard to review and hard to defend. Build the chain explicitly as the procedure is performed.

### Escalate issues that exceed the performer's authority or competence

Some findings during performance require escalation: a suspected fraud, a control deficiency that may be material, a judgement beyond the performer's experience. Escalate promptly to the appropriate level (senior, manager, partner), document the escalation and its resolution, and do not conclude on the matter without that input. Performing within competence and escalating beyond it is a mark of rigour, not weakness.

## Common Traps

- **Silently substituting items or procedures** when execution is difficult, breaking the link between design and evidence.
- **Selecting "convenient" items** instead of the items the selection method produced, destroying the sample's properties.
- **Reconstructing evidence from memory** days after performance, producing weaker and less accurate documentation.
- **Drifting toward lighter testing** as the sample progresses, compromising uniform rigour.
- **Glossing over deviations** to keep the sample "clean," distorting the results and missing broader issues.
- **Shortcutting under deadline pressure** and presenting a degraded procedure as complete.
- **Modifying procedures silently** rather than deliberately with documented rationale and review.
- **Implicit links between evidence and conclusion**, leaving the workpaper hard to review and defend.
- **Concluding on matters beyond competence** without escalation, exposing the conclusion to challenge.
- **Accepting incomplete evidence** because the full evidence is hard to obtain, rather than extending effort or modifying deliberately.

## Self-Check

- Did I perform the procedure exactly as designed, and document any deviation with its cause and resolution?
- Did I select items per the documented method, treating untestable selected items per the methodology rather than silently replacing them?
- Is the evidence captured contemporaneously and completely — items, evidence, results, exceptions, performer, date?
- Did I apply the same rigour to each item, avoiding drift toward lighter testing?
- Did I document and evaluate every deviation and exception, following the methodology rather than glossing them?
- Under fieldwork pressure, did I resist shortcutting and either extend the timeline or modify the procedure deliberately?
- Where execution required modification, did I document the change, rationale, effect on evidence sufficiency, and obtain review?
- Does the workpaper build an explicit chain from assertion and procedure through selection, evidence, deviations, to conclusion?
- Did I escalate matters beyond my competence or authority — suspected fraud, material deficiencies, difficult judgements — and document the resolution?
