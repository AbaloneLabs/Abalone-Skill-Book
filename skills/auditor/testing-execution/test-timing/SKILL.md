# Test Timing

Test timing is when, during the engagement and relative to the balance sheet date, each procedure is performed. Timing is not a scheduling convenience; it is a substantive dimension of evidence. A procedure performed at the wrong time can produce evidence about the wrong moment — an existence test performed months before year-end does not support a year-end existence assertion without roll-forward; a cut-off test performed only at year-end may miss the transactions that determine the boundary. The discipline is to align each procedure's timing with the assertion it tests, to perform interim procedures only with appropriate roll-forward, and to recognise where timing constraints require additional or alternative procedures.

## Core Rules

### Align procedure timing with the assertion

Different assertions are sensitive to timing in different ways:

- **Balance at period-end** (existence of receivables, inventory, cash): the procedure must produce evidence about the balance as of the balance sheet date, either by being performed at that date or by being rolled forward from interim.
- **Transactions over the period** (revenue, expenses): the procedure must cover the whole period, with samples spread across it.
- **Cutoff**: the procedure must examine transactions on both sides of the period boundary.
- **Disclosures and subsequent events**: the procedure must extend to the events and conditions through the date of the auditor's report.

State, for each procedure, the point in time or the period it must address, and confirm the timing provides evidence about that point or period. A procedure whose timing does not match its assertion provides evidence about the wrong thing.

### Perform interim procedures only with appropriate roll-forward

Interim procedures (performed before the balance sheet date) can improve efficiency, but for period-end balance assertions they require roll-forward to year-end:

- test the balance or transactions at an interim date;
- perform procedures to bridge the gap from interim to year-end (testing of subsequent transactions, analytical procedures, controls over the period between interim and year-end);
- evaluate whether the roll-forward procedures provide sufficient evidence about the period-end balance.

The roll-forward is part of the evidence; without it, the interim procedure does not support the year-end assertion. Document the roll-forward procedures and their sufficiency. The longer the gap between interim testing and year-end, the more extensive the roll-forward must be.

### Time cut-off procedures to span the boundary in both directions

Cut-off assertions require examining transactions on both sides of the period boundary:

- transactions recorded just before period-end, to confirm they belong in the current period (vouch to shipping, receipt, or delivery evidence);
- transactions recorded just after period-end, to confirm they belong in the prior period (examine subsequent entries for items that should have been accrued).

Testing only one direction is a common error that leaves half the cut-off assertion unsupported. Perform cut-off testing in both directions, and over a window wide enough to catch any timing manipulation.

### Extend subsequent events procedures to the report date

Subsequent events procedures must cover the period from the balance sheet date to the date of the auditor's report (or the date sufficient appropriate evidence is obtained):

- review events and transactions in the subsequent period that may indicate conditions requiring adjustment or disclosure;
- read board minutes, review significant subsequent transactions, and inquire of management about subsequent developments;
- update the procedures as the report date approaches, so the evidence covers the full subsequent period.

A subsequent events review performed early and not updated leaves a gap between the last review and the report date. Build subsequent events procedures into the close timeline so they extend to the report date.

### Spread transaction testing across the whole period

For assertions about transactions over the period (revenue occurrence, expense accuracy), spread the sample across the full period:

- include items from each quarter or month;
- include items from high-risk points (close, peak volume, system changes);
- include items from different operators, locations, or systems where relevant.

Concentrating the sample in one part of the period leaves the rest untested. The period-spanning discipline applies to transaction testing just as it does to control testing.

### Recognise where timing constraints require alternative procedures

Some procedures cannot be performed at the ideal time: physical inventory may be counted at a date other than year-end; confirmations may not return by the report date; key personnel may be unavailable. Where timing constraints prevent the ideal procedure:

- perform alternative or additional procedures to obtain the needed evidence (roll-forward inventory counts, alternative procedures for unreturned confirmations);
- evaluate whether the alternatives provide sufficient appropriate evidence;
- if they do not, consider a scope limitation or a qualified opinion.

Do not accept a timing-compromised procedure as if it were fully effective; either supplement it or recognise the limitation.

### Time control testing to support period-wide reliance

Control testing that supports reliance across the period must be timed to cover the period:

- test instances spread across the period, including period-end;
- for controls that changed during the period, test each version of the control in the period it operated;
- for controls that operate only at certain points (period-end close controls), test at those points.

Control testing concentrated at one point in the period does not support period-wide reliance; the timing must match the period the reliance is meant to cover.

### Align timing with risk and materiality

Higher-risk and more material assertions warrant timing closer to the period-end, where the evidence is most directly about the reported balance. Lower-risk assertions may tolerate interim testing with lighter roll-forward. Calibrate timing to risk: perform period-end procedures for the highest-risk, most material balances; use interim procedures with roll-forward where risk and materiality permit.

### Build timing into the engagement schedule, not as an afterthought

Timing decisions affect the engagement schedule: interim testing requires early access to data and personnel; year-end procedures require scheduling around the close; subsequent events require procedures through the report date. Build the timing into the schedule from the planning stage, so that the right procedures are performed at the right times and the team is not forced into timing compromises by a schedule that did not account for them.

## Common Traps

- **Performing existence procedures at interim without roll-forward**, leaving a gap to the year-end balance.
- **Testing cut-off in only one direction**, leaving half the assertion unsupported.
- **Concentrating transaction samples in one part of the period**, leaving the rest untested.
- **Performing subsequent events procedures early and not updating** to the report date.
- **Accepting timing-compromised procedures as fully effective** without supplementation or recognition of limitation.
- **Concentrating control testing at one point**, undermining period-wide reliance.
- **Using interim testing for high-risk, material balances** without sufficient roll-forward; **Treating timing as a scheduling matter** rather than a substantive dimension of evidence
- **Failing to build timing into the engagement schedule**, forcing compromises that weaken the evidence; **Not testing each version of a control** that changed during the period, missing the version that operated at the relevant time

## Self-Check

- For each procedure, did I state the point in time or the period it must address, and confirm the timing provides evidence about that point or period?
- Where I performed interim procedures for period-end balances, did I perform and document sufficient roll-forward to year-end?
- Does my cut-off testing span the period boundary in both directions, over a window wide enough to catch timing manipulation?
- Do my subsequent events procedures extend to the report date, updated as the date approaches?
- Are my transaction samples spread across the whole period, including high-risk points and different operators, locations, and systems?
- Where timing constraints prevented the ideal procedure, did I perform alternatives, evaluate their sufficiency, and recognise any limitation?
- Is my control testing timed to cover the period of reliance, including period-end and each version of a control that changed?
- Did I calibrate timing to risk and materiality — period-end for the highest-risk, most material balances?
- Did I build timing decisions into the engagement schedule from planning, so the right procedures are performed at the right times?
