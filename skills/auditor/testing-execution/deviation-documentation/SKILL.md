# Deviation Documentation

A deviation is an instance where a control did not operate as designed, or where a sampled item contains error. Deviations are not failures of the audit; they are evidence, often the most informative evidence in the sample. The discipline is to document each deviation completely and honestly, to evaluate whether it is isolated or indicative of a broader problem, and to follow through to the correct effect on reliance or on the substantive conclusion. The characteristic failure is to minimise or rationalise deviations — treating them as one-offs, explaining them away, or failing to extend the sample when the methodology requires it — so that a control or population that is actually weak appears clean.

## Core Rules

### Document each deviation completely and specifically

A deviation documented vaguely cannot be evaluated. For each, record:

- the specific item and what was expected vs what was found;
- the nature of the deviation (control not performed, performed by the wrong person, performed late, performed incompletely; transaction error of a specific type);
- the cause, if identifiable;
- the effect, including any financial statement impact;
- how the deviation was resolved or corrected.

A line that says "minor deviation, no impact" is not documentation; it is a conclusion without a basis. The detail is what lets the deviation be evaluated and what lets a reviewer assess whether the treatment was appropriate.

### Distinguish control deviations from errors in tests of details

The two contexts call for different evaluation:

- **Control deviation**: the control did not operate as designed for a sampled item. The question is whether the deviation rate, actual or projected, exceeds the tolerable rate, and what that means for control reliance.
- **Error in a test of details**: a sampled item is misstated. The question is the projected misstatement relative to tolerable misstatement, and the qualitative implications.

Apply the correct evaluation framework for the type of test. Confusing the two — projecting a control deviation as a monetary misstatement, or treating a detail error only as a control issue — produces the wrong conclusion.

### Evaluate whether the deviation is isolated or systemic

A single deviation may be an isolated lapse or the visible tip of a systemic problem. Evaluate:

- is there a pattern across the deviations found (same operator, same period, same system, same transaction type)?
- does the deviation reflect a design weakness or an execution failure?
- does the deviation indicate fraud, override, or management pressure?
- are there environmental factors (reorganisation, system change, staff turnover, period-end pressure) that could have produced a cluster?

Isolated deviations are treated per the methodology; clusters or systemic patterns warrant deeper investigation, a raised risk assessment, and a different response than the methodology's standard deviation handling.

### Follow the methodology for sample extension

Most methodologies require extending the sample when deviations are found in a test of controls, to determine whether the deviation rate is acceptable. Apply the extension:

- extend by the specified amount or to the specified level;
- test the additional items with the same rigour;
- if further deviations arise, follow the methodology's escalation (further extension, or conclude the control cannot be relied upon).

Do not avoid extension by rationalising the initial deviations as one-offs. Extension is the mechanism that distinguishes an isolated lapse from a control that cannot be relied upon; skipping it leaves the reliance conclusion unsupported.

### Assess the qualitative implications, not only the quantitative

Some deviations are qualitatively serious even if their projected magnitude is small:

- deviations indicating fraud or override;
- deviations by senior personnel or in areas of segregation sensitivity;
- deviations that defeat the specific risk the control was meant to address;
- deviations clustered at period-end or in high-risk processes.

Qualitative seriousness can require reducing or withdrawing reliance, or designing targeted substantive procedures, even where the quantitative deviation rate appears tolerable. Record the qualitative assessment explicitly.

### Determine the effect on control reliance

For tests of controls, the deviation evaluation drives the reliance conclusion:

- deviation rate at or below tolerable, with no qualitative concerns: reliance may be maintained (subject to extension results);
- deviation rate above tolerable, or qualitative concerns: reliance is reduced or withdrawn, and the substantive approach must be adjusted (more extensive, more persuasive, or different procedures).

State the reliance conclusion explicitly and link it to the deviation evaluation. A control with a high deviation rate that is nonetheless "relied upon" without explanation is an unsupported conclusion.

### Determine the effect on the substantive conclusion

For tests of details, errors drive the conclusion on whether the population is materially misstated:

- project the errors to the population per the methodology;
- consider the qualitative characteristics of the errors;
- compare projected misstatement (plus an allowance for sampling risk) to tolerable misstatement;
- if projected misstatement is material, request adjustment or extend procedures.

Do not treat a sample with errors as "clean" because the projected amount is below tolerable without considering the qualitative implications and the allowance for undetected error.

### Consider whether deviations indicate a control deficiency for reporting

Deviations may indicate a control deficiency that must be communicated to management and those charged with governance:

- a control that operates with a high deviation rate may be a significant deficiency or material weakness;
- systemic deviations may indicate a design weakness that warrants remediation;
- deviations indicating fraud or override are serious matters for communication.

Evaluate deviations not only for their effect on the current audit but for their effect on the entity's control environment and for the auditor's reporting obligations.

### Document the aggregate evaluation and the conclusion

Aggregate the deviation evaluation across the sample and across related procedures:

- total deviations found and their nature;
- projected deviation rate or misstatement;
- qualitative assessment;
- sample extension performed and its results;
- effect on reliance or on the substantive conclusion;
- any control deficiency identified and its severity.

The aggregate documentation is what supports the conclusion. A conclusion that rests on deviations without their aggregate evaluation is indefensible.

## Common Traps

- **Minimising or rationalising deviations** as one-offs without evaluation, so a weak control or population appears clean.
- **Vague deviation documentation** ("minor issue, no impact") that cannot be evaluated or reviewed.
- **Confusing control deviations with detail errors**, applying the wrong evaluation framework.
- **Failing to extend the sample** when the methodology requires it, leaving the reliance conclusion unsupported.
- **Ignoring qualitative implications** — fraud, override, clustering — that are serious regardless of projected magnitude.
- **Maintaining reliance on a control with a high deviation rate** without explanation or adjustment to the substantive approach.
- **Treating a details sample with errors as clean** because the projected amount is below tolerable, without considering qualitative implications or sampling allowance.
- **Missing the control-deficiency reporting implication** of systemic or serious deviations; **Failing to aggregate** the deviation evaluation across the sample and related procedures, so the conclusion lacks a basis

## Self-Check

- Is each deviation documented completely — item, expected vs found, nature, cause, effect, resolution — not just labelled "minor"?
- Did I apply the correct evaluation framework — control deviation vs detail error — to each?
- Did I evaluate whether each deviation is isolated or systemic, looking for patterns in operator, period, system, and transaction type?
- Did I follow the methodology for sample extension when deviations were found, and test additional items with the same rigour?
- Did I assess the qualitative implications — fraud, override, clustering, defeat of the specific risk — independently of the projected magnitude?
- For control deviations, did I state the effect on reliance explicitly and adjust the substantive approach where reliance is reduced or withdrawn?
- For detail errors, did I project misstatement, consider qualitative implications and sampling allowance, and compare to tolerable misstatement?
- Did I consider whether the deviations indicate a control deficiency that must be communicated, and assessed its severity?
- Did I aggregate the deviation evaluation across the sample and related procedures to support the conclusion?
