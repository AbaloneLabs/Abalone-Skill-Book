# Audit Quality Assurance And Engagement Quality Review

Audit quality is not guaranteed by hiring capable people. Even experienced teams produce work that contains judgment errors, incomplete documentation, missed risks, or conclusions that outrun the evidence. Quality assurance and engagement quality review exist to catch these failures before the report or opinion is issued, when correction is still inexpensive. A review that rubber-stamps the file, that checks formatting instead of substance, or that happens too late to change the conclusion provides the appearance of quality without the substance. The harm is that stakeholders rely on assurance that was never properly validated.

Use this skill when performing engagement quality review, supervising audit work, inspecting workpapers, evaluating whether an engagement meets professional standards, conducting internal quality reviews, or deciding whether an audit opinion or report can be released. The goal is to ensure quality review challenges substance, not only form, and that it occurs early enough to matter.

## Core Rules

### Separate Quality Review From Performance And Supervision

There are distinct layers of review, and confusing them weakens all of them. Supervision happens during the work and guides the team. Performance review checks that assigned work meets standards before it is considered complete. Engagement quality review is an independent challenge of significant judgments and conclusions before issuance.

Clarify roles:

- the performer does the work and documents it;
- the supervisor reviews for completeness and correctness during fieldwork;
- the engagement partner or director owns the final opinion and significant judgments;
- the engagement quality reviewer independently challenges significant judgments and the overall conclusion;
- internal quality inspection reviews completed engagements after the fact for systemic lessons.

Engagement quality review is not a second performance of the audit. It is a focused, independent challenge of the matters that carry the most risk of being wrong. If the reviewer simply re-performs routine checks, the significant judgments go unchallenged.

### Define When Engagement Quality Review Is Required

Not every engagement requires a formal engagement quality review, but high-risk ones do. Define the triggers in advance rather than deciding after the fact.

Typical triggers include:

- listed entity or public interest entity audits;
- engagements involving complex estimates, fair value, or significant judgment;
- audits with significant fraud risk or suspected fraud;
- first-year audits or audits with prior-year qualifications;
- engagements where management integrity is in question;
- audits involving going concern uncertainty;
- engagements with disputed accounting or scope limitations;
- any engagement where the firm's policy mandates review.

When in doubt about whether review is required, err toward review. Document the decision either way.

### Focus Review On Significant Judgments And Risk Areas

The reviewer's time is finite. Spending it on formatting, tickmark consistency, or routine procedures means the matters most likely to be wrong receive no challenge. Prioritize the areas where judgment, estimation, and conclusion carry the most risk.

Focus on:

- risk assessment and whether it drove the procedures;
- materiality and performance materiality judgments;
- significant risks and the response to each;
- key estimates, fair value, and management judgments;
- areas involving management override of controls;
- sufficiency and appropriateness of evidence for significant accounts;
- evaluation of misstatements, including uncorrected errors;
- going concern assessment;
- subsequent events evaluation;
- the form and basis of the opinion or conclusion;
- adequacy of disclosures and presentation;
- independence and ethics compliance for the engagement.

A review that confirms the easy items are correct but never questions the hard ones has failed its purpose.

### Challenge, Do Not Confirm

Effective quality review requires skepticism about the team's conclusions, not deference to them. The reviewer should ask whether the evidence supports the conclusion, whether alternative conclusions were considered, and whether the documentation would withstand external inspection or challenge.

Ask:

- what evidence supports this significant judgment?
- what would have to be true for this conclusion to be wrong?
- were contradictory indicators considered and resolved?
- is the conclusion consistent across related workpapers?
- does the documentation allow an independent reader to understand the basis?
- were consultations, if any, appropriately documented and followed?
- has management's representation been corroborated rather than merely accepted?

If the reviewer cannot reach the same conclusion from the file, the team must either strengthen the evidence or revise the conclusion. The reviewer does not take over the engagement but can withhold approval for issuance.

### Time The Review To Influence The Outcome

A quality review performed only after the report is drafted and management has been told the conclusion is too late to change anything meaningful without embarrassment or delay. The review must be timed so that significant judgments can still be challenged and additional procedures can still be performed.

Plan review at:

- planning stage, to challenge risk assessment and strategy;
- key interim points, for significant estimates or complex areas;
- before the opinion or conclusion is finalized;
- before sensitive communications to governance or regulators.

Late review tends to become a documentation cleanup exercise rather than a substantive challenge. Build review milestones into the engagement timeline.

### Evaluate Documentation Against Standards

Workpapers are the evidence of what was done and why. Quality review must assess whether the documentation meets the applicable standard, not merely whether it exists.

Check:

- does the documentation show what was tested, how, by whom, and when?
- are significant judgments and conclusions, and their basis, recorded?
- is there a clear link from risk to procedure to evidence to conclusion?
- are exceptions, contradictions, and unresolved matters documented?
- are consultations and their resolution recorded?
- is the documentation sufficient for an experienced auditor with no prior involvement to understand the work?
- are dates, signatures, and review evidence present?

Do not accept undocumented conclusions. If the team's reasoning lives only in their memory, the file is incomplete regardless of how sound the reasoning may be.

### Review Independence, Ethics, And Scope Compliance

Quality is not only about technical audit work. The engagement must also satisfy independence, ethics, and scope requirements, and these are often assumed rather than checked.

Confirm:

- independence declarations are complete and current for all team members;
- non-audit services provided do not create prohibited threats;
- fees and relationships do not create undue dependence;
- the engagement scope matches what was agreed or required;
- any scope limitations are documented and their effect on the conclusion assessed;
- required communications to governance and regulators were made;
- the engagement letter or charter terms were honored.

A technically excellent audit performed by a non-independent team or outside its authorized scope is still a failed engagement.

### Capture Lessons For Systemic Improvement

Individual engagement review catches errors on that file. Quality assurance at the firm or function level uses patterns across engagements to improve the system. Both matter.

Feed review findings into:

- training and methodology updates;
- common error trends shared across teams;
- partner and staff performance evaluation;
- methodology or tool improvements;
- risk assessment updates for future engagements;
- root cause analysis of recurring deficiencies.

A review that finds the same error on every engagement but never changes the process is not improving quality, only documenting its absence.

### Preserve The Reviewer's Independence And Authority

The engagement quality reviewer must have sufficient authority to withhold approval and sufficient independence to challenge the engagement team. A reviewer who reports to the engagement partner, who depends on the partner for evaluation, or who cannot stop issuance has no real power.

Ensure:

- the reviewer is not part of the engagement team;
- the reviewer has appropriate competence and authority;
- the reviewer's approval is required before issuance where mandated;
- disagreements between reviewer and team are escalated and resolved;
- the reviewer is not subject to undue influence from the engagement partner or client.

If the reviewer's role is ceremonial, quality review provides false assurance.

## Common Traps

### Reviewing Form Instead Of Substance

Checking tickmarks, formatting, and signatures is easy and visible but does not test whether the significant judgments are correct. Substance review is harder and matters more.

### Rubber-Stamping Under Deadline Pressure

When issuance is imminent, reviewers face pressure to approve quickly. Approval under pressure that skips challenge is worse than no review, because it lends false credibility to unexamined conclusions.

### Reviewing Too Late To Change Anything

If review happens only after the conclusion is communicated, it becomes a cleanup or justification exercise. Time the review so additional procedures remain possible.

### Reviewer Lacks Independence Or Authority

A reviewer who reports to the engagement partner or who cannot withhold approval cannot provide a genuine independent challenge. The role becomes ceremonial.

### Confirming Routine Work While Ignoring Hard Judgments

Spending review time on low-risk procedures leaves the estimates, overrides, and opinion-forming judgments unexamined. Allocate review effort to where being wrong is most costly.

### Treating Documentation Gaps As Cosmetic

Missing or thin documentation on a significant judgment is a substantive deficiency, not a formatting issue. The file must allow an independent reader to reach the conclusion; if it cannot, the work is incomplete.

### Assuming Independence Compliance Without Checking

Independence and ethics failures invalidate otherwise sound work. Verify declarations, non-audit services, and relationships rather than assuming the team handled them.

### No Feedback Loop

Finding the same deficiencies repeatedly without changing methodology, training, or staffing means the review process detects failure but never prevents it. Close the loop with systemic improvement.

## Self-Check

- Are supervision, performance review, engagement quality review, and post-issuance inspection distinguished, with each performing its intended function?
- Are the triggers for mandatory engagement quality review defined in advance, and is the decision to review or not documented?
- Does review focus on significant judgments, risk areas, estimates, overrides, and the opinion basis rather than routine procedures and formatting?
- Does the reviewer challenge conclusions with skepticism, asking what evidence supports them and what would make them wrong?
- Is the review timed so that significant judgments can still be challenged and additional procedures performed before issuance?
- Does the documentation allow an independent experienced auditor to understand the work, the judgments, and their basis?
- Have independence, ethics, non-audit services, scope compliance, and required communications been verified, not assumed?
- Does the reviewer have genuine independence, competence, and authority, including the ability to withhold approval?
- Are disagreements between reviewer and engagement team escalated and resolved rather than suppressed?
- Are review findings fed back into training, methodology, risk assessment, and systemic improvement so recurring deficiencies are addressed?
