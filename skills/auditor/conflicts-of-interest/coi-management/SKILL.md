# COI Management

Once a conflict of interest is disclosed, it must be managed. Management means applying safeguards that reduce the conflict's threat to objectivity to an acceptable level, or concluding that no safeguard is sufficient and the auditor must be reassigned or the engagement declined. The failure mode of conflict management is the nominal safeguard: a review by a colleague who rubber-stamps the work, a disclosure to a party who cannot act, or a written acknowledgment that changes nothing about how the engagement is conducted. Nominal safeguards allow the conflict to persist while creating the appearance of management. Real management requires safeguards proportionate to the threat, verified to be effective, and revisited as the engagement evolves. The central judgment is recognizing when management has reduced the conflict enough to continue, and when it has not, so that continuation would mean proceeding under impaired objectivity.

Use this skill when managing disclosed conflicts, selecting safeguards, assessing whether residual conflict is acceptable, or deciding between management and avoidance. The goal is to ensure conflicts are genuinely neutralized or, where they cannot be, that the work is reassigned or declined.

## Core Rules

### Match Safeguards To The Significance Of The Threat

Conflicts vary in severity, and safeguards must be proportionate. A minor familiarity threat may be managed with independent review; a significant financial interest or self-review threat may require removal from the engagement. Applying a one-size-fits-all safeguard, typically the lightest available, under-manages significant conflicts.

Match safeguards by:

- assessing the significance of the threat (how strongly could the interest influence judgment, and on what aspects?);
- selecting safeguards whose strength corresponds to the threat, not whose convenience corresponds to preference;
- escalating to stronger safeguards, reassignment, or withdrawal when weaker safeguards are insufficient;
- documenting the assessment that the selected safeguards reduce the threat to an acceptable level.

A safeguard that is weaker than the threat it addresses is a rationalization, not a control.

### Use Independent Review That Is Substantive

Independent review is a common safeguard for familiarity and self-review threats, but it is effective only if the reviewer is genuinely independent and the review is substantive. A reviewer who reports to the conflicted auditor, who shares the conflict, or who reviews for form rather than judgment provides no real protection.

Ensure independent review is substantive by:

- assigning a reviewer with no involvement in the conflicted work and no conflicting interest of their own;
- giving the reviewer authority to require changes, not merely to advise;
- directing the review at the significant judgments the conflict could affect, not at procedural completeness;
- documenting the reviewer's independent conclusion and any required changes;
- ensuring the reviewer has the seniority and expertise to challenge credibly.

Nominal review, performed to satisfy a requirement without genuine challenge, is the most common failed safeguard. Review must be real to count.

### Remove The Conflicted Auditor From Affected Work Where Needed

For significant conflicts, the most effective safeguard is removing the conflicted auditor from the aspects of the engagement the conflict could affect, or from the engagement entirely. Partial removal, where the auditor continues on unaffected areas, may suffice for some conflicts; full removal or reassignment is required where the conflict pervades the work.

Determine the scope of removal by:

- identifying which aspects of the engagement the conflict could influence;
- removing the auditor from decisions, procedures, or conclusions in those aspects;
- assessing whether the conflict is limited enough that continued involvement elsewhere is acceptable;
- reassigning the auditor entirely where the conflict is pervasive or where partial involvement creates an appearance problem;
- documenting the scope of removal and the rationale.

Continued involvement under a significant conflict, even with review, may not reduce the threat to an acceptable level. Removal is the cleaner safeguard where doubt exists.

### Restrict Information Flow Where Appropriate

Some conflicts arise from, or are worsened by, the auditor having access to information that creates a divided loyalty, such as confidential information from a related party or a competing interest. Restricting the flow of such information to the conflicted auditor can reduce the threat.

Apply information restrictions by:

- identifying information that creates or worsens the conflict;
- ensuring the conflicted auditor does not receive or use that information;
- using separate engagement teams, ethical walls, or information barriers where the conflict involves concurrent or related interests;
- documenting the restrictions and monitoring compliance;
- recognizing that information restrictions alone may be insufficient where the conflict also involves relationship or self-interest.

Information barriers are a tool, not a complete solution. They address information-based conflicts but not those rooted in relationship or financial interest.

### Assess Whether Residual Conflict Is Acceptable

After safeguards are applied, the auditor must assess whether the residual threat is acceptable. Acceptability depends on the significance of the remaining threat, the nature of the engagement, and the expectations of those relying on the work. Some residual conflict is manageable; some is not.

Assess acceptability by asking:

- does the residual threat still have a material ability to influence the significant judgments?
- would a reasonable, informed third party, knowing the conflict and the safeguards, conclude that objectivity is maintained?
- are the safeguards robust and verifiable, or nominal and dependent on the auditor's self-restraint?
- does the engagement's significance, public-interest nature, or sensitivity warrant a lower tolerance for residual conflict?

If the residual threat is not acceptable, management has failed and the response must escalate to reassignment or withdrawal.

### Recognize When Management Is Insufficient

Not every conflict can be managed. Some threats are so fundamental that no safeguard reduces them to an acceptable level: a direct financial interest in the outcome, a close family relationship with the decision-maker, a self-review of the auditor's own prior management decisions. In these cases, management is not the answer; avoidance is.

Recognize management insufficiency when:

- the conflict involves a direct, significant interest in the engagement's outcome;
- the conflict is pervasive, affecting most or all significant judgments;
- the available safeguards do not credibly remove the ability to influence conclusions;
- the appearance of impairment is so severe that confidence cannot be sustained;
- standards or regulations prohibit the relationship or service regardless of safeguards.

When management is insufficient, the only ethical responses are reassignment of the conflicted auditor or declining or withdrawing from the engagement. Continuing under an unmanageable conflict produces a conclusion that cannot be trusted.

### Monitor Safeguards Throughout The Engagement

Safeguards applied at engagement acceptance may degrade or become insufficient as the engagement evolves. A conflict that seemed manageable may grow more significant; a safeguard that was effective may be circumvented by familiarity or pressure. Monitoring ensures safeguards remain effective over the life of the engagement.

Monitor by:

- reviewing the conflict and safeguards at key milestones or periodically;
- reassessing when the engagement's scope, personnel, or circumstances change;
- checking that independent reviewers remain independent and substantive;
- being alert to signs that the conflict is influencing judgments, such as unusual sympathy for management's position;
- escalating to reassignment or withdrawal if safeguards prove insufficient.

A safeguard applied once and never revisited provides no assurance that it continued to work. Management is ongoing, not a one-time decision.

## Common Traps

### Nominal Safeguards

A review or disclosure that looks like management but does not reduce the threat is a rationalization. Safeguards must be substantive.

### One-Size-Fits-All Responses

Applying the lightest safeguard regardless of threat severity under-manages significant conflicts. Match safeguards to the threat.

### Independent Review Without Independence Or Authority

A reviewer who is not independent, or who can only advise, provides no protection. Review must be independent, substantive, and authoritative.

### Continuing When Removal Is Required

Persisting with a conflicted auditor under safeguards that do not suffice produces an untrustworthy conclusion. Remove or reassign when needed.

### Information Barriers Treated As Complete Solutions

Ethical walls address information conflicts but not relationship or financial-interest conflicts. Use alongside other safeguards where needed.

### Assuming Acceptability Without Assessment

Applying safeguards and assuming the residual conflict is acceptable, without explicit assessment, skips the key judgment. Assess explicitly.

### Set-And-Forget Safeguards

Safeguards applied at acceptance and never revisited may degrade. Monitor throughout the engagement.

## Self-Check

- Are safeguards matched in strength to the significance of each conflict threat, with escalation when weaker safeguards are insufficient?
- Where independent review is used, is the reviewer genuinely independent, authoritative, focused on significant judgments, and documented?
- Is the conflicted auditor removed from affected aspects, or from the engagement entirely, where the conflict's significance requires it?
- Are information restrictions applied where the conflict involves access to sensitive or competing information, with compliance monitored?
- Has the residual conflict been explicitly assessed for acceptability, from both a factual and a reasonable-third-party perspective?
- Is the point at which management is insufficient recognized, leading to reassignment or withdrawal rather than continuation under an unmanageable conflict?
- Are safeguards monitored and reassessed throughout the engagement, especially when scope, personnel, or circumstances change?
- Is the assessment that residual threat is acceptable documented, with the reasoning and the safeguards relied upon?
- For public-interest or sensitive engagements, is the tolerance for residual conflict appropriately lower?
- Could an independent reviewer confirm that the conflict was genuinely reduced to an acceptable level, rather than nominally managed?
