# Specialist And Co-Sourcing

Some audit areas require expertise no generalist team possesses: valuation of complex instruments, actuarial estimates, IT system testing, or knowledge of a foreign component. The decision to use a specialist or to co-source is a competence decision, and it must be made deliberately. Agents often treat specialist involvement as a procurement task, engaging a provider and accepting their output without evaluating competence, scope, or objectivity. The auditor cannot shed responsibility by delegating; the specialist's work becomes part of the audit evidence, and the auditor must understand, evaluate, and conclude on it. A specialist whose scope, assumptions, or objectivity the auditor never examined produces evidence the auditor cannot actually rely on.

Use this skill when deciding whether specialist or co-sourced support is needed, when engaging and scoping specialists or component auditors, and when evaluating and relying on their work. The goal is specialist involvement that genuinely fills a competence gap and produces evidence the auditor can stand behind.

## Core Rules

### Identify When Specialist Expertise Is Required

The first question is whether the engagement needs expertise the team lacks. This should be assessed against the significant risks and complex areas, not left to emerge during fieldwork.

Specialist expertise is typically required for:

- valuation of complex financial instruments, intangibles, or real estate;
- actuarial estimates for insurance, pensions, or warranties;
- complex tax positions or transfer pricing;
- IT environments requiring systems, security, or data expertise;
- engineering, environmental, or technical assessments;
- foreign components requiring local knowledge or language;
- forensic or fraud investigations where specialized skills are needed.

Document the areas where specialist expertise is required and how the team's own competence falls short. Proceeding without a needed specialist is a competence gap, not an efficiency.

### Evaluate The Specialist's Competence And Objectivity

Whether the specialist is the auditor's specialist or management's specialist, the auditor must evaluate competence and objectivity before relying on the work.

Evaluate competence by considering:

- the specialist's professional qualifications, certifications, and experience;
- reputation and standing in the field;
- experience with the specific type of asset, estimate, or issue;
- prior work for the entity or the auditor and its reliability.

Evaluate objectivity by considering:

- whether the specialist is independent of management and the entity;
- any relationships, financial interests, or undue influence;
- whether management selected the specialist and whether that affects independence;
- for management's specialists, whether management can influence the assumptions or outcome.

Document the evaluation. A specialist whose competence or objectivity is unexamined cannot be relied upon, and the auditor remains responsible for the conclusion.

### Distinguish The Auditor's Specialist From Management's Specialist

The source of the specialist changes the auditor's procedures. The two are not interchangeable.

For the auditor's specialist:

- the auditor engages and directs the specialist;
- the auditor evaluates competence and objectivity;
- the auditor considers the specialist's findings as audit evidence;
- the auditor may need to involve the specialist in evaluating management's process.

For management's specialist:

- management engaged the specialist, which raises objectivity questions;
- the auditor must understand management's process for selecting and directing the specialist;
- the auditor must evaluate the specialist's competence, objectivity, and work;
- the auditor should test the data and assumptions management gave the specialist;
- the auditor may need to engage their own specialist to challenge management's specialist.

Do not treat management's specialist's report as auditor evidence without these additional procedures.

### Define The Scope Of Specialist Work Clearly

Specialist work must be scoped to address the specific audit objective. Vague scope produces work that does not answer the audit question.

Define scope by specifying:

- the specific asset, estimate, or issue to be addressed;
- the assertion or audit objective the work supports;
- the valuation date or reference date;
- the assumptions, inputs, and methodology to be used or evaluated;
- the form of the specialist's conclusion and the required documentation;
- the deadline and the coordination with the audit timeline;
- access to information, data, and personnel the specialist needs.

Confirm the scope in writing. A specialist who works to their own scope may produce a thorough result that does not address the audit objective.

### Evaluate The Specialist's Assumptions And Methodology

The auditor cannot simply accept the specialist's number. The auditor must understand and evaluate the assumptions, inputs, and methodology.

Evaluate:

- whether the assumptions are reasonable and consistent with external evidence;
- whether the methodology is appropriate and commonly accepted in the field;
- whether the inputs are complete and accurate, including data provided by management;
- the sensitivity of the conclusion to changes in assumptions;
- whether the conclusion falls within a range and where management's point estimate sits;
- for management's specialist, whether assumptions appear biased toward a favorable outcome.

Where assumptions or methodology are questionable, challenge the specialist, engage the auditor's own specialist, or expand procedures. Do not accept a conclusion whose basis is not understood.

### Coordinate Component Auditors And Co-Sourced Providers

Component auditors and co-sourced providers perform work that feeds the group or lead auditor's conclusion. The lead auditor remains responsible and must direct and evaluate their work.

For component auditors and co-sourced providers:

- evaluate competence, independence, and regulatory standing;
- determine materiality and confirm it is appropriate for the group;
- communicate significant risks, required procedures, and reporting requirements;
- determine the involvement required, from review of working papers to attendance;
- review the work performed and resolve any differences;
- integrate the component findings into the group conclusion.

Reliance on a component auditor without evaluation and involvement transfers responsibility the lead auditor cannot shed.

### Document The Reliance And The Conclusion

The engagement record must show why the specialist was used, how competence and objectivity were evaluated, what scope was set, and how the conclusion was reached.

Document:

- the area requiring specialist expertise and the team's competence gap;
- the specialist engaged, their competence and objectivity evaluation;
- the scope, assumptions, and methodology, and the auditor's evaluation of each;
- the specialist's conclusion and the auditor's assessment of it;
- for management's specialist, the additional procedures performed;
- the effect on the audit conclusion and any challenges or differences resolved.

A specialist conclusion in the file without this documentation is evidence the auditor cannot defend.

## Common Traps

### Treating Specialist Engagement As Procurement

Engaging a provider and accepting the output without evaluating competence, scope, or objectivity leaves the auditor relying on evidence they never examined.

### Accepting Management's Specialist As Auditor Evidence

Management's specialist requires additional procedures: understanding management's selection process, testing data and assumptions, and possibly engaging the auditor's own specialist to challenge.

### Vague Specialist Scope

A specialist working to their own scope may produce a thorough result that does not address the audit objective. Scope must be defined to the audit question.

### Accepting The Number Without Understanding The Assumptions

The auditor must understand and evaluate the assumptions, inputs, and methodology, including sensitivity. Accepting a conclusion whose basis is not understood is not reliance.

### Relying On Component Auditors Without Evaluation

The lead auditor cannot shed responsibility by delegating. Component auditors must be evaluated, directed, and their work integrated into the group conclusion.

### Proceeding Without A Needed Specialist

Treating a competence gap as an efficiency, rather than engaging a specialist, produces work in a complex area performed without the required expertise.

### Leaving Specialist Reliance Undocumented

A specialist conclusion without documentation of competence, objectivity, scope, and evaluation is evidence the auditor cannot defend under review.

## Self-Check

- Are the areas requiring specialist expertise identified against the significant risks and complex areas, with the team's competence gap documented?
- Is the specialist's competence and objectivity evaluated before reliance, whether the specialist is the auditor's or management's?
- Are the auditor's specialist and management's specialist distinguished, with the additional procedures for management's specialist performed?
- Is the specialist's scope defined to address the specific audit objective, including assertions, valuation date, assumptions, and required conclusion?
- Are the specialist's assumptions, inputs, and methodology evaluated, including sensitivity and consistency with external evidence?
- Are component auditors and co-sourced providers evaluated, directed, and their work integrated into the group conclusion?
- Is the reliance documented, including the competence gap, evaluation, scope, conclusion, and any challenges or differences resolved?
- Could the auditor explain and defend the specialist's conclusion, rather than merely citing it, under review?
