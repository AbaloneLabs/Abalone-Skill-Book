# Team Composition And Competence

Audit quality is bounded by the competence of the people who perform and review the work. A perfectly designed plan fails if the team lacks the skills to execute it. Agents often staff engagements by availability rather than by fit, assigning complex areas to whoever is free, or they assume that senior involvement in planning translates to senior execution in the field. The result is work performed by people without the judgment the area requires, reviewed too late or too lightly to catch the gaps. Competence is not a generic attribute; it is specific to the risk, the industry, the IT environment, and the specialized areas involved. A team whose composition cannot be tied to the engagement's risks is a quality risk waiting to materialize.

Use this skill when staffing the engagement, when assigning team members to areas, when determining supervision and review depth, and when competence gaps emerge during fieldwork. The goal is a team whose skills visibly match the engagement's risks and specialized needs.

## Core Rules

### Match Team Skills To The Engagement's Specific Risks

Staffing should follow the risk profile. The significant risks, the industry, the IT environment, and the specialized areas each call for specific competence.

Match skills to:

- the industry and its accounting and regulatory features;
- the significant risks, which need experienced judgment;
- complex estimates and fair-value areas, which need valuation or specialist knowledge;
- the IT environment, which may need IT audit expertise;
- fraud risks, which need skeptical and experienced practitioners;
- group components in foreign or specialized operations, which need local or sector knowledge.

Document the engagement's competence requirements and how each is met by the team. A team staffed without reference to the specific risks is a generic team on a specific engagement.

### Ensure The Right Depth Of Experience On Significant Areas

Experience depth matters most where judgment matters most. Significant risks and complex areas should be staffed and supervised by experienced personnel, not left to junior staff with light oversight.

Ensure experience depth by:

- assigning experienced seniors or managers to significant-risk areas;
- having partners and managers involved in risk assessment and key judgments, not only at completion;
- ensuring that estimates, fair value, and fraud areas have senior review throughout, not only at the end;
- avoiding the pattern where juniors perform all fieldwork and seniors appear only to review.

The pattern of junior execution with late senior review is acceptable for routine areas but dangerous for significant risks where judgment must be exercised as the work is performed.

### Plan Supervision And Review To Match Staff Experience

Supervision and review depth should scale inversely with the experience of the performer. Less experienced staff require closer supervision and more detailed review.

Calibrate supervision and review by:

- matching the reviewer's experience to the complexity of the area, not only the performer's;
- increasing review detail for junior-performed work and significant-risk areas;
- ensuring hot or on-the-job review for complex areas, rather than only cold review at completion;
- confirming that the reviewer has the expertise to challenge, not just to check formatting;
- documenting review notes and their clearance, so review is evidence of supervision.

Review is not a formality. A reviewer without the expertise to challenge the substance provides supervision in name only.

### Identify And Fill Competence Gaps Deliberately

Few teams have every skill internally. The discipline is to identify gaps and fill them, rather than to proceed with insufficient competence and hope review catches the gaps.

Common gaps and how to fill them:

- valuation or actuarial expertise: engage an auditor's specialist;
- IT and systems expertise: involve IT auditors for complex environments;
- tax expertise: involve tax specialists for complex positions;
- language or local knowledge: use component auditors or local staff;
- industry-specific knowledge: provide training or bring in experienced practitioners.

Document identified gaps and how each was filled. Proceeding with a known gap unfilled is a quality risk that must be escalated.

### Address Competence Issues That Emerge During Fieldwork

Competence gaps sometimes surface only during fieldwork, when work product reveals that the performer lacks the needed judgment. These must be addressed, not papered over.

When competence issues emerge:

- assess whether the work performed is reliable or needs to be redone;
- reassign the area to more experienced personnel or add supervision;
- provide coaching or training if the gap is addressable within the engagement;
- escalate to engagement leadership if the gap affects a significant area;
- document the issue and the response, including any rework.

Ignoring competence issues to protect the timeline or the relationship produces work that cannot be relied on and that will fail under review.

### Maintain Independence And Ethics Alongside Competence

Competence is necessary but not sufficient. Team members must also be independent and ethically fit for the engagement.

Confirm for each team member:

- independence from the client, including financial and relationships;
- absence of conflicts of interest;
- compliance with non-audit services restrictions;
- appropriate licensing or professional qualifications where required;
- completion of required ethics and independence training.

A competent but non-independent team member is a disqualification, not an asset. Screen the team for ethics and independence at assignment and throughout.

### Document Team Composition And Competence For The Engagement Record

The engagement record should show who did what, with what competence, and under what supervision. This is both a quality and a defensibility requirement.

Document:

- the team roster with roles, experience, and assignments;
- the competence requirements and how each was met;
- specialist involvement and the basis for their use;
- supervision and review arrangements, including who reviewed what;
- any competence gaps identified and how they were filled;
- independence and ethics clearances.

A team composition that exists only in a scheduler cannot be defended. The engagement record must show that the team was fit for the engagement.

## Common Traps

### Staffing By Availability Rather Than Fit

Assigning areas to whoever is free, rather than to whoever has the competence for the risk, produces work that lacks the judgment the area requires.

### Junior Execution With Late Senior Review On Significant Risks

Significant risks need experienced judgment during execution, not only at review. Late review cannot recover judgment that was absent when the work was performed.

### Review Without Expertise To Challenge

A reviewer who checks formatting but cannot challenge the substance provides supervision in name only. The reviewer's expertise must match the area's complexity.

### Proceeding With Known Competence Gaps Unfilled

Identifying a gap and not filling it, whether for cost or timeline, is a quality risk that must be escalated, not absorbed.

### Ignoring Competence Issues That Surface In Fieldwork

When work product reveals a competence gap, the work may be unreliable. Reassign, rework, or escalate rather than paper over the issue.

### Forgetting Independence And Ethics Screening

A competent but non-independent team member is a disqualification. Screen for independence and ethics, not only competence.

### Leaving Team Composition Undocumented

A team that exists only in a scheduler cannot be defended under review or inspection. Document roles, competence, supervision, and clearances.

## Self-Check

- Is the team staffed to match the engagement's specific risks, industry, IT environment, and specialized areas, with the fit documented?
- Are significant risks and complex areas staffed and supervised by experienced personnel, with senior involvement throughout rather than only at completion?
- Is supervision and review depth scaled to the experience of the performer and the complexity of the area, with reviewers expert enough to challenge?
- Are competence gaps identified and filled, through specialists, IT auditors, training, or reassignment, rather than left unaddressed?
- Are competence issues that emerge during fieldwork addressed through reassignment, rework, or escalation, rather than ignored?
- Are independence, conflicts, and ethics screened for each team member at assignment and throughout?
- Is team composition, competence, supervision, specialist involvement, and ethics clearance documented in the engagement record?
- Could a reviewer confirm from the documentation that the team was collectively fit for the engagement's risks and specialized needs?
