# End-To-End Walkthrough

A walkthrough is the auditor's opportunity to see the process as it actually runs, not as it is documented. By tracing a single transaction from initiation through to the financial statements, the auditor confirms that the process operates as described, locates where controls sit, and spots the gaps, workarounds, and manual interventions that documentation hides. Agents often substitute a read of the procedure manual or a management description for an actual walkthrough, or they perform a walkthrough that follows only the happy path and never asks what happens when something goes wrong. Both produce an understanding of a process that does not match reality, and that misunderstanding propagates into every later decision about risk, controls, and procedures.

Use this skill before assessing risk and designing procedures for a process, when confirming the process operates as described, and when validating understanding of how transactions flow. The goal is a verified, evidence-backed understanding of the process as it actually runs, including its exceptions and workarounds.

## Core Rules

### Trace A Transaction From Initiation To The Financial Statements

A walkthrough is not a description; it is a trace. Select an actual transaction and follow it from its origin through every step to its appearance in the financial statements.

Follow the transaction through:

- initiation, such as a customer order, a purchase request, or a payroll action;
- authorization and approval steps;
- system entry and processing;
- matching, reconciliation, or exception handling;
- posting to the sub-ledger and general ledger;
- classification and presentation in the financial statements;
- disclosure where relevant.

At each step, obtain the actual document, system screen, or record, not a description of it. A walkthrough that relies on narrative without evidence is an interview, not a walkthrough.

### Confirm The Process Operates As Described

The walkthrough's purpose is to confirm or contradict the documented understanding. Discrepancies between the documented process and the actual process are the most valuable findings.

Confirm by comparing:

- the documented procedure against what staff actually do;
- the systems described against the systems actually used;
- the approval thresholds stated against those actually enforced;
- the timing described against when steps actually occur;
- the segregation described against who actually performs each step.

Where the actual process differs from the documented one, update the understanding and assess why the difference exists. A documented process that no one follows is a control that does not operate.

### Identify Where Controls Sit In The Flow

A walkthrough locates controls in their actual position in the process. This is essential for later control assessment, because a control's position determines what it can prevent or detect.

For each control, note:

- where in the process it occurs, before or after the risk it addresses;
- who performs it and whether they are independent;
- what information it uses and where that information comes from;
- what it does when it finds an exception;
- whether it is preventive or detective, manual or automated;
- whether it leaves evidence of its operation.

Map controls to the flow so that coverage gaps, where a risk has no control, are visible. A process with risks that no control addresses is a process with an exposure.

### Probe The Exception Paths, Not Only The Happy Path

The happy path is what staff show you. The exception paths are where errors, fraud, and control failures actually occur. A walkthrough that follows only routine transactions misses the real risk.

Probe exceptions by asking:

- what happens when an approval is missing or late;
- what happens when a system rejects or flags an item;
- what happens when a match fails or a reconciliation breaks;
- how manual workarounds are invoked and who authorizes them;
- how errors are corrected and who can post corrections;
- what happens to voided, reversed, or adjusted items;
- how non-routine or unusual transactions are handled.

Document the exception paths and the controls, if any, that govern them. A process whose exception handling is uncontrolled is a process where manipulation can hide.

### Walk Through More Than One Transaction Type Where They Differ

A single transaction may not represent the whole process. Where transaction types differ materially, walk through representative examples of each.

Walk through separately where:

- different product or service lines have different flows;
- different locations or entities process differently;
- high-value, manual, or unusual transactions follow a different path;
- system-generated and manually entered items are handled differently;
- domestic and foreign transactions differ in controls or cutoff.

Select transactions that exercise the variation in the process, not only the most common type. A walkthrough of only the routine path leaves variant paths unexamined.

### Use Inquiry Corroborated By Evidence

Walkthroughs rely on inquiry, but inquiry alone is not sufficient. Each significant step should be corroborated by inspection of the actual record or system.

Corroborate by:

- inspecting the initiating document or system record;
- observing the system processing or the screen used;
- inspecting the approval evidence and the approver's identity;
- inspecting the reconciliation or exception log;
- tracing the posting to the ledger and confirming the amount.

Inquiry that is not corroborated by evidence is a description, not a walkthrough. Document the evidence inspected at each step, not only the explanation given.

### Document The Walkthrough As A Verified Understanding

The walkthrough documentation is the foundation for risk assessment and procedure design. It must be specific, evidence-backed, and updated for differences found.

Document:

- the transaction traced, including identifier and date;
- each step with the evidence inspected;
- the controls noted, their position, performer, and type;
- the exception paths and how they are handled;
- discrepancies between the documented and actual process;
- the conclusion on whether the process operates as described.

A walkthrough documented as a generic narrative, with no transaction traced and no evidence cited, cannot support the risk assessment that follows.

## Common Traps

### Substituting The Procedure Manual For A Walkthrough

Reading the documented process confirms only what is written, not what happens. The walkthrough must trace an actual transaction with evidence.

### Following Only The Happy Path

Routine transactions rarely reveal risk. The exception paths, workarounds, and manual interventions are where errors and fraud concentrate.

### Relying On Inquiry Without Corroboration

Asking staff what they do produces a description. Inspecting the actual records and systems produces a walkthrough. Corroborate each significant step.

### Walking Through Only One Transaction Type

Where transaction types differ, a single walkthrough misses variant paths. Walk through representative examples of each materially different type.

### Noting Controls Without Locating Them In The Flow

A list of controls without their position in the process cannot show coverage gaps. Map each control to where it occurs and what risk it addresses.

### Ignoring Discrepancies Between Documented And Actual Process

When the actual process differs from the documented one, the difference is the finding. Update the understanding and assess why the gap exists.

### Documenting A Generic Narrative

A walkthrough with no traced transaction, no evidence, and no specifics cannot support the risk assessment. The documentation must be specific and verified.

## Self-Check

- Is an actual transaction traced from initiation through recording to the financial statements, with evidence inspected at each step?
- Is the process confirmed to operate as described, with discrepancies between documented and actual process identified and assessed?
- Are controls located in the flow, with position, performer, type, information source, and exception handling noted for each?
- Are exception paths and manual workarounds probed, not only the happy path, given that exceptions are where risk concentrates?
- Are materially different transaction types walked through separately, so variant paths are examined?
- Is inquiry corroborated by inspection of documents, systems, and records, rather than relying on description alone?
- Is the walkthrough documented as a verified understanding, with the traced transaction, evidence, controls, exceptions, and conclusion?
- Does the documented walkthrough provide a specific, evidence-backed foundation for the risk assessment and procedure design that follow?
