# Flowchart Documentation

A flowchart is how the auditor turns a verbal or observed understanding of a process into a structured, reviewable artifact. A good flowchart makes the process, its control points, and its exception paths visible at a glance; a bad one is a decorative diagram that omits the steps that matter. Agents frequently produce flowcharts that show only the happy path, omit system boundaries and handoffs, or copy last year's diagram without confirming it still reflects reality. Each failure turns the flowchart into a liability: it looks authoritative, so reviewers rely on it, but it misrepresents the process and hides the gaps where risk lives. A defensible flowchart is one that an informed reader could use to locate every control, every handoff, and every exception path in the process.

Use this skill when documenting a process visually, when mapping controls into a flow, and when updating or validating an existing flowchart. The goal is an accurate, complete, decision-useful representation of the as-is process.

## Core Rules

### Document The As-Is Process, Not The As-Designed Or As-Desired

A flowchart must represent what actually happens, including workarounds and informal steps, not what the manual says should happen. The gap between as-designed and as-is is where control failures hide.

Capture the as-is by:

- basing the flowchart on the walkthrough of actual transactions, not the procedure manual;
- including informal steps, workarounds, and manual interventions observed;
- noting where the actual flow diverges from the documented process;
- marking steps that are nominally performed but routinely skipped.

If the flowchart matches the manual but not the walkthrough, it documents a fiction. Reconcile the two and explain the differences.

### Show The Full Path From Initiation To Recording

A flowchart that begins mid-process or ends before the ledger hides the boundaries where risk enters and where it is recorded. Show the complete path.

Include:

- initiation of the transaction or event;
- all authorization, approval, and processing steps;
- system entries, interfaces, and handoffs;
- reconciliation, matching, and exception handling;
- posting to the sub-ledger and general ledger;
- classification and presentation in the financial statements.

A flowchart that stops at "posted to system" without reaching the ledger leaves the recording and classification steps uncontrolled in the diagram.

### Depict System And Manual Handoffs Explicitly

Many control failures occur at handoffs: between systems, between people, and between manual and automated steps. These boundaries must be visible.

Depict handoffs by:

- marking where a transaction moves from one system to another, including interfaces and data transfers;
- marking where a step moves from automated to manual or vice versa;
- identifying who performs each manual step and their role;
- noting where data is re-entered, exported, or manipulated outside the core system;
- flagging handoffs where information could be lost, altered, or delayed.

Handoffs are control risk points. A flowchart that glosses over them hides exactly where errors and manipulation occur.

### Map Control Points Into The Flow

Controls are not separate from the process; they sit within it. The flowchart should show where each control occurs and what it addresses.

For each control point, show:

- where in the flow the control occurs;
- whether it is preventive or detective, manual or automated;
- who performs it and their independence;
- what risk or assertion it addresses;
- what happens when the control flags an exception.

A flowchart with process steps but no control points cannot support control coverage analysis. The reader should be able to see, for each major risk, whether a control addresses it and where.

### Capture Exception And Error Paths, Not Only The Happy Path

The happy path is the easy part to draw. The exception paths, where items are rejected, held, corrected, or escalated, are where the process is stress-tested and where risk concentrates.

Capture exception paths by:

- showing what happens when an approval is missing or a match fails;
- showing how rejected items are corrected, reprocessed, or written off;
- showing who can override a control and how the override is logged;
- showing how voided, reversed, and adjusted items are handled;
- showing the path for non-routine or unusual transactions.

A flowchart with no exception branches implies a process that never fails, which no real process does. The exception branches are the most decision-useful part of the diagram.

### Use Consistent Symbols And Clear Labels

A flowchart is a communication tool. Inconsistent symbols, unclear labels, and cluttered layout reduce its usefulness and invite misreading.

Maintain consistency by:

- using standard symbols for process steps, decisions, documents, systems, and data stores;
- labeling each step with a clear, specific description, not a vague verb;
- numbering or referencing steps so they can be cited in risk and control documentation;
- keeping the layout readable, with logical flow direction and minimal crossing lines;
- including a legend if non-standard symbols are used.

A flowchart that only its author can interpret has failed its purpose. Clarity and consistency make it reviewable.

### Validate And Date The Flowchart Against The Walkthrough

A flowchart is a point-in-time representation. Processes change, and an undated, unvalidated flowchart becomes a source of error rather than understanding.

Validate by:

- confirming the flowchart against the current walkthrough, not last year's;
- dating the flowchart and recording who prepared and reviewed it;
- noting the period the flowchart represents and any known changes since;
- updating the flowchart when walkthroughs or fieldwork reveal changes;
- reconciling the flowchart to the documented understanding and explaining differences.

An undated flowchart copied forward is a liability. The validation and date confirm it represents the as-is process for the period under audit.

## Common Traps

### Documenting The As-Designed Instead Of The As-Is

A flowchart that matches the manual but not the walkthrough documents a fiction. Base the diagram on observed reality, including workarounds.

### Showing Only The Happy Path

A flowchart with no exception branches implies a process that never fails. The exception paths are where risk concentrates and where the diagram is most useful.

### Omitting System And Manual Handoffs

Handoffs between systems, people, and manual and automated steps are where control failures occur. A flowchart that glosses over them hides the risk points.

### Listing Process Steps Without Control Points

A flowchart must show where controls sit and what they address. Process steps alone cannot support control coverage analysis.

### Stopping Before The Ledger

A flowchart that ends at "posted to system" leaves recording and classification unrepresented. Show the full path to the financial statements.

### Using Inconsistent Or Unclear Symbols

A flowchart only its author can interpret has failed. Use consistent symbols, clear labels, and a readable layout.

### Copying Last Year's Flowchart Without Validation

An undated, unvalidated flowchart becomes a source of error. Confirm it against the current walkthrough and date it.

## Self-Check

- Does the flowchart represent the as-is process observed in the walkthrough, including workarounds, rather than the as-designed or as-desired?
- Does it show the full path from initiation through recording to the financial statements, not stopping mid-process?
- Are system and manual handoffs, including interfaces and re-entry points, depicted explicitly as control risk points?
- Are control points mapped into the flow, showing position, type, performer, and the risk each addresses?
- Are exception and error paths, including rejections, overrides, and adjustments, captured rather than omitted?
- Are symbols consistent, labels clear, and steps numbered or referenced so the diagram is reviewable and citable?
- Is the flowchart validated against the current walkthrough, dated, and reconciled to the documented understanding?
- Could an informed reader use the flowchart to locate every control, handoff, and exception path in the process?
