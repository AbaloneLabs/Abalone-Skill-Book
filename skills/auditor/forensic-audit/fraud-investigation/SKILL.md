# Fraud Investigation

Fraud investigations fail most often not because the fraud is too clever, but because the investigator tips off the subject, destroys or contaminates evidence, exceeds the engagement scope, or reaches a conclusion before the evidence supports it. Fraud is deliberately concealed, so normal audit procedures that look for error will usually miss it. Agents tend to underweight the legal and evidentiary consequences of every step: who is told, what is preserved, what is documented, and what is said in interviews. A botched investigation can destroy a recoverable loss, expose the entity to defamation or wrongful-termination claims, and make later civil or criminal action impossible.

Use this skill before and during any fraud investigation: when predication exists or is being assessed, when building a fraud hypothesis, when preserving evidence, when planning interviews, and when coordinating scope and reporting with legal counsel. The goal is to investigate lawfully, preserve admissible evidence, avoid tipping off the subject, and reach conclusions that are defensible.

## Core Rules

### Establish Predication Before Acting

Predication is the factual basis that makes an investigation reasonable and lawful. Acting without it risks harassment, privacy violations, and claims that the investigation was pretextual. Predication should be documented before surveillance, data collection, interviews, or accusations begin.

Document the predication:

- the specific allegation or anomaly;
- source of the concern (whistleblower, analytics, tip, audit finding, vendor complaint);
- date received and who received it;
- documents or data already supporting concern;
- the apparent scheme and parties involved;
- why the concern is credible;
- the legal or policy basis to investigate;
- who authorized the investigation.

Reassess predication as evidence develops. If predication weakens, narrow or stop the work rather than fishing.

### Define And Respect The Engagement Scope

Scope creep is dangerous in fraud work. An investigator authorized to examine expense fraud cannot, without authorization, expand into payroll, executive accounts, or personal devices. Define what is in scope, what is out of scope, and the escalation path if scope expansion is warranted.

Confirm in writing:

- objectives of the investigation;
- entities, locations, systems, accounts, and periods in scope;
- who the client or sponsor is;
- reporting line and confidentiality boundaries;
- whether the work is privileged and under what doctrine;
- what happens if criminal conduct or regulatory issues are found;
- authority to access systems, email, and devices;
- budget and timeline;
- the form and recipients of the final report.

When new issues surface, document them and seek authorization before expanding.

### Preserve Evidence And Chain Of Custody Immediately

Evidence is fragile. Emails can be deleted, files overwritten, logs rotated, and devices wiped, sometimes automatically. The first priority once an investigation is authorized is preservation, not analysis. A break in chain of custody can render evidence inadmissible and undermine the entire case.

Preservation steps:

- issue a litigation or preservation hold to relevant custodians;
- suspend routine deletion, backup overwrite, and log rotation;
- image devices and capture systems before forensic review;
- record who collected what, when, from where, and how;
- store originals read-only and work from copies;
- log every access, transfer, and handling of evidence;
- use tamper-evident storage where physical evidence exists;
- photograph or video physical scenes before moving anything;
- preserve metadata, not just file contents.

Never investigate on the original media. Never let the subject handle or "find" their own evidence.

### Build A Fraud Hypothesis Using The Fraud Triangle

A structured hypothesis prevents drift. The fraud triangle frames why people commit fraud: pressure (motive), opportunity (weak control or access), and rationalization (justification). Map evidence to each leg so the theory is testable rather than assumed.

For each suspected scheme, articulate:

- the pressure: financial difficulty, targets, addiction, lifestyle, coercion;
- the opportunity: override authority, missing segregation, system access, weak approvals;
- the rationalization: perceived unfairness, entitlement, "borrowing," retaliation;
- the scheme: how funds or value move out and how it is concealed;
- the parties: who acts, who benefits, who is complicit;
- the concealment: falsified documents, entries, reconciliations, or approvals;
- the conversion: where the proceeds go;
- the time window: when it began and whether it continues.

Test the hypothesis against evidence and revise it; do not fit the evidence to a fixed theory.

### Coordinate With Legal Counsel Early And Continuously

Fraud intersects employment law, privacy law, criminal law, regulatory disclosure, and privilege. Counsel should shape the investigation structure, the privilege posture, interview strategy, and reporting. Investigators who proceed without counsel risk waiving privilege, violating employee rights, or creating statements harmful to the entity.

Coordinate with counsel on:

- whether the investigation is conducted at counsel's direction to support privilege;
- preservation hold language and recipients;
- lawful access to email, devices, and systems;
- interview warnings and the right to representation;
- jurisdictional privacy and labor law constraints;
- when and how to involve HR, internal audit, or law enforcement;
- mandatory disclosure obligations (regulators, auditors, insurers);
- whistleblowers and anti-retaliation protections;
- the wording of conclusions to avoid defamation;
- the audience and distribution of any report.

Document legal direction so privilege and scope decisions are defensible.

### Conduct Interviews Strategically And Last

Interviews are powerful but risky. Once a subject or witness is interviewed, the investigation is effectively disclosed to them, and their statements can shape, contaminate, or derail the evidence. Interview after documents and data have been gathered and analyzed, so questions are precise and contradictions can be tested.

Sequence and plan interviews:

- interview peripheral witnesses and document custodians first;
- move toward more involved parties;
- interview the subject last, when evidence is strongest;
- prepare an interview outline tied to evidence, not a script;
- confirm authority to interview and any required representation;
- use two interviewers and contemporaneous notes;
- avoid accusations in early interviews;
- test inconsistencies against documents already obtained;
- preserve statements accurately and have them reviewed;
- never promise confidentiality you cannot guarantee.

Do not confront a subject before evidence is preserved; do not let interview notes contradict the underlying documents.

### Maintain Confidentiality And Need-To-Know

Fraud investigations depend on secrecy until disclosure is intentional and authorized. Leaks warn the subject, trigger evidence destruction, and damage reputations and careers. Confidentiality must be enforced on people, data, and communications.

Control:

- the list of people who know the investigation exists;
- access to the investigation workspace, files, and email thread;
- printing, forwarding, and screenshot controls;
- code names or initials for subjects in working files;
- secure storage of physical and electronic evidence;
- separation from normal audit or HR files;
- communications only through approved channels;
- restrictions on discussing the matter in open offices or chats.

Assume the subject will notice any change in access, calendar holds, or data pulls; coordinate timing to avoid premature disclosure.

### Quantify Loss And Distinguish Findings From Suspicion

A defensible investigation separates what is proven, what is likely, and what is suspected. Overstating loss or guilt creates legal and reputational risk; understating it understates recoveries and control failures. Quantify conservatively and show the basis.

For each finding, state:

- the assertion proven by documents versus inferred;
- the amount supported by direct evidence;
- the estimated range where amounts are uncertain;
- the period affected;
- the parties responsible, by role and evidence;
- the control failures that enabled the scheme;
- the recovery potential and sources;
- the limitations of the work performed.

Avoid characterizing conduct as criminal unless counsel and the evidence clearly support it.

## Common Traps

### Confronting The Subject Too Early

A premature confrontation gives the subject time to destroy evidence, coordinate stories, or resign before the loss is quantified. Build the documentary case first; confront only when evidence is preserved and counsel approves.

### Investigating On Live Systems And Original Media

Working on the original device or database overwrites timestamps, logs, and deleted-file remnants, and gives the subject a defense that evidence was altered. Always image and work from copies under chain of custody.

### Skipping The Preservation Hold

If routine deletion continues, the very emails or logs that prove the fraud may disappear, and the entity may face sanctions for spoliation. Issue the hold before, not after, collecting evidence.

### Treating An Anomaly As Proven Fraud

A red flag, a duplicate payment, or an unusual journal entry is a lead, not a conclusion. Investigate to rule out error, system issue, or legitimate business reason before alleging fraud.

### Expanding Scope Without Authorization

Discovering unrelated misconduct and investigating it anyway can exceed authority, breach privacy, and taint the original engagement. Document the new issue and seek authorization through counsel.

### Waiving Privilege Through Loose Communication

Privilege can be lost by copying too many people, circulating drafts broadly, or mixing investigative work with ordinary business advice. Route communications through counsel and limit distribution to need-to-know.

### Relying On Confessions Or Witness Statements Alone

Witnesses lie, misremember, and have motives; confessions can be coerced or retracted. Corroborate statements with documents, data, and system evidence before relying on them.

### Publishing Accusatory Findings Too Widely

A report distributed beyond the authorized audience can trigger defamation claims, prejudice ongoing proceedings, and destroy recoveries. Control distribution and let counsel approve final wording.

## Self-Check

- Is predication documented and authorized before collection, interviews, or accusations began?
- Is the engagement scope defined in writing, including systems, periods, parties, and the escalation path for expansion?
- Has a preservation hold been issued and have originals been imaged and stored read-only under chain of custody?
- Does each fraud hypothesis map evidence to pressure, opportunity, rationalization, scheme, concealment, and conversion?
- Is legal counsel directing the investigation structure, privilege posture, interview plan, and reporting?
- Are interviews sequenced from peripheral witnesses to the subject, conducted after documents are gathered, with two interviewers and accurate notes?
- Is confidentiality enforced through need-to-know access, code names, and controlled communications?
- Does each finding distinguish proven, likely, and suspected conduct with supporting evidence and stated limitations?
- Are loss amounts quantified conservatively with a documented basis and a stated recovery potential?
- Are conclusions worded to avoid defamation and distributed only to the authorized audience?
