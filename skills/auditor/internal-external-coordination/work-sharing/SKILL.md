# Work Sharing

Work-sharing between internal and external audit goes beyond informal coordination: it is a formal arrangement in which one party's working papers, procedures, or results are made available for the other to use. Done well, it produces an audit that is deeper and more efficient than either party could achieve alone. Done carelessly, it creates a false sense of coverage, because the receiving party treats shared work as equivalent to its own without confirming that it meets the receiving party's evidentiary and documentation standards. Agents who treat work-sharing as a simple file transfer miss that shared working papers carry confidentiality obligations, ownership limits, and evidentiary conditions that must be satisfied before the work can support an opinion. The harm is concentrated when a misstatement traces back to shared work that was never evaluated: the external auditor has relied on evidence it never validated, and the arrangement provides no defense.

Use this skill when establishing work-sharing arrangements between internal and external auditors, when determining how shared working papers can be used, or when setting the conditions under which shared work can be relied upon. The goal is a work-sharing arrangement that is formal, documented, confidentiality-protected, and evidentiarily sound, so that shared work genuinely supports the receiving party's conclusions rather than merely appearing to.

## Core Rules

### Establish The Arrangement Formally Before Any Sharing

Work-sharing must rest on a formal understanding that defines what is shared, why, and under what conditions. Informal sharing, where one party simply hands over files, leaves confidentiality, ownership, and evidentiary expectations undefined and creates disputes when problems arise. The arrangement should be documented and agreed before fieldwork, not constructed retroactively.

Formal arrangement elements:

- the scope of work to be shared, including engagements, controls, and procedures;
- the purpose of sharing, whether for reliance, reference, or corroboration;
- the standards under which the shared work was performed and documented;
- the ownership of the working papers and the limits on the receiving party's use;
- the confidentiality and non-disclosure obligations of both parties;
- the access protocols, including who may view the working papers and how;
- the process for resolving questions about the shared work;
- the duration of the arrangement and post-engagement access rights.

### Confirm Shared Work Meets The Receiving Party's Evidentiary Standard

Shared working papers are only useful if they meet the evidentiary standard of the party that intends to use them. Internal audit's documentation, prepared under its standards, may not satisfy the external auditor's documentation requirements, and vice versa. The receiving party must evaluate the work against its own standard before relying on it.

Evidentiary standard confirmation:

- the procedures performed are appropriate to the receiving party's relevant assertions and risks;
- the evidence gathered is sufficient and appropriate, not merely voluminous;
- the conclusions are supported by the documented evidence and are not overstated;
- the documentation is complete, cross-referenced, and reviewable by the receiving party;
- exceptions and errors were evaluated and followed up, not left open;
- the work covers the complete population or a representative sample for the receiving party's needs;
- significant judgments in the shared work are explained and supported.

### Evaluate The Performing Party's Objectivity And Competence

The reliability of shared work depends on who performed it. The receiving party must evaluate the objectivity and competence of the performing party for the specific work, because work performed by a non-object or unqualified party cannot be relied upon regardless of how thoroughly it is documented. This evaluation is a precondition, not a formality.

Objectivity and competence evaluation:

- the performing party's organizational position and reporting lines;
- the independence of the performing party from the area or outcome measured;
- the qualifications, certifications, and experience of the individuals who performed the work;
- the performing party's methodology, supervision, and quality program;
- any threats, self-interest, self-review, familiarity, and the safeguards applied;
- the performing party's history of reliable work and willingness to report unfavorable findings;
- for internal audit, functional reporting to governance and freedom from management veto.

### Set Confidentiality And Access Protocols That Protect Both Parties

Shared working papers contain sensitive information about the entity, its controls, and its weaknesses. Uncontrolled access exposes both parties to confidentiality breaches and to the risk that findings are misused or disclosed. Protocols must protect the information while permitting the legitimate use for which sharing was intended.

Confidentiality and access protocols:

- define who within each party may access the shared working papers;
- use secure channels and repositories with access logging where appropriate;
- prohibit onward sharing to third parties without explicit written consent;
- clarify how each party may reference the other's work in its own reporting;
- address the handling of particularly sensitive findings, such as fraud indicators;
- establish obligations upon termination of the arrangement, including return or destruction;
- reconcile the arrangement with each party's professional and legal confidentiality duties.

### Clarify Ownership And Permitted Use Of Shared Work

Working papers remain the property of the party that created them, and the receiving party's right to use them is limited. Confusion about ownership leads to disputes, especially when the receiving party wants to incorporate shared work into its own file or cite it in its report. Ownership and use must be explicit.

Ownership and use clarifications:

- the performing party retains ownership of its working papers;
- the receiving party may use the work for the agreed purpose, typically the current engagement;
- the receiving party may not incorporate the work into a permanent file as its own without permission;
- citation of the other party's work in reporting must follow the agreed conventions;
- the receiving party may not rely on the work for engagements outside the arrangement;
- modifications, annotations, or derivative work must be handled per the agreement;
- the basis for any reliance is documented in the receiving party's own file, not assumed.

### Conduct Joint Planning So Shared Work Is Fit For Purpose

Work-sharing is most effective when the parties plan jointly, so that the work performed by one is shaped to be useful to the other. Joint planning aligns scope, methodology, timing, and documentation expectations before the work is performed, rather than trying to retrofit shared work to the receiving party's needs afterward.

Joint planning elements:

- identify the risks, assertions, and controls where shared work can support the receiving party;
- agree on the methodology, sample sizes, and documentation format for that work;
- align timing so the work is performed and reviewed when the receiving party needs it;
- agree on the definition of exceptions and the threshold for escalation;
- designate points of contact and the cadence of coordination during fieldwork;
- anticipate the questions the receiving party will need answered in the working papers;
- document the joint plan so both parties work to the same expectations.

### Define The Conditions Under Which Shared Work Can Be Relied Upon

Reliance on shared work is a distinct decision from sharing itself. The receiving party must determine, based on evaluation, whether the shared work can be relied upon for its purposes, and to what extent. Reliance conditions must be defined and documented, and the receiving party must perform whatever corroboration is needed.

Reliance conditions:

- the performing party's objectivity and competence have been evaluated and are adequate;
- the shared work meets the receiving party's evidentiary and documentation standard;
- the scope of the work aligns with the receiving party's relevant assertions and risks;
- the receiving party has reviewed the working papers, not merely the summary or conclusions;
- the receiving party has performed reperformance or independent testing of a portion where appropriate;
- the conclusions are consistent with the receiving party's other evidence;
- the extent of reliance is calibrated to the risk, materiality, and judgment of the area.

### Preserve Each Party's Independence And Reporting Responsibility

Work-sharing must not blur the independence or reporting responsibility of either party. The external auditor remains solely responsible for its opinion, and internal audit remains responsible to governance. Sharing work does not create joint opinions or shared responsibility, and the arrangement must safeguard each party's independence.

Independence and responsibility safeguards:

- the external auditor forms its opinion independently, even when relying on shared work;
- internal audit's reporting line to governance is not compromised by the arrangement;
- neither party adopts the other's conclusions without its own evaluation;
- the arrangement does not create a self-review or advocacy threat for either party;
- significant findings are reported by each party through its own reporting lines;
- disputes over findings or reliance are resolved without compromising either party's position;
- the arrangement is disclosed to governance where appropriate, particularly reliance decisions.

### Document The Work-Sharing Arrangement And Reliance Decisions

The arrangement, the evaluations, and the reliance decisions must be documented to a standard that an experienced reviewer can follow. Documentation is what makes the arrangement defensible and the reliance traceable. Undocumented work-sharing is indistinguishable from informal borrowing when the file is reviewed.

Documentation requirements:

- the formal arrangement, including scope, ownership, confidentiality, and access;
- the evaluation of the performing party's objectivity and competence;
- the confirmation that shared work meets the receiving party's evidentiary standard;
- the joint plan and the alignment of scope, methodology, and timing;
- the specific work relied upon and the extent of reliance;
- the corroboration or reperformance performed by the receiving party;
- the basis for each reliance decision and the safeguards applied.

## Common Traps

### Informal File Transfer Without A Formal Arrangement

Handing over working papers without a documented arrangement leaves confidentiality, ownership, and evidentiary expectations undefined. Disputes and unsupported reliance follow when problems emerge.

### Assuming Shared Work Meets The Receiving Party's Standard

Work performed under one party's standards may not satisfy the other's evidentiary or documentation requirements. Assuming equivalence without evaluation produces reliance on work that cannot support the conclusion.

### Relying On Summaries Instead Of Working Papers

Accepting the performing party's summary or conclusions without reviewing the underlying working papers relies on assertions rather than evidence. The receiving party must examine the work, not the headline.

### Sharing Without Confidentiality Protocols

Uncontrolled access to working papers exposes sensitive findings about controls and weaknesses. Confidentiality and access protocols must protect the information and define permitted use.

### Blurring Ownership And Permitted Use

Treating shared working papers as the receiving party's own, or relying on them beyond the agreed engagement, creates ownership disputes and oversteps the arrangement. Ownership and use must be explicit.

### Joint Planning That Becomes Joint Opinion

Collaboration that merges the parties' conclusions compromises the independence of each. The external auditor's opinion and internal audit's reporting to governance remain separate responsibilities.

### Reliance Without Corroboration

Accepting shared work wholesale, without reperformance or independent testing of a portion, leaves the receiving party's conclusion resting on unevaluated evidence. Corroboration is usually required, especially for significant areas.

### Undocumented Arrangements And Reliance

Work-sharing and reliance decisions that are not documented cannot be defended. An experienced reviewer must be able to trace what was shared, what was evaluated, and what was relied upon.

## Self-Check

- Is the work-sharing arrangement formal and documented, covering scope, purpose, standards, ownership, confidentiality, and access, before any sharing occurs?
- Has the shared work been confirmed to meet the receiving party's evidentiary and documentation standard, not merely the performing party's?
- Has the performing party's objectivity and competence been evaluated for the specific work, with threats and safeguards documented?
- Are confidentiality and access protocols established, defining who may view the working papers and how sensitive findings are handled?
- Is ownership of the working papers and the permitted use by the receiving party explicitly clarified, including limits on incorporation and citation?
- Has joint planning aligned scope, methodology, timing, and documentation expectations so the shared work is fit for the receiving party's purpose?
- Are the conditions for reliance defined, including review of working papers, corroboration, and consistency with other evidence?
- Does the arrangement preserve each party's independence and reporting responsibility, with the external auditor forming its opinion alone?
- Is reliance on shared work supported by documented evaluation and appropriate reperformance or independent testing?
- Are the arrangement, evaluations, joint plan, and reliance decisions documented to a standard an experienced reviewer can follow?
- Are significant findings reported by each party through its own reporting lines, without compromise to either party's position?
