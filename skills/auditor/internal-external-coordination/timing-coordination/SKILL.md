# Timing Coordination

The timing of audit work is not a logistical afterthought; it is an evidentiary and dependency problem that determines whether coordinated coverage actually holds together. Internal audit and the external auditor operate on different clocks: internal audit runs continuously across the year, while the external auditor concentrates fieldwork around interim and year-end windows. When these clocks are not synchronized, the external auditor arrives to rely on work that internal audit has not finished, or internal audit performs work whose results are stale by the time the external auditor needs them. Agents who treat timing as a scheduling convenience miss that it governs whether reliance is even possible, whether roll-forward is needed, and whether the reporting deadline can be met. The harm is concrete: reliance decisions made under deadline pressure, period-end gaps from interim work never rolled forward, and reporting deadlines missed because a dependency was discovered too late.

Use this skill when synchronizing the timing of internal and external audit activities, when resolving scheduling dependencies, or when reconciling reporting deadlines and calendar conflicts across parties. The goal is a timing plan in which every dependency is sequenced, every period-end is covered, and the reporting deadline is protected by a realistic critical path rather than wishful scheduling.

## Core Rules

### Build A Shared Calendar Before Fieldwork Begins

Timing coordination starts with a single shared calendar that lays out each party's planned fieldwork, milestones, and deadlines against a common timeline. Without it, dependencies are invisible until they bite. The calendar should be built early, agreed by all parties, and maintained as conditions change.

Shared calendar elements:

- the external auditor's interim and year-end fieldwork windows;
- internal audit's engagement schedule across the year, including relevant engagements;
- the entity's financial close calendar and reporting deadlines;
- specialist availability, IT, valuation, actuarial, against the windows that need them;
- regulatory and board reporting dates that constrain the audit;
- holidays, blackouts, and system change freezes that affect access;
- milestones for completion, review, and sign-off by each party;
- the dates by which relied-upon work must be finalized for the receiving party to evaluate it;
- regulatory submission windows and board meeting dates that fix the latest acceptable audit completion.

### Align Interim And Year-End Work Deliberately

The split between interim and year-end work is a timing decision with evidentiary consequences. Interim work performed by either party must be rolled forward to year-end, and the parties must agree on who performs the roll-forward and how. Misaligned interim work leaves period-end gaps or forces redundant year-end testing.

Interim and year-end alignment:

- identify which procedures are appropriate for interim and which must occur at or after year-end;
- agree on which party performs interim work and whether the other can leverage or rely on it;
- plan the roll-forward procedures needed to extend interim conclusions to the period end;
- confirm that year-end testing covers the period not addressed by interim work;
- align the timing of interim work so its results are still relevant when year-end work begins;
- avoid interim work so early that subsequent changes render it stale without heavy roll-forward;
- document the interim-to-year-end bridge so the period-end coverage is demonstrable;
- consider the risk of intervening fraud or override when deciding how much to rely on interim conclusions;
- align the interim cutoff date across parties so that roll-forward periods are consistent and comparable.

### Sequence Dependent Work So Inputs Are Ready When Needed

Much coordinated work is sequential: the external auditor cannot evaluate internal audit's work until it is complete and reviewed; substantive reductions cannot be taken until controls testing supports them; estimates cannot be tested until the specialist's valuation is available. A timing plan that ignores these dependencies strands teams and forces rushed, unsupported decisions under deadline pressure.

Dependency sequencing:

- internal audit work intended for external reliance should be completed and reviewed before the external auditor evaluates it;
- controls testing that supports a controls-reliance approach must precede the related substantive reductions;
- specialist work feeding estimates should be timed to inform the auditor's evaluation, not arrive after;
- workpapers relied upon must be finalized, not draft, before reliance is placed;
- build buffer for review cycles, questions, and revisions before the dependent step begins;
- identify the critical path, the longest dependency chain, and protect it from slippage;
- agree on escalation if a dependency slips so the receiving party is not surprised;
- sequence the evaluation of relied-upon work early enough to allow expansion if reliance proves unsupported;
- flag any dependency that crosses organizational boundaries, such as a service auditor report, and track it separately.

### Protect The Reporting Deadline Through A Realistic Critical Path

The reporting deadline is the immovable constraint against which all timing is judged. A plan that looks feasible on paper but ignores the critical path collapses when any dependency slips. The critical path must be identified, protected, and monitored, with contingency for the most likely sources of slippage.

Critical path protection:

- map the longest chain of dependent activities from fieldwork start to report issuance;
- identify the activities whose delay would directly push the reporting deadline;
- build contingency into those activities rather than assuming on-time delivery;
- sequence the highest-risk, highest-judgment work early enough to allow for issues;
- avoid back-loading significant risk procedures to the final days before deadline;
- monitor progress against the critical path throughout the engagement;
- have a pre-agreed escalation if the critical path is threatened.

### Reconcile Calendar Conflicts Across Parties And The Entity

Calendar conflicts are the everyday friction of timing coordination: both parties want the same people during the close, specialists are booked elsewhere, and the entity imposes blackouts. Conflicts that are not reconciled in advance become bottlenecks that compress fieldwork and degrade evidence. Reconciliation should be proactive, not reactive.

Calendar conflict reconciliation:

- identify overlapping demands on the same personnel, systems, or locations;
- stagger fieldwork windows so the entity is not serving all parties at once;
- book scarce specialists and key personnel well in advance;
- respect the entity's close blackout and system freeze periods;
- coordinate with the entity's calendar so audit work does not disrupt the close;
- resolve conflicts through the coordination point before they affect fieldwork;
- document the reconciled schedule so all parties work to the same plan.

### Time Reliance Decisions To Evidence Availability

Reliance is not just a question of whether; it is a question of when. A reliance decision made before the relied-upon work is complete and reviewed is premature and unsupported. Timing coordination must ensure that reliance decisions occur when the evidence is actually available and evaluated, not when the schedule demands a decision.

Reliance timing discipline:

- do not place reliance until the relied-upon work is complete, reviewed, and evaluated;
- schedule the external auditor's evaluation of internal audit's work after that work is finalized;
- build time into the schedule for the evaluation, including questions and follow-up;
- if work is not ready in time, do not force reliance; perform the external procedures instead;
- document the date and basis of each reliance decision against the work's completion;
- revisit reliance timing if the relied-upon work is revised after the decision.

### Manage The Roll-Forward And Period-End Gap Explicitly

When work is performed at interim, the period between interim and year-end is a gap that must be addressed. The roll-forward is not automatic; it is a set of procedures that must be planned, performed, and documented. A timing plan that uses interim work without planning the roll-forward leaves the period-end untested.

Roll-forward and gap management:

- identify the duration of the gap between interim testing and the period end;
- design roll-forward procedures proportional to the gap and the risk;
- agree on which party performs the roll-forward and how it is documented;
- consider whether late-period changes require additional year-end procedures;
- test cutoff and late adjustments separately, as these are not covered by interim work;
- confirm that the combined interim and roll-forward evidence covers the full period;
- document the period-end coverage so a reviewer can see no gap remains;
- reassess the roll-forward scope if material changes or unusual transactions occur in the gap period;
- ensure year-end substantive procedures are not skipped on the assumption that interim work already covered them.

### Build Contingency For Slippage And Late Information

Timing plans assume timely delivery of information and work, but audits rarely go to plan. Late-adjusting entries, slow close, unavailable personnel, and deficient relied-upon work all disrupt the schedule. A robust timing plan builds contingency for these disruptions rather than assuming they will not occur.

Contingency planning:

- identify the information and work most likely to arrive late;
- build slack into the schedule before the reporting deadline;
- pre-plan the external procedures that would replace unsupported reliance;
- agree on the trigger and process for expanding procedures if relied-upon work fails;
- plan for key-person unavailability with cross-staffing or backup contacts;
- maintain a reserve of capacity for late-emerging risks and investigations;
- revisit the timing plan at each coordination milestone and adjust;
- agree in advance which procedures can be compressed and which must not be, so that slippage is absorbed without cutting required evidence.

## Common Traps

### Scheduling Without A Shared Calendar

Each party planning to its own calendar, without a shared view, makes dependencies and conflicts invisible until they disrupt fieldwork. A shared calendar is the foundation of timing coordination.

### Interim Work Without A Roll-Forward Plan

Using interim work to reduce year-end effort, without planning and performing the roll-forward, leaves the period-end gap unaddressed. The roll-forward is a required procedure, not an option.

### Forcing Reliance Before Work Is Complete

Placing reliance to meet a schedule, before the relied-upon work is finalized and evaluated, is premature and unsupported. Reliance timing must follow evidence availability, not deadline pressure.

### Ignoring The Critical Path

A plan that looks balanced but ignores the longest dependency chain collapses when any link slips. The critical path must be identified and protected.

### Back-Loading Significant Risk Procedures

Deferring significant risk and high-judgment procedures to the final days before deadline leaves no time to address issues. Significant risks should be timed to allow for findings and expansion.

### Unreconciled Calendar Conflicts

Competing demands on the same personnel or systems, left unresolved, compress fieldwork and degrade evidence. Conflicts must be reconciled proactively through the coordination point.

### No Contingency For Slippage

A plan with no slack assumes perfect delivery, which rarely happens. Slippage in relied-upon work or late information then pushes the deadline with no recovery path.

### Treating Timing As Logistics Rather Than Evidence

Timing decisions, interim versus year-end, roll-forward, reliance sequencing, directly affect the sufficiency and appropriateness of evidence. Treating them as mere scheduling ignores their evidentiary consequences.

## Self-Check

- Is a shared calendar built and agreed before fieldwork, covering each party's windows, the close, specialist availability, and reporting deadlines?
- Is the interim and year-end split deliberate, with roll-forward procedures planned for any interim work and the period-end gap explicitly addressed?
- Are dependencies sequenced so that relied-upon work is complete and reviewed before the receiving party evaluates or relies on it?
- Is the critical path from fieldwork start to report issuance identified, protected, and monitored, with contingency for slippage?
- Are calendar conflicts across parties and the entity reconciled proactively, with scarce resources booked in advance?
- Are reliance decisions timed to occur only when the relied-upon work is complete, reviewed, and evaluated, not when the schedule demands?
- Is the roll-forward between interim testing and year-end designed, performed, and documented, with cutoff and late adjustments tested separately?
- Is contingency built into the schedule for late information, deficient relied-upon work, and key-person unavailability?
- Are significant risk and high-judgment procedures timed early enough to allow for findings and expansion before the deadline?
- Is the timing plan revisited at each coordination milestone and adjusted as conditions change?; does the timing plan preserve, rather than compromise, the sufficiency and appropriateness of evidence across the full period?
