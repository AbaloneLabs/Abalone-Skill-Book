# Cyber-Enabled Fraud Risk

Cyber-enabled fraud blends traditional fraud schemes with digital attack methods. Business email compromise directs fraudulent payments to attacker-controlled accounts; ransomware encrypts records and extorts payments; account takeover enables unauthorized transactions; and compromised credentials allow manipulation of accounting systems. These schemes produce financial statement impact through stolen assets, fraudulent disbursements, contingent liabilities for breach notification and remediation, and impaired reliability of the accounting records themselves. The recurring failure is treating cyber incidents as an IT or security matter separate from the financial statement audit, or assuming that because the breach is technical its financial impact is immaterial or outside the audit's scope. In reality, a material cyber incident can misstate assets and revenue, create undisclosed contingencies, and call into question the integrity of the records on which the audit relies. The auditor must assess cyber-enabled fraud as a financial statement risk and design a response that addresses both detection and the implications for record reliability.

Use this skill when assessing cyber-enabled fraud risk, when designing procedures to detect fraudulent transactions arising from compromised systems, and when evaluating the financial statement implications of a cyber incident. The goal is a fraud risk assessment that includes cyber-enabled schemes and a response that addresses detection, contingency evaluation, and the reliability of the accounting records.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Financial Statement Impact Of Cyber-Enabled Fraud

Cyber incidents are not only IT security events. They produce direct financial statement impact through stolen or misappropriated assets, fraudulent disbursements, contingent liabilities for notification and remediation, and potential impairment of the reliability of accounting records and controls.

Understand that:

- business email compromise and account takeover can produce fraudulent disbursements that misstate cash and expenses;
- ransomware can disrupt operations, impair the ability to prepare records, and create going concern or contingency exposure;
- stolen data creates contingent liabilities for notification, remediation, regulatory fines, and litigation;
- compromised accounting systems or credentials can undermine the reliability of the records and the controls on which the audit relies.

Understanding the financial dimensions prevents treating cyber incidents as outside the audit's scope.

### Identify Cyber-Enabled Fraud Risk Indicators

Certain entity characteristics and environments elevate cyber-enabled fraud risk. Identifying these indicators directs attention to where schemes are most likely and where procedures should focus.

Indicators include:

- high volumes of electronic payments or wire transfers with weak authorization controls;
- reliance on email-based approval or payment instruction workflows vulnerable to compromise;
- prior cyber incidents, breaches, or unresolved vulnerabilities;
- weak identity and access management, including shared credentials or excessive privileges;
- third-party or supply-chain dependencies with system access to the entity's environment;
- operations in regulated sectors with breach notification obligations and associated fines;
- a pattern of phishing, social engineering, or unauthorized access attempts.

The presence of multiple indicators elevates cyber-enabled fraud risk and warrants a tailored response.

### Assess The Risk Of Fraudulent Transactions From Compromised Systems

Cyber-enabled fraud often produces fraudulent transactions that appear legitimate because they were authorized within compromised systems or workflows. The auditor must assess where such transactions are most likely and design procedures to detect them.

Assess by:

- identifying payment and disbursement processes vulnerable to business email compromise or account takeover;
- evaluating the strength of multi-factor authentication, segregation, and approval controls over electronic payments;
- considering whether accounting system credentials or privileged access could enable manipulation of records;
- assessing whether prior incidents have been fully remediated or whether residual vulnerability remains;
- considering the completeness and accuracy of records prepared from systems that were compromised.

The assessment must consider how a compromise would translate into fraudulent transactions or record manipulation.

### Design Procedures To Detect Cyber-Enabled Fraud

Procedures to detect cyber-enabled fraud must address the digital attack methods and the transactions they produce. They often combine transaction testing, system and log review, and inquiries, supported by IT and forensic specialists.

Design by:

- testing electronic payment and wire transfer transactions for proper authorization, especially those matching compromise patterns such as urgent or off-cycle payments to new accounts;
- examining email-based approval and instruction workflows for signs of compromise;
- reviewing access logs, privileged access, and system changes for unauthorized activity;
- performing analytical procedures on disbursements, vendor additions, and banking details for unusual patterns;
- inquiring of management, IT, and security personnel about incidents, vulnerabilities, and remediation;
- considering the use of forensic and IT specialists where risk is elevated or an incident is suspected.

Procedures must be tailored to the digital attack methods, not generic disbursement testing.

### Evaluate The Reliability Of Records From Compromised Systems

A cyber incident that compromises the accounting system or records raises a fundamental question: can the records on which the audit relies be trusted? The auditor must evaluate whether the compromise affected the integrity, completeness, or accuracy of the records and, if so, what additional procedures are needed to establish reliability.

Evaluate by:

- determining which systems, periods, or data were affected by the compromise;
- assessing whether the compromise could have altered, deleted, or fabricated transactions;
- considering the strength of system logs, backups, and reconciliation controls in detecting and recovering from manipulation;
- where reliability is impaired, designing procedures to reconstruct or independently verify affected records;
- concluding whether the records provide a reliable basis for the audit.

Treating compromised records as reliable without evaluation undermines the foundation of the audit.

### Evaluate Disclosure And Contingency Obligations

Where a cyber incident has occurred, the auditor must evaluate disclosure and contingency obligations. Notification costs, remediation, regulatory fines, and litigation may require accrual or disclosure. The evaluation must consider the likelihood and estimability of the exposure.

Evaluate by:

- identifying the potential exposures, including notification, remediation, fines, and litigation;
- assessing whether exposures are probable and estimable, requiring accrual, or reasonably possible, requiring disclosure;
- considering whether the entity has appropriately evaluated and disclosed the exposures;
- reviewing legal counsel responses and management's assessment;
- determining whether the exposures, individually or aggregated, are material to the financial statements.

Failure to evaluate disclosure and contingency obligations leaves material liabilities undisclosed.

### Consider The Implications For Controls And Going Concern

A material cyber incident can affect the control environment and, in severe cases, the entity's ability to continue as a going concern. The auditor must consider these broader implications, not only the immediate transactions.

Consider by:

- assessing the impact of the incident on the control environment and on specific controls relied upon;
- where the incident disrupts operations or destroys records, considering going concern implications;
- evaluating whether management's remediation plan is adequate and whether residual risk remains;
- communicating the implications to those charged with governance.

A cyber incident is not an isolated event; its control and going concern implications must be considered.

## Common Traps

### Treating Cyber Incidents As An IT Matter Outside The Audit

Cyber incidents have direct financial statement impact. Treating them as outside the audit's scope leaves misstated assets, undisclosed contingencies, and unreliable records.

### Assuming Technical Breaches Have Immaterial Financial Impact

A technical breach can produce material fraudulent disbursements, contingent liabilities, and record impairment. The assumption of immateriality is often false.

### Using Generic Disbursement Testing

Generic testing does not detect cyber-enabled fraud. Procedures must target the digital attack methods and compromise patterns.

### Failing To Evaluate Record Reliability After Compromise

Compromised records may be unreliable. Failing to evaluate reliability undermines the foundation of the audit.

### Missing Disclosure And Contingency Obligations

Cyber incidents create notification, remediation, fine, and litigation exposures. Missing these leaves material liabilities undisclosed.

### Underestimating Control And Going Concern Implications

A material incident affects controls and may affect going concern. Underestimating these leaves broader risks unaddressed.

### Avoiding Specialist Involvement

Cyber-enabled fraud often requires IT and forensic expertise. Avoiding specialists understates the detection and evaluation capacity.

## Self-Check

- Does the fraud risk assessment include cyber-enabled fraud, with attention to financial statement impact including stolen assets, fraudulent disbursements, and contingencies?
- Have cyber-enabled fraud risk indicators been identified, including electronic payment volumes, email-based workflows, prior incidents, and access management weaknesses?
- Has the risk of fraudulent transactions from compromised systems been assessed, including how a compromise would translate into transactions or record manipulation?
- Are procedures designed to detect cyber-enabled fraud tailored to the digital attack methods, including transaction testing, log review, and inquiries?
- Where systems or records have been compromised, has the reliability of those records been evaluated, with additional procedures designed where reliability is impaired?
- Where a cyber incident has occurred, have disclosure and contingency obligations been evaluated, including notification, remediation, fines, and litigation?
- Have the implications for the control environment and going concern been considered, particularly for material incidents?
- Where risk is elevated or an incident is suspected, have IT and forensic specialists been engaged or coordinated with?
- Is the assessment free of treating cyber incidents as an IT matter or assuming technical breaches have immaterial financial impact?
- Could an independent reviewer confirm that cyber-enabled fraud risk was assessed and addressed with procedures and disclosure evaluation calibrated to the risk?
