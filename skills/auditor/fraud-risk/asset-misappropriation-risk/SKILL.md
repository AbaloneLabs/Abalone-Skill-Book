# Asset Misappropriation Risk

Misappropriation of assets is the theft or misuse of an entity's assets by employees, management, or third parties. It encompasses cash schemes such as skimming, larceny, and fraudulent disbursements, as well as theft of inventory, supplies, and fixed assets. While often smaller in individual amount than fraudulent financial reporting, asset misappropriation is the most common form of occupational fraud, and aggregated over time it can become material to the financial statements. The auditor's responsibility is to obtain reasonable assurance that the financial statements are free of material misstatement, whether caused by fraud or error, and material asset misappropriation falls within that responsibility. The recurring failure is dismissing asset misappropriation as an operational or HR matter outside the audit's scope, or assuming that because individual schemes appear small they cannot aggregate to materiality. Both errors leave theft schemes undetected and the financial statements potentially misstated.

Use this skill when assessing the risk of asset misappropriation, when designing procedures to detect theft schemes, and when evaluating whether identified misappropriation could result in material misstatement. The goal is a fraud risk assessment that includes asset misappropriation alongside fraudulent financial reporting, with procedures calibrated to the schemes most likely in the entity.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Scope Of The Auditor's Responsibility

The auditor's responsibility for fraud covers both fraudulent financial reporting and misappropriation of assets. The focus of the audit is on fraud that could result in material misstatement. For asset misappropriation, this means theft schemes that, individually or aggregated, could materially misstate the financial statements.

Understand that:

- the responsibility extends to asset misappropriation that could be material, not only to fraudulent financial reporting;
- small or immaterial schemes may aggregate to materiality over time and warrant attention;
- the assessment must consider which assets are most susceptible and which schemes are most likely;
- detection focuses on schemes that could produce material misstatement, not on uncovering every instance of theft.

Understanding the scope prevents both dismissing asset misappropriation and over-extending into immaterial operational matters.

### Identify Susceptible Assets And Likely Schemes

Different assets and environments present different misappropriation risks. Cash is the most commonly targeted asset, but inventory, supplies, intellectual property, and fixed assets are also vulnerable. The schemes vary: skimming removes cash before it enters the records; larceny removes cash after it enters the records; fraudulent disbursements move cash out through bogus vendors, payroll, or expense schemes.

Identify by:

- considering which assets are most liquid, portable, or difficult to trace;
- assessing cash receipting and disbursement processes for weak controls and access;
- evaluating inventory controls, particularly for high-value or easily resold items;
- considering payroll and expense processes for ghost employees or inflated claims;
- reviewing vendor master data for shell vendors, related parties, or duplicate payments.

The identification must be specific to the entity's assets, processes, and control weaknesses.

### Consider The Fraud Triangle For Perpetrators

Asset misappropriation, like all fraud, is driven by the fraud triangle: pressure, opportunity, and rationalization. For asset schemes, opportunity is often the dominant factor, created by weak segregation of duties, excessive access, or absent oversight. Pressure may arise from personal financial difficulty, gambling, addiction, or resentment. Rationalization allows the perpetrator to justify the theft.

Consider by:

- identifying where weak segregation of duties or excessive access creates opportunity;
- assessing whether roles with cash, vendor, payroll, or inventory access also have reconciliation or approval authority;
- considering personal or financial pressures that may affect employees in sensitive roles;
- recognizing that long-tenured, trusted employees can be perpetrators, and that trust is not a control.

The fraud triangle analysis directs attention to where opportunity and pressure converge.

### Design Procedures To Detect Cash Schemes

Cash schemes are the most common form of asset misappropriation. The procedures must address the specific schemes most likely in the entity's environment, based on the risk assessment.

Design by:

- for skimming, considering procedures that address off-book theft, such as analytical comparisons of revenue to expectations and surprise counts;
- for larceny, testing bank deposits and reconciliations for timing and completeness;
- for fraudulent disbursements, testing vendor master data for shell or related-party vendors, examining supporting documentation for disbursements, and reperforming reconciliations;
- for payroll schemes, testing for ghost employees, inflated hours, or unauthorized rate changes;
- for expense schemes, testing expense reimbursements for duplication, policy violations, or unsupported claims.

Procedures must be tailored to the schemes identified as likely, not generic.

### Design Procedures To Detect Inventory And Asset Theft

Inventory and fixed asset theft present different detection challenges than cash. Physical controls, access, and recordkeeping are central, and analytical procedures may reveal shrinkage or unexplained reductions.

Design by:

- observing physical inventory counts and comparing to recorded amounts;
- performing analytical procedures on inventory shrinkage, turnover, and cost ratios;
- testing fixed asset additions and disposals for proper authorization and existence;
- considering high-value, portable, or easily resold items for enhanced scrutiny;
- reviewing access controls over warehouses, yards, and asset storage.

Procedures must address the physical and recordkeeping aspects of asset theft.

### Evaluate Aggregation And Materiality

Individual asset misappropriation schemes may appear small, but fraud is often recurring and can aggregate over time. The auditor must consider whether schemes that appear immaterial individually could aggregate to materiality, particularly over multiple periods.

Evaluate by:

- considering the duration over which a scheme could operate undetected;
- assessing whether control weaknesses would allow recurring theft;
- aggregating identified or suspected misappropriation across accounts and periods;
- concluding whether the aggregated amount could be material to the financial statements.

Dismissing small schemes without considering aggregation can leave material misstatement undetected.

### Respond To Identified Misappropriation

Where asset misappropriation is identified or suspected, the auditor must respond. The response includes investigating the scope, considering the implications for the financial statements, and communicating the matter appropriately.

Respond by:

- investigating the scope and amount of the misappropriation;
- determining whether the amount is material and whether the financial statements require adjustment;
- considering whether the misappropriation indicates broader control failures or fraud;
- communicating the matter to management and, where appropriate, those charged with governance;
- considering the implications for the audit opinion and for the continuation of the engagement.

Identified misappropriation cannot be dismissed as operational; its financial statement implications must be evaluated.

## Common Traps

### Dismissing Asset Misappropriation As Operational

Asset misappropriation that could be material is within the audit's scope. Dismissing it as an HR or operational matter leaves material misstatement risk unaddressed.

### Assuming Small Schemes Cannot Aggregate

Fraud is often recurring. Small schemes can aggregate to materiality over time and must be considered for aggregation.

### Treating Trust As A Control

Long-tenured, trusted employees can be perpetrators. Trust is not a control; segregation of duties and oversight are.

### Ignoring Segregation Of Duties Weaknesses

Weak segregation, particularly in cash, vendor, and payroll functions, creates the opportunity for misappropriation. Ignoring these weaknesses understates risk.

### Using Generic Procedures Not Tailored To Schemes

Cash, inventory, and disbursement schemes require different procedures. Generic testing may miss the specific schemes most likely in the entity.

### Missing Shell Vendors And Related Parties

Fraudulent disbursements often flow through shell or related-party vendors. Missing these in vendor master data testing leaves a common scheme undetected.

### Failing To Respond To Identified Misappropriation

Identified misappropriation must be investigated, evaluated for materiality, and communicated. Dismissing it as immaterial without aggregation analysis fails the responsibility.

## Self-Check

- Does the fraud risk assessment include asset misappropriation alongside fraudulent financial reporting, with attention to schemes that could be material?
- Have susceptible assets and likely schemes been identified, specific to the entity's assets, processes, and control weaknesses?
- Has the fraud triangle, particularly opportunity created by weak segregation and access, been considered for perpetrators in sensitive roles?
- Are procedures designed to detect cash schemes, including skimming, larceny, and fraudulent disbursements, tailored to the schemes identified as likely?
- Are procedures designed to detect inventory and fixed asset theft, including physical counts, analytical procedures, and access controls?
- Has aggregation across accounts and periods been considered, so that recurring schemes are not dismissed as immaterial?
- Where misappropriation is identified or suspected, has it been investigated, evaluated for materiality, and communicated appropriately?
- Is the assessment free of dismissing asset misappropriation as operational or assuming small schemes cannot aggregate?
- Could an independent reviewer confirm that asset misappropriation risk was assessed and addressed with procedures calibrated to the entity's schemes?
- Is the treatment of trust as a substitute for controls avoided, with segregation and oversight assessed objectively?
