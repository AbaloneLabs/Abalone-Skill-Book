# Fraud Triangle Assessment

The fraud triangle is the lens that explains why fraud happens: pressure provides the motive, opportunity provides the path, and rationalization provides the justification. Auditors use it not to diagnose psychology but to identify where, in this specific entity, the conditions for fraud are present. Agents often treat fraud risk as a generic overlay, applying the same boilerplate to every engagement, or they assess only opportunity (weak controls) and ignore pressure and rationalization. That produces a fraud risk assessment that names fraud without locating it. A meaningful assessment identifies the specific pressures on this management, the specific opportunities in these processes, and the specific attitudes that would allow rationalization, then targets procedures at those points.

Use this skill during fraud risk brainstorming, before designing fraud-specific responses, and whenever assessing whether the conditions for management or employee fraud are present. The goal is a specific, entity-tailored fraud risk assessment that drives targeted procedures, not a generic fraud acknowledgment.

## Core Rules

### Conduct Structured Fraud Risk Brainstorming

Fraud risk assessment begins with brainstorming among the engagement team, conducted with professional skepticism and free of management's influence. The session should surface where fraud could occur and who could commit it, without anchoring on last year's conclusions.

During brainstorming:

- set aside assumptions that management is honest because they appear cooperative;
- consider both fraudulent financial reporting and misappropriation of assets;
- brainstorm how and where in the financial statements fraud could occur and be concealed;
- consider the risk of management override of controls as a pervasive risk;
- identify external and internal pressures on management and employees;
- consider opportunities created by weak, bypassed, or overridable controls;
- discuss known or suspected fraud, allegations, or whistleblower concerns.

Document the brainstorming, including the risks identified and the responses planned. A brainstorming session with no documented risks or responses is not a fraud assessment.

### Assess Pressure Specific To This Entity And Management

Pressure provides the motive for fraud. It must be assessed specifically, because generic pressure applies to everyone and locates nothing.

Sources of pressure to evaluate:

- financial pressure to meet earnings, growth, or analyst expectations;
- covenant, debt, or financing pressure that threatens default or access to capital;
- compensation or bonus tied to results, thresholds, or targets;
- regulatory or licensing pressure to meet thresholds;
- pressure from a pending transaction such as an acquisition, IPO, or refinancing;
- personal pressure on owners or managers, such as guarantees, lifestyle, or debt;
- pressure to reverse a decline or avoid a loss;
- competitive or industry pressure threatening survival.

Document the specific pressures present and which financial statement areas they would most likely affect. Pressure on earnings points to revenue and expense manipulation; pressure on covenants points to leverage and ratio metrics.

### Assess Opportunity Created By The Control Environment

Opportunity is the path fraud takes. It is created by control weaknesses, but also by design choices, automation gaps, and override pathways that may not appear as weaknesses on paper.

Sources of opportunity to evaluate:

- areas with weak segregation of duties, especially in cash, journal entries, and disbursements;
- estimates and judgments that management can influence within a wide range;
- complex or unusual transactions that are hard to scrutinize;
- automated controls that management can override or reconfigure;
- related-party transactions that bypass normal controls;
- period-end adjusting entries and top-side journal entries;
- access to systems that record or alter transactions;
- locations or processes with limited oversight or remote management;
- revenue recognition points where cutoff and timing can be manipulated.

Document where opportunity exists and how a fraudster could exploit it. Opportunity without a path is not a located risk.

### Assess Rationalization And Attitude

Rationalization is the justification that allows a person to commit fraud while viewing themselves as honest. It is the hardest component to assess, but attitude and ethical climate provide signals.

Indicators of rationalization risk:

- a culture that tolerates aggressive accounting or rule-bending;
- a history of disputes with auditors, regulators, or auditors over accounting;
- management that is overly evasive, defensive, or dominant;
- a pattern of explaining away exceptions or unfavorable evidence;
- high turnover in finance, internal audit, or compliance roles;
- prior fraud, allegations, or ethics concerns;
- a tone at the top that prioritizes results over integrity;
- family or personal relationships that complicate objectivity.

Rationalization does not prove fraud, but its presence, combined with pressure and opportunity, raises the fraud risk profile and warrants heightened skepticism.

### Treat Management Override As A Pervasive Risk

Management override of controls is a risk on every audit, because management can bypass the very controls designed to prevent fraud. It cannot be eliminated by controls and must be addressed with specific procedures.

Override pathways to address:

- testing journal entries, especially manual, late-period, round-number, and top-side entries;
- reviewing accounting estimates for bias and override;
- evaluating significant unusual transactions for substance over form;
- examining the business rationale for transactions that appear designed to manipulate;
- corroborating inquiry and challenging management explanations rather than accepting them.

Document the override response separately, because it is pervasive and not limited to a single account.

### Identify Presumptive Fraud Risks

Certain fraud risks are presumed present unless the auditor can show they are not, given the entity's circumstances.

Presumptive fraud risks:

- fraudulent revenue recognition, including premature recognition, cutoff manipulation, channel stuffing, and fictitious revenue;
- management override of controls;
- related-party transactions and significant unusual transactions near period end.

For each presumptive risk, either design a specific response or document why the risk is not present in this entity. Dismissing a presumptive risk without reasoning is not acceptable.

### Translate The Assessment Into Targeted, Unpredictable Procedures

A fraud risk assessment that does not change the procedures is decoration. Each identified fraud risk must drive a specific, often unpredictable, response.

Fraud-specific responses:

- test journal entries with focus on the identified override pathways;
- perform substantive procedures at period end rather than interim for cutoff-sensitive areas;
- confirm balances and transactions with external parties;
- examine revenue recognition for side agreements, bill-and-hold, or round-tripping;
- test bank and vendor details for redirection risk;
- perform procedures management cannot anticipate, varying location, timing, and selection;
- corroborate management explanations with independent evidence;
- increase skepticism and challenge convenient or self-serving explanations.

Document how each identified fraud risk maps to a specific response.

## Common Traps

### Applying Generic Fraud Risk Boilerplate

Using the same fraud risk language on every engagement locates no risk and drives no targeted procedures. The assessment must be entity-specific.

### Assessing Only Opportunity

Opportunity (weak controls) is the easiest component to see, but pressure provides the motive and rationalization provides the willingness. All three must be assessed.

### Skipping Or Under-Documenting Brainstorming

A brainstorming session with no documented risks or responses is not a fraud assessment. The session must surface and record specific risks.

### Dismissing Presumptive Fraud Risks Without Reasoning

Revenue recognition fraud and management override are presumed present. Dismissing them without documented reasoning breaches the standard.

### Treating Override As An Ordinary Control Weakness

Management override bypasses controls by design. It cannot be addressed by relying on controls and requires specific override-focused procedures.

### Accepting Convenient Management Explanations

Skepticism must rise with fraud risk. Self-serving explanations require corroboration, not acceptance.

### Letting The Fraud Assessment Drive No Change In Procedures

If the procedures are identical to a no-fraud-risk audit, the assessment added nothing. Each identified risk must change the nature, timing, or extent of testing.

## Self-Check

- Was structured fraud risk brainstorming conducted and documented, surfacing specific risks rather than generic acknowledgment?
- Are the pressures on this management, including earnings, covenants, compensation, financing, and personal pressures, assessed specifically and linked to affected financial statement areas?
- Are the opportunities for fraud, including segregation weaknesses, estimates, override pathways, and related-party transactions, located to specific processes and paths?
- Are rationalization and attitude signals, including culture, defensiveness, turnover, and prior concerns, considered alongside pressure and opportunity?
- Is management override treated as a pervasive risk with specific override-focused procedures, rather than as an ordinary control weakness?
- Are the presumptive fraud risks, revenue recognition and override, either addressed with specific responses or documented as not present with reasoning?
- Does each identified fraud risk map to a targeted, often unpredictable, procedure that differs from routine testing?
- Is professional skepticism elevated throughout, with convenient explanations corroborated rather than accepted?
