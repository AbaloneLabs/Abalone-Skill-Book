# Journal Entry Testing

Journal entry testing is the auditor's primary procedure for addressing the risk of management override of internal control. Because management can direct, authorize, or conceal entries that manipulate the financial statements, the auditor must test journal entries for evidence of fraud, bias, or inappropriate adjustment. The recurring failure is treating journal entry testing as a mechanical exercise, selecting a handful of entries by convenience, or relying on a generic checklist without tailoring the testing to the entity's specific fraud risks and systems. Effective journal entry testing requires understanding how and where entries are made, using the fraud risk assessment to focus the testing, and applying computer-assisted techniques to identify the unusual or inappropriate entries that a manual sample would miss. The objective is not to test every entry but to test the right entries, the ones most likely to reveal fraud if it exists.

Use this skill when designing and performing journal entry testing, when addressing the risk of management override, and when evaluating the business rationale of significant unusual transactions. The goal is testing that is risk-focused, technologically supported, and capable of detecting fraud that other procedures would miss.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Requirement And Its Fraud Focus

Journal entry testing is required to address the risk of management override of internal control, which is a significant risk in every audit. The testing is not a routine substantive procedure; it is a fraud-response procedure. Its design must reflect that purpose.

Understand that:

- the testing addresses management override, a presumptive significant risk;
- the objective is to identify entries made to manipulate, conceal, or misstate, not to verify routine entries;
- the testing must be unpredictable from management's perspective, so that it could catch concealed fraud;
- the testing complements, but does not replace, other fraud-response procedures.

Understanding the fraud focus prevents reducing the testing to a mechanical exercise.

### Understand How And Where Entries Are Made

Effective testing requires understanding the entity's journal entry processes, systems, and controls. Entries may be manual or automated, recurring or non-routine, initiated at the corporate level or in subsidiaries, and posted through the general ledger or through subledgers.

Understand by:

- mapping the systems and interfaces through which entries reach the general ledger;
- identifying who has the ability to initiate, approve, and post entries, especially manual entries;
- distinguishing manual from automated entries and recurring from non-routine entries;
- understanding the controls over entry initiation, approval, and posting;
- identifying the periods of highest risk, particularly period-end and post-closing.

Understanding the entry landscape focuses testing on where fraud is most likely.

### Use The Fraud Risk Assessment To Focus Testing

Journal entry testing must be tailored to the entity's specific fraud risks. A generic, untargeted test is unlikely to detect fraud. The fraud risk assessment identifies the assertions, accounts, and entry types most susceptible to manipulation.

Focus by:

- identifying the accounts and assertions most susceptible to fraud, based on the risk assessment;
- targeting entries that affect those accounts, particularly at period-end or post-closing;
- considering entries made by senior personnel, by those with unusual access, or outside normal workflows;
- focusing on entries with characteristics associated with fraud, such as round amounts, weekend or holiday postings, or entries to unrelated accounts.

Risk-focused testing concentrates effort where fraud is most likely to appear.

### Apply Computer-Assisted Audit Techniques

The volume of journal entries in most entities is too large for manual testing. Computer-assisted audit techniques (CAATs) enable the auditor to test the full population, identify anomalies, and select high-risk entries for investigation. CAATs are not optional where the population is large; they are the appropriate tool.

Apply by:

- obtaining the complete population of journal entries in electronic form, reconciled to the general ledger;
- performing completeness and integrity checks on the data extracted;
- applying risk-based filters and criteria to identify unusual or suspicious entries;
- selecting high-risk entries for detailed examination of support and business rationale;
- using the results to refine the testing and identify additional risk areas.

CAATs transform journal entry testing from a sample-based exercise into a population-level fraud detection procedure.

### Evaluate The Business Rationale Of Significant Unusual Transactions

Beyond individual entries, the auditor must evaluate the business rationale of significant unusual transactions. Transactions that lack apparent business purpose, are overly complex, or involve unidentified parties may indicate fraud, including concealed related-party arrangements.

Evaluate by:

- identifying significant unusual transactions, particularly those identified through journal entry testing or analytics;
- inquiring of management about the business purpose and the parties involved;
- assessing whether the explanation is plausible and supported by the surrounding evidence;
- considering whether the transaction appears designed to conceal, misstate, or achieve a particular accounting result.

Transactions without a genuine business rationale are a red flag that demands further investigation.

### Examine Supporting Evidence For Selected Entries

For entries selected for testing, the auditor must examine the supporting evidence, not merely confirm that the entry was posted. The examination should verify that the entry is supported, properly approved, and accurately recorded.

Examine by:

- obtaining the supporting documentation for the entry, including approvals and underlying transactions;
- verifying that the entry is accurately recorded and posted to the correct accounts and periods;
- assessing whether the entry is consistent with the entity's accounting policies and the applicable framework;
- identifying entries that lack support, are unapproved, or appear manipulative.

Examining support distinguishes legitimate entries from those that may reflect fraud or error.

### Assess Implications Of Identified Anomalies

Where journal entry testing identifies anomalies, unsupported entries, or suspicious transactions, the auditor must assess the implications. A single anomaly may be an error; a pattern may indicate fraud. The assessment must consider both the individual entry and the broader picture.

Assess by:

- investigating each identified anomaly to determine its cause;
- determining whether anomalies are isolated or form a pattern;
- considering whether anomalies indicate management override, fraud, or a control deficiency;
- where fraud is suspected, expanding procedures and considering the implications for the engagement;
- documenting the anomalies, the investigation, and the conclusions.

Identified anomalies cannot be dismissed without investigation; their implications must be assessed.

## Common Traps

### Treating Testing As A Mechanical Exercise

Journal entry testing is a fraud-response procedure. Mechanical, convenience-based selection defeats its purpose.

### Using Generic, Untargeted Testing

Untargeted testing is unlikely to detect fraud. The testing must be tailored to the entity's specific fraud risks.

### Ignoring CAATs For Large Populations

Manual testing of a large population misses anomalies. CAATs are the appropriate tool for population-level testing.

### Failing To Understand The Entry Landscape

Without understanding how and where entries are made, testing cannot focus on where fraud is likely.

### Accepting Entries Without Examining Support

Confirming that an entry was posted is not enough. The supporting evidence and approval must be examined.

### Dismissing Anomalies Without Investigation

Anomalies may indicate fraud or control failure. Dismissing them without investigation leaves risk unaddressed.

### Overlooking Business Rationale Of Unusual Transactions

Transactions without business purpose may indicate fraud. Overlooking rationale leaves concealed arrangements undetected.

## Self-Check

- Is journal entry testing understood and designed as a fraud-response procedure addressing management override, not a routine substantive test?
- Is the entity's journal entry process, including systems, interfaces, access, and controls, understood and mapped?
- Is the testing tailored to the entity's specific fraud risks, focusing on susceptible accounts, assertions, and entry types?
- Where the population is large, are computer-assisted audit techniques used to test the full population and identify anomalies?
- Are significant unusual transactions evaluated for business rationale, particularly those lacking apparent purpose or involving unidentified parties?
- For selected entries, is supporting evidence examined, including approvals, accuracy, and consistency with accounting policies?
- Where anomalies are identified, are they investigated, assessed for pattern and implication, and documented?
- Could an independent reviewer confirm that the testing is risk-focused, technologically supported, and capable of detecting fraud that manual sampling would miss?
- Is the testing free of mechanical, convenience-based selection and generic, untargeted criteria?
- Are the implications of identified anomalies assessed for fraud, override, and control deficiency, with procedures expanded where warranted?
