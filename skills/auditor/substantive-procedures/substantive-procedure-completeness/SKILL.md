# Substantive Procedure Completeness

Substantive completeness is the question of whether the overall set of substantive procedures, taken together, provides sufficient appropriate evidence for all material assertions of all significant accounts. It is possible to perform many procedures well and still have a coverage gap — an assertion or a population that nothing directly addresses, or a risk that fell between the detail test and the analytical procedure because each assumed the other covered it. The discipline is to map every material assertion to the procedure(s) that provide evidence for it, confirm there are no gaps, and confirm the combined evidence is sufficient and appropriate before the opinion is formed.

## Core Rules

### Map every significant account to all its relevant assertions

For each significant account, list every assertion that is relevant (existence, completeness, accuracy, cutoff, valuation, rights, classification, presentation). Not every assertion is relevant to every account, but the relevant set must be identified explicitly. Receivables carry existence, valuation, rights, completeness, and cutoff; a prepaid expense carries existence, accuracy, and amortisation (valuation). Missing an assertion in the mapping is the first source of coverage gaps, because a procedure cannot cover an assertion no one identified.

### Link each assertion to the procedure(s) that provide evidence

For each assertion, identify the specific procedure(s) that provide evidence — detail test, analytical procedure, or both — and confirm the linkage is real, not assumed. A detail test of receivable confirmations provides existence evidence; it does not provide valuation evidence unless the confirmation result is combined with ageing and subsequent-cash analysis. State, for each assertion, which procedure(s) support it and what kind of evidence they provide. Assertions supported only by an assumption ("the control covers it") without a linked substantive procedure are gaps unless control reliance has been properly established.

### Confirm each material assertion has direct, targeted coverage

For each material assertion, confirm there is at least one procedure whose evidence directly addresses it:

- **Existence**: confirmations, physical observation, vouching to source.
- **Completeness**: source-based tracing, cut-off in both directions, subsequent-events review.
- **Valuation**: allowances, impairment, net realisable value, fair value evidence.
- **Rights**: confirmations of pledges/factoring, review of contracts.
- **Accuracy**: recalculation, re-performance.
- **Cutoff**: transactions around the boundary in both directions.
- **Presentation/disclosure**: agreement to disclosures, completeness of disclosure checklist.

An assertion covered only indirectly (e.g., existence "covered" by an analytical procedure) is weakly covered; for material assertions, prefer direct, targeted evidence.

### Avoid the "between two procedures" gap

A common coverage gap arises when two procedures each appear to cover an assertion but neither actually does, because each assumed the other handled it. For example, a detail test assumes the analytical procedure covers valuation, while the analytical procedure assumes the detail test covers it; valuation ends up covered by neither. Resolve this by explicitly assigning each assertion to a primary procedure and confirming the assignment is real. The matrix of assertions to procedures should have no cell that is covered by mutual assumption.

### Ensure the combined evidence is sufficient and appropriate

Completeness is not only about presence of procedures but about whether they collectively provide sufficient appropriate evidence. Assess:

- is the evidence from enough sources (external, internal, documentary, analytical)?
- is it persuasive enough for the risk (more persuasive for higher risk)?
- do the procedures cover the whole population at risk, including all locations and periods?
- do they address the identified risks specifically, not just the account generically?

A complete-looking set of procedures that collectively provides thin evidence for a high-risk assertion is not actually complete; it needs strengthening.

### Confirm coverage across all locations, components, and periods

Substantive completeness includes geographic and temporal coverage. Confirm:

- all components or locations within scope have evidence (using materiality and scoping decisions);
- procedures cover the full period, not just year-end, where the assertion relates to the period (e.g., revenue over the year);
- interim procedures are rolled forward to year-end where the assertion is a period-end balance;
- cut-off procedures span the period boundary in both directions.

A location or a period left without coverage is a completeness gap, even if the account-level procedures are well designed.

### Address completeness and cutoff assertions with deliberate, source-based procedures

Completeness and cutoff are the assertions most often left under-covered, because they require reaching outside the ledger. Ensure, for each relevant account, that:

- completeness is tested by source-based procedures (subsequent disbursements, unmatched receiving, shipping-to-revenue tracing), not only by record-based sampling;
- cutoff is tested by examining transactions on both sides of the period boundary, in both directions.

Defaulting to record-based testing for these assertions is a structural gap that no amount of sample size can fix.

### Include disclosure and presentation coverage

Substantive completeness extends to disclosures, not only to the primary statements. Confirm procedures cover:

- the existence and accuracy of disclosed amounts, commitments, and contingencies;
- the completeness of required disclosures (using a disclosure checklist tied to the framework);
- the appropriateness of classification and presentation;
- related-party disclosures, subsequent events, and segment information.

Disclosures are often under-tested relative to the primary statements; a complete strategy covers both.

### Re-assess completeness when risks or findings change

Substantive completeness is not static. If procedures identify errors, control deficiencies, or new risks during the audit, the original coverage may no longer be sufficient. Re-assess:

- does a finding in one area indicate a risk in another that lacks coverage?
- does a control deficiency require expanded substantive procedures?
- do errors found indicate the procedures were insufficient and need extension?

Build the completeness assessment as a living map, updated as the audit progresses, not a one-time plan.

## Common Traps

- **Missing an assertion in the account-to-assertion mapping**, so no procedure is ever designed for it.
- **Assuming a procedure covers an assertion it does not** — e.g., confirmation covering valuation, or an analytical procedure covering existence.
- **The "between two procedures" gap**, where each procedure assumes the other covers an assertion that neither does.
- **Covering material assertions only indirectly or with thin evidence**, treating presence of a procedure as sufficient coverage.
- **Leaving a location, component, or period without coverage**, despite account-level procedures being well designed.
- **Defaulting to record-based testing for completeness and cutoff**, which structurally cannot test those assertions.
- **Under-testing disclosures and presentation** relative to the primary statements.
- **Treating the completeness map as a one-time plan** rather than updating it as findings and risks emerge.
- **Concluding sufficient appropriate evidence without explicitly aggregating** the evidence across procedures and assertions.

## Self-Check

- Did I map every significant account to all its relevant assertions, missing none?
- For each assertion, did I link it to the specific procedure(s) that provide evidence, and confirm the linkage is real?
- Does each material assertion have direct, targeted coverage, not just indirect or assumed coverage?
- Did I resolve every "between two procedures" gap by explicitly assigning each assertion to a primary procedure?
- Is the combined evidence across procedures sufficient and appropriate — enough sources, persuasive enough for the risk, covering the whole population?
- Did I confirm coverage across all in-scope locations, components, and periods, with roll-forward where interim?
- Are completeness and cutoff tested by deliberate, source-based procedures, not only record-based sampling?
- Did I include disclosure and presentation coverage, using a disclosure checklist tied to the framework?
- Did I re-assess completeness when findings, errors, or new risks emerged, extending procedures where needed?
- Can I aggregate the evidence across all procedures and assertions to support the conclusion that sufficient appropriate evidence has been obtained?
