# Analytical Procedure Design

Substantive analytical procedures evaluate financial information through analysis of relationships — ratios, trends, reasonableness comparisons — to identify and investigate differences between expected and recorded amounts. When well designed, they can provide direct evidence about an assertion, sometimes replacing or reducing tests of details, especially for predictable, stable populations. When poorly designed, they produce a comforting comparison that has no real evidential value: an expectation so loose that any result would "pass," or a relationship so unpredictable that no meaningful expectation can be formed. The discipline is to design analytical procedures whose expectations are precise enough to detect a material misstatement and whose corroboration is strong enough to support reliance.

## Core Rules

### Assess whether an analytical procedure is appropriate for the assertion at all

Not every assertion lends itself to analytical evidence. Analytical procedures work best where the relationship being tested is predictable and stable, so a meaningful expectation can be formed. They are strongest for:

- **Predictable relationships**: interest expense from debt balances and rates, depreciation from asset bases and rates, payroll from headcount and rates, rental income from occupancy and rates.
- **Stable accounts**: recurring expenses, period-over-period balances with low volatility.
- **Volume-driven accounts**: revenue, cost of sales, commissions, where a driver (units, production, sales) explains the amount.

They are weaker or inappropriate where relationships are volatile, highly discretionary, or driven by judgement (certain estimates, fair value adjustments, one-off items). For those, tests of details or specific procedures are more reliable. State explicitly whether an analytical procedure can, in principle, provide sufficient evidence for the assertion before investing in the design.

### Develop a precise, independent expectation

The evidential value of an analytical procedure is entirely in the precision and independence of the expectation. A weak expectation produces a weak procedure. Build expectations from:

- **Independent drivers**: production volumes, headcount, interest rates, floor space, units shipped — data the auditor can corroborate independently of the account being tested.
- **Prior-period and industry data**: adjusted for known changes.
- **Interrelationships**: between accounts (e.g., commission expense should track commissioned revenue at the contractual rate).

Avoid expectations that simply compare the current balance to last year's without adjustment, or that rest on management's own forecast without corroboration. The expectation must be precise enough that a material misstatement would cause an identifiable difference; if the expectation could accommodate a materially wrong answer, it has no evidential value.

### Calibrate precision to materiality and to the risk

The expectation's precision must be finer than the misstatement the procedure is meant to detect. For a high-risk, material assertion, the expectation must be tight: a difference of, say, 5% might be tolerable for a low-risk account but would mask a material misstatement in a large, high-risk one. Ask: if the recorded amount were overstated by a material amount, would my expectation reveal it? If not, the expectation is too loose for this assertion, regardless of how reasonable it looks.

### Investigate and explain differences with corroboration, not narration

When recorded amounts differ from expectation, the difference must be investigated and explained with corroborated evidence — not with a narrative that merely describes plausible causes. Acceptable corroboration includes:

- specific transactions or events that account for the difference, verified by the auditor;
- changes in the business (volume, mix, price) confirmed independently;
- errors identified and corrected.

An explanation that "sales grew because of strong demand" without corroboration is a description, not evidence. Require the explanation to be specific, quantified, and independently confirmed before accepting the difference.

### Decide whether the analytical procedure can replace or reduce detail testing

A well-designed analytical procedure can, in the right circumstances, provide sufficient evidence on its own or reduce the extent of detail testing. The conditions are demanding:

- the relationship is highly predictable and stable;
- the expectation is precise and based on corroborated, independent data;
- the procedure is corroborated by other evidence (controls, prior-year comparisons, industry data);
- the risk of material misstatement is not so high that only detail evidence will suffice.

Where these hold, an analytical procedure may be the primary substantive evidence. Where they do not — high risk, volatile relationship, weak expectation — detail testing remains necessary, and the analytical procedure is corroboration, not a substitute.

### Combine analytical and detail procedures deliberately

The strongest substantive strategies often combine both: an analytical procedure to provide overall reasonableness and identify anomalies, plus detail testing of specific items or samples to provide direct evidence on the highest-risk transactions. Design the combination so each procedure addresses what the other cannot:

- analytical for coverage and reasonableness across the whole population;
- detail for precision and direct evidence on large, unusual, or high-risk items.

State how the combination provides sufficient appropriate evidence, rather than running both in parallel without considering their complementary roles.

### Use analytical procedures as risk assessment and as final review, with different standards

Analytical procedures serve three purposes, each with different design demands:

- **Risk assessment** (planning): looser expectations are acceptable; the goal is to identify risks and unusual patterns to direct further work.
- **Substantive** (evidence): precise, corroborated expectations as described above.
- **Final review** (overall conclusion): broad, reasonableness-focused, to confirm no inconsistencies remain.

Do not use a risk-assessment-quality analytical procedure as substantive evidence; the standards are different and substituting one for the other is a common error.

### Consider data quality and granularity

An analytical procedure is only as good as the data behind it. Confirm:

- the data is complete and accurate for the period (tied to the ledger, reconciled);
- the granularity is sufficient to detect a material difference (monthly data may reveal what annual data masks);
- the drivers are measured on a consistent basis (no changes in scope, definition, or classification that would distort the comparison).

Data quality problems silently undermine analytical procedures; a precise expectation built on incomplete data provides false assurance.

### Document the expectation, the comparison, the investigation, and the conclusion

For each substantive analytical procedure, document: the expectation and how it was developed (including the drivers and their corroboration), the recorded amount, the difference, the investigation of any difference and its corroboration, and the conclusion. Without this documentation, the procedure cannot be reviewed or relied upon. A line that says "analytical procedures performed; no significant differences" is not documentation of a substantive procedure.

## Common Traps

- **Using analytical procedures where the relationship is not predictable** (estimates, discretionary items, one-offs), expecting evidence they cannot provide.
- **Building expectations from uncorroborated management forecasts** or from last year's balance without adjustment, producing expectations too loose to detect material misstatement.
- **Setting precision too coarse** for the materiality and risk, so a materially wrong answer would still "pass."
- **Accepting narrative explanations of differences** without specific, quantified, corroborated causes.
- **Treating a risk-assessment analytical procedure as substantive evidence**, substituting a looser standard where a tighter one is required.
- **Using analytical procedures to replace detail testing in high-risk areas** where only detail evidence is sufficiently persuasive.
- **Running analytical and detail procedures in parallel** without designing them to complement each other.
- **Building precise expectations on incomplete or inconsistent data**, providing false assurance.
- **Documenting the procedure as "performed; no differences"** without the expectation, comparison, investigation, and conclusion.

## Self-Check

- Is an analytical procedure appropriate for this assertion — is the relationship predictable and stable enough to form a meaningful expectation?
- Is my expectation precise and based on independently corroborated drivers, not on management forecasts or unadjusted prior-year balances?
- Is the precision calibrated so that a material misstatement would produce an identifiable difference — could a materially wrong answer still pass?
- For any difference between expectation and recorded amount, did I investigate and explain it with specific, quantified, corroborated evidence, not narrative?
- Have I assessed whether the analytical procedure can replace or reduce detail testing, applying the demanding conditions, or whether it is corroboration only?
- Where I combine analytical and detail procedures, did I design each to address what the other cannot, and state how the combination provides sufficient appropriate evidence?
- Am I using the right standard for the purpose — substantive precision, not risk-assessment looseness, where the procedure provides evidence?
- Did I confirm the data is complete, accurate, and at sufficient granularity, with consistent measurement of drivers?
- Is the procedure documented — expectation, comparison, difference, investigation, corroboration, conclusion — so it can be reviewed and relied upon?
