# Non-Audit Service Self-Review

Independence requires that the auditor not be in a position of auditing its own work. When a firm provides non-audit services to an audit client, services such as bookkeeping, financial statement preparation, valuation, actuarial, internal audit outsourcing, human resources, or certain tax and advisory work, it may create a self-review threat in which the audit team is effectively reviewing outputs the firm itself produced. The recurring failure is treating non-aaudit services as categorically permissible or as a matter of fee revenue, without evaluating whether the specific service creates a self-review threat that cannot be reduced to acceptable levels. Independence rules, whether under the AICPA, SEC, PCAOB, or IESBA, prohibit or restrict specific services and require evaluation of threats for those that are permitted. The auditor must approach non-audit services as an independence question first and a service question second.

Use this skill when evaluating whether a non-audit service creates a self-review threat, when applying safeguards, and when deciding whether a service must be declined. The goal is independence preserved over revenue, with each service evaluated for its threat before it is accepted or continued.

## Core Rules

### Treat Non-Audit Services As An Independence Question First

The decision to provide a non-audit service is, before anything else, an independence decision. The question is not whether the firm is capable of the service or whether it is profitable, but whether providing it creates a threat to independence that cannot be reduced to acceptable levels.

Treat by:

- evaluating every proposed non-audit service against the applicable independence rules before acceptance;
- recognizing that certain services are prohibited outright, regardless of safeguards;
- recognizing that permitted services may still create threats that require safeguards or that may, in context, be unacceptable;
- documenting the evaluation and the basis for the accept-or-decline decision.

Treating services as a revenue question first risks accepting engagements that compromise independence.

### Identify Services That Create A Self-Review Threat

The core threat from non-audit services is self-review: the audit team reviewing subject matter that the firm produced. Certain services are particularly likely to create this threat.

Identify threats from:

- bookkeeping or accounting services that prepare records the audit will test;
- financial statement preparation services that produce the statements the audit will opine on;
- valuation and actuarial services whose results the audit will evaluate;
- internal audit outsourcing that has the firm performing controls or risk functions the audit will rely on;
- designing or implementing financial information systems that the audit will test;
- certain tax services, particularly advocacy and transactions affecting the financial statements;
- human resources services affecting the selection or placement of officers in financial reporting roles.

Each of these creates a self-review threat that must be evaluated and, in many cases, is prohibited.

### Apply The Prohibitions And Restrictions Of The Applicable Rules

Independence rules prohibit or restrict specific non-audit services. The applicable rules depend on the engagement: SEC and PCAOB rules for public companies, AICPA for private, IESBA for international, and GAGAS for governmental. The auditor must apply the rules that govern the specific engagement.

Apply by:

- identifying the applicable independence framework for the engagement;
- applying the prohibited-services list, declining any service that is prohibited;
- for permitted services, applying the restrictions and required safeguards;
- recognizing that rules differ across frameworks, and that the most restrictive applicable rule governs;
- documenting the framework applied and the conclusions.

Applying the wrong framework, or the least restrictive one, can leave the firm in violation.

### Apply Safeguards Where Permitted Services Create Threats

For services that are permitted but create threats, safeguards may reduce the threat to acceptable levels. Safeguards must be substantive, not nominal, and their effectiveness must be evaluated in context.

Apply safeguards by:

- using professionals outside the audit team, and outside the firm where required, to perform the service;
- ensuring the client, not the firm, makes all management decisions and takes responsibility for the service output;
- establishing policies and procedures that prevent the audit team from relying on its own work without independent review;
- requiring the client to review and accept responsibility for the service output;
- documenting the safeguards applied and the evaluation of their effectiveness.

Nominal safeguards, such as a disclaimer that the client is responsible, do not reduce a self-review threat to acceptable levels.

### Evaluate Tax Services For Self-Review And Advocacy Threats

Tax services warrant specific attention because they span a range from compliant preparation to advocacy. Tax return preparation is generally permitted; tax advocacy before tribunals, and tax services affecting amounts reflected in the financial statements, may create threats.

Evaluate by:

- distinguishing compliant tax services, such as return preparation, from advocacy and transactional services;
- assessing whether the tax service affects amounts or disclosures the audit will test;
- declining tax advocacy services where prohibited;
- ensuring tax personnel do not make management decisions on transactions affecting the statements;
- evaluating the cumulative effect of multiple tax services.

Tax services are not categorically permissible; their specific nature must be evaluated.

### Evaluate Contingent Fee And Fee Arrangements

Fee arrangements can create independence threats beyond the nature of the service. Contingent fees, in which the firm's fee depends on the outcome, create a self-interest threat. Certain fee arrangements are prohibited or restricted.

Evaluate by:

- declining contingent fee arrangements for services to audit clients where prohibited;
- assessing whether fee arrangements create undue dependence on a single client;
- evaluating the cumulative effect of non-audit fees relative to audit fees, where rules set thresholds;
- documenting fee arrangements and the independence evaluation.

Fee arrangements are part of the independence picture, not merely a commercial matter.

### Document The Evaluation And Safeguards

Every non-audit service evaluation must be documented, including the threat assessment, the applicable rules, the safeguards applied, and the basis for the accept-or-decline decision. Documentation supports the independence assertion and enables review.

Document by:

- recording the service proposed or provided and the applicable framework;
- documenting the threat assessment and whether the service is prohibited, permitted with safeguards, or permitted without safeguards;
- recording the safeguards applied, where applicable, and the evaluation of their effectiveness;
- retaining the documentation for review by engagement quality control, regulators, or inspectors.

Undocumented evaluations cannot be reviewed or defended, and may leave the firm unable to demonstrate independence.

## Common Traps

### Treating Non-Audit Services As A Revenue Question First

The first question is independence, not capability or fee. Treating it as revenue risks accepting engagements that compromise independence.

### Assuming Permitted Services Are Categorically Safe

Permitted services may still create threats requiring safeguards. Permission is not a blanket approval.

### Applying Nominal Safeguards

A disclaimer that the client is responsible does not reduce a self-review threat. Safeguards must be substantive.

### Applying The Wrong Or Least Restrictive Framework

Rules differ across frameworks. The applicable and most restrictive rule governs.

### Treating Tax Services As Categorically Permissible

Tax services span preparation to advocacy. Their specific nature must be evaluated.

### Ignoring Contingent Fees And Fee Dependence

Fee arrangements create self-interest threats. They are part of the independence picture.

### Failing To Document The Evaluation

Undocumented evaluations cannot be reviewed or defended. Documentation is essential.

## Self-Check

- Is every non-audit service evaluated as an independence question first, before capability or fee considerations?
- Are services that create self-review threats identified, including bookkeeping, financial statement preparation, valuation, internal audit outsourcing, system design, and certain tax and HR services?
- Are the prohibitions and restrictions of the applicable framework applied, with prohibited services declined?
- For permitted services that create threats, are substantive safeguards applied and their effectiveness evaluated?
- Are tax services evaluated for their specific nature, distinguishing preparation from advocacy and transactional services?
- Are fee arrangements, including contingent fees and fee dependence, evaluated for self-interest threats?
- Is every evaluation documented, including the threat assessment, applicable rules, safeguards, and the accept-or-decline decision?
- Could an independent reviewer confirm that independence was preserved over revenue and that no prohibited or threat-creating service was accepted without adequate safeguards?
- Is the evaluation free of treating services as categorically permissible or applying nominal safeguards?
- Is the most restrictive applicable framework applied, rather than the least restrictive?
