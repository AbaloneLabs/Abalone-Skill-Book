# Independence Safeguards And Network

Independence is not only an individual or engagement-level matter. Threats can arise anywhere in a firm's network, and an independence breach at one office, affiliate, or network firm can compromise the independence of the audit engagement. The recurring failure is treating independence as confined to the engagement team, without considering how threats at related entities, network firms, or shared service centers propagate to the audit. A firm's independence framework must operate at the firm and network level, with monitoring, safeguards, and communication that ensure threats are identified and addressed wherever they arise. The network concept, common control, and shared brand create the connection through which a threat at one entity affects another, and the auditor must understand and manage that connection.

Use this skill when evaluating network-level independence threats, when applying firm-wide and network safeguards, and when determining whether a threat at a network firm affects the audit engagement. The goal is an independence framework that operates across the firm and network, with threats identified and addressed wherever they arise.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Network Concept And Its Independence Implications

A network is a group of firms, or a firm and its affiliates, that operate under common control, share a brand, or present themselves to the public as connected. Independence rules generally treat a threat at a network firm as a threat to the audit firm, because the connection implies shared interests, common control, or a public perception of association.

Understand that:

- the network concept creates independence connections across firms that share control, brand, or presentation;
- a threat at a network firm, such as a prohibited non-audit service or financial interest, can compromise the audit firm's independence;
- the definition of network varies by framework, and the auditor must apply the applicable definition;
- the implications extend to shared service centers, common quality control, and cross-firm personnel.

Understanding the network concept prevents treating related firms as independent of each other for independence purposes.

### Identify Threats That Propagate Across The Network

Certain threats, when they arise at a network firm, propagate to the audit firm. The auditor must identify these threats and address them as if they arose at the audit firm itself.

Identify threats from:

- financial interests in the audit client held by personnel of a network firm;
- non-audit services provided to the audit client by a network firm;
- business relationships between a network firm and the audit client;
- employment of a network firm's former personnel by the audit client, in positions affecting the audit;
- family or personal relationships across network firms and the client;
- loans and guarantees between network firms and the client.

Each of these, if it arose at the audit firm, would be evaluated for independence; arising at a network firm, it must be evaluated with the same rigor.

### Apply Firm-Wide And Network-Level Safeguards

Independence safeguards must operate at the firm and network level, not only at the engagement team. These safeguards include policies, monitoring, communication, and structural separation.

Apply by:

- maintaining firm-wide and network-wide independence policies that apply to all firms and personnel in the network;
- implementing monitoring systems that track independence compliance across the network;
- establishing communication channels through which threats identified at any firm are reported and addressed;
- using structural separation, such as separate legal entities, offices, or reporting lines, where appropriate;
- ensuring shared service centers and common functions operate under independence controls.

Engagement-level safeguards alone are insufficient where threats can arise elsewhere in the network.

### Determine Whether A Network Firm Threat Affects The Audit

Not every event at a network firm affects the audit engagement. The auditor must determine whether a specific threat, given its nature and the network connection, compromises the audit firm's independence.

Determine by:

- assessing the nature of the threat and the closeness of the network connection;
- for threats that would compromise independence if they arose at the audit firm, treating them as compromising;
- for threats that are isolated or where the network connection is weak, evaluating whether safeguards reduce the threat to acceptable levels;
- where the threat cannot be reduced, addressing it through withdrawal, personnel changes, or other remediation.

A blanket assumption that network events do not affect the audit is inconsistent with the network concept.

### Address Breaches Promptly And Document Remediation

When an independence breach occurs at a network firm, it must be addressed promptly. The remediation depends on the nature of the breach and its effect on the audit.

Address by:

- identifying the breach, its source, and its scope across the network;
- removing the threatening interest, relationship, or service;
- where personnel are involved, removing them from positions affecting the audit;
- evaluating whether the breach affected the audit's conduct or conclusions;
- documenting the breach, the remediation, and the evaluation of its effect.

Unaddressed or undocumented breaches leave the independence assertion unsupported and may require reporting.

### Coordinate Independence Communication Across The Network

Independence communication must flow across the network, so that threats identified at any firm are known to the audit firm. Without effective communication, a threat at an affiliate can persist undetected.

Coordinate by:

- establishing channels for reporting independence threats across the network;
- requiring network firms to report threats affecting audit clients of other network firms;
- maintaining a central record of threats, breaches, and remediation across the network;
- ensuring the audit engagement team is informed of relevant threats identified elsewhere.

Without communication, the network safeguards cannot operate, and threats can persist undetected.

### Document Network-Level Independence Monitoring

Network-level independence monitoring must be documented, including the policies, the monitoring systems, the threats identified, and the remediation performed. Documentation supports the independence assertion and enables review.

Document by:

- maintaining the network independence policies and the framework applied;
- recording the monitoring activities performed and their scope;
- documenting threats identified at network firms, the evaluation of their effect, and the remediation;
- retaining the documentation for review by engagement quality control, regulators, or inspectors.

Undocumented network monitoring cannot be reviewed or defended.

## Common Traps

### Treating Independence As Engagement-Team Only

Threats can arise anywhere in the network. Treating independence as confined to the engagement team misses network-level threats.

### Assuming Network Events Do Not Affect The Audit

The network concept creates independence connections. Assuming events at affiliates do not affect the audit is inconsistent with the concept.

### Failing To Identify Propagating Threats

Financial interests, services, relationships, and employment across the network propagate. Failing to identify them leaves threats unaddressed.

### Relying On Engagement-Level Safeguards Alone

Engagement-level safeguards are insufficient where threats arise elsewhere. Firm-wide and network safeguards are required.

### Failing To Communicate Across The Network

Without communication, threats at affiliates persist undetected. Communication channels are essential.

### Leaving Breaches Unaddressed Or Undocumented

Breaches must be remediated and documented. Unaddressed breaches leave the independence assertion unsupported.

### Applying The Wrong Network Definition

The definition of network varies by framework. Applying the wrong definition can leave connections unaddressed.

## Self-Check

- Is the network concept understood, including how common control, shared brand, or public presentation create independence connections across firms?
- Are threats that propagate across the network identified, including financial interests, non-audit services, relationships, employment, and loans at network firms?
- Are firm-wide and network-level safeguards applied, including policies, monitoring, communication, and structural separation?
- For threats identified at network firms, is it determined whether they affect the audit engagement, with the same rigor as if they arose at the audit firm?
- Are breaches addressed promptly, with the threat removed, personnel reassigned where needed, and the effect on the audit evaluated?
- Are communication channels established so that threats at any network firm are reported to and known by the audit firm?
- Is network-level independence monitoring documented, including policies, monitoring, threats, and remediation?
- Could an independent reviewer confirm that independence operates across the firm and network, with threats addressed wherever they arise?
- Is the application free of treating independence as engagement-team only or assuming network events do not affect the audit?
- Is the applicable network definition applied, rather than a narrower or broader one that misstates the connections?
