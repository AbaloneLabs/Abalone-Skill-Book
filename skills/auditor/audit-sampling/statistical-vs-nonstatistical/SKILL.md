# Statistical vs Nonstatistical Sampling

The choice between statistical and nonstatistical sampling is not a technicality; it determines what conclusions can be drawn, how sample size is justified, how results are projected, and how sampling risk is quantified. Both approaches can provide sufficient appropriate evidence when properly applied, but they are not interchangeable. The recurring failure is to use a nonstatistical approach while drawing statistical-style conclusions (claiming a measured confidence level, or projecting results as if a random sample supported it), or to default to nonstatistical sampling out of habit where a statistical approach would provide stronger, more defensible evidence for a high-risk area.

## Core Rules

### Choose the approach based on the conclusion needed, not on familiarity

The deciding question is: what kind of conclusion does this procedure need to support?

- **Statistical sampling** allows the auditor to quantify sampling risk, project misstatements to the population with a defined confidence, and conclude at a stated level of assurance. It is appropriate where the population is large, where a quantified conclusion strengthens the audit response, and where random selection is feasible.
- **Nonstatistical sampling** reaches a conclusion through judgement rather than measured probability. It is appropriate where the population is small, where judgement-based selection of high-value or high-risk items is more efficient, where the cost of statistical design exceeds its benefit, or where random selection is impractical.

Neither is inherently superior; each is appropriate to different circumstances. Choose deliberately based on the needed conclusion, not by default.

### Apply the requirements of whichever approach is chosen, consistently

Each approach carries its own discipline:

- **Statistical sampling** requires: a defined population, random (probability-based) selection, a sample size derived from statistical formulae considering desired confidence, tolerable and expected misstatement (details) or tolerable and expected deviation rate (controls), and evaluation using statistical projection or acceptance tables.
- **Nonstatistical sampling** requires: a defined population, judgement-based selection (often targeted at high-value or high-risk items), a sample size informed by risk assessment but not formula-driven, and evaluation through judgement that considers both the sample results and their qualitative implications.

The cardinal error is mixing the two: selecting items judgementally but projecting as if randomly, or claiming a confidence level the selection method does not support. Apply one discipline end-to-end.

### Recognise when statistical sampling is advantageous or necessary

Statistical sampling is advantageous when:

- the population is large and homogeneous, making judgement-based coverage inefficient;
- the auditor needs a defensible, quantified conclusion (common for high-risk areas or contentious balances);
- the entity or a regulator is likely to challenge the sample, and a measured confidence level strengthens the response;
- projection of misstatement to the population is needed with a defined precision.

It is rarely necessary to use statistical sampling for every procedure; targeted nonstatistical sampling of key items is often more efficient. Reserve statistical sampling for the populations where its quantification adds real value.

### Recognise when nonstatistical sampling is more efficient

Nonstatistical sampling is often more efficient when:

- the population contains a few high-value or high-risk items that warrant individual examination regardless of sampling approach (key-item testing);
- judgement can target the items most likely to contain misstatement (recent additions, related-party, unusual entries);
- the population is small enough that a statistical approach adds design cost without proportional benefit;
- 100% examination of a stratum is feasible and removes sampling risk for that stratum.

Nonstatistical sampling is not a weaker option; it is a different option. Its efficiency comes from targeting, but it cannot support the quantified conclusions that statistical sampling can.

### Understand the projection and evaluation differences

The two approaches evaluate results differently, and confusing them invalidates conclusions:

- **Statistical projection**: misstatements found in a random sample are projected to the population using statistical methods, with a sampling allowance (precision) that reflects the risk the projection is wrong. The conclusion compares the projected misstatement plus allowance to tolerable misstatement.
- **Nonstatistical evaluation**: misstatements found are considered qualitatively and projected judgementally, with an allowance for sampling risk based on judgement rather than formula. The conclusion is that the population is or is not materially misstated, without a measured confidence.

Do not project nonstatistical sample results as if they were statistical. A judgemental sample of high-value items does not support a statistical projection to the remaining population; it supports a judgement about the population informed by those items.

### Treat sampling risk explicitly in both approaches

Sampling risk — the risk the sample is not representative — exists in both approaches. The difference is how it is handled:

- In statistical sampling, sampling risk is quantified and built into the sample size and evaluation (confidence level, precision).
- In nonstatistical sampling, sampling risk is addressed judgementally, by selecting more items in higher-risk areas, examining all key items, and applying a conservative allowance in evaluation.

Ignoring sampling risk in nonstatistical sampling (treating the sample as if it were the whole population) is a common error. Always state the allowance for sampling risk, even when it is judgemental.

### Document the approach and its rationale

Whichever approach is used, document:

- the population and sampling unit;
- the approach chosen and why (the needed conclusion, the population characteristics);
- the selection method (random, systematic, targeted, haphazard);
- the sample size and how it was determined;
- the evaluation method, including how sampling risk was addressed;
- the conclusion and how it supports the audit opinion.

Documentation is what makes the sampling defensible. A conclusion without documented approach and evaluation is an assertion, not evidence.

### Use 100% examination and targeted testing where they remove sampling risk

For some populations, sampling is unnecessary: examine 100% of items above a threshold, or all items in a high-risk stratum, and sampling risk is eliminated for those items. Combine 100% examination of key items with sampling of the residual population for efficiency. This hybrid is common and effective: it concentrates effort where it matters and uses sampling only for the homogeneous remainder.

## Common Traps

- **Mixing statistical and nonstatistical methods** — judgemental selection with statistical projection, or a claimed confidence level the selection does not support.
- **Defaulting to nonstatistical sampling out of habit** where statistical sampling would provide a stronger, more defensible conclusion for a high-risk population.
- **Projecting nonstatistical sample results as if they were statistical**, overstating the precision of the conclusion.
- **Ignoring sampling risk in nonstatistical sampling**, treating the sample as the population.
- **Choosing the approach without considering the conclusion needed**, leading to a method that cannot support the intended opinion.
- **Failing to document the approach, selection method, sample size rationale, and evaluation**, leaving the conclusion indefensible.
- **Overusing statistical sampling where targeted key-item testing would be more efficient**, adding design cost without proportional benefit.
- **Assuming statistical sampling is always stronger** — its strength is quantification, not coverage; a well-targeted nonstatistical sample can provide equivalent or better evidence for certain populations; **Forgetting that 100% examination of key items removes sampling risk** for those items, allowing a smaller, focused sample for the remainder

## Self-Check

- Did I choose between statistical and nonstatistical sampling based on the conclusion needed and the population characteristics, not by default?
- Did I apply the discipline of the chosen approach end-to-end, without mixing selection, sizing, or evaluation methods?
- Where I used statistical sampling, did I use random selection, formula-based sizing, and statistical evaluation with quantified sampling risk?
- Where I used nonstatistical sampling, did I document the judgement-based selection, the sizing rationale, and a judgemental allowance for sampling risk?
- Did I avoid projecting nonstatistical results as if they were statistical, and avoid claiming a confidence level the method does not support?
- Is the sampling approach documented — population, approach, selection method, sample size, evaluation, and conclusion — so the conclusion is defensible?
- Did I consider 100% examination of key items to remove sampling risk for the highest-value or highest-risk stratum, with sampling for the residual?
- Does the chosen approach match the risk: statistical for large, homogeneous, high-stakes populations where quantification adds value; nonstatistical for targeted, judgement-driven coverage?
