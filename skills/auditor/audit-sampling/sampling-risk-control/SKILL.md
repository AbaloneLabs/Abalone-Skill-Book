# Sampling Risk Control

Sampling risk is the risk that the auditor's conclusion, based on a sample, differs from the conclusion that would be reached if the entire population were tested. It is unavoidable in sampling, but it is controllable through sample design and must be accounted for in interpreting results. The recurring failure is treating sampling risk as a theoretical concept rather than a practical driver of sample size and conclusion, or confusing the two directions of sampling risk, incorrect acceptance and incorrect rejection, which have very different consequences. Incorrect acceptance, accepting a population that is in fact misstated, is the more dangerous error because it leads directly to an inappropriate audit opinion. Incorrect rejection, rejecting a population that is in fact fairly stated, leads to expanded testing but not to an inappropriate opinion. The auditor must design samples to limit incorrect acceptance to acceptable levels and must interpret results in light of both directions of risk.

Use this skill when designing samples to control sampling risk, when setting acceptable risk levels, and when interpreting sample results in light of sampling risk. The goal is sampling that limits the risk of inappropriate acceptance to a level consistent with the assessed risk, and conclusions that account for sampling risk rather than ignoring it.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish The Two Directions Of Sampling Risk

Sampling risk has two directions, and they have very different consequences. Confusing them leads to misdesigned samples and misinterpreted results.

Distinguish by:

- risk of incorrect acceptance: the risk that the sample supports a conclusion that the population is fairly stated when it is in fact materially misstated. This is the more dangerous error, as it leads to an inappropriate audit opinion.
- risk of incorrect rejection: the risk that the sample does not support a conclusion that the population is fairly stated when it is in fact fairly stated. This leads to expanded testing or adjustment but not to an inappropriate opinion.

Understanding the asymmetry focuses design and interpretation on the more dangerous direction.

### Set Acceptable Sampling Risk Based On Assessed Risk

The acceptable level of sampling risk, particularly the risk of incorrect acceptance, must be set based on the assessed risk of material misstatement. Higher assessed risk warrants a lower acceptable sampling risk and a larger sample.

Set by:

- linking acceptable sampling risk to the assessed risk of material misstatement for the relevant assertion;
- for higher-risk assertions, setting a lower acceptable risk of incorrect acceptance and a correspondingly larger sample;
- for lower-risk assertions, permitting a higher acceptable risk and a smaller sample;
- documenting the basis for the acceptable risk level.

Defaulting acceptable risk to a standard level, without linking it to assessed risk, misaligns the sample with the actual risk.

### Design Samples To Limit Sampling Risk To Acceptable Levels

Sample size is the primary tool for controlling sampling risk. Larger samples reduce sampling risk. The sample must be sized so that the risk of incorrect acceptance is at or below the acceptable level.

Design by:

- using the acceptable risk of incorrect acceptance, tolerable misstatement, expected misstatement, and population characteristics to determine sample size;
- for statistical sampling, using formulas or tables that link sample size to the risk parameters;
- for nonstatistical sampling, using judgment informed by the same parameters, recognizing that the risk is not quantified;
- verifying that the resulting sample size limits sampling risk to the acceptable level.

A sample sized without reference to the risk parameters does not control sampling risk.

### Account For Sampling Risk In Interpreting Results

Sampling risk must be accounted for when interpreting sample results. A sample with no exceptions does not prove the population is free of misstatement; it supports a conclusion only within the bounds of sampling risk. A sample with exceptions may still support a conclusion if the projected misstatement, adjusted for sampling risk, is below tolerable.

Account by:

- for statistical sampling, calculating the upper limit on misstatement, which includes projected misstatement plus an allowance for sampling risk;
- comparing the upper limit to tolerable misstatement, accepting the population only if the upper limit is below tolerable;
- for nonstatistical sampling, applying judgment to the results in light of the unquantified sampling risk;
- recognizing that zero exceptions in a small sample does not support a strong conclusion about the population.

Interpreting results without accounting for sampling risk overstates the support the sample provides.

### Expand The Sample Or Adjust When Results Exceed Tolerable

Where sample results, adjusted for sampling risk, exceed tolerable misstatement, the auditor must respond. The response may include expanding the sample, adjusting the population, or qualifying the conclusion.

Respond by:

- expanding the sample to reduce sampling risk and refine the projection;
- where misstatements are identified, requesting that management adjust the affected balances;
- where the adjusted results still exceed tolerable, qualifying the conclusion or performing additional procedures;
- documenting the response and the basis for the final conclusion.

Accepting a population whose upper limit exceeds tolerable, without response, fails to control sampling risk.

### Consider The Effect Of Anomalous And Projected Misstatements

Misstatements found in a sample may be anomalous, isolated to specific items, or indicative of a broader pattern. The evaluation must distinguish between the two and project accordingly.

Consider by:

- determining whether a misstatement is anomalous, affecting only the specific item, or indicative of a pattern;
- for anomalous misstatements, not projecting to the population but evaluating the specific item;
- for pattern misstatements, projecting to the population and adding the sampling risk allowance;
- documenting the treatment of each misstatement.

Treating pattern misstatements as anomalous understates the projected misstatement; treating anomalous misstatements as pattern overstates it.

### Distinguish Sampling Risk From Nonsampling Risk

Sampling risk arises from testing a sample rather than the full population. Nonsampling risk arises from factors unrelated to sample selection, such as using inappropriate procedures, misinterpreting evidence, or failing to recognize misstatements. Both must be managed, but they require different controls.

Distinguish by:

- recognizing that sampling risk is reduced by larger samples and appropriate selection methods;
- recognizing that nonsampling risk is reduced by appropriate procedures, competent execution, and effective supervision and review;
- not attributing all audit risk to sampling; nonsampling risk is often the larger component;
- managing both through sample design and through procedure design and quality control.

Confusing the two leads to misdirected effort, such as enlarging samples when the real problem is procedure design.

## Common Traps

### Confusing Incorrect Acceptance With Incorrect Rejection

The two directions have different consequences. Incorrect acceptance leads to inappropriate opinions; incorrect rejection leads to expanded work.

### Defaulting Acceptable Risk Without Linking To Assessed Risk

Acceptable sampling risk must be set based on assessed risk. Defaulting to a standard level misaligns the sample.

### Interpreting Results Without Accounting For Sampling Risk

Zero exceptions in a small sample does not prove the population is clean. Sampling risk must be accounted for in the conclusion.

### Accepting Populations Whose Upper Limit Exceeds Tolerable

Where the upper limit exceeds tolerable, the population must not be accepted without response. Expansion, adjustment, or qualification is required.

### Treating Pattern Misstatements As Anomalous

Pattern misstatements must be projected. Treating them as anomalous understates the projected misstatement.

### Confusing Sampling Risk With Nonsampling Risk

Sampling risk is reduced by larger samples; nonsampling risk by better procedures and execution. Confusing them misdirects effort.

### Assuming A Large Sample Eliminates Sampling Risk

A large sample reduces but does not eliminate sampling risk. The risk remains and must be accounted for.

## Self-Check

- Are the two directions of sampling risk, incorrect acceptance and incorrect rejection, distinguished, with design focused on the more dangerous incorrect acceptance?
- Is acceptable sampling risk set based on the assessed risk of material misstatement, with lower acceptable risk for higher-risk assertions?
- Is the sample sized to limit the risk of incorrect acceptance to the acceptable level, using the risk parameters rather than defaulted sizes?
- Are sample results interpreted with sampling risk accounted for, including the upper limit for statistical sampling?
- Is the population accepted only if the upper limit or risk-adjusted projection is below tolerable misstatement?
- Where results exceed tolerable, is the sample expanded, the population adjusted, or the conclusion qualified?
- Are anomalous and pattern misstatements distinguished, with projection applied appropriately to each?
- Is sampling risk distinguished from nonsampling risk, with both managed through their respective controls?
- Could an independent reviewer confirm that sampling risk is controlled to acceptable levels and accounted for in the conclusion?
- Is the interpretation free of assuming zero exceptions proves the population is clean, or that a large sample eliminates sampling risk?
