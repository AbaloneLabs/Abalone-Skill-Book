# Presentation Skills

An audit presentation is a translation: the engagement's significant results must be conveyed from the auditor, who lived the work, to an audience that did not, in a short time and without losing the substance. The failure modes are familiar. A presentation that recites everything bores the audience before the significant matters arrive. One that is too thin leaves the audience unconvinced the work was adequate. Dense slides invite the audience to read rather than listen. A presenter who is nervous, rushed, or unable to handle the room loses credibility regardless of the quality of the work behind them. Presentation skill is not decoration; it is the means by which sound audit work reaches and persuades the people who must act on it. A well-presented weak engagement can land better than a poorly presented strong one, which is precisely why the skill matters: the audience's understanding, not the auditor's, is what determines whether the findings lead to action.

Use this skill when preparing or delivering a verbal presentation of audit results, structuring content for an audience, using visuals, managing delivery, or ensuring the presentation conveys the significant results accurately. The goal is a presentation that holds attention, conveys the significant results faithfully, and leaves the audience with the understanding and conviction needed to act.

## Core Rules

### Structure The Presentation Around The Audience's Need

The audience approaches the presentation with questions: what did you find, what does it mean, what should we do. Structure should answer those questions in order, not follow the chronology of the fieldwork.

Structure by:

- opening with the overall conclusion or the most significant message;
- following with the significant findings, ranked by importance;
- providing the basis and context concisely for each;
- closing with what is needed from the audience, decisions, actions, or attention;
- holding detail for questions or backup.

A presentation structured to the audience's need keeps attention on what matters. One structured to the auditor's process loses the room.

### Lead With The Conclusion

Audiences engage most when they know the answer early. A presentation that builds suspense through methodology delays the message and tests patience. State the conclusion first, then support it.

Lead with:

- the overall conclusion or opinion;
- the single most significant finding or risk;
- a brief framing of scope so the conclusion is understood.

The audience can then listen to the supporting detail with the conclusion in mind, evaluating it rather than waiting for the punchline.

### Calibrate Depth And Pace To The Audience

Audiences vary in expertise and attention. A presentation must be accessible without being shallow, and paced so the audience can absorb each point before the next.

Calibrate by:

- explaining technical matters in plain terms, with depth available on request;
- providing context for non-specialists so they grasp significance;
- pacing slowly enough for absorption, especially on complex findings;
- reading the audience's engagement and adjusting pace in response;
- not overfilling the time, leaving room for questions.

A presentation pitched only at experts excludes part of the audience; one pitched only at generalists wastes the rest. Aim for accessibility with substance underneath.

### Use Visuals To Clarify, Not To Carry The Message

Visuals should make the significant messages clearer. When slides carry the whole message, the audience reads instead of listening, and the presenter becomes redundant.

Use visuals by:

- keeping each slide focused on one message;
- minimizing text so the audience listens to the presenter;
- using charts or tables only where they clarify comparison or trend;
- ensuring figures and ratings match the report exactly;
- being able to deliver the presentation without the slides if needed.

The presenter carries the message; the visuals support it. A presentation dependent on reading slides to the audience has failed its purpose.

### Practice Delivery To Convey Confidence And Accuracy

Delivery affects credibility as much as content. A presenter who is rushed, hesitant, or clearly reading notes signals uncertainty, even about sound conclusions. Practice builds the composure that lets the substance come through.

Practice by:

- rehearsing aloud, including transitions and timing;
- anticipating questions and practicing concise responses;
- checking that figures and statements match the report;
- managing filler words, pace, and volume;
- preparing for the likely failure modes, such as technology problems or hostile questions.

Confidence in delivery is earned through preparation, not performed. A prepared presenter lets the audience focus on the message rather than the messenger.

### Manage Nerves And The Room

Presenting significant findings to senior or skeptical audiences is inherently stressful, and unmanaged nerves undermine the message. The presenter must manage their own state and the room's dynamics.

Manage by:

- using preparation and practice to reduce anxiety;
- pausing rather than rushing when uncertain, to gather thought;
- maintaining composure under challenge, responding with evidence not emotion;
- engaging the audience through eye contact and addressing questions directly;
- not being defensive when findings are questioned, but standing behind supported conclusions.

Composure under pressure reinforces the credibility of the findings. Visible anxiety or defensiveness can undercut even sound work.

### Ensure Verbal Consistency With The Written Report

What is said verbally must match what is written. A presentation that softens findings or introduces conclusions not in the report creates inconsistency that undermines both.

Ensure consistency by:

- using the same severity ratings and characterizations as the report;
- not softening findings verbally to avoid discomfort;
- not introducing new conclusions or implications unsupported by the report;
- being ready to refer to the report for detail and to stand behind it;
- reconciling the presentation's examples and figures to the written record.

The audience should hear and read the same engagement. Drift between verbal and written creates doubt about which to believe.

### Close With Clear Next Steps

The presentation should end with what happens next, so the audience leaves knowing what is expected of them and what the auditor will do.

Close with:

- the decisions or actions requested from the audience;
- the timeline for the report's issuance and any follow-up;
- the remediation tracking process where applicable;
- an invitation for questions or further discussion.

A presentation that ends on the last finding without framing next steps leaves the audience uncertain what to do with what they heard.

## Common Traps

### Reciting Everything Instead Of Selecting

A presentation that covers all findings loses the audience before the significant ones. Select for significance.

### Building Suspense Instead Of Leading With The Conclusion

Methodology-first structure tests patience. Lead with the answer.

### Slides That Carry The Whole Message

Dense slides invite reading over listening. Use visuals to clarify, with the presenter carrying the message.

### Pitching Only At Experts Or Only At Generalists

The former excludes part of the audience; the latter wastes the rest. Calibrate for accessibility with depth.

### Unpracticed, Nervous Delivery

Hesitation and rushing signal uncertainty. Practice to convey composure and accuracy.

### Softening Findings Verbally

Verbal characterization weaker than the written report undermines the audit. Match severity.

### Defensive Responses To Challenge

Defensiveness undercuts credibility. Respond with evidence and composure.

### Ending Without Next Steps

A presentation that ends on the last finding leaves the audience uncertain what to do. Close with clear next steps.

## Self-Check

- Is the presentation structured around the audience's need, leading with the conclusion and significant findings?
- Does it open with the overall conclusion rather than building through methodology?
- Is the depth and pace calibrated to the audience, accessible to non-specialists with substance available?
- Do visuals clarify the message rather than carry it, with minimal text and figures matching the report?
- Has delivery been practiced, with timing, transitions, and anticipated questions rehearsed?
- Are nerves and room dynamics managed, with composure maintained under challenge?
- Is the verbal presentation consistent with the written report, using the same ratings and characterizations without softening?
- Does the presentation close with clear next steps, decisions requested, timeline, and follow-up?
- Could an audience member who did not read the report grasp the significant results and what is expected of them?
