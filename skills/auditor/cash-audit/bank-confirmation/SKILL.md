# Bank Confirmation

Bank confirmations are one of the most reliable forms of external evidence an auditor can obtain, which is exactly why they are a frequent target of manipulation. A confirmation that the auditor did not actually control, or that passed through the client's hands, is worth little more than a piece of paper management wrote itself. The recurring failures are not usually arithmetic; they are procedural. The client controls the address, intercepts the response, alters a figure, or quietly omits an account, and the auditor records a clean confirmation that never touched the bank. Done casually, the procedure produces false assurance and can mask kiting, undisclosed debt, pledged collateral, or window-dressing around the period end.

Use this skill when designing the bank confirmation process, deciding which accounts and relationships to confirm, controlling the mailing and return of forms, evaluating responses and exceptions, following up on non-responses, and judging when alternative procedures are sufficient. The goal is a confirmation process whose chain of custody is unbroken and whose scope covers every account and obligation that could be material.

## Core Rules

### Confirm Every Account And Relationship, Not Only The Obvious Ones

The confirmation population is defined by the existence of a banking relationship, not by whether the account appears on the general ledger at year-end. An account closed two days before the balance sheet date, a loan drawn down after period end but secured against period-end assets, or a zero-balance facility can all be highly material to the audit.

Identify and confirm:

- all demand, savings, and time deposit accounts in the entity's name;
- accounts in the name of subsidiaries, branches, or related entities;
- loan, overdraft, and credit facilities, drawn or undrawn;
- guarantee and letter-of-credit commitments issued by or for the entity;
- safe deposit boxes or custody arrangements;
- trust, escrow, or client-money accounts the entity controls;
- accounts used for payroll, merchant acquiring, or restricted funds;
- accounts disclosed as pledged, frozen, or subject to legal restriction.

Reconcile the list of accounts to the trial balance, the bank reconciliations, and any schedule of facilities. An account that exists operationally but is absent from the confirmation list is a red flag.

### Maintain Strict Control Over The Confirmation Cycle

The integrity of a confirmation depends on the auditor controlling the process from request to receipt. Any break in that control destroys the evidential value.

Control requirements:

- the auditor, not the client, prepares or approves the request and selects the recipient;
- the request is mailed using the auditor's own stationery and return envelope, addressed to a verified bank contact;
- the bank's address is independently verified against the bank's website, regulatory filings, or prior correspondence, not copied from a client-provided list;
- the client signs the request in the auditor's presence, but the auditor retains physical custody of the mailing;
- responses return directly to the auditor's office, never routed through the client;
- electronic confirmations use a reputable platform with authenticated responder access;
- any response received via the client, or bearing signs of having passed through the client, is treated as compromised.

Document who prepared, mailed, received, and opened each confirmation. If custody cannot be established, the confirmation is not reliable evidence.

### Use The Standard Confirmation Form And Tailor Where Needed

Standard bank confirmation forms are designed to elicit balances, loan balances, interest, and contingent liabilities in a structured way. They reduce the risk that the auditor forgets to ask about a guarantee or an off-balance-sheet exposure.

The standard form should request:

- account numbers, names, and currencies for each account;
- the balance and whether it is debit or credit at the confirmation date;
- loan principal, interest, and maturity terms;
- collateral pledged by or to the entity;
- contingent liabilities, guarantees, and letters of credit;
- any restrictions, set-off rights, or frozen balances;
- confirmation of whether the bank holds any assets in custody;
- the authorized signatory's name, title, and contact details.

Tailor the form when the engagement involves specialized products such as derivatives, structured deposits, or sweep facilities. A generic form that omits the entity's actual exposures is not adequate.

### Confirm Loans And Collateral Specifically

Loan confirmations are not cash confirmations with a debt line added. They must capture the full obligation and the security behind it, because undisclosed or misstated debt and collateral are common fraud vectors.

For each loan or facility, confirm:

- principal balance and currency at the confirmation date;
- interest rate, basis, and accrued interest;
- repayment schedule, maturity, and any breach of covenant;
- whether the facility is drawn, undrawn, or cancelled;
- collateral or security pledged, with descriptions and values;
- personal guarantees from owners or directors;
- cross-default or acceleration clauses;
- any reclassification, restructuring, or waiver in the period;
- balances of any related-party loans routed through the bank.

Compare the confirmed terms to the loan agreement and to management's debt schedule. A confirmation that disagrees with the contractual schedule requires resolution, not acceptance of the higher or lower figure.

### Follow Up On Non-Responses Actively

A bank that does not respond has not confirmed anything. Silence is not negative assurance. Non-responses must be chased, and if they persist, alternative procedures must fill the gap.

Follow-up actions:

- send a second request to a named contact after a defined interval;
- escalate through the bank's audit or compliance desk;
- confirm by phone with a documented call to a verified contact, followed by written confirmation;
- request the client's cooperation to release the bank from confidentiality if needed;
- set a deadline after which alternative procedures begin.

Do not let a non-response drift to the report date unresolved. The longer it sits, the harder it is to obtain evidence that is contemporaneous with the balance sheet date.

### Define And Perform Alternative Procedures When Confirmation Fails

When a confirmation is not returned, or returns unusable, alternative procedures must provide equivalent assurance about existence and completeness.

Acceptable alternative procedures include:

- subsequent clearing of the balance through the bank statement after period end;
- review of year-end bank statements and reconciliations for the specific account;
- tracing deposits and disbursements around the period end;
- examination of cut-off statements obtained directly from the bank;
- review of loan agreements, drawdown notices, and interest computations;
- confirmation of facilities through other documentary evidence;
- analytical review of interest expense against average balances.

Document why confirmation failed and why the alternative is sufficient. If alternatives cannot close the gap, consider a scope limitation.

### Evaluate Exceptions And Discrepancies Before Accepting Them

A response that disagrees with the books is a finding, not a nuisance. Discrepancies may reveal errors, fraud, timing differences, or undisclosed accounts.

Investigate each exception by:

- obtaining management's explanation and corroborating it independently;
- determining whether the difference is a timing item, a reconciling item, or a misstatement;
- checking whether the reconciling item clears shortly after period end;
- assessing whether the exception indicates an undisclosed account or obligation;
- considering whether the exception is isolated or part of a pattern;
- escalating items that suggest kiting, window-dressing, or undisclosed debt.

A reconciling item that never clears, or a balance that appears only at period end, may indicate intentional manipulation.

### Watch For Fraud Indicators In The Confirmation Process

Cash and debt are among the most frequently fraudulently reported balances. The confirmation process is both a detection tool and a fraud target.

Fraud risk indicators:

- the client insists on a specific bank contact or mailing address;
- confirmations are returned unusually quickly with no internal bank routing marks;
- the response format differs from the bank's standard form;
- a single signatory covers multiple unrelated banks;
- balances shift dramatically around the period end and reverse shortly after;
- transfers between accounts at the same bank mask overdrafts;
- the client offers to hand-carry requests or responses;
- electronic confirmations come from non-domain email addresses;
- accounts are opened or closed in a narrow window around year-end.

When indicators appear, increase skepticism, expand procedures, and consider whether the engagement team needs fraud specialists or a modified opinion.

### Time The Confirmation To The Right Date

Confirmations are normally obtained as of the balance sheet date, but interim or subsequent-date confirmations are used in some engagements. The date chosen affects what the evidence supports.

Timing considerations:

- a period-end confirmation supports existence at the balance sheet date directly;
- an interim confirmation requires roll-forward procedures to year-end;
- a subsequent-date confirmation supports subsequent events and may support existence with roll-back;
- cut-off statements obtained shortly after period end support completeness and reconciliation;
- confirmations sent too early or too late may not capture period-end balances.

Document the confirmation date and the procedures that bridge it to the balance sheet date.

## Common Traps

### Letting The Client Control Mailing Or Receipt

When the client prepares, addresses, or receives the confirmation, the evidence is worthless. Even an honest client creates the appearance of control that undermines the procedure. The auditor must retain custody end to end.

### Confirming Only General Ledger Accounts

Restricting confirmations to accounts on the trial balance misses closed accounts, undisclosed loans, and off-balance-sheet facilities. The population is the set of banking relationships, not the set of ledger lines.

### Accepting A Photocopy Or Email As A Response

A scanned image or unverified email can be fabricated. Only a response received directly from the bank, through controlled channels or an authenticated platform, is reliable. Treat anything routed through the client as compromised.

### Treating Silence As Negative Assurance

A bank that does not respond has confirmed nothing. Negative assurance is not a substitute for positive confirmation of cash and debt balances. Silence requires follow-up and alternative procedures.

### Ignoring Loan And Collateral Details

Confirming only the principal balance misses covenants, collateral, guarantees, and contingent liabilities. A clean-looking loan confirmation can hide pledged assets or a covenant breach that changes the audit conclusion.

### Accepting Unexplained Reconciling Items

A reconciling difference that never clears, or that appears only at period end, may be a misstatement or a fraud signal. Investigate until the difference is explained and corroborated, not merely noted.

### Missing Kiting And Window-Dressing Patterns

Transfers between banks near period end, with the credit recorded before the debit, can inflate balances. The confirmation alone does not reveal kiting; it must be combined with transfer schedule testing and interbank cutoff analysis.

### Using An Unverified Bank Address

An address provided by the client may route the confirmation to an accomplice or a private mailbox. Independently verify the bank's address before mailing.

## Self-Check

- Does the confirmation population include every account, loan, facility, and custody relationship, including closed, zero-balance, and related-entity accounts?
- Is the chain of custody documented from preparation through mailing to receipt, with the client never controlling the request or response?
- Has the bank's address and contact been independently verified rather than taken from a client-provided list?
- Does the form request balances, loans, collateral, guarantees, contingent liabilities, and restrictions, not only cash balances?
- Are loan confirmations compared to the loan agreements and debt schedule, with covenants and security captured?
- Is there a documented follow-up process for non-responses, with a deadline that triggers alternative procedures?
- When confirmation fails, do alternative procedures provide equivalent assurance about existence and completeness, and is the sufficiency documented?
- Are exceptions investigated, corroborated, and resolved rather than noted and ignored?
- Have fraud indicators in the confirmation process been considered, and are suspicious patterns escalated?
- Is the confirmation date appropriate, with roll-forward or roll-back procedures documented where it differs from the balance sheet date?; could another auditor reconstruct the confirmation process and conclude the evidence is reliable from the documentation alone?
