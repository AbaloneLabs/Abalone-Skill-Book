# Cash Count

Counting cash sounds simple, and that is precisely why it is done badly. A casual count, performed at a convenient time, witnessed by the custodian who knows it is coming, with unreimbursed vouchers folded into the total, produces a number that matches the ledger and proves almost nothing. The point of a cash count is not to verify arithmetic; it is to test whether the cash the entity says it holds actually exists, is under proper custody, and reconciles to the recorded balance without manipulation. Cash on hand is small relative to total assets in most entities, but it is the most liquid, most easily diverted, and most often used to conceal fraud through IOUs, padded petty cash, or commingled personal and business funds.

Use this skill when planning and performing cash counts, observing petty cash or till funds, reconciling physical currency to recorded balances, evaluating custody and reimbursement controls, or counting foreign currency holdings. The goal is a count that is unannounced, properly witnessed, completely documented, and genuinely reconciled to the books.

## Core Rules

### Plan The Count For Surprise And Completeness

A count scheduled in advance loses most of its evidential value, because the custodian has time to restore shortages, remove personal funds, or rebalance the box. Surprise is a control feature, not a discourtesy.

Plan around:

- which funds, locations, and custodians will be counted;
- whether all funds will be counted simultaneously to prevent borrowing between tills;
- the timing, chosen to catch normal operating balances without warning;
- whether to count at period end, at an interim date, or unannounced during fieldwork;
- who will count, who will witness, and who will record;
- how to handle funds the auditor cannot reach, such as remote sites or safes;
- whether to coordinate with multiple custodians so none can cover another's shortage.

If a count cannot be a surprise, document why and strengthen the reconciliation and subsequent procedures.

### Establish Custody Before Counting

Before any currency moves, establish who is responsible for the fund and have that person present. Counting a fund whose custodian is absent, or whose responsibility is unclear, creates disputes later if a shortage appears.

At the start:

- identify the custodian and confirm their responsibility in writing;
- require the custodian to be present throughout the count;
- have the custodian confirm the fund is intact and disclose any IOUs or personal items;
- record the time the fund was last reconciled and last reimbursed;
- note whether the fund is locked, who holds keys and combinations, and whether dual control applies;
- ensure the auditor never takes sole possession of the cash.

If the custodian leaves the room mid-count, stop and resume only when they return. The auditor should never be left alone with funds that are not theirs.

### Use A Structured Count Sheet

The count sheet is the primary record and must capture enough detail to be reproduced and reviewed. A total written on a sticky note is not documentation.

The count sheet should record:

- the fund name, location, and custodian;
- the count date and time;
- the auditor and witness performing the count;
- each denomination and quantity counted;
- coin, notes, and any foreign currency listed separately;
- unreimbursed vouchers, receipts, and IOUs listed individually with dates and amounts;
- personal funds or non-business items, if any;
- the total physical cash counted;
- the total of vouchers and IOUs;
- the combined total compared to the recorded imprest or ledger balance;
- the custodian's signature acknowledging the count and the figures.

Both the auditor and the custodian should sign. A count sheet signed only by the auditor is weaker evidence.

### Handle Unreimbursed And Disbursable Funds Correctly

Petty cash funds are usually maintained on an imprest basis: the total of cash plus unreimbursed vouchers equals the fixed fund amount. Misclassifying vouchers as cash, or ignoring them, distorts the count.

Apply:

- list every voucher, receipt, and IOU separately with payee, purpose, date, and amount;
- assess whether vouchers are genuine business expenses with supporting receipts;
- treat unsupported IOUs or personal advances as potential misappropriation;
- confirm the fund is at or near its imprest level, or explain the shortfall;
- check that vouchers are for the current reimbursement cycle and not stale;
- verify that large or unusual vouchers are properly approved;
- flag vouchers that pre-date the last reimbursement and should already have been replenished.

A fund full of old, unsupported vouchers is a warning sign regardless of whether the total reconciles.

### Reconcile The Count To The Recorded Balance

The count must be tied to the books, not left as a standalone number. A reconciled count tests existence and completeness together.

Reconcile by:

- comparing cash plus vouchers to the recorded petty cash or cash on hand balance;
- identifying and explaining any difference;
- determining whether differences are timing items, errors, or shortages;
- tracing any recorded entries between the last reconciliation and the count;
- confirming the recorded balance reflects all reimbursements and disbursements to date;
- testing whether disbursements recorded after the count are supported by vouchers in the box;
- evaluating whether shortages or overages are isolated or recurring.

A difference that is explained as a timing item must clear in the subsequent entries; one that lingers is a finding.

### Count Foreign Currency Properly

When the entity holds foreign currency, the count and the valuation both matter. Counting foreign notes at face value and ignoring the exchange rate understates or overstates the balance.

For foreign currency:

- count each currency separately and record the denomination breakdown;
- use the exchange rate appropriate to the measurement basis, spot at the count date for existence, period-end rate for balance sheet valuation;
- confirm the source and reliability of the rate used;
- assess whether the entity holds speculative or hedging currency and whether it is properly classified;
- check that foreign currency is retranslated consistently with accounting policy;
- consider whether physical holdings of foreign cash indicate unrecorded offshore activity.

Document the rates used and the valuation, not only the physical count.

### Observe Custody And Security Controls

The count is also an opportunity to evaluate whether cash is safeguarded against loss and theft. Weak custody turns a reconciled fund into an easy target.

Observe and document:

- whether the fund is kept in a locked box, safe, or drawer;
- who holds keys and combinations, and whether dual control exists;
- whether the custodian reconciles the fund regularly and retains records;
- whether access is restricted to authorized personnel;
- whether the entity insures cash on hand or relies on custody;
- whether funds are commingled with personal money or other entity accounts;
- whether the fund exceeds a prudent maximum and should be reimbursed more often.

Custody weaknesses should be reported to management even when the count itself is clean.

### Time The Count Relative To The Balance Sheet Date

Cash on hand is often counted at an interim date because period-end surprise counts are impractical. The date chosen determines what procedures are needed to bridge to year-end.

Timing considerations:

- a period-end count directly supports the balance sheet balance;
- an interim count requires roll-forward procedures to the balance sheet date;
- a subsequent count supports existence but requires roll-back for completeness;
- counts of high-risk funds should be as close to period end as feasible;
- document all intervening receipts and disbursements between the count and the balance sheet date.

State the count date and the bridging procedures clearly in the workpapers.

### Document So The Count Can Be Defended

A cash count is often challenged when a shortage is later discovered. The documentation must show that the count was proper, witnessed, and reconciled.

Record:

- the surprise nature of the count and any deviation with reasons;
- the custodian's presence and acknowledgment;
- the full denomination and voucher breakdown;
- the reconciliation to the ledger with explanation of differences;
- the custody and security observations;
- the signatures of auditor and custodian;
- the timing and any roll-forward or roll-back procedures.

A count that is not documented is, for evidential purposes, a count that did not happen.

## Common Traps

### Scheduling The Count In Advance

A count the custodian expects is a count that can be staged. Shortages are restored, personal funds removed, and the fund rebalanced before the auditor arrives. Preserve surprise wherever possible.

### Ignoring Unreimbursed Vouchers And IOUs

Treating the cash only and ignoring vouchers understates the fund and hides unsupported advances. The imprest total is cash plus vouchers, and every voucher must be scrutinized.

### Counting Without The Custodian Present

Counting a fund whose custodian is absent lets the custodian later disclaim any shortage. The custodian must be present and acknowledge the count and result.

### Accepting A Total Without A Denomination Breakdown

A single total cannot be reviewed or reproduced. Without a denomination and voucher breakdown, the workpaper cannot support the conclusion.

### Letting Shortages Be Explained Away Verbally

A shortage explained as a timing difference that never clears is a misappropriation until proven otherwise. Corroborate every explanation with subsequent entries or documentation.

### Forgetting Foreign Currency Valuation

Counting foreign notes at face value ignores the exchange rate and the accounting policy. Both the physical count and the retranslation must be documented.

### Counting Funds Sequentially Instead Of Simultaneously

When multiple funds exist, counting them one at a time lets a custodian borrow from one to cover another. Simultaneous counts prevent cross-covering.

### Treating The Count As A Mere Formality

A clean count does not excuse weak custody or stale vouchers. The count is also a control and fraud-detection procedure, and its observations should inform the findings.

## Self-Check

- Was the count planned for surprise, with simultaneous counts where multiple funds exist, and is any loss of surprise documented with reasons?
- Was the custodian identified, present throughout, and required to acknowledge the count and figures in writing?
- Does the count sheet record denominations, coin, foreign currency, vouchers, IOUs, and personal items separately, with signatures?
- Are unreimbursed vouchers scrutinized for genuineness, support, approval, and age, with unsupported IOUs flagged?
- Is the count reconciled to the recorded balance, with differences explained, corroborated, and evaluated as timing, error, or shortage?
- Is foreign currency counted separately and retranslated using an appropriate and documented rate?
- Are custody and security controls observed and documented, with weaknesses reported?
- Is the count date appropriate, with roll-forward or roll-back procedures bridging to the balance sheet date where needed?
- Is the documentation complete enough that another auditor could reproduce the count and defend the conclusion?
- Are shortages or overages investigated rather than accepted on verbal explanation, and are patterns across funds considered?
