# Transaction Disclosure

Related-party transactions are not prohibited, but they are suspect, and disclosure is what makes them tolerable to users. The danger is not that related parties exist; it is that their terms, balances, and commitments are understated, misclassified, or omitted so that the financial statements present an arm's-length picture that the underlying dealings do not support. Auditors often verify that some disclosure exists and stop there, when the real work is testing whether the disclosure is complete, whether the arm's-length assertion is supportable, and whether the terms are consistent with what actually happened. A disclosure that lists a transaction but hides its pricing, or that omits a guarantee, leaves users with a false sense of transparency.

Use this skill when evaluating the completeness and accuracy of related-party disclosures, testing arm's-length assertions, assessing materiality of related-party transactions, or verifying disclosure of balances, commitments, and guarantees. The goal is disclosure that a user could rely on to understand the true economics of the entity's related-party dealings.

## Core Rules

### Confirm The Population Before Testing Disclosure

Disclosure completeness cannot be tested against an incomplete transaction list. Begin from the corroborated related-party list and build the transaction population independently of management's disclosure.

Build the population from:

- the general ledger by counterparty, matched to the related-party list;
- board minutes and conflict-of-interest declarations;
- contracts, leases, and financing agreements with related parties;
- procurement and sales records filtered for related counterparties;
- guarantee and commitment registers;
- expense records for compensation, consulting, and reimbursements to related parties;
- intercompany and group cash movements;
- acquisitions, disposals, and restructurings involving related parties.

Reconcile the resulting population to the disclosed transactions. Any disclosed item not in the population, or any population item not disclosed, must be explained.

### Apply Materiality To Disclosure Decisions

Not every related-party transaction must be individually disclosed, but the materiality judgment for related parties is stricter than for arm's-length items because of the qualitative risk.

Consider material based on:

- quantitative size relative to overall materiality and to the relevant line item;
- qualitative significance, including any related-party transaction regardless of size when fraud or management self-interest is present;
- effect on key metrics, ratios, covenants, or trends;
- whether terms deviate from market in a way that distorts results;
- concentration of transactions with a single related party;
- transactions that change control, ownership, or capital structure;
- compensation and loans to key management personnel, which generally require disclosure by category.

Apply qualitative materiality liberally; a small self-dealing transaction can be highly material to users' assessment of governance.

### Test The Arm's-Length Assertion

Management often asserts that related-party transactions were conducted on arm's-length terms. This assertion is a representation that must be tested, not accepted, because it directly affects whether the reported amounts reflect economic reality.

Test by:

- comparing prices to independent market transactions or published benchmarks;
- obtaining and reviewing independent valuation reports where relied upon;
- comparing terms, interest rates, and maturities to those available from third parties;
- assessing the methodology and qualifications of any expert management relied on;
- evaluating whether the assertion is supported for each material transaction, not stated generically;
- considering whether the counterparty had a real alternative to the stated terms;
- testing whether the assertion is contradicted by the transaction's economics.

Where the arm's-length assertion cannot be supported, the disclosure must say so, and the financial impact must be considered.

### Verify Disclosure Of Terms And Pricing

Disclosure that names a related party and a transaction type, but omits the terms, is incomplete. Users need the pricing, the basis, and the amounts to assess the impact.

Verify disclosure of:

- the nature of the relationship and the transaction;
- the amounts of the transactions and outstanding balances;
- the pricing basis and whether terms differ from those available to third parties;
- commitments and contingent liabilities, including guarantees, given or received;
- provisions for doubtful related-party balances and the basis for them;
- amounts written off or forgiven in the period;
- significant terms that could affect future cash flows, such as long-dated contracts or renewals.

A disclosure that names the party but not the price is not adequate for a material transaction.

### Verify Compensation And Key Management Disclosure

Key management personnel compensation is a required disclosure by category and is frequently incomplete or misclassified. Test it specifically because it is both a related-party and a governance matter.

Verify disclosure of:

- short-term benefits, including salaries and bonuses;
- post-employment benefits and pension contributions;
- other long-term benefits;
- termination benefits;
- share-based payment;
- the total for key management personnel as a group;
- any loans or advances to key management personnel, with terms and balances.

Confirm the categories match the framework's required presentation and that the totals reconcile to the ledger.

### Test Completeness Of Balances, Commitments, And Guarantees

Outstanding balances and off-balance-sheet commitments with related parties are often the most consequential disclosures because they create ongoing exposure. Omitting a guarantee can conceal a material contingent liability.

Test completeness of:

- receivables from and payables to related parties at period end;
- loans and advances, with terms, interest, and security;
- guarantees provided to or by related parties;
- commitments to purchase from, sell to, or invest in related parties;
- contingent liabilities arising from related-party dealings;
- pledges of assets securing related-party obligations;
- intercompany balances that are not eliminated on consolidation.

Trace each to contracts and registers, and confirm the disclosure captures the full exposure, not only the period's transactions.

### Evaluate Whether Disclosure Enables Understanding

The standard for disclosure is not bare mention but whether a user can understand the effect of the transactions on the financial statements. Read the disclosure as a user would.

Assess whether the disclosure explains:

- why the party is related and the nature of the relationship;
- what was transacted and on what terms;
- the amounts involved in the period and the balances outstanding;
- the commitments and contingencies created;
- whether terms are arm's-length and the basis for that assertion;
- the impact on the entity's results, financial position, and cash flows;
- any changes from the prior period in the nature or scale of dealings.

If a reasonable user could not understand the impact from the disclosure, it is deficient even if technically present.

### Reconcile Disclosures Across Notes And Statements

Related-party information appears in several places: the related-party note, the compensation note, commitments and contingencies, segment reporting, and sometimes the equity or borrowings notes. Inconsistency across these is a red flag.

Reconcile:

- the related-party note to the key management compensation note;
- guarantees in the related-party note to the contingencies note;
- intercompany balances to the consolidation eliminations;
- related-party revenue and expense to segment disclosures where applicable;
- share-based payment to the equity note;
- related-party borrowings to the debt note.

Discrepancies between notes often indicate incomplete or inaccurate disclosure and must be resolved.

### Consider The Impact Of Non-Disclosure On The Opinion

When related-party disclosure is materially incomplete or misleading, the consequence is not a management letter comment; it is a scope limitation or a qualified or adverse opinion. Judge the severity against the framework and the users.

Evaluate:

- whether the omitted or misstated disclosure is material quantitatively or qualitatively;
- whether management has refused to provide information necessary to identify or quantify the transactions;
- whether the lack of disclosure prevents the financial statements from giving a true and fair view;
- whether the matter should be emphasized or a qualification is required;
- whether the matter must be reported to those charged with governance or to regulators.

Do not downgrade the seriousness of incomplete related-party disclosure because the amounts look small; the governance signal is often the dominant issue.

## Common Traps

### Testing Disclosure Existence Instead Of Completeness

Confirming that a related-party note exists is not the same as confirming it is complete. The omitted transaction or guarantee is the real risk.

### Accepting The Arm's-Length Assertion Without Evidence

Management's assertion that terms are arm's-length is a claim that must be tested. Accepting it generically, transaction by transaction, lets distorted pricing pass as transparent.

### Disclosing The Party But Not The Price

Naming a related party without the amounts, terms, or pricing leaves users unable to assess impact. Material transactions require material detail.

### Treating Related-Party Materiality As Purely Quantitative

Small self-dealing transactions can be qualitatively material because they signal governance failure or self-interest. Apply qualitative materiality aggressively in this area.

### Missing Guarantees And Commitments

Period transactions are usually disclosed; off-balance-sheet guarantees and commitments are frequently omitted. These create the largest hidden exposure.

### Inconsistent Disclosure Across Notes

The related-party note says one thing and the contingencies or compensation note says another. Reconciliation across notes catches these gaps.

### Ignoring Loans And Advances To Management

Loans to directors or key managers on favorable terms are both a related-party and a governance issue and generally require specific disclosure. Their omission is a common deficiency.

### Underreacting To Non-Disclosure

Incomplete related-party disclosure is not a minor presentation point. When material, it can drive a qualification, and the response must match the severity.

## Self-Check

- Was the related-party transaction population built independently from the ledger, contracts, and registers, then reconciled to management's disclosure?
- Has materiality been applied with qualitative rigor, recognizing that self-dealing and governance-signaling transactions can be material regardless of size?
- Has the arm's-length assertion been tested against market evidence or valuation, with unsupported assertions identified?
- Does the disclosure capture terms, pricing, amounts, balances, commitments, and guarantees, not only the names of the parties?
- Is key management personnel compensation disclosed by the required categories, reconciled to the ledger?
- Are off-balance-sheet commitments, guarantees, and contingent liabilities with related parties fully disclosed and traced to supporting agreements?
- Would a reasonable user understand the nature, terms, and impact of the transactions from the disclosure as written?
- Are related-party disclosures across all notes consistent and reconciled to each other?
- Has the impact of any non-disclosure or misstatement on the audit opinion been evaluated, with escalation where warranted?
- Could another auditor review the disclosure testing and conclude the related-party note is complete, accurate, and understandable?
