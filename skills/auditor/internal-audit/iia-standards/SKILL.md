# IIA Standards

The Institute of Internal Auditors' Global Internal Audit Standards, within the International Professional Practices Framework, define the mandatory requirements for the professional practice of internal auditing. Conformance with these standards is what gives an internal audit activity its claim to professionalism and what allows its assurance to be relied upon. The standards cover the purpose, ethics, positioning, resources, operations, and communication of the activity, and they establish the requirements against which the activity is assessed. The challenge is that conformance is not a one-time achievement; it is an ongoing state that must be maintained as the activity, the organization, and the standards themselves evolve. An activity that conforms in form but not in substance, that claims conformance without genuinely meeting the requirements, or that is unaware of where it falls short, provides assurance that rests on an unsupported foundation.

Use this skill when applying the IIA standards, assessing conformance, designing or improving an internal audit activity, or evaluating whether practice meets mandatory requirements. The goal is to ensure the activity conforms to the standards in substance, not merely in assertion, and uses the standards as the basis for continuous improvement.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Framework's Structure And Authority

The IPPF distinguishes mandatory guidance, the Standards and the Definition of Internal Auditing, from recommended guidance that supports but does not mandate practice. The Global Internal Audit Standards are organized into domains covering the purpose of internal auditing, ethics and professionalism, positioning the function, managing the function, performing engagements, and communicating results. Understanding this structure is the foundation for applying the standards correctly.

Understand the framework by:

- recognizing that the Standards are mandatory and that conformance is required, not optional;
- distinguishing the Standards' mandatory requirements from recommended guidance that informs but does not bind;
- familiarizing yourself with the domains and the specific requirements within each;
- recognizing that the Code of Ethics underpins the Standards and is itself mandatory.

Treating recommended guidance as mandatory over-burdens the activity; treating mandatory requirements as optional undermines conformance. Know which is which.

### Establish Independence And Positioning Per The Standards

The Standards require that the internal audit activity be positioned to achieve its purpose, with independence supported by the reporting line, the charter, and the board's oversight. Independence is both organizational, the position that frees the activity from interference, and individual, the objectivity of each internal auditor. Positioning failures are among the most common conformance gaps.

Establish positioning by:

- ensuring the chief audit executive reports functionally to the board or audit committee, with appropriate access and accountability;
- confirming the internal audit charter, approved by the board, defines the purpose, authority, responsibility, and independence;
- protecting the chief audit executive's appointment, removal, and compensation from management influence;
- ensuring the activity has unrestricted access to the board and audit committee;
- assessing individual objectivity and managing threats per the standards.

Independence that exists on paper but not in practice, such as a reporting line that is functional in name only, does not meet the standards. Assess substance, not form.

### Resource The Activity To Meet Its Mandate

The Standards require that the internal audit activity be resourced to achieve its purpose, with competent personnel, sufficient numbers, and appropriate development. Under-resourcing, whether in headcount, skills, or development, is a conformance gap that directly affects the activity's ability to deliver assurance.

Resource appropriately by:

- assessing the skills and numbers required to deliver the audit plan against the organization's risk profile;
- identifying competency gaps and addressing them through hiring, training, or co-sourcing;
- providing continuing professional development to maintain and build competence;
- ensuring the chief audit executive has authority over resource decisions;
- recognizing when the plan exceeds the activity's resourced capacity and adjusting scope or seeking additional resources.

An activity that approves a plan it cannot competently deliver sets itself up for conformance failure and weak engagements.

### Perform Engagements To Professional Standards

The Standards set requirements for how engagements are performed, including planning, risk assessment, objectives and scope, procedures, evidence, conclusions, and communication. Each engagement must conform to these requirements for its results to carry the authority of professional practice.

Perform to standards by:

- planning each engagement based on a risk assessment of the area under review;
- defining objectives, scope, and criteria that align with the risk assessment;
- performing procedures sufficient to achieve the objectives, with evidence to support conclusions;
- communicating results that include objectives, scope, findings, conclusions, and recommendations;
- obtaining management's responses and tracking them to resolution;
- documenting the engagement in working papers that support the conclusions.

Engagements performed without risk-based planning, with insufficient evidence, or with conclusions not supported by the work, do not conform regardless of how the report is framed.

### Communicate Results Effectively And Without Filter

The Standards require that the activity communicate results to the appropriate levels, including the board, without management filtering or alteration that would mislead. The chief audit executive must have direct access to the board and must report significant findings, risks, and the activity's performance.

Communicate per standards by:

- ensuring the chief audit executive can communicate directly with the board and audit committee;
- reporting significant findings, control issues, and risks to governance without management filtering;
- including in governance reporting the activity's plan, performance, conformance, and improvement actions;
- not allowing management to soften, delay, or suppress significant findings before they reach governance;
- documenting any attempts to restrict communication and escalating appropriately.

A reporting line that requires management approval before findings reach the board does not meet the standards. Communication independence is mandatory.

### Assess Conformance Through Quality Assurance

The Standards require that the activity assess its conformance through an internal assessment and, at least every five years, an external assessment by a qualified, independent reviewer. The external assessment is the authoritative test of conformance, and its results must be communicated to the board.

Assess conformance by:

- conducting ongoing internal monitoring of conformance with the standards;
- commissioning an external assessment at least every five years, by a qualified independent assessor;
- communicating the external assessment results, including any non-conformance, to the board;
- developing and tracking corrective action for identified gaps;
- not claiming full conformance where gaps exist, and disclosing partial conformance where applicable.

An activity that has never undergone external assessment, or that claims conformance without addressing known gaps, does not meet the standards' quality assurance requirement.

### Use The Standards As A Basis For Continuous Improvement

Conformance is not a static state. The standards evolve, the organization changes, and the activity's practice develops. The standards should be used not only as a pass-fail test but as a framework for continuous improvement, identifying where practice can strengthen and tracking progress over time.

Use the standards for improvement by:

- reviewing practice against the standards periodically, not only at external assessment time;
- identifying areas where the activity exceeds the minimum and where it falls short;
- benchmarking against leading practice and peer activities;
- incorporating standards updates and emerging guidance into methodology and practice;
- reporting improvement progress to governance.

An activity that treats the standards as a hurdle to clear every five years, rather than a framework to practice within continuously, misses their value as a driver of professional development.

## Common Traps

### Conformance In Form But Not Substance

Positioning, charter, and reporting that meet the standards on paper but not in practice, such as a reporting line that is functional in name only, do not constitute conformance. Assess substance.

### Under-Resourcing Relative To The Plan

Approving a plan that exceeds the activity's resourced capacity, in headcount or skills, sets up conformance failure and weak engagements. Resource to the mandate.

### Engagements Without Risk-Based Planning

Performing engagements procedurally, without risk assessment driving objectives and procedures, does not conform regardless of the report's framing. Plan based on risk.

### Management Filtering Of Communication

Reporting lines or practices that allow management to soften or suppress findings before they reach governance breach the communication requirements. Protect direct access.

### No External Assessment

Failing to commission the required external assessment, or claiming conformance without it, leaves the activity's conformance unverified. Assess externally at least every five years.

### Claiming Full Conformance With Known Gaps

Asserting full conformance while aware of areas that fall short misleads governance and the users of assurance. Disclose partial conformance and corrective action.

### Standards As Hurdle, Not Framework

Treating the standards as a periodic test rather than a continuous practice framework misses their improvement value. Use them ongoing.

## Self-Check

- Is the framework's structure and authority understood, with mandatory requirements distinguished from recommended guidance and the Code of Ethics recognized as foundational?
- Is the activity positioned for independence per the standards, with functional reporting to the board, an approved charter, protected appointment and compensation, and unrestricted board access, in substance not only form?
- Is the activity resourced, in competent personnel, numbers, and development, to deliver its plan against the organization's risk profile?
- Are engagements performed to professional standards, with risk-based planning, defined objectives and scope, sufficient procedures and evidence, supported conclusions, and complete communication?
- Are results communicated to governance without management filtering, with the chief audit executive having direct board access and significant findings reaching those charged with governance?
- Is conformance assessed through internal monitoring and external assessment at least every five years, with results communicated to the board?
- Are conformance gaps disclosed honestly, with corrective action developed and tracked, rather than claiming full conformance where it does not exist?
- Are the standards used as a framework for continuous improvement, with periodic self-review and incorporation of updates, rather than only as a periodic hurdle?
- Is individual objectivity assessed and managed per the standards, with threats identified and safeguarded?
- Could an external assessor confirm that the activity conforms to the standards in substance, and where it does not, that the gaps are disclosed and being addressed?
