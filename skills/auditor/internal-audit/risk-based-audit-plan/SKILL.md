# Risk-Based Internal Audit Plan

Internal audit resources are finite, and the demands on them are not. A risk-based audit plan is the mechanism by which internal audit allocates its limited resources to the engagements that matter most to the organization. The recurring failure is building the plan from last year's plan, adding or removing engagements incrementally, without a fresh assessment of the current risk landscape, or treating the plan as a list of audits to perform rather than a strategic allocation of coverage. A risk-based plan is not a catalog of possible audits; it is a deliberate prioritization that aligns internal audit coverage with the organization's objectives, risks, and stakeholder needs. Getting the plan wrong means the most significant risks go unaudited while resources are spent on lower-value, routine coverage.

Use this skill when developing or reviewing a risk-based internal audit plan, when prioritizing engagements, and when allocating resources across the audit portfolio. The goal is a plan that directs internal audit resources to the engagements that address the organization's most significant risks and strategic priorities.

## Core Rules

### Base The Plan On A Current Risk Assessment

A risk-based plan must be grounded in a current assessment of the organization's risks. Using last year's plan, or a static list of auditable entities, without a fresh risk assessment, leaves the plan misaligned with the current risk landscape.

Base by:

- performing or updating a risk assessment that identifies the organization's significant risks;
- considering the organization's objectives, strategy, and the risks that could prevent their achievement;
- incorporating input from management, the audit committee, and other assurance providers;
- reassessing the risk landscape at least annually and updating the plan as conditions change.

A plan built on a stale risk assessment directs resources to last year's risks, not today's.

### Align With Organizational Objectives And Strategy

Internal audit exists to help the organization achieve its objectives. The plan must align with the organization's strategy, not merely with its processes or compliance obligations.

Align by:

- understanding the organization's strategic objectives and the risks to their achievement;
- prioritizing engagements that address risks to strategy execution, not only operational or compliance risks;
- considering the information, governance, and reporting needs of senior management and the board;
- ensuring the plan reflects the organization's current priorities, including new initiatives, transformations, and changes.

A plan that audits processes without connecting them to objectives misses the strategic value internal audit can provide.

### Prioritize Engagements Based On Risk

Not all engagements are equally valuable. The plan must prioritize engagements based on the significance of the risks they address, the adequacy of other assurance, and the potential for internal audit to add value.

Prioritize by:

- ranking potential engagements by the significance of the risk addressed, considering likelihood and impact;
- considering the adequacy of management's controls and other assurance over each risk;
- factoring in the potential for fraud, noncompliance, or reputational impact;
- recognizing that some risks warrant recurring coverage while others may be one-time or periodic;
- being willing to defer or drop lower-priority engagements to free resources for higher-priority ones.

Prioritization requires tradeoffs; the plan cannot cover everything, and attempting to do so dilutes focus.

### Consider The Assurance Map And Avoid Duplication

Internal audit is one of several assurance providers. The plan should consider the assurance provided by management, external audit, regulators, and other functions, to avoid duplication and to focus internal audit where its independence and perspective add the most value.

Consider by:

- mapping the assurance provided across the significant risks;
- identifying where internal audit's coverage complements, rather than duplicates, other assurance;
- coordinating with external audit to leverage work and avoid redundant coverage;
- focusing internal audit on areas where its independence, objectivity, or whole-organization perspective is uniquely valuable.

Ignoring the assurance map leads to duplicated effort and missed coverage where internal audit is uniquely positioned.

### Allocate Resources Realistically

The plan must be executable with the available resources. Over-committing the plan to more engagements than the team can deliver, or to engagements requiring expertise the team lacks, sets the plan up for failure.

Allocate by:

- assessing the team's capacity, skills, and availability realistically;
- matching engagements to the team's competencies, or planning for specialist or co-sourced support where needed;
- building in flexibility for unforeseen demands, investigations, or requests from the audit committee;
- recognizing that a smaller number of well-executed engagements is more valuable than a larger number of rushed ones.

An over-committed plan produces shallow engagements that fail to deliver the assurance they promise.

### Build In Flexibility And Responsiveness

The risk landscape changes during the year, and the plan must be able to respond. A rigid plan that cannot accommodate new risks, emerging issues, or requests from the audit committee becomes obsolete.

Build by:

- reserving capacity for unforeseen demands and emerging risks;
- establishing a process for updating the plan during the year as conditions change;
- engaging with the audit committee and management to identify emerging priorities;
- being willing to reprioritize or defer planned engagements when higher-priority needs arise.

A rigid plan, maintained despite changed conditions, directs resources away from the risks that matter most at the time.

### Communicate And Obtain Buy-In

The plan must be communicated to, and accepted by, the audit committee and senior management. Their buy-in ensures the plan reflects the organization's priorities and that internal audit has the mandate to execute it.

Communicate by:

- presenting the plan to the audit committee, with the risk assessment and prioritization rationale;
- explaining the tradeoffs, including what is not being audited and why;
- obtaining formal approval of the plan from the audit committee;
- keeping the audit committee informed of plan changes during the year.

A plan executed without audit committee buy-in lacks the mandate and support needed for effectiveness.

## Common Traps

### Building The Plan From Last Year's Plan

A plan built incrementally from the prior year reflects last year's risks. A fresh risk assessment is required.

### Treating The Plan As A List Rather Than A Prioritization

The plan is a strategic allocation of finite resources. Treating it as a catalog of audits dilutes focus.

### Ignoring The Assurance Map

Other assurance providers cover some risks. Ignoring this leads to duplication and missed unique coverage.

### Over-Committing Resources

An over-committed plan produces shallow engagements. Resources must be allocated realistically.

### Maintaining A Rigid Plan Despite Change

The risk landscape changes. A rigid plan directs resources away from current priorities.

### Auditing Processes Without Connecting To Objectives

Processes matter insofar as they affect objectives. Auditing processes without the objective connection misses strategic value.

### Executing Without Audit Committee Buy-In

The plan needs the audit committee's mandate. Executing without buy-in undermines effectiveness.

## Self-Check

- Is the plan based on a current risk assessment that identifies the organization's significant risks, not last year's plan?
- Does the plan align with the organization's strategic objectives and the risks to their achievement?
- Are engagements prioritized by the significance of the risk addressed, with tradeoffs made to focus on higher-priority coverage?
- Is the assurance map considered, with internal audit focused where its independence and perspective add the most value and duplication avoided?
- Are resources allocated realistically, matching engagements to capacity and competencies, with specialist support planned where needed?
- Is flexibility built in, with capacity reserved for emerging risks and a process for updating the plan during the year?
- Is the plan communicated to and approved by the audit committee, with tradeoffs explained?
- Could an independent reviewer confirm that the plan directs internal audit resources to the organization's most significant risks and strategic priorities?
- Is the application free of building from last year's plan, treating the plan as a list, or ignoring the assurance map?
- Are resources allocated realistically, with a smaller number of well-executed engagements preferred over a larger number of rushed ones?
