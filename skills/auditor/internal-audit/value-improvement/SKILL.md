# Value Improvement

Internal audit's primary purpose is assurance, but it is also expected to add value by identifying opportunities for improvement and offering constructive recommendations. This dual role is a strength when managed well: the auditor's broad view of the organization surfaces improvements that siloed functions miss. It becomes a weakness when value-add is pursued at the expense of assurance, when recommendations are generic platitudes that add no real value, or when the auditor crosses from advising into making decisions that compromise independence. Value improvement is not about being liked or about padding the report with suggestions; it is about using the insight gained during assurance to identify specific, practical improvements that management can act on, while preserving the boundary that keeps the auditor advisory rather than operational.

Use this skill when developing improvement recommendations, communicating value, balancing assurance and advisory input, or ensuring recommendations are practical and do not impair independence. The goal is to provide genuine, actionable improvement input without compromising the assurance role.

## Core Rules

### Ground Recommendations In What The Engagement Found

Valuable recommendations emerge from the evidence and observations of the engagement, not from generic best practice trotted out for every client. A recommendation that the entity should "strengthen controls" or "improve documentation" adds nothing because it is not connected to the specific condition found. The most valuable recommendations address the specific root causes identified during the engagement.

Ground recommendations by:

- tying each recommendation to a specific finding, condition, or root cause documented in the engagement;
- explaining how the recommendation addresses the cause, not merely the symptom;
- drawing on the auditor's observation of what works elsewhere in the organization or in comparable entities;
- avoiding boilerplate suggestions that could appear in any report regardless of the engagement.

A recommendation that could be copied into any report is not a recommendation; it is filler. Specificity is what makes recommendations valuable.

### Make Recommendations Specific And Actionable

A recommendation that management cannot act on adds no value and erodes the credibility of the report. Recommendations should be specific enough that management understands what is being suggested, practical enough that it can be implemented, and clear enough that progress can be measured.

Make recommendations actionable by:

- stating specifically what improvement is suggested, not merely that something should be improved;
- identifying the objective the recommendation aims to achieve, so management can consider alternative approaches;
- avoiding prescriptive detail that dictates the solution, which crosses into management's decision;
- considering feasibility, including cost, effort, and organizational constraints;
- framing the recommendation so management can assess it and decide how to proceed.

Recommendations that are too vague to act on, or too prescriptive to leave room for management judgment, both fail. The aim is a clear suggestion that management can evaluate and own the implementation of.

### Preserve The Boundary Between Advising And Deciding

Value improvement is advisory; it does not make management decisions. When the auditor designs the solution, specifies the system, or dictates the process, the function has crossed into management and compromised its independence, creating a self-review threat for any future assurance on the implemented solution.

Preserve the boundary by:

- framing recommendations as suggestions for management to evaluate, not as decisions to be implemented;
- leaving the design and implementation of solutions to management;
- declining requests to take operational responsibility, even temporarily, for processes or controls;
- recognizing when a value-add request is actually a request to perform a management function;
- documenting the advisory nature of the input.

The auditor who implements cannot independently assure. The boundary is what preserves the capacity for future assurance.

### Prioritize Recommendations By Impact And Feasibility

Not all recommendations carry equal value, and presenting them as an undifferentiated list overwhelms management and obscures the most important. Recommendations should be prioritized so management can focus on the highest-impact, most feasible improvements first.

Prioritize by:

- assessing the risk reduction or benefit each recommendation offers;
- considering the effort, cost, and disruption of implementation;
- distinguishing quick wins from larger initiatives that require sustained effort;
- presenting recommendations in a way that highlights priority, such as by risk addressed or by urgency;
- recognizing that some recommendations, while valid, may not be worth the cost and letting management make that judgment.

A prioritized set of recommendations respects management's finite capacity and directs attention where it adds the most value.

### Avoid Recommendation Overload

Reports that contain dozens of recommendations, many minor, dilute attention from the significant improvements and create the impression that everything is wrong. Recommendation overload also creates a remediation burden that management cannot meet, leading to partial or cosmetic action.

Avoid overload by:

- focusing recommendations on significant findings and root causes, not on every minor observation;
- consolidating related recommendations rather than listing each separately;
- distinguishing recommendations from observations that do not require action;
- resisting the temptation to demonstrate thoroughness by quantity of recommendations;
- recognizing that a few well-chosen recommendations add more value than many marginal ones.

Quality and focus matter more than quantity. A report with five high-value recommendations is more useful than one with fifty of varying merit.

### Communicate Value Without Compromising The Assurance Message

Value-add should complement, not obscure, the assurance conclusions. A report that leads with praise and recommendations can soften the impact of significant findings, leaving governance with an unduly favorable impression. The assurance message, including significant deficiencies and risks, must be clear and not buried under constructive suggestions.

Balance communication by:

- presenting assurance conclusions and findings clearly, before or alongside recommendations;
- not using value-add language to soften or qualify significant findings;
- ensuring governance understands the assurance picture, not only the improvement opportunities;
- recognizing that value-add is secondary to the independent assessment, not a substitute for it.

Value improvement enhances the report; it does not replace the assurance that is the engagement's primary deliverable.

### Let Management Own The Decision To Implement

Recommendations are suggestions; the decision to implement, and the approach, belong to management. The auditor should not press for implementation of every recommendation, should accept management's reasoned decision not to implement some, and should track the risk where recommendations are declined, not the non-implementation itself.

Respect management ownership by:

- presenting recommendations for management's evaluation, not as demands;
- accepting management's reasoned decision to decline or modify a recommendation, while documenting the residual risk;
- tracking remediation of findings, which management has accepted, rather than pressing for every suggestion;
- recognizing that management may have information or constraints that justify a different approach.

The auditor's role ends at the recommendation; management's begins at the decision. Forcing implementation crosses the advisory boundary and undermines the relationship.

## Common Traps

### Generic Boilerplate Recommendations

Suggestions that could appear in any report add no value and signal that the engagement produced no specific insight. Ground recommendations in findings.

### Over-Prescriptive Recommendations

Dictating the solution crosses into management and creates self-review threats. Advise, do not decide.

### Recommendation Overload

Dozens of minor recommendations dilute focus and overwhelm remediation capacity. Prioritize and consolidate.

### Value-Add Softening Assurance

Leading with praise and suggestions, obscuring significant findings, misleads governance about the assurance picture. Keep assurance clear.

### Pressing For Implementation

Treating recommendations as demands, rather than suggestions for management's decision, crosses the advisory boundary. Let management own the decision.

### Recommendations Disconnected From Root Cause

Suggestions that address symptoms, not causes, produce cosmetic fixes that do not prevent recurrence. Tie recommendations to causes.

### Quantity Over Quality

Demonstrating thoroughness through the number of recommendations, rather than their value, produces marginal suggestions. Focus on impact.

## Self-Check

- Are recommendations grounded in specific findings and root causes documented in the engagement, rather than generic best practice?
- Is each recommendation specific and actionable, stating the improvement and objective while leaving solution design to management?
- Is the boundary between advising and deciding preserved, with the auditor not designing, implementing, or taking operational responsibility?
- Are recommendations prioritized by impact and feasibility, so management can focus on the highest-value improvements?
- Is recommendation overload avoided, with focus on significant findings and consolidation of related suggestions?
- Does value-add complement rather than obscure the assurance message, with significant findings clear and not softened?
- Is management's ownership of the implementation decision respected, with declined recommendations documented for residual risk rather than pressed?
- Are recommendations feasible, considering cost, effort, and organizational constraints?
- Is the quality and focus of recommendations emphasized over their quantity?
- Could management act on the recommendations and own the implementation, with the auditor remaining advisory throughout?
