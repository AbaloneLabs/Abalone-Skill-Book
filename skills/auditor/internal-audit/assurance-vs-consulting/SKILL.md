# Assurance Vs Consulting

Internal audit provides two distinct types of service: assurance, which is an objective examination of evidence to provide an independent assessment, and consulting, which is advisory activity intended to add value without the assurance conclusion. The distinction matters because the two roles create different obligations, rest on different bases of independence, and interact differently with management. The danger is that the boundary blurs. A consulting engagement drifts into making decisions that management should make; an assurance engagement is framed as consulting to avoid the rigor of assurance standards; or a consulting engagement on a process later becomes the subject of an assurance review, creating a self-review threat that undermines the assurance conclusion. Managing the assurance-consulting boundary is central to internal audit's credibility, because a function that cannot distinguish the two, or that lets consulting contaminate assurance, loses the independence that gives its assurance any value.

Use this skill when classifying a requested engagement, defining scope to reflect the correct role, managing threats from prior consulting, or preserving objectivity across both service types. The goal is to ensure assurance and consulting are clearly distinguished and that consulting does not impair the independence assurance requires.

## Core Rules

### Classify The Engagement By Its Objective, Not Its Label

Whether an engagement is assurance or consulting is determined by its objective, not by what it is called. Assurance aims to evaluate and conclude against criteria; consulting aims to advise and improve without a conclusion against criteria. The same topic, risk management, process design, controls, can be the subject of either, depending on what the engagement is meant to deliver.

Classify by asking:

- is the deliverable an independent conclusion against criteria, or advice and recommendations?
- does the engagement require the objectivity and evidence basis of assurance, or the collaborative, advisory nature of consulting?
- who is the primary client, governance for assurance, or management for consulting, and what do they expect?
- would the work, if performed, create a self-review threat to future assurance on the same area?

Misclassification, especially framing assurance as consulting to reduce rigor, or consulting as assurance to lend it authority, distorts the engagement and its deliverable. Classify by objective and adjust scope and approach accordingly.

### Preserve Independence As The Assurance Foundation

Assurance rests on independence, both in fact and in appearance. The internal audit function must be positioned to provide assurance free from interference, and individual engagements must be performed without conflicts that could bias the conclusion. Consulting, by contrast, is inherently collaborative with management and does not require the same independence, though it still requires objectivity.

Preserve independence for assurance by:

- ensuring the function reports to a level that supports independence, typically the audit committee or board;
- confirming that assurance engagement objectives, scope, and conclusions are not directed or influenced by management;
- assessing whether prior consulting or other involvement creates a threat to the assurance engagement;
- applying safeguards, including using different personnel, where threats exist;
- declining assurance where independence cannot be maintained.

Assurance performed without independence is not assurance; it is an internal review with no claim to credibility. Protect the foundation.

### Manage The Self-Review Threat From Consulting

The most significant threat consulting creates is self-review: when the function has previously provided consulting on a process, control, or system, and later performs assurance on the same area, it is asked to evaluate its own prior advice. The incentive to find the prior work adequate is strong, and the appearance of impairment is unavoidable.

Manage the self-review threat by:

- identifying, before accepting an assurance engagement, any prior consulting the function provided in the area;
- assessing the significance of the threat, considering the nature and recency of the consulting and its relationship to the assurance scope;
- applying safeguards, such as using personnel who did not participate in the consulting, or independent review;
- where the threat is significant and cannot be mitigated, declining the assurance engagement or disclosing the limitation;
- documenting the threat assessment and the safeguards applied.

The cleanest safeguard is separation of personnel. Where the same individuals consult and then assure the same area, the assurance conclusion carries an unavoidable asterisk.

### Define Consulting Scope To Avoid Management Decisions

Consulting engagements advise management; they do not make management decisions. When consulting drifts into designing and implementing solutions, taking on operational responsibility, or making decisions that management should own, the function compromises its independence and creates self-review threats for any future assurance.

Keep consulting within bounds by:

- defining the deliverable as advice, recommendations, or facilitation, not implementation or decision-making;
- ensuring management retains ownership and accountability for decisions and implementation;
- avoiding taking on roles or responsibilities that are properly management's;
- documenting the boundary between advisory input and management decision;
- recognizing when a consulting request is actually a request to perform a management function, and declining or reframing it.

A function that becomes the designer and implementer of solutions cannot later independently assure those solutions. The boundary protects future assurance capability.

### Apply The Right Standards And Rigor To Each Type

Assurance and consulting engagements are governed by different standards and require different rigor. Assurance engagements follow assurance standards, with requirements for planning, risk assessment, evidence, conclusion, and reporting. Consulting engagements follow consulting standards, which are less prescriptive and emphasize agreement with the client on objectives and scope. Applying the wrong framework produces an engagement that is either over-rigged for consulting or under-rigged for assurance.

Apply the right framework by:

- following assurance standards for assurance engagements, including the requirements for independence, evidence, and conclusion;
- following consulting standards for consulting engagements, with agreed objectives and scope;
- not reducing assurance rigor by framing work as consulting, nor inflating consulting by framing it as assurance;
- documenting the applicable framework and the engagement's conformity with it.

The framework is not a label; it defines what the engagement must do to deliver its type of value credibly.

### Communicate The Role Clearly To Stakeholders

Stakeholders, including management, governance, and the audited area, should understand whether an engagement is assurance or consulting, because the deliverable and its authority differ. Assurance carries the weight of an independent conclusion; consulting carries the weight of advice. Confusion about which was delivered leads to misreliance.

Communicate clearly by:

- stating the engagement type in the engagement letter or charter;
- describing the deliverable accurately, as a conclusion for assurance or recommendations for consulting;
- ensuring governance understands which engagements are assurance and which are consulting, and the different authority each carries;
- avoiding language in consulting reports that implies assurance conclusions, or vice versa.

A consulting report read as an assurance conclusion misleads governance about what was independently evaluated. Clarity protects the function's credibility and the users' understanding.

### Balance The Portfolio Deliberately

Internal audit functions typically provide both assurance and consulting, and the balance between them is a strategic choice. Too much consulting can erode independence and the capacity for assurance; too little can limit the function's value and its understanding of the business. The balance should be deliberate, aligned with the function's mandate and governance expectations.

Balance the portfolio by:

- defining in the charter the relative emphasis on assurance and consulting;
- ensuring assurance, the function's core purpose, is not crowded out by consulting demand;
- recognizing that consulting demand often grows because it is welcomed by management, while assurance is not, and managing the drift;
- reporting the portfolio balance to governance periodically.

A function that has drifted to predominantly consulting, without deliberate decision, may be providing value but has quietly abandoned the independent assurance role that is its reason for existing.

## Common Traps

### Framing Assurance As Consulting To Reduce Rigor

Calling an assurance engagement consulting to avoid independence and evidence requirements produces a weaker deliverable under a softer label. Classify by objective.

### Consulting That Drifts Into Management Decisions

Designing and implementing solutions in consulting engagements compromises independence and creates self-review threats. Keep consulting advisory.

### Same Personnel Consulting Then Assuring

Using the same individuals to consult and then assure the same area creates an unmitigated self-review threat. Separate personnel.

### Applying The Wrong Framework

Following consulting standards for assurance work, or assurance standards for consulting, mismatches rigor to type. Apply the right framework.

### Ambiguous Reporting

Consulting reports that imply assurance conclusions, or assurance reports framed as advice, confuse stakeholders. Communicate the type clearly.

### Portfolio Drift Toward Consulting

Allowing consulting to crowd out assurance, because it is welcomed by management, erodes the core purpose. Balance deliberately.

### Independence Eroded Without Recognition

Gradual loss of independence through accumulated consulting involvement, without recognizing the threat, undermines assurance credibility. Assess threats proactively.

## Self-Check

- Is each engagement classified by its objective, with assurance delivering independent conclusions against criteria and consulting delivering advice?
- Is independence preserved as the foundation of assurance, with reporting lines, freedom from direction, and threat assessment supporting it?
- Are self-review threats from prior consulting identified before assurance acceptance, with safeguards or declination where threats are significant?
- Is consulting scope defined to avoid management decisions, implementation, and operational responsibility that compromise independence?
- Is the correct framework applied to each engagement type, with assurance rigor for assurance and agreed-scope consulting standards for consulting?
- Is the engagement type communicated clearly to stakeholders, with deliverables described accurately and no implication of assurance in consulting reports?
- Is the assurance-consulting portfolio balance deliberate, aligned with the charter and governance expectations, and not drifted toward consulting by default?
- Are threats from accumulated consulting involvement recognized and managed over time?
- Is the function's core assurance purpose protected from being crowded out by consulting demand?
- Could governance distinguish which engagements provided independent assurance and which provided advice, and understand the different authority each carries?
