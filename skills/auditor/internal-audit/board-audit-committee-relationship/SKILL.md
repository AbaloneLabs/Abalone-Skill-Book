# Board And Audit Committee Relationship

The audit committee is internal audit's primary governance stakeholder and the source of its independence. The reporting line to the audit committee, the quality of communication, and the committee's effectiveness in providing oversight and support determine whether internal audit can function independently and add value. The recurring failure is treating the audit committee relationship as a reporting obligation, delivering routine updates and findings without engaging the committee on the significant risks and judgments, or allowing the relationship to be mediated by management in ways that compromise independence. An effective relationship is a two-way partnership: internal audit provides the committee with the insight it needs to govern, and the committee provides internal audit with the mandate, access, and protection it needs to be effective. Getting this relationship right is central to the function's effectiveness; getting it wrong neutralizes internal audit's independence and value.

Use this skill when managing the internal audit relationship with the board and audit committee, when determining reporting and communication, and when handling matters that require board attention. The goal is a relationship that secures internal audit's independence, provides the committee with effective oversight, and ensures significant matters reach the board.

## Core Rules

### Secure Functional Reporting To The Audit Committee

Internal audit's independence depends on its reporting line. Functional reporting to the audit committee, covering areas such as the charter, the plan, the budget, and the chief audit executive's appointment and performance, is the foundation of independence.

Secure by:

- ensuring the audit committee approves the internal audit charter, which defines the function's purpose, authority, and responsibility;
- having the audit committee approve the internal audit plan and any significant changes;
- ensuring the audit committee approves the internal audit budget and resources;
- having the audit committee concur in the appointment, performance evaluation, and compensation of the chief audit executive.

Without functional reporting to the audit committee, internal audit's independence is compromised, regardless of administrative reporting lines.

### Communicate Effectively And Proportionately

Effective communication with the audit committee requires delivering the right information, in the right form, at the right time. Too little leaves the committee unable to govern; too much buries the significant in the routine.

Communicate by:

- tailoring communications to the committee's needs, focusing on significant risks, findings, and judgments;
- providing concise, decision-useful reports rather than voluminous detail;
- reporting on the results of engagements, the status of the plan, and emerging risks;
- flagging significant matters promptly, rather than waiting for the next scheduled meeting;
- being available between meetings for consultation on significant issues.

Effective communication respects the committee's time and attention while ensuring it has what it needs to govern.

### Report Significant Findings And Risks

The audit committee relies on internal audit to surface significant findings, risks, and judgments that require its attention. Internal audit must report these matters clearly, with the context needed for the committee to act.

Report by:

- identifying findings, risks, and judgments that warrant audit committee attention, based on significance and governance relevance;
- presenting the matters with sufficient context, including the risk, the root cause, and management's response;
- being willing to report matters that management may prefer to keep private, where they are significant;
- following up on previously reported matters to confirm resolution.

Filtering significant findings, particularly to avoid conflict with management, denies the committee the information it needs.

### Handle Disagreements With Management Appropriately

Disagreements between internal audit and management are inevitable and can be productive. Where a disagreement on a significant matter cannot be resolved at the management level, it must be escalated to the audit committee.

Handle by:

- attempting to resolve disagreements with management through evidence, consultation, and process;
- where a significant disagreement persists, escalating to the audit committee with a clear presentation of the positions;
- presenting the disagreement objectively, without minimizing management's position or overstating internal audit's;
- documenting the disagreement, the escalation, and the resolution.

Suppressing significant disagreements, to preserve relationships with management, denies the committee the chance to govern.

### Maintain Independence From Management

Internal audit's administrative reporting line may run through management, but its functional independence must be preserved. Management must not be able to direct internal audit's scope, suppress findings, or influence the chief audit executive's performance evaluation inappropriately.

Maintain by:

- ensuring the chief audit executive has direct access to the audit committee and its chair;
- protecting internal audit's scope from management direction, with the plan approved by the audit committee;
- ensuring the chief audit executive's performance evaluation and compensation are not unduly influenced by management;
- documenting any attempts by management to influence internal audit's work and reporting them to the committee.

Independence compromised by management influence neutralizes internal audit's value to the organization.

### Provide Private Session Access

The audit committee and the chief audit executive should have the ability to meet privately, without management present. Private sessions allow the committee to hear matters that may be sensitive to management and to assess the chief audit executive's perspective independently.

Provide by:

- ensuring private sessions between the audit committee and the chief audit executive are a regular practice;
- using private sessions to surface sensitive matters, management relationship issues, or concerns about the control environment;
- protecting the chief audit executive from any retaliation for raising matters in private session;
- documenting that private sessions occurred, without necessarily documenting the confidential content.

Without private session access, sensitive matters may never reach the committee, and the chief audit executive may be inhibited from raising them.

### Evaluate The Committee's Effectiveness

The audit committee's effectiveness in overseeing internal audit is itself a matter for evaluation. A committee that is disengaged, lacks financial or governance literacy, or defers to management cannot provide effective oversight.

Evaluate by:

- assessing whether the committee provides effective oversight, challenge, and support;
- considering the committee's composition, including financial literacy and independence;
- identifying gaps in the committee's effectiveness and raising them constructively;
- where the committee's effectiveness is limited, adjusting internal audit's communication and support to compensate.

An ineffective audit committee leaves internal audit without the governance support it needs, and the function must adapt its approach accordingly.

## Common Traps

### Treating The Audit Committee As A Reporting Obligation

The committee is a governance partner, not a recipient of routine updates. Treating it as an obligation wastes the relationship.

### Filtering Significant Findings To Avoid Conflict

Significant findings must reach the committee. Filtering them denies the committee the information it needs to govern.

### Allowing Management To Mediate The Relationship

Management must not mediate internal audit's relationship with the committee in ways that compromise independence.

### Suppressing Disagreements To Preserve Relationships

Significant disagreements must be escalated. Suppressing them denies the committee the chance to govern.

### Burying The Significant In Routine Reporting

Too much routine detail obscures the significant. Communication must be tailored and decision-useful.

### Omitting Private Session Access

Without private sessions, sensitive matters may never surface. Access must be protected.

### Overlooking The Committee's Own Effectiveness

The committee's effectiveness affects internal audit's support. Gaps must be identified and addressed.

## Self-Check

- Is functional reporting to the audit committee secured, covering the charter, plan, budget, and the chief audit executive's appointment and performance?
- Is communication tailored to the committee's needs, focusing on significant risks, findings, and judgments, in concise, decision-useful form?
- Are significant findings, risks, and judgments reported to the committee with sufficient context for governance action?
- Are disagreements with management handled through escalation where they cannot be resolved, with objective presentation of the positions?
- Is internal audit's independence from management maintained, with direct access to the committee and protection from inappropriate influence?
- Are private sessions between the committee and the chief audit executive a regular practice, with retaliation protection?
- Is the committee's own effectiveness evaluated, with gaps identified and internal audit's approach adjusted to compensate?
- Could an independent reviewer confirm that the relationship secures internal audit's independence and provides the committee with effective oversight?
- Is the application free of treating the committee as an obligation, filtering findings, or allowing management to mediate the relationship?
- Are significant disagreements escalated rather than suppressed, and is private session access protected?
