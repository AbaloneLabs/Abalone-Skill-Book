# Quality Assurance And Improvement Program

A quality assurance and improvement program (QAIP) is the mechanism by which an internal audit function demonstrates that it meets professional standards and continuously improves. The IIA standards require every internal audit function to maintain a QAIP that covers all aspects of the function, including internal and external assessments. The recurring failure is treating the QAIP as a compliance exercise, performing perfunctory internal reviews and scheduling an external assessment to satisfy the standard, without using the findings to drive real improvement. A QAIP that does not lead to improvement is a ritual, not a quality program. Effective quality assurance is embedded in the function's daily work, supported by periodic internal assessment and rigorous external assessment, and it produces findings that are acted upon, not filed.

Use this skill when designing or evaluating a quality assurance and improvement program, when conducting internal or external assessments, and when using quality findings to drive improvement. The goal is a QAIP that demonstrates conformance with standards, drives continuous improvement, and provides confidence to stakeholders.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Scope Of A QAIP

A QAIP is not a single review. It covers all aspects of the internal audit function, including its charter, independence, staffing, methodology, engagement performance, and reporting. The scope is the function as a whole, not only individual engagements.

Understand that:

- the QAIP covers both the function's conformance with standards and its effectiveness in adding value;
- it includes internal assessments, both ongoing and periodic, and external assessments at least every five years;
- it evaluates the function against the IIA standards, the function's charter, and stakeholder expectations;
- the findings must drive improvement, not merely demonstrate conformance.

Understanding the scope prevents reducing the QAIP to a perfunctory engagement review.

### Establish Ongoing Internal Monitoring

Ongoing internal monitoring is embedded in the function's daily work. It includes supervision, review, engagement-level quality checks, and performance metrics that provide real-time insight into quality.

Establish by:

- defining supervision and review expectations for every engagement, calibrated to risk and complexity;
- implementing engagement-level quality checks, such as working paper review and report review;
- tracking performance metrics, such as recommendation acceptance rates, audit cycle times, and stakeholder satisfaction;
- identifying and addressing quality issues as they arise, not waiting for periodic assessment.

Ongoing monitoring catches quality issues early, before they affect the function's outputs.

### Conduct Periodic Internal Assessments

Periodic internal assessments provide a more comprehensive view of the function's conformance and effectiveness, conducted by qualified internal staff independent of the engagements reviewed.

Conduct by:

- scheduling periodic internal assessments, typically annually, with sufficient resources and independence;
- selecting a representative sample of engagements and function-level processes for review;
- evaluating conformance with standards, the charter, and methodology;
- identifying both conformance gaps and opportunities for improvement;
- documenting the assessment, the findings, and the improvement actions.

Periodic internal assessments provide depth that ongoing monitoring cannot, and they prepare the function for external assessment.

### Arrange External Assessments

External assessments provide an independent view of the function's conformance and effectiveness, conducted by qualified assessors from outside the function. The IIA standards require external assessment at least every five years.

Arrange by:

- engaging a qualified external assessor, such as another organization's internal audit function or an external provider;
- ensuring the assessor is independent, competent, and familiar with the applicable standards;
- scoping the assessment to cover the function's conformance, effectiveness, and improvement opportunities;
- providing the assessor with access to engagements, documentation, and stakeholders;
- obtaining a formal opinion on conformance and a report with recommendations.

External assessment provides the independent validation that internal assessment alone cannot.

### Evaluate Conformance And Effectiveness

The QAIP evaluates both conformance, whether the function follows the standards, and effectiveness, whether the function adds value and meets stakeholder needs. Both dimensions matter, and a function can conform without being effective, or be effective without fully conforming.

Evaluate by:

- assessing conformance with each applicable standard, identifying partial or non-conformance;
- assessing effectiveness through stakeholder feedback, recommendation outcomes, and the function's contribution to objectives;
- recognizing that conformance without effectiveness, or effectiveness without conformance, both indicate improvement needs;
- documenting both dimensions in the assessment report.

Evaluating only conformance, or only effectiveness, misses half the quality picture.

### Use Findings To Drive Improvement

The purpose of the QAIP is improvement. Findings, whether from ongoing monitoring, periodic internal assessment, or external assessment, must be translated into improvement actions, tracked to completion, and evaluated for effect.

Use by:

- developing an improvement plan that addresses each finding, with owners and timelines;
- tracking improvement actions to completion and reporting progress to the audit committee;
- evaluating whether the improvements had the intended effect on quality;
- incorporating lessons learned into the function's methodology, training, and supervision.

Findings filed without action are wasted; the QAIP must close the loop from finding to improvement.

### Report Results To Stakeholders

The results of the QAIP must be communicated to stakeholders, particularly the audit committee and senior management. Transparency about quality, including gaps and improvement actions, builds confidence and demonstrates accountability.

Report by:

- communicating QAIP results to the audit committee at least annually;
- being transparent about conformance gaps, improvement actions, and progress;
- reporting the results of external assessment, including the formal opinion;
- using the reporting to reinforce the function's commitment to quality and improvement.

Hiding quality gaps undermines confidence; transparency, paired with improvement, builds it.

## Common Traps

### Treating The QAIP As A Compliance Exercise

The QAIP exists to drive improvement, not merely to satisfy a standard. Treating it as compliance wastes its value.

### Reducing Internal Assessment To Perfunctory Review

Ongoing and periodic internal assessments must be substantive. Perfunctory review misses real quality issues.

### Scheduling External Assessment Without Acting On Findings

External assessment provides findings that must drive improvement. Scheduling it without acting wastes the investment.

### Evaluating Only Conformance Or Only Effectiveness

Both dimensions matter. Evaluating only one misses half the quality picture.

### Filing Findings Without Improvement Actions

Findings must translate into tracked improvement actions. Filing them without action is wasted effort.

### Hiding Quality Gaps From Stakeholders

Transparency about gaps, paired with improvement, builds confidence. Hiding gaps undermines it.

### Overlooking Ongoing Monitoring In Favor Of Periodic Assessment

Ongoing monitoring catches issues early. Relying only on periodic assessment allows issues to persist.

## Self-Check

- Is the QAIP understood to cover all aspects of the function, including conformance and effectiveness, not only individual engagements?
- Is ongoing internal monitoring embedded in daily work, with supervision, review, and performance metrics?
- Are periodic internal assessments conducted by qualified, independent internal staff, covering a representative sample?
- Are external assessments arranged at least every five years, with qualified, independent assessors and appropriate scope?
- Does the assessment evaluate both conformance with standards and effectiveness in adding value?
- Are findings translated into an improvement plan with owners, timelines, and tracking to completion?
- Are QAIP results communicated transparently to the audit committee, including gaps and improvement actions?
- Could an independent reviewer confirm that the QAIP drives continuous improvement, not merely compliance?
- Is the application free of treating the QAIP as compliance, perfunctory review, or filing findings without action?
- Are both conformance and effectiveness evaluated, with improvement actions evaluated for their effect on quality?
