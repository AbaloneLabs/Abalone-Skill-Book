# Audit Strategy Memorandum

The audit strategy is the high-level approach that frames the engagement before the detailed audit plan is built. It is the bridge between risk assessment and the specific procedures that follow. Where the plan answers "what will we do," the strategy answers "how, in broad strokes, will we approach this engagement." Agents frequently collapse strategy into plan, producing a list of procedures without an overarching approach, or they write a strategy that merely restates the risk assessment without translating it into directional choices about scope, emphasis, timing, team, and reporting. A strategy that does not make choices adds no value, because it leaves the detailed plan to be assembled from habit rather than from a deliberate approach to this engagement's risks.

Use this skill after completing or substantially progressing the risk assessment and before building the detailed audit plan, and whenever a change in circumstances requires a revised overall approach. The goal is a documented strategy that makes the engagement's direction, emphasis, and constraints explicit, so the detailed plan follows from a deliberate approach rather than from default.

## Core Rules

### Separate Strategy From Plan By Purpose

The strategy and the plan serve different purposes, and confusing them produces a document that is neither. The strategy sets the overall approach; the plan translates that approach into specific procedures, populations, samples, and timing. The strategy is shorter, more directional, and decided earlier; the plan is more detailed and developed once the strategy is set.

The strategy should determine:

- the scope and reporting objectives of the engagement;
- the overall approach, substantive versus controls-reliance, and where each applies;
- the emphasis on particular areas, risks, or locations;
- materiality and performance materiality at the overall level;
- the timing of fieldwork and the use of interim work;
- the team structure, specialists, and review points;
- the reporting deliverables and deadlines;
- the reliance on internal audit, experts, or the work of others;
- the approach to group audits and components if applicable.

If the strategy does not make these choices, it is a restatement of risk, not a strategy.

### Translate The Risk Assessment Into Directional Choices

The strategy's core function is to convert the assessed risks into an overall approach. A risk assessment that identifies significant risks in revenue, estimates, and override should produce a strategy that emphasizes those areas with more persuasive evidence, period-end timing, and unpredictable procedures, not a generic program spread evenly across the statements.

Translate risk into strategy by:

- identifying which assertions and areas drive the most risk and directing emphasis there;
- deciding where a controls-reliance approach is viable and where a substantive-only approach is required;
- setting the overall materiality and performance materiality with reference to user needs and risk;
- determining where interim testing can be used and where period-end testing is mandatory;
- identifying where specialists, experts, or IT auditors are needed;
- deciding the locations or components to be visited or scoped.

A strategy that does not change when the risk profile changes is not responsive to risk.

### Set Materiality At The Strategy Stage

Overall materiality and performance materiality are strategic decisions, because they frame the precision of the entire engagement and the amount of work required. Setting them at the plan stage, after procedures are designed, risks tailoring the threshold to the work already planned rather than the reverse.

Set materiality strategically by:

- identifying the benchmark and its rationale, tied to user needs;
- considering qualitative factors that may lower the threshold;
- setting performance materiality with reference to the risk of aggregation of uncorrected errors;
- documenting the basis so the threshold can be defended and, if necessary, revised;
- recognizing that materiality for components or locations may differ from overall.

Materiality set without reference to risk, or copied from last year without reconsideration, weakens the strategy.

### Decide The Timing Approach Deliberately

Timing is a strategic lever. The choice between interim and period-end testing, and the sequencing of locations and components, affects both efficiency and the control of detection risk. The strategy should set the timing approach, with the detailed schedule developed in the plan.

Decide timing by:

- identifying which areas require period-end or post-period-end testing, such as cutoff, inventory, and estimates;
- determining where interim testing is efficient and what roll-forward procedures will bridge to period end;
- sequencing multi-location or group work to manage resources and deadlines;
- accounting for reporting deadlines that constrain how late testing can occur;
- recognizing that significant risks may require testing closer to or after period end.

Defaulting to last year's timing without reconsideration leaves detection risk unmanaged where risks have shifted.

### Resource The Engagement To The Strategy

The strategy should identify the resources, skills, and structure needed to execute the approach. An approach that requires fair value specialists, IT auditors, or component teams must be resourced accordingly, or the strategy is not executable.

Resource the strategy by:

- identifying the skills required by the significant risks and complex areas;
- determining whether specialists or experts are needed and engaging them early;
- structuring the team to provide appropriate supervision, review, and engagement quality review;
- allocating effort to the areas of emphasis identified by the risk assessment;
- recognizing constraints on availability, particularly during peak periods.

A strategy that assumes resources the firm does not have is a strategy that will fail in execution.

### Define Reliance On The Work Of Others

The strategy should decide, at a high level, whether and how the engagement will rely on the work of internal audit, other auditors, specialists, or service organization controls. These reliance decisions shape the engagement and must be made before the detailed plan.

Define reliance by:

- assessing whether internal audit can be used and for what, subject to independence, competence, and objectivity;
- determining the approach to components and other auditors in a group engagement;
- deciding where experts will be used and how their work will be evaluated;
- identifying reliance on SOC reports for service organizations and what complementary controls must be tested.

Reliance decided ad hoc during fieldwork is poorly supported and difficult to defend.

### Make The Strategy A Living Document

Circumstances change during the engagement, and the strategy must be revised when they do. A strategy frozen at planning loses relevance when risks emerge, controls fail, or scope limitations arise. The strategy should be revisited and updated, with changes documented.

Revise the strategy when:

- significant risks emerge or change during fieldwork;
- controls are found ineffective, changing the viable approach;
- scope limitations or unavailable evidence arise;
- materiality needs revision based on new information;
- the entity undergoes significant change, such as acquisition or restructuring.

A strategy that cannot be revised is not a strategy; it is a historical artifact.

## Common Traps

### Collapsing Strategy Into Plan

Producing a detailed procedure list without an overarching approach leaves the engagement without direction and makes the plan a collection of habits.

### Restating Risk Without Making Choices

A strategy that lists risks without translating them into scope, emphasis, timing, and resourcing adds no value and does not guide the plan.

### Copying Last Year's Strategy

Risks change, and a strategy rolled forward without reconsideration may not respond to the current risk profile.

### Setting Materiality To Fit The Work

Choosing a threshold that justifies the work already planned, rather than setting the threshold first and designing work to it, inverts the logic.

### Defaulting To Interim Timing Without Roll-Forward

Interim testing is efficient but leaves a period-end gap. Without roll-forward, detection risk at year-end is uncontrolled.

### Assuming Resources That Do Not Exist

A strategy requiring specialists or capacity the firm lacks is not executable and will degrade during fieldwork.

### Freezing The Strategy At Planning

A strategy that is not revised when risks emerge or controls fail no longer reflects the engagement being performed.

## Self-Check

- Is the strategy distinct from the detailed plan, setting overall scope, approach, emphasis, timing, materiality, and resourcing rather than listing procedures?
- Does the strategy translate the risk assessment into directional choices, with emphasis directed to the significant risks?
- Is overall and performance materiality set at the strategy stage, with a documented basis tied to user needs and risk, before detailed procedures are designed?
- Is the timing approach decided deliberately, with period-end testing where required and documented roll-forward where interim testing is used?
- Are the resources, skills, specialists, and review structure identified to match the approach, and are they actually available?
- Is reliance on internal audit, other auditors, experts, and service organization controls defined at the strategy stage, not decided ad hoc?
- Is the strategy a living document, revised and documented when risks, controls, materiality, or scope change during the engagement?
- Could an independent reviewer see how the detailed plan follows from a deliberate overall approach rather than from default or last year?
- Does the strategy make choices that would differ if the risk profile differed, confirming it is responsive rather than generic?
- Are the reporting objectives, deliverables, and deadlines reflected in the strategy so the approach is feasible within the engagement window?
