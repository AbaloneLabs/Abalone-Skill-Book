# Audit Planning And Risk Assessment

Audit planning is not administrative setup. It is the point where the auditor decides what the engagement is trying to prove, what could go wrong, what criteria apply, what evidence will be persuasive, and what work is necessary. Weak planning creates audit work that looks busy but does not answer the audit objective. A checklist can be complete while the wrong risks remain untested.

Use this skill before planning a financial, operational, compliance, internal control, performance, vendor, grant, security, or process audit. The goal is to prevent the agent from jumping to procedures before defining objectives, scope, risk, criteria, materiality, responsibility, and evidence strategy.

## Core Rules

### Define The Engagement Type And Objective

Start by identifying the type of engagement and the question it must answer. Different engagements require different criteria, evidence, assurance levels, and reporting language.

Clarify:

- financial statement audit;
- internal control audit;
- compliance audit;
- operational audit;
- performance audit;
- vendor or third-party audit;
- grant or contract audit;
- security or process audit;
- readiness assessment;
- agreed-upon procedures;
- internal review that is not an audit.

Write the objective in testable language. "Review expenses" is not enough. "Determine whether travel expenses for Q2 were approved, supported, business-related, and recorded in the correct period according to policy" is audit-ready.

### Identify Criteria Before Testing

An audit compares evidence against criteria. Criteria may come from accounting standards, law, regulation, contracts, grant terms, policies, control frameworks, service-level agreements, board approvals, management assertions, or documented procedures.

Before testing, identify:

- source of criteria;
- version or effective date;
- responsible owner;
- whether criteria are mandatory or advisory;
- whether criteria are clear enough to test;
- conflicts between criteria;
- exceptions or scope exclusions;
- how criteria apply to the period under audit.

Do not create findings from preferences. If there is no valid criterion, the auditor can identify a concern or improvement opportunity, but should not present it as noncompliance without explaining the basis.

### Understand The Entity And Process

Risk assessment begins with understanding the environment. The auditor needs to understand how the process actually works, not only how the policy says it works.

Gather:

- organization structure;
- process owners;
- systems used;
- transaction flow;
- approval points;
- manual workarounds;
- outsourced service providers;
- changes during the period;
- prior audit results;
- known incidents;
- complaints or hotline reports;
- management incentives;
- regulatory or contractual pressure;
- key reports and data sources.

Walk through a transaction or activity from initiation to reporting. If the walkthrough cannot be explained clearly, planning is not complete.

### Assess Risk At The Right Level

Audit risk should be assessed where misstatement, noncompliance, inefficiency, fraud, or control failure could occur. High-level risk statements are useful only if they lead to targeted procedures.

Assess risk by:

- objective;
- account or disclosure;
- assertion;
- process step;
- control activity;
- location;
- system;
- population;
- transaction type;
- management estimate;
- compliance requirement.

For financial audits, consider relevant assertions such as existence, completeness, accuracy, valuation, rights and obligations, cutoff, classification, and presentation. For operational and compliance audits, translate the objective into equivalent testable assertions.

### Consider Materiality And Significance

Materiality is not only a numeric threshold. Significance can depend on users, legal requirements, fraud risk, public sensitivity, covenant impact, management compensation, control failure, or repeated small errors.

Consider:

- quantitative size;
- qualitative sensitivity;
- regulatory or contractual consequences;
- effect on users' decisions;
- impact on key metrics;
- risk of fraud or override;
- prior-period issues;
- repeated pattern;
- whether the matter affects vulnerable groups, public funds, or safety.

Set planning thresholds before testing where appropriate, but remain willing to revise them if new information changes the risk assessment.

### Link Planned Procedures To Risks

Every planned procedure should answer a risk, assertion, criterion, or objective. If a procedure does not connect to the audit plan, remove it or explain its purpose.

For each significant risk, document:

- risk statement;
- affected objective or assertion;
- criteria;
- population;
- expected evidence;
- planned procedure;
- timing;
- sample approach if applicable;
- person responsible;
- how exceptions will be evaluated.

Avoid generic procedures such as "review documents" without specifying which documents, for what attribute, against which criterion, and what conclusion the evidence supports.

### Plan Evidence Sufficiency And Reliability

Evidence should be sufficient and appropriate. Sufficiency concerns quantity. Appropriateness concerns relevance and reliability. A large volume of weak evidence does not fix poor relevance.

Evaluate:

- source independence;
- original versus copy;
- external versus internal evidence;
- system reliability;
- completeness of population;
- accuracy of extracted data;
- competence of person providing evidence;
- timing of evidence;
- whether evidence supports the exact assertion;
- whether contradictory evidence exists.

If the audit depends on system-generated reports, plan how report completeness and accuracy will be tested.

### Preserve Independence And Objectivity

Auditors must avoid planning work that audits their own decisions or relies on management without challenge. Internal auditors and external auditors face different independence rules, but objectivity is always relevant.

Check:

- prior involvement in designing the process;
- financial or personal relationships;
- management pressure;
- self-review threat;
- advocacy threat;
- familiarity threat;
- access restrictions;
- conflicts of interest;
- use of specialists or other auditors.

If independence or objectivity is impaired, disclose, mitigate, reassign, or decline the work depending on applicable standards.

### Plan Supervision, Review, And Communication

Audit quality depends on supervision and review. The plan should say who performs work, who reviews it, how issues are escalated, and when management or governance will be updated.

Plan:

- engagement owner;
- team roles;
- review points;
- consultation triggers;
- escalation for suspected fraud or illegal acts;
- timing of status meetings;
- evidence request protocol;
- management representation needs;
- governance communications;
- documentation standards.

Do not wait until reporting to discover that a key issue required earlier communication.

## Common Traps

### Starting With Last Year's Audit Program

Prior programs are useful but dangerous if copied blindly. New systems, new contracts, turnover, regulatory changes, new products, remote work, outsourcing, and prior findings can change risk. Reuse only after updating the risk assessment.

### Confusing Process Knowledge With Audit Evidence

Understanding the process is not the same as testing it. A walkthrough can inform risk assessment, but it does not usually prove that controls operated throughout the period or that transactions complied with criteria.

### Planning Around Available Data Instead Of Audit Objectives

Auditors often test whatever data is easiest to extract. The audit objective should drive the evidence request. If the available data does not answer the objective, identify the limitation and adjust the plan.

### Ignoring Scope Boundaries

A plan should define what is excluded as well as included. Period, location, entity, system, transaction type, and criteria exclusions should be explicit. Hidden scope limits create misleading conclusions.

### Treating Materiality As Purely Numeric

A small issue can matter when it involves fraud, legal violation, executive override, public money, safety, privacy, repeated control failure, or a sensitive disclosure. Document qualitative significance.

### Failing To Reassess Risk During Fieldwork

Planning is not frozen. New exceptions, missing evidence, contradictory explanations, or data quality problems can change risk. Update the plan when evidence points to a different risk profile.

### Letting Management Define The Conclusion

Management can provide context and evidence, but the auditor must decide whether evidence supports the conclusion. Do not write the audit plan around management's preferred answer.

## Self-Check

- Is the engagement type and objective stated in testable language?
- Are valid criteria identified with source, version, effective date, and scope?
- Does the plan reflect how the process actually works, including systems, owners, changes, and workarounds?
- Are risks assessed at the objective, assertion, process, population, and system level where relevant?
- Are materiality and significance considered both quantitatively and qualitatively?
- Does every planned procedure map to a risk, criterion, assertion, or objective?
- Is the expected evidence sufficient, appropriate, reliable, and tied to the exact assertion?
- Are system-generated reports and data extracts planned for completeness and accuracy testing?
- Have independence, objectivity, conflicts, and self-review threats been considered?
- Are supervision, review, escalation, and communication steps defined before fieldwork begins?
