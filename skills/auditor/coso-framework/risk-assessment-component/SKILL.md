# Risk Assessment Component Evaluation

The risk assessment component of COSO is the entity's own process for identifying, analysing, and responding to risks to the achievement of its objectives — including financial reporting objectives. This is not the auditor's risk assessment; it is management's. But the two are deeply connected: a mature entity risk assessment process produces a reliable view of where financial reporting risks concentrate, which the auditor can leverage, while an absent or cosmetic process forces the auditor to build the risk picture independently and signals a broader control weakness. Confusing the two assessments, or treating management's risk register as a substitute for the auditor's, is a recurring error.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish the entity's risk assessment from the auditor's, and use the relationship deliberately

The entity's risk assessment is a control — management's mechanism for knowing where its risks lie and responding to them. The auditor's risk assessment is the procedure that drives audit scope. They overlap in subject matter but differ in purpose and ownership. Evaluate the entity's process on its own merits (does it exist, is it robust, does it drive action?), then decide how much to leverage it. A strong entity process can focus the auditor's procedures; a weak one is itself a finding and forces the auditor to do the work from scratch.

### Confirm the entity's process actually covers financial reporting objectives, not only strategic and operational ones

Many entities run an enterprise risk assessment that is heavily weighted toward strategic, operational, and compliance risk and gives financial reporting only cursory treatment. Examine whether the process explicitly identifies risks to reliable financial reporting — misstatement risk in significant accounts, new accounting standards, estimation uncertainty, complex transactions, fraud, and IT-dependent processes. If financial reporting risk is absent or shallow, the process is incomplete for internal control over financial reporting purposes, regardless of how sophisticated it looks for strategic risk.

### Assess the four steps of a credible risk assessment process

A mature entity risk assessment moves through identifiable stages. Confirm each is present and substantive:

1. **Objective setting** — are financial reporting objectives (accuracy, completeness, compliance with the applicable framework) clearly defined and communicated?
2. **Risk identification** — are risks to those objectives identified at a useful level of granularity, drawing on changes in the business, prior errors, and external factors?
3. **Risk analysis and assessment** — are identified risks evaluated for likelihood and impact, and prioritised?
4. **Risk response** — are specific responses designed (avoid, reduce, share, accept), assigned to owners, and resourced?

A process that identifies risks but never analyses or responds to them is incomplete; a process that responds to risks never formally identified is ad hoc. Both are weaknesses.

### Evaluate whether the process is forward-looking and change-oriented

The highest-value part of any risk assessment is how it handles change. New products, new systems, acquisitions, new accounting standards, new regulations, leadership turnover, and rapid growth all create or shift risk. Assess whether the entity's process:

- has a mechanism to surface risks from change events rather than only repeating last year's register;
- is refreshed at a frequency that matches the pace of business change;
- involves the people closest to the changed processes, not only senior management.

A risk register that is a rolled-forward copy of last year's, with the same risks in the same order, is a strong indicator that the process is ceremonial.

### Test whether risk responses are real and owned

A risk that is listed but has no response, no owner, and no deadline is not actually managed. For a sample of identified risks, confirm:

- there is a defined response (a control, a mitigation, an acceptance decision with rationale);
- the response has an accountable owner with the authority to act;
- there is a mechanism to confirm the response is operating (this connects to the monitoring component);
- residual risk after the response is understood and accepted at an appropriate level.

Risk responses that exist only in the register, with no evidence of implementation or follow-up, indicate the process is documentation rather than management.

### Assess the linkage from risk assessment to control activities

The COSO components are meant to be integrated: risks identified in the risk assessment component should drive the design of control activities, and changes in risk should trigger changes in controls. Trace a few identified financial reporting risks through to the specific controls that address them. If the linkage is broken — risks are listed in one document, controls are documented in another, and the two never connect — the framework is not operating as intended, and the entity's view of its control coverage is unreliable.

### Leverage the entity's output carefully, with corroboration

Where the entity's risk assessment is robust, it can sharpen the auditor's risk focus and identify risks the auditor might otherwise underweight. But leverage is not substitution. Corroborate management's risk views against independent evidence: prior-period audit findings, error patterns, industry trends, walkthrough observations, and analytical procedures. Management may under-state risks that reflect poorly on them (estimation aggressiveness, override, control gaps in growth areas). The auditor's risk assessment remains the auditor's responsibility.

### Treat a weak or absent entity risk assessment as a control deficiency and a risk amplifier

If the entity has no process to identify and respond to financial reporting risk, or the process is purely formal, this is both a control deficiency (the risk assessment component is ineffective) and a reason to raise the auditor's assessed risk: an entity that does not know where its risks lie is more likely to have unmanaged exposures. Reflect this in higher inherent risk assessments, broader scope, and more persuasive evidence, rather than treating the absence as neutral.

## Common Traps

- **Confusing the entity's risk assessment with the auditor's**, and either duplicating effort or substituting management's register for independent audit judgement.
- **Accepting an enterprise risk register as covering financial reporting** when it is actually weighted to strategic and operational risk and omits reporting risk.
- **Treating a risk register as evidence of a process.** The register is an output; the process is identification, analysis, response, and follow-up.
- **Rolling forward last year's risk assessment without challenge.** A static register in a changing business is a red flag, not reassurance.
- **Accepting risks with no response, owner, or follow-up as "managed."** Listing is not managing.
- **Failing to trace identified risks through to the controls that respond to them**, missing the integration that makes COSO coherent.
- **Leveraging management's risk views without corroboration**, especially for risks management has incentive to understate (estimation, override, aggressive accounting).
- **Treating an absent or weak risk assessment as neutral** rather than as both a deficiency and a reason to raise assessed risk; **Over-weighting sophistication of risk quantification** (heat maps, scoring models) over whether the process actually changes behaviour and controls

## Self-Check

- Did I clearly separate my evaluation of the entity's risk assessment process from my own audit risk assessment, and decide deliberately how much to leverage the former?
- Does the entity's process explicitly cover financial reporting objectives and risks, not only strategic and operational ones?
- Can I confirm each of the four stages — objective setting, risk identification, analysis, and response — is present and substantive, not just documented?
- Is the process forward-looking and responsive to change events, or is it a rolled-forward copy of prior years?
- For a sample of identified risks, is there a real response, an accountable owner, and evidence of follow-up?
- Did I trace at least a few identified financial reporting risks through to the specific control activities that respond to them?
- Where I leveraged management's risk views, did I corroborate them against independent evidence, especially for risks management has incentive to understate?
- If the entity's risk assessment is weak or absent, did I record it as a control deficiency and reflect it in higher assessed risk and broader scope, rather than treating it as neutral?
