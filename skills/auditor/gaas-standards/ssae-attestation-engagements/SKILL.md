# SSAE Attestation Engagements

Attestation engagements, governed by the Statements on Standards for Atestation Engagements (SSAE, currently SSAE 18), cover a range of services in which a practitioner issues a report on subject matter, or an assertion about subject matter, that is the responsibility of another party. They include examinations, reviews, and agreed-upon procedures, and they cover subjects such as controls at service organizations (SOC reports), compliance, forecasts, and performance measures. The recurring failure is treating an attestation engagement as if it were an audit, applying audit standards and audit procedures without recognizing that attestation has its own framework, its own levels of assurance, and its own requirements. Each type of attestation engagement, examination, review, and agreed-upon procedures, provides a different level of assurance and follows different procedures and reporting. The practitioner must apply the correct attestation standard, match the procedures to the engagement type, and report in the form that the standard requires.

Use this skill when performing an attestation engagement, when determining the engagement type and applicable standard, and when applying the procedures and reporting requirements. The goal is an attestation engagement that applies the correct SSAE standard, matches procedures to the engagement type, and reports appropriately.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Attestation From Audit

Attestation is a distinct service from an audit. An audit provides reasonable assurance on financial statements; attestation provides assurance, at varying levels, on subject matter or an assertion about subject matter. The frameworks, procedures, and reporting differ.

Distinguish by:

- recognizing that attestation is governed by SSAE, not by GAAS for audits;
- understanding that the subject matter of attestation is broader than financial statements, encompassing controls, compliance, forecasts, and performance;
- applying the SSAE framework, procedures, and reporting, rather than audit standards;
- identifying when a service is attestation, and when it falls under a different framework such as SSARS for compilations and reviews of historical financial statements.

Treating an attestation engagement as an audit leads to misapplied standards and inappropriate reporting.

### Determine The Engagement Type: Examination, Review, Or Agreed-Upon Procedures

SSAE defines three types of attestation engagement, each providing a different level of assurance and following different procedures.

Determine by:

- examination engagements, which provide reasonable assurance and result in an opinion;
- review engagements, which provide limited assurance and result in a conclusion;
- agreed-upon procedures engagements, which provide no assurance but report factual findings from procedures agreed with the parties;
- matching the engagement type to the needs of the intended users and the nature of the subject matter;
- documenting the engagement type and the basis for the selection.

Selecting the wrong engagement type, or applying procedures from one type to another, produces inappropriate assurance and reporting.

### Identify The Subject Matter And Criteria

Attestation requires a subject matter and criteria against which it is evaluated. The subject matter must be identifiable and capable of consistent measurement against the criteria.

Identify by:

- defining the subject matter clearly, whether it is controls, compliance, a forecast, or performance measures;
- identifying the criteria against which the subject matter is evaluated, which must be suitable, available, and relevant;
- confirming the criteria are established by a recognized body, by law or regulation, or by the engaging party where appropriate;
- evaluating whether the subject matter and criteria are appropriate for the engagement type.

Without suitable criteria, the subject matter cannot be evaluated and the engagement cannot proceed.

### Evaluate Management's Assertion Or Measurement

In many attestation engagements, the practitioner reports on an assertion about the subject matter made by management, rather than directly on the subject matter. The practitioner must evaluate whether management's assertion is appropriately stated.

Evaluate by:

- obtaining management's assertion about the subject matter;
- evaluating whether the assertion is fairly stated, based on the criteria;
- performing procedures sufficient to support the conclusion on the assertion;
- recognizing that the practitioner's responsibility is to the assertion, with management retaining responsibility for the subject matter.

Accepting management's assertion without evaluation fails the practitioner's responsibility.

### Apply Procedures Matched To The Engagement Type

Each engagement type requires different procedures. Examinations require sufficient evidence to support reasonable assurance; reviews require primarily inquiry and analytical procedures to support limited assurance; agreed-upon procedures require performance of the specific procedures agreed.

Apply by:

- for examinations, obtaining sufficient appropriate evidence to support a reasonable assurance opinion;
- for reviews, performing inquiry and analytical procedures sufficient to support a limited assurance conclusion, and identifying any basis for modification;
- for agreed-upon procedures, performing only the procedures agreed with the parties, and reporting the factual findings;
- not exceeding the engagement type's procedures, such as performing examination-level procedures in a review engagement.

Mismatched procedures produce inappropriate assurance and misstate the engagement's value.

### Apply The Reporting Requirements For Each Engagement Type

Each engagement type has specific reporting requirements. The form, content, and level of assurance in the report differ across examination, review, and agreed-upon procedures.

Apply by:

- for examinations, issuing an opinion that expresses reasonable assurance;
- for reviews, issuing a conclusion that expresses limited assurance, including any modifications;
- for agreed-upon procedures, issuing a report of factual findings, with no assurance or conclusion;
- using the prescribed report form for each engagement type, including required elements such as the subject matter, criteria, and the practitioner's responsibility;
- restricting the report's use where required, particularly for agreed-upon procedures engagements.

Using the wrong report form, or expressing the wrong level of assurance, is a reporting failure.

### Apply Independence And Quality Requirements

SSAE imposes independence and quality requirements on attestation engagements, similar to but distinct from those for audits. The practitioner must be independent, unless the engagement type permits otherwise, and must meet the firm's quality control standards.

Apply by:

- evaluating independence under the SSAE framework, recognizing that some engagements, such as agreed-upon procedures, may have different independence considerations;
- applying the firm's quality control system to attestation engagements;
- ensuring personnel are competent in the subject matter and the SSAE framework;
- documenting independence and quality compliance.

Treating attestation independence as automatic, or applying audit independence without considering SSAE specifics, risks impairment.

## Common Traps

### Treating Attestation As An Audit

Attestation has its own framework. Treating it as an audit leads to misapplied standards and reporting.

### Selecting The Wrong Engagement Type

Each type provides different assurance. Selecting the wrong type misstates the engagement's value.

### Mismatching Procedures To The Engagement Type

Procedures must match the type. Examination procedures in a review, or vice versa, produce inappropriate assurance.

### Using The Wrong Report Form

Each type has specific reporting. The wrong form misstates the assurance and the engagement.

### Accepting Management's Assertion Without Evaluation

The practitioner must evaluate the assertion. Accepting it without evaluation fails the responsibility.

### Overlooking Suitable Criteria

Without suitable criteria, the subject matter cannot be evaluated. Criteria must be identified and assessed.

### Assuming Independence Is Automatic

SSAE independence must be evaluated. Assuming it, without considering SSAE specifics, risks impairment.

## Self-Check

- Is attestation distinguished from audit, with the SSAE framework applied rather than GAAS?
- Is the engagement type, examination, review, or agreed-upon procedures, determined based on the users' needs and the subject matter?
- Are the subject matter and suitable criteria identified, with the criteria available, relevant, and established by a recognized body or the engaging party where appropriate?
- Where the engagement reports on an assertion, is management's assertion evaluated rather than accepted without evaluation?
- Are procedures matched to the engagement type, with sufficient evidence for examinations, inquiry and analytics for reviews, and only agreed procedures for AUPs?
- Are the reporting requirements for each engagement type applied, with the correct form, content, and level of assurance?
- Are independence and quality requirements applied under SSAE, with documentation of compliance?
- Could an independent reviewer confirm that the correct SSAE standard is applied, procedures match the engagement type, and reporting is appropriate?
- Is the application free of treating attestation as an audit, selecting the wrong engagement type, or mismatching procedures?
- Are suitable criteria identified, management's assertion evaluated, and the correct report form used?
