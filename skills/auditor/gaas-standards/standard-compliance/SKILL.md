# Standard Compliance

Audit standards are not a backdrop to the work; they are the rules that make the work an audit rather than a review. The persistent failure is treating compliance as a documentation layer added at the end, when in fact compliance must shape planning, execution, review, and reporting from the start. Standards also change, sometimes materially, and a firm that does not track effective dates and amend its methodology will perform engagements under superseded requirements without knowing it. Layered on top of the performance standards is a quality management regime, ISQM, SAQM, or equivalent, that governs the firm-level system within which every engagement sits. An audit can be well-executed and still non-compliant if the firm's quality system is deficient or if the wrong standard version was applied.

Use this skill when ensuring engagement or firm-level compliance with applicable standards, tracking and implementing standard updates, designing or evaluating a quality management system, applying engagement quality review, or assessing independence within the standards framework. The goal is compliance that is designed into the engagement and demonstrable on inspection.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Correct Standard Set For Each Engagement

Compliance begins with applying the right standard. A single firm may operate under GAAS, ISA, PCAOB, and GAGAS simultaneously across different engagements, and each has distinct requirements.

Identify by:

- whether the entity is an issuer, non-issuer, government body, or recipient of government funding;
- whether PCAOB standards apply due to SEC registration or oversight;
- whether GAGAS, the yellow book, applies due to government audit requirements;
- whether ISAs apply as adopted in the relevant jurisdiction;
- whether any supplemental requirements from a regulator, lender, or grantor overlay the base standard;
- whether components or subsidiaries are subject to a different framework.

Document the applicable standard set on each engagement and confirm it at acceptance, continuance, and reporting. Applying the wrong framework is a foundational compliance failure.

### Track Standard Updates And Effective Dates Systematically

Standards evolve continuously, and each pronouncement has its own effective date and transition provisions. A firm without a tracking mechanism will drift into non-compliance.

Track by:

- maintaining a register of current standards and pending pronouncements;
- recording issuance dates, effective dates, and early-adoption provisions for each;
- assigning responsibility for monitoring updates from each standard-setter;
- mapping each update to the affected methodology, templates, and training;
- confirming the applicable version for each engagement's period and report date;
- reviewing the register at engagement acceptance and before report issuance.

An audit performed under a superseded standard, even one issued only months earlier, is non-compliant for its report date if the new standard was effective.

### Implement Quality Management Standards At The Firm Level

Quality management standards, such as ISQM 1, SAQM, or the equivalent, require the firm to operate a system of quality management rather than rely on ad hoc controls. The engagement sits inside this system.

Implement by:

- establishing quality objectives that address leadership, governance, ethics, engagement performance, and resources;
- identifying and assessing risks to achieving those objectives;
- designing and applying responses to the assessed risks;
- operating a monitoring and remediation process that detects and fixes deficiencies;
- evaluating the system at least annually and documenting the evaluation;
- assigning clear roles for the system's operation and oversight.

A compliant engagement in a firm without a functioning quality system is fragile and unlikely to withstand inspection.

### Apply Engagement Quality Review Where Required

Engagement quality review (EQR) provides an independent check on significant judgments and the conclusion before the report is issued. The standards define when EQR is mandatory and what it must cover.

Apply EQR by:

- identifying engagements that require EQR under the applicable standard or firm policy;
- selecting an EQR partner or reviewer who is independent and competent;
- ensuring the review occurs before the report is released, not after;
- focusing the review on significant judgments, risks, conclusions, and the report;
- documenting the EQR findings, objections raised, and their resolution;
- obtaining the EQR reviewer's sign-off as a condition of report release.

An EQR performed after issuance, or that merely ticks the file, does not satisfy the standard's intent.

### Embed Independence Within The Standards Framework

Independence is not only an ethical obligation; it is a requirement embedded in the auditing standards themselves. A breach of independence invalidates the engagement regardless of work quality.

Embed by:

- maintaining an independence monitoring system covering the firm and network;
- identifying restricted entities, affiliates, and covered persons;
- evaluating non-audit services and relationships for threats;
- applying safeguards where permitted and declining where not;
- re-evaluating independence at acceptance, during the engagement, and at the report date;
- documenting independence evaluations on each engagement file.

Independence must be active and current at the report date, not merely at planning.

### Meet Documentation Requirements As A Compliance Matter

Documentation is where compliance is proven. Standards require documentation that enables an experienced auditor to understand the work, evidence, and conclusions, and that demonstrates compliance with each relevant requirement.

Ensure documentation:

- shows the nature, timing, and extent of procedures performed;
- records the evidence obtained and the conclusions reached;
- demonstrates compliance with specific standard requirements, not generic references;
- captures significant judgments and the basis for them;
- meets retention periods required by the applicable standard;
- is prepared contemporaneously and reviewed before report release.

Work that was performed but not documented is, for compliance purposes, work that cannot be proven to have been performed.

### Distinguish Performance Compliance From Documentation Compliance

An engagement can be substantively sound yet documentation-deficient, or thoroughly documented yet substantively weak. Both are compliance failures, and inspection will catch either.

Guard against:

- performing the work but not documenting it to the required standard;
- documenting procedures that were not actually performed or were performed differently;
- over-relying on templates that create the appearance of compliance without substance;
- letting the documentation drive the work rather than the work driving the documentation;
- treating review notes as administrative rather than as evidence of quality control.

Compliance requires both the substance of the work and the documentation that proves it, consistently.

### Evaluate The Firm's Compliance Through Monitoring

A firm cannot claim compliance without monitoring it. Monitoring and remediation are required components of the quality system and the basis for any compliance assertion.

Evaluate by:

- performing internal inspections of selected engagements on a risk basis;
- reviewing the engagement files for adherence to the applicable standards;
- identifying root causes of deficiencies, not just symptoms;
- implementing remedial action and tracking it to completion;
- assessing the system's effectiveness at least annually;
- incorporating external inspection and peer review results.

A firm that does not monitor cannot know whether it is compliant, and a clean self-assessment without monitoring is not credible.

### Reconcile Multiple Frameworks On Cross-Framework Engagements

Some engagements, particularly group audits or multinational clients, span more than one framework. Reconciliation is required so that each component meets its own standard and the group meets the group standard.

Reconcile by:

- identifying the framework applicable to each component and to the group;
- mapping the differences in requirements across the frameworks;
- ensuring component work meets the more demanding requirement where they diverge;
- documenting the basis for relying on work performed under a different framework;
- confirming the group report conforms to the group's applicable standard.

Assuming that one framework satisfies another, without explicit reconciliation, is a compliance gap.

## Common Traps

### Applying The Wrong Standard Set

Defaulting to a familiar standard without confirming issuer status, government funding, or jurisdictional adoption leads to a non-compliant engagement from the outset.

### Drifting On Effective Dates

Failing to track when new standards become effective means performing engagements under superseded requirements. The register must be current and consulted at each report date.

### Treating Quality Management As Administrative

ISQM and equivalent standards are operative requirements, not paperwork. A firm without a functioning quality system cannot reliably produce compliant engagements.

### EQR As A Late Or Token Check

An EQR performed after report release, or that does not engage with significant judgments, does not satisfy the standard. It must be timely and substantive.

### Independence Evaluated Only At Planning

Independence can be impaired at any point, including by services provided mid-engagement. Re-evaluation at the report date is required.

### Documentation That Creates Appearance Without Substance

Templates filled in to look compliant, without underlying work, fail inspection. Substance and documentation must align.

### Monitoring That Does Not Drive Remediation

Identifying deficiencies without root-cause analysis and corrective action leaves the system non-compliant. Monitoring must close the loop.

### Assuming Framework Equivalence

GAAS, ISA, PCAOB, and GAGAS overlap but are not identical. Assuming one satisfies another without reconciliation produces silent non-compliance on cross-framework engagements.

## Self-Check

- Has the correct standard set, GAAS, ISA, PCAOB, GAGAS, or a combination, been identified and documented for the engagement, including any overlays?
- Is there a current register of standards and pending pronouncements with effective dates, consulted at acceptance and report issuance?
- Does the firm operate a quality management system, ISQM or SAQM, with objectives, risk assessment, responses, monitoring, and annual evaluation?
- Is engagement quality review applied where required, performed before report release by an independent reviewer, and documented with resolution of objections?
- Is independence monitored actively and re-evaluated at acceptance, during the engagement, and at the report date, with documentation on each file?
- Does the engagement documentation demonstrate compliance with specific standard requirements, prepared contemporaneously and meeting retention rules?
- Are substance and documentation aligned, with no template-driven appearance of compliance unsupported by underlying work?
- Is the firm's monitoring program performing risk-based inspections, identifying root causes, and completing remediation?
- On cross-framework engagements, are the differences reconciled so each component and the group meet their applicable standards?
- Could an external inspector or peer reviewer conclude, from the firm's system and the engagement file, that the work complies with the applicable standards in substance and form?
