# AICPA Standards

AICPA Generally Accepted Auditing Standards govern audits of non-issuer, private companies in the United States, and they are codified in the AU-C sections issued through Statements on Auditing Standards. The recurring failure is treating AICPA GAAS as interchangeable with PCAOB standards or ISA, when in fact each framework has distinct definitions, report formats, and requirements. An auditor who applies PCAOB language to a private company audit, or who assumes an ISA procedure satisfies an AU-C requirement, produces work that looks professional but is non-compliant. AICPA engagements also sit inside a broader professional ecosystem, the Code of Professional Conduct, peer review, and the SSAE attestation framework, and competence in GAAS requires understanding how these pieces fit together.

Use this skill when applying AICPA GAAS, performing a non-issuer audit, navigating the AU-C structure, evaluating compliance with the AICPA Code, preparing for peer review, or distinguishing AICPA work from PCAOB or ISA engagements. The goal is an engagement that is compliant with the correct framework and defensible under peer review.

## Core Rules

### Confirm The Engagement Is A Non-Issuer AICPA Engagement

The first decision is whether AICPA GAAS actually applies. Applying the wrong framework from the start contaminates the entire engagement, because report language, independence rules, and procedural requirements differ.

Confirm:

- whether the entity is an issuer subject to PCAOB oversight or a non-issuer under AICPA GAAS;
- whether the entity has registered securities or files with the SEC, which may trigger PCAOB standards;
- whether any subsidiary or component is subject to a different framework;
- whether the engagement is an audit, a review, a compilation, or an attestation engagement;
- whether a specific user, regulator, or lender has imposed additional requirements.

A private company audit defaults to AICPA GAAS, but confirm the issuer status before assuming so, because the consequences of error run through every later decision.

### Navigate The AU-C Structure Correctly

AICPA GAAS is organized in the AU-C sections, prefixed by number ranges for general principles, risk, evidence, reporting, and special considerations. Knowing the structure prevents citing the wrong section and missing a requirement.

Use the structure:

- AU-C 200s for general principles and responsibilities, including independence and quality;
- AU-C 300s for risk assessment and materiality;
- AU-C 400s and 500s for evidence, sampling, and specific balance areas;
- AU-C 600s for using the work of others and group audits;
- AU-C 700s for forming the opinion and the auditor's report;
- AU-C 800s for special considerations and special-purpose frameworks.

Cite the specific AU-C section that governs each procedure, and verify the current version, since SASs amend these sections over time.

### Track SAS Amendments And Effective Dates

GAAS is not static. Statements on Auditing Standards amend the AU-C sections, and each SAS has an effective date that may differ from its issuance date. Applying an outdated version is a compliance failure.

Track by:

- maintaining a current GAAS reference reflecting issued SASs and their effective dates;
- confirming the effective dates for the period under audit and the report date;
- identifying early-adoption provisions where relevant;
- checking for SASs that supersede or modify prior requirements;
- ensuring the firm's methodology and templates reflect the current GAAS.

An audit performed under superseded GAAS, even if well-executed, is non-compliant for its report date.

### Apply The AICPA Code Of Professional Conduct

GAAS does not stand alone; it sits within the AICPA Code of Professional Conduct, which governs independence, integrity, objectivity, and general standards. A GAAS-compliant audit performed by an auditor who lacks independence is invalid.

Apply the Code by:

- evaluating independence in fact and in appearance before and during the engagement;
- identifying non-audit services that may impair independence under the Code's rules;
- assessing threats such as self-review, advocacy, familiarity, undue influence, and financial self-interest;
- applying safeguards where permitted and disqualifying where not;
- confirming compliance with the general standards of professional competence, due care, and planning and supervision;
- documenting independence evaluations for the engagement.

Independence is not a one-time check; it must be maintained and re-evaluated throughout the engagement and at the report date.

### Distinguish AICPA GAAS From PCAOB And ISA

Because the three frameworks overlap in substance but diverge in detail, auditors who work across them frequently import requirements from the wrong one. Recognize the differences that matter.

Key distinctions:

- report format and required sections differ between AICPA GAAS, PCAOB, and ISA;
- definitions of materiality, related parties, and key audit matters are not identical;
- PCAOB standards impose additional requirements for issuers, including specific Form filings;
- ISA may require key audit matters and critical audit matters in circumstances where AICPA GAAS does not;
- independence rules differ in detail between the AICPA Code, SEC, and IFAC requirements;
- documentation and retention periods differ across frameworks.

When an auditor works under multiple frameworks, build a cross-reference that flags the differences rather than assuming convergence.

### Understand Peer Review Exposure

AICPA engagements are subject to peer review, and the firm's peer review program evaluates compliance with GAAS and the Code. Work that passes fieldwork but fails documentation or independence will surface in peer review.

Prepare for peer review by:

- ensuring the engagement file meets GAAS documentation standards;
- confirming independence evaluations are complete and current;
- maintaining a system of quality control consistent with the firm's peer review scope;
- selecting engagements for review that represent the firm's risk profile;
- responding to peer review findings with corrective action.

Peer review is not a separate exercise; it is a lens on whether the engagement was actually compliant, so the work should be peer-review-ready throughout.

### Recognize When SSAE Attestation Standards Apply

Not every assurance engagement is an audit. The Statements on Standards for Attestation Engagements govern examination, review, and agreed-upon procedures engagements that are not financial statement audits.

Distinguish by:

- confirming whether the engagement is an audit under GAAS or an attestation engagement under SSAE;
- using the correct practitioner's report format for examination, review, or agreed-upon procedures;
- applying the subject matter criteria appropriate to the attestation;
- recognizing that SSAE engagements have their own independence and evidence requirements;
- avoiding the temptation to apply audit procedures to a non-audit attestation or vice versa.

Misclassifying the engagement type leads to the wrong report and the wrong procedural framework.

### Address Quality Management And General Standards

AICPA GAAS and the related quality management standards govern the environment in which audits are performed. A compliant audit requires a compliant firm environment.

Address:

- the firm's system of quality management, including leadership, governance, and risk evaluation;
- engagement quality, including acceptance, continuance, performance, and completion;
- competence and capabilities of the engagement team;
- engagement performance, supervision, and review;
- ethical requirements, including independence;
- monitoring and remediation of the quality system.

A GAAS-compliant audit in a firm without an adequate quality system is fragile and will not withstand peer review or inspection.

### Form The Report Under AICPA GAAS

The AICPA auditor's report has a specific structure and required elements. Deviations from the required format are non-compliant regardless of the underlying work.

Confirm the report:

- includes the title, addressee, opinion, and basis for opinion sections;
- identifies the applicable framework and states whether it is a special-purpose framework;
- includes the auditor's responsibilities and the inherent limitations sections;
- addresses any modifications, whether qualified, adverse, or disclaimer;
- adds emphasis-of-matter or other-matter paragraphs only where AU-C criteria are met;
- is signed, dated, and includes the location, consistent with current GAAS.

Use the current report template, because SAS amendments have changed required elements over time, and an outdated template is a compliance failure.

## Common Traps

### Applying PCAOB Or ISA Requirements To A Non-Issuer Audit

Importing PCAOB report language or ISA key audit matters into an AICPA engagement produces a non-compliant report. Confirm the framework before drafting.

### Citing Outdated AU-C Sections

GAAS evolves through SASs, and superseded sections may still appear in old templates. Verify the current version effective for the report date.

### Treating Independence As A Single Checkbox

Independence under the AICPA Code requires evaluation of threats and safeguards throughout the engagement. A one-time sign-off does not satisfy the requirement.

### Confusing Audits With SSAE Attestations

Applying audit procedures and an audit report to an agreed-upon procedures or examination engagement is a framework error. Match the engagement type to the correct standard.

### Ignoring Effective Dates Of New SASs

A new SAS may be issued but not yet effective, or effective but not yet reflected in templates. Applying the wrong version for the period under audit is non-compliant.

### Assuming GAAS, PCAOB, And ISA Are Interchangeable

The frameworks converge in substance but diverge in detail. Assuming a procedure or report element from one satisfies another leads to silent non-compliance.

### Letting Documentation Fall Below Peer Review Standards

Work that is substantively adequate but poorly documented will fail peer review. Documentation must meet GAAS regardless of how sound the fieldwork was.

### Overlooking Quality Management Requirements

GAAS audits require a supporting quality system. Treating quality management as a separate administrative matter disconnects the engagement from its compliance foundation.

## Self-Check

- Has it been confirmed that the engagement is a non-issuer audit governed by AICPA GAAS, with no PCAOB or SEC overlay?
- Are procedures and citations mapped to the correct current AU-C sections, with SAS effective dates verified for the period and report date?
- Is the firm's GAAS reference and methodology current, reflecting all issued and effective SASs?
- Has independence under the AICPA Code been evaluated in fact and appearance, with threats and safeguards documented throughout the engagement?
- Are the differences between AICPA GAAS, PCAOB, and ISA understood, with a cross-reference used where the auditor works across frameworks?
- Is the engagement file peer-review-ready, meeting GAAS documentation and quality control standards?
- Has the engagement type been correctly classified as an audit or an SSAE attestation, with the matching report and procedures applied?
- Does the firm's system of quality management support the engagement, with leadership, risk evaluation, and monitoring in place?
- Does the auditor's report conform to the current AICPA GAAS format, with required sections, signatures, and any modifications per AU-C?
- Could a peer reviewer conclude from the file that the engagement complies with AICPA GAAS and the Code in substance and in form?
