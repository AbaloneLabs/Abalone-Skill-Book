# GAGAS (Yellow Book) Standards

Government Auditing Standards, known as GAGAS or the Yellow Book, issued by the U.S. Government Accountability Office (GAO), govern audits of government entities and of recipients of federal financial assistance. GAGAS incorporates AICPA GAAS for financial audits but imposes additional requirements in the areas of independence, ethics, quality control, continuing professional education, and reporting. The recurring failure is treating a GAGAS audit as equivalent to a standard GAAS audit with minor reporting additions, without recognizing that GAGAS imposes substantive additional requirements throughout the engagement. For recipients of federal assistance, GAGAS audits often take the form of single audits under the Uniform Guidance, which layer further requirements on top. The auditor must apply the correct framework, meet its additional requirements, and navigate the relationship between GAGAS, the Single Audit Act, and the Uniform Guidance.

Use this skill when applying GAGAS to a government or federally-funded engagement, when handling GAGAS-specific reporting, and when navigating the single audit requirements. The goal is an audit that applies GAGAS correctly and meets its additional requirements, alongside any single audit obligations.

## Core Rules

### Confirm The Engagement Requires GAGAS

The first step is to confirm that the engagement requires GAGAS. GAGAS applies to audits of government entities, of recipients of federal financial assistance subject to the Single Audit Act, and where required by law, regulation, or agreement.

Confirm by:

- determining whether the entity is a government entity or a recipient of federal assistance subject to single audit;
- identifying whether law, regulation, or agreement requires GAGAS;
- recognizing that GAGAS may be required even where the entity is not a traditional government body, such as for certain non-profits receiving federal funds;
- documenting the basis for applying GAGAS.

Applying standard GAAS to an engagement that requires GAGAS leaves the additional requirements unmet.

### Apply The Additional Independence Requirements

GAGAS imposes independence requirements that go beyond AICPA GAAS, including a conceptual framework for evaluating threats, specific prohibitions, and requirements for both the firm and individual auditors.

Apply by:

- applying the GAGAS conceptual framework to identify and evaluate threats to independence;
- recognizing GAGAS-specific prohibitions, which may be more restrictive than AICPA in areas such as non-audit services;
- ensuring both the firm and individual auditors meet GAGAS independence requirements, including the absence of impairing interests and relationships;
- documenting the independence evaluation under GAGAS, not merely under AICPA.

Treating GAGAS independence as equivalent to AICPA understates the requirements and risks impairment.

### Meet The Continuing Professional Education Requirements

GAGAS requires auditors performing GAGAS engagements to complete specific continuing professional education (CPE), including a specified number of hours in the direct completion of GAGAS subjects over a defined period.

Meet by:

- ensuring each auditor performing GAGAS work meets the CPE requirements, including the GAGAS-specific hours;
- tracking CPE completion and retaining documentation;
- ensuring the firm's CPE tracking system identifies GAGAS-qualified personnel;
- not assigning personnel who do not meet the CPE requirements to GAGAS engagements.

Assigning personnel without the required GAGAS CPE compromises the engagement's compliance.

### Apply The Quality Control Requirements

GAGAS imposes quality control requirements on firms performing GAGAS engagements, including a system of quality control, leadership, and monitoring. These requirements are in addition to AICPA quality control standards.

Apply by:

- maintaining a system of quality control that addresses GAGAS requirements, including leadership, independence, and engagement performance;
- establishing policies and procedures that address GAGAS-specific quality elements;
- monitoring the system's effectiveness, including through internal inspections;
- documenting the quality control system and its operation.

A quality control system designed only for AICPA may not meet GAGAS requirements.

### Apply GAGAS-Specific Reporting Requirements

GAGAS reporting differs from standard GAAS in specific respects. The auditor's report must identify that the audit was conducted in accordance with GAGAS, and GAGAS requires reporting on internal control over financial reporting and on compliance.

Apply by:

- stating in the auditor's report that the audit was conducted in accordance with GAGAS, in addition to AICPA GAAS;
- reporting on internal control over financial reporting, including significant deficiencies and material weaknesses identified;
- reporting on compliance with laws, regulations, and provisions of contracts or grant agreements that could have a material effect on the financial statements;
- issuing the GAGAS report in the prescribed form, including any required separate reports or combined presentation.

Omitting the GAGAS reporting elements, or using a standard GAAS report, fails the requirements.

### Navigate The Single Audit Requirements

For recipients of federal assistance meeting expenditure thresholds, the audit is a single audit under the Uniform Guidance, which layers requirements on top of GAGAS, including testing of major programs, compliance, and reporting.

Navigate by:

- determining whether the engagement is a single audit, based on federal expenditure thresholds;
- identifying major programs using the risk-based approach required by the Uniform Guidance;
- testing compliance and internal control over the major programs;
- issuing the single audit reporting package, including the schedule of expenditures of federal awards and the data collection form;
- meeting the reporting deadlines, which may be shorter than for a standard audit.

Treating a single audit as a standard GAGAS audit, without the Uniform Guidance requirements, leaves major program compliance untested.

### Coordinate With The Federal Oversight And Pass-Through Entities

Single audits serve federal oversight and pass-through entities that provide the federal funding. The auditor must coordinate reporting and communication with these entities.

Coordinate by:

- issuing the reporting package in the form required for submission to the Federal Audit Clearinghouse;
- communicating findings to pass-through entities where required;
- meeting the submission deadlines, which affect the entity's continued eligibility for federal funding;
- ensuring the reporting package is complete and accurate for federal oversight purposes.

Failure to meet single audit reporting and submission requirements can affect the entity's federal funding eligibility.

## Common Traps

### Treating GAGAS As Standard GAAS With Minor Reporting Additions

GAGAS imposes substantive additional requirements throughout the engagement. Treating it as GAAS-plus-reporting understates the obligations.

### Applying AICPA Independence Without The GAGAS Framework

GAGAS independence is more restrictive. Applying only AICPA risks impairment.

### Assigning Personnel Without GAGAS CPE

GAGAS requires specific CPE. Assigning personnel without it compromises compliance.

### Using A Standard GAAS Report

GAGAS reporting requires specific elements, including the GAGAS statement and internal control and compliance reporting.

### Treating A Single Audit As A Standard GAGAS Audit

Single audits layer Uniform Guidance requirements. Treating them as standard GAGAS leaves major program compliance untested.

### Missing Single Audit Reporting And Submission Deadlines

Single audit reporting has deadlines tied to funding eligibility. Missing them affects the entity's federal funding.

### Overlooking Quality Control Requirements

GAGAS quality control is in addition to AICPA. A system designed only for AICPA may not meet GAGAS.

## Self-Check

- Is it confirmed that the engagement requires GAGAS, with the basis documented?
- Are the additional GAGAS independence requirements applied, including the conceptual framework and specific prohibitions, beyond AICPA?
- Do all assigned personnel meet the GAGAS CPE requirements, including the GAGAS-specific hours?
- Does the firm's quality control system address GAGAS requirements, in addition to AICPA?
- Are GAGAS-specific reporting requirements applied, including the GAGAS statement and reporting on internal control and compliance?
- Where the engagement is a single audit, are the Uniform Guidance requirements navigated, including major program identification, compliance testing, and the reporting package?
- Are reporting and submission coordinated with federal oversight and pass-through entities, with deadlines met?
- Could an independent reviewer confirm that GAGAS is applied correctly and that any single audit requirements are met?
- Is the application free of treating GAGAS as standard GAAS-plus-reporting, applying only AICPA independence, or using a standard GAAS report?
- Are single audit requirements, where applicable, addressed rather than overlooked, with reporting and submission deadlines met?
