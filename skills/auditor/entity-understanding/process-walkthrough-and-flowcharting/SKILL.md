# Process Walkthrough And Flowcharting

A walkthrough is the auditor's primary method for confirming how a process actually works. It traces a single transaction or activity from initiation through processing, approval, recording, and reporting, and it confirms where the key controls sit, who performs them, what evidence they leave, and where the gaps are. Done well, a walkthrough reveals design weaknesses, unrecorded workarounds, system handoffs that lose information, and points where fraud or error could enter undetected. Done poorly, it becomes a transcription of the policy manual that tells the auditor nothing about reality. Agents frequently treat walkthroughs as a box to tick rather than as the diagnostic step that drives risk assessment and control testing.

Use this skill before testing controls, before finalizing the risk assessment for a process, and whenever a process, system, or handoff needs to be understood and documented. The goal is to produce a defensible record of the real process and a flowchart precise enough to locate where things could go wrong.

## Core Rules

### Define The Walkthrough Scope And Objective

A walkthrough is not a general chat about a department. It targets a specific process, cycle, or transaction type and a specific objective.

Before starting, define:

- the process or cycle in scope, such as order-to-cash, procure-to-pay, payroll, close, or grant disbursement;
- the transaction class or population to trace;
- the assertions or risks the walkthrough must inform;
- the period the walkthrough covers;
- whether it supports a controls-reliance approach or only risk assessment;
- the level of detail required, end-to-end or focused on a high-risk segment.

A walkthrough with no defined objective tends to produce a narrative that cannot be linked back to risk or to planned procedures.

### Trace One Real Transaction End To End

The most reliable walkthrough follows an actual transaction from start to finish rather than asking for a generic description. Select a real item and follow it.

For the selected transaction, observe and document:

- how it was initiated and by whom;
- what system or form captured it;
- what approvals were required and obtained;
- how it was processed and posted;
- what controls touched it and when;
- what evidence each step left behind;
- how it flowed to the general ledger and to reporting;
- where handoffs between people, systems, or departments occurred;
- how exceptions were handled;
- how it appeared in period-end balances and disclosures.

A real transaction exposes manual workarounds, spreadsheet bridges, and informal approvals that a policy description hides. If the walkthrough cannot be completed because a step is unclear, that is itself a finding about the process.

### Confirm The Actual Process, Not The Documented One

Policies describe the intended process. Walkthroughs exist to confirm what actually happens. The gap between the two is where risk concentrates.

Probe for:

- steps performed differently from the written procedure;
- manual workarounds for system limitations;
- informal approvals by email or verbal sign-off;
- spreadsheets or local databases that bridge systems;
- controls that are bypassed under time pressure;
- segregation-of-duties compromises when staff are absent;
- system-generated reports that are manually adjusted before use;
- exceptions that are handled outside the normal workflow;
- recent changes not yet reflected in documentation.

When the actual process differs from the documented one, record the actual process and assess whether the controls still address the risk. A control that exists only in the policy is not operating.

### Identify The Controls Embedded In The Process

As the transaction is traced, mark where controls occur and what each control does.

For each control, capture:

- the risk or assertion it addresses;
- the process step where it sits;
- who performs it and their independence;
- whether it is preventive or detective;
- whether it is manual, system-automated, or IT-dependent;
- its frequency and timing;
- the population it covers;
- the evidence it produces;
- what happens when an exception is found;
- whether it depends on another control or report.

This control inventory becomes the basis for selecting key controls to test. A walkthrough that does not locate controls cannot support a controls-reliance approach.

### Document Handoffs And System Boundaries

Most errors and fraud enter at handoffs, where information moves between people, departments, or systems. Map them explicitly.

Common handoff risk points:

- between front-office initiation and back-office processing;
- between an operating system and the general ledger;
- between a spreadsheet and a core system;
- between an entity and a service organization or outsourcer;
- between subsidiaries and the consolidation process;
- between local books and statutory or tax reporting;
- between manual data entry and automated validation;
- between exception handling and the normal workflow.

At each handoff, ask whether data is complete, accurate, authorized, and timely, and whether any control reconciles the two sides. Unreconciled handoffs are a prime source of undetected error.

### Produce A Flowchart That Matches The Narrative

A flowchart makes the process and its controls visible at a glance. It should be consistent with the walkthrough narrative and precise enough to locate risk.

A useful flowchart shows:

- the start and end points of the process;
- each significant step in sequence;
- decision points and approval gates;
- systems and forms used at each step;
- where controls occur, marked distinctly;
- handoffs between people, departments, or systems;
- exception paths, not only the happy path;
- evidence generated at each step;
- the link to the general ledger and reporting.

Use standard shapes and keep it readable. A flowchart that only shows the happy path, or that omits handoffs and exceptions, misrepresents the process and hides risk. Update the flowchart when the process changes.

### Link The Walkthrough To Risk And Assertions

The walkthrough is not an end in itself. Its output must drive risk assessment and procedure design.

For each significant step or handoff, identify:

- what could go wrong and which assertion is affected;
- whether a control addresses that risk and how well;
- whether the risk is inherent, fraud-related, or system-driven;
- whether the step concentrates judgment, estimate, or override;
- whether the step depends on information produced by the entity;
- whether the step is a candidate for substantive or controls-based testing.

This linkage turns the walkthrough from documentation into analysis. Without it, the walkthrough sits unused while risk is assessed from assumptions.

### Corroborate Through Inquiry At Multiple Levels

A walkthrough based only on a manager's description can miss what actually happens at the desk. Corroborate by speaking to the people who perform each step.

Corroborate by:

- inquiring of the person who initiates the transaction;
- inquiring of the person who approves or reviews;
- inquiring of the person who reconciles or posts;
- observing the system in use where possible;
- inspecting the actual evidence for the traced transaction;
- comparing the narrative to system logs or audit trails;
- cross-checking with a second transaction to test consistency.

Discrepancies between levels are diagnostic. If the clerk describes a workaround the manager never mentioned, the documented process is incomplete.

### Document And Retain The Walkthrough Evidence

The walkthrough must be documented well enough that another auditor could understand the process, locate the controls, and see how risk was assessed.

Retain:

- the selected transaction and its identifiers;
- the step-by-step narrative with owners and systems;
- copies or references to the evidence inspected;
- the flowchart;
- the list of controls identified with their attributes;
- the handoffs and reconciliation points;
- the risks and assertions linked to each step;
- notes on gaps between documented and actual process;
- the names and roles of people interviewed;
- the date and period covered.

A walkthrough with no retained evidence cannot be reviewed or relied upon and provides weak support for a controls-reliance conclusion.

## Common Traps

### Transcribing The Policy Instead Of Walking The Process

Reading the procedure manual and writing it up produces a description of the intended process, not the real one. Always trace an actual transaction and confirm what happens.

### Walking Only The Happy Path

Following a clean, routine transaction hides the exception paths, workarounds, and error handling where problems concentrate. Deliberately inquire about exceptions and edge cases.

### Stopping The Walkthrough When A Step Is Unclear

If a step cannot be explained, that is a finding, not a reason to move on. Unclear handoffs and unexplained reconciliations are exactly where risk hides.

### Omitting Handoffs And System Boundaries

Flowcharts that jump from one department to another without showing the handoff conceal unreconciled data movements. Map every boundary and ask what reconciles it.

### Treating The Walkthrough As Testing

A walkthrough confirms understanding and design. It usually does not prove that a control operated throughout the period. Do not conclude operating effectiveness from a single walkthrough.

### Ignoring IT-Dependent Controls

Many controls rely on system reports, automated validations, or interfaces. If the walkthrough does not address how the system produces and protects that information, the control understanding is incomplete.

### Failing To Update The Walkthrough After Change

Processes change through system migrations, reorganizations, and new products. A walkthrough from a prior period that is not refreshed can describe a process that no longer exists.

### Disconnecting The Walkthrough From Risk Assessment

A beautifully documented process that never feeds into identified risks and planned procedures is wasted effort. Every significant step should inform a risk or a testing decision.

## Self-Check

- Is the walkthrough scope and objective defined, including the process, transaction class, assertions, and period?
- Is a real transaction traced end to end from initiation through posting to reporting, with evidence inspected at each step?
- Does the walkthrough confirm the actual process, including workarounds, spreadsheets, and informal approvals, rather than only the documented policy?
- Is each control located in the process with its risk, owner, type, frequency, evidence, and exception handling captured?
- Are handoffs between people, departments, and systems mapped, with reconciliation or lack of it identified at each boundary?
- Does the flowchart show steps, decision points, controls, handoffs, exception paths, and evidence, consistent with the narrative?
- Is each significant step linked to a risk and assertion, and to a decision about substantive or controls-based testing?
- Has the walkthrough been corroborated through inquiry and observation at multiple levels, not only with a manager?
- Is the walkthrough evidence retained, including the transaction, narrative, flowchart, controls, risks, and people interviewed?
- Has the walkthrough been refreshed for process or system changes so that it describes the process as it currently operates?
