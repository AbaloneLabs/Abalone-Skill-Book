# Internal Audit Role

Internal audit can be a powerful ally to the external auditor, but only if it is competent, objective, and independent. When those conditions hold, the external auditor can leverage internal audit's coverage, coordinate testing, and avoid duplication. When they do not, internal audit's work cannot be relied upon, and treating it as if it could leads to gaps in coverage that look filled but are not. Agents often accept internal audit reports at face value, or conversely ignore internal audit entirely, both of which are errors. The disciplined approach is to evaluate internal audit as a function, decide deliberately what (if anything) to rely on, and document the basis for that decision.

Use this skill when evaluating the internal audit function, when deciding whether to rely on internal audit work, and when coordinating coverage. The goal is to leverage internal audit where it is reliable and to avoid relying on it where it is not.

## Core Rules

### Evaluate Internal Audit's Competence

Competence determines whether internal audit's work can be relied upon. Assess the function's capability before relying on any of its work.

Assess competence by considering:

- the professional qualifications and certifications of internal audit staff;
- their experience with the entity, the industry, and the specific areas tested;
- the methodology and standards internal audit follows, such as IIA standards;
- the quality of internal audit's working papers and reports;
- the training and professional development provided;
- the function's resources relative to its scope.

Document the competence assessment. A function that lacks the qualifications or methodology to perform reliable work cannot be relied upon, regardless of how active it appears.

### Evaluate Internal Audit's Objectivity And Independence

Objectivity is as important as competence. Internal audit must be able to assess areas without management influence to produce work the external auditor can use.

Assess objectivity by considering:

- to whom internal audit reports, ideally the audit committee, not management;
- whether internal audit's plan is set independently or directed by management;
- whether internal audit staff are free to report findings without fear of reprisal;
- whether internal audit assesses areas where management has incentive to hide issues;
- whether internal audit rotates staff to avoid familiarity threats;
- whether internal audit performs operational tasks that compromise its independence.

A function that reports to management, follows a management-directed plan, or performs operational duties is not sufficiently objective for external audit reliance.

### Decide Whether And How To Rely On Internal Audit Work

Reliance is a deliberate decision, not an automatic one. Decide what, if anything, to rely on based on the competence and objectivity assessment.

Options for reliance:

- use internal audit's work as a factor in risk assessment and understanding, without relying on it for direct evidence;
- rely on internal audit's testing of a specific area, after evaluating the work and adjusting scope;
- coordinate testing so internal audit and external audit cover complementary areas without duplication;
- direct internal audit to perform specific procedures for the external audit, with the external auditor controlling scope and reviewing results;
- rely on internal audit's monitoring and follow-up of control remediation.

For any reliance, document the evaluation of the specific work, not just the function. A reliable function can produce unreliable work if the scope or execution was weak.

### Evaluate The Specific Work Before Relying On It

Even a competent and objective function produces work of varying quality. Evaluate the specific work before relying on it.

Evaluate the specific work by considering:

- whether the scope addresses the external auditor's objective and assertion;
- whether the population was complete and the sample adequate;
- whether the procedures were appropriate and sufficiently rigorous;
- whether exceptions were properly evaluated and followed up;
- whether the documentation supports the conclusion;
- whether the conclusion is consistent with the external auditor's other evidence.

If the specific work does not meet these tests, do not rely on it, even if the function is generally sound. Adjust the scope or perform the work independently.

### Coordinate To Avoid Duplication And Leverage Coverage

Where internal audit is reliable, coordination improves efficiency and coverage. The goal is complementary, not duplicated, effort.

Coordinate by:

- discussing internal audit's plan and the external audit plan to identify overlaps and gaps;
- assigning specific areas or locations to internal audit where its coverage is reliable;
- having the external auditor focus on significant risks and judgmental areas;
- scheduling work so internal audit's results are available when the external auditor needs them;
- agreeing on documentation standards so internal audit's workpapers are usable.

Document the coordination and the division of responsibility. Duplication wastes effort; uncoordinated gaps leave areas uncovered.

### Consider The Effect Of Internal Audit On Risk Assessment

A strong internal audit function can lower the assessed risk in areas it covers reliably, while a weak or absent function leaves risk unmitigated.

Consider:

- whether internal audit's ongoing monitoring supports a lower control risk in covered areas;
- whether internal audit's findings reveal control weaknesses that raise risk;
- whether the absence of internal audit in a high-risk area leaves it unmonitored;
- whether internal audit's presence deters fraud and override through its activity.

Use the internal audit assessment to calibrate risk, but never let it substitute for the external auditor's own procedures on significant risks.

### Maintain The External Auditor's Responsibility

The external auditor cannot shed responsibility by relying on internal audit. The opinion remains the external auditor's, and reliance is a decision about evidence, not about accountability.

Maintain responsibility by:

- making the reliance decision deliberately and documenting it;
- evaluating both the function and the specific work;
- not relying on internal audit for significant risks without the external auditor's own procedures;
- reviewing internal audit's work with the same skepticism as any other evidence;
- being willing to override internal audit's conclusions when the external auditor's evidence differs.

Reliance is a tool for efficiency and coverage, not a transfer of the audit obligation.

## Common Traps

### Accepting Internal Audit Reports At Face Value

A report is a conclusion, not evidence of its own reliability. Evaluate the function and the specific work before relying on it.

### Ignoring Internal Audit Entirely

Even where internal audit cannot be relied upon for direct evidence, its reports, findings, and risk assessments inform the external auditor's understanding and risk assessment.

### Relying Without Evaluating Objectivity

A competent function that reports to management or follows a management-directed plan is not objective enough for reliance.

### Relying On The Function But Not The Specific Work

A reliable function can produce unreliable work. Evaluate the specific scope, population, procedures, and documentation before relying.

### Duplicating Internal Audit's Work Unnecessarily

Where internal audit is reliable, coordination avoids duplication and frees the external auditor to focus on significant risks.

### Letting Internal Audit Substitute For External Procedures On Significant Risks

Significant risks require the external auditor's own tailored procedures. Internal audit can supplement but not replace them.

### Forgetting That Reliance Does Not Transfer Responsibility

The external auditor's opinion remains their own. Reliance is a decision about evidence, not about accountability.

## Self-Check

- Is internal audit's competence assessed, including qualifications, experience, methodology, working paper quality, and resources?
- Is internal audit's objectivity and independence assessed, including reporting line, plan independence, freedom from reprisal, and absence of operational duties?
- Is the decision to rely on internal audit deliberate, with the basis documented, rather than automatic?
- Is the specific work evaluated before reliance, including scope, population, procedures, exception handling, documentation, and consistency with other evidence?
- Is coverage coordinated to avoid duplication and leverage internal audit's reliable areas, with the division of responsibility documented?
- Is the effect of internal audit on risk assessment considered, including where its presence supports lower risk and where its absence leaves risk unmitigated?
- Is the external auditor's responsibility maintained, with reliance treated as an evidence decision and significant risks still receiving the external auditor's own procedures?
- Could the auditor explain, from the documentation, what internal audit work was relied on, why, and how it was evaluated?
