# The 3E Framework

The economy, efficiency, and effectiveness (3E) framework is the foundational structure of performance and value-for-money auditing. Each E asks a distinct question: economy asks whether inputs were acquired at the right cost, efficiency asks whether inputs were converted to outputs with minimal waste, and effectiveness asks whether outputs achieved the intended outcomes. The central judgment problem is that the three dimensions can conflict and trade off against one another, and agents frequently collapse them into a single "value" judgment, optimize one E at the expense of another, or apply financial-audit materiality logic where outcome-based reasoning is required.

## Core Rules

### Define each E precisely for the engagement before measuring

The three dimensions sound intuitive but require operational definition for each program:

- **Economy** — acquiring inputs of the appropriate quality and quantity at the lowest reasonable cost, at the right time and place. Focus on input procurement.
- **Efficiency** — the relationship between inputs consumed and outputs produced; maximizing output per unit of input, or minimizing input per unit of output. Focus on the input-to-output conversion process.
- **Effectiveness** — the extent to which objectives are achieved and intended outcomes realized. Focus on the output-to-outcome relationship.

Write the operational definition into the audit criteria. A vague definition ("spend wisely") produces untestable conclusions.

### Establish measurable criteria for each dimension

Each E requires criteria against which performance is assessed. Criteria may be:

- absolute targets from legislation, regulation, or approved plans;
- benchmarks from comparable programs, prior periods, or peer entities;
- professional or technical standards;
- internally set performance commitments.

State the source and authority of each criterion. Avoid criteria that are merely the entity's own unverified aspirations unless no external benchmark exists, in which case document the limitation. Criteria must be specific enough that a reader can see exactly what "good" looks like.

### Separate the three dimensions in analysis, integrate them in conclusion

Analyze each E independently first. A program may be economical (cheap inputs) yet inefficient (wasteful conversion) and ineffective (poor outcomes). Or it may be effective yet uneconomical. Only after assessing each dimension separately should you consider the integrated picture, because trade-offs are meaningful only when the individual dimensions are clear. Conflating them produces conclusions like "good value" that obscure where the real performance issues lie.

### Handle the trade-offs between dimensions explicitly

The three Es frequently conflict:

- buying the cheapest inputs (economy) may reduce output quality and undermine effectiveness;
- maximizing output per input (efficiency) may sacrifice outcomes if outputs are of the wrong kind;
- achieving outcomes (effectiveness) may require premium inputs that hurt economy.

Identify which trade-offs the program design intended and which are unintended consequences. Do not declare a failure on one E without considering whether it was an accepted cost of success on another. Report the trade-off, do not hide it.

### Distinguish outputs from outcomes rigorously

Effectiveness is about outcomes, not outputs. Counting services delivered, beneficiaries reached, or units produced measures outputs, not whether the program's purpose was achieved. Establish the causal or contributory chain from inputs to outputs to intermediate outcomes to final outcomes. Recognize that outcomes are often influenced by external factors beyond the program's control; attribute outcomes carefully and avoid claiming full credit or blame where multiple causes are at play.

### Choose indicators that match the dimension being assessed

A common error is using an output measure to assess economy or an input measure to assess effectiveness. Map each indicator to the correct E:

- economy indicators: unit cost, price variance, procurement savings, input quality conformance;
- efficiency indicators: cost per unit of output, output per staff hour, cycle time, resource utilization rates;
- effectiveness indicators: outcome achievement rates, target attainment, beneficiary impact, long-term effect measures.

Mixed indicators produce category errors and misleading conclusions.

### Address data availability and quality for each dimension

The three Es often differ in data difficulty. Economy data (prices, quantities) is usually available from procurement and finance systems. Efficiency data requires reliable output measurement, which may be weak. Effectiveness data requires outcome measurement, which is often incomplete, delayed, or confounded by external factors. Assess data quality for each dimension separately and qualify conclusions where data is weak. Do not let data availability silently determine which dimensions are reported.

### Consider equity and sustainability as potential fourth dimensions

Many modern performance audit frameworks extend the 3Es with equity (fairness of distribution) and sustainability (environmental and intergenerational impact). Determine whether the engagement mandate requires these additional dimensions. If the program has significant distributional or environmental effects, excluding them may itself be a limitation worth reporting.

## Common Traps

- **Collapsing the 3Es into a single value judgment.** Reporting "good value for money" without showing which dimensions performed well and which poorly.
- **Output-as-outcome confusion.** Measuring effectiveness using output counts (services delivered) rather than outcome achievement (problem solved).
- **Optimizing one E at the expense of others.** Praising economy while ignoring that cheap inputs caused effectiveness failure.
- **Criteria sourced from aspiration.** Using the entity's own unverified targets as criteria without external benchmark or validation.
- **Data-availability bias.** Over-reporting economy (easy data) and under-reporting effectiveness (hard data), skewing the conclusion.
- **Ignoring trade-offs.** Reporting a dimension as a failure without acknowledging it was an accepted cost of success elsewhere.
- **Indicator-category errors.** Using an input or output measure to assess a dimension it does not represent.
- **External-factor blindness.** Attributing outcome changes wholly to the program without considering confounding external causes.
- **Static snapshot.** Assessing a single year without trend, hiding whether performance is improving or deteriorating.
- **Equity and sustainability omission.** Ignoring distributional or environmental effects that materially affect the program's overall performance story.

## Self-Check

- Have I written operational definitions of economy, efficiency, and effectiveness specific to this program, not generic dictionary definitions?
- Is each dimension assessed against measurable criteria with a documented source and authority?
- Did I analyze each E independently before integrating, so that trade-offs are visible rather than hidden in a blended conclusion?
- Are my effectiveness indicators measuring outcomes (not outputs), and have I addressed external confounding factors in outcome attribution?
- Does each indicator map correctly to the dimension it is used to assess, with no category errors?
- Have I assessed and reported data quality limitations for each dimension, especially the harder-to-measure effectiveness data?
- Have I explicitly identified and reported trade-offs between the dimensions rather than treating one E's failure as unconditional?
- If the program has material equity or sustainability effects, have I considered whether to extend beyond the 3Es and reported any decision to exclude them?
