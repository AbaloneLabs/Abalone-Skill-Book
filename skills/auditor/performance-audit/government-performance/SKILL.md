# Government Performance Auditing

Government performance auditing holds public sector entities accountable for the economical, efficient, and effective use of public resources and the achievement of intended outcomes. It differs from private-sector performance review because the resources are public, the accountability flows to citizens and legislatures, and the objectives are mandated by statute or policy rather than shareholder value. The central judgment problem is that government objectives are often politically contested, multiple, and deliberately broad, making it hard to define "success" cleanly; agents frequently import private-sector logic, avoid politically sensitive findings, or produce reports that describe activity without rendering judgment on public value.

## Core Rules

### Anchor the audit in the public accountability mandate

Government performance audits exist to serve the public and its elected representatives, not management. The primary accountability relationship is to the legislature and, through it, to citizens. This shapes every decision: scope selection should reflect public significance, evidence standards must withstand public and political scrutiny, and reporting must be accessible to non-specialist readers. Do not allow the audited entity's preferences to determine what is reported or how.

### Define objectives against the mandated purpose, not political preferences

Government programs exist to achieve objectives set in legislation, regulation, or approved policy. Establish the authoritative objective before assessing performance. Where objectives are broad or contested:

- cite the enabling legislation or policy that defines the program's purpose;
- identify any formal performance targets or service standards;
- acknowledge where objectives are multiple or in tension and assess against all of them;
- avoid substituting the auditor's preferred objective or a political faction's interpretation for the mandated one.

Report where objectives are unclear, outdated, or contradictory, because that is itself a performance issue, but do not resolve political disputes by audit fiat.

### Apply the public-sector materiality lens

Materiality in government performance audit is broader than financial. Consider:

- the scale of public expenditure involved;
- the number and vulnerability of affected citizens;
- the salience of the issue to Parliament or legislature;
- the risk to public safety, rights, or trust;
- whether the issue reflects systemic weakness versus isolated error.

A program may be financially small yet highly material because it serves vulnerable populations or touches fundamental rights. Conversely, a large program with minor process weaknesses may be less material than its budget suggests. Set materiality against public significance, not just dollars.

### Navigate political sensitivity without abandoning judgment

Government performance audits often touch politically charged topics. Maintain independence by:

- grounding findings in evidence and criteria, not political alignment;
- applying consistent standards regardless of which party is in government;
- avoiding language that could be read as partisan endorsement or attack;
- presenting the government's response and planned action fairly;
- being willing to report adverse findings even when politically inconvenient.

Independence does not mean avoiding difficult topics; it means addressing them with evidence and balance. Self-censorship to avoid political friction betrays the public accountability mandate.

### Assess stewardship of public resources specifically

A core government audit theme is stewardship: whether public money, assets, and authority have been used for the purposes authorized and protected from waste, loss, or misuse. Evaluate:

- whether spending was within appropriation and authorized purpose;
- whether assets are safeguarded and maintained;
- whether authority (regulatory, licensing, procurement) was exercised properly;
- whether conflicts of interest and integrity risks were managed.

Stewardship failures are particularly serious in the public sector because they breach the trust placed in public officials.

### Report for the public and the legislature, not just management

Government performance audit reports have multiple audiences with different needs:

- **the legislature** needs clear findings and recommendations to inform oversight and action;
- **the public** needs accessible language to understand how public resources were used;
- **the audited entity** needs specific, actionable recommendations to improve;
- **the media** needs accurate, quotable content to report responsibly.

Write for all of these. Avoid jargon, explain technical concepts, use visual summaries, and ensure the headline findings are unambiguous. A report that only specialists can interpret fails the public accountability function.

### Evaluate equity and access in public service delivery

Government services carry a particular obligation to serve all eligible citizens fairly. Assess whether services reach intended beneficiaries, whether access barriers disadvantage particular groups, and whether outcomes are equitable across populations. In government performance audit, equity is not an optional add-on; it is often central to whether the program is achieving its public purpose. Disaggregate findings by relevant demographics.

### Consider the interaction with the broader public sector system

Government programs rarely operate in isolation. They interact with other programs, levels of government, and delivery partners. Assess coherence and coordination: does the program duplicate or conflict with others, are handoffs between agencies effective, does fragmented governance undermine outcomes? System-level findings are often more valuable than entity-level ones because they point to structural reform.

### Follow up on prior recommendations and commitments

Government performance audit has a longitudinal dimension. Track whether prior audit recommendations were implemented, whether committed remediation actually delivered results, and whether problems persist. Repeated findings on the same issue across audits signal systemic failure that warrants escalation to the legislature. A single audit snapshot misses the pattern.

## Common Traps

- **Private-sector logic import.** Applying shareholder-value or profit-maximization reasoning to programs whose purpose is public service, not financial return.
- **Political self-censorship.** Softening, omitting, or reframing adverse findings to avoid political friction, betraying the public mandate.
- **Partisan framing.** Using language or emphasis that favors one political position, compromising perceived independence.
- **Activity description without judgment.** Reporting what the program did without concluding whether it achieved public value.
- **Single-objective assumption.** Assessing against one objective when the program has multiple, possibly conflicting, mandated objectives.
- **Financial materiality only.** Dismissing as immaterial programs that are financially small but serve vulnerable populations or fundamental rights.
- **Jargon-heavy reporting.** Writing for specialists and excluding the public and legislative audience that the audit serves.
- **Equity omission.** Reporting aggregate performance without examining whether services reach and serve all eligible groups fairly.
- **Entity-only lens.** Evaluating one agency in isolation and missing system-level fragmentation, duplication, or coordination failure.
- **Snapshot myopia.** Failing to connect current findings to prior audits, missing persistent systemic issues.

## Self-Check

- Is the audit anchored in the public accountability mandate to the legislature and citizens, rather than oriented to management preferences?
- Have I established the authoritative mandated objective (legislation, policy, approved targets) and assessed against it, without substituting a preferred interpretation?
- Does my materiality assessment reflect public significance (expenditure, affected citizens, rights, safety, trust), not just financial scale?
- Have I maintained independence by grounding findings in evidence and criteria, avoiding partisan language, and reporting adverse findings despite political sensitivity?
- Have I specifically assessed stewardship of public resources: authorized purpose, asset safeguarding, proper exercise of authority, and integrity?
- Is the report accessible to the legislature and public, with clear headline findings, minimal jargon, and fair presentation of the entity's response?
- Have I evaluated equity and access, disaggregating outcomes to show whether services reach and serve all eligible groups?
- Have I considered the program's interaction with the broader public sector system, including duplication, coordination, and governance fragmentation?
- Have I connected current findings to prior audit recommendations to identify persistent systemic issues warranting legislative attention?
