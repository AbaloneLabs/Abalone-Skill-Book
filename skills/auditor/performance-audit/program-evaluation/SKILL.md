# Program Evaluation

Program evaluation within a performance audit assesses whether a program or policy is working, for whom, under what conditions, and at what cost. It goes beyond measuring activity to judging merit, worth, and contribution to outcomes. The central judgment problem is causal attribution: observed outcomes may result from the program, from external factors, from other interventions, or from chance. Agents frequently confuse describing a program with evaluating it, claim causation from correlation, or evaluate only what is easy to measure while ignoring the harder questions of impact and distribution.

## Core Rules

### Establish the evaluation questions before selecting methods

Begin with the questions the evaluation must answer, not the data that happens to be available. Typical evaluation questions address:

- **relevance** — is the program addressing a real need and aligned with current priorities?;
- **coherence** — does the program complement or duplicate other interventions?;
- **effectiveness** — did the program achieve its objectives?;
- **efficiency** — were resources used economically relative to results?;
- **impact** — what difference did the program make compared to the counterfactual?;
- **sustainability** — will benefits endure after the program ends?;
- **equity** — are benefits and burdens distributed fairly across groups?.

Each question dictates the evidence and design required. Methods chosen before questions are settled produce evaluations that answer the wrong things.

### Build or validate a theory of change and logic model

A program's theory of change describes the causal reasoning linking inputs to activities to outputs to outcomes to impact. A logic model operationalizes it. Before evaluating, establish whether a credible theory of change exists and validate it against the program's actual design. If the theory of change is missing, implausible, or contested, that is itself a finding: a program whose causal logic is unclear cannot be meaningfully evaluated for effectiveness. Test each link in the causal chain, not just the endpoints.

### Address causal attribution explicitly

The hardest part of program evaluation is establishing that observed outcomes were caused by the program rather than alternatives. Consider the strength of available attribution evidence:

- **experimental or quasi-experimental designs** (comparison groups, difference-in-differences, regression discontinuity) provide stronger attribution;
- **contribution analysis** establishes a plausible causal story when full counterfactuals are unavailable;
- **descriptive before-and-after** shows change but cannot rule out external causes;
- **output reporting alone** establishes activity but not impact.

Match the attribution claim to the strength of the design. Do not claim the program "caused" outcomes based on before-and-after data alone.

### Distinguish the levels of evaluation: design, implementation, results

A complete evaluation examines three levels:

- **design quality** — are the objectives clear, the theory of change plausible, the targeting appropriate, the indicators defined?;
- **implementation fidelity** — was the program delivered as designed, reaching the intended beneficiaries at the intended intensity?;
- **results and impact** — were outputs produced and outcomes achieved, and can they be attributed to the program?.

Weakness at the design level often explains failure at the results level. Evaluating only results without examining design and implementation produces findings that cannot be acted upon.

### Assess outcomes for different beneficiary groups, not just averages

Aggregate outcomes can hide important distributional effects. A program may show positive average impact while leaving the most vulnerable groups worse off or unreached. Disaggregate outcomes by relevant dimensions: income, geography, gender, age, disability, ethnicity, or other stratifications relevant to the program's equity objectives. Equity analysis is not optional decoration; it is often where the most important findings lie.

### Use mixed methods to triangulate

Quantitative data establishes what happened and to what magnitude; qualitative data explains why and how. Strong evaluations combine:

- administrative and monitoring data for output and reach;
- surveys or administrative data for outcome measurement;
- interviews and focus groups for implementation experience and unintended effects;
- site visits and observation for operational reality;
- document review for design and governance.

Triangulation across methods strengthens conclusions; reliance on a single method leaves them vulnerable to that method's weaknesses.

### Consider unintended effects, positive and negative

Programs often produce effects their designers did not anticipate: displacement of other services, market distortion, dependency, stigma, or windfall gains. Actively seek unintended consequences through interviews, open-ended survey items, and comparison with non-beneficiary groups. Negative unintended effects can outweigh intended benefits and are frequently missed by output-focused evaluation.

### Evaluate sustainability and durability of benefits

A program that achieves outcomes only while funded may not represent good value if benefits collapse when funding ends. Assess whether the program has built capacity, changed systems, or created self-sustaining change. Consider financial, institutional, and environmental sustainability. A positive short-term impact with no sustainability plan is a qualified success at best.

### Acknowledge limitations and avoid overclaiming

Every evaluation has limitations: data gaps, attribution uncertainty, selection bias, time constraints. State them explicitly and bound the conclusions accordingly. An honest evaluation that says "the evidence supports a contribution, but causation is not established" is more credible and useful than an overclaimed definitive verdict that cannot withstand scrutiny.

## Common Traps

- **Description masquerading as evaluation.** Reporting what the program did (activities, outputs) without judging whether it worked.
- **Correlation-as-causation.** Claiming the program caused observed outcomes from before-and-after data without a counterfactual or comparison group.
- **Method-first design.** Choosing methods before settling evaluation questions, producing answers to questions nobody asked.
- **Ignoring the theory of change.** Evaluating results without examining whether the causal logic was ever plausible.
- **Average-only outcomes.** Reporting aggregate impact while masking that vulnerable groups were unreached or harmed.
- **Results-only evaluation.** Assessing impact without examining design and implementation, leaving findings unactionable.
- **Unintended-effects blindness.** Focusing only on intended outcomes and missing displacement, distortion, or harm.
- **Single-method overreliance.** Depending on one data source and inheriting all its biases.
- **Short-termism.** Evaluating immediate outputs and ignoring whether benefits will endure.
- **Overclaiming.** Presenting uncertain attribution as proven impact, undermining credibility when challenged.

## Self-Check

- Have I defined the evaluation questions first, then selected methods, rather than the reverse?
- Is there a validated theory of change and logic model, and have I tested the causal links rather than only the endpoints?
- Have I addressed causal attribution with a design whose strength matches the claims I make (experimental, contribution analysis, or descriptive)?
- Did I evaluate design quality, implementation fidelity, and results, not just outcomes?
- Are outcomes disaggregated by relevant beneficiary groups so distributional and equity effects are visible?
- Have I used mixed methods to triangulate, rather than relying on a single data source?
- Did I actively seek unintended effects, both positive and negative, beyond the program's stated objectives?
- Have I assessed sustainability and durability of benefits, not just short-term impact?
- Are the evaluation's limitations explicitly stated, and are conclusions bounded to what the evidence supports?
