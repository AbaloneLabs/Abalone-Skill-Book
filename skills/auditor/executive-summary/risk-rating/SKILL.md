# Risk Rating

A risk rating is the shorthand that tells readers how seriously to take a finding, and it is also the single most disputed element of any report. Management argues ratings should be lower; auditors under deadline pressure inflate ratings to ensure attention; inconsistent ratings across findings make the scale meaningless. A rating is a judgment compressed into a word or a number, and like all such compressions it is only useful if the underlying judgment is sound and the criteria are consistent. A rating that does not reflect the actual risk misleads readers about where to direct attention and resources. The skill is in defining a clear rating scale, applying it through consistent criteria that combine quantitative and qualitative factors, calibrating across findings so the scale means the same thing throughout, and defending each rating with documented reasoning rather than impression.

Use this skill when assigning risk ratings to findings, defining or applying a rating scale, combining factors into a rating, calibrating ratings for consistency, or defending ratings against challenge. The goal is a set of ratings that accurately reflect risk, are applied consistently, and direct attention proportionate to significance.

## Core Rules

### Define A Clear, Documented Rating Scale

A rating scale only works if its levels are defined. Without definitions, high, medium, and low mean whatever each rater thinks, and consistency is impossible.

Define the scale by:

- stating what each level means in terms of impact, likelihood, and exposure;
- distinguishing, where relevant, severity from urgency;
- defining the thresholds, financial or qualitative, that separate levels;
- documenting the scale so all raters apply it the same way;
- using consistent terminology across the engagement and over time.

A documented scale lets a reviewer test whether a rating follows from the criteria, rather than from the rater's impression.

### Combine Quantitative And Qualitative Factors

Risk is not only a dollar amount. A rating based solely on financial impact misses the qualitative severity of fraud, override, compliance, or governance matters. A rating must combine both dimensions.

Combine factors by:

- assessing financial materiality and potential exposure;
- assessing qualitative severity, including fraud, override, legal, safety, reputational, and governance implications;
- assessing likelihood of occurrence or recurrence;
- assessing breadth of impact across processes, locations, or populations;
- combining the factors into an overall rating, with the highest applicable dimension often driving the rating.

A small-dollar fraud may rate higher than a large-dollar clerical error because qualitative severity dominates. Make the combination explicit, not intuitive.

### Apply Criteria Consistently Across Findings

Consistency is what makes a rating scale meaningful. If similar findings receive different ratings, the scale loses credibility and readers cannot rely on it.

Apply consistently by:

- rating each finding against the same defined criteria;
- comparing each rating to ratings on similar findings in the same engagement;
- checking that findings with similar impact and likelihood receive similar ratings;
- documenting the basis for each rating so consistency can be reviewed;
- having ratings reviewed by a second person to counter individual bias.

Inconsistency is often invisible to the individual rater. A review step that compares ratings across findings is essential to catch it.

### Calibrate To Avoid Inflation And Deflation

Both inflation and deflation destroy a rating scale's usefulness. If everything is high, nothing is; if severe findings are rated medium to avoid conflict, the scale conceals risk.

Avoid inflation by:

- reserving the highest rating for findings that genuinely warrant it;
- not rating findings high to ensure management attention;
- using lower ratings or observations for matters that do not meet the threshold.

Avoid deflation by:

- not downgrading a rating to avoid disagreement with management;
- preserving the risk-based rating even where management prefers lower;
- documenting the basis so a downgrade is visible and must be justified.

The rating must follow the risk, not the desired reaction. Document any departure from the criteria-based rating and the reason.

### Distinguish Inherent From Residual Risk Where Relevant

Some findings concern risks that are partially mitigated by compensating controls. The rating should reflect the residual risk, the exposure after considering what controls do operate, not only the inherent risk.

Consider residual risk by:

- assessing the exposure if the failed control were the only control;
- considering whether compensating controls operate and have been tested;
- rating based on the net exposure after compensating controls, where they are reliable;
- noting where compensating controls are assumed but untested, which leaves the rating closer to inherent.

A finding rated high on inherent risk may be medium on residual if tested compensating controls reduce the exposure. State which basis the rating reflects.

### Rate The Finding, Not The Recommendation

A common confusion is letting the ease of the fix influence the rating. A finding that is easy to fix is still a high-risk finding until it is fixed; the rating reflects the risk, not the remediation effort.

Keep them separate by:

- rating the risk the finding creates, regardless of how easily it can be addressed;
- not downgrading a rating because the fix is simple or already planned;
- communicating urgency separately, since an easy fix may warrant fast action;
- recognizing that a quick fix does not reduce the significance of the condition that existed.

The rating tells the reader how serious the matter is; the recommendation and timeframe tell them what to do and when.

### Document The Basis For Each Rating

A rating without its basis cannot be reviewed, defended, or applied consistently elsewhere. The basis should be recorded for each significant finding.

Document:

- the factors considered, quantitative and qualitative;
- the impact, likelihood, and breadth assessments;
- the residual risk basis, including any compensating controls relied upon;
- the comparison to similar findings for consistency;
- any adjustment from the criteria-based rating and the reason.

A documented basis lets a reviewer, a successor, or a regulator see why a finding was rated as it was, and lets the rating withstand challenge.

## Common Traps

### Undefined Or Inconsistent Rating Scale

Without definitions, ratings reflect individual impression. Define and document the scale.

### Rating On Dollar Amount Alone

Qualitative severity often dominates. Combine quantitative and qualitative factors.

### Inconsistent Ratings Across Similar Findings

This destroys the scale's credibility. Compare and review ratings for consistency.

### Inflation To Ensure Attention

If everything is high, nothing is. Reserve high ratings for genuine significance.

### Deflation To Avoid Disagreement

Downgrading to please management conceals risk. Preserve the risk-based rating.

### Letting Ease Of Fix Drive The Rating

A simple fix does not reduce the risk that existed. Rate the finding, communicate urgency separately.

### Ignoring Residual Versus Inherent Risk

Failing to consider compensating controls overstates risk; assuming untested controls understates it. Assess residual risk and state the basis.

### Ratings Without Documented Basis

A rating that cannot be explained cannot be defended. Document the factors and reasoning.

## Self-Check

- Is the rating scale defined and documented, with clear meaning for each level in terms of impact, likelihood, and exposure?
- Does each rating combine quantitative and qualitative factors, including fraud, override, compliance, and governance severity?
- Are ratings applied consistently across findings, with similar findings receiving similar ratings and a review step to catch inconsistency?
- Is inflation avoided by reserving high ratings for genuine significance, and deflation avoided by preserving risk-based ratings despite management preference?
- Where compensating controls exist, does the rating reflect residual risk based on tested controls, with the basis stated?
- Does the rating reflect the finding's risk rather than the ease of the fix, with urgency communicated separately?
- Is the basis for each significant rating documented, including factors, residual risk basis, consistency comparison, and any adjustment?
- Could each rating be defended to a reviewer or regulator on the basis of the documented criteria and reasoning?
