# Actionable Recommendations

An executive reading an audit summary wants to know not only what was found but what to do about it. A recommendation that an executive can approve, assign, and track is actionable; one that states an aspiration, such as "strengthen the control environment," is not. The difference matters because executives act on recommendations, allocate resources against them, and are held accountable for their completion. A vague recommendation cannot be implemented, cannot be tracked, and cannot be verified, so it produces no remediation while creating the impression that the matter has been addressed. The skill is in converting the audit's analysis into recommendations that are specific enough to act on, owned by someone with authority, bounded by a realistic timeframe, and tied clearly to the risk they reduce, so that executive approval translates into actual change.

Use this skill when formulating recommendations for an executive summary or governance audience, ensuring recommendations are actionable and trackable, or converting observations into actions executives can approve and monitor. The goal is recommendations that, when approved and implemented, actually reduce the identified risk and that can be verified as complete.

## Core Rules

### Make Each Recommendation Specific Enough To Act On

A recommendation must tell the reader what is to be done with enough specificity that the action can be understood, assigned, and implemented. Vague recommendations invite nominal compliance.

Make recommendations specific by:

- stating the action expected, such as implement, configure, assign, reconcile, or report;
- defining the scope, which process, system, population, or location;
- specifying, where relevant, the frequency, threshold, or trigger;
- avoiding aspirational verbs like strengthen, enhance, or improve without a concrete action.

"Implement a monthly reconciliation of the suspense account, reviewed by the controller, with exceptions escalated to finance leadership" is actionable. "Strengthen controls over suspense accounts" is not.

### Assign Clear Ownership

An actionable recommendation has an owner, the role or person responsible for implementation. Without ownership, the recommendation drifts and no one is accountable.

Assign ownership by:

- identifying the role or function responsible, such as the controller, CIO, or compliance officer;
- naming a specific executive sponsor where the matter is significant;
- ensuring the owner has the authority and resources to implement;
- avoiding ownership assigned to a committee or "management" generically, which diffuses accountability.

The executive audience approves the recommendation and the owner accepts it. Diffuse ownership produces diffuse, often absent, remediation.

### Set A Realistic, Risk-Appropriate Timeframe

A recommendation without a timeframe has no urgency and no basis for tracking. The timeframe should be realistic given the complexity and calibrated to the risk.

Set timeframes by:

- bounding the recommendation with a target completion date or period;
- making the timeframe shorter for higher-risk or urgent matters;
- accounting for the complexity, such as system changes that require longer;
- distinguishing interim milestones from final completion for multi-phase remediation;
- avoiding open-ended language like "as soon as possible" or "ongoing."

A timeframe lets governance track progress and escalate slippage. Without it, recommendations linger indefinitely in "in progress."

### Tie The Recommendation To The Risk It Reduces

An executive needs to understand why a recommendation matters, not only what it asks for. Tying the action to the risk lets the executive weigh the effort against the benefit and prioritize.

Tie to risk by:

- stating the risk or exposure the finding creates;
- explaining how the recommended action reduces that risk;
- noting any residual risk that would remain after implementation;
- distinguishing preventive from detective effects.

This transforms the recommendation from a demand into a reasoned proposal, which executives are more likely to approve and resource.

### Ensure The Recommendation Is Verifiable

A recommendation should be framed so that its completion can be verified later. If the action cannot be tested for completion, remediation cannot be confirmed.

Ensure verifiability by:

- specifying the evidence that would demonstrate completion, such as a configured rule, a signed-off report, or a tested sample;
- defining what result constitutes successful implementation;
- aligning the recommendation with the verification approach the auditor will later apply;
- avoiding actions whose completion is inherently subjective, like "improve awareness."

A verifiable recommendation closes cleanly when done and stays open, honestly, when it is not. An unverifiable one produces disputes about whether it was completed.

### Address Root Cause, Not Symptom

An actionable recommendation that addresses the symptom will be implemented and the problem will recur. Executives who approve a symptom-level recommendation believe the matter is resolved when it is not.

Address root cause by:

- connecting the action to the cause identified in the finding;
- checking that the action would prevent recurrence, not only detect it;
- ensuring the action covers the full affected population, not only the sample;
- resisting recommendations that address a more convenient problem than the finding described.

Executives rely on the auditor to recommend actions that actually fix the problem. Symptom-level recommendations betray that reliance.

### Calibrate Recommendation Volume For Executive Attention

An executive summary should not overwhelm with recommendations. A long list dilutes attention from the significant actions and signals an inability to prioritize.

Calibrate by:

- including in the executive summary only the recommendations tied to significant findings;
- grouping related recommendations where they address a common theme;
- moving lower-priority recommendations to the detailed report;
- sequencing recommendations so the most important are prominent.

Executives can approve and track a focused set of significant actions more effectively than an exhaustive catalog. Reserve the summary for what warrants executive attention.

### Frame As Guidance, Retaining Management's Decision Role

Even when actionable, a recommendation is guidance, not a directive. The framing should preserve management's authority to choose the approach, while making the objective and rationale clear.

Frame by:

- using recommendation language, not imperative commands;
- stating the objective and letting management determine detailed design;
- acknowledging that alternative approaches achieving the same risk reduction are acceptable;
- not taking on implementation responsibility, which would impair independence.

Actionable does not mean prescriptive. It means specific enough to act on while leaving the design and decision to management.

## Common Traps

### Aspirational Verbs Without Concrete Action

"Strengthen," "enhance," or "improve" without a specific action cannot be implemented or verified. State the concrete action.

### No Clear Owner

Ownership assigned to "management" or a committee diffuses accountability. Assign to a role with authority.

### Open-Ended Timeframes

"As soon as possible" or "ongoing" provide no basis for tracking or escalation. Set a realistic, risk-appropriate date.

### Disconnected From Risk

A recommendation without its rationale reads as an arbitrary demand. Tie the action to the risk it reduces.

### Unverifiable Actions

Actions whose completion is subjective produce disputes about remediation. Specify the evidence of completion.

### Addressing Symptom, Not Cause

Symptom-level recommendations lead to claimed remediation and recurrence. Address the root cause.

### Overwhelming The Executive With Volume

A long list dilutes attention. Include only significant, prioritized recommendations in the summary.

### Overstepping Into Directives

Recommendations framed as commands overstep the auditor's role. Frame as guidance while preserving specificity.

## Self-Check

- Is each recommendation specific enough to act on, with a concrete action, defined scope, and relevant frequency or trigger?
- Does each recommendation have a clear owner, a role or person with authority and resources to implement?
- Is each bounded by a realistic, risk-appropriate timeframe, with milestones for multi-phase remediation?
- Is the recommendation tied to the risk it reduces, with residual risk noted, so executives can weigh effort against benefit?
- Is the recommendation framed to be verifiable, with defined evidence of completion and a clear pass or fail result?
- Does the recommendation address the documented root cause and cover the full affected population, not only the symptom or sample?
- Is the volume calibrated, with only significant, prioritized recommendations in the executive summary and the rest in the detail?
- Is the recommendation framed as guidance, preserving management's decision role while remaining specific and actionable?
- Could an executive approve, assign, track, and later verify this recommendation as complete?
