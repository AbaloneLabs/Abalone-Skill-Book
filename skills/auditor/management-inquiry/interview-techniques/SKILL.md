# Interview Techniques

Inquiry is one of the auditor's primary sources of information, and it is also one of the most easily mishandled. Unlike documentary evidence, oral information is shaped in real time by the questions asked, the relationship between interviewer and interviewee, and the incentives of the person answering. An auditor who asks leading questions will hear confirmation of whatever they implied; an auditor who accepts the first fluent answer will mistake confidence for accuracy; an auditor who frames the conversation as a friendly chat will collect opinions dressed as facts. The skill in audit interviewing is not extracting a confession but creating the conditions in which accurate, complete information can emerge, and then treating every answer as a claim to be corroborated rather than a fact to be recorded.

Use this skill when planning and conducting audit interviews with management, process owners, or staff, when designing question lines, when probing and challenging explanations, or when evaluating the reliability of oral information. The goal is to obtain information that is accurate and complete enough to be useful, while never mistaking inquiry for evidence.

## Core Rules

### Prepare The Interview Around What You Need To Learn

An unstructured interview produces unstructured information. Before the conversation, define what you need to understand and design questions that surface it. Preparation also signals competence, which affects how seriously the interviewee engages.

Prepare by:

- listing the specific facts, processes, risks, or judgments you need to understand;
- reviewing prior audit findings, walkthroughs, and documentation so questions are informed;
- identifying who has the knowledge and authority to answer each area;
- anticipating the likely answers and the follow-up questions they would require;
- deciding the sequence, from broad understanding to specific detail;
- identifying sensitive areas that require careful framing.

A prepared interviewer can recognize when an answer is incomplete or evasive because they know what a complete answer should contain.

### Use Open Questions To Discover, Closed Questions To Confirm

The form of the question determines the kind of answer. Open questions invite the interviewee to describe, explain, or narrate, and they reveal what the interviewee considers important. Closed questions confirm specific facts. Both have a role, and using only one type distorts the result.

Open questions:

- how does this process work when an exception occurs?
- walk me through what happens from the point of approval to posting;
- what would you say are the main risks in this area?

Closed questions:

- does the system prevent posting without approval?
- who reviews this report, and how often?
- was this control in place during the entire period?

Start open to discover the interviewee's understanding, then close to confirm specific facts. Leading questions that embed the expected answer, such as "you do review this monthly, right?", should be avoided because they invite agreement rather than truth.

### Probe Explanations Rather Than Accepting The First Answer

The first answer is often a summary, a rehearsed account, or a convenient framing. Probing tests whether the explanation holds and whether the interviewee actually knows what they are describing.

Probe by:

- asking for a specific example or the most recent instance;
- asking what happens in the unusual or exception case, not only the normal one;
- asking how the interviewee knows, and what evidence supports the explanation;
- asking what would happen if the control failed, and whether anyone would notice;
- asking about changes during the period, staffing, or systems;
- returning to a point later in the interview to test consistency.

Evasive or inconsistent answers are themselves evidence. Note where the interviewee could not answer, gave a different answer than expected, or deflected, and pursue those areas through other evidence.

### Separate Fact From Opinion And Representation

Interviewees blend fact, opinion, and self-serving representation. The auditor must distinguish them, because only facts are directly usable as evidence, and even facts require corroboration.

Distinguish:

- fact: a verifiable statement about what happened or how something works;
- opinion: the interviewee's judgment about adequacy, risk, or cause;
- representation: an assertion made for the auditor's benefit, often about completeness or compliance.

Treat opinions as leads to investigate, not as conclusions. Treat representations as claims requiring corroboration. Record each answer with a note of its nature, so a reviewer can see what was fact and what was assertion.

### Corroborate Significant Oral Information Through Other Evidence

Inquiry alone does not provide sufficient evidence for significant matters. Oral explanations must be corroborated through documents, system evidence, observation, or third-party confirmation before they are relied upon.

Corroborate by:

- tracing the described process to actual transactions and documentation;
- observing the control in operation rather than relying on the description;
- confirming balances or transactions with third parties;
- comparing the explanation to system configurations, logs, or reports;
- testing whether the described exception handling actually occurs.

An uncorroborated explanation, however detailed and confident, remains a claim. The corroboration, not the explanation, is the evidence.

### Manage The Relationship Without Compromising Objectivity

Interviews are conducted with people the auditor will continue to work with, and a productive relationship helps obtain information. But the relationship must not become so comfortable that the auditor stops challenging, or so adversarial that the interviewee stops cooperating.

Balance by:

- being professional, respectful, and clear about the engagement's purpose;
- not softening necessary challenge to avoid discomfort;
- not sharing preliminary conclusions that could shape later answers;
- not accepting hospitality or favors that create a sense of obligation;
- documenting disagreements factually rather than arguing them in the room.

Objectivity is maintained through the questions and the corroboration, not through coldness. A courteous interviewer who nonetheless probes and corroborates obtains better information than either a friendly one who does not.

### Document The Inquiry While It Is Reliable

Memory of an interview degrades quickly, and reconstructed notes are less reliable than contemporaneous ones. Document the inquiry promptly, capturing what was asked, what was answered, and the nature of each answer.

Document:

- the date, attendees, and roles;
- the topics covered and the sequence;
- the substance of each significant answer, in the interviewee's terms where possible;
- any evasiveness, inconsistency, or inability to answer, with the area noted;
- the follow-up or corroboration planned for significant claims.

Where a representation is significant, reduce it to writing through a representation letter or a signed summary, because oral representations are the weakest form of evidence.

## Common Traps

### Asking Leading Questions That Invite Agreement

Questions that embed the expected answer produce agreement, not truth. Phrase questions so the interviewee can disagree or correct without friction.

### Accepting The First Fluent Answer

A confident, fluent answer is not necessarily a correct one. Fluent respondents can be repeating a rehearsed account or masking a gap. Probe for specifics and examples.

### Treating Inquiry As Sufficient Evidence

Oral information is a lead, not evidence, for significant matters. Without corroboration, it cannot support a conclusion, no matter how detailed.

### Conducting An Unstructured Conversation

Without preparation and a question plan, the interview drifts and omits the areas that matter. The interviewee, not the auditor, ends up setting the agenda.

### Letting Comfort Suppress Challenge

A friendly relationship can make challenge feel rude. The auditor who stops probing to preserve warmth collects opinions and misses the gaps.

### Sharing Preliminary Conclusions

Telling the interviewee what you expect to find shapes their answers toward confirmation. Keep preliminary views out of the conversation.

### Failing To Note Evasiveness Or Inconsistency

The most informative parts of an interview are often where the interviewee could not or would not answer. If these are not noted, the signal is lost.

### Reconstructing Notes Later From Memory

Notes written hours or days later omit detail and introduce reconstruction error. Document contemporaneously or immediately after.

## Self-Check

- Was the interview prepared with a defined list of what needed to be learned and informed questions?
- Were open questions used to discover and closed questions used to confirm, with leading questions avoided?
- Were explanations probed for examples, exception cases, and supporting evidence rather than accepted at first answer?
- Were facts distinguished from opinions and representations, with each answer recorded by nature?
- Is significant oral information corroborated through documents, observation, system evidence, or third parties before being relied upon?
- Was the relationship managed professionally without softening necessary challenge or accepting favors that create obligation?
- Were preliminary conclusions withheld from the conversation to avoid shaping answers?
- Was evasiveness, inconsistency, or inability to answer noted as a signal for follow-up?
- Was the inquiry documented contemporaneously, capturing topics, substance, nature of answers, and planned corroboration?
- Were significant representations reduced to writing through a representation letter or signed summary?
