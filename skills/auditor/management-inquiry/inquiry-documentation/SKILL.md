# Inquiry Documentation

Inquiry produces information that exists only in the moment it is given. Unlike a document that can be re-read or a balance that can be re-tested, an oral answer leaves no trace unless the auditor records it, and the recording must be good enough that a reviewer, a successor auditor, or a regulator can later understand what was asked, what was answered, and what it meant. Poor inquiry documentation is one of the most common weaknesses in audit files: notes that capture a conclusion without the question, that paraphrase an answer into something the interviewee did not say, that omit who spoke and when, or that silently drop the parts of the answer that were evasive or contradictory. The discipline of inquiry documentation is to record enough that the oral evidence is preserved as reliably as the documentary evidence, and to record it in a way that does not distort what was actually said.

Use this skill when documenting audit inquiries, interviews, or management responses in workpapers, when deciding what level of detail to capture, when recording the nature and reliability of oral information, or when ensuring inquiry-based evidence can withstand review. The goal is to create a contemporaneous, accurate, and complete record of inquiry that supports the conclusions drawn from it.

## Core Rules

### Document Inquiry Contemporaneously

Memory of a conversation degrades within hours, and notes reconstructed later are unreliable and vulnerable to challenge. Inquiry should be documented during the conversation or immediately after, while the substance is fresh.

Contemporaneous documentation:

- captures the actual words and examples used by the interviewee;
- preserves the sequence and context of the exchange;
- records evasiveness, hesitation, or contradiction as observed;
- avoids the reconstruction errors that inflate or smooth what was said.

If notes must be made after a delay, note the delay and the basis for the reconstruction. Where the matter is significant, consider having the interviewee review and confirm a written summary.

### Capture Who, When, And In What Capacity

Inquiry evidence is only as strong as its attribution. A note that records an answer without identifying who gave it, when, and in what role cannot be verified or relied upon.

Record for each inquiry:

- the date and, where relevant, the time;
- the full name and title of each person interviewed;
- the capacity in which they answered, such as process owner, preparer, or approver;
- the auditor or auditors present;
- the format, in person, by phone, by email, or in a meeting;
- whether the response was oral, written, or confirmed in writing.

An answer from the person with direct knowledge and authority carries different weight than one relayed second-hand, and the documentation must let a reviewer see that distinction.

### Record The Question And The Answer, Not Only The Conclusion

A common failure is to record the auditor's conclusion from an inquiry without recording the question asked or the answer given. This makes the conclusion unreviewable, because a reviewer cannot see what supported it.

For each significant inquiry, record:

- the question asked, in enough detail to show what was being sought;
- the answer given, in the interviewee's terms where possible;
- any follow-up questions and the responses;
- the auditor's interpretation or conclusion, clearly separated from the raw answer.

If the workpaper states only "management confirmed the control operates," a reviewer cannot tell what was asked, what management actually said, or whether the confirmation was meaningful. The question and answer must precede the conclusion.

### Preserve The Substance In The Interviewee's Terms

Paraphrasing can subtly change meaning, especially when the auditor paraphrases toward the answer they expected. Preserve the interviewee's actual language for significant matters, so the record reflects what they said rather than what the auditor heard.

Preserve substance by:

- using direct quotation or close paraphrase for key statements;
- recording specific examples, figures, and names the interviewee cited;
- noting qualifications, such as "to my knowledge" or "as far as I am aware";
- avoiding the temptation to tidy ambiguous or imprecise language into something cleaner.

A cleaned-up answer that sounds more definite than the original is a distortion, even if unintentional. The record should reflect the uncertainty that was actually present.

### Distinguish Fact, Opinion, And Representation In The Record

Interviewees provide a mix of verifiable facts, personal opinions, and self-serving representations. The documentation must distinguish them, because each carries different evidentiary weight and requires different follow-up.

Mark each significant item as:

- fact: a verifiable statement about what happens or how something works;
- opinion: the interviewee's judgment about adequacy, risk, or cause;
- representation: an assertion made for the auditor, often about completeness or compliance.

This distinction lets a reviewer see which answers need corroboration and which are the interviewee's own view. It also prevents opinions from being treated as evidence.

### Note Evasiveness, Inconsistency, And Inability To Answer

The most informative parts of an interview are often where the interviewee could not or would not answer. These signals are easily lost if not recorded, yet they often point to the areas that need deeper testing.

Record:

- questions the interviewee could not answer, and the topic;
- answers that changed during the interview or conflicted with prior statements;
- deflections, such as redirecting to a different topic or deferring to someone not present;
- hesitation, unusual confidence, or convenient timing in responses;
- topics where access or information was restricted.

These notes are not accusations; they are observations that guide where corroboration and additional procedures are most needed.

### Tie Inquiry To Corroboration And Conclusion

Inquiry documentation should not stand alone for significant matters. The workpaper should show how the oral information was corroborated and how it contributed to the conclusion.

Tie the record together by:

- cross-referencing the documents, system evidence, or observations that corroborate key answers;
- noting where corroboration confirmed, qualified, or contradicted the oral account;
- linking the inquiry to the specific conclusion or finding it supports;
- identifying any significant matter that remains reliant on the inquiry alone, and why.

A workpaper that records an interview but never connects it to corroboration or conclusion leaves the inquiry hanging, with no visible role in the audit's results.

### Reduce Significant Representations To Writing

For significant representations, oral inquiry is insufficient as a final record. Reduce them to a signed representation letter or a written confirmation that the interviewee has reviewed and acknowledged.

Reduce to writing when:

- the matter is material or involves management's assertions and intentions;
- the representation concerns completeness, fraud, contingencies, or subsequent events;
- the auditor will rely on the representation in forming a conclusion;
- the matter may be subject to later dispute.

The written record transforms an oral claim into a documented position that management has taken, which is stronger evidence than a contemporaneous note of a conversation.

## Common Traps

### Recording Only The Conclusion

A note that states a conclusion without the question and answer cannot be reviewed or verified. Always record the exchange that led to the conclusion.

### Reconstructing Notes From Memory Later

Notes made hours or days later omit detail and introduce smoothing. Document contemporaneously, and note any delay.

### Paraphrasing Toward The Expected Answer

Cleaning up ambiguous language into something more definite distorts the record. Preserve the interviewee's terms and qualifications.

### Omitting Who, When, And Capacity

An answer without attribution cannot be weighed. Record the people, dates, roles, and format for every inquiry.

### Failing To Distinguish Fact From Opinion

Treating an interviewee's opinion as evidence overstates the support for a conclusion. Mark the nature of each significant answer.

### Losing The Signals Of Evasiveness

The parts where the interviewee could not or would not answer are often the most important. If not recorded, the signal is lost and the area goes untested.

### Leaving Inquiry Uncorroborated For Significant Matters

Oral inquiry alone does not support significant conclusions. Cross-reference the corroboration and show how the inquiry contributed to the result.

### Treating A Conversation As A Representation

For significant matters, an oral exchange is not enough. Obtain a written representation or confirmed summary so management's position is documented.

## Self-Check

- Was the inquiry documented contemporaneously, during or immediately after the conversation, with any delay noted?
- Does each record capture the date, attendees, their titles and capacities, the format, and who was present?
- Are the questions and answers recorded, not only the auditor's conclusion, so a reviewer can follow the exchange?
- Is the substance preserved in the interviewee's terms, including qualifications and specific examples, rather than cleaned-up paraphrase?
- Are facts, opinions, and representations distinguished and marked in the record?
- Are evasiveness, inconsistency, inability to answer, and access restrictions noted as signals for follow-up?
- Is the inquiry tied to corroboration through cross-references, and is its contribution to the conclusion shown?
- For significant matters, has the representation been reduced to a signed letter or written confirmation rather than left as an oral note?
- Can an independent reviewer reconstruct what was asked, what was answered, and why it matters from the documentation alone?
