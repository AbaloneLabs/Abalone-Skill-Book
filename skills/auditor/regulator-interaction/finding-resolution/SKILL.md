# Regulatory Finding Resolution

When a regulator issues a finding, a criticism, or a matter requiring attention (MRA) or matter requiring immediate attention (MRIA), the audit team inherits a remediation obligation that is distinct from ordinary audit follow-up. The regulator will verify whether the finding was truly fixed, and an inadequate response can convert a criticism into enforcement. Agents often mishandle resolution because they treat it like internal remediation: they write a plan, claim it is done, and move on. The harm this prevents is concrete. A superficial root cause analysis produces a fix that fails verification, a remediation plan that misses the regulator's actual concern gets rejected, and an unresolved finding lingers as a repeat criticism in the next cycle. Resolution must be precise, evidence-based, and calibrated to what the regulator will actually test.

Use this skill when responding to regulatory findings, examination criticisms, MRAs, MRIAs, or supervisory letters, and when developing, tracking, and verifying remediation. The goal is to resolve the underlying concern to the regulator's satisfaction with a defensible, root-cause-based, and verified corrective action.

## Core Rules

### Decode The Finding Before Designing A Fix

A regulatory finding is written in supervisory language and may not name the root cause explicitly. Before designing remediation, decode what the regulator actually found and why.

Decode:

- the specific control, process, or practice criticized;
- the standard, rule, or expectation it was measured against;
- the evidence the regulator relied on;
- the severity classification (MRA, MRIA, criticism, observation);
- the regulator's stated expectation for remediation;
- whether the finding is isolated or systemic;
- whether it overlaps with internal audit or external audit findings.

Restate the finding in operational terms and confirm understanding with the regulator where ambiguous. Fixing the wrong problem wastes the remediation cycle.

### Perform A Genuine Root Cause Analysis

Regulators increasingly expect root cause analysis, not symptom treatment. A finding fixed at the symptom level will fail verification or recur.

Perform root cause analysis that identifies:

- the process, design, or control failure that allowed the condition;
- whether the cause is people, process, technology, governance, or culture;
- contributing factors such as incentives, turnover, or system limitations;
- whether the cause affects other processes or engagements;
- whether prior remediation attempted the same area and failed;
- the depth at which intervention is required to prevent recurrence.

Document the analysis method (for example, five whys, fishbone, fault tree) and the evidence supporting the identified cause. Avoid stopping at the first plausible explanation.

### Design Remediation That Matches The Cause And The Regulator's Concern

The corrective action must address the root cause and must demonstrably resolve the concern the regulator raised. Two separate tests.

Ensure the action:

- targets the identified root cause, not a convenient symptom;
- covers the full affected population, period, and scope;
- includes preventive controls, not only detective ones;
- specifies the control or process change, not only a policy;
- defines measurable success criteria;
- assigns a responsible owner with authority to implement;
- sets a realistic date with milestones;
- identifies dependencies and risks to completion.

If the action would not, if implemented, prevent the criticized condition, redesign it.

### Distinguish Implementation From Effective Operation

Regulators verify that remediation works, not just that it was launched. Plan for evidence of both.

Implementation evidence:

- policy or procedure issued;
- system configuration changed;
- roles or access updated;
- training delivered;
- new report or monitoring created.

Effective operation evidence:

- sample of transactions showing the control operated over a period;
- logs showing reviews occurred at required frequency;
- exception tracking showing deviations were caught and resolved;
- evidence the control operated across the full population, not a single point.

Match the verification evidence to what the regulator will test. For an MRA about a control failing in practice, a new policy alone will not pass verification.

### Build A Credible Remediation Plan For The Regulator

The remediation plan submitted to the regulator is a commitment. It must be credible, specific, and achievable, because missed commitments damage credibility.

A credible plan includes:

- a clear restatement of the finding and root cause;
- the specific corrective actions with owners and dates;
- the evidence that will demonstrate completion;
- the verification approach and who performs it;
- interim milestones for longer remediations;
- risk acceptance for any residual risk remaining;
- communication cadence with the regulator.

Avoid over-promising dates to appear responsive. A missed date is worse than a realistic one.

### Track Corrective Actions To Completion With Discipline

Remediation plans fail most often in execution, not design. Track each action with discipline.

Track:

- action, owner, due date, and current status;
- dependencies and blockers;
- evidence received and reviewed;
- verification performed and result;
- deviations from the plan and revised dates;
- items at risk of missing committed dates;
- escalation of overdue or stalled items.

Review the tracker on a regular cadence and escalate items that are slipping before they miss the regulator's date, not after.

### Prepare For Regulator Verification Of Remediation

The regulator will return to verify. Prepare the evidence and the demonstration, not just the claim.

Prepare by:

- assembling the evidence package mapped to each corrective action;
- performing internal verification before the regulator arrives;
- identifying and closing any gaps found in internal verification;
- ensuring consistency between the evidence and prior representations;
- preparing walkthroughs and demonstrations of the changed controls;
- documenting the population and period over which operation is shown.

If internal verification shows the fix is not yet effective, do not claim completion. Report honest status and request time.

### Communicate Resolution Status Honestly And Timely

Regulators value honest, timely communication. Surprises damage credibility more than bad news.

Communicate:

- status updates on the agreed cadence;
- any change in scope, owner, or date promptly;
- completion with supporting evidence;
- partial completion with what remains and revised dates;
- any new issues discovered during remediation;
- requests for extension with justification before the deadline.

### Escalate Unresolved And Disputed Findings

Not all findings resolve cleanly. Some are disputed, some stall, and some recur. Have a path for each.

Escalate when:

- remediation cannot meet the committed date with a credible plan;
- the regulator and the team disagree on whether the finding is resolved;
- the same finding recurs after claimed remediation;
- root cause analysis reveals a deeper governance or cultural issue;
- remediation requires resources or authority beyond the team.

Escalation paths include senior management, the board or audit committee, and the regulator itself. Document the escalation and the response.

## Common Traps

### Fixing The Symptom, Not The Cause

A control or policy that addresses the surface condition without touching the root cause will fail verification or recur. Always trace to cause first.

### Claiming Completion On Implementation Alone

Issuing a policy or changing a setting proves the fix was launched, not that it works. Provide operating evidence for findings about control effectiveness.

### Missing The Regulator's Actual Concern

The finding may describe a symptom of a concern the regulator cares about more broadly. Decode the concern before designing the fix, or the plan will be rejected.

### Over-Promising Dates

Committing to dates that cannot be met to appear responsive damages credibility when missed. Commit to realistic dates with milestones.

### Stale Tracker With No Escalation

A remediation log that is never reviewed becomes a list of overdue items. Review regularly and escalate slippage before deadlines pass.

### Inconsistent With Prior Representations

Evidence or statements that contradict what was previously told to the regulator or in the audit file create exposure. Reconcile before submitting.

### Treating Verification As A Formality

If internal verification is perfunctory, the regulator's verification will find what was missed. Verify rigorously before claiming completion.

### Disputing A Finding Without Evidence

Disagreeing with a finding is legitimate, but only with testable counter-evidence. Bare disagreement damages credibility and does not resolve the matter.

## Self-Check

- Has the finding been decoded to identify the specific control, standard, evidence, and regulator expectation before remediation was designed?
- Does the root cause analysis reach the actual cause using a documented method, rather than stopping at the first plausible explanation?
- Does each corrective action target the root cause, cover the full affected population, and include preventive controls and measurable success criteria?
- Is the verification evidence matched to the finding's nature, with operating effectiveness evidence for findings about control failures?
- Does the remediation plan submitted to the regulator restate the finding and root cause, with specific actions, owners, dates, and verification approach?
- Are corrective actions tracked with discipline, including dependencies, evidence, deviations, and escalation of at-risk items?
- Has internal verification been performed before claiming completion, with gaps closed and consistency with prior representations confirmed?
- Is resolution status communicated honestly and timely, with changes, partial completion, and extension requests reported promptly?
- Are unresolved, disputed, or recurring findings escalated through the appropriate path with documented response?
- Has the team avoided claiming completion on implementation evidence alone for findings that concern operating effectiveness?
- Are evidence packages mapped to each corrective action and ready for regulator verification, including walkthroughs and population coverage?
