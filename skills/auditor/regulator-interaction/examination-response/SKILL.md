# Examination Response

A regulatory examination or inspection is a high-stakes, externally imposed process in which the auditor's work is itself under review. Whether the examiner is a banking regulator, an insurance department, the SEC, or the PCAOB, the auditor's response shapes the outcome. Agents often stumble here because examinations feel like a request to cooperate, and the instinct is to produce everything quickly. The harm this prevents is concrete: over-broad production, inconsistent statements that contradict workpapers, waived privilege, missed deadlines that trigger enforcement, and a record that makes a defensible audit look weak. A disciplined examination response protects the audit, the firm, and the client while satisfying legitimate regulatory needs.

Use this skill when an examination or inspection notice arrives, when coordinating document production, when defining the scope of requests, or when managing the timeline and communications with examiners. The goal is to respond completely and accurately to legitimate requests while controlling scope, preserving privilege, and maintaining consistency with the audit record.

## Core Rules

### Establish Governance And A Single Coordination Point Before Producing Anything

An examination generates many requests from many people. Without governance, responses become inconsistent, duplicated, or over-broad. Set up coordination before the first document goes out.

Establish:

- a single engagement-level coordinator for the examination;
- a tracking log of every request, response, and due date;
- roles for document collection, legal review, and quality review;
- escalation paths for scope disputes and sensitive requests;
- communication protocols with the examiner;
- secure channels for production and storage;
- the client's involvement and approval where required.

Do not let individual team members respond directly to examiner requests without coordination.

### Define The Scope Of Each Request Precisely

Regulator requests are often broad and can be read either narrowly or expansively. The auditor should interpret scope deliberately, not default to producing everything available.

For each request, determine:

- the exact documents, periods, and accounts covered;
- whether it seeks the audit file, client records, or both;
- whether it targets a specific engagement or the firm's practices;
- the legal basis and authority for the request;
- whether it exceeds the regulator's jurisdiction;
- whether narrower production satisfies the legitimate need;
- whether the request is mandatory or discretionary;
- whether the period or population referenced matches the engagement records.

Where scope is ambiguous, seek clarification in writing rather than guessing. Document the agreed scope, and revisit it if the examiner later asks for more than the original request implied. A written scope understanding protects both sides and prevents scope creep during the examination.

### Preserve Privilege And Confidentiality Deliberately

Examinations routinely touch privileged and confidential material. Production without a privilege review can waive protections permanently.

Protect:

- attorney-client privileged communications;
- attorney work product;
- audit workpapers to the extent protected by applicable rules;
- confidential client information and trade secrets;
- personal data subject to privacy law;
- internal firm deliberative and quality review materials.

Use privilege logs, redactions, and confidentiality agreements where appropriate. Note that PCAOB and some regulators have statutory access rights that override certain privileges; understand which rights apply before withholding.

### Ensure Consistency With Workpapers And Prior Statements

The fastest route to an examination finding is a response that contradicts the audit file. Everything produced must be reconciled to the workpapers and to prior communications.

Reconcile:

- narratives and explanations against the audit file;
- risk assessments against documented risks;
- conclusions against documented conclusions;
- timelines against the engagement records;
- prior responses to the client, committee, or other regulators;
- oral statements made to examiners with the written record.

If a discrepancy is found, investigate and correct before production rather than papering over it.

### Manage The Timeline And Sequencing Proactively

Examinations have deadlines that, if missed, can escalate to subpoenas or enforcement. Manage the timeline as an active process, not a passive calendar.

Manage:

- the due date for each request and interim milestones;
- dependencies on the client, component auditors, or specialists;
- review and approval cycles before production;
- the examiner's own milestones and inspection windows;
- competing demands across multiple requests;
- buffer for rework if the examiner rejects a production.

Communicate proactively with the examiner about timing, especially if a deadline cannot be met. Silence invites escalation.

### Coordinate With The Client Without Surrendering Control

The examination may concern the client's financial reporting, but the auditor's workpapers and conclusions are the auditor's to produce and explain. Coordinate without ceding control.

Coordinate:

- align on factual matters where client and auditor records overlap;
- obtain client input on confidentiality and privilege;
- avoid producing client records without the client's awareness where required;
- maintain separate positions where auditor and client differ;
- keep the client informed of scope and progress;
- avoid being used as a channel to manage the client's regulator relationship.

### Document The Examination Record Defensibly

The examination itself generates a record that may be reviewed later. Document responses and interactions so they are defensible on later review.

Document:

- each request received, with date and source;
- the scope interpretation and any clarifications;
- the documents produced, with dates and references;
- documents withheld and the basis;
- meetings and calls with examiners, with attendees and topics;
- oral explanations given and their basis in the file;
- any disputes and their resolution.

Do not rely on memory later. The examination record should stand on its own.

### Recognize When To Involve Counsel And escalate

Some examinations or specific requests warrant legal counsel and escalation within the firm. Recognize these early.

Involve counsel or escalate when:

- the request concerns possible fraud, illegal acts, or enforcement;
- privilege or confidentiality is at stake;
- the scope exceeds the regulator's apparent authority;
- the examiner alleges a deficiency in the audit;
- the matter could affect the opinion or require restatement;
- there is a conflict between the client's interests and the auditor's duties.

## Common Traps

### Over-Producing To Appear Cooperative

Producing more than requested to show cooperation can waive privilege, expose unrelated matters, and create new lines of inquiry. Produce what is required, properly scoped and reviewed.

### Inconsistent Statements Across Requests

Telling one examiner one thing and another something else, or contradicting the workpapers, is a serious exposure. Reconcile every response to the file.

### Defaulting To Broad Scope Interpretation

Reading a request expansively because it is easier than negotiating scope creates unnecessary risk. Clarify and narrow where appropriate.

### Waiving Privilege Through Careless Production

Handing over privileged material without review can waive protections permanently. Conduct privilege review and use logs and redactions.

### Letting The Client Drive The Response

The client may want to manage the examination narrative. The auditor controls its own workpapers and explanations and must not become the client's agent.

### Missing Deadlines Silently

A missed deadline without communication triggers escalation. If a deadline is at risk, notify the examiner and propose a realistic date.

### Treating Oral Explanations As Informal

Oral statements to examiners become part of the record and can be quoted back. Treat oral explanations with the same care as written ones, and document them.

### Ignoring Prior Communications

Statements previously made to the committee, the client, or other regulators may resurface. Reconcile current responses with the full prior record.

## Self-Check

- Is there a single coordinator and a tracking log for every request, response, and due date?
- Has the scope of each request been interpreted precisely, with clarifications sought and documented where ambiguous?
- Have privileged, confidential, and personal materials been reviewed and protected through logs, redactions, or agreements?
- Is every narrative and explanation reconciled to the audit workpapers and prior statements before production?
- Is the examination timeline managed proactively, with dependencies identified and the examiner informed of any at-risk deadlines?
- Is the client coordinated with, without the auditor surrendering control of its workpapers and conclusions?
- Is the examination record documented defensibly, including requests, scope, productions, withholdings, and interactions?
- Have matters involving fraud, enforcement, privilege disputes, or alleged deficiencies been escalated and referred to counsel where appropriate?
- Are oral explanations treated with the same rigor as written responses and documented?
- Has the auditor avoided over-production that could waive privilege or open unrelated lines of inquiry?; are disputes over scope or authority documented with their resolution?
