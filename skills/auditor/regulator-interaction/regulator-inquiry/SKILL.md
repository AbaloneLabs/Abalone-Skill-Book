# Regulator Inquiry Response

A regulator inquiry is narrower than a full examination but no less consequential. It may arrive as a phone call, an email, a subpoena, or an informal request for information, and the auditor must judge what is being asked, what must be answered, and what must not be said. Agents often mishandle inquiries because they seem minor: a quick question deserves a quick answer. The harm this prevents is serious. A casual, incomplete, or inaccurate response can be treated as a misrepresentation, can obstruct an investigation, or can create a record that contradicts the audit file. The line between cooperation and self-incrimination, and between responsiveness and over-disclosure, requires deliberate judgment.

Use this skill when any regulator or supervisory authority contacts the auditor with a question or request, whether formal or informal. The goal is to respond accurately and completely to legitimate requests while controlling scope, protecting privilege, involving counsel where needed, and never obstructing a lawful process.

## Core Rules

### Classify The Inquiry Before Responding

The nature of the inquiry determines the obligations. Classify it deliberately before drafting any response.

Classify:

- formal versus informal (subpoena, order, letter, email, call);
- mandatory versus discretionary (statutory demand versus voluntary request);
- the authority and jurisdiction of the requester;
- the subject matter and the engagement it relates to;
- whether it concerns the client, the audit, or the firm;
- whether it signals an investigation or enforcement posture;
- whether it implicates the auditor or only the client.

Record the classification and its basis. Different classifications trigger different duties.

### Determine The Legal Duty To Respond

Not every request must be answered, and not every answer must be complete. Determine the legal duty precisely.

Assess:

- whether a statute or rule compels response;
- the consequences of non-response or refusal;
- whether the request is within the regulator's authority;
- whether confidentiality or privilege limits disclosure;
- whether client consent is required to disclose client information;
- whether the auditor has an independent duty to report (for example, illegal acts).

When the duty is unclear, obtain legal advice before responding. Do not assume cooperation is mandatory.

### Engage Counsel Early For Sensitive Inquiries

Inquiries touching on fraud, illegal acts, enforcement, or privilege require counsel. Engage early, not after a problematic response is made.

Involve counsel when:

- the inquiry relates to possible fraud or illegal acts;
- the auditor or firm may be a subject of investigation;
- privilege or work product may be implicated;
- the inquiry seeks testimony or sworn statements;
- the request is broad, ambiguous, or potentially over-reaching;
- the matter could affect the audit opinion or require restatement.

Counsel helps shape scope, protects privilege, and ensures the response is both complete and safe.

### Ensure Accuracy And Completeness Of Every Response

A response to a regulator is a formal record. It must be accurate, complete within the scope answered, and free of misleading omissions.

Ensure:

- factual statements are supported by the audit file;
- dates, amounts, and parties are verified;
- the response addresses what was actually asked;
- material qualifications are not omitted;
- uncertainty is stated rather than papered over;
- the response is internally consistent;
- the response is consistent with prior statements and the opinion.

A misleadingly partial answer can be treated as a misrepresentation even if each sentence is literally true.

### Control Scope And Avoid Volunteering Beyond The Question

Cooperation does not require volunteering information beyond the question. Over-disclosure can open new lines of inquiry, waive privilege, or harm the client or firm.

Control scope by:

- answering the specific question asked;
- not narrating unrelated audit areas;
- not speculating about matters not inquired into;
- not offering opinions unless specifically requested and supported;
- not producing documents beyond the request;
- seeking clarification when the question is ambiguous.

If the regulator wants more, let it ask. Do not expand the record unprompted.

### Preserve Privilege And Confidentiality

Inquiries often seek privileged or confidential material. Protect it appropriately.

Protect:

- attorney-client communications and work product;
- audit workpapers to the extent protected;
- confidential client and firm information;
- personal data under privacy law;
- internal deliberative and quality review material.

Use privilege logs, redactions, and protective agreements. Distinguish between regulators with statutory access rights and those without.

### Document The Inquiry And Response Fully

The inquiry and response create a record that may be reviewed later, including in enforcement. Document them defensibly.

Document:

- the request received, with date, source, and form;
- the classification and legal assessment;
- counsel involvement and advice;
- the scope of the response;
- the documents or information provided;
- material withheld and the basis;
- oral communications and their substance;
- follow-up requests and responses.

Do not rely on memory. The record should stand alone.

### Avoid Any Conduct That Could Be Obstruction

Obstruction is a serious offense and can arise from conduct that feels like normal cooperation. Recognize the line.

Avoid:

- destroying, altering, or concealing records;
- delaying production to wear down the regulator;
- coordinating stories with the client in a misleading way;
- making incomplete or evasive statements;
- omitting material context that changes meaning;
- intimidating witnesses or discouraging cooperation.

When in doubt, produce accurate, complete, and timely responses within the proper scope, and let counsel handle difficult judgments.

### Coordinate With The Client Appropriately

The client may have its own regulator relationship and its own counsel. Coordinate without surrendering the auditor's independent duties.

Coordinate:

- inform the client of inquiries concerning it where appropriate;
- align on factual matters in shared records;
- respect the client's privilege and confidentiality;
- maintain separate positions where auditor and client differ;
- do not become the client's agent in managing the regulator.

## Common Traps

### Treating An Informal Inquiry As Trivial

A phone call or casual email from a regulator can be the start of a formal process. Treat every inquiry with the same rigor as a written request until classified otherwise.

### Responding Before Classifying The Duty

Answering before understanding whether response is mandatory, discretionary, or prohibited can create exposure. Classify first, respond second.

### Volunteering Beyond The Question

Offering extra context to appear helpful can open new inquiries or waive privilege. Answer the question asked.

### Inconsistent With The Workpapers

A response that contradicts the audit file is a serious exposure. Reconcile every factual statement to the file.

### Omitting Qualifications That Change Meaning

A statement that is literally true but misleading by omission can be treated as a misrepresentation. Include material qualifications.

### Failing To Involve Counsel Early

Waiting until a response is drafted to involve counsel limits its value. Engage counsel when the inquiry is sensitive.

### Confusing Cooperation With Self-Incrimination

Cooperating beyond legal obligation, or volunteering material that incriminates the firm or client unnecessarily, is not required. Let counsel calibrate.

### Allowing The Client To Dictate The Auditor's Response

The client may want to shape the auditor's response to protect itself. The auditor responds independently and accurately.

## Self-Check

- Has the inquiry been classified as formal or informal, mandatory or discretionary, and within the requester's authority?
- Has the legal duty to respond been determined, with counsel engaged for sensitive matters?
- Is every factual statement in the response supported by the audit file and consistent with prior statements and the opinion?
- Does the response address the specific question asked without volunteering unrelated information?
- Are privileged, confidential, and personal materials protected through logs, redactions, or agreements?
- Is the response complete within its scope, with material qualifications included rather than omitted?
- Has conduct been reviewed to ensure nothing could constitute obstruction, including no alteration or concealment of records?
- Is the inquiry and response fully documented, including classification, counsel advice, scope, productions, and oral communications?
- Has the client been coordinated with appropriately without the auditor surrendering independent control of its response?
- Are oral communications treated with the same care as written responses and documented?; has the auditor avoided letting the client dictate a response that would be inaccurate or incomplete?
