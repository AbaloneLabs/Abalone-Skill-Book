# Other Information Reporting

Annual reports and similar documents contain, alongside the audited financial statements, a substantial amount of other information: management commentary, operational reviews, sustainability disclosures, non-GAAP measures, and narrative discussion. This other information is outside the scope of the audit opinion, but the auditor has a defined responsibility to read it, consider whether it is materially inconsistent with the audited financial statements or the auditor's knowledge, and respond appropriately when it is. The recurring failure is treating the other information as someone else's problem, reading it superficially or not at all, or assuming that because it is unaudited the auditor has no responsibility. In fact, users often read the annual report as a whole, and materially misleading other information can undermine the credibility of the audited statements it accompanies. The auditor's responsibility is limited but real, and discharging it requires reading with attention, not rubber-stamping.

Use this skill when the engagement involves a document containing audited financial statements and other information, when reading and evaluating the other information, and when responding to identified inconsistencies or misstatements of fact. The goal is to discharge the auditor's responsibility for other information without overstating it, and to ensure that materially misleading information accompanying the audited statements is identified and addressed.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Scope And Limits Of The Responsibility

The auditor's responsibility for other information is specific and bounded. It does not extend to providing assurance on the other information, and it does not require the auditor to audit it. The responsibility is to read the other information, consider whether it is materially inconsistent with the audited financial statements or with the auditor's knowledge obtained in the audit, and respond when a material inconsistency or misstatement of fact is identified.

Recognize that the responsibility:

- applies to other information in a document containing audited financial statements, such as an annual report;
- is a reading and consideration responsibility, not an audit or assurance responsibility;
- focuses on material inconsistency with the financial statements or with the auditor's knowledge;
- extends to misstatements of fact, not only financial inconsistencies;
- does not require the auditor to express a conclusion on the other information as a whole.

Understanding the boundaries prevents both under-discharge, ignoring the other information, and over-reach, treating it as if it were audited.

### Obtain The Other Information And Plan The Reading

The auditor must obtain the other information before the audit report is issued, where practicable, and plan to read it with the same attention given to the financial statements. The reading should be structured, not casual, because material inconsistencies are easy to miss in a lengthy document.

Plan by:

- identifying the document or documents that contain the other information;
- obtaining the other information before report issuance, or agreeing a timeline where it is not yet available;
- identifying the sections most likely to contain financial figures, non-GAAP measures, or claims tied to the audited statements;
- reading with the financial statements and audit knowledge in mind, not in isolation.

A disorganized or rushed reading, late in the engagement, is where inconsistencies are missed.

### Identify Material Inconsistency With The Financial Statements

A material inconsistency exists when the other information and the audited financial statements are inconsistent and the inconsistency is material. The inconsistency may be in figures, in characterization, or in the impression created. The auditor must identify such inconsistencies and respond.

Look for inconsistencies in:

- financial figures cited in the narrative that differ from the audited statements;
- non-GAAP or alternative performance measures that are reconciled incorrectly or misleadingly;
- characterizations of results, trends, or position that conflict with the audited statements;
- segment or geographic data that does not agree with the audited disclosures;
- selected financial data, five-year summaries, or key metrics that contain errors.

A material inconsistency is not a matter of opinion; it is a factual conflict with the audited statements that users would notice and that could mislead.

### Identify Misstatement Of Fact

Beyond financial inconsistency, the other information may contain a misstatement of fact: a statement about a matter other than the financial statements that appears to be incorrectly stated. The auditor's responsibility arises when the misstatement is material, judged in the context of the other information and the document as a whole.

Identify misstatements of fact in:

- operational or non-financial claims that conflict with the auditor's knowledge;
- descriptions of events, transactions, or circumstances that appear incorrect;
- sustainability, environmental, or governance claims that are contradicted by evidence obtained in the audit;
- legal, regulatory, or ownership statements that appear inaccurate.

The auditor is not expected to verify every fact, but a material misstatement of fact that comes to attention during reading must be addressed.

### Respond To Identified Inconsistencies Or Misstatements

When a material inconsistency or misstatement of fact is identified, the auditor must respond. The response depends on the nature of the matter and whether it relates to the financial statements or to other information.

Respond by:

- discussing the matter with management to understand the basis;
- requesting that management correct the inconsistency or misstatement;
- where the matter relates to the financial statements, determining whether it indicates a misstatement requiring correction;
- where the matter is in the other information and management refuses to correct, considering further action, including reporting the matter in the audit report.

The response escalates if management does not address a material inconsistency or misstatement that could mislead users.

### Report When Management Does Not Correct A Material Inconsistency

Where a material inconsistency or misstatement of fact in the other information is not corrected, and the matter is material, the auditor may need to report it. The form of reporting depends on the applicable standard, but it may include an other-matter or emphasis paragraph, or other communication, to alert users.

Report by:

- following the applicable standard's requirements for reporting uncorrected material inconsistencies;
- considering whether the matter warrants refusal to provide the document, withdrawal, or communication to governance;
- documenting the inconsistency, the discussions with management, and the basis for the reporting decision.

Silently accepting materially misleading other information, because it is unaudited, fails the responsibility and exposes users to misleading information.

### Handle Timing When Other Information Is Not Yet Available

The other information may not be available when the audit report is issued, particularly if the annual report is finalized after the financial statements. The auditor must plan for this and, where required, commit to reading the other information when it becomes available and taking action if a material inconsistency is later found.

Handle timing by:

- agreeing with management when the other information will be available;
- where it is not available before report issuance, following the standard's requirements for subsequent reading;
- committing, where required, to taking action if a material inconsistency is identified after issuance, including requesting correction or further communication.

Treating unavailable other information as no longer the auditor's concern fails the responsibility, which extends to information available after the report.

## Common Traps

### Treating Other Information As Someone Else's Problem

The other information is unaudited, but the auditor has a defined responsibility to read and consider it. Ignoring it fails that responsibility.

### Reading Superficially Or Late

A rushed or disorganized reading, late in the engagement, is where material inconsistencies are missed. Read with attention and structure.

### Confusing Opinion Scope With Responsibility

The auditor does not opine on the other information, but the responsibility to read, consider, and respond is real. The absence of an opinion does not mean the absence of responsibility.

### Missing Non-Financial Misstatements Of Fact

The responsibility extends to material misstatements of fact, not only financial inconsistencies. Operational and non-financial claims can mislead users too.

### Accepting Management's Refusal To Correct Silently

Where management will not correct a material inconsistency, the auditor must consider reporting or other action, not silently accept.

### Abandoning Responsibility When Information Is Late

Other information available after the report still falls within the responsibility. Plan for and commit to subsequent reading and action.

### Overstating Assurance On Other Information

The responsibility is to read and consider, not to assure. Do not express a conclusion on the other information beyond what the standard requires.

## Self-Check

- Is the scope and limit of the responsibility for other information understood, as a reading and consideration duty, not an audit or assurance duty?
- Has the other information been obtained before report issuance where practicable, with a plan and timeline where it is not?
- Is the reading structured and attentive, focused on sections most likely to contain financial figures, non-GAAP measures, and claims tied to the audited statements?
- Have material inconsistencies with the audited financial statements been identified, including figures, characterizations, and selected data?
- Have material misstatements of fact, including operational and non-financial claims, been identified where they come to attention?
- Where an inconsistency or misstatement is identified, has it been discussed with management and correction requested?
- Where management refuses to correct a material inconsistency, has the applicable standard's reporting or escalation requirements been followed?
- Where other information is not yet available, has the auditor committed to subsequent reading and action if a material inconsistency is later found?
- Is the responsibility discharged without overstating assurance on the other information?
- Could an independent reviewer confirm that the other information was read and considered, and that material inconsistencies or misstatements were addressed rather than ignored?
