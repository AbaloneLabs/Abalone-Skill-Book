# Audit Findings And Communication

Audit reporting is not a place to surprise readers with unsupported opinions. A strong finding explains the criteria, condition, cause, effect, evidence, and recommendation in a way that is fair, traceable, and useful. A weak finding says something is "inadequate" without stating compared to what, how it was tested, what failed, why it matters, and what should change. Reporting also requires care because audit language can imply assurance, blame, legal conclusions, or scope coverage the work does not support.

Use this skill before writing audit findings, management letters, board communications, issue logs, remediation plans, internal audit reports, vendor audit summaries, compliance findings, or performance audit conclusions. The goal is to help the agent communicate audit results without overstating evidence or hiding important limitations.

## Core Rules

### Build Findings From Criteria, Condition, Cause, And Effect

A finding should usually answer four questions: what should be, what is, why the difference exists, and why it matters. Not every engagement requires all elements in the same form, but the auditor should deliberately decide what is needed.

Develop:

- criteria: the standard, policy, law, contract, control, objective, or expectation;
- condition: the facts found through testing;
- cause: the reason the condition occurred;
- effect: the actual or potential consequence;
- recommendation: action that addresses the cause;
- evidence: procedures and support behind the condition;
- scope: population and period tested.

Avoid writing recommendations before understanding cause. If the cause is wrong, the recommendation will be superficial.

### State Scope And Evidence Limits Clearly

Readers need to understand what the audit covered and what it did not cover. This prevents users from applying conclusions beyond the evidence.

State:

- period audited;
- entity, location, system, or process;
- population;
- sample size and method;
- criteria used;
- procedures performed;
- reliance on management data;
- unavailable evidence;
- exclusions;
- limitations;
- assurance level if applicable.

Do not write "the process is effective" if the work only tested one location, one month, or one attribute. Match the conclusion to the scope.

### Separate Facts, Judgments, And Recommendations

Audit communication should make it clear which statements are tested facts, which are auditor judgments, and which are recommended actions.

Use discipline:

- facts should be supported by workpapers;
- judgments should explain reasoning and criteria;
- recommendations should connect to cause and risk;
- management responses should be labeled as management responses;
- unresolved disagreements should be described fairly.

Do not blend management's explanation into the auditor's conclusion unless the explanation was tested and supported.

### Quantify Effect When Practical

A finding is stronger when the effect is concrete. Effect can be financial, operational, compliance-related, reputational, security-related, customer-facing, reporting-related, or control-related.

Quantify:

- dollar amount;
- number of exceptions;
- percentage error rate;
- affected population;
- delay days;
- duplicate payments;
- missed approvals;
- unsupported transactions;
- unauthorized users;
- potential penalty exposure;
- service-level impact.

If effect cannot be quantified, explain potential effect and why quantification was not practical. Do not invent precision.

### Classify Severity Consistently

Severity should follow defined criteria, not tone. A finding's severity may depend on likelihood, magnitude, control importance, legal exposure, fraud risk, safety, public interest, recurrence, and management's remediation history.

Define or apply severity based on:

- material weakness, significant deficiency, control deficiency, or equivalent control categories;
- high, medium, low operational risk;
- regulatory or contractual impact;
- financial exposure;
- fraud or override risk;
- repeated prior issue;
- remediation urgency;
- affected users or stakeholders.

Do not downgrade severity simply because management disagrees or because the issue is uncomfortable.

### Write Recommendations That Address Root Cause

Recommendations should be actionable and proportionate. They should fix the reason the issue occurred, not only the symptom.

Good recommendations specify:

- responsible owner;
- process or control change;
- evidence expected after remediation;
- timing or priority;
- system or policy update if needed;
- training if knowledge caused the issue;
- monitoring or exception reporting;
- retesting expectation.

Avoid vague recommendations such as "management should ensure compliance." Say what control, evidence, review, or system change would make compliance more likely.

### Preserve Fairness And Right Of Response

Audit reports should be fair. Management should have an opportunity to review factual accuracy, provide context, and respond. The auditor still owns the conclusion.

Before finalizing:

- share draft findings through the agreed process;
- validate factual details;
- request missing evidence by a clear deadline;
- distinguish new evidence from explanations;
- include management response where required;
- resolve or document disagreements;
- update findings when evidence changes;
- preserve independence from management's preferred wording.

Fairness does not mean weakening supported conclusions.

### Communicate Sensitive Matters Appropriately

Some matters require restricted communication or escalation. Fraud indicators, illegal acts, security vulnerabilities, privacy breaches, harassment, retaliation, conflicts of interest, and legal privilege issues may need special handling.

Consider:

- who is authorized to receive the information;
- whether legal counsel should be involved;
- whether governance needs direct communication;
- whether management might be implicated;
- confidentiality and whistleblower protection;
- report distribution restrictions;
- redaction;
- evidence preservation;
- law enforcement or regulator obligations.

Do not include sensitive details in broad reports when a restricted communication is more appropriate.

### Track Management Action And Follow-Up

Reporting is not the end if follow-up is part of the engagement. Remediation should be tracked until implemented and verified.

Track:

- finding owner;
- agreed action;
- due date;
- status;
- evidence requested;
- implementation date;
- retest procedure;
- retest result;
- residual risk;
- closure approval.

Do not close findings based only on intent, meeting notes, or policy drafts. Verify operation where the issue involved a control.

## Common Traps

### Writing A Finding Without A Criterion

If the report does not state the basis for judgment, readers cannot distinguish an audit finding from a preference. Always anchor the issue in a standard, policy, contract, law, control objective, or agreed expectation.

### Making The Condition Broader Than The Evidence

If the auditor tested 25 transactions and found 4 exceptions, say that. Do not imply every transaction was wrong unless the evidence supports that conclusion.

### Recommending Training For Every Problem

Training may help when lack of knowledge caused the issue. It does not fix bad system design, missing controls, excessive access, poor incentives, unclear ownership, or lack of monitoring.

### Hiding Limitations In Footnotes

Scope limits, missing evidence, unreliable data, or management-imposed restrictions can change the conclusion. State material limitations clearly enough that readers do not over-rely on the report.

### Letting Management Rewrite The Finding

Management can correct facts and provide response, but should not remove supported criteria, condition, effect, or severity because the language is inconvenient.

### Overstating Assurance

Do not say "all controls are effective" when only selected controls were tested. Do not imply audit-level assurance for advisory work, readiness assessments, or agreed-upon procedures.

### Reporting Sensitive Details Too Broadly

Fraud indicators, security weaknesses, personal data, and legal matters can create harm if distributed widely. Use restricted channels when needed.

## Self-Check

- Does each finding include criteria, condition, cause, effect, recommendation, evidence, and scope where relevant?
- Is the conclusion limited to the period, population, sample, procedures, and evidence actually tested?
- Are facts, auditor judgments, recommendations, and management responses clearly separated?
- Is effect quantified where practical, and are uncertainty or limits explained when quantification is not practical?
- Is severity assigned using defined risk criteria rather than tone or management preference?
- Does the recommendation address root cause and specify the control, process, owner, timing, and evidence expected?
- Has management had a fair chance to correct factual errors and provide response without taking over the audit conclusion?
- Are sensitive matters escalated, restricted, redacted, or handled through legal or governance channels when appropriate?
- Are unresolved disagreements, missing evidence, and scope limitations documented clearly?
- Is follow-up designed to verify implementation and operating effectiveness before closure?
