# Comparative And Restatement Reporting

Prior-period financial statements are often presented alongside the current period for comparability, and those prior-period figures may be restated to correct an error, reflect a change in accounting principle, or reclassify amounts. The auditor's responsibility extends to the comparative information and to deciding how the audit report addresses restated figures, reissuance of a prior report, and the audit status of comparative information that was not previously audited. The recurring failure is treating comparative figures as a passive carry-forward, assuming the prior opinion still applies without reconsideration, or mishandling the distinction between restatement, reissuance, and dual dating. These errors can leave users with an opinion that no longer reflects the restated figures, or with comparative information whose audit status is unclear. The auditor must think deliberately about what is being compared, why prior figures changed, and how the report must respond.

Use this skill when prior-period figures are presented, when those figures are restated or reclassified, when a prior audit report may need reissuance, or when comparative information of differing audit status is presented. The goal is an audit report that correctly reflects the audit status of each period presented and the reasons for any changes to prior figures.

## Core Rules

### Establish The Audit Status Of Each Period Presented

The first step is to determine the audit status of each period of comparative information. Audited comparative figures carry the auditor's opinion; unaudited comparative figures do not. The report must make the status of each period clear to users.

Establish status by:

- identifying which periods are presented as comparative information;
- determining whether each period was audited, and by whom;
- confirming whether the prior-period audit report, if any, remains appropriate for the figures now presented;
- ensuring the report clearly identifies the audited periods and the status of any unaudited comparative information.

Ambiguity about audit status misleads users who may assume all presented figures carry the same level of assurance.

### Distinguish Corresponding Figures From Comparative Financial Statements

Reporting frameworks and jurisdictions differ in how comparative information is presented. Two common approaches are corresponding figures, where prior-period amounts are shown as comparative to the current period within a single set of statements, and comparative financial statements, where full prior-period statements are presented as a separate audited period. The auditor's reporting approach differs between the two.

Distinguish by:

- confirming which presentation approach the entity uses and the applicable framework's requirements;
- for corresponding figures, determining whether the prior-period figures are consistent with the current period and whether any restatement is disclosed;
- for comparative financial statements, issuing an opinion that covers both periods and references the prior-period auditor where required.

Conflating the two approaches leads to an opinion that does not match the presentation.

### Address Restatement Of Prior-Period Figures

When prior-period figures are restated to correct a material error, the auditor must determine whether the restatement is appropriate, whether the prior-period audit report requires modification, and how the current report addresses the restated figures. Restatement is not a clerical update; it reflects a correction of error that may change the basis on which the prior opinion was issued.

Address restatement by:

- evaluating whether the restatement corrects a prior error in accordance with the applicable framework;
- confirming that the entity has disclosed the nature of the error and the effect of the restatement;
- determining whether the prior-period opinion, as originally issued, remains appropriate for the restated figures;
- where the prior opinion would not remain appropriate, modifying the current report to address the restatement, consistent with the presentation approach.

Failing to reconsider the prior opinion in light of the restatement leaves a report that may no longer be supported.

### Decide Whether Reissuance Of A Prior Report Is Required

In some circumstances, the auditor must reissue or update the prior-period audit report, rather than simply carrying the original opinion forward. Reissuance may be required when the prior-period figures are restated, when the prior report contained a modification that no longer applies, or when subsequent events or additional information affects the prior figures.

Decide on reissuance by:

- assessing whether the prior-period figures have changed in a way that affects the prior opinion;
- considering whether the prior report's modifications or emphases remain relevant;
- where reissuance is required, following the applicable standard's requirements for the reissued report, including dual dating where appropriate;
- coordinating with any predecessor auditor where a prior period was audited by a different firm.

Simply carrying forward an opinion that no longer reflects the restated or reconsidered figures fails the responsibility.

### Handle Dual Dating And The Date Of The Report

When a prior-period report is reissued or when the current report addresses prior-period figures, the date of the report and any dual dating must be considered. The report date establishes the cutoff for the auditor's responsibility for subsequent events, and dual dating may be used to limit responsibility to specific subsequent events or procedures.

Handle dating by:

- dating the report no earlier than the date sufficient appropriate audit evidence is obtained;
- using dual dating where the report is updated for a specific subsequent event or procedure, to limit the extended responsibility to that matter;
- ensuring the date reflects when the auditor obtained evidence supporting the opinion on both current and prior periods.

An incorrect or unconsidered report date can extend or misstate the auditor's responsibility for subsequent events.

### Address Changes In Accounting Principle

A change in accounting principle, as distinct from a correction of error, requires disclosure and may require specific reporting treatment. The auditor must determine whether the change is appropriate, whether disclosure is adequate, and whether the report must draw attention to the change.

Address changes in principle by:

- evaluating whether the change is justified and applied in accordance with the framework;
- confirming the entity has disclosed the nature of the change and its effect;
- considering whether an emphasis-of-matter or other reporting treatment is required to draw users' attention to the change.

Treating a change in principle as if it were a restatement, or vice versa, mischaracterizes the nature of the change.

### Report When Comparative Information Is Unaudited

Where comparative information is presented but was not audited, the auditor must ensure the report clearly identifies that the comparative figures are unaudited and that the opinion does not cover them. Users must not be left to assume that unaudited comparative figures carry the auditor's assurance.

Report unaudited comparatives by:

- ensuring the report states that the comparative information is unaudited;
- confirming that the opinion scope is limited to the audited period;
- where required by the framework or jurisdiction, considering whether an other-matter paragraph is needed.

Presenting unaudited comparative figures without clear identification misleads users about the scope of assurance.

## Common Traps

### Treating Comparative Figures As A Passive Carry-Forward

Comparative figures are not automatically covered by the prior opinion. Their audit status, consistency, and any restatement must be considered.

### Conflating Restatement With Change In Principle

A correction of error and a change in accounting principle have different reporting treatments. Conflating them mischaracterizes the nature of the change.

### Failing To Reconsider The Prior Opinion

When prior figures are restated, the prior opinion must be reconsidered. It may no longer be appropriate for the restated figures.

### Mishandling Dual Dating

Dual dating limits responsibility to specific subsequent events. Misuse can extend or misstate the auditor's responsibility.

### Leaving Unaudited Comparatives Ambiguous

Unaudited comparative figures must be clearly identified. Ambiguity misleads users about the scope of assurance.

### Ignoring Predecessor Auditor Considerations

When a prior period was audited by a different firm, coordination and reporting requirements apply. Ignoring these leads to gaps or conflicts.

### Assuming The Original Report Date Still Applies

Restatement, reissuance, and reconsideration may change the report date. The date must reflect when evidence supporting the opinion was obtained.

## Self-Check

- Is the audit status of each period of comparative information established and clearly reflected in the report?
- Is the presentation approach, corresponding figures or comparative financial statements, correctly identified and matched to the reporting approach?
- Where prior-period figures are restated, has the prior opinion been reconsidered and, if necessary, modified or reissued?
- Has the need for reissuance of a prior report been assessed, including where the prior figures have changed or the prior modification no longer applies?
- Is the report date, including any dual dating, correct and reflective of when evidence supporting the opinion was obtained?
- Where there is a change in accounting principle, has the change been evaluated, disclosure confirmed, and any required reporting treatment applied?
- Where comparative information is unaudited, is it clearly identified as such, with the opinion scope limited to the audited period?
- Where a prior period was audited by a predecessor, have coordination and reporting requirements been followed?
- Does the report correctly distinguish restatement from change in principle, and apply the appropriate treatment to each?
- Could an independent reviewer confirm that the audit status, restatement treatment, and report dating are correct and consistent with the applicable standard?
