# Governance And Required Communications

Communication with those charged with governance is a required element of every audit, not an optional courtesy. The applicable auditing standards specify matters that must be communicated, and the form, timing, and content of those communications affect whether governance can fulfill its oversight role. The recurring failure is treating governance communication as a final meeting where the auditor presents the finished report, rather than as a two-way dialogue throughout the engagement that informs governance, allows challenge, and surfaces matters while they can still influence the audit and the financial statements. When significant findings, difficult judgments, independence matters, or control deficiencies are communicated only at the end, or buried in a standard letter, governance loses the opportunity to act, and the auditor loses the benefit of governance oversight.

Use this skill when planning and conducting communications with governance, when determining required communications, and when deciding the form, timing, and content of reporting. The goal is communications that meet the standard's requirements, serve governance's oversight role, and preserve the auditor's independence and direct access.

## Core Rules

### Identify Those Charged With Governance Correctly

Communication must be directed to the right people. Those charged with governance are the individuals or bodies with responsibility for overseeing the entity's financial reporting and, where applicable, for overseeing the audit. The identity and structure vary: a board of directors, an audit committee, a supervisory board, a sole owner-manager, or a public-sector governing body.

Identify by:

- determining the governance structure and who holds oversight of financial reporting;
- identifying the audit committee or equivalent, where one exists;
- recognizing when governance is concentrated in a single owner-manager, which changes the communication approach;
- confirming who has authority to receive and act on audit communications;
- where governance is layered, determining the appropriate level for each type of communication.

Communicating to the wrong level, such as to management when the standard requires governance, does not satisfy the requirement.

### Plan Two-Way Communication Throughout The Engagement

Governance communication is not a single event; it is a process that should run throughout the engagement. Early communication establishes the terms, sets expectations, and allows governance to raise concerns. Mid-engagement communication surfaces significant findings while they can be addressed. Final communication reports conclusions and required matters.

Plan by:

- agreeing the form, timing, and content of communications at the start, with governance;
- establishing the auditor's direct access to governance, not filtered through management;
- scheduling communication points that align with key milestones: planning, significant findings, and reporting;
- confirming that governance can initiate communication with the auditor as well.

A communication plan that exists only at the end forfeits the oversight value governance provides during the engagement.

### Communicate The Required Matters

The auditing standards specify matters that must be communicated to governance. These are not discretionary; their omission is a compliance failure. The auditor must understand which matters the applicable standard requires and ensure each is communicated.

Required matters typically include:

- the auditor's responsibilities and the scope of the audit;
- significant findings, including accounting policies, estimates, and significant risks;
- significant difficulties encountered during the audit;
- significant deficiencies and material weaknesses in internal control;
- the auditor's independence and, where applicable, the firm's relationships that may bear on independence;
- uncorrected misstatements and management's judgments about them;
- significant disagreements with management, if any;
- expected modifications to the opinion or report;
- other information and the auditor's responsibilities regarding it;
- fraud or suspected fraud identified during the audit.

Tailor the communication to the engagement's circumstances, but do not omit a required matter because it is inconvenient or uncomfortable.

### Communicate Significant Findings And Judgments Meaningfully

The communication of significant findings is where governance oversight adds the most value. But a communication that lists findings without explaining their significance, or that reports judgments without explaining the alternatives considered, gives governance little to work with. The communication should convey what matters and why.

Communicate meaningfully by:

- explaining the significant accounting policies, estimates, and judgments, including the range of possible outcomes;
- describing significant risks and how the audit responded to them;
- reporting significant difficulties, such as restrictions on scope or unavailability of evidence;
- presenting uncorrected misstatements and their aggregate effect;
- explaining the auditor's view on management's significant judgments, including any concerns about bias.

A communication that merely lists items, without their significance, does not enable governance to exercise oversight.

### Communicate Control Deficiencies At The Appropriate Level

Control deficiencies must be communicated to governance, but the level of communication depends on severity. Significant deficiencies and material weaknesses require communication to those charged with governance; lesser deficiencies may be communicated to management. The auditor must classify deficiencies correctly and communicate each at the level the standard requires.

Communicate deficiencies by:

- classifying each deficiency as control deficiency, significant deficiency, or material weakness;
- communicating significant deficiencies and material weaknesses to governance, in writing where required;
- communicating lesser deficiencies to management, with a summary to governance where appropriate;
- explaining the risk each deficiency addresses, the potential effect, and the recommended remediation.

A material weakness communicated only to management, or softened in its severity, fails the communication requirement and misleads governance about the state of control.

### Communicate Independence Matters Transparently

Where independence rules require communication to governance, the auditor must disclose the relationships and services that may bear on independence, and the safeguards applied. This communication supports governance's oversight of the auditor's independence.

Communicate independence by:

- disclosing relationships and services that may create threats;
- explaining the safeguards applied to reduce threats to an acceptable level;
- confirming, where required, that the firm is independent;
- addressing non-audit services and fees in the context of independence.

A generic independence statement, without the specific relationships and services, does not meet the communication requirement.

### Preserve Direct Access And Independence In Communication

The auditor's communication with governance must be direct and must not be filtered or altered by management. Where management controls what reaches governance, the auditor's independence is compromised, and significant matters may be suppressed.

Preserve direct access by:

- communicating directly with governance or the audit committee, not through management;
- resisting any attempt by management to soften, delay, or filter communications;
- documenting any restriction on communication and escalating where necessary;
- maintaining the right to meet governance without management present.

A reporting line that requires management approval before findings reach governance does not meet the standard.

## Common Traps

### Treating Communication As A Final Meeting

Communicating only at the end forfeits the oversight value governance provides during the engagement and prevents governance from acting on matters while they can be addressed.

### Communicating To The Wrong Level

Reporting to management when the standard requires governance, or vice versa, does not satisfy the requirement.

### Omitting Required Matters

Required communications are not discretionary. Omitting an uncomfortable matter, such as a disagreement or a material weakness, is a compliance failure.

### Listing Without Explaining Significance

A communication that lists findings without their significance gives governance nothing to act on. Explain what matters and why.

### Softening Severity To Avoid Discomfort

Downgrading a material weakness or a significant finding because it is uncomfortable misleads governance and breaches the communication requirement.

### Allowing Management To Filter Communication

Communication filtered or altered by management loses its independence and may suppress significant matters.

### Generic Independence Statements

A boilerplate independence letter, without the specific relationships and safeguards, does not meet the communication requirement.

## Self-Check

- Have those charged with governance been correctly identified, and is communication directed to the appropriate level for each matter?
- Is communication planned as a two-way process throughout the engagement, with form, timing, and content agreed at the start?
- Are all matters required by the applicable standard communicated, including responsibilities, significant findings, difficulties, deficiencies, independence, misstatements, disagreements, and fraud?
- Are significant findings and judgments communicated with their significance, alternatives, and the auditor's view, rather than as a bare list?
- Are control deficiencies classified and communicated at the level their severity requires, with material weaknesses and significant deficiencies reaching governance?
- Are independence matters, including relationships, services, and safeguards, communicated transparently and specifically?
- Is the auditor's communication direct to governance, free from management filtering or alteration?
- Are significant disagreements, restrictions, and uncomfortable matters communicated honestly rather than softened or omitted?
- Is the communication documented, including what was communicated, to whom, when, and any restrictions encountered?
- Could governance exercise effective oversight based on the communications received, and could an independent reviewer confirm the required matters were communicated at the right level and time?
