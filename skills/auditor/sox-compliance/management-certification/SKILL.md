# Management Certification

SOX Sections 302 and 906 require the principal executive and financial officers to certify personally that the financial statements and disclosures are fairly presented and that they are responsible for the controls that support them. These certifications are not ceremonial; they create personal liability and are enforced. Agents frequently treat certification support as a documentation exercise — confirming that a sub-certification form was signed — rather than examining whether the underlying process actually gathers, evaluates, and escalates the information the officers are certifying. The harm is that officers certify disclosures they did not meaningfully evaluate, and the disclosure controls that are supposed to catch material information gaps are never tested.

Use this skill when reviewing the support for 302/906 certifications, when testing disclosure controls and procedures, when evaluating sub-certification processes, and when assessing whether management's representations on ICFR and the financial statements are supportable. The goal is to confirm that the certification is backed by a real, tested process — not merely a signed form.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish What Each Certification Requires

Section 302 and Section 906 certifications impose different obligations, and conflating them leads to incomplete support. Section 302 covers responsibility for the report, the design and effectiveness of disclosure controls and procedures, ICFR conclusions, and disclosures of significant changes and material weaknesses. Section 906 criminal liability covers the fairness of the financial statements and their conformity with GAAP. Agents must know exactly what each officer is signing.

Confirm support for, at minimum:

- the signing officers reviewed the report;
- based on their knowledge, the financial statements do not contain a material misstatement;
- the financial statements and other financial information fairly present the financial condition and results;
- the signing officers are responsible for establishing and maintaining disclosure controls and procedures;
- the officers have evaluated the effectiveness of disclosure controls and procedures as of a date within 90 days of filing;
- the officers have disclosed significant changes in ICFR and whether corrective actions are planned for significant deficiencies and material weaknesses;
- the 906 criminal certification that the financial statements comply with applicable GAAP requirements.

### Test Disclosure Controls And Procedures, Not Just ICFR

Disclosure controls and procedures (DC&P) are broader than ICFR: they are designed to ensure that information required to be disclosed is recorded, processed, summarized, and reported within the required time, and accumulated and communicated to management for decisions. Agents often focus on ICFR and treat DC&P as a conclusion by reference. Test DC&P as a distinct control objective.

Test DC&P by examining:

- the process for identifying required disclosures beyond the financial statements (MD&A, risk factors, commitments, contingencies, related parties);
- the mechanisms for capturing material non-financial information and getting it to the disclosure committee and certifying officers;
- the timeliness of information flow relative to filing deadlines;
- the disclosure committee's operation, membership, and meeting cadence;
- the evaluation of subsequent events and new information before filing;
- the controls over completeness and accuracy of disclosures sourced from outside the general ledger.

### Evaluate The Sub-Certification Process For Real Content

Sub-certifications are how information flows up to the certifying officers from business unit and function leaders. A sub-certification that is a boilerplate form, signed without review of the underlying data, provides no real support. Evaluate whether the sub-certification process actually gathers meaningful representations and escalates issues.

Assess sub-certifications for:

- whether the questions are specific to the certifier's area and the period;
- whether the certifier has a basis for the representations (not just a signature);
- whether exceptions, control issues, and changes are required to be disclosed;
- whether the process reaches the right people (controllers, unit leaders, tax, legal, HR, IT);
- whether follow-up occurs on items raised in sub-certifications;
- whether the disclosure committee reviews and challenges sub-certifications before they roll up to the officers;
- whether prior-period issues are tracked to resolution within the process.

### Confirm CEO/CFO Responsibility For The Financial Statements And Disclosures

The certifying officers must take responsibility for the financial statements and the disclosures, which means they must be genuinely involved in their review, not merely presented with a final draft. Agents should evaluate whether the officers have substantive engagement with the content they certify.

Confirm the officers:

- review draft financial statements and disclosures and document that review;
- are involved in evaluating significant accounting positions, estimates, and judgments;
- review significant new accounting standards and their adoption;
- are informed of significant transactions, restatements, and errors;
- participate in or receive the results of the disclosure committee's evaluation;
- receive and consider the auditor's communications, including significant deficiencies and material weaknesses.

### Evaluate The ICFR Representations In The Certification

Section 302 requires the officers to report on ICFR: the conclusion on effectiveness, significant changes, and disclosure of significant deficiencies and material weaknesses. The certification's ICFR representations must align with management's 404 assessment and with the auditor's findings. Agents must reconcile these representations to avoid inconsistency.

Reconcile:

- the effectiveness conclusion in the 302 certification with management's 404 assessment;
- the disclosure of any material weaknesses, including those identified late in the process;
- significant changes in ICFR and planned corrective actions;
- any significant deficiencies disclosed to the auditor and whether they are reported externally;
- consistency between the certification language and the financial statement disclosures on ICFR.

### Evaluate The Disclosure Committee's Substantive Role

The disclosure committee is the body that assembles, evaluates, and recommends disclosure decisions to the certifying officers. A committee that exists on paper but does not meet, does not challenge content, or lacks the right membership provides no real assurance. Evaluate whether the committee functions as a genuine control over disclosure completeness and accuracy.

Confirm the committee:

- has appropriate membership (controller, general counsel, tax, investor relations, treasury, risk, and business leaders as relevant);
- meets on a cadence aligned to filing deadlines, with documented minutes;
- reviews the full set of disclosures, not just the financial statements;
- challenges estimates, new accounting positions, and sensitive disclosures;
- coordinates with the external auditor on disclosure matters;
- escalates unresolved issues to the certifying officers and the audit committee.

### Assess The Timing Of The Evaluation Within 90 Days

Section 302 requires the disclosure controls evaluation as of a date within 90 days prior to the filing. Agents should confirm the evaluation date is within the window and that the evaluation reflects conditions as of that date, not a stale prior assessment.

Confirm:

- the evaluation date is within 90 days of the filing date;
- the evaluation reflects the control environment and disclosures as of that date;
- any changes between the evaluation date and the filing date are considered;
- the certifying officers actually performed or oversaw the evaluation as of that date.

### Evaluate The Process For Late-Breaking Information

Information that arises between the evaluation date and the filing — a subsequent event, a new lawsuit, a control failure, a restatement — can change what the certification should disclose. Agents should test whether there is a process to capture and escalate such information rather than certifying on a frozen evaluation.

Confirm the process captures:

- subsequent events and their disclosure implications;
- litigation, investigations, and contingencies arising before filing;
- control deficiencies or fraud discovered after the evaluation date;
- changes in estimates or significant transactions;
- any matter that would require amendment of the certification or the disclosures.

### Link Certification Support To Evidence, Not Assertion

Every representation in a certification should trace to underlying evidence: review documentation, sub-certifications, disclosure committee minutes, and ICFR evaluation workpapers. A certification supported only by the officers' assertion is not supportable. Build the evidentiary link explicitly.

Trace each representation to:

- the financial statement review evidence signed or retained by the officers;
- the disclosure committee minutes and supporting analyses;
- the sub-certifications and their supporting documentation;
- the ICFR evaluation workpapers and deficiency analysis;
- the auditor's communications on deficiencies and material weaknesses;
- the subsequent-events review and disclosure support.

## Common Traps

### Treating Certification As A Signed Form

A certification supported only by a signature, without underlying review and evaluation evidence, is not defensible and exposes the officers to personal liability.

### Collapsing DC&P Into ICFR

Disclosure controls are broader than ICFR and must be evaluated separately. Treating an ICFR conclusion as a DC&P conclusion misses non-financial and timing-related disclosure risks.

### Boilerplate Sub-Certifications

Sub-certifications with generic questions signed without review of the underlying data provide no real support and mask unreported issues.

### Ignoring The 90-Day Evaluation Window

An evaluation dated outside the 90-day window, or not updated for late changes, does not satisfy the timing requirement and may not reflect current conditions.

### Inconsistent ICFR Representations

The 302 certification's ICFR conclusion must match the 404 assessment and the auditor's findings. Inconsistency between these is a red flag for reviewers and regulators.

### Missing Late-Breaking Information

Certifying on a frozen evaluation without a process to capture subsequent events, new litigation, or control failures leads to certifications that omit material disclosures.

### Officers Not Substantively Involved

If the certifying officers merely receive a final draft without genuine review and engagement, their responsibility representations are not supportable.

### Omitting Material Weakness Disclosure

Material weaknesses identified late, even after the evaluation date, must be disclosed. Omitting them to preserve an effectiveness conclusion is a serious certification failure.

### Treating The Disclosure Committee As A Formality

A disclosure committee that meets without challenging content, without the right members, or without documented deliberation provides no real assurance that disclosures are complete and accurate.

## Self-Check

- Are the distinct requirements of Section 302 and Section 906 each addressed with specific support?
- Has disclosure controls and procedures been tested as a separate control objective, including capture of non-financial and timing-sensitive information?
- Do sub-certifications contain specific, period-relevant questions with a documented basis, reaching the right functions and business units?
- Are the certifying officers substantively involved in reviewing financial statements, estimates, significant transactions, and auditor communications?
- Is the ICFR conclusion in the 302 certification reconciled to management's 404 assessment and the auditor's findings?
- Is the disclosure controls evaluation dated within 90 days of filing and reflective of conditions as of that date?
- Is there a process to capture and escalate late-breaking information (subsequent events, litigation, control failures) before filing?
- Does every certification representation trace to underlying evidence — review docs, disclosure committee minutes, sub-certifications, ICFR workpapers?
- Are any material weaknesses, including those identified late, disclosed in the certification and the financial statements?
- Are significant deficiencies and material weaknesses communicated to the auditor consistent with the certification representations?
