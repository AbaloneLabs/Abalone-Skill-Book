# SOX Section 404 Testing

Section 404 requires both management to assess and the auditor to opine on the effectiveness of internal control over financial reporting (ICFR), and the discipline that makes this work is the top-down, risk-based approach: start from the financial statement risks that matter, scope down to the controls that address them, and test those controls to the precision the risk requires. Agents frequently invert this discipline — they start from last year's control list, test broadly, and then try to rationalize the scope afterward. The harm is an inefficient audit that still misses the controls that actually drive material misstatement risk, and a deficiency evaluation that is disconnected from the financial statement assertions it is supposed to protect.

Use this skill when planning and executing a 404 engagement: when scoping significant accounts and locations, when performing walkthroughs, when designing tests of design and operating effectiveness, when relying on IT general controls and SOC reports, and when evaluating deficiencies for the management report and the auditor's opinion. The goal is a risk-anchored scope, evidence that supports an effectiveness conclusion, and a defensible severity classification of any deficiencies.

## Core Rules

### Anchor Scope To Financial Statement Risk, Not Last Year's List

A 404 scope must begin with the financial statements: which accounts, disclosures, and assertions present a reasonable possibility of material misstatement. Only controls that address those risks belong in scope. Starting from the prior-year control inventory, or from the process narratives, produces a scope that may be thorough but is not risk-justified, and it tends to omit new risks while retaining controls that no longer matter.

Build scope top-down:

- identify significant accounts and disclosures by size, susceptibility, and qualitative factors;
- determine the relevant assertions for each significant account;
- identify the risks of material misstatement to those assertions;
- identify the processes and the controls (manual, automated, and IT-dependent) that address those risks;
- include controls over significant business cycles, estimates, journal entries, and the financial close;
- document the rationale for excluding accounts, locations, or controls as low risk.

### Identify Significant Accounts And Locations Defensibly

Significant accounts are those with a reasonable possibility of a material misstatement, considering size and susceptibility, and scoping must cover the locations and business units that drive them. Agents often scope by revenue or asset concentration alone, which can miss a smaller location with complex transactions or weak controls.

Assess significance by:

- quantitative size relative to materiality and performance materiality;
- susceptibility to error, fraud, or estimate-driven misstatement;
- qualitative factors such as related parties, new accounting standards, or prior-period restatements;
- the presence of significant estimates and judgments;
- coverage of locations based on specific risk and coverage percentage thresholds;
- the risk that a non-significant location aggregates into a material misstatement.

### Perform Walkthroughs To Confirm Design, Not To Test Operation

Walkthroughs confirm that the auditor understands the flow of transactions and that controls are designed appropriately to address the identified risks. A walkthrough is a design-level procedure, not operating-effectiveness testing, and conflating the two is a common error. Use walkthroughs to trace a transaction from initiation through recording and to confirm control design before committing to testing.

Through walkthroughs, confirm:

- the flow of significant transactions from initiation through the financial statements;
- that the controls identified are actually performed as described;
- that the control addresses the specific risk and assertion;
- who performs the control, with what authority, and using what information;
- where IT systems capture, process, and report the data the control relies on;
- the points at which misstatement could occur and how the control addresses them;
- that design deficiencies are identified before operating testing begins.

### Test Design Effectiveness Before Operating Effectiveness

A control cannot be tested for operating effectiveness if its design is flawed. Evaluate design effectiveness first: does the control, individually or with other controls, provide reasonable assurance of preventing or detecting a material misstatement? Only controls that pass the design test proceed to operating-effectiveness testing.

For design effectiveness, evaluate:

- whether the control addresses the specific risk at sufficient precision;
- whether the control is performed by competent people with appropriate authority;
- whether the control has clear triggers, evidence, and follow-up;
- whether complementary controls compensate for a weaker primary control;
- whether automated controls depend on IT general controls that support their reliability;
- whether the combined set of controls covers the assertion end to end.

### Test Operating Effectiveness With Sufficient And Appropriate Evidence

Operating-effectiveness testing determines whether the control functioned throughout the period of reliance. The sample size, the nature of the evidence, and the timing must match the risk and the control frequency. Agents often under-test by relying on inspection of a few samples or by accepting evidence that does not show the control actually operated.

Match testing to the control:

- determine the nature of evidence needed (reperformance, inspection, observation, inquiry plus corroboration);
- set sample sizes appropriate to the risk and the control frequency (daily, weekly, monthly, quarterly, point-in-time);
- cover the period of reliance, not just a convenient window;
- for automated controls, test once plus the supporting IT general controls rather than repeated sampling;
- for management review controls, test the evidence of review, the precision of the review, and follow-up on exceptions;
- test the completeness and accuracy of information the control relies on;
- evaluate deviations for their nature, cause, and whether they indicate a deficiency.

### Test IT General Controls That Support Relied-Upon Controls

Automated controls and IT-dependent manual controls are only as reliable as the IT general controls (ITGCs) that govern the underlying systems. Agents often test the automated control but skip or under-test the ITGCs, which undermines the basis for relying on the automation. Test the ITGCs that are relevant to the systems supporting in-scope controls.

Test the relevant ITGCs:

- logical access over the applications and data supporting in-scope controls;
- change management for application and relevant infrastructure changes;
- IT operations, including job scheduling, backup, and incident handling;
- interface controls where data moves between systems;
- the acquisition, implementation, and configuration of the application;
- the link between each ITGC and the specific automated or IT-dependent control it supports.

### Use Service Organization Controls (SOC 1) Correctly

When a service organization performs processes relevant to ICFR, the auditor may rely on a SOC 1 report, but only if the report covers the relevant period, the relevant control objectives, and the user organization's complementary controls. Agents frequently accept a SOC 1 without confirming these conditions, which provides no real assurance.

When relying on a SOC 1:

- confirm it is a Type 2 report covering the user organization's period of reliance;
- map the service organization's control objectives to the user's relevant assertions;
- identify and test the user organization's complementary user controls;
- evaluate any qualified or adverse opinions and exceptions in the report;
- assess the service auditor's scope and whether sub-service organizations are carved out or inclusive;
- perform procedures over the completeness and accuracy of data transmitted to and from the service organization.

### Distinguish Management's Assessment From The Auditor's Opinion

Management issues the assessment of ICFR effectiveness; the auditor issues a separate opinion. The two can diverge, and the auditor must reach an independent conclusion rather than ratify management's. Agents must keep the evidence and conclusions separate even when the testing is coordinated.

Maintain independence of conclusion:

- base the opinion on the auditor's own risk assessment and testing, not on management's process alone;
- evaluate management's process for its adequacy, including its documentation and evaluation of deficiencies;
- reach an independent conclusion on deficiency severity and overall effectiveness;
- address any disagreements between management's assessment and the auditor's evidence;
- ensure the auditor's report clearly states the opinion and its basis, separate from management's report.

### Evaluate Deficiency Severity Using The Right Framework

Control deficiencies must be evaluated as either control deficiencies, significant deficiencies, or material weaknesses, using both qualitative and quantitative indicators. The severity determination drives both management's report and the auditor's opinion, and a misclassification changes the conclusion. Aggregate deficiencies before concluding, because individually inconsequential deficiencies can combine into a material weakness.

Evaluate severity by considering:

- the likelihood that a deficiency could allow a misstatement and the magnitude of potential misstatement;
- qualitative indicators of material weakness (restatement, identified fraud, oversight of external auditors ineffective, significant deficiency not remediated);
- aggregation of deficiencies that affect the same account, assertion, or process;
- whether compensating controls reduce the severity, and whether they were tested;
- the effect on the auditor's ability to plan the substantive audit;
- whether the deficiency is remediated and, if so, whether remediation was tested for operating effectiveness.

## Common Traps

### Starting Scope From Last Year's Control List

Inheriting the prior-year scope without re-anchoring to current-year financial statement risk retains irrelevant controls and omits new risks, producing a scope that is not risk-justified.

### Treating Walkthroughs As Operating Testing

A walkthrough confirms design and understanding; it does not provide evidence that the control operated throughout the period. Conflating the two under-tests operating effectiveness.

### Under-Testing Management Review Controls

Review controls are often accepted on the existence of a signature or an email, without testing the precision of the review, the completeness and accuracy of the data reviewed, or follow-up on exceptions.

### Relying On Automated Controls Without Testing ITGCs

An automated control is only reliable if the underlying ITGCs (access, change, operations) support it. Testing the automation once without the ITGCs provides no assurance over the period.

### Accepting A SOC 1 Without Confirming Period And Scope

A Type 1 report, a report covering the wrong period, or one whose control objectives do not map to the user's assertions provides no basis for reliance.

### Evaluating Deficiencies In Isolation

Deficiencies affecting the same account or assertion can aggregate into a material weakness. Evaluating each in isolation understates severity.

### Ratifying Management's Assessment Without Independent Evidence

The auditor's opinion must rest on independent testing and judgment. Adopting management's conclusion without independent support is a fundamental 404 failure.

### Skipping Completeness And Accuracy Of Reports Used By Controls

A control that reviews a report is only effective if the report is complete and accurate. Failing to test the underlying data undermines the control's reliability.

## Self-Check

- Is the 404 scope anchored top-down to significant accounts, assertions, and risks, with a documented rationale for exclusions?
- Are significant accounts and locations identified by size, susceptibility, and qualitative factors, with coverage meeting the relevant thresholds?
- Do walkthroughs confirm control design and transaction flow before operating-effectiveness testing begins?
- Is design effectiveness evaluated before any control is tested for operating effectiveness?
- Does operating-effectiveness testing use appropriate evidence, sample sizes, timing, and period coverage for each control's risk and frequency?
- Are the IT general controls supporting relied-upon automated and IT-dependent controls tested, with the linkage documented?
- For any SOC 1 reliance, is the report a Type 2 covering the period, mapped to relevant assertions, with complementary user controls tested?
- Is the auditor's opinion based on independent evidence and judgment, distinct from management's assessment?
- Are deficiencies aggregated and evaluated for severity using likelihood, magnitude, and qualitative indicators, with compensating controls tested?
- Is any remediated deficiency supported by tested evidence of operating effectiveness before it is relied upon?
