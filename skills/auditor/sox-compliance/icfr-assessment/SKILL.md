# ICFR Assessment

Assessing internal control over financial reporting is a judgment exercise built on a framework, not a checklist. The COSO framework defines five integrated components — control environment, risk assessment, control activities, information and communication, and monitoring — and effectiveness requires that all five operate together across entity-level and process-level controls. Agents tend to collapse this into a list of controls tested in isolation, which produces an effectiveness conclusion that is not actually supported. The harm is a material weakness that is missed because deficiencies were never aggregated, an IT dependency that was never tested, or a control environment weakness that was treated as narrative rather than as a scope driver.

Use this skill when assessing ICFR for a 404 management assessment or an integrated audit, when applying the COSO framework, when evaluating the relationship between entity-level and process-level controls, and when determining whether deficiencies aggregate into a material weakness. The goal is an effectiveness conclusion grounded in all five COSO components and a severity determination that reflects aggregation and qualitative indicators.

## Core Rules

### Apply COSO As Five Integrated Components

COSO is not five separate categories to document independently; effectiveness requires that the components operate together. A strong set of control activities cannot compensate for a weak control environment or absent risk assessment, and monitoring only provides assurance if the underlying controls it monitors are themselves effective. Assess the components in relation to one another.

Assess each component and its integration:

- control environment: integrity, ethics, board oversight, organizational structure, and human resource policies;
- risk assessment: the entity's process for identifying and analyzing financial reporting risks, including fraud risk;
- control activities: the policies and procedures, at the right precision, that address the identified risks;
- information and communication: the capture and flow of relevant information internally and externally;
- monitoring: ongoing evaluations, separate evaluations, and the evaluation and communication of deficiencies;
- whether weaknesses in one component undermine reliance on controls in another.

### Distinguish Entity-Level From Process-Level Controls By Precision

Entity-level controls operate across the organization (tone at the top, monitoring, IT general controls, the financial close); process-level controls operate within a specific transaction cycle. The decisive question is always precision: does the entity-level control operate precisely enough to prevent or detect a material misstatement in a specific account? Only precise entity-level controls can reduce process-level scope.

Apply the precision test:

- indirect or imprecise controls (ethics policy, tone) support the environment but do not reduce process scope;
- direct but high-level controls (enterprise dashboards) may detect large anomalies but not account-level errors;
- direct and precise controls (detailed review of a reconciled estimate) can substitute for specific process controls;
- map each entity-level control to the specific account, assertion, and risk it mitigates at a stated precision;
- reflect entity-level weaknesses in higher process-level risk and broader testing.

### Test The IT General Control Dependency

Automated and IT-dependent manual controls are only as reliable as the IT general controls (ITGCs) governing the underlying systems. Agents frequently test the application control without testing whether access, change management, and IT operations support its reliability over the period. Test the ITGCs that are relevant to each relied-upon control, and document the linkage.

Test the relevant ITGCs:

- logical access to the applications and data supporting in-scope controls;
- change management for application and infrastructure changes;
- IT operations, including job scheduling, backup, and interfaces;
- the acquisition, implementation, and configuration of the application;
- the completeness and accuracy of system-generated reports and data feeds;
- the link between each ITGC and the specific control it supports.

### Evaluate Fraud-Specific Controls Separately

Fraud risk requires its own control evaluation because fraud is concealed and ordinary error controls often fail to detect it. Assess whether the entity has controls over the fraud risks identified in its risk assessment: management override, journal entry manipulation, revenue recognition manipulation, and significant unusual estimates. Do not assume that controls effective against error are effective against fraud.

Evaluate fraud controls for:

- controls over journal entries and other adjustments, including who can post and who reviews;
- controls over significant estimates and bias, including review and challenge of assumptions;
- controls over revenue recognition, including cutoff and the risk of premature recognition;
- controls over management override, including the tone at the top and oversight;
- the whistleblower hotline and anti-retaliation protections as a fraud detection mechanism;
- the entity's response to identified fraud risks, not merely their documentation.

### Test Design And Operating Effectiveness In Sequence

A control must be designed effectively before its operating effectiveness can be tested. Evaluate design first: does the control, alone or with others, provide reasonable assurance of preventing or detecting material misstatement? Only controls that pass the design test proceed to operating testing, and operating testing must match the control's frequency and risk.

Sequence the testing:

- evaluate design effectiveness and the precision of the control;
- confirm the control is performed by competent personnel with appropriate authority;
- test operating effectiveness with evidence, sample size, and timing matched to risk and frequency;
- cover the full period of reliance, not a convenient window;
- evaluate deviations for their nature, cause, and severity implications.

### Test The Completeness And Accuracy Of Information Used By Controls

A control that reviews a report, a reconciliation, or a dashboard is only as effective as the information it relies on. Agents frequently test that the review occurred but never test whether the underlying report is complete and accurate, which undermines the entire control. Test the completeness and accuracy (C&A) of the information each in-scope control depends on.

Test C&A by confirming:

- the source system and the report or extract the control uses;
- the parameters, logic, and any manual manipulation between source and report;
- reconciliation of report totals to the general ledger or other authoritative source;
- the controls over interface feeds and data transfers between systems;
- the controls over any manual adjustments or reclassifications applied to the report;
- that the C&A testing covers the same period as the control's operating testing.

### Aggregate Deficiencies Before Concluding Severity

Deficiency severity is the most consequential judgment in ICFR assessment, and aggregation is where it most often fails. Individually inconsequential deficiencies that affect the same account, assertion, or process can combine into a material weakness. Evaluate deficiencies individually and then aggregate them before reaching a severity conclusion.

Aggregate and evaluate by considering:

- deficiencies affecting the same account, assertion, component, or process;
- the likelihood a deficiency could allow a misstatement and the magnitude of potential misstatement;
- qualitative indicators of material weakness (restatement, identified fraud, ineffective oversight of external reporting, significant deficiency not remediated);
- whether compensating controls reduce severity, and whether those controls were tested;
- the effect on the auditor's substantive procedures and on the ability to plan the audit.

### Determine Material Weaknesses Using Both Indicators

A material weakness is a deficiency, or combination, such that there is a reasonable possibility that a material misstatement will not be prevented or detected on a timely basis. Both quantitative magnitude and qualitative indicators drive the determination, and certain indicators are presumptive. Agents must apply the indicators rather than relying on a dollar threshold alone.

Recognize material weakness indicators:

- restatement of prior financial statements to correct a material misstatement;
- identification of fraud by management or employees that is material;
- ineffective oversight of financial reporting and ICFR by the audit committee;
- identification of a significant deficiency not remediated within a reasonable period;
- a reasonable possibility that a material misstatement would not be prevented or detected;
- the need for the auditor to lower control risk below the maximum as impractical.

### Test Remediation Before Relying On It

A deficiency reported as remediated must be supported by tested evidence that the new or modified control is designed and operating effectively, not merely by management's assertion that it was fixed. Remediation occurring late in the period may not support an effective conclusion for the full period. Test remediation to the same standard as any other control.

Test remediation by confirming:

- the new or modified control is designed to address the root cause, not the symptom;
- the control operated for a sufficient period to support effectiveness;
- operating effectiveness is tested with appropriate evidence and sample size;
- any related ITGCs supporting the remediated control are also tested;
- the prior-period deficiency is clearly described and the basis for concluding it is remediated is documented.

## Common Traps

### Documenting COSO Components In Isolation

Treating the five components as separate documentation sections, rather than assessing their integration, produces a conclusion that ignores how a weak component undermines the others.

### Relying On Imprecise Entity-Level Controls

Counting tone at the top or an ethics policy as reducing process scope, without the precision test, over-relies on entity-level controls and under-tests the process.

### Testing Application Controls Without ITGCs

An automated control is only reliable if the underlying ITGCs support it; testing the control once without the ITGCs provides no period assurance.

### Assuming Error Controls Stop Fraud

Controls effective against ordinary error often fail against concealed fraud. Failing to evaluate fraud-specific controls separately leaves the fraud risk unaddressed.

### Skipping Design Before Operating Testing

Testing operating effectiveness of a poorly designed control wastes effort and produces a conclusion that is not supported, because the control cannot be effective if its design is flawed.

### Evaluating Deficiencies In Isolation

Deficiencies affecting the same area can aggregate into a material weakness. Evaluating each in isolation understates severity and misstates the effectiveness conclusion.

### Relying On A Dollar Threshold Alone

Material weakness determination is qualitative as well as quantitative. Restatements, fraud, and ineffective oversight are presumptive indicators regardless of dollar size.

### Accepting Remediation Without Testing

A remediated deficiency must be supported by tested evidence of design and operating effectiveness. Accepting management's assertion that it is fixed is not sufficient.

### Ignoring Period Coverage In Operating Testing

Testing a control only at year-end, or in a narrow window, does not support effectiveness throughout the period of reliance. Period coverage must match the period over which the conclusion is drawn.

## Self-Check

- Are the five COSO components assessed as integrated, with weaknesses in one component reflected in reliance on others?
- Is each entity-level control subjected to the precision test before it is allowed to reduce process-level scope?
- Are the IT general controls supporting relied-upon automated and IT-dependent controls tested, with the linkage documented?
- Are fraud-specific controls (journal entries, estimates, revenue, override, hotline) evaluated separately from error controls?
- Is design effectiveness evaluated before operating effectiveness, with operating testing matched to risk and frequency?
- Are deficiencies aggregated across accounts, assertions, and processes before severity is concluded?
- Is material weakness determination based on both quantitative magnitude and qualitative indicators, including presumptive ones?
- Are compensating controls tested before they are used to reduce deficiency severity?
- Is any reported remediation supported by tested evidence of design and operating effectiveness over a sufficient period?
- Does the overall effectiveness conclusion rest on all five components operating together, not on a checklist of controls tested?
