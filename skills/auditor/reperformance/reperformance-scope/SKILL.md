# Reperformance Scope

Reperformance is persuasive evidence, but only for the items, the period, and the conditions actually tested. The scope of reperformance, what is reperformed, how many items, at what points in time, and under what conditions, determines what conclusion the evidence can support. An auditor who reperforms a control once at year-end and concludes it operated effectively all year has overstated the evidence. An auditor who reperforms every item when a sample would suffice has wasted effort without strengthening the conclusion in proportion. Scoping reperformance is the judgment of matching the extent of the procedure to the risk it addresses, the type of control, and the conclusion needed, so that the evidence is sufficient without being wasteful and the conclusion drawn is no broader than the work supports.

Use this skill when deciding what to reperform, how extensively, across what period and population, when scoping reperformance for different control types, or when evaluating whether the extent of reperformance supports the intended conclusion. The goal is to define a reperformance scope that is risk-appropriate, type-appropriate, and sufficient to support the conclusion without overreach.

## Core Rules

### Match The Extent Of Reperformance To Risk

Not all controls or amounts warrant the same depth of reperformance. The extent should be driven by the risk of the area, the significance of the control, and the consequences if it fails. A blanket approach, the same sample size for every control, misallocates effort.

Increase the extent of reperformance when:

- the control addresses a significant risk, such as revenue recognition or management override;
- the area has a history of errors or prior findings;
- the control is the primary preventive or detective control for a material process;
- management has incentive to misstate, or the area involves significant judgment.

A lower extent may be acceptable for lower-risk controls, but the rationale for the reduced extent should be documented, not assumed.

### Distinguish The Scope For Manual And Automated Controls

The scoping logic differs fundamentally between manual and automated controls, because automated controls, once configured, operate consistently across the population, while manual controls depend on the person performing them each time.

For manual controls:

- reperformance should cover multiple points in time throughout the period, because operation by one person at one time does not prove operation at other times;
- sample across different operators where multiple people perform the control;
- include periods of change, such as staff turnover or system transitions, when failure is more likely.

For automated controls:

- a single reperformance, combined with testing of the system configuration and the IT general controls that govern changes, can support a conclusion across the period;
- test that the configuration was in place throughout the period, including any changes and their authorization;
- do not assume the automated control operated correctly if IT general controls are weak, because unauthorized changes could have altered it.

Applying manual-control scoping to an automated control wastes effort, while applying automated-control scoping to a manual control overstates the evidence.

### Cover The Relevant Period, Not Only A Convenient Point

A common scoping error is to reperform at a single point, often year-end, and treat it as representative of the period. For manual controls, this is rarely sufficient, because operation varies over time.

Cover the period by:

- selecting items from across the period, including interim and year-end;
- including periods of higher risk, such as quarter-ends, peak volumes, or post-change;
- avoiding concentration in a single month or a single operator's work;
- considering whether the control was in place for the entire period or implemented partway through.

If the control was changed during the period, scope reperformance to cover both the pre-change and post-change operation, and confirm the change was authorized and effective.

### Define The Population And Ensure Complete Coverage

Reperformance scope depends on a correctly defined population. If the population is incomplete or misdefined, the sample is drawn from the wrong set and the conclusion does not apply to what matters.

Define the population by:

- extracting the complete set of items the control should have operated on;
- reconciling the population total to the ledger or system to confirm completeness;
- removing items appropriately, such as voided or reversed items, and documenting why;
- stratifying where the population has distinct layers, such as high-value and low-value items.

A population that omits a segment, such as a location, a product line, or a system, produces a conclusion that does not cover that segment. State explicitly what the population includes and excludes.

### Choose A Sampling Approach Appropriate To The Objective

The sampling method for reperformance should match what the auditor needs to conclude. Different objectives call for different approaches.

Approaches include:

- statistical sampling, when a quantified conclusion about deviation rate is needed;
- judgmental, targeted sampling, when the focus is on high-risk or unusual items;
- 100 percent reperformance, for very high-risk items or small key populations;
- stratified sampling, when the population has layers with different risk profiles.

Document the approach, the sample size, the selection method, and the basis for each. A sample size chosen without reference to risk, tolerable rate, or expected deviation is not defensible.

### Ensure The Scope Supports Only The Conclusion Drawn

The conclusion from reperformance must match the scope performed. Overstating the conclusion beyond the scope is a fundamental error that undermines the audit's reliability.

Align conclusion to scope by:

- stating the conclusion for the population and period actually tested;
- not extending a conclusion about a sample to a guarantee about the whole population without addressing sampling risk;
- not extending a conclusion about one location or system to others not tested;
- acknowledging limitations, such as a single-point reperformance, in the conclusion.

If the scope does not support the conclusion needed, the answer is to expand the scope or modify the conclusion, not to stretch the wording.

### Document The Scope Decisions And Their Basis

Scoping decisions are judgments that a reviewer must be able to follow and challenge. The workpaper should show not only what was reperformed but why that extent was chosen.

Document:

- the control or amount and its risk significance;
- the population definition, reconciliation, and any stratification;
- the sampling approach, size, and selection method, with the rationale;
- the period coverage and the points in time selected;
- the basis for any reduced or expanded extent;
- how the scope supports the conclusion reached.

A scope that appears in the file without its rationale cannot be evaluated for appropriateness, and a conclusion that appears without its scope cannot be evaluated for overreach.

## Common Traps

### Reperforming Once At Year-End And Concluding For The Period

For manual controls, a single-point reperformance does not prove operation throughout the period. Cover multiple points in time.

### Applying Manual-Control Scoping To Automated Controls

Sampling an automated control across the period, when a single reperformance plus IT general controls testing would suffice, wastes effort without adding evidence.

### Applying Automated-Control Scoping To Manual Controls

Treating a single reperformance of a manual control as sufficient overstates the evidence, because manual operation varies.

### Drawing The Sample From An Incomplete Population

An omitted location, system, or item type means the conclusion does not cover it. Reconcile the population and state what is included.

### Uniform Sample Sizes Without Risk Justification

The same extent for every control misallocates effort. Tie the extent to risk and document the rationale.

### Overstating The Conclusion Beyond The Scope

A conclusion that extends beyond the population, period, or sample tested is not supported. Align the conclusion to the scope or expand the scope.

### Ignoring Periods Of Change

Staff turnover, system changes, and process transitions are when controls fail. Include these periods in the scope.

## Self-Check

- Is the extent of reperformance matched to the risk of the area, the significance of the control, and the consequence of failure, with the rationale documented?
- Is the scoping logic appropriate to the control type, with multiple points in time for manual controls and configuration plus IT general controls for automated controls?
- Does the scope cover the relevant period, including higher-risk periods and any changes, rather than concentrating at a single convenient point?
- Is the population completely defined and reconciled, with inclusions, exclusions, and stratification documented?
- Is the sampling approach, size, and selection method appropriate to the objective, with the basis for each documented?
- Is the conclusion limited to what the scope supports, without extending to populations, periods, or locations not tested?
- Are scope decisions and their rationale documented so a reviewer can follow and challenge them?
- Where the control changed during the period, does the scope cover both before and after the change, and is the change's authorization confirmed?
