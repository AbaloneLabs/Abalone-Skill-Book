# Independent Recalculation

Recalculation is one of the most persuasive forms of audit evidence, because a correctly recomputed amount is hard to dispute. But its persuasiveness hides a subtle failure mode: the auditor who simply re-runs management's formula on management's data has confirmed only that the arithmetic is consistent, not that the inputs, assumptions, or method were correct. True independent recalculation means rebuilding the amount from its own basis, using the auditor's own selection of inputs and, where relevant, the auditor's own assumptions, then comparing the result to what was recorded. The difference between mechanical re-running and genuine independent recalculation is the difference between confirming a calculation and testing whether it should have been made that way at all. Auditors who confuse the two lend the appearance of strong evidence to conclusions that rest entirely on management's choices.

Use this skill when recalculating accruals, depreciation, provisions, allocations, interest, or any computed balance, when building an independent expectation to compare against recorded amounts, or when evaluating differences between a recalculation and the recorded figure. The goal is to produce recalculation evidence that tests the substance of the amount, not merely the consistency of the arithmetic.

## Core Rules

### Rebuild The Amount From Its Own Basis, Not From Management's Output

The strongest recalculation reconstructs the amount from the underlying data and rules, rather than re-entering management's figures into management's formula. This tests whether the inputs and method are appropriate, not just whether the math is consistent.

Rebuild from the basis by:

- identifying the source data that should drive the amount, such as invoices, rates, headcount, or asset registers;
- selecting or testing that source data independently, rather than accepting management's summarized inputs;
- applying the relevant rule, rate, or formula, including any thresholds or caps;
- confirming the period, population, and cutoff the calculation should cover;
- comparing the independently built result to the recorded amount.

If the recalculation uses only the figures management already summarized, it confirms consistency but not correctness. The independent selection of inputs is what gives the procedure its evidentiary force.

### Test The Inputs, Not Only The Arithmetic

A recalculation is only as good as the data and assumptions that feed it. The most common reason a correctly calculated amount is still wrong is that an input was incorrect, stale, or inappropriately chosen.

Test the inputs by:

- tracing rates, prices, and parameters to authoritative sources or agreements;
- confirming the source data, such as the fixed asset register or payroll master, is complete and current;
- testing assumptions, such as useful lives, bad debt rates, or completion percentages, against evidence and history;
- checking that the population used in the calculation matches the population that should be included;
- verifying cutoff, ensuring the calculation covers the right period and excludes items outside it.

Document which inputs were tested and how, because an untested input is an assumption, not evidence.

### Apply The Correct Method, And Question Management's Method If It Differs

Before recalculating, confirm the method the framework or agreement requires. If management used a different method, the recalculation should apply the correct method, not reproduce management's error.

Method considerations:

- does the applicable standard or contract specify the method, rate, or basis?
- is management's method consistent with prior periods, and if changed, is the change justified?
- are estimates based on a defensible approach, or on a convenient assumption?
- are allocations applied on a reasonable and consistent basis?

If management's method is wrong, reproducing it confirms the error. Recalculate using the correct method, quantify the difference, and evaluate whether it is a misstatement.

### Build An Independent Expectation For Judgmental Amounts

For estimates and judgmental amounts, recalculation often takes the form of building an independent expectation or range, then comparing it to management's point estimate. This is more informative than re-running management's calculation, because it tests whether the recorded figure falls within a reasonable range.

Build an independent expectation by:

- using historical data, industry benchmarks, or external evidence to develop a range;
- identifying the key assumptions and testing their reasonableness;
- considering the sensitivity of the result to changes in assumptions;
- assessing whether management's point estimate sits at an aggressive end of the range;
- documenting the expectation, the range, and the comparison to the recorded amount.

If management's estimate falls outside the auditor's reasonable range, that is evidence of potential bias or error, even if the calculation itself is internally consistent.

### Handle Differences Between Recalculation And Recorded Amount

When the independent recalculation differs from the recorded amount, the difference must be evaluated, not ignored or silently adjusted away. The evaluation determines whether the difference is a misstatement, a timing difference, or a difference in method.

Evaluate differences by:

- determining whether the difference arises from an input error, a method error, or a judgment;
- assessing whether the difference is isolated or likely to pervade the population;
- considering whether the difference indicates bias in management's estimates;
- quantifying the difference and comparing it to tolerable misstatement;
- proposing correction of any identified misstatement and evaluating the effect of uncorrected differences.

A difference is not automatically a misstatement, but it always requires an explanation. Documenting the cause and the conclusion is what makes the recalculation defensible.

### Document The Recalculation So It Can Be Reproduced

A recalculation that cannot be reproduced by a reviewer is weak evidence, because the reviewer cannot confirm the inputs, method, or result. The workpaper should allow another auditor to rebuild the same figure and reach the same result.

Document:

- the amount tested and its source;
- the source data used, with references to where it was obtained;
- the method, rates, and assumptions applied, and their basis;
- the calculation itself, in enough detail to follow;
- the result and the comparison to the recorded amount;
- the evaluation of any difference and the conclusion.

A spreadsheet or calculation tool should be retained or referenced, with inputs traceable to source, so the recalculation is transparent rather than a black box.

## Common Traps

### Re-Running Management's Formula On Management's Data

This confirms consistency, not correctness. Independent recalculation requires independent selection or testing of inputs.

### Confirming The Arithmetic But Not The Inputs

A correctly calculated amount built on a wrong rate or stale data is still wrong. Test the inputs as rigorously as the math.

### Reproducing Management's Wrong Method

If management's method is incorrect, reproducing it confirms the error. Apply the correct method and quantify the difference.

### Ignoring Differences Below A Threshold

A small difference may indicate a systemic input error or a bias in estimates. Investigate the cause before dismissing the amount.

### Treating The Recalculation As Proof Of The Whole Balance

Recalculation of a sample or a single amount does not prove the entire population. Relate the procedure to the scope tested and the sampling approach.

### Failing To Document Inputs And Method

A result without the supporting inputs and method cannot be reproduced or reviewed. The calculation must be transparent.

### Accepting Management's Estimate Without An Independent Range

For judgmental amounts, re-running the calculation is weak. Build an independent expectation or range to test whether the estimate is reasonable.

## Self-Check

- Does the recalculation rebuild the amount from its underlying basis and source data, rather than re-running management's summarized inputs?
- Have the inputs, rates, parameters, and assumptions been tested against authoritative sources and confirmed complete and current?
- Is the method applied the correct one under the framework or agreement, and has management's method been questioned where it differs?
- For judgmental amounts, has an independent expectation or range been built and compared to management's point estimate?
- Have differences between the recalculation and recorded amount been evaluated for cause, isolated versus systemic nature, and potential bias?
- Is the difference quantified, compared to tolerable misstatement, and proposed for correction where it is a misstatement?
- Is the recalculation documented with source data, method, assumptions, calculation, result, and conclusion so it can be reproduced?
- Is the scope of the recalculation clear, so the conclusion is not overstated to cover a population or period not tested?
