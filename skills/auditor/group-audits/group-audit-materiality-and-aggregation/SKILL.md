# Group Audit Materiality and Aggregation

Materiality in a group audit is a single concept applied across multiple components, and its misapplication is a frequent source of audit failure. The group engagement team sets group materiality, but component materiality must be lower to provide a cushion for aggregation of uncorrected misstatements. If component materiality equals group materiality, there is no protection against errors accumulating across components until they become material to the group. Aggregation risk is the defining technical challenge of group audits: many small, individually immaterial misstatements can sum to a material amount.

This skill covers the materiality judgments specific to group audits: setting group materiality, allocating component materiality, defining clearly trivial thresholds, and evaluating aggregated uncorrected misstatements against group materiality.

This guidance addresses audit materiality judgment. It does not replace ISA 320, ISA 600 (Revised), or firm methodology. Materiality determinations must be documented and subject to engagement partner approval.

## Core Rules

### Set group materiality first, based on group financial information

Group materiality is determined by the group engagement team based on an appropriate benchmark applied to group financial information (e.g., a percentage of group profit before tax, revenue, or net assets, depending on the group's circumstances). The benchmark choice and percentage must be justified, not inherited. Document why the chosen benchmark is appropriate for this group and why the percentage is suitable given user expectations and the group's stability.

### Set component materiality below group materiality

When a component auditor performs an audit, component materiality must be lower than group materiality. The differential provides a cushion so that uncorrected misstatements identified at components can aggregate without exceeding group materiality. The size of the differential depends on the number of significant components, the expected level of misstatements, and the group's risk profile. Document the percentage applied and the rationale; a common range is 50–80% of group materiality for significant components, but the figure must be justified, not defaulted.

### Define a clearly trivial threshold for each level

Below component materiality, set a clearly trivial threshold (often 1–5% of component materiality) below which misstatements need not be accumulated. This threshold must be documented at both component and group levels. Misstatements above clearly trivial but below materiality must be accumulated and evaluated for aggregation.

### Evaluate aggregation across all components, not only significant ones

The group engagement team must aggregate uncorrected misstatements from all components—significant and non-significant—and from group-level procedures, and compare the total to group materiality. Even when no single component's misstatements are material, the aggregate may be. The aggregation must include group-level misstatements from consolidation, intercompany eliminations, and group-level estimates that no component audit captures.

### Reassess materiality if conditions change

If the group's financial results differ materially from those used to set materiality (e.g., a significant decline in profit discovered during the audit), reassess materiality and the effect on component materiality and on accumulated misstatements. Document the trigger and the revised determination.

### Communicate materiality to component auditors clearly

The group engagement team must communicate component materiality and the clearly trivial threshold to component auditors and require them to accumulate and report misstatements above the clearly trivial threshold. Ambiguity in communication leads to component auditors applying their own materiality, undermining aggregation.

## Common Traps

### Setting component materiality equal to group materiality

This is the most common and most dangerous error. It leaves no aggregation cushion and can render the group opinion unsupported if uncorrected errors accumulate. Component materiality must be lower, with documented rationale.

### Aggregating only significant component misstatements

Non-significant components and group-level procedures also generate misstatements. Aggregating only significant component errors misses the accumulation that often drives the group conclusion.

### Forgetting group-level misstatements in aggregation

Consolidation adjustments, intercompany eliminations, and group-level estimates (e.g., goodwill impairment, group-level provisions) generate misstatements that no component audit captures. These must be included in the aggregation against group materiality.

### Defaulting materiality percentages without justification

Benchmarks and percentages must be justified for the specific group. Defaulting to a prior-year figure or a generic percentage without considering the group's current circumstances, user expectations, and volatility is a documentation and quality failure.

### Failing to communicate thresholds to component auditors

If component auditors are not told the component materiality and clearly trivial threshold, they may apply their own, leading to misstatements being treated as trivial at component level that aggregate to material at group level.

## Self-Check

- Is group materiality set by the group engagement team based on an appropriate benchmark applied to group financial information, with documented justification?
- Is component materiality set below group materiality, with the percentage and rationale documented?
- Is a clearly trivial threshold defined at both component and group levels?
- Does the aggregation of uncorrected misstatements include all components (significant and non-significant) and group-level procedures?
- Have I included group-level misstatements from consolidation, intercompany, and group-level estimates in the aggregation?
- Have I communicated component materiality and the clearly trivial threshold clearly to component auditors?
- Have I reassessed materiality if the group's results changed materially during the audit?
- Has the engagement partner approved the materiality determination and the aggregation conclusion?
