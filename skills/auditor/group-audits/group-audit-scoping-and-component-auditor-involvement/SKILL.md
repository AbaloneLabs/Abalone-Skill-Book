# Group Audit Scoping and Component Auditor Involvement

A group audit is not a collection of standalone component audits stitched together. It is a single engagement in which the group engagement team takes responsibility for the opinion on the group financial statements and must design procedures that respond to the risks of material misstatement at the group level. The revised ISA 600 fundamentally changed the framework: the group engagement team must understand the group, identify significant components, and determine the appropriate involvement with each component, rather than defaulting to reliance on component auditor work.

This skill covers the scoping judgments that determine the structure of the entire engagement: which components are significant, what type of involvement is required for each, how materiality flows from the group to components, and how the group engagement team directs, supervises, and reviews component auditor work. Getting scoping wrong propagates errors through the entire audit: under-scoping misses material risks, while over-scoping wastes effort and creates coordination failures.

This guidance addresses audit planning judgment. It does not replace the applicable auditing standards, firm methodology, or professional judgment required for a specific engagement. All scoping decisions must be documented and subject to the engagement partner's review.

## Core Rules

### Start from the group, not from the components

The group engagement team must first obtain an understanding of the group, its structure, its business activities, and the components that comprise it. Only then can significance be assessed. The trap is to inherit a prior-year component list and treat it as fixed. Reassess the component structure each year: acquisitions, divestitures, reorganizations, and changes in business mix alter which components are significant.

### Apply the significance threshold rigorously

Under ISA 600 (Revised), a component is significant when, individually or in aggregate with other components, it is of individual financial significance to the group or due to its specific nature or circumstances, including specific risks of material misstatement. Apply both tests: a component may not be individually financially significant but may carry significant risk (e.g., a component holding complex financial instruments, a related-party-laden entity, or one undergoing a significant transaction). Document the significance determination for every component, including the rationale for components deemed not significant.

### Determine the type of involvement per component, not a blanket approach

For significant components, the group engagement team must perform, or the component auditor must perform on its behalf, an audit of the component's financial information. For components that are not significant but that are relevant to the group, the group team performs specified audit procedures at the group level. For non-significant components, analytical procedures at group level may suffice. Match the involvement to the risk, and document why the chosen involvement responds to the assessed risks.

### Allocate materiality deliberately and document it

Group materiality is set by the group engagement team. Component materiality, where component auditors perform audits, is set by the group team (or by the component auditor with group team approval) and must be lower than group materiality. The threshold below group materiality must be documented, including the rationale for the percentage applied. A common error is allowing component materiality to equal group materiality, which leaves no coverage for aggregation of uncorrected errors across components.

### Evaluate component auditor competence and independence before relying

When using a component auditor, the group engagement team must evaluate whether the component auditor is independent, has competence for the specific component's risks, operates in an applicable framework, and has adequate regulatory oversight. Document the evaluation. Where the component auditor's framework differs, the group team must determine whether supplementary procedures are needed to bridge the gap.

### Establish direction, supervision, and review of component auditor work

The group engagement team must be involved in the component auditor's risk assessment, be involved in determining the scope of the component work, and review the component auditor's documentation. Reliance is not passive. Document the nature and extent of involvement and the conclusions drawn from review of component work.

## Common Traps

### Inheriting prior-year scoping without reassessment

Components change. A component that was not significant last year may become significant through growth, a new business line, or a significant transaction. Reassess significance each year rather than rolling forward the prior-year component map.

### Using a single significance test

Significance is not only financial size. A small component can be significant due to specific risk: complex estimates, related-party transactions, fraud risk, or non-routine transactions. Applying only a revenue or asset threshold misses risk-driven significant components.

### Setting component materiality equal to group materiality

This leaves no aggregation cushion and can cause the group opinion to be unsupported if uncorrected errors accumulate across components. Component materiality must be lower, and the rationale documented.

### Treating component auditor reliance as a black box

The group team cannot simply accept a component auditor's report. It must understand the component auditor's procedures, evaluate findings, and determine their effect on the group audit. Passive reliance is a standard violation and an audit risk.

### Forgetting aggregation risk across non-significant components

Even when no single non-significant component is material, the aggregate of uncorrected misstatements across many components can be material to the group. The group team must perform procedures to address aggregation risk, typically analytical procedures at group level covering the remaining components.

## Self-Check

- Have I obtained an understanding of the group structure and reassessed component significance for the current year, rather than rolling forward prior-year scoping?
- Have I applied both the individual financial significance test and the specific nature/circumstances/risk test to every component?
- Is the type of involvement for each component documented and matched to the assessed risk?
- Is component materiality set below group materiality, with the percentage and rationale documented?
- Have I evaluated the component auditor's independence, competence, framework, and oversight, and documented the evaluation?
- Have I documented the direction, supervision, and review of component auditor work, including involvement in risk assessment and scope?
- Have I addressed aggregation risk across non-significant components through group-level analytical procedures?
- Has the engagement partner reviewed and approved the group audit strategy and component scoping?
