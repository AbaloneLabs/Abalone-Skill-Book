# Significant Components and Risk Assessment

The identification of significant components is the load-bearing decision in a group audit. It determines where audit effort is concentrated, which components receive full audits, and which risks the group engagement team must address with its own procedures. Under-identification leaves material risks unaddressed; over-identification spreads effort so thin that no component receives adequate attention. The judgment is not mechanical: a component's significance flows from both its financial weight and the specific risks it carries.

This skill covers the risk assessment judgments that drive component significance: how to identify significant components, how to assess component-level risks including fraud and significant risks, and how to determine whether a risk requires a group-level response, a component-level response, or both.

This guidance addresses audit risk assessment judgment. It does not replace ISA 315, ISA 600 (Revised), or firm methodology. All risk assessments must be documented, linked to planned procedures, and subject to engagement partner review.

## Core Rules

### Assess significance on both financial weight and specific risk

A component is significant if it is of individual financial significance to the group, or if its specific nature or circumstances give rise to significant risks of material misstatement to the group. Apply both lenses. Financial significance is typically assessed against group revenue, profit, assets, or cash flows—use the measure most relevant to the group's performance. Specific risk significance arises from complex transactions, estimates, related parties, fraud risk, regulatory exposure, or non-routine events.

### Identify significant risks at the component level and escalate

The group engagement team must identify significant risks—risks requiring special audit consideration—at the group level and at significant component levels. A risk that is significant at a component may be significant at the group even if the component is not individually financially significant. Examples include revenue recognition in a component under growth pressure, management override in a component with weak controls, or fair value estimates in a component holding complex assets.

### Treat fraud risk as a group-level concern

Fraud risk does not respect component boundaries. The group engagement team must identify and assess fraud risks across the group, including the risk of management override of controls, and design group-level responses. A component where fraud indicators are present may be significant even if financially small. Do not allow component-level fraud risk assessment to substitute for group-level fraud risk assessment; both are required.

### Map related-party and group-level transactions to components

Intercomponent transactions, related-party transactions, and group-level transactions (transfers of assets, intercompany financing, management charges) are a frequent source of fraud and error. Map these transactions to the components involved and assess whether they create significant risk. Consolidation adjustments, elimination entries, and the completeness of intercompany balances are group-level risks that no single component audit will catch.

### Reassess significance when conditions change during the audit

Significance is not fixed at planning. If, during the audit, a component experiences a significant transaction, a deterioration in conditions, or a fraud indicator, reassess its significance and adjust the involvement. Document the trigger and the revised determination.

### Link each significant risk to a specific response

Every significant risk must be linked to a planned response that is specific, not generic. A risk assessment that lists risks without linking each to a tailored procedure is incomplete. For significant risks, the response typically includes the nature, timing, and extent of procedures that are more persuasive than for non-significant risks.

## Common Traps

### Using revenue or assets alone to determine significance

A single financial metric misses risk-driven significance. A component with modest revenue but complex fair value estimates, heavy related-party activity, or regulatory exposure may be significant. Apply the specific nature and circumstances test deliberately, not as a formality.

### Treating fraud risk as a component-only matter

Fraud risk, especially management override, must be assessed at the group level. Component auditors may identify component fraud risks, but the group team retains responsibility for group-level fraud risk assessment and responses.

### Ignoring consolidation and intercompany risk

The group financial statements are not the sum of component statements; consolidation adjustments and intercompany eliminations are a distinct risk area. Under-assessing consolidation completeness risk leaves a gap that no component audit covers.

### Failing to escalate component significant risks to the group

A risk significant at a component may warrant group-level attention. The trap is treating component significant risks as fully addressed by the component audit, without considering whether the group team needs additional procedures or involvement.

### Leaving risk assessments unlinked to procedures

A risk register that does not connect each significant risk to a specific, tailored response fails the basic requirement that the audit respond to assessed risks. Generic responses ("perform additional procedures") are not adequate for significant risks.

## Self-Check

- Have I assessed each component for both individual financial significance and specific nature/circumstances/risk significance?
- Have I identified significant risks at both the group and component levels, and documented why each is significant?
- Have I assessed fraud risk, including management override, at the group level, not only at component level?
- Have I mapped intercomponent, related-party, and consolidation transactions to components and assessed the resulting risks?
- Have I reassessed component significance when conditions changed during the audit, and documented the trigger?
- Is every significant risk linked to a specific, tailored audit response, not a generic procedure?
- Have I considered whether component significant risks require additional group-level procedures?
- Has the engagement partner reviewed the risk assessment and the linkage between risks and responses?
