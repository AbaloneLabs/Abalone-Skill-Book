# Known Versus Projected Errors

In substantive sampling, the auditor encounters two distinct kinds of misstatement that must never be confused. A known misstatement is an error actually identified in a specific item tested; it exists, it is documented, and its amount is real. A projected error is an estimate of the misstatement likely present in the untested population, extrapolated from the sample using a defined method. Confusing the two, or treating the projection as if it were a measured fact, produces conclusions that are either understated or overstated. Auditors sometimes add up only the known errors and declare the population fine, ignoring what the sample implies about the rest; or they treat the projection as a precise figure and demand an adjustment for an amount that was never actually observed. The discipline lies in keeping the two concepts separate, handling each correctly, and combining them in the right way to evaluate whether the population is fairly stated.

Use this skill when evaluating sample results in substantive testing, projecting misstatements to a population, comparing known and projected errors to tolerable misstatement, deciding on adjustments and further testing, or documenting the basis for a conclusion on a sampled population. The goal is to draw a defensible conclusion that accounts for both the errors actually found and the errors the sample implies exist.

## Core Rules

### Keep Known And Projected Errors Conceptually Separate

The distinction must be maintained throughout evaluation and documentation. Known errors are facts about tested items; projected errors are estimates about untested items. Mixing them obscures the basis for the conclusion.

Define clearly:

- known misstatement: the sum of actual errors identified in specific sampled items;
- projected misstatement: the estimate of error in the untested population, derived by a defined projection method;
- the projection method used, such as ratio projection, difference projection, or mean-per-unit, and why it is appropriate;
- anomalous errors: errors so unusual they are excluded from projection and evaluated separately.

State both figures separately in the workpaper, then combine them for evaluation against tolerable misstatement. Never present the projection alone as if it were the measured total, and never evaluate only the known errors as if the sample implied nothing about the rest.

### Project Using A Method Appropriate To The Population

The projection method depends on how the population and the errors behave. Applying the wrong method produces a projection that does not reflect the population's characteristics.

Common methods:

- difference projection: average error per sampled item, multiplied by population size, suitable when errors do not vary with item size;
- ratio projection: ratio of total error to total book value in the sample, applied to population book value, suitable when errors tend to scale with item size;
- mean-per-unit: average sampled value multiplied by population size, used in certain statistical approaches;
- stratified projection: projecting within strata and combining, used when the population has distinct layers.

Document the method chosen and the reasoning. If the sample contains zero errors, the projection may be zero, but that does not eliminate sampling risk; evaluate the upper limit on misstatement, not only the point projection.

### Evaluate Known And Projected Errors Together Against Tolerable Misstatement

The evaluation combines the known errors, the projected error, and where applicable an allowance for sampling risk, and compares the total to tolerable misstatement. The conclusion depends on where the combination falls.

Evaluate:

- if known plus projected misstatement is well below tolerable misstatement, with allowance for sampling risk, the population is accepted as not materially misstated;
- if the total approaches or exceeds tolerable misstatement, the population may be materially misstated and further action is required;
- if the upper limit on misstatement exceeds tolerable misstatement, sampling risk alone may prevent a clean conclusion.

Do not conclude the population is fine merely because known errors are small. The projection and sampling risk must be considered. Conversely, do not demand an adjustment for the full projected amount if the projection's upper limit, not its point estimate, is what drives the concern.

### Decide On Adjustments, Further Testing, Or Qualification Based On The Combination

When the combination of known and projected errors is significant, the auditor has a sequence of responses, and the choice depends on the magnitude and the cause.

Response options:

- request management to correct the known misstatements, which removes them from the evaluation;
- request management to examine the population and correct identified errors, which can reduce both known and projected error;
- expand sampling to reduce sampling risk and refine the projection, when the conclusion is borderline;
- adjust the audit approach, such as testing 100 percent of high-value items;
- consider a qualified or adverse conclusion if material misstatement remains uncorrected and unaddressed.

The decision should be explicit. Known errors should generally be proposed for correction. Projected errors are not proposed for correction dollar-for-dollar, because they are estimates, but they drive the conclusion about whether the population is materially misstated.

### Handle Anomalous Errors Separately

An anomalous error is one that is so unusual, such as an isolated processing failure on a single large item, that projecting it to the population would be misleading. Anomalous errors are excluded from the projection and evaluated individually.

Treat an error as anomalous only when:

- it has a specific, identifiable cause that does not affect the rest of the population;
- it is genuinely isolated, not part of a pattern;
- the auditor has investigated and confirmed it does not recur.

Exclude the anomalous error from projection, but still include its known amount in the total misstatement evaluation. Document why the error was treated as anomalous, because excluding errors from projection without justification understates the implied population error.

### Consider Qualitative Characteristics Alongside The Quantitative Evaluation

Even when known and projected errors are below tolerable misstatement, qualitative factors can change the conclusion. A projected error driven by fraud, override, or a systematic cause is more serious than its amount suggests.

Consider:

- whether the errors are random or share a systematic cause;
- whether the errors indicate fraud, override, or intentional misstatement;
- whether the errors recur across periods or locations;
- whether the errors affect a high-risk area such as revenue or estimates.

A small projected error with a fraudulent or systematic cause may require expanded procedures, governance communication, or a different conclusion than the dollar amount alone would suggest.

### Document The Projection, The Evaluation, And The Conclusion

The workpaper must allow a reviewer to follow the path from sample results to conclusion. Conclusions that appear without the projection arithmetic cannot be verified.

Document:

- the population, sampling method, sample size, and selection approach;
- each known error, its amount, and its cause;
- the projection method, the calculation, and the projected amount;
- any anomalous errors and the basis for excluding them from projection;
- the allowance for sampling risk or the upper limit on misstatement;
- the comparison of the combined total to tolerable misstatement;
- the conclusion and the action taken, such as adjustment, expansion, or acceptance.

## Common Traps

### Evaluating Only Known Errors And Ignoring The Projection

Summing the errors actually found and ignoring what the sample implies about the untested population understates misstatement and produces unjustified clean conclusions.

### Treating The Projection As A Precise Measured Amount

The projection is an estimate. Demanding an adjustment for the exact projected figure, or treating it as a known fact, overstates certainty and confuses estimate with measurement.

### Using An Inappropriate Projection Method

Applying difference projection when errors scale with item size, or vice versa, produces a projection that does not reflect the population. Match the method to the error behavior.

### Excluding Errors From Projection Without Justification

Calling errors anomalous to avoid a larger projection understates the implied population error. Reserve anomalous treatment for genuinely isolated errors with confirmed non-recurring causes.

### Ignoring Sampling Risk When The Sample Has Zero Errors

A zero-error sample does not guarantee a zero-error population. Evaluate the upper limit on misstatement, which reflects sampling risk, not only the point projection of zero.

### Failing To Propose Correction Of Known Errors

Known errors are real misstatements in real items. They should generally be proposed for adjustment, not absorbed into a projection that is then compared to tolerable misstatement.

### Letting A Small Dollar Amount Suppress Qualitative Concerns

A projected error below tolerable misstatement may still be significant if driven by fraud, override, or a systematic cause. Do not let the arithmetic override the qualitative evaluation.

## Self-Check

- Are known misstatements and projected misstatements defined, calculated, and documented separately before being combined for evaluation?
- Is the projection method appropriate to the population and the error behavior, with the reasoning documented?
- Is the combined total of known errors, projected error, and sampling risk allowance compared to tolerable misstatement to reach the conclusion?
- Has the response, adjustment, expansion, or qualification, been chosen explicitly based on the magnitude and cause of the combination?
- Are anomalous errors identified, excluded from projection only with justification, and still included as known amounts in the total evaluation?
- Has sampling risk, including the upper limit on misstatement for zero-error samples, been considered rather than relying on the point projection alone?
- Have known errors been proposed for correction rather than absorbed silently into the projection?
- Have qualitative factors, such as fraud, override, or systematic cause, been considered alongside the quantitative evaluation?
- Does the workpaper document the population, sample, each known error, the projection calculation, the comparison to tolerable misstatement, and the resulting conclusion?
