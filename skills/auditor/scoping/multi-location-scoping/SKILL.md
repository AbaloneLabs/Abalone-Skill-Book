# Multi-Location Scoping

When an entity operates across many locations, the auditor cannot test everything everywhere. Multi-location scoping is the judgment of where to look, how hard, and through whom. Agents often default to visiting the same locations each year, or they treat all locations uniformly and waste effort on immaterial sites while under-covering the significant ones. The disciplined approach identifies which locations are financially significant, which carry particular risk, and which can be addressed through analytical procedures, component auditors, or specific procedures rather than full visits. A multi-location strategy that cannot explain why each location received the attention it did is not a strategy; it is a schedule.

Use this skill when planning a group or multi-location audit, when deciding location coverage and visits, when relying on component auditors, and when allocating work across geographically dispersed operations. The goal is a risk-based coverage strategy that directs effort to the locations that matter.

## Core Rules

### Identify Financially Significant Components First

Not all locations are equal. Begin by identifying which components are financially significant to the group, because these drive the depth of required procedures.

A component is typically financially significant when:

- its revenue, profit, assets, or other benchmark exceeds a threshold of group materiality;
- it carries a significant risk due to the nature of its business or transactions;
- it holds significant related-party or unusual transactions;
- it is central to the group's strategy, financing, or compliance;
- it has a high growth rate or volatility relative to the group.

For financially significant components, perform an audit using the group materiality, or specific procedures combining review and analytical work. Document the significance determination and the threshold used.

### Assess Risk Significance Beyond Financial Size

Some locations are risk-significant even when not financially large. A small location can carry disproportionate risk because of what happens there.

Consider risk significance when a location:

- processes complex or unusual transactions, such as intercompany or transfer pricing;
- operates in a high-risk geography with corruption, sanction, or regulatory exposure;
- holds significant estimates, fair-value assets, or off-balance-sheet structures;
- has weak controls, high turnover, or limited oversight;
- is newly acquired or recently restructured;
- is subject to specific regulatory or licensing requirements;
- processes transactions for other components or acts as a shared service center.

Risk-significant locations may warrant targeted procedures even when their financial size is below the significance threshold.

### Decide The Coverage Approach Per Location

Once significance and risk are assessed, decide how each location will be covered. Uniform coverage is inefficient; the approach should fit the location's profile.

Coverage approaches:

- full audit at group materiality, for financially significant components;
- specific procedures, such as analytical review and targeted testing, for risk-significant but smaller components;
- analytical procedures at group level, for non-significant components, to confirm nothing unusual;
- reliance on a component auditor, where a competent component auditor performs the work;
- no separate procedures, for clearly immaterial and low-risk locations, with the group team performing overall analytical procedures.

Document the approach per location and the reasoning. The aggregate of approaches must cover the group's significant risks.

### Evaluate And Rely On Component Auditors Deliberately

Where a component auditor performs work, the group auditor remains responsible for the group opinion. Reliance must be deliberate, not automatic.

Before relying on a component auditor:

- evaluate the component auditor's competence, independence, and regulatory standing;
- determine the component auditor's materiality and confirm it is appropriate for the group;
- understand the component auditor's planned procedures and significant risks;
- determine the involvement required, which may range from review of working papers to attendance and direction;
- communicate the group's requirements, including significant risks, group materiality, and reporting deadlines;
- consider whether ethics, quality, or regulatory differences require additional involvement.

Document the evaluation and the nature of involvement. Reliance without evaluation transfers responsibility the group auditor cannot shed.

### Address Intercompany And Shared-Process Risk

Multi-location entities carry intercompany and shared-process risk that single-location audits do not. Elimination entries, transfer pricing, and shared service centers create opportunities for error and manipulation.

Assess:

- whether intercompany balances are properly eliminated on consolidation;
- whether transfer pricing is consistent with the arm's-length principle and tax rules;
- whether shared service centers process transactions for multiple components with consistent controls;
- whether intercompany transactions are near period end and may not have been recorded consistently;
- whether one component's results depend on another's reporting timing.

Design procedures that cross locations, such as reconciling intercompany balances from both sides and testing elimination entries.

### Calibrate Coverage To The Control Environment

The control environment varies across locations and affects how much coverage each needs. A strong local environment may support lighter coverage; a weak one demands more.

Consider:

- whether local management is competent and independent of pressure to manipulate;
- whether local controls are designed and operating, or whether they are nominal;
- whether internal audit provides coverage that the external auditor can leverage;
- whether there is a history of local findings or restatements;
- whether governance and oversight reach the location effectively.

Use the environment assessment to adjust the coverage approach, and document the basis for lighter or heavier coverage.

### Document The Multi-Location Strategy As A Coherent Whole

The coverage decisions across locations must add up to a coherent group strategy. Document the strategy so a reviewer can see the whole picture.

Document:

- the significance and risk assessment per location;
- the coverage approach per location and the reasoning;
- the component auditors relied on and the involvement provided;
- the intercompany and shared-process procedures;
- how the aggregate of procedures covers the group's significant risks;
- the materiality used at group and component level.

A location-by-location list without a group-level explanation is not a strategy. The documentation must show how the pieces combine to support the group opinion.

## Common Traps

### Visiting The Same Locations Every Year Out Of Habit

Defaulting to prior-year locations without reconsidering significance and risk wastes effort on sites that no longer matter and misses emerging significant locations.

### Treating All Locations Uniformly

Uniform coverage over-tests immaterial locations and under-covers significant or risk-significant ones. Coverage must fit each location's profile.

### Relying On Component Auditors Without Evaluation

The group auditor cannot shed responsibility by delegating. Reliance without evaluating competence, independence, and materiality is indefensible.

### Ignoring Risk-Significant Small Locations

A small location with complex intercompany, high-risk geography, or weak controls can carry disproportionate risk. Financial size alone does not capture significance.

### Overlooking Intercompany And Shared-Process Risk

Intercompany balances, transfer pricing, and shared service centers create cross-location risk that single-location procedures miss. Design procedures that cross locations.

### Failing To Reconcile Component And Group Materiality

If the component auditor's materiality is too high for the group, errors material to the group may pass undetected at component level. Confirm materiality is appropriate.

### Documenting Locations Without A Group-Level Strategy

A list of locations and approaches without a group-level explanation does not show how the pieces combine to support the opinion. The strategy must be coherent as a whole.

## Self-Check

- Are financially significant components identified using a documented threshold of group materiality, with full audit or specific procedures planned?
- Are risk-significant locations, including those with complex intercompany, high-risk geography, or weak controls, identified and given targeted procedures regardless of size?
- Is the coverage approach, full audit, specific procedures, analytical review, or component reliance, tailored to each location's profile and documented?
- Are component auditors evaluated for competence, independence, and materiality before reliance, with the nature of involvement documented?
- Are intercompany balances, transfer pricing, and shared service centers addressed with cross-location procedures?
- Is coverage calibrated to the local control environment, with lighter or heavier coverage justified and documented?
- Does the documented multi-location strategy explain how the aggregate of procedures covers the group's significant risks and supports the group opinion?
- Are group and component materiality reconciled so that errors material to the group cannot pass undetected at component level?
