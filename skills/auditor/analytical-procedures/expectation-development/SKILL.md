# Expectation Development

The expectation is the heart of a substantive analytical procedure. It is the auditor's independent prediction of what the recorded amount should be, developed before (or independently of) looking at the recorded result. The evidential value of the entire procedure rests on the expectation: a precise, well-corroborated expectation can detect a material misstatement and provide substantive evidence; a loose expectation built from the recorded amount itself, or from uncorroborated management forecasts, will accommodate almost any result and provides no evidence at all. The discipline is to build expectations from independent drivers, to make them precise enough to matter, and to document their basis so the conclusion is defensible.

## Core Rules

### Build expectations before or independently of the recorded amount

The expectation must be formed independently of the number it is testing. If the auditor builds the expectation by looking at the recorded amount and rationalising why it is reasonable, the procedure is circular and has no evidential value. Develop the expectation from drivers and relationships established first, then compare to the recorded amount. Where prior-year or management data is used, confirm it is corroborated and adjusted for known changes, not simply accepted because it produces a number close to the recorded amount.

### Identify independent, corroborable drivers

Strong expectations rest on drivers that can be confirmed independently of the account being tested. Good drivers include:

- **Operational volumes**: units produced, units sold, headcount, floor space, flight hours, bed nights.
- **External rates**: market interest rates, statutory tax rates, published tariffs, contracted commission rates.
- **Contractual terms**: lease rates, royalty percentages, depreciation schedules.
- **Physical or countable measures**: barrels, tonnes, megawatt-hours, transactions processed.

Weak drivers include management forecasts (which may be biased or aspirational), last year's amount without adjustment, and amounts derived from the same ledger as the account under test. Prefer drivers whose value the auditor can confirm from outside the accounting system.

### Quantify the relationship between driver and account

Move from "this account is driven by that factor" to a quantified relationship:

- interest expense = average debt × average rate;
- depreciation = asset base × depreciation rate (by class);
- commission expense = commissioned revenue × contractual rate;
- payroll expense = headcount × average compensation, adjusted for changes.

State the relationship as a formula or model, with the inputs and their values. A quantified relationship can be tested for precision; a qualitative relationship ("payroll tracks headcount") cannot.

### Adjust for known changes and one-off items

Expectations must reflect the current period's realities. Adjust the base relationship for:

- changes in rates, prices, or terms during the period;
- acquisitions, divestitures, or new product lines;
- one-off items in the prior period that do not recur;
- changes in accounting policy or classification;
- seasonality and number of trading days.

An unadjusted prior-year comparison ignores everything that changed; a well-adjusted expectation isolates the effect of the changes so the remaining difference is meaningful.

### Calibrate precision to materiality and risk

The expectation must be precise enough that a material misstatement would produce a detectable difference. Establish:

- what magnitude of difference would indicate a material misstatement for this account?
- how tight is the expectation — could a materially wrong recorded amount still fall within the expected range?
- is the precision finer than the relevant threshold, or coarser?

If the expectation's natural variability is larger than the material misstatement threshold, it cannot provide substantive evidence at that threshold, regardless of how reasonable it looks. Either tighten the expectation (better drivers, more granularity, segmentation) or recognise that the procedure provides only broad reasonableness, not substantive evidence.

### Use granularity and segmentation to sharpen precision

Aggregated expectations are often too coarse. Sharpen by:

- **Segmenting** by product, location, or customer, where drivers differ across segments.
- **Using monthly or quarterly data** rather than annual, to reduce the noise that annual totals mask.
- **Stratifying by driver level** (e.g., separate expectations for high-rate and low-rate debt).

Granularity reduces within-group variability and tightens the expectation, often turning a procedure too coarse at the aggregate level into one precise enough at the segment level.

### Corroborate the drivers independently

Each driver's value should be confirmed from a source independent of the account under test:

- headcount from HR records or payroll system, not from management assertion;
- production volumes from operational systems or production reports;
- interest rates from loan agreements or market data;
- contractual rates from the contracts themselves.

Drivers taken from the same ledger as the account under test create circularity: the expectation and the recorded amount share a source and will tend to agree regardless of correctness. Independent corroboration of drivers is what makes the expectation a genuine check.

### Define the investigation threshold before comparing

Before comparing expectation to recorded amount, define what magnitude of difference will trigger investigation. Setting the threshold after seeing the result invites rationalisation. The threshold should reflect:

- the materiality of the account;
- the normal variability of the relationship;
- the risk assessment for the assertion.

Differences above the threshold are investigated and corroborated; differences below are documented but not pursued. A pre-set threshold disciplines the evaluation and makes it defensible.

### Document the expectation, its basis, and its precision

For each substantive analytical procedure, document:

- the account and assertion tested;
- the drivers and their independently corroborated values;
- the quantified relationship (formula or model);
- the adjustments for known changes;
- the resulting expectation and its precision (range or point);
- the investigation threshold;
- the comparison to recorded amount, any difference, and the investigation.

This documentation is what makes the procedure substantive evidence rather than a comfort check. An expectation without documented basis and precision cannot be reviewed or relied upon.

## Common Traps

- **Building the expectation from the recorded amount**, making the procedure circular and evidentially worthless.
- **Using uncorroborated management forecasts or unadjusted prior-year amounts** as drivers, producing expectations too loose to detect material misstatement.
- **Leaving the relationship qualitative** ("tracks headcount") without quantifying it, so precision cannot be assessed.
- **Failing to adjust for known changes** (rates, scope, policy, one-offs), so differences reflect changes rather than misstatements.
- **Setting precision coarser than the materiality threshold**, so a materially wrong answer would still pass.
- **Using aggregated annual data** where monthly or segmented data would tighten the expectation.
- **Taking drivers from the same ledger as the account under test**, creating circularity.
- **Defining the investigation threshold after seeing the result**, inviting rationalisation of inconvenient differences; **Documenting "no significant differences" without the expectation, drivers, relationship, adjustments, and precision**, leaving the procedure indefensible

## Self-Check

- Did I build the expectation independently of the recorded amount, from corroborated drivers established first?
- Are the drivers independent and corroborable — operational volumes, external rates, contractual terms — rather than management forecasts or same-ledger amounts?
- Is the relationship quantified as a formula or model with stated inputs, not left qualitative?
- Did I adjust for known changes in rates, scope, policy, and one-off items so the remaining difference is meaningful?
- Is the expectation's precision finer than the material misstatement threshold for this account — could a materially wrong answer still pass?
- Did I use granularity and segmentation (product, location, monthly data) to tighten the expectation where the aggregate was too coarse?
- Did I corroborate each driver's value from a source independent of the account under test?
- Did I define the investigation threshold before comparing expectation to recorded amount?
- Is the expectation, its drivers, relationship, adjustments, precision, threshold, comparison, and investigation documented so the procedure is substantive evidence?
