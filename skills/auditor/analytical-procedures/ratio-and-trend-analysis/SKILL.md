# Ratio and Trend Analysis

Ratio and trend analysis compares financial data across periods, against expectations, and against industry benchmarks to identify unusual relationships that may indicate risk or misstatement. It is one of the most efficient procedures available, capable of covering whole populations quickly, but its value depends entirely on interpreting the results correctly. The two characteristic failures are the opposite extremes: dismissing every difference with a plausible explanation (false comfort), and chasing every minor fluctuation as if it were a signal (wasted effort). The discipline is to distinguish meaningful signals from noise, to corroborate explanations rather than accept narratives, and to let the analysis direct further work rather than substitute for it.

## Core Rules

### Select ratios and trends that map to the risks and accounts that matter

Not every ratio is informative for every audit. Select ratios and trends that relate to the significant accounts and the identified risks:

- **Gross margin, revenue by segment, days sales outstanding** for revenue and receivables.
- **Inventory turnover, days inventory on hand, write-down ratios** for inventory.
- **Current ratio, quick ratio, debt service coverage** for liquidity and going concern.
- **Expense ratios** (expense to revenue) for operating expenses and accruals.

Choose ratios whose movement would indicate a risk relevant to the assertions under audit. A library of generic ratios applied uniformly provides less signal than a targeted set chosen for the entity's risk profile.

### Compare against multiple reference points, not only prior year

A single comparison (current vs prior year) gives limited signal. Build a richer picture by comparing against:

- **Prior periods** — multiple years, to distinguish trend from one-off.
- **Budget or forecast** — management's own expectations, which if missed may indicate pressure or misstatement.
- **Industry or peer benchmarks** — adjusted for differences in business model, to spot divergence.
- **Interim periods** — monthly or quarterly data within the year, to spot timing or concentration issues.
- **Non-financial drivers** — units, headcount, capacity, to test whether financial results are consistent with operational reality.

Each reference point answers a different question; using several triangulates the signal and reduces the chance of misinterpreting a single comparison.

### Distinguish meaningful signal from noise

Not every fluctuation indicates risk. Assess each difference by:

- **Magnitude relative to materiality and to normal variability** — a 3% swing in a volatile account is noise; a 3% swing in a stable, material account may be signal.
- **Direction and consistency** — a one-month spike differs from a sustained trend; consistent directional movement is more likely meaningful.
- **Corroboration across ratios** — if margin, turnover, and DSO all move consistently, the signal is stronger than a single isolated ratio.
- **Explanation availability** — a difference with no ready explanation is more concerning than one with a clear, corroborated cause.

Develop a threshold or judgement for what magnitude of difference warrants investigation, calibrated to the account's materiality and volatility, so effort is concentrated on genuine signals.

### Investigate and corroborate explanations, do not accept narratives

When a difference is significant enough to investigate, the explanation must be corroborated, not narrated. Acceptable corroboration includes:

- specific, quantified transactions or events that account for the difference, verified by the auditor;
- operational changes (volume, mix, price, capacity) confirmed independently;
- errors identified and corrected;
- external factors (market, regulatory) confirmed by evidence.

An explanation that "margins fell due to competitive pressure" without quantification or corroboration is a narrative, not evidence. Require explanations to be specific and independently confirmed before accepting them as resolving the difference.

### Use non-financial data to test the credibility of financial trends

Financial results should be consistent with operational reality. Compare financial trends to non-financial drivers:

- revenue per unit shipped, per customer, per employee;
- cost per unit produced, per hour worked;
- capacity utilisation against production volume;
- headcount against payroll expense.

Divergence between financial and non-financial trends is a strong signal: revenue rising while units shipped fall, or payroll flat while headcount grows, indicates something that warrants investigation. Non-financial data is often harder to manipulate and provides an independent check on the financials.

### Segment the analysis to avoid masking by aggregation

Aggregated data can mask material movements in components. Segment the analysis by:

- product line, customer segment, or geography;
- month or quarter, to spot timing issues hidden in annual totals;
- location or business unit, especially in multi-location entities.

A stable total revenue may conceal a declining core product offset by a new, lower-margin product; a stable annual expense may conceal a Q4 spike. Segment to where the risk lives, not only at the consolidated level.

### Use trend analysis to direct further work, not to conclude

Ratio and trend analysis is powerful for identifying where to look, weaker for proving the answer. A clean trend analysis provides comfort that nothing obvious is wrong, but it rarely provides sufficient evidence on its own for a material assertion. Use it to:

- identify risks that need targeted detail or analytical procedures;
- confirm that areas assessed as low risk remain reasonable;
- provide overall reasonableness as part of a combined evidence strategy.

Where a trend identifies a concern, design a specific procedure to address it; do not let the trend analysis itself be the only response.

### Consider the reliability and comparability of the underlying data

The analysis is only as good as the data. Confirm:

- the data is complete and accurate for each period compared;
- accounting policies are consistent across periods (or adjusted for known changes);
- scope is consistent (acquisitions, divestitures, reclassifications adjusted out);
- industry benchmarks are genuinely comparable (similar business model, scale, geography).

Data quality and comparability problems silently distort trends; a trend built on inconsistent data provides false signal or false comfort.

### Apply different standards for risk assessment, substantive, and final review

Ratio and trend analysis serves different purposes with different standards:

- **Risk assessment**: looser, exploratory, to identify risks and direct work.
- **Substantive**: precise expectations, corroborated explanations, as described in the analytical-procedure-design skill.
- **Final review**: broad, reasonableness-focused, to confirm overall consistency before the opinion.

Match the rigour to the purpose; a risk-assessment-quality analysis used as substantive evidence is a common and serious error.

## Common Traps

- **Applying a generic library of ratios** without targeting the entity's significant accounts and risks.
- **Comparing only to prior year**, missing the richer signal from budget, industry, interim, and non-financial comparisons.
- **Accepting narrative explanations** of differences without specific, quantified, corroborated causes.
- **Chasing every minor fluctuation** as if it were signal, wasting effort and diluting focus.
- **Dismissing significant differences** with plausible-sounding explanations, providing false comfort.
- **Analysing only at the consolidated level**, allowing aggregation to mask material movements in components.
- **Using trend analysis as the sole substantive evidence** for a material assertion, where it can only direct further work.
- **Building trends on inconsistent data** (policy changes, scope changes, non-comparable benchmarks), distorting the signal.
- **Using risk-assessment-quality analysis as substantive evidence**, substituting a looser standard where a tighter one is required.

## Self-Check

- Did I select ratios and trends that map to the significant accounts and identified risks, rather than applying a generic library?
- Did I compare against multiple reference points — prior periods, budget, industry, interim, non-financial — not only prior year?
- Did I distinguish meaningful signal from noise using magnitude, direction, corroboration, and materiality, and concentrate effort on genuine signals?
- For each significant difference, did I investigate and corroborate the explanation with specific, quantified, independent evidence, not narratives?
- Did I use non-financial data to test the credibility of financial trends, investigating divergences?
- Did I segment the analysis by product, customer, geography, and period to avoid masking by aggregation?
- Am I using trend analysis to direct further work and provide overall reasonableness, not as the sole substantive evidence?
- Did I confirm the data is complete, accurate, and comparable across periods, with policy and scope changes adjusted?
- Did I match the rigour to the purpose — risk assessment, substantive, or final review — rather than using one standard for all three?
