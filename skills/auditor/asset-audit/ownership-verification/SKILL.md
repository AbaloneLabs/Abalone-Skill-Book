# Ownership Verification

Ownership verification produces evidence that the entity holds the rights or controls the benefits that an asset's balance sheet presence implies. Agents tend to conflate physical possession with ownership, but the rights and obligations assertion is distinct: a machine on the factory floor may be leased, inventory in the warehouse may be consigned in, and securities in an account may be pledged as collateral. The judgment problem is that ownership evidence is often documentary and external, scattered across title registries, lease agreements, security agreements, and registration filings, and the absence of a lien or encumbrance is harder to prove than its presence.

Failing to verify ownership leaves the rights assertion unsupported and can mask material misclassification, such as operating leases presented as owned assets or pledged assets presented as unencumbered. The skill is about identifying the legal form of ownership or control for each material asset class and obtaining evidence specific to that form.

Use this skill whenever the audit program calls for rights and obligations testing across cash, securities, inventory, property, intellectual property, or any asset whose title or control status is material. The goal is to confirm that the entity owns or controls what it records and to identify any encumbrance, restriction, or third-party claim that affects presentation and disclosure.

## Core Rules

### Separate Possession From Ownership and Control

Physical presence proves existence, not title. The first step in ownership verification is to determine, for each material asset, whether the entity owns it outright, controls it under a lease or similar arrangement, or merely holds it on consignment or for a third party. Conflating these states is the root of most rights and obligations errors.

- Confirm whether each significant fixed asset is owned, leased, or held under a hire-purchase or similar arrangement.
- For inventory, identify goods held on consignment that belong to a supplier and exclude them from the entity's records.
- Identify consigned goods out that the entity owns but are held by a customer or agent.
- For cash, confirm the account is in the entity's name and that the entity controls the funds.
- For securities, confirm beneficial ownership through the custodian, not merely custody.
- Distinguish assets held under finance leases, which are recognized, from operating leases, which may not be.
- Flag any asset whose possession and ownership clearly diverge for further investigation.
- Reconcile the legal form of holding to the accounting treatment applied.
- Identify assets held in trust or as a fiduciary that must be excluded from the entity's balance sheet.

### Obtain and Inspect Title Evidence for Real Property

Real property ownership is established by title documents and registry records, not by occupation. The auditor must inspect the relevant evidence and confirm it is in the entity's name and free of undisclosed encumbrance. Title is the asset class where ownership evidence is most formal and most verifiable.

- Inspect title deeds or equivalent registry extracts for land and buildings.
- Confirm the title is registered in the entity's legal name and matches the asset description.
- Search the relevant land or property registry for liens, mortgages, and caveats.
- Reconcile the property on the fixed asset register to the title document by address and identifier.
- For recently acquired property, confirm the transfer was registered and the consideration matches.
- Identify any property held through a special purpose vehicle or intermediate entity and trace control.
- Assess whether jointly held property is correctly reflected in the entity's share.
- Confirm that property acquired with government grants or incentives carries no undisclosed restrictions.
- Verify that improvements on leased land are accounted for consistently with the lease term.
- Confirm that property held under joint venture or partnership arrangements is reflected at the correct proportionate or equity-method amount.

### Evaluate Lease Classification and Right-of-Use Assets

Leases are a primary source of ownership ambiguity. Whether an asset is owned, finance-leased, or operating-leased changes both the balance sheet and the disclosure. The auditor must test classification against the substance of the arrangement, not merely the label.

- Review lease agreements for terms that transfer substantially all risks and rewards of ownership.
- Test whether right-of-use assets are recognized for leases meeting the recognition criteria.
- Confirm the lease term reflects enforceable options to extend or terminate.
- Evaluate the discount rate used to measure lease liabilities for reasonableness.
- Identify sale-leaseback transactions and test whether gain recognition is appropriate.
- Assess whether short-term and low-value lease exceptions are applied correctly.
- Reconcile lease accounting to the underlying contractual obligations.
- Flag leases with related parties for additional scrutiny of terms and classification.
- Test whether lease incentives, variable payments, and residual guarantees are correctly measured.
- Assess whether lease modifications and remeasurements during the period are accounted for correctly.

### Confirm Inventory Rights and Consigned Goods

Inventory ownership is complicated by consignment, bill-and-hold, and conditional sale arrangements. The auditor must determine which goods on the entity's premises belong to it and which goods it owns that are held elsewhere. Rights errors here directly affect both inventory and payables.

- Confirm goods on the floor belong to the entity and exclude supplier-consigned inventory.
- Identify and confirm goods the entity owns that are held by consignees or agents.
- Test bill-and-hold arrangements for revenue recognition and ownership transfer criteria.
- Evaluate whether goods in transit at year-end are correctly included or excluded based on shipping terms.
- Confirm the accounting for return rights and consignment-out arrangements.
- Reconcile consignment agreements to the recorded inventory and receivable balances.
- Assess whether inventory held by processors or toll manufacturers is confirmed and owned.
- Identify any inventory pledged as collateral and confirm the related disclosure.
- Verify the treatment of inventoriable costs for goods shipped on approval or with right of return.
- Confirm that goods at toll processors or subcontractors are confirmed directly and reconciled to production records.

### Identify Pledged, Collateralized, and Restricted Assets

Assets pledged as security for borrowings or other obligations remain on the balance sheet but carry restrictions that must be disclosed. The auditor must identify these restrictions because they affect presentation and may signal liquidity risk. Encumbrances are often documented in loan agreements that are not obviously linked to specific assets.

- Review loan and credit agreements for collateral pledges and asset restrictions.
- Confirm whether cash balances are restricted, escrowed, or subject to compensating balance requirements.
- Identify securities and inventory pledged under repurchase or securities lending agreements.
- Confirm that pledged assets are still presented on the balance sheet and appropriately disclosed.
- Test the carrying amount of pledged assets for impairment where restrictions affect recoverability.
- Reconcile pledged asset schedules from lenders to the entity's records.
- Assess whether restrictions on remitting dividends or transferring assets between subsidiaries are disclosed.
- Identify any assets subject to attachment, seizure, or legal claim.
- Evaluate whether guarantees issued over assets of subsidiaries or affiliates are disclosed.
- Identify any assets subject to retention-of-title clauses from suppliers that remain legally owned by the seller.

### Verify Intellectual Property and Intangible Rights

Intangibles have no physical form, so ownership is established entirely by registration, contract, and legal opinion. The auditor must confirm that the entity holds the rights the balance sheet implies and that those rights are enforceable. Intangible ownership is the area where legal evidence is most indispensable.

- Inspect patent, trademark, and copyright registrations and confirm the entity is the registered owner.
- Review license agreements to determine whether the entity is licensor or licensee.
- Confirm the geographic scope, duration, and exclusivity of intangible rights.
- Identify intangibles acquired in business combinations and trace them to the purchase agreement.
- Assess whether internally generated intangibles meet the criteria for capitalization.
- Evaluate legal challenges, oppositions, or infringement claims that may impair the rights.
- Confirm renewal dates and maintenance fees are current for registered rights.
- Reconcile the intangible asset register to the underlying legal documentation.
- Test whether in-licensed technology is correctly distinguished from owned intellectual property.
- Assess whether franchise rights and broadcasting licenses are supported by enforceable agreements and within their term.

### Use External Confirmations and Searches

Ownership evidence is strongest when it comes from independent sources. Title registries, lenders, attorneys, and counterparties can corroborate or contradict the entity's assertions. The auditor should use these sources rather than relying on internally generated schedules.

- Obtain lender confirmations of collateral and pledged assets.
- Search public registries for liens, charges, and security interests against the entity and its assets.
- Request attorney confirmation of ownership disputes, claims, and encumbrances.
- Confirm consignment and bailment arrangements directly with counterparties.
- For significant acquisitions, obtain independent legal opinion on title transfer.
- Use corporate registry searches to confirm the entity's legal status and authority to hold assets.
- Corroborate related-party asset transfers with independent evidence of terms and value.
- Document the source and independence of each ownership confirmation.
- Where confirmations are not returned, perform alternative procedures and assess the reliability of alternatives.
- Consider negative confirmations only where the risk of misstatement is low and the population is large.

### Assess Disclosure of Rights, Restrictions, and Encumbrances

Ownership findings must be reflected in the financial statements, not just the workpapers. Restrictions, pledges, and encumbrances are disclosure requirements that follow directly from rights testing. A clean ownership conclusion with a missing disclosure is still a deficiency.

- Confirm pledged and restricted assets are disclosed in the notes.
- Verify disclosure of significant restrictions on the use or disposal of assets.
- Confirm that assets held for sale or held under operating leases are separately presented where required.
- Test disclosure of rights of use and lease commitments for consistency with the lease testing.
- Confirm that contingencies related to ownership claims are cross-referenced to the contingency workpapers.
- Assess whether related-party asset holdings and restrictions are adequately disclosed.
- Verify that encumbrances on assets of subsidiaries are consolidated and disclosed appropriately.
- Confirm that restrictions arising from government grants or regulatory regimes on asset use are disclosed.
- Assess whether assets subject to operating lease are correctly presented as right-of-use assets with corresponding lease liabilities.

## Common Traps

### Equating Possession With Ownership

An asset on the premises may belong to a supplier on consignment, and an asset the entity owns may sit in a customer's warehouse. The trap is the visual comfort of seeing the asset. Confirm legal title or control separately from physical location.

### Missing a Lien or Encumbrance Through Incomplete Search

A title deed in the entity's name does not prove the asset is unencumbered. The trap is stopping at the deed and skipping the registry search for charges and security interests. Always search the relevant registry for liens and mortgages.

### Misclassifying a Lease

Treating a finance lease as operating, or vice versa, misstates both assets and liabilities. The trap is relying on the contractual label rather than the substance of risks and rewards. Test classification against the economic substance of the arrangement.

### Overlooking Consigned Inventory

Consigned-in goods inflate inventory and equity if recorded as the entity's own. Consigned-out goods are omitted if not tracked. The trap is assuming warehouse contents equal owned inventory. Confirm consignment terms with counterparties.

### Ignoring Pledged Assets in Loan Agreements

Collateral pledges buried in loan covenants are easy to miss if the auditor reviews the loan balance but not the agreement terms. The trap is treating the debt workpaper as sufficient. Read the security and covenant sections of financing agreements.

### Assuming Internally Generated Intangibles Are Owned

Capitalized development costs or brands may not meet the recognition criteria, or the rights may be challenged. The trap is accepting the capitalized balance without legal support. Confirm registration and recognition criteria separately.

### Forgetting Restricted Cash

Escrow accounts, compensating balances, and legally restricted deposits may look like ordinary cash. The trap is confirming the balance without testing for restrictions. Classify and disclose restricted cash correctly.

### Accepting Related-Party Transfers at Face Value

Asset transfers between related parties may not reflect arm's-length terms or genuine title transfer. The trap is treating them as ordinary transactions. Scrutinize the substance and disclosure of related-party asset movements.

## Self-Check

- For each material asset class, did you determine whether the entity owns, controls, or merely holds the asset?
- Were title deeds or registry extracts inspected for real property and reconciled to the fixed asset register?
- Were public registries searched for liens, charges, mortgages, and security interests?
- Was lease classification tested against the substance of the arrangement, not just the contractual label?
- Were consigned-in goods excluded from inventory and consigned-out goods confirmed and included?
- Were loan and credit agreements reviewed for collateral pledges and asset restrictions?
- Were pledged and restricted assets still presented on the balance sheet and appropriately disclosed?
- For intangibles, were registrations, licenses, and legal rights confirmed and reconciled to the asset register?
- Were ownership confirmations obtained from independent external sources where possible?
- Are disclosures of restrictions, encumbrances, and pledged assets consistent with the ownership testing?; were related-party asset transfers scrutinized for substance and arm's-length treatment?
