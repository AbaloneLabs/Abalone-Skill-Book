# Existence Verification

Existence verification is the audit work that produces evidence an asset recorded in the ledger corresponds to something real at the balance sheet date. Agents tend to treat it as a mechanical counting exercise, but the real judgment problem is that existence evidence is assertion-specific and direction-sensitive: a count proves the item exists, it does not prove the entity owns it, values it correctly, or that all items are recorded. Overstated assets and fictitious entries are among the most common fraud concealment techniques, so existence procedures must be designed to resist manipulation, not merely to reconcile to a listing. The work also spans assets that cannot be touched at all, such as cash on deposit, securities in a custodial account, or goods consigned to a customer, each of which requires a different evidence source.

Failing to design existence procedures with fraud resistance in mind produces evidence that looks complete on paper but collapses under scrutiny, because a client intent on overstating results will exploit any predictable, mechanical procedure. The skill is therefore about evidence design, not arithmetic, and about staying honest regarding what each procedure does and does not prove.

Use this skill whenever the audit program calls for physical observation, third-party confirmations, tag tracing, or existence testing across any asset class. The goal is to obtain sufficient appropriate evidence that recorded assets are real, while staying clear about what that evidence does and does not prove, and about how the evidence bridges from the observation date to the reporting date.

## Core Rules

### Match the Procedure to the Asset Class

Existence evidence is not interchangeable across asset types. The source of evidence must fit the nature of the asset, and a procedure valid for one class can be meaningless or even misleading for another. Selecting the right procedure begins with understanding how the asset is held, where it is held, and what independent corroboration is available. A bank confirmation proves cash exists; a physical inspection proves a machine exists; neither proves anything about the other.

- Cash on hand: count on a surprise basis, control the funds until recorded, and reconcile to the bank reconciliation and cutoff.
- Cash on deposit: obtain a bank confirmation, not a bank statement alone; confirm closed and zero-balance accounts too.
- Marketable securities: independent custodian confirmation, inspect certificates or electronic holdings reports.
- Inventory: attend and observe the physical count, test counts, and evaluate count instructions and controls.
- Property, plant and equipment: physical inspection of selected items, vouch additions to invoices and tagging records.
- Intangibles: inspect legal agreements, registration certificates, and capitalization support; existence is documentary rather than physical.
- Assets held by others: direct written confirmation from the holder, never management representation alone.
- Prepaid and deferred assets: existence is established by underlying contracts and payment evidence, not physical inspection.
- Construction in progress: inspect the site, support costs with invoices and progress reports, and assess percentage complete.
- Resources or biological assets: consider specialist valuation and physical observation of the living resource.
- Cryptographic or digital assets: confirm holdings through signed wallet proofs or independent custodian reports rather than screenshots.

### Observe the Direction of Testing

Existence is tested by tracing from the records to the physical asset or external evidence. Tracing in the other direction tests completeness, not existence. Mixing the two directions is a common cause of insufficient evidence and a frequent finding in inspection reviews, because the workpaper appears to support the assertion when it actually supports a different one. The direction must be deliberate and labeled, not an accident of how the sample was drawn.

- Select items from the fixed asset register and physically locate them on the floor.
- Select inventory items from the perpetual ledger and confirm they are on the floor during the count.
- Trace recorded securities to the custodian's holding report by CUSIP and quantity.
- Do not count floor-to-ledger items and label that as existence testing.
- Document the direction explicitly in the workpaper so a reviewer can follow the logic.
- Where both directions are tested, label each clearly as existence or completeness.
- Tie the recorded cost of inspected fixed assets to the vendor invoice and capitalization approval.
- For samples drawn by the client, re-perform the selection independently to preserve direction integrity.
- Stratify the sample so that high-value items receive proportionate attention without abandoning the record-to-asset direction.

### Distinguish Existence From Completeness and Ownership

A located asset proves existence only. It does not establish title, correct valuation, or that all assets are recorded. State the assertion being tested in every workpaper and resist scope creep that lets a single procedure silently cover assertions it was not designed for. A successful existence test often tempts the auditor to conclude too much, which is exactly where coverage gaps appear in inspection.

- Existence: is the recorded item real?
- Completeness: are all real items recorded?
- Rights and obligations: does the entity own or control it?
- Valuation: is it carried at an appropriate amount?
- Presentation and disclosure: is it classified and described correctly?
- Confirm you are not implicitly claiming more than the procedure supports.
- Cross-reference each assertion to the separate workpaper that actually tests it.
- Flag any asset whose existence is confirmed but whose title or valuation remains open.
- Record the specific evidence that closes existence, and list the separate evidence still required for the other assertions.

### Apply Surprise and Unpredictability

Predictable existence procedures are easy to circumvent, especially where fraud risk is elevated. Build in elements of surprise so that a client intent on concealing an overstatement cannot stage the evidence in advance. Unpredictability is not a nicety; it is a substantive response to the risk of management override and concealed fraud.

- Vary the timing of cash counts rather than announcing them on a fixed schedule.
- Select inventory locations not visited in prior years, including off-site and third-party warehouses.
- Include low-value, dormant, and zero-balance accounts in confirmation samples.
- Rotate the sample of fixed assets inspected across the audit cycle.
- Avoid telling client staff which items will be tested before the test begins.
- Consider unannounced visits for high-risk locations or where control weaknesses exist.
- Test a mix of high-value and high-volume items rather than only the largest balances.
- Use computer-assisted techniques to identify unusual or anomalous transactions for testing.
- Vary the auditors assigned to a location so that familiarity does not erode professional skepticism.

### Handle Assets Held by Third Parties

Assets held by custodians, banks, consignees, or public warehouses cannot be physically inspected by the auditor and require external corroboration. The independence and competence of the holder is part of the evidence, not a background detail. A confirmation signed by a related party is materially weaker than one signed by an independent custodian, and the audit response must reflect that difference.

- Send confirmations directly and control both the mailing and the return.
- Confirm the holder actually holds the asset for the entity, not for another party.
- For inventory at public warehouses, consider whether to observe the count or rely on a third-party report.
- For consigned goods out, confirm with the consignee and reconcile to consignment agreements.
- Evaluate the competence and independence of the third party, including any related-party relationship.
- For material holdings, consider whether a report on the holder's internal controls is available.
- Reconcile confirmed balances to the general ledger and investigate all differences.
- Where the holder is a related party, obtain additional corroborating evidence to offset the lack of independence.
- Re-confirm material third-party holdings at year-end, not merely at an interim date, unless a reliable roll-forward supports the interim balance.

### Evaluate the Client's Count Instructions and Controls

When observing inventory, the reliability of the count depends on the client's process. Evaluate instructions and controls before relying on the numbers, because a poorly controlled count cannot be salvaged by testing more tags. The auditor is observing a process, not just verifying a number, and the quality of that process determines how much substantive testing can be reduced.

- Review written count instructions for completeness, clarity, and consistency across locations.
- Confirm counters are independent of custody responsibilities where possible.
- Verify pre-numbered tags, count sheets, and reconciliation procedures are in use.
- Observe the handling of damaged, obsolete, and slow-moving items during the count.
- Test the accuracy of the final compilation from tags to the inventory listing.
- Evaluate how the client resolves recount differences and whether adjustments are approved.
- Confirm all locations, including consigned and third-party sites, are included in the count scope.
- Assess whether the perpetual system is reliable enough to support reduced substantive testing.
- Observe whether obsolete and slow-moving goods are segregated and tagged so they are not commingled with saleable stock.

### Document Conditions at the Time of Observation

Existence evidence is only valid for the moment of observation. The workpaper must record what was seen and how it bridges to the balance sheet date, because conditions change and a reviewer must be able to reconstruct the basis of the conclusion. Thin documentation is a frequent inspection finding even when the fieldwork was adequate.

- Record the date, time, location, and personnel present at the observation.
- Note the condition of assets observed, including signs of obsolescence, damage, or disuse.
- Perform roll-forward or roll-back procedures when the count date differs from the reporting date.
- Reconcile observed quantities to the perpetual ledger and investigate differences.
- Retain count sheets, tag copies, and notes as part of the audit file.
- Photograph significant or unusual items where it aids documentation.
- Record the count team's treatment of goods in transit and goods on the loading dock.
- Document the last receipt number and last shipment number for cutoff purposes.
- Note any goods in transit, goods on consignment, or items held for others that were observed but excluded from the entity's count.

### Address Inability to Observe

Sometimes observation is impossible, such as a first-year engagement with an interim count date already past. The auditor must decide whether alternative procedures are sufficient or whether a scope limitation exists. The decision must be made and escalated before the report date, not rationalized after the fact.

- Consider whether roll-forward from the last observed count is reliable and supported.
- Evaluate the client's perpetual inventory system and cycle-counting program.
- Assess whether the inability affects the ability to issue an unmodified opinion.
- Document the alternative procedures performed and their limitations honestly.
- Escalate scope limitations to the engagement partner promptly.
- Consider the effect on the opinion paragraph if the limitation is material and pervasive.
- Reassess risk and expand other procedures if existence evidence cannot be obtained.

### Assess Control Reliance for Existence Testing

Where the entity maintains a robust perpetual inventory system with disciplined cycle counting, the auditor may reduce but not eliminate substantive existence testing, and the decision must be supported by evidence that the controls actually operate rather than by their mere existence. Control reliance shifts where the effort concentrates, it does not remove the need to observe or confirm. The strength of the perpetual system must be tested, not assumed, and any reliance must be documented with the specific controls relied upon.

- Test the accuracy of the perpetual ledger by comparing recorded quantities to physical counts.
- Evaluate the cycle-counting program for frequency, coverage, and investigation of differences.
- Confirm that adjustments to the perpetual system are reviewed and approved by competent staff.
- Assess whether the system covers all locations and inventory categories, including consigned goods.
- Determine the reduction in substantive testing that control reliance supports and document the basis.
- Reassess control reliance when control deficiencies or errors are identified during testing.
- Confirm that any reliance on a service organization's controls is supported by a current SOC 1 report.

## Common Traps

### Treating a Floor-to-Ledger Trace as Existence Testing

Selecting items from the warehouse floor and finding them in the ledger tests completeness, not existence. Reviewers and regulators catch this mislabeling, and it leaves the existence assertion unsupported while giving a false sense of coverage. The trap is seductive because the work ties out neatly, but it answers the wrong question. Always start from the record and locate the asset.

### Relying on the Bank Statement Instead of a Bank Confirmation

A bank statement provided by the client is client-controlled evidence and does not confirm balances, accounts, or liabilities directly with the bank. It can miss closed accounts, undisclosed facilities, and contingent liabilities that the standard confirmation captures. The trap is that the statement looks authoritative and ties to the ledger, so it feels sufficient. A direct bank confirmation, including the standard liability and collateral sections, is the appropriate evidence.

### Observing Inventory Without Testing the Compilation

Standing in the warehouse and watching people count is not sufficient if the tags are never tied to the final inventory listing. Errors, alterations, and missing tags can occur during compilation after the auditors leave. The trap is treating presence at the count as the entire procedure. The auditor must test the accuracy of the roll-up from tags to the recorded balance.

### Ignoring Assets the Entity Does Not Physically Hold

Consigned inventory out, goods at processors, cash in escrow, and securities held by a custodian are easily overlooked because they are not on site. Omitting them leaves a gap in existence coverage that fraud can exploit precisely because no one is looking. The trap is building the sample only from what is visible. Maintain a list of off-site and third-party-held assets and confirm each independently.

### Announcing the Count Timing in Advance

When the client knows exactly when cash or inventory will be counted, there is time to temporarily cover shortages or move goods between locations. Predictability undermines the evidentiary value of the procedure and gives a misleadingly clean result. The trap is the convenience of a scheduled count. Build surprise into timing and location selection.

### Confusing Existence With Valuation

Locating an asset does not tell you it is worth its carrying amount. Damaged, obsolete, or slow-moving inventory may exist but require a write-down, and idle fixed assets may need impairment testing. The trap is closing the valuation assertion implicitly when the count succeeds. Separate the existence conclusion from the valuation conclusion and do not let a successful count close out valuation testing.

### Skipping Roll-Forward When the Count Date Differs

When inventory is counted before or after the balance sheet date, the observed quantities must be reconciled to the reporting date through purchases, sales, and production records. Skipping this step leaves a gap that can hide material misstatement in the intervening period. The trap is treating the observed count as the year-end balance. Document the roll-forward and test its components.

### Accepting a Custodian Confirmation Without Checking Independence

A confirmation from a related party or affiliated custodian is not independent evidence. The same risk applies to warehousemen or brokers with financial ties to the client. The trap is that a signed confirmation looks like strong evidence regardless of who signed it. Evaluate the holder's independence and corroborate where doubt exists.

## Self-Check

- Does each existence workpaper state the assertion tested and the direction of testing (record-to-asset)?
- For physical assets, were items selected from the records and then located, rather than floor-to-ledger?
- Were cash counts performed on a surprise basis with control maintained until the balance was recorded?
- Did bank confirmations include liability, collateral, and contingent sections, and were closed and zero-balance accounts confirmed?
- For inventory, were count instructions evaluated, the compilation tested, and roll-forward performed if the count date differed from the reporting date?
- Were all assets held by third parties identified and confirmed directly with the independent holder?
- Did fixed asset testing trace selected register items to physical assets and supporting invoices?
- Were surprise and unpredictability elements built into timing, location, and sample selection?
- Is the existence conclusion kept separate from ownership, valuation, and completeness conclusions?
- Where observation was impossible, were alternative procedures documented and assessed for sufficiency, and was any scope limitation escalated?
- Were off-site, consigned, and third-party-held assets explicitly included in the existence scope?
