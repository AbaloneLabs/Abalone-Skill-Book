# Valuation Testing

Valuation testing addresses whether assets are carried at amounts that are supportable under the applicable framework, and it is where judgment, estimate, and market data converge. Agents often treat valuation as a recalculation of a formula, but the real problem is that valuation depends on assumptions about an uncertain future, and management has both incentive and opportunity to choose assumptions that flatter the balance sheet. The work spans inventory net realizable value, securities measured at fair value, depreciable lives, intangible impairment, goodwill, and long-lived asset impairment, each governed by different rules and each vulnerable to different manipulation.

Failing to challenge valuation assumptions produces a clean workpaper that nonetheless misses material overstatement, because the math is often correct given inputs that were never examined. The skill is about testing the inputs, the process, and the bias, not about reproducing a spreadsheet.

Use this skill whenever the audit program includes valuation, impairment, fair value, depreciation, or write-down testing. The goal is to obtain evidence that carrying amounts are appropriate and that the assumptions, inputs, and methods behind them are reasonable, well-supported, and free from management bias.

## Core Rules

### Identify the Governing Framework and Model

Valuation rules differ by framework and by asset class, and applying the wrong model produces a structurally correct but substantively wrong conclusion. Before testing any number, confirm which standard governs and which model it prescribes. The framework determines whether impairment is triggered or measured first, whether reversals are permitted, and which inputs are admissible.

- Confirm whether the reporting framework is IFRS, US GAAP, or another local standard, because impairment triggers differ.
- For inventory, apply lower of cost and net realizable value, and confirm the cost flow assumption in use.
- For long-lived assets held for use, test for impairment only when triggers exist, then measure by the applicable model.
- For goodwill, confirm whether the annual test is required and whether the qualitative assessment was performed.
- For financial instruments, confirm the measurement category (amortized cost, FVOCI, FVTPL) and the applicable level.
- Identify whether the entity uses an allowed alternative such as the revaluation model, and test its consistency.
- Confirm that the same model is applied consistently period to period, with any change justified and disclosed.
- Verify the entity's cost accounting system correctly feeds inventory cost into the NRV comparison.

### Challenge Management's Assumptions

Valuation conclusions are only as good as the assumptions behind them. Management's assumptions about growth, discount rates, terminal values, useful lives, and recovery prospects are the most common locus of overstatement. The auditor's job is to challenge these, not to accept the model's output.

- Compare growth and margin assumptions to historical performance and industry benchmarks.
- Test the discount rate for reasonableness against market data and the entity's cost of capital.
- Evaluate whether terminal value or perpetuity assumptions are supportable and not overly optimistic.
- Assess whether useful life estimates reflect asset condition, technology cycles, and usage patterns.
- For NRV, test selling price, costs to complete, and costs to sell against current market evidence.
- Identify assumptions that are highly sensitive and perform sensitivity analysis on them.
- Look for assumptions that changed from the prior year without a corresponding change in circumstances.
- Test whether cash flow forecasts used for impairment are consistent with budgets approved by management and the board.

### Evaluate the Independence and Reliability of Inputs

Fair value inputs vary in reliability by level. Level 1 quoted prices in active markets are strong evidence; Level 3 unobservable inputs require the most challenge. The auditor must understand the input hierarchy and adjust the depth of testing accordingly.

- For Level 1, confirm quoted prices on the measurement date and the appropriate market.
- For Level 2, corroborate observable inputs and confirm the valuation technique is appropriate.
- For Level 3, develop a point estimate or range and compare it to management's estimate.
- Test the data sources for Level 3 inputs, including broker quotes and pricing services.
- Evaluate whether markets were active or inactive, and whether adjustments for inactive markets are warranted.
- Assess whether management's valuation process produces consistent and repeatable results.
- Consider whether a pricing specialist or valuation expert is needed for complex instruments.
- Assess whether broker quotes are binding or indicative, and whether multiple sources corroborate each other.
- Test whether any adjustments to observable inputs, such as blockage factors, are supported and consistently applied.

### Use Specialists Appropriately

Complex valuations, such as derivatives, private equity, or specialized property, often require a valuation specialist. The decision to use a specialist is a matter of competence and materiality, and the specialist's work must be evaluated, not merely accepted. Management's specialist is not a substitute for the auditor's own specialist when independence or competence is in question.

- Determine whether the asset's complexity and materiality warrant an auditor-employed specialist.
- Evaluate the specialist's qualifications, independence, and methodology.
- Assess whether management's specialist is independent and competent, and corroborate where needed.
- Compare the auditor's specialist conclusion to management's estimate and investigate differences.
- Document the specialist's findings and how they were used in reaching the audit conclusion.
- Confirm the specialist used the same measurement date and framework as the entity.
- Evaluate whether the specialist had access to all relevant data, including proprietary or non-public inputs.

### Test Impairment Indicators and Triggers

Impairment is often a matter of timing: an asset that should have been written down earlier is the most common finding. The auditor must actively look for triggers rather than wait for management to identify them. A passive trigger review lets overstatement persist.

- Review internal indicators: obsolescence, physical damage, idle assets, declining performance.
- Review external indicators: market decline, technological change, regulatory shifts, interest rate moves.
- For goodwill, assess whether events or changes indicate the reporting unit's fair value is below carrying amount.
- For inventory, identify slow-moving, obsolete, or damaged items through aging and turnover analysis.
- For long-lived assets, test whether undiscounted cash flows exceed carrying amount when triggers exist.
- Investigate assets that have been impaired in prior periods for reversal indicators where permitted.
- Consider whether declining segment performance or restructuring signals impairment.
- Review post-year-end events for evidence of impairment that existed at the reporting date, such as asset sales below carrying amount.

### Assess Useful Lives and Depreciation

Useful life estimates drive depreciation expense and carrying amounts, and they are easily left unchanged long after circumstances have shifted. A review of useful lives is a valuation procedure, not a disclosure formality. Shortened lives accelerate expense; inappropriately long lives defer it and overstate assets.

- Review the useful life policy for each asset class for reasonableness and consistency.
- Test whether residual values are appropriate or should be zero given expected usage.
- Confirm the depreciation method reflects the pattern of economic benefit consumption.
- Identify assets still in service beyond their estimated useful life and assess remaining life.
- For intangibles with finite lives, test the remaining life against the period of expected benefit.
- Assess whether component accounting is applied to significant parts with different lives.
- Recalculate depreciation on a sample of additions and disposals for accuracy.
- Confirm that fully depreciated assets still in use are still controlled and not written off prematurely to reduce asset counts.

### Evaluate Estimation Uncertainty and Disclosure

Valuation estimates carry uncertainty that must be communicated to users. The auditor must confirm that disclosure of key assumptions, sensitivity, and level classification is adequate and consistent with the testing performed. Disclosure gaps are a common deficiency even when the numbers are right.

- Confirm the fair value hierarchy level is disclosed for each relevant asset class.
- Test that key assumptions and estimation uncertainty are disclosed for significant estimates.
- Verify sensitivity disclosures for highly uncertain estimates such as goodwill and Level 3 instruments.
- Confirm transfers between hierarchy levels are disclosed with the reasons for the transfer.
- Check that impairment losses recognized and reversal of prior impairments are disclosed.
- Confirm that methods and significant unobservable inputs are described for Level 3 measurements.
- Assess whether disclosure is consistent with the conclusions reached in the audit workpapers.
- Verify that the carrying amount of each asset class is reconciled to the fixed asset or investment ledger.

### Perform Retrospective Review for Bias

Comparing prior-year estimates to actual outcomes is a powerful bias detector. Consistent optimism in one direction signals management bias that must be addressed in the current year's estimates. Retrospective review is not optional; it is a primary procedure for evaluating estimation risk.

- Compare prior-year estimates to current-year actuals for warranty, bad debt, and impairment.
- Identify a pattern of conservative or aggressive estimation and assess its implications.
- Use the retrospective review to inform the current year's risk assessment and testing depth.
- Discuss identified bias with management and assess the response.
- Document the retrospective review and its effect on the current year's conclusions.
- Consider whether the cumulative effect of multiple small assumption changes materially altered the valuation.

### Evaluate Write-Downs and Reversals

Once an asset is written down, the new carrying amount becomes the cost basis, and subsequent recoveries are governed by framework-specific reversal rules that the auditor must apply carefully. US GAAP generally prohibits reversing inventory and long-lived asset write-downs, while IFRS permits reversal of inventory write-downs and certain impairment losses (excluding goodwill), and applying the wrong rule either hides recovery or improperly inflates assets. The auditor must confirm that any reversal is supported by evidence of a genuine change in circumstances, not by a desire to smooth earnings.

- Confirm whether the governing framework permits reversal of the specific write-down.
- Test that any reversal does not exceed the carrying amount that would have existed absent the original write-down.
- Verify that reversal evidence reflects conditions that arose after the original impairment, not a correction of an overstatement.
- For inventory, confirm that written-down items are restored only up to the new NRV and not above original cost.
- For goodwill, confirm that no reversal is recognized even when conditions improve.
- Assess whether a pattern of large write-downs followed by reversals signals earnings management.
- Reconcile recognized reversals to the ledger and confirm they are disclosed consistently with the testing.
- Assess whether assets held for sale are measured at the lower of carrying amount and fair value less costs to sell.
- Confirm that assets reclassified as held for sale cease to be depreciated from the reclassification date.
- Evaluate whether the recoverable amount of a cash-generating unit is supported by recent and credible cash flow forecasts.
- Confirm that impairment losses allocated to individual assets within a cash-generating unit are applied pro rata unless a rational basis supports another method.

## Common Traps

### Accepting the Model Without Testing the Inputs

A correctly calculated model fed by untested assumptions produces a precise but unreliable number. The trap is the aura of precision that a spreadsheet generates, which makes the inputs feel validated when they were never examined. Always trace back to the source assumptions and market data.

### Relying on Management's Specialist Without Independence Assessment

A valuation prepared by a specialist with ties to management or incentive to support a target is not independent evidence. The trap is that the specialist's report looks authoritative. Evaluate the specialist's independence and competence, and engage the auditor's own specialist where warranted.

### Treating Impairment as Management's Call

Management may delay recognizing impairment to avoid a charge, and a passive auditor enables that delay. The trap is accepting management's assertion that no triggers exist without independent review. Actively search for triggers in both internal and external evidence.

### Ignoring the Difference Between Impairment Frameworks

IFS reverses certain impairments when conditions improve; US GAAP generally does not. Applying the wrong reversal rule produces a material error that looks procedurally correct. Confirm the governing framework before concluding on reversal.

### Using Stale or Inactive Market Data

Broker quotes from inactive markets or prices from a different date can materially misstate fair value. The trap is that a quote is a quote, regardless of market conditions. Assess market activity and the appropriateness of any adjustments.

### Overlooking Useful Life Creep

Extending useful lives or residual values is a subtle way to reduce expense and overstate assets. The trap is that small changes look immaterial individually but compound across the asset base. Review changes in life estimates for justification and consistency.

### Under-Disclosing Estimation Uncertainty

Failing to disclose key assumptions and sensitivity leaves users unaware of how much the valuation depends on judgment. The trap is treating disclosure as boilerplate. Tie disclosure testing to the specific assumptions that drove the valuation.

### Skipping the Retrospective Review

Failing to compare last year's estimates to this year's actuals forfeits the strongest evidence of bias. The trap is treating each year's estimate in isolation. Make the retrospective comparison a standing procedure.

## Self-Check

- Did you confirm the governing framework and the prescribed valuation model before testing?
- Were management's key assumptions challenged against historical, market, and industry evidence?
- For fair value measurements, were inputs classified by level and tested with depth matching the level?
- Was an auditor specialist used where complexity or materiality warranted, and was the specialist's work evaluated?
- Were impairment triggers actively sought from both internal and external evidence, not merely accepted from management?
- Were useful lives, residual values, and depreciation methods tested for reasonableness and consistency?
- For inventory, was net realizable value tested against current selling prices and costs to complete?
- Was a retrospective review of prior estimates performed and used to assess management bias?
- Are disclosure of key assumptions, estimation uncertainty, hierarchy levels, and sensitivity consistent with the testing performed?
- Were highly sensitive assumptions subjected to sensitivity analysis?; is the valuation conclusion documented with the evidence supporting each material assumption?
