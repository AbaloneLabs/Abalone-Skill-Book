# Recommendations Drafting

A finding without a recommendation is a complaint; a recommendation without a root cause is a guess. The recommendation is where the audit's analysis turns into something the entity can act on, and it is also where the auditor's credibility is most exposed. A recommendation that addresses the symptom rather than the cause will be implemented and the problem will recur. A recommendation so vague that management can claim compliance by drafting a policy fixes nothing. A recommendation so prescriptive that it dictates management's business decisions oversteps the auditor's role and invites rejection. Drafting good recommendations is the discipline of connecting the corrective action to the documented cause, making it specific enough to be implemented and verified, and framing it as guidance rather than command, so that ownership remains with management.

Use this skill when drafting audit recommendations, tying corrective actions to root causes, specifying scope and ownership, or framing recommendations that are constructive, actionable, and appropriately bounded. The goal is recommendations that, if implemented, would actually reduce the risk the finding identified, and that management can realistically execute and the auditor can later verify.

## Core Rules

### Tie The Recommendation To The Documented Root Cause

The most important property of a recommendation is that it addresses the cause of the finding, not merely its symptom. A recommendation that patches the observed condition while leaving the cause untouched guarantees recurrence.

Tie to cause by:

- restating the cause identified in the finding, such as a design flaw, missing monitoring, or resource gap;
- explaining how the recommended action addresses that specific cause;
- checking that the action would prevent recurrence, not only detect it after the fact;
- avoiding recommendations that address a different, more convenient problem than the finding described.

If the cause was a control environment weakness, a recommendation to add a single control does not address it. Match the breadth of the recommendation to the breadth of the cause.

### Make Recommendations Specific And Verifiable

A recommendation must be specific enough that management knows what to do and the auditor can later verify whether it was done. Vague recommendations, such as "improve controls" or "enhance monitoring," cannot be implemented meaningfully and cannot be verified.

Make recommendations specific by:

- stating the action expected, such as implementing a reconciliation, configuring a system rule, or assigning ownership;
- defining the scope, which processes, populations, or systems the action covers;
- specifying, where relevant, the frequency, threshold, or trigger for the action;
- identifying a suggested owner or role responsible for implementation;
- proposing a realistic timeframe keyed to the risk and complexity.

Specific does not mean prescriptive. State what outcome the action should achieve and the nature of the action, while leaving the detailed design to management unless the auditor's expertise adds value.

### Address The Full Affected Population, Not Only The Sample

Findings often arise from a sample, but the recommendation must address the whole population at risk, not only the items that triggered the finding. A recommendation confined to the sample leaves the rest of the population exposed.

Ensure population coverage by:

- stating that the corrective action should apply across the affected process, location, or system;
- confirming the recommendation covers the period going forward, and any remediation of prior-period items where relevant;
- avoiding language that could be read as fixing only the specific exceptions found.

A recommendation to "correct the three exceptions identified" is weaker than one to "implement the control across the population and correct all affected items."

### Frame Recommendations As Guidance, Not Commands

The auditor recommends; management decides and implements. Recommendations that read as directives overstep the auditor's role, especially in internal audit where the auditor advises governance rather than managing the entity. Overstepping invites rejection and blurs accountability.

Frame appropriately by:

- using recommendation language, such as "we recommend," rather than imperative commands;
- presenting the recommendation as a means to reduce the identified risk, with the rationale;
- acknowledging that management may choose an alternative approach that achieves the same objective;
- leaving the detailed design and resourcing decisions to management;
- not taking on implementation responsibility, which would impair independence for future assurance.

Management owns the decision and the implementation. The auditor owns the analysis and the recommendation's connection to the risk.

### Calibrate Recommendation Strength To Risk

Not every finding warrants an equally forceful recommendation. The strength and urgency of the recommendation should match the risk of the finding, so that attention and resources are directed where they matter most.

Calibrate by:

- making high-risk findings, such as fraud, override, or material weakness, the subject of strong, time-bound recommendations;
- allowing medium-risk findings to carry recommendations with reasonable but less urgent timeframes;
- using observations or suggestions for lower-risk matters, clearly distinguished from formal recommendations;
- not inflating minor matters with heavyweight recommendations, which dilutes attention to the significant ones.

A management letter where every item is "urgent" trains readers to ignore urgency. Reserve force for the matters that warrant it.

### Ensure Recommendations Are Realistic And Implementable

A recommendation that the entity cannot realistically implement, due to cost, capability, system constraints, or authority, will be ignored or nominally adopted, and the finding will remain unremediated. Recommendations should be ambitious enough to address the risk but grounded in what is feasible.

Ensure realism by:

- considering the cost and effort relative to the risk being addressed;
- accounting for system, resourcing, or authority constraints identified during the audit;
- discussing feasibility with management during the engagement, without surrendering the recommendation's objective;
- offering, where appropriate, a range of options that would achieve the risk-reduction objective.

A recommendation that ignores feasibility is easily dismissed. One that acknowledges constraints while holding to the objective is harder to reject and more likely to result in real remediation.

### Present The Risk Reduction The Recommendation Achieves

A recommendation should make clear what risk it reduces, so management and governance can weigh the action against the benefit. A recommendation presented without its rationale reads as an arbitrary demand.

Present the rationale by:

- stating the risk or exposure the finding creates;
- explaining how the recommended action reduces that risk;
- noting any residual risk that would remain even after implementation;
- distinguishing preventive from detective effects.

This lets governance judge whether the action is worth the cost and gives management a basis for prioritization.

## Common Traps

### Addressing The Symptom, Not The Cause

A recommendation that patches the observed condition while leaving the cause untouched guarantees the problem recurs. Tie the action to the documented cause.

### Vague Recommendations That Cannot Be Verified

"Improve controls" or "enhance monitoring" cannot be implemented or verified. Be specific about the action, scope, owner, and timeframe.

### Fixing Only The Sample Items

A recommendation confined to the exceptions found leaves the population exposed. Address the full affected population.

### Overstepping Into Management's Decisions

Dictating detailed design or taking implementation responsibility oversteps the auditor's role and impairs independence. Recommend; let management decide and implement.

### Uniform Urgency That Dilutes Attention

If every recommendation is urgent, none is. Calibrate strength and timeframe to risk.

### Ignoring Feasibility

A recommendation the entity cannot implement will be dismissed or nominally adopted. Account for cost, capability, and constraints.

### Recommendations Without Stated Risk Reduction

A recommendation without its rationale reads as arbitrary. Explain what risk the action reduces.

### Conflicting With Other Recommendations Or Findings

Recommendations that contradict each other or that conflict with findings elsewhere confuse management. Reconcile the set before issuance.

## Self-Check

- Does each recommendation address the documented root cause, and would implementing it prevent recurrence rather than only detect it?
- Is each recommendation specific enough to be implemented and verified, with action, scope, owner, and timeframe?
- Does the recommendation cover the full affected population and forward-looking remediation, not only the sample items?
- Is the recommendation framed as guidance, leaving design and implementation decisions to management without overstepping?
- Is the strength and urgency calibrated to the risk, with high-risk matters carrying forceful, time-bound recommendations?
- Is the recommendation realistic given cost, capability, system, and authority constraints, with options offered where appropriate?
- Does the recommendation state the risk it reduces and any residual risk remaining, so governance can weigh action against benefit?
- Are the recommendations reconciled with each other and with the findings, free of contradiction?
- Are observations and suggestions distinguished from formal recommendations so attention focuses on what matters?
