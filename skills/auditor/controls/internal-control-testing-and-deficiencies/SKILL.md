# Internal Control Testing And Deficiencies

Internal control testing asks whether controls are designed to prevent or detect important problems and whether they operated as intended. A control can exist on paper and still fail. A control can operate sometimes and still be unreliable. A control deficiency can be minor, significant, or severe depending on likelihood, magnitude, exposure, and compensating controls. Agents often miss this by listing controls without testing whether they address the risk.

Use this skill before reviewing control design, testing control operation, assessing segregation of duties, evaluating access controls, classifying deficiencies, or writing control findings. The goal is to force a risk-control-evidence chain rather than a generic "controls look adequate" conclusion.

## Core Rules

### Start With The Risk The Control Addresses

A control is meaningful only in relation to a risk. Do not evaluate a control in isolation.

For each control, identify:

- risk or assertion addressed;
- process step;
- control objective;
- owner;
- frequency;
- population covered;
- evidence produced;
- system or manual component;
- preventive or detective nature;
- dependency on other controls;
- failure consequence.

If no one can explain what risk the control addresses, the control may be unnecessary, incomplete, or misunderstood.

### Distinguish Design Effectiveness From Operating Effectiveness

Design effectiveness asks whether the control, if performed as described, would prevent or detect the risk. Operating effectiveness asks whether the control actually operated consistently and by a competent person during the period.

For design, ask:

- Is the control precise enough?
- Does it cover the whole population?
- Is the owner independent enough?
- Is the control timely?
- Does it use reliable information?
- Does it define exception follow-up?
- Does it leave evidence?

For operation, test:

- control occurred;
- timing matched required frequency;
- reviewer had appropriate authority and competence;
- evidence shows actual review;
- exceptions were investigated;
- follow-up was completed;
- control operated throughout the period.

Do not conclude a control is effective just because it is documented in a policy.

### Identify Key Controls And Dependencies

Not all controls deserve the same testing. Key controls address significant risks, important assertions, compliance requirements, or fraud pathways. Dependencies can make a control fail even when the reviewer signs off.

Identify controls over:

- approvals;
- reconciliations;
- segregation of duties;
- system access;
- change management;
- data interfaces;
- report generation;
- journal entries;
- vendor setup;
- payroll;
- revenue recognition;
- estimates;
- close process;
- physical assets;
- compliance reporting.

Also test information used in the control. If a reconciliation relies on a system report, the report's completeness and accuracy may need testing.

### Test Precision And Review Quality

Review controls often fail because the reviewer signs a report without investigating anomalies. A signature alone is not evidence of an effective review.

Evaluate:

- what the reviewer compared;
- thresholds used;
- variance explanations required;
- source data reviewed;
- evidence of challenge;
- exception log;
- follow-up actions;
- reviewer competence;
- time spent if relevant;
- whether review occurred before the risk materialized.

A control that says "manager reviews report" is weak unless the review criteria and response to exceptions are clear.

### Evaluate Segregation Of Duties And Access

Access can override process controls. A person who can create vendors, approve invoices, release payments, and reconcile the bank can conceal errors or fraud.

Review incompatible duties for:

- vendor creation and payment approval;
- payroll setup and payroll release;
- sales order entry and credit approval;
- inventory custody and inventory records;
- journal entry preparation and approval;
- user access administration and transaction posting;
- cash handling and bank reconciliation;
- system development and production deployment.

If perfect segregation is impossible, identify compensating controls such as independent review, bank alerts, owner review, dual approval, exception reporting, or external oversight.

### Assess Control Deficiency Severity

A deficiency exists when a control is missing, poorly designed, not operating, not documented, or not precise enough to prevent or detect the risk. Severity depends on likelihood and magnitude, not only whether an error was found.

Assess:

- what could go wrong;
- likelihood of failure;
- maximum potential impact;
- actual exceptions found;
- affected accounts or obligations;
- fraud exposure;
- duration of failure;
- frequency of control;
- compensating controls;
- remediation status;
- recurrence of prior issues.

A control deficiency can be serious even if no misstatement has yet been detected.

### Consider Fraud, Override, And Sensitive Areas

Controls should be tested more skeptically where management override, collusion, incentives, or sensitive assets exist.

High-risk areas include:

- manual journal entries;
- estimates and reserves;
- revenue cutoff;
- side agreements;
- related-party transactions;
- vendor bank changes;
- refunds and credits;
- payroll changes;
- inventory adjustments;
- executive expenses;
- system administrator access;
- late period adjustments.

If management can override the control without detection, the control may not address the risk.

### Track Remediation To Verified Operation

Management may propose remediation, but remediation is not complete until the redesigned control is implemented and evidence shows it operated for an appropriate period.

Track:

- root cause;
- corrective action;
- control owner;
- implementation date;
- revised procedure;
- evidence produced;
- training or access change;
- testing period;
- retest result;
- residual risk.

Do not close a control finding because management promised a future change.

## Common Traps

### Equating A Policy With A Control

A policy describes what should happen. A control is an activity that prevents, detects, or corrects a risk and leaves evidence. Policies need operating controls behind them.

### Treating Sign-Off As Sufficient Evidence

A sign-off proves someone signed. It does not prove the reviewer compared the right data, investigated exceptions, or understood the risk. Review the underlying evidence.

### Ignoring Information Produced By The Entity

Controls often rely on reports. If the report is incomplete or inaccurate, the control may fail. Test key report logic, parameters, and reconciliation to source data.

### Overlooking User Access And Change Management

Transaction controls can be bypassed by excessive access or untested system changes. Access and change management are often root causes of downstream errors.

### Downgrading Deficiencies Because No Error Was Found

The absence of a detected error does not prove the control is effective. Severity considers what could happen if the control fails.

### Double Counting Compensating Controls

A compensating control must be precise, timely, reliable, and independent enough to reduce risk. A vague management review is not automatically compensating.

### Closing Findings On Management Assertion

A remediation statement is not evidence. Retest the redesigned control and document operation before closing.

## Self-Check

- Is each control linked to a specific risk, assertion, process step, owner, frequency, evidence, and failure consequence?
- Are design effectiveness and operating effectiveness evaluated separately?
- Have key controls and control dependencies been identified based on risk and significance?
- Are review controls tested for precision, challenge, exception follow-up, and reliable source data?
- Are segregation of duties, access rights, administrator privileges, and change management considered?
- Is deficiency severity assessed using likelihood, magnitude, fraud exposure, duration, frequency, and compensating controls?
- Are management override, collusion, related parties, late adjustments, and sensitive transactions considered?
- Does each deficiency identify root cause and effect rather than only describing a missing document?
- Is remediation tracked through implementation and retesting, not just management promise?
- Are control conclusions supported by evidence that the control operated as designed throughout the relevant period?
