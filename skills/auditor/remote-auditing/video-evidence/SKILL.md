# Video Evidence

Video can be powerful audit evidence, but it is uniquely easy to misread. A recording compresses time, hides what is outside the frame, and depends entirely on who aimed the lens and when. Agents tend to over-trust video because it looks concrete and objective: a warehouse full of boxes, a meter reading, a sealed container all appear self-evidently real. Yet video proves only what was captured, in the direction it was captured, during the window it ran. It cannot establish what was just out of frame, whether items were staged before the recording, whether the timestamp is accurate, or whether the operator omitted an area. Treating a video as equivalent to physical observation silently inflates its evidentiary value.

Use this skill when video is the primary or supporting evidence for an observation procedure, when inventory or assets are being observed by live stream or recording, or when evaluating whether video is reliable enough for the conclusion. The goal is to extract the real evidentiary value of video, corroborate its weak points, and avoid asserting more than the recording can support.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Live Video From Recorded Video

Live and recorded video carry different evidentiary characteristics, and the auditor must treat them differently. Live video allows the auditor to direct the operator, request repositioning, and verify surroundings in real time, but it cannot be re-examined later unless recorded. Recorded video can be reviewed repeatedly but offers no opportunity to redirect, and its provenance must be established.

Decide and document for each use:

- whether the feed is live or recorded, and how that was confirmed;
- for live video, whether the session was recorded and by whom;
- for recorded video, when and by whom it was captured;
- whether the auditor could direct the camera in real time;
- whether the recording is continuous or edited into segments;
- whether gaps, cuts, or loops are detectable.

Recorded video without provenance is weaker than live observation. Live video that is not recorded cannot be re-reviewed and depends on contemporaneous documentation.

### Verify Authenticity, Time, And Place

A video's value collapses if its origin, timestamp, or location cannot be trusted. Staging, relabeling, and reusing footage from another period or site are real risks, especially for high-value or geographically dispersed assets.

Corroborate authenticity and context by:

- requiring visible date, time, and location markers in frame where possible;
- cross-checking the recording against an independent clock or system log;
- matching visible features to floor plans, coordinates, or prior visits;
- requesting a live re-verification of a sample of items shown in a recording;
- confirming the device used and whether its clock was synchronized;
- checking for metadata such as creation time, device ID, and GPS;
- comparing footage across multiple cameras or angles where available.

If time and place cannot be independently corroborated, the video should be treated as unverified and supported by other procedures.

### Control Who Operates The Camera

The operator controls the evidence. A client employee who selects the angle, the route, and the items shown is effectively curating the observation. Independence of observation requires independence of control, or at least documented direction by the auditor.

Establish and document:

- who operated the camera and their role and relationship to the asset;
- whether the auditor directed movement, zoom, and framing;
- whether the operator could omit or stage areas without detection;
- whether a second person or independent party was present;
- whether the auditor could request unscheduled or random re-panning;
- the basis for trusting the operator if they are not independent.

Where the operator is not independent, the video is closer to management representation and must be corroborated.

### Recognize The Limits Of Video For Inventory Observation

Video is a partial substitute for physical inventory observation. It cannot confirm quantity with the same reliability as a count, cannot verify condition or obsolescence reliably, and cannot detect items staged behind or beneath what is visible. It is especially weak for high-value, small, or liquid assets.

Account for these limits by:

- reconciling observed items to perpetual records and prior counts;
- requesting counts be performed on camera by independent staff;
- selecting sample locations for closer inspection or zoom;
- testing condition through specific, directed requests rather than overview shots;
- corroborating with third-party warehouse receipts or custodian confirmations;
- extending testing where video cannot verify quantity or condition;
- considering whether drone footage adds coverage or merely breadth.

Coverage of many locations on video is not the same as reliable observation of each location. Breadth does not equal sufficiency.

### Require Corroboration For Significant Video Evidence

Video should rarely stand alone for a material conclusion. Its weaknesses, framing, staging, and provenance, are best addressed by independent evidence that either confirms or challenges what was shown.

Pair video with:

- physical or system counts reconciled to the recording;
- third-party confirmations from custodians or warehouse operators;
- system logs, scanner reads, or RFID data tied to the same window;
- prior-period observations showing continuity;
- analytical procedures on related balances or movements;
- reperformance of counts on a sample of items;
- management representations where appropriate, clearly labeled as such.

The more material or fraud-sensitive the area, the stronger and more independent the corroboration must be.

### Manage Recording Consent And Privacy

Recording people, premises, and proprietary operations raises legal and ethical obligations that vary by jurisdiction. Failing to obtain consent or violating privacy rules can invalidate the evidence and create liability.

Before recording, confirm:

- applicable consent and notification requirements for each jurisdiction;
- whether signs, verbal notice, or written consent are required;
- whether employees, contractors, or the public may appear on camera;
- restrictions on recording proprietary processes, screens, or security controls;
- data protection rules for any personal data captured in the recording;
- the entity's own policies on recording in its facilities;
- how recordings will be stored, who may access them, and retention limits.

Document consent and the legal basis for recording. When in doubt, record only what is necessary and redact or restrict access to sensitive portions.

### Establish Chain Of Custody For Video Files

A video file is evidence, and like any evidence it must be protected from alteration, loss, or substitution. Files are easily edited, re-encoded, or overwritten, and metadata can be stripped or faked.

Maintain chain of custody by:

- capturing or transferring the file to a controlled repository promptly;
- recording who created, received, stored, and accessed the file;
- preserving original metadata and avoiding re-encoding where possible;
- using hashes or checksums to detect later modification;
- storing working copies separately from originals;
- restricting edit and delete permissions to named individuals;
- documenting any processing, redaction, or format conversion applied.

A video whose custody cannot be traced is vulnerable to challenge and should be treated cautiously.

### Document What The Video Proves And Does Not Prove

The workpaper should state precisely what assertion the video supports and acknowledge its limits. Overstating what a recording demonstrates is a common and serious documentation failure.

For each video used as evidence, record:

- the assertion or procedure it supports;
- the location, date, time, and participants;
- who operated the camera and the auditor's degree of direction;
- what was visible and what was not;
- corroborating evidence obtained;
- known limitations, such as framing, condition, or quantity;
- the conclusion drawn and the basis for it.

A reviewer should be able to understand the evidentiary weight of the video without watching it, and locate and view it if needed.

## Common Traps

### Treating Video As Equivalent To Physical Observation

Video is a representation, not presence. It cannot verify condition, detect staging, or confirm quantity as reliably as a directed physical count, and overstating its equivalence inflates the conclusion.

### Trusting The Timestamp Without Corroboration

Device clocks drift, can be changed, and can be absent from the frame. A timestamp printed on a video is not proof of when it was recorded unless independently corroborated.

### Letting The Client Curate The Frame

When the client chooses the route and framing, the auditor observes what the client chose to show. Without the ability to redirect or verify surroundings, the video is management representation.

### Confusing Coverage With Sufficiency

Drone or multi-site footage that covers many locations looks thorough but may verify little about each location. Breadth of coverage does not establish that the evidence supports the assertion.

### Ignoring What Is Outside The Frame

Staged items, omitted aisles, and obscured conditions are invisible in a recording. The absence of evidence in the video is not evidence of absence in reality.

### Failing To Preserve The Original File

Re-encoding, renaming, or editing a video can destroy metadata and create doubts about authenticity. Originals must be preserved with a documented chain of custody.

### Recording Without Consent Or Legal Clearance

Recording people or proprietary operations without proper notice or consent can breach privacy law, invalidate the evidence, and damage the engagement relationship.

### Relying On A Single Uncorroborated Recording

A single recording, especially of a material or fraud-sensitive area, is weak on its own. Significant video evidence should be paired with independent corroboration.

## Self-Check

- Is each piece of video evidence classified as live or recorded, with the basis for that classification documented?
- Have time, place, and authenticity been corroborated independently, rather than relying on an on-screen timestamp alone?
- Is it documented who operated the camera, their independence, and the degree of direction the auditor exercised?
- Are the known limits of video for inventory or asset observation, such as quantity, condition, and staging, acknowledged and addressed with corroboration?
- Has significant video evidence been paired with independent corroboration such as counts, confirmations, or system logs?
- Were recording consent, privacy, and legal requirements identified and documented before recording occurred?
- Is there a documented chain of custody for each video file, including creation, transfer, storage, access, and any processing?
- Does the workpaper state precisely what assertion the video supports and what it does not prove?
- Could an experienced reviewer understand the evidentiary weight of the video and locate the original file from the documentation?
- Does the conclusion reflect the actual reliability of the video rather than overstating what the recording demonstrates?
