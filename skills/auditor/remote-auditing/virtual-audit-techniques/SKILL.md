# Virtual Audit Techniques

Remote auditing expands where work can be performed, but it does not relax the standard for sufficient appropriate evidence. The danger is that a video walkthrough, a shared screen, or a virtual data room feels like a site visit while quietly narrowing what the auditor can actually observe, test, and corroborate. The camera only shows what the operator chooses to point at, the data room only contains what the client chose to upload, and a remote interview removes the informal cues and spontaneous access that physical presence provides. Agents miss these issues because the digital artifacts look complete and professional, and because the convenience of remote work discourages the friction of insisting on additional procedures.

Use this skill when planning a remote or hybrid engagement, when substituting virtual walkthroughs, video observation, or screen-share for physical procedures, or when deciding whether a remote method is adequate or whether on-site work is required. The goal is to obtain persuasive evidence while recognizing the structural limits of remote techniques and preserving professional skepticism.

## Core Rules

### Decide Remote Versus On-Site Based On Risk, Not Convenience

Remote methods are a technique, not a default. Whether they are adequate depends on the assertions to be tested, the assessed risk, the nature of the assets or controls, and the reliability of the entity's systems and people.

Assess whether remote is sufficient by considering:

- inherent and fraud risk of the area;
- materiality or significance of the balance, process, or control;
- whether the evidence can be fully tested without physical access;
- the complexity and uniqueness of the operations or assets;
- the strength of the entity's IT general controls and access security;
- prior-year exceptions or known deficiencies;
- regulatory or standard-specific on-site requirements;
- whether corroborating procedures can close the remote evidence gap.

High-risk areas involving physical assets, complex operations, weak controls, or heightened fraud risk often require physical presence. Document the decision and its rationale either way.

### Compensate For The Loss Of Physical Presence

Physical presence provides incidental evidence that remote work removes: the auditor sees the building, the warehouse, the staff, the culture of compliance, and can walk to an unplanned location. Remote techniques must deliberately recreate some of that coverage.

Build compensating procedures such as:

- randomized or unannounced remote observation windows;
- requests to re-pan or reposition the camera to verify surroundings;
- cross-checking live video against floor plans, asset registers, or system data;
- confirming counts against perpetual records and prior observations;
- obtaining independent confirmation from third parties;
- extending analytical or substantive procedures;
- performing dual procedures where one remote method corroborates another.

Do not treat a guided tour as observation. Observation implies the auditor controls what is examined.

### Control The Remote Observation, Not Just Receive It

The credibility of a virtual walkthrough depends on who controls the camera, what is shown, and whether the auditor can direct attention. A client-operated, client-framed feed is a presentation, not independent observation.

Establish control by:

- having the auditor direct camera movement, zoom, and repositioning;
- requesting real-time display of identifying markers, dates, or system screens;
- using the entity's own timestamps, scanner reads, or RFID output to corroborate;
- observing at random times rather than scheduled showcase moments;
- requiring the operator to show context beyond the immediate subject;
- recording or capturing the session per policy and consent rules;
- documenting who operated the device and their relationship to the process.

If the auditor cannot direct the observation, the evidence should be treated as management representation and corroborated independently.

### Design Virtual Walkthroughs To Test Process, Not Just Describe It

A walkthrough that merely confirms the documented process adds little. A useful virtual walkthrough tests whether the process actually operates as described, including handoffs, approvals, and exceptions.

For each virtual walkthrough, capture:

- the process step being observed;
- the person performing it and their role;
- the system or record in use, shown on screen or in view;
- the control activity and who performs it;
- evidence of approval, segregation, or reconciliation;
- how exceptions are handled in practice;
- discrepancies between the described and observed process;
- the date, time, and participants of the session.

Walkthroughs are a starting point for testing, not a substitute for it. Identify where additional procedures are needed.

### Conduct Remote Interviews With Skepticism And Structure

Remote interviews remove body language, shared documents, and the ability to point at a screen together. They also make it easier for the interviewee to consult others off-camera or read from a script.

Strengthen remote interviews by:

- confirming the identity and role of each participant;
- stating that the session is for audit purposes and how it will be used;
- asking open questions before sharing what is already known;
- requesting spontaneous demonstration of a task, not a description;
- probing specific transactions rather than general policy;
- noting when answers appear rehearsed, evasive, or inconsistent;
- corroborating significant statements with documents or system evidence;
- documenting who said what, with dates and cross-references.

Inquiry alone is rarely sufficient. Treat interview content as one input that must be supported by other evidence.

### Govern Screen-Sharing And System Access As Evidence

Screen-sharing lets the auditor see the entity's systems, but the view is ephemeral and easy to manipulate. A live screen is stronger than a screenshot, and direct read-only access is stronger than a shared screen.

When using screen-share or remote access:

- confirm the system, environment, and period being shown;
- have the auditor navigate rather than only watch the client navigate;
- capture the session per policy and document key screens;
- reconcile what is shown to reports, ledgers, or exports;
- verify the user identity, role, and permissions of the logged-in account;
- confirm whether the environment is production or a copy;
- note any inability to access areas, periods, or records.

Do not rely on a single screen-share as proof of a population, a control, or a balance. Tie what is observed to documented evidence.

### Rely On Data Rooms With Discipline

A virtual data room centralizes documents, but its completeness and organization are controlled by the client. The existence of a folder is not evidence that the folder is complete or authoritative.

When relying on a data room:

- request an index and reconcile it to the audit's evidence needs;
- note upload dates, versions, and watermarks;
- confirm whether documents are originals, copies, or redacted;
- track which documents were reviewed, by whom, and when;
- flag documents that are locked, expired, or placeholder;
- reconcile data room contents to source systems where possible;
- preserve access logs showing what the auditor accessed.

A well-stocked data room can still omit the one document that matters. Maintain an open-item list tied to specific procedures.

### Protect Confidentiality And Security Of Remote Sessions

Remote work spreads evidence across platforms, devices, and networks that the audit firm does not fully control. Each channel is a confidentiality and integrity risk.

Apply controls such as:

- using firm-approved, encrypted conferencing and file-transfer tools;
- verifying participant identity before sharing sensitive content;
- avoiding recording without documented consent and legal clearance;
- restricting downloads and exports of sensitive data;
- storing evidence in controlled repositories, not local drives or chats;
- redacting personal or regulated data where full detail is unnecessary;
- following data residency and privacy rules for each jurisdiction;
- terminating access promptly when team members rotate off the engagement.

A confidentiality breach in a remote session can compromise the engagement and the entity.

### Recognize When Remote Methods Are Insufficient

Some procedures cannot be replicated remotely without unacceptable loss of reliability. The auditor must be willing to conclude that on-site work is required rather than rationalize a weaker substitute.

On-site is typically required when:

- physical assets are material and cannot be reliably observed remotely;
- fraud risk is elevated and physical presence is a deterrent or detector;
- controls depend on physical segregation, custody, or safeguards;
- the entity's systems or connectivity are unreliable;
- remote observation has produced inconsistent or unverifiable results;
- regulators, standards, or the engagement letter mandate presence;
- the area is complex, unique, or new with no prior baseline.

Document the trigger that made on-site work necessary and the procedures performed there.

## Common Traps

### Treating A Guided Video Tour As Independent Observation

When the client chooses the route, the framing, and the timing, the auditor sees a curated view. Observation requires the ability to direct attention and verify surroundings; a guided tour is closer to a management representation.

### Assuming The Data Room Is Complete

A data room reflects what the client uploaded. Missing documents, redactions, and locked files are common. Reconcile the index to the audit's evidence needs and track gaps as open items.

### Letting Convenience Drive The Remote Decision

Remote work is cheaper and faster, which creates subtle pressure to declare it sufficient. The decision should follow risk and evidence needs, not travel budget or scheduling ease.

### Accepting A Single Screen-Share As Proof

A live screen proves only what was shown in that moment. Without reconciliation to reports, exports, or the ledger, a screen-share is weak evidence for populations, controls, or balances.

### Losing Skepticism Because The Medium Feels Professional

Polished video, structured interviews, and organized data rooms can create a false sense of thoroughness. Professional presentation is not a substitute for tested, corroborated evidence.

### Failing To Document Remote Procedures Adequately

Because remote work is fast and ephemeral, auditors often fail to capture what was observed, who controlled the camera, and how the evidence was obtained. A session that is not documented cannot be reviewed or relied upon.

### Overlooking Confidentiality Risks Of Remote Channels

Personal devices, consumer messaging apps, and unencrypted transfers can expose sensitive data. Using non-approved tools, even once, can create a breach and an independence or compliance problem.

### Declaring An Area Complete Based On Coverage Rather Than Evidence

Showing every location on video is not the same as obtaining sufficient appropriate evidence for each assertion. Coverage is about breadth; sufficiency is about whether the evidence supports the conclusion.

## Self-Check

- Was the remote-versus-on-site decision based on assessed risk, materiality, and evidence needs rather than convenience, with the rationale documented?
- Did the auditor control or direct the observation, rather than only receiving a client-framed view, and is that control documented?
- Are virtual walkthroughs testing actual operation of the process, with discrepancies between described and observed activity captured?
- Are remote interviews corroborated by documents or system evidence rather than treated as standalone proof?
- Has each screen-share or remote access session been reconciled to reports, ledgers, or exports, with user identity and environment confirmed?
- Has the data room index been reconciled to the audit's evidence needs, with upload dates, versions, redactions, and gaps tracked?
- Are confidentiality and security controls applied to all remote channels, including approved tools, access verification, and controlled storage?
- Has the auditor identified and documented any area where remote methods are insufficient and on-site work is required?
- Does the documentation allow an experienced auditor to understand what was observed remotely, who controlled it, and how the conclusion is supported?
- Does the conclusion reflect the actual reliability of remote evidence, with corroborating procedures where the remote method alone was weak?
