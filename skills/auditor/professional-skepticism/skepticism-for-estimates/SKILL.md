# Skepticism For Estimates

Accounting estimates are the area where professional skepticism matters most and is most difficult to sustain. Estimates depend on assumptions about uncertain future events, on models that translate those assumptions into measurements, and often on management's judgment. Management has both the discretion and the incentive to shape estimates toward desired results, and the assumptions that drive the bias are often plausible individually while producing a biased aggregate. The recurring failure is accepting estimates because they are supported by a model, a specialist, or plausible assumptions, without challenging whether the assumptions are reasonable, whether the process is free from bias, and whether the evidence is sufficient. Skepticism for estimates is not generic suspicion; it is a structured challenge of the assumptions, the process, and the potential for bias, supported by independent expectation and retrospective review.

Use this skill when applying skepticism to estimates and fair value measurements, when challenging management's estimation process, and when detecting bias in estimates. The goal is estimate testing that goes beyond accepting plausible assumptions and produces an independent, evidence-based conclusion.

## Core Rules

### Recognize That Estimates Are Where Bias Most Often Hides

Estimates depend on assumptions about the future, and assumptions about the future are inherently difficult to challenge. This combination of discretion and plausible deniability makes estimates the area where management bias most often distorts the financial statements. The auditor must approach estimates expecting that bias may be present, not assuming it is absent.

Recognize by:

- understanding that estimates are inherently judgment-intensive and vulnerable to bias;
- identifying the estimates that have the greatest effect on results and the most subjective assumptions;
- expecting that bias, if present, will appear in plausible assumptions rather than obvious errors;
- recognizing that individual estimates may appear reasonable while the aggregate is biased.

Approaching estimates expecting possible bias is the starting point for effective skepticism.

### Challenge The Assumptions, Not Just The Model

A model can be technically correct while producing a biased result if its assumptions are biased. The auditor's challenge must focus on the assumptions, particularly the most sensitive and most subjective ones, not merely on the model's mechanics.

Challenge by:

- identifying the key assumptions that most affect the estimate;
- testing each key assumption against historical experience, current conditions, and external evidence;
- looking for assumptions that are at the aggressive end of a reasonable range;
- considering whether assumptions are consistent across related estimates and across the entity;
- performing sensitivity analysis to show how changes in assumptions move the estimate.

Accepting the model without challenging the assumptions leaves the most vulnerable element unexamined.

### Develop An Independent Expectation

The strongest form of skepticism for estimates is to develop an independent expectation, using the auditor's own assumptions, model, or specialist, and to compare it to management's estimate. This provides a benchmark that is not dependent on accepting management's process.

Develop by:

- deriving a point estimate or range using the auditor's own approach;
- comparing the independent expectation to management's estimate and evaluating the difference;
- where the difference is significant, investigating the assumptions and inputs that drive it;
- where management's estimate falls outside the auditor's reasonable range, requiring adjustment or expanded procedures.

An independent expectation counters the tendency to accept management's estimate as the starting point.

### Perform Retrospective Review To Detect Bias

A retrospective review compares prior-period estimates to actual outcomes. It is a powerful tool for detecting bias, because patterns of over- or under-estimation are often visible only in hindsight and across periods.

Perform by:

- comparing prior-period estimates to the outcomes now known;
- identifying systematic over- or under-estimation that may indicate bias;
- assessing whether the patterns suggest that management's process is result-oriented;
- using the findings to calibrate skepticism for the current-period estimates.

Retrospective review reveals bias that current-period testing alone may miss.

### Look For Result-Oriented Patterns

Bias often appears as patterns: estimates that consistently favor the desired result, changes in method or assumption that move the estimate in a favorable direction, or assumptions that are always at the aggressive end of the range. The auditor must look for these patterns, not only at the individual estimate level.

Look for:

- estimates that consistently produce favorable results;
- changes in method or assumption that are not clearly justified but move the estimate favorably;
- assumptions that are consistently at the aggressive end of a reasonable range;
- estimates that, in aggregate, produce results that are implausibly favorable.

Patterns of result-oriented estimation are a strong indicator of bias that requires expanded procedures.

### Evaluate Whether Evidence Is Sufficient

Skepticism requires that the evidence supporting an estimate be sufficient, not merely present. Plausible assumptions, without supporting evidence, are not enough. The auditor must evaluate whether the evidence supports the estimate and whether additional procedures are needed.

Evaluate by:

- assessing whether each key assumption is supported by appropriate evidence, not merely plausible;
- identifying assumptions that are unsupported or rely on management's assertion alone;
- where evidence is insufficient, expanding procedures, requiring additional support, or using specialists;
- concluding whether the evidence supports the estimate or whether adjustment is required.

Accepting plausible but unsupported assumptions fails the sufficiency requirement.

### Use Specialists And Independent Challenge

Complex or subjective estimates often require specialist involvement and independent challenge. The auditor should use valuation, engineering, or other specialists where the estimate's complexity warrants, and should ensure that conclusions are subject to independent challenge.

Use by:

- engaging specialists for complex or highly subjective estimates;
- evaluating the specialist's assumptions, methods, and conclusions, not merely accepting them;
- subjecting estimate conclusions to independent review, particularly for significant estimates;
- documenting the specialist's work and the challenge applied.

Specialists expand the capacity to challenge complex estimates, but their work must be evaluated and integrated.

## Common Traps

### Accepting Estimates Because They Have A Model Or Specialist

A model or specialist does not make an estimate correct. Assumptions and specialist work must be challenged.

### Challenging The Model Without The Assumptions

Bias enters through assumptions, not only models. Assumptions must be the focus of challenge.

### Failing To Develop An Independent Expectation

Without an independent benchmark, management's estimate is accepted by default.

### Missing Result-Oriented Patterns

Individual estimates may appear reasonable while the aggregate is biased. Patterns must be assessed.

### Accepting Plausible But Unsupported Assumptions

Plausibility is not sufficiency. Assumptions must be supported by evidence.

### Avoiding Specialists For Complex Estimates

Complex estimates often require specialist expertise. Avoiding specialists understates the challenge capacity.

### Treating Retrospective Review As Optional

Retrospective review is a powerful, efficient bias-detection tool. Skipping it leaves bias undetected.

## Self-Check

- Is it recognized that estimates are where management bias most often hides, and is the audit approached expecting possible bias?
- Are the key assumptions, particularly the most sensitive and subjective, challenged against historical, current, and external evidence?
- Is an independent expectation developed to benchmark management's estimate, rather than accepting it as the starting point?
- Is a retrospective review performed to detect patterns of bias across periods?
- Are result-oriented patterns assessed, including consistent favorable outcomes, unjustified method changes, and aggressive-range assumptions?
- Is the sufficiency of evidence supporting each key assumption evaluated, with expansion or adjustment where evidence is insufficient?
- Are specialists used for complex or subjective estimates, with their work evaluated and integrated?
- Could an independent reviewer confirm that estimate testing goes beyond accepting plausible assumptions and produces an evidence-based conclusion?
- Is the application free of accepting estimates because of a model or specialist, challenging only the model, or accepting plausible but unsupported assumptions?
- Are significant estimates subject to independent challenge and, where warranted, specialist involvement?
