# Corroboration Requirement

Management representations are a necessary part of an audit. Management knows the entity, its decisions, and its intentions, and the auditor legitimately seeks their explanations. But a representation is the weakest form of evidence because it is self-serving and uncorroborated. Treating a representation as sufficient evidence, simply because it is plausible or because it comes from a senior source, is one of the most common paths to audit failure. The corroboration requirement is the discipline of seeking independent support for significant assertions, evaluating the reliability of the support obtained, and recognizing when a representation is standing alone where it should not. Corroboration does not mean distrusting management; it means not relying on trust alone for conclusions that matter.

Use this skill when evaluating whether representations are sufficiently corroborated, determining what additional evidence is needed, assessing evidence reliability, or recognizing over-reliance on management assertions. The goal is to ensure significant conclusions rest on corroborated evidence, not on representations accepted at face value.

## Core Rules

### Treat Representations As Starting Points, Not Conclusions

A management representation identifies what management asserts and provides a direction for testing. It is not itself the evidence that proves the assertion. The auditor's role is to take the representation and determine what independent evidence would support or refute it.

Apply this by:

- for each significant representation, identifying what independent evidence would corroborate it;
- obtaining that evidence through documents, systems, third parties, observation, or recalculation;
- assessing whether the evidence obtained actually supports the representation or merely fails to contradict it;
- distinguishing a representation that summarizes verified facts from one that asserts an unverified judgment or intention.

A representation that summarizes facts the auditor has independently verified adds little risk; one that asserts a judgment, estimate, or intention with no independent support carries significant risk and requires proportionate corroboration.

### Match Corroboration Depth To Significance And Risk

Not every representation requires the same depth of corroboration. Routine, low-risk assertions may require light corroboration; significant, judgment-heavy, or fraud-risk assertions require robust independent support. Calibrating corroboration effort to significance prevents both under-testing of important matters and wasted effort on trivial ones.

Calibrate by considering:

- the materiality or significance of the amount or matter represented;
- whether the representation involves judgment, estimate, or intention, which are inherently less verifiable;
- the fraud risk or override risk associated with the area;
- the reliability of management's process and controls underlying the representation;
- whether contradictory indicators exist that heighten the need for corroboration.

For high-significance, high-judgment, or high-risk representations, corroboration should be robust and from independent sources. For low-risk routine matters, reviewing supporting documentation may suffice.

### Evaluate The Reliability Of Corroborating Evidence

Not all corroboration is equal. The reliability of evidence depends on its source, nature, and the controls over its preparation. Evidence from an independent external source is generally more reliable than evidence generated internally; evidence generated by the auditor is more reliable than evidence provided by the entity; and original documents are more reliable than copies or summaries.

Evaluate reliability by considering:

- is the source independent of the entity, or does it originate from management or the entity's systems?
- was the evidence created in the ordinary course of business, or prepared specifically for the audit?
- is it original documentation, or a copy, summary, or reconstruction?
- what controls operated over its preparation, and are those controls reliable?
- does the evidence directly address the assertion, or only an adjacent point?

Corroboration that comes from the same origin as the representation provides little independent support. Two documents that both originate from management, and that agree, are not independent corroboration of each other.

### Recognize When A Representation Is Standing Alone

A common failure is a conclusion that appears supported but actually rests on a single uncorroborated representation, with other evidence addressing only adjacent or routine matters. The auditor must be able to identify, for each significant conclusion, what evidence supports it and whether that evidence is genuinely independent of the representation.

Test for standalone representations by asking:

- if I set aside management's representation, what evidence remains to support this conclusion?
- does the remaining evidence independently support the conclusion, or does it depend on the representation being true?
- have I obtained evidence from a source that does not trace back to management?

If setting aside the representation leaves the conclusion unsupported, the representation is standing alone and further corroboration is required.

### Corroborate Explanations For Anomalies And Contradictions

When the auditor identifies an anomaly, contradiction, or unexpected result, management typically offers an explanation. The temptation is to accept the explanation and move on, especially if it is plausible. But an explanation for an anomaly is itself a representation that requires corroboration, particularly because anomalies often signal underlying issues.

Corroborate explanations by:

- testing the explanation against independent evidence rather than accepting its plausibility;
- considering whether the explanation accounts for all aspects of the anomaly or only some;
- looking for whether similar explanations have been offered for prior anomalies and whether they held up;
- assessing whether the explanation is consistent with other evidence or creates new contradictions.

A plausible explanation that is not tested is an uncorroborated representation. Anomalies deserve resolution through evidence, not through narrative.

### Obtain Written Representations While Understanding Their Limits

Written representation letters are a required component of an audit, and they serve important functions, including documenting management's responsibility and capturing representations on significant matters. But a signed representation letter does not corroborate the representations it contains. It converts oral representations into written form; it does not make them independent evidence.

Use written representations appropriately by:

- obtaining them for the matters required by standards;
- recognizing that they are not a substitute for other evidence on significant matters;
- not reducing other procedures because a written representation has been obtained;
- assessing whether management is reluctant to provide a representation, which may itself be a signal;
- understanding that a representation letter does not protect the auditor if the underlying matter was not otherwise supported.

The representation letter is a complement to evidence, not a replacement for it. Its presence does not relieve the corroboration requirement.

### Pursue Corroboration Until The Conclusion Is Supported Or Qualified

If corroboration cannot be obtained for a significant representation, the auditor faces a choice: perform additional procedures, seek alternative evidence, or qualify or disclaim the conclusion. Accepting an uncorroborated representation as sufficient, because corroboration proved difficult, is not an acceptable resolution.

Resolve insufficient corroboration by:

- performing additional or alternative procedures to seek independent support;
- considering whether the inability to corroborate is itself a significant finding or risk indicator;
- qualifying the conclusion or expressing a scope limitation where corroboration cannot be obtained;
- documenting the procedures performed, the inability to corroborate, and the effect on the conclusion.

The difficulty of obtaining corroboration does not lower the standard. If the evidence is insufficient, the conclusion must reflect that, through qualification, disclaimer, or a modified finding, rather than through acceptance of the unsupported representation.

## Common Traps

### Treating Representations As Evidence

Accepting a plausible representation as sufficient, without independent support, is the core corroboration failure. Representations are starting points.

### Corroboration From The Same Origin

Two management-source documents that agree are not independent corroboration. Seek evidence that does not trace back to management.

### Plausible Explanations Accepted Without Testing

An explanation for an anomaly feels like resolution but is itself an uncorroborated representation. Test it.

### Over-Reliance On The Representation Letter

Treating the signed letter as corroboration relieves the auditor of nothing. It documents representations; it does not prove them.

### Uniform Corroboration Depth

Applying the same light corroboration to high-risk and low-risk matters under-tests the significant ones. Calibrate to significance.

### Accepting Unobtainable Corroboration As A Reason To Conclude

If corroboration cannot be obtained, the answer is more work or a qualified conclusion, not acceptance of the unsupported representation.

### Confusing Lack Of Contradiction With Support

Evidence that fails to contradict a representation does not positively support it. Seek evidence that affirms, not merely fails to deny.

## Self-Check

- Is each significant management representation treated as a starting point requiring independent corroboration, rather than as evidence in itself?
- Is the depth of corroboration calibrated to the significance, judgment content, and risk of the representation, with robust support for high-risk matters?
- Is the reliability of corroborating evidence evaluated for source independence, ordinary-course origin, originality, and controls, recognizing that same-origin evidence is not independent?
- For each significant conclusion, can the auditor identify what evidence remains if the representation is set aside, and does that evidence independently support the conclusion?
- Are explanations for anomalies and contradictions corroborated through testing rather than accepted on plausibility?
- Are written representations obtained as required while their evidentiary limits are understood, with no reduction in other procedures as a result?
- Where corroboration cannot be obtained, are additional procedures performed or the conclusion qualified, rather than the unsupported representation accepted?
- Is the distinction between evidence that affirms a representation and evidence that merely fails to contradict it recognized?
- Is management reluctance to provide a representation assessed as a potential signal?
- Could an independent reviewer confirm that significant conclusions rest on corroborated evidence rather than on representations standing alone?
