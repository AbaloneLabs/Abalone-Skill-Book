# Healthcare Audit

Healthcare entities operate where clinical practice, third-party payment, and heavy regulation meet, and their financial statements are dominated by estimates and rules that have no parallel in ordinary commerce. Revenue is not a posted price; it is a net amount after contractual allowances, denials, and government reimbursement formulas that adjust months or years after service. Agents who audit healthcare as if it were a retail or manufacturing business misread the largest accounts. Charity care, bad debt, and contractual allowances are easy to confuse, and misclassifying them distorts both revenue and the disclosures regulators and bondholders rely on. The harm is concrete: overstated net patient revenue, misstated charity care in community benefit reports, and cost report settlements that reverse into material losses.

Use this skill when planning or performing an audit of a hospital, health system, clinic, physician group, managed care organization, or other healthcare entity, when testing revenue cycle and reimbursement, when evaluating charity care and contractual allowances, or when assessing regulatory compliance including HIPAA, 340B, and cost report obligations. The goal is to audit revenue and estimates through the lens of the reimbursement system that actually determines the numbers, and to keep clinical, regulatory, and financial assertions consistent.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Revenue Cycle End To End Before Testing Revenue

Healthcare revenue is produced by a long cycle from registration through coding, billing, adjudication, denial management, and collection. Each stage introduces estimates and risk, and the net revenue recorded is the residual after contractual adjustments. Testing the ending balance without understanding the cycle tests the wrong thing.

Map and assess the revenue cycle:

- registration, insurance verification, and prior authorization controls;
- charge capture and the link between clinical documentation and charges;
- coding accuracy, including ICD and CPT coding and the role of certified coders;
- charge description master maintenance and price accuracy;
- claim submission, editing, and clearinghouse controls;
- payer adjudication, remittance posting, and denial management;
- the estimation of contractual allowances and implicit price concessions;
- bad debt recognition and the distinction from contractual adjustments.

### Test Net Patient Revenue And Contractual Allowances As Significant Estimates

The contractual allowance is the difference between gross charges and the amount the entity expects to collect, and it is often the largest estimate on the statement. It depends on payer mix, contract terms, historical collection, and government fee schedules. Understating the allowance overstates revenue and earnings.

Contractual allowance and revenue considerations:

- understand the methodology for estimating contractual allowances by payer and service line;
- test the completeness and accuracy of payer mix and gross charge data;
- evaluate the reasonableness of expected reimbursement using contract terms and fee schedules;
- assess the estimation of implicit price concessions for self-pay patients after policy adjustments;
- perform retrospective review of prior estimates against actual collections to detect bias;
- test the treatment of denials, underpayments, and appeals in the estimate;
- reconcile the allowance rollforward and the presentation of net patient revenue.

### Distinguish Charity Care, Bad Debt, And Discounts Correctly

Healthcare entities reduce amounts owed for several reasons that look similar but are reported differently: charity care for patients who cannot pay, bad debt for those who will not, and discounts for prompt payment or uninsured discounts. Misclassifying among them distorts revenue, bad debt, and the community benefit disclosure that bondholders and regulators scrutinize.

Classification considerations:

- charity care is generally not reported as revenue at all under current guidance, given the implicit price concession policy;
- bad debt is recorded when an amount due is deemed uncollectible after assessment;
- uninsured discounts and prompt-pay discounts reduce gross charges or revenue, not charity care;
- the charity care policy must be documented, applied consistently, and supported by eligibility determination;
- community benefit reporting should reconcile to the charity care and means-tested amounts;
- disclosures of charity care at cost should be supported by a reasonable cost-finding method.

### Audit Third-Party Reimbursement And Settlement Estimates

Medicare, Medicaid, and commercial payers settle on formulas and cost reports that adjust long after the service date. The estimated settlement, including prospective and retrospective elements, is a significant estimate that can reverse materially. Agents must understand whether the entity is a provider, a managed care plan, or both.

Reimbursement and settlement considerations:

- understand the reimbursement basis, fee-for-service, DRG, capitation, or cost-based, by payer;
- test the Medicare cost report preparation and the controls over its submission;
- evaluate the estimated settlement and the accrual for known or probable adjustments;
- assess the adequacy of reserves for overpayment recoupment, RAC audits, and reopenings;
- consider the impact of program audits, zone program integrity contractors, and whistleblower exposure;
- test the timing of revenue recognition for managed care premiums and the related medical loss ratio;
- evaluate risk-sharing and incentive arrangements that adjust premium revenue.

### Test Cost Report Compliance And Related Settlements

For providers paid on a cost basis, the Medicare cost report is both a regulatory filing and a driver of revenue. Errors in cost allocation, wage index, or pass-through amounts create settlement adjustments and regulatory exposure. Treat the cost report as connected to the audit, not as a separate compliance task.

Cost report considerations:

- reconcile cost report totals to the general ledger and the trial balance;
- test the allocation of costs across cost centers using the applicable worksheet methodology;
- verify the wage index, geographic adjustments, and outlier calculations;
- assess the settlement estimate and the accrual for probable adjustments;
- review prior-year settlements and reopenings for indication of systematic error;
- consider the effect of new program rules, such as payment system updates, on the settlement;
- coordinate with the cost report preparer and understand their control framework.

### Evaluate Charity Care And Community Benefit Reporting

Not-for-profit hospitals must report community benefit to justify tax exemption and to satisfy bond covenants and regulators. The components, charity care, means-tested programs, unreimbursed Medicaid, health professions education, research, and community health operations, must be supported and consistently defined. Overstating community benefit invites regulatory challenge.

Community benefit considerations:

- confirm the methodology aligns with the applicable guidance, such as the IRS Form 990 Schedule H framework;
- test charity care at cost using a reasonable cost-to-charge ratio or cost-finding method;
- verify means-tested program shortfalls and unreimbursed Medicaid costs;
- distinguish community benefit from bad debt and from revenue shortfalls on paying patients;
- assess the community health needs assessment and implementation strategy requirements;
- reconcile community benefit totals to the underlying accounting records.

### Audit Provider Compensation And Arrangements For Compliance

Provider compensation, especially productivity-based pay, medical directorships, and recruitment arrangements, is a high-risk area for fraud and abuse because of the Stark Law and Anti-Kickback Statute. Compensation that is not fair market value or that varies with the volume of referrals creates legal and financial statement exposure.

Provider compensation considerations:

- test whether compensation meets a personal services or employment exception under Stark;
- evaluate fair market value and commercial reasonableness of medical director and call coverage agreements;
- assess productivity-based compensation for a documented, consistent methodology;
- review recruitment agreements, guarantees, and forgiveness for compliance and proper accounting;
- consider the financial statement implications of self-disclosure or repayment of noncompliant amounts;
- evaluate the adequacy of the compliance program and its monitoring of arrangements.

### Address HIPAA, 340B, And Regulatory Compliance Exposure

Healthcare entities carry regulatory obligations that create contingent liabilities and disclosures even when they are not part of the core financials. HIPAA breaches, 340B drug pricing compliance, and conditions of participation failures can produce fines, restitution, and reputational harm. Scope these deliberately rather than ignoring them.

Regulatory compliance considerations:

- assess HIPAA security and privacy incidents for breach notification and potential fines;
- evaluate 340B program compliance, including eligibility, ceiling price, and duplicate discount risk;
- review compliance with conditions of participation and quality reporting that affect reimbursement;
- consider False Claims Act exposure from billing errors, upcoding, or medically unnecessary services;
- assess the adequacy of reserves and disclosures for known investigations and self-disclosures;
- coordinate with the compliance officer and legal counsel on the status of open matters.

### Apply Skepticism To Estimates And To The Clinical-Financial Link

Healthcare estimates, contractual allowances, settlements, charity care, and reserves, are large, judgmental, and prone to management bias. The link between clinical documentation and the financial numbers is where errors and fraud enter. Maintain skepticism toward estimates that conveniently improve earnings.

Estimate and link considerations:

- challenge assumptions that lower contractual allowances or settlements without support;
- test the completeness and accuracy of clinical data feeding charges and coding;
- perform retrospective look-back on prior estimates for systematic optimism;
- corroborate management explanations with external payer data and remittance evidence;
- consider the effect of payer mix shifts and volume changes on estimate reasonableness;
- evaluate whether disclosure of estimation uncertainty is adequate for the most sensitive areas.

## Common Traps

### Treating Gross Charges As The Revenue Base

Gross charges bear little relation to amounts collected, because most payers pay contracted rates or government fee schedules far below charges. Testing gross charges without understanding the netting through contractual allowances audits a number that does not represent economic reality.

### Confusing Charity Care With Bad Debt

Recording uncollectible self-pay amounts as bad debt when the patient was eligible for charity care overstates bad debt and understates community benefit. The distinction turns on the entity's policy and the patient's circumstances, not on whether collection failed.

### Accepting The Contractual Allowance Without Retrospective Testing

The contractual allowance is an estimate, and estimates can be biased. Accepting the current estimate without comparing prior estimates to actual collections allows systematic understatement to persist and revenue to be overstated over time.

### Ignoring The Cost Report Because It Is A Separate Filing

The Medicare cost report drives cost-based settlement revenue and is filed under certification. Treating it as outside the audit misses the connection to revenue and the exposure from misallocated costs or reopenings.

### Overlooking Stark And Anti-Kickback Risk In Compensation

Compensation arrangements that vary with referral volume or exceed fair market value violate federal law and create repayment and disclosure exposure. Reviewing compensation only for accounting accuracy, without the compliance lens, misses the legal contingency.

### Missing 340B Compliance Exposure

The 340B program is heavily scrutinized, and errors in eligibility, ceiling price, or duplicate discounts can trigger repayment and enforcement. Treating 340B as a pharmacy operational matter rather than a financial statement risk understates the contingency.

### Understating Settlement Reserves To Smooth Earnings

Third-party settlements and recoupments are estimates that can be tuned to produce a desired result. Understating reserves for RAC audits or known reopenings smooths earnings now and creates a larger reversal later. Challenge the reserve adequacy.

### Ignoring The Community Benefit Disclosure For Not-For-Profits

Community benefit reporting on Form 990 Schedule H and bond disclosures is scrutinized by regulators and the public. Inflating community benefit with bad debt or unsupported costs invites challenge to tax exemption and creates reputational risk.

## Self-Check

- Has the revenue cycle been mapped end to end, with controls assessed at registration, charge capture, coding, billing, adjudication, and collection?
- Is net patient revenue tested through the contractual allowance estimate, with payer mix, contract terms, fee schedules, and prior-period retrospective review considered?
- Are charity care, bad debt, and discounts distinguished correctly and consistently with the entity's policy and applicable guidance?
- Are third-party reimbursement and settlement estimates, including cost report settlements and recoupment reserves, tested for reasonableness and adequacy?
- Is the Medicare cost report reconciled to the general ledger, with cost allocations, wage index, and settlement accruals assessed?
- Is community benefit reporting supported by a reasonable cost method and reconciled to underlying records, excluding bad debt and unsupported amounts?
- Are provider compensation arrangements tested for Stark and Anti-Kickback compliance, fair market value, and commercial reasonableness?
- Are HIPAA, 340B, and other regulatory compliance matters assessed for contingent liability and disclosure, with reserves for known investigations?
- Have healthcare estimates been challenged for management bias, with retrospective look-back and corroboration from external payer data?
- Is the clinical-to-financial link, documentation to charges to coding to billing, tested for completeness and accuracy?
- Are disclosures of estimation uncertainty adequate for the most sensitive areas, such as settlements, contractual allowances, and charity care?
