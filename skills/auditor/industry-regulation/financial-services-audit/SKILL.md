# Financial Services Audit

Financial services entities are audited under two overlapping regimes at once: the financial reporting framework that produces GAAP statements, and the regulatory framework that keeps the institution solvent and permitted to operate. Agents who treat a bank or insurer like an ordinary commercial entity miss the point. The balance sheet is dominated by financial instruments measured at fair value or amortized cost, the income statement is driven by estimates of loss and market movement, and the regulatory reports, call reports, statutory statements, and capital ratios are often more consequential to the entity than the audited financials. A clean audit opinion paired with an understated allowance or a miscomputed capital ratio produces real harm: regulatory action, restated filings, and loss of public confidence.

Use this skill when planning or performing an audit of a bank, savings institution, credit union, insurance company, investment manager, broker-dealer, or similar regulated financial entity, when testing regulatory capital or call report data, when evaluating loan loss reserves and investment valuations, or when preparing for regulator interaction. The goal is to audit both the financial statements and the regulatory submissions as one connected system, so that estimates, valuations, and ratios are consistent across every place they appear.

## Core Rules

### Map The Regulatory Architecture Before Designing Procedures

Every financial services audit is shaped by a specific regulator and a specific set of rules. Designing procedures without first mapping which rules apply produces work that tests the wrong numbers against the wrong benchmarks. Identify the charter, the primary regulator, and the reporting regime before scoping.

Establish the regulatory frame:

- the primary federal and state regulators, such as the OCC, Federal Reserve, FDIC, state banking or insurance commissioner, SEC, or FINRA;
- the capital framework, Basel III for large banks, RBC for insurers, net capital for broker-dealers;
- the required regulatory reports, call reports for banks, statutory statements and annual statements for insurers, Form ADV or focus reports for others;
- the examination cycle and any open supervisory matters, consent orders, or MRAs;
- the legal lending limits, investment authority constraints, and statutory restrictions on the entity's activities;
- the applicable accounting standards overlay, including ASC 326 for credit losses and specialized insurance or investment company guidance.

### Treat Regulatory Capital As A Primary Audit Target

Regulatory capital ratios are not a footnote; they are often the single most important number the entity reports. Understatement of risk-weighted assets or overstatement of capital can mask insolvency and trigger enforcement. Test capital with the same rigor as a significant account.

Capital testing considerations:

- reconcile regulatory capital components to the general ledger and to the audited financial statements;
- test risk-weighting of assets under the standardized or advanced approaches, including off-balance-sheet exposures and derivatives;
- verify capital deductions and adjustments, including goodwill, intangibles, and deferred tax assets;
- confirm thresholds for capital conservation, countercyclical, and systemic buffers;
- test the numerator components, CET1, additional Tier 1, and Tier 2, for eligibility and regulatory limits;
- evaluate whether the entity correctly applies transition provisions for new or amended rules;
- consider the impact of stress testing, CCAR or DFAST results, and capital action disclosures.

### Audit The Allowance For Credit Losses As A Significant Estimate

Under CECL and similar current expected credit loss models, the allowance is a forward-looking estimate that materially affects earnings and capital. It is a significant risk in virtually every lending institution and must receive a tailored, skeptical response. The allowance is also where management bias most commonly hides.

CECL and allowance considerations:

- understand management's estimation method, whether pooling, individual review, or a hybrid, and its consistency with policy;
- test the completeness and accuracy of loan-level data feeding the model, including risk ratings, balances, and origination data;
- evaluate the reasonableness of macroeconomic forecasts, qualitative factors, and the reasonable and supportable period;
- assess model governance, validation, and backtesting, and whether model changes were approved;
- challenge management's adjustments and overrides, especially those that lower the allowance;
- perform a look-back on prior-period estimates to detect systematic bias or optimism;
- test the layering for acquired, PCI, and TDR or modified loans under current guidance;
- reconcile the allowance rollforward and ensure disclosures of estimation uncertainty are adequate.

### Test Investment Portfolios And Fair Value Hierarchies

Investments are often the largest asset and the largest source of estimation uncertainty. Classification, valuation, and other-than-temporary impairment decisions all carry audit risk, especially for level 3 instruments with unobservable inputs. Confirm that the valuation method matches the classification and that the evidence supports the level claimed.

Investment and valuation considerations:

- confirm holdings and income with custodians and counterparties;
- verify classification as held-to-maturity, available-for-sale, or trading, and test for intent and ability assertions;
- obtain and test fair value for level 1, level 2, and level 3 instruments, using independent pricing where possible;
- evaluate the auditor's use of a valuation specialist for complex or illiquid instruments;
- test transfers between hierarchy levels and the disclosure of level 3 rollforward;
- assess other-than-temporary impairment conclusions against credit and market evidence;
- for insurers, test the separation of investment income, realized gains, and unrealized gains by segment and statutory treatment.

### Audit Derivatives And Hedging For Substance And Effectiveness

Derivatives are off-balance-sheet in notion but can dominate earnings and equity through OCI. The accounting, hedge effectiveness, and counterparty risk all need specific procedures, and the notional volume often dwarfs the entity's capital. Treat derivatives as a significant area whenever the entity uses them.

Derivative and hedging considerations:

- confirm notional amounts, fair values, and collateral with counterparties;
- test hedge documentation, including the risk management objective and designated hedged item, executed at inception;
- assess effectiveness testing method and results for fair value, cash flow, and net investment hedges;
- test the reclassification of amounts from OCI to earnings and the related disclosures;
- evaluate counterparty credit risk, netting arrangements, and collateral thresholds;
- consider whether speculative or undesignated positions exist and how they are reported;
- reconcile derivative gains and losses to the income statement and OCI.

### Test Fiduciary And Asset Management Activities Separately

Banks, trust companies, and investment managers hold assets in a fiduciary capacity that is off-balance-sheet to the institution but creates regulatory and reputational risk. A failure in segregation, custody, or fair dealing can trigger enforcement and restitution. Do not treat fiduciary assets as outside the audit's concern.

Fiduciary considerations:

- confirm segregation of fiduciary assets from bank assets and verify custody controls;
- test the completeness and accuracy of fee revenue and its basis, including asset-based, transactional, and incentive fees;
- evaluate compliance with the Prudent Investor Rule or applicable fiduciary standards;
- review self-dealing, principal transactions, and conflicts of interest for prohibited activity;
- test the valuation of assets under administration and the accuracy of client statements;
- assess litigation and surcharge exposure and whether a liability or disclosure is required;
- consider regulatory examination findings on fiduciary activities and management's response.

### Integrate Regulatory Reporting Into The Audit

The call report, annual statement, or focus report is filed under oath or certification and is subject to regulatory penalties for misstatement. Much of its data derives from the same records as the financial statements, so an inconsistency between the two is a red flag. Treat regulatory reporting as a connected deliverable, not a separate compliance task.

Regulatory reporting integration:

- reconcile key call report or statutory statement schedules to the audited financials and to the general ledger;
- test the regulatory adjustments that differ from GAAP, such as RAP versus GAAP differences;
- verify the accuracy of data feeding ratios, such as loan-to-deposit, liquidity, and capital metrics;
- review the certification process and the controls over report preparation;
- assess whether identified financial statement misstatements also affect regulatory filings;
- evaluate the timeliness of filings and any amendments or restatements during the period;
- coordinate with the entity's regulatory reporting team and understand their control framework.

### Engage Regulators And Examiners With Discipline

Regulator interaction is part of the audit of a financial institution, and it cuts both ways. Examiners hold information the auditor needs, and the auditor holds information examiners need. Mismanaging this interaction can breach confidentiality, create liability, or hide problems that both parties should see.

Regulator engagement considerations:

- understand the auditor's legal and professional obligations to communicate with examiners;
- request and review the most recent examination report, MRAs, and enforcement actions;
- communicate significant audit findings, accounting matters, and disagreements as required;
- coordinate timing of audit fieldwork with the examination cycle to share information appropriately;
- maintain the independence of the auditor's judgment and do not defer conclusions to the examiner;
- document communications and the basis for any reliance on examiner work, which is generally limited;
- escalate disputes between management and the regulator that affect accounting or disclosure.

### Apply Heightened Skepticism To Related-Party And Insider Activity

Financial institutions are prone to related-party lending, insider transactions, and affiliate dealings that are regulated and disclosed. These transactions are a classic fraud and abuse vector and a frequent focus of enforcement. Do not accept them at face value.

Related-party considerations:

- identify insiders, principal shareholders, and affiliates under the applicable definition;
- test compliance with legal lending limits and section 23A or 23B-type restrictions;
- evaluate the terms of related-party loans for favorability and adequacy of collateral;
- review affiliate transactions for fairness, proper allocation, and required disclosures;
- assess whether related-party balances are properly presented and disclosed in the financials;
- consider the regulatory consequences of insider abuse and whether a referral is warranted.

## Common Traps

### Auditing Only The Financial Statements And Ignoring Regulatory Reports

Treating the call report or statutory statement as someone else's problem leaves the auditor exposed, because the same underlying records feed both. An error in the allowance or capital computation flows into the regulatory filing, and a clean financial statement opinion does not protect against a misstated regulatory submission.

### Accepting Management's Allowance Without Challenging Inputs

CECL models are complex, and management's inputs, qualitative factors, and overrides are where bias enters. Accepting the model output without testing the data, challenging the forecasts, and looking back at prior bias produces an allowance that may be comfortable rather than correct.

### Treating Risk-Weighted Assets As A Mechanical Calculation

Risk-weighting involves judgments about exposure classification, collateral, guarantees, and netting. Applying the weights mechanically without testing the underlying exposures understates risk-weighted assets and overstates capital. The denominator matters as much as the numerator.

### Relying On Broker Quotes For Level 3 Valuations

A broker quote for an illiquid instrument is often a rounded indication, not an executed price, and may reflect the broker's interest. Relying on it without understanding the methodology, the level of the market, and corroborating evidence overstates the reliability of the fair value.

### Overlooking Hedge Documentation Defects

Hedge accounting requires documentation at inception designating the hedged item, the derivative, and the risk management objective. A defect in inception documentation cannot be cured retroactively and forces de-designation. Discovering the defect at year-end is too late.

### Deferring To The Examiner's Conclusions

Examiners and auditors have different objectives and different evidence. Deferring to the examiner on an accounting matter, or assuming an area is covered because the examiner reviewed it, abandons the auditor's own responsibility. Coordinate, but do not delegate judgment.

### Ignoring Fiduciary Exposure Because It Is Off-Balance-Sheet

Fiduciary assets do not appear on the balance sheet, so it is easy to treat them as out of scope. But surcharge litigation, regulatory enforcement, and restitution obligations can create material liabilities and disclosures. Scope fiduciary activity deliberately.

### Missing The Effect Of A Rule Change Or Transition

Banking and insurance rules change frequently, with transition periods and phase-ins. Applying the prior rule, or applying the new rule without the transition, produces misstated capital and disclosures. Track effective dates and transition mechanics for every relevant amendment.

## Self-Check

- Has the regulatory architecture, primary regulator, capital framework, and required reports been mapped before procedures were designed?
- Are regulatory capital ratios tested as a primary target, with risk-weighted assets, capital deductions, and buffer thresholds reconciled to the ledger and financials?
- Is the allowance for credit losses treated as a significant estimate, with model inputs, forecasts, qualitative factors, overrides, and prior-period bias challenged?
- Are investment portfolios tested for classification, fair value hierarchy, level 3 instruments, and other-than-temporary impairment, with specialist involvement where needed?
- Are derivatives and hedges tested for notional confirmation, inception documentation, effectiveness, reclassification, and counterparty risk?
- Are fiduciary and asset management activities scoped deliberately, with segregation, fees, conflicts, and surcharge exposure considered?
- Is regulatory reporting integrated into the audit, with call report or statutory schedules reconciled to the audited financials?
- Has the most recent examination report and any MRAs or enforcement actions been obtained and assessed for audit implications?
- Are related-party and insider transactions identified, tested against lending limits, and evaluated for terms, fairness, and disclosure?
- Has the impact of any rule change or transition provision been correctly applied to both capital and disclosures?
- Are communications with regulators handled within legal and professional limits, with the auditor's independent judgment preserved?
