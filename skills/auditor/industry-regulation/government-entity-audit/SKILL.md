# Government Entity Audit

Government entities are not audited like commercial enterprises because they are not run to make a profit and their financial statements serve citizens, creditors, and oversight bodies rather than investors. Fund accounting separates resources by purpose and legal restriction, the budget is a legal document as much as a plan, and intergovernmental grants carry compliance conditions that can claw back revenue. Agents who apply commercial audit instincts produce work that misses budgetary violations, misclassified funds, and grant compliance failures that are the real risks in the public sector. The harm is not just a restatement; it is a loss of public trust, bond covenant breaches, and disallowed grant funding that the entity must repay.

Use this skill when planning or performing an audit of a state, local, or special-purpose government, when applying GASB standards and fund accounting, when testing budgetary compliance, or when auditing grants, capital assets, infrastructure, or pension and OPEB liabilities. The goal is to audit within the governmental framework, where legal compliance and fund integrity matter as much as fair presentation, and to connect the basic financial statements to the compliance and single audit obligations that ride alongside them.

## Core Rules

### Apply The Right Measurement Focus And Basis Of Accounting Per Fund

Governmental accounting rests on the distinction between fund financial statements and government-wide statements, and between the current financial resources measurement focus and the economic resources focus. Applying the wrong basis to a fund produces misstated results and violates the framework. Establish the structure before testing any balance.

Fund structure and basis considerations:

- governmental funds use the current financial resources measurement focus and the modified accrual basis;
- proprietary funds use the economic resources measurement focus and the full accrual basis;
- fiduciary funds, including pension trust and agency funds, use the economic resources focus and accrual basis;
- government-wide statements convert governmental fund data to full accrual and eliminate interfund activity;
- reconciliations between fund and government-wide statements must be prepared and tested;
- each fund must meet the criteria for its type and not be misclassified for presentation advantage;
- permanent, special revenue, capital projects, and debt service funds each have distinct purposes and should not be used as holding accounts for unrestricted activity;
- confirm that major fund criteria are applied to determine which funds are presented separately, and that the general fund is always presented as major.

### Test Budgetary Compliance As A Legal Matter

For most governments, the adopted budget is a legal instrument, and expenditures in excess of appropriations, or expenditures from the wrong budgetary fund, are violations of law even when the accounting is correct. Budgetary comparison statements are required, and variances can trigger findings. Treat budget integrity as a compliance objective, not a presentation detail.

Budgetary compliance considerations:

- confirm the legally adopted budget and any amendments during the period;
- test expenditures against appropriations at the legal level of control, which may be department, program, or object;
- identify expenditures in excess of appropriations, interfund transfers requiring approval, and unauthorized uses;
- evaluate budgetary basis differences between the budget and GAAP and reconcile them;
- test the budget-to-actual comparison statement and the required variance columns;
- assess whether material budget violations require findings or management letter comments;
- test encumbrances and their treatment in the budgetary basis and year-end carryover;
- evaluate compliance with legal reserve and working capital requirements imposed by statute or bond covenant.

### Audit Grants And Intergovernmental Revenue For Eligibility And Compliance

Grants and intergovernmental revenue are often a government's largest revenue source, and they come with eligibility, allowable cost, and reporting conditions. A disallowed cost converts revenue into a repayment liability. When federal funding is involved, the Single Audit or program-specific audit requirements attach.

Grant and intergovernmental revenue considerations:

- identify all federal awards and determine whether the Single Audit threshold and requirements apply;
- test revenue recognition under the appropriate time of availability and eligibility standards;
- verify compliance with allowable cost, permissible activities, and matching or maintenance of effort requirements;
- test the schedule of expenditures of federal awards for completeness and accuracy;
- evaluate the adequacy of the SEFA and its reconciliation to the underlying records;
- assess compliance with the uniform guidance requirements for major programs and the related audit opinions;
- consider pass-through entity monitoring and subrecipient monitoring obligations;
- test the recognition of grant revenue against eligibility and the period of availability, distinguishing reciprocal from nonreciprocal and time-restricted awards;
- evaluate the accrual for disallowed costs, questioned costs, and known or probable repayments, and the adequacy of related disclosures.

### Test Capital Assets And Infrastructure Including The Modified Approach

Governments hold extensive infrastructure, roads, bridges, water systems, that may be reported under the depreciation method or the modified approach. The choice and the supporting asset records are a significant area. Incomplete or inaccurate capital asset records are one of the most common government audit deficiencies.

Capital asset and infrastructure considerations:

- verify the capitalization threshold and policy and its consistent application;
- test the capital asset subsidiary ledger for completeness, including infrastructure;
- assess whether infrastructure is depreciated or reported under the modified approach, and whether the conditions for the modified approach are met;
- for the modified approach, test the asset management system and the documented condition assessments;
- evaluate the capitalization of constructed assets and the cutoff of projects in progress;
- test disposals, transfers between funds, and impairment of capital assets;
- reconcile capital asset balances between fund and government-wide statements;
- test depreciation methods and useful lives for reasonableness and consistent application across asset classes;
- assess whether donated, contributed, and foreclosed capital assets are recorded at fair value with appropriate documentation.

### Audit Pension And OPEB Liabilities And Disclosures

Most governments participate in cost-sharing multiple-employer or agent pension plans, and the net pension and OPEB liabilities are among the largest items on the statement of net position. The accounting is complex, the assumptions are sensitive, and the disclosures are extensive. This is a significant estimate requiring specialist involvement.

Pension and OPEB considerations:

- determine the type of plan, single-employer, agent, or cost-sharing, and the applicable accounting;
- test the employer's proportionate share and the basis for allocation in cost-sharing plans;
- evaluate the actuarial assumptions, including the discount rate, mortality, and salary scale;
- assess the actuary's qualifications and the actuarial valuation's date and methodology;
- test the inputs to the actuarial valuation, including census data and plan provisions;
- evaluate the recognition of deferred inflows and outflows and the components of pension expense;
- assess the adequacy of required disclosures, including the schedule of changes in net pension liability;
- evaluate contributions made versus required contributions and the impact on the funded status and disclosures;
- consider the sensitivity of the liability to changes in the discount rate and other key assumptions.

### Preserve Fund Integrity And Test Interfund Activity

Governments use funds to segregate resources by purpose and legal restriction, and interfund borrowing, transfers, and advances can mask deficits or violate restrictions. A fund that appears solvent only because it holds advances from another fund may be structurally in deficit. Test fund integrity as a distinct objective.

Fund integrity and interfund considerations:

- test that resources restricted to a purpose are used only for that purpose;
- evaluate interfund transfers for legality, authorization, and proper classification;
- assess interfund receivables and payables for collectibility and whether advances should be equity;
- identify funds with structural deficits or with balances below required reserves;
- test due from and due to other governments and interfund due to and due from;
- evaluate whether interfund activity is properly eliminated in government-wide statements;
- assess whether transfers in and out are recorded in the same period and properly matched between funds.

### Identify And Report On Compliance And Legal Requirements

Government audits routinely include compliance reporting beyond the basic financial statements, such as the Single Audit, state-specific compliance, and bond covenant compliance. Missing a compliance requirement because it is not part of the financial statement opinion is a failure of the engagement scope. Understand the full reporting package.

Compliance and reporting considerations:

- identify federal, state, and local compliance requirements applicable to the entity;
- perform the Single Audit, including major program determination and the schedule of findings and questioned costs;
- test compliance with bond covenants, debt limits, and reserve requirements;
- evaluate compliance with state statutory requirements, such as tax rate and levy limits;
- assess the management's discussion and analysis as required supplementary information;
- report findings, questioned costs, and the summary schedule of prior audit findings.

### Evaluate The Reporting Model And Required Supplementary Information

Government financial statements include required supplementary information, budgetary comparison schedules, pension and OPEB schedules, and infrastructure condition data under the modified approach, that are part of the audit but not part of the basic statements. Omitting or misstating RSI is an audit failure even when the basic statements are correct.

Reporting model considerations:

- confirm the presence and format of all required basic statements and notes;
- test the budgetary comparison schedule as RSI for governmental funds;
- assess the pension and OPEB RSI schedules for accuracy and consistency with the actuarial valuations;
- evaluate the management's discussion and analysis for consistency with the statements;
- test combining and individual fund statements where required;
- confirm that the introductory section and statistical section, where applicable, are complete;
- verify that the basic financial statements include the government-wide and fund-level statements, reconciliations, and notes to the financial statements;
- assess whether component unit reporting, blending versus discrete presentation, is correctly applied for related entities.

## Common Traps

### Applying Commercial Accrual Accounting To Governmental Funds

Governmental funds use modified accrual and a current financial resources focus, not full accrual. Recognizing revenue or expenditures on a commercial basis distorts fund results and the budget-to-actual comparison, and it violates the framework.

### Treating The Budget As A Presentation Detail Rather Than A Legal Document

The budget is legally binding, and exceeding appropriations is a violation regardless of accounting correctness. Reviewing the budget-to-actual schedule without testing expenditures against appropriations at the legal level of control misses the real compliance risk.

### Misclassifying Funds To Improve Presentation

Classifying an activity as proprietary or fiduciary when it should be governmental, or splitting funds to avoid showing a deficit, misleads users. Fund type must follow the criteria, not the desired appearance of the statements.

### Ignoring Single Audit Requirements On Federal Awards

When federal expenditures exceed the threshold, a Single Audit is required, with major program determination, compliance testing, and a schedule of findings. Treating federal grants as ordinary revenue and omitting the Single Audit is a scope failure.

### Accepting The Pension Liability Without Actuarial Review

The net pension liability is a complex actuarial estimate with sensitive assumptions. Accepting management's number without evaluating the actuary, the assumptions, and the census data inputs leaves a significant estimate untested.

### Incomplete Capital Asset Records

Governments commonly have incomplete or outdated capital asset ledgers, especially for infrastructure. Relying on the ledger without testing completeness and condition understates assets and depreciation, and defeats the modified approach if used.

### Missing Interfund Borrowing That Masks Deficits

Advances and due-to balances between funds can make a structurally deficit fund look solvent. Treating interfund balances as routine without assessing collectibility and legality hides the true financial condition of individual funds.

### Omitting Required Supplementary Information

RSI is part of the audit. Skipping the budgetary comparison schedule, pension schedules, or MD&A review because they are not basic statements leaves required information unexamined and is an audit failure.

## Self-Check

- Is the correct measurement focus and basis of accounting applied per fund type, with governmental funds on modified accrual and proprietary and fiduciary funds on full accrual?
- Are reconciliations between fund financial statements and government-wide statements prepared and tested?
- Is budgetary compliance tested at the legal level of control, with expenditures in excess of appropriations and unauthorized uses identified?
- Are grants and intergovernmental revenue tested for eligibility, allowable cost, and compliance, with the Single Audit performed where federal awards exceed the threshold?
- Is the schedule of expenditures of federal awards complete, accurate, and reconciled to underlying records, with major programs properly determined?
- Are capital assets and infrastructure tested for completeness, with the modified approach conditions verified if elected?
- Are pension and OPEB liabilities evaluated for plan type, proportionate share, actuarial assumptions, actuary qualifications, and census data inputs?
- Is fund integrity preserved, with interfund transfers, advances, and due-to balances tested for legality, collectibility, and proper elimination?
- Is the full compliance reporting package, including Single Audit findings, bond covenants, and state requirements, scoped and completed?
- Is required supplementary information, including budgetary comparison, pension schedules, and MD&A, tested for accuracy and consistency?; are combining and individual fund statements and statistical sections complete where required?
