# External Confirmation Design

External confirmation is one of the most persuasive forms of audit evidence because it is obtained directly from a third party, independent of both management and the entity's records. A properly designed and controlled confirmation provides direct evidence about existence, rights, and accuracy of balances and transactions. A poorly designed or loosely controlled confirmation produces evidence that looks strong but is not: responses routed through management, confirmations of the wrong assertion, exceptions dismissed without investigation, or non-responses treated as acceptable. The recurring failure is treating confirmation as a mechanical mail-merge exercise, where the auditor sends standard letters and records responses, without designing the confirmation to test the specific assertion or controlling the process to preserve reliability. The result is persuasive-looking evidence that does not actually support the conclusion.

Use this skill when designing confirmation procedures, when controlling the confirmation process, when evaluating responses and exceptions, and when deciding whether confirmation evidence is sufficient. The goal is to obtain confirmation evidence that is directly relevant, reliably controlled, and properly evaluated, so that the conclusion rests on genuinely independent third-party evidence.

## Core Rules

### Match The Confirmation To The Specific Assertion

Confirmation is most powerful for certain assertions and weak or irrelevant for others. A receivable confirmation provides direct evidence of existence and rights, but it does not test valuation, completeness, or cutoff. Designing the confirmation to test the right assertion is the first decision.

Match confirmation to assertion by:

- existence and rights: confirm balances directly with debtors, banks, or custodians;
- completeness of liabilities: confirmation is weaker, because a creditor may not respond about an unrecorded balance; supplement with other procedures;
- accuracy: confirm specific transaction terms, balances, or terms that affect measurement;
- contingent terms: confirm side agreements, holdbacks, or special terms that affect revenue recognition or valuation.

Do not assume a confirmation covers all assertions. State which assertion each confirmation addresses and supplement with other procedures where confirmation does not reach.

### Decide What To Confirm And With Whom

The choice of counterparty and the content of the confirmation determine its evidential value. Confirming the wrong party, or confirming a balance when the risk is in terms, produces evidence that does not address the risk.

Decide by:

- confirming with independent third parties: banks, customers, vendors, legal counsel, custodians, or regulators;
- selecting counterparties based on risk, materiality, and the assertion, not only on size;
- including specific information: balance, terms, contingent arrangements, or status of disputes;
- considering confirmation of unusual or related-party balances where existence or terms are at risk.

A confirmation that asks only "please confirm the balance" misses terms, side agreements, and disputes that often hold the real risk.

### Control The Confirmation Process To Preserve Reliability

The reliability of confirmation depends on the auditor maintaining control from initiation to receipt. If management intercepts, alters, or influences the process, the response loses its independence and its evidential value. Process control is not a formality; it is what makes the evidence trustworthy.

Maintain control by:

- the auditor, not management, sending the confirmation requests;
- using the auditor's letterhead and including a request to reply directly to the auditor;
- addressing confirmations based on independently verified addresses, not addresses provided solely by management;
- providing a prepaid return envelope or electronic reply mechanism directed to the auditor;
- following up on non-responses through the auditor's own channels;
- for electronic confirmations, using authenticated systems or verified portals, not email replies that can be spoofed.

Any breach of process control, such as management delivering responses or addresses that cannot be verified, undermines the reliability of the evidence obtained.

### Choose Positive Versus Negative Confirmation Deliberately

Positive confirmation requires the recipient to respond whether or not they agree; non-response is not evidence. Negative confirmation asks the recipient to respond only if they disagree; non-response is treated as implicit agreement. The two differ greatly in evidential strength.

Choose based on risk:

- positive confirmation is required for higher-risk assertions and provides reliable evidence only when responses are received;
- negative confirmation is appropriate only where risk is low, the population is large, the balances are small and uniform, and the auditor reasonably expects recipients to respond to disagreements;
- negative confirmation provides weaker evidence because non-response may reflect indifference rather than agreement.

Using negative confirmation for a high-risk assertion, because it is easier and produces fewer exceptions, leaves detection risk too high.

### Handle Non-Responses With Alternative Procedures

Non-response to a positive confirmation is not evidence that the balance is correct; it is a gap that must be filled. For each non-response, the auditor must perform alternative procedures that provide the evidence the confirmation was meant to provide.

Perform alternative procedures by:

- examining subsequent cash receipts or payments that corroborate the balance;
- vouching the balance to shipping documents, invoices, or underlying contracts;
- testing the aging and the composition of the balance;
- confirming through other independent evidence where possible.

If alternative procedures cannot be performed, or if they do not provide sufficient evidence, the auditor has a scope limitation that may require modification.

### Evaluate Responses And Investigate Exceptions

Responses, when received, must be evaluated. Exceptions, where the confirmed amount differs from the recorded amount, must be investigated, not dismissed. An exception may indicate an error, a timing difference, a dispute, or a control failure, and each possibility has different implications.

Evaluate by:

- reconciling exceptions to determine whether they represent misstatements, timing differences, or disputes;
- determining whether exceptions are isolated or indicate a systemic problem;
- assessing whether exceptions suggest the population contains more errors than the sample revealed;
- considering whether to expand testing based on the nature and frequency of exceptions.

Dismissing exceptions as "timing differences" without evidence, or accepting management's explanation without corroboration, undermines the evidential value of the confirmation.

### Recognize Indicators Of Unreliable Or Fraudulent Responses

In rare but serious cases, confirmation responses may be unreliable or fraudulent: routed through management, fabricated, or coerced. The auditor should remain alert to indicators that a response is not genuinely independent.

Watch for:

- responses received through management rather than directly to the auditor;
- responses that are unusually uniform, perfect, or inconsistent with other evidence;
- addresses or signatories that cannot be verified;
- a high rate of "no exceptions" responses that contrasts with other risk indicators;
- electronic responses from unverified email addresses or non-authenticated systems.

Where reliability is in question, the auditor must obtain corroborating evidence through other means and, if necessary, treat the confirmation as unreliable.

## Common Traps

### Confirming The Wrong Assertion

A confirmation of balance does not test valuation, completeness, or cutoff. Match the confirmation to the specific assertion at risk.

### Losing Control Of The Process

Allowing management to send, receive, or deliver confirmations destroys the independence that makes the evidence persuasive.

### Using Negative Confirmation For High Risk

Negative confirmation is weak evidence appropriate only for low-risk, uniform populations. Using it for higher risk leaves detection risk too high.

### Treating Non-Response As Agreement

Non-response to a positive confirmation is a gap, not evidence. Alternative procedures are required.

### Dismissing Exceptions Without Investigation

Exceptions may indicate errors, disputes, or systemic problems. Investigating and corroborating is required, not explaining away.

### Confirming Only Large Balances

Risk may concentrate in unusual, related-party, or disputed balances that are not the largest. Select counterparties based on risk, not only size.

### Accepting Unverified Electronic Responses

Email replies can be spoofed. Use authenticated confirmation systems or verify the source.

## Self-Check

- Is each confirmation designed to test a specific assertion, with the content, counterparty, and selection matched to the risk?
- Does the confirmation request specific and relevant information, including terms, contingent arrangements, and disputes, rather than only a balance?
- Is the confirmation process controlled by the auditor from initiation to receipt, with independently verified addresses and direct reply to the auditor?
- Is positive confirmation used for higher-risk assertions, with negative confirmation reserved for low-risk, uniform populations?
- Are non-responses to positive confirmations addressed through alternative procedures that provide the intended evidence?
- Are exceptions investigated to determine whether they represent misstatements, timing differences, disputes, or systemic problems?
- Is the auditor alert to indicators of unreliable or fraudulent responses, and are corroborating procedures performed where reliability is in question?
- Are counterparties selected based on risk, materiality, and the assertion, including unusual, related-party, and disputed balances?
- Is the conclusion supported by the confirmation evidence obtained, with gaps and limitations acknowledged rather than concealed?
- Could an independent reviewer confirm that the confirmation evidence is directly relevant, reliably controlled, and properly evaluated?
