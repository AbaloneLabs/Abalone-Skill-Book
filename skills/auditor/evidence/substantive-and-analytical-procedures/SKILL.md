# Substantive And Analytical Procedures

Substantive procedures and analytical procedures are the two families of evidence-gathering that respond directly to risks at the assertion level. Substantive testing examines individual transactions and balances. Analytical procedures evaluate relationships among data to identify unexpected patterns. Neither family is inherently stronger than the other; the right mix depends on the assertion, the predictability of the relationship, the reliability of the data, and the risk being addressed. The most common failure is choosing a procedure because it is convenient rather than because it responds to the specific risk, then claiming the assertion is covered when the evidence never addressed it.

Use this skill before designing substantive tests, planning analytical procedures, choosing between detail testing and analytics, investigating expected-versus-actual differences, or concluding on an account balance. The goal is to align each procedure with a specific assertion and risk, and to avoid treating a routine variance explanation as sufficient evidence.

## Core Rules

### Map Every Procedure To A Specific Assertion

Procedures are persuasive only when they address the assertion at risk. A confirmation tests existence and rights, but it does not test valuation or completeness. A recalculation tests accuracy but not whether the transaction occurred or belonged in the period.

For each procedure, identify:

- the assertion being addressed, such as existence, completeness, rights and obligations, valuation, accuracy, cutoff, classification, or presentation;
- the specific risk that prompted the procedure;
- the population and period covered;
- the evidence obtained;
- how the result supports or refutes the assertion.

If a procedure is not tied to an assertion, it is documentation, not evidence.

### Choose Substantive Detail Testing Where Precision Matters

Detail testing examines individual items and is most persuasive when the auditor needs precise evidence about specific transactions or balances. It is typically appropriate for large, unusual, or high-risk items, and when analytics alone cannot provide sufficient evidence.

Common substantive procedures include:

- external confirmation of balances and terms;
- inspection of contracts, invoices, and supporting documents;
- recalculation of amounts, accruals, and amortization;
- reperformance of controls-related calculations;
- physical observation of inventory or assets;
- vouching recorded items to underlying support for existence;
- tracing unrecorded items to the records for completeness;
- examining subsequent events and settlements.

Detail testing is especially important for assertions like existence, rights, and valuation where the auditor must verify specific facts about specific items.

### Use Analytical Procedures Where Relationships Are Predictable

Analytical procedures compare recorded amounts to expectations developed independently. They are powerful when the relationship is predictable, the data is reliable, and the expected amount can be estimated with reasonable precision.

Suitable situations include:

- revenue compared to prior periods, budgets, or industry benchmarks;
- gross margin analysis across periods or product lines;
- expense ratios and per-unit metrics;
- interest expense compared to debt balances and rates;
- payroll compared to headcount;
- inventory turnover;
- allowance changes relative to receivable aging;
- tax expense relative to pre-tax income.

Develop an expectation before looking at the result. The expectation should come from a source independent of the recorded amount, such as history, budgets, nonfinancial measures, or industry data.

### Assess Predictability And Data Reliability Before Relying On Analytics

Analytical procedures are only as reliable as the relationship and the data behind them. A relationship that changed during the period, or data that is unreliable, weakens the procedure.

Evaluate:

- whether the relationship is stable and predictable;
- whether the data is complete and accurate;
- whether the data source is independent of the process being tested;
- whether the period is comparable, including business changes, acquisitions, or reorganizations;
- whether seasonality affects the comparison;
- whether the expectation is precise enough to detect a material misstatement.

If the relationship is volatile or the data is unreliable, either corroborate the analytics with detail testing or do not rely on them for the primary evidence.

### Define What Counts As An Investigable Difference

An analytical procedure is not complete when the numbers are compared. It is complete when the auditor determines whether the difference is expected, explains it with corroborated evidence, or treats it as a potential misstatement.

Define in advance:

- the threshold above which a difference is investigated;
- the expected direction and magnitude of normal variation;
- the sources of explanation that will be accepted;
- whether management explanations require corroboration.

A difference within threshold may be accepted, but the threshold should be tied to materiality and the precision of the expectation, not to convenience.

### Investigate Differences With Professional Skepticism

When a difference exceeds the threshold, investigate it. Do not accept the first plausible explanation without support.

Investigation should:

- identify the specific items or periods driving the difference;
- obtain corroborating evidence for the explanation;
- distinguish a real business cause from a recording error;
- consider whether the difference indicates fraud, cutoff problems, or estimate bias;
- document the explanation and the evidence supporting it;
- conclude whether the difference is acceptable or a potential misstatement.

A plausible story is not an explanation. Corroborated evidence is.

### Combine Procedures To Cover Assertions Adequately

A single procedure rarely covers all assertions for an account. Revenue, for example, may require cutoff testing for the cutoff assertion, confirmations or tracing for existence, analytics for completeness and reasonableness, and contract review for rights and presentation.

Build a procedure set that together covers:

- existence and occurrence;
- completeness;
- rights and obligations;
- valuation and allocation;
- accuracy;
- cutoff;
- classification;
- presentation and disclosure.

Where one procedure is weak for an assertion, add another. Do not claim coverage based on a procedure that does not address the assertion.

### Respond To Risk With Procedure Intensity

Higher risk warrants more persuasive evidence, more extensive testing, or procedures closer to the transaction and balance date.

Increase intensity when:

- fraud risk is present;
- management override is possible;
- estimates and judgment are significant;
- prior-year errors existed;
- the process is manual or recently changed;
- the assertion is complex or hard to verify.

For high-risk areas, consider substantive procedures performed at or near period end, larger or targeted samples, external evidence, and corroboration of management estimates through specialists or independent data.

### Test Estimates And Fair Values With Discipline

Estimates, reserves, and fair value measurements are inherently judgmental and are common sources of misstatement. Testing them requires evaluating the process, the assumptions, and the outcome.

For estimates, consider:

- the process management used to develop the estimate;
- the assumptions and whether they are reasonable and consistent;
- comparable data from independent sources;
- subsequent events that confirm or refute the estimate;
- specialist involvement for complex valuations;
- sensitivity of the estimate to assumption changes;
- bias in prior estimates compared to outcomes.

Do not accept an estimate because management prepared it. Evaluate the evidence behind the assumptions.

## Common Traps

### Relying On Analytics Where The Relationship Is Unpredictable

If revenue is driven by unpredictable one-time contracts, period-over-period comparison is weak evidence. Use detail testing where the relationship is not stable.

### Accepting Management Explanation Without Corroboration

Management often explains a variance with a reason that sounds right. Unless the reason is corroborated by independent evidence, it is an assertion, not an explanation.

### Testing Existence But Claiming Completeness

Vouching recorded items to support proves the items exist, but it cannot prove that all items were recorded. Completeness requires tracing from source to records, the opposite direction.

### Treating A Recalculation As Evidence Of Valuation

Recalculating an accrual proves the arithmetic is correct. It does not prove the underlying assumption is reasonable. Valuation requires testing the assumption, not just the math.

### Using Subsequent Settlement As The Only Procedure

Subsequent cash receipt can confirm existence and collectibility, but it may not address cutoff, rights, or valuation if the receipt relates to a different period or arrangement.

### Ignoring The Direction Of Testing

Testing from the records to the source tests existence or occurrence. Testing from the source to the records tests completeness. Using the wrong direction can leave an assertion unaddressed while looking thorough.

### Overstating What A Procedure Covers

A single confirmation does not cover valuation, classification, or completeness. State what each procedure addresses and add procedures where coverage is thin.

## Self-Check

- Is each procedure mapped to a specific assertion and to the risk that prompted it?
- Are substantive detail tests used where precision over specific items is required, such as existence, rights, and valuation?
- Are analytical procedures applied only where the relationship is predictable and the data is reliable?
- Is an independent expectation developed before the recorded amount is evaluated?
- Is the investigable difference threshold tied to materiality and the precision of the expectation?
- Are differences investigated with corroborated evidence rather than accepted management explanation?
- Does the combined procedure set cover all relevant assertions, including completeness through the correct direction of testing?
- Are estimates and fair values tested for process, assumptions, independent data, subsequent events, and bias?
- Does procedure intensity respond to assessed risk, fraud, override, and complexity?
- Does the conclusion state which assertions are supported, by which evidence, and which assertions remain limited?
