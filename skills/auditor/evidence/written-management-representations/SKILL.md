# Written Management Representations

Written representations from management are a required part of an audit, but they are not, and can never be, a substitute for other evidence. Their role is specific: they confirm management's responsibility for the financial statements, they record management's assertions on matters where it has unique knowledge, and they reduce the risk of misunderstanding about what management has told the auditor. The recurring failure is inverting this role, treating the representation as evidence that closes a gap where procedures could not reach. When a representation letter is used to cover missing evidence, untested assertions, or unresolved exceptions, the audit has accepted management's word in place of the independent evidence the standard requires. A representation obtained after the conclusion, or drafted to fit whatever the auditor decided, is not a representation; it is a document manufactured to complete the file.

Use this skill when obtaining, drafting, and evaluating written representations, when deciding which representations are required, and when management refuses or qualifies a representation. The goal is to use representations for their proper purpose, corroborated by other evidence, and never as a substitute for the procedures the audit requires.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Proper Role And Limits Of Representations

A written representation is management's acknowledgment of its responsibilities and assertions. It is necessary but not sufficient. It does not, by itself, provide audit evidence that reduces detection risk, and it cannot substitute for procedures that the auditor could not or did not perform.

Recognize that representations:

- confirm management's responsibility for the financial statements and internal control;
- record management's assertions on matters within its unique knowledge, such as intentions, plans, or future events;
- confirm that management has provided all relevant information and records;
- reduce the risk of misunderstanding between management and the auditor;
- do not constitute sufficient appropriate evidence on their own for any material assertion.

Treating a representation as evidence that closes a procedural gap is the core error this skill prevents.

### Obtain The Required Representations

Certain representations are required by the applicable standard, and their absence is a scope limitation. The auditor must obtain these representations, in writing, from management with appropriate responsibility and knowledge.

Required representations typically include:

- management's responsibility for the financial statements and the applicable framework;
- management's responsibility for the design and implementation of internal control;
- that management has provided all financial records and related information;
- that all transactions have been recorded and are reflected in the financial statements;
- that management believes the effects of uncorrected misstatements are immaterial;
- representations regarding subsequent events, litigation and claims, related parties, estimates, and fraud.

The specific required representations depend on the framework. Confirm which are required and obtain each, tailored to the engagement's circumstances.

### Draft The Representation To Reflect What Was Actually Discussed

The representation letter should record what management actually asserted during the engagement, not what the auditor wishes it had asserted. A representation drafted to support a conclusion, after the fact, with language management did not originate, is not a genuine representation.

Draft by:

- basing the representation on actual discussions, inquiries, and management's stated positions;
- using language that management understands and can genuinely affirm;
- avoiding wording that subtly shifts responsibility or manufactures assurance;
- dating the representation as of the audit report date, or close to it, so it covers events through the end of fieldwork;
- having it signed by those with appropriate responsibility and knowledge, typically the CEO and CFO.

A representation that management signs without understanding, or that records assertions management did not make, provides no genuine evidence and may itself be misleading.

### Corroborate Significant Representations With Independent Evidence

Because representations are the weakest form of evidence, significant representations must be corroborated by other procedures. The corroboration does not need to re-prove everything, but it must provide independent support for the matters represented.

Corroborate by:

- testing the underlying transactions, balances, or events the representation concerns;
- obtaining external evidence where the representation involves third parties;
- examining supporting documentation for estimates, intentions, and plans;
- comparing the representation to other evidence obtained during the audit;
- identifying and resolving any contradiction between the representation and other evidence.

A significant representation that stands alone, with no corroboration, cannot support a conclusion on a material matter.

### Handle Refusal Or Qualification Of A Representation

If management refuses to provide a required representation, or qualifies it in a way that undermines its purpose, the auditor faces a scope limitation. The response is not to proceed as if the representation were obtained, but to assess the effect on the audit and the opinion.

Respond to refusal or qualification by:

- discussing the matter with management and governance to understand the reason;
- assessing whether the refusal relates to a material matter;
- considering whether the refusal indicates a broader integrity or control problem;
- where the matter is material, modifying the opinion, typically through a scope qualification or disclaimer;
- documenting the refusal and its effect on the engagement.

A refusal to provide a required representation is a serious matter. It may indicate that management cannot or will not stand behind the assertions on which the financial statements rest.

### Do Not Use Representations To Cover Procedural Gaps

The most dangerous misuse of representations is to substitute them for procedures the auditor could not perform. Where evidence is unavailable, the correct response is a scope limitation and possible modification, not a representation that asserts the matter is fine.

Avoid misuse by:

- recognizing that a representation cannot replace procedures that were not performed;
- distinguishing between matters within management's unique knowledge, where a representation is appropriate, and matters that require independent evidence;
- documenting the procedures performed, not only the representation obtained;
- escalating to a scope limitation where evidence is genuinely unavailable.

Using a representation to manufacture assurance where evidence is missing is a fundamental failure that can invalidate the engagement.

### Recognize Indicators That A Representation May Be Unreliable

In some cases, a representation may be unreliable because management lacks the knowledge, has an incentive to misrepresent, or is itself implicated in the matter. The auditor should assess whether the representation is credible given the circumstances.

Watch for:

- representations from individuals who lack responsibility or knowledge of the matter;
- representations that conflict with other evidence or with management's prior statements;
- representations on matters where management has a strong incentive to misrepresent;
- representations obtained under pressure or after disagreement;
- a pattern of management providing whatever representation the auditor requests.

Where reliability is in question, seek additional corroboration and consider whether the representation can be relied upon at all.

## Common Traps

### Treating Representations As Substitute Evidence

A representation cannot close a procedural gap or reduce detection risk on its own. It must be corroborated.

### Drafting To Fit The Conclusion

A representation manufactured after the conclusion, with language management did not originate, is not a genuine representation.

### Omitting Required Representations

Certain representations are mandatory. Their absence is a scope limitation, not a minor documentation gap.

### Accepting Refusal Without Consequence

Management refusal to provide a required representation, on a material matter, requires opinion modification, not silent acceptance.

### Using Representations For Matters Requiring Independent Evidence

Matters such as existence, valuation, or accuracy require independent procedures, not management's assertion.

### Signing Without Understanding

A representation signed by management that does not understand its content provides no genuine evidence.

### Ignoring Contradictions With Other Evidence

A representation that conflicts with other evidence must be resolved, not preferred because it is convenient.

## Self-Check

- Are written representations understood as necessary but not sufficient, confirming responsibility and assertions rather than substituting for evidence?
- Are all required representations obtained, tailored to the engagement's circumstances and the applicable framework?
- Is the representation letter based on actual discussions and management's genuine assertions, dated appropriately, and signed by those with responsibility and knowledge?
- Are significant representations corroborated by independent procedures, with contradictions identified and resolved?
- If management refuses or qualifies a required representation, is the effect on the audit assessed and the opinion modified where the matter is material?
- Are representations not used to cover procedural gaps, with scope limitations acknowledged and addressed through modification where evidence is unavailable?
- Are indicators of unreliable representations, such as lack of knowledge, incentive to misrepresent, or conflict with other evidence, recognized and addressed?
- Are matters within management's unique knowledge distinguished from matters requiring independent evidence, with representations used only for the former?
- Is the documentation of representations supported by the procedures performed, so the file shows corroboration rather than reliance on assertion alone?
- Could an independent reviewer confirm that the representations serve their proper role and that no conclusion rests on a representation standing alone?
