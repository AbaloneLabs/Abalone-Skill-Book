# Information Produced By The Entity Testing

Almost every modern audit depends on reports, extracts, and data produced by the entity's own systems. The auditor tests samples from these reports, controls operate on them, and analytical procedures are built on them. Yet the reports themselves are evidence whose reliability must be established before anything built on them can be trusted. The recurring failure is treating entity-produced reports as given, assuming that a system-generated extract is accurate because it came from a computer, and performing all subsequent testing on a population whose completeness and accuracy were never validated. When the report is missing manual entries, excludes a location, or uses the wrong filter, every sample drawn from it, every control that relies on it, and every analytical conclusion built on it inherits the error. Testing the report is not a preliminary formality; it is the foundation on which the rest of the evidence rests.

Use this skill before using any entity-produced report, extract, or dataset as audit evidence or as the basis for a control, and whenever the reliability of entity-prepared information is in question. The goal is to establish, through testing, that the information is complete, accurate, and valid for its intended use, before relying on it.

## Core Rules

### Recognize That Entity-Produced Information Is Evidence Requiring Testing

A report generated by the entity's system is not inherently reliable simply because it is system-produced. Systems produce incorrect reports when filters are wrong, when manual entries are excluded, when interfaces fail, or when the logic is mis-specified. The auditor must treat entity-produced information as evidence whose accuracy and completeness must be tested, not assumed.

Apply testing to:

- any report or extract used to select samples or populations;
- any report used by a control the auditor intends to rely on;
- any data used in analytical procedures;
- any schedule or reconciliation provided by management that supports a balance;
- any extract used for data analytics or journal entry testing.

If the audit relies on the report, the report's reliability must be established first.

### Test Completeness: Confirm Nothing Material Is Missing

Completeness asks whether the report contains all the transactions or records it should. A report that excludes manual entries, a location, a transaction type, or a period segment produces a population that looks complete but is not, and testing from it will miss the excluded items entirely.

Test completeness by:

- reconciling the report total to the general ledger or another authoritative control total;
- confirming the population includes all relevant entities, locations, and transaction types;
- verifying that manual, off-system, or late entries are captured, not silently excluded;
- checking that the date range and status filters include all in-scope transactions;
- comparing record counts and amounts to independent expectations.

A report that does not reconcile to the control total, or whose population cannot be explained, is incomplete and cannot be relied upon without resolving the discrepancy.

### Test Accuracy: Confirm Values And Fields Are Correct

Accuracy asks whether the values and fields in the report are correct. A report may be complete but contain errors in amounts, dates, account codes, or other key fields, which would corrupt any testing or analytics built on it.

Test accuracy by:

- tracing a sample of report items back to source records to confirm amounts and attributes;
- recalculating derived fields, such as aging, totals, or subtotals;
- verifying key fields, such as dates, account codes, and identifiers, against source data;
- checking for duplicate records or data corruption;
- confirming currency, rounding, and unit handling where relevant.

Errors in key fields can invalidate sampling, analytics, and control reliance, even when the report reconciles in total.

### Test Validity And Logic: Confirm The Report Does What It Claims

Validity asks whether the report's logic and parameters produce the intended output. A report may be complete and accurate for what it extracts, but if the logic excludes the wrong transactions or applies the wrong criteria, it is valid for the wrong question.

Test validity by:

- inspecting the report specification, parameters, and filters against the intended purpose;
- testing the report with known transactions that should and should not appear, to confirm it behaves as intended;
- confirming that the logic has not changed during the period without the auditor's knowledge;
- verifying that the report is generated from the authoritative system, not a secondary or manual copy.

A report whose logic is mis-specified produces a population that answers a different question than the one the audit is asking.

### Reconcile To Authoritative Sources

Reconciliation is the most powerful completeness and accuracy test. A report that reconciles to the general ledger, a subledger, or another authoritative total provides evidence that its population and totals are consistent with the entity's records. A report that does not reconcile has a discrepancy that must be understood before the report can be used.

Reconcile by:

- tying report totals to the general ledger or trial balance;
- tying subledger reports to their control accounts;
- reconciling extracts to system-generated counts and amounts;
- investigating and resolving any unreconciled difference, no matter how small, if it could indicate a population gap.

An unreconciled difference is not a rounding issue until proven to be one. It may indicate excluded transactions, duplication, or logic errors.

### Assess The System And Controls Behind The Report

The reliability of a report is influenced by the reliability of the system and controls that produce it. A report from a well-controlled system, with effective IT general controls and tested report logic, is more reliable than one from a system with weak change management or uncontrolled manual adjustments.

Assess the system by:

- evaluating IT general controls over the system, particularly change and access controls;
- confirming that report logic changes are controlled and documented;
- assessing whether manual adjustments to the report are controlled and approved;
- considering whether the system has a history of reliable reporting or known issues.

Where system controls are weak, the report requires more extensive completeness and accuracy testing before it can be relied upon.

### Document The Testing And Its Conclusion

The testing of entity-produced information must be documented so that a reviewer can see what was tested, how, and what conclusion was reached about reliability. A report used throughout the audit without documented testing leaves the foundation of the evidence unverified.

Document by:

- recording the report source, generation date, parameters, and population;
- documenting the completeness, accuracy, and validity tests performed;
- recording reconciliations and the resolution of any differences;
- stating the conclusion on the report's reliability for its intended use;
- cross-referencing the report testing to the procedures that rely on it.

Documentation that shows the report was tested makes the rest of the audit's evidence defensible; its absence leaves the foundation assumed rather than established.

## Common Traps

### Assuming System-Generated Means Accurate

Computers produce incorrect reports when logic, filters, or interfaces are wrong. System generation does not establish reliability.

### Skipping Completeness Testing

A report that excludes manual entries, a location, or a transaction type looks complete but is not. Reconcile to the control total.

### Testing Samples From An Untested Population

All sampling, analytics, and control reliance built on an untested report inherit the report's errors. Test the report first.

### Ignoring Unreconciled Differences

A difference between the report and the control total may indicate excluded transactions or duplication. Investigate before using the report.

### Accepting The Report Logic Without Inspection

A report with mis-specified filters or logic answers the wrong question. Inspect the specification and test with known transactions.

### Not Considering System And Control Reliability

A report from a poorly controlled system is less reliable. Weak IT general controls require more extensive report testing.

### Using A Manual Copy Or Secondary Extract

Reports copied, exported, or manually adjusted after generation may differ from the authoritative source. Use the system-generated original.

## Self-Check

- Is entity-produced information recognized as evidence requiring testing for completeness, accuracy, and validity before it is relied upon?
- Has completeness been tested by reconciling the report to the general ledger or authoritative control total, with population scope, manual entries, and filters confirmed?
- Has accuracy been tested by tracing items to source records, recalculating derived fields, and verifying key fields?
- Has validity been tested by inspecting report logic and parameters and testing with known transactions that should and should not appear?
- Have unreconciled differences been investigated and resolved, rather than dismissed as rounding?
- Has the reliability of the system and its IT general controls been assessed, with more extensive testing where controls are weak?
- Is the report an authoritative system-generated original, not a manual copy or secondary extract?
- Is the testing documented, including source, parameters, tests performed, reconciliations, and the conclusion on reliability?
- Does the documentation cross-reference the report testing to the procedures that rely on it, so the foundation is visible?
- Could an independent reviewer confirm that the report's reliability was established before it was used, rather than assumed?
