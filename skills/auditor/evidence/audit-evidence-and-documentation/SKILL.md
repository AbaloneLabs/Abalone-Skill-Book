# Audit Evidence And Documentation

Audit evidence is useful only when it supports the exact conclusion being drawn. A workpaper can be long and still fail if it does not show the procedure performed, the population tested, the evidence obtained, the exceptions found, the reasoning applied, and the conclusion reached. Good documentation should allow an experienced auditor with no prior involvement to understand what was done and why the conclusion is supported.

Use this skill before performing audit testing, documenting workpapers, evaluating samples, relying on reports, reviewing evidence, or deciding whether a conclusion is ready. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating screenshots, spreadsheets, interviews, or management explanations as sufficient without testing relevance and reliability.

## Core Rules

### Tie Evidence To A Specific Assertion Or Criterion

Evidence should support a specific audit objective, criterion, risk, or assertion. Do not collect documents first and decide later what they prove.

For each test, document:

- audit objective;
- criterion or assertion;
- population;
- period covered;
- procedure performed;
- evidence source;
- attribute tested;
- exception definition;
- result;
- conclusion.

A vendor invoice may prove amount and vendor but not approval, business purpose, receipt of goods, correct period, or compliance with contract terms. Name the assertion being tested.

### Evaluate Sufficiency And Appropriateness Separately

Sufficiency is about quantity. Appropriateness is about relevance and reliability. More documents do not help if they do not address the objective.

Assess relevance:

- Does the evidence relate to the exact period?
- Does it test the right population?
- Does it address the right attribute?
- Does it prove the assertion or only suggest it?
- Does it include all required facts?

Assess reliability:

- Is the source independent?
- Is the evidence original, system-generated, or manually prepared?
- Can it be altered?
- Is the system reliable?
- Is the preparer competent?
- Is there corroborating evidence?
- Does contradictory evidence exist?

Strong evidence is both relevant and reliable. Weak evidence should be corroborated or treated as a limitation.

### Test Population Completeness And Accuracy

Sampling or analytics are only meaningful if the population is complete and accurate. An extract missing canceled transactions, manual entries, late adjustments, inactive vendors, or one location can mislead the audit.

Before testing, verify:

- source system;
- extraction date;
- period filters;
- included and excluded statuses;
- entity and location filters;
- reconciliation to general ledger or control total;
- record count;
- total amount;
- key fields;
- duplicate handling;
- manual adjustments;
- data owner confirmation.

If the population cannot be reconciled or validated, document the limitation and reconsider the testing approach.

### Design Procedures That Match The Risk

Testing should respond to risk. Low-risk routine attributes may support limited testing. High-risk areas require more persuasive evidence, larger or targeted samples, additional procedures, or specialist involvement.

Match procedures to:

- inherent risk;
- control risk;
- fraud risk;
- prior exceptions;
- transaction complexity;
- management judgment;
- manual intervention;
- system changes;
- data reliability;
- materiality or significance.

Use inquiry alone only when it is sufficient for the objective. For many audit conclusions, inquiry must be corroborated by inspection, observation, reperformance, recalculation, confirmation, analytics, or other evidence.

### Use Sampling Deliberately

Sampling is not simply choosing examples. The sample should be tied to the population, risk, expected exception rate, tolerable deviation or misstatement, and conclusion needed.

Document:

- sampling method;
- population size;
- sample size;
- selection rationale;
- random, systematic, haphazard, stratified, or targeted approach;
- high-value or high-risk items selected separately;
- replacement rules;
- exception evaluation;
- projection method if applicable;
- limitation on conclusion.

Targeted samples can identify issues but may not support a population-level conclusion. Random or statistical samples can support broader conclusions when designed correctly.

### Handle Exceptions With Discipline

An exception is not automatically a finding, but it is also not something to explain away casually. Each exception should be evaluated against criteria and risk.

For each exception, determine:

- what criterion was not met;
- whether evidence is missing or the activity failed;
- whether the exception is isolated or systemic;
- monetary or operational impact;
- control implication;
- root cause;
- whether management explanation is supported;
- whether additional testing is needed;
- whether the population conclusion changes.

Do not replace missing evidence with a confident story. If management says the document exists, obtain it or document that it was not provided.

### Consider Contradictory Evidence

Audit quality depends on professional skepticism. Evidence inconsistent with the expected conclusion must be retained, evaluated, and resolved. Ignoring contradictory evidence weakens the entire workpaper.

Examples:

- report totals do not tie to ledger;
- policy says approval is required but transactions lack approval;
- interview statements conflict;
- system timestamp differs from document date;
- email contradicts formal representation;
- same vendor appears under multiple names;
- reconciliations contain stale items;
- management explanation changes after challenge.

Document the contradiction, follow-up procedures, resolution, and effect on the conclusion.

### Document Work So It Can Be Reperformed Or Reviewed

Workpapers should show what was done without relying on the preparer's memory.

Include:

- purpose;
- source documents;
- procedure;
- population;
- sample;
- tickmark legend;
- reviewer notes;
- preparer and date;
- reviewer and date;
- exceptions;
- conclusion;
- cross-references;
- unresolved items;
- evidence location.

If a reviewer cannot tell what was tested, which evidence was used, or how the conclusion follows, the workpaper is not ready.

### Protect Evidence Integrity And Confidentiality

Audit evidence often contains payroll, tax, customer, vendor, bank, legal, health, security, or personal data. Preserve integrity and confidentiality.

Control:

- access permissions;
- redaction where full detail is unnecessary;
- secure transfer;
- file naming;
- version control;
- chain of custody for sensitive evidence;
- retention period;
- deletion rules;
- restrictions on sharing screenshots or exports.

Do not store sensitive evidence in uncontrolled notes, chats, or personal drives.

## Common Traps

### Treating Inquiry As Proof

Interviews help understand processes and explain exceptions, but unsupported inquiry rarely proves that a control operated or a transaction complied. Corroborate important statements.

### Accepting Screenshots Without Context

Screenshots can be altered, incomplete, or captured after the fact. Document source, date, preparer, system, transaction ID, and what the screenshot proves. Use system reports or direct access where stronger evidence is needed.

### Testing A Sample From The Wrong Population

If the population excludes the risky transactions, the test is weak. Check filters, date ranges, statuses, locations, manual entries, and system migrations before selecting samples.

### Overgeneralizing From Targeted Testing

Targeted testing is useful for high-risk items, but it does not automatically support a population-wide error rate. State what the test can and cannot support.

### Failing To Retain Contradictory Evidence

Deleting or ignoring evidence that challenges the conclusion undermines documentation. Retain the evidence and document how it was resolved.

### Writing Conclusions That Do Not Match Results

If exceptions were found, the conclusion should explain their effect. Do not write "no issues noted" when issues were noted but judged immaterial or isolated.

### Using Workpaper Templates As A Substitute For Thought

Templates standardize documentation, but they cannot decide whether evidence is persuasive. Remove irrelevant template sections and add procedures where risk requires them.

## Self-Check

- Does each workpaper identify the objective, criterion, assertion, population, period, procedure, evidence, result, and conclusion?
- Is evidence evaluated for both sufficiency and appropriateness?
- Was population completeness and accuracy tested before sampling or analytics?
- Do procedures match assessed risk, complexity, materiality, fraud risk, and data reliability?
- Is the sampling method documented with population, sample size, selection rationale, and conclusion limits?
- Are exceptions evaluated for criterion failure, impact, cause, control implication, and need for additional testing?
- Is contradictory evidence retained, investigated, and resolved in the documentation?
- Could an experienced auditor understand what was done and why the conclusion is supported without asking the preparer?
- Are sensitive evidence, exports, screenshots, and workpapers protected by access and retention controls?
- Does the conclusion match the actual results and limitations of the work performed?
