# Economy Assessment

Economy is the first of the operational dimensions: acquiring the resources the organization needs, of the right quality and quantity, at the right cost and time. It is about the input side, before efficiency and effectiveness come into play. Economy seems straightforward, because it is often equated with getting the lowest price, but this is where the assessment goes wrong. Lowest-price acquisition that brings poor quality, excess quantity, late delivery, or hidden downstream cost is not economical; it is false economy. True economy balances acquisition cost against quality, quantity, timing, and total cost of ownership, recognizing that a cheap input that causes rework, failure, or risk may cost more overall than a higher-priced alternative. The economy assessment examines how resources are sourced, whether the organization pays a fair and competitive price for what it genuinely needs, and whether acquisition practices support or undermine the downstream objectives of efficiency and effectiveness.

Use this skill when assessing acquisition and procurement economy, evaluating sourcing practices, examining the balance of cost, quality, and quantity, or developing economy findings. The goal is to assess whether resources are acquired economically in the full sense, not merely at the lowest invoice price.

## Core Rules

### Define Economy As Optimal Acquisition, Not Lowest Price

Economy is acquiring the right resources, of the right quality and quantity, at the right cost and time. The emphasis on "right" across all dimensions distinguishes economy from mere cheapness. An economical acquisition meets the organization's actual needs at a fair and competitive cost, without overpaying and without sacrificing what is needed to secure a low price.

Define economy by assessing:

- is the resource of the quality the operation requires, neither higher than needed nor lower than acceptable?
- is the quantity appropriate to need, without excess that creates waste or shortage that disrupts operations?
- is the cost fair and competitive for the quality and quantity acquired, reflecting market conditions and volume?
- is the timing appropriate, so resources are available when needed without excessive inventory or rush premiums?

Lowest price is economical only when it also meets the quality, quantity, and timing requirements. Otherwise it is a saving that costs more elsewhere.

### Evaluate Total Cost Of Ownership, Not Just Acquisition Price

The invoice price is only part of what a resource costs the organization. Total cost of ownership includes acquisition, delivery, installation, operation, maintenance, support, training, and disposal. A resource with a low acquisition price but high operating, maintenance, or failure cost may be more expensive overall than a higher-priced alternative. Economy assessment must consider the full cost, not just the purchase.

Evaluate total cost by:

- identifying the costs beyond acquisition, including operating, maintenance, support, and end-of-life;
- comparing alternatives on total cost, not just purchase price;
- recognizing hidden costs such as integration, training, downtime, or disposal;
- considering the cost of poor quality, including rework, warranty, and reputational impact;
- assessing whether a low-price acquisition has created downstream costs that exceed the saving.

False economy, cheap now, expensive later, is a common finding. Total cost reveals where the apparent saving is illusory.

### Assess Whether Procurement Achieves Competitive And Fair Pricing

Economy requires that the organization pay a fair and competitive price for what it acquires. This does not always mean the lowest price, but it means the price reflects market conditions, the quality and quantity purchased, and appropriate negotiation. Procurement practices that do not test the market, that rely on single sources without justification, or that accept quoted prices without challenge, may result in overpayment.

Assess pricing by:

- examining whether procurement uses competitive processes where appropriate, such as bidding or market testing;
- evaluating single-source or sole-source arrangements for justification and value;
- comparing prices paid to market benchmarks, prior prices, or prices for comparable purchases;
- assessing whether volume, terms, or relationships are leveraged to achieve fair pricing;
- identifying instances of overpayment, split purchases, or avoidance of competitive thresholds.

Overpayment is a direct economy failure. The assessment should identify where the organization is paying more than it should for what it needs.

### Balance Cost Against Quality And Risk

Economy is not achieved by sacrificing quality or increasing risk to secure a lower price. Resources that are too low in quality, or that introduce risk through unreliable supply or poor performance, undermine the operations they support. The economical choice is the one that meets quality and risk requirements at a fair cost, not the one that strips these out to reduce price.

Balance by assessing:

- does the acquired resource meet the quality requirements of the operation it supports?
- does the lower-cost option introduce risk, such as supply interruption, failure, or compliance exposure?
- is the quality reduction, if any, a deliberate and documented decision, or an unintended consequence of cost pressure?
- would a higher-quality or more reliable alternative reduce total cost or risk enough to justify its higher price?

An economy finding that recommends a lower-price option without considering quality and risk is incomplete and potentially harmful. The balance must be explicit.

### Examine Quantity And Timing For Appropriateness

Economy includes acquiring the right quantity at the right time. Over-acquisition ties up capital and creates waste through obsolescence, spoilage, or storage cost; under-acquisition disrupts operations and may force rush purchases at premium prices. Similarly, acquiring too early creates inventory cost; acquiring too late creates disruption or rush premiums. Economy assessment should examine whether quantity and timing are well managed.

Examine by:

- assessing whether quantities purchased align with need, considering usage rates, lead times, and storage costs;
- evaluating inventory levels for excess, obsolescence, or spoilage that indicate over-acquisition;
- examining rush or premium purchases that may indicate poor planning or under-acquisition;
- assessing the balance between holding cost and stockout risk in inventory decisions.

Quantity and timing are economy dimensions that procurement assessments often overlook. Both affect the true cost of acquisition.

### Identify The Root Causes Of Uneconomical Practice

Economy findings are most valuable when they identify why uneconomical practice occurs. Root causes may include weak procurement controls, decentralized purchasing without leverage, poor specifications, supplier dependence, urgency-driven buying, or incentives that reward low price regardless of total cost. Addressing the cause produces sustained improvement.

Identify root causes by:

- analyzing procurement processes for weaknesses in competition, specification, or approval;
- examining purchasing patterns for decentralization, fragmentation, or threshold avoidance;
- assessing specifications for over-specification, which raises cost, or under-specification, which creates quality and risk cost;
- considering management practices and incentives that may drive uneconomical decisions;
- distinguishing causes within procurement's control from those requiring broader organizational change.

A finding that identifies the cause enables effective recommendations. One that reports only the overpayment leaves management to investigate.

### Connect Economy To Efficiency And Effectiveness

Economy is the input dimension, and it interacts with efficiency and effectiveness. Economical acquisition supports efficiency by providing the right inputs at fair cost; uneconomical acquisition undermines it. Similarly, economy that sacrifices quality or reliability undermines effectiveness downstream. The assessment should consider these connections, so economy findings are not developed in isolation from their operational consequences.

Connect by:

- assessing whether uneconomical acquisition has created efficiency problems, such as rework or excess processing;
- evaluating whether cost-driven acquisition choices have affected effectiveness or quality;
- recognizing where economy improvements would support or require changes in efficiency and effectiveness;
- framing findings in terms of the overall operational impact, not just the input cost.

An economy finding that ignores its downstream effects is incomplete. The organization needs to understand how acquisition practice supports or undermines the operations it feeds.

## Common Traps

### Equating Economy With Lowest Price

Acquiring at the lowest price without regard to quality, quantity, timing, or total cost is false economy. Define economy as optimal acquisition.

### Ignoring Total Cost Of Ownership

Comparing alternatives on acquisition price alone misses operating, maintenance, and failure costs that may make the cheap option more expensive overall. Evaluate total cost.

### Overlooking Quality And Risk Effects

Recommending lower-cost options that sacrifice quality or increase risk undermines the operations supported. Balance cost against quality and risk explicitly.

### Quantity And Timing Neglected

Focusing on unit price while ignoring over- or under-acquisition, inventory cost, and rush premiums misses economy dimensions. Examine quantity and timing.

### Symptom Findings Without Root Cause

Reporting overpayment or waste without identifying why it occurs leaves management to investigate. Identify root causes.

### Single-Source Without Justification

Accepting single-source arrangements without evaluating their justification or value may result in overpayment. Assess sourcing critically.

### Economy Assessed In Isolation

Evaluating acquisition cost without considering effects on efficiency and effectiveness misses the operational impact. Connect the dimensions.

## Self-Check

- Is economy defined as acquiring the right resources of right quality and quantity at right cost and time, rather than as lowest price?
- Is total cost of ownership evaluated, including operating, maintenance, support, and end-of-life costs, not just acquisition price?
- Does the assessment examine whether procurement achieves competitive and fair pricing through appropriate processes and benchmarking?
- Is cost balanced against quality and risk, with lower-cost options assessed for their effect on the operations supported?
- Are quantity and timing examined for appropriateness, including inventory levels, obsolescence, and rush purchases?
- Are root causes of uneconomical practice identified, including process, specification, sourcing, and incentive factors?
- Is false economy, cheap acquisition creating downstream cost, identified where it exists?
- Are economy findings connected to their effects on efficiency and effectiveness, rather than assessed in isolation?
- Are findings presented with measured support, including price comparisons, total cost analysis, and quantified gaps?
- Could management understand from the assessment whether resources are acquired economically in the full sense, not merely at low invoice price?
