# Materiality Revision During Audit

Materiality set at planning is provisional. It reflects what the auditor knew at the start, and what the auditor knows at the start is never the whole picture. During fieldwork the benchmark may move, restatements may surface, fraud risk may emerge, controls may fail, and scope may change. Each of these can make the planning threshold wrong. Agents frequently set materiality once, file it, and never revisit it, which leaves the audit testing against a threshold that no longer matches the risk and the economics. A materiality figure that is not revisited is not a conservative choice; it is a stale one.

Use this skill whenever fieldwork surfaces information that could change the benchmark, the risk profile, the control environment, or the scope, and whenever deciding whether to revise overall materiality, performance materiality, or tolerable misstatement during the engagement. The goal is to keep the threshold current and to propagate any revision through sample sizes, procedures, and previously evaluated errors.

## Core Rules

### Treat Planning Materiality As Provisional

The default posture should be that planning materiality may need revision, not that it is fixed. Build a deliberate checkpoint into the engagement rather than assuming the planning figure survives to completion.

Recognize materiality as provisional because:

- the benchmark figure is often unaudited at planning and changes as fieldwork progresses;
- risk assessment deepens during walkthroughs and testing;
- control conclusions emerge only after operating effectiveness testing;
- fraud indicators and unusual transactions can surface late;
- scope and entity boundaries can shift during the engagement.

Schedule a materiality reassessment at a defined fieldwork milestone, and revisit it whenever a triggering event occurs.

### Identify The Triggers That Require Reassessment

Revision is not optional when certain information surfaces. Treat the following as triggers that demand a documented reassessment.

Reassess materiality when:

- the benchmark figure changes materially after planning, due to restatement, audit adjustment, late entries, or error correction;
- new fraud risk, illegal act, or related-party matter emerges;
- going-concern doubts increase or a material uncertainty arises;
- a control deficiency is found that raises expected misstatement;
- a covenant or regulatory threshold is closer than expected at planning;
- the scope changes, expands, is restricted, or new components are added;
- errors cluster in one account or assertion, indicating higher risk than planned;
- qualitative facts emerge that were unknown at planning;
- a significant transaction, acquisition, or disposal occurs during the audit period;
- prior-period errors are discovered that affect comparability.

Each trigger should prompt a documented decision: revise, or consciously confirm the existing threshold with reasons.

### Distinguish Benchmark Movement From Percentage Adjustment

Two distinct revisions can occur, and they should be reasoned separately.

Benchmark movement: the underlying figure (profit, revenue, net assets) changes because of audit adjustments, restatements, or late entries. If the benchmark moves materially, overall materiality calculated from it moves too, and the revision is mechanical but must be documented.

Percentage or threshold adjustment: the auditor's judgment about the appropriate percentage or the performance materiality buffer changes because risk, controls, or qualitative factors have shifted. This is a judgment revision and requires explicit reasoning.

Both can occur in the same engagement. Separate them so the revision is transparent.

### Propagate The Revision Through Performance Materiality And Sample Sizes

Revising overall materiality is not enough. The downstream thresholds and procedures must follow.

When overall materiality is revised:

- recalculate performance materiality and confirm the percentage still fits the updated risk picture;
- recalculate tolerable misstatement for affected populations;
- reassess whether planned sample sizes still drive detection risk low enough at the new thresholds;
- consider whether completed testing remains sufficient or whether additional items must be tested;
- update the planned extent of procedures for areas not yet tested.

A revised overall figure with unchanged sample sizes is a half-revision that leaves the testing misaligned with the new threshold.

### Re-Evaluate Previously Identified Misstatements

When materiality is revised, items already evaluated against the old threshold must be re-evaluated against the new one.

Re-evaluate:

- uncorrected misstatements previously concluded immaterial, which may now be material;
- projected misstatement from sampling, which must be compared to the new tolerable limit;
- qualitative items set aside, which may now cross a boundary at the revised threshold;
- corrected items, to confirm correction was complete and accurate under the new figure.

Document the re-evaluation. An item concluded immaterial under a stale threshold cannot stand without reconsideration.

### Handle Downward Versus Upward Revision Differently

The direction of revision carries different consequences and requires different discipline.

Downward revision (tighter threshold): usually increases sample sizes, expands procedures, and may surface previously immaterial items as material. It typically arises from emerging risk, control failure, or benchmark decline. Apply it prospectively and re-evaluate prior items.

Upward revision (looser threshold): can appear convenient because it reduces work, and for that reason it requires stronger justification. Do not revise upward simply to make accumulated errors disappear. Upward revision is defensible when the benchmark was understated at planning and the corrected figure genuinely supports a higher threshold, and even then the reasoning must be explicit and the impact on conclusions documented.

### Document The Revision Trail

Revisions are frequently scrutinized by reviewers and inspectors. The trail must show what changed, why, and what followed.

Record:

- the trigger that prompted reassessment;
- the old and new overall materiality, performance materiality, and tolerable misstatement;
- whether the change was benchmark movement, percentage adjustment, or both;
- the effect on sample sizes, procedures, and completed testing;
- the re-evaluation of previously identified misstatements;
- the date of revision and the responsible auditor's approval.

A revision with no documented propagation is incomplete and indefensible.

### Coordinate Revision With The Reporting Conclusion

Materiality at completion, not at planning, is what governs the opinion. Ensure the final threshold aligns with the reporting evaluation.

Confirm that:

- the threshold used to evaluate uncorrected misstatements at completion is the revised, current figure;
- any modification of opinion reflects the final materiality;
- key audit matters and emphasis-of-matter paragraphs are consistent with the final threshold;
- comparative figures and prior-period adjustments are evaluated against the appropriate period's materiality.

Reporting against a planning threshold that was superseded during fieldwork is a reporting error.

## Common Traps

### Setting Materiality Once And Never Revisiting

The most common failure. Planning materiality becomes wrong when the benchmark moves, risk emerges, or controls fail, and an unrevised threshold leaves the audit testing the wrong level of risk.

### Revising Overall Materiality But Not Sample Sizes

Recalculating the top figure without propagating it through performance materiality, tolerable misstatement, and sample sizes is a half-revision that leaves testing misaligned.

### Revising Upward To Make Errors Disappear

An upward revision that conveniently clears accumulated misstatements is indefensible unless the benchmark genuinely supports it. Reviewers and inspectors treat self-serving upward revisions with suspicion.

### Failing To Re-Evaluate Prior Items Against The New Threshold

Items concluded immaterial under the old threshold may be material under the revised one. Skipping re-evaluation leaves stale conclusions in the workpapers.

### Treating A Trigger As Routine Without Documenting The Decision

Even when the auditor confirms the existing threshold after a trigger, the confirmation must be documented. An undocumented trigger is indistinguishable from an ignored one.

### Using Planning Materiality For The Final Reporting Evaluation

Materiality at completion governs the opinion. Reporting against a superseded planning figure is a reporting error.

### Letting Management Pressure Drive The Direction Of Revision

Management may push for upward revision to reduce findings. The direction and magnitude of revision must follow the evidence and the risk, not management preference.

## Self-Check

- Is planning materiality treated as provisional, with a scheduled reassessment checkpoint built into the engagement?
- Are the triggers for reassessment, including benchmark movement, fraud risk, control failure, scope change, and going-concern developments, recognized and acted on?
- When revision occurs, are benchmark movement and percentage adjustment reasoned separately and documented?
- Is the revision propagated through performance materiality, tolerable misstatement, sample sizes, and planned procedures?
- Are previously identified misstatements re-evaluated against the revised threshold, with the re-evaluation documented?
- Is the direction of revision justified, with upward revision subject to stronger scrutiny because of its convenience?
- Is the full revision trail recorded, including trigger, old and new thresholds, effect on testing, and approval?
- Is the reporting conclusion evaluated against the final, revised materiality rather than the planning figure?
