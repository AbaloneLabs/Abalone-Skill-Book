# Single Audit (Uniform Guidance)

A Single Audit is not a financial audit with a few extra schedules. It is a separate compliance audit governed by 2 CFR 200 (Uniform Guidance) that tests whether the entity administered Federal awards in compliance with applicable requirements and whether it has internal control over compliance sufficient to provide reasonable assurance. Agents frequently miss this distinction and underweight the mechanics that drive the whole engagement: a misstated SEFA, a wrong major-program determination, a misapplied Compliance Supplement, or a skipped data collection form can render the audit noncompliant and expose the entity to funding sanctions. The decisions are sequential and interdependent, so an error early in the process compounds through reporting.

Use this skill throughout a Single Audit engagement: when confirming the audit is required and properly scoped, when building and auditing the SEFA, when determining major programs, when applying the Compliance Supplement, when testing controls over compliance, and when assembling the reporting package. The goal is a defensible major-program determination, appropriately scoped compliance testing, and a complete and accurate reporting package that withstands clearinghouse submission and oversight review.

## Core Rules

### Confirm The Single Audit Is Required And Properly Triggered

A Single Audit is triggered when a non-Federal entity expends Federal awards at or above the threshold in a fiscal year. Agents sometimes assume the audit is required based on revenue recognition or grant agreements without confirming the actual expenditures of Federal awards, or they overlook the alternative of a program-specific audit. Establish the trigger before planning the work.

Confirm:

- the entity expended Federal awards at or above the Single Audit threshold in the fiscal year;
- the expenditures are measured on the expenditure basis (or the applicable basis for loans, loan guarantees, or non-cash assistance);
- whether the entity is a recipient, subrecipient, or both, and how clusters and passthrough awards are treated;
- whether a program-specific audit is permitted and elected in lieu of a Single Audit for an otherwise qualifying program;
- the reporting entity boundary and whether component units or related organizations are included;
- the due date of the reporting package relative to the entity's fiscal year-end and any extensions.

### Build And Audit The SEFA Accurately

The Schedule of Expenditures of Federal Awards is the foundation of the entire Single Audit; every later decision flows from it. A SEFA that omits programs, misclassifies CFDA/ALN numbers, double-counts passthrough funds, or misstates expenditures distorts the major-program determination and the audit opinion. Audit the SEFA with the same rigor as the financial statements.

Verify on the SEFA:

- all Federal programs and their Assistance Listing Numbers (ALN, formerly CFDA), including passthrough awards;
- expenditures on the correct basis, reconciled to the underlying accounting records;
- loan and loan guarantee programs at the proper amounts (often including the loan balance outstanding);
- non-cash assistance (commodities, surplus property) valued appropriately;
- clusters of programs identified and aggregated where the cluster is recognized;
- amounts passed through to subrecipients shown separately from direct expenditures;
- known or questioned adjustments to the SEFA reflected before major-program determination;
- reconciliation of total SEFA expenditures to the audited financial statements.

### Apply The Major Program Determination Steps Correctly

Major program determination is the single most consequential and most error-prone step in a Single Audit. It uses a defined risk-based approach with specific steps and thresholds, and applying the wrong step or threshold changes what is tested and reported. Work through the steps deliberately and document the basis for each program selected.

Work through the determination:

- identify Type A (larger) and Type B programs using the percentage thresholds;
- apply the risk-based criteria to Type A programs (recent audit findings, current-year changes, new programs, inherent risk);
- assess Type B programs using the sliding-scale risk assignment based on the number and size of Type B programs;
- ensure the coverage percentage of total Federal expenditures is met (generally the lower of the risk-based percentage or a fixed percentage, scaled by findings history);
- include any program deemed higher risk by the auditor or required to be treated as major;
- treat clusters as a single program for risk assessment and coverage;
- document the rationale for each low-risk and high-risk conclusion, not just the final selection.

### Use The Compliance Supplement As The Primary Compliance Authority

The OMB Compliance Supplement is the authoritative source of compliance requirements for most Federal programs, and it specifies which requirements are direct and material for each program. Agents often test generic compliance instead of the Supplement's program-specific requirements, which means the testing does not address what actually matters. Use the current-year Supplement and apply any program-specific addenda.

For each major program, extract from the Supplement:

- the compliance requirements deemed direct and material (often selected from a standard set such as activities allowed, allowable costs, eligibility, equipment, matching, reporting, subrecipient monitoring);
- the program-specific guidance that modifies or adds to the generic requirements;
- the audit objectives and suggested audit procedures;
- any references to program statutes, regulations, or award agreements that supplement the Supplement;
- the current-year version date, since requirements change annually;
- any open Supplement sections that direct the auditor to award terms when the program is not yet listed.

### Test Internal Control Over Compliance, Not Just Compliance

Uniform Guidance requires the auditor to obtain an understanding of internal control over compliance for each major program, assess control risk, and test controls where the planned approach relies on them. Agents frequently perform compliance testing without first mapping the controls that should prevent or detect noncompliance, leaving the control risk assessment unsupported. Design compliance testing around the controls and the direct compliance requirements together.

Address control over compliance:

- understand the controls relevant to each direct and material compliance requirement;
- assess control risk and decide whether to test controls or take a primarily substantive approach;
- test the operating effectiveness of controls when relying on them or when required by the planned approach;
- link control deficiencies to the specific compliance requirements they affect;
- consider the effect of IT general controls on automated compliance controls;
- evaluate whether identified deficiencies are significant deficiencies or material weaknesses in compliance.

### Evaluate And Report Findings With Required Elements

Single Audit findings must contain specific elements to be reportable and to support corrective action. A finding that omits the condition, the criterion, the cause, the effect, and the recommendation is deficient, and a questioned cost without a clear basis invites dispute. Draft each finding with the required structure and tie amounts to evidence.

For each finding, include:

- the program title and ALN;
- the federal award year and the applicable compliance requirement;
- the condition (what was found);
- the criterion (the requirement violated, with citation);
- the cause (why it happened);
- the effect or potential effect, including questioned costs with a documented basis;
- the recommendation;
- the views of responsible officials;
- a cross-reference to the summary schedule of prior audit findings if the issue is repeated.

### Assemble The Complete Reporting Package And Data Collection Form

The Single Audit reporting package has required components and a separate data collection form submitted to the Federal Audit Clearinghouse. Omitting a component or misreporting on the data collection form can invalidate the submission and jeopardize the entity's funding. Assemble the package deliberately and reconcile the form to the underlying reports.

Confirm the package and form include:

- the auditor's reports on the financial statements, internal control over financial reporting, and compliance (as applicable);
- the report on internal control over compliance and on compliance with each major program;
- the schedule of expenditures of Federal Awards and notes;
- the schedule of findings and questioned costs;
- the summary schedule of prior audit findings and the corrective action plan;
- the data collection form, completed and certified, reconciled to the reports;
- distribution to the Federal Audit Clearinghouse and any required passthrough or oversight entities within deadlines.

### Manage Subrecipient Monitoring And Passthrough Obligations

When a non-Federal entity passes Federal awards to subrecipients, monitoring those subrecipients is both a compliance requirement and a recurring source of findings. Agents often test the recipient's own expenditures but treat subrecipient monitoring as a paperwork review, missing that the recipient must evaluate subrecipient risk, monitor activities, and ensure required audits are performed and resolved. Test monitoring as a substantive compliance area.

Confirm the recipient:

- made subrecipient awards under proper agreements specifying Federal award information and requirements;
- performed a risk assessment of each subrecipient;
- monitored activities through reporting, site visits, or other means;
- verified subrecipients that meet the Single Audit threshold obtained and submitted a Single Audit;
- issued management decisions on subrecipient audit findings within required timeframes;
- tracked and resolved questioned costs and corrective action at the subrecipient level.

## Common Traps

### Auditing The SEFA As An Afterthought

Treating the SEFA as a clerical schedule rather than a primary audit deliverable leads to omitted programs and misstated expenditures that corrupt the entire major-program determination.

### Applying The Wrong Major-Program Step

Selecting the wrong determination step, misapplying the Type A/B thresholds, or miscalculating the coverage percentage changes what is tested and can produce an opinion on the wrong programs.

### Testing Generic Compliance Instead Of Supplement Requirements

Running standard compliance procedures without anchoring them to the Compliance Supplement's direct and material requirements means the testing does not address the risks that matter for that program.

### Using An Outdated Compliance Supplement

The Supplement is updated annually. Applying the prior-year version can lead to testing the wrong requirements or missing new program-specific guidance.

### Omitting Required Finding Elements

A finding missing the criterion, cause, effect, or questioned-cost basis is not reportable in the required form and weakens corrective action and oversight.

### Skipping The Data Collection Form Reconciliation

The data collection form must agree with the reports. Submitting a form that does not reconcile to the schedule of findings or the SEFA creates a noncompliant submission.

### Overlooking Subrecipient Monitoring

When the entity passes funds to subrecipients, monitoring those subrecipients is itself a compliance requirement. Failing to test passthrough monitoring misses a recurring area of noncompliance.

### Missing Cluster Treatment and confusing Questioned Costs With Noncompliance Without Cost

Programs that form a recognized cluster must be assessed as a single program. Treating clustered programs individually distorts risk assessment and coverage.

A questioned cost requires a documented basis and a tie to a specific unallowable or unsupported expenditure. Reporting a questioned cost where none exists, or omitting one where it does, misstates the schedule of findings.

## Self-Check

- Has the Single Audit trigger been confirmed based on actual expenditures of Federal awards at or above the threshold, and is the reporting entity boundary correct?
- Is the SEFA complete, reconciled to the financial statements, with all ALNs, clusters, passthrough amounts, and non-cash assistance correctly stated?
- Was the major program determination worked through the correct steps, with Type A/B assignments, risk conclusions, and coverage percentage documented?
- Does compliance testing for each major program anchor to the current-year Compliance Supplement's direct and material requirements?
- Has internal control over compliance been understood, assessed, and tested where relied upon, with deficiencies evaluated for severity?
- Does each finding contain the program, ALN, criterion, condition, cause, effect, recommendation, questioned-cost basis, and views of responsible officials?
- Are repeated findings cross-referenced to the summary schedule of prior audit findings?
- Is the summary schedule of prior audit findings and the corrective action plan included and reconciled?
- Has subrecipient monitoring been tested as a substantive compliance area, including risk assessment, performance review, and resolution of subrecipient audit findings?
- Is the data collection form completed, certified, and reconciled to the reports and the SEFA?; has the complete reporting package been submitted to the Federal Audit Clearinghouse and distributed to required passthrough or oversight entities within deadlines?
