# OMB Compliance

OMB compliance testing under 2 CFR 200 (Uniform Guidance) is where the substance of federal award administration is examined: whether costs are allowable, allocable, and reasonable; whether procurement followed the required standards; whether cash was managed to minimize the time between drawdown and disbursement; and whether subrecipients were properly overseen. Agents tend to treat these as checkbox tests because the rules read as procedural, but each requirement encodes a judgment about public money that, if violated, leads to disallowed costs, repayments, and funding consequences. The harm from a superficial compliance review is that unallowable spending is certified as compliant and only surfaces later under a federal review.

Use this skill when testing any OMB compliance requirement for a federal award, when evaluating the allowability of specific expenditures, when assessing procurement or cash management, and when reviewing pass-through and subrecipient monitoring. The goal is to test each requirement against the actual facts and the governing authority, not against a generic template, and to tie every conclusion to a citable rule.

## Core Rules

### Apply The Cost Principles As A Three-Part Test

The 2 CFR 200 cost principles determine whether a cost charged to a federal award is allowable, and the test has three interlocking parts: the cost must be allowable, allocable, and reasonable. Agents often confirm allowability (is this type of cost permitted?) and stop there, missing allocation (is it assignable to this award on a defensible basis?) and reasonabteness (would a prudent person pay this amount?). All three must be satisfied.

Test each cost against:

- allowability: is the cost type permitted by the cost principles and the award terms, and not specifically unallowable;
- allocability: is the cost assignable to the award on a basis that produces an equitable share, with a documented allocation methodology;
- reasonableness: is the nature and amount of the cost what a prudent person would incur under comparable circumstances;
- consistency: is the cost treated consistently as a direct or indirect cost in like circumstances;
- adequate documentation: is there source documentation supporting the amount, purpose, and business purpose;
- conformance with award-specific limitations, such as prior approval requirements for certain costs.

### Recognize Specifically Unallowable Costs

The cost principles identify categories of cost that are unallowable or allowable only with limitations or prior approval. Agents must know these categories rather than relying on management's assertion that costs are allowable, because charging an unallowable cost is a direct compliance failure regardless of how it was documented.

Watch for, and test, costs that are:

- unallowable as listed (e.g., alcoholic beverages, entertainment, lobbying, certain fundraising, advertising, fines and penalties, bad debts, certain contributions and donations);
- allowable only with limitations (e.g., certain compensation, travel, consultant costs, interest, equipment and supplies thresholds);
- allowable only with prior approval (e.g., certain equipment, foreign travel, participant support costs, changes in scope);
- subject to cost-sharing or matching treatment;
- affected by the salary cap limitations for certain federally funded personnel;
- indirect costs claimed directly, or vice versa, in violation of consistent treatment.

### Test Procurement Against The Uniform Guidance Standards

2 CFR 200 procurement standards require that procurement be conducted in a manner that provides full and open competition, avoids conflicts of interest, and uses the appropriate method of procurement for the dollar value. Agents frequently accept that a purchase was made without examining whether the method, competition, and documentation met the standard, which is where most procurement findings arise.

Confirm procurement compliance:

- the method of procurement matched the dollar threshold (micro-purchase, small purchase, sealed bids, competitive proposals, noncompetitive);
- competition was full and open unless a sole-source justification met the standard;
- conflicts of interest were identified and managed;
- the entity used its own documented procurement policies that conform to the Uniform Guidance;
- cost or price analysis was performed as required;
- required contract provisions (the Uniform Guidance procurement clauses) were included;
- records retain enough detail to show the basis for contractor selection;
- no prohibited or suspended parties were awarded.

### Verify Cash Management To Minimize Federal Cash On Hand

The cash management standard requires that federal cash be drawn down only as needed to pay actual disbursements, minimizing the time between receipt of funds and payment. Agents often overlook this because it seems administrative, but excessive drawdowns are a measurable, reportable compliance failure and can indicate broader weaknesses.

Test cash management:

- the timing of drawdowns relative to actual disbursements;
- whether the entity used advance or reimbursement methods appropriately;
- interest earned on federal cash advances accounted for and remitted as required;
- whether the entity maintained procedures to minimize the time between drawdown and disbursement;
- bank reconciliation evidence showing federal cash balances;
- pass-through entities' timing of subaward payments relative to the subrecipient's need.

### Evaluate Program-Specific Compliance Requirements

Beyond the cost principles and cross-cutting standards, each federal program has specific compliance requirements that flow from its authorizing statute, regulations, and award terms. Agents who test only the generic Uniform Guidance requirements miss program-specific obligations such as eligibility, matching, level of effort, earnings limitations, or service delivery standards. Anchor program-specific testing to the governing authority.

Identify and test:

- eligibility requirements for beneficiaries or activities;
- matching, cost-sharing, or maintenance-of-effort requirements;
- activity or use restrictions specific to the program;
- reporting requirements (program performance and financial);
- property and equipment standards (title, use, and disposition);
- any prior-approval or special award conditions;
- the OMB Compliance Supplement guidance for the program.

### Test Subrecipient Monitoring And Pass-Through Requirements

Pass-through entities must monitor subrecipient activity and ensure subrecipients meet audit and compliance requirements. This is both a Uniform Guidance requirement and a frequent source of findings. Agents should test monitoring as a substantive activity, not a documentation review.

Confirm the pass-through entity:

- made subawards under agreements containing the required federal award information;
- evaluated subrecipient risk and tailored monitoring accordingly;
- reviewed subrecipient performance and financial reports;
- verified required audits were obtained and findings resolved through management decisions;
- monitored activities through site visits, desk reviews, or other means proportionate to risk;
- enforced corrective action on subrecipient findings;
- distinguished subrecipients from contractors, since the monitoring duties differ.

### Distinguish Direct From Indirect Costs And Test Consistency

The Uniform Guidance requires consistent treatment of costs: a cost cannot be charged directly to an award if it has been treated as indirect elsewhere, and vice versa. Agents should test for consistency and for the proper negotiation and application of indirect cost rates.

Verify:

- the entity's negotiated indirect cost rate agreement and its proper application;
- consistent treatment of costs across awards;
- that costs in the indirect cost base are not also charged directly (double recovery);
- that direct-charged costs meet the direct cost standard and have proper documentation;
- any use of the de minimis indirect cost rate is within the permitted conditions.

### Document The Authority For Each Conclusion

Every compliance conclusion must tie to a citable authority: the cost principle, the procurement standard, the award term, or the program regulation. A conclusion that a cost is "allowable" without the citation is not defensible. Build the citation into the workpaper at the point of conclusion.

Cite for each test:

- the specific 2 CFR 200 section or program regulation;
- the award agreement term where relevant;
- the entity policy where it conforms to the federal standard;
- the source document supporting the transaction;
- the basis for any judgment (allocation methodology, reasonableness basis, sole-source justification).

### Test Property, Equipment, And Records Retention Standards

Federal awards carry standards for the title, use, and disposition of property and equipment acquired with federal funds, and for the retention of financial and program records. Agents often skip these because the assets or records may not be material to the financial statements, but they are independent compliance obligations with their own requirements and timeframes.

Verify:

- title and use conditions on federally funded equipment and real property are observed;
- inventory records of federally funded assets are maintained and current;
- disposition of assets follows the required process and any proceeds are handled correctly;
- records are retained for the required period from the date of submission of the final report;
- records are available for audit and review by the federal awarding agency, pass-through entity, and GAO;
- any required real-property use and disposition conditions are tracked over the asset's useful life.

## Common Traps

### Confirming Allowability And Stopping

Allowability is only one of three tests. A cost can be an allowable type yet fail allocation or reasonableness, and confirming only allowability lets unallowable charges through.

### Accepting Management's Assertion On Unallowable Costs

Relying on management's representation that no unallowable costs were charged, without testing for the specific unallowable categories, is a common and avoidable deficiency.

### Treating Procurement As A Documentation Check

Verifying that purchase orders exist without examining competition, method, conflicts, and contract provisions misses the substance of the procurement standards where findings concentrate.

### Ignoring Cash Drawdown Timing

Failing to measure the gap between drawdown and disbursement lets excessive federal cash on hand go unreported, even though it is a measurable compliance failure.

### Testing Generic Uniform Guidance Without Program-Specific Requirements

Program-specific obligations such as eligibility, matching, and reporting are often where noncompliance occurs; generic testing misses them entirely.

### Confusing Subrecipients With Contractors

The monitoring and compliance duties differ for subrecipients and contractors. Misclassifying a relationship applies the wrong requirements and the wrong monitoring.

### Allowing Double Recovery Of Costs

Charging a cost directly while also including it in the indirect cost base double-recovers the cost and is a direct violation of consistent treatment.

### Concluding Without Citing Authority

A compliance conclusion without a citation to the governing rule is not defensible and will not withstand federal review or a finding dispute.

## Self-Check

- Is each cost tested against allowability, allocability, reasonableness, consistency, and adequate documentation, not allowability alone?
- Have specifically unallowable and limited-allowability cost categories been tested rather than accepted on management's representation?
- Does procurement testing confirm method, competition, conflicts, contract provisions, and price analysis appropriate to the dollar threshold?
- Has cash management been tested for the timing of drawdowns versus disbursements and the treatment of interest earned?
- Have program-specific compliance requirements (eligibility, matching, effort, reporting, property) been identified and tested against their governing authority?
- Is subrecipient monitoring tested as a substantive activity, including risk evaluation, performance review, audit follow-up, and corrective action?
- Has consistent treatment of direct and indirect costs been tested, including the proper application of the negotiated indirect cost rate?
- Is each subrecipient relationship correctly classified as subrecipient or contractor, with the matching requirements applied?
- Does every compliance conclusion cite the specific 2 CFR 200 section, program regulation, award term, or conforming policy?
- Are disallowed or unsupported costs quantified with a documented basis and tied to the specific transactions and authority?
