# Completeness of Liabilities

Completeness of liabilities is the work that produces evidence the balance sheet includes all obligations the entity owes at the reporting date. Agents tend to focus on verifying recorded amounts and overlook the harder question of what is missing, but understated liabilities are among the most common forms of earnings management and fraud, because omitting an expense directly inflates profit. The judgment problem is that completeness is direction-sensitive in the opposite way from existence: you must trace from external evidence and subsequent events back to the records, and an unrecorded item leaves no trail in the ledger to sample from. The procedures must therefore look outside the accounting system to receiving reports, cash disbursements after period-end, vendor statements, and commitments.

Failing to design completeness procedures with the right direction and the right evidence sources produces a clean ledger that nonetheless omits material obligations, and the omission is invisible to vouching and recalculation. The skill is about hunting for what is not there, using evidence generated after the balance sheet date and from parties outside the entity.

Use this skill whenever the audit program includes search for unrecorded liabilities, accounts payable cutoff, accrued expense testing, subsequent disbursement review, or commitments and contingencies. The goal is to obtain sufficient appropriate evidence that all material liabilities are recorded in the correct period.

## Core Rules

### Test in the Completeness Direction

Completeness is tested by starting from evidence of an obligation and tracing forward to confirm it is recorded. Starting from the ledger and vouching to support tests existence and overstatement, not completeness. Reversing the direction is the single most common completeness failure.

- Select cash disbursements after period-end and trace to the payable or accrual recorded at year-end.
- Select unmatched receiving reports and investigate whether a payable was recorded.
- Review vendor statements received after year-end for items dated on or before the reporting date.
- Trace subsequent accruals and reversing entries back to the period to which they relate.
- Use open purchase order and commitment files to identify obligations not yet invoiced.
- Confirm that tracing direction is labeled completeness, not existence, in every workpaper.
- Re-perform cutoff on a sample of receipts and shipments near period-end.
- Confirm that the population of subsequent disbursements includes manual checks, wire transfers, and electronic payments, not just the check register.

### Perform the Search for Unrecorded Liabilities

The search for unrecorded liabilities is the primary completeness procedure for accounts payable and accruals. It examines disbursements and entries after the balance sheet date to find payments for goods or services received before that date that were not accrued. The period covered must extend far enough to capture obligations that would normally be paid within the operating cycle.

- Select cash disbursements for a defined period after year-end, typically four to six weeks.
- Examine the underlying support to determine when the goods or services were received.
- Investigate items received before year-end that were not recorded as a payable or accrual.
- Evaluate the population from which disbursements are selected to ensure it is complete and includes all bank accounts.
- Consider whether the search period should be extended for slow-paying vendors or unusual items.
- Document each exception and propose adjustment where the amount is material.
- Assess whether identified omissions indicate a broader pattern requiring expanded testing.
- Consider whether the search should include debit notes, credit memos, and non-trade payables that bypass the standard voucher process.

### Test Accounts Payable Cutoff

Cutoff testing ensures that goods received before year-end are recorded in the same period as the related payable, and that goods received after year-end are excluded. Cutoff errors shift expense and payables between periods and are a frequent source of misstatement. The test must cover both purchases and returns.

- Obtain the last receiving report number and last shipping document number before year-end.
- Trace selected pre-year-end receiving reports to the payable and purchase records.
- Trace selected post-year-end receiving reports to confirm they are excluded from the period.
- Test the matching of invoices to receiving reports for timing and completeness.
- Evaluate the entity's cutoff procedures and the reliability of the three-way match.
- Investigate invoices dated near year-end that lack matching receiving documentation.
- Confirm that returns and allowances after year-end relating to pre-year-end purchases are handled correctly.
- Test that freight, duty, and other landed costs incurred before year-end are included in the period's inventory and payables.

### Evaluate Accrued Expenses and Estimates

Accruals are estimates of obligations incurred but not yet invoiced, and they are vulnerable to both omission and understatement. The auditor must test the completeness of the accrual population and the reasonableness of the amounts. Common accruals include payroll, bonuses, utilities, professional fees, and warranties.

- Obtain a schedule of accrued expenses and reconcile the total to the general ledger.
- Compare the current year accrual balances to prior year and investigate significant changes.
- Test the computation of payroll and payroll tax accruals at year-end.
- Evaluate bonus and incentive accruals against the relevant plans and board approvals.
- Review accruals for professional fees, utilities, and rent by reference to subsequent payments.
- Assess whether service periods spanning year-end are fully accrued.
- Investigate accrual balances that reversed immediately after year-end, which may indicate smoothing.
- Test that accruals for recurring expenses, such as rent, insurance, and subscriptions, reflect the portion of service consumed by year-end.

### Review Unmatched Receiving Reports and Open Invoices

Receiving reports without matching invoices, and invoices held without entry, are signals of unrecorded liabilities. The auditor must review the open files and understand why items remain unmatched. A buildup of unmatched items indicates a control weakness and a completeness risk.

- Obtain the listing of unmatched receiving reports as of year-end and investigate aging items.
- Review the invoice log or voucher register for invoices received but not yet recorded.
- Evaluate management's explanation for unmatched items and test for unrecorded obligations.
- Assess whether the matching process is timely and whether cutoff controls operate effectively.
- Consider whether unmatched items indicate delayed invoicing by related parties.
- Test a sample of unmatched items for subsequent recording and proper period allocation.
- Evaluate the effect of any control deficiency on the extent of substantive testing.
- Trace a sample of unmatched items to their subsequent recording and confirm the period of recognition is correct.

### Examine Commitments and Contingencies

Commitments to purchase goods or services, and contingencies that may give rise to liabilities, must be identified and evaluated for both recognition and disclosure. Some commitments do not meet the recognition threshold but still require disclosure. The auditor must look beyond the ledger to contracts and board minutes.

- Review minutes of the board and committees for authorization of commitments.
- Examine significant contracts and purchase commitments for obligations extending beyond the period.
- Identify commitments related to capital expenditure, take-or-pay, and long-term supply agreements.
- Evaluate loss contingencies for probability and estimability, and assess recognition versus disclosure.
- Review correspondence with attorneys and the legal department for unasserted claims.
- Assess whether guarantees and indemnities create potential obligations requiring disclosure.
- Confirm that identified commitments and contingencies are disclosed consistently with the testing.
- Review executive expense reports and management representations for indications of obligations not captured in the accrual process.

### Use External Confirmations and Vendor Statements

Vendor statements and confirmations provide independent evidence of amounts owed. They are particularly useful for completeness because they originate outside the entity. The auditor must control the process and reconcile differences.

- Send accounts payable confirmations to selected vendors, including some with zero balances.
- Reconcile vendor statements to the accounts payable subsidiary ledger.
- Investigate reconciling items, especially items on the statement not in the ledger.
- Evaluate the reliability of confirmations and perform alternative procedures for non-responses.
- Consider confirming with vendors with whom there has been little or no activity.
- Use confirmations to test for related-party payables that may be omitted or understated.
- Assess whether confirmation evidence is corroborated by subsequent disbursement testing.
- Send confirmations to a sample of vendors with debit balances, which may indicate misclassified receivables or overpayments.

### Evaluate Subsequent Events for Liabilities

Events between the balance sheet date and the audit report date can reveal liabilities that existed at year-end. Bankruptcy filings, new litigation, debt agreements, and restructuring announcements can all signal previously unrecorded obligations. The subsequent events review is a completeness procedure, not just a disclosure formality.

- Review cash disbursements, board minutes, and legal correspondence in the subsequent period.
- Identify new borrowings, refinancings, and debt covenant modifications.
- Investigate subsequent settlements of disputes that indicate year-end obligations.
- Review post-year-end payroll and contractor payments for services rendered before year-end.
- Assess whether subsequent events confirm or contradict the recorded liability balances.
- Consider whether subsequent events indicate the need for additional disclosure.
- Review post-year-end refinancing and debt modifications for terms that create new or changed obligations at year-end.

### Evaluate Related-Party and Off-Balance-Sheet Liabilities

Related-party obligations and off-balance-sheet arrangements are where liabilities are most easily omitted or understated, because they sit outside the normal procurement and payables cycle and management has both motive and means to keep them out of view. The auditor must actively search for guarantees, intercompany balances, special purpose entities, and variable interest arrangements that create obligations not visible in the trade payables ledger. A completeness program that ignores these structures can produce a clean payables cutoff while missing material obligations entirely.

- Identify all related parties and obtain a schedule of balances and transactions with them.
- Review intercompany accounts for unfunded or non-arm's-length obligations that may be omitted.
- Examine guarantees, comfort letters, and letters of support provided to or by related parties.
- Assess whether special purpose or variable interest entities create consolidating obligations.
- Review executive compensation, loans to officers, and related-party accruals for completeness.
- Test whether off-balance-sheet arrangements such as factoring or securitization are properly disclosed or de-recognized.
- Review factoring arrangements to determine whether receivables were truly sold or merely financed, creating a retained obligation.
- Assess whether the entity is a primary or agent in arrangements that affect the grossing of payables and receivables.
- Confirm that commitments under take-or-pay contracts are evaluated for onerous-contract provisions where applicable.
- Confirm that related-party payables are measured on arm's-length terms and adequately disclosed.

## Common Traps

### Testing in the Wrong Direction

Vouching recorded payables to invoices tests existence and overstatement, not completeness. The trap is that the work ties out neatly while leaving omitted liabilities undetected. Always start from external or subsequent evidence and trace to the records.

### Truncating the Search Period Too Early

A search for unrecorded liabilities that ends too soon misses obligations paid on longer cycles. The trap is the convenience of a short window. Extend the period to cover the normal operating and payment cycle.

### Ignoring Unmatched Receiving Reports

Receiving reports without invoices are a direct signal of an obligation not yet recorded. The trap is assuming the matching process will catch them later. Review the unmatched file and test for unrecorded payables.

### Forgetting Accruals That Reverse Immediately

An accrual that reverses the day after year-end may be a placeholder rather than a real estimate. The trap is accepting the balance without understanding its basis. Investigate the nature and support of immediately reversing accruals.

### Overlooking Related-Party Payables

Obligations to related parties may be omitted, understated, or recorded on non-market terms. The trap is treating related-party balances like ordinary trade payables. Identify related parties and scrutinize their balances and terms.

### Missing Commitments Through Incomplete Contract Review

Significant commitments can be buried in supply, take-or-pay, or capital contracts that are not part of the accounts payable cycle. The trap is limiting the review to the ledger. Examine contracts and board authorizations.

### Relying on Management's Accrual List Without Challenge

Accepting management's schedule of accruals at face value tests arithmetic, not completeness. The trap is that the list may omit entire categories of obligation. Independently identify accrual categories and test their completeness.

### Confusing Disclosure With Recognition

Some commitments and contingencies require disclosure even when they do not meet recognition criteria. The trap is treating a recognition conclusion as the end of the work. Assess disclosure separately for each identified item.

## Self-Check

- Was the search for unrecorded liabilities performed in the completeness direction, tracing from subsequent disbursements and external evidence to the records?
- Did the subsequent disbursement search cover a period long enough to capture the normal payment cycle, across all bank accounts?
- Were unmatched receiving reports and held invoices reviewed and investigated for unrecorded obligations?
- Was accounts payable cutoff tested using the last receipt and shipment numbers before and after year-end?
- Were accrued expenses reconciled to the ledger, compared to prior year, and tested for reasonableness?
- Were commitments and contingencies identified through contract and board minute review and evaluated for recognition and disclosure?
- Were vendor confirmations and statements used to corroborate completeness, including for zero-balance and low-activity vendors?
- Were related-party payables identified and scrutinized for omission, understatement, and non-market terms?
- Were immediately reversing accruals investigated for their nature and support?
- Did the subsequent events review identify liabilities existing at year-end?
- Is each completeness workpaper labeled with the assertion and direction tested?
