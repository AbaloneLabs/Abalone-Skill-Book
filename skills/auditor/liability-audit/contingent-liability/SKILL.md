# Contingent Liability

Contingent liability testing addresses obligations whose existence, amount, or timing depends on a future event that is not wholly within the entity's control, such as the outcome of litigation, regulatory action, environmental remediation, or a guarantee being called. Agents tend to treat contingencies as a disclosure checklist, but the real judgment problem is that management has strong incentives to understate or avoid recognizing loss contingencies, and the evidence often sits with attorneys and external parties who speak a language of probability rather than accounting. The auditor must translate legal uncertainty into accounting thresholds and obtain evidence from independent sources rather than relying on management's comfort with its own legal position.

Failing to probe contingencies deeply leaves material obligations undisclosed or unrecorded, and the consequences are severe because contingencies often crystallize into large, sudden losses. The skill is about identifying the full population of potential obligations, obtaining independent legal evidence, and applying the recognition and disclosure thresholds rigorously.

Use this skill whenever the audit program includes contingencies, commitments, litigation, claims, guarantees, environmental matters, or attorney letter review. The goal is to identify all material contingencies, evaluate each against the recognition and disclosure criteria, and confirm that the financial statements present them correctly.

## Core Rules

### Identify the Full Population of Contingencies

The first risk is missing a contingency entirely. The auditor must look broadly across legal correspondence, board minutes, regulatory filings, and management inquiry to build a complete list. A contingency that is never identified cannot be evaluated or disclosed.

- Inquire of management about pending, threatened, or unasserted litigation and claims.
- Review board minutes, audit committee minutes, and legal committee minutes for authorized actions and disputes.
- Examine legal expense accounts and correspondence with outside counsel for indications of disputes.
- Review regulatory correspondence, inspections, and enforcement matters.
- Identify guarantees, indemnities, and warranties provided to or by the entity.
- Review environmental assessments, site reports, and regulatory notices.
- Consider tax disputes and transfer pricing exposures as contingencies.
- Reconcile the population to the prior year's schedule and investigate additions and deletions.
- Review tax correspondence and transfer pricing documentation for disputed assessments and potential exposures.
- Examine minutes of the health, safety, and environmental committees for incidents that may give rise to claims.

### Obtain and Evaluate Attorney Letters

Attorney letters are the primary independent evidence for litigation and claims. The auditor must control the process, ensure the letters address the right questions, and evaluate the responses carefully. A perfunctory letter exchange provides no real evidence.

- Send the audit inquiry letter to all attorneys the entity has used in the period, with management's involvement limited to listing the matters.
- Request that attorneys list pending or threatened litigation and provide their assessment of the likely outcome.
- Ask attorneys to identify unasserted claims where the probability of assertion is more than remote.
- Evaluate the format, date, and responsiveness of each attorney letter.
- Follow up on vague, qualified, or unresponsive letters.
- Assess whether the matters listed by attorneys agree with management's list.
- Consider whether the entity has used attorneys not disclosed to the auditor.
- Evaluate the competence and independence of the attorneys providing responses.
- Consider whether the entity has used in-house counsel or foreign counsel not covered by the standard inquiry letter.
- Follow up on any matter where the attorney's response differs materially from management's characterization.

### Apply the Recognition and Disclosure Thresholds

Contingencies are governed by probability thresholds that determine whether a loss is accrued, disclosed only, or neither. The auditor must apply the thresholds rigorously to each matter, because management may resist accrual even when the criteria are met. The thresholds differ between frameworks, so the governing standard must be confirmed first.

- Confirm the governing framework, because probable, reasonably possible, and remote differ in definition.
- Accrue a loss when it is probable and estimable under the applicable framework.
- Disclose a loss that is reasonably possible, even if not accrued.
- Evaluate whether a range of possible loss can be estimated and whether the accrual reflects the best estimate.
- For matters where no amount within the range is a better estimate, apply the framework's rule for range accrual.
- Confirm that remote contingencies are evaluated for disclosure where required.
- Document the threshold analysis for each material contingency.
- Cross-reference the threshold conclusion to the disclosure and accrual workpapers.
- Document the rationale for the probability assessment of each material matter with reference to the evidence obtained.

### Evaluate Estimability of the Loss

Even when a loss is probable, the amount must be estimable before it can be accrued. The auditor must assess whether management's estimate, or a range, is supportable. Where the amount cannot be estimated, disclosure rather than accrual may be required.

- Obtain management's basis for the estimated amount or range.
- Corroborate the estimate with attorney assessments, settlement history, and expert input.
- Evaluate whether a single best estimate or a range is more appropriate.
- Assess whether the low end of a range is used to minimize the accrual.
- Consider the effect of insurance recoveries and whether they are recognized appropriately.
- Test whether related costs, such as legal fees, are included or excluded consistently.
- Reassess prior-year accruals against current developments and adjust.
- Evaluate whether the accrual reflects the most likely outcome or the minimum of the range, consistent with the framework.
- Assess whether settlement authority limits or insurance deductibles affect the amount to be accrued.

### Review Guarantees and Indemnities

Guarantees and indemnities create contingent obligations that may require both recognition and disclosure. The terms must be reviewed to determine the maximum exposure and the conditions under which the guarantee would be called. Related-party guarantees warrant particular scrutiny.

- Identify all guarantees issued, including those to subsidiaries, joint ventures, and related parties.
- Review the terms to determine the maximum potential payment and the conditions for payment.
- Assess whether the guarantee meets the criteria for recognition at fair value.
- Evaluate the creditworthiness of the guaranteed party as a indicator of likelihood.
- Confirm disclosure of the maximum exposure and the recognition, if any.
- Identify indemnities given to directors, officers, and advisors.
- Assess whether guarantees of third-party debt create consolidation considerations.
- Evaluate whether intra-group guarantees create separate disclosure or recognition requirements in the parent's separate financial statements.

### Assess Environmental and Regulatory Contingencies

Environmental remediation and regulatory matters often involve significant uncertainty and specialist input. The auditor must identify the sites and matters, obtain specialist assessments, and evaluate the recognition and disclosure. These contingencies can be material and long-tailed.

- Identify sites subject to remediation, investigation, or regulatory action.
- Obtain environmental assessments and remediation cost estimates from qualified specialists.
- Evaluate the stage of assessment and whether costs can be estimated reliably.
- Assess the allocation of costs among potentially responsible parties.
- Consider the effect of insurance, indemnities, and cost-recovery actions.
- Review regulatory enforcement matters for fines, penalties, and compliance costs.
- Evaluate whether environmental contingencies require accrual, disclosure, or both.
- Consider whether newly acquired operations introduced environmental or legal contingencies that must be evaluated.
- Assess whether changes in environmental regulation increase the likelihood or amount of remediation obligations.

### Evaluate Subsequent Events for Contingencies

Events between the balance sheet date and the audit report date can resolve or reveal contingencies. Settlements, judgments, and new claims in the subsequent period inform the year-end evaluation. The subsequent events review is an integral part of contingency testing.

- Review settlements, judgments, and court decisions in the subsequent period.
- Identify new claims, lawsuits, or regulatory actions arising after year-end.
- Assess whether subsequent events confirm or contradict the year-end accrual or disclosure.
- Determine whether events providing additional evidence of conditions at year-end require adjustment.
- Identify non-adjusting events requiring disclosure.
- Document the subsequent events cutoff date and the procedures performed.
- Inquire of management about any events after the reporting date up to the audit report date that may affect contingency conclusions.

### Assess Disclosure Adequacy

Contingency disclosure must give users enough information to assess the risk. The auditor must confirm that disclosure includes the nature, the estimate or range, the uncertainty, and any movement during the period. Boilerplate disclosure is a deficiency when the contingency is material.

- Confirm disclosure of the nature of each material contingency.
- Test disclosure of the estimated loss or range, and the basis for the estimate.
- Verify disclosure of contingencies not accrued because they are not estimable.
- Confirm disclosure of guarantees, including maximum exposure and recognition.
- Assess whether disclosure of environmental and regulatory matters is specific.
- Test disclosure of changes in accruals during the period and the reasons for them.
- Confirm that contingencies are presented in the correct categories and notes.
- Verify that contingent liabilities are not netted against contingent assets unless the framework permits set-off.

### Evaluate Contingent Assets and Insurance Recoveries

Contingent assets and insurance recoveries are the mirror image of contingent liabilities and are often overlooked because they do not appear on the balance sheet until virtually certain. The recognition and disclosure rules for contingent assets are generally more restrictive than for liabilities, and insurance recoveries tied to losses must be assessed for realizability before they are netted or disclosed. The auditor must apply the framework's asymmetry carefully, because recognizing a contingent asset too early overstates assets while missing it understates them.

- Identify contingent assets arising from pending claims, insurance, and potential recoveries from third parties.
- Apply the recognition threshold for contingent assets, which is typically virtual certainty under IFRS.
- For insurance recoveries, confirm that recovery is probable and the amount can be reliably estimated before recognition.
- Assess whether insurance proceeds are grossed up or netted against the related loss, consistent with the framework.
- Evaluate whether indemnification rights from sellers in a business combination create contingent assets requiring evaluation.
- Confirm that contingent assets are disclosed where inflow is probable but not virtually certain.
- Reconcile recognized insurance recoveries to correspondence with insurers and settlement agreements.

## Common Traps

### Treating the Attorney Letter as a Formality

A rushed or boilerplate attorney letter provides no real evidence about the likely outcome of litigation. The trap is treating the letter as a procedural step rather than a source of substantive evidence. Evaluate the content and follow up on vague responses.

### Relying on Management's Assessment of Probability

Management may characterize a likely loss as merely possible to avoid accrual. The trap is accepting management's characterization without independent corroboration. Obtain the attorney's assessment and apply the thresholds independently.

### Missing Unasserted Claims

Unasserted claims where assertion is probable must be evaluated, but they are easy to overlook because no complaint has been filed. The trap is limiting the review to pending litigation. Ask attorneys specifically about unasserted claims.

### Applying the Wrong Threshold

The definitions of probable, reasonably possible, and remote differ between frameworks, and applying the wrong definition misstates the conclusion. The trap is assuming the thresholds are universal. Confirm the governing framework first.

### Under-Disclosing the Range of Loss

Even when an accrual is recorded, the full range of possible loss must often be disclosed. The trap is recording the best estimate and omitting the range. Tie the disclosure to the range evaluated during testing.

### Overlooking Guarantees to Related Parties

Guarantees to subsidiaries or affiliates may be treated as routine intercompany support and overlooked as contingencies. The trap is assuming related-party support carries no risk. Review all guarantees for terms, exposure, and disclosure.

### Ignoring Insurance and Indemnity Recoveries

Insurance and indemnity recoveries can reduce the net exposure, but their recognition depends on realizability. The trap is netting recoveries without testing their probability. Assess recoveries separately and disclose where appropriate.

### Stopping at the Accrual Decision

Deciding to accrue is not the end of the work; disclosure of the nature, range, and uncertainty is also required. The trap is treating the accrual as sufficient. Complete the disclosure testing for each material contingency.

## Self-Check

- Was a complete population of contingencies built from management inquiry, board minutes, legal correspondence, and regulatory review?
- Were attorney letters sent to all attorneys used during the period, and were the responses evaluated for content and responsiveness?
- Were recognition and disclosure thresholds applied to each material contingency under the correct governing framework?
- For probable losses, was the estimability of the amount or range assessed and corroborated with independent evidence?
- Were guarantees and indemnities identified, with terms, maximum exposure, and recognition evaluated?
- Were environmental and regulatory contingencies assessed with specialist input where needed?
- Did the subsequent events review identify settlements, judgments, and new claims affecting year-end contingencies?
- Were unasserted claims with probable assertion specifically identified and evaluated?
- Is disclosure of the nature, estimate, range, and uncertainty of each material contingency specific and adequate?
- Were insurance and indemnity recoveries assessed for realizability and disclosed where appropriate?
- Are contingency conclusions documented with the threshold analysis and supporting evidence for each matter?
