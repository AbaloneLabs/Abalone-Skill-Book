# Governance Findings

Some audit findings do not concern a process or a balance; they concern governance itself. A board that lacks the expertise to oversee a critical risk, an audit committee that does not meet often enough or challenge management, a tone at the top that tolerates cutting corners, a whistleblower process that routes complaints to the very people they concern, an ethics program that exists on paper but not in practice. These findings are among the most significant an audit can surface, because governance weakness undermines every control that depends on oversight. They are also among the hardest to report, because the audience for the finding is often the governance body the finding concerns. The skill is in identifying governance weaknesses with the same rigor as operational ones, evaluating them against defensible criteria, and communicating them in a way that informs rather than alienates the very people who must act on them.

Use this skill when identifying or evaluating findings about governance, board or committee effectiveness, tone at the top, ethics culture, or oversight structures, or when deciding how to communicate governance matters to the body they concern. The goal is to surface governance weaknesses accurately and constructively, without softening them into invisibility or delivering them in a way that prevents remediation.

## Core Rules

### Recognize Governance Weakness As A Distinct Finding Category

Governance weakness is not just background context for other findings; it can be a finding in itself, and often the root cause behind multiple operational findings. Failing to recognize it as a distinct category causes the auditor to report symptoms while leaving the governance cause unaddressed.

Recognize governance findings when evidence shows:

- the board or committee lacks the composition, expertise, or time to oversee a significant risk;
- oversight is nominal, with challenges to management rare or ineffective;
- tone at the top tolerates non-compliance, override, or ethical shortcuts;
- the ethics or compliance program is not reinforced or enforced;
- the whistleblower process lacks independence or protection;
- governance structure creates conflicts or diffuses accountability.

When multiple operational findings share a governance root cause, name the governance weakness explicitly rather than reporting only the downstream symptoms.

### Evaluate Governance Against Defensible Criteria

Governance findings must be evaluated against criteria just as rigorously as operational findings. Without criteria, the finding becomes the auditor's opinion about how the board should behave, which is easily dismissed.

Use defensible criteria such as:

- regulatory or listing requirements for board composition, independence, and expertise;
- recognized governance codes or frameworks applicable to the entity;
- the entity's own charter, bylaws, and governance policies;
- professional standards for audit committee responsibilities;
- the entity's stated ethics and compliance commitments.

Cite the specific criterion the governance practice falls short of. A finding that "the audit committee should meet more often" is weak; one that references the requirement for quarterly meetings and the actual frequency is defensible.

### Distinguish Observation Of Governance From Opinion About It

The auditor observes governance practices through evidence, meetings attended, minutes reviewed, challenges observed or absent, follow-up on prior findings. The finding must rest on these observations, not on the auditor's preference for a governance style.

Ground findings in observation by:

- citing specific evidence, such as minutes, attendance, expertise gaps, or lack of challenge;
- quantifying where possible, such as meeting frequency, executive session occurrence, or training hours;
- distinguishing what was observed from what the auditor infers;
- avoiding findings based on the auditor's ideal governance model rather than applicable criteria.

A governance finding grounded in observed evidence and applicable criteria is far harder to dismiss than one based on preference.

### Identify The Root Cause Link To Operational Findings

Governance findings are most persuasive when their effect is visible in operational results. Showing that governance weakness is the root cause of multiple operational findings connects the abstract to the concrete and demonstrates why it matters.

Identify the link by:

- tracing operational findings, such as recurring control failures or unremediated issues, to governance causes;
- showing where lack of oversight allowed the operational condition to persist;
- demonstrating how tone at the top influenced the control environment;
- connecting governance gaps to specific risks the entity faces.

A governance finding that explains why operational problems keep recurring is more compelling than one reported in isolation.

### Communicate To The Body The Finding Concerns

The central difficulty of governance findings is that the audience is the subject. Reporting a governance weakness to the very board or committee it concerns requires care to inform without provoking defensiveness that prevents remediation.

Communicate effectively by:

- framing the finding in terms of the entity's objectives and risks, not as criticism of individuals;
- grounding every statement in evidence and criteria;
- acknowledging context and any constraints the governance body faces;
- offering constructive observations on how the weakness could be addressed;
- preserving the auditor's conclusion even where it is unwelcome.

The goal is a finding the governance body can hear, understand, and act on, not one that triggers a defensive rejection. Candor and respect are both required.

### Escalate When The Governance Body Is The Impediment

Sometimes the governance weakness is so fundamental, or the body's response so inadequate, that reporting to that body is insufficient. The auditor must recognize when escalation beyond the immediate governance body is necessary.

Escalate when:

- the audit committee or board lacks the independence or will to address the finding;
- the finding concerns integrity, fraud, or illegal acts at the governance level;
- the governance body attempts to suppress or alter the finding;
- the entity has no effective governance body to receive the finding.

Escalation paths may include a higher governance level, regulators, or, for internal audit, the full board or an independent body. Document the escalation and the response.

### Connect Governance Findings To Risk And Strategy

Governance findings are most relevant when tied to the risks and strategic objectives the entity faces. A governance weakness that affects a critical risk warrants more attention than one in a peripheral area.

Connect by:

- linking the governance gap to the entity's most significant risks;
- showing how the weakness affects strategic objectives or viability;
- assessing whether the governance structure is adequate for the entity's complexity and risk profile;
- considering emerging risks the governance body may not yet be equipped to oversee.

This positions the governance finding as integral to the entity's success, not as an abstract critique.

## Common Traps

### Treating Governance As Background, Not A Finding

Reporting operational symptoms while leaving the governance root cause unaddressed guarantees recurrence. Name governance weakness as a finding.

### Findings Based On Preference, Not Criteria

A finding about how the board should behave, without applicable criteria, is opinion and easily dismissed. Cite defensible criteria.

### Softening To Avoid Offending The Audience

Because the audience is the subject, there is pressure to soften. A governance finding softened into invisibility fails its purpose.

### Reporting Governance Findings In Isolation

A governance finding without its link to operational consequences seems abstract. Connect it to the risks and recurring issues it causes.

### Provoking Defensive Rejection

Findings framed as personal criticism trigger defensiveness that prevents remediation. Frame in terms of objectives, evidence, and criteria.

### Failing To Escalate When The Body Is The Impediment

When the governance body cannot or will not address the finding, reporting only to them is insufficient. Escalate appropriately.

### Ignoring Tone At The Top

Tone at the top shapes the entire control environment but is hard to evidence. Gather observations, such as enforcement of ethics policies and response to prior issues, to support findings.

### Assuming Governance Adequacy From Structure Alone

A board that looks right on paper, with committees and charters, may still fail in practice. Observe actual operation, not only structure.

## Self-Check

- Are governance weaknesses recognized as distinct findings, especially when they are the root cause of multiple operational issues?
- Is each governance finding evaluated against defensible criteria, such as regulatory requirements, governance codes, or the entity's own policies?
- Are findings grounded in observed evidence, distinguishing observation from the auditor's preference or ideal model?
- Is the link between governance weakness and operational findings, risks, and strategy made explicit?
- Is the finding communicated to the governance body in a way that informs without provoking defensive rejection, grounded in evidence and criteria?
- Where the governance body is itself the impediment, is the matter escalated to an appropriate higher level or regulator?
- Are tone at the top, ethics culture, and oversight effectiveness assessed through observable evidence, not assumed from structure?
- Does the finding explain why the governance weakness matters to the entity's objectives, risks, and viability?
- Is the auditor's conclusion preserved even where the governance body prefers a softer characterization?
