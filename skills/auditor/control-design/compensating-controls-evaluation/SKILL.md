# Compensating Controls Evaluation

A compensating control is a secondary control that reduces the risk created by the absence or weakness of a primary control. The temptation is to treat any nearby control as compensating and thereby declare a deficiency managed. In reality, most candidate compensating controls are weaker, later, less comprehensive, or less reliably operated than the primary control they are meant to replace, and over-reliance on them is one of the most common reasons a known deficiency still leads to a material error.

## Core Rules

### Establish the specific deficiency before evaluating any compensating control

A compensating control can only be judged against a precisely defined gap. Before accepting a compensating arrangement, state:

1. What is the primary control that is missing or weak?
2. What specific error or risk does that primary control prevent or detect?
3. What assertion and transaction class does it cover?
4. What is the population at risk?

Only once the gap is precisely defined can you judge whether a candidate control actually addresses the same risk. A control that mitigates a different risk is not compensating — it is simply another control.

### Test whether the compensating control addresses the same risk, not just a related one

A common error is to accept a control that covers part of the risk and treat it as full compensation. For example, a missing segregation-of-duties control over payment initiation might be "compensated" by a bank reconciliation — but the reconciliation detects unauthorised payments after the money has left the account, which is a different (and much weaker) form of protection than preventing them. Ask: does this control prevent or detect the exact error that the missing primary control would have caught, at a comparable point in the process?

### Apply a stricter strength test than for primary controls

A compensating control must be at least as effective as the primary control it replaces, after accounting for:

- **Timing**: detective-after-the-fact is weaker than preventive; a control that finds the error weeks later is weaker than one that finds it the same day.
- **Coverage**: does it cover the whole population at risk, or only a subset (e.g., a sample review rather than full reconciliation)?
- **Reliability**: is it performed consistently, by competent people, with documented evidence?
- **Independence**: is the compensating control operated by someone other than the person whose duties created the deficiency?

If the compensating control is weaker on any of these dimensions, it provides only partial mitigation, and the residual deficiency must still be evaluated for significance.

### Confirm the compensating control is actually operating, not merely designed

A designed-but-not-operated compensating control provides zero mitigation. For each candidate, obtain evidence that it:

- was in operation throughout the period at risk, not just at year-end;
- is performed by a person with the competence and authority to act;
- produces documented evidence (signed reviews, reconciliations with investigation of differences, exception logs);
- has actually caught or corrected errors, demonstrating it works in practice.

A compensating control that has never found anything may be operating well, or may be cosmetic — the difference matters and evidence of effective operation resolves it.

### Evaluate whether the compensating control is itself dependent on the deficient control

A subtle but serious trap: the proposed compensating control may rely on the same system, person, or process that is deficient. For example, if the deficiency is weak access control over the ERP, a "compensating" review of an exception report generated by that same ERP inherits the access weakness — a privileged user could suppress the exceptions. Trace the dependency: if the compensating control shares the root cause of the deficiency, it does not compensate.

### Distinguish compensating controls from corrections and from routine controls

- A **routine control** that was always part of the process is not compensating; it is just the normal control structure.
- A **correction** of the underlying deficiency (fixing the segregation, implementing the missing validation) eliminates the gap rather than compensating for it.
- A **compensating control** is an additional or alternative control specifically relied upon to reduce the risk of the uncorrected gap.

Only the third category should be labelled compensating. Mislabelling routine controls as compensating inflates the apparent strength of the control environment.

### Factor compensating controls into deficiency severity, not into existence of the deficiency

The presence of a compensating control does not erase the deficiency; it reduces the likelihood or magnitude of the resulting misstatement, which feeds the assessment of whether the deficiency is significant or material. Evaluate:

- Without the compensating control, how large could the misstatement be?
- With the compensating control, what is the residual likelihood and magnitude?
- Is the residual risk still above the threshold for a significant deficiency or material weakness?

Document the compensating control's contribution explicitly so the severity conclusion is defensible. Do not let the compensating control silently downgrade a deficiency to "no issue."

### Escalate when compensating controls are insufficient, informal, or person-dependent

A compensating control that depends on a single key person, that is not documented, or that has no evidence of consistent operation should be treated as weak or non-existent for severity purposes. Likewise, a control that management asserts exists but cannot demonstrate with evidence cannot be relied upon. When in doubt, evaluate the deficiency as if the compensating control were absent, and let the evidence raise the conclusion rather than the assertion lower it.

## Common Traps

- **Accepting a nearby control as compensating without mapping it to the specific risk.** A reconciliation is not a universal compensating control; it must address the same error as the missing primary control.
- **Treating a detective control as full compensation for a missing preventive control.** Detection after the fact is weaker, especially for irreversible transactions like payments.
- **Relying on a compensating control that depends on the same deficient system or person.** Shared root cause means no real mitigation.
- **Assuming a control is operating because management says so.** Demand evidence of operation across the period, not just a description.
- **Letting the compensating control silently erase the deficiency instead of reducing its severity.** The deficiency still exists; only its assessed severity is affected.
- **Accepting a sample-based review as compensation for a population-wide gap.** Partial coverage is partial mitigation, not full.
- **Over-relying on a single key person as the compensating control.** If that person is absent, reassigned, or pressured, the mitigation vanishes; person-dependent controls are fragile.
- **Treating a year-end-only compensating control as covering a full-year risk.** Timing gaps leave large windows unprotected; **Confusing a routine control that was always present with a true compensating control**, which inflates the assessed strength of the environment

## Self-Check

- Have I precisely defined the deficiency — the missing primary control, the specific risk, the assertion, and the population at risk — before judging any compensating control?
- Does the candidate compensating control address the exact same risk as the missing primary control, at a comparable point and with comparable coverage?
- Have I tested that the compensating control actually operated throughout the period, with documented evidence, not just at a single point?
- Is the compensating control independent of the person or system whose weakness created the deficiency?
- Have I assessed the compensating control on timing, coverage, reliability, and independence, and concluded it is at least as strong as the primary control it replaces — or explicitly recognised the residual gap?
- Did I factor the compensating control into the severity of the deficiency rather than using it to claim the deficiency does not exist?
- If the compensating control depends on a single key person, have I considered what happens when that person is unavailable?
- For any compensating control I accepted, can I explain in one sentence why it reduces the residual risk below the significant-deficiency or material-weakness threshold — and is that explanation supported by evidence rather than assertion?
