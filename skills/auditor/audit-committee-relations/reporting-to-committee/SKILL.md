# Reporting To The Audit Committee

Reporting to the audit committee is not a courtesy update; it is a regulated communication that carries legal, professional, and reputational weight. Under ISA 260 and PCAOB AS 1301 the external auditor is required to communicate specific matters that the committee needs to oversee financial reporting and the audit itself. Agents tend to under-deliver here because the requirements feel procedural: they summarize the plan, list risks generically, and avoid uncomfortable topics such as management disagreements, aggressive estimates, or fraud risks. The harm this prevents is twofold. A weak communication leaves the committee unable to challenge management, while a careless communication can waive privilege, disclose matters to the wrong audience, or create a record that contradicts the workpapers.

Use this skill when drafting the audit committee letter, planning memorandum, significant-risk summary, critical audit matter (CAM) communications, or year-end reporting package. The goal is to give the committee the specific, decision-relevant information it needs to oversee the audit and financial reporting, while keeping the communication consistent with the work performed and the applicable standard.

## Core Rules

### Communicate The Required Matters, Not A Subset

Both ISA 260 and PCAOB AS 1301 enumerate matters that must be communicated. Agents often cover the comfortable ones and omit the uncomfortable ones. Work through the required list deliberately and confirm each is addressed.

Required topics typically include:

- the auditor's responsibilities and the scope of the audit;
- the overall audit strategy, plan, and timing;
- significant risks and material risks of material misstatement;
- materiality, performance materiality, and the threshold for uncorrected errors;
- significant risks of fraud, including management override;
- significant accounting policies, including changes and controversial choices;
- significant estimates and judgments, including management's process;
- significant unusual transactions and their business rationale;
- critical audit matters, where applicable under PCAOB standards;
- significant deficiencies and material weaknesses in internal control;
- disagreements with management, and how they were resolved;
- going concern evaluation and related events or conditions;
- other information, such as the annual report consistency;
- fraud or illegal acts identified, and any uncorrected misstatements.

If a required matter is not relevant, state that it was considered and why no communication is needed, rather than silently omitting it.

### Make The Plan And Scope Decision-Useful

The committee needs to understand what the audit will and will not cover, where the team focused effort, and why. Generic statements such as "we performed a risk-based audit" are not useful.

Communicate specifically:

- entities, locations, and components included or excluded;
- materiality basis and how it was set;
- reliance on internal controls and the nature of testing;
- areas of significant risk and the planned response;
- use of internal audit, the work of others, or component auditors;
- timing of significant procedures and key deliverables;
- limitations on scope, access, or evidence;
- significant changes to the plan during the engagement.

The committee should be able to ask informed questions, not be reassured that everything is fine.

### Describe Significant Risks With Enough Specificity

Significant risks warrant heightened audit attention and warrant committee attention. Describe them in a way that connects the risk to the financial statements and to the audit response.

For each significant risk, state:

- the assertion-level risk and the related account or disclosure;
- why it is significant (complexity, judgment, change, fraud, external factors);
- the inherent risk factors present;
- the planned or performed audit response;
- controls the auditor intends to test or rely on;
- any matters that required the auditor's professional skepticism;
- the outcome or status at the reporting date.

Avoid boilerplate risk language that could apply to any company. If the description could be moved to another client unchanged, it is too generic.

### Handle Critical Audit Matters And Significant Unusual Transactions Carefully

CAMs (under PCAOB) and significant unusual transactions are high-scrutiny areas. They must be described accurately and consistently with the audit opinion and workpapers.

For CAMs, ensure:

- the matter was communicated or is required to be communicated;
- the description explains why the matter is material and how it was addressed;
- the description does not duplicate the opinion language improperly;
- the matter is not generic to all audits of the company.

For significant unusual transactions, communicate:

- the nature and business rationale;
- the accounting treatment and why it may be challenging;
- whether the transaction is with a related party;
- the audit procedures performed;
- management's process and the evidence obtained;
- any concerns about the form, substance, or completeness of disclosure.

### Report Disagreements With Management Honestly

Disagreements with management are a required communication, and agents often soften them to preserve the relationship. Softening defeats the purpose. The committee needs to know where management and the auditor differed and how it was resolved.

Report:

- the matter in dispute and its financial statement impact;
- management's position and the auditor's position;
- the evidence each side relied on;
- whether the matter was resolved, and how;
- whether the resolution changed the audit opinion or required modification;
- any uncorrected differences remaining at the reporting date.

Do not characterize a disagreement as a routine discussion if it concerned accounting treatment, disclosure, or audit scope.

### Address Going Concern And Subsequent Events

Going concern is a recurring area of committee concern and regulator scrutiny. Communicate the auditor's evaluation, not management's assertion alone.

Communicate:

- management's going concern evaluation and the period assessed;
- events or conditions that cast significant doubt;
- management's plans to mitigate and the auditor's assessment of feasibility;
- adequacy of related disclosures;
- emphasis-of-matter or qualification if warranted;
- subsequent events reviewed and any that affect the conclusion.

If there is substantial doubt, say so plainly and explain the basis.

### Preserve Consistency With Workpapers And The Opinion

Everything told to the committee must be consistent with the audit file and the issued opinion. Inconsistency is a finding waiting to happen.

Check consistency between:

- the committee communication and the audit opinion;
- the risks described and the risk assessments in the file;
- the significant deficiencies reported and the deficiency evaluation;
- the going concern statements and the conclusion documented;
- the CAMs and the workpapers supporting them;
- any oral remarks versus the written record.

### Respect Privilege, Confidentiality, And Distribution

Committee communications can create or waive privilege and can disclose sensitive matters. Manage distribution deliberately.

Consider:

- whether legal counsel should review legally sensitive communications;
- whether fraud or illegal-act communications need restricted distribution;
- whether draft communications should be marked privileged and confidential;
- the authorized recipient list and copy list;
- retention of the final communication in the audit file;
- consistency between written and oral communications.

## Common Traps

### Treating The Communication As A Status Update

A status update says progress is on track. A required communication conveys specific oversight matters. Do not substitute one for the other.

### Omitting Uncomfortable Matters

Disagreements, fraud risks, aggressive estimates, and significant deficiencies are exactly what the committee most needs. Omitting them to avoid friction defeats the purpose of the communication and can be a professional standards violation.

### Generic Risk Descriptions

If the significant-risk section reads like a template, the committee cannot exercise oversight. Tie each risk to the specific account, assertion, and response.

### Inconsistency With The Opinion Or File

Telling the committee the audit is clean while the file shows unresolved misstatements, or describing a risk differently than in the opinion, creates exposure. Reconcile before sending.

### Overstating Comfort With Controls

Saying controls are effective when only a subset was tested, or when significant deficiencies exist, misleads the committee. Report control conclusions precisely.

### Premature Going Concern Reassurance

Reassuring the committee that going concern is fine before completing the evaluation, or before reviewing management's mitigation plans, can create a record that is later contradicted.

### Disclosing Matters To Too Broad An Audience

Fraud allegations, whistleblower matters, or illegal-act indicators may need restricted distribution. Sending the full communication to a wide management copy list can waive privilege or compromise investigations.

### Letting Management Draft The Communication

Management may offer to draft or edit the committee letter. The auditor owns the required communications. Management may provide factual corrections, but the auditor controls content, tone, and conclusions.

## Self-Check

- Does the communication address each required matter under ISA 260 or PCAOB AS 1301, with omissions explained rather than silent?
- Is the audit plan and scope described specifically enough for the committee to ask informed questions about coverage and limitations?
- Does each significant risk connect to a specific account, assertion, and audit response rather than boilerplate?
- Are critical audit matters and significant unusual transactions described consistently with the opinion and workpapers?
- Are disagreements with management reported honestly, including each side's position and the resolution?
- Is the going concern evaluation communicated with its basis, and is any substantial doubt stated plainly?
- Are significant deficiencies and material weaknesses reported with severity and supporting evidence?
- Is the communication reconciled against the audit opinion, risk assessments, and deficiency evaluations before issuance?
- Are sensitive matters distributed through restricted channels with privilege and confidentiality preserved?
- Does the auditor retain ownership of content and conclusions, with management limited to factual corrections?; is the final communication retained in the audit file, consistent with any oral remarks made to the committee?
