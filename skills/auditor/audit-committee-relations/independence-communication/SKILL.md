# Independence Communication

Auditor independence is the foundation of the audit's value, and the audit committee is the body that oversees it. Communicating independence is therefore not a formality; it is a substantive disclosure of relationships, services, fees, and safeguards that could threaten objectivity. Agents often treat this as a checklist exercise: they confirm the firm is independent, note that no prohibited services were provided, and move on. The harm this prevents is real. A missed self-interest threat, an undisclosed non-audit service, or an expired pre-approval can trigger restatements, regulator action, loss of the engagement, and loss of public confidence in the financial statements.

Use this skill when preparing the annual independence letter, pre-approval requests, fee disclosures, or communications about relationships and services that may affect independence. The goal is to give the committee an accurate, complete picture of threats and safeguards so it can discharge its oversight duty, and to document that the auditor considered independence with professional skepticism rather than assuming it.

## Core Rules

### Communicate Independence As A Continuous, Not Annual-Only, Duty

Independence is not assessed once a year. Relationships, services, and personnel changes occur throughout the engagement, and the committee needs timely information when something changes.

Communicate independence:

- at least annually, through a formal independence letter;
- when new non-audit services are proposed and pre-approval is sought;
- when new relationships arise (hiring, lending, business relationships);
- when partner or senior team rotation occurs;
- when fee structures change materially;
- when a threat is identified that requires committee attention;
- before any service that could create a prohibited relationship.

Do not wait for the annual cycle if a mid-year event affects independence.

### Identify All Threat Categories Before Declaring Independence

Independence can be impaired by several threat types. Agents often check the obvious financial ones and miss the others. Work through the categories deliberately.

Consider:

- self-interest threats (fees, loans, financial interests, contingent fees);
- self-review threats (non-audit services that become audit evidence);
- advocacy threats (acting for the client in litigation or securities work);
- familiarity threats (long association, family relationships, former employees);
- intimidation threats (client pressure, threats of replacement);
- management participation threats (making decisions or acting as management);
- undue influence threats (gifts, hospitality, dominance by a dominant personality).

For each threat present, describe the threat, the safeguard applied, and the residual risk.

### Apply Non-Audit Service Rules Precisely

Non-audit services are a primary independence risk surface. PCAOB and SEC rules, and equivalent national standards, prohibit certain services outright and restrict others. Agents must apply the rules to the specific service, not assume general permissibility.

Restricted or prohibited areas commonly include:

- bookkeeping and accounting services;
- designing or implementing financial information systems that the firm audits;
- valuation and appraisal services where results are material;
- actuarial services related to amounts in the financial statements;
- internal audit outsourcing services;
- management functions or human resources decisions;
- investment banking or broker-dealer services;
- legal and expert services unrelated to the audit;
- services involving contingent fees.

For each permitted non-audit service, confirm scope, safeguards, and that it does not create a self-review threat.

### Obtain And Document Pre-Approval Correctly

Under PCAOB rules, non-audit services require pre-approval by the audit committee, with specific delegation and dollar thresholds. The mechanics matter.

Ensure pre-approval covers:

- the specific service category, not a blanket approval;
- the engagement and the responsible partner;
- the fee estimate or range;
- the committee's authority to delegate to the chair within limits;
- the timing of approval before work begins;
- documentation of the approval in the audit file;
- the distinction between audit, audit-related, tax, and other services.

Pre-approval obtained after the work began is not valid pre-approval. Track the approval status before services commence.

### Disclose Fees Transparently And In The Required Categories

Fee disclosure reveals the economic relationship between the firm and the client. A high ratio of non-audit to audit fees signals a self-interest threat. Disclose fees in the required categories.

Disclose:

- audit fees for the financial statement audit;
- audit-related fees (quarterly reviews, comfort letters, subsidiary audits);
- tax fees (compliance, advisory, planning);
- all other fees;
- fees for the prior year for comparison;
- any contingent or success-based fees;
- significant changes in fee mix or magnitude.

Explain any fee relationships that could create threats, such as a dominant client relative to firm revenue.

### Address Partner And Team Rotation

Rotation rules exist to limit familiarity threats. Communicate the rotation status of the key personnel and any deviations.

Communicate:

- the engagement partner's tenure against the rotation limit;
- the concurring or reviewing partner's tenure;
- senior managers and specialists with long association;
- any difficult or unusual rotation circumstances;
- cooling-off periods for former audit personnel joining the client;
- whether the client has hired any audit team members during the year.

If a rotation is approaching or overdue, say so and describe the plan.

### Describe Safeguards And Their Limits

When threats exist, safeguards reduce them. But safeguards are not magic; some threats cannot be safeguarded away. Be honest about residual risk.

Typical safeguards include:

- rotation of senior personnel;
- second partner review;
- independent quality review;
- internal independence confirmation processes;
- separation of audit and non-audit teams;
- restriction of access and information;
- committee oversight and challenge.

If a threat is so significant that safeguards cannot reduce it to acceptable, the firm must decline or resign, not communicate a comfortable conclusion.

### Reconcile The Independence Conclusion With The Audit File

The independence communication must be consistent with the independence determination in the audit file. Do not tell the committee one thing while the file shows unresolved threats.

Reconcile:

- the threats disclosed to the committee with those in the independence checklist;
- the services listed with the engagement records;
- the fee disclosure with the billing system;
- the rotation status with personnel records;
- any exceptions or breaches with their resolution.

## Common Traps

### Treating Independence As A Yes Or No

Independence is a judgment about threats and safeguards, not a binary. A flat statement that "the firm is independent" without describing threats considered is incomplete and non-compliant.

### Assuming Permitted Means Safe

A service may be technically permitted but still create a self-review or familiarity threat. Evaluate substance, not only the rule's letter.

### Backdated Or Blanket Pre-Approval

Obtaining approval after work started, or relying on a blanket approval that does not specify services, does not satisfy pre-approval rules. Track approval timing and specificity.

### Hiding The Fee Mix

If non-audit fees are large relative to audit fees, the self-interest threat is real. Disclosing only audit fees hides the economic dependency.

### Missing Familiarity And Hiring Threats

Long-tenured partners and recent hires of audit staff into client roles are familiarity threats. Omitting them because they feel personal or awkward undermines the communication's purpose.

### Relying On Safeguards For Prohibited Services

Some services are prohibited regardless of safeguards. Do not describe a safeguard as a substitute for a prohibition.

### Letting The Client Frame The Independence Letter

Management may want the independence letter to be brief and reassuring. The auditor owns the independence communication and must describe threats and safeguards objectively.

### Forgetting Component And Affiliate Relationships

Independence extends to components, affiliates, and entities the firm's network serves. A relationship at an affiliate can impair independence for the parent audit.

## Self-Check

- Is independence communicated at least annually and whenever a new service, relationship, or personnel change arises?
- Are all threat categories considered and documented, not only the obvious financial ones?
- Are non-audit services evaluated against specific prohibitions and restrictions, with permitted services confirmed against self-review risk?
- Is pre-approval obtained before work begins, specific to the service category and fee, and documented in the file?
- Are fees disclosed in the required categories with prior-year comparison and explanation of any threatening fee mix?
- Is partner and team rotation status communicated, with overdue or approaching rotations flagged?
- Are safeguards described honestly with their limits, and is any non-reducible threat escalated rather than rationalized?
- Is the independence conclusion reconciled with the threats, services, fees, and rotation status in the audit file?
- Are component, affiliate, and network relationships considered for independence effects?
- Does the auditor retain ownership of the independence communication, with management limited to factual input?; are any independence breaches or exceptions disclosed to the committee with their resolution and any remedial action?
