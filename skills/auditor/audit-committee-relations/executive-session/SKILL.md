# Executive Session With The Audit Committee

An executive session is a private meeting between the auditor and the audit committee without management present. It exists precisely because some of the most important oversight matters cannot be raised openly in front of the people they concern. Agents often underuse executive sessions or fill them with routine updates, because raising concerns about management's accounting quality, pressure on the audit team, or competence is uncomfortable. The harm this prevents is significant: without a candid private channel, fraud risks, management override, and tone-at-the-top problems can fester unreported to the body charged with overseeing them. Conversely, an unstructured or poorly documented executive session can create confusion about what was communicated and what the committee directed.

Use this skill when planning executive sessions, deciding what to raise, conducting the session, documenting it, and deciding when to escalate. The goal is to use the private channel to surface oversight-critical matters that management's presence would suppress, while keeping the discussion fair, factual, and properly recorded.

## Core Rules

### Treat The Executive Session As A Required, Structured Channel

Executive sessions are not optional or ceremonial. For listed companies and many regulated entities, holding a private session with the external auditor is a governance expectation and often a requirement. Plan for it deliberately each cycle.

Plan:

- the frequency of executive sessions across the audit cycle;
- the topics reserved for private discussion versus open session;
- who attends from the audit team and the committee;
- the materials or talking points prepared in advance;
- the time allocated, sufficient for substantive discussion;
- the follow-up actions and how they are tracked.

Do not let executive sessions shrink to a five-minute courtesy at the end of a packed agenda.

### Prepare A Candid Agenda Of Oversight-Critical Topics

The value of the session depends on what is raised. Prepare topics that genuinely require management's absence. Generic status updates waste the opportunity.

Topics that typically belong in executive session:

- quality of accounting judgments and aggressiveness of estimates;
- pressure on the audit team to accept positions, timing, or scope limits;
- tone at the top and the control environment;
- fraud risks, including management override of controls;
- competence and integrity of senior financial management;
- disagreements with management and their resolution;
- significant unusual transactions and their business rationale;
- adequacy and independence of the internal audit function;
- whistleblower or ethics-line matters affecting financial reporting;
- indications of management bias in estimates and disclosures;
- retention or replacement of senior management or the CFO;
- any restriction on audit access to people, systems, or records.

If nothing on the agenda requires privacy, the session is likely under-planned.

### Distinguish Open-Session From Executive-Session Content

Some matters benefit from management hearing the auditor's view directly; others require private discussion first. Decide deliberately where each topic belongs.

Reserve for executive session:

- matters where management may be implicated or pressured;
- assessments of management competence or integrity;
- fraud or override concerns;
- disagreements not yet resolved;
- sensitive whistleblower or ethics matters;
- committee direction on scope or approach.

Address in open session:

- routine plan and progress updates;
- resolved technical accounting matters;
- control deficiencies already agreed with management;
- factual findings with agreed recommendations.

When unsure, default to raising the matter privately first and letting the committee decide whether to address it openly.

### Use Professional Skepticism And Evidence, Not Opinion

Executive session is where the auditor's judgment matters most, but judgment must rest on evidence. Do not turn the session into venting or speculation.

Ground each topic in:

- specific transactions, accounts, or events observed;
- the audit procedures performed and their results;
- the evidence supporting the concern;
- the auditor's professional judgment, clearly labeled as such;
- the criteria or standard against which the concern is measured;
- the range of possible conclusions and their implications.

Avoid characterizing individuals without factual basis. Describe behavior and evidence, not personalities.

### Raise Pressure And Scope Issues Promptly

One of the most important uses of executive session is to report pressure on the audit team. Management pressure to reduce scope, accept aggressive positions, or meet deadlines is a governance matter that the committee needs to hear.

Raise:

- attempts to limit access to information or personnel;
- pressure to change timing or scope of procedures;
- threats of fee reduction tied to audit findings;
- late or incomplete information that impaired the audit;
- attempts to influence the opinion or remove disclosures;
- intimidation of audit staff during fieldwork.

Do not absorb pressure silently and report only after the audit is complete. Timely reporting lets the committee act.

### Document The Session Accurately And Discreetly

Because executive sessions involve sensitive matters, documentation requires care. Too little record creates ambiguity about what was communicated; too much detail in the wrong place can breach confidentiality or compromise investigations.

Document:

- the date, attendees, and topics discussed;
- the matters raised by the auditor;
- the committee's questions and direction;
- any actions agreed and owners;
- the auditor's conclusions on matters raised;
- any matters the committee asked to be followed up.

Keep the documentation in a controlled location, with distribution limited to those authorized. Reflect the session in the audit file at the appropriate level of detail.

### Decide When To Escalate Beyond The Committee

Sometimes the committee's response is insufficient, or the matter exceeds the committee's authority. The auditor must know when to escalate further.

Escalate when:

- the committee declines to act on a significant fraud or override risk;
- management interference continues despite committee direction;
- the matter involves illegal acts requiring regulator notification;
- the committee itself appears compromised or unwilling to challenge management;
- the auditor concludes the issue materially affects the opinion and is unresolved.

Escalation paths include the full board, regulators, or, in extreme cases, resignation. Document the escalation decision and its basis.

### Coordinate With Internal Audit Without Surrendering Judgment

Internal audit often participates in or informs executive sessions. Their perspective is valuable, but the external auditor must form independent conclusions.

Coordinate by:

- sharing relevant findings within permitted boundaries;
- understanding internal audit's view of the control environment;
- avoiding reliance that bypasses the auditor's own testing;
- maintaining separate conclusions on risks and deficiencies;
- respecting internal audit's reporting line to the committee.

## Common Traps

### Filling Executive Session With Routine Updates

If the session covers the same content as the open meeting, the private channel is wasted. Reserve it for matters that require privacy.

### Avoiding The Uncomfortable Topic

The matters most needing a private channel are the hardest to raise: competence, pressure, fraud, integrity. Avoiding them defeats the session's purpose.

### Speculation Without Evidence

Raising concerns without supporting evidence undermines the auditor's credibility and can be unfair. Ground every concern in procedures performed and facts observed.

### Letting Management Attend Part Of The Session

Even partial management presence suppresses candor on the topics that follow. Hold a fully private portion each time, even if some topics are then discussed with management.

### No Documentation Of Direction Received

If the committee directs the auditor on scope, escalation, or follow-up, that direction must be recorded. Ambiguity later about what was agreed creates risk.

### Over-Documenting Sensitive Detail

Recording names, allegations, or investigation detail in broadly accessible files can breach confidentiality or prejudice proceedings. Match the record's detail to its audience.

### Treating Internal Audit's View As The Auditor's Conclusion

Internal audit's assessment is informative, not determinative. The external auditor must reach and report its own conclusions.

### Failing To Follow Up On Committee Direction

If the committee asks for follow-up and it is not tracked, the matter disappears. Maintain a follow-up log and report status in the next session.

## Self-Check

- Is an executive session planned and held at appropriate frequency, with adequate time and a prepared agenda?
- Does the agenda include oversight-critical topics such as accounting quality, pressure, fraud, competence, and internal audit, rather than routine updates?
- Has each topic been assigned to open or executive session deliberately, with sensitive matters defaulted to private?
- Is every concern grounded in specific evidence, procedures, and criteria rather than opinion or speculation?
- Are pressure and scope-limitation issues raised promptly rather than absorbed until the audit is complete?
- Is the session documented with date, attendees, topics, committee direction, and follow-up actions, at an appropriate level of detail and access?
- Are matters requiring escalation beyond the committee identified, with escalation paths and documented basis?
- Is internal audit's perspective considered without replacing the auditor's independent conclusions?
- Are committee-directed follow-ups tracked and reported in subsequent sessions?
- Is the executive session record kept in a controlled location with restricted distribution?; has the auditor avoided characterizing individuals without factual basis, focusing on behavior and evidence?
