# Fieldwork Execution And Progress Management

Fieldwork is where the audit plan becomes evidence. It is also where plans quietly drift. A test is skipped because data is late, a sample is reduced because time is short, a reviewer signs off without reading the workpaper, and an open item is carried forward until it is forgotten. The result is an engagement that looks complete on the status sheet but is missing the evidence needed to support the conclusion. Managing fieldwork is not administrative housekeeping; it is the control that prevents evidence gaps from being hidden behind a green status.

Use this skill before executing fieldwork, tracking progress, supervising team members, managing open items, deciding whether to extend or reduce testing, or concluding that an area is complete. The goal is to keep the work tied to the planned objectives, to surface shortfalls early, and to prevent schedule pressure from degrading evidence quality.

## Core Rules

### Execute Against The Plan, Not Around It

Fieldwork should follow the planned scope, objectives, and procedures. Deviations are legitimate, but they must be deliberate and documented, not silent drift.

Before and during execution, keep visible:

- the planned objectives and assertions for each area;
- the procedures to be performed;
- the populations and samples;
- the evidence to be obtained;
- the responsible team member;
- the reviewer;
- the due date;
- dependencies on client deliverables.

When a procedure changes, record what changed, why, who approved it, and whether the change affects the conclusion the work can support. Convenience is not a sufficient reason to reduce testing in a high-risk area.

### Track Open Items And Evidence Gaps Explicitly

The single most common fieldwork failure is losing track of what was promised but never received. Open items should be tracked in a way that makes gaps visible and actionable.

For each open item, track:

- description of the evidence or document requested;
- the procedure it supports;
- the requester and the client contact;
- date requested;
- follow-up dates;
- current status;
- impact if not received;
- escalation owner;
- deadline beyond which the gap becomes a scope limitation.

An open item that supports a key procedure is not a minor administrative detail. If it remains open at the conclusion deadline, it may force a qualified conclusion or additional procedures.

### Manage Client Deliverables And PBC Actively

The prepared-by-client list drives much of fieldwork timing. Late or incomplete deliverables cascade into compressed testing and rushed review.

Manage the list by:

- confirming completeness and format of each deliverable on receipt;
- reconciling data to source systems or control totals before relying on it;
- flagging incomplete, reformatted, or partial extracts;
- requesting replacements when the data does not match the population;
- avoiding the temptation to test whatever was provided instead of what was needed.

Do not begin testing from an extract that has not been reconciled. A clean-looking spreadsheet that does not tie to the ledger is not a population.

### Supervise And Review With Substance

Supervision is not a signature. Review should confirm that the work addresses the objective, that the evidence supports the conclusion, and that exceptions were evaluated.

Effective review includes:

- checking that the procedure performed matches the planned procedure;
- verifying population completeness and sample selection;
- reading the conclusion against the actual results;
- confirming exceptions were investigated, not explained away;
- challenging unsupported assumptions;
- ensuring cross-references are accurate;
- confirming the workpaper could be understood by an experienced auditor with no prior involvement.

Review notes should be cleared, not acknowledged. An open review note at sign-off is an unresolved quality issue.

### Recognize And Control Scope Creep

Scope changes during fieldwork are sometimes necessary and sometimes a distraction. The auditor should distinguish deliberate risk-based expansion from uncontrolled scope creep.

Legitimate scope expansion occurs when:

- testing reveals a risk not identified in planning;
- a control deficiency requires additional procedures;
- analytics identify an unexpected pattern;
- a new transaction type falls within the period.

Concerning scope creep occurs when:

- the team accepts adjacent requests that are not in scope;
- management redirects attention to convenient areas;
- testing expands without a documented risk rationale;
- effort shifts away from high-risk areas toward easy ones.

Document any scope change, the reason, and the effect on budget, timing, and conclusions. Escalate significant changes to the engagement leader.

### Surface Delays And Resource Constraints Early

Schedule pressure is a root cause of weak evidence. When fieldwork falls behind, the response should be to surface the problem and rebalance, not to compress testing silently.

Escalate early when:

- key data is delayed;
- client availability is poor;
- team members are overloaded;
- complex areas need more time than budgeted;
- a specialist is needed but not engaged;
- review capacity is insufficient for the volume produced.

Options include resequencing work, reallocating staff, extending the timeline, narrowing scope with documented justification, or escalating to leadership. Silent compression of testing is not an acceptable option.

### Maintain Independence And Objectivity During Fieldwork

Fieldwork often involves close collaboration with client staff, which can erode independence if not managed. The auditor should remain objective and avoid performing or approving client work.

Guard against:

- preparing or posting client entries;
- approving client transactions;
- relying on client staff to perform audit procedures without oversight;
- accepting client-prepared schedules without testing;
- letting client urgency override audit judgment;
- becoming an advocate for a preferred outcome.

Independence is a state of mind as well as a formal requirement. If the auditor is functioning as part of management, the evidence loses its independence and its persuasiveness.

### Define What Complete Means Before Declaring It

An area is complete only when the planned procedures are performed, the evidence is obtained, exceptions are resolved, review notes are cleared, and the conclusion is supported. A status of done should mean done, not waiting on something.

Before marking an area complete, confirm:

- all planned procedures were performed or documented substitutions made;
- all key open items were received or their absence evaluated;
- exceptions were investigated and concluded;
- review notes were cleared;
- the conclusion matches the evidence;
- cross-references are complete;
- workpapers are finalized and signed.

## Common Traps

### Treating The Status Sheet As Reality

A green status does not prove the evidence exists. The status should reflect verified completion, not optimism or pressure to show progress.

### Testing Whatever Was Provided

When a client delivers a convenient extract, the temptation is to test it. If it is not the right population, the testing is wasted and the conclusion unsupported.

### Reducing Testing Under Time Pressure

Compressing a sample or skipping a procedure to meet a deadline degrades evidence. Surface the delay and rebalance instead.

### Acknowledging Review Notes Without Clearing Them

A review note that is read but not resolved leaves a gap. Notes must be cleared, with the resolution documented.

### Forgetting Open Items At Conclusion

Open items that supported key procedures cannot be abandoned at sign-off. If the evidence was never received, the conclusion must reflect that limitation.

### Letting Client Urgency Set Audit Priorities

Client pressure to finish, or to focus on areas management prefers, can distort fieldwork. Priorities should follow risk and the plan, not convenience.

### Drifting Into A Management Role

Helping the client close the books or prepare schedules may seem efficient, but it compromises independence. The auditor evaluates management work; the auditor does not perform it.

## Self-Check

- Is fieldwork being executed against the planned objectives, procedures, populations, and samples, with any deviations documented and approved?
- Are open items tracked with requester, date, status, impact, escalation owner, and a deadline beyond which the gap becomes a scope limitation?
- Has each client deliverable been reconciled to a control total before testing began?
- Does review confirm procedure alignment, population completeness, exception evaluation, and conclusion support, with notes cleared rather than acknowledged?
- Are scope changes documented with risk rationale and approved, distinguishing legitimate expansion from uncontrolled creep?
- Are delays, resource constraints, and missing specialists escalated early rather than absorbed through compressed testing?
- Has independence been preserved, with the auditor avoiding preparation or approval of client transactions and entries?
- Before an area is marked complete, have all planned procedures, evidence, exception resolutions, cleared review notes, and supported conclusions been confirmed?
- Does the conclusion for each area reflect the evidence actually obtained, including any unresolved open items?
- Could an experienced reviewer reconstruct what was done, what was open, and why the area was concluded from the fieldwork documentation?
