# Management Inquiry And Representations

Inquiry is one of the auditor's most used and most overrated procedures. It is essential for understanding the business, the process, the events of the period, and management's view of risks and estimates. But inquiry, by itself, rarely proves that a control operated, that a transaction was valid, or that a balance is correct. Management has knowledge the auditor needs, and management also has incentives that can color what it says. The auditor's job is to extract useful information while never mistaking a statement for proof. Representations, whether oral or written, are supporting evidence and sometimes required evidence, but they are not a substitute for testing.

Use this skill before interviewing management or process owners, seeking explanations for exceptions, relying on management statements, drafting or obtaining a representation letter, or deciding whether inquiry is enough. The goal is to gather information effectively, corroborate what matters, and avoid treating confident assertions as conclusions.

## Core Rules

### Treat Inquiry As Evidence Gathering, Not As Proof

Inquiry is powerful for understanding and explanation. It is weak for proving that something happened or that a control operated. Distinguish the two uses.

Inquiry is appropriate for:

- understanding the business, process, and system;
- identifying risks, events, and changes during the period;
- obtaining management's view of estimates and judgments;
- explaining exceptions and variances;
- learning about related parties, commitments, and contingencies;
- confirming that no undisclosed matters exist.

Inquiry alone is usually insufficient for:

- proving a control operated throughout the period;
- verifying existence, accuracy, or valuation of a balance;
- confirming completeness of a population;
- supporting a conclusion on a high-risk assertion.

When inquiry is the only evidence for an important matter, corroborate it or treat the conclusion as limited.

### Plan The Inquiry Before Conducting It

Good inquiry is structured. Walking into a meeting with a vague question produces a vague answer. Plan what you need to learn and from whom.

For each inquiry, prepare:

- the objective and the assertion or risk it relates to;
- the specific questions, ordered from open to specific;
- the person best positioned to answer, considering knowledge and independence;
- follow-up questions for likely responses;
- documents to request that support the answers;
- areas where corroboration will be needed.

Start with open questions to understand, then narrow to specifics to confirm. Leading questions that telegraph the desired answer produce unreliable responses.

### Choose The Right Source And Consider Motivation

The person answering affects the reliability of the response. Someone who performed the process knows it; someone who owns the risk may have an incentive to minimize it.

Consider:

- whether the source has direct knowledge or secondhand familiarity;
- whether the source has incentive to overstate or understate;
- whether the source is independent of the process being described;
- whether corroborating the response with a different source is feasible;
- whether the source was involved in prior issues or disagreements.

Where management has strong incentive to present a favorable view, such as over estimates, contingencies, or fraud-related matters, apply greater skepticism and seek independent corroboration.

### Corroborate Important Statements

Statements that affect a conclusion should be corroborated with evidence from independent sources or other procedures. Corroboration converts an assertion into support.

Corroborate by:

- inspecting underlying documents;
- reperforming calculations;
- confirming with external parties;
- comparing to system data or independent reports;
- checking consistency with other interviews;
- reviewing subsequent events;
- obtaining written representation where appropriate.

Document what was said, who said it, when, and what corroboration was obtained. An uncorroborated statement should be flagged as such.

### Obtain Written Representation For Required And Significant Matters

Written representations are formal statements from management that the auditor uses as evidence. For some engagements they are required; for others they are prudent support for significant judgments.

A representation letter or set of representations should cover:

- responsibility for the financial statements or subject matter;
- completeness of information provided;
- management's judgments and estimates;
- subsequent events;
- related parties and disclosures;
- fraud or noncompliance of which management is aware;
- plans or intentions affecting carrying values;
- uncorrected misstatements and management's rationale;
- compliance with laws, contracts, and covenants.

The representation should be dated appropriately, addressed to the auditor, and signed by those with authority and knowledge. A representation does not eliminate the need for other evidence, and it cannot cure a scope limitation.

### Handle Contradictory Or Evasive Responses

When responses conflict with other evidence, or when a respondent is evasive, the auditor should not simply move on. The contradiction may signal a real problem.

Respond by:

- restating the question in different terms;
- asking for specific examples or documents;
- consulting a different source;
- performing additional procedures;
- documenting the inconsistency;
- considering whether the evasion indicates concealment or fraud;
- escalating if the matter is significant.

A respondent who cannot or will not answer a direct question about a significant matter is itself audit-relevant information.

### Maintain Professional Skepticism Throughout

Skepticism is not hostility. It is a mindset that questions whether the evidence supports the conclusion, especially when management is cooperative and the answers are convenient.

Apply skepticism by:

- not accepting the first answer to a significant question without support;
- looking for evidence that contradicts management's view;
- considering whether incentives, pressure, or culture could affect candor;
- probing estimates and judgments for bias;
- watching for changes in explanation over time;
- treating unusually smooth or perfect results with caution.

Cooperative management can still be wrong, and cooperative management can still be concealing a problem. Skepticism protects against both.

### Preserve Independence When Relying On Management

Relying on management statements does not mean adopting management's conclusions. The auditor evaluates the statement, not merely receives it.

Maintain independence by:

- forming an independent expectation before hearing the answer;
- testing rather than accepting management-prepared summaries;
- avoiding reliance on management's assurance language as a substitute for procedures;
- documenting the auditor's own conclusion, not management's;
- refusing to let management draft the audit conclusion or finding;
- escalating pressure to change a conclusion based on management preference.

If the auditor's conclusion mirrors management's preferred wording without independent support, independence has been compromised.

### Escalate Indicators Of Misrepresentation Or Concealment

Some responses go beyond ordinary optimism and suggest active concealment. These must be escalated.

Escalate when inquiry reveals:

- inconsistent or changing explanations;
- refusal to provide documents that should exist;
- answers that contradict system data or external evidence;
- patterns of missing or altered records;
- threats, intimidation, or attempts to limit scope;
- indications of related-party or undisclosed arrangements;
- awareness of fraud or noncompliance that was not previously disclosed.

Escalation should follow the engagement's defined path to the engagement leader, governance, legal counsel, or the appropriate authority, depending on the matter.

## Common Traps

### Treating A Confident Answer As Proof

Confidence is not evidence. A well-spoken answer without corroboration is still just a statement.

### Accepting Management's Explanation For An Exception Without Corroboration

Explanations are leads. Unless supported by independent evidence, the exception remains unresolved.

### Using Inquiry As The Only Procedure For A High-Risk Assertion

For existence, valuation, completeness, or control operation, inquiry alone is rarely enough. Add inspection, confirmation, recalculation, or analytics.

### Letting Management Draft The Representation To Avoid Disagreement

The auditor should propose the representation content based on what is needed. Management edits for accuracy, not to remove uncomfortable matters.

### Failing To Corroborate Estimates And Judgments

Estimates are inherently subjective and susceptible to bias. Test the process, assumptions, and subsequent outcomes rather than accepting the estimate as stated.

### Ignoring Evasive Or Contradictory Responses

Evasion is information. Document it, investigate, and escalate if the matter is significant.

### Dating The Representation Letter Incorrectly

The representation should be dated as of the audit report date or the conclusion date, not backdated or left undated. An undated or misdated letter undermines its evidentiary value.

### Relying On Representation To Cure A Scope Limitation

If evidence was unavailable, a representation cannot replace it. A scope limitation may require a qualified conclusion regardless of what management asserts.

## Self-Check

- Is inquiry used for understanding and explanation while corroboration is obtained for matters that affect a conclusion?
- Was the inquiry planned with objectives, specific questions, the right source, and follow-ups before it was conducted?
- Has the reliability of each source been considered, including knowledge, independence, and incentive to misstate?
- Are important statements corroborated by independent evidence, with the corroboration documented?
- Does the written representation cover required and significant matters, is it appropriately dated, and is it signed by those with authority and knowledge?
- Are contradictory or evasive responses investigated, documented, and escalated when significant?
- Is professional skepticism applied, including independent expectations, contradiction-seeking, and scrutiny of estimates for bias?
- Has independence been preserved, with the auditor forming and documenting its own conclusion rather than adopting management's wording?
- Are indicators of misrepresentation, concealment, fraud, or noncompliance escalated through the defined path?
- Is it clear in the documentation which conclusions rest on inquiry alone, which are corroborated, and which remain limited?
