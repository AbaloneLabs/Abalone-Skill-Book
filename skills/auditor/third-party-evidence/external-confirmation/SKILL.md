# External Confirmation

External confirmation is the process of obtaining and evaluating direct written response from a third party to corroborate information in the financial statements. It is among the most persuasive forms of audit evidence for existence and rights assertions, precisely because it comes from outside the entity. But its value depends entirely on the auditor controlling the process: maintaining control over the recipient, the content, and the return path. Where the entity intermediates the confirmation, the evidence is compromised; where non-responses are not followed up with alternative procedures, the procedure is incomplete. The discipline is to control the process end-to-end and to evaluate responses with appropriate scepticism.

## Core Rules

### Maintain control over the entire confirmation process

The integrity of confirmation evidence rests on the auditor controlling the process:

- **Selection**: the auditor selects the items to confirm, not management.
- **Recipient and address**: the auditor verifies the recipient's address independently (prior confirmations, public records, direct knowledge) and does not accept addresses provided solely by management.
- **Sending**: the auditor sends the request, using a method the auditor controls (auditor-controlled portal, direct mail from the auditor's office), not via the entity.
- **Return**: responses return directly to the auditor, not through the entity.

If the entity intermediates any step — provides unverified addresses, sends the requests, receives the responses — the evidence is compromised and cannot be relied upon at full strength. Document the control at each step.

### Design the confirmation request to obtain relevant, specific evidence

The request should ask for specific information that directly corroborates the assertion:

- the balance or transaction amount as of a stated date;
- the terms (credit terms, contingent features, pledges, factoring) that bear on rights and valuation;
- any amounts due to the third party that bear on completeness;
- a statement that the third party should respond whether or not the stated amount agrees.

Blank confirmations (asking the third party to state the balance without prompting) provide stronger evidence than positive confirmations that ask the third party to confirm a stated amount, because they require the third party to supply the number. Negative confirmations (respond only if you disagree) provide weaker evidence and are appropriate only for low-risk, homogeneous populations.

### Verify the recipient independently before sending

Confirmation evidence is only as good as the recipient's identity and independence. Verify the recipient's address and identity from a source independent of management:

- prior-year confirmation files;
- the third party's published address or website;
- independent databases or directories;
- direct telephone verification (not a number provided by management).

Do not send confirmations to addresses provided solely by management, especially for related parties or newly added counterparties. A confirmation sent to a controlled address returns a controlled response.

### Decide between positive and negative confirmation based on risk

- **Positive confirmation**: the third party responds whether or not the amount agrees. Stronger evidence; appropriate for material, high-risk balances and when the population is not large and homogeneous.
- **Negative confirmation**: the third party responds only if it disagrees. Weaker evidence (silence is taken as agreement); appropriate only for low-risk, homogeneous populations where the auditor expects few errors and has strong other evidence.

Reserve negative confirmations for genuinely low-risk situations; for material or high-risk balances, use positive confirmation.

### Follow up non-responses with alternative procedures

Non-response to a positive confirmation is not evidence that the balance is correct; it is a lack of evidence. For each non-response, perform alternative procedures to obtain the evidence:

- examine subsequent cash receipt of the balance;
- vouch the balance to shipping documents, contracts, or other source evidence;
- examine the customer order, the shipping/billing documents, and the reconciliation to the ledger.

Document the alternative procedures performed and the evidence obtained. A non-response with no alternative procedure leaves the assertion unsupported.

### Evaluate responses with professional scepticism

Responses are not automatically reliable. Evaluate:

- **Oral responses**: weaker than written; corroborate in writing where possible.
- **Exceptions and disagreements**: investigate each, determine whether it indicates an error, and resolve it.
- **Unusual or suspicious responses**: a response that arrives unusually fast, from an unusual address, or with unusual formatting may be fraudulent; verify its authenticity.
- **Responses routed through the entity**: treat as compromised; the auditor has lost control.

A response that disagrees with the recorded amount is evidence of a potential misstatement and must be investigated, not dismissed as a third-party error.

### Confirm the right assertions

Confirmation is strong for existence and rights, weaker for completeness and valuation:

- **Existence**: strong — the third party acknowledges the balance.
- **Rights**: strong — the third party can confirm whether the asset is pledged, factored, or subject to lien.
- **Completeness**: weak — a confirmation of recorded receivables does not detect unrecorded receivables; use source-based procedures for completeness.
- **Valuation**: indirect — confirmation confirms the balance exists but not that it is collectible; combine with ageing and subsequent-cash analysis for valuation.

Do not assume a confirmation covers assertions it does not; design separate procedures for completeness and valuation.

### Control the timing and cut-off

Confirmations are typically sent near the balance sheet date to corroborate the period-end balance. Where sent at interim, roll forward to year-end with appropriate procedures. For cut-off, combine confirmation with examination of transactions around the period boundary. Document the confirmation date and its relationship to the assertion being tested.

### Assess the aggregate sufficiency of confirmation evidence

Confirmation of a sample provides evidence about the sampled items and, through projection or evaluation, about the population. Assess:

- did enough responses (or alternative procedures) provide sufficient evidence for the assertion?
- were the non-responses adequately covered by alternative procedures?
- do the confirmed balances, taken with other evidence, support the conclusion?

If response rates are low and alternative procedures do not fill the gap, the confirmation procedure has not provided sufficient evidence, and additional procedures are needed.

## Common Traps

- **Letting the entity intermediate the process** — providing addresses, sending requests, or receiving responses — compromising the evidence.
- **Sending confirmations to unverified addresses**, especially for related parties or new counterparties, where a controlled address returns a controlled response.
- **Using negative confirmations for material or high-risk balances**, where their weak evidence is insufficient.
- **Treating non-response as evidence of correctness**, rather than as a lack of evidence requiring alternative procedures.
- **Accepting oral or unusual responses without corroboration or authentication**.
- **Assuming confirmation covers completeness and valuation**, when it primarily covers existence and rights.
- **Dismissing exceptions as third-party errors** rather than investigating them as potential misstatements.
- **Low response rates with insufficient alternative procedures**, leaving the assertion unsupported.
- **Confirming at interim without roll-forward**, leaving a gap to year-end for a period-end assertion.
- **Sending the confirmation without a statement to respond regardless of agreement**, weakening positive confirmation evidence.

## Self-Check

- Did the auditor control the entire process — selection, recipient verification, sending, and return — without the entity intermediating?
- Was the recipient's address verified independently of management before sending?
- Is the confirmation type (positive, blank, negative) appropriate to the risk and materiality of the balance?
- For each non-response, were alternative procedures performed and documented to obtain the evidence?
- Were responses evaluated with scepticism — exceptions investigated, oral responses corroborated, unusual responses authenticated?
- Am I confirming the right assertions (existence, rights) and using separate procedures for completeness and valuation?
- Is the confirmation timing appropriate to the assertion, with roll-forward where interim?
- Do the confirmed balances plus alternative procedures, in aggregate, provide sufficient evidence for the assertion, or are additional procedures needed?
- Did the request ask the third party to respond whether or not the amount agrees, to support positive confirmation?
