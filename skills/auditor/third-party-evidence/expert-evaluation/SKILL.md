# Expert Evaluation

Many audit areas require specialised skill or knowledge the auditor does not possess: fair value measurements of complex instruments, valuations of intangible assets in a business combination, actuarial estimates of pension obligations, reserves for environmental liabilities, and estimates of oil and gas reserves. In these areas the auditor relies on the work of an expert — either management's expert or the auditor's own expert. Reliance is not automatic; the auditor must evaluate the expert's competence, capability, and objectivity, understand and challenge the expert's work, and conclude whether it is adequate for audit purposes. Treating an expert's valuation as a black box that the auditor cannot question is the central failure mode.

## Core Rules

### Evaluate the expert's competence and capability before relying

Confirm the expert has the qualifications and experience relevant to the specific subject:

- professional credentials, licences, or recognition in the field;
- experience with the specific type of asset, liability, or estimate (not just general expertise);
- familiarity with the applicable framework (e.g., the relevant valuation or accounting standard);
- reputation, including any disciplinary or regulatory history.

General expertise is not enough; the expert must be capable in the specific subject and context. For management's expert, also consider whether the expert's competence is undermined by any relationship with the entity.

### Evaluate the expert's objectivity

Objectivity is as important as competence, and is the dimension most often underweighted. Assess:

- for management's expert: the nature of the relationship with the entity (employee, recurring contractor, related party), and whether the expert has interests that could impair objectivity (contingent fees, dependence on the engagement, options or equity);
- for the auditor's expert: independence from the entity and from the audit team's own objectivity;
- whether the expert's work is subject to management pressure or direction that could bias the outcome.

Where objectivity is impaired or unclear, place less reliance on the expert's work and obtain more corroborating evidence, or engage the auditor's own independent expert.

### Understand the expert's assumptions, data, and method

You do not need to replicate the expert's expertise, but you must understand the work well enough to evaluate it. Obtain and understand:

- the method or model used, and why it is appropriate to the subject;
- the key assumptions, especially those that are significant, subjective, or sensitive;
- the source data, and whether it is reliable and appropriate;
- the range of reasonably possible outcomes given the assumptions.

If you cannot understand the method and assumptions well enough to challenge them, you cannot evaluate the expert's work; obtain assistance (including the auditor's own expert) to reach that understanding.

### Challenge the significant assumptions and data

Do not accept the expert's assumptions uncritically. Evaluate:

- are the assumptions reasonable and supported, in the context of the entity, the market, and past experience?
- are they consistent with other audit evidence (e.g., business plans, market data, industry trends)?
- are they overly optimistic or conservative in a direction that benefits management?
- is the source data reliable, complete, and appropriate, and has it been corroborated?

Particularly for management's expert, the assumptions are where bias most often enters — a discount rate a point lower, a growth rate a point higher, a useful life a year longer. Test the sensitivity of the outcome to the significant assumptions; if a reasonable change in an assumption produces a materially different answer, the estimate is sensitive and warrants deeper challenge.

### Assess whether the expert's work is adequate for audit purposes

The expert's work is adequate for audit purposes only if:

- its objective and scope align with what the auditor needs for the relevant assertion;
- its method and assumptions are appropriate and well-supported;
- its data is reliable;
- its conclusions are reasonable in the context of other evidence.

If the work falls short — wrong scope, unsupported assumptions, unreliable data, or a conclusion inconsistent with other evidence — do not rely on it. Obtain additional procedures, engage the auditor's own expert, or qualify the conclusion as appropriate.

### Corroborate the expert's conclusion with other evidence

An expert's conclusion should not stand alone as the sole evidence for a material assertion. Corroborate it with:

- comparison to prior-period values and to the entity's own historical experience;
- comparison to market or industry data where available;
- subsequent events that confirm or refute the estimate;
- the entity's actual results against the assumptions, where time has passed.

A conclusion that disagrees with other evidence is not necessarily wrong, but the disagreement must be understood and resolved, not ignored.

### Consider the effect of estimation uncertainty

For estimates with high uncertainty (significant, sensitive assumptions; wide range of outcomes), the expert's point estimate is only one value within a range. Evaluate:

- the range of reasonably possible outcomes;
- whether the recorded amount falls within a reasonable range;
- whether the uncertainty warrants specific disclosure or sensitivity analysis in the financial statements.

High estimation uncertainty may require the auditor to test a range rather than a point estimate, and to consider whether management's judgement was reasonable within that range.

### Engage the auditor's own expert where management's expert is insufficient

Where management's expert lacks competence or objectivity, where the assumptions cannot be adequately challenged, or where the subject is sufficiently material and complex, engage the auditor's own expert. The auditor's expert provides independent expertise that lets the auditor evaluate management's expert and reach an independent conclusion. Using the auditor's expert does not transfer the audit judgement; the auditor remains responsible for the conclusion and must direct, evaluate, and challenge the auditor's expert's work.

### Document the evaluation and the conclusion

For reliance on any expert, document:

- the expert's identity, competence, capability, and objectivity, and how each was evaluated;
- the expert's method, significant assumptions, and source data;
- the auditor's evaluation of the method, assumptions, and data, including challenges and their resolution;
- the corroboration with other evidence;
- the conclusion on whether the expert's work is adequate and the effect on the audit.

A conclusion that relies on an expert without this documentation is indefensible; the documentation is what shows the reliance was reasoned, not blind.

## Common Traps

- **Treating the expert's valuation as a black box** that cannot be questioned, when understanding and challenging the method and assumptions is the auditor's responsibility.
- **Evaluating competence without evaluating objectivity**, missing bias from relationships, contingent fees, or management pressure.
- **Accepting significant assumptions uncritically**, especially where they are optimistic in management's favour.
- **Failing to test sensitivity** of the outcome to significant assumptions, missing estimates where a small change produces a materially different answer.
- **Relying on the expert's conclusion without corroboration** from other evidence.
- **Using management's expert where objectivity or competence is impaired** without engaging the auditor's own expert.
- **Treating the auditor's expert's work as transferring the judgement**, rather than as input the auditor must direct, evaluate, and challenge.
- **Ignoring estimation uncertainty** for sensitive estimates, where a range rather than a point estimate is the appropriate evaluation; **Documenting the conclusion without documenting the evaluation** of competence, objectivity, method, assumptions, and corroboration

## Self-Check

- Did I evaluate the expert's competence and capability for the specific subject, not just general expertise?
- Did I evaluate the expert's objectivity — relationships, interests, pressure — and reduce reliance or use my own expert where objectivity is impaired?
- Do I understand the expert's method, significant assumptions, and source data well enough to challenge them?
- Did I challenge the significant assumptions and data for reasonableness, support, consistency with other evidence, and directional bias?
- Did I test the sensitivity of the outcome to significant assumptions, especially for material and subjective estimates?
- Is the expert's work adequate for audit purposes — aligned scope, appropriate method, supported assumptions, reliable data, reasonable conclusion?
- Did I corroborate the expert's conclusion with other evidence and resolve any disagreements?
- For high-uncertainty estimates, did I consider the range of reasonably possible outcomes and whether disclosure or sensitivity analysis is required?
- Where management's expert was insufficient, did I engage the auditor's own expert and direct, evaluate, and challenge that work?
- Is the evaluation of competence, objectivity, method, assumptions, data, corroboration, and conclusion documented so the reliance is defensible?
