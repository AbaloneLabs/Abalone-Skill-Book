# Review Evidence Trail

The review evidence trail is what demonstrates that the audit was planned, performed, and supervised to the required standard. It is not the substantive evidence itself (the confirmations, recalculations, inspections) but the documentation that shows each procedure was performed, the evidence obtained was evaluated, conclusions were reached, and those conclusions were reviewed by an appropriately senior auditor. A file with strong substantive evidence but a weak review trail is hard to defend: it cannot show that the work was supervised, that judgements were challenged, or that the conclusions were reached competently. The discipline is to build the review trail as the work proceeds, not to reconstruct it at the end.

## Core Rules

### Document the full chain: objective, procedure, evidence, evaluation, conclusion

Every workpaper should read as a complete chain, not as a collection of documents. For each procedure:

- **Objective**: what assertion or risk does this address?
- **Procedure**: what was done, specifically?
- **Evidence**: what was examined (documents, confirmations, data), with source and date?
- **Evaluation**: how was the evidence evaluated, including exceptions and their resolution?
- **Conclusion**: what does the evidence support about the assertion?

A workpaper that presents evidence without stating the conclusion leaves the reviewer to infer it; a workpaper that states a conclusion without showing the evidence leaves it unsupported. The chain must be complete and explicit.

### Capture review notes and their clearance

Review notes are the primary evidence that the work was challenged and improved through review. For each review:

- record the review notes raised (questions, challenges, requested additional work);
- record the response and any additional work performed;
- record the clearance of each note by the reviewer.

Cleared review notes show that the reviewer engaged critically and that the team responded. A file with no review notes may indicate flawless work, but more often indicates superficial review; a file with notes that are never cleared indicates incomplete review. Both are weaknesses.

### Confirm appropriate review by sufficiently senior personnel

Review must be performed by personnel with the competence and authority appropriate to the judgements involved:

- routine workpapers by seniors or managers;
- significant judgements and risk areas by the manager and engagement partner;
- engagement quality review for audits requiring it, by an independent partner.

Confirm the review signatures (or equivalent electronic sign-off) are present, dated, and correspond to the appropriate level. A workpaper with no review sign-off, or reviewed only by a peer of the preparer, does not demonstrate adequate supervision.

### Ensure review is timely, not back-loaded

Review conducted in a single batch at the end of the fieldwork is weaker than review conducted as the work proceeds. Timely review:

- catches errors and gaps while they can still be fixed efficiently;
- allows the team to adjust scope based on findings;
- demonstrates ongoing supervision rather than after-the-fact rationalisation.

Build review milestones into the audit schedule, especially for high-risk areas, so that significant judgements are reviewed before the team moves on. A file where all review is dated in the final week signals back-loaded review.

### Document the evaluation of exceptions and contradictions

The review trail must show not only what was found but how exceptions, contradictions, and unexpected results were evaluated:

- an exception that arose and how it was resolved (error corrected, false positive, explained and corroborated);
- a contradiction between procedures (e.g., analytical procedure flagged a concern that detail testing did not confirm) and how it was reconciled;
- a judgement that was difficult and how it was resolved.

Exceptions and contradictions are where misstatements and weak judgements hide. A review trail that shows them being engaged with, rather than glossed, is strong evidence of a competent audit.

### Demonstrate sufficient appropriate evidence was obtained

The review trail should allow a reviewer to conclude that sufficient appropriate evidence was obtained for the opinion. This means:

- the procedures performed map to the risks identified;
- the evidence is of appropriate type and persuasiveness for the risk;
- the coverage is complete across material accounts, assertions, locations, and periods;
- any limitations or scope restrictions are documented and addressed.

The engagement partner's conclusion that sufficient appropriate evidence was obtained should be supported by the file, not merely asserted in the final review.

### Capture significant judgements and consultations

Significant judgements — materiality, risk assessments, going concern, critical estimates, group scoping — and any consultations (with specialists, national offices, technical teams) should be documented in the file, including:

- the judgement or issue;
- the alternatives considered;
- the conclusion and its basis;
- the consultation, if any, and its outcome.

These are the areas most scrutinised in inspection; a review trail that shows the judgement was made deliberately, with consultation where appropriate, is defensible. A judgement that appears in the file only as a conclusion, with no basis or alternatives, is exposed.

### Ensure the file stands alone without oral explanation

The ultimate test of the review trail is whether an independent reviewer — an engagement quality reviewer, a regulator, a successor auditor — can understand the audit, follow the evidence, and agree with the conclusions without any oral explanation from the team. Build the file to that standard:

- assumptions and context stated, not assumed;
- abbreviations and entity-specific terms explained;
- cross-references complete;
- conclusions explicit and supported.

A file that requires the team to explain it has not been documented to standard.

## Common Traps

- **Presenting evidence without a conclusion**, leaving the reviewer to infer what the evidence supports.
- **Stating conclusions without showing the evidence**, leaving them unsupported.
- **A file with no review notes**, which may indicate superficial review rather than flawless work.
- **Review notes that are never cleared**, indicating incomplete review.
- **Review by insufficiently senior personnel** for the judgements involved, or missing review sign-off.
- **Back-loaded review** all dated in the final week, signalling after-the-fact rather than ongoing supervision.
- **Glossing exceptions and contradictions** rather than documenting their evaluation and resolution.
- **Significant judgements appearing only as conclusions**, without basis, alternatives, or consultation; **A file that requires oral explanation** to be understood, failing the stand-alone test

## Self-Check

- Does each workpaper document the complete chain — objective, procedure, evidence, evaluation, conclusion — explicitly?
- Are review notes recorded, responded to, and cleared by the reviewer, demonstrating critical engagement?
- Is review performed by sufficiently senior personnel, with signatures present, dated, and at the appropriate level?
- Is review timely, with milestones built into the schedule for high-risk areas, rather than back-loaded?
- Are exceptions, contradictions, and unexpected results documented with their evaluation and resolution?
- Does the review trail support the conclusion that sufficient appropriate evidence was obtained for the opinion?
- Are significant judgements and consultations documented with basis, alternatives, and outcomes?
- Does the file stand alone — can an independent reviewer understand it and agree with the conclusions without oral explanation?
