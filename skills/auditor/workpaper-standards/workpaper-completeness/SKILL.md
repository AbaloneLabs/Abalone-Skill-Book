# Workpaper Completeness

Workpaper completeness is the final assurance that the audit file, taken as a whole, supports the opinion. It is possible to perform good work throughout the engagement and still have an incomplete file: a planned procedure that was never documented, a significant account with no workpaper, an open item that was never resolved, or a conclusion that does not tie to the opinion. The discipline is to perform a deliberate completeness review against the audit plan, the risks, and the financial statements, so that nothing material is missing and the file can be defended.

## Core Rules

### Reconcile the file to the audit plan

The audit plan defined the procedures necessary to address the identified risks. Reconcile the file to the plan:

- every planned procedure either has a workpaper or a documented reason for change;
- changes to the plan (additional procedures, modified scope) are documented with rationale;
- no planned procedure silently disappeared.

A file that does not reconcile to its plan cannot demonstrate that the identified risks were addressed. The reconciliation is the first completeness check.

### Confirm coverage of all significant accounts and assertions

Map the significant accounts and their relevant assertions to the workpapers, and confirm each has coverage:

- every significant account has a lead schedule tying to the financial statements;
- every material assertion for each account has at least one supporting workpaper;
- coverage extends to all in-scope locations, components, and periods.

Use the assertion-to-procedure matrix (from substantive-procedure-completeness) as the checklist. Any cell without a workpaper is a gap; any gap must be filled or explained.

### Confirm all financial statement amounts are tied to support

Every material amount in the financial statements should trace through the file to its support:

- the lead schedule agrees to the trial balance and to the financial statements;
- the lead schedule references the supporting workpapers;
- the supporting workpapers document the evidence and conclusion.

An amount in the financial statements that cannot be traced to a workpaper is unsupported. Perform a tie-out from the financial statements to the lowest level of support for material items.

### Resolve all open items, exceptions, and unrecorded differences

Before the file is complete, every open item must be resolved:

- exceptions identified during testing, with their investigation and resolution;
- unrecorded differences and proposed adjustments, with their disposition (booked or passed, with rationale);
- passed adjustments aggregated and evaluated against materiality;
- contingencies and uncertainties, with their evaluation.

Open items left unresolved at file completion are both a completeness gap and a risk that a material matter was not addressed. Track open items to zero, or document why an item remains open and how it was considered.

### Confirm sufficient appropriate evidence for the opinion

The file must support the engagement partner's conclusion that sufficient appropriate evidence was obtained. Confirm:

- the evidence addresses the identified risks;
- the evidence is of appropriate type and persuasiveness for each material assertion;
- the evidence covers the whole population at risk (locations, periods, components);
- any scope limitations are documented and their effect considered.

This is an aggregate judgement, not a per-workpaper check. Step back and ask whether the file, as a whole, supports the opinion being issued.

### Confirm significant judgements are documented

Significant judgements — materiality, risk assessments, going concern, critical estimates, group scoping, reliance decisions — must be documented with their basis:

- the judgement and the alternatives considered;
- the evidence and reasoning supporting the conclusion;
- any consultation and its outcome.

These are the areas most likely to be challenged. A file where a significant judgement appears only as a conclusion, without basis, is incomplete in the most important respect.

### Verify disclosures are complete and supported

Completeness extends to disclosures, which are often under-documented relative to the primary statements:

- each required disclosure under the framework has a workpaper;
- disclosed amounts are supported and agreed to the underlying records;
- the disclosure checklist is complete, with items addressed or marked not applicable with rationale;
- subsequent events, related parties, and contingencies are specifically addressed.

An unsupported or missing disclosure is a completeness gap even when the primary statements are fully supported.

### Confirm the file is finalised, signed, and dated

Completeness includes the administrative finalisation:

- all workpapers are final, not draft;
- preparation and review sign-offs are present and dated;
- the report date aligns with the completion of fieldwork and the evaluation of subsequent events;
- the file is locked or otherwise protected from uncontrolled change after finalisation.

A file that is still being changed after the report date, or that lacks sign-offs, is not complete regardless of the substantive content.

### Perform an independent completeness review

The final completeness check should be performed by someone with sufficient perspective — typically the manager or engagement partner, and for some audits the engagement quality reviewer — who looks across the whole file rather than within individual workpapers. This review asks: does the file, as a whole, support the opinion? Are there gaps, orphan items, or unresolved matters? The independent review catches what the team, close to the detail, may miss.

## Common Traps

- **A planned procedure with no workpaper and no documented reason for change**, leaving an identified risk unaddressed.
- **A significant account or assertion with no coverage**, slipping through because the matrix was not used as a checklist.
- **A financial statement amount that cannot be traced to support**, leaving a number in the opinion unsupported.
- **Open items, exceptions, or passed adjustments left unresolved** at file completion.
- **Concluding sufficient appropriate evidence without aggregating** across the file to confirm it.
- **Significant judgements documented only as conclusions**, without basis, alternatives, or consultation.
- **Under-documenting disclosures** relative to the primary statements.
- **A file still being changed after the report date**, or lacking final sign-offs.
- **Skipping the independent cross-file review**, missing gaps that the close team cannot see.
- **Treating completeness as a clerical task** rather than a substantive assurance that the file supports the opinion.

## Self-Check

- Does the file reconcile to the audit plan, with every planned procedure documented or its change explained?
- Does every significant account and material assertion have coverage, verified against the assertion-to-procedure matrix?
- Can every material financial statement amount be traced through the file to its support?
- Are all open items, exceptions, and passed adjustments resolved or documented as considered?
- Does the file, taken as a whole, support the conclusion that sufficient appropriate evidence was obtained for the opinion?
- Are significant judgements documented with basis, alternatives, evidence, and any consultation?
- Are all required disclosures complete, supported, and tied to a disclosure checklist?
- Is the file finalised — workpapers final, sign-offs present and dated, report date aligned, file protected from uncontrolled change?
- Has an independent cross-file review confirmed that the whole file supports the opinion, catching gaps the close team may miss?
