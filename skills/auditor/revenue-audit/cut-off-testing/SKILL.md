# Cut-Off Testing

Cutoff testing determines whether transactions are recorded in the period in which they belong, and it is one of the most common sources of material misstatement because the boundary is thin and the incentives to shift it are strong. Agents frequently test the last few shipments before year-end and the first few after, then conclude cutoff is correct, while the real risks lie in the shipping terms that govern transfer, the goods still in transit, the services partially performed, and the returns that arrive after period end. A clean cutoff sample is meaningless if the population excludes manual entries, if the shipping terms were misread, or if the cutoff was manipulated through side agreements.

Use this skill when testing revenue or expense cutoff around period end, evaluating goods-in-transit, services-in-progress, cash receipts timing, or post-period returns. The goal is to confirm that transactions fall in the correct period under the applicable recognition basis and to quantify and project any cutoff misstatement found.

## Core Rules

### Anchor Cutoff To The Recognition Basis, Not The Shipping Date

Cutoff is not an accounting ritual performed on shipping logs; it is the application of the recognition standard to the period boundary. The correct period depends on when control transfers, when risk and reward pass, or when service performance occurs, all of which depend on terms and substance. Testing dates without testing terms produces a false sense of comfort.

Anchor each test to:

- the applicable revenue or expense recognition basis;
- the actual transfer-of-control point for each transaction;
- the shipping terms that govern title and risk;
- whether the entity is principal or agent for the transaction;
- the service-performance measure for over-time revenue;
- the expense recognition trigger (receipt, use, consumption, invoice);
- any contract-specific terms that override defaults;
- the entity's documented cutoff policy and its consistency;
- differences between book cutoff and tax cutoff if relevant.

State the recognition rule before selecting or evaluating any sample.

### Read And Apply The Actual Shipping Terms

Shipping terms determine when title and risk transfer, and they are routinely misread or assumed. FOB shipping point and FOB destination produce opposite cutoff conclusions, and variations such as FOB origin, delivered duty paid, or ex-works change the analysis further. A single misread term can move a material shipment between periods.

For each shipment, confirm:

- the exact Incoterm or domestic shipping term used;
- whether title transfers at origin or destination;
- who bears transit risk and freight;
- the documented terms versus the entity's assumed default;
- whether terms are consistent across the population or vary by customer;
- whether the term in the system matches the term on the contract and the bill of lading;
- whether freight terms and title terms differ;
- the effect of any title-retention clauses;
- the treatment of drop shipments and third-party logistics;
- customer-specific overrides in master data.

Never assume FOB destination because the entity usually ships that way; read the term.

### Test Both Sides Of The Period Boundary

Cutoff risk exists on both sides of the boundary. Recording a shipment made after period end in the prior period overstates revenue, and recording a period-end shipment in the next period understates it. Testing only one direction misses half the risk and biases the conclusion.

Test both directions by:

- selecting the last shipments, invoices, and receipts before period end;
- selecting the first shipments, invoices, and receipts after period end;
- tracing each to the recorded period under the correct terms;
- comparing goods-in-transit at period end to the recorded revenue;
- testing the last and first journal entries around the boundary;
- examining manual and adjusting entries near period end;
- reviewing reversing entries in the early new period;
- testing both revenue and the related cost of sales cutoff together;
- confirming consistency of treatment across locations and product lines;
- documenting each item's correct period and any misstatement.

### Reconcile Goods-In-Transit To Recorded Revenue

Goods in transit at period end are the most error-prone cutoff area because their correct period depends entirely on terms. A shipment in transit under FOB destination belongs in the next period; the same shipment under FOB shipping point belongs in the current period. Agents often ignore in-transit items because they are not yet delivered.

For goods in transit at period end, determine:

- the shipping term for each in-transit shipment;
- the correct recording period under that term;
- whether the entity recorded revenue in the correct period;
- whether cost of sales and inventory were recorded consistently;
- the value of in-transit goods and their materiality;
- whether title passed before or after the boundary;
- the treatment of customer-held or consignment stock;
- the effect of any bill-and-hold arrangements;
- whether freight and transfer-of-control dates align;
- the population completeness of the in-transit listing.

### Test Services And Over-Time Performance Separately

Service revenue cutoff cannot be tested by shipping logs because there is no shipment. The cutoff depends on the measure of progress, the period the service was performed, and any milestone terms. Agents who apply goods-style cutoff to services miss the entire population.

For service and over-time revenue, test:

- the input or output measure used to recognize progress;
- services performed but not yet recorded at period end;
- amounts recorded for services not yet performed;
- milestone or percentage-of-completion cutoff;
- unbilled revenue and the basis for its measurement;
- deferred revenue and whether it relates to future performance;
- contract modifications affecting the measure at period end;
- the consistency of the measure across the period;
- costs incurred consistent with revenue recognized;
- the cutoff of related costs and subcontractor performance.

### Coordinate Cutoff With Cash Receipts And Returns

Cutoff is interconnected: revenue cutoff affects returns reserves, and cash receipts cutoff reveals timing of customer acceptance. Testing revenue cutoff in isolation can miss returns that reverse the revenue or cash that proves the wrong period. Coordinate the related populations.

Coordinate testing of:

- cash received after period end relating to period-end shipments;
- returns processed after period end relating to prior-period revenue;
- the returns reserve and its consistency with cutoff conclusions;
- credit notes issued after period end for prior-period sales;
- the cutoff of sales returns and the related inventory;
- cash receipts recorded in the wrong period;
- the aging of unbilled and deferred items;
- subsequent events that adjust period-end balances;
- the consistency of cutoff across revenue, cost, returns, and cash;
- the net effect of any offsetting cutoff errors.

### Identify And Project Cutoff Misstatements

A cutoff error is only useful if it is evaluated for materiality and projected where appropriate. Agents often list exceptions without quantifying their effect on the period or considering whether they are systematic. Each exception should be tied to its effect on the financial statements.

For each cutoff exception, determine:

- the amount and the period it was recorded versus should have been;
- the effect on revenue, cost of sales, receivables, and inventory;
- whether the error is isolated or indicative of a pattern;
- whether the error is directional and biased toward overstating revenue;
- the population from which the exception arose;
- the projection method if sampling was used;
- the effect on related balances and reserves;
- whether management has corrected or intends to correct;
- the qualitative impact regardless of quantitative materiality;
- the conclusion on whether cutoff is materially correct.

### Watch For Period-End Manipulation And Override

Cutoff is a prime target for earnings management because small shifts move large amounts. Hold-and-release of shipments, last-minute journal entries, and reopening the period are common techniques. Cutoff testing should include skepticism about whether the boundary was respected.

Look for:

- unusual spikes in the last days of the period;
- shipments held or released to meet targets;
- manual journal entries near or after period end;
- reopening of closed periods;
- revenue concentrated in a few large transactions at the boundary;
- changes to shipping terms or cutoff policy near period end;
- side agreements granting return or cancellation rights;
- transactions with related parties near the boundary;
- reversals of period-end entries in the early new period;
- management explanations that conflict with the documented terms.

## Common Traps

### Testing Dates Without Reading Terms

A shipment dated before year-end belongs in the next period if terms are FOB destination. Always read and apply the actual shipping term.

### Sampling Only One Side Of The Boundary

Testing only pre-period-end items misses revenue deferred into the next period. Test both the last items before and the first items after the boundary.

### Ignoring Goods In Transit

In-transit goods are where most cutoff errors hide because their correct period depends entirely on terms. Reconcile the in-transit listing to recorded revenue.

### Applying Goods Cutoff Logic To Services

Service revenue has no shipment; cutoff depends on the measure of progress. Test performed-but-unrecorded and recorded-but-unperformed services separately.

### Excluding Manual And Adjusting Entries

Routine shipping-log testing misses the manual entries where manipulation occurs. Include journal entries near and after period end in the population.

### Isolating Revenue Cutoff From Returns

A clean revenue cutoff with an understated returns reserve still misstates net revenue. Test returns and credit notes in the same period.

### Listing Exceptions Without Quantifying Impact

A cutoff error has no meaning until its effect on the period is measured. Quantify each exception and project where appropriate.

### Assuming Terms From Past Practice

FOB destination last year does not mean FOB destination this year. Confirm terms for each material transaction from source documents.

## Self-Check

- Is each cutoff test anchored to the recognition basis and transfer-of-control point, not just to dates?
- Are shipping terms read and applied for each material transaction, with FOB origin versus destination correctly distinguished?
- Are both the last items before and the first items after the period boundary tested?
- Is the goods-in-transit listing at period end reconciled to recorded revenue under the correct terms?
- Are services and over-time revenue tested using the measure of progress, separate from goods cutoff?
- Are revenue cutoff, cost of sales cutoff, returns reserves, and cash receipts cutoff tested together?
- Are manual and adjusting entries near and after period end included in the population?
- Is each cutoff exception quantified for its effect on revenue, cost, receivables, and inventory?
- Where sampling was used, is the misstatement projected using an appropriate method?
- Are period-end spikes, hold-and-release patterns, and reversals investigated for earnings management?
