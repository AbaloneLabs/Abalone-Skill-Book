# Subsequent Events Adjustment and Disclosure Decisions

Identifying a subsequent event is only the first step; the harder judgment is what to do about it. The treatment turns on a single question: did the event provide additional evidence of a condition that existed at the reporting date, or did it arise from a new condition after that date? Adjusting events change the numbers; non-adjusting events change the disclosures. Getting this wrong in either direction misstates the financial statements or buries material information. Beyond the binary, the auditor must judge materiality, the adequacy of disclosure, and whether the event warrants an Emphasis of Matter in the audit report.

This skill covers the decision logic for subsequent events treatment: adjusting versus non-adjusting classification, materiality of non-adjusting events, adequacy of disclosure, and the use of Emphasis of Matter or Other Matter paragraphs.

This guidance addresses audit judgment. It does not replace ISA 560, ISA 701, ISA 706, applicable accounting standards, or firm methodology. Treatment decisions must be documented and subject to engagement partner review.

## Core Rules

### Apply the "condition existed at reporting date" test

The dividing line between adjusting and non-adjusting is whether the event provides evidence of a condition that existed at the reporting date. A customer that went bankrupt after year-end but was already in financial difficulty at year-end provides evidence of a pre-existing impairment—adjusting. A fire that destroyed a factory after year-end reflects a new condition—non-adjusting, disclosed if material. Apply the test rigorously to each event and document the reasoning, because the accounting treatment flows directly from it.

### Require adjustment for adjusting events

When an event provides additional evidence of a condition existing at the reporting date, the financial statements must be adjusted. Common examples: settlement of litigation confirming a year-end obligation; receipt of information about a year-end estimate (e.g., inventory net realizable value, fair value of an investment); bankruptcy of a customer existing at year-end. Do not allow these to be merely disclosed; disclosure does not cure a misstated balance.

### Require disclosure for material non-adjusting events

Non-adjusting events that are material—such that non-disclosure would influence the decisions of users—must be disclosed. Disclosure typically includes the nature of the event and an estimate of its financial effect, or a statement that an estimate cannot be made. The materiality threshold for disclosure is judged by the event's potential effect on users' decisions, not only on the current period numbers.

### Evaluate disclosure adequacy, not only presence

A disclosure that names the event but omits the financial effect, or that buries a material event in a generic note, may be present yet inadequate. Evaluate whether the disclosure enables users to understand the nature and financial effect of the event. Where disclosure is inadequate, require management to enhance it before the report is issued.

### Consider an Emphasis of Matter for appropriately disclosed events

Where a subsequent event is material and disclosed, and the auditor judges that fundamental to user understanding, an Emphasis of Matter paragraph may be added to draw attention. Emphasis of Matter does not modify the opinion; it points to disclosure that is already adequate. Do not use Emphasis of Matter to compensate for inadequate disclosure—fix the disclosure first. Document the rationale for adding (or not adding) the paragraph.

### Handle facts discovered after the report date deliberately

If, after the report date but before issuance, the auditor becomes aware of a fact that may require adjustment or disclosure, additional procedures are required. If management adjusts or discloses, the auditor applies procedures and may issue a new or amended report. If management does not act and the fact is material, the auditor must consider notification to those charged with governance and, depending on the framework, refusal to issue the report or withdrawal.

## Common Traps

### Disclosing an adjusting event instead of adjusting

The most frequent error is treating an event that confirms a year-end condition as non-adjusting and disclosing it. This leaves the balance sheet misstated. The test is the existence of the condition at year-end, not the timing of the event.

### Burying material non-adjusting events in generic notes

A material non-adjusting event disclosed without its financial effect, or hidden in a boilerplate note, fails the disclosure objective. Evaluate the quality of disclosure, not merely its presence.

### Using Emphasis of Matter to fix disclosure

Emphasis of Matter draws attention to adequate disclosure; it does not substitute for it. If disclosure is inadequate, require enhancement, not an emphasis paragraph.

### Applying current-period materiality to disclosure decisions

The materiality of a non-adjusting event for disclosure is judged by its effect on users' understanding, which may differ from current-period quantitative materiality. A post-year-end acquisition or catastrophe may warrant disclosure even if its quantified effect is below materiality, because of its qualitative significance.

### Mishandling facts discovered after the report date

Delay, silence, or informal handling of facts discovered after the report date but before issuance creates significant risk. Follow the framework: additional procedures, management action, and report implications, with documentation and governance communication.

## Self-Check

- For each material subsequent event, have I applied the "condition existed at reporting date" test and documented the basis for adjusting versus non-adjusting classification?
- Are adjusting events reflected in adjusted numbers, not merely disclosed?
- Are material non-adjusting events disclosed with their nature and estimated financial effect, and is the disclosure adequate for user understanding?
- Have I judged disclosure materiality by user impact, not only current-period quantitative thresholds?
- Where I added an Emphasis of Matter, is the underlying disclosure adequate, and is the rationale documented?
- For facts discovered after the report date but before issuance, have I applied the required additional procedures and addressed management action and report implications?
- Have I documented the conclusions on treatment for every material subsequent event?
- Has the engagement partner reviewed the subsequent events treatment decisions?
