# Subsequent Events, Going Concern, and Litigation Interaction

Subsequent events do not exist in isolation. The most consequential ones—loss of financing, adverse litigation outcomes, customer failures, regulatory action—intersect with two other high-risk audit areas: going concern and litigation/contingencies. An event that is non-adjusting for the balance sheet may nonetheless destroy the going concern assumption, or confirm a previously disclosed contingency. The auditor must evaluate subsequent events not only for their direct accounting effect but for their interaction with these related judgments. Treating these areas as separate workstreams misses the connections where audit risk concentrates.

This skill covers the intersection judgments: how subsequent events affect going concern evaluation, how post-reporting-date litigation and claims interact with contingencies and provisions, and how to coordinate the three workstreams so that evidence gathered in one informs the others.

This guidance addresses audit judgment at the intersection of standards. It does not replace ISA 560, ISA 570, ISA 580, applicable accounting standards, or firm methodology. Intersection conclusions must be documented and subject to engagement partner and, where required, independent review.

## Core Rules

### Evaluate every material subsequent event for going concern impact

A subsequent event that is non-adjusting for balance sheet purposes may nonetheless indicate that the going concern assumption is no longer appropriate or that a material uncertainty exists. Loss of a major customer, inability to refinance maturing debt, regulatory action threatening operations, or a catastrophic event after year-end must be evaluated for going concern implications even though they arose after the reporting date. Coordinate the subsequent events review with the going concern evaluation so that such events are not processed only through the subsequent events lens.

### Use post-reporting-date litigation to confirm or refute year-end provisions

Litigation and claims often resolve after the reporting date. A settlement, judgment, or adverse development provides evidence of the obligation that existed at year-end and may confirm that a provision was adequate, inadequate, or should have been recognized. Conversely, a favorable outcome may indicate a provision was overstated. Evaluate post-reporting-date legal developments against year-end provisions and contingencies, and adjust or disclose accordingly. Coordinate with the legal counsel inquiry and the contingencies workstream.

### Treat management override and fraud indicators across the intersection

Subsequent events that suggest management override or fraud—a restatement of prior periods discovered after year-end, whistleblower allegations, regulatory investigation—must be evaluated for their effect on going concern, on litigation and contingencies, and on the overall audit. These events cut across all three areas and may require expanded procedures and a reassessment of risk.

### Reassess the adequacy of related disclosures holistically

When a subsequent event affects going concern or confirms a contingency, the related disclosures must be evaluated together. A going concern disclosure, a contingency disclosure, and a subsequent events note that tell inconsistent stories undermine the financial statements. Ensure the disclosures are coherent and that each cross-references the others where appropriate.

### Coordinate timing of the three workstreams

Going concern evaluation, litigation/contingencies review, and subsequent events review must converge at the end of the audit. Evidence gathered late in the engagement—legal counsel responses, refinancing confirmations, post-year-end trading results—often bears on all three. Structure the workstreams so that final conclusions are reached together, not in sequence where early conclusions are overtaken by late evidence.

### Document the intersection conclusions explicitly

Because the intersection is where risk concentrates, document not only the conclusions within each area but the cross-area reasoning: why a particular event is non-adjusting but going-concern-relevant; why a litigation outcome confirms a year-end provision; why the disclosures are coherent across the three areas.

## Common Traps

### Processing subsequent events in a silo

Treating subsequent events review as a standalone procedure, separate from going concern and litigation, misses the connections. A loss of financing is processed as a non-adjusting event and disclosed, without triggering a going concern reassessment.

### Failing to use post-year-end litigation evidence

Legal counsel responses and post-year-end developments are powerful evidence about year-end provisions and contingencies. The trap is gathering this evidence for the litigation workstream but not feeding it back into provision adequacy.

### Inconsistent disclosures across the three areas

A going concern note that downplays risk, a contingency note that discloses a material claim, and a subsequent events note that describes a financing failure can contradict each other. Review the disclosures as a set for coherence.

### Reaching going concern or litigation conclusions too early

Conclusions reached at interim, before subsequent events and late legal evidence are available, may be overtaken. Defer final conclusions until the workstreams converge at the end of the engagement.

### Underestimating fraud and override indicators

Subsequent events suggesting fraud or override have effects that ripple across going concern, litigation, and the overall audit. Treating them narrowly—as a single disclosure—understates the risk and the required response.

## Self-Check

- For every material subsequent event, have I evaluated its effect on the going concern assumption, not only its balance sheet treatment?
- Have I used post-reporting-date litigation and claims developments to confirm or refute the adequacy of year-end provisions and contingencies?
- Have I evaluated subsequent events suggesting fraud or management override for their cross-area impact on going concern, litigation, and overall audit risk?
- Are the going concern, contingency, and subsequent events disclosures coherent and consistent with each other?
- Have I structured the three workstreams to converge at the end of the engagement, so that final conclusions incorporate late evidence?
- Have I documented the cross-area reasoning for events that affect more than one area?
- Have I reassessed earlier conclusions in light of late subsequent events and legal developments?
- Has the engagement partner reviewed the intersection conclusions, and where required, has independent review been obtained?
