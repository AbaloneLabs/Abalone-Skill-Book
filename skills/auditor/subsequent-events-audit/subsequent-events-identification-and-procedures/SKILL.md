# Subsequent Events Identification and Procedures

Events that occur after the reporting date but before the audit report is issued can change whether the financial statements give a true and fair view. Some events provide additional evidence of conditions that existed at the reporting date and require adjustment; others relate to conditions arising after the reporting date and require disclosure. The auditor's obligation under ISA 560 is to design and perform procedures to identify events up to the date of the audit report, not merely to wait for management to volunteer information. The risk is that material subsequent events—litigation settlements, customer failures, asset impairments, financing collapses—are discovered after the report is issued, undermining the opinion and exposing the auditor to liability.

This skill covers the procedures and judgments for identifying and evaluating subsequent events: defining the active and subsequent periods, designing inquiry and reading procedures, distinguishing adjusting from non-adjusting events, and determining the appropriate financial statement treatment.

This guidance addresses audit procedures. It does not replace ISA 560, applicable law, or firm methodology. All subsequent events procedures and conclusions must be documented and subject to engagement partner review.

## Core Rules

### Define the periods precisely

The "active period" runs from the start of the reporting period to the reporting date (the date the financial statements are authorized for issue). The "subsequent period" runs from the reporting date to the date of the auditor's report, and the auditor must extend procedures to the date as near as practicable to the report date. For events discovered between the report date and the date the financial statements are issued, additional procedures and potential action are required. Know which period you are in, because the obligations differ.

### Design proactive procedures, do not rely on management

The auditor must actively design procedures to identify subsequent events, not wait for management to report them. Required procedures include inquiring of management and those charged with governance about events since the reporting date, reviewing management's processes for identifying such events, reading minutes of meetings of those charged with governance, reviewing interim financial statements and other reports, and inquiring of legal counsel. Document each procedure and its results.

### Distinguish adjusting from non-adjusting events

Adjusting events provide additional evidence of conditions that existed at the reporting date and require adjustment to the financial statements (e.g., settlement of a pre-year-end court case confirming a present obligation; bankruptcy of a customer existing at year-end confirming receivable impairment). Non-adjusting events arise from conditions that came into existence after the reporting date and require disclosure if material (e.g., a major fire or acquisition after year-end). The classification drives the treatment; document the basis for each material event's classification.

### Evaluate conditions pointing to going concern problems

Subsequent events that indicate going concern difficulties (loss of a major customer, inability to refinance, regulatory action, catastrophic event) must be evaluated for their effect on the going concern assumption and the related disclosures, even if they are non-adjusting for balance sheet purposes.

### Address events discovered after the report date but before issuance

If the auditor becomes aware of a material event after the report date but before the financial statements are issued, the auditor must apply additional procedures and determine whether management will adjust or disclose. If management does not, the auditor must consider the implications for the report, including potential modification or addition of an Emphasis of Matter, and in some jurisdictions, withdrawal.

### Obtain a written representation from management

Obtain a written representation from management and, where appropriate, those charged with governance, that all events subsequent to the reporting date and for which adjustment or disclosure is required have been adjusted or disclosed. This representation complements, but does not substitute for, the auditor's own procedures.

## Common Traps

### Treating subsequent events review as a formality

Procedures performed mechanically—reading minutes, asking a generic question—often miss material events. The risk concentrates where conditions are uncertain at year-end: litigation, large receivables, financing, inventory obsolescence, and contingencies. Focus inquiry on these areas.

### Confusing adjusting and non-adjusting events

Misclassification leads to the wrong treatment: adjusting a non-adjusting event distorts the balance sheet, while merely disclosing an adjusting event leaves a material misstatement. The test is whether the event provides evidence of a condition that existed at the reporting date.

### Cutting procedures too early

Procedures performed at interim dates, well before the report date, miss events occurring in the gap. Extend procedures to as near as practicable to the report date, and document the date through which procedures were performed.

### Ignoring going concern signals in subsequent events

A non-adjusting event (e.g., loss of financing after year-end) may nonetheless indicate that the going concern assumption is no longer appropriate or that a material uncertainty exists. Evaluate subsequent events for going concern implications separately from their balance sheet treatment.

### Relying on management's representation alone

The written representation is required but is not a substitute for the auditor's own procedures. Management may be unaware of, or unwilling to disclose, material events. The auditor's independent procedures are the primary evidence.

## Self-Check

- Have I defined the active period, subsequent period, and report date precisely, and know which obligations apply to each?
- Did I design and perform proactive procedures (inquiry, reading minutes, reviewing interim reports, legal counsel inquiry) rather than relying on management?
- Did I focus inquiry on areas of year-end uncertainty: litigation, receivables, financing, inventory, contingencies?
- For each material subsequent event, have I documented whether it is adjusting or non-adjusting, and the basis for classification?
- Have I evaluated subsequent events for going concern implications, separate from balance sheet treatment?
- Do I have procedures in place for events discovered after the report date but before issuance, including the implications for the report?
- Have I obtained a written representation from management about subsequent events, while not treating it as a substitute for my own procedures?
- Have I documented the date through which subsequent events procedures were performed?
