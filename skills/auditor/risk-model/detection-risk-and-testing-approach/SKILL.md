# Detection Risk And Testing Approach

Detection risk is the one component of audit risk the auditor directly controls. It is set by the nature, timing, and extent of procedures, and it must be driven low enough to offset the inherent and control risk the auditor has assessed. The testing approach, substantive or controls-reliance, is the strategic decision that frames how detection risk is managed. Agents often default to one approach out of habit, set sample sizes without reference to the required detection risk, or treat the three levers of nature, timing, and extent as interchangeable. Each error leaves detection risk higher than the auditor claims, which is the precise condition under which material misstatement goes undetected.

Use this skill before choosing the overall testing approach, before setting the nature, timing, and extent of procedures, and whenever detection risk must be driven lower in response to assessed risk. The goal is a deliberate, risk-responsive approach in which every lever is set with reference to how low detection risk must go.

## Core Rules

### Understand That Detection Risk Is Set By The Auditor's Own Procedures

Unlike inherent and control risk, which the auditor assesses, detection risk is controlled by what the auditor does. This makes it the lever the auditor must reason about most carefully.

Detection risk is driven down by:

- more persuasive and independent evidence, such as external confirmation or physical inspection;
- larger sample sizes and broader populations;
- more reliable procedure types, such as recalculation over inquiry;
- timing closer to or after period end;
- procedures that management cannot anticipate.

The higher the inherent and control risk, the lower detection risk must be driven, and the more these levers must be applied. Setting detection risk without reference to assessed risk is the core failure this skill prevents.

### Choose The Overall Approach Based On Risk, Not Habit

Two broad strategies exist, and the choice should follow the risk assessment, not convenience or last year's approach.

A substantive approach gathers evidence primarily through substantive testing of balances and transactions. It is appropriate when:

- controls are weak, absent, or untested;
- the population is small enough to test substantially in full;
- controls cannot be reliably tested;
- the cost of testing controls exceeds the benefit;
- significant risks require direct substantive evidence.

A controls-reliance approach tests controls to reduce control risk and then performs reduced substantive procedures. It is appropriate when:

- controls are well-designed and operating;
- populations are large and repetitive;
- automation makes controls reliable and testable;
- testing controls is more efficient than testing every transaction.

A hybrid is common: rely on automated controls for completeness and accuracy while performing substantive procedures on judgmental items. Document the basis for the chosen strategy per significant area.

### Set Nature To Match The Required Persuasiveness

Nature is the type of procedure, and procedures differ in persuasiveness. Higher risk demands more persuasive evidence.

Order of persuasiveness, roughly:

- external confirmation and physical inspection are highly persuasive because they come from independent sources;
- recalculation and reperformance are persuasive because they are objective;
- inspection of documents is moderately persuasive, depending on whether the document is external or internal;
- analytical procedures are persuasive when expectations are precise but weaker when relationships are loose;
- inquiry alone is the weakest evidence and is never sufficient for high-risk areas.

Match the nature to the risk. A high-risk assertion tested only by inquiry has detection risk set far too high.

### Set Timing To Cover The Period-End Risk

Timing is when the procedure is performed. Higher risk, especially for cutoff and late-period manipulation, pushes testing toward or after period end.

Timing considerations:

- interim testing reduces effort but leaves a gap to period end that must be covered by roll-forward procedures;
- period-end or post-period-end testing is required for cutoff-sensitive items, late adjustments, and inventory observation;
- for significant risks such as revenue cutoff, testing at interim without roll-forward leaves detection risk uncontrolled at year-end;
- controls testing must cover the relevant period, not just a single point, to support an operating effectiveness conclusion.

Document the timing and, where interim testing is used, the roll-forward procedures that bridge to period end.

### Set Extent To Drive Sampling Risk Low Enough

Extent is how much testing is done. Higher risk means larger samples, broader populations, and lower tolerable misstatement.

Extent drivers:

- higher inherent or control risk increases sample size;
- lower tolerable misstatement increases sample size;
- higher expected error rate increases sample size;
- greater need for assurance increases sample size;
- larger and more heterogeneous populations may increase sample size.

Sampling risk must be considered so the chosen extent actually drives detection risk low enough. A sample size copied from a template without reference to risk, tolerable limits, and expected error does not control detection risk.

### Give Significant Risks Tailored, Not Routine, Responses

Significant risks require more than standard substantive testing. The response must be specific to the risk.

For significant risks:

- design procedures that address the specific assertion and the specific fraud or override pathway;
- use more persuasive evidence, such as external confirmation for receivables or physical inspection for inventory;
- perform unpredictable procedures that management cannot anticipate;
- test journal entries for override risk, especially manual, late-period, and top-side entries;
- review estimates for bias and override;
- examine significant unusual transactions for substance over form.

Generic substantive procedures spread evenly across the audit do not constitute a tailored response to a significant risk.

### Reassess Detection Risk When Fieldwork Changes The Picture

Detection risk is set based on assessed inherent and control risk. When those assessments change, detection risk must be reset.

Reassess when:

- exceptions or errors cluster in one area, indicating higher risk than planned;
- controls are found ineffective, raising control risk;
- contradictory evidence or explanations appear;
- analytical procedures show unexpected relationships;
- fraud indicators emerge;
- scope limitations arise.

When assessed risk rises, detection risk must be driven lower, which means expanding nature, timing, or extent. Document the reassessment and the expanded procedures.

## Common Traps

### Defaulting To One Approach Out Of Habit

Choosing substantive or controls-reliance based on last year or firm preference, rather than the current risk assessment, produces an approach that may not fit the risk.

### Setting Sample Size Without Reference To Detection Risk

A sample size copied from a template does not control detection risk unless it responds to the assessed risk, tolerable limits, and expected error.

### Using Inquiry As The Primary Response To High Risk

Inquiry alone is weak evidence. High-risk assertions demand corroboration, external evidence, recalculation, or inspection.

### Performing All Testing At Interim Without Roll-Forward

Interim testing reduces effort but leaves the period-end gap unaddressed. Without roll-forward, detection risk at year-end is uncontrolled.

### Treating Nature, Timing, And Extent As Interchangeable

The three levers serve different purposes. More extent cannot compensate for the wrong nature, and period-end timing cannot compensate for an unpersuasive procedure type.

### Giving Significant Risks Routine Responses

Listing a risk as significant and then performing the same substantive procedures as for ordinary items is not a tailored response and leaves detection risk too high for that risk.

### Failing To Reassess When Risk Rises

Sticking to the planned response when controls fail or fraud indicators appear means detection risk is now set too high for the actual risk.

## Self-Check

- Is detection risk understood as the component the auditor controls through nature, timing, and extent, and is it set with reference to assessed inherent and control risk?
- Is the overall approach, substantive, controls-reliance, or hybrid, chosen based on the current risk assessment rather than habit, with the basis documented per significant area?
- Is the nature of procedures matched to the required persuasiveness, with high-risk areas receiving external confirmation, inspection, or recalculation rather than inquiry alone?
- Is timing set to cover period-end risk, with roll-forward procedures documented where interim testing is used?
- Is extent set to drive sampling risk low enough, with sample sizes justified by risk, tolerable limits, and expected error rather than copied from a template?
- Are significant risks given tailored, risk-specific responses, including unpredictable procedures and override-focused testing?
- Is there a documented trigger to reassess detection risk and expand procedures when fieldwork surfaces exceptions, control failures, or fraud indicators?
- Does every procedure map back to the assessed risk it addresses, so that detection risk is demonstrably controlled for each significant area?
