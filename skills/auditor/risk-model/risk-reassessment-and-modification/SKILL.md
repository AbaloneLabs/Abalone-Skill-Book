# Risk Reassessment And Modification

Risk assessment is not a one-time event performed at planning. It is a continuous process that runs through the engagement, and it must be updated when new information emerges, when conditions change, or when results differ from expectations. The recurring failure is treating the initial risk assessment as fixed, proceeding with the originally planned procedures regardless of what the audit reveals, and failing to recognize that new information can render the original response insufficient. A risk assessment that is never revisited is a risk assessment that may no longer reflect reality by the time the opinion is issued. The auditor must remain alert to information that changes the risk picture and must be willing to modify the response, even significantly, when the evidence demands it. Reassessment is not a sign of poor planning; it is the sign of an audit that responds to what it learns.

Use this skill when new information emerges during the audit, when conditions change, when results differ from expectations, and when deciding whether the planned procedures remain sufficient. The goal is a risk assessment that reflects current conditions and a response that is modified promptly when the evidence requires it.

## Core Rules

### Treat Risk Assessment As Continuous

Risk assessment begins at planning but does not end there. Throughout the engagement, the auditor performs procedures, obtains evidence, and learns about the entity and its environment. Each piece of information has the potential to confirm, refine, or overturn the initial assessment. Treating risk assessment as a planning-only exercise leaves the audit locked into a response that may no longer be appropriate.

Treat assessment as continuous by:

- remaining alert, throughout the engagement, to information that bears on risk;
- considering whether each significant finding, deviation, or unexpected result affects the risk assessment;
- creating explicit checkpoints during the engagement to revisit the assessment, not only at planning;
- recognizing that the final risk assessment, reflected in the opinion, may differ from the planning assessment.

A continuous mindset ensures the assessment evolves with the evidence.

### Identify Triggers For Reassessment

Certain developments should trigger a deliberate reassessment of risk. Recognizing these triggers promptly is essential to keeping the response aligned with reality.

Triggers include:

- identification of a control deficiency, particularly one that affects a relied-upon control;
- unexpected results from substantive procedures, such as uncorrected misstatements or patterns of error;
- evidence of fraud, however small, or of management override;
- changes in the entity's business, ownership, or management;
- changes in the external environment, including market, regulatory, or economic developments;
- identification of related parties or transactions not previously known;
- subsequent events that reveal risks not considered at planning;
- inconsistencies between the auditor's expectations and management's representations or explanations.

Each trigger warrants a deliberate consideration of whether the risk assessment must change.

### Evaluate Whether Planned Procedures Remain Sufficient

When a trigger arises, the auditor must evaluate whether the originally planned procedures remain sufficient to address the revised risk. Procedures designed for one risk picture may be inadequate for another. The evaluation must be substantive, not a rubber-stamp confirmation that the original plan stands.

Evaluate by:

- comparing the revised risk to the nature, timing, and extent of planned procedures;
- identifying gaps where the planned procedures do not address the new or elevated risk;
- considering whether additional assertions, locations, or populations now require testing;
- determining whether the timing of procedures must shift, for example from interim to year-end;
- concluding whether the original plan can stand, with modification, or must be substantially revised.

A substantive evaluation, not a reflexive confirmation, keeps the response adequate.

### Modify The Nature, Timing, And Extent Of Procedures

Where reassessment indicates the original response is insufficient, the auditor must modify the procedures. Modification can take three dimensions: nature, what procedures are performed; timing, when they are performed; and extent, how many items or how much coverage.

Modify by:

- changing the nature of procedures, for example adding confirmations or expert involvement where analytical procedures alone are insufficient;
- shifting timing, for example moving substantive testing from interim to year-end where control risk has increased;
- expanding extent, for example increasing sample sizes or testing additional locations or populations;
- adding procedures for newly identified risks or assertions that were not in the original plan.

Modification must be calibrated to the revised risk, not applied uniformly.

### Respond To Control Deficiencies And Unexpected Results

Control deficiencies and unexpected substantive results are among the most common reassessment triggers. A relied-upon control that is found not to operate effectively undermines the basis for reduced substantive testing. Unexpected misstatements may indicate a broader problem that the planned procedures would not catch.

Respond by:

- where a relied-upon control fails, assessing whether reliance can continue and, if not, expanding substantive testing to compensate;
- where unexpected misstatements are identified, investigating the root cause and determining whether the planned procedures would detect similar issues;
- where a pattern of error emerges, considering whether it indicates systemic problems, fraud, or a broader scope of misstatement;
- documenting the deficiency or result, the reassessment, and the modified response.

Ignoring these triggers, or treating them as isolated, leaves the audit response inadequate to the actual risk.

### Respond To Evidence Of Fraud Or Override

Evidence of fraud, even if not material in isolation, is a severe trigger. Fraud indicates that the entity's representations and controls may not be reliable, and that the risk picture has fundamentally changed. Management override, in particular, can invalidate the reliance placed on controls.

Respond by:

- treating any evidence of fraud as a potential indicator of a broader problem;
- reconsidering the reliability of management representations and the integrity of the control environment;
- expanding procedures to address the possibility of broader misstatement or concealment;
- considering the implications for the engagement itself, including the ability to continue;
- consulting with appropriate parties, including those charged with governance and, where required, legal advisors.

Fraud evidence cannot be treated as an isolated issue; it demands a fundamental reconsideration of the risk response.

### Document The Reassessment And Modified Response

Every reassessment and modification must be documented. The documentation must explain the trigger, the revised assessment, the evaluation of whether planned procedures remained sufficient, and the modified response. This documentation supports the opinion and enables review.

Document by:

- recording the trigger and the information that gave rise to the reassessment;
- documenting the revised risk assessment and the reasoning behind it;
- recording the evaluation of the original plan and the decision to modify;
- linking the modified procedures to the revised risk;
- ensuring the final risk assessment, reflected in the opinion, is supported by the documentation.

Undocumented reassessment cannot be reviewed or defended and leaves the opinion unsupported.

## Common Traps

### Treating Risk Assessment As A Planning-Only Exercise

Risk assessment is continuous. A planning-only mindset leaves the audit locked into a response that may no longer be appropriate.

### Missing Or Ignoring Reassessment Triggers

Control deficiencies, unexpected results, fraud evidence, and environmental changes are triggers. Missing or ignoring them leaves the response inadequate.

### Reflexively Confirming The Original Plan

When a trigger arises, the evaluation must be substantive. Reflexively confirming the original plan fails to address the revised risk.

### Failing To Modify All Three Dimensions

Modification includes nature, timing, and extent. Changing only one dimension may not fully address the revised risk.

### Treating Fraud Evidence As Isolated

Fraud evidence, even if not material in isolation, demands fundamental reconsideration. Treating it as isolated understates the risk.

### Failing To Document The Reassessment

Undocumented reassessment cannot be reviewed or defended. The trigger, revised assessment, and modified response must be recorded.

### Maintaining Reliance On A Failed Control

Where a relied-upon control fails, reliance must be reconsidered and substantive testing expanded. Maintaining reliance leaves the audit exposed.

## Self-Check

- Is risk assessment treated as continuous throughout the engagement, with explicit checkpoints to revisit the assessment?
- Are reassessment triggers, including control deficiencies, unexpected results, fraud evidence, environmental changes, and new information, recognized and acted upon promptly?
- When a trigger arises, is the evaluation of whether planned procedures remain sufficient substantive, rather than a reflexive confirmation?
- Where procedures are modified, are nature, timing, and extent all considered and calibrated to the revised risk?
- Are control deficiencies and unexpected results investigated for root cause and broader implications, with reliance and substantive testing adjusted?
- Is evidence of fraud or management override treated as a severe trigger that demands fundamental reconsideration of the risk response?
- Is every reassessment and modification documented, including the trigger, revised assessment, evaluation, and modified procedures?
- Does the final risk assessment, reflected in the opinion, match the conditions at the time of the opinion, not the conditions at planning?
- Could an independent reviewer confirm that the audit response evolved with the evidence and that no trigger was ignored?
- Is the reassessment free of a planning-only mindset or reflexive confirmation of the original plan?
