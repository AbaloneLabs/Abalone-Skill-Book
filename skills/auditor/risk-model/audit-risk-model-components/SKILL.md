# Audit Risk Model Components

The audit risk model is the logic that connects what could go wrong, whether controls reduce that exposure, and how much evidence the auditor must gather. Its purpose is to make audit risk deliberate rather than accidental. Agents often treat the model as a formula to compute a number, or worse, skip it entirely and run a standard program of procedures. Both mistakes produce work that feels thorough but is not aimed at the risks that matter. The model is a reasoning tool: it tells the auditor that when inherent and control risk are high, detection risk must be driven low, and that means more persuasive evidence, larger samples, and work closer to period end.

Use this skill before deciding the nature, timing, and extent of audit procedures, before combining the risk components into an overall response, and whenever the relationship between the components must be reasoned through and documented. The goal is to make every procedure a deliberate response to assessed risk, not a habit.

## Core Rules

### Understand Each Component And What Drives It

The model has three components, and each reflects a different source of risk. Confusing them produces mis-targeted procedures.

- Inherent risk is the susceptibility of an assertion to misstatement before considering controls. It reflects the nature of the item, complexity, judgment, external factors, and fraud pressure. The auditor does not control it; the auditor assesses it.
- Control risk is the risk that controls will not prevent or detect a misstatement. It reflects control design and operating effectiveness. The auditor can choose to rely on controls, but only after testing them.
- Detection risk is the risk that the auditor's own procedures will not catch a misstatement that exists. It is the component the auditor directly controls through the nature, timing, and extent of testing.

Keep the components distinct. Inherent risk is about the item, control risk is about the entity's controls, and detection risk is about the auditor's work.

### Reason About The Combination, Do Not Calculate To False Precision

The model is commonly expressed as the product of the three components, but it is a reasoning tool, not a calculation to a spurious decimal.

The core logic:

- higher inherent risk plus higher control risk demands lower detection risk;
- lower detection risk means more persuasive evidence, larger samples, more reliable procedures, and timing closer to period end;
- if controls are strong and tested, control risk can be assessed lower, allowing a higher acceptable detection risk and reduced substantive testing;
- if inherent risk is low and controls are strong, the audit can be more efficient.

Do not compute a precise detection-risk percentage and present it as if it were a measured quantity. Use the model to set the direction and intensity of testing.

### Assess Inherent Risk At The Assertion Level

Inherent risk is not a single rating for an account. It is assessed per relevant assertion, because an item can be high risk for one assertion and low for another. Inventory may be high risk for valuation and existence but low for classification.

For each significant area, document:

- which assertions are relevant;
- the inherent risk factors present for each assertion;
- the assessed level of inherent risk and the reasoning.

Factors that raise inherent risk include complexity, judgment, estimation uncertainty, fraud susceptibility, volume and volatility, unusual or non-routine transactions, related-party dealings, prior-period errors, changes in policy or systems, external pressure, reliance on third-party data, fair-value measurement with unobservable inputs, and cutoff sensitivity.

### Assess Control Risk Honestly Before Relying On Controls

Assessing control risk as low is a decision to rely on controls, and it carries an obligation to test them. Low control risk is not a default; it is a conclusion earned by evidence.

Before relying on controls:

- confirm the control is designed to address the specific risk;
- test design effectiveness;
- test operating effectiveness across the period;
- consider whether the control is manual or automated and the reliability of each;
- evaluate who performs the control and whether they are independent and competent;
- assess whether the control produces evidence of its operation;
- consider whether management can override it.

If controls are weak, absent, or untested, control risk stays high or maximum, and the response must be more substantive. Do not claim low control risk because a policy exists.

### Translate Combined Risk Into Nature, Timing, And Extent

Once inherent and control risk are assessed, detection risk is set by adjusting three levers. Each lever responds to higher combined risk.

Nature: the type of procedure. Higher risk demands more persuasive and more independent evidence, such as external confirmations or direct inspection rather than inquiry alone.

Timing: when the procedure is performed. Higher risk pushes testing toward or after period end, because interim testing may not capture late changes. Roll-forward procedures are needed when interim work is performed.

Extent: how much testing is done. Higher risk means larger samples, broader populations, and lower tolerable misstatement. Sampling risk must be considered so the chosen extent actually drives detection risk low enough.

Document how each lever was set in response to the assessed risk.

### Recognize When Combined Risk Creates A Significant Risk

Some combinations of inherent and control risk produce a significant risk, which requires special audit consideration and a tailored response. Significant risks are not handled with routine procedures.

Treat as significant risks, typically:

- management override of controls;
- fraud in revenue recognition;
- complex or subjective estimates and fair-value measurements;
- related-party transactions;
- significant unusual transactions near period end;
- going-concern uncertainties;
- valuation of hard-to-value assets;
- compliance with restrictive covenants or regulation.

For each significant risk, document a response that is specific to that risk, addressing the assertion, the fraud angle, the estimation uncertainty, or the override pathway.

### Reassess The Model When Fieldwork Changes The Picture

The model is not frozen at planning. Fieldwork findings can change the assessed risk and therefore the required response.

Reassess when:

- exceptions, errors, or uncorrected misstatements cluster in one area;
- controls are found to be absent or ineffective;
- contradictory evidence or explanations appear;
- analytical procedures show unexpected relationships;
- fraud indicators emerge;
- scope limitations or access restrictions arise;
- management is evasive or uncooperative;
- a related party or unusual transaction surfaces late.

When risk rises, expand the nature, timing, or extent of procedures, and document why the original response was no longer sufficient.

## Common Traps

### Skipping The Model And Running A Standard Program

Applying last year's procedures without re-assessing inherent and control risk produces work that is not aimed at the current risk profile. A busy audit is not the same as a risk-responsive audit.

### Calculating Detection Risk To False Precision

The model is a reasoning tool. Computing a precise detection-risk percentage implies a false precision and obscures the real judgment.

### Rating Control Risk Low Without Testing Controls

Claiming low control risk to justify lighter testing, without testing design and operating effectiveness, is both a logic error and a standards breach. Reliance must be earned by evidence.

### Assessing Inherent Risk At The Account Level Only

An account can be high risk for one assertion and low for another. Account-level ratings mis-target procedures and miss assertion-specific exposures.

### Treating Significant Risks With Routine Procedures

Listing a risk as significant and then performing the same substantive procedures as for ordinary items is not a tailored response. Significant risks need specific, often more skeptical, procedures.

### Freezing The Model At Planning

Sticking to the planned response when controls fail or fraud indicators appear means the audit is now testing the wrong level of risk. Reassessment and expansion must follow.

### Using Inquiry As The Primary Response To High Risk

Inquiry alone is weak evidence, especially for fraud or override. High combined risk demands corroboration, external evidence, recalculation, or inspection.

## Self-Check

- Are inherent risk, control risk, and detection risk understood as distinct components, with their different sources kept separate?
- Is the model used as reasoning to set the direction and intensity of testing, rather than calculated to a spurious decimal?
- Is inherent risk assessed at the assertion level, with the specific factors raising it documented per assertion?
- Is control risk assessed honestly, with any reliance on controls supported by tested design and operating effectiveness?
- Are nature, timing, and extent each set deliberately in response to combined risk, with higher risk driving more persuasive evidence, period-end timing, and greater extent?
- Are significant risks identified and given tailored, risk-specific responses rather than routine procedures?
- Is there a documented trigger to reassess the model and expand procedures when fieldwork surfaces exceptions, control failures, or fraud indicators?
- Does every planned procedure map back to an assessed risk so that no work is unexplained and no significant risk is left without a response?
