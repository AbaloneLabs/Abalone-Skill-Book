# Inherent Risk Assessment

Inherent risk is the susceptibility of an assertion to misstatement before controls are considered. It is the starting point of risk assessment, because it identifies where the audit must look hardest regardless of what controls exist. Agents frequently rate inherent risk loosely at the account level, apply a generic "moderate" to everything, or inflate it to justify a standard program. Each of these defeats the purpose. Inherent risk is assertion-specific: inventory is high risk for valuation and existence but low for classification, and a single rating cannot capture that. When inherent risk is assessed carelessly, the audit's effort is misallocated and assertion-specific exposures are missed.

Use this skill before assessing risk for any significant area, before rating assertions, and whenever identifying what could go wrong in the absence of controls. The goal is a documented, assertion-level assessment that drives targeted procedures rather than a generic program.

## Core Rules

### Assess Inherent Risk At The Assertion Level

Inherent risk is not a property of an account; it is a property of an assertion about an account. Assess each relevant assertion separately.

Common assertions and their differing risk profiles:

- existence or occurrence: whether the item exists or the transaction actually happened;
- completeness: whether all items that should be recorded are recorded;
- accuracy: whether amounts are recorded correctly;
- valuation or allocation: whether the item is carried at the right amount;
- rights and obligations: whether the entity owns or controls the asset or owes the obligation;
- cutoff: whether transactions are recorded in the correct period;
- classification: whether items are presented in the right category;
- presentation and disclosure: whether items are properly described and disclosed.

For each significant account, identify which assertions are relevant and rate each. An account rated low overall may still have a high-risk assertion that demands targeted testing.

### Identify The Factors That Raise Inherent Risk

Inherent risk rises from characteristics of the item, the transaction, the environment, and the people involved. Enumerate the factors present for each assertion.

Factors that raise inherent risk:

- complexity of calculation or estimate;
- significant judgment or subjectivity;
- uncertainty about future events affecting the balance;
- susceptibility to fraud or management override;
- volume and volatility of transactions;
- unusual or non-routine transactions;
- related-party dealings;
- prior-period errors;
- changes in accounting policy, systems, or personnel;
- external economic, regulatory, or market pressure;
- reliance on third-party or service-organization data;
- items measured at fair value with unobservable inputs;
- cutoff-sensitive revenue or expense streams.

Document which factors are present and how they affect each assertion. A factor list with no link to a specific assertion is not an assessment.

### Recognize The Assertions And Items That Are Inherently High Risk

Certain assertions and items are almost always high inherent risk because of their nature. Treat them as presumptively high risk unless evidence shows otherwise.

Typically high inherent risk:

- revenue recognition, especially cutoff and occurrence near period end;
- valuation of inventory, investments, and hard-to-value assets;
- fair-value measurements using significant unobservable inputs;
- accounting estimates and provisions requiring judgment;
- related-party transactions and significant unusual transactions;
- goodwill and intangible asset impairment;
- going-concern assessments and liquidity estimates;
- contingencies, litigation, and warranties;
- deferred revenue and long-term contract accounting;
- tax positions and uncertain tax benefits.

For these, default to a high inherent risk rating and document the specific factors, rather than searching for reasons to rate them lower.

### Evaluate Estimation Uncertainty And Judgment Separately

Estimates deserve special treatment because their risk comes from uncertainty and judgment, not from transaction volume. Assess the estimation process, not just the final number.

For estimates and fair value, assess:

- the degree of estimation uncertainty, meaning how much the estimate could vary with different assumptions;
- the subjectivity of the inputs, especially unobservable inputs;
- the range of reasonable outcomes;
- whether external market evidence corroborates or contradicts management's assumptions;
- the history of prior estimate accuracy and any pattern of bias;
- the complexity of the model or methodology;
- the competence and independence of those making the estimate.

High estimation uncertainty often makes the item a significant risk requiring a tailored response, including possible use of an auditor's specialist.

### Consider External And Environmental Pressure

Inherent risk is not only about the transaction; it is about the environment in which it occurs. Pressure can make otherwise routine items risky.

Environmental factors that raise inherent risk:

- economic downturn, inflation, or currency volatility affecting valuation and going concern;
- regulatory change affecting recognition, measurement, or disclosure;
- industry disruption or rapid technological change;
- competitive pressure to meet earnings or growth targets;
- liquidity constraints or covenant pressure;
- ownership or management changes that disrupt continuity;
- geographic or political risk affecting foreign operations;
- public scrutiny or reputational sensitivity.

When environmental pressure is present, raise inherent risk for the affected assertions and document the linkage.

### Distinguish Inherent Risk From Control Risk

Inherent risk is assessed before considering controls. A common error is to lower inherent risk because a control exists. That control belongs in the control risk assessment.

Keep them separate:

- inherent risk asks: how susceptible is this assertion to misstatement by its nature, regardless of controls?
- control risk asks: do the entity's controls reduce that susceptibility?

Assess inherent risk first, then assess whether controls mitigate it. Strong controls lower control risk, not inherent risk. This separation keeps the logic of the risk model intact and prevents double-counting.

### Document The Assessment So Procedures Can Be Targeted

Inherent risk assessment exists to drive procedures. If the assessment does not translate into targeted testing, it has failed its purpose.

For each significant area, document:

- the relevant assertions and their inherent risk ratings;
- the specific factors driving each rating;
- whether the item is a significant risk and why;
- the implications for the nature, timing, and extent of procedures;
- any environmental or contextual factors that elevate risk.

The documentation should allow a reviewer to see exactly why a high-risk assertion received more extensive testing than a low-risk one.

## Common Traps

### Rating Inherent Risk At The Account Level

A single account rating hides assertion-specific exposure. Inventory is high risk for valuation and existence but low for classification. Assess per assertion.

### Lowering Inherent Risk Because A Control Exists

That control belongs in the control risk assessment. Inherent risk is assessed before controls. Conflating them double-counts and distorts the model.

### Applying A Generic Moderate To Everything

A blanket moderate rating drives no targeted procedures and signals that the assessment was not actually performed. It is the most common form of risk-assessment theater.

### Ignoring Estimation Uncertainty

Treating an estimate like a routine transaction misses the real risk, which is the range of reasonable outcomes and the subjectivity of inputs. Estimates need process-level assessment.

### Forgetting Environmental And Fraud Pressure

Economic, regulatory, and earnings pressure can make routine items risky. Inherent risk assessed in a vacuum misses the environment that drives misstatement.

### Inflating Inherent Risk To Justify A Standard Program

Rating everything high to defend a pre-existing program is the inverse error. It produces over-testing of low-risk areas and still misses assertion-specific exposures.

### Leaving The Assessment Disconnected From Procedures

An inherent risk rating with no link to the nature, timing, or extent of testing is documentation, not assessment. The assessment must drive the procedures.

## Self-Check

- Is inherent risk assessed at the assertion level, with each relevant assertion rated separately rather than a single account-level rating?
- Are the specific factors raising inherent risk, such as complexity, judgment, fraud susceptibility, volume, and related-party involvement, enumerated and linked to each assertion?
- Are the presumptively high-risk items, including revenue cutoff, estimates, fair value, and related parties, treated as high risk unless evidence shows otherwise?
- For estimates and fair value, are estimation uncertainty, input subjectivity, range of outcomes, and prior estimate bias assessed at the process level?
- Are external and environmental pressures, including economic, regulatory, and earnings pressure, considered and linked to affected assertions?
- Is inherent risk kept separate from control risk, so that controls lower control risk rather than inherent risk?
- Does the documented assessment translate directly into targeted procedures, with high-risk assertions receiving more extensive testing than low-risk ones?
- Could a reviewer follow the reasoning from the factor list to the rating to the planned procedures without gaps?
