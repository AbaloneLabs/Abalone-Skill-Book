# Control Risk Assessment

Control risk is the risk that a misstatement that could occur in an assertion and that could be material will not be prevented, or detected and corrected, on a timely basis by the entity's internal control. The auditor's assessment of control risk directly drives the nature, timing, and extent of substantive testing: where controls are assessed as effective and the auditor intends to rely on them, substantive testing can be reduced; where controls are weak or untested, the auditor must perform more extensive substantive procedures. The recurring failure is assessing control risk by default, either setting it at maximum to avoid testing controls, or setting it at low without performing the testing required to support reliance. Both errors distort the audit response. Setting control risk artificially high leads to over-testing and inefficiency; setting it artificially low without support undermines the audit's risk response and leaves material misstatement risk unaddressed. Control risk assessment is a deliberate judgment that must be supported by evidence of design and operating effectiveness.

Use this skill when assessing control risk for an assertion, process, or class of transactions, when deciding whether to test and rely on controls, and when determining the risk response that flows from the assessment. The goal is a control risk assessment that is supported, defensible, and correctly translated into the nature, timing, and extent of further audit procedures.

## Core Rules

### Understand What Control Risk Represents

Control risk is a component of audit risk, alongside inherent risk and detection risk. It reflects the risk that the entity's internal controls, if relied upon, would fail to prevent or detect and correct a material misstatement. Control risk is a function of the entity's control environment, not of the auditor's procedures, though the auditor can choose whether to test controls and rely on them.

Recognize that:

- control risk is assessed at the assertion level, not merely at the process or account level;
- a control risk assessment must be supported by evidence of control design and operating effectiveness if the auditor intends to rely on controls;
- control risk interacts with inherent risk: the higher the inherent risk, the more the auditor relies on either strong controls or extensive substantive procedures;
- setting control risk below maximum commits the auditor to testing controls to support that assessment.

Understanding the component prevents treating control risk as a free parameter that can be set for convenience.

### Decide Whether To Assess Control Risk Below Maximum

A deliberate decision is required on whether to assess control risk below maximum. Assessing below maximum means the auditor intends to rely on controls to reduce substantive testing. This requires testing the controls. The alternative is a substantive-only approach, where control risk is assessed at or near maximum and the auditor relies entirely on substantive procedures.

Decide by:

- considering whether the entity has controls that, if effective, would reduce the risk of material misstatement for the relevant assertions;
- weighing the efficiency of relying on controls against the cost of testing them;
- for assertions where controls are weak, manual, or absent, defaulting to a substantive-only approach rather than unsupported reliance;
- for high-volume, routine transactions, considering whether automated or IT-dependent controls make reliance efficient.

The decision should be driven by whether reliable controls exist and whether testing them is more efficient than substantive-only testing.

### Evaluate Control Design Before Operating Effectiveness

Before testing whether a control operates effectively, the auditor must determine whether it is suitably designed to meet its objective. A control that is poorly designed cannot operate effectively, no matter how consistently it is applied. Design assessment is a precondition for operating effectiveness testing.

Evaluate design by:

- walking through the control with management to understand how it operates;
- determining whether the control, as designed, would prevent or detect and correct the misstatements it addresses;
- identifying who performs the control, what evidence it produces, and how exceptions are handled;
- concluding whether the control is suitably designed before committing resources to operating effectiveness testing.

Testing operating effectiveness of a poorly designed control wastes effort and produces unsupported reliance.

### Test Operating Effectiveness With Sufficient Samples

Where the auditor intends to rely on a control, operating effectiveness must be tested across the relevant period and population. The extent of testing depends on the frequency of the control, the risk of the assertion, and the planned degree of reliance.

Test by:

- determining the appropriate sample size based on the control's frequency and the planned reliance;
- selecting items from across the entire period, not just a convenient window;
- inspecting evidence of performance, re-performing the control, or observing it where appropriate;
- evaluating deviations to determine whether they indicate a control deficiency that undermines reliance.

A few walkthrough items do not support operating effectiveness conclusions. Reliance requires testing across the period and population.

### Consider The Type Of Control: Manual, Automated, And IT-Dependent

The type of control affects how it is tested and how its effectiveness is assessed. Manual controls depend on the consistency and competence of the person performing them. Automated controls, once implemented, tend to perform consistently but depend on general IT controls. IT-dependent manual controls depend on both the system and the human interaction.

Consider by:

- for manual controls, focusing on who performs them, segregation of duties, and evidence of performance;
- for automated controls, testing general IT controls, configuration, and change management to support reliance;
- for IT-dependent controls, testing both the system element and the manual element;
- recognizing that automated controls, if general IT controls are strong, may support greater reliance and larger populations.

Treating all controls the same ignores the different reliability and testing demands of each type.

### Translate The Assessment Into The Substantive Response

The control risk assessment is not an end in itself. It drives the nature, timing, and extent of substantive procedures. A lower control risk assessment, supported by testing, allows reduced substantive testing; a higher assessment requires more extensive, often substantive-only, procedures.

Translate by:

- adjusting the substantive approach based on the supported control risk level;
- where control risk is low, reducing the extent of substantive testing and considering timing at interim rather than year-end;
- where control risk is high or maximum, expanding substantive testing, often at year-end, and reducing reliance on analytical procedures alone;
- documenting the link between the control risk assessment and the substantive procedures performed.

An assessment that does not translate into the substantive response provides no audit value and may misstate the risk coverage.

### Reassess Control Risk If Conditions Change

Control risk is assessed based on conditions at the time of assessment. If conditions change during the audit, for example a control breaks down, a key person leaves, or a deficiency is identified, the assessment may need to be revised and the substantive response adjusted.

Reassess by:

- monitoring for changes in the control environment, personnel, systems, or processes during the engagement;
- where a control deviation or deficiency is identified, determining whether it undermines the planned reliance;
- where reliance is undermined, expanding substantive testing to compensate;
- documenting the reassessment and the revised risk response.

A static control risk assessment, maintained despite changed conditions, leaves the audit response misaligned with actual risk.

## Common Traps

### Setting Control Risk At Maximum By Default To Avoid Testing Controls

Defaulting to maximum avoids control testing but leads to over-testing and inefficiency, and may miss the opportunity to rely on strong controls.

### Setting Control Risk Low Without Testing

Assessing control risk low without testing design and operating effectiveness is unsupported and undermines the risk response.

### Testing Operating Effectiveness Without Evaluating Design

Testing a poorly designed control for operating effectiveness wastes effort. Design must be assessed first.

### Insufficient Sample Sizes For Reliance

A few walkthrough items do not support operating effectiveness. Reliance requires testing across the period and population.

### Ignoring General IT Controls For Automated Reliance

Automated and IT-dependent controls depend on general IT controls. Ignoring these undermines reliance on the automated element.

### Failing To Translate Assessment Into Substantive Response

A control risk assessment that does not change the substantive procedures provides no value and may misstate risk coverage.

### Maintaining A Static Assessment Despite Changed Conditions

If controls break down or conditions change, the assessment must be revised and the response adjusted. A static assessment misaligns the audit with actual risk.

## Self-Check

- Is control risk assessed at the assertion level, with an understanding that it is a function of the entity's controls, not the auditor's procedures?
- Has a deliberate decision been made on whether to assess control risk below maximum, based on whether reliable controls exist and whether reliance is efficient?
- Where control risk is assessed below maximum, has control design been evaluated and found suitable before testing operating effectiveness?
- Has operating effectiveness been tested with sufficient samples across the relevant period and population, not just a few walkthrough items?
- Has the type of control, manual, automated, or IT-dependent, been considered in determining how to test and how much to rely?
- Does the control risk assessment translate into a defensible substantive response, with reduced testing where reliance is supported and expanded testing where it is not?
- Where conditions change during the audit, has the control risk assessment been reassessed and the response adjusted?
- Is the link between the control risk assessment and the substantive procedures documented and defensible?
- Could an independent reviewer confirm that the control risk level is supported by evidence and correctly drives the substantive approach?
- Is the assessment free of defaulting to maximum to avoid work, or to low without support?
