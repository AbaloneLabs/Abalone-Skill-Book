# Reliance Criteria

Auditors routinely encounter work performed by others that could be relevant to their own engagement: internal audit's testing, a management specialist's valuation, an actuary's reserve calculation, or a service auditor's SOC report on a cloud provider. The decision to rely on such work is not a shortcut; it is an evidentiary judgment that transfers some of the auditor's evidentiary burden to a third party. Agents who rely without explicit criteria produce files that look efficient but rest on work the auditor never evaluated. The harm is concentrated and severe: when a misstatement traces back to a specialist's flawed assumption or a service organization's control failure, the auditor who relied on it without criteria has no defense, because the reliance was never justified by evidence.

Use this skill when establishing criteria to rely on the work of internal auditors, specialists, management experts, or service organizations, when documenting the basis for reliance, or when determining the limits of reliance and how much independent corroboration is still required. The goal is to make reliance a structured decision governed by competence, objectivity, work adequacy, and scope alignment, so that the auditor's opinion never rests on unexamined third-party work.

## Core Rules

### Treat Reliance As A Transfer Of Evidentiary Responsibility

When the auditor relies on another party's work, that work becomes part of the audit evidence supporting the opinion. The auditor does not shed responsibility by relying on others; the auditor remains responsible for the sufficiency and appropriateness of the total evidence. Reliance must therefore meet the same standard the auditor would apply to their own work.

Reliance-as-evidence principles:

- relied-upon work is audit evidence and must be evaluated as such;
- the auditor remains responsible for forming the opinion, regardless of who performed the underlying work;
- reliance does not reduce the auditor's accountability for the conclusion reached;
- the combined evidence from reliance and the auditor's own work must be sufficient and appropriate;
- reliance is permissible only where the standards explicitly allow it and the criteria are met.

### Assess Competence Of The Party Whose Work Is Relied Upon

Competence is the first criterion for any reliance decision. A party that lacks the qualifications, experience, or methodological rigor to perform the work cannot produce evidence the auditor can use, regardless of how objective or cooperative they are. Competence must be assessed for the specific work and the specific individual or function.

Competence assessment factors:

- professional qualifications, certifications, and licensing relevant to the work;
- experience with the specific subject matter, industry, or methodology involved;
- the party's track record, including prior errors or quality findings;
- for specialists, reputation and standing in the relevant profession;
- for internal functions, the methodology, supervision, and quality program;
- the currency of the party's knowledge, including awareness of recent standards or market changes;
- the party's understanding of the auditor's evidentiary and documentation needs.

### Assess Objectivity And Independence From The Outcome

Objectivity is the second criterion, and for some reliance decisions independence from the measured outcome is required. A management specialist whose compensation depends on a favorable valuation, or an internal function that reports to the area it audits, may be competent but not objective. Objectivity must be assessed for both the party and the specific engagement.

Objectivity assessment factors:

- organizational position and reporting lines of the party;
- any financial, personal, or organizational interest in the outcome of the work;
- for specialists, whether the auditor or the entity engaged the specialist, and the specialist's relationship to management;
- for internal functions, freedom from management influence over scope and conclusions;
- any threats, self-interest, self-review, advocacy, familiarity, or intimidation, and safeguards applied;
- the party's history of independence from the measured outcome;
- whether the party has made management decisions in the area, which would impair objectivity.

### Evaluate The Adequacy Of The Work Performed

Even a competent and objective party may produce work that is not adequate for the auditor's purposes. The work must be evaluated for whether it used appropriate methods, reliable data, sound assumptions, sufficient procedures, and reached conclusions supported by evidence. Adequacy is assessed against the auditor's evidentiary standard, not the performing party's own standard.

Work adequacy factors:

- the methodology used is appropriate and consistent with professional or industry practice;
- the source data is complete, accurate, and reliable;
- the assumptions used are reasonable, supported, and consistent with external evidence;
- the procedures performed are sufficient to support the conclusions reached;
- the conclusions are supported by the documented evidence and not overstated;
- significant judgments are explained and supported;
- the work addresses the relevant assertions and risks, not adjacent ones.

### Confirm Scope Alignment With The Auditor's Objectives

Reliance is only useful if the work performed actually addresses what the auditor needs. A specialist's valuation may be excellent but cover a different asset or date than the auditor requires; a SOC report may test controls that are not the ones the auditor intends to rely on. Scope alignment must be confirmed before reliance, not assumed.

Scope alignment factors:

- the subject matter, population, and period covered by the work match the auditor's needs;
- the assertions addressed by the work correspond to the auditor's relevant assertions;
- the control objectives in a SOC report correspond to the user entity controls the auditor intends to rely on;
- the cutoff date or period of the work is relevant to the auditor's reporting date;
- the level of detail and granularity meets the auditor's evidentiary needs;
- any limitations, exclusions, or qualifications in the work are understood and acceptable;
- the work covers the complete population or a representative sample, not an unrepresentative subset.

### Evaluate Sufficiency Of Evidence Provided By The Work

Reliance must contribute sufficient appropriate evidence to the auditor's conclusion. A single piece of corroborating work is rarely sufficient on its own for a significant area, and the auditor must determine how much additional evidence is needed. Sufficiency is judged in combination with the auditor's own procedures.

Sufficiency considerations:

- the persuasiveness and reliability of the evidence the work provides;
- the correlation between the work's evidence and the risk of material misstatement in the area;
- the amount of independent corroboration the auditor will still obtain;
- the consistency of the work's conclusions with other audit evidence;
- whether the work covers the highest-risk transactions or only routine ones;
- the materiality and significance of the area to which reliance is applied;
- the sampling approach and sample size if the work is based on testing.

### Recognize Limits On Reliance And Areas Where It Is Not Permitted

Reliance has limits. Some judgments cannot be delegated, some areas require the auditor's own procedures regardless of others' work, and some parties can never be objective enough to rely on. The auditor must know where reliance is not permitted and where only limited reliance is appropriate.

Reliance limits:

- the auditor's opinion and the overall conclusion cannot be delegated;
- significant risks and fraud risks generally require the auditor's own tailored procedures;
- management representations are not audit evidence and cannot be relied upon as such;
- work performed by a party lacking independence from the outcome cannot be relied upon for objective evidence;
- reliance on a service organization's controls is limited by the type of report, Type I versus Type II, and the complementary user entity controls;
- a specialist engaged and directed by management requires more auditor evaluation than one engaged by the auditor;
- reliance cannot substitute for procedures required by standards to be performed by the auditor.

### Document The Reliance Decision And Its Basis

Every reliance decision must be documented so that an experienced reviewer can understand what was relied upon, the criteria evaluated, and the conclusion reached. Documentation is what distinguishes a deliberate reliance decision from an assumption. Undocumented reliance cannot be defended.

Documentation requirements:

- identification of the party whose work was relied upon and the specific work used;
- the assessment of competence, including qualifications and experience;
- the assessment of objectivity and any threats and safeguards;
- the evaluation of work adequacy, including methodology, data, and assumptions;
- the scope alignment analysis and any limitations noted;
- the extent of reliance and the corresponding reduction or adjustment in the auditor's procedures;
- the additional evidence the auditor obtained independently to achieve sufficiency.

### Apply Different Criteria For Different Categories Of Reliance

The criteria for reliance differ by category. Internal audit, management's specialist, the auditor's own specialist, and a service auditor's report each carry different objectivity profiles and require different evaluation emphasis. Applying one generic checklist to all of them misses category-specific risks.

Category-specific criteria:

- for internal audit, emphasize function-level objectivity, competence, and systematic discipline;
- for management's specialist, emphasize independence from the outcome and adequacy of assumptions;
- for the auditor's own specialist, emphasize the auditor's direction, supervision, and evaluation of the specialist's work;
- for a service auditor's SOC report, emphasize the type of report, the control objectives, the period covered, and complementary user entity controls;
- for other assurance providers, emphasize the framework under which the assurance was provided and its relevance;
- for each category, confirm that the specific criteria are met before relying.

## Common Traps

### Relying On A Name Without Evaluating The Work

Accepting work because it was performed by a well-known firm or a credentialed specialist, without evaluating the methodology, data, and assumptions, substitutes reputation for evidence. Credentials do not establish adequacy.

### Confusing Objectivity With Competence

A highly credentialed specialist who lacks independence from the outcome is not objective, and an independent party who lacks qualifications is not competent. Both criteria must be met; one cannot substitute for the other.

### Relying On Work Whose Scope Does Not Match The Auditor's Needs

A SOC report or specialist's report may be excellent but cover different controls, assets, or periods than the auditor requires. Relying on it without confirming scope alignment uses evidence that does not actually address the auditor's objectives.

### Treating A Type I SOC Report As If It Were Type II

A Type I report addresses control design at a point in time, not operating effectiveness over a period. Relying on a Type I report to support controls-reliance over the audit period overstates what the report supports.

### Relying On Management's Specialist Without Evaluating Assumptions

A valuation or reserve prepared by management's specialist is management's assertion, supported by a specialist. The auditor must evaluate the specialist's assumptions, data, and methodology, not merely accept the conclusion. Delegation of judgment is not permitted.

### Assuming More Reliance Than The Evidence Supports

Placing heavy reliance on a single piece of third-party work, without independent corroboration, leaves the conclusion resting on one source. Sufficiency requires combination with the auditor's own evidence, especially in significant areas.

### Failing To Recognize Where Reliance Is Not Permitted

Some areas require the auditor's own procedures regardless of others' work, such as significant risks, fraud risks, and the overall opinion. Relying on others in these areas abandons the required tailored response.

### Undocumented Or Boilerplate Reliance

Documenting reliance with generic language that does not identify the specific work, the criteria evaluated, or the extent of reliance is not documentation. An experienced reviewer must be able to see the basis for the decision.

## Self-Check

- Is reliance treated as a transfer of evidentiary responsibility, with the auditor retaining accountability for the sufficiency and appropriateness of total evidence?
- Has the competence of the party whose work is relied upon been assessed for the specific work, including qualifications, experience, and methodology?
- Has objectivity, including independence from the measured outcome, been assessed for both the party and the specific engagement, with threats and safeguards documented?
- Has the adequacy of the work been evaluated against the auditor's evidentiary standard, including methodology, data, assumptions, and supported conclusions?
- Has scope alignment been confirmed, ensuring the work covers the same subject matter, population, period, and assertions the auditor needs?
- Is the sufficiency of evidence judged in combination with the auditor's own procedures, with independent corroboration obtained where needed?
- Are the limits on reliance recognized, with significant risks, fraud risks, and the overall opinion receiving the auditor's own tailored procedures?
- Is the reliance decision documented to a standard that an experienced reviewer can follow, including the party, the work, the criteria, and the extent?
- Are category-specific criteria applied for internal audit, management's specialist, the auditor's specialist, and service auditor reports, rather than a generic checklist?
- For SOC reports, is the type of report, the control objectives, the period covered, and the complementary user entity controls evaluated before reliance?
- Is reliance revisited if the work is found deficient, the party's objectivity is impaired, or new risks emerge during the audit?
