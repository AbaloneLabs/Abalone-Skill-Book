# Internal Audit Work Reliance

External auditors are often tempted to rely on internal audit's work because it appears to save time and cost, but reliance is a decision that carries real evidentiary weight and cannot be made casually. Internal audit sits inside the organization, reports to governance, and serves management's objectives, which means its work is useful evidence but never a substitute for the external auditor's own judgment. Agents who treat internal audit's workpapers as a free extension of their own file produce audits that are efficient on paper but unsupported in fact. The harm surfaces when a misstatement later emerges in an area where reliance was placed without evaluating objectivity, competence, and work quality: the external auditor's opinion is undermined and the reliance decision cannot be justified.

Use this skill when an external auditor is deciding whether to use internal audit work, when evaluating the quality of the internal audit function, when choosing between using internal audit's work product and using internal auditors as direct assistance, or when determining how much to reduce the external auditor's own procedures in light of reliance. The goal is to make reliance a deliberate, evidence-based decision that preserves the external auditor's independence and sufficiency of evidence.

## Core Rules

### Establish Whether The Function Meets The Threshold For Any Reliance

Reliance is not available as a default. Before any internal audit work can be considered, the external auditor must conclude that the function is, in aggregate, capable of producing work that is objective and competent enough to be relevant to the audit. This is a preliminary, function-level assessment, separate from the evaluation of any specific engagement.

Function-level threshold factors:

- the internal audit function's organizational status and reporting lines, including functional reporting to those charged with governance;
- the charter, mandate, and scope of the function and whether it is restricted by management;
- the function's role in the entity's risk management and internal control framework;
- whether the function applies a recognized professional standard, such as the IIA's International Professional Practices Framework;
- the existence of a quality assurance and improvement program, including external assessments;
- the function's overall reputation for independence and for willingness to report unfavorable findings.

### Evaluate Objectivity As A Precondition For Reliance

Objectivity is the single most important attribute, because competent work performed by a non-object function cannot be relied upon. Objectivity has two dimensions: the organizational independence of the function and the individual objectivity of the auditors who performed the specific work. Both must be assessed and documented.

Objectivity considerations:

- confirm functional reporting to the audit committee or board, not solely to management;
- assess whether the chief audit executive can set scope and report findings without management veto;
- evaluate whether internal auditors are free from management influence over their conclusions;
- check that the auditors who performed the work did not recently hold operational roles in the area;
- consider whether any auditor has familiarity, personal, or self-review threats in the area;
- review whether internal audit has a history of softening or withholding findings to suit management;
- assess rotation practices and whether long-tenured auditors have lost independence in their areas.

### Evaluate Competence Before Relying On The Work

Competence determines whether the work, even if objective, is of a quality that the external auditor can use. Competence is a function of qualifications, training, experience, supervision, and the function's methodology. A well-intentioned but unqualified function produces work that looks complete but lacks the rigor to support an external opinion.

Competence considerations:

- review the qualifications, certifications, and continuing professional education of internal audit staff;
- assess the experience of the specific auditors who performed the work being considered;
- evaluate the function's methodology, manuals, and engagement-level procedures;
- consider the supervision and review structure within the function;
- review the results of the function's external quality assessment and internal monitoring;
- assess whether the function uses appropriate sampling, evidence, and documentation standards;
- consider the function's use of specialists and technology-assisted audit techniques.

### Apply A Systematic And Disciplined Approach To Specific Work

Even an objective and competent function produces work that must be evaluated at the engagement level before reliance. The external auditor must determine whether the specific work is relevant to the audit objectives, adequately planned, properly performed, sufficiently documented, and subject to appropriate review. This is the work-by-work evaluation that follows the function-level assessment.

Work-level evaluation factors:

- the work was planned by internal audit using a risk-based approach relevant to the external auditor's risks;
- the scope, objectives, and criteria of the internal audit engagement align with the external auditor's needs;
- the procedures performed are appropriate to the assertions and risks the external auditor seeks to cover;
- the evidence gathered is sufficient and appropriate, not merely voluminous;
- the conclusions reached are supported by the documented evidence and are not overstated;
- the working papers are adequately documented, cross-referenced, and reviewable;
- the work was subject to internal supervision and review before completion;
- exceptions and errors were appropriately evaluated and followed up.

### Determine The Extent Of Reliance Deliberately

Reliance is not binary. The external auditor may rely on internal audit's work for some procedures and not others, and may reduce but not eliminate the external auditor's own procedures. The extent of reliance must be calibrated to the assessed objectivity, competence, and work quality, and to the risk and materiality of the area. Higher risk and higher materiality permit less reliance.

Extent calibration factors:

- the significance of the area to the audit and the risks of material misstatement;
- the degree of judgment involved, with more judgment permitting less reliance;
- the amount and persuasiveness of evidence the internal audit work provides;
- the sufficiency of additional evidence the external auditor will still obtain independently;
- the results of the external auditor's reperformance or testing of a sample of internal audit's work;
- the consistency of internal audit's findings with the external auditor's other evidence;
- the impact of any identified deficiencies in internal audit's work on the extent of reliance.

### Distinguish Reliance From Direct Assistance

There are two distinct ways the external auditor may use internal auditors: relying on internal audit's own work product, and using internal auditors as direct assistance under the external auditor's direction and supervision. These are governed differently, and confusing them produces the wrong level of external auditor involvement. Direct assistance means the external auditor owns the work; reliance means the external auditor evaluates work that internal audit owns.

Direct assistance considerations:

- direct assistance requires the external auditor to direct, supervise, and review the internal auditors' work;
- the external auditor must assess objectivity and competence of the specific individuals used as direct assistance;
- internal audit management must not object to the use of its staff for direct assistance;
- the external auditor determines the nature, timing, and extent of the work, not internal audit;
- direct assistance is often appropriate for routine, low-judgment procedures under close supervision;
- direct assistance is generally not appropriate for significant judgments or high-risk areas;
- documentation must clearly identify work performed as direct assistance and the external auditor's involvement.

### Adjust External Auditor Scope Based On Reliance, Not Convenience

When reliance is placed, the external auditor may reduce the nature, timing, or extent of certain procedures, but the reduction must be justified by the quality of the internal audit work and must not compromise sufficiency of evidence. Reducing scope because internal audit performed work, without evaluating that work, is a standards breach. The adjustment must be deliberate and documented.

Scope adjustment discipline:

- identify the specific external auditor procedures affected by reliance and how they are reduced;
- ensure the combined evidence from reliance and remaining external work is sufficient and appropriate;
- do not reduce procedures in significant risk areas below the level the risk requires;
- maintain external auditor involvement in judgments, estimates, and high-risk assertions;
- perform reperformance or independent testing of a portion of internal audit's work as corroboration;
- document the basis for each scope reduction and the evidence supporting it;
- revisit the adjustment if fieldwork reveals weaknesses in internal audit's work.

### Document The Reliance Decision And Its Basis

The reliance decision must be documented to the standard that a reviewer can understand what was relied upon, why, and to what extent. Undocumented reliance is indistinguishable from no reliance when the file is reviewed, and it cannot be reconstructed after a problem emerges. Documentation is part of the decision, not an afterthought.

Documentation requirements:

- the function-level assessment of objectivity, competence, and systematic discipline;
- the engagement-level evaluation of the specific work relied upon;
- the nature, timing, and extent of the external auditor's procedures to evaluate internal audit's work;
- the extent of reliance and the corresponding reduction in external auditor procedures;
- any reperformance, testing, or corroboration performed by the external auditor;
- the basis for any decision to use direct assistance and the supervision applied;
- any threats identified and the safeguards applied to address them.

### Reassess Reliance When Conditions Change

Reliance is a point-in-time judgment that can be invalidated by events during the audit. If internal audit's work is found to be deficient, if the function's independence is impaired, or if new risks emerge, the reliance decision must be revisited and the external auditor's procedures expanded. Holding to a reliance decision in the face of contrary evidence abandons the basis for it.

Reassessment triggers:

- exceptions or errors found when testing internal audit's work;
- evidence that internal audit's conclusions were unsupported or overstated;
- changes in the function's leadership, reporting lines, or mandate during the audit;
- management interference with internal audit's scope or findings;
- emergence of new significant risks not covered by internal audit's work;
- restatements or findings in areas where reliance was previously placed.

## Common Traps

### Treating Reliance As A Cost-Saving Default

Assuming reliance to reduce fee pressure, before evaluating objectivity, competence, and work quality, produces reliance that cannot be defended. Reliance must be earned by evidence, not assumed for efficiency.

### Evaluating Objectivity But Not Competence, Or Vice Versa

An objective but incompetent function, or a competent but non-object function, cannot be relied upon. Both attributes must be assessed and documented; one does not compensate for the absence of the other.

### Relying On Work Without Reviewing The Working Papers

Accepting internal audit's conclusions or summary reports without reviewing the underlying working papers relies on assertions rather than evidence. The external auditor must evaluate the work, not the headline.

### Confusing Reliance With Direct Assistance

Using internal auditors to perform work the external auditor directs, but documenting it as reliance, understates the external auditor's involvement and supervision. The two modes are governed differently and must be distinguished.

### Reducing Scope In Significant Risk Areas Based On Reliance

Significant risks require tailored external auditor responses. Reducing procedures in these areas based on internal audit work, without independent corroboration, leaves the highest-risk areas with the least external evidence.

### Failing To Reperform Or Test Any Of Internal Audit's Work

Reliance does not mean accepting internal audit's work wholesale. Some reperformance or independent testing of a sample of internal audit's procedures is usually needed to corroborate the work's reliability.

### Holding To Reliance After Deficiencies Emerge

If testing reveals that internal audit's work is deficient, the reliance decision must be revisited and external procedures expanded. Continuing to rely on work that has shown weaknesses abandons the evidentiary basis.

### Undocumented Reliance

Placing reliance without documenting the function-level and work-level evaluations, the extent of reliance, and the scope adjustments leaves the file unable to demonstrate that reliance was justified. Undocumented reliance is not reliance.

## Self-Check

- Has a function-level assessment of internal audit's objectivity and competence been performed and documented before any reliance?
- Is objectivity assessed at both the organizational and individual level, with reporting lines, scope-setting authority, and rotation considered?
- Is competence assessed through qualifications, experience, methodology, supervision, and quality program results?
- For each engagement relied upon, is the work evaluated for relevance, scope alignment, procedure adequacy, evidence sufficiency, and documented conclusions?
- Is the extent of reliance calibrated to the risk, materiality, and judgment of the area, with higher risk permitting less reliance?
- Are reliance and direct assistance correctly distinguished, with direct assistance directed, supervised, and reviewed by the external auditor?
- Is every scope reduction justified by the quality of internal audit's work and documented with the supporting basis?
- Has the external auditor performed reperformance or independent testing of a portion of internal audit's work to corroborate reliability?
- Is the reliance decision, including the function-level and work-level evaluations, the extent of reliance, and scope adjustments, fully documented?
- Is there a documented trigger to reassess reliance when internal audit's work shows deficiencies, independence is impaired, or new risks emerge?; does reliance preserve, rather than compromise, the sufficiency and appropriateness of audit evidence and the external auditor's independent judgment?
