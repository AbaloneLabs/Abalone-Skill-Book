# Retention Policy

Audit documentation retention is a legal and professional obligation, not a storage housekeeping task. Retain too little or too briefly and the firm cannot defend a past audit during an inspection, investigation, or claim; retain carelessly and the firm exposes confidential client information, breaches data-protection law, or holds contradictory superseded records that should have been disposed of under a defensible schedule. Agents often assume retention is someone else's problem and that "keep everything forever" is safe, but indefinite retention creates its own legal and confidentiality risks and is rarely compliant. The harm this prevents is losing the ability to substantiate a prior audit, breaching confidentiality through poor storage or disposal, or failing a regulator's document-request because the retention rule was never actually applied.

Use this skill whenever a retention period is set or applied, whenever working papers are stored, transferred, or disposed of, and whenever a legal hold or terminated engagement raises retention questions. The goal is a retention posture that meets the longest applicable requirement, protects confidentiality throughout the retention period, and disposes of records only under a defensible, documented schedule.

## Core Rules

### Apply The Longest Applicable Retention Requirement

Retention is governed by multiple authorities, and the correct period is the longest that applies to the engagement, not the average or the most convenient. ISA, AICPA, PCAOB, local regulators, and client contracts can each impose different periods, and they do not offset each other.

Identify and apply:

- ISA 230 and national equivalents (commonly a minimum of five years);
- AICPA requirements for private company engagements;
- PCAOB requirements for public company audits (commonly at least seven years);
- securities regulator and stock exchange rules;
- tax authority record-retention rules;
- client contract or engagement letter terms;
- local statutory limitation periods for liability claims.

### Confirm Ownership And Rights Before Retaining Or Releasing

Working papers generally belong to the audit firm, but the client owns its own records and may have contractual rights to copies or access. Confusion about ownership causes both wrongful withholding and wrongful release. Establish ownership and access rights at engagement setup, not at disposal.

Clarify in advance:

- that working papers are the property of the firm;
- that source documents provided by the client remain the client's property;
- any contractual right of the client to copies or access;
- restrictions on releasing working papers to third parties;
- the firm's right to retain working papers after the engagement ends;
- how ownership is affected if the firm is acquired or dissolved.

### Protect Confidentiality Throughout The Retention Period

Retention is not just keeping records; it is keeping them secure for the entire period. A retained file that is accessible to the wrong people, stored unencrypted, or left in an unmanaged location is a confidentiality breach waiting to happen.

Maintain confidentiality by:

- storing retained files in access-controlled, encrypted repositories;
- restricting access to authorized personnel with a documented basis;
- auditing access to retained files periodically;
- avoiding unencrypted local copies, personal drives, or email stores;
- applying the same confidentiality controls to backups and archives;
- ensuring third-party storage providers meet the firm's security standard.

### Apply Legal Holds That Override Normal Disposal

A legal hold suspends the normal retention schedule and requires preservation of relevant records, often beyond the standard period. Failing to apply a hold, or disposing of records subject to a hold, can constitute obstruction or spoliation with serious consequences.

Manage legal holds by:

- identifying the trigger (litigation, investigation, regulator request);
- defining the scope of records to be preserved;
- issuing the hold promptly to all custodians;
- suspending any scheduled disposal for affected records;
- documenting the hold and its scope;
- releasing the hold only on documented authority.

### Dispose Of Records Only Under A Defensible Schedule

Disposal is as important as retention. Keeping records indefinitely beyond requirement creates risk and cost; disposing of them early destroys defensibility. Disposal must follow a documented schedule and be evidenced.

For any disposal:

- confirm the retention period has genuinely expired;
- confirm no legal hold or investigation is in effect;
- use a disposal method that prevents reconstruction;
- record what was disposed of, when, by whom, and under what authority;
- retain the disposal log permanently;
- never dispose selectively in a way that could appear to destroy unfavorable evidence.

### Handle Cross-Border Retention Differences

Multinational engagements can be subject to retention rules that differ by country, and data-protection laws may restrict where data can be stored or how long it can be held. A single global rule can violate local law in one direction or the other.

Address cross-border issues by:

- identifying every jurisdiction whose rules may apply;
- applying the longest period across all relevant jurisdictions;
- checking data-residency and data-export restrictions;
- reconciling retention obligations with data-minimization duties;
- documenting the jurisdictional analysis for the engagement;
- consulting local counsel where rules conflict.

### Retain Documentation For Terminated Engagements

A terminated or withdrawn engagement still generates retention obligations, and sometimes heightened ones because withdrawal can attract scrutiny. The documentation must be retained as if the engagement had completed, and the reasons for termination documented.

For terminated engagements:

- retain all documentation prepared up to termination;
- document the reasons for termination or withdrawal;
- apply the standard retention period from the date of termination;
- confirm no disposal occurs while any review is pending;
- preserve communications relevant to the termination decision;
- flag the file for heightened retention if withdrawal was contentious.

### Document The Retention Decision For Each Engagement

Retention should be a documented decision per engagement, not an assumption. The record should show what period applies, why, and who approved it, so that years later the basis is still clear.

Document for each engagement:

- the applicable requirements and the chosen period;
- the jurisdictional analysis if multinational;
- the storage location and access controls;
- any legal holds applied or released;
- the scheduled disposal date and authority;
- the person responsible for the retention decision.

### Plan For Media And Format Obsolescence

Records retained for years must remain readable for years. File formats, software, and storage media become obsolete, and a file that cannot be opened is effectively lost even if it was retained. Plan for readability across the full retention horizon.

Guard against obsolescence by:

- retaining records in durable, open formats (e.g., PDF/A, plain data files);
- migrating records when formats or systems are retired;
- verifying periodically that archived files still open and render correctly;
- retaining the software or viewers needed to read proprietary formats;
- keeping backups that meet the same retention and readability standard;
- documenting the format and migration history of retained records.

## Common Traps

### Defaulting To A Single Period Without Checking Authorities

Assuming five or seven years covers everything ignores the variety of requirements that can apply. Always identify every applicable authority and apply the longest period.

### Keeping Everything Indefinitely As A Safe Default

Indefinite retention is not safe; it increases storage cost, confidentiality exposure, and conflicts with data-minimization law. Retain for the required period and dispose under a defensible schedule.

### Failing To Apply A Legal Hold

Disposing of records that were subject to a hold can amount to spoliation. Treat any litigation, investigation, or regulator interest as a trigger to suspend disposal immediately.

### Disposing Without Evidence

Disposing of records without a log leaves no proof that disposal was authorized and timely. Record what was disposed, when, by whom, and under what authority, and retain the log.

### Storing Retained Files Without Ongoing Confidentiality Controls

A file retained for seven years must remain secure for seven years, not just at the start. Apply and audit access controls throughout the entire retention period.

### Ignoring Cross-Border Conflicts

A global retention rule can breach local data-protection or data-residency law. Analyze each jurisdiction and reconcile retention duties with data-minimization obligations.

### Treating Terminated Engagements As Exempt From Retention

Terminated engagements carry the same, and sometimes heightened, retention duties. Retain the documentation and document the reasons for termination.

### Selective Disposal That Looks Like Destruction Of Evidence

Disposing of some records while retaining others, without a defensible schedule, can appear to destroy unfavorable evidence. Dispose only under a uniform, documented schedule.

## Self-Check

- Has the longest applicable retention requirement been identified across ISA, AICPA, PCAOB, regulators, tax, and contract?
- Are ownership of working papers and client access rights established and documented?
- Are retained files stored with encryption, access control, and periodic access audits for the full period?
- Is there a process to identify triggers and apply legal holds that suspend scheduled disposal?
- Does every disposal confirm expiry, absence of holds, secure method, and a retained disposal log?
- Have cross-border jurisdictional requirements and data-protection duties been analyzed and documented?
- Are terminated or withdrawn engagements retained and documented with the reasons for termination?
- Is the retention decision, period, basis, and responsible person documented for each engagement?
- Are disposal actions performed only under a uniform, defensible schedule, never selectively?
- Are third-party storage providers held to the firm's security and confidentiality standards?
- Could the firm, years later, demonstrate both what was retained and what was disposed of, and why?
- Are retained records stored in durable formats and migrated to avoid media and format obsolescence?
- Is the retention decision, applicable period, and responsible person documented for each engagement?
