# Equity Transaction Testing

Equity transaction testing produces evidence that movements in shareholders' equity are authorized, properly measured, and correctly classified. Agents tend to treat equity as a mechanical roll-forward of account balances, but the real judgment problem is that equity transactions are governed by legal and contractual authority, carry classification consequences between permanent equity and liabilities, and are a frequent vehicle for related-party manipulation and earnings management through instruments that are debt in substance but equity in form. A share issuance that lacks board authorization, or a convertible instrument misclassified as equity, distorts both the balance sheet and earnings per share.

Failing to test equity transactions for authority, substance, and classification leaves the capital structure misstated and can mask dilution, undisclosed commitments, or misclassified obligations. The skill is about tracing each material equity movement to its authorization, measuring it at the correct amount, and classifying it under the substance of the arrangement.

Use this skill whenever the audit program includes stock issuances, repurchases, treasury stock, conversions, splits, or equity-classified instruments. The goal is to confirm that equity transactions are valid, authorized, measured correctly, and properly presented and disclosed.

## Core Rules

### Verify Authorization for Every Material Transaction

Equity transactions require formal authorization that the auditor must independently confirm. A transaction without proper authority is not valid regardless of its accounting. The authority evidence must be traced to the specific transaction, not assumed from general policy.

- Obtain board and shareholder minutes authorizing issuances, repurchases, and splits.
- Confirm the authorization specifies the number of shares, price, class, and parties where relevant.
- Verify compliance with the corporate constitution, charter, and applicable company law.
- Confirm that authorized share capital limits are not exceeded by the transactions.
- Test that preemptive rights, where they exist, were observed or waived properly.
- Reconcile the authorized, issued, and outstanding share counts at period-end.
- Identify any transactions requiring regulatory filing or approval and confirm completion.
- Confirm that transactions comply with stock exchange listing rules and any takeover or securities regulations.

### Test Stock Issuances for Validity and Measurement

Each issuance must be traced from authorization through receipt of proceeds to the ledger. The measurement depends on the consideration received, which may be cash, non-cash assets, services, or conversion of another instrument. Non-cash consideration raises fair value questions that must be addressed.

- Trace issuances to board authorization, subscription or purchase agreements, and cash receipts.
- For non-cash consideration, evaluate the fair value of the assets or services received.
- Confirm the allocation between par value and additional paid-in capital.
- Test that issuance costs are accounted for consistently with the applicable framework.
- Verify that share counts in the capital ledger agree with the registrar or transfer agent.
- Reconcile the proceeds to the bank and the equity ledger.
- Identify issuances to related parties and assess the terms for arm's-length treatment.
- Confirm that shares issued for services are measured at the fair value of the services or the shares, whichever is more reliably measurable.

### Account for Treasury Stock Correctly

Repurchases of the entity's own shares create treasury stock that must be accounted for under the applicable method. The carrying and reissuance of treasury shares are common sources of error, particularly where shares are reissued at prices that differ from cost. Treasury stock transactions must not produce gains or losses in income.

- Confirm repurchases are authorized and comply with legal limits on buybacks.
- Test the cost of repurchased shares and the carrying of treasury stock.
- Verify that reissuances are recorded at cost, with differences taken to equity, not income.
- Reconcile treasury share counts to the registrar and the ledger.
- Confirm that treasury shares are presented as a deduction from equity, not as an asset.
- Evaluate the treatment of fractional shares and odd-lot buybacks.
- Assess whether repurchases from related parties were on arm's-length terms.
- Confirm that treasury shares are not treated as outstanding for earnings per share or voting purposes while held.

### Evaluate Stock Splits and Stock Dividends

Splits and stock dividends change the number of shares without changing total equity, but they require recasting of par value, per-share amounts, and earnings per share. Errors in the recasting distort comparative figures. The effective date and the treatment of fractional shares must be handled correctly.

- Confirm the authorization and terms of the split or stock dividend.
- Test the recalculation of par value and shares outstanding.
- Verify retrospective adjustment of comparative earnings per share and per-share amounts.
- Confirm the accounting for fractional shares or cash-in-lieu arrangements.
- Reconcile the share counts before and after the split to the registrar.
- Assess whether a large stock dividend requires transfer from retained earnings.
- Confirm disclosure of the split and its effective date.
- Verify that the share premium or additional paid-in capital is adjusted correctly when par value changes in a split.

### Test Conversions and Exchanges

Conversions of debt or preferred stock into common equity change both the liability or preference equity and the common equity. The accounting depends on the terms and the framework, particularly whether the carrying amount of the converted instrument is reclassified or remeasured. Conversion terms must be read carefully for induced conversion premiums.

- Trace conversions to the underlying instrument terms and authorization.
- Test the reclassification of carrying amounts from debt or preferred to common equity.
- Evaluate any induced conversion or modification premium for correct accounting.
- Confirm that unamortized issuance costs or discounts are handled correctly on conversion.
- Reconcile the converted shares to the registrar and the equity ledger.
- Assess whether conversion affects earnings per share retrospectively.
- Identify conversions involving related parties for additional scrutiny.
- Assess whether the conversion ratio was modified or induced, creating additional consideration or expense.

### Classify Instruments as Equity or Liability

The classification of complex instruments between equity and liability is one of the most error-prone areas. Classification depends on the substance of the instrument, including settlement options, redemption features, and contingent provisions. Misclassification misstates both the balance sheet and, often, interest expense.

- Evaluate preferred shares for mandatory redemption or dividend features that require liability classification.
- Test convertible instruments for the appropriate split between liability and equity components.
- Assess whether put or call options on the entity's own shares require liability treatment.
- Evaluate contingent settlement provisions and their effect on classification.
- Confirm that compound instruments are bifurcated correctly at inception and remeasured where required.
- Review subsequent changes in classification and the accounting for reclassifications.
- Confirm disclosure of the classification basis and significant terms of complex instruments.
- Evaluate whether embedded derivatives within convertible or compound instruments are separately accounted for.

### Account for Equity Issuance Costs

Costs directly attributable to issuing equity are generally deducted from equity, not expensed, but the boundary with other costs is not always clear. The treatment of costs related to failed issuances or to instruments that are liabilities requires care. Consistency with the framework is essential.

- Identify costs directly attributable to the equity issuance.
- Confirm that direct issuance costs are deducted from equity, net of any tax effect.
- Test the treatment of costs related to failed or abandoned issuances.
- Evaluate the treatment of costs for instruments classified as liabilities.
- Confirm that recurring legal and accounting fees are expensed, not capitalized.
- Reconcile the net proceeds to the gross proceeds and the identified costs.
- Confirm that costs allocated to a combined debt and equity issuance are split on a reasonable basis.

### Reconcile and Disclose the Equity Roll-Forward

The statement of changes in equity must reconcile the opening and closing balances for each component, with each material movement supported. Disclosure must describe the transactions, the terms of complex instruments, and the rights attached to each class. The roll-forward is where equity testing converges.

- Reconcile opening balances, transactions, other comprehensive income, and closing balances for each equity component.
- Confirm that each material movement is supported by a tested transaction.
- Verify disclosure of the terms and rights of each class of shares and equity instrument.
- Test disclosure of restrictions on retained earnings and dividends.
- Confirm presentation of treasury stock, non-controlling interests, and accumulated other comprehensive income.
- Reconcile the equity note to the general ledger and the financial statements.
- Confirm that non-controlling interest transactions, such as partial acquisitions and step acquisitions, are measured and disclosed correctly.

### Evaluate Business Combination and Non-Controlling Interest Effects

Equity is affected by business combinations and transactions with non-controlling interests, which change the composition of shareholders' equity in ways that are easy to misstate. A step acquisition, a buyout of minority shareholders, or a loss of control all trigger remeasurement that flows through equity or income depending on the transaction structure. The auditor must confirm that the equity effects of these transactions are measured at fair value where required and that goodwill or gain on bargain purchase is correctly reflected.

- Confirm that consideration transferred in a business combination is measured at fair value, including equity instruments issued.
- Test the remeasurement of any previously held interest in a step acquisition to fair value.
- Evaluate the gain or loss recognized on disposal or partial disposal of a subsidiary that results in loss of control.
- Confirm that non-controlling interests are measured at fair value or proportionate share of identifiable net assets, consistently with the elected policy.
- Reconcile the goodwill or bargain purchase gain arising from the combination to the purchase price allocation.
- Assess whether contingent consideration is classified as a liability or equity and remeasured appropriately.
- Confirm that equity issued as consideration is reflected in the roll-forward at the acquisition-date fair value.
- Assess whether transactions with owners in their capacity as owners are presented separately from transactions with non-owners in other comprehensive income.
- Confirm that reclassification adjustments between retained earnings and accumulated other comprehensive income are correctly presented.
- Verify that equity-settled share-based payment additions to equity are reflected in the roll-forward and reconciled to the compensation workpapers.
- Confirm that any equity issued to an employee benefit trust is eliminated on consolidation and not double-counted as outstanding shares.

## Common Traps

### Treating the Roll-Forward as the Audit

Reconciling opening to closing balances proves arithmetic, not validity or authority. The trap is the false comfort of a roll-forward that ties. Trace each material movement to authorization and evidence.

### Missing Board Authorization for a Specific Transaction

General authority to issue shares does not cover a specific issuance at specific terms. The trap is assuming authorization exists without locating the specific resolution. Obtain the minutes for each material transaction.

### Misclassifying a Complex Instrument

A redeemable preferred share or a convertible with a cash settlement option may be a liability, not equity. The trap is relying on the instrument's name rather than its substance. Test classification against the settlement and redemption features.

### Ignoring Non-Cash Consideration

Shares issued for assets, services, or debt forgiveness require fair value measurement of the consideration. The trap is recording the shares at par or nominal value. Measure non-cash consideration at fair value.

### Recording a Gain or Loss on Treasury Stock

Reissuing treasury shares above or below cost does not produce income. The trap is treating the difference as a gain or loss. Take the difference to equity, not the income statement.

### Overlooking Related-Party Issuances

Shares issued to related parties at below-market prices may be a disguised distribution or compensation. The trap is treating them as ordinary issuances. Assess the terms and consider compensation or distribution accounting.

### Forgetting Earnings Per Share Effects

Splits, conversions, and issuances change the weighted average share count and require retrospective EPS adjustment. The trap is using the current share count for comparatives. Recast EPS for all relevant periods.

### Under-Disclosing Terms and Rights

The rights, terms, and restrictions attached to each equity class must be disclosed. The trap is treating the equity note as a share count table. Disclose redemption, conversion, voting, and dividend rights.

## Self-Check

- Was each material equity transaction traced to specific board or shareholder authorization?
- Were stock issuances traced from authorization through proceeds to the ledger, with non-cash consideration measured at fair value?
- Was treasury stock accounted for under the correct method, with reissuance differences taken to equity?
- Were stock splits and stock dividends reflected in par value, share counts, and retrospective EPS?
- Were conversions of debt or preferred stock traced to instrument terms, with carrying amounts reclassified correctly?
- Were complex instruments classified as equity or liability based on substance, including settlement and redemption features?
- Were equity issuance costs accounted for consistently with the applicable framework?
- Does the equity roll-forward reconcile each component, with every material movement supported?
- Are related-party equity transactions identified and assessed for arm's-length terms?
- Is disclosure of terms, rights, restrictions, and EPS effects complete and consistent with the testing?
- Are authorized, issued, and outstanding share counts reconciled to the registrar at period-end?
