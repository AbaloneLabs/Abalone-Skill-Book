# Stock-Based Compensation Audit

Stock-based compensation audit produces evidence that equity compensation is measured at grant-date fair value, recognized over the appropriate service period, and correctly adjusted for modifications, cancellations, and forfeitures. Agents tend to treat stock compensation as a formulaic expense accrual, but the real judgment problem is that grant-date fair value depends on assumptions and models chosen by management, vesting conditions change the recognition pattern, and modifications and cancellations create remeasurement events that are easy to miss. Because stock compensation is a significant expense and a common retention and earnings management tool, weak testing leaves material misstatement and hides the true cost of compensation.

Failing to challenge the valuation model and assumptions, or to catch modifications and cancellations, produces an expense that looks precise but is built on untested inputs. The skill is about testing the grant, the valuation, the recognition, and the subsequent events, and about coordinating with the equity, tax, and related-party areas.

Use this skill whenever the audit program includes stock options, restricted stock, performance awards, or any equity-settled compensation. The goal is to confirm that grant-date fair value is supportable, expense is recognized over the correct period, modifications are accounted for, and disclosures are complete.

## Core Rules

### Obtain and Understand the Full Award Population

The starting point is a complete population of grants, including new grants, modifications, cancellations, and forfeitures during the period. A population that omits cancelled or modified awards cannot support a complete expense conclusion. The auditor must reconcile the population to the equity ledger and the cap table.

- Obtain the complete grant register, including grant date, number of shares, exercise price, and vesting terms.
- Reconcile the grant register to the equity ledger and the share registrar.
- Identify grants to related parties, executives, and employees for appropriate scrutiny.
- Confirm the population includes international and subsidiary-level grants.
- Reconcile opening unvested shares, grants, forfeitures, vesting, and closing unvested shares.
- Identify any grants from prior periods that were modified or cancelled in the current period.
- Confirm that the population covers all plan types, including evergreen and evergreen-style provisions.
- Reconcile the grant register to the board or compensation committee approvals for each grant.

### Test Grant-Date Fair Value

Grant-date fair value is the foundation of the expense, and it must be measured at the grant date using an appropriate model. The model and assumptions are where the most significant estimation risk sits. The auditor must understand the model, challenge the assumptions, and consider using a specialist.

- Confirm the valuation model used is appropriate for the award type and terms.
- For options, evaluate whether Black-Scholes, binomial, or Monte Carlo is appropriate given the terms.
- Test the risk-free interest rate against the term of the award and the grant date.
- Test expected volatility against historical and implied volatility of comparable entities.
- Test expected term, including post-vesting exercise behavior, for support.
- Test the dividend yield assumption against the entity's dividend policy and history.
- For awards with market conditions, confirm the model captures the condition and the grant-date fair value reflects it.
- Consider engaging a valuation specialist for complex or market-condition awards.
- Test whether the model used reflects the substantive terms of the award, including post-vesting restrictions and transferability limits.

### Evaluate Vesting Conditions and Recognition Pattern

Vesting conditions determine the expense recognition pattern. Service conditions require straight-line recognition over the service period; performance conditions require estimating the number of awards expected to vest; market conditions are reflected in grant-date fair value rather than in the estimate of vesting. Confusing these conditions misstates the timing and amount of expense.

- Classify each award's vesting conditions as service, performance, or market.
- For service conditions, confirm expense is recognized straight-line over the requisite service period.
- For performance conditions, confirm expense is based on the best estimate of awards expected to vest.
- For market conditions, confirm the condition is reflected in grant-date fair value and expense is recognized regardless of whether the condition is met.
- Test the requisite service period, including awards with graded or cliff vesting.
- Evaluate the accounting for graded vesting, including the choice of straight-line versus accelerated attribution.
- Reassess performance condition estimates each reporting date and adjust prospectively.
- Confirm that the service period reflects the period over which the employee is required to provide service, not merely the vesting date.

### Test Forfeiture Assumptions

Forfeitures reduce the expense recognized. Management may estimate forfeitures or account for them when they occur. The estimate is a source of bias, because a low forfeiture assumption overstates vesting and expense, while a high assumption understates it.

- Confirm whether the entity estimates forfeitures or accounts for them as they occur.
- If estimated, test the historical forfeiture rate and the methodology for reasonableness.
- Evaluate whether the estimate reflects distinct groups with different forfeiture characteristics.
- Reconcile actual forfeitures to the estimate and assess the need for revision.
- Assess whether the forfeiture estimate has been adjusted to manage expense.
- Confirm the accounting for forfeitures of awards with performance or market conditions.
- Evaluate whether the forfeiture rate is applied consistently across awards with similar employee populations and risk profiles.

### Account for Modifications and Cancellations

Modifications and cancellations are remeasurement events that are easy to overlook. A modification can increase or decrease expense depending on whether the fair value of the modified award exceeds the original. Cancellations and repricings have specific accounting that must be applied.

- Identify all modifications during the period and determine whether they are favorable or unfavorable.
- For modifications, measure the incremental fair value and recognize additional expense if favorable.
- Test the accounting for changes in vesting conditions, including acceleration.
- Identify cancellations and confirm they are accounted for in the period of cancellation.
- Evaluate the accounting for repricings, including whether they are treated as modifications or exchanges.
- Assess the accounting for cash settlements of equity awards and the resulting reclassification.
- Confirm that modifications of awards with performance or market conditions are evaluated for their effect on recognized expense.
- Assess whether a modification that extends the exercise window or reduces the price is accounted for as a new grant or a modification.

### Evaluate the Tax Effects

Stock compensation creates tax effects that differ between financial reporting and tax reporting, including deferred tax assets and uncertain tax positions. The accounting depends on whether the award is equity-settled or liability-settled and on the jurisdiction's tax rules. Errors here affect both the tax provision and equity.

- Confirm whether the tax deduction is fixed at grant or based on the share price at vesting or exercise.
- For equity-classified awards, confirm the deferred tax asset is measured based on the intrinsic value at vesting.
- Test the recognition of excess tax benefits or deficiencies in equity or income, consistent with the framework.
- Evaluate the valuation allowance on deferred tax assets generated by stock compensation.
- Assess the accounting for disallowed deductions and uncertain tax positions.
- Reconcile the tax effect to the tax provision and the equity accounts.
- Confirm disclosure of the tax effects of stock compensation.
- Assess whether the entity has recognized a valuation allowance against deferred tax assets generated solely by stock compensation deductions.

### Reconcile Expense to the Ledger and Notes

Stock compensation expense must be reconciled from the grant-level computation to the ledger and the notes. The reconciliation catches errors in classification, period, and amount. It also confirms that the expense is presented in the correct income statement line.

- Reconcile total recognized expense to the general ledger by award type and expense category.
- Confirm the classification of expense into cost of sales, research and development, and selling and administrative.
- Reconcile the unrecognized compensation cost and the weighted-average remaining service period for disclosure.
- Confirm that capitalized stock compensation, if any, is appropriately included in inventory or assets.
- Reconcile the equity accounts affected, including additional paid-in capital and deferred compensation.
- Test the cash flow presentation of tax benefits from stock compensation.
- Confirm that excess tax benefits classified as financing inflows are separated from operating cash flows correctly.

### Assess Disclosure Completeness

Stock compensation disclosure is extensive and must include the valuation methods, assumptions, expense recognized, and activity during the period. The disclosure is part of the assertion, not optional context. Boilerplate disclosure is a deficiency when the awards are material.

- Confirm disclosure of the valuation model and the significant assumptions for each award type.
- Test disclosure of the total expense recognized and the line items in which it is included.
- Verify disclosure of the unrecognized compensation cost and the weighted-average period.
- Confirm disclosure of the activity during the period, including grants, vesting, forfeitures, and cancellations.
- Test disclosure of the tax effects and any excess tax benefits.
- Confirm disclosure of modifications, including the incremental fair value recognized.
- Assess whether the disclosure of related-party grants is adequate.
- Confirm that the fair value of awards granted to non-employees is measured at the service receipt date or grant date consistent with the framework.

### Evaluate International and Multi-Jurisdictional Plans

Stock-based compensation often spans multiple jurisdictions, each with its own tax rules, securities regulations, and plan structures, and the auditor must confirm that the accounting reflects the specific terms of each local plan rather than a single global assumption. Cross-border plans create transfer pricing, withholding, and currency considerations that affect both the expense and the equity accounts. A failure to disaggregate the population by plan and jurisdiction hides errors that aggregate testing cannot detect.

- Identify all local and regional plans and confirm each is approved and governed by its own terms.
- Test that expense is recognized in the correct legal entity for awards granted by a parent on behalf of subsidiaries.
- Evaluate the recharge or transfer pricing arrangements for stock compensation between parent and subsidiary entities.
- Assess the effect of foreign currency on the measurement of awards denominated in a currency other than the functional currency.
- Confirm that withholding tax obligations on cross-border awards are accrued and disclosed.
- Reconcile subsidiary-level expense to the consolidated stock compensation expense.
- Evaluate whether local securities law exemptions relied upon for unregistered grants remain valid.
- Assess whether employee stock purchase plans with a discount or look-back provision are accounted for under the compensation or non-compensation approach.
- Confirm that the population includes awards assumed in a business combination and that their remaining service period is correctly determined.
- Test whether awards with reload or tandem features are accounted for according to their specific terms.

## Common Traps

### Accepting the Black-Scholes Output Without Testing Assumptions

A Black-Scholes value is only as good as its inputs, and volatility and expected term are the most judgmental. The trap is the false precision of a formula. Test each assumption against independent evidence.

### Confusing Performance and Market Conditions

Market conditions are built into grant-date fair value; performance conditions are not. The trap is reversing expense for a failed market condition or recognizing expense regardless of a performance condition. Classify conditions carefully and apply the correct accounting.

### Missing a Modification

Modifications are easy to miss because they occur outside the normal grant cycle. The trap is treating the award as unchanged. Identify all modifications and measure their incremental fair value.

### Using an Inappropriate Model for the Terms

A plain Black-Scholes model does not capture early exercise, market conditions, or indexed features. The trap is defaulting to one model for all awards. Match the model to the terms of the award.

### Understating Volatility to Reduce Expense

Volatility is a significant driver of option value, and understating it reduces expense. The trap is accepting a volatility assumption that is low without support. Test volatility against historical and implied data.

### Forgetting the Requisite Service Period for Graded Vesting

Graded vesting can be expensed straight-line or by accelerated attribution, but the requisite service period must be applied correctly. The trap is expensing over the wrong period. Confirm the attribution method and period.

### Ignoring Tax Effects of Equity Awards

The tax accounting for stock compensation is complex and error-prone, particularly excess benefits and deferred taxes. The trap is treating it as a simple deduction. Apply the specific tax accounting for equity awards.

### Under-Disclosing Assumptions and Activity

Disclosure of valuation methods, assumptions, and period activity is required and is often incomplete. The trap is treating disclosure as boilerplate. Tie each disclosure element to the testing performed.

## Self-Check

- Was a complete population of grants, modifications, cancellations, and forfeitures obtained and reconciled to the equity ledger?
- Was grant-date fair value tested, including the model and each significant assumption, with a specialist used where warranted?
- Were vesting conditions classified as service, performance, or market, with the correct recognition pattern applied to each?
- Was the forfeiture assumption tested for reasonableness and reconciled to actual forfeitures?
- Were all modifications and cancellations identified, with incremental fair value and remeasurement accounted for correctly?
- Were the tax effects of stock compensation evaluated, including excess benefits and deferred tax assets?
- Is total recognized expense reconciled to the general ledger and the correct income statement classification?
- Are capitalized stock compensation amounts, if any, included in inventory or assets appropriately?
- Is disclosure of valuation methods, assumptions, expense, activity, and tax effects complete and consistent with the testing?
- Are related-party and executive grants identified and given appropriate scrutiny?
- Is the unrecognized compensation cost and weighted-average remaining service period disclosed and supported?
