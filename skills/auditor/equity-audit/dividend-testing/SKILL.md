# Dividend Testing

Dividend testing produces evidence that distributions to shareholders are authorized, lawful, correctly measured, and recorded in the proper period. Agents tend to treat dividends as a straightforward cash outflow, but the real judgment problem is that dividend legality depends on corporate law and the entity's own constitutional documents, distributions can be in cash, stock, or property, and the declaration, record, and payment dates each carry distinct accounting consequences. An unlawful or unauthorized dividend can create personal liability for directors and a receivable from shareholders, while a misdated accrual shifts retained earnings and liabilities between periods.

Failing to test dividends for legality and authorization leaves the entity exposed to distributions it was not permitted to make, and failing to test the dates and amounts leaves the financial statements misstated. The skill is about confirming authority, legality, and measurement, and about distinguishing the accounting for each type of distribution.

Use this skill whenever the audit program includes dividend declarations, payments, reinvestment plans, stock dividends, or accrued preferred dividends. The goal is to confirm that dividends are properly authorized, lawful, measured correctly, recorded in the right period, and adequately disclosed.

## Core Rules

### Verify Declaration and Authorization

A dividend is not valid until it is formally declared by the appropriate body. The auditor must confirm the declaration, the authority for it, and the terms. A dividend paid without declaration is a different transaction and may be recoverable from shareholders.

- Obtain the board resolution or shareholder resolution declaring the dividend.
- Confirm the body declaring the dividend had authority under the corporate constitution and law.
- Verify the resolution specifies the class, amount per share, and the declaration, record, and payment dates.
- Test compliance with any shareholder agreement or preferred stock terms that govern dividends.
- Confirm that the declaration complies with debt covenants restricting distributions.
- Reconcile the declared amount to the dividend payable recorded.
- Identify any dividends proposed but not declared at period-end and assess disclosure versus accrual.
- Confirm that interim dividends paid during the year were each separately authorized and properly recorded.

### Test Dividend Legality

Dividends may only be paid out of distributable reserves as defined by applicable law. Paying a dividend when distributable reserves are insufficient is unlawful and can create director liability and a receivable from shareholders. The auditor must confirm the legal basis for the distribution.

- Determine the legal test for distributable profits under the applicable company law.
- Compute distributable reserves using the legal basis, which may differ from the accounting retained earnings.
- Confirm that the dividend does not exceed the available distributable reserves.
- Identify any solvency or capital impairment test that must also be met.
- Assess the effect of accumulated losses or revaluation reserves on distributability.
- Consider restrictions in loan agreements that limit distributions beyond the legal test.
- Document the legality analysis and the basis for the conclusion.
- Consider the effect of prior-year errors or restatements on the computation of distributable reserves.

### Apply the Correct Dates and Cutoff

The declaration, record, and payment dates drive the accounting. A dividend becomes a liability at the declaration date for cash dividends, and the population entitled to payment is fixed at the record date. Errors in applying these dates shift the liability and the retained earnings reduction between periods.

- Recognize the dividend payable at the declaration date, not the record or payment date.
- Confirm the record date used to determine shareholders entitled to the dividend.
- Test that dividends declared before period-end but paid after are accrued as a liability.
- Test that dividends declared after period-end are not accrued, even if relating to the period.
- Reconcile the dividend payable to the subsequent payment and the bank.
- Confirm cutoff for interim dividends paid during the year and the final dividend proposed.
- Investigate any dividend recorded in a period inconsistent with its declaration date.
- Confirm that dividends are presented in the statement of changes in equity, not as an expense in profit or loss.

### Distinguish Cash, Stock, and Property Dividends

The accounting differs by the form of distribution. Cash dividends reduce retained earnings and create a liability; stock dividends reclassify amounts within equity; property dividends require fair value measurement of the distributed assets. Confusing the forms misstates both equity and, in some cases, income.

- For cash dividends, confirm reduction of retained earnings and recognition of a payable.
- For stock dividends, confirm reclassification from retained earnings to share capital, with the amount depending on the size of the distribution.
- For property dividends, measure the distributed assets at fair value and recognize any gain or loss.
- Confirm that large stock dividends are accounted for at market value where required by the framework.
- Test that scrip or stock dividends are reflected in the share count and capital accounts.
- Evaluate the accounting for spin-offs and distributions of subsidiary shares.
- Confirm that the form of distribution is disclosed.
- Evaluate whether a large stock dividend requires reclassification at market value rather than par, depending on the framework.

### Evaluate Dividend Reinvestment Plans

Dividend reinvestment plans (DRIPs) allow shareholders to receive additional shares instead of cash. The accounting depends on whether the plan is a genuine equity issuance or a financing arrangement, and on the price at which shares are issued. DRIPs can obscure the cash dividend figure and the equity issuance.

- Obtain the plan terms and confirm the pricing mechanism for issued shares.
- Confirm that shares issued under the plan are recorded at the issue price, with par to share capital and the excess to paid-in capital.
- Reconcile the shares issued under the plan to the registrar and the equity ledger.
- Assess whether the plan creates a liability for any cash element or deferred settlement.
- Evaluate whether the plan is with an employee benefit trust or other structure requiring consolidation analysis.
- Confirm disclosure of the plan and the shares issued during the period.
- Test the treatment of any discount to market price as a possible distribution or compensation.
- Reconcile the cash actually paid under the DRIP versus the gross dividend declared to avoid misstating the financing and distribution totals.

### Test Accrued Dividends on Preferred Stock

Preferred dividends accumulate if not declared, depending on the terms of the instrument. Cumulative preferred dividends not declared may require disclosure even if not accrued as a liability. The distinction between accrual and disclosure depends on the instrument terms and the framework.

- Confirm whether the preferred stock is cumulative or non-cumulative.
- For cumulative preferred, compute dividends in arrears and confirm disclosure.
- Test whether undeclared cumulative dividends meet the criteria for liability recognition under the framework.
- For non-cumulative preferred, confirm no accrual is made until declaration.
- Evaluate participating or preferential dividend features and their accounting.
- Reconcile the preferred dividend computation to the instrument terms and the share count.
- Assess the effect of dividends in arrears on going concern and liquidity disclosure.
- Confirm that participating preferred dividend features are evaluated and the allocation to each class is computed correctly.

### Test Compliance With Covenants and Restrictions

Debt agreements, preference share terms, and regulatory regimes often restrict dividends. Breaching a restriction can trigger default or create a constructive obligation. The auditor must identify all restrictions and test compliance.

- Review loan agreements for dividend and distribution covenants.
- Confirm compliance with restricted payment provisions in bond indentures.
- Test compliance with regulatory capital or solvency restrictions for regulated entities.
- Identify restrictions in preference share terms on dividends to other classes.
- Assess whether a covenant breach affects the classification of debt as current or triggers default.
- Confirm disclosure of restrictions on the ability to pay dividends.
- Consider whether restrictions on subsidiaries' dividends affect consolidated distributable reserves.
- Assess whether a breach of distribution covenants triggers cross-default under other facilities.

### Reconcile and Disclose Dividends

Dividends must be reconciled from declaration through payment and disclosed with enough detail for users to understand the distributions. Disclosure includes dividends per share, the aggregate amount, dividends payable at period-end, and any restrictions. The dividend reconciliation is where the testing converges.

- Reconcile declared, paid, and payable dividends for each class.
- Confirm dividends per share agree to the declared rate and the share count.
- Verify disclosure of dividends declared after the reporting date.
- Confirm disclosure of dividends in arrears on cumulative preferred stock.
- Test disclosure of restrictions on distributions and their effect.
- Reconcile the dividend note to the statement of changes in equity and the cash flow statement.
- Confirm that dividends paid are correctly classified as financing activities in the cash flow statement.

### Evaluate Constructive Dividends and Disguised Distributions

Not all distributions are labeled as dividends, and constructive or disguised distributions are a frequent source of misstatement and tax exposure. Loans to shareholders forgiven below market terms, personal expenses paid by the entity, and transfers of assets to owners at less than fair value may all be distributions in substance. The auditor must look beyond the dividend account to identify transactions that economically transfer value to owners without formal declaration.

- Review related-party loans and advances to shareholders for terms that indicate a distribution in substance.
- Assess transactions where the entity sold assets to shareholders at below-market prices.
- Evaluate personal expenses paid by the entity on behalf of owners or directors.
- Consider whether the forgiveness of shareholder loans constitutes a constructive dividend.
- Review compensation paid to owner-managers for reasonableness relative to services rendered.
- Assess whether guarantees provided for the benefit of shareholders carry below-market terms requiring recognition.
- Confirm that identified constructive distributions are recorded and disclosed as dividends where required.
- Evaluate whether shareholder loans that are indefinitely extended without repayment terms should be reclassified as equity or treated as distributions.
- Assess whether the entity has made distributions in violation of solvency tests that may be recoverable from directors.
- Review tax correspondence to confirm that constructive distributions have been properly reported to tax authorities.

## Common Traps

### Accruing a Declared Dividend in the Wrong Period

The liability arises at declaration, not at record or payment. The trap is dating the accrual to the payment date. Recognize the payable at the declaration date.

### Paying a Dividend Without Legal Distributable Reserves

Accounting retained earnings may exceed legal distributable profits, especially after revaluations or losses. The trap is assuming retained earnings equals distributable reserves. Compute distributable reserves on the legal basis.

### Treating a Stock Dividend Like a Cash Dividend

Stock dividends reclassify within equity and do not create a liability. The trap is reducing retained earnings and recording a payable. Apply the equity reclassification treatment.

### Missing Cumulative Preferred Dividends in Arrears

Undeclared cumulative dividends may require disclosure even when not accrued. The trap is ignoring them because no liability was recognized. Compute and disclose dividends in arrears.

### Ignoring Covenant Restrictions on Dividends

A dividend that breaches a debt covenant can trigger default. The trap is testing the dividend without reading the covenants. Review all distribution restrictions.

### Overlooking Property or Scrip Dividends

Distributions of assets or additional shares carry different measurement and disclosure. The trap is treating them as ordinary cash dividends. Apply the correct accounting to each form.

### Misclassifying Proposed Dividends

A dividend proposed after period-end is a non-adjusting event requiring disclosure, not an accrual. The trap is accruing it because it relates to the period's results. Disclose, do not accrue.

### Forgetting DRIP Effects on Equity and Cash

A reinvestment plan issues shares and reduces the cash dividend actually paid. The trap is reporting the gross cash dividend without showing the equity issuance. Present the DRIP shares as an equity transaction.

## Self-Check

- Was each dividend traced to a formal declaration by an authorized body, with terms confirmed?
- Was the legality of the distribution tested against distributable reserves and solvency requirements?
- Was the dividend payable recognized at the declaration date, with correct cutoff across periods?
- Were cash, stock, and property dividends distinguished and accounted for under the correct treatment?
- For stock dividends, was the reclassification amount consistent with the framework and the size of the distribution?
- Were dividend reinvestment plan shares reconciled to the registrar and recorded at the issue price?
- Were cumulative preferred dividends in arrears computed and disclosed, with liability recognition assessed?
- Were debt covenants, preference terms, and regulatory restrictions on dividends reviewed for compliance?
- Are proposed but undeclared dividends disclosed rather than accrued?
- Is the dividend reconciliation supported from declaration through payment, and consistent with the cash flow and equity statements?; are dividends per share, restrictions, and dividends in arrears adequately disclosed?
