# Going Concern Assessment and Evidence

The going concern assumption underpins the preparation of financial statements on a basis other than liquidation. When the assumption is inappropriate, assets must be written down to recoverable amounts and liabilities reclassified, fundamentally changing the picture presented. The auditor's obligation under ISA 570 is to obtain sufficient appropriate evidence about whether management's use of the going concern basis is appropriate, and about whether a material uncertainty exists that may cast significant doubt on the entity's ability to continue. This is one of the highest-risk areas in audit: getting it wrong means opining on financial statements that may be fundamentally misstated, and the consequences fall on the auditor when the entity fails shortly after.

This skill covers the assessment judgments: evaluating management's process, the period of assessment, the adequacy of cash flow forecasts, the supporting evidence, and the events or conditions that may cast significant doubt.

This guidance addresses audit judgment. It does not replace ISA 570, applicable law, or firm methodology. Going concern conclusions must be documented, subject to engagement partner review, and where required, independent/quality management review.

## Core Rules

### Evaluate management's assessment process, not only its conclusion

The auditor must evaluate management's process for the going concern assessment: the assumptions underlying cash flow forecasts, the reliability of historical data, the process for generating forecasts, and management's response to identified events or conditions. Do not accept the conclusion without understanding and testing the process. Where management has not performed an assessment, or the period is less than 12 months from the reporting date, the auditor must request that management extend it.

### Confirm the period of assessment is at least 12 months

The assessment must cover at least 12 months from the end of the reporting period (or from the date of approval, depending on framework). A shorter period does not comply. Verify the period covered and obtain evidence that management's forecasts extend sufficiently.

### Scrutinize cash flow forecasts with professional skepticism

Cash flow forecasts are the heart of most going concern assessments, and they are inherently uncertain and susceptible to management optimism. Evaluate the key assumptions: revenue growth, margin assumptions, collection of receivables, timing of cash inflows, capex deferrals, and financing availability. Compare forecasts to historical performance and to subsequent actual results where available. Test the sensitivity of the forecast to adverse but plausible variations. A forecast that depends on uninterrupted growth, full collection, or new financing that is not yet secured is high-risk.

### Identify events or conditions that may cast significant doubt

The auditor must remain alert throughout the audit for events or conditions that, individually or collectively, may cast significant doubt on going concern: net liability or net current liability positions, repeated operating losses, loan covenant breaches or near-breaches, denial of credit, loss of a major customer or supplier, labor disputes, regulatory action, and dependence on new financing or asset disposals to meet obligations. Document the events or conditions identified and their evaluation.

### Evaluate the adequacy of management's mitigation plans

Where events or conditions create doubt, evaluate management's plans to mitigate: cost reductions, asset disposals, refinancing, new equity, or suspension of distributions. Assess whether the plans are feasible, within management's control, and sufficient to address the shortfall. Plans that depend on third parties (lenders, investors) not yet committed provide less support. Test the financial effect of the plans in the forecast.

### Determine whether a material uncertainty exists

A material uncertainty exists when events or conditions, individually or collectively, may cast significant doubt on the entity's ability to continue, such that disclosure is necessary. Determine whether a material uncertainty exists based on the evidence, regardless of management's view. If a material uncertainty exists and is adequately disclosed, issue an unmodified opinion with a Material Uncertainty Related to Going Concern (MURGC) section. If not adequately disclosed, modify the opinion.

## Common Traps

### Accepting management's forecast at face value

Forecasts that assume growth, full collection, or secured financing are the most common source of going concern failures. Test the assumptions against historical and subsequent evidence, and apply sensitivity analysis. Optimism bias is the central risk.

### Over-relying on management's mitigation plans

Plans that depend on uncommitted third parties (refinancing, new investors) or on actions outside management's control provide weak support. Evaluate feasibility and control, and discount plans that are speculative.

### Truncating the assessment period

An assessment that covers less than 12 months does not comply. Verify the period and require extension where necessary.

### Failing to identify cumulative indicators

No single indicator may be decisive, but the combination—recurring losses, covenant pressure, declining liquidity, customer concentration—may collectively cast significant doubt. Evaluate indicators in aggregate.

### Confusing "material uncertainty" with "inappropriate going concern basis"

A material uncertainty means the basis is still appropriate but disclosure is required; the basis becomes inappropriate only when the entity cannot continue at all. Do not default to an inappropriate-basis conclusion when the evidence supports a material uncertainty, nor the reverse.

## Self-Check

- Have I evaluated management's going concern assessment process, including assumptions, data, and forecasts, not only the conclusion?
- Does the assessment cover at least 12 months from the reporting date?
- Have I tested the cash flow forecast's key assumptions against historical and subsequent evidence, and applied sensitivity analysis?
- Have I identified and documented events or conditions that may cast significant doubt, individually and collectively?
- Have I evaluated the feasibility, control, and sufficiency of management's mitigation plans, discounting speculative or third-party-dependent plans?
- Have I determined whether a material uncertainty exists, based on the evidence and regardless of management's view?
- Where a material uncertainty exists, is the disclosure adequate, and is the report treatment (MURGC section) appropriate?
- Has the engagement partner reviewed the conclusion, and where required, has independent review been obtained?
