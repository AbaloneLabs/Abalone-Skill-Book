# Going Concern Material Uncertainty and Reporting

The going concern conclusion drives a specific set of reporting outcomes, and selecting the wrong one is a serious audit failure. Under ISA 570, the reporting outcomes form a decision tree: if the going concern basis is appropriate and no material uncertainty exists, an unmodified opinion is issued; if a material uncertainty exists and is adequately disclosed, an unmodified opinion with a Material Uncertainty Related to Going Concern (MURGC) section; if a material uncertainty exists but disclosure is inadequate, a qualified or adverse opinion; if the auditor cannot obtain sufficient evidence, a disclaimer or qualified opinion; and if the going concern basis is inappropriate, a qualified or adverse opinion. The distinctions are precise and the consequences of error are significant.

This skill covers the reporting decision logic: how to move from the assessment conclusion to the correct report outcome, how to draft the MURGC section, and how to handle the cases of inadequate disclosure, scope limitation, and inappropriate basis.

This guidance addresses audit reporting judgment. It does not replace ISA 560, ISA 570, ISA 705, applicable law, or firm methodology. Reporting decisions must be documented and subject to engagement partner and, where required, independent review.

## Core Rules

### Map the conclusion to the reporting outcome precisely

The reporting outcome follows directly from two findings: (1) is the going concern basis appropriate? and (2) is a material uncertainty adequately disclosed? Work through the decision tree explicitly: appropriate basis, no material uncertainty → unmodified; appropriate basis, material uncertainty, adequate disclosure → unmodified with MURGC; appropriate basis, material uncertainty, inadequate disclosure → qualified or adverse; unable to obtain sufficient evidence → qualified or disclaimer; inappropriate basis → qualified or adverse. Document the path taken.

### Draft the MURGC section to standard

When a material uncertainty exists and is adequately disclosed, the MURGC section must: state that a material uncertainty exists that may cast significant doubt on the entity's ability to continue; confirm that the opinion is not modified; describe the principal events or conditions giving rise to the uncertainty; and refer to the related note. The wording is prescribed; do not paraphrase loosely. The MURGC section is placed immediately after the opinion.

### Reserve qualified/adverse for inadequate disclosure or inappropriate basis

A qualified or adverse opinion for going concern arises when disclosure of a material uncertainty is inadequate, or when the going concern basis is inappropriate and the financial statements do not reflect the consequences. Do not use a qualification where an MURGC section is the correct outcome—qualification in that case overstates the problem and misleads users.

### Use disclaimer or qualification for inability to obtain evidence

Where management has not performed an assessment or the auditor cannot obtain sufficient appropriate evidence about going concern (e.g., no forecasts, no mitigation plan, no financing evidence), the scope limitation may be so pervasive that a disclaimer is warranted; otherwise, a qualification. Document the nature and pervasiveness of the limitation.

### Evaluate the appropriateness of the going concern basis separately

A material uncertainty assumes the going concern basis is still appropriate. The basis becomes inappropriate when the entity cannot continue at all—liquidation is unavoidable, financing is unobtainable, operations will cease. In that case, the financial statements must be prepared on a liquidation basis, and failure to do so warrants a qualified or adverse opinion. Distinguish carefully between "material uncertainty" (basis appropriate, disclosure required) and "inappropriate basis" (basis must change).

### Coordinate with subsequent events and emphasis of matter

Going concern conclusions are informed by subsequent events (e.g., post-year-end refinancing or its failure). Coordinate the timing of the going concern conclusion with the subsequent events review. Do not duplicate an Emphasis of Matter with a MURGC; the MURGC is the prescribed mechanism for going concern emphasis.

## Common Traps

### Defaulting to a qualification when MURGC is correct

A qualification where an MURGC section is required overstates the problem and misleads users into thinking the financial statements are misstated, when in fact they are fairly stated with a disclosed uncertainty.

### Loose MURGC drafting

The MURGC wording is prescribed for good reason: users must understand that the opinion is unmodified, that an uncertainty exists, and where to find the disclosure. Paraphrasing risks obscuring these elements.

### Failing to distinguish material uncertainty from inappropriate basis

Concluding "inappropriate basis" when the evidence supports only a material uncertainty forces an unnecessary qualification or adverse opinion. The test is whether the entity can continue at all, not whether it faces significant challenges.

### Mishandling inability to obtain evidence

When forecasts or mitigation evidence is unavailable, the issue is scope limitation, not a going concern conclusion. Apply the scope limitation framework (qualification or disclaimer) rather than forcing a going concern opinion.

### Duplicating MURGC and Emphasis of Matter

Using both a MURGC section and an Emphasis of Matter for the same going concern issue is redundant and confusing. The MURGC is the prescribed mechanism.

## Self-Check

- Have I mapped the going concern conclusion to the reporting outcome through the explicit decision tree, and documented the path?
- Where a material uncertainty exists and is adequately disclosed, is the MURGC section drafted to the prescribed standard and placed after the opinion?
- Have I reserved qualified or adverse opinion for inadequate disclosure or inappropriate basis, not for an adequately disclosed material uncertainty?
- Where I cannot obtain sufficient evidence, have I applied the scope limitation framework (qualification or disclaimer) based on pervasiveness?
- Have I carefully distinguished material uncertainty (basis appropriate) from inappropriate basis (entity cannot continue)?
- Have I coordinated the going concern conclusion with the subsequent events review?
- Have I avoided duplicating MURGC and Emphasis of Matter for the same issue?
- Has the engagement partner reviewed the reporting outcome, and where required, has independent review been obtained?
