# Going Concern Forecasts, Covenants, and Financing Evidence

The evidentiary core of a going concern assessment is the cash flow forecast and the evidence supporting its key drivers: revenue, margins, working capital movements, capital expenditure, and—critically—financing. Forecasts are inherently uncertain and susceptible to management optimism, and the auditor's job is to test them against evidence rather than accept them. The most common going concern failures arise not from missing forecasts but from forecasts that rested on untested assumptions: revenue growth that did not materialize, covenant compliance that was marginal or breached, and refinancing that was assumed but never secured. The auditor who treats the forecast as a document to be read rather than tested has not obtained sufficient evidence.

This skill covers the evidentiary judgments: testing forecast assumptions, evaluating covenant compliance and breach consequences, and obtaining evidence on financing availability and refinancing commitments.

This guidance addresses audit evidence judgment. It does not replace ISA 570, ISA 580, applicable law, or firm methodology. Evidence conclusions must be documented and subject to engagement partner review.

## Core Rules

### Test forecast assumptions against evidence, not plausibility

A forecast that reads plausibly is not the same as a forecast supported by evidence. Test the key assumptions: revenue growth against order books, pipeline, historical seasonality, and market data; gross margin against recent actuals and cost trends; working capital movements against collection and payment patterns; capex against committed and budgeted projects. Where an assumption departs materially from historical experience, require management's support and test it. Document the evidence relied upon for each material assumption.

### Compare forecast to subsequent actual results

Where the reporting date has passed and subsequent actual results are available, compare forecast to actual. Persistent shortfalls between forecast and actual are a powerful indicator that the forecast is optimistic. Subsequent results that track or exceed the forecast provide corroboration. Use the subsequent period evidence; it is often the most reliable indicator of forecast reliability.

### Evaluate covenant compliance precisely, including headroom

Obtain the loan agreement and test compliance with each covenant as at the reporting date and projected through the assessment period. Calculate headroom—the margin by which the entity complies—and assess its sensitivity to forecast variations. Marginal headroom is a significant going concern indicator. Where a breach has occurred or is probable, evaluate the consequences: acceleration rights, cross-default, and the lender's actual or likely response. A technical breach that the lender has waived is different from one that triggers acceleration.

### Obtain evidence on financing availability, not assertions

Financing is often the decisive variable. Distinguish between committed and uncommitted facilities: a committed facility with adequate headroom provides strong support; an uncommitted facility, or one subject to conditions, provides weaker support. For refinancing of maturing debt, obtain evidence of the status: term sheets, lender commitments, board minutes, or correspondence. A refinancing that is "expected" or "in discussion" is not evidence of availability. Where financing depends on a third party not yet committed, discount it heavily in the assessment.

### Apply sensitivity analysis to the forecast

Test the forecast against adverse but plausible scenarios: revenue 10–20% below plan, margin compression, delayed customer collection, capex acceleration, loss of a major customer, covenant breach. If the entity remains solvent only under the base case and fails under plausible adverse scenarios, a material uncertainty likely exists. Document the scenarios tested and the results.

### Evaluate the reliability of mitigation plans

Where the forecast depends on mitigation (cost cuts, disposals, financing), evaluate whether each plan is within management's control, feasible in the timeframe, and sufficient. Cost cuts within management's control and already underway are stronger than disposals dependent on market conditions. Document the assessment of each plan's reliability.

## Common Traps

### Accepting forecasts without testing assumptions

Reading a forecast and concluding it "looks reasonable" is not testing. Each material assumption must be supported by evidence and tested against historical and subsequent data.

### Ignoring marginal covenant headroom

Headroom that is positive but small is a going concern indicator, because modest forecast variation can trigger breach. Assess the sensitivity of headroom, not only whether covenants are currently met.

### Treating uncommitted financing as available

Uncommitted facilities, facilities subject to conditions, or refinancing "in discussion" are not evidence of availability. The trap is treating management's expectation of financing as if it were committed.

### Overlooking cross-default and acceleration

A breach of one covenant can trigger acceleration and cross-default across the debt structure, converting a manageable breach into a liquidity crisis. Evaluate the consequences of breach across the financing structure, not only the breached facility.

### Failing to use subsequent actual results

Subsequent actual results are often the most reliable evidence of forecast reliability, yet are sometimes ignored because they fall "after year-end." Use them; they are within the assessment period and highly relevant.

## Self-Check

- Have I tested each material forecast assumption (revenue, margin, working capital, capex) against evidence, not merely plausibility?
- Have I compared the forecast to subsequent actual results and evaluated persistent shortfalls?
- Have I tested covenant compliance as at the reporting date and through the assessment period, and assessed headroom sensitivity?
- For any breach or probable breach, have I evaluated acceleration, cross-default, and the lender's likely response?
- Have I distinguished committed from uncommitted financing, and obtained evidence (not assertions) on refinancing status?
- Have I applied sensitivity analysis to the forecast under adverse but plausible scenarios?
- Have I evaluated the reliability, control, feasibility, and sufficiency of each mitigation plan?
- Have I documented the evidence relied upon and the conclusions reached, and has the engagement partner reviewed them?
