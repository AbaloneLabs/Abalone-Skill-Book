# Retesting Approach

When management claims a finding is remediated, the auditor must decide how to retest the corrective action before accepting it. Retesting is not a repeat of the original test; it is a distinct procedure designed to confirm that the new or changed control actually operates effectively. The design of the retest determines what conclusion it can support. A retest that examines one item immediately after implementation proves the control can work, not that it operates over time. A retest that relies on management's own testing without assessing its competence and objectivity inherits whatever weaknesses that testing contained. A retest scoped for a manual control as if it were automated overstates the evidence. The skill is in designing a retest whose scope, extent, and independence match the risk of the finding, the type of control, and the conclusion needed, so that closure or reliance rests on evidence genuinely sufficient to support it.

Use this skill when designing a retest of remediated controls or findings, deciding retest scope and extent, choosing between independent retest and reliance on management testing, or ensuring retest evidence supports the closure decision. The goal is a retesting approach that produces sufficient, appropriate evidence to confirm remediation without wasteful over-testing.

## Core Rules

### Design The Retest Around What Must Be Confirmed

A retest should be designed to answer a specific question: does the corrective action operate effectively to reduce the risk the finding identified? The design follows from that question, not from a generic retest template.

Design by defining:

- the control or process change being retested;
- the population and period over which operation must be shown;
- the attribute or evidence that confirms operation;
- the sample size or method appropriate to the risk;
- who performs the retest and with what independence;
- what result constitutes pass versus fail.

A retest designed without these elements produces data but not a defensible conclusion. State what the retest is meant to establish before performing it.

### Match Retest Scope To The Original Finding's Nature

The retest must address the same risk and population as the original finding. A retest that covers a narrower scope than the original finding can appear to pass while leaving the broader risk unaddressed.

Match scope by:

- covering the same population the original finding affected, not a subset;
- addressing the same control objective, such as authorization, completeness, or accuracy;
- including the same types of transactions or conditions that originally failed;
- verifying the fix applies across locations, systems, or units where the finding applied.

If the original finding concerned approvals across all regions, a retest confined to one region does not confirm remediation. Match the retest's reach to the finding's reach.

### Distinguish Manual And Automated Control Retesting

The retest logic differs between manual and automated controls, because automated controls operate consistently once configured while manual controls depend on the person performing them each time.

For manual controls:

- retest across multiple points in time and, where relevant, multiple operators;
- include periods after the change has settled, when workarounds may reappear;
- test that evidence of operation, such as sign-offs, is retained.

For automated controls:

- a single retest, combined with confirmation the configuration is in place and unchanged, can support a conclusion across the period;
- test that the configuration was not reverted and that IT general controls governing changes are adequate;
- do not assume continued operation if IT general controls are weak.

Applying automated logic to a manual control overstates the evidence; applying manual logic to an automated control wastes effort.

### Decide Between Independent Retest And Reliance On Management Testing

The auditor can retest independently or rely on management's testing of the corrective action, but the two are not equivalent. Reliance is appropriate only where management's testing is competent, objective, and sufficient.

Rely on management testing only when:

- management's testing is documented and objective, not a self-assessment summary;
- the testers are competent and independent of the control's operation;
- the scope, sample, and method are adequate for the risk;
- the auditor has assessed and accepted the testing's quality.

Otherwise, perform an independent retest. For higher-risk findings, independent retesting is generally required, because the consequences of accepting inadequate remediation are greatest where the risk is highest.

### Calibrate Retest Extent To Risk

The extent of retesting should match the risk of the finding and the consequences of failed remediation. A blanket extent misallocates effort.

Increase retest extent when:

- the finding was high-risk or a significant deficiency;
- prior remediation attempts failed or the issue recurred;
- the corrective action is complex or recently implemented;
- management has a history of partial fixes.

A lower extent may be acceptable for lower-risk findings, with the rationale documented. The risk of the original finding, not the verifier's convenience, sets the extent.

### Define Pass And Fail Criteria Before Retesting

The retest's conclusion depends on what result constitutes success. Defining pass and fail criteria in advance prevents subjective interpretation after the fact.

Define criteria by:

- stating the deviation rate or condition that would constitute a fail;
- specifying how anomalies or exceptions will be evaluated;
- determining in advance whether a single failure triggers non-closure or further investigation;
- documenting the basis for the criteria relative to the risk.

A retest that reports "no significant issues" without defined criteria cannot be evaluated. Predetermined criteria make the conclusion reviewable.

### Document The Retest So It Supports The Closure Decision

The retest documentation must allow a reviewer to see what was tested, how, with what result, and how that supports the closure or reliance decision.

Document:

- the control or action retested and its link to the original finding;
- the population, period, sample, and selection method;
- the procedures performed and the evidence examined;
- the result, including any exceptions and their evaluation;
- the conclusion on whether remediation is confirmed;
- the basis for closure or for keeping the finding open.

A closure decision that rests on an undocumented or vague retest cannot be defended. The retest record is the foundation of the closure.

## Common Traps

### Retesting Only Immediately After Implementation

A point-in-time retest proves the control can work, not that it operates over time. Retest across an appropriate period, especially for manual controls.

### Retesting A Narrower Scope Than The Original Finding

A retest confined to a subset can pass while the broader risk remains. Match the retest's reach to the finding's reach.

### Applying Automated Logic To Manual Controls

This overstates the evidence, because manual operation varies. Use manual-control retest logic for manual controls.

### Relying On Management Testing Without Assessment

Accepting management's self-assessment as verification inherits its weaknesses. Assess competence, objectivity, and sufficiency before relying.

### Uniform Retest Extent

The same extent for every finding misallocates effort. Calibrate to risk and document the rationale.

### Undefined Pass And Fail Criteria

"No significant issues" without criteria is not a defensible conclusion. Define what constitutes pass and fail in advance.

### Retest Documentation That Cannot Support Closure

A vague retest record cannot defend the closure decision. Document procedures, evidence, result, and conclusion specifically.

### Closing On Implementation Evidence For An Operating Effectiveness Finding

If the original finding was about operation, implementation evidence alone does not confirm remediation. Retest for operating effectiveness.

## Self-Check

- Is the retest designed around a specific question about whether the corrective action operates effectively, with population, period, attribute, and pass or fail criteria defined?
- Does the retest scope match the original finding's nature, population, and control objective, rather than a narrower subset?
- Is the retest logic appropriate to the control type, with multiple points in time for manual controls and configuration plus IT general controls for automated controls?
- Is the choice between independent retest and reliance on management testing based on an assessment of competence, objectivity, and sufficiency, with independent retest for higher-risk findings?
- Is the retest extent calibrated to the risk of the finding and the consequences of failed remediation, with the rationale documented?
- Are pass and fail criteria defined before retesting, so the conclusion is not subjective after the fact?
- Does the retest documentation specify what was tested, how, with what result, and how it supports the closure or reliance decision?
- Would an independent reviewer agree that the retest evidence is sufficient to confirm remediation or to justify keeping the finding open?
