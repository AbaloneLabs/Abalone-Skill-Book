# Status Tracking

The remediation tracker is the instrument by which an audit function knows whether its findings led to change. It is also, in practice, where remediation goes to die quietly. Trackers collapse into a binary open or closed that conceals the real states: actions partially done, overdue without credible plan, abandoned and replaced, disputed between auditor and management. Items linger in "in progress" for years because no one reviews them. Management reports status that the auditor accepts without evidence, and the tracker records optimism rather than reality. The skill is in maintaining a tracker that reflects the actual state of each finding, that distinguishes the meaningful statuses, that flags aging and overdue items for escalation, and that drives closure only on verified evidence. A tracker that reflects reality lets governance see whether remediation is working; one that reflects hope conceals failure until a problem recurs.

Use this skill when maintaining a remediation or open-issue tracker, recording and updating status, managing aging items, handling partial or disputed remediation, or escalating non-remediation and recurrence. The goal is a tracker that accurately reflects the state of each finding and drives remediation to verified closure.

## Core Rules

### Track Meaningful Statuses, Not A Binary Open Or Closed

A binary open or closed status hides the real states findings pass through. Remediation is frequently partial, overdue, disputed, or abandoned, and the tracker must capture these distinctions to reflect reality.

Track statuses such as:

- open, not yet started: agreed action exists but no implementation;
- in progress: implementation underway, with milestones and dates;
- implemented, not verified: action claims complete but not yet retested;
- partially implemented: some elements done, others outstanding;
- verified and closed: implementation confirmed through retest;
- overdue: committed date passed without credible completion;
- disputed: management and auditor disagree on the finding or its closure;
- abandoned or replaced: original action dropped, alternative to be assessed.

Collapsing these into open or closed conceals stale, failed, or contested remediation and prevents effective governance oversight.

### Record The Basis For Each Status

A status without its basis is an assertion. Each status entry should record what supports it, so a reviewer or successor can see why the finding is where it is.

Record for each status:

- the agreed action and owner;
- the implementation evidence reviewed, if any;
- the verification performed and its result, if any;
- the committed and actual dates;
- the reason for any delay or change in approach;
- the source of the status, management report or auditor verification.

A tracker that says "in progress" without milestones, dates, or evidence is not tracking; it is recording hope.

### Manage Aging And Overdue Items Actively

Items that sit in "in progress" indefinitely are a common failure. The tracker must flag aging and overdue items and drive them toward resolution or escalation.

Manage aging by:

- recording the original committed date for each action;
- flagging items that pass their committed date without credible completion;
- requiring a revised plan with a new date for overdue items, not indefinite extension;
- scheduling periodic status reviews of the entire tracker, not only when a report is due;
- escalating items that miss milestones repeatedly or without justification.

A tracker that is never reviewed becomes a graveyard. Periodic review and escalation of aging items are what keep remediation moving.

### Handle Partial Remediation Explicitly

Remediation is often partial: some actions done, some late, some replaced. The tracker must reflect partial states honestly rather than forcing a binary judgment.

Handle partial remediation by:

- recording which elements of the action plan are complete and which remain;
- keeping the finding open with the outstanding elements documented;
- assessing whether the completed elements reduce risk pending full remediation;
- setting dates for the remaining elements and tracking them.

A finding with three agreed actions, one done and two overdue, is not closed or cleanly open; it is partially remediated, and the tracker must show that so governance understands the residual risk.

### Handle Disputed Remediation And Findings

Sometimes management disputes the finding, the severity, or whether remediation is sufficient. The tracker must capture the dispute and the auditor's position, not silently adopt management's view.

Handle disputes by:

- recording the nature of the disagreement;
- documenting the auditor's position and its basis;
- noting whether management provided testable counter-evidence or only assertion;
- keeping the finding open or at its original severity until the dispute is resolved on evidence;
- escalating significant disputes to governance.

A dispute does not close a finding. It keeps it open, with the disagreement visible, until evidence resolves it.

### Escalate Non-Remediation And Recurrence

When management fails to remediate within a reasonable time, or when the same issue recurs after claimed remediation, escalation is required. This is a governance matter, not a clerical update.

Escalate when:

- a high-risk finding remains open beyond its committed date without a credible revised plan;
- the same root cause appears in a new finding, signaling failed prior remediation;
- management disputes the finding but provides no testable counter-evidence;
- remediation evidence is missing, contradictory, or appears fabricated;
- a control verified as remediated later fails in subsequent testing.

Escalation paths include the audit committee, board, regulator, or executive sponsor. Document the escalation, the recipient, and the response.

### Drive Closure Only On Verified Evidence

Closure is a decision that the risk has been reduced, and it must rest on verified evidence, not on management's assertion or the passage of time. The tracker should record the verification that supports each closure.

Drive verified closure by:

- requiring verification evidence before moving a finding to closed;
- recording the verification procedure, sample, period, and result;
- noting the residual risk after remediation;
- identifying the closure approver and date;
- retaining the closed finding for trend and recurrence analysis.

Closure on self-report or convenience produces trackers that look clean while the underlying risks persist. Verified closure is what makes the tracker trustworthy.

### Report Tracker Reality To Governance

The tracker's value depends on governance seeing its actual state. Reporting a sanitized version that hides overdue, partial, or disputed items defeats oversight.

Report to governance:

- the number and risk of open findings, by status and age;
- overdue items and the credibility of revised plans;
- partial and disputed remediation, with residual risk;
- recurrence of prior findings, which signals failed remediation;
- trends in remediation timeliness and verification rates.

An honest tracker report lets governance judge whether management's remediation is effective and where intervention is needed.

## Common Traps

### Binary Open Or Closed Status

This hides partial, overdue, disputed, and abandoned states. Track meaningful statuses.

### Status Without Basis

"In progress" without milestones, dates, or evidence records hope, not reality. Record the basis for each status.

### Unreviewed Trackers Becoming Graveyards

Items that sit indefinitely without review never close. Schedule periodic reviews and flag aging items.

### Forcing Partial Remediation Into A Binary Judgment

Partial remediation has residual risk that must be visible. Record which elements are done and which remain.

### Silently Adopting Management's View In Disputes

A dispute does not close a finding. Record the disagreement and the auditor's position.

### Failing To Escalate Non-Remediation And Recurrence

Stale or recurring issues require governance attention. Escalate rather than absorb.

### Closing On Self-Report Or Convenience

Closure must rest on verified evidence. Trackers that close on assertion conceal persistent risk.

### Sanitized Governance Reporting

Hiding overdue and disputed items defeats oversight. Report the tracker's actual state.

## Self-Check

- Does the tracker capture meaningful statuses, including partial, overdue, disputed, and abandoned, rather than a binary open or closed?
- Does each status entry record its basis, including agreed action, evidence reviewed, verification result, dates, and source?
- Are aging and overdue items flagged, reviewed periodically, and required to provide credible revised plans?
- Is partial remediation tracked explicitly, with completed and outstanding elements and residual risk visible?
- Are disputes between management and the auditor recorded, with the auditor's position and basis documented?
- Are non-remediation and recurrence escalated to governance, with the escalation and response documented?
- Is closure driven only by verified evidence, with the verification procedure, result, residual risk, and approver recorded?
- Does governance reporting reflect the tracker's actual state, including open counts by risk and age, overdue items, disputes, and recurrence?
- Are closed findings retained for trend and recurrence analysis rather than deleted?
