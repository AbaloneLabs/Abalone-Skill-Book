# Opinion Formulation

The opinion or conclusion is the single most consequential sentence in an audit. It is what users rely upon, what regulators scrutinize, and what the engagement is ultimately judged by. Formulating it is not a matter of selecting a template; it is the culmination of all the evidence, requiring the auditor to decide whether the evidence supports an unmodified conclusion, whether a misstatement or limitation is material enough to require modification, and if so, what form the modification should take. The categories, unmodified, qualified, adverse, and disclaimer, are not interchangeable shades of severity. Each carries a specific meaning, and choosing the wrong one, such as a qualification where an adverse opinion is required, or a disclaimer where a qualification would do, misleads users about the seriousness of the matter. The discipline of opinion formulation is to let the evidence determine the category and to word the conclusion so that it means exactly what the evidence supports.

Use this skill when formulating an audit opinion or assurance conclusion, deciding whether to modify, choosing the type of modification, drafting opinion language, or ensuring the conclusion matches the evidence and assurance level. The goal is a conclusion that is correct in category, precise in language, and defensible against the work performed.

## Core Rules

### Let The Evidence Determine The Opinion Category

The opinion must follow from the evidence, not from a desired outcome or from management's preference. The auditor's first task is to determine, from the accumulated evidence, which category of conclusion the engagement supports.

Determine the category by assessing:

- whether sufficient appropriate evidence was obtained to support a conclusion;
- whether the subject matter is free from material misstatement, or conforms to the criteria;
- whether any misstatement or deviation is material, and if so whether it is pervasive;
- whether any scope limitation is material, and if so whether it is pervasive;
- whether the engagement provides reasonable or limited assurance.

The category flows from these assessments. An auditor who has decided the category before completing the evidence has reversed the proper order.

### Understand When Each Modification Is Required

The four opinion categories respond to different conditions, and confusing them produces a conclusion that misrepresents the engagement's findings.

Unmodified, or unqualified:

- sufficient appropriate evidence was obtained;
- the subject matter is free from material misstatement;
- no material scope limitation exists.

Qualified:

- a material but not pervasive misstatement exists, or a material but not pervasive scope limitation exists;
- the conclusion states that, except for the effects of the matter, the subject matter is fairly stated.

Adverse:

- a material and pervasive misstatement exists;
- pervasiveness means the misstatement affects so many items or is so fundamental that a qualification is inadequate.

Disclaimer:

- a material and pervasive scope limitation exists, or sufficient appropriate evidence could not be obtained;
- pervasiveness means the possible effects are so widespread that a qualification is inadequate.

The distinction between material and pervasive is the hinge between qualified and the more severe categories. A matter that is material but confined supports a qualification; a matter that is pervasive requires adverse or disclaimer.

### Apply The Material Versus Pervasive Distinction Correctly

The most common error in opinion formulation is misjudging pervasiveness, either overstating it to justify an adverse opinion or understating it to avoid one. Pervasiveness is a high bar and must be assessed against the evidence.

A matter is pervasive when:

- it affects a large portion of the subject matter, not a single line item;
- it is fundamental to the user's understanding, such as overall presentation or going concern;
- a qualification would not adequately inform users of the seriousness;
- it undermines the reliability of the subject matter as a whole.

A matter confined to one account, one process, or one disclosure, even if material, is generally not pervasive and supports a qualification. Reserve adverse and disclaimer for matters that genuinely permeate the subject matter.

### Match Opinion Language To The Assurance Level

The wording of the conclusion must reflect the level of assurance the engagement provides. Reasonable and limited assurance use different language, and using reasonable-assurance wording for limited-assurance work overstates the result.

Match language by:

- using opinion language, such as "present fairly, in all material respects," for reasonable-assurance engagements that support an opinion;
- using conclusion language appropriate to limited assurance, such as "nothing has come to our attention," reflecting the reduced level;
- for agreed-upon procedures, presenting findings of procedures performed without an opinion or conclusion;
- naming the subject matter, the criteria, and the framework in the conclusion.

An opinion implies a level of work and assurance. If the engagement did not provide that level, the language must not imply it did.

### Draft The Basis For Modification Clearly

When the opinion is modified, the report must include a clear basis for the modification, separate from the opinion itself. This basis lets the reader understand what drove the modification and how serious it is.

The basis for modification should:

- describe the matter causing the modification, in specific terms;
- quantify the financial effects where possible, or explain why they cannot be quantified;
- explain why the matter is material, or material and pervasive;
- for a scope limitation, describe the procedures that could not be performed and why.

The basis and the opinion must be consistent. A modification without a clear basis, or a basis that does not support the category chosen, undermines the report's credibility.

### Handle Scope Limitations Differently From Misstatements

Scope limitations and misstatements lead to different modifications, and the auditor must keep the reasoning distinct. A misstatement is a matter of substance; a scope limitation is a matter of evidence.

For a material misstatement:

- the opinion is qualified or adverse, depending on pervasiveness;
- the basis describes the misstatement and its effects.

For a material scope limitation:

- the opinion is qualified or a disclaimer, depending on pervasiveness;
- the basis describes the procedures that could not be performed and the possible effects.

A disclaimer for a scope limitation is not the same as an adverse opinion for a misstatement, even though both are severe. The auditor must be clear about which condition exists.

### Reconcile The Opinion To The Engagement's Purpose And Users

The opinion must be appropriate for the engagement's purpose, the applicable framework, and the intended users. An opinion drafted for one context may be misleading in another.

Reconcile by:

- confirming the framework applied is acceptable and consistently applied;
- ensuring the opinion addresses the subject matter the engagement covered;
- considering whether emphasis-of-matter or other-matter paragraphs are needed to draw attention to disclosed matters without modifying the opinion;
- confirming the opinion is addressed to the intended users and reflects any restriction on use.

An opinion that drifts from the engagement's purpose, or that implies a broader conclusion than the engagement supports, is not defensible.

## Common Traps

### Deciding The Category Before Completing The Evidence

Choosing the opinion early and fitting the evidence to it reverses the proper order. Let the evidence determine the category.

### Misjudging Pervasiveness

Overstating pervasiveness to justify an adverse opinion, or understating it to avoid one, misleads users. Reserve adverse and disclaimer for genuinely pervasive matters.

### Using Reasonable-Assurance Language For Limited-Assurance Work

This overstates the result. Match the wording to the assurance level the engagement provides.

### Qualifying When An Adverse Opinion Is Required

A qualification for a pervasive misstatement understates the seriousness. If the matter is pervasive, an adverse opinion is required.

### Disclaiming When A Qualification Would Do

A disclaimer for a material but confined scope limitation overstates the limitation. Match the modification to the materiality and pervasiveness.

### A Modification Without A Clear Basis

A modified opinion must be accompanied by a basis that explains the matter and why it warrants the category chosen. A modification without a basis is indefensible.

### Confusing Scope Limitation With Misstatement

The two lead to different modifications. Be clear about whether the issue is a matter of substance or a matter of evidence.

### Implying Opinion For Non-Opinion Work

Agreed-upon procedures and readiness assessments should not use opinion language. Present findings, not an opinion.

## Self-Check

- Is the opinion category determined by the evidence, including evidence sufficiency, material misstatement, and scope limitation, rather than by a desired outcome?
- Is the distinction between material and pervasive applied correctly, with adverse and disclaimer reserved for genuinely pervasive matters?
- Does the opinion language match the assurance level, with opinion wording for reasonable assurance and appropriate conclusion wording for limited assurance?
- For a modified opinion, is there a clear basis describing the matter, its effects, and why it is material or pervasive?
- Is a scope limitation distinguished from a misstatement, leading to the correct modification type?
- Is the opinion reconciled to the engagement's purpose, framework, intended users, and any restriction on use?
- Are emphasis-of-matter or other-matter paragraphs used appropriately to draw attention without modifying the opinion?
- Is the opinion drafted so that it means exactly what the evidence supports, no more and no less, and withstands scrutiny from regulators and users?
