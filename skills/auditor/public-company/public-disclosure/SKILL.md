# Public Disclosure

Public company disclosure is governed by a principle of fairness and completeness: material information must reach the market broadly and on time, and selective disclosure to favored analysts or investors is prohibited. Agents often treat disclosure as a filing task and miss the judgment that drives it — whether information is material, whether it has been selectively disclosed, whether a subsequent event must be recognized or only disclosed, and whether forward-looking statements qualify for safe harbor. The harm is an enforcement action for selective disclosure, a market reaction to information that should have been released earlier, or a securities litigation exposure from a misleading forward-looking statement.

Use this skill when evaluating any public company disclosure obligation, when assessing Reg FD compliance, when reviewing earnings releases and investor communications, when determining the materiality of undisclosed information, and when handling subsequent events, related-party, and executive compensation disclosures. The goal is disclosure that is fair, complete, timely, and supported by a reasonable basis.

## Core Rules

### Apply The Reg FD Selective Disclosure Prohibition

Regulation FD prohibits the selective disclosure of material nonpublic information to securities market professionals and certain shareholders. When an issuer intentionally discloses material nonpublic information, it must do so publicly and simultaneously; when it discloses unintentionally, it must make public disclosure promptly. Agents must understand which communications trigger Reg FD and how to structure disclosure to comply.

Apply Reg FD:

- identify in advance which communications (earnings calls, investor meetings, conferences, one-on-ones) could involve material nonpublic information;
- ensure intentional disclosures of material nonpublic information are made publicly and simultaneously (e.g., through a furnished 8-K or widely disseminated release);
- ensure unintentional disclosures are followed by prompt public disclosure;
- brief spokespersons on what is and is not public, and what may be said;
- avoid confirming or denying selectively when analysts inquire about material nonpublic information;
- maintain controls that route investor communications through disclosure review.

### Assess Materiality For Disclosure Timing

Materiality drives whether and when information must be disclosed. Information is material if there is a substantial likelihood that a reasonable investor would consider it important, considering both quantitative and qualitative factors. Agents must assess materiality deliberately, because both over- and under-disclosure carry consequences.

Assess materiality by considering:

- quantitative significance relative to the company's financial position and results;
- qualitative factors (the nature of the information, its effect on trends, its relevance to investor decisions);
- whether the information would alter the total mix of information available;
- the effect on the company's reputation, liquidity, or ability to raise capital;
- pending developments whose disclosure could be misleading in isolation;
- the difference between materiality for financial statement recognition and materiality for separate timely disclosure.

### Structure Earnings Releases With Complete And Balanced Content

Earnings releases must present results fairly and not omit information necessary to make the statements not misleading. Agents frequently focus on the non-GAAP measures and headline results and underweight the required context, the GAAP reconciliation, and the cautionary statements. Review the release for completeness and balance before issuance.

Confirm the release includes:

- the GAAP results, presented with at least equal prominence to non-GAAP measures;
- complete reconciliations of any non-GAAP measures to the most directly comparable GAAP measure;
- the reasons management believes the non-GAAP measures are useful;
- cautionary statements for forward-looking information, with the safe harbor legend;
- any material subsequent events or developments known at release;
- consistency between the release and the eventual 10-K/10-Q filing;
- a clear statement of the reporting period and any changes in basis of presentation.

### Handle Subsequent Events With Recognition Versus Disclosure Logic

Subsequent events must be evaluated through the date the financial statements are issued, and the agent must distinguish those that require adjustment (recognition) from those that require disclosure only. Agents often apply one treatment uniformly, which misstates either the financial statements or the disclosures. Apply the recognition-versus-disclosure logic to each event.

For each subsequent event:

- determine whether it provides additional evidence about conditions that existed at the balance sheet date (recognition event) or reflects conditions arising after (disclosure event);
- recognize recognition events by adjusting the financial statements;
- disclose disclosure events if their non-disclosure would make the statements misleading;
- evaluate the effect of subsequent events on going concern;
- confirm the evaluation extends through the issuance date of the financial statements;
- ensure consistency between the subsequent event disclosure and the financial statement amounts.

### Disclose Related Party Transactions Completely

Related party transactions must be disclosed so investors can understand the nature of the relationships and their financial effect. Agents must ensure the disclosure captures the required elements and that the entity's process for identifying related party transactions is effective. Incomplete related party disclosure is a frequent comment area.

Confirm related party disclosure includes:

- the nature of the relationship and the transaction;
- the dollar amounts of the transactions and amounts due to or from the related party;
- the terms and any unusual or favorable terms;
- the business purpose and the effect on the financial statements;
- transactions with principal shareholders and management;
- guarantees, commitments, and contingencies involving related parties;
- any required separate disclosure of compensation arrangements.

### Disclose Executive Compensation Per The Applicable Rules

Executive compensation disclosure is detailed and prescriptive, covering the summary compensation table, the compensation discussion and analysis, and related tables and narratives. Agents must apply the applicable rules (for IPOs, smaller reporting companies, or large accelerated filers) and ensure the disclosure is internally consistent and reconciles to the underlying arrangements.

Confirm executive compensation disclosure:

- includes the required tables for the applicable filer category;
- reconciles the compensation amounts to the underlying plan records and agreements;
- explains the compensation philosophy and the decisions for the named executive officers;
- discloses related-party transactions with officers and directors;
- addresses performance targets and the level of disclosure required where targets are omitted;
- is consistent across the compensation discussion, the tables, and the notes.

### Use The Forward-Looking Statement Safe Harbor Correctly

Forward-looking statements are protected by a safe harbor if they are identified as forward-looking, accompanied by meaningful cautionary statements, and made in good faith with a reasonable basis. Agents must ensure forward-looking statements are properly identified and accompanied by cautionary statements that address the specific risks, not generic boilerplate. Misuse of the safe harbor removes the protection.

For forward-looking statements:

- identify the statements as forward-looking;
- accompany them with meaningful cautionary statements identifying important risk factors;
- tailor the cautionary statements to the actual forward-looking statements, not generic boilerplate;
- ensure the statements have a reasonable basis and are made in good faith;
- avoid selectively updating forward-looking statements in a way that could mislead;
- confirm the safe harbor legend and the applicable statutory or judicial framework.

### Manage Timely Disclosure Of Material Developments

Beyond periodic filings, issuers must disclose material developments on a timely basis, often through an 8-K, to keep the market informed and avoid a misleading silence. Agents must recognize when silence becomes misleading and when a development crosses the materiality threshold requiring prompt disclosure. The decision is time-sensitive and often made under incomplete information.

Manage timely disclosure by:

- monitoring developments (acquisitions, financings, litigation, regulatory actions, product issues) for materiality as they arise;
- deciding whether a development requires an 8-K or other prompt disclosure;
- recognizing when silence in the face of a rumor or partial leak becomes misleading;
- coordinating disclosure with the disclosure committee, legal, and investor relations;
- ensuring disclosure is made before any selective communication of the same information;
- documenting the materiality assessment and the disclosure decision and timing.

### Maintain Disclosure Controls Over Investor Communications

Disclosure obligations extend beyond periodic filings to all investor communications, conference presentations, website content, and social media. Agents must ensure there are controls that review these communications for material nonpublic information and consistency with filed disclosures. Uncontrolled investor communications are a leading source of Reg FD and selective disclosure issues.

Confirm controls over:

- investor presentations, roadshows, and conference materials;
- website content and social media posts by the company and senior executives;
- scripts and talking points for earnings calls and investor meetings;
- the review and approval process for any new material communication;
- the consistency of all communications with the most recent filings;
- the routing of analyst inquiries through a controlled disclosure function.

## Common Traps

### Selective Disclosure To Favored Analysts

Sharing material nonpublic information with favored analysts or investors before public release is a direct Reg FD violation, regardless of intent.

### Materiality Assessed On Quantity Alone

Materiality has qualitative dimensions that quantitative thresholds miss. A small transaction can be material if it signals a strategic shift or affects investor decisions.

### Earnings Release Overweighting Non-GAAP Measures

Presenting non-GAAP measures more prominently than GAAP, or with incomplete reconciliations, is both a Reg G and a disclosure fairness failure.

### Uniform Subsequent Event Treatment

Applying recognition or disclosure uniformly to all subsequent events misstates either the financial statements or the required disclosures.

### Incomplete Related Party Disclosure

Omitting the nature, amounts, or business purpose of related party transactions is a frequent and avoidable comment area.

### Generic Cautionary Statements

Boilerplate cautionary statements that do not address the specific risks of the forward-looking statements do not provide safe harbor protection.

### Uncontrolled Investor Communications

Social media posts, conference remarks, and website content that bypass disclosure review are a leading source of selective disclosure and consistency issues.

### Inconsistent Compensation Disclosure

Executive compensation tables, narratives, and notes that do not reconcile to the underlying arrangements draw comments and undermine credibility.

### Treating Website And Social Media As Outside Disclosure

Company websites, executive social media, and conference webcasts are disclosure channels. Assuming they are outside the disclosure regime leads to unreviewed material statements and Reg FD exposure.

## Self-Check

- For any communication involving material nonpublic information, has Reg FD been applied, with simultaneous or prompt public disclosure?
- Has materiality for disclosure timing been assessed using both quantitative and qualitative factors, not quantity alone?
- Does the earnings release present GAAP results with at least equal prominence, with complete non-GAAP reconciliations and cautionary statements?
- Has each subsequent event been classified as recognition or disclosure, with the financial statements and disclosures adjusted accordingly?
- Does related party disclosure include the nature, amounts, terms, business purpose, and effect of the transactions?
- Does executive compensation disclosure include the required tables, reconcile to underlying arrangements, and address performance targets?
- Are forward-looking statements identified, accompanied by tailored cautionary statements, and supported by a reasonable basis?
- Are investor presentations, website content, and social media subject to disclosure review for material nonpublic information and consistency?
- Is the evaluation of subsequent events and going concern extended through the issuance date of the financial statements?
- Are all investor communications consistent with the most recent filings and free of selective disclosure?
