# SEC Reporting

SEC reporting is a regime where form, timing, and disclosure substance are all enforced, and a filing that is technically present can still be deficient if it omits required information, misstates a non-GAAP measure, or fails the Reg S-X presentation rules. Agents often approach SEC filings as a financial-statement exercise and underweight the regulatory layer: the MD&A narrative, the risk factors, the critical accounting estimates, the non-GAAP reconciliations, and the XBRL tagging each carry independent compliance obligations. The harm is a comment letter, a restatement, or a disclosure enforcement action that traces back to a gap the financial statements themselves did not reveal.

Use this skill when reviewing or supporting any periodic or current SEC filing, when evaluating financial statement presentation against Reg S-X, when drafting MD&A and related disclosures, and when responding to SEC comments. The goal is a filing that satisfies each applicable form requirement, presents financial statements in conformity with Reg S-X, and supports its narrative disclosures with underlying analysis.

## Core Rules

### Confirm The Correct Form And Its Specific Requirements

Each SEC form (10-K, 10-Q, 8-K, and others) has specific required items, prescribed order, and timing. Agents must begin from the form's requirements, not from a prior filing, because requirements change and because a prior filing may itself have been incomplete. Build a checklist from the current form requirements and the applicable Reg S-K and S-X items.

Confirm for the form:

- the required items and their prescribed order;
- the applicable filing deadline and any acceleration;
- the financial statement requirements under Reg S-X (annual or interim, audited or reviewed);
- the interactive data (XBRL) tagging requirement and the exhibits required;
- the certifications (302/906) and any required consents and reports;
- whether the filer's status (large accelerated, accelerated, smaller reporting, emerging growth) changes any requirement.

### Apply Regulation S-X To Financial Statement Presentation

Regulation S-X governs the form, content, and presentation of financial statements in SEC filings, and it layers requirements on top of GAAP: specific schedules, the basis of consolidation, the treatment of foreign private issuers, and the presentation of interim and annual data. Agents must verify presentation against S-X, not merely against GAAP, because S-X can require more (or different) presentation.

Verify against S-X:

- the required financial statements and periods (annual and interim columns);
- the basis of consolidation and the treatment of variable interest and equity-method entities;
- required schedules (e.g., valuation and qualifying accounts, supplementary financial information);
- the requirements for acquired businesses, pro forma, and segment reporting;
- the rules for parent-only versus consolidated financial statements where applicable;
- compliance with industry-specific guides where relevant (e.g., banking, insurance, oil and gas).

### Make MD&A A Substantive Analysis, Not A Restatement

MD&A must provide a narrative explanation of the financial statements that allows investors to see the company through management's eyes: the drivers of results, liquidity and capital resources, and known trends and uncertainties. Agents frequently produce MD&A that recaps the numbers without explaining why they changed, which fails the purpose and invites comment letters. Build MD&A from the underlying drivers.

Ensure MD&A addresses:

- the results of operations, with a meaningful discussion of why revenues, margins, and expenses changed;
- liquidity and capital resources, including material cash requirements and known trends;
- off-balance sheet arrangements and contractual obligations;
- critical accounting estimates, including the methodology, assumptions, and sensitivity;
- known trends, demands, commitments, events, and uncertainties expected to affect results;
- the comparison to prior periods with enough specificity to be useful;
- forward-looking information supported by a reasonable basis.

### Disclose Critical Accounting Estimates With Real Specificity

Critical accounting estimates are those that are both highly uncertain and material. The disclosure must explain the methodology, the significant assumptions, and the sensitivity to change, not merely name the estimate. Agents often list the estimates without explaining the judgment, which fails the disclosure objective.

For each critical estimate, disclose:

- the nature of the estimate and why it is critical;
- the methodology and the significant assumptions used;
- how the estimate is developed and who reviews it;
- the sensitivity to changes in assumptions, with a reasonable range where practicable;
- the effect of changes in prior-period estimates;
- the relationship to the audited financial statement amounts.

### Draft Risk Factors Tied To The Actual Business

Risk factors must be specific to the company and its industry, not generic boilerplate. SEC review focuses on whether the risks are tailored, material, and current. Agents should draft risk factors from the actual business model, competitive landscape, regulatory environment, and recent events, and update them as circumstances change.

Ensure risk factors:

- are specific to the company, industry, and geography;
- address the most material risks, including those that make the offering or investment speculative;
- cover operational, financial, regulatory, legal, cybersecurity, and market risks;
- are updated for recent acquisitions, new products, regulatory changes, and emerging risks;
- are not so generic that they could apply to any company;
- cross-reference related disclosures (MD&A, notes) where appropriate.

### Apply The Non-GAAP Measures Rules Strictly

Non-GAAP measures are permitted but tightly regulated: they must be reconciled to the most directly comparable GAAP measure, presented with no greater prominence than the GAAP measure, and accompanied by the required explanations. Agents often fail the prominence rule or the reconciliation, both of which draw comments. Apply the rules measure by measure.

For each non-GAAP measure, confirm:

- a reconciliation to the most directly comparable GAAP measure;
- the GAAP measure presented with equal or greater prominence;
- the reasons management believes the measure is useful;
- the method of calculation applied consistently period to period;
- exclusion of individually tailored accounting principles;
- no misleading labels or descriptions;
- the treatment of forward-looking non-GAAP measures and the required reconciliations.

### Apply XBRL Tagging Accurately

Interactive data (XBRL) tagging is a required part of the filing, and errors in tagging are treated as filing deficiencies. Tags must match the financial statement amounts, the taxonomy version must be current, and the block-tagging and detail-tagging requirements must be met. Agents should not treat XBRL as a clerical step.

Confirm XBRL:

- uses the current accepted taxonomy;
- tags face financial statements, notes, schedules, and the applicable narrative disclosures;
- matches the tagged amounts to the as-filed financial statements;
- applies detail tagging where required, not only block tagging;
- includes the required elements for each form;
- is validated and free of errors before submission.

### Manage 8-K Trigger Events And Timing

Form 8-K has a short filing window (generally four business days) for many triggering events, and missing the window is itself a violation. Agents must know which events trigger a filing and act promptly, because the events (material agreements, acquisitions, executive changes, financial restatements, regulation FD disclosures) are time-sensitive and often overlap with other obligations.

Track 8-K triggers including:

- entry into or termination of material definitive agreements;
- completion of acquisitions or dispositions of significant assets;
- bankruptcy or receivership;
- changes in certifying officers or directors;
- changes in accountants or disagreements;
- financial restatements or non-reliance on prior financial statements;
- material impairments, exit or disposal activities, and other cost-reduction events;
- regulation FD disclosures and other events the registrant considers material.

### Manage SEC Comment Letter Responses With Evidence

A comment letter response is a formal record that must be accurate, complete, and supported. Agents must understand each comment, determine the correct response (including any required amendment or supplemental disclosure), and document the basis. Incomplete or evasive responses invite escalation and enforcement.

For each comment:

- understand the staff's concern and the underlying disclosure;
- determine whether a filing amendment, supplemental disclosure, or prospective change is required;
- draft a responsive answer supported by analysis and evidence;
- address every sub-part of the comment;
- confirm consistency between the response and the underlying financial statements and disclosures;
- obtain necessary internal and auditor support before responding;
- consider whether the response creates new disclosure obligations.

## Common Traps

### Starting From Last Year's Filing

Beginning from the prior filing inherits its gaps and misses changed requirements. Start from the current form and regulation requirements each year.

### Treating MD&A As A Numbers Recap

Reciting the change in amounts without explaining the drivers fails the purpose of MD&A and invites comments asking for the real analysis.

### Listing Critical Estimates Without The Judgment

Naming estimates without methodology, assumptions, and sensitivity does not satisfy the disclosure objective.

### Boilerplate Risk Factors

Generic risk factors that could apply to any company draw comments and fail the tailoring requirement.

### Non-GAAP Prominence And Reconciliation Failures

Presenting the non-GAAP measure more prominently than GAAP, or with an incomplete reconciliation, are the most common non-GAAP deficiencies.

### Treating XBRL As Clerical

Tagging errors are filing deficiencies. Leaving XBRL to a clerical step without validation against the financial statements is a frequent failure.

### Evasive Comment Letter Responses

Partial or unsupported responses escalate the review. Each comment must be addressed fully and with evidence.

### Ignoring Reg S-X Presentation Layer

Conforming to GAAP does not guarantee conformity with Reg S-X, which can require additional schedules, periods, or presentations.

## Self-Check

- Does the filing use the correct form, with all required items in the prescribed order and filed by the applicable deadline?
- Are the financial statements presented in conformity with Regulation S-X, including required schedules, periods, and industry-specific guidance?
- Does MD&A explain the drivers of results, liquidity, and trends, rather than restating the numbers?
- Does each critical accounting estimate disclosure include methodology, significant assumptions, and sensitivity?
- Are risk factors specific to the company and updated for recent events, not generic boilerplate?
- Is each non-GAAP measure reconciled, presented with no greater prominence than GAAP, and accompanied by the required explanation?
- Is XBRL tagging applied with the current taxonomy, validated, and reconciled to the as-filed financial statements?
- Are the required certifications, consents, and exhibits included and accurate?
- For any comment letter, is each comment addressed fully, with evidence, and consistent with the underlying disclosures?
- Has the filer's status (accelerated, smaller reporting, emerging growth) been confirmed and its effect on requirements applied?
