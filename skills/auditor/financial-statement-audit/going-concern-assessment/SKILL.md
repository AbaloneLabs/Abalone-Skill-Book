# Going Concern Assessment

The going concern assumption, that the entity will continue to operate for the reasonable period ahead, underlies the preparation of financial statements on a going concern basis. When conditions or events raise substantial doubt about the entity's ability to continue, the auditor must evaluate management's assessment, perform procedures to address the uncertainty, evaluate the adequacy of disclosures, and determine the appropriate reporting treatment. The recurring failure is treating going concern as a binary check at the end of the audit, or accepting management's forecast at face value without evaluating its assumptions and the feasibility of mitigation plans. Going concern is a forward-looking, judgment-intensive assessment that requires structured procedures, skeptical evaluation of forecasts and mitigation, and careful calibration of the reporting response. Getting it wrong, either missing real going concern doubt or overstating doubt that has been alleviated, produces a misleading audit report.

Use this skill when evaluating going concern, when assessing management's evaluation and forecasts, and when determining the reporting treatment for going concern uncertainty. The goal is a going concern assessment supported by procedures and evidence, with reporting calibrated to the actual level of doubt.

## Core Rules

### Identify Conditions And Events That Raise Substantial Doubt

Going concern evaluation begins with identifying conditions and events that may raise substantial doubt about the entity's ability to continue. These indicators are both financial and operational, and they may arise throughout the audit, not only at the end.

Identify by:

- reviewing financial indicators, such as negative trends, recurring losses, working capital deficiencies, and inability to meet obligations;
- considering operational indicators, such as loss of a key customer or supplier, labor issues, or regulatory action;
- evaluating the entity's access to financing and the status of debt covenants;
- reviewing management's own evaluation, including any disclosed doubts;
- identifying subsequent events that bear on going concern.

The identification must be active and ongoing, not a single end-of-audit check.

### Evaluate Management's Going Concern Assessment

Management is responsible for evaluating the entity's ability to continue as a going concern. The auditor's first layer of procedures evaluates management's assessment, including its process, its assumptions, and its conclusions.

Evaluate by:

- understanding management's evaluation process, including the period covered and the data used;
- assessing the reasonableness of management's assumptions, particularly about future cash flows and financing;
- evaluating the conclusions management reached and the consistency with disclosed conditions;
- considering whether management's evaluation is adequately supported and free from undue optimism.

Accepting management's assessment without evaluating its process and assumptions fails the auditor's responsibility.

### Perform Procedures To Address The Uncertainty

Where conditions raise substantial doubt, the auditor must perform additional procedures to address the uncertainty. These procedures focus on the feasibility of management's plans and the adequacy of disclosure.

Perform by:

- reviewing the entity's cash flow forecasts and comparing them to historical accuracy and current conditions;
- evaluating the feasibility of management's mitigation plans, such as asset sales, debt restructuring, or new financing;
- testing the support for forecast assumptions, including the status of financing arrangements and covenant compliance;
- reading minutes, contracts, and correspondence for indications of financing or restructuring activity;
- considering the effect of subsequent events on the going concern assessment.

Procedures must address both the forecast and the feasibility of mitigation, not merely confirm the forecast's existence.

### Evaluate The Feasibility Of Management's Mitigation Plans

Management's plans to alleviate going concern doubt, such as disposing of assets, restructuring debt, or obtaining new financing, must be evaluated for feasibility. A plan that is merely intended, without concrete steps and support, does not alleviate doubt.

Evaluate by:

- assessing whether the plan has been specifically articulated, not merely stated as an intention;
- evaluating whether the plan's components are feasible, given market conditions and the entity's circumstances;
- testing the support for the plan, such as executed agreements, commitments from lenders, or identified buyers;
- considering the timing of the plan's effect relative to the reasonable period;
- distinguishing plans that are already being implemented from those that remain aspirational.

A plan without concrete support does not alleviate substantial doubt, regardless of management's confidence.

### Evaluate The Adequacy Of Going Concern Disclosures

Where substantial doubt exists, or where conditions have been alleviated but emphasis is warranted, the financial statements must include adequate disclosure. The auditor must evaluate whether the disclosures meet the applicable framework's requirements.

Evaluate by:

- confirming the disclosures describe the conditions and events giving rise to the doubt;
- assessing whether the disclosures address management's evaluation and mitigation plans;
- confirming the disclosures are consistent with the auditor's procedures and conclusions;
- where conditions have been alleviated, confirming the disclosures still draw attention to the conditions where required.

Inadequate disclosure, where required, is itself a basis for qualification.

### Determine The Reporting Treatment

The reporting treatment depends on whether substantial doubt exists, has been alleviated, or remains after considering mitigation. The applicable standards set specific reporting requirements for each outcome.

Determine by:

- where substantial doubt exists and is not alleviated, including an emphasis-of-matter paragraph that draws attention to the going concern disclosure;
- where substantial doubt is alleviated by management's plans, considering whether emphasis is warranted given the conditions;
- where conditions or disclosures are inadequate, qualifying or disclaiming as appropriate;
- following the applicable standard's specific wording and placement requirements for going concern reporting.

Mis-calibrating the reporting treatment, either overstating or understating the doubt, produces a misleading report.

### Consider The Reasonable Period And Its Boundaries

Going concern evaluation covers a reasonable period, typically twelve months from the balance sheet date under many frameworks. The auditor must understand the period that applies and evaluate conditions and mitigation within that boundary.

Consider by:

- confirming the reasonable period under the applicable framework;
- evaluating forecasts and mitigation within that period;
- recognizing that conditions beyond the period may be relevant context but do not, by themselves, drive the going concern conclusion;
- documenting the period applied and the evaluation within it.

Applying the wrong period, or evaluating mitigation outside it, misstates the going concern assessment.

## Common Traps

### Treating Going Concern As An End-Of-Audit Check

Going concern indicators arise throughout the audit. A single end-of-audit check misses them.

### Accepting Management's Forecast At Face Value

Management's forecast must be evaluated for assumptions and feasibility. Accepting it without evaluation fails the responsibility.

### Treating Intended Plans As Alleviating Doubt

Plans without concrete support do not alleviate doubt. Feasibility must be evaluated.

### Missing Inadequate Disclosure

Where disclosure is required, its adequacy must be evaluated. Inadequate disclosure is itself a qualification basis.

### Mis-Calibrating The Reporting Treatment

Overstating or understating doubt produces a misleading report. The treatment must match the actual level of doubt.

### Applying The Wrong Reasonable Period

The period varies by framework. Applying the wrong one misstates the assessment.

### Ignoring Subsequent Events

Subsequent events may affect going concern. Ignoring them leaves the assessment incomplete.

## Self-Check

- Are conditions and events raising substantial doubt actively identified throughout the audit, not only at the end?
- Is management's going concern evaluation assessed for process, assumptions, and conclusions, not merely accepted?
- Are additional procedures performed where doubt exists, addressing both the forecast and the feasibility of mitigation?
- Are management's mitigation plans evaluated for feasibility, with concrete support required rather than intentions?
- Is the adequacy of going concern disclosures evaluated against the applicable framework's requirements?
- Is the reporting treatment calibrated to the actual level of doubt, following the applicable standard's requirements?
- Is the reasonable period under the applicable framework confirmed, with evaluation performed within its boundaries?
- Are subsequent events considered for their effect on the going concern assessment?
- Could an independent reviewer confirm that the going concern assessment is supported by procedures and evidence, with reporting calibrated to the actual doubt?
- Is the application free of treating going concern as an end-of-audit check, accepting forecasts at face value, or treating intended plans as alleviating doubt?
