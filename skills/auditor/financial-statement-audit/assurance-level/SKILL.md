# Assurance Level

Assurance is not a single thing; it comes in levels, and the level determines what the engagement can claim, what procedures are required, and how the conclusion is expressed. The two principal levels are reasonable assurance, provided by an audit, which is a high but not absolute level obtained by reducing risk to an acceptably low level, and limited assurance, provided by a review, which is a meaningful but lower level obtained by reducing risk to a level acceptable in the circumstances but greater than for an audit. Confusing these levels, or claiming more assurance than the engagement provides, is one of the most consequential errors in assurance work. A review conclusion expressed as if it were an audit opinion misleads users into relying on it as if it carried audit-level assurance. An audit that provides less than reasonable assurance, by under-scoping, delivers less than its type promises. The discipline is to know what level the engagement provides, calibrate the work to it, and express the conclusion in terms that match.

Use this skill when determining the assurance level of an engagement, calibrating procedures, drafting conclusions, or evaluating whether expressed assurance matches the work performed. The goal is to ensure the engagement delivers and communicates the assurance it claims, no more and no less.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Assurance Level At Engagement Acceptance

The assurance level is determined by the engagement type and the applicable standard, and it should be identified at the outset so that scope, procedures, and reporting are calibrated to it. An audit provides reasonable assurance; a review provides limited assurance; agreed-upon procedures provide no assurance, only factual findings; a compilation provides no assurance. Each has distinct objectives, procedures, and conclusion forms.

Identify the level by:

- confirming the engagement type and the applicable assurance standard;
- understanding the assurance level that type provides, reasonable, limited, or none;
- communicating the level to the engaging party so expectations align;
- ensuring the engagement terms and the planned work reflect the identified level.

An engagement whose level is unclear at the outset tends to drift, with procedures and conclusions that do not match a consistent assurance basis.

### Calibrate Procedures To The Assurance Level

The depth and extent of procedures must correspond to the assurance level. Reasonable assurance requires sufficient appropriate evidence to reduce risk to an acceptably low level, which demands comprehensive procedures including risk assessment, controls testing where relevant, and substantive procedures on significant areas. Limited assurance requires a combination of inquiry, analytical procedures, and discussion sufficient to reduce risk to a level acceptable for the engagement, which is less than for an audit but still meaningful.

Calibrate by:

- for reasonable assurance, performing the full suite of procedures required to reduce risk to an acceptably low level;
- for limited assurance, performing the inquiry and analytical procedures sufficient to identify matters that may require adjustment, without the depth of an audit;
- for agreed-upon procedures, performing only the specific procedures agreed and reporting factual findings;
- documenting the basis for the procedure mix and its correspondence to the assurance level.

Procedures that are too light for the claimed assurance level under-deliver; procedures that are too heavy for a limited-assurance engagement over-deliver and may blur the engagement type. Match procedures to level.

### Express The Conclusion In Terms That Match The Level

The conclusion must be expressed in language that accurately conveys the assurance level. A reasonable assurance conclusion states a positive conclusion, such as that the subject matter is fairly presented. A limited assurance conclusion states that nothing has come to the practitioner's attention that causes them to believe the subject matter is not fairly presented, a negative or caveat form that signals the lower level. Agreed-upon procedures report findings without a conclusion.

Express appropriately by:

- using positive conclusion language for reasonable assurance;
- using the negative, nothing-has-come-to-our-attention form for limited assurance, which signals the lower assurance;
- avoiding language in a limited assurance report that implies audit-level certainty;
- for agreed-upon procedures, reporting factual findings without an assurance conclusion;
- ensuring the report's wording cannot be read as a higher level than provided.

The conclusion language is the primary signal to users of the assurance level. Imprecise wording, especially in limited assurance reports, can mislead users into over-relying on the conclusion.

### Do Not Claim More Assurance Than The Engagement Provides

The most serious assurance-level failure is claiming more than the engagement delivers. A review presented as if it were an audit, a limited assurance conclusion phrased as a positive opinion, or an agreed-upon procedures report that implies comfort, each overstates what was done and misleads users. The pressure to provide stronger-sounding conclusions, because users want more comfort, must be resisted.

Avoid overclaiming by:

- ensuring the engagement type, procedures, and conclusion language are consistent;
- resisting requests to phrase a limited assurance conclusion more positively to satisfy a user;
- declining to attach an assurance conclusion to agreed-upon procedures findings;
- communicating clearly when a request seeks assurance the engagement type cannot provide;
- recognizing that overclaiming exposes the practitioner to liability when users rely on assurance that was not actually provided.

The assurance level is a constraint on what can be claimed, not a marketing variable. Honesty about the level protects both users and the practitioner.

### Recognize What Each Level Can And Cannot Detect

The assurance level determines the detection capability of the engagement. Reasonable assurance reduces, but does not eliminate, the risk of material misstatement; some misstatements may go undetected. Limited assurance reduces the risk to a level acceptable for the engagement but greater than for an audit; the likelihood that a material matter will be detected is lower than in an audit. Agreed-upon procedures detect only what the specific procedures were designed to find.

Recognize detection limits by:

- communicating in the report that reasonable assurance is not absolute and that some material misstatements may not be detected;
- understanding that limited assurance provides less detection capability and expressing the conclusion accordingly;
- recognizing that agreed-upon procedures findings are limited to the procedures performed and imply nothing about areas not covered;
- calibrating user expectations to what the level can realistically deliver.

Users who understand the limits rely on the conclusion appropriately; those who assume more than the level provides are misled, sometimes catastrophically.

### Align Scope, Materiality, And Assurance Level

Scope and materiality interact with the assurance level. Reasonable assurance engagements typically apply lower materiality thresholds and broader scope than limited assurance engagements, reflecting the greater detection capability required. Misaligned materiality, too high for the assurance level, undermines the level claimed.

Align by:

- setting materiality appropriate to the engagement type and the users' needs;
- recognizing that limited assurance engagements may apply different materiality considerations than audits;
- ensuring the scope covers the areas necessary to support the claimed level;
- documenting the materiality basis and its consistency with the assurance level.

A reasonable assurance engagement with materiality set as if for a review, or vice versa, produces a mismatch between the work and the claimed assurance.

### Handle Requests For Higher Assurance Than The Engagement Provides

Users sometimes request more assurance than the engagement type provides, often because a third party, such as a lender or regulator, wants the comfort of an audit but the entity has commissioned a review. The practitioner must handle these requests honestly, explaining the difference and declining to overstate the assurance.

Handle requests by:

- explaining the assurance level the engagement provides and its limits;
- declining to rephrase or recharacterize the conclusion to imply more;
- offering, where appropriate, to perform additional work to upgrade to a higher assurance engagement, with the corresponding procedures;
- documenting the request and the response.

Overstating assurance to satisfy a request trades honesty for convenience and exposes the practitioner and the user to the consequences of misreliance.

## Common Traps

### Limited Assurance Expressed As Audit-Level

Phrasing a review conclusion positively, or implying audit-level certainty, overstates the assurance. Use the negative form for limited assurance.

### Procedures Too Light For The Claimed Level

Performing review-level procedures in an audit, or minimal procedures in a review, under-delivers the assurance claimed. Calibrate procedures to level.

### Agreed-Upon Procedures With Comfort Language

Attaching assurance conclusions to factual findings overstates what agreed-upon procedures provide. Report findings only.

### Overclaiming Under User Pressure

Rephrasing conclusions to satisfy users who want more comfort misleads. Decline to overstate.

### Materiality Mismatched To Level

Setting materiality too high for the assurance level undermines the claimed assurance. Align materiality with level.

### Blurring Engagement Types

Allowing scope or reporting to drift between audit, review, and agreed-upon procedures creates inconsistency. Keep types distinct.

### Assuming Users Understand The Levels

Many users do not distinguish reasonable from limited assurance. Communicate the level and its limits clearly.

## Self-Check

- Is the assurance level identified at engagement acceptance, consistent with the engagement type and applicable standard, and communicated to the engaging party?
- Are procedures calibrated to the level, with the full suite for reasonable assurance and the inquiry and analytical mix for limited assurance?
- Is the conclusion expressed in language matching the level, positive for reasonable assurance and negative for limited assurance?
- Does the engagement avoid claiming more assurance than it provides, resisting pressure to phrase conclusions more strongly than the level supports?
- Are the detection limits of each level recognized and communicated, so users understand what may and may not be detected?
- Are scope and materiality aligned with the assurance level, without mismatches that undermine the claimed assurance?
- Are requests for higher assurance than the engagement provides handled honestly, with explanation and, where appropriate, an offer to upgrade with corresponding procedures?
- Are engagement types kept distinct, without drift in scope or reporting between audit, review, and agreed-upon procedures?
- Is the assurance level and its basis documented, so a reviewer can confirm the work matches the claimed level?
- Could a user, reading the report, understand precisely what level of assurance was and was not provided?
