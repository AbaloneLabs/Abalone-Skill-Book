# Comparative Figures And Consistency

Comparative figures allow users to compare the current period with the prior period, and that comparison is meaningful only if the figures are presented on a consistent basis. Reclassifications, changes in accounting policy, and restatements all affect consistency, and each must be evaluated, properly accounted for, and adequately disclosed. The recurring failure is treating comparative figures as a passive carry-forward of the prior-period statements, without considering whether reclassifications or policy changes require adjustment, or accepting management's reclassifications without evaluating whether they are appropriate and consistently applied. Consistency is not automatic; it is a judgment that requires the auditor to evaluate whether the comparative figures truly provide a comparable basis and whether the disclosures support the comparison. The reporting implications, including restatement and emphasis, depend on getting this right.

Use this skill when evaluating the consistency of comparative figures, when handling reclassifications and changes in policy, and when determining the reporting implications of adjustments to comparative figures. The goal is comparative figures that provide a genuinely comparable basis and disclosures that support the comparison.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Purpose And Limits Of Consistency

Consistency does not mean that nothing can ever change. Reclassifications, changes in accounting policy, and corrections of error are all legitimate, provided they are properly accounted for and disclosed. The purpose of the consistency evaluation is to ensure that comparative figures provide a comparable basis and that users understand any changes.

Understand that:

- consistency enables meaningful period-to-period comparison;
- changes, including reclassifications, policy changes, and corrections, are permitted but must be properly accounted for and disclosed;
- the auditor must evaluate whether the comparative figures provide a comparable basis, not merely whether they match the prior report;
- the disclosures must allow users to understand the effect of any changes.

Understanding the purpose prevents both demanding false consistency and accepting changes without disclosure.

### Evaluate Reclassifications For Appropriateness

Reclassifications move amounts between line items without changing totals, typically to conform the prior period's presentation to the current period. Reclassifications must be appropriate, consistently applied, and disclosed.

Evaluate by:

- understanding the basis for each reclassification and whether it conforms the presentation;
- assessing whether the reclassification is appropriate and consistently applied;
- confirming that totals, such as net income and equity, are unaffected by reclassifications;
- ensuring the nature and effect of material reclassifications are disclosed;
- where a reclassification is inappropriate, requesting correction.

Accepting reclassifications without evaluation can mislead users about the nature of prior-period amounts.

### Evaluate Changes In Accounting Policy

A change in accounting policy, such as adoption of a new standard or a voluntary change in method, affects the comparability of periods. The change must be accounted for in accordance with the framework and adequately disclosed.

Evaluate by:

- identifying any changes in accounting policy between periods;
- assessing whether the change is justified and applied in accordance with the framework;
- confirming the framework's required accounting, which may include retrospective application with restated comparatives;
- ensuring the disclosures describe the nature of the change, the reasons, and the effect on affected line items;
- where the change is not properly accounted for or disclosed, qualifying as appropriate.

Treating a policy change as a reclassification, or failing to apply the required accounting, misstates the comparatives.

### Evaluate Corrections Of Error

A correction of error addresses a material misstatement in prior-period financial statements that was not detected at the time. Corrections require retrospective restatement, with the comparatives restated as if the error had never occurred.

Evaluate by:

- identifying whether a prior-period error exists and its nature;
- assessing whether the correction is accounted for by retrospective restatement;
- confirming that the comparatives are restated and the error's effect is disclosed;
- considering whether the error indicates a control deficiency or fraud;
- where the correction is not properly accounted for, qualifying as appropriate.

Treating a correction of error as a routine adjustment, without retrospective restatement, misstates the comparatives.

### Evaluate The Adequacy Of Comparative Disclosures

Where changes have occurred, the disclosures must allow users to understand their nature and effect. The auditor must evaluate whether the comparative disclosures are adequate.

Evaluate by:

- confirming the disclosures describe any reclassifications, policy changes, or corrections;
- assessing whether the disclosures quantify the effect on affected line items;
- ensuring the disclosures are clear and allow users to understand the comparison;
- where disclosures are inadequate, requesting enhancement or qualifying as appropriate.

Inadequate disclosure, where changes have occurred, undermines the comparability the figures are meant to provide.

### Determine The Reporting Implications

Where comparative figures are adjusted, restated, or reclassified, the reporting implications must be considered. The report may need to draw attention to the restatement, reference a predecessor, or address a modification.

Determine by:

- where comparatives are restated for a correction of error, considering whether an emphasis-of-matter or other reporting treatment is required;
- where the prior period was audited by a predecessor, determining whether to reference the predecessor;
- where the prior-period opinion requires modification, addressing it in the current report;
- following the applicable standard's specific requirements for reporting on adjusted or restated comparatives.

Mis-handling the reporting implications can leave users without the information they need to understand the changes.

### Confirm Consistency Of Accounting Policies Disclosed

The disclosure of significant accounting policies must be consistent with the presentation, including any changes. The auditor must confirm that the policy disclosures reflect the actual presentation and any changes.

Confirm by:

- reviewing the significant accounting policies disclosure for consistency with the presentation;
- ensuring any changes in policy are described, with the reasons and effect;
- where the disclosure is inconsistent with the presentation, requesting correction;
- documenting the consistency evaluation.

Inconsistent policy disclosures mislead users about the basis of presentation.

## Common Traps

### Treating Comparative Figures As Passive Carry-Forward

Comparative figures must be evaluated for consistency, not merely carried forward. Changes must be addressed.

### Accepting Reclassifications Without Evaluation

Reclassifications must be appropriate, consistent, and disclosed. Accepting them without evaluation can mislead users.

### Confusing Policy Changes With Reclassifications

Policy changes require retrospective application; reclassifications do not. Confusing them misstates the accounting.

### Treating Corrections Of Error As Routine Adjustments

Corrections require retrospective restatement. Treating them as routine adjustments misstates the comparatives.

### Accepting Inadequate Comparative Disclosures

Where changes occur, disclosures must describe their nature and effect. Inadequate disclosure undermines comparability.

### Mis-Handling Reporting Implications

Restatements, predecessor involvement, and modifications have specific reporting requirements. Mis-handling them misleads users.

### Overlooking Policy Disclosure Consistency

The policy disclosure must match the presentation. Inconsistencies mislead users about the basis.

## Self-Check

- Is the purpose of consistency understood, allowing legitimate changes while requiring proper accounting and disclosure?
- Are reclassifications evaluated for appropriateness, consistent application, and disclosure?
- Are changes in accounting policy identified, evaluated for justification and framework compliance, and properly accounted for?
- Are corrections of error identified, accounted for by retrospective restatement, and adequately disclosed?
- Is the adequacy of comparative disclosures evaluated, including the nature and effect of any changes?
- Are the reporting implications of adjusted, restated, or reclassified comparatives determined and applied?
- Is the consistency of the significant accounting policies disclosure with the presentation confirmed?
- Could an independent reviewer confirm that comparative figures provide a genuinely comparable basis and that disclosures support the comparison?
- Is the application free of treating comparatives as carry-forward, accepting reclassifications without evaluation, or confusing policy changes with reclassifications?
- Are corrections of error treated as retrospective restatements, not routine adjustments?
