# Opening Balances And First-Year Engagements

A first-year engagement, or the first audit of an entity after a change in auditors, presents a distinctive challenge: the auditor must obtain sufficient appropriate evidence about opening balances, which were not audited by the current firm and which form the foundation for the current period's results. The recurring failure is treating opening balances as automatically carried forward, or relying on the predecessor's report without evaluating its scope and qualifications, or failing to obtain evidence about opening balances that could contain misstatements affecting the current period. Opening balances matter because errors in them propagate: an overstated opening inventory becomes an overstated cost of sales or retained earnings. The auditor must approach opening balances deliberately, obtaining evidence about their correctness, evaluating the predecessor's work where relevant, and handling the reporting implications, including whether to reference the predecessor, with care.

Use this skill when auditing opening balances in a first-year engagement, when evaluating a predecessor auditor's work, and when determining the reporting treatment for opening balances. The goal is sufficient appropriate evidence about opening balances and a report that correctly reflects the audit's scope and any reliance on a predecessor.

## Core Rules

### Understand Why Opening Balances Matter

Opening balances are the prior period's closing balances, brought forward. They matter because errors in them affect the current period: an overstated opening inventory understates cost of sales when sold; an overstated opening retained earnings overstates equity throughout the period. Even where the current period's transactions are correct, misstated opening balances produce misstated current period results.

Understand that:

- opening balances are the foundation for the current period's results and balances;
- errors in opening balances propagate into the current period, affecting income, equity, and other balances;
- the auditor must obtain evidence about opening balances, not merely accept them as carried forward;
- the scope of the opening balances audit depends on whether the prior period was audited and by whom.

Understanding why opening balances matter prevents treating them as a clerical carry-forward.

### Determine Whether The Prior Period Was Audited

The approach to opening balances depends on whether the prior period was audited, and if so, whether the audit was performed by a predecessor that issued an unmodified report. The approach differs between audited and unaudited prior periods.

Determine by:

- confirming whether the prior period financial statements were audited;
- if audited, identifying the predecessor auditor and obtaining, with client permission, the predecessor's audit documentation;
- if unaudited, recognizing that the auditor must obtain evidence about opening balances through other means.

The determination shapes the procedures and the potential for referencing the predecessor in the report.

### Evaluate The Predecessor Auditor's Work

Where the prior period was audited by a predecessor, the current auditor may be able to rely on that work, but only after evaluating its scope, competence, and independence. Reliance is not automatic.

Evaluate by:

- obtaining client permission and requesting access to the predecessor's audit documentation;
- assessing the predecessor's independence, competence, and the scope of their audit;
- reviewing the predecessor's report, including any modifications or emphasis paragraphs;
- evaluating whether the predecessor's procedures support the opening balances for the relevant assertions;
- where the predecessor's work cannot be relied upon, obtaining independent evidence about the opening balances.

Blind reliance on the predecessor's report, without evaluation, fails the responsibility.

### Obtain Evidence About Opening Balances

Regardless of whether the prior period was audited, the auditor must obtain sufficient appropriate evidence about opening balances. Where the predecessor's work supports some balances, additional procedures may focus on others; where no prior audit exists, the auditor must obtain evidence through current-period and other procedures.

Obtain by:

- performing procedures on the current period's activity to confirm that opening balances roll forward correctly;
- for significant opening balances, obtaining direct evidence through confirmation, inspection, or other procedures;
- reviewing the entity's reconciliation of opening balances to prior audited or unaudited statements;
- evaluating whether opening balances reflect appropriate accounting policies applied consistently;
- identifying any uncorrected misstatements in opening balances and their effect on the current period.

The procedures must provide evidence about the opening balances, not merely confirm their existence.

### Determine Whether To Reference The Predecessor

Where the prior period was audited by a predecessor and the current report covers comparative figures, the current auditor may need to decide whether to reference the predecessor. Referencing indicates that the prior-period opinion was issued by the predecessor, not the current firm.

Determine by:

- following the applicable standard's requirements on predecessor referencing;
- where the prior-period figures are presented as comparative financial statements, deciding whether to reference based on the standard and the circumstances;
- ensuring the reference is clear and does not imply a division of responsibility that the standard does not support;
- where referencing is used, ensuring the report clearly identifies the predecessor's opinion and its date.

Incorrect or unnecessary referencing misleads users about the scope of the current auditor's work.

### Handle Material Misstatements In Opening Balances

Where opening balances contain material misstatements, the auditor must address them, regardless of whether the prior period was audited. The misstatement may require adjustment, disclosure, or reporting treatment.

Handle by:

- determining the nature and materiality of the misstatement;
- requesting that management adjust the opening balances;
- where adjustment is required by the framework and management refuses, qualifying or disclaiming on the affected balances;
- considering the implications for the current period's results and for the opinion;
- documenting the misstatement, the discussions with management, and the resolution.

Ignoring material misstatements in opening balances leaves the current period's results misstated.

### Consider Accounting Policy Consistency

Opening balances must reflect accounting policies applied consistently with the current period. A change in policy between periods, or an inconsistency, must be evaluated and addressed.

Consider by:

- confirming that the accounting policies reflected in opening balances are consistent with the current period;
- where a change in policy occurred, evaluating whether it is properly accounted for and disclosed;
- where an inconsistency exists that is not properly addressed, qualifying as appropriate;
- documenting the consideration of policy consistency.

Inconsistent policies between periods, not properly accounted for, are a basis for qualification.

## Common Traps

### Treating Opening Balances As Automatic Carry-Forward

Opening balances form the foundation for current results. Treating them as clerical carry-forward leaves errors undetected.

### Blindly Relying On The Predecessor's Report

Reliance on the predecessor requires evaluation of scope, competence, and independence. Blind reliance fails the responsibility.

### Failing To Obtain Evidence Where No Prior Audit Exists

Where the prior period was unaudited, the auditor must obtain evidence through other means. Failing to do so leaves opening balances unsupported.

### Incorrect Or Unnecessary Predecessor Referencing

Referencing must follow the standard's requirements. Incorrect referencing misleads users about scope.

### Ignoring Material Misstatements In Opening Balances

Material misstatements in opening balances must be addressed. Ignoring them leaves the current period misstated.

### Overlooking Accounting Policy Consistency

Policies must be consistent across periods. Inconsistencies must be evaluated and addressed.

### Assuming Current-Period Correctness Implies Opening Balance Correctness

Current-period transactions can be correct while opening balances are misstated. Both must be tested.

## Self-Check

- Is it understood why opening balances matter, and that errors in them propagate into the current period?
- Is it determined whether the prior period was audited, and if so, by whom?
- Where a predecessor auditor exists, is their work evaluated for scope, competence, and independence before reliance?
- Is sufficient appropriate evidence obtained about opening balances, through the predecessor's work, current-period procedures, or direct testing?
- Is the decision to reference the predecessor made in accordance with the applicable standard, with clear identification of the predecessor's opinion?
- Where opening balances contain material misstatements, are they addressed through adjustment, disclosure, or reporting treatment?
- Is accounting policy consistency between opening balances and the current period confirmed, with changes properly accounted for?
- Could an independent reviewer confirm that opening balances are supported by evidence and that the report correctly reflects scope and any predecessor reliance?
- Is the application free of treating opening balances as carry-forward, blindly relying on the predecessor, or failing to obtain evidence where no prior audit exists?
- Are material misstatements in opening balances addressed rather than ignored?
