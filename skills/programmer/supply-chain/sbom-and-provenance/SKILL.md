# SBOM And Provenance

An SBOM (Software Bill of Materials) is the inventory of what a software artifact contains — every direct and transitive dependency, with versions and origins — and provenance is the verifiable evidence of how that artifact was built (from what sources, by what build process, with what controls). Together they answer the two questions that matter for supply-chain trust: what is in this artifact, and can I verify how it was made. The judgment problem is that both are easy to generate and easy to ignore: an SBOM that is incomplete (missing transitive dependencies) or stale (generated at the wrong stage, not reflecting the final artifact) gives false confidence; provenance that is generated but never verified proves nothing, because the trust comes from the verification, not the generation. The discipline is to generate the SBOM at the right stage (from the final artifact, including transitive dependencies), to produce provenance that captures the build process with integrity (SLSA attestation, signed and tamper-evident), to verify provenance at promotion gates (so an artifact without verifiable provenance cannot proceed), and to make builds reproducible so that the same inputs produce a bit-identical artifact, which is the strongest provenance check. An SBOM no one consults and provenance no one verifies are paperwork; an SBOM used to answer "are we exposed" and provenance used to gate promotion are supply-chain security.

Agents tend to generate SBOMs and provenance as compliance artifacts and then not use them. The harm appears as vulnerabilities that take days to attribute (because the SBOM was incomplete or stale), as artifacts promoted without verifiable provenance (because generation was enabled but verification was not enforced), and as build processes that are trusted but not reproducible (so there is no way to confirm the artifact matches the source). The judgment is to generate the SBOM from the final artifact with full transitive coverage, to produce and sign provenance that captures the build with integrity, to enforce verification at gates so unprovenanced artifacts cannot proceed, and to pursue reproducible builds as the strongest integrity check. The value of SBOM and provenance is realized when they are queried and enforced, not when they are filed.

## Core Rules

### Generate The SBOM From The Final Artifact With Full Transitive Coverage

An SBOM's value is answering "is this artifact exposed to vulnerability X," and that requires the SBOM to reflect the final artifact, including every transitive dependency. An SBOM generated from the source manifest (before resolution) or missing transitive dependencies will not answer the question.

- **Generate the SBOM from the built artifact, not the declared manifest.** The manifest says what was requested; the artifact contains what was actually resolved and included. The SBOM must reflect the artifact.
- **Include transitive dependencies, not only direct ones.** Most vulnerabilities are in transitive dependencies; an SBOM listing only direct dependencies misses the exposure.
- **Regenerate when the artifact changes.** An SBOM is tied to a specific artifact version; a stale SBOM does not reflect the current artifact and gives false confidence.
- **Make the SBOM queryable and consulted.** An SBOM filed and never queried provides no value; integrate it into the vulnerability-response process so exposure questions are answered from it.

### Produce Provenance That Captures The Build With Integrity

Provenance is the verifiable evidence of how an artifact was built. To be trustworthy, it must be tamper-evident (signed, so alteration is detectable), generated by the build process (not self-reported by the builder), and detailed enough to reason about (source, build parameters, dependencies, build platform).

- **Generate provenance as part of the build, attested by the build platform.** The build platform (CI/CD) is in a position to attest truthfully about what it built; self-attestation by the developer is weaker because it can be falsified.
- **Sign the provenance and the artifact.** Signatures prove the attestation's origin and the artifact's integrity; unsigned provenance can be tampered with undetectably.
- **Capture enough detail to reason about.** The source (commit, repo), the build instructions, the dependencies, the build platform identity — enough to assess whether the artifact is trustworthy and to reproduce it.
- **Use a framework like SLSA to structure the provenance and its controls.** SLSA levels provide a common vocabulary for build integrity (source integrity, build platform trust, tamper resistance); use them to communicate and to set targets.

### Verify Provenance At Promotion Gates

Provenance generation without verification proves nothing; the trust comes from enforcing that only artifacts with verifiable, expected provenance can proceed. Verification belongs at promotion gates (build-to-release, release-to-deploy), so an artifact without provenance, or with provenance that does not match expectations, is blocked.

- **Enforce provenance verification at gates.** A release or deploy pipeline should reject artifacts whose provenance is missing, unsigned, or does not match the expected build (wrong source, wrong builder, unexpected parameters).
- **Verify the signature and the attestation's content.** A valid signature from an unexpected builder, or a valid attestation showing the artifact was built from an unexpected source, should both block.
- **Make verification automatic and blocking, not advisory.** A gate that warns but proceeds provides no security; it must block to enforce trust.
- **Verify transitively where it matters.** For high-assurance artifacts, the provenance of the build platform and its dependencies matters too; the chain of trust extends as far as the assurance requires.

### Pursue Reproducible Builds As The Strongest Integrity Check

A reproducible build is one where the same sources and build environment produce a bit-identical artifact, verified by independent rebuilders. This is the strongest provenance check: if anyone can rebuild the artifact from the published source and get the same bytes, the artifact is proven to correspond to the source, with no opportunity for the build platform to have injected anything.

- **Aim for reproducibility where feasible.** Reproducible builds eliminate the need to trust the build platform, because the output is verifiable by anyone.
- **Eliminate sources of non-determinism** (timestamps, paths, random, build environment differences) that prevent bit-identical reproduction.
- **Use independent rebuilders to verify.** A second party rebuilding from the published source and confirming identical bytes is the verification; a single builder's claim is not.

### Treat SBOM And Provenance As Operational, Not Compliance

The value of SBOM and provenance is realized when they are queried and enforced operationally — when a new vulnerability triggers an SBOM query to find exposed artifacts, when a promotion gate blocks an unprovenanced build, when a reproducible-build check catches a tampered artifact. Treat them as operational tools, and the compliance follows; treat them as compliance, and the operation never benefits.

## Common Traps

### SBOM Missing Transitive Dependencies

An SBOM listing only direct dependencies, missing the transitive dependencies where most vulnerabilities live, so exposure queries return false negatives. Generate from the final artifact with full transitive coverage.

### Stale Or Wrong-Stage SBOM

An SBOM generated from the manifest before resolution, or not regenerated when the artifact changes, not reflecting the final artifact. Generate from the built artifact and regenerate on change.

### Provenance Generated But Never Verified

Provenance attestation enabled but no gate verifies it, so artifacts without or with unexpected provenance proceed anyway. The trust is in the verification; enforce it at promotion gates, blocking and automatic.

### Self-Attested Provenance

Provenance self-reported by the developer rather than attested by the build platform, which can be falsified. Generate provenance as part of the build, attested by the platform.

### Unsigned Provenance Or Artifact

Provenance or artifacts that are not signed, so tampering is undetectable. Sign both the provenance and the artifact.

### Verification That Warns But Does Not Block

A provenance gate that warns on mismatch but proceeds, providing no security. Make verification blocking and automatic.

### Non-Reproducible Builds Trusted Anyway

Build processes that are trusted without reproducibility, so there is no way to confirm the artifact matches the source or to detect build-platform tampering. Pursue reproducible builds and independent verification where assurance matters.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The SBOM is generated from the final built artifact (not the declared manifest), includes full transitive dependency coverage, is regenerated when the artifact changes, and is queryable and integrated into the vulnerability-response process so exposure questions are answered from it.
- [ ] Provenance is generated as part of the build, attested by the build platform (not self-reported by the developer), signed (provenance and artifact), detailed enough to reason about (source, build instructions, dependencies, builder identity), and structured using a framework like SLSA with its controls.
- [ ] Provenance verification is enforced at promotion gates (build-to-release, release-to-deploy): artifacts with missing, unsigned, or mismatched provenance (wrong source, wrong builder, unexpected parameters) are blocked automatically, not warned; and verification extends transitively where assurance requires.
- [ ] Reproducible builds are pursued where feasible: sources of non-determinism are eliminated, and independent rebuilders verify that the same sources produce bit-identical artifacts, eliminating the need to trust the build platform.
- [ ] SBOM and provenance are treated as operational tools (queried on new vulnerabilities, enforced at gates, verified by rebuilds) rather than compliance paperwork, so their value is realized in practice.
- [ ] The highest-risk cases were verified — an SBOM query that found an exposed transitive dependency, a gate that blocked an unprovenanced artifact, a signature that detected tampering, and a reproducible-build check that confirmed source-to-artifact correspondence — not only the clean generation step.
