# Shortage Communication and Formulary Adaptation

A shortage response that is clinically correct at the pharmacist's bench still fails if it is not communicated, because every other clinician in the chain (the prescriber who does not know the drug changed, the nurse who does not know the concentration doubled, the patient who does not know the new bottle looks different) is set up to make an error. Shortage management is largely a communication and systems problem: the substitute must be added to the formulary, the order sets and the electronic health record must reflect the change, the new product's concentration and packaging must be checked for look-alike risk, and the people who order, prepare, administer, and take the drug must all be told what changed and why. The danger is that the pharmacist solves the clinical substitution and assumes the system will catch up, when in fact the system defaults to the old drug, the old concentration, and the old look, and the uncommunicated change becomes a wrong-drug, wrong-concentration, or wrong-rate error. The pharmacist's discipline is to treat communication and formulary adaptation as part of the clinical intervention, not as paperwork that follows it.

## Core Rules

### Communicate the Shortage and the Substitute to Every Affected Party

A shortage that affects ordering, preparation, administration, or counseling must be communicated to each affected party with the information they need. Prescribers need to know what is unavailable, what the substitute is, and any dose or monitoring changes. Nurses need to know the new product, its concentration, its appearance, and any administration change. Patients need to know the new bottle may look different, why it changed, and that the medicine is still theirs. The pharmacist must use the channels that reach each group (alerts, memos, huddles, counseling) and must communicate proactively rather than waiting for confusion to surface at the point of care. A shortage known only to the pharmacy is a latent error for everyone else.

### Update Order Sets, the EHR, and the Formulary Before the Substitute Is Used

The substitute must be built into the systems that generate and display orders before it reaches patients, because systems left on the old drug will reject the new one, display the wrong default dose, or fail to flag the change. The pharmacist must update the formulary, the computerized order entry templates and order sets, the automated dispensing cabinet, the IV room references, the labeling templates, and any decision support so that the substitute appears correctly and the old drug is suppressed or flagged. A substitute dispensed against un-updated systems forces workarounds and invites error, because the clinician sees one thing on screen and another on the shelf.

### Assess Look-Alike, Sound-Alike and Concentration Risks of the New Product

Shortage substitutes often introduce new look-alike or concentration risks that did not exist with the original. The substitute may share a name or packaging with another drug, may come in a concentration different from the institution's standard, or may have a different total volume or vial size that changes the math. The pharmacist must perform a risk assessment of the new product (tall-man lettering, separation in storage, distinct labeling, concentration change alerts), update the labeling and storage to mitigate confusion, and flag the concentration change explicitly wherever the drug appears. A new concentration that is not flagged is a tenfold-error waiting to happen at the point of preparation or administration.

### Coordinate the Shortage Response Across the Pharmacy and Care Teams

A shortage response that lives only in the dispensing pharmacy breaks down at the IV room, the automated cabinet, the nursing unit, and the clinic. The pharmacist must coordinate the response across the teams that touch the drug: the buyers who source the substitute, the IV room that compounds it, the informatics team that updates the systems, the clinical pharmacists who verify and counsel, and the nursing educators who prepare staff. A designated shortage coordinator or huddle ensures that the pieces move together, that no team is working from outdated information, and that the response is consistent across sites. Fragmented coordination produces inconsistent practice and error.

### Time the Switch and Manage the Transition Inventory

The transition from the old drug to the substitute must be timed so that the old product is exhausted or removed before the new one is introduced, preventing both products from being available simultaneously and confused. The pharmacist must manage the transition inventory (run down the old stock, quarantine or return remaining stock, bring in the substitute), align the system updates with the physical switch, and avoid a window in which both concentrations or both products are on the shelf. A mixed inventory during transition is the highest-risk period for wrong-product and wrong-concentration errors.

### Document the Shortage Response and Capture Lessons Learned

Each shortage response should be documented (what was unavailable, what substitute was chosen, what systems and communications were updated, how long the shortage lasted, what problems arose) so the institution builds a record it can learn from. The pharmacist should capture what worked and what failed, feed the lessons into the formulary and shortage-preparedness process, and identify single-source or fragile products that warrant contingency planning. Shortages recur, and an institution that documents its responses is faster and safer the next time.

## Common Traps

### The Uncommunicated Substitution

The pharmacist solves the shortage at the bench but does not tell the prescriber, the nurse, or the patient, and the change surfaces as confusion at the point of care. The mechanism is that the clinical problem was solved, so the work feels complete. The false signal is that the substitute is in the system, so everyone will see it. The harm is that the prescriber continues to order the old drug, the nurse does not recognize the new product, or the patient worries the wrong medicine was dispensed, and the uncommunicated change becomes an error or a loss of trust; communication is part of the intervention.

### The Un-Updated Order Set

The substitute is stocked but the order set and the EHR still default to the old drug, so prescribers continue to order it and the pharmacy must work around the system. The mechanism is that the drug is physically available, so the operational problem feels solved. The false signal is that the substitute is on the shelf, so ordering will follow. The harm is that the system generates orders for a drug that is not available, forces manual workarounds, and may default to the wrong dose or concentration for the substitute; the order entry templates must be updated before the substitute is used.

### The Unflagged Concentration Change

The substitute comes in a different concentration, the change is not flagged in labeling or systems, and a dose is prepared using the old concentration assumption. The mechanism is that the drug name is the same, so the concentration feels the same. The false signal is that the product is the same drug, so the math is unchanged. The harm is that a doubled or halved concentration delivers a proportional overdose or underdose, and the error is invisible until the patient reacts; every concentration change must be explicitly flagged wherever the drug appears.

### The Mixed-Inventory Transition

The old product and the substitute are both on the shelf during the transition, and a dose is pulled from the wrong stock. The mechanism is that both are available, so both are usable. The false signal is that the inventory is being used efficiently, so the transition is fine. The harm is that two concentrations or two products in the same location is the classic setup for a wrong-product error, and the transition window must be managed so the old stock is removed before the new stock is used.

### The Fragmented Response

Each pharmacy area handles the shortage independently, so the IV room, the cabinet, the clinic, and the dispensing pharmacy act on different information. The mechanism is that each area solved its own piece. The false signal is that each area is responding, so the institution is responding. The harm is that the pieces are inconsistent (different substitutes, different concentrations, different communications), and the inconsistency itself becomes an error source; coordination must be centralized so the response is coherent.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The standard shortage communication is delivered, but the unusual case (a high-alert drug requiring double-check revalidation, a pediatric liquid whose concentration change affects every active prescription, a discharge patient whose outpatient pharmacy will not have the substitute) is handled generically. The trap is that the standard path is sound while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the broadcast and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- Did I communicate the shortage and the substitute to every affected party (prescribers, nurses, patients) with the specific information each needs, using channels that reach them proactively?
- Did I update the formulary, order sets, EHR templates, automated cabinets, IV references, and labeling so the substitute appears correctly and the old drug is suppressed or flagged before use?
- Did I perform a look-alike, sound-alike, and concentration risk assessment on the new product and apply mitigation (tall-man lettering, separation, distinct labeling, concentration alerts)?
- Did I coordinate the response across buyers, IV room, informatics, clinical pharmacists, and nursing educators so the pieces move together and are consistent across sites?
- Did I time the switch and manage transition inventory so the old product is removed before the substitute is used, avoiding a mixed-inventory window?
- Did I document the shortage response and capture lessons learned to speed and improve the next response?
- Does the output stay within the agent's scope, deferring final authority, licensed judgment, or specialist sign-off to the qualified person where the question exceeds the agent's competence?
- Is the reasoning documented clearly enough that another practitioner could review the basis and reproduce the conclusion?
