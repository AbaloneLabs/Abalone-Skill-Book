# Therapeutic Substitution During Drug Shortages

A drug shortage forces the pharmacist to do something that normal practice is designed to avoid: change a medication that a prescriber chose, for a patient the pharmacist may not know well, under time pressure, often without the prescriber at the table. A therapeutic substitution is not a simple swap of one name for another; it is a clinical decision that reopens questions the original order had already settled (is the alternative effective for this specific indication, at this dose, in this patient with these allergies and interactions, by this route). The danger is that shortage pressure rewards the fastest substitute, and the fastest substitute is often the one that matches the drug class but ignores the indication-specific efficacy, the dose-equivalence gap, the allergy or interaction the new drug introduces, or the monitoring the new drug requires. The pharmacist's discipline is to treat every shortage substitution as a fresh clinical verification, not a name replacement, and to refuse substitutions that are not clinically equivalent even when the shortage demands an answer.

## Core Rules

### Confirm the Substitution Is Clinically Equivalent for the Specific Indication

Two drugs in the same class are not interchangeable for every indication. A drug may be approved and effective for one indication but not another, and the alternative chosen for a shortage may cover the class's common use while failing the patient's specific indication (for example, a substituted antibiotic that does not reach the infection site, or a substituted antiepileptic that lacks data in the seizure type). The pharmacist must verify the alternative is effective for the patient's documented indication, not merely that it belongs to the same class, and must escalate to the prescriber when the equivalence is uncertain. Class-level similarity is a starting point, not a conclusion.

### Verify Dose Equivalence and Pharmacokinetic Differences

Even when the alternative is effective, the dose is rarely a one-to-one swap. The substitute may have different potency, different bioavailability, different half-life requiring a different interval, different tissue penetration, or different metabolism that matters in organ failure. The pharmacist must determine the equivalent dose of the substitute for the indication, adjust the interval, and confirm the new regimen achieves the intended exposure. A dose carried over by name (the same milligram number on a different drug) is a common source of under- or overdosing during shortages. Renal and hepatic adjustments must be rechecked for the new drug, because the original drug's adjustments do not transfer.

### Re-Screen Allergies, Interactions, and Contraindications for the New Drug

A substitution introduces a new molecule, and the allergy, interaction, and contraindication profile must be re-evaluated for that molecule. The patient may be allergic to the substitute, the substitute may interact with the patient's other medications in a way the original did not, or the substitute may be contraindicated in a comorbidity (for example, a substituted drug with QT prolongation in a patient on other QT drugs, or a beta-lactam cross-reactivity concern). The pharmacist must run the full interaction and allergy screen against the substitute as if it were a new order, not assume that a same-class drug inherits a clean profile. A substitution that introduces a new interaction or allergy is an iatrogenic harm caused by the shortage response.

### Preserve Therapeutic and Monitoring Continuity for Narrow-Index Drugs

For narrow-therapeutic-index drugs (anticonvulsants, warfarin, immunosuppressants, thyroid hormones, some psychotropics), substitution carries special risk because small pharmacokinetic differences can cause loss of efficacy or toxicity, and these patients are often stabilized on a specific product. The pharmacist must treat substitutions in this group with heightened caution, arrange monitoring (drug levels, INR, clinical signs) around the switch, inform the patient and prescriber of the change and its monitoring plan, and avoid repeated back-and-forth switching that destabilizes control. For some narrow-index drugs, substitution should be avoided or undertaken only with prescriber agreement and enhanced monitoring.

### Follow Institutional Policy and Legal Authority for the Substitution

Therapeutic substitution during a shortage is governed by institutional policy, formulary protocols, and state law, which define what substitutions a pharmacist may make independently and which require prescriber notification or approval. The pharmacist must know the scope of authority under the current shortage protocol (which may allow same-class substitution under defined conditions) and must obtain prescriber agreement where the protocol or the law requires it. Even when independent substitution is permitted, the prescriber should be notified, and the substitution and its rationale must be documented in the patient record so the change is visible to the care team.

### Document the Shortage, the Rationale, and the Monitoring Plan

Every shortage substitution must be documented with the specific shortage (the unavailable drug and the reason), the chosen alternative and the rationale (why this drug, this dose, this monitoring), the allergies and interactions rechecked, the prescriber notification or agreement, and the monitoring plan for the switch. This documentation supports continuity of care, allows the team to reverse the substitution when the shortage resolves, and creates the institutional record needed for shortage management and regulatory review. An undocumented substitution is invisible to the team and dangerous when the shortage ends.

## Common Traps

### The Same-Class Equivalence Assumption

The original drug is unavailable, a same-class drug is chosen, and the pharmacist assumes class membership guarantees equivalence. The mechanism is that the drugs share a mechanism and an indication list. The false signal is that the substitute is in the same class, so it must work for this patient. The harm is that the substitute may not cover the specific indication, site, or organism, and the patient fails therapy; equivalence must be verified for the documented indication, not assumed from the class.

### The Carried-Over Dose

The substitute is given at the same milligram dose and interval as the original, without adjusting for potency, bioavailability, or half-life. The mechanism is that the dose number feels transferable. The false signal is that the regimen looks the same, so the exposure must be the same. The harm is that the substitute may be more or less potent, may need a different interval, or may behave differently in organ failure, producing underdosing with treatment failure or overdosing with toxicity; the equivalent dose must be calculated for the new drug.

### The Inherited Clean Profile

Because the original drug had no interaction or allergy issues, the pharmacist assumes the substitute is also clean and does not re-screen. The mechanism is that the verification felt complete for the original order. The false signal is that the patient tolerated the original, so they will tolerate the substitute. The harm is that the substitute is a different molecule with its own interaction and allergy profile, and an unflagged interaction or allergy causes a new adverse event that is blamed on the patient rather than on the unverified substitution.

### The Silent Narrow-Index Switch

A narrow-therapeutic-index drug is substituted silently during a shortage, with no level monitoring and no patient or prescriber notification, and the patient loses control of their disease. The mechanism is that the substitute is "equivalent" on paper. The false signal is that the substitution was made within the class, so control will continue. The harm is that small pharmacokinetic differences in narrow-index drugs cause breakthrough seizures, rejection, thrombosis, or relapse, and the harm is attributed to disease progression rather than to the unmonitored switch.

### The Undocumented Substitution

The substitution is made to keep operations moving and is not documented in the patient record or communicated to the prescriber. The mechanism is that the immediate need was met. The false signal is that the patient received a working medication, so the care was delivered. The harm is that the team does not know the drug changed, monitoring is not arranged, and when the shortage resolves the patient may be switched back abruptly or left on the substitute indefinitely; documentation is what makes a shortage substitution safe and reversible.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The standard substitution protocol is applied, but the unusual case (a pregnant patient, a pediatric patient needing a liquid the substitute lacks, a patient on a complex regimen with multiple interactions, a patient in a trial requiring the specific drug) is handled generically. The trap is that the standard path is sound while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the protocol and never tested the clinical boundary.

## Self-Check

- Did I confirm the chosen alternative is clinically effective for the patient's specific documented indication, not merely that it belongs to the same drug class?
- Did I calculate the equivalent dose and interval for the substitute based on its potency, bioavailability, half-life, and organ-function requirements, rather than carrying over the original milligram number?
- Did I re-run the full allergy, interaction, and contraindication screen against the substitute as if it were a new order?
- For narrow-therapeutic-index drugs, did I arrange enhanced monitoring, notify the patient and prescriber, and avoid destabilizing back-and-forth switching?
- Did I act within the institutional shortage protocol and state-law scope of authority, obtaining prescriber agreement where required and notifying the prescriber in every case?
- Did I document the specific shortage, the alternative chosen, the rationale, the rechecked allergies and interactions, the prescriber notification, and the monitoring plan?
- Does the output stay within the agent's scope, deferring final authority, licensed judgment, or specialist sign-off to the qualified person where the question exceeds the agent's competence?
- Is the reasoning documented clearly enough that another practitioner could review the basis and reproduce the conclusion?
