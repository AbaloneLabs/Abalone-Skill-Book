# Shortage Triage and Clinical Prioritization

When a drug is genuinely scarce, the pharmacist stops being a dispenser and becomes an allocator, and allocation is a moral and clinical act that determines who gets effective treatment and who gets a lesser option or none. The hard part is not finding an alternative; it is deciding, under real scarcity, which patients have the strongest claim on the limited supply, and doing so by a rule that is clinically defensible and equitable rather than by who orders first, who is loudest, or who has the most connections. The danger is that scarcity rewards hoarding and first-come-first-served behavior, which concentrates scarce drug on lower-acuity or early requests while patients with the strongest clinical need (the septic patient, the pediatric patient with no alternative, the patient for whom the substitute is contraindicated) receive nothing. The pharmacist's discipline is to build and apply an explicit prioritization rule based on clinical acuity, evidence of benefit, and the absence of alternatives, to make allocation transparent and reviewable, and to resist both the pressure to hoard and the pressure to dispense on request alone.

## Core Rules

### Define Clinical Priority by Acuity, Evidence, and Lack of Alternatives

A defensible allocation rule ranks patients by three criteria: the acuity of their need (a life-threatening indication outranks a routine one), the strength of evidence that this specific drug is necessary (an indication where the drug is first-line and irreplaceable outranks one where it is one of several options), and the absence of a workable alternative (a patient allergic to or failing the substitutes outranks a patient who can use them). The pharmacist must translate these criteria into a concrete ranking for the scarce drug, so that allocation decisions are made against the rule rather than case by case under pressure. The rule should be written down, agreed with the medical team, and applied consistently.

### Distinguish Irreplaceable From Substitutable Use Before Allocating

Not every use of a scarce drug is equally necessary. Some indications have effective substitutes and some do not; some uses are supported by strong evidence and some by habit. The pharmacist must classify the requests for the scarce drug into irreplaceable uses (no acceptable alternative, strong evidence, high acuity) and substitutable uses (a reasonable alternative exists), and must direct the limited supply to the irreplaceable uses first. A common failure is to allocate proportionally to all requesters regardless of substitutability, which dilutes the scarce drug away from the patients who truly have no other option.

### Prevent Hoarding and Request Inflation

Scarcity triggers hoarding: units order more than they need, request par levels inflated against the shortage, or stockpile "just in case," and the scarce drug disappears into inventories while patients go without. The pharmacist must centralize control of the scarce drug, move from automatic restocking to case-by-case approval, require clinical justification for each request, and audit usage against actual administration. Request inflation is a predictable response to scarcity, and the allocation rule must assume it and counter it through centralized review rather than trusting requested quantities.

### Apply Equity Principles Alongside Clinical Priority

Clinical priority is necessary but not sufficient; allocation must also be equitable, meaning that patients with equal need receive equal access regardless of which unit, clinician, or institution they happen to be under. The pharmacist must guard against systematic advantage (a unit that orders aggressively capturing disproportionate supply, well-connected clinicians getting stock for their patients, larger or wealthier sites outcompeting smaller ones) and must apply the same clinical rule across all requesters. Equity also means that non-clinical factors (insurance status, social standing, ability to advocate) do not determine access, and that the rule is applied consistently enough to be auditable.

### Build the Rule Collaboratively and Communicate It

An allocation rule that the pharmacist imposes unilaterally will be bypassed or resisted; a rule built with the medical team, pharmacy leadership, and where relevant an ethics committee is understood and followed. The pharmacist must convene the relevant stakeholders, agree the prioritization criteria, document the rule, and communicate it to all requesters so that allocation is transparent and predictable. Transparency reduces the pressure on individual pharmacists, because requesters know the rule and can see that decisions follow it, and it creates the shared accountability that shortage management requires.

### Monitor, Adjust, and Reverse as the Shortage Evolves

A shortage is dynamic: supply may worsen, improve, or shift to a different product, and the allocation rule must be reviewed and adjusted as conditions change. The pharmacist must monitor actual supply and demand, track whether the rule is directing drug to the highest-need patients, identify unintended consequences (a rule that starves a legitimate use), and adjust the criteria as the situation evolves. When the shortage resolves, the pharmacist must reverse the contingency measures and restore normal allocation, because crisis rules left in place become inappropriate constraints.

## Common Traps

### First-Come-First-Served Allocation

The scarce drug is allocated to whoever requests it first, in the order requests arrive, without ranking by clinical need. The mechanism is that queue order feels fair and neutral. The false signal is that requests are being filled in order, so the process is equitable. The harm is that early, lower-acuity requests capture the supply before higher-acuity patients are even identified, and the patient with the strongest claim receives nothing because they arrived second; allocation must be by clinical priority, not by arrival time.

### Proportional Distribution That Dilutes Scarce Drug

To avoid conflict, the pharmacist allocates a share of the scarce drug to every requesting unit proportional to their usual use. The mechanism is that proportional sharing feels evenhanded. The false signal is that every unit received some, so the distribution is fair. The harm is that scarce drug is spread across substitutable uses while the irreplaceable, highest-need uses remain under-supplied; allocation must prioritize irreplaceable uses over substitutable ones, even if that means some units receive none.

### Hoarding Disguised as Patient Care

A unit requests large quantities citing patient care, but the quantity exceeds realistic patient need and the excess is held in inventory. The mechanism is that the request is framed clinically. The false signal is that the request is for patients, so it must be legitimate. The harm is that hoarding removes scarce drug from the shared pool, and patients elsewhere with equal or greater need go without; centralized approval and clinical justification per request are required to distinguish real need from hoarding.

### The Loud-Clinician Override

A prominent or persistent clinician secures stock for their patients by repeated request or seniority, bypassing the allocation rule. The mechanism is that seniority and persistence feel like legitimate priority. The false signal is that the clinician is advocating for important patients, so the allocation is justified. The harm is that the rule is undermined, other equally needy patients lose access, and the allocation becomes driven by advocacy rather than by clinical criteria; the rule must be applied consistently regardless of who requests.

### The Static Rule in a Dynamic Shortage

An allocation rule is set at the start of the shortage and never revisited, even as supply and demand shift. The mechanism is that the rule was carefully built, so it feels durable. The false signal is that the rule is in place, so allocation is under control. The harm is that conditions change (supply worsens, a new alternative appears, a patient population emerges that the rule did not anticipate), and the static rule either over-restricts or misallocates; the rule must be monitored and adjusted as the shortage evolves.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The standard allocation rule is applied, but the unusual case (a pediatric patient with no alternative formulation, a pregnant patient where substitutes are contraindicated, a patient in a clinical trial requiring the specific drug) is handled by the generic ranking. The trap is that the standard rule is sound while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the criteria and never tested the clinical boundary.

## Self-Check

- Did I define a written allocation rule based on clinical acuity, strength of evidence for the specific indication, and the absence of workable alternatives, agreed with the medical team?
- Did I classify requests into irreplaceable versus substitutable uses and direct limited supply to irreplaceable uses first, rather than distributing proportionally to all requesters?
- Did I centralize control of the scarce drug, require clinical justification per request, and audit usage to prevent hoarding and request inflation?
- Did I apply the rule equitably across all requesters, guarding against advantage from unit, clinician seniority, site size, or non-clinical factors?
- Did I build the rule collaboratively with stakeholders and communicate it transparently so allocation decisions are predictable and auditable?
- Did I monitor supply and demand, adjust the rule as the shortage evolves, and reverse contingency measures when the shortage resolves?
- Does the output stay within the agent's scope, deferring final authority, licensed judgment, or specialist sign-off to the qualified person where the question exceeds the agent's competence?
- Is the reasoning documented clearly enough that another practitioner could review the basis and reproduce the conclusion?
