# Type 2 Diabetes Oral and Injectable Agent Selection

Non-insulin agent selection in type 2 diabetes is no longer a simple stepwise ladder of metformin then sulfonylurea then basal insulin. The modern decision is dominated by patient-specific cardiorenal risk and weight considerations that change which drug class is first-line for which patient. The pharmacist's judgment problem is that the number of agents is large, the evidence base is heterogeneous, the comorbidity-driven contraindications are dense, and the cost and access barriers are real. A default of "metformin for everyone, then add something" is no longer defensible, because a patient with established atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease, heart failure, or chronic kidney disease derives mortality and hospitalization benefit from specific classes regardless of A1c. The pharmacist must integrate glycemic efficacy with cardiorenal protection, hypoglycemia risk, weight effects, tolerability, renal dosing, and access before recommending a regimen.

## Core Rules

### Anchor Selection to Cardiorenal Comorbidity First, Then Glycemic Target

Before considering A1c reduction, classify the patient's cardiorenal status: established atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease (ASCVD) or high risk, heart failure (HFrEF or HFpEF), and chronic kidney disease with albuminuria or reduced eGFR. Patients with ASCVD or high ASCVD risk should receive a GLP-1 receptor agonist with proven cardiovascular benefit or an SGLT2 inhibitor with proven cardiovascular benefit as an add-on to or instead of later agents. Patients with heart failure should receive an SGLT2 inhibitor (proven to reduce heart failure hospitalization) and should generally avoid thiazolidinediones, which cause fluid retention and worsen heart failure. Patients with CKD and albuminuria should receive an SGLT2 inhibitor with proven renal benefit when eGFR permits; GLP-1 receptor agonists are an alternative or addition. These benefits are independent of glycemic control and should drive selection before the A1c target.

### Keep Metformin as First-Line When Tolerated and Not Contraindicated

Metformin remains first-line for most patients because of glycemic efficacy, weight neutrality or modest loss, low hypoglycemia risk, low cost, and long safety experience. Confirm no contraindication: eGFR below 30 mL/min is a contraindication, and the dose should be reduced at eGFR 30 to 45. Acute kidney injury, contrast procedures with holding protocols, unstable heart failure with hypoperfusion, and significant hepatic impairment increase lactic acidosis risk and warrant caution or hold. Counsel on gastrointestinal tolerability by starting low and titrating, and use extended-release for patients who cannot tolerate immediate-release. Metformin does not cause hypoglycemia alone, which is a major advantage in older adults.

### Prefer GLP-1 Receptor Agonists Over Insulin When Injectable Therapy Is Needed

When an injectable is indicated and insulin is not yet required, a GLP-1 receptor agonist is generally preferred over basal insulin because of greater A1c reduction, weight loss rather than weight gain, lower hypoglycemia risk, and proven cardiovascular and renal benefit for specific agents. Select an agent with proven cardiorenal benefit when the patient has those comorbidities. Counsel on the titration schedule to manage nausea, the injection technique, the storage requirements, and the contraindications (personal or family history of medullary thyroid carcinoma or MEN 2). Recognize that GLP-1 receptor agonists slow gastric emptying and can affect absorption of oral medications, and that they should be held before procedures if gastroparesis is a concern.

### Reserve Sulfonylureas and TZDs for Specific, Justified Situations

Sulfonylureas (glipizide, glimepiride, glyburide) are inexpensive and effective but carry higher hypoglycemia risk, weight gain, and in the case of glyburide, substantial risk in renal impairment and older adults. Reserve them for patients with cost barriers who need glycemic efficacy and who can tolerate the hypoglycemia risk; prefer glipizide in older adults and renal impairment. Thiazolidinediones (pioglitazone) are effective and have possible cardiovascular benefit but cause fluid retention, weight gain, bone fracture risk, and are contraindicated in heart failure and active bladder cancer. Use them selectively and with clear justification. DPP-4 inhibitors are weight-neutral and well tolerated but have modest efficacy and no proven cardiorenal advantage; avoid saxagliptin and alogliptin in heart failure due to heart failure hospitalization risk.

### Adjust Aggressively for Renal Function

Renal function changes both the safety and the dose of nearly every agent. Metformin dose is reduced or held below eGFR thresholds. Sulfonylureas, especially glyburide, accumulate and cause hypoglycemia and require avoidance or dose reduction. Most GLP-1 receptor agonists need no adjustment but some have renal thresholds; SGLT2 inhibitors have eGFR thresholds for initiation and continued use that vary by agent and indication. DPP-4 inhibitors require dose reduction by agent-specific rules. The pharmacist must check the current eGFR before every recommendation and must recognize that acute kidney injury changes the picture immediately, often requiring temporary cessation of metformin and sulfonylureas.

### Reconcile Glycemic Target, Weight Goal, and Cost and Access

A lower A1c target is appropriate for younger, healthier patients; a relaxed target is appropriate for older adults, those with limited life expectancy, and those with hypoglycemia history, because tight control in these groups causes harm. Weight effects matter: SGLT2 inhibitors and GLP-1 receptor agonists promote weight loss, insulin, sulfonylureas, and TZDs cause weight gain, and metformin and DPP-4 inhibitors are neutral. Cost and access are clinical issues, not administrative afterthoughts; a patient who cannot afford or cannot obtain a GLP-1 receptor agonist will not take it, and the pharmacist must know formulary status, prior authorization requirements, and lower-cost alternatives.

### Sequence and Combine Rationally, and Know When to Add Insulin

Combine agents with complementary mechanisms (metformin plus SGLT2 inhibitor plus GLP-1 receptor agonist) rather than stacking agents within the same class. Add insulin when the A1c remains above target despite two or three non-insulin agents, when there is evidence of insulin deficiency (unintended weight loss, marked hyperglycemia, ketosis), or when acute illness or steroids cause severe hyperglycemia. When insulin is added, continue metformin and consider continuing the GLP-1 receptor agonist for weight and cardiorenal benefit. Do not abandon the cardiorenal-protective agents just because insulin was added.

## Common Traps

### Selecting by A1c Alone and Ignoring Cardiorenal Status

The pharmacist selects the next agent purely by expected A1c reduction, defaulting to a sulfonylurea or insulin because they lower glucose fast. The mechanism is that A1c is the visible outcome measure. The false signal is that a bigger A1c drop means a better choice. The harm is that the patient with heart failure or CKD is denied an SGLT2 inhibitor that would reduce hospitalization and progression, and instead receives a drug that causes hypoglycemia and weight gain. Cardiorenal status must be classified before the A1c target drives the choice.

### Prescribing a Thiazolidinedione in Heart Failure

The pharmacist adds pioglitazone for glycemic control in a patient with heart failure. The mechanism is that the drug is effective and possibly cardioprotective in some populations. The false signal is that cardiovascular benefit in one population generalizes to heart failure. The harm is that thiazolidinediones cause fluid retention and can precipitate or worsen heart failure, and they are contraindicated in NYHA class III or IV heart failure. The pharmacist must screen for heart failure before recommending a TZD.

### Overlooking Sulfonylurea Hypoglycemia in Older Adults and Renal Impairment

The pharmacist recommends glyburide for an older adult with reduced eGFR because it is inexpensive. The mechanism is that cost drives the choice. The false signal is that all sulfonylureas are interchangeable. The harm is that glyburide has a long duration and active metabolites that accumulate in renal impairment, and it is a leading cause of severe hypoglycemia in older adults, associated with falls, fractures, and death. Glipizide is preferred when a sulfonylurea is needed in these populations, and the lowest effective dose should be used.

### Defaulting to Insulin When a GLP-1 Receptor Agonist Is Indicated

The pharmacist moves to basal insulin when an injectable is needed because insulin is familiar. The mechanism is that insulin feels like the standard next step. The false signal is that insulin is the default injectable. The harm is that the patient gains weight, incurs hypoglycemia risk, and misses the cardiorenal and weight benefits of a GLP-1 receptor agonist that would have been preferred when not contraindicated. Injectable selection should default to GLP-1 receptor agonist first when no insulin-deficiency indication exists.

### Ignoring Renal Dose Adjustments

The pharmacist recommends a full dose of a renally cleared agent without checking the current eGFR. The mechanism is that the dose was correct at the last visit. The false signal is that the dose is stable. The harm is that renal function declines silently in diabetes, and an unadjusted dose of metformin, a DPP-4 inhibitor, or a sulfonylurea accumulates and causes lactic acidosis, hypoglycemia, or other toxicity. The eGFR must be checked at every recommendation.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The standard sequencing is handled well, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the common path is well-managed while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the typical case and never tested the boundary such as pancreatitis history, gastroparesis, pregnancy, or steroid-induced hyperglycemia.

## Self-Check

- Did I classify the patient's cardiorenal status (ASCVD, heart failure, CKD with albuminuria) and let that drive class selection before considering A1c reduction?
- Did I confirm metformin is appropriate (no eGFR below 30, no acute kidney injury, no unstable heart failure) and counsel on tolerability titration?
- When an injectable was needed without an insulin-deficiency indication, did I prefer a GLP-1 receptor agonist with proven cardiorenal benefit over basal insulin?
- Did I reserve sulfonylureas and thiazolidinediones for justified situations, avoid glyburide in older adults and renal impairment, and avoid TZDs in heart failure?
- Did I check the current eGFR and adjust or avoid agents (metformin, sulfonylureas, DPP-4 inhibitors, SGLT2 inhibitors by agent-specific thresholds) accordingly?
- Did I set an A1c target appropriate to the patient's age, life expectancy, comorbidities, and hypoglycemia risk rather than defaulting to a tight target?
- Did I reconcile cost, formulary status, and access, and offer a viable alternative when the preferred agent is unaffordable or unavailable?
- Are assumptions, uncertainties, and confidence levels stated explicitly rather than buried in a confident-sounding recommendation?
