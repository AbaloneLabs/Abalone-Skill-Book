# Thyroid Hormone Replacement and Antithyroid Therapy

Thyroid therapy looks deceptively simple because the drugs are few and the monitoring test is a single number, but the management is dominated by narrow therapeutic windows, drug-food and drug-drug absorption interactions that silently sabotage replacement, and rare but life-threatening adverse effects of antithyroid drugs. The pharmacist's judgment problem is that levothyroxine is one of the most commonly mismanaged chronic medications: patients take it with calcium or iron, take it with food, have it disrupted by new interacting drugs, or receive inconsistent products, and the resulting TSH swings are misread as dose failures. On the antithyroid side, the danger is under-recognition of agranulocytosis and hepatotoxicity, and the misapplication of propylthiouracil outside the settings where it is preferred. The pharmacist must manage both the dose and the absorption environment, and must treat thyroid therapy as a dynamic balance with the patient's changing physiology, especially in pregnancy.

## Core Rules

### Titrate Levothyroxine by TSH, Incrementally, With the Correct Recheck Interval

Levothyroxine dose changes take five to six weeks to fully reflect in the TSH because of the long half-life of thyroxine and the pituitary feedback loop. Titrate by 12.5 to 25 mcg increments based on the TSH drawn at least six weeks after the last change, never sooner. The target TSH depends on the patient: most adults target the middle of the reference range; older adults and those with atrial fibrillation or osteoporosis risk target a slightly higher TSH to avoid over-replacement; pregnant patients target a trimester-specific lower range. Do not chase a single out-of-range TSH without confirming adherence, absorption interactions, and timing of the dose relative to the lab draw, because a TSH drawn shortly after a missed or mistimed dose misrepresents the steady state.

### Control the Absorption Environment Before Changing the Dose

Levothyroxine absorption is the most common silent cause of unstable replacement. The patient must take it on an empty stomach, ideally 30 to 60 minutes before breakfast, or consistently at bedtime three to four hours after the last meal. Calcium, iron, magnesium, aluminum antacids, bile acid sequestrants, proton pump inhibitors, sucralfate, and soy and coffee all impair absorption and must be separated by at least four hours (or for some, avoided entirely). Before increasing a dose for a high TSH, the pharmacist must ask about new supplements, new medications, and changes in meal timing, because the correct fix is usually separation or timing, not a higher dose that will overshoot once the interaction is removed. Pregnancy, weight gain, and malabsorption disorders increase requirements; document and anticipate these.

### Maintain Product Consistency and Address Generic Switch Concerns

Levothyroxine has a narrow therapeutic index, and small differences in bioavailability between products can move the TSH out of range. While FDA-approved generics are considered therapeutically equivalent, the practical reality is that frequent switching between manufacturers can destabilize control. The pharmacist should aim for product consistency: when a patient is stable, keep them on the same product; if a switch is necessary, recheck the TSH at six weeks. Document the product and the lot if possible. For patients who remain unstable despite consistent product and correct timing, investigate malabsorption, adherence, and concomitant conditions before escalating dose further.

### Select Antithyroid Drug by Patient and Clinical Setting

For hyperthyroidism, methimazole is preferred over propylthiouracil for most patients because it is more convenient (once daily dosing), more effective, and has a lower risk of hepatotoxicity and agranulocytosis. Propylthiouracil is preferred in the first trimester of pregnancy (methimazole is teratogenic in the first trimester), in thyroid storm (because it blocks peripheral T4 to T3 conversion), and in patients intolerant to methimazole. Switch from propylthiouracil to methimazole after the first trimester in pregnancy. The starting dose of methimazole depends on severity; titrate down as the patient becomes euthyroid, typically over weeks to months, and add a beta-blocker for symptomatic control of adrenergic symptoms.

### Monitor Aggressively for the Life-Threatening Adverse Effects of Antithyroid Drugs

Antithyroid drugs carry two rare but dangerous risks that the pharmacist must monitor and counsel on: agranulocytosis and severe hepatotoxicity. Counsel every patient to report fever and sore mouth or throat immediately, because agranulocytosis typically presents within the first months and can be fatal if the drug is continued. A baseline and periodic complete blood count can be considered, but the cornerstone is patient counseling and immediate evaluation of febrile illness. For hepatotoxicity, counsel on jaundice, dark urine, abdominal pain, and fatigue; propylthiouracil carries higher hepatotoxicity risk and baseline plus periodic liver function tests are warranted. PTU also carries a risk of ANCA-associated vasculitis with long-term use, which is another reason to limit its duration.

### Manage Thyroid Therapy in Pregnancy With Heightened Vigilance

Pregnancy substantially increases levothyroxine requirements, often by 30 to 50 percent, because of increased thyroxine-binding globulin, placental deiodinase, and fetal demand. The pharmacist must anticipate an increase requirement early in pregnancy, advise the patient to increase the dose upon a positive pregnancy test in consultation with the prescriber, and monitor the TSH every four weeks during the first half of pregnancy and at least once in the second half. Undertreatment in pregnancy carries fetal neurodevelopmental risk; overtreatment carries fetal and maternal cardiac risk. For hyperthyroidism in pregnancy, use propylthiouracil in the first trimester and switch to methimazole after, with the lowest effective dose to avoid fetal hypothyroidism.

### Coordinate Conversion Between Products and Parenteral Therapy

When converting between levothyroxine and desiccated thyroid or liothyronine, recognize that combination or T3-containing products are not standard first-line and that conversion is not one-to-one. Desiccated thyroid has variable potency and is not recommended as first-line. Liothyronine has a short half-life and requires divided dosing; it is reserved for specific cases under endocrinology guidance. For patients unable to take oral levothyroxine (nothing by mouth, severe malabsorption), parenteral levothyroxine at approximately half the oral dose is used, with monitoring, because oral bioavailability is roughly 70 percent. Do not convert carelessly; verify the conversion and the monitoring plan.

## Common Traps

### Increasing the Dose for an Absorption Interaction

The patient has a high TSH and the pharmacist increases the levothyroxine dose without asking about calcium, iron, food, or new interacting drugs. The mechanism is that the high TSH looks like under-dosing. The false signal is that the dose is insufficient. The harm is that the patient now takes a higher dose layered on impaired absorption, and when the interaction resolves (supplement stopped, timing corrected), the patient becomes over-replaced with atrial fibrillation or osteoporosis risk. The absorption environment must be assessed before any dose increase.

### Drawing TSH Too Soon After a Dose Change

The pharmacist checks the TSH two weeks after a dose change and adjusts again. The mechanism is impatience and the desire for rapid feedback. The false signal is that the current TSH reflects the new dose. The harm is that levothyroxine takes five to six weeks to reach steady state in the TSH, and acting on an early value causes overshoot and oscillation. The recheck interval must be at least six weeks.

### Using Propylthiouracil Outside Its Preferred Settings

The pharmacist continues propylthiouracil long-term because it was the initial drug. The mechanism is inertia once a patient is stable. The false signal is that the drug is working, so it should continue. The harm is that propylthiouracil carries higher hepatotoxicity risk and ANCA-associated vasculitis risk with long-term use, and methimazole is preferred for chronic therapy except in the first trimester of pregnancy, thyroid storm, or methimazole intolerance. The pharmacist should flag patients on long-term PTU for review and conversion.

### Missing Agranulocytosis Counseling

The pharmacist dispenses methimazole without counseling on fever and sore throat. The mechanism is that agranulocytosis is rare, so it feels unlikely. The false signal is that routine dispensing does not require urgent counseling. The harm is that agranulocytosis presents abruptly, is potentially fatal if the drug is continued, and depends on the patient recognizing the symptoms and stopping the drug immediately. Every patient starting or continuing an antithyroid drug must receive this counseling at every fill.

### Ignoring Pregnancy-Driven Requirement Increases

A pregnant patient on stable levothyroxine develops a rising TSH and the pharmacist attributes it to non-adherence. The mechanism is that the prior dose was stable, so instability is assumed to be behavioral. The false signal is that the patient is skipping doses. The harm is that pregnancy physiologically increases levothyroxine requirement by 30 to 50 percent, and failing to anticipate and increase the dose risks fetal neurodevelopmental harm. The pharmacist must recognize pregnancy as a trigger for proactive dose increase and closer monitoring.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical replacement scenario is handled thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-managed while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary such as amiodarone-induced thyroid disease, subacute thyroiditis, or post-radioactive-iodine hypothyroidism.

## Self-Check

- Did I titrate levothyroxine by 12.5 to 25 mcg increments with a TSH recheck at least six weeks after the last change, using a target appropriate to the patient's age, cardiac status, and pregnancy status?
- Before increasing a dose for a high TSH, did I assess the absorption environment (calcium, iron, antacids, proton pump inhibitors, food timing, new interacting drugs)?
- Did I aim for product consistency and plan a TSH recheck after any product switch?
- For antithyroid therapy, did I select methimazole as first-line and reserve propylthiouracil for first-trimester pregnancy, thyroid storm, or methimazole intolerance, and plan a switch after the first trimester?
- Did I counsel every antithyroid drug patient on fever and sore throat (agranulocytosis) and on jaundice, dark urine, and abdominal pain (hepatotoxicity), and arrange baseline or periodic monitoring where indicated?
- For pregnant patients on levothyroxine, did I anticipate a 30 to 50 percent dose increase, advise increasing upon a positive pregnancy test, and plan TSH monitoring every four weeks in the first half?
- Did I verify any conversion between levothyroxine, desiccated thyroid, liothyronine, or parenteral forms rather than assuming one-to-one equivalence?
- Are assumptions, uncertainties, and confidence levels stated explicitly rather than buried in a confident-sounding dose recommendation?
