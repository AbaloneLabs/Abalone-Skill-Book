# Pharmacy Resident Precepting and Evaluation

A pharmacy residency is a far higher-stakes educational enterprise than a student rotation. Residents hold a licensed pharmacist credential, deliver billable clinical services, staff as part of the workforce, and are being groomed for independent specialist practice, often within a single year. The judgment problem for the residency preceptor is that the resident must be simultaneously trained, supervised, evaluated, and used, and the balance among these shifts week to week as the resident matures. Residency preceptors most often fail not in clinical teaching but in evaluation discipline: they give rich verbal coaching all year and then produce a summative evaluation that cannot distinguish a strong resident from a marginal one, or they discover a competence gap in the final month that was visible at month three but never documented. Residency evaluation is governed by ASHP standards and a residency advisory committee, which means every evaluation is auditable and every undocumented concern is a liability.

## Core Rules

### Precept Against ASHP Goals and Objectives, Calibrated to the Program

PGY1 and PGY2 residencies are structured around required and elective goals, objectives, and competency areas defined by ASHP and customized by the program. Every learning experience (rotation, longitudinal, staffing) must map to specific objectives, and every evaluation must be conducted against those objectives, not against a general impression of the resident. The preceptor must know which objectives a given experience is responsible for, design activities that produce evidence for those objectives, and evaluate only against them. Evaluating a resident against criteria that are not in the program's goals, or failing to evaluate against criteria that are, both invalidate the evaluation and create appeal risk. The preceptor should keep the program's goal-and-objective set as the working reference for every encounter.

### Use the Four Preceptor Roles Deliberately

ASHP defines four preceptor roles that progress as the resident matures: instruct (tell and show), model (demonstrate while resident observes), coach (resident performs with preceptor feedback), and facilitate (resident performs independently with preceptor available). The trap is to default to one role, usually coach, for the entire year. The preceptor must deliberately select the role for each task based on the resident's current entrustment level for that specific task, and must move toward facilitation as competence is demonstrated. A resident who is still being instructed in month ten on tasks they have mastered is being underutilized and will not reach independent practice; a resident who is being facilitated in month two on tasks they have not mastered is a patient-safety risk. The role selection is a deliberate decision, recorded and revisited.

### Build Documentation That Supports Both Formative and Summative Evaluation

Residency evaluation requires both frequent formative feedback (weekly or per-encounter) and periodic summative evaluations (quarterly, semiannual, final). The formative documentation is the evidence base for the summative evaluation; without it, the summative is an unsupported opinion. The preceptor must document specific observed behaviors, the context, the standard expected, and the gap (if any) for every significant encounter, using the program's evaluation tool. A summative evaluation that says "needs improvement in clinical reasoning" without cited formative examples is indefensible. The preceptor should treat documentation as a continuous activity, not a quarterly scramble, and should review the running record before each formal evaluation to ensure the summative reflects the full pattern, not the most recent week.

### Manage the Resident Project With Milestones and Real Mentorship

The residency project (research, quality improvement, or medication-use evaluation) is a frequent source of resident failure and preceptor frustration. The project must be scoped at the start to be achievable within the year, must have a written timeline with milestones (proposal approval, IRB or exemption, data collection complete, analysis complete, draft manuscript, final presentation), and must have a designated project preceptor who meets with the resident on a fixed cadence. The most common failure mode is a project that drifts because no one enforced the milestones until the spring, leaving the resident to scramble a shallow product in the final weeks. The preceptor must enforce the timeline rigorously, treat missed milestones as a formative concern requiring a remediation plan, and ensure the project produces a defensible scholarly deliverable.

### Separate Staffing Service From Educational Evaluation

Residents typically staff as part of their training, and there is a recurring confusion between evaluating the resident's staffing performance (which is a workforce concern) and evaluating their educational progress (which is a residency concern). The preceptor must keep these distinct. Staffing competency (accuracy, throughput, reliability, teamwork) is evaluated against staffing standards and may affect the resident's continued employment; educational competency (clinical reasoning, evidence evaluation, communication, professionalism) is evaluated against ASHP goals. A resident can be strong educationally and weak operationally, or vice versa, and conflating the two produces evaluations that mislead the residency advisory committee. Each domain needs its own criteria and its own documentation.

### Address Professionalism and Behavioral Concerns Early and Formally

The most difficult residency evaluations concern professionalism: reliability, receptiveness to feedback, interpersonal conduct, ethical behavior, and self-directedness. These are competencies, not personality traits, and they are evaluated against the program's standards. The trap is to tolerate professionalism concerns because the resident is clinically strong, or to avoid the difficult conversation until the final evaluation. The preceptor must raise any professionalism concern at the first observation, document it specifically, create an improvement plan with the program director, and recognize that unremediated professionalism failures are the most common reason a resident does not complete or cannot be recommended for a position. Clinical strength does not offset a professionalism deficit, because professionalism deficits cause harm in independent practice.

## Common Traps

### The Halo Evaluation From a Strong Clinician

The resident is clinically articulate and produces impressive presentations, and the preceptor carries that positive impression into every objective, including ones where the resident is actually weak (staffing reliability, professionalism, evidence appraisal). The mechanism is that one salient strength dominates global judgment, and the false signal is that a strong presenter is strong everywhere. The harm is that genuine gaps in less visible competencies go unevaluated and unremediated, and the resident graduates with a blind spot that surfaces in independent practice. Each objective must be evaluated on its own evidence.

### Verbal Coaching Without Running Documentation

The preceptor gives excellent verbal feedback throughout the year but records almost nothing until the summative evaluation is due. The mechanism is that verbal feedback feels sufficient in the moment, and the false signal is that "I coached them, so the gap is closed." The harm is that the summative evaluation cannot be defended, a marginal resident cannot be flagged for remediation with evidence, and the program director cannot see the pattern early enough to intervene. Documentation is the residency evaluation system; treating it as optional paperwork undermines the entire program.

### Project Drift and the Spring Scramble

The resident and preceptor agree on a project in the fall but do not enforce milestones, and by spring the data are uncollected, the analysis undone, and the manuscript unwritten. The mechanism is that the timeline feels long and clinical service feels urgent, and the false signal is that there is always next month. The harm is a shallow, late, low-quality project that fails to meet the scholarly requirement, embarrasses the program at regional meetings, and may jeopardize the resident's completion. Project milestones must be enforced with the same rigor as clinical evaluations.

### Facilitating Too Early on Unmastered Tasks

Because the resident is licensed and often impressive, the preceptor moves to the facilitator role on tasks the resident has not yet demonstrated, allowing independent clinical decisions without verification. The mechanism is that the resident's license and presentation lower vigilance, and the false signal is that a licensed pharmacist resident can be trusted to act independently. The harm is a patient exposed to an unverified recommendation, and a resident who is set up to fail when their actual competence is tested. Entrustment is earned task by task on demonstrated performance, never granted on credential.

### Conflating Staffing and Educational Evaluation

The preceptor lets a resident's strong (or weak) staffing performance bleed into the educational evaluation, or vice versa. The mechanism is that the same person is observed in both contexts and the impressions merge, and the false signal is that performance is a single trait. The harm is an evaluation that misrepresents the resident to the advisory committee and to future employers, masking either a clinical gap behind good staffing or a staffing gap behind good clinical work. The two domains require separate criteria and separate documentation.

### Tolerating Professionalism Deficits in a Strong Clinician

The resident is clinically excellent but unreliable, dismissive of feedback, or difficult interpersonally, and the preceptor downgrades the concern because the clinical work is strong. The mechanism is that clinical output is visible and valuable, and the false signal is that clinical strength compensates for behavior. The harm is that the professionalism deficit is never remediated and becomes the defining limitation in the resident's independent career, and the preceptor who failed to document it bears responsibility. Professionalism is a core competency evaluated on its own merits.

## Self-Check

- Is every learning experience and evaluation mapped to specific ASHP goals and objectives, with activities designed to produce evidence for those objectives?
- Am I deliberately selecting among the four preceptor roles (instruct, model, coach, facilitate) based on the resident's demonstrated entrustment for each task, rather than defaulting to one role?
- Is there running formative documentation of specific observed behaviors, so that the summative evaluation is evidence-based rather than impression-based?
- Does the residency project have a written milestone timeline (proposal, IRB, data, analysis, draft, final) that is enforced, with missed milestones triggering a remediation plan?
- Are staffing competency and educational competency evaluated against separate criteria and separate documentation, without one bleeding into the other?
- Are professionalism concerns raised at first observation, documented specifically, and managed with the program director, rather than deferred to the final evaluation?
- Does each summative rating rest on cited formative examples, so that a strong resident and a marginal resident can be distinguished defensibly?
- Does the output defer final residency decisions to the preceptor, residency program director, and advisory committee where the question exceeds the agent's competence?
