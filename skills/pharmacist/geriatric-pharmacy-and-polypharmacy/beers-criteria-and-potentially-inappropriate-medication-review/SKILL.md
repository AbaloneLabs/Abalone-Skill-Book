# Beers Criteria and Potentially Inappropriate Medication Review

The Beers Criteria are the most widely used framework for identifying potentially inappropriate medications (PIMs) in older adults, and the pharmacist's judgment problem is that they are easy to apply mechanically and easy to dismiss. Applied mechanically, every flagged drug becomes a target for discontinuation regardless of whether it is actually inappropriate for the specific patient, producing over-zealous changes that destabilize a controlled condition. Dismissed reflexively, the criteria become a paperwork exercise and the older adult continues on benzodiazepines, anticholinergics, and long-acting sulfonylureas that silently cause falls, cognitive decline, hypoglycemia, and bleeding. The pharmacist must use the Beers Criteria as a structured prompt for clinical reasoning, weighing the strength of the evidence, the patient's specific risk factors and goals, and the availability of safer alternatives, then documenting a deliberate decision for each flagged medication.

## Core Rules

### Treat the Beers Criteria as a Prompt for Reasoning, Not an Automatic Stop List

The Beers Criteria identify medications that are potentially inappropriate, meaning the potential harm generally outweighs the potential benefit in older adults, but they do not mandate discontinuation in every patient. For each flagged medication, the pharmacist must assess whether the indication is still valid, whether the patient is experiencing or is at high risk for the specific harm the criteria cite, whether a safer alternative exists, and whether the patient's goals and life expectancy justify the risk. A benzodiazepine in a frail 85-year-old with falls is a clear discontinuation target; the same drug in an 80-year-old with severe refractory anxiety after alternatives have failed may be a documented, justified choice. The output of a Beers review is a reasoned decision per drug, not a blanket list of stops.

### Prioritize the Highest-Harm Categories First

Not all PIMs carry equal risk, and the pharmacist should prioritize review by harm severity. The highest-harm categories include benzodiazepines and other sedative-hypnotics (falls, fractures, cognitive impairment, delirium), first-generation antihistamines and other strong anticholinergics (cognitive decline, urinary retention, constipation, falls), long-acting sulfonylureas such as glyburide (prolonged severe hypoglycemia), nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (gastrointestinal bleeding, renal injury, heart failure exacerbation, hypertension), and skeletal muscle relaxants (sedation, falls, weakness with limited benefit). Address these categories first because the evidence of harm is strongest and the alternatives are usually available. Lower-priority flags (such as long-term proton pump inhibitors) still warrant review but carry less immediate risk.

### Apply Disease-Specific and Drug-Interaction Sections, Not Just the Drug List

The Beers Criteria include not only medications to avoid in most older adults but also medications to avoid in specific diseases (heart failure, dementia, history of falls, chronic kidney disease, lower urinary tract symptoms, Parkinson disease) and medications requiring dose adjustment or to be used with caution. The pharmacist must apply all three layers: the general avoid list, the disease-specific list against the patient's conditions, and the caution list. A drug that is acceptable in a healthy older adult may be inappropriate in one with dementia, falls, or heart failure. Cross-referencing the patient's full problem list against the disease-specific table is where much of the value lies.

### Incorporate the Patient's Goals, Life Expectancy, and Function

The decision to continue, change, or stop a PIM depends on the patient's goals of care, life expectancy, and functional status, not only on the drug. In a patient with limited life expectancy and a goal of comfort, the calculus shifts: a benzodiazepine for terminal agitation may be appropriate even though it is flagged. In a robust older adult with a long life expectancy and a goal of independence, the same drug is a clear risk to falls and cognition and should be addressed. Ask about goals and function before deciding, and align the medication decision with them. A PIM review without this context produces decisions that are clinically correct but wrong for the patient.

### Plan the Discontinuation Safely, Not Abruptly

Discontinuing a PIM, especially a benzodiazepine, opioid, or anticholinergic taken long-term, requires a taper to avoid withdrawal, rebound, or destabilization. Plan a slow taper (benzodiazepine dose reduction of 10 to 25 percent every one to two weeks), substitute a safer alternative where appropriate (an SSRI or nonpharmacologic therapy for anxiety), monitor for withdrawal symptoms and for recurrence of the original condition, and schedule follow-up. Abrupt discontinuation causes withdrawal, delirium, or symptom recurrence that erodes trust and often leads to reinstatement. The discontinuation plan is part of the PIM review, not a separate step.

### Document the Decision and the Reasoning

For every flagged medication, document the decision (continue, change, taper, stop) and the reasoning (the indication, the risk assessment, the alternative considered, the patient's preference). This documentation serves three purposes: it communicates the deliberate decision to the care team, it protects against the assumption that the medication was overlooked, and it provides a baseline for the next review. A PIM that is continued with documented justification is appropriate care; a PIM that is continued without documentation is a gap. The pharmacist should make the reasoning visible.

### Reassess at Every Transition and Periodically

PIM review is not a one-time activity. Medications accumulate at every transition (hospital admission, discharge, new specialist visit), and a patient who was appropriate at the last review may have new flags. Reassess at admission, at discharge, after any new prescription, and at least annually in the outpatient setting. Build the Beers review into medication reconciliation and comprehensive medication review workflows so that it recurs rather than depending on a one-off audit.

## Common Traps

### Applying the Criteria as an Automatic Stop List

The pharmacist flags every Beers medication for discontinuation without assessing the indication, the alternatives, or the patient's goals. The mechanism is that the criteria are authoritative and convenient. The false signal is that a flagged drug is an inappropriate drug. The harm is destabilization of controlled conditions, withdrawal, and symptom recurrence when a justified medication is stopped reflexively. Each flag requires a reasoned decision, not an automatic stop.

### Dismissing the Criteria as Inapplicable

The pharmacist treats the Beers Criteria as a paperwork exercise and continues all flagged medications. The mechanism is that the drugs were prescribed by a physician and seem to be working. The false signal is that the patient is stable, so the medications are fine. The harm is that the silent harms of benzodiazepines, anticholinergics, and long-acting sulfonylureas (falls, cognitive decline, hypoglycemia) accumulate unrecognized and are attributed to aging rather than to the medications. The criteria are a prompt for active reasoning, not a formality.

### Stopping a Benzodiazepine Abruptly

The pharmacist recommends immediate discontinuation of a long-term benzodiazepine. The mechanism is that the drug is flagged as inappropriate. The false signal is that stopping an inappropriate drug is always the right move. The harm is withdrawal seizures, rebound anxiety, delirium, and destabilization, and the patient often reinstates the drug or seeks it elsewhere. Long-term benzodiazepines require a slow taper with monitoring.

### Ignoring the Disease-Specific and Caution Sections

The pharmacist applies only the general avoid list and misses that a drug is inappropriate for the patient's specific condition. The mechanism is that the general list is the most familiar part. The false signal is that the general list covers the important risks. The harm is that drugs acceptable in a healthy older adult but inappropriate in dementia, falls, heart failure, or chronic kidney disease continue, causing the specific harms the disease section was designed to prevent. All three layers must be applied.

### Continuing a PIM Without Documented Justification

The pharmacist reviews the list, decides to continue a flagged drug, but does not document why. The mechanism is that a continue decision feels like no action. The false signal is that no change means no documentation needed. The harm is that the next reviewer cannot tell whether the drug was deliberately continued or overlooked, and the assumption is that it was missed, prompting repeated and conflicting review. Every flagged drug requires a documented decision.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical community-dwelling older adult is reviewed well, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the common path is handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the typical case and never tested the boundary such as end-of-life comfort care, dementia with severe behavioral symptoms, or the patient on a PIM with no available alternative.

## Self-Check

- Did I treat each Beers flag as a prompt for clinical reasoning rather than an automatic discontinuation, assessing the indication, risk, alternatives, and patient goals?
- Did I prioritize the highest-harm categories (benzodiazepines, strong anticholinergics, long-acting sulfonylureas, NSAIDs, muscle relaxants) first?
- Did I apply all three layers of the criteria (general avoid list, disease-specific list against the patient's conditions, and caution list with dose adjustments)?
- Did I incorporate the patient's goals of care, life expectancy, and functional status into the decision for each flagged medication?
- For any discontinuation, did I plan a safe taper, substitute an alternative where appropriate, and schedule monitoring for withdrawal and symptom recurrence?
- Did I document the decision and the reasoning for every flagged medication, including the justification for continued use?
- Did I build PIM review into transitions of care and periodic comprehensive medication review rather than treating it as a one-time audit?
- Are assumptions, uncertainties, and confidence levels stated explicitly rather than buried in a confident-sounding recommendation?
