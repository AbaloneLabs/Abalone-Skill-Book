# Deprescribing and Fall Risk Mitigation in Older Adults

Falls are among the most devastating and most preventable events in older adults, causing fractures, head injury, loss of independence, and death, and medications are among the most modifiable fall risk factors. The pharmacist's judgment problem is that deprescribing and fall-risk mitigation are not simply stopping drugs; they are a sequenced, patient-centered process that must preserve treatment of legitimate conditions while reducing the medications that increase fall risk, must manage withdrawal and rebound, and must engage a patient who may resist stopping a drug they believe is helping. The danger is twofold: leaving fall-increasing drugs in place because each seems individually justified, and removing drugs so aggressively that the underlying condition destabilizes and the patient falls for a different reason. Successful deprescribing is deliberate, sequenced, monitored, and paired with nonpharmacologic fall-risk reduction.

## Core Rules

### Identify Fall-Increasing Drugs Systematically Across All Classes

Fall risk comes from many drug classes, not only the obvious sedatives. Screen the regimen for benzodiazepines and Z-drugs (sedation, impaired balance), opioids (sedation, orthostasis), first-generation antihistamines and other anticholinergics (sedation, blurred vision, cognitive impairment), antihypertensives (orthostatic hypotension, especially when intensified), diuretics (volume depletion, electrolyte disturbance, nocturia), antipsychotics (sedation, orthostasis, gait disturbance), gabapentinoids (sedation, ataxia), hypoglycemics (hypoglycemia causing falls), and sedating antidepressants. A patient may fall because of the interaction of several modest-risk drugs rather than one high-risk drug, so the full regimen must be reviewed. Polypharmacy itself (typically five or more medications) is an independent fall risk factor, and reducing the total count reduces risk beyond the effect on any single drug.

### Prioritize by Fall Risk Magnitude and by the Patient's Specific Risk Profile

Not all fall-increasing drugs carry equal risk, and the pharmacist should prioritize by the magnitude of risk and by the patient's specific vulnerabilities. The highest-risk drugs are benzodiazepines, antipsychotics, opioids, and first-generation antihistamines; prioritize these first. Within antihypertensives, prioritize those causing the most orthostasis (alpha-blockers, high-dose diuretics, aggressive multi-drug regimens) and intensify review in patients with documented orthostatic hypotension, recent falls, frailty, or cognitive impairment. Correlate the medication list with the patient's fall history: a drug started shortly before the falls began is a stronger target than a long-standing stable drug. The prioritization must reflect both the pharmacologic risk and the patient's actual pattern.

### Engage the Patient in Shared Decision-Making and Address the Belief That the Drug Helps

Deprescribing fails when the patient is unconvinced. Many fall-increasing drugs are drugs the patient values: a benzodiazepine for sleep, an opioid for pain, an antihistamine for allergies. The pharmacist must explain the link between the drug and the fall risk in concrete terms, present the evidence that deprescribing reduces falls, offer alternatives and a taper plan, and let the patient participate in the decision. Frame the change as a positive step toward independence and safety, not as withdrawal of treatment. A patient who understands the rationale and the plan is far more likely to adhere to a taper than one who is simply told to stop. Document the patient's preference and the agreed plan.

### Plan a Sequenced, Incremental Taper Rather Than Abrupt Discontinuation

Abrupt discontinuation of benzodiazepines, opioids, gabapentinoids, or antihypertensives causes withdrawal, rebound symptoms, or destabilization that can itself cause falls. Plan a slow, sequenced taper: reduce benzodiazepines by 10 to 25 percent every one to two weeks with a long-acting substitution if needed; reduce opioids gradually with monitoring for withdrawal; reduce antihypertensives one drug at a time with blood pressure monitoring; taper gabapentinoids over weeks. Tackle one drug or class at a time so that effects can be attributed and the plan adjusted, rather than changing everything at once. Schedule monitoring visits during the taper to catch withdrawal, symptom recurrence, or new falls early.

### Preserve Treatment of Conditions Whose Untreated State Increases Fall Risk

Deprescribing must not abandon treatment of conditions whose untreated state also causes falls. Uncontrolled pain causes immobility and deconditioning that increase fall risk; untreated depression causes inattention and deconditioning; severe orthostatic hypotension from undertreated Parkinson disease or autonomic failure causes falls; uncontrolled diabetes causes neuropathy and vision problems. The goal is the right medications at the right doses, not the fewest medications. For each drug targeted for deprescribing, assess whether the underlying condition is adequately addressed by an alternative, and ensure that reducing one risk does not create another.

### Pair Medication Reduction With Nonpharmacologic Fall-Risk Strategies

Medication reduction is most effective when paired with other fall-risk interventions. Refer for physical therapy and a strength and balance program (the most evidence-supported single intervention), address vision and hearing, evaluate footwear and home hazards (throw rugs, lighting, grab bars), assess and treat vitamin D deficiency, evaluate for postural hypotension and advise on hydration and slow position changes, and review assistive devices. A pharmacist who only reduces medications without connecting the patient to these interventions addresses one risk factor among many. Coordinate with the primary care provider, physical therapy, and the family.

### Reassess and Reinforce at Every Transition and Periodically

Fall risk and medications change at every transition and with each new prescription, so deprescribing and fall-risk review must recur. Reassess at admission, at discharge, after any fall, after any new prescription, and at least annually. A patient whose medications were optimized at the last review may have acquired a new sedative or had an antihypertensive intensified at a hospital discharge. Build fall-risk medication review into transitions of care and comprehensive medication review so that the silent accumulation of fall-increasing drugs is caught before the next fall.

## Common Traps

### Stopping Fall-Increasing Drugs Abruptly

The pharmacist recommends immediate discontinuation of a benzodiazepine or antihypertensive to reduce fall risk. The mechanism is that the drug causes falls, so removing it quickly seems right. The false signal is that faster removal means faster risk reduction. The harm is withdrawal seizures and rebound anxiety for benzodiazepines, hypertensive rebound for some antihypertensives, and destabilization that can itself cause falls and erode trust. Fall-increasing drugs require sequenced, monitored tapers.

### Leaving Fall-Increasing Drugs in Place Because Each Seems Justified

The pharmacist reviews each drug and finds a legitimate indication for each, so concludes none should be changed. The mechanism is that each drug is defensible in isolation. The false signal is that an indicated drug is a safe drug. The harm is that the cumulative fall risk of several individually justified drugs persists, and the patient falls from the interaction rather than from any single drug. The regimen must be assessed for cumulative fall risk, not only individual indications.

### Deprescribing Without Patient Engagement

The pharmacist and prescriber decide to stop a drug the patient values without explaining the rationale or offering alternatives. The mechanism is that the clinical decision feels sufficient. The false signal is that the right decision will be followed. The harm is non-adherence to the taper, the patient resuming the drug elsewhere, or loss of trust in the care team. The patient must understand the link between the drug and falls and participate in the plan.

### Removing a Drug and Creating a New Fall Risk

The pharmacist stops an antihypertensive to reduce orthostasis, and the patient's blood pressure rises to a level that causes a hypertensive fall or stroke, or stops an opioid and the patient's uncontrolled pain causes immobility and deconditioning. The mechanism is that the drug was a fall risk, so removing it reduces risk. The false signal is that fewer fall-increasing drugs always means fewer falls. The harm is that the untreated condition creates a different fall or harm pathway. Each deprescribing decision must account for the effect of the untreated condition.

### Focusing Only on Medications and Missing the Nonpharmacologic Interventions

The pharmacist reduces the medications and considers the fall-risk mitigation complete. The mechanism is that medications are the pharmacist's domain. The false signal is that medication reduction is sufficient. The harm is that the patient remains at high fall risk from deconditioning, home hazards, vision impairment, and other factors that medication reduction does not address. Fall-risk mitigation requires the nonpharmacologic interventions, and the pharmacist should refer and coordinate.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical community-dwelling older adult is managed well, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the common path is handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the typical case and never tested the boundary such as end-of-life comfort, advanced dementia with behavioral symptoms, or the patient on a fall-increasing drug with no safer alternative.

## Self-Check

- Did I screen the full regimen for fall-increasing drugs across all classes (benzodiazepines, opioids, anticholinergics, antihypertensives, diuretics, antipsychotics, gabapentinoids, hypoglycemics) and assess total medication count?
- Did I prioritize by fall-risk magnitude and by the patient's specific risk profile (orthostasis, frailty, cognitive impairment, recent falls, fall timing relative to new drugs)?
- Did I engage the patient in shared decision-making, explain the link between the drug and falls, present alternatives, and document the patient's preference?
- Did I plan a sequenced, incremental taper (one drug at a time, 10 to 25 percent reductions, scheduled monitoring) rather than abrupt discontinuation?
- Did I ensure that deprescribing does not abandon treatment of conditions whose untreated state increases fall risk (pain, depression, orthostatic hypotension, neuropathy)?
- Did I pair medication reduction with nonpharmacologic fall-risk interventions (strength and balance program, vision and hearing, home hazards, vitamin D, footwear)?
- Did I build fall-risk medication review into transitions of care, after any fall, and periodic comprehensive medication review?
- Are assumptions, uncertainties, and confidence levels stated explicitly rather than buried in a confident-sounding recommendation?
