# Anticholinergic and Cognitive Burden Reduction

Anticholinergic burden is one of the most underrecognized and modifiable causes of cognitive decline, delirium, falls, urinary retention, constipation, and dry mouth in older adults, and the pharmacist's challenge is that it accumulates silently across multiple prescribers and over-the-counter products. A single drug with modest anticholinergic activity may be tolerable; the sum of several such drugs (an overactive bladder agent, a first-generation antihistamine taken for sleep, a tricyclic for neuropathy, an antidepressant, an antispasmodic) produces a total burden that crosses the threshold into confusion, falls, and functional decline. The judgment problem is that each drug was prescribed for a legitimate indication, none was flagged individually as dangerous, and the harm is invisible until the patient presents with delirium or a fall attributed to aging. The pharmacist must quantify the cumulative burden, recognize the high-burden drugs that drive most of the risk, and reduce burden through substitution and deprescribing while preserving treatment of the underlying conditions.

## Core Rules

### Quantify the Cumulative Burden, Not Just Individual Drugs

Anticholinergic toxicity is a function of the total regimen, not of any single drug. Use a validated anticholinergic burden scale (the Anticholinergic Cognitive Burden scale, the Anticholinergic Risk Scale, or the American Geriatrics Society Beers Criteria) to score each medication and sum the total. A patient on three drugs each scored 1 may have a total burden equivalent to a single high-burden drug, and the cumulative effect on cognition and function is what drives harm. Quantifying the total burden reframes a regimen that looks unremarkable drug-by-drug into one that is clearly problematic in aggregate, and it gives the prescriber and patient a concrete measure to track as medications are changed.

### Identify and Prioritize the High-Burden Drugs That Drive Most Risk

A small number of drugs carry disproportionate anticholinergic burden and should be the first targets for review. These include oxybutynin and other overactive bladder agents (especially immediate-release oxybutynin), first-generation antihistamines (diphenhydramine, chlorpheniramine, hydroxyzine), tricyclic antidepressants (amitriptyline, imipramine, doxepin at higher doses), skeletal muscle relaxants (cyclobenzaprine, orphenadrine), antispasmodics (dicyclomine, hyoscyamine), and certain antipsychotics and antiemetics (promethazine, olanzapine, clozapine). Review every older adult's regimen for these drugs specifically, because they account for most of the preventable anticholinergic harm. A patient on none of these is likely to have low burden even if several low-scoring drugs are present.

### Recognize the Hidden Sources: Over-the-Counter and Sleep Aids

Much anticholinergic burden comes from products the patient does not think of as medications: over-the-counter sleep aids containing diphenhydramine (often marketed as nighttime pain relievers), allergy products, cold and flu remedies, and motion sickness drugs. Always ask directly about over-the-counter and as-needed products, because patients rarely volunteer them and they frequently contain potent anticholinergics. A patient whose prescription list looks clean may be taking diphenhydramine nightly for sleep, which alone produces substantial cognitive burden over time. The hidden sources are often the easiest to discontinue because safer alternatives exist.

### Correlate Burden With the Patient's Symptoms and Function

Anticholinergic burden causes measurable symptoms: dry mouth, blurred vision, constipation, urinary retention or incontinence (paradoxically, in a patient treated for overactive bladder), tachycardia, orthostatic hypotension, sedation, confusion, and delirium. Ask the patient about these symptoms and correlate them with the burden score, because a high burden with no symptoms may warrant monitoring while a high burden with new cognitive or functional decline warrants urgent reduction. The correlation strengthens the case for change with the prescriber and the patient, who may not have connected their confusion, falls, or dry mouth to their medications. Function (cognition, mobility, continence) is the outcome that matters, not the score alone.

### Substitute Lower-Burden Alternatives for the Underlying Indication

Reducing burden does not mean abandoning treatment of the underlying condition; it means choosing lower-burden alternatives. For overactive bladder, prefer mirabegron (a beta-3 agonist with no anticholinergic activity) or topical or extended-release formulations that cross the blood-brain barrier less. For depression, prefer an SSRI or SNRI over a tricyclic. For neuropathic pain, prefer gabapentinoids or duloxetine over amitriptyline. For allergies, prefer a second-generation antihistamine (loratadine, cetirizine) over diphenhydramine. For sleep, prefer cognitive behavioral therapy and, if a drug is needed, a lower-burden agent. The substitution must address the indication, not just remove the offending drug.

### Reduce Burden Incrementally and Monitor for Symptom Recurrence

When substituting or deprescribing, reduce burden incrementally rather than changing everything at once, especially for drugs taken long-term. Introduce the alternative, allow it to take effect, then taper and discontinue the high-burden drug. Monitor for recurrence of the original symptom (worsening urinary urgency, return of depression, insomnia), for withdrawal effects, and for improvement in cognition and function, which may take weeks to manifest after discontinuation. An abrupt change that destabilizes the patient erodes trust and is often reversed. Document the timeline and the expected monitoring so the plan survives transitions.

### Reassess Burden at Every Transition and Periodically

Anticholinergic burden accumulates at transitions and with each new prescription, so it must be reassessed at admission, at discharge, after any new prescription, and at least annually. Build burden scoring into medication reconciliation and comprehensive medication review. A patient whose burden was acceptable at the last review may have acquired a new overactive bladder agent or sleep aid from another prescriber. Periodic reassessment catches the silent accumulation that drives the gradual decline attributed to aging.

## Common Traps

### Reviewing Drugs Individually and Missing the Cumulative Burden

The pharmacist checks each drug against the Beers list and finds none individually flagged as high-burden, so concludes the regimen is acceptable. The mechanism is that the review is drug-by-drug. The false signal is that no single high-burden drug means no anticholinergic problem. The harm is that the cumulative burden of several modest-burden drugs crosses the toxicity threshold and causes cognitive and functional decline that is missed. The total burden must be quantified, not just individual drugs screened.

### Missing Over-the-Counter and Sleep Aid Sources

The pharmacist reviews the prescription list and finds low burden, missing that the patient takes diphenhydramine nightly for sleep. The mechanism is that over-the-counter products are not on the prescription list. The false signal is that the prescription review is complete. The harm is that the single highest-burden product the patient takes is never addressed, and the cognitive decline continues despite a clean prescription review. Over-the-counter and as-needed products must be asked about directly.

### Removing the Drug Without Treating the Underlying Condition

The pharmacist stops oxybutynin or amitriptyline to reduce burden without substituting an alternative. The mechanism is that the burden is the identified problem. The false signal is that removing the offending drug solves the problem. The harm is that the underlying condition (overactive bladder, neuropathic pain, depression) recurs untreated, the patient suffers, and the drug is often reinstated. Burden reduction must include substitution or an alternative treatment plan for the indication.

### Treating the Score Without Correlating to Symptoms

The pharmacist calculates a high burden score and recommends changes without assessing whether the patient has symptoms of anticholinergic toxicity. The mechanism is that the score is objective and actionable. The false signal is that a high score alone justifies change. The harm is that changes may be made for a patient who is tolerating the regimen well, while the patient with a modest score and clear symptoms of toxicity is overlooked. The score must be correlated with the patient's actual cognitive, functional, and autonomic symptoms.

### Changing Everything at Once

The pharmacist substitutes or stops several high-burden drugs simultaneously to reduce the score quickly. The mechanism is that the goal is rapid burden reduction. The false signal is that faster is better. The harm is that the patient experiences multiple simultaneous changes, withdrawal, recurrence of several conditions, and an inability to tell which change caused which effect, eroding trust and often leading to reinstatement of all drugs. Burden reduction should be incremental and monitored.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical community-dwelling older adult is reviewed well, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the common path is handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the typical case and never tested the boundary such as Parkinson disease psychosis requiring antipsychotics with anticholinergic profiles, or severe refractory overactive bladder where alternatives have failed.

## Self-Check

- Did I quantify the cumulative anticholinergic burden using a validated scale (Anticholinergic Cognitive Burden, Anticholinergic Risk Scale, or Beers) rather than reviewing drugs individually?
- Did I identify and prioritize the high-burden drugs (oxybutynin, diphenhydramine, tricyclics, muscle relaxants, antispasmodics, promethazine) that drive most of the risk?
- Did I ask directly about over-the-counter and as-needed products, especially sleep aids and allergy and cold remedies, that are frequent hidden sources?
- Did I correlate the burden score with the patient's symptoms (dry mouth, constipation, blurred vision, urinary symptoms, sedation, confusion, falls) and functional status?
- For each high-burden drug, did I identify a lower-burden alternative that treats the underlying indication (mirabegron, SSRI, gabapentinoid, second-generation antihistamine)?
- Did I plan incremental substitution or deprescribing with monitoring for symptom recurrence, withdrawal, and improvement in cognition and function?
- Did I build burden reassessment into transitions of care and periodic comprehensive medication review?
- Are assumptions, uncertainties, and confidence levels stated explicitly rather than buried in a confident-sounding recommendation?
