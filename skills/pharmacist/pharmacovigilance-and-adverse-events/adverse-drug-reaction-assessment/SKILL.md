# Adverse Drug Reaction Assessment

Adverse drug reactions are common, often subtle, and frequently misattributed — blamed on the patient's underlying disease, on aging, or on bad luck, when the actual cause is a drug the patient is taking. The pharmacist's role in ADR assessment is to be the person who systematically considers the drug as a cause of every new or worsening symptom, because no one else in the care team is positioned to do so with the same drug expertise, and because the default attribution (to the disease, to age) is the reason so many ADRs persist until they cause serious harm. The danger is the twin failures of under-attribution (missing the drug cause, so the patient is treated for a "new condition" with yet another drug — the prescribing cascade) and over-attribution (blaming the drug for a symptom that is actually the disease, so a needed drug is stopped and the disease flares). The skilled pharmacist uses a structured causality framework, considers the temporal and pharmacologic evidence, and resists both defaults.

## Core Rules

### Suspect the Drug First for Every New or Worsening Symptom

The first principle of ADR assessment is to maintain a high index of suspicion that a drug may be causing any new symptom or any worsening of an existing condition, particularly in patients on multiple medications, older adults, and patients with organ impairment. The pharmacist must, for every reported symptom or new clinical event, ask: could this be caused by one of the patient's medications? This question is asked more often than it is answered yes, but it must be asked, because the alternative — assuming the symptom is the disease or a new condition — is the cognitive default that allows ADRs to persist. Common ADR presentations that are frequently misattributed include: cognitive changes and delirium in older adults (often anticholinergic or sedative drugs, attributed to "dementia" or "aging"); falls (sedatives, antihypertensives, hypoglycemics, attributed to " frailty"); constipation (opioids, anticholinergics, calcium channel blockers, treated with laxatives rather than addressing the cause); edema (calcium channel blockers, NSAIDs, treated with diuretics); and cough (ACE inhibitors, treated with antibiotics for a "respiratory infection"). The pharmacist's discipline is to ask the drug question before accepting the symptom as a new condition.

### Apply a Structured Causality Assessment Framework

Causality — whether the drug actually caused the event — is rarely certain, and the pharmacist must use a structured framework rather than impressionistic judgment. A practical framework considers: temporal relationship (did the symptom begin after the drug was started or after a dose increase, with a plausible time course for the drug's pharmacology?); biological plausibility (is the symptom a known effect of this drug or drug class, consistent with its mechanism?); dechallenge (did the symptom improve or resolve when the drug was stopped or the dose reduced?); rechallenge (did the symptom recur when the drug was restarted — though rechallenge is ethically constrained for serious reactions); and alternative explanations (has another cause — the disease, another drug, a new condition — been reasonably excluded?). Each factor strengthens or weakens the causal case, and the assessment concludes with a categorization (certain, probable, possible, unlikely) that reflects the strength of the evidence. The framework forces the pharmacist to consider all the evidence rather than anchoring on the first plausible explanation, and it produces a defensible assessment that can be communicated to the prescriber.

### Assess Severity, Seriousness, and the Required Action

Not all ADRs require the same response, and the pharmacist must assess both severity (the intensity of the reaction) and seriousness (whether the reaction meets regulatory criteria: death, life-threatening, hospitalization, persistent disability, congenital anomaly, or an important medical event). A mild, expected reaction (mild nausea from an antibiotic) may warrant continuation with supportive care and counseling; a moderate reaction (a rash that is not progressing) may warrant holding the drug and contacting the prescriber; a serious reaction (anaphylaxis, severe skin reactions like Stevens-Johnson syndrome, hepatotoxicity, severe bleeding, arrhythmia) requires immediate action — stopping the drug, emergency care, and never re-exposing the patient to the causative agent. The pharmacist must distinguish reactions that require immediate permanent avoidance of the drug (serious immunologic reactions, severe skin reactions) from those that may be managed with rechallenge at a lower dose under monitoring (many type A, dose-related reactions), because the wrong categorization either endangers the patient (re-exposing after a serious reaction) or deprives them of a needed drug (permanently avoiding after a mild reaction).

### Document and Report the Reaction Through the Appropriate Channels

Every significant ADR must be documented in the patient's record (so the reaction and the causative drug are flagged for future care), communicated to the prescriber (so the regimen can be adjusted), and, for serious or unexpected reactions, reported to the relevant pharmacovigilance system (the FDA MedWatch program in the United States, or the equivalent national system). Reporting is not optional and not limited to reactions the pharmacist is certain about — the systems depend on reports of suspected reactions, and underreporting is the single greatest limitation of post-marketing surveillance. The pharmacist must report serious reactions even when causality is only possible, must report reactions to new drugs (where the safety profile is still being defined), and must report reactions in populations underrepresented in trials (children, the elderly, pregnant patients). The report must include enough detail (the drug, the dose, the timeline, the reaction, the outcome, the patient characteristics) to be useful, because a report without detail cannot contribute to signal detection.

### Prevent Recurrence Through the Patient's Record and Counseling

An ADR that is identified, assessed, and managed but not recorded in a way that prevents re-exposure will recur at the next prescription, often from a different prescriber who does not know about the prior reaction. The pharmacist must ensure the reaction and the causative drug (with cross-reactive drugs noted — the cephalosporin reaction flags the beta-lactam class, the sulfa reaction flags sulfonamides) are documented in the patient's allergy and ADR record, with the reaction type and severity, so that the pharmacy system and the prescriber are alerted at the next prescription event. The pharmacist must counsel the patient on the reaction (what drug caused it, what symptom to watch for, what to do if prescribed a related drug) so the patient can advocate for themselves, because the patient is often the last check before a re-exposure. The pharmacist must distinguish a true allergy (which requires avoidance) from an intolerance (which may be manageable) in the record, because an inaccurate "allergy" label deprives the patient of first-line drugs for years.

### Respect Scope and Escalation Boundaries

Know where the agent's authority and competence end. When the question requires a license, a specialist's judgment, a final approval, or expertise the agent does not hold, the correct action is to escalate rather than to produce a confident answer that overreaches. Scope discipline protects the recipient from harm caused by an unqualified conclusion and protects the agent from liability. State explicitly when the output is advisory and must be confirmed by the qualified person.

## Common Traps

### The Disease Attribution Default

A new symptom appears in a patient with a chronic disease, and the symptom is attributed to the disease progressing rather than to a drug. The mechanism is that the disease is the salient explanation and the patient already has it. The false signal is that the symptom is consistent with the disease, so the disease must be the cause. The harm is that the actual drug cause is not addressed, the symptom is treated with another drug (the prescribing cascade), and the regimen grows while the original ADR persists. The pharmacist must ask the drug question for every new symptom, particularly in patients with chronic disease where the disease is the easy explanation.

### Impressionistic Causality

The pharmacist forms an impression of whether the drug caused the reaction based on a single factor (often the temporal relationship alone) without considering the full evidence. The mechanism is that one plausible factor feels sufficient. The false signal is that the timing fits, so the drug must be the cause (or does not fit, so it cannot be). The harm is that impressionistic assessment both over-attributes (stopping a needed drug for a symptom that was actually the disease) and under-attributes (continuing a drug that is causing harm), because a single factor is unreliable. The structured framework, considering all factors, is the protection against both errors.

### Re-exposing After a Serious Reaction

A patient with a documented serious reaction to a drug (anaphylaxis, severe skin reaction) is prescribed the same or a cross-reactive drug again, and the pharmacist dispenses it because the reaction is "in the past" or the prescriber ordered it. The mechanism is that the prescription is current and the reaction feels historical. The false signal is that the prescription exists, so it must be safe. The harm is that serious immunologic reactions recur on re-exposure, often more severely, and can be fatal. The pharmacist must check the allergy and ADR record before dispensing and must intervene — contacting the prescriber, refusing to dispense, recommending an alternative — when a serious reaction is documented to the prescribed drug or a cross-reactive class.

### The "Allergy" Label That Is Actually an Intolerance

A patient reports nausea from a drug decades ago, recorded as an "allergy," and the patient is deprived of first-line drugs (a penicillin "allergy" that was actually nausea leads to broader-spectrum antibiotics with more adverse effects and resistance). The mechanism is that the allergy label is treated as an absolute contraindication. The false signal is that the record says allergy, so the drug must be avoided. The harm is the loss of optimal therapy and the use of inferior alternatives, sometimes for decades. The pharmacist must clarify the reaction type when obtaining the history, must distinguish intolerance (manageable, may permit use) from true allergy (requires avoidance), and must update the record so the label reflects the actual reaction.

### Failure to Report Because Causality Is Uncertain

The pharmacist suspects a drug caused a serious reaction but does not report it because they are not certain. The mechanism is that reporting feels appropriate only for confirmed reactions. The false signal is that uncertainty disqualifies the report. The harm is that post-marketing surveillance depends on reports of suspected reactions, and the requirement of certainty suppresses the reports that the system needs to detect signals — particularly for new drugs and rare reactions where individual certainty is rarely achievable. The pharmacist must report suspected serious reactions even at possible causality, because the system aggregates reports to detect patterns no individual could confirm.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- For every new or worsening symptom in a patient on medications, did I ask whether a drug could be the cause before accepting the disease or a new condition as the explanation?
- Did I assess causality using a structured framework (temporal relationship, biological plausibility, dechallenge, rechallenge, alternative explanations) and categorize the assessment (certain, probable, possible, unlikely) rather than relying on impression?
- Did I assess both severity and seriousness, and did I distinguish reactions requiring permanent avoidance (serious immunologic, severe skin) from those manageable with rechallenge at lower dose under monitoring?
- Did I document the reaction in the patient's record, communicate it to the prescriber, and report serious or unexpected reactions to the pharmacovigilance system (FDA MedWatch or equivalent) even at possible causality?
- Did I record the reaction with the causative drug and cross-reactive class, the reaction type, and the severity, distinguishing true allergy from intolerance so the patient is not deprived of first-line drugs based on an inaccurate label?
- Did I counsel the patient on the reaction (the causative drug, the symptom to watch for, what to do if prescribed a related drug) so they can advocate for themselves at future encounters?
- Does the output stay within the agent's scope, deferring final authority, licensed judgment, or specialist sign-off to the qualified person where the question exceeds the agent's competence?
- Is the reasoning documented clearly enough that another practitioner could review the basis and reproduce the conclusion?
