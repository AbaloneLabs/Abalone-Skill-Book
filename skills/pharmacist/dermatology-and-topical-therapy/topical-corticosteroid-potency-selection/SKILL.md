# Topical Corticosteroid Potency Selection

Topical corticosteroids are prescribed by potency class, not by molecule, and the same drug appears in multiple potencies depending on concentration and vehicle. The judgment problem is that potency is driven by four interacting variables (the molecule, its concentration, the vehicle, and the skin site), and a wrong choice is rarely caught at the point of dispensing because the tube looks identical regardless of whether it is a low-potency face preparation or a super-high-potency scalp agent. The harm runs in two directions: under-potency fails to control the disease and erodes adherence, while over-potency causes skin atrophy, telangiectasia, hypopigmentation, rebound flares, and, in children or under occlusion, systemic absorption with hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) suppression. The pharmacist must read the prescription as a potency decision, not just a drug name.

## Core Rules

### Select Potency From the Body Site First

Anatomical skin thickness is the single strongest determinant of how much steroid reaches the dermis, and therefore of effective potency. Thin and highly permeable skin (eyelids, face, neck, intertriginous areas such as axillae and groin, and the scrotum) absorbs steroid many times faster than the extremities, so a molecule that is low-potency on the back becomes high-potency on the face. Reserve low-potency agents (hydrocortisone 1 to 2.5 percent, desonide, hydrocortisone valerate in some classifications) for the face, flexures, and infants. Thick, lichenified, or palmoplantar skin requires high to super-high potency (clobetasol, halobetasol, betamethasone dipropionate augmented) because the stratum corneum blocks penetration of weaker agents. The scalp tolerates and often needs higher potency but in a vehicle that penetrates hair. Always translate the site into an expected potency band before checking whether the chosen molecule fits.

### Match Potency to the Diagnosis and Disease Severity

Potency should follow the condition, not habit. Acute contact dermatitis and localized nummular eczema often resolve with a short course of a mid-potency agent, whereas plaque psoriasis, lichen planus, and lichen simplex chronicus generally require high to super-high potency to penetrate the thickened plaque. Use the lowest potency that controls the disease for the shortest time consistent with response, then step down. For chronic diseases requiring repeated courses, plan a maintenance strategy (weekend pulse dosing, rotation to a lower potency, or a steroid-sparing agent) at the outset rather than allowing open-ended daily high-potency use, which is where most atrophy and HPA suppression originate.

### Account for the Vehicle Because It Changes Effective Potency

Vehicle is not cosmetic; it is a potency modifier. Ointments increase penetration through occlusion and hydration of the stratum corneum, so the same molecule in an ointment is more potent than in a cream, and a foam or solution can approach ointment penetration on hairy skin while remaining cosmetically acceptable. Creams suit weeping or acute intertriginous lesions where an ointment would macerate, but they contain preservatives and may sting. Gels, foams, solutions, lotions, and shampoos are preferred for hairy areas. When a prescriber switches a patient from a cream to an ointment of the same molecule and concentration, the pharmacist should recognize that effective potency has risen and that duration or quantity may need to be reduced to compensate.

### Define Quantity, Duration, and Tapering Explicitly

A prescription for a tube without a quantity, a duration, or application instructions invites overuse. Use the fingertip unit (approximately 0.5 grams covers two adult palms) to translate body surface area into grams needed per application and per course, and dispense a quantity that matches the intended duration. Set a defined treatment course (commonly one to two weeks for acute flares) with a review point, and counsel the patient not to use high-potency agents continuously beyond that window without reassessment. For chronic conditions, agree on a step-down plan: control with the higher potency, then taper to a lower potency or to intermittent weekend use. Patients who are simply handed a tube of clobetasol with refills are the ones who return with striae and telangiectasia.

### Protect Children, Pregnant Patients, and High-Absorption Sites

Children have a higher surface-area-to-mass ratio and thinner skin, so systemic absorption is proportionally greater and HPA suppression occurs at lower potencies and shorter durations. Prefer low-potency agents on the face and flexures of children, limit mid-potency to short courses on the trunk and limbs, and avoid super-high potency in infants unless under specialist supervision. In pregnancy, low to mid potency is preferred where possible; high and super-high potency are used only briefly when benefit clearly outweighs risk, because first-trimester exposure to potent agents has been associated with low birth weight in some studies. Under occlusion (wet wraps, diapers, tight dressings), reclassify the effective potency upward and shorten duration, because occlusion multiplies absorption.

### Screen for Interactions, Contraindications, and Concomitant Steroid Burden

Topical steroids can worsen untreated infection (tinea, candidiasis, impetigo, scabies, rosacea, perioral dermatitis) and a misdiagnosed fungal infection given a steroid will flare dramatically. Confirm the diagnosis is inflammatory before dispensing and flag a request for an antifungal if the presentation is ambiguous. Add the topical steroid to the patient's total corticosteroid burden: a patient already on inhaled or oral steroids for asthma or autoimmune disease has a higher cumulative suppression risk, and the topical contribution is easy to overlook. Counsel on application with clean hands, separation from contact with infants and pets, and avoidance of the eye unless an ophthalmologist has directed periocular use.

## Common Traps

### Defaulting to the Most Familiar Molecule Regardless of Site

A pharmacist or prescriber reaches for hydrocortisone or betamethasone valerate because the name is familiar, and applies it to whatever site is involved without checking whether that molecule at that concentration is appropriate for eyelid versus palm versus scalp. The mechanism is cognitive economy: one remembered drug is easier than consulting a potency chart. The false signal is that the tube was prescribed, so it must be correct. The harm is that a low-potency agent on a thick plaque fails and the patient abandons treatment, or a high-potency agent applied to the face for weeks produces perioral dermatitis, atrophy, and glaucoma risk from periocular absorption. The corrective is to verify site-appropriate potency for every new prescription.

### Treating Potency as a Property of the Molecule Alone

Because a single molecule such as betamethasone or triamcinolone appears across several potency classes depending on concentration, ester, and vehicle, a pharmacist who reads only the molecule may assume a fixed potency. The mechanism is that drug names feel like a stable reference. The false signal is that betamethasone is betamethasone. The harm is that betamethasone dipropionate augmented ointment is super-high potency while betamethasone valerate lotion is mid potency, and confusing them produces either a severe flare from under-treatment or atrophy from over-treatment. The corrective is to read the molecule, the ester, the concentration, and the vehicle together and place the product in its potency class.

### Ignoring Quantity and Refills, Enabling Chronic Overuse

The prescription is verified for drug and dose but not for the quantity dispensed or the number of refills, so a patient receives multiple tubes of a super-high-potency steroid with automatic refills. The mechanism is that verification focuses on correctness of the agent, not on the total exposure. The false signal is that a valid refill schedule is safe. The harm is months of daily high-potency application that produces irreversible striae, telangiectasia, purpura, and biochemical HPA suppression that may take months to recover. The corrective is to confirm that high-potency agents have a defined course and a limited or zero refill plan, with reassessment before continuation.

### Assuming Occlusion and Wet Wraps Are Always Safe Adjuncts

A patient or prescriber adds occlusive dressings or wet wraps to boost effect without recognizing that occlusion multiplies absorption, often by an order of magnitude. The mechanism is that occlusion is taught as a way to make a weak steroid work. The false signal is that more penetration equals better control. The harm is that a mid-potency agent under occlusion behaves like a super-high-potency agent and rapidly produces systemic effects, especially in children with widespread atopic dermatitis. The corrective is to reclassify effective potency upward whenever occlusion is used and to limit the combination to short, specialist-supervised courses.

### Underestimating Systemic Risk in Infants and Over Large Areas

Because topical steroids are applied to the skin, the assumption is that they act locally and carry little systemic risk. The mechanism is the contrast with oral steroids, which are feared, while topical agents are perceived as mild. The false signal is that topical equals safe. The harm is that infants treated over a large body surface area, especially with mid to high potency and occlusion, develop iatrogenic Cushing syndrome, growth failure, and adrenal crisis under stress. The corrective is to calculate the total weekly dose relative to body weight, prefer low potency and limited body surface area in infants, and arrange baseline and follow-up growth and adrenal surveillance for extensive use.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical adult limb or trunk presentation is handled well, but the unusual site such as the eyelid, scrotum, or an open wound, or the unusual patient such as a neonate, a pregnant patient, or someone with rosacea, is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is safe while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- Did I translate the prescribed molecule, ester, concentration, and vehicle into a potency class, rather than assuming potency from the drug name alone?
- Is the chosen potency appropriate for the body site, recognizing that thin and intertriginous skin absorbs far more and that thick palmoplantar skin requires higher potency?
- Does the potency match the diagnosis and severity, with a plan to step down once control is achieved rather than open-ended daily use?
- Did I verify the quantity dispensed using a fingertip-unit estimate and set a defined course duration with a review point?
- Did I confirm the diagnosis is inflammatory and not an untreated fungal, bacterial, or parasitic infection that a steroid would worsen?
- For children, pregnant patients, large body surface area, or occlusion, did I reclassify effective potency upward and arrange appropriate surveillance?
- Did I limit or remove refills on high and super-high potency agents so that continuation requires reassessment?
- Did I add the topical steroid to the patient's total corticosteroid burden and check for additive suppression risk?
