# Topical Vehicle and Formulation Choice

The vehicle is the base in which an active drug is delivered, and in topical therapy it is not an inert filler but a determinant of efficacy, tolerability, and adherence as important as the drug itself. The judgment problem is that prescribers often write for the molecule and let the default vehicle fall out of the electronic record, while the patient's disease state, skin site, and daily life decide whether that vehicle works. A correct drug in the wrong vehicle fails: an ointment on an oozing acute eczema macerates the skin, a gel on fissured skin stings so the patient stops using it, and a greasy ointment on the scalp is never applied because it ruins the hair. The pharmacist must read the vehicle as a clinical decision and intervene when it does not fit.

## Core Rules

### Match the Vehicle to the Acuity and Surface Condition of the Lesion

The state of the skin surface determines which vehicle hydrates or dries, soothes or irritates. Acute, weeping, or exudative lesions need drying, cooling formulations such as wet dressings, lotions, or aqueous creams that allow evaporation; an occlusive ointment traps moisture and macerates an already weeping surface, worsening the condition. Subacute lesions with scaling and mild erythema suit creams or gels that hydrate without full occlusion. Chronic, dry, lichenified lesions need the emollient and occlusive effect of ointments to hydrate the stratum corneum and drive the active in. The pharmacist should confirm that the vehicle matches the phase of the lesion and should question an ointment on an acute weeping eruption or a drying lotion on a chronic fissured plaque.

### Use Occlusive Vehicles to Increase Penetration and Potency

Ointments, and to a lesser degree heavy creams, occlude the skin and hydrate the stratum corneum, which increases drug penetration several-fold compared with a cream of the same concentration. This is therapeutically useful for thick, chronic lesions but it also raises effective potency and systemic absorption, so the same molecule in an ointment behaves more powerfully than in a cream. When a prescriber switches a patient from a cream to an ointment to improve response, the pharmacist should recognize that the patient is now receiving a more potent exposure and may need a shorter duration or a smaller quantity. Foams and certain enhanced vehicles can achieve ointment-like penetration with better cosmetic acceptability, which is valuable for hairy areas and for patients who reject greasy bases.

### Select Hair- and Cosmetically-Sensitive Sites for Foams, Solutions, Gels, and Lotions

The scalp, beard, chest, and back are covered with hair, and an ointment or heavy cream mats the hair and is cosmetically unacceptable, which guarantees non-adherence. Foams, solutions, gels, lotions, and shampoos are designed for these sites: they penetrate hair, dry quickly, and leave no residue. The face also demands cosmetic acceptability; patients will not use a preparation that shines or feels greasy for weeks. Matching the vehicle to the patient's tolerance is a clinical act, because a vehicle the patient will not apply is a vehicle that does not work, regardless of the drug it carries. When a patient reports non-adherence, the first question is often about the vehicle, not the drug.

### Anticipate Vehicle-Specific Irritation and Sensitization

Creams, lotions, and gels contain preservatives, emulsifiers, propylene glycol, and fragrances that ointments usually lack, and these excipients cause contact sensitization, stinging, and burning in a meaningful fraction of patients, especially on damaged skin. Propylene glycol in particular stings on fissured or eroded skin and is a common reason for apparent treatment failure. Patients with sensitive skin, atopy, or known contact allergy may need a preservative-free ointment even when a cream would otherwise be convenient. The pharmacist should identify the likely irritant when a patient reports stinging and should consider a switch to an ointment, a different base, or a compounded preservative-free formulation rather than assuming the active drug is the problem.

### Quantify the Prescription in Meaningful Units and Counsel on Application

A tube without a defined quantity or application instruction leads both to overuse and to under-adherence, because the patient guesses how much to apply. Translate the affected area into grams using the fingertip-unit rule, where one fingertip unit is roughly 0.5 grams and covers two adult palms, and dispense a quantity that covers the intended course. Counsel on application to dry skin unless otherwise directed, on washing hands before and after, on the order of application when multiple products are used (medicated before emollient, with a waiting interval), and on avoiding mucous membranes and the eye. For combination products, confirm the patient understands whether the product is for a defined area and course, because fixed combinations can over-treat one component while under-treating another.

### Reconcile Vehicle With Patient Lifestyle, Cost, and Adherence

The pharmacologically optimal vehicle is useless if the patient cannot or will not use it. A manual worker who washes hands repeatedly cannot maintain an ointment on the hands during the day; a patient who shares a household may not accept a malodorous preparation; an expensive branded foam may sit unfilled because of cost. Adherence to topical therapy is notoriously poor, and the vehicle is the largest modifiable factor. The pharmacist should ask about daily routine, acceptability, and cost, and should propose a vehicle the patient will actually use, even if it is pharmacologically slightly less optimal, because a used lesser vehicle outperforms an unused ideal one.

## Common Traps

### Choosing the Vehicle by Default Rather Than by Lesion and Site

The electronic record defaults to a cream or ointment and the prescription is verified without considering whether that vehicle suits the lesion phase or body site. The mechanism is workflow efficiency: the default is fast and looks complete. The false signal is that the prescription was generated, so the vehicle must be acceptable. The harm is that an ointment on an acute weeping eczema macerates the skin and prolongs the flare, or a drying lotion on a chronic fissured plaque cracks the skin further and the patient stops treatment. The corrective is to confirm the vehicle against the lesion phase and site for every new topical prescription.

### Assuming Creams Are Milder and Therefore Safer Than Ointments

Because creams feel lighter and less occlusive, prescribers and patients assume they are gentler and safer. The mechanism is the intuitive link between greasiness and strength. The false signal is that a lighter base means a milder effect. The harm is twofold: the cream under-treats a thick chronic lesion because it does not penetrate, and its preservatives and propylene glycol sting and sensitize damaged skin that an ointment would have tolerated. The corrective is to treat the vehicle as a penetration and tolerability modifier, not as a strength indicator, and to match occlusion to the lesion.

### Dismissing Patient Reports of Stinging as Drug Allergy

A patient reports burning after applying a topical agent and the conclusion is allergy to the active drug, leading to discontinuation of an effective treatment. The mechanism is that stinging feels like an allergic reaction. The false signal is that discomfort equals intolerance to the drug. The harm is that the actual culprit, usually propylene glycol, a preservative, or the alcohol base of a gel or solution, is never identified, and the patient is denied a useful drug class or switched unnecessarily. The corrective is to consider vehicle excipients as the cause of stinging, especially on damaged skin, and to trial a different vehicle or a preservative-free ointment before abandoning the drug.

### Overlooking That a Vehicle Switch Changes Effective Potency

A patient is switched from a cream to an ointment of the same molecule and concentration to improve response, and the prescription is continued at the same quantity and duration. The mechanism is that the molecule and concentration are unchanged, so exposure is assumed unchanged. The false signal is that identical labeling means identical effect. The harm is that the ointment delivers substantially more drug through occlusion, raising effective potency and systemic absorption, which can produce atrophy or hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal suppression, especially in children and under occlusion. The corrective is to re-evaluate quantity and duration whenever the vehicle changes in the occlusive direction.

### Ignoring Cosmetic Acceptability and Cost as Drivers of Non-Adherence

The vehicle is selected for pharmacologic perfection and the patient is handed a greasy, malodorous, or expensive preparation with no discussion of daily life. The mechanism is that adherence is treated as the patient's responsibility rather than as a prescribing variable. The false signal is that the right drug has been prescribed, so success depends on the patient. The harm is silent non-adherence: the tube sits unused, the disease appears refractory, and the prescriber escalates to systemic therapy when a different vehicle would have sufficed. The corrective is to ask about acceptability and cost and to choose a vehicle the patient will actually apply.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The standard adult trunk or limb is handled well, but the unusual site such as the scalp, eyelid, nail fold, or mucous membrane, or the unusual patient such as an infant, an atopic with multiple contact allergies, or a patient on occlusive dressings, is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is safe while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- Did I confirm the vehicle matches the lesion phase, using drying formulations for acute weeping lesions and occlusive ointments for chronic lichenified skin?
- Is the vehicle appropriate for the body site, with foams, solutions, gels, and lotions for hairy areas and cosmetic acceptability for the face?
- When a vehicle was switched toward greater occlusion, did I recognize that effective potency and systemic absorption rose and reassess quantity and duration?
- Did I consider vehicle excipients, especially propylene glycol, preservatives, and alcohol bases, as the cause of reported stinging before blaming the active drug?
- Did I quantify the prescription in fingertip units and counsel on application technique, order of products, and avoidance of mucous membranes?
- Did I ask about daily routine, cosmetic acceptability, and cost, and choose a vehicle the patient will actually use?
- For combination products, did I confirm the patient understands the intended area and duration, so that one component is not over-applied?
- Did I verify that a vehicle chosen for an infant, a pregnant patient, or an atopic with contact allergies avoids known irritants and sensitizers?
