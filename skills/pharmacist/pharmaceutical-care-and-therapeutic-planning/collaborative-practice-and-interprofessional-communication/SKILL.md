# Collaborative Practice and Interprofessional Communication

The pharmacist's clinical impact is mediated through communication — the recommendation to the prescriber, the coordination with the nurse, the counseling of the patient, the handoff to the next provider — and the quality of that communication determines whether the pharmacist's expertise reaches the patient or is lost in the handoff. The danger is that the communication is treated as a notification (the message sent) rather than as a clinical intervention (the recommendation designed to be understood, accepted, and acted upon). A recommendation buried in a long note that the prescriber does not read, a concern expressed so tentatively that it is dismissed, a collaborative practice agreement (CPA) exceeded because the boundary was not clear, a handoff that drops the critical information — each represents a communication failure that prevents the pharmacist's work from producing the patient benefit it was meant to produce. The pharmacist's discipline is to communicate with the clinical precision and the persuasive structure that makes the recommendation actionable, to operate within the collaborative scope that is defined, and to escalate the concerns that exceed the communication channel.

## Core Rules

### Structure Therapeutic Recommendations for Clarity, Evidence, and Action

A therapeutic recommendation to a prescriber must be structured so the prescriber can understand the issue, evaluate the rationale, and act on the recommendation without further investigation. The pharmacist must structure the recommendation with: the issue (the specific drug-therapy problem identified, stated concisely), the evidence (the clinical data that supports the issue — the lab value, the interaction, the symptom, the guideline), the recommendation (the specific action proposed — the dose change, the drug change, the discontinuation, the addition), the rationale (the clinical reasoning that connects the evidence to the recommendation, including the guideline or the evidence base), and the follow-up (the proposed monitoring and the timeframe). The recommendation must be specific (the exact drug, dose, and instruction, not "consider adjusting"), must be evidence-based (the guideline citation, the study, the mechanism), and must be patient-specific (the recommendation that fits this patient's conditions, not the generic guideline). The pharmacist must communicate in the channel the prescriber uses and expects (the electronic message, the phone call, the note, the face-to-face), must match the urgency to the clinical significance (the phone call for the urgent interaction, the message for the routine recommendation), and must close the loop (the confirmation that the recommendation was received and the action taken).

### Operate Within the Collaborative Practice Agreement Scope and Boundaries

A collaborative practice agreement (CPA) delegates specific clinical authority to the pharmacist (the initiation, adjustment, or discontinuation of drug therapy under a defined protocol), and the pharmacist must operate within the scope the CPA defines. The pharmacist must know the specific authority granted by each CPA (which drugs, which conditions, which actions — initiate, adjust, discontinue; which monitoring, which patient populations), must operate within it (the CPA-authority action taken independently, with documentation), and must not exceed it (the action outside the CPA requires the prescriber's order). The pharmacist must recognize the CPA boundaries: the patient who does not meet the CPA's inclusion criteria, the drug that is not on the CPA's drug list, the action that is not permitted by the CPA, the condition that has changed and falls outside the CPA's scope. When the boundary is reached, the pharmacist must escalate to the prescriber (the recommendation, the request for the order, the referral) rather than exceed the CPA, because the CPA is the legal authority and exceeding it is practicing without authority. The pharmacist must document the CPA-authority actions (the assessment, the action, the protocol reference, the outcome) and must communicate the actions to the prescriber per the CPA's terms (the routine notification, the periodic review), because the CPA does not eliminate the prescriber's oversight; it delegates the operational authority within the oversight.

### Coordinate Care Across the Interprofessional Team to Prevent Fragmentation

The patient's care involves multiple providers (the prescriber, the specialist, the nurse, the care manager, the pharmacist, the social worker), and the coordination across the team prevents the fragmentation that produces conflicting plans, duplicate therapies, missed monitoring, and confused patients. The pharmacist must know the team (who is responsible for what — the diagnosis, the prescribing, the administration, the monitoring, the education, the coordination), must communicate the pharmacist's role and actions to the team (the recommendation made, the intervention delivered, the monitoring planned), and must coordinate the overlapping responsibilities (the shared monitoring, the handoff at the transition, the resolution of the conflicting recommendation). The pharmacist must recognize the points where the coordination is most fragile (the transition between settings, the handoff between providers, the addition of a new specialist, the change in the care plan) and must reinforce the coordination at those points (the medication reconciliation, the communicated plan, the documented handoff). The pharmacist must avoid the siloed practice (the pharmacist's plan that is not communicated, the recommendation that conflicts with the specialist's plan, the monitoring that is duplicated or missed) that the coordination is meant to prevent.

### Escalate Clinical Concerns With the Appropriate Urgency and Channel

Some clinical concerns exceed the routine communication and require escalation — the urgent or the serious issue that needs immediate attention, the disagreement that needs resolution, the system gap that needs a process change. The pharmacist must recognize the concerns that warrant escalation: the urgent safety issue (the severe interaction, the contraindication, the dispensing error that reached the patient), the unresolved disagreement (the recommendation refused without a clinical rationale, the therapy continued despite the evidence), the pattern that indicates a system problem (the recurring error type, the protocol gap, the training need). The pharmacist must escalate with the appropriate urgency (the immediate phone call for the urgent safety issue, the formal message for the disagreement, the committee referral for the system problem) and through the appropriate channel (the prescriber for the therapy issue, the medical director for the disagreement, the quality committee for the system problem). The pharmacist must document the escalation (the concern, the channel, the recipient, the response) and must follow through to the resolution, because the escalation that is initiated but not followed through produces no resolution and the concern persists.

### Document the Communication for Continuity, Accountability, and Quality

The communication — the recommendation, the coordination, the escalation — must be documented for continuity (the next provider can see what was communicated and what the response was), for accountability (the pharmacist's clinical judgment and actions are recorded), and for quality (the communication patterns and the outcomes can be reviewed and improved). The pharmacist must document the recommendation (the issue, the evidence, the recommendation, the rationale, the follow-up), the response (the prescriber's acceptance, modification, or refusal, with the rationale), the coordination (the team communication, the handoff, the shared plan), and the escalation (the concern, the channel, the response). The documentation must be in the patient's record (the clinical communication) and in the pharmacy's record (the intervention log), must be timely (at the point of the communication, not deferred), and must be accurate (the actual communication, not the intended). The pharmacist must recognize that the undocumented communication is, for practical purposes, a communication that did not occur (because it cannot be demonstrated, retrieved, or continued), and the documentation is the completion of the communication.

## Common Traps

### Burying the Recommendation in a Long Note or Message

The recommendation embedded in a lengthy note or message (the comprehensive review with the recommendation in the middle) is not seen by the prescriber, who skims or does not read the full text, and the recommendation is not acted upon. The trap is that the comprehensive note feels thorough (all the information is there), so the structure is not optimized for the action, and the recommendation is lost in the volume. The harm is the inaction — the drug-therapy problem persists because the recommendation was not received, and the patient does not get the benefit of the pharmacist's assessment. The fix is to lead with the recommendation (the specific action first, the supporting evidence second, the comprehensive review as the appendix), to use the subject line or the heading to flag the action needed, and to match the length to the channel (the concise message for the routine recommendation, the detailed note for the complex case).

### Exceeding the CPA Scope Because the Action "Makes Clinical Sense"

The CPA defines the scope of the pharmacist's independent authority, and the action outside the scope (the drug not on the CPA list, the patient outside the inclusion criteria, the action not permitted) requires the prescriber's order, even when the action makes clinical sense. The trap is that the action feels appropriate (the dose adjustment that is clearly indicated, the drug that is clearly the better choice), so the pharmacist takes it independently, and the CPA boundary is exceeded. The harm is the practice outside authority (the legal exposure, the liability void, the board violation), and the harm occurs even when the clinical decision was correct, because the authority boundary exists independently of the clinical appropriateness. The fix is to know the CPA scope precisely, to operate within it, and to escalate to the prescriber for the action outside the scope, treating the boundary as the legal control rather than the clinical suggestion.

### Communicating So Tentatively That the Concern Is Dismissed

The pharmacist's concern, expressed so tentatively ("just wanted to mention," "you might consider," "not sure if this matters") that the prescriber perceives it as a minor or optional note, is dismissed, and the underlying clinical issue persists. The trap is that the tentative expression feels respectful (not challenging the prescriber), so it is the default style, and the clinical significance is understated, and the concern is not weighted appropriately. The harm is the unresolved issue (the interaction, the contraindication, the inappropriate dose) that the tentative communication did not surface effectively, and the patient who suffers the consequence. The fix is to communicate the concern with the clinical significance it warrants (the specific issue, the evidence, the recommendation, the urgency), to be respectful without being tentative, and to escalate (the phone call, the repeated message, the formal escalation) when the concern is not addressed.

### Failing to Close the Loop on a Recommendation

The recommendation is communicated (the message sent, the note written), and the pharmacist considers the communication complete, but the prescriber's response and the action taken are not confirmed, and the recommendation may not have been received, read, or acted upon. The trap is that the message sent feels like the completion (the communication was made), so the follow-up is not done, and the open loop (the recommendation without the confirmed response) persists. The harm is the inaction that is not recognized — the pharmacist assumes the recommendation was acted upon, the prescriber did not see it, and the drug-therapy problem persists without the pharmacist's knowledge. The fix is to close the loop (the confirmation of the receipt, the response, the action), to follow up when the response is not received within the expected timeframe, and to escalate when the action is not taken, treating the closed loop as the completion of the communication.

### Communicating Only to the Pharmacy Record and Not to the Patient's Care Team

The pharmacist documents the recommendation, the intervention, and the follow-up in the pharmacy record, but does not communicate it to the patient's care team (the prescriber, the specialist, the nurse), and the team is unaware of the pharmacist's assessment and actions. The trap is that the pharmacy documentation feels like the completion (the work is recorded), so the team communication is skipped, and the care plan is fragmented (the team's plan does not include the pharmacist's recommendation, the monitoring is duplicated or missed, the patient receives conflicting information). The harm is the uncoordinated care that the communication was meant to prevent, and the patient who is caught between the uncoordinated providers. The fix is to communicate the pharmacist's assessment, recommendation, and actions to the care team (the prescriber, the relevant specialists, the care manager), to document the communication, and to integrate the pharmacist's plan with the team's plan, treating the team communication as the implementation of the pharmacist's work rather than an optional notification.

## Self-Check

- For each therapeutic recommendation, did I structure it for clarity and action (the issue, the evidence, the specific recommendation, the rationale, the follow-up), lead with the action, and match the channel and urgency to the significance?
- If operating under a CPA, did I confirm the action is within the CPA scope (the drug, the condition, the action, the patient population), document the CPA-authority action, and escalate to the prescriber for actions outside the scope?
- Did I coordinate with the interprofessional team — communicating the pharmacist's role and actions, reinforcing the coordination at the fragile points (transitions, handoffs, new specialists), and avoiding the siloed practice?
- For clinical concerns warranting escalation (urgent safety issues, unresolved disagreements, system patterns), did I escalate with the appropriate urgency and channel, document the escalation, and follow through to the resolution?
- Did I close the loop on each recommendation (the confirmation of the receipt, the response, the action), and did I follow up or escalate when the response was not received?
- Did I document the communication (the recommendation, the response, the coordination, the escalation) in the patient's record and the pharmacy's intervention log, timely and accurately?
- Did I communicate the pharmacist's assessment and actions to the patient's care team, not only to the pharmacy record, so the care plan is integrated rather than fragmented?
