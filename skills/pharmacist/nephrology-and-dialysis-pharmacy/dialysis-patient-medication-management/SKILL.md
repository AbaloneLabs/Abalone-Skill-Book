# Dialysis Patient Medication Management

The dialysis patient is the most medication-intensive patient in the pharmacy, often taking ten or more drugs across cardiovascular, anemia, mineral, infection, and symptomatic indications, and the dialysis itself alters the pharmacokinetics of many of those drugs in ways that are not captured by any standard dosing table. The judgment problem is that a dose that was correct for the patient with stage four chronic kidney disease becomes wrong once dialysis begins, because dialysis removes some drugs entirely, removes others partially, and leaves many untouched, and the prescriber's list is rarely re-evaluated against the new clearance reality. The harm is twofold: the drug that is removed by dialysis and never supplemented leaves the patient under-treated (the missed antibiotic dose, the subtherapeutic anticonvulsant), and the drug that is not removed but continues to be dosed for a kidney that no longer functions accumulates between sessions and reaches toxicity (the gabapentin sedation, the beta-blocker bradycardia). The pharmacist's discipline is to classify every drug by its dialyzability, to schedule the dosing around the dialysis schedule, and to rebuild the regimen for the dialysis patient rather than carrying forward the pre-dialysis list.

## Core Rules

### Classify Every Drug by Its Dialyzability Before Scheduling the Dose

The first question for every drug on the dialysis patient's list is whether dialysis removes it, and the answer determines the dosing strategy. The dialyzability of a drug depends on its molecular weight, its water solubility, its protein binding, and its volume of distribution, and these properties vary widely across the common drugs. The pharmacist must consult the authoritative dialysis dosing references (the drug label, the renal dosing handbook, the dialysis-specific references) for each drug, and must classify it as significantly removed by hemodialysis (requiring a supplemental or post-dialysis dose), partially removed (sometimes requiring adjustment), or not removed (dosed independently of dialysis). The pharmacist must recognize that peritoneal dialysis removes drugs differently and generally less than hemodialysis, that the high-flux dialyzers remove larger molecules than the older low-flux membranes, and that the duration and flow rates of the session affect the removal. The pharmacist must not assume that a drug is or is not dialyzed based on the class alone, because drugs within a class differ (among the beta-blockers, atenolol is significantly removed while carvedilol is not), and must verify each drug individually.

### Schedule the Dosing Around the Dialysis Session for the Removed Drugs

The drugs that are significantly removed by hemodialysis must be scheduled so that the dose is not wasted by the session and so that the therapeutic level is maintained. The pharmacist must identify the drugs that require the post-dialysis supplemental dose (the dose given after the session to replace what was removed, such as certain antibiotics and anticonvulsants), and must coordinate the timing with the dialysis unit so that the dose is administered after the session rather than before. For the drugs that are given independently of dialysis but are partially removed, the pharmacist must ensure that the dose on the dialysis day is timed to avoid the immediate pre-dialysis window, so that the drug is not cleared before it acts. The pharmacist must recognize that the thrice-weekly hemodialysis schedule creates a long interdialytic interval (the two-day gap over the weekend), during which the fluid, the potassium, and the blood pressure can rise, and the medications that manage these (the diuretics in the residual kidney function, the potassium binders) must be adjusted for that interval. The pharmacist must coordinate with the dialysis unit, because the unit often administers the intravenous drugs (the iron, the vitamin D analogs, the antibiotics during the session) and holds the oral drugs, and the regimen must be consistent across the in-unit and the at-home settings.

### Rebuild the Regimen for the Dialysis Patient Rather Than Carrying Forward the Pre-Dialysis List

The transition to dialysis is the moment to rebuild the medication regimen, because the pre-dialysis list was built for a patient with residual kidney function and different goals, and much of it becomes unnecessary, unsafe, or redundant. The pharmacist must review every drug for its ongoing indication and safety in the dialysis patient: the renin-angiotensin inhibitors that were kidney-protective in the earlier disease may be continued for the cardiovascular and the antiproteinuric benefit but require the potassium monitoring, the diuretics may be retained for the residual kidney function but are often ineffective in the anuric patient, the oral hypoglycemics require re-evaluation (the metformin is generally contraindicated, the sulfonylureas accumulate, the SGLT2 inhibitors are not initiated in dialysis), and the statin therapy is generally not initiated in dialysis but may be continued if already established. The pharmacist must identify and discontinue the drugs that are no longer indicated, the drugs that accumulate to harm, and the duplicate therapies, and must rebuild the regimen around the dialysis-specific needs: the anemia management, the mineral and bone disorder management, the cardiovascular protection, the infection prophylaxis, and the symptom control. The deprescribing is as important as the prescribing, because the dialysis patient's pill burden is a primary driver of the non-adherence and the adverse events.

### Manage the Dialysis-Specific Medication Classes With Precision

The dialysis patient takes several medication classes that are specific to the end-stage renal disease, and each requires the precise management. For the anemia, the erythropoiesis-stimulating agents and the iron are titrated to the target hemoglobin range, avoiding both the under-treatment (the symptomatic anemia, the transfusion) and the over-treatment (the thrombosis, the cardiovascular risk at the high hemoglobin). For the mineral and bone disorder, the phosphate binders are taken with meals to bind the dietary phosphate, the active vitamin D analogs and the calcimimetics manage the secondary hyperparathyroidism, and the balance of the calcium, the phosphate, and the parathyroid hormone must be managed together to avoid both the vascular calcification and the adynamic bone disease. For the anticoagulation, the dialysis circuit often requires the heparin during the session, and the systemic anticoagulation (for the atrial fibrillation, the thrombosis) requires the drug selection that accounts for the dialysis clearance (the warfarin is the traditional choice, the apixaban is used with the caution and the dose adjustment, the dabigatran and the rivaroxaban are generally avoided). The pharmacist must know the evidence and the cautions for each class and must manage the targets and the interactions.

### Address the Polypharmacy, the Adherence, and the Drug Interactions

The dialysis patient's pill burden is among the highest of any patient population, and the adherence and the interaction risks scale with the burden. The pharmacist must reconcile the medications at every encounter, because the dialysis patient sees multiple providers (the nephrologist, the cardiologist, the primary care, the dialysis unit, the vascular access surgeon) and the lists diverge. The pharmacist must identify the drug interactions, particularly the phosphate binders that bind many co-administered drugs (the antibiotics, the levothyroxine, the immunosuppressants) and must be separated in time, and the interactions among the cardiovascular and the symptomatic drugs. The pharmacist must address the adherence through the simplification, the pill organizers, the coordination with the dialysis unit for the in-unit administration, and the counseling that accounts for the patient's fatigue, the financial burden, and the complex schedule. The pharmacist must also screen for the over-the-counter and the herbal products that are unsafe in dialysis (the NSAIDs, the magnesium-containing antacids, the potassium-containing salt substitutes, the certain herbal products).

### Respect Scope and Escalation Boundaries

Know where the agent's authority and competence end. The dialysis regimen changes, the anemia and the mineral disorder titration, and the management of the dialysis complications require the nephrologist and the dialysis team. When the question requires a license, a specialist's judgment, a final approval, or expertise the agent does not hold, the correct action is to escalate rather than to produce a confident answer that overreaches. Scope discipline protects the recipient from harm caused by an unqualified conclusion and protects the agent from liability. State explicitly when the output is advisory and must be confirmed by the qualified person.

## Common Traps

### Treating the Pre-Dialysis Dose as Correct After Dialysis Begins

The pharmacist continues the medication regimen from the pre-dialysis stage without re-evaluating the doses against the dialysis clearance, and the drugs that are removed by dialysis are under-dosed while the drugs that are not removed accumulate. The mechanism is that the list was working before, so it must still be correct. The false signal is that the regimen is established and stable. The harm is that the dialysis removes the drugs differently than the failing kidney did, so the antibiotic that was therapeutic is now subtherapeutic after the session, and the gabapentin that was adjusted is now accumulating between the sessions, and the patient suffers both the under-treatment and the toxicity simultaneously. The pharmacist must re-evaluate every drug when the dialysis begins.

### Assuming the Dialyzability From the Drug Class

The pharmacist assumes that all the drugs in a class are dialyzed the same way, and applies the dosing strategy of one drug to another. The mechanism is that the class feels uniform. The false signal is that the beta-blockers or the antibiotics behave the same within the class. The harm is that the dialyzability varies widely within a class (the atenolol is significantly removed while the carvedilol is not, the vancomycin is removed while the linezolid is not), and the wrong assumption produces either the wasted pre-dialysis dose or the uncorrected accumulation, depending on the direction of the error. The pharmacist must verify each drug individually against the dialysis reference.

### Giving the Removed Drug Immediately Before the Dialysis Session

The pharmacist schedules a dialyzed drug for the morning of the dialysis day, and the session clears the drug before it can act. The mechanism is that the morning dose is the standard schedule. The false signal is that the dose was given on time. The harm is that the drug is removed by the session that follows, so the patient receives no therapeutic effect from that dose, and for the antibiotics and the anticonvulsants this produces the subtherapeutic level and the treatment failure or the seizure. The pharmacist must time the dialyzed drugs to avoid the pre-session window and must coordinate the post-dialysis supplemental dose.

### Continuing the Drugs That Are No Longer Indicated or Safe in Dialysis

The pharmacist carries forward the metformin, the NSAID, the high-dose diuretic in the anuric patient, or the duplicate therapies from the pre-dialysis list, without recognizing that the dialysis patient's physiology has changed. The mechanism is that the drug was prescribed for a reason, and the reason was not re-examined. The false signal is that the drug is still on the list, so it must still be needed. The harm is that the metformin accumulates to the lactic acidosis, the NSAID worsens the residual function and the cardiovascular risk, the diuretic is ineffective and adds to the pill burden, and the duplicates interact. The pharmacist must deprescribe the drugs that are no longer indicated or safe.

### Ignoring the Phosphate Binder Interactions

The pharmacist schedules the phosphate binders with the other oral medications at the mealtime, and the binders adsorb the co-administered drugs and reduce their absorption. The mechanism is that the binders are taken with meals, and the other drugs are also taken with meals. The false signal is that the mealtime dosing is convenient and correct for both. The harm is that the phosphate binders (the sevelamer, the lanthanum, the calcium acetate) bind the antibiotics, the levothyroxine, the immunosuppressants, and the other drugs, reducing their efficacy and producing the treatment failure that is attributed to the disease rather than to the interaction. The pharmacist must separate the binders from the interacting drugs by at least one to two hours.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- Did I classify every drug on the dialysis patient's list by its dialyzability (significantly removed, partially removed, not removed) using the authoritative reference, and did I verify each drug individually rather than assuming from the class?
- For the drugs that are significantly removed by hemodialysis, did I arrange the post-dialysis supplemental dose or the timing that avoids the pre-session window, and did I coordinate with the dialysis unit?
- Did I re-evaluate the entire regimen when the dialysis began, deprescribing the drugs that are no longer indicated or unsafe (the metformin, the NSAIDs, the ineffective diuretics, the duplicates) rather than carrying forward the pre-dialysis list?
- Did I manage the dialysis-specific classes (the anemia agents, the phosphate binders, the vitamin D analogs, the calcimimetics, the anticoagulation) to their targets, avoiding both the under-treatment and the over-treatment?
- Did I address the phosphate binder interactions by separating the binders from the co-administered oral drugs by at least one to two hours?
- Did I reconcile the medications across the multiple providers (the nephrologist, the cardiologist, the dialysis unit) and screen for the over-the-counter and the herbal products that are unsafe in dialysis (the NSAIDs, the magnesium antacids, the potassium salt substitutes)?
- Does the output stay within the agent's scope, deferring the regimen changes, the anemia and mineral disorder titration, and the complication management to the nephrologist and the dialysis team where the question exceeds the agent's competence?
- Is the dialysis regimen documented clearly enough that the dialysis unit, the nephrologist, and the patient can follow the schedule and reproduce the decisions?
