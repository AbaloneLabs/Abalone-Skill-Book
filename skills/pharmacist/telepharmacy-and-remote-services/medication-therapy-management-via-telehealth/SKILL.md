# Medication Therapy Management via Telehealth

Medication therapy management (MTM) is a clinical service whose value depends on a thorough, face-to-face-equivalent conversation with the patient about every medication they take, and telehealth changes both the reach and the risk of that conversation. Telehealth extends MTM to patients who cannot come to the pharmacy — the homebound, the rural, the working adult who cannot take time off — and that reach is the service's promise. The danger is that the remote channel degrades the quality of the encounter in ways the pharmacist does not notice: the audio-only call cannot show the pill bottles the patient is actually taking, the video visit cannot capture the supplements in the kitchen cabinet, the patient at home is distracted by family or work and gives less complete answers, and the technical friction of the platform causes the patient to disengage before the review is complete. The result is an MTM encounter that is documented as complete but that missed the duplicate therapy, the adherence problem, or the adverse effect that was the reason to do the review. The pharmacist's discipline is to deliver the five core MTM elements with the same rigor remotely as in person, to use the channel's strengths while compensating for its limits, to document and bill accurately, and to measure whether the service actually resolved drug-related problems rather than merely counting completed visits.

## Core Rules

### Deliver All Five Core MTM Elements, Adapted to the Channel

MTM is defined by five core elements: medication therapy review (MTR), personal medication record (PMR), medication action plan (MAP), intervention and referral, and documentation and follow-up. A telehealth MTM service is not a lighter version of MTM; it must deliver all five elements, adapted to the remote channel. The MTR is the comprehensive review of all prescription, over-the-counter, herbal, and supplement medications, and in a telehealth visit the pharmacist must explicitly ask the patient to gather and show or read every bottle and package, because the visual cue of the medication shelf is absent. The PMR is the consolidated, patient-friendly record of all medications with indications and instructions, and it must be provided to the patient through a channel they can access (secure portal, mail, email with consent). The MAP is the patient-facing action plan with to-do items, and it must be written in plain language and confirmed by teach-back. Intervention and referral must be completed for each drug-related problem identified, with communication to the prescriber. Documentation and follow-up close the loop and schedule the next contact. Skipping any element to fit the visit time degrades the service to something other than MTM.

### Compensate for the Limits of Audio-Only and Video Encounters

The telehealth channel limits what the pharmacist can observe, and the workflow must compensate. In an audio-only call, the pharmacist cannot see the patient's medications, their pill organizer, their injection technique, or non-verbal cues of confusion or distress; the pharmacist must ask the patient to read labels, to describe their organizer, and to demonstrate technique verbally, and must use structured questions to compensate for the missing visual information. In a video visit, the pharmacist can see bottles and technique if the patient positions the camera, but resolution and lighting limit detail, and the pharmacist must ask the patient to hold labels close and to show each bottle individually. The pharmacist must recognize that patients under-report medications they do not consider "real" drugs (over-the-counter, herbal, as-needed, samples, borrowed) and must ask explicitly for each category, because these are frequent sources of interactions and duplications. When the channel cannot support an adequate review (for example, a patient with complex therapy on a poor audio connection), the pharmacist must reschedule, switch channels, or refer to an in-person visit rather than document an incomplete review as complete.

### Build and Deliver the Personal Medication Record and Action Plan

The PMR and MAP are the tangible outputs the patient takes from the encounter, and they must be accurate, complete, and accessible. The PMR must include every medication the patient is actually taking — not only the prescribed list — with the drug name, dose, route, frequency, indication, and the prescriber, and must note allergies and adverse drug reactions. Discrepancies between the prescribed list and what the patient actually takes (a stopped medication, a doubled dose, a borrowed pill) are the most common findings of an MTR and must be reconciled and communicated to the prescriber. The MAP must list the patient's action items in plain language (for example, "take your lisinopril in the morning," "call your doctor about the leg swelling"), and the pharmacist must confirm by teach-back that the patient understands each item. The PMR and MAP must be delivered through a channel the patient can use — a patient portal, secure email with consent, postal mail, or a printed copy at pickup — and the pharmacist must verify receipt, because a document trapped in a portal the patient cannot access is of no value.

### Document and Bill Accurately Under Part D and CPT Codes

MTM documentation and billing must reflect the service actually delivered and must comply with the applicable payer rules. Medicare Part D MTM programs require eligible beneficiaries to be offered comprehensive MTM services with defined elements, and the documentation must capture the MTR, the PMR, the MAP, interventions and referrals, and follow-up, with the drug-related problems identified and resolved. CPT codes for MTM (such as the codes for MTM services by level and time, and the codes for pharmacists where recognized) describe specific services and time ranges, and the code selected must match the service delivered and the time documented; time must be recorded, and the service must meet the code's requirements. The pharmacist must not bill for a service that was not fully delivered (for example, a review that did not produce a PMR and MAP), must not double-bill across payers, and must maintain documentation sufficient to support the claim on audit. Where billing rules are unclear, the pharmacist must consult the payer or a qualified compliance resource.

### Engage the Patient Actively in the Remote Setting

Patient engagement is the limiting factor in telehealth MTM, because the patient can disengage more easily than in person. The pharmacist must set expectations at scheduling (the time required, the need to gather medications, the importance of a quiet setting), must confirm the patient's identity and consent at the start, must use open-ended questions and teach-back throughout, and must watch for signs of disengagement (short answers, background noise, multitasking) and address them. The pharmacist should tailor the encounter to the patient's health literacy, language, and sensory or cognitive limitations, and must arrange interpreter or accessibility services where needed. Follow-up scheduling at the end of the encounter, with a confirmed contact method, increases completion of the care plan and the next review. Engagement is not a soft skill; it determines whether the clinical content of the MTM reaches the patient at all.

### Measure Outcomes That Reflect Clinical Effectiveness, Not Just Activity

The value of an MTM service is measured by the drug-related problems identified and resolved, the clinical and adherence outcomes achieved, and the patient's experience — not by the number of visits completed. The pharmacist must track, at minimum, the number and categories of drug-related problems identified (adverse drug reactions, interactions, duplications, untreated indications, dosing problems, adherence problems), the proportion resolved, the interventions accepted by prescribers, adherence changes (such as proportion of days covered for target medications), clinical outcomes where measurable (blood pressure, A1C, INR), and patient-reported satisfaction. Process measures alone (visits completed, time spent) do not demonstrate effectiveness and can mask a service that completes encounters without resolving problems. The pharmacist must use the outcome data to improve the service, identifying whether problems are being missed (a low problem-finding rate may indicate incomplete reviews) or unresolved (a low resolution rate may indicate weak prescriber communication).

### Document the Basis and the Reasoning

Every conclusion should be traceable to its evidence, assumptions, and alternatives considered. Record not only the outcome but the reasoning path: what was checked, what was ruled out, what uncertainty remains, and what would change the conclusion. Documentation that captures the basis allows another practitioner to review, reproduce, or challenge the work, and it prevents confident conclusions from becoming unrepeatable assertions. A decision made without a recorded basis cannot be audited, improved, or safely handed off.

## Common Traps

### The Incomplete Review Documented as Complete

The telehealth visit runs short or the connection is poor, and the pharmacist documents the MTM as complete despite not reviewing all medications. The mechanism is that the visit occurred and some content was covered. The false signal is that an encounter took place, so the service was delivered. The harm is that the unreviewed medications are where the drug-related problems hide — the over-the-counter NSAID causing hypertension, the duplicate therapy, the borrowed pill — and an incomplete review documented as complete both bills for undelivered service and misses the harm the review was meant to find. The pharmacist must reschedule or refer when the review cannot be completed.

### Trusting the Prescribed List Over the Patient's Actual Use

The pharmacist reviews the medication list from the record and does not ask the patient what they actually take. The mechanism is that the prescribed list is authoritative and convenient. The false signal is that the list reflects the therapy, so the review is grounded. The harm is that patients routinely stop, double, borrow, and supplement medications, and the discrepancy between prescribed and actual is the most common source of drug-related problems. The pharmacist must ask explicitly for every category of medication the patient actually takes.

### The PMR and MAP Trapped in an Inaccessible Portal

The pharmacist completes the PMR and MAP and uploads them to the patient portal, but the patient cannot or does not access the portal. The mechanism is that the documents were produced and stored. The false signal is that the deliverables exist, so the patient has them. The harm is that a document the patient never receives cannot guide their behavior, and the action plan fails for lack of reach. The pharmacist must verify a channel the patient can use and confirm receipt.

### Billing for a Service That Did Not Meet the Code

The pharmacist bills an MTM CPT code for an encounter that did not include all required elements or the documented time. The mechanism is that an encounter occurred and time was spent. The false signal is that the visit was clinical, so the code applies. The harm is that billing for a service not fully delivered is an inaccurate claim, vulnerable to audit and recoupment, and it inflates measures of service delivered while the patient received less. The code must match the service and time actually documented.

### Low Problem-Finding Interpreted as a Clean Patient

An MTM service identifies few drug-related problems across many patients, and this is interpreted as evidence that the patients are well-managed. The mechanism is that a low problem count feels like a good outcome. The false signal is that few problems were found, so few problems exist. The harm is that a low problem-finding rate often indicates that the reviews are incomplete or shallow, and the actual problems go unresolved. The pharmacist must benchmark the problem-finding rate and investigate rates that are implausibly low.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- Did I deliver all five core MTM elements (medication therapy review, personal medication record, medication action plan, intervention and referral, documentation and follow-up), adapted to but not weakened by the telehealth channel?
- Did I compensate for the limits of audio-only or video by asking the patient to gather and show or read every bottle and by explicitly asking about over-the-counter, herbal, as-needed, sample, and borrowed medications?
- Did I reconcile the prescribed list against the patient's actual use and communicate clinically significant discrepancies to the prescriber?
- Did I build an accurate PMR and a plain-language MAP, confirm understanding by teach-back, and deliver them through a channel the patient can access, verifying receipt?
- Did I document and bill under the CPT or Part D MTM code that matches the service actually delivered and the time recorded, without billing for incomplete encounters?
- Did I actively engage the patient, addressing disengagement, arranging interpreter or accessibility services where needed, and scheduling follow-up with a confirmed contact method?
- Did I measure outcomes (drug-related problems identified and resolved, prescriber acceptance, adherence, clinical measures, patient experience) rather than only process measures, and use the data to improve the service?
- Does the output stay within the agent's scope, deferring diagnostic and therapeutic decisions to the prescriber and escalating billing and compliance questions to the payer, board of pharmacy, or qualified compliance counsel where the question exceeds the agent's competence?
- Is the reasoning documented clearly enough that another practitioner could review the basis and reproduce the conclusion?
