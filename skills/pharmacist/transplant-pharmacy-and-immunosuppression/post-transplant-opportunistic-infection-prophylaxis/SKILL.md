# Post-Transplant Opportunistic Infection Prophylaxis

Solid organ transplant recipients are rendered vulnerable to a predictable calendar of opportunistic infections by the very immunosuppression that protects the graft, and prophylaxis is the structured defense that prevents the most dangerous of these. The judgment problem is that prophylaxis is not a single pill applied to all recipients: the pathogens, the timing, the duration, and the agents all depend on the organ, the donor-recipient serologic mismatch, the intensity of immunosuppression, and the patient's tolerance and interactions. A prophylaxis plan that is too narrow leaves the patient exposed to preventable disease, while one that is too broad or too long adds toxicity, resistance, cost, and interactions. The pharmacist must build a prophylaxis plan that matches the patient's risk and must revisit it every time the immunosuppression or the clinical situation changes.

## Core Rules

### Tailor Cytomegalovirus Prophylaxis to the Serologic Mismatch and the Organ

Cytomegalovirus is among the most consequential opportunistic infections after transplant, and the prophylaxis strategy is driven primarily by the donor-recipient serologic status, with the highest risk in a seronegative recipient of a seropositive organ. Prophylaxis, typically with valganciclovir, or alternatively a preemptive monitoring strategy with treatment of early viral replication, is chosen based on risk, organ type, and center protocol, with lung and other high-risk recipients generally receiving longer and more intense prophylaxis. The pharmacist must confirm the serostatus is known, that the chosen strategy matches the risk, and that the valganciclovir dose is adjusted for renal function, because ganciclovir and valganciclovir accumulate in renal impairment and cause bone marrow suppression. The duration of prophylaxis follows the period of greatest risk, typically the first months, extended in high-risk groups.

### Provide Universal Pneumocystis Prophylaxis in the Early Post-Transplant Period

Pneumocystis pneumonia is a preventable, frequently fatal infection in immunosuppressed transplant recipients, and prophylaxis is standard for at least the early months and often longer, commonly with trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole. For patients who cannot tolerate trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole due to allergy, intolerance, or interaction, alternatives include atovaquone, dapsone with glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase screening, or inhaled or intravenous pentamidine, each with its own limitations. The pharmacist should confirm prophylaxis is in place, that the chosen agent suits the patient's allergies and renal function, and that glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase deficiency is excluded before dapsone. Discontinuing pneumocystis prophylaxis too early, or during any intensification of immunosuppression, is a common and dangerous gap.

### Add Fungal Prophylaxis Where Organ and Risk Justify It

Fungal prophylaxis is not universal; it is reserved for recipients at elevated risk, including lung, liver, pancreas, and small bowel transplant recipients, and patients with additional risk factors such as prolonged or intense immunosuppression, renal failure, or retransplantation. The agent, typically an azole such as fluconazole or a broader-spectrum triazole in higher-risk groups, and the duration are chosen by organ and risk. The pharmacist must be alert to the profound interaction between azoles and calcineurin inhibitors, which can multiply calcineurin inhibitor levels and cause toxicity, and must plan dose adjustment and level monitoring whenever an azole is started or stopped. Fungal prophylaxis is a benefit that comes with a measurable interaction cost, and both must be managed together.

### Vaccinate Before Transplant and Use Non-Live Vaccines After

Prevention of community-acquired infection through vaccination is part of prophylaxis, but transplant immunosuppression constrains it. Live vaccines are generally contraindicated after transplant because of the risk of uncontrolled vaccine-strain infection, so live vaccines such as measles-mumps-rubella and varicella must be given before transplant when feasible. Inactivated and recombinant vaccines, including influenza, pneumococcal, hepatitis B, and others, are given after transplant, with timing optimized to the patient's immune recovery to maximize response. The pharmacist should review the vaccination history before transplant to close gaps with live vaccines while still possible, and should ensure the post-transplant schedule of inactivated vaccines is planned and documented. Household contacts should also be up to date to reduce exposure.

### Reassess Prophylaxis Whenever Immunosuppression Changes

Prophylaxis is calibrated to the intensity of immunosuppression, so whenever the regimen changes, the prophylaxis must be reassessed. An intensification of immunosuppression for rejection treatment, with high-dose steroids, antibody therapy, or increased baseline agents, raises the risk of cytomegalovirus, pneumocystis, and fungal infection and often warrants restarting or extending prophylaxis that had been discontinued. A reduction in immunosuppression may allow prophylaxis to be safely stopped, reducing toxicity and cost. The pharmacist should treat any rejection episode or immunosuppression change as a trigger to review the prophylaxis plan, because the calendar that justified the original plan no longer applies.

### Screen for and Manage Interactions and Toxicity Across the Whole Prophylaxis Set

Transplant patients take many drugs at once, and the prophylaxis agents interact with the immunosuppressants and with each other. Trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole can raise potassium and creatinine and interact with other nephrotoxins; azoles profoundly affect calcineurin inhibitor levels; valganciclovir causes bone marrow suppression that overlaps with other myelosuppressive agents. The pharmacist must review the complete medication list for additive toxicity and interactions each time a prophylaxis agent is added, removed, or changed, and must monitor renal function, electrolytes, and blood counts across the prophylaxis period. Prophylaxis that prevents one infection while causing renal failure or neutropenia has simply traded one harm for another.

## Common Traps

### Applying One-Size-Fits-All Prophylaxis Regardless of Organ and Risk

The same prophylaxis is applied to every recipient regardless of organ, serostatus, and immunosuppression intensity. The mechanism is that a standard protocol is easier than individualized risk assessment. The false signal is that prophylaxis is uniform across transplant. The harm is under-protection of high-risk recipients such as cytomegalovirus mismatched or lung transplant patients who develop preventable disease, and over-treatment of low-risk recipients who accumulate toxicity and interactions unnecessarily. The corrective is to tailor the pathogens, agents, and duration to the organ and the individual risk profile.

### Using Standard Valganciclovir Dosing Without Renal Adjustment

Valganciclovir is dosed at a standard regimen without adjustment for renal function. The mechanism is that the standard dose is memorized and renal function is assumed stable. The false signal is that the prescribed dose is correct as written. The harm is accumulation in renal impairment with severe bone marrow suppression, neutropenia, thrombocytopenia, and infection, because ganciclovir is renally cleared and transplant recipients frequently have fluctuating renal function. The corrective is to adjust valganciclovir to current renal function and to recheck the dose as renal function changes.

### Stopping Pneumocystis Prophylaxis Too Early or During Intensified Immunosuppression

Pneumocystis prophylaxis is stopped at a fixed time and not restarted when immunosuppression is intensified for rejection. The mechanism is that prophylaxis is treated as a calendar event rather than a risk-dependent decision. The false signal is that the prophylaxis period is over. The harm is pneumocystis pneumonia during a rejection episode, when the patient is most vulnerable, a frequently fatal and entirely preventable complication. The corrective is to extend or restart pneumocystis prophylaxis whenever immunosuppression is intensified.

### Adding an Azole Without Adjusting the Calcineurin Inhibitor

Fungal prophylaxis with an azole is started and the calcineurin inhibitor level is not checked until the next routine visit. The mechanism is that the interaction is known but not translated into a monitoring plan. The false signal is that the azole was prescribed appropriately, so the interaction is handled. The harm is a multiplied calcineurin inhibitor level with acute nephrotoxicity and neurotoxicity, because azoles inhibit the metabolism of tacrolimus and cyclosporine. The corrective is to reduce and monitor the calcineurin inhibitor whenever an azole is started and to readjust when it is stopped.

### Overlooking Live Vaccine Contraindication After Transplant

A live vaccine is given after transplant, or recommended without recognizing the contraindication. The mechanism is that vaccination is assumed safe across the board. The false signal is that immunization is always protective and never harmful. The harm is vaccine-strain infection in a heavily immunosuppressed patient, which can be severe. The corrective is to give live vaccines before transplant when feasible, to avoid them after transplant, and to ensure household contacts are vaccinated to reduce exposure.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The standard adult kidney recipient on a stable regimen is handled well, but the unusual case such as a pediatric recipient, a lung or small bowel recipient with much higher risk, a cytomegalovirus mismatched patient, a pregnant transplant recipient, a patient with glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase deficiency, or a non-adherent patient is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is safe while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- Did I tailor cytomegalovirus prophylaxis or preemptive monitoring to the donor-recipient serostatus, organ type, and risk, with valganciclovir dose adjusted for renal function?
- Is pneumocystis prophylaxis in place for the appropriate period, with an alternative agent chosen and screened where trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole is not tolerated?
- Did I reserve fungal prophylaxis for recipients at elevated risk, choosing the agent and duration by organ and risk factors?
- Whenever an azole was added or removed, did I adjust and monitor the calcineurin inhibitor level to prevent toxicity or under-exposure?
- Did I review vaccination status before transplant to give live vaccines while still feasible, and plan the post-transplant inactivated vaccine schedule?
- Did I reassess the prophylaxis plan whenever immunosuppression was intensified for rejection or otherwise changed?
- Did I screen the full prophylaxis set for additive toxicity and interactions, monitoring renal function, electrolytes, and blood counts?
- For special situations such as pediatrics, pregnancy, high-risk organs, glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase deficiency, or non-adherence, did I individualize the prophylaxis rather than apply the standard path?
