# Discharge Medication Reconciliation and Counseling

Discharge is the most dangerous transition in the medication continuum, because the patient moves from a controlled environment — where medications are verified, dispensed, administered, and monitored — to an uncontrolled environment where they must obtain, understand, afford, and self-administer a regimen that may have changed substantially during the hospitalization. The judgment problem is that discharge reconciliation is performed under time pressure, with incomplete information about the patient's post-discharge capacity, and the consequences of error — readmission, adverse drug event, therapeutic failure — manifest after the patient has left the system's direct observation.

## Core Rules

### Reconcile Against Both the Pre-Admission List and the Inpatient Course

Discharge reconciliation requires two comparisons: the discharge regimen against the pre-admission home medication list (what changed and why), and the discharge regimen against the final inpatient orders (what was the patient actually receiving in the hospital). Medications started inpatient may need to be continued, tapered, or stopped; medications held inpatient may need to be resumed or permanently discontinued; dose changes made for acute illness may need to be adjusted back to chronic maintenance or maintained at the new level. For each medication on the discharge list, document whether it is a continuation of a home medication, a new initiation, a modification, or a resumption after a hold, and the rationale for the decision.

### Resolve Every Discrepancy Before the Patient Leaves

Every unresolved discrepancy at discharge propagates into the community, where no pharmacist is checking. Common discharge discrepancies include: inpatient medications that were continued by default but have no outpatient indication; home medications that were held inpatient and never explicitly resumed or discontinued; duplicate therapy from the same drug class; medications that interact with newly started agents; and dose changes made for acute illness that were not reconciled back to chronic dosing. Resolve each discrepancy by contacting the medical team before discharge — do not defer to the outpatient provider, because the patient may not follow up for weeks, and the discrepancy operates in the interim.

### Generate Clear, Patient-Centered Discharge Instructions

The discharge medication list must be comprehensible to the patient and caregiver, not just to clinicians. Use plain language, not generic names alone if the patient knows the brand. For each medication, state the name, the indication in the patient's terms ("for your heart rhythm," not "antiarrhythmic"), the dose, the frequency, and key administration instructions (with food, in the morning, separate from other medications). Highlight what is new, what changed, and what was stopped — the patient must understand how the discharge regimen differs from what they were taking before admission, because confusion between old and new regimens is a primary cause of post-discharge error. Avoid the trap of handing the patient a computer-generated list that is accurate but incomprehensible.

### Prioritize Counseling for High-Risk Medications and Complex Regimens

While every patient benefits from counseling, certain situations carry elevated post-discharge risk and require intensive counseling: new anticoagulants (bleeding risk, monitoring, interaction with food and medications), new insulin or changed diabetes regimens (hypoglycemia, technique, monitoring), medications with narrow therapeutic indices (warfarin, digoxin, lithium, anticonvulsants), regimens requiring tapering (corticosteroids, opioids, benzodiazepines), medications with critical administration technique (inhalers, injectables), and any regimen involving more than five medications or multiple daily doses. For these patients, use teach-back — ask the patient to explain the regimen back to you — to verify comprehension rather than assuming that nodding indicates understanding.

### Address Access, Affordability, and Adherence Barriers Before Discharge

A discharge prescription the patient cannot afford, cannot obtain, or cannot administer is not a discharge plan — it is a setup for non-adherence and readmission. Before discharge, assess the patient's insurance coverage for each prescribed medication, identify cost barriers, and where possible select affordable alternatives or connect the patient to patient assistance programs. Assess whether the patient can physically obtain the medications — transportation, pharmacy access, caregiver support — and arrange delivery or a pharmacy that can fill the full regimen. Assess administration capacity — can the patient swallow pills, use an inhaler, self-inject, read the labels — and provide aids, training, or alternative formulations where needed. These barriers are not peripheral; they are often the decisive factor in whether the regimen actually reaches the patient's body.

### Communicate the Discharge Regimen to the Next Provider

The patient's outpatient providers — primary care, specialists, the pharmacy — need accurate, timely information about the discharge regimen, including what changed, what is new, what requires monitoring, and when follow-up is needed. A discharge summary that lists medications without context does not enable safe follow-up. Communicate the rationale for changes, the monitoring plan (what labs, what symptoms, what timeframe), and any medications that were held and may need to be reconsidered. Where possible, send the discharge medication list electronically to the patient's pharmacy and primary care provider, because the patient carrying a paper copy that may be lost or ignored is a fragile single point of failure.

## Common Traps

### Continuing Inpatient Medications Without an Outpatient Indication

During hospitalization, medications are started for acute indications — proton pump inhibitors for stress ulcer prophylaxis, sleep agents, antipsychotics for delirium, IV diuretics, broad-spectrum antibiotics. The trap is carrying these onto the discharge list by default, without evaluating whether each has an ongoing outpatient indication. The harm is that the patient leaves the hospital on medications they do not need, which may interact with their chronic regimen, cause adverse effects, and become permanent additions to their medication list through subsequent visits where "continue current medications" propagates the error. The mechanism is that stopping requires an active decision while continuing is the path of least resistance. The corrective is to evaluate every inpatient-initiated medication for outpatient indication and to stop those without one.

### Failing to Resume Held Home Medications

Medications held during hospitalization — for procedures, for acute illness, due to interactions — may be unintentionally omitted from the discharge regimen. The trap is that the hold was documented as an inpatient decision, but no one explicitly reversed it at discharge, and the patient leaves without a medication they need chronically. The harm is therapeutic failure — breakthrough seizures from an omitted anticonvulsant, rejection from an omitted immunosuppressant, thrombosis from an omitted antiplatelet — that manifests after discharge. The corrective is to review every held home medication at discharge, decide explicitly whether to resume, discontinue, or modify, and document the decision.

### Handing Over an Incomprehensible Discharge List

The computer-generated discharge medication list is typically accurate, comprehensive, and utterly incomprehensible to patients — dense with generic names, devoid of indication context, and silent on what changed. The trap is treating the generation of this list as the discharge counseling, when in fact the patient cannot use it. The harm is that the patient, unable to understand the list, defaults to their pre-admission regimen, ignores new medications, or takes medications incorrectly. The corrective is to translate the list into patient-centered language, to highlight changes, to use teach-back to verify comprehension, and to recognize that an accurate list the patient does not understand is functionally useless.

### Ignoring Cost and Access Until the Patient Fails to Fill

The most common cause of post-discharge non-adherence is not patient choice but access barriers — the medication is too expensive, the pharmacy is too far, the prior authorization is pending, the patient cannot get an appointment. The trap is completing the discharge reconciliation without addressing these barriers, assuming that the prescription is equivalent to the patient receiving the medication. The harm is that the patient leaves with a plan that fails at the pharmacy counter, and the readmission occurs before anyone identifies the access barrier. The corrective is to assess cost, coverage, and access before discharge, to select affordable alternatives where possible, and to arrange pharmacy services that bridge the gap.

### Not Communicating Changes to the Outpatient Provider

The patient's primary care provider and specialists often learn about the hospitalization and medication changes from the patient themselves, at a follow-up visit weeks later, by which time the changes may have caused harm. The trap is assuming that the discharge summary reaches the outpatient provider in time, when in practice it may be delayed, unread, or routed to the wrong provider. The harm is that the outpatient provider cannot monitor, adjust, or reconcile what they do not know about, and medication errors propagate. The corrective is to communicate the discharge regimen and rationale directly and promptly to the outpatient provider and pharmacy, not solely through the discharge summary.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- Did I reconcile the discharge regimen against both the pre-admission home list and the final inpatient orders?
- Did I resolve every discrepancy — unintended omissions, inpatient medications without outpatient indication, held home medications not resumed — before the patient left?
- Did I evaluate every inpatient-initiated medication for ongoing outpatient indication and stop those without one?
- Did I generate patient-centered discharge instructions that the patient can understand, highlighting what is new, changed, and stopped?
- Did I provide intensive counseling with teach-back for high-risk medications and complex regimens?
- Did I assess and address cost, coverage, access, and administration capacity before discharge?
- Did I communicate the discharge regimen, rationale, and monitoring plan directly to the outpatient provider and pharmacy?
- Did I arrange follow-up and monitoring appropriate to the regimen's risk level?
