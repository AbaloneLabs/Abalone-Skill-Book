# Admission Medication Reconciliation and History-Taking

Admission medication reconciliation is the process of creating an accurate, complete list of every medication a patient was taking before arrival — prescription, over-the-counter, herbal, supplement, as-needed, and recreational — and comparing it against the admission orders to identify and resolve discrepancies. It sounds administrative, but it is one of the highest-leverage safety interventions in pharmacy, because the admission medication list propagates through the entire hospitalization and into discharge, and errors at this origin point become errors at every subsequent decision. The judgment problem is that patients are poor historians under the best conditions, and admission conditions — acute illness, pain, cognitive impairment, language barriers, and the stress of hospitalization — are the worst conditions.

## Core Rules

### Use Multiple Sources and Never Rely on Patient Recall Alone

Patient self-report is the starting point, not the endpoint, of medication reconciliation. Patients forget medications, confuse names and doses, omit supplements and as-needed medications they do not consider "real" medications, and may not know the doses of medications managed by family members or caregivers. Corroborate self-report with at least one independent source: the outpatient pharmacy fill history, the primary care provider's medication list, prior hospital discharge summaries, the patient's medication bottles, and family or caregiver input. Where sources conflict, investigate rather than guessing — a discrepancy between the pharmacy fill history and the patient's report may indicate non-adherence, a recent change, or a misunderstanding that affects therapy.

### Capture the Complete Medication Universe, Not Just Chronic Prescriptions

The medication list must include every substance the patient takes that could affect therapy: prescription medications, over-the-counter medications (especially NSAIDs, antihistamines, PPIs, and aspirin, which carry bleeding, sedation, and interaction risks), herbal and dietary supplements (which interact with anticoagulants, immunosuppressants, and many others), vitamins, as-needed medications used regularly or intermittently, inhalers, eye drops, topical medications, hormonal contraceptives, and recreational substances including alcohol, cannabis, and illicit drugs. Each of these can interact with inpatient therapy, affect anesthesia, alter coagulation, or explain the patient's presentation. Do not narrow the list to chronic prescriptions, because the omitted as-needed NSAID may be the cause of the gastrointestinal bleed that brought the patient in.

### Document Dose, Route, Frequency, Last Dose, and Adherence for Each Medication

For each medication, capture not just the name but the full picture: the prescribed dose, route, frequency, the time of the last dose taken, and the patient's actual adherence pattern. The last dose time is critical for medications with timing-dependent effects — anticoagulants, insulin, anti-seizure medications, dopaminergic agents in Parkinson's disease — where knowing the gap since the last dose determines whether to continue, hold, or bridge. Adherence matters because a patient who is prescribed an antihypertensive but does not take it is not "on" that medication, and continuing it inpatient based on the prescription rather than the reality may produce hypotension. Ask explicitly about adherence rather than assuming the prescription reflects reality, because non-adherence is common and clinically significant.

### Reconcile Against Admission Orders and Resolve Every Discreason

Once the complete home medication list is assembled, compare it systematically against the admission orders. For each home medication, determine whether it was continued, held, modified, or discontinued, and whether the decision is documented and appropriate. Common discrepancies include medications omitted entirely (the patient was on it at home but it does not appear on admission orders), dose or frequency changes without documented rationale, duplications (two medications in the same class), and therapeutic overlaps that create interaction risk. Resolve every discrepancy by contacting the admitting team — do not leave discrepancies to propagate, because an omitted critical medication (anticonvulsant, immunosuppressant, antiretroviral) can cause harm within hours.

### Prioritize High-Risk Medications for Immediate Reconciliation

While every discrepancy matters, certain medications cause immediate, severe harm if omitted, duplicated, or incorrectly dosed at admission. Prioritize reconciliation of anticoagulants (bridging, holding for procedures, interaction with new medications), antiplatelet agents, insulin and oral hypoglycemics, anticonvulsants (breakthrough seizures from omission), immunosuppressants (transplant rejection), antiretrovirals (HIV resistance from missed doses), corticosteroids (adrenal crisis from abrupt cessation), dopaminergic agents for Parkinson's (severe rigidity, neuroleptic malignant syndrome from omission), and thyroid medications. For these medications, reconcile immediately upon admission rather than waiting for the standard reconciliation timeline, because the window for harm from omission is short.

### Document the Reconciliation Clearly for Downstream Providers

The admission reconciliation is the foundation for the discharge reconciliation, and clarity at admission prevents errors at discharge. Document the verified home medication list, the admission decisions for each medication (continued, held, modified, discontinued, with rationale), the discrepancies found and how they were resolved, and the sources used to verify the history. This documentation must be accessible to every provider who will touch the medication regimen during and after the hospitalization, because a reconciliation that exists only in the pharmacist's notes is invisible to the team and ineffective as a safety intervention.

## Common Traps

### Accepting the Patient's Verbal List as Complete

The most common reconciliation failure is accepting the patient's verbal account as the complete and accurate medication list. The mechanism is that taking a thorough history is time-consuming, corroborating with pharmacy records requires additional steps, and the patient's confident recollection creates an illusion of completeness. The harm is that omitted medications — supplements, as-needed agents, recently stopped medications, medications managed by others — go unreconciled, creating interaction risk, duplication, and missed indications throughout the hospitalization. The corrective is to always corroborate self-report with at least one independent source and to investigate discrepancies rather than accepting the first account.

### Omitting Supplements, Over-the-Counter, and Recreational Substances

Patients — and some clinicians — do not consider over-the-counter medications, herbal supplements, vitamins, or recreational substances to be "medications," and so they are omitted from the reconciliation. The trap is that these substances carry significant clinical risk: St. John's Wort interacts with dozens of medications, garlic and ginkgo increase bleeding risk, NSAIDs cause renal injury and gastrointestinal bleeding, alcohol affects anesthesia and withdrawal risk, and cannabis interacts with sedatives and psychiatric medications. The harm is that the inpatient team is blind to these risks because they were never captured. The corrective is to ask explicitly and non-judgmentally about each category, using plain language rather than the word "medication" alone, which patients interpret narrowly.

### Continuing the Prescribed Regimen Without Assessing Adherence

A patient who is prescribed metoprolol 50 mg twice daily but takes it inconsistently is not "on" metoprolol 50 mg twice daily, and continuing that dose inpatient — where adherence is guaranteed — may produce bradycardia or hypotension in a patient whose body has adapted to intermittent exposure. The trap is reconciling to the prescription rather than to the patient's actual intake, which overestimates the patient's medication exposure and creates risk when inpatient administration enforces the prescribed dose. The corrective is to ask explicitly about adherence — "How often do you actually take it? Have you missed doses recently?" — and to adjust inpatient therapy based on actual rather than prescribed exposure, particularly for cardiovascular and neurological medications.

### Missing the Last Dose Time for Timing-Critical Medications

For most medications, the exact time of the last dose is a detail; for some, it is the decisive clinical fact. The trap is not asking about last dose time for anticoagulants (which determines procedural holding and bridging), insulin (which determines glucose management and hypoglycemia risk), anticonvulsants (which determines seizure risk and loading), anti-Parkinsonian agents (which determines motor control and medication timing), and opioids and benzodiazepines (which determines tolerance, withdrawal, and dosing). The harm is that without the last dose time, the team cannot make informed decisions about continuation, holding, bridging, or loading, and the patient may experience preventable harm from a gap or overlap. The corrective is to capture last dose time for every timing-critical medication during the initial reconciliation.

### Failing to Resolve Discrepancies and Leaving Them to Propagate

Identifying discrepancies is only half of reconciliation; the other half is resolving them. The trap is documenting discrepancies — "patient reports warfarin but it is not on admission orders" — without contacting the team to resolve them, leaving the discrepancy to propagate through the hospitalization and into discharge. The mechanism is that documentation feels like action, and the pharmacist may assume the team will notice and address the discrepancy, when in fact the team may not read the reconciliation note. The corrective is to treat every unresolved discrepancy as an active intervention: contact the team, communicate the discrepancy, recommend a resolution, and document the outcome.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- Did I corroborate the patient's self-reported medication history with at least one independent source — pharmacy fill history, provider list, prior records, bottles, or family?
- Did I capture the complete medication universe including over-the-counter, herbal, supplement, as-needed, inhaler, topical, and recreational substances?
- Did I document dose, route, frequency, last dose time, and actual adherence for each medication, not just the name?
- Did I prioritize immediate reconciliation of high-risk medications — anticoagulants, insulin, anticonvulsants, immunosuppressants, antiretrovirals, corticosteroids, anti-Parkinsonian agents?
- Did I compare the home list against admission orders systematically and resolve every discrepancy by contacting the team?
- Did I ask explicitly about adherence rather than assuming the prescription reflects reality?
- Did I capture last dose time for timing-critical medications?
- Did I document the reconciliation clearly enough that downstream providers can use it for discharge planning?
