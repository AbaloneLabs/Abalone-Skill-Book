# Transitions of Care Across Levels of Care

Medication safety at transitions is not limited to admission and discharge from the hospital. Patients move across multiple levels of care — ICU to ward, hospital to skilled nursing facility, SNF to home with home health, emergency department to observation to admission, hospital to hospice — and each transition is a point where medications can be lost, duplicated, incorrectly modified, or inappropriately continued. The judgment problem is that each receiving setting has different formularies, different staffing, different monitoring capacity, and different expectations, and a medication regimen appropriate for one setting may be unsafe or unmanageable in the next.

## Core Rules

### Reconcile at Every Transition, Not Just Admission and Discharge

Every change in care level is a medication reconciliation opportunity and risk. When a patient transfers from ICU to ward, reconcile: vasoactive infusions are being weaned, sedation is being transitioned, prophylaxis regimens may change, and medications held in ICU may need resumption. When a patient transfers from hospital to SNF, reconcile: the SNF formulary may not carry the same agents, the SNF nursing ratio affects what regimens are manageable, and medications appropriate for monitored settings may be unsafe in a lower-acuity environment. Treat every transition as a fresh reconciliation — assemble the current regimen, compare it to the receiving orders, and resolve discrepancies before the transfer is complete.

### Adapt the Regimen to the Receiving Setting's Capacity

Each care setting has different capacity for medication administration, monitoring, and response. A regimen that is safe in the ICU — with continuous monitoring, rapid response, and one-to-one nursing — may be unsafe on a general ward, in a SNF, or at home. Insulin sliding scales that require frequent glucose checks may be unmanageable in a SNF with limited nursing. Anticoagulants that require lab monitoring may be impractical where lab draw frequency is limited. Medications requiring refrigeration, specialized administration, or rapid availability of reversal agents may be inappropriate for home discharge. Evaluate the regimen against the receiving setting's capacity and modify where the setting cannot safely support the current plan.

### Manage Formulary Conversion at Each Transfer

Different care settings have different formularies, and a direct transfer of the medication list may produce therapeutic gaps, duplications, or errors. Convert to the receiving formulary's equivalent agent — not just the same drug class but the specific agent, dose, and formulation that the receiving pharmacy stocks. Document the conversion rationale, because the next provider needs to understand why the patient's regimen changed at transfer. Common conversions include therapeutic equivalents within a class (one proton pump inhibitor for another), formulation changes (IV to oral, extended-release to immediate-release with dosing adjustment), and agent substitutions where the receiving formulary does not carry the sending agent.

### Ensure Monitoring Continuity Across the Transition

Medications that require monitoring — anticoagulants with INR, digoxin with levels and potassium, lithium with levels and renal function, anticonvulsants with levels, immunosuppressants with trough levels — must have a monitoring plan that survives the transition. The sending provider must communicate what monitoring is needed, at what interval, and to whom results should be reported. The receiving provider must accept responsibility for completing the monitoring and acting on results. A gap in monitoring continuity — the INR is due two days after SNF transfer but no one orders it — is a common and dangerous transition failure. Specify the monitoring plan explicitly in the transfer documentation, including the responsible provider and the timeframe.

### Communicate the Clinical Narrative, Not Just the Medication List

A medication list without context is a source of error at transition. The receiving provider needs the clinical narrative: why each medication was started or changed, what the therapeutic goals are, what adverse effects have occurred, what has been tried and failed, what monitoring is pending, and what the anticipated trajectory is. A patient transferred to a SNF on five new medications without context forces the SNF provider to guess at rationale, which produces errors. Communicate the narrative in the transfer documentation, highlighting medications that are time-sensitive, high-risk, newly initiated, or undergoing active titration.

### Manage Time-Sensitive Medications During the Transfer Process

The transfer process itself — transport, waiting, intake at the receiving facility — creates windows where time-sensitive medications are missed. Parkinsonian medications, anticonvulsants, antiretrovirals, immunosuppressants, and analgesics have timing windows where delay causes clinical deterioration. Plan for medication administration during the transfer gap: send doses with the patient, communicate the last dose time and next dose time, and ensure the receiving facility administers on schedule. Do not assume that the transfer will be quick or that the receiving facility will immediately resume the schedule, because transfers are frequently delayed and intake processes defer medication administration.

## Common Traps

### Treating Transfer as a Data Forwarding Exercise Rather Than Clinical Reconciliation

The administrative definition of transfer — forwarding the medication list and orders to the receiving facility — feels complete but is clinically inadequate. The trap is treating the transfer as accomplished when the paperwork is sent, without evaluating whether the regimen is appropriate for the receiving setting, whether discrepancies exist, and whether the receiving provider understands the clinical context. The harm is that errors embedded in the transfer propagate in a setting where no one has the context to catch them. The mechanism is that data transfer is measurable and completable while clinical reconciliation is open-ended and requires judgment. The corrective is to treat transfer as a clinical event requiring active reconciliation, not a data forwarding task.

### Continuing ICU Regimens on the Ward Without Modification

ICU regimens are optimized for a monitored, high-acuity environment with continuous nursing, and direct continuation onto a general ward can be unsafe. The trap is transferring the patient with their ICU medication list unchanged, assuming that the ward will manage it. The harm includes: continued vasoactive or sedative infusions without ICU monitoring, insulin regimens calibrated to ICU glucose patterns that produce hypoglycemia on the ward, deep sedation that compromises airway safety without ICU observation, and prophylaxis regimens (stress ulcer, deep vein thrombosis) that may be inappropriate at lower acuity. The corrective is to actively reconcile and modify the regimen at ICU-to-ward transfer, scaling down to what the ward can safely manage.

### Failing to Convert to the Receiving Formulary

Each facility's formulary is different, and a medication list transferred without formulary conversion produces errors: the receiving pharmacy substitutes a different agent without clinical guidance, the patient misses doses while the substitution is negotiated, or a therapeutic duplicate is created when the receiving provider adds their formulary equivalent without realizing the original is still listed. The trap is assuming that the medication list will transfer cleanly when the agents may not exist in the receiving formulary. The corrective is to identify formulary gaps before transfer, to specify the conversion explicitly, and to communicate with the receiving pharmacy to confirm availability and equivalence.

### Losing Monitoring Responsibility in the Handoff

Monitoring responsibility is often ambiguous at transition — the sending provider assumes the receiver will monitor, the receiver assumes the sender communicated what is needed, and no one owns the task. The trap is that monitoring falls through the gap, and the patient arrives at the next adverse event — a supratherapeutic INR with bleeding, a toxic digoxin level, a subtherapeutic anticonvulsant with breakthrough seizure — because no one completed the monitoring that would have caught it. The corrective is to specify the monitoring plan, the interval, and the responsible provider in the transfer documentation, and to confirm acceptance by the receiving team.

### Missing Time-Sensitive Medications During Transfer Delays

Transfers are routinely delayed — waiting for transport, waiting for bed assignment, waiting for intake — and during these delays, time-sensitive medications are missed. The trap is not planning for medication administration during the gap, assuming the transfer will be quick. The harm is clinical deterioration — rigidity from missed Parkinsonian doses, breakthrough seizures from missed anticonvulsants, pain crises from missed analgesics, rejection risk from missed immunosuppressants — that occurs during the transfer and is attributed to the patient's condition rather than to the missed dose. The corrective is to send time-sensitive medications with the patient, to communicate the dosing schedule, and to verify that the receiving facility administers on time.

## Self-Check

- Did I reconcile medications at every transition — not just admission and discharge, but ICU to ward, hospital to SNF, SNF to home, ED to admission?
- Did I evaluate whether the current regimen is safe and manageable in the receiving setting's capacity for administration, monitoring, and response?
- Did I convert to the receiving formulary, specifying equivalent agents, doses, and formulations, and documenting the rationale?
- Did I specify the monitoring plan — what, when, by whom — and confirm acceptance by the receiving provider?
- Did I communicate the clinical narrative, not just the medication list, so the receiving provider understands rationale and goals?
- Did I plan for time-sensitive medications during the transfer gap, sending doses with the patient and verifying on-time administration?
- Did I modify ICU or high-acuity regimens to what the ward or lower-acuity setting can safely manage?
- Did I confirm that the receiving facility can actually obtain and administer each medication on the transferred regimen?
