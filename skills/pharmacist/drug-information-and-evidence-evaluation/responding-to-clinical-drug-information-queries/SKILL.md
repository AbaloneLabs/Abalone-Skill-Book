# Responding to Clinical Drug Information Queries

Drug information response is the pharmacist's role as the medication expert who translates the literature into a clinical decision. A prescriber asks whether a drug is safe in pregnancy, a nurse asks whether two drugs are compatible in the same IV line, a patient asks whether their supplement interacts with their anticoagulant — and the answer must be accurate, current, and calibrated to the strength of the evidence behind it. The danger is that the pharmacist treats the query as a search task — type the question into a database, copy the answer, send it back — without formulating the actual clinical question, without verifying that the source is current and applicable, and without distinguishing what the evidence shows from what the reference asserts. When the response becomes a database lookup, the pharmacist delivers confident answers built on weak or outdated evidence, and the clinical decision is made on a foundation the pharmacist never examined.

## Core Rules

### Formulate the Specific Clinical Question Before Searching

A drug information request arrives as a vague or multi-part question: "Is this antibiotic safe in renal failure?" Before searching, the pharmacist must reformulate it into a structured, answerable question that specifies the patient population, the drug, the outcome, and the context — for example, "In an elderly patient with a creatinine clearance of 25 mL/min, what is the risk of nephrotoxicity and the required dose adjustment for this specific antibiotic?" This reformulation is not pedantry; it determines what evidence is relevant. A search conducted on the vague original question will return general safety information that may not address the patient's actual circumstance, while a search on the structured question will surface the specific data the decision requires. The pharmacist must also identify what kind of question it is — dosing, safety in a special population, compatibility, therapeutic equivalence, place in therapy — because each type requires a different evidence hierarchy and a different set of sources.

### Search the Primary Literature, Not Just the Tertiary Summary

Tertiary references (drug compendia, clinical decision support, textbook summaries) are the starting point, not the endpoint. They summarize the evidence, but the summary may be outdated, may reflect the author's interpretation rather than the underlying data, and may not include the most recent studies. For questions where the clinical decision carries significant risk — safety in pregnancy or lactation, dosing in a population not covered by the label, a drug interaction with limited data — the pharmacist must go to the primary literature: the original study, the case report, the pharmacokinetic analysis. The pharmacist must know which databases to search (PubMed, Embase, the Cochrane Library, the Reprotox or LactMed databases for pregnancy and lactation), how to construct a search that captures the relevant evidence without being overwhelmed by noise, and when the absence of primary evidence is itself the answer — "there are no data" is a more honest and useful response than a confident extrapolation from a tertiary source.

### Grade the Strength of the Evidence Behind the Answer

A recommendation based on a randomized controlled trial is not equivalent to a recommendation based on a single case report, a pharmacokinetic extrapolation, or expert opinion. The pharmacist must communicate the strength of the evidence alongside the answer, so the prescriber can weigh the recommendation appropriately. A response that says "this drug is safe in pregnancy" without noting that the statement rests on a small prospective cohort and a handful of case reports gives the prescriber false confidence; a response that says "the evidence is limited to observational data, but no signal of teratogenicity has been identified" allows the prescriber to make a calibrated decision. The pharmacist must also distinguish between absence of evidence (no studies have been done) and evidence of absence (studies have found no harm) — these are very different statements, and conflating them produces either false reassurance or false alarm.

### Deliver the Answer in the Clinical Timeframe and Format Required

A drug information response is useful only if it arrives when the decision is being made and in a form the requester can act on. A prescriber waiting at the bedside for a compatibility answer needs a verbal response in minutes, not a written report in two days; a patient asking about a supplement interaction needs a clear, non-technical explanation, not a citation list. The pharmacist must assess the urgency of the request and match the depth of the response to the available time — a rapid, focused answer with the key point and the evidence grade for an urgent question, and a comprehensive written response with full literature search for a non-urgent policy or formulary question. The pharmacist must also confirm that the answer was received and understood, particularly for verbal responses where a misheard dose or interaction can cause direct harm.

### Document the Question, the Search, the Answer, and the Evidence

Every drug information response must be documented, not as bureaucratic record-keeping, but as the evidence trail that allows the response to be verified, updated, and reused. The documentation must include the original question as asked, the reformulated question, the sources searched, the key evidence found, the recommendation, and the evidence grade. This documentation protects the patient (the recommendation can be reviewed if new evidence emerges), protects the pharmacist (the reasoning is visible if the outcome is questioned), and serves the institution (recurring questions can be answered from the documented response rather than re-searched). A response delivered verbally and never documented is, for practical purposes, a response that never existed if it is later questioned.

### Respect Scope and Escalation Boundaries

Know where the agent's authority and competence end. When the question requires a license, a specialist's judgment, a final approval, or expertise the agent does not hold, the correct action is to escalate rather than to produce a confident answer that overreaches. Scope discipline protects the recipient from harm caused by an unqualified conclusion and protects the agent from liability. State explicitly when the output is advisory and must be confirmed by the qualified person.

## Common Traps

### The Tertiary Source That Has Not Been Updated

A pharmacist responds to a question about drug safety in pregnancy by checking a standard drug compendium, finding a statement that the drug is "contraindicated in pregnancy," and relaying it to the prescriber. The mechanism of the trap is that the compendium's statement was based on animal studies from twenty years ago, and more recent prospective human data — published in the primary literature but not yet reflected in the tertiary summary — has shown no increased risk. The harm is that the prescriber, acting on the outdated contraindication, stops a needed medication or switches to a less effective alternative, and the patient's underlying condition worsens during pregnancy. The false signal is that a published reference is current, when in fact tertiary sources lag behind the primary literature by months to years, and the lag is longest for the questions where the data is sparsest.

### Conflating Absence of Evidence With Evidence of Safety

A prescriber asks whether a newly approved drug is safe in lactation. The pharmacist searches, finds no studies on the drug in breast milk, and reports that "there are no data indicating harm." The mechanism of the trap is that "no data indicating harm" is linguistically close to "no harm," but they are fundamentally different statements — the former means no one has looked, the latter means people have looked and found nothing. The harm is that the prescriber interprets the absence of data as evidence of safety, clears the patient to breastfeed, and if the drug does transfer into milk and harm the infant, the harm was caused not by the drug alone but by the pharmacist's failure to distinguish the two. The correct response is "there are no data; the drug's molecular properties suggest it is likely to transfer into milk; a safer alternative with established lactation data should be preferred."

### Answering the Question Asked Without Identifying the Underlying Need

A nurse asks "what is the half-life of this drug?" The pharmacist provides the half-life. The mechanism of the trap is that the nurse's actual clinical need was to know how long to wait after the dose before drawing a level, or how long the drug would interact with a newly started medication — and the half-life, while technically correct, does not answer the underlying question. The harm is that the nurse acts on the half-life in a way that does not serve the clinical need, the level is drawn at the wrong time, or the interaction window is miscalculated. The pharmacist must always ask why the information is needed before providing it, because the requester's framing of the question often omits the context that determines what answer is actually useful. The false signal is that the question as asked is the question to answer, when in fact it is often a proxy for a different question the requester could not formulate.

### The Confident Recommendation Built on a Single Case Report

A prescriber asks about a rare drug interaction. The pharmacist searches and finds a single published case report describing the interaction. The mechanism of the trap is that a case report is the lowest level of evidence — it describes one event, with no control, no measurement of confounders, and no ability to distinguish causation from coincidence — but it is also the only evidence available, and the pharmacist, needing to provide an answer, treats it as definitive. The harm is that the prescriber, receiving a confident-sounding recommendation, changes the regimen based on an observation that may not generalize — the interaction may have been caused by the patient's other conditions, by a different drug, or by chance. The correct response communicates the evidence grade explicitly: "this interaction is described in a single case report; causality is uncertain; monitor if the combination is necessary, but do not assume the interaction is established."

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

### Treating a confident conclusion as a substitute for evidence

A well-written, confident-sounding conclusion is accepted as proof. The trap is that the tone of certainty masks unstated assumptions, missing verification, or unresolved uncertainty, and the confident framing prevents anyone from asking the question that would expose the gap.

## Self-Check

- Did I reformulate the request into a structured clinical question specifying the population, drug, outcome, and context, or did I search on the vague original question?
- Did I go to the primary literature for questions where the clinical decision carries significant risk, or did I rely solely on a tertiary summary that may be outdated?
- Did I communicate the strength of the evidence alongside the answer, distinguishing a randomized trial from a case report from an extrapolation from absence of data?
- Did I distinguish "absence of evidence" (no studies) from "evidence of absence" (studies found no harm), or did I let the requester interpret the gap as safety?
- Did I ask why the information was needed before answering, to ensure I addressed the underlying clinical question rather than the literal question asked?
- Did I document the question, the search strategy, the sources, the answer, and the evidence grade, so the response can be verified and updated if new evidence emerges?
- Does the output stay within the agent's scope, deferring final authority, licensed judgment, or specialist sign-off to the qualified person where the question exceeds the agent's competence?
- Is the reasoning documented clearly enough that another practitioner could review the basis and reproduce the conclusion?
