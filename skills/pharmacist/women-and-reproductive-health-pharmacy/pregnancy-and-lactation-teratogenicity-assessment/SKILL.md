# Pregnancy and Lactation Teratogenicity Assessment

The medication use in pregnancy and lactation is a high-stakes decision because two patients are exposed (the mother and the developing fetus or the nursing infant), the evidence is limited by the ethical constraints on the research in pregnancy, and the fear of harm produces both the over-avoidance (the undertreatment of the maternal condition, which also harms the fetus) and the under-avoidance (the exposure to the teratogen). The danger is that the outdated risk categories (the FDA letters) are used as the sole reference when they have been replaced by the narrative format, that the teratogenicity is assessed without considering the gestational age (the critical periods of the organogenesis), that the lactation assessment conflates the drug in the milk with the harm to the infant (many drugs transfer but are not absorbed or are not toxic), and that the maternal condition is undertreated because of the fear. The pharmacist's discipline is to use the current evidence-based references, to assess the risk in the context of the gestational age and the maternal condition, to weigh the risk of the medication against the risk of the untreated disease, and to counsel with the balanced and the specific information.

## Core Rules

### Use the Current Evidence-Based References, Not the Outdated Risk Categories

The FDA pregnancy risk categories (A, B, C, D, X) were replaced in 2015 by the Pregnancy and Lactation Labeling Rule (PLLR), which provides the narrative summaries of the risk, the clinical considerations, and the data, because the letter categories were oversimplified and misleading (a category C drug could be safer than a category B drug depending on the context). The pharmacist must use the current references: the PLLR labeling, the Briggs textbook (Drugs in Pregnancy and Lactation), the LactMed database (the NIH database for the lactation), the reproductive toxicology services (REPROTOX, TERIS, Micromedex REPRORISK), and the current guidelines. The pharmacist must not rely on the letter categories alone, must recognize that the absence of data is not the evidence of harm (many drugs have no human data but are widely used without the apparent teratogenicity), and must distinguish the animal data (which may not predict the human risk) from the human data. The current references are the protection against the outdated and the misleading information.

### Assess the Risk in the Context of the Gestational Age

The teratogenicity is the gestational-age-dependent: the pre-implantation phase (the first 2 weeks, the all-or-nothing period), the organogenesis (weeks 3 to 8, the period of the highest teratogenic susceptibility, when the organs are forming), and the fetal period (week 9 to term, the period of the growth and the functional development, with the lower structural teratogenicity but the risks of the growth restriction, the functional effects, and the late effects). The pharmacist must determine the gestational age and must assess the risk in the context: the isotretinoin and the thalidomide are the teratogens throughout, the valproate and the carbamazepine are the neural tube defect risks in the early pregnancy, the ACE inhibitors and the ARBs cause the fetal renal damage in the second and third trimesters, the NSAIDs cause the fetal renal and the premature closure of the ductus in the third trimester, the warfarin causes the fetal warfarin syndrome throughout. The gestational-age-specific assessment is the protection against the generic and the wrong risk assessment.

### Weigh the Medication Risk Against the Risk of the Untreated Disease

The medication use in pregnancy is not the binary of safe versus harmful; it is the comparison of the medication risk against the risk of the untreated maternal disease, because the untreated disease also harms the fetus. The uncontrolled asthma causes the fetal hypoxia and the growth restriction, the uncontrolled diabetes causes the congenital anomalies and the macrosomia, the uncontrolled hypertension causes the placental insufficiency and the fetal growth restriction, the untreated depression causes the poor prenatal care and the postpartum relapse, the untreated epilepsy causes the seizures that harm the fetus. The pharmacist must assess both risks and must recommend the medication when the benefit of the disease control outweighs the medication risk, must select the safest effective option (the metformin over the sulfonylurea for the diabetes, the sertraline over the paroxetine for the depression, the lamotrigine over the valproate for the epilepsy), and must communicate the balanced risk to the patient and the prescriber. The under-treatment of the maternal condition out of the fear is the harm that the balanced assessment prevents.

### Assess the Lactation Risk on the Drug Transfer and the Infant Exposure

The lactation risk is the function of the drug transfer into the milk (the milk-to-plasma ratio, the oral bioavailability, the half-life, the protein binding) and the infant exposure (the infant's ability to absorb and to metabolize the drug, the age, the feeding pattern). The pharmacist must use the LactMed and the Briggs references to assess the transfer and the exposure, must recognize that many drugs transfer into the milk but are not absorbed by the infant or are not toxic at the transferred dose, and must distinguish the drugs to avoid (the radioactive isotopes, the certain chemotherapy, the amiodarone, the lithium in some cases, the high-dose aspirin) from the drugs that are compatible with the breastfeeding (most antibiotics, most antidepressants, most antihypertensives, the acetaminophen, the ibuprofen, the heparin). The pharmacist must consider the timing of the dose relative to the feeding (the taking the dose after the feeding, the avoiding the feeding at the peak concentration), the infant's age (the preterm and the neonate are more vulnerable), and the alternatives (the temporary pumping and discarding, the alternative drug). The lactation assessment is the specific, drug-and-infant-specific judgment, not the generic avoidance.

### Identify the Known Teratogens and the Contraindicated Drugs Explicitly

There are the known teratogens and the contraindicated drugs that must be identified explicitly and avoided, and the pharmacist must know them: the isotretinoin and the other retinoids (the severe craniofacial and cardiac defects), the thalidomide and the lenalidomide (the limb defects), the valproate (the neural tube defects and the developmental delay), the methotrexate (the multiple anomalies), the mycophenolate (the fetal malformations), the warfarin and the other oral anticoagulants (the fetal warfarin syndrome), the ACE inhibitors and the ARBs in the second and third trimester (the fetal renal damage), the tetracyclines (the tooth discoloration and the bone effects), the aminoglycosides (the fetal ototoxicity, though the risk is debated). The pharmacist must ensure the contraception for the women of the childbearing potential on the known teratogens (the isotretinoin and the mycophenolate programs), must counsel on the pregnancy planning and the preconception switch to the safer alternatives, and must document the counseling.

### Respect Scope and Escalation Boundaries

Know where the agent's authority and competence end. The teratogenicity assessment in the complex or the high-risk cases often requires the obstetrician, the maternal-fetal medicine specialist, the genetic counselor, or the reproductive toxicology service. When the question requires a license, a specialist's judgment, a final approval, or expertise the agent does not hold, the correct action is to escalate rather than to produce a confident answer that overreaches. Scope discipline protects the recipient from harm caused by an unqualified conclusion and protects the agent from liability. State explicitly when the output is advisory and must be confirmed by the qualified person.

## Common Traps

### Using the Outdated FDA Letter Categories as the Sole Reference

The pharmacist relies on the FDA pregnancy risk categories (A, B, C, D, X) as the sole reference for the teratogenicity assessment. The mechanism is that the letter categories are the familiar and the simple framework. The false signal is that the letter category represents the risk. The harm is that the categories were oversimplified and misleading (a category C drug could be safer than a category B drug in the context), and the reliance on them produces the wrong decisions (the avoidance of the effective drug, the use of the riskier drug). The pharmacist must use the current PLLR narrative labeling and the evidence-based references (Briggs, REPROTOX, TERIS) rather than the letter categories.

### Treating the Absence of Data as the Evidence of Harm

The drug has no human pregnancy data (only the animal data, or no data), and the pharmacist concludes the drug is harmful or should be avoided. The mechanism is that the absence of the data feels like the unknown risk. The false signal is that the lack of the data means the drug is dangerous. The harm is that many drugs have no human data but are widely used without the apparent teratogenicity, and the avoidance of the effective drug leads to the under-treatment of the maternal condition, which also harms the fetus. The pharmacist must distinguish the absence of the data from the evidence of harm and must assess the risk in the context of the animal data, the mechanism, and the disease.

### Continuing the ACE Inhibitor or the ARB Into the Second and Third Trimester

The patient on the ACE inhibitor or the ARB becomes or is pregnant, and the medication is continued into the second and third trimester without the switch. The mechanism is that the ACE inhibitors are common and the first trimester risk was debated. The false signal is that the ACE inhibitor was tolerated in the first trimester. The harm is that the ACE inhibitors and the ARBs cause the fetal renal damage (the oligohydramnios, the renal failure), the skull hypoplasia, and the death in the second and third trimester, and the continuation produces the severe fetal harm. The pharmacist must recommend the switch to the safer antihypertensive (the labetalol, the nifedipine, the methyldopa) as soon as the pregnancy is recognized.

### Under-treating the Maternal Condition Out of the Fear

The pregnant patient has the asthma, the depression, the hypertension, or the epilepsy, and the condition is under-treated because the medications are feared. The mechanism is that the medication risk is visible, and the disease risk is invisible. The false signal is that the avoidance of the medication is the safe choice. The harm is that the untreated maternal disease (the uncontrolled asthma, the depression, the hypertension, the epilepsy) also harms the fetus, often more than the carefully selected medication, and the under-treatment produces the maternal and the fetal harm that the balanced assessment would have prevented. The pharmacist must weigh both risks and recommend the disease control with the safest effective option.

### Conflating the Drug in the Milk With the Harm to the Infant

The drug transfers into the breast milk, and the pharmacist concludes the drug is harmful to the nursing infant and recommends the discontinuation of the breastfeeding or the medication. The mechanism is that the presence of the drug in the milk feels like the risk. The false signal is that the drug in the milk means the drug harms the infant. The harm is that many drugs transfer into the milk but are not absorbed by the infant or are not toxic at the transferred dose, and the unnecessary discontinuation of the breastfeeding deprives the infant of the benefits and the medication deprives the mother of the treatment. The pharmacist must assess the transfer and the infant exposure with the LactMed and the Briggs references.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- Did I use the current evidence-based references (the PLLR narrative labeling, the Briggs textbook, the LactMed, the REPROTOX, the TERIS) rather than the outdated FDA letter categories, for the teratogenicity and the lactation assessment?
- Did I determine the gestational age and assess the teratogenicity risk in the context (the organogenesis for the structural defects, the fetal period for the growth and the functional effects, the second and third trimester for the ACE inhibitors and the ARBs and the NSAIDs)?
- Did I weigh the medication risk against the risk of the untreated maternal disease, and recommend the disease control with the safest effective option when the benefit of the control outweighs the medication risk?
- For the lactation, did I assess the drug transfer (the milk-to-plasma ratio, the oral bioavailability, the half-life) and the infant exposure (the age, the feeding pattern), and distinguish the drugs to avoid from the drugs compatible with the breastfeeding?
- Did I identify the known teratogens and the contraindicated drugs explicitly (the isotretinoin, the thalidomide, the valproate, the methotrexate, the mycophenolate, the warfarin, the ACE inhibitors and ARBs in the second and third trimester), ensure the contraception for the women of the childbearing potential, and counsel on the preconception switch?
- Did I counsel the patient with the balanced and the specific information (the risk, the benefit, the alternatives, the monitoring), and document the counseling?
- Does the output stay within the agent's scope, deferring the complex or the high-risk cases to the obstetrician, the maternal-fetal medicine specialist, or the reproductive toxicology service where the question exceeds the agent's competence?
- Is the assessment documented clearly enough that another practitioner could review the basis and reproduce the recommendation?
