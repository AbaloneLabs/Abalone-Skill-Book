# Menopause Hormone Therapy Management

Menopausal hormone therapy is the most effective treatment for the vasomotor symptoms (the hot flashes, the night sweats) and the genitourinary syndrome of menopause (the vaginal dryness, the dyspareunia, the urinary symptoms), but its use is constrained by the risks (the breast cancer, the cardiovascular events, the venous thromboembolism, the stroke) that depend on the formulation, the route, the dose, the timing, and the patient's risk profile. The danger is that the hormone therapy is either over-feared (the patient who would benefit is denied the relief because of the undifferentiated fear from the Women's Health Initiative) or under-cautious (the patient with the contraindication is given the systemic therapy without the screening), and that the local vaginal estrogen for the genitourinary syndrome is conflated with the systemic therapy and withheld from the patients who need it. The pharmacist's discipline is to differentiate the systemic from the local therapy, to screen the patient for the contraindications, to use the lowest effective dose for the shortest necessary duration in the appropriate window, and to select the formulation that minimizes the risk.

## Core Rules

### Differentiate the Systemic From the Local Therapy by the Indication

The systemic hormone therapy (the oral tablets, the transdermal patches, the gels, the sprays) delivers the estrogen to the systemic circulation and treats the vasomotor symptoms, the systemic symptoms, and the bone loss, but it carries the systemic risks (the VTE, the stroke, the breast cancer with the combined therapy). The local vaginal estrogen (the creams, the tablets, the rings, the low-dose formulations) delivers the estrogen locally to the vaginal and the urethral tissues and treats the genitourinary syndrome of menopause, with the minimal systemic absorption and the minimal systemic risk. The pharmacist must differentiate the two by the indication: the systemic therapy for the vasomotor symptoms, the local therapy for the genitourinary syndrome. The pharmacist must recognize that the local vaginal estrogen is often appropriate even in the patients with the contraindications to the systemic therapy (the breast cancer survivors, the VTE history), with the oncology consultation, because the systemic absorption is minimal and the symptom relief is significant. The conflation of the local with the systemic therapy is the common error that withholds the effective and the safe local therapy from the patients who need it.

### Screen for the Contraindications Before the Systemic Therapy

The systemic hormone therapy has the contraindications that must be screened before the initiation: the current or the past breast cancer (the estrogen-dependent), the estrogen-dependent gynecologic cancers (the endometrial), the undiagnosed vaginal bleeding, the active liver disease, the active or the history of the venous thromboembolism, the active or the recent arterial thromboembolic disease (the stroke, the myocardial infarction), the uncontrolled hypertension, and the pregnancy. The pharmacist must screen for these conditions, must not recommend the systemic therapy in the contraindicated patients, and must recommend the non-hormonal alternatives (the SSRIs and the SNRIs like the paroxetine, the venlafaxine, the escitalopram; the gabapentin; the clonidine; the oxybutynin) for the vasomotor symptoms in the contraindicated patients. The pharmacist must also assess the risk factors that increase the systemic risk (the age over 60, the more than 10 years since the menopause, the obesity, the immobility) and must weigh them in the decision.

### Apply the Timing and the Duration Principles

The systemic hormone therapy has the timing window: the initiation within 10 years of the menopause and before the age of 60 (the window of opportunity) is associated with the favorable benefit-to-risk ratio (the symptom relief, the bone protection, the possible cardiovascular benefit), while the initiation after the age of 60 or the more than 10 years since the menopause is associated with the increased risks (the cardiovascular events, the stroke, the dementia) that may outweigh the benefits. The pharmacist must apply the timing principles: the systemic therapy is the most favorable when started in the window, and the caution is required when starting outside the window. The duration must be the shortest necessary to manage the symptoms, with the periodic reassessment (the annual review of the ongoing need, the tapering and the discontinuation trial), because the vasomotor symptoms often resolve over the years, and the prolonged therapy increases the risks (the breast cancer with the combined therapy beyond 3 to 5 years). The pharmacist must counsel on the duration and the periodic reassessment.

### Select the Formulation and the Route to Minimize the Risk

The formulation and the route of the systemic hormone therapy affect the risk profile, and the pharmacist must select the option that minimizes the risk for the patient. The transdermal route (the patch, the gel, the spray) is associated with the lower VTE and the stroke risk than the oral route (the first-pass effect of the oral estrogen increases the clotting factors), and is the preferred route for the patients with the VTE risk factors (the obesity, the immobility, the age). The lowest effective dose should be used (the lower doses are effective for the symptoms and carry the lower risks). The progestogen is required for the women with the intact uterus (to prevent the endometrial hyperplasia and the cancer from the unopposed estrogen), and the choice of the progestogen (the micronized progesterone, the medroxyprogesterone, the norethindrone, the levonorgestrel) affects the adverse effect profile. The tissue-selective estrogen complex (the conjugated estrogen with the bazedoxifene, a SERM that protects the endometrium) is an option that avoids the progestogen. The pharmacist must select the formulation, the route, and the dose based on the patient's uterus status, the risk factors, and the preferences.

### Provide the Endometrial Protection for the Women With the Uterus

The systemic estrogen without the progestogen causes the endometrial hyperplasia and the endometrial cancer in the women with the intact uterus, and the endometrial protection is the mandatory component of the systemic therapy for these women. The pharmacist must ensure the progestogen is added (the continuous combined regimen for the postmenopausal women, the sequential regimen for the perimenopausal women with the cyclic withdrawal bleeding), must verify the adequate dose and the duration of the progestogen (the continuous daily, or the 12 to 14 days per month for the sequential), and must counsel on the expected bleeding patterns (the irregular bleeding in the first 6 months of the continuous regimen, the regular withdrawal bleeding with the sequential). The pharmacist must ensure the investigation of the unexpected bleeding (the bleeding after the amenorrhea, the heavy or the prolonged bleeding), because the unexpected bleeding may signal the endometrial pathology and requires the evaluation. The women without the uterus (the post-hysterectomy) do not need the progestogen and should receive the estrogen alone.

### Respect Scope and Escalation Boundaries

Know where the agent's authority and competence end. The hormone therapy initiation, the complex risk assessment (the breast cancer history, the VTE history), and the unexpected bleeding evaluation require the prescriber, the gynecologist, and sometimes the oncologist. When the question requires a license, a specialist's judgment, a final approval, or expertise the agent does not hold, the correct action is to escalate rather than to produce a confident answer that overreaches. Scope discipline protects the recipient from harm caused by an unqualified conclusion and protects the agent from liability. State explicitly when the output is advisory and must be confirmed by the qualified person.

## Common Traps

### Conflating the Local Vaginal Estrogen With the Systemic Therapy

The patient with the genitourinary syndrome of menopause (the vaginal dryness, the dyspareunia) is denied the local vaginal estrogen because the hormone therapy is feared, especially in the breast cancer survivor. The mechanism is that the local estrogen is a hormone therapy, so the systemic risk is assumed. The false signal is that the local estrogen carries the systemic risk. The harm is that the local vaginal estrogen has the minimal systemic absorption and the minimal systemic risk, and the withholding deprives the patient of the effective relief of the distressing symptoms, with the oncology consultation for the cancer survivors. The pharmacist must differentiate the local from the systemic therapy and must recommend the local therapy for the genitourinary syndrome.

### Initiating the Systemic Therapy Outside the Timing Window

The patient over 60 or the more than 10 years since the menopause is started on the systemic hormone therapy for the vasomotor symptoms without the consideration of the timing. The mechanism is that the symptoms are present, so the therapy is initiated. The false signal is that the symptom relief justifies the therapy at any age. The harm is that the initiation outside the window (over 60, or the more than 10 years since the menopause) is associated with the increased risks of the cardiovascular events, the stroke, and the dementia, and the risk may outweigh the benefit. The pharmacist must apply the timing principles and must use the caution or the non-hormonal alternatives outside the window.

### Omitting the Progestogen for the Woman With the Uterus

The woman with the intact uterus is given the systemic estrogen without the progestogen, and the endometrium is exposed to the unopposed estrogen. The mechanism is that the estrogen alone is simpler or the progestogen is overlooked. The false signal is that the estrogen is the hormone therapy. The harm is that the unopposed estrogen causes the endometrial hyperplasia and the endometrial cancer, and the omission of the progestogen produces the preventable cancer. The pharmacist must ensure the progestogen is added for the women with the intact uterus, in the adequate dose and the adequate duration, and must reserve the estrogen alone for the women without the uterus.

### Using the Oral Route in the Patient With the VTE Risk Factors

The patient with the VTE risk factors (the obesity, the immobility, the age, the prior VTE history that is not an absolute contraindication) is given the oral estrogen rather than the transdermal. The mechanism is that the oral route is the familiar and the cheaper route. The false signal is that the oral estrogen is the standard. The harm is that the oral estrogen (with the first-pass effect) increases the clotting factors and the VTE risk more than the transdermal, and the patient with the risk factors has the higher absolute risk of the clot. The pharmacist must select the transdermal route for the patients with the VTE risk factors.

### Continuing the Combined Therapy Beyond 3 to 5 Years Without Reassessment

The patient on the combined estrogen-progestogen therapy continues for the years beyond the symptom resolution without the reassessment or the discontinuation trial. The mechanism is that the therapy is working, so it is continued. The false signal is that the ongoing therapy is the safe choice. The harm is that the combined therapy beyond 3 to 5 years is associated with the increased breast cancer risk, and the prolonged therapy increases the risk without the ongoing benefit if the symptoms have resolved. The pharmacist must recommend the periodic reassessment (the annual review) and the discontinuation trial.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- Did I differentiate the systemic hormone therapy (for the vasomotor symptoms) from the local vaginal estrogen (for the genitourinary syndrome), and recognize that the local therapy is often appropriate even in the patients with the systemic contraindications?
- Did I screen for the contraindications to the systemic therapy (the breast cancer, the endometrial cancer, the undiagnosed bleeding, the active liver disease, the VTE history, the arterial thromboembolic disease, the uncontrolled hypertension), and recommend the non-hormonal alternatives for the vasomotor symptoms in the contraindicated patients?
- Did I apply the timing principles (the initiation within 10 years of the menopause and before 60 for the favorable ratio, the caution outside the window), and the duration principles (the shortest necessary, the periodic reassessment, the discontinuation trial)?
- Did I select the formulation, the route, and the dose to minimize the risk (the transdermal for the VTE risk factors, the lowest effective dose), and provide the endometrial protection (the progestogen) for the women with the intact uterus?
- Did I counsel on the expected bleeding patterns (the irregular in the first 6 months of the continuous, the withdrawal with the sequential) and ensure the investigation of the unexpected bleeding?
- Did I recommend the periodic reassessment (the annual review) and the consideration of the discontinuation trial, especially for the combined therapy beyond 3 to 5 years?
- Does the output stay within the agent's scope, deferring the initiation, the complex risk assessment, and the unexpected bleeding evaluation to the prescriber, the gynecologist, or the oncologist where the question exceeds the agent's competence?
- Is the selection and the counseling documented clearly enough that another practitioner could review the basis and reproduce the recommendation?
