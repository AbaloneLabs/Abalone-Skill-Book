# Hormonal Contraception Selection and Safety Screening

Hormonal contraception is one of the most commonly used medication classes, and the selection is often treated as routine when it is in fact a clinical decision with real risks: the combined methods (the pills, the patch, the ring) carry the venous thromboembolism risk of the estrogen, the progestin-only methods have their own adverse effect profiles, and the wrong method in the wrong patient produces harm (the stroke in the migraine with aura, the clot in the smoker over 35, the pregnancy from the enzyme-inducing drug interaction). The danger is that the safety screening is reduced to a checklist that is completed without understanding, that the contraindications are missed because the right question was not asked (the migraine aura, the postpartum status, the breastfeeding), and that the drug interactions are overlooked (the enzyme inducers that reduce the efficacy). The pharmacist's discipline is to perform the safety screening against the evidence-based framework (the CDC Medical Eligibility Criteria), to match the method to the patient's risk profile and preferences, and to counsel on the correct use and the missed-dose management.

## Core Rules

### Screen Every Patient Against the CDC Medical Eligibility Criteria

The contraceptive selection must begin with the safety screening, and the screening must use the evidence-based framework rather than the ad hoc judgment. The CDC Medical Eligibility Criteria (MEC) classifies the conditions into four categories (1: no restriction, 2: advantages generally outweigh risks, 3: risks generally outweigh advantages, 4: unacceptable health risk), separately for each method (the combined hormonal, the progestin-only, the IUDs, the barrier). The pharmacist must screen for the conditions that change the category: the hypertension, the smoking (especially over 35), the migraine (with aura is the critical distinction), the venous thromboembolism history, the breast cancer, the liver disease, the postpartum status (the timing and the breastfeeding), the diabetes with complications, the lupus, the sickle cell, and the solid organ transplant. The pharmacist must apply the MEC category to the method under consideration, must not recommend the category 4 methods and must use caution with the category 3 methods, and must document the screening. The screening is the protection against the harm, and the screening that is skipped is the harm waiting to occur.

### Distinguish the Combined Methods From the Progestin-Only Methods by the Estrogen Risk

The combined hormonal contraceptives (the combined oral pills, the transdermal patch, the vaginal ring) contain the estrogen (typically ethinyl estradiol) and the progestin, and the estrogen carries the venous thromboembolism risk, the stroke risk, and the myocardial infarction risk, which are amplified by the smoking, the hypertension, the migraine with aura, the obesity, the immobility, and the age over 35. The progestin-only methods (the progestin-only pills, the depot medroxyprogesterone injection, the etonogestrel implant, the levonorgestrel IUD) do not contain the estrogen and do not carry the estrogen risks, and they are the methods of choice for the patients with the estrogen contraindications (the smoking over 35, the migraine with aura, the hypertension, the VTE history, the postpartum and the breastfeeding). The pharmacist must recognize when the patient has the estrogen contraindication and must steer toward the progestin-only method, and must recognize that the progestin-only methods have their own considerations (the DMPA and the bone density with the long-term use, the irregular bleeding with all the progestin-only methods, the contraceptive efficacy differences).

### Address the Drug Interactions That Reduce the Contraceptive Efficacy

The hormonal contraceptives are metabolized by the cytochrome P450 enzymes, and the enzyme inducers reduce the contraceptive hormone levels and the efficacy, producing the unintended pregnancies. The pharmacist must screen for the enzyme inducers: the rifampin and the rifabutin (the strong inducers, the contraindication to the hormonal contraception), the certain anticonvulsants (the phenytoin, the carbamazepine, the phenobarbital, the primidone, the topiramate at high doses, the oxcarbazepine), the certain antiretrovirals (some protease inhibitors and non-nucleoside reverse transcriptase inhibitors), and the St. John's wort. The pharmacist must recognize that the enzyme inducers reduce the efficacy of all the hormonal methods except the DMPA and the copper IUD (which are not affected), and must recommend the backup method (the condom) or the alternative method (the IUD, the DMPA) during the inducer use and for 28 days after. The pharmacist must also recognize that the broad-spectrum antibiotics do not reduce the efficacy (the prior concern was not supported by the evidence), and must not recommend the backup for the routine antibiotics.

### Match the Method to the Patient's Preferences and the Adherence Realities

The contraceptive efficacy depends on the adherence, and the methods vary in the adherence demands: the long-acting reversible contraceptives (the etonogestrel implant, the IUDs) require no adherence after the placement and have the lowest typical-use failure rates (under 1 percent), the DMPA requires the quarterly injection, the combined and the progestin-only pills require the daily adherence (with the typical-use failure rates of 7 to 9 percent), the patch requires the weekly change, and the ring requires the monthly change. The pharmacist must match the method to the patient's ability and willingness to adhere, must present the long-acting reversible options as the first-line for the patients who want the reliable contraception without the daily demands, and must respect the patient's preferences (the control, the reversibility, the cycle control, the non-contraceptive benefits, the privacy). The pharmacist must counsel on the adverse effects (the irregular bleeding, the nausea, the breast tenderness, the mood changes) and the time to the onset of the contraceptive efficacy (the backup needed for the first 7 days when starting certain methods).

### Counsel on the Missed-Dose and the Missed-Method Management

The missed doses and the missed methods are the primary cause of the contraceptive failure in the adherence-dependent methods, and the management must be clear and the patient must know it before the missed dose occurs. The pharmacist must counsel on the missed-pill management: for the combined pill, the missed one pill is taken as soon as remembered and the next at the usual time (no backup), the missed two or more pills requires taking the active pills and using the backup for 7 days and considering the emergency contraception if the intercourse occurred. For the progestin-only pill, the missed pill (more than 3 hours late) requires the backup for 48 hours and the emergency contraception consideration. For the patch and the ring, the detachment or the late removal has the specific management. The pharmacist must counsel on the emergency contraception (the ulipristal acetate, the levonorgestrel, the copper IUD) as the backup for the contraceptive failure, with the timing (the sooner the more effective, up to 5 days). The counseling must be clear, written, and verified with the teach-back.

### Respect Scope and Escalation Boundaries

Know where the agent's authority and competence end. The contraceptive provision often involves the prescriber, the collaborative practice agreement, and the clinical examination (the blood pressure, the history). When the question requires a license, a specialist's judgment, a final approval, or expertise the agent does not hold, the correct action is to escalate rather than to produce a confident answer that overreaches. Scope discipline protects the recipient from harm caused by an unqualified conclusion and protects the agent from liability. State explicitly when the output is advisory and must be confirmed by the qualified person.

## Common Traps

### Missing the Migraine With Aura Contraindication to the Combined Method

The patient reports the migraines, and the combined hormonal contraceptive is dispensed without determining whether the migraines have the aura. The mechanism is that the migraines are noted, but the aura is not asked about. The false signal is that the migraines are common and benign. The harm is that the migraine with aura is the category 4 contraindication to the combined hormonal contraception (the ischemic stroke risk is substantially elevated), and the combined method in this patient produces the stroke that the screening would have prevented. The pharmacist must ask specifically about the aura (the visual, the sensory, the motor symptoms preceding the headache) and must steer the aura patient to the progestin-only method.

### Overlooking the Smoking Over 35 Contraindication

The patient over 35 who smokes is given the combined hormonal contraceptive without the smoking status being asked or the age being considered. The mechanism is that the smoking and the age are not part of the routine screening. The false signal is that the combined pill is the standard contraceptive. The harm is that the smoking over 35 is the category 4 contraindication to the combined hormonal contraception (the cardiovascular risk is substantially elevated), and the combined method in this patient produces the myocardial infarction or the stroke. The pharmacist must ask the smoking status and the age and must steer the smoking-over-35 patient to the progestin-only method.

### Missing the Enzyme-Inducer Drug Interaction

The patient on the rifampin, the phenytoin, or the certain antiretrovirals is given the hormonal contraceptive without the interaction being addressed. The mechanism is that the interaction is not in the routine screening. The false signal is that the contraceptive is effective regardless of the other medications. The harm is that the enzyme inducers reduce the contraceptive hormone levels and the efficacy, and the patient has the unintended pregnancy that the interaction caused. The pharmacist must screen for the enzyme inducers and must recommend the backup method or the alternative method (the DMPA, the copper IUD) that is not affected.

### Recommending the Backup for the Routine Antibiotics

The patient on the hormonal contraceptive is prescribed a broad-spectrum antibiotic, and the pharmacist counsels to use the backup method during the antibiotic course. The mechanism is that the prior teaching linked the antibiotics to the contraceptive failure. The false signal is that the antibiotics reduce the efficacy. The harm is that the unnecessary backup counseling causes the confusion, the unnecessary condom use, and the distrust of the contraceptive, and it distracts from the real interactions (the enzyme inducers) that do reduce the efficacy. The pharmacist must not recommend the backup for the routine antibiotics and must reserve the interaction concern for the enzyme inducers.

### Under-counseling on the Missed-Dose Management

The patient is given the contraceptive pills without the clear counseling on the missed-dose management, and when the pills are missed, the patient does not know what to do. The mechanism is that the daily use is counseled, but the missed scenario is not. The false signal is that the daily use is the only instruction. The harm is that the missed pills are the primary cause of the contraceptive failure, and the patient who does not know the management either takes no action (and risks the pregnancy) or takes the wrong action (and either over-treats or under-treats). The pharmacist must counsel the missed-dose management clearly, in writing, and verified with the teach-back.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- Did I screen every patient against the CDC Medical Eligibility Criteria for the conditions that change the category (the hypertension, the smoking, the migraine with aura, the VTE history, the postpartum and breastfeeding status, the diabetes, the liver disease)?
- Did I distinguish the combined methods (with the estrogen risk) from the progestin-only methods, and did I steer the patients with the estrogen contraindications (the smoking over 35, the migraine with aura, the hypertension, the VTE history) to the progestin-only methods?
- Did I screen for the enzyme-inducing medications (the rifampin, the enzyme-inducing anticonvulsants, the certain antiretrovirals, the St. John's wort) and recommend the backup method or the alternative method (the DMPA, the copper IUD) during the inducer use and for 28 days after?
- Did I match the method to the patient's adherence ability and preferences, presenting the long-acting reversible contraceptives as the first-line for the reliable contraception, and respecting the patient's preferences for the control, the reversibility, and the non-contraceptive benefits?
- Did I counsel on the missed-dose and the missed-method management (the combined pill, the progestin-only pill, the patch, the ring) and on the emergency contraception, clearly, in writing, and verified with the teach-back?
- Did I avoid the unnecessary backup counseling for the routine antibiotics, reserving the interaction concern for the enzyme inducers?
- Does the output stay within the agent's scope, deferring the clinical examination, the prescriber confirmation, and the method placement to the qualified person where the question exceeds the agent's competence?
- Is the screening and the selection documented clearly enough that another practitioner could review the basis and reproduce the recommendation?
