# Compounding and Dosing for Veterinary Patients

Veterinary compounding and dosing differ from human pharmacy in ways that are easy to underestimate, and the gap is where most of the harm occurs. A dose is not transferable across species, a flavor that delights a dog can be rejected or toxic to a cat, a dosage form that works for a human cannot be assumed for a horse or a parrot, and the federal rules that govern compounding for animals are distinct from those that govern human compounding. The danger is that the preparation looks correct — the right drug, a plausible concentration, a clean label — while the dose is wrong for the species, the vehicle is unsuitable for the patient, or the compound itself is illegal because it was made from bulk drug for a food animal or in violation of the FD&C Act. The pharmacist's discipline is to treat every veterinary prescription as species-specific, to verify the dose against a veterinary reference rather than extrapolating from human data, to select vehicles and flavors appropriate to the species, and to apply the FDA compounding rules for animals, deferring therapeutic and diagnostic decisions to the veterinarian of record.

## Core Rules

### Calculate Every Veterinary Dose Against Species-Specific References, Never Human Extrapolation

A veterinary dose is never a human dose scaled down by weight. Drug disposition differs dramatically across species: cats are deficient in glucuronidation and cannot clear many drugs that dogs tolerate; horses have a large hindgut where oral antibiotics disrupt the microbiome and can cause colitis; ruminants' forestomach alters oral drug absorption; birds and reptiles have variable body temperatures that change kinetics. The pharmacist must obtain the patient's actual current body weight in the correct unit (kilograms for small animals, grams for very small exotics, pounds commonly for horses, with explicit unit conversion), confirm the species and, where it matters, the breed, and verify the mg/kg dose and interval against a current veterinary reference such as Plumb's Veterinary Drug Handbook, the formulary section of the Veterinary Pharmacology and Therapeutics text, or a peer-reviewed veterinary guideline. Allometric scaling (for example the equation-based interspecies scaling using body weight to the 0.67 or 0.75 power) can support cross-species estimates but must not replace a published species-specific dose when one exists. The calculated dose must be checked against the maximum dose for that species and the known toxic threshold, and any dose outside the expected range must be clarified with the prescribing veterinarian before dispensing, because the veterinarian — not the pharmacist — owns the therapeutic decision.

### Match the Dosage Form and Route to the Species and the Patient

The dosage form that is convenient for a human is frequently wrong for an animal. Cats are notoriously difficult to dose orally and often require small-volume liquids, transdermal preparations (for select drugs such as methimazole), or palatable forms; dogs often accept chewable tablets, pastes, or flavored suspensions but vary by individual; horses are dosed orally as pastes, suspensions via nasogastric tube, or by oral dosing syringe, and their large volume of distribution often makes the injectable route more practical; exotics (rabbits, ferrets, birds, reptiles) require very small, accurately measurable volumes and often a specific route dictated by their anatomy. The pharmacist must confirm the route is feasible for the owner to administer (an owner who cannot give a cat a pill will not comply, and non-adherence becomes the treatment failure), select a concentration that allows accurate measurement of the small dose, and verify that the formulation is stable and compatible with the route. A transdermal preparation must only be used for drugs with evidence of absorption by that route in that species; assuming transdermal absorption from human data leads to underdosing and treatment failure. When the route or form is impractical, the pharmacist should raise the concern with the veterinarian rather than silently dispensing a form the owner cannot use.

### Select Flavors and Vehicles That Are Safe and Palatable for the Target Species

Flavoring is not cosmetic in veterinary compounding; it determines whether the animal receives the medication at all, and the wrong flavor or vehicle can be directly toxic. Cats lack a sweet receptor and generally reject sweet flavors; they often accept fish, liver, or poultry bases, and many cats will not accept any oral medication, making palatable or transdermal options essential. Dogs are often attracted to sweet and meaty flavors, and xylitol — a common sweetener in human products and some flavor bases — is highly toxic to dogs and must never be used. Onions, garlic, chocolate, grape, and macadamia flavor components are toxic across species and must be excluded. Horses accept molasses, apple, and anise flavors but the vehicle must be stable in a large-volume oral form. The pharmacist must verify that every excipient, flavor, and base in the compound is safe for the target species, must use only ingredients with an established safety profile in that species, and must document the source and lot of each component for traceability.

### Apply the FDA Compounding Rules for Animal Patients, Including the Bulk Drug Restrictions

Compounding for animal patients is governed by provisions of the FD&C Act and FDA guidance that differ from human compounding under 503A and 503B. For non-food-producing animals, compounding from bulk drug substances is permitted only when there is no approved animal or human drug that can be used as written or with minor alteration, and only to produce a preparation needed for the specific patient's diagnosed condition; FDA maintains a list of bulk drug substances for compounding for certain non-food species. For food-producing animals (cattle, pigs, poultry, lactating dairy animals, aquaculture, and any animal intended for human consumption), compounding from bulk drugs is prohibited except under very narrow conditions, because of the risk of drug residues in the human food supply. The pharmacist must determine whether the patient is a food animal before compounding, must refuse to compound from bulk for a food animal absent the specific narrow exception, and must document the basis for any compound. When an approved animal drug exists, it must be used in preference to compounding; compounding of a copy of an approved animal drug is prohibited. All compounding must be performed from finished approved products whenever possible, and the beyond-use date must follow USP standards appropriate to the preparation.

### Establish and Document the Beyond-Use Date, Stability, and Storage for Each Compound

Veterinary compounded preparations frequently lack the stability data that commercial products carry, and the assigned beyond-use date must be conservative and evidence-based. The pharmacist must assign a BUD based on the formulation's stability (the drug in the specific vehicle, at the specific concentration, at the storage temperature), the route of administration, and USP compounding chapters where applicable, and must default to the shorter of the stability-supported date and the default USP BUD when specific data are absent. Suspensions, transdermal gels, and flavored chewables each have distinct stability considerations; a suspension may settle or degrade faster than the default BUD assumes. The label must state the BUD, the storage condition, and any handling instruction, and the owner must be counseled that a compounded preparation has a shorter shelf life than a commercial product. Stability must not be assumed from a different concentration or a different vehicle; a study supporting one formulation does not transfer to another.

### Counsel the Owner on Administration, Adherence, and Adverse Signs

The animal's owner is the actual administrator, and the counseling must equip them to give the medication correctly and to recognize problems. The pharmacist must demonstrate the administration technique for the chosen form and route, must explain the timing and relationship to feeding (some drugs must be given with food, others on an empty stomach, and this varies by species), must explain what to do if the animal vomits or rejects a dose, and must describe the expected effect and the adverse signs that warrant calling the veterinarian. The owner must be told never to use the medication in another animal without veterinary direction, never to use human products as substitutes, and never to adjust the dose. For transdermal preparations, the owner must be warned to wear gloves and to keep the application site away from other animals and people. Adherence in veterinary patients is often worse than in human patients because the animal cannot understand the rationale and may resist; the pharmacist should anticipate this and propose practical strategies.

### Document the Basis and the Reasoning

Every conclusion should be traceable to its evidence, assumptions, and alternatives considered. Record not only the outcome but the reasoning path: what was checked, what was ruled out, what uncertainty remains, and what would change the conclusion. Documentation that captures the basis allows another practitioner to review, reproduce, or challenge the work, and it prevents confident conclusions from becoming unrepeatable assertions. A decision made without a recorded basis cannot be audited, improved, or safely handed off.

## Common Traps

### The Human Dose Scaled by Weight

The pharmacist receives a veterinary prescription and, lacking a veterinary reference, calculates the dose by scaling the human dose to the animal's weight. The mechanism is that the human dose is readily available and the arithmetic is straightforward. The false signal is that the calculation produced a number, so a dose has been determined. The harm is that species differences in metabolism, clearance, and sensitivity make the scaled dose wrong — sometimes dramatically, as with acetaminophen in cats or NSAIDs across species — and the patient is overdosed, underdosed, or poisoned. The pharmacist must use a veterinary reference for every veterinary dose and must never extrapolate from human data.

### Flavor or Vehicle Toxic to the Species

A flavored suspension is compounded with a palatable base chosen for general appeal, without checking whether the flavor or excipient is safe for the species. The mechanism is that the flavor is marketed as pet-friendly or is used in human compounding. The false signal is that the ingredient is food-grade, so it must be safe. The harm is that species-specific toxicities are invisible in the labeling: xylitol is a dog toxin, onion and garlic components damage red blood cells in cats and dogs, and a "safe" human flavor can sicken or kill the patient. Every excipient must be verified safe for the target species.

### Compounding From Bulk for a Food Animal

A dairy goat or a backyard flock chicken is prescribed a compounded medication, and the pharmacist prepares it from bulk drug because that is the only practical source. The mechanism is that the patient is an animal and the bulk drug is available. The false signal is that compounding for an animal patient is routine. The harm is that compounding from bulk for a food animal is prohibited except in narrow exceptions, because unapproved bulk-drug residues enter the milk, meat, or eggs and reach the human food supply, with no established tolerance or withdrawal time. The pharmacist must determine food-animal status before compounding and must refuse the bulk compound.

### Assuming Transdermal Absorption Across Species

A drug is formulated as a transdermal gel for a cat because the cat will not take oral medication, and the pharmacist assumes the drug will absorb based on human transdermal data. The mechanism is that the transdermal route is attractive for a non-compliant patient. The false signal is that the formulation is a gel applied to the skin, so it must deliver the drug. The harm is that transdermal absorption is highly drug- and species-specific; many drugs do not absorb transdermally in cats at all, and the patient is effectively untreated while the owner believes the medication is working. Transdermal use must be supported by species-specific evidence.

### The Approved Animal Drug Exists

A compound is prepared for an animal patient when an FDA-approved animal drug of the same active ingredient and suitable strength is commercially available. The mechanism is that the veterinarian wrote for a compound and the pharmacist filled it. The false signal is that a prescription was written, so compounding is appropriate. The harm is that compounding a copy of an available approved animal drug is prohibited, the compounded product lacks the stability and bioavailability data of the approved product, and the patient may receive an inferior preparation. The pharmacist must verify that no approved animal drug is available before compounding.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- Did I verify the dose against a current veterinary reference for the specific species, using the patient's actual current weight in the correct unit, rather than scaling from a human dose?
- Did I confirm the species and, where relevant, the breed (for example MDR1-sensitive collies), and check for species-specific contraindications and toxicities before dispensing?
- Did I select a dosage form and route that the owner can realistically administer, and a concentration that allows accurate measurement of the small dose?
- Did I verify that every flavor, base, and excipient is safe for the target species, excluding xylitol for dogs and other species-specific toxins?
- Did I determine whether the patient is a food-producing animal, and if so, did I refuse compounding from bulk absent a narrow documented exception and consult FARAD for withdrawal guidance?
- Did I assign a conservative, evidence-based beyond-use date appropriate to the formulation and storage condition, and label the BUD, storage, and handling clearly?
- Did I counsel the owner on administration technique, timing with food, what to do if a dose is rejected, expected effects, adverse signs, and the rule against using the medication in other animals?
- Does the output stay within the agent's scope, deferring diagnosis and therapeutic decisions to the veterinarian of record and escalating regulatory questions to the board of pharmacy or FDA-CVM where the question exceeds the agent's competence?
- Is the reasoning documented clearly enough that another practitioner could review the basis and reproduce the conclusion?
