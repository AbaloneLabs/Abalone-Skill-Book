# Veterinary Drug Interactions and Contraindications

Veterinary drug safety cannot be carried over from human pharmacy, and the assumption that it can is the source of catastrophic, preventable harm. A drug that is first-line in humans can be lethal in a cat; a common sweetener is a dog toxin; a topical insecticide used safely on dogs kills cats; an interaction that is minor in one species is severe in another; and a drug given to a dairy cow can contaminate the milk that a human infant drinks. The danger is that the medication passes every human-oriented safety check — the dose looks reasonable, the interaction software returns no alert, the drug is in stock — while it is in fact contraindicated, toxic, or illegal for the species in front of the pharmacist. The pharmacist's discipline is to screen every veterinary prescription against species-specific toxicity data and breed-specific sensitivities, to evaluate interactions using veterinary references rather than human databases, to determine food-animal residue obligations before dispensing, and to defer diagnosis and therapeutic decisions to the veterinarian while owning the medication-safety review.

## Core Rules

### Screen Every Prescription Against Species-Specific Toxicities Before Dispensing

Species-specific toxicity is the single largest category of preventable veterinary dispensing harm, and it is invisible to human-trained intuition. Acetaminophen is lethal to cats because cats are deficient in glucuronidation, the metabolic pathway that clears the drug, causing methemoglobinemia and irreversible red blood cell damage at doses that are therapeutic in humans and tolerated by dogs. Xylitol, used as a sweetener in human products and occasionally in compounded formulations, causes dose-dependent hypoglycemia and hepatic necrosis in dogs. Permethrin and other synthetic pyrethroids, used in canine flea spot-on products, cause severe tremors, seizures, and death in cats; a cat groomed by or housed with a treated dog is also at risk. NSAID sensitivities differ across species: cats are especially sensitive to NSAID half-life accumulation, acetaminophen is not a veterinary NSAID at all, and certain NSAIDs safe in dogs are toxic in cats or have narrow margins. The pharmacist must verify each drug against a veterinary reference for the target species before dispensing, must treat any cross-species prescription as a red flag, and must query the veterinarian when a species-inappropriate drug appears.

### Evaluate Drug Interactions Using Veterinary References, Not Human Databases

Interaction screening software calibrated to human patients will miss or misclassify interactions that matter in animals and will flag interactions that are irrelevant. The pharmacist must use veterinary-specific references (Plumb's, the Veterinary Information Network, peer-reviewed veterinary pharmacology sources) to evaluate interactions, because the pharmacokinetics and the clinical relevance differ by species. Concurrent NSAID and corticosteroid administration is a recognized cause of gastrointestinal ulceration in dogs and must be flagged; serotonin syndrome risk with tramadol and monoamine oxidase inhibitors or selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors applies across species but with different thresholds; the cytochrome P450 induction patterns differ between dogs and humans, so an interaction that is significant in humans may be attenuated in dogs. The pharmacist must consider the species, the patient's organ function, and the concurrent therapies, and must communicate clinically significant interactions to the veterinarian with a recommendation rather than a silent override or a silent fill.

### Identify Breed-Specific Genetic Sensitivities, Especially the MDR1 Mutation

Certain breeds carry genetic polymorphisms that change drug safety, and the most clinically important in dogs is the MDR1 (ABCB1) gene mutation, common in collies, Australian shepherds, Shetland sheepdogs, Old English sheepdogs, and other herding breeds. Dogs homozygous for the mutation lack functional P-glycoprotein at the blood-brain barrier, allowing certain drugs to accumulate in the central nervous system and causing neurotoxicity. Affected drugs include ivermectin (at doses used for heartworm prevention the risk is low, but at higher demodex doses it is significant), selamectin, moxidectin, loperamide, vincristine, vinblastine, doxorubicin, and digoxin. The pharmacist must recognize when a prescription involves an MDR1-affected drug in a susceptible breed, must verify with the veterinarian whether the dog has been genotyped or whether the dose is within the safe range, and must flag the concern rather than dispense silently. Other breed or species sensitivities exist (for example, dobermans and sulfonamides) and must be checked against the reference.

### Determine Food-Animal Status and Establish Withdrawal Times Before Dispensing

For any food-producing animal — cattle, pigs, poultry, lactating dairy animals, sheep, goats intended for meat or milk, farmed fish, and any animal whose milk, meat, or eggs will enter the human food supply — the pharmacist must determine withdrawal times before dispensing, because drug residues in human food are a public health and regulatory concern. The withdrawal time is the period after the last dose during which the milk, meat, or eggs must be discarded because residues exceed the established tolerance. Withdrawal times are product-, species-, route-, and dose-specific and cannot be assumed from a different product or species. The pharmacist must consult the product label for approved withdrawal times, and for extra-label use must consult the Food Animal Residue Avoidance Databank (FARAD), which provides withdrawal guidance for extra-label and compound situations. The pharmacist must communicate the withdrawal time to the veterinarian and the owner in writing, must document the basis, and must refuse to dispense without establishing a withdrawal time when one is required.

### Verify the Dose and Interval Against the Maximum and the Toxic Threshold

Veterinary doses have narrower published ranges than human doses in many cases, and the gap between therapeutic and toxic can be small. The pharmacist must verify that the prescribed dose falls within the published range for the species and indication, must check the total daily dose against the maximum, and must verify the interval against the clearance of the drug in that species. A dose above the published range may be intentional (the veterinarian may be treating a resistant condition) but must be confirmed with the prescriber and documented. A dose that is correct in one species may be excessive in another (for example, a dog dose applied to a cat for a drug with feline-specific accumulation). The pharmacist must treat any out-of-range dose as a query, not an override, and must record the veterinarian's confirmation.

### Counsel the Owner on Toxic Exposures, Adverse Signs, and Household Hazards

The owner is the front-line observer of adverse effects and the most common source of accidental toxic exposure. The pharmacist must counsel the owner on the common adverse effects of the dispensed medication, the signs that warrant immediate veterinary contact, and the household hazards that cause species-specific poisoning: xylitol in sugar-free gum and peanut butter given to dogs, acetaminophen and ibuprofen in human bottles left within reach, permethrin dog products applied to cats, lily toxicity in cats, rodenticides, chocolate, grapes and raisins, and human antidepressants. The owner must be given the ASPCA Animal Poison Control number (888-426-4435) and instructed to call for any suspected exposure. Counseling must be documented and should verify, by teach-back, that the owner can state the danger signs and the emergency number.

### Document the Basis and the Reasoning

Every conclusion should be traceable to its evidence, assumptions, and alternatives considered. Record not only the outcome but the reasoning path: what was checked, what was ruled out, what uncertainty remains, and what would change the conclusion. Documentation that captures the basis allows another practitioner to review, reproduce, or challenge the work, and it prevents confident conclusions from becoming unrepeatable assertions. A decision made without a recorded basis cannot be audited, improved, or safely handed off.

## Common Traps

### The Human Database Interaction Screen

The pharmacist runs the veterinary prescription through a human-oriented interaction checker, sees no alert, and dispenses. The mechanism is that the screening tool is the one routinely used in the pharmacy. The false signal is that the software returned a clean result, so the prescription is safe. The harm is that human databases do not encode species-specific toxicities (acetaminophen in cats is not an interaction, it is a species contraindication), do not include veterinary-specific interactions, and may flag irrelevant human interactions, leading to both missed harm and false alarms. Veterinary interactions must be screened with veterinary references.

### Acetaminophen or NSAID to a Cat

A prescription or an owner's request involves acetaminophen or a human NSAID for a cat, and the pharmacist proceeds because the drug is routine in humans. The mechanism is that the drug is familiar. The false signal is that the dose was calculated by weight, so it must be acceptable. The harm is that cats cannot glucuronidate acetaminophen and are exquisitely sensitive to NSAID accumulation, and a single therapeutic-appearing dose can cause fatal methemoglobinemia or organ failure. The pharmacist must refuse and contact the veterinarian.

### Permethrin Dog Product on or Near a Cat

A canine permethrin spot-on is dispensed for a household that also has a cat, without warning. The mechanism is that the product is labeled for dogs and the prescription is for the dog. The false signal is that the product is an over-the-counter flea treatment, so it must be low risk. The harm is that permethrin is highly toxic to cats through direct application and through grooming contact with a treated dog, causing seizures and death. The pharmacist must warn the owner and ensure separation of treated and untreated animals.

### The MDR1 Drug in a Herding Breed

An ivermectin, loperamide, or chemotherapy drug is dispensed to a collie or Australian shepherd without checking MDR1 status. The mechanism is that the drug is commonly used in dogs. The false signal is that the breed is a dog, so canine dosing applies. The harm is that MDR1-mutant homozygous dogs develop neurotoxicity from P-glycoprotein substrate drugs at doses that are safe in normal dogs, and the result can be fatal. The pharmacist must flag MDR1-affected drugs in susceptible breeds and confirm genotype or safe dosing with the veterinarian.

### Assuming a Withdrawal Time From a Different Product

A food animal is dispensed a drug extra-label, and the pharmacist assigns a withdrawal time from a different approved product or species. The mechanism is that some withdrawal number is better than none. The false signal is that a withdrawal time was provided, so the residue risk is managed. The harm is that withdrawal times are product- and species-specific, and an assumed time can be far too short, allowing residues above tolerance into the food supply and exposing the public and the producer to regulatory action. The pharmacist must consult FARAD for extra-label withdrawal guidance.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- Did I screen the drug against species-specific toxicities for the exact patient species (acetaminophen in cats, xylitol in dogs, permethrin in cats, NSAID differences) before dispensing?
- Did I evaluate drug interactions using veterinary-specific references rather than a human interaction database, and did I communicate clinically significant interactions to the veterinarian?
- Did I consider breed-specific genetic sensitivities, especially MDR1-affected drugs in herding breeds, and confirm genotype or safe dosing with the prescriber?
- Did I determine whether the patient is a food-producing animal and, if so, establish a product- and species-specific withdrawal time, consulting FARAD for any extra-label use?
- Did I verify the prescribed dose falls within the published range for the species and indication, and confirm any out-of-range dose with the veterinarian before dispensing?
- Did I counsel the owner on adverse signs, household toxic hazards, and the ASPCA Animal Poison Control number, verified by teach-back?
- Does the output stay within the agent's scope, deferring diagnosis and therapeutic decisions to the veterinarian of record and escalating residue or regulatory questions to FARAD, the board of pharmacy, or FDA-CVM where the question exceeds the agent's competence?
- Is the reasoning documented clearly enough that another practitioner could review the basis and reproduce the conclusion?
