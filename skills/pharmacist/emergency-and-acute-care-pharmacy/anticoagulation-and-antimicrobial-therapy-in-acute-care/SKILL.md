# Anticoagulation and Antimicrobial Therapy in Acute Care

Anticoagulation and antimicrobial therapy are the two drug classes that most frequently cause preventable harm in acute care — bleeding and thrombosis from mismanaged anticoagulation, resistance and toxicity and C. difficile from mismanaged antibiotics — and the pharmacist is the primary operational steward of both. The danger is that these therapies are managed dose-by-dose without the stewardship framework that prevents the predictable harms: an anticoagulant started without renal assessment, a heparin infusion titrated without the anti-Xa or aPTT monitoring, a broad-spectrum antibiotic continued without the de-escalation plan, a vancomycin dose not adjusted to the trough or AUC. Each decision, taken in isolation, may seem reasonable, but the cumulative pattern produces the harm that stewardship is designed to prevent. The pharmacist's discipline is to manage each patient's anticoagulation and antimicrobial therapy within the stewardship framework — the protocol, the monitoring, the de-escalation plan, the transition plan — and to recognize that the operational decisions are the stewardship decisions.

## Core Rules

### Assess Renal and Hepatic Function Before Every Anticoagulant and Antimicrobial Dose Decision

Renal and hepatic function determine the clearance and the dose of most anticoagulants and many antimicrobials, and the acute care patient's function is dynamic (changing with illness, hemodynamics, contrast, and recovery), so the function must be assessed before each dose decision, not assumed from a baseline. For anticoagulants, the renal function determines the dose and the choice (the direct oral anticoagulants [DOACs] require dose adjustment or avoidance in renal impairment; low-molecular-weight heparin requires adjustment in severe impairment; unfractionated heparin is preferred in renal failure for its short half-life and reversibility). For antimicrobials, the renal function determines the dose and the interval of renally cleared drugs (vancomycin, many beta-lactams, aminoglycosides, fluoroquinolones), and the hepatic function determines the dose of hepatically cleared drugs. The pharmacist must obtain the current renal function (the creatinine clearance or eGFR, calculated from the current creatinine, not a historical value) and the hepatic function before each significant dose decision, must adjust the dose to the current function, and must reassess when the function changes (the patient who develops acute kidney injury on day 3 of therapy needs the dose reassessed on day 3, not continued at the day-1 dose). A dose continued at the baseline function in a patient whose function has changed accumulates or underdoses, and the harm (bleeding from accumulated anticoagulant, toxicity from accumulated antimicrobial, treatment failure from underdosing) is the predictable consequence.

### Monitor Anticoagulation to the Target and Adjust by the Protocol

Anticoagulation monitoring — the aPTT or anti-Xa for unfractionated heparin, the anti-Xa for low-molecular-weight heparin in specific patients, the PT/INR for warfarin, the specific monitoring for DOACs in certain situations — is the control that keeps the therapy in the therapeutic window, and the pharmacist must apply the monitoring protocol and adjust the dose to the target. For heparin infusions, the pharmacist must interpret the aPTT or anti-Xa against the protocol's nomogram, must adjust the rate per the nomogram (not by clinical impression), must consider the confounders (a prolonged aPTT from a factor deficiency rather than heparin effect, an anti-Xa that does not reflect the anticoagulant effect if drawn at the wrong time), and must reassess at the defined interval. For warfarin, the pharmacist must interpret the INR against the target, must adjust the dose per the algorithm, must consider the interacting factors (the antibiotic that raised the INR, the dietary change, the missed dose), and must manage the supratherapeutic INR with vitamin K or reversal per the protocol. For DOACs, the pharmacist must verify the indication-specific dose, the renal-adjusted dose, and the absence of major interactions, and must recognize the situations where DOAC levels or anti-Xa monitoring is needed (urgent surgery, bleeding, thrombolysis). The pharmacist must not manage anticoagulation by impression; the protocol and the monitoring are the controls that prevent both bleeding (over-anticoagulation) and thrombosis (under-anticoagulation).

### Apply Antimicrobial Pharmacokinetic and Pharmacodynamic Monitoring

Antimicrobial pharmacokinetic (PK) and pharmacodynamic (PD) monitoring — the vancomycin trough or AUC, the aminoglycoside peak and trough, the beta-lactam trough for severe infections — is the control that keeps the antimicrobial in the effective and safe concentration range, and the pharmacist must apply it for the drugs that require it. For vancomycin, the pharmacist must monitor to the AUC (the current standard for serious MRSA infections) or the trough (where AUC is not feasible), must adjust the dose to the target, must consider the renal function and the dialysis status, and must recognize the nephrotoxicity risk (which increases with concurrent nephrotoxins, high troughs, and prolonged courses). For aminoglycosides, the pharmacist must monitor the peak and the trough (or apply the extended-interval dosing approach with the appropriate monitoring), must adjust to the target, and must manage the nephrotoxicity and ototoxicity risks. For beta-lactams in severe infections, the pharmacist may apply prolonged or continuous infusion to optimize the PD (the time above the minimum inhibitory concentration), and must monitor the renal function for the dose adjustment. The pharmacist must not dose these drugs without the monitoring, because the effective and safe range is narrow and the monitoring is the control that keeps the therapy within it.

### Drive Antimicrobial De-Escalation and Duration Optimization

Antimicrobial stewardship in acute care depends on the operational decisions that de-escalate broad therapy and limit the duration to the evidence-based minimum, and the pharmacist is the operational driver of both. De-escalation: when culture and sensitivity results return, the pharmacist must prompt the transition from the empiric broad-spectrum therapy to the narrowest effective agent (the vancomycin stopped if MRSA is not present, the carbapenem de-escalated to a narrower beta-lactam based on sensitivities, the double anaerobic coverage narrowed to single). Duration: the pharmacist must apply the evidence-based duration for the infection (the shorter durations now recommended for pneumonia, intra-abdominal infection, pyelonephritis, and others), must prompt the stop or the transition to oral at the appropriate day, and must manage the pressure to continue longer than necessary (the "just in case" continuation that drives resistance, C. difficile, and toxicity). The pharmacist must track the patient's therapy against the stewardship benchmarks (the day-3 culture review, the day-5 duration review, the IV-to-oral conversion) and must communicate the recommendations to the team, because the de-escalation and the duration optimization are the decisions that determine whether the antimicrobial use is appropriate or excessive.

### Manage the Transition of Anticoagulation and Antimicrobial Therapy Across Care Levels

The transition of anticoagulation and antimicrobial therapy from the acute care setting to the next level (home, rehabilitation, long-term care) is the point where stewardship and monitoring are most often lost, and the pharmacist must manage the transition explicitly. For anticoagulation, the transition includes: the bridging plan (if switching from heparin to warfarin, or from a DOAC to warfarin), the discharge prescription with the correct drug, dose, and duration, the monitoring plan (the INR follow-up for warfarin, the renal function follow-up for DOACs), the patient education (the adherence, the bleeding signs, the drug and dietary interactions, the monitoring appointments), and the communication to the outpatient provider. For antimicrobials, the transition includes: the discharge prescription with the correct drug, dose, duration, and the indication, the plan for any remaining IV therapy (outpatient parenteral antimicrobial therapy [OPAT] if needed), the monitoring plan (the renal function, the drug levels if continued), and the communication to the outpatient provider. The pharmacist must not assume the transition will happen by itself; the explicit plan, the education, and the communication are the controls that prevent the transition failures (the patient who stops the anticoagulant because they did not understand, the patient who continues the antibiotic longer than prescribed, the outpatient provider who does not know the monitoring plan).

## Common Traps

### Continuing the Admission Dose of an Anticoagulant or Antimicrobial Without Reassessing Renal Function

The admission dose is calculated from the admission renal function, and the acute care patient's renal function changes (improves with hydration, declines with illness or contrast or nephrotoxins), so the admission dose may become inappropriate within days. The trap is that the admission dose is on the order, so it continues by default, and the reassessment (which requires obtaining the current creatinine and recalculating) is not triggered, so the dose continues unchanged while the function changes. The harm is accumulation (bleeding from the anticoagulant, toxicity from the antimicrobial) if the function declines, or underdosing (treatment failure, resistance selection) if the function improves and the dose is not increased. The fix is to reassess renal function at a defined interval (daily for heparin and vancomycin, every few days for stable therapy) and to adjust the dose to the current function, treating the dose as dynamic rather than fixed.

### Titrating a Heparin Infusion by Impression Rather Than the Nomogram

The heparin nomogram specifies the rate adjustment for each aPTT or anti-Xa value, and the nomogram is the control that produces consistent, protocol-driven titration. The trap is that the pharmacist (or the team) adjusts the rate by clinical impression (the patient is "doing well," so a small adjustment; the patient is "bleeding," so a large reduction) rather than by the nomogram, and the impression-driven titration produces wider variability in anticoagulation and more time outside the therapeutic range. The harm is both bleeding (over-anticoagulation from impression-driven increases) and thrombosis (under-anticoagulation from impression-driven conservatism), and the variability defeats the purpose of the nomogram. The fix is to titrate by the nomogram, to document the nomogram-based adjustment, and to deviate from the nomogram only with documented clinical justification (a bleeding event, a planned procedure) and not by default impression.

### Continuing Broad-Spectrum Empiric Therapy After Culture Results Return

Empiric broad-spectrum therapy is appropriate before the pathogen is known, but once the culture and sensitivity return, the therapy should be de-escalated to the narrowest effective agent, and the continuation of the broad therapy is the most common stewardship failure. The trap is that the broad therapy is "working" (the patient is improving), so the team continues it rather than de-escalating, and the continuation drives resistance, C. difficile risk, and toxicity, while providing no additional benefit over the narrower agent. The harm is the cumulative effect of broad-spectrum overuse across patients and the institution, and the individual patient's increased risk of complications. The fix is to review the cultures at day 2-3 (when results return), to prompt the de-escalation to the narrowest effective agent, and to document the recommendation and the team's response.

### Dosing Vancomycin to a Trough Without Adjusting for the AUC or the Renal Function Change

Vancomycin dosing has shifted from trough-based to AUC-based monitoring for serious MRSA infections, and the pharmacist who continues to dose to a trough target may underdose or overdose relative to the AUC target. Additionally, the vancomycin dose must be adjusted for renal function, and the function changes in acute care, so a dose that was appropriate on day 1 may be excessive by day 5. The trap is that the dosing is driven by the trough habit and the admission renal function, without the AUC calculation or the renal reassessment, and the result is nephrotoxicity (from excessive exposure) or treatment failure (from inadequate exposure). The harm is acute kidney injury (which extends the hospitalization and the morbidity) or persistent infection. The fix is to apply the current AUC-based monitoring for serious infections, to adjust the dose to the renal function at each assessment, and to recognize the nephrotoxicity risk factors (concurrent piperacillin-tazobactam, high troughs, prolonged course).

### Discharging the Patient Without an Explicit Anticoagulation or Antimicrobial Transition Plan

The discharge is the point where the acute care stewardship ends and the outpatient management begins, and the absence of an explicit transition plan leaves the patient to manage the therapy without the monitoring, the education, and the follow-up that the therapy requires. The trap is that the acute care team considers the therapy "done" when the patient is discharged, so the transition (the bridging, the monitoring, the education, the outpatient communication) is not planned, and the patient stops the anticoagulant, continues the antibiotic indefinitely, or misses the monitoring. The harm is the thrombosis or bleeding from the anticoagulation failure, the resistance or the C. difficile from the antimicrobial continuation, or the lost follow-up that the outpatient provider did not know to provide. The fix is to plan the transition explicitly at discharge — the drug, the dose, the duration, the monitoring, the education, the outpatient communication — and to verify the patient's understanding and the follow-up before the discharge.

## Self-Check

- Before each significant anticoagulant or antimicrobial dose decision, did I assess the current renal function (from the current creatinine, not a historical value) and the hepatic function, and did I adjust the dose to the current function?
- For heparin infusions, did I titrate by the protocol nomogram (not by impression), interpret the aPTT or anti-Xa against the target, consider the confounders, and reassess at the defined interval?
- For vancomycin, did I apply the AUC-based monitoring for serious infections (or the trough where appropriate), adjust for the renal function, and recognize the nephrotoxicity risk factors?
- For antimicrobials, did I review the cultures at day 2-3, prompt the de-escalation to the narrowest effective agent, and apply the evidence-based duration with the IV-to-oral conversion at the appropriate point?
- Did I apply PK/PD monitoring for the drugs that require it (vancomycin, aminoglycosides, prolonged-infusion beta-lactams) and adjust to the effective and safe concentration range?
- At discharge, did I create an explicit transition plan for the anticoagulation or antimicrobial therapy — the drug, dose, duration, bridging if needed, monitoring, patient education, and communication to the outpatient provider?
- Did I avoid continuing the admission dose without reassessment, titrating by impression, continuing broad empiric therapy after cultures, and discharging without a transition plan?
