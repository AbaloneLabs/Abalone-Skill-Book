# Drug Cost Analysis and Formulary Decisions

Formulary and drug-budget decisions look like financial exercises but are actually clinical decisions with financial framing: a formulary addition or deletion determines which therapies patients can access, a therapeutic interchange redirects patients from one agent to another that may differ in efficacy or tolerability, and a cost-effectiveness analysis that is built on biased inputs produces a confident-looking recommendation that is clinically wrong. The judgment problem is that the inputs that drive these analyses — acquisition cost, efficacy estimates, adverse-event rates, quality-of-life weights, market share assumptions — are uncertain and often supplied by parties with an interest in the outcome, and the analyst who accepts them uncritically produces a recommendation that harms patients or wastes scarce budget while appearing rigorous. The pharmacist's role is to build analyses on transparent, verifiable assumptions, to distinguish acquisition cost from total cost of care, to present the P&T committee with both the clinical evidence and the financial impact, and to defer the final formulary decision to the committee while ensuring the analysis is sound.

## Core Rules

### Separate Acquisition Cost from Total Cost of Care

A drug's cost is not its list price or even its invoice price; it is the total cost of using the drug in the patient population, which includes acquisition cost, administration cost, monitoring cost, waste, treatment of adverse events, and the downstream cost of success or failure (length of stay, readmission, switching to a more expensive alternative). A cheaper acquisition cost can be more expensive in total if the drug requires more monitoring, has more adverse events, or fails more often and requires escalation. Use the correct acquisition cost basis for the analysis: wholesale acquisition cost (WAC) is the manufacturer's published list price and overstates what most payers pay; average wholesale price (AWP) is a markup benchmark and overstates even more; average sales price (ASP) reflects actual sales net of rebates for Medicare Part B drugs and is closer to reality; 340B cost (for covered entities) and group purchasing organization (GPO) contract price reflect the institution's actual paid price and are the right basis for an institutional budget impact model. State which basis is used and why, and never compare a WAC for one drug against a 340B cost for another.

### Build Cost-Effectiveness Analyses on Transparent, Sourced Inputs

Cost-effectiveness analysis (CEA) compares the incremental cost of one strategy against another per unit of health gained, most commonly expressed as the incremental cost-effectiveness ratio (ICER): the difference in cost divided by the difference in effect (often quality-adjusted life-years, QALYs). The ICER is only as reliable as its inputs, and each input — the efficacy difference, the adverse-event rate difference, the utility weight assigned to each health state, the time horizon, the discount rate — should be sourced to a published trial, a meta-analysis, or a clearly stated assumption, and should be tested in a sensitivity analysis. A QALY is a composite of survival and quality of life, and the utility weights that feed it are subjective and population-dependent; small changes in utility can swing the ICER dramatically. Run one-way sensitivity analysis (varying each input across its plausible range) and scenario analysis (best-case and worst-case), and report the range of ICERs, not just the point estimate. An ICER presented as a single number conceals the uncertainty that dominates the decision.

### Build Budget Impact Models on Realistic Market Share and Population Assumptions

A budget impact model (BIM) answers a different question than CEA: not "is this drug cost-effective per unit of health?" but "what will adding this drug to the formulary cost or save the budget over a defined period?" The BIM is driven by the eligible population size, the market share the new drug is expected to capture, the displacement of existing therapies, and the per-patient cost difference. The most common error is an unrealistic market-share assumption: a manufacturer's model may assume rapid uptake that the institution cannot achieve (due to prescribing habits, restrictions, or prior authorization), overstating the budget impact. Build the BIM on the institution's actual patient volumes and current prescribing patterns, model a range of uptake scenarios, and include the offsetting savings from displaced therapies and avoided downstream costs. The BIM should be reproducible from institutional data, not from manufacturer-supplied assumptions.

### Prepare the Formulary Monograph as a Balanced Clinical and Financial Document

The formulary monograph is the document the P&T committee uses to decide, and it must present both the clinical evidence and the financial impact in a balanced, transparent way. Structure the monograph to include: the pharmacology and mechanism, the FDA-approved indications and the off-label uses under consideration, the pivotal trial evidence (with effect sizes, confidence intervals, and populations), the adverse-event profile and how it compares to alternatives, the dosing and administration (including any special handling or monitoring), the acquisition cost and the budget impact, the place in therapy relative to existing formulary alternatives, and a recommendation with its rationale. Present the evidence at face value: do not overstate efficacy from a surrogate endpoint when a hard endpoint is available, do not generalize trial results to a population the trial excluded, and do not bury a serious adverse event in a footnote. The recommendation should follow from the evidence, not precede it.

### Design Therapeutic Interchange on Evidence and Monitoring, Not Cost Alone

Therapeutic interchange — substituting one drug for another within the same class, or a proton pump inhibitor for another, or a formulary agent for a non-formulary one — is a legitimate tool when the interchange is clinically equivalent and the protocol is monitored for outcomes. Design the protocol on evidence of equivalence (same mechanism, comparable efficacy in the relevant population, comparable safety), not on cost alone, because a cheaper drug that is less effective or less tolerated will increase total cost through failure, switching, and adverse events. Define the patient populations excluded from the interchange (documented intolerance, specific indication, prior failure of the formulary agent), build a monitoring plan to track outcomes after implementation, and set a review date to evaluate whether the interchange achieved its clinical and financial goals. A therapeutic interchange that is implemented and never audited can silently harm patients whose outcomes were never measured.

### Follow the P&T Committee Process and Disclose Conflicts

The P&T committee is the governance body that owns formulary decisions, and the pharmacist's role is to support it with sound analysis, not to make the decision unilaterally. Follow the institution's process for monograph submission, evidence presentation, conflict of interest disclosure (including any manufacturer funding, speaker honoraria, or consulting relationships), and recusal where a conflict exists. Present the evidence and the recommendation, answer questions, and accept the committee's decision even when it differs from the recommendation — the committee weighs factors (access, equity, contract obligations, clinical preference) that the analyst may not see. Document the decision and its rationale, and implement it through the formulary, order sets, and clinical decision support so that the decision actually changes practice.

## Common Traps

### Comparing List Prices Across Products

The analyst compares the WAC of drug A against the WAC of drug B and concludes A is cheaper, but the institution's actual acquisition cost (after rebates, GPO contracts, or 340B pricing) is the opposite. The mechanism is that WAC is publicly available and easy to compare, while actual paid price requires internal data. The false signal is that the comparison looks rigorous because both numbers are cited. The harm is a recommendation that increases cost or disadvantages the cheaper-in-reality option, and a loss of credibility when the actual invoices arrive. The corrective is to base the comparison on the institution's actual acquisition cost, to disclose the basis, and to avoid cross-basis comparisons.

### Treating a Surrogate Endpoint as a Hard Outcome in the ICER

A CEA uses a reduction in a biomarker (blood pressure, cholesterol, tumor shrinkage) as the effectiveness input, treating it as equivalent to a reduction in strokes, mortality, or quality of life, when the link between the surrogate and the hard outcome is unproven for the drug in question. The mechanism is that surrogate endpoints are available from short trials and produce clean numbers. The false signal is that the ICER looks precise and the drug looks cost-effective. The harm is a recommendation that the drug is good value when its actual effect on patient-important outcomes is unknown or, in some cases, harmful despite the surrogate improvement. The corrective is to use hard endpoints where available, to model the surrogate-to-hard-outcome link explicitly with its uncertainty, and to flag the limitation.

### Manufacturer-Supplied Model Treated as Neutral

A manufacturer provides a polished cost-effectiveness or budget impact model, and the analyst runs it with default inputs and presents the output as the institution's analysis. The mechanism is that the model is convenient and the inputs look reasonable. The false signal is that the model produces a number, so the analysis is done. The harm is that the model's structure and defaults embed assumptions that favor the manufacturer (optimistic efficacy, high market share, favorable utility weights, omitted comparators), and the resulting recommendation is biased while appearing objective. The corrective is to treat any third-party model as a starting point, to interrogate every input against independent sources, to rerun with institutional data, and to disclose the model's origin.

### Ignoring Monitoring and Administration Costs

The acquisition cost is compared and the cheaper drug is selected, but the cheaper drug requires therapeutic drug monitoring, a longer infusion, special handling, or more clinic visits, and the total cost is higher. The mechanism is that acquisition cost is the visible number and the ancillary costs are dispersed across departments. The false signal is that the drug budget line decreased. The harm is that total cost of care increased, shifted to the laboratory, the infusion center, or the nursing unit, and the formulary decision did not achieve its goal. The corrective is to include monitoring, administration, waste, and adverse-event costs in the model.

### Therapeutic Interchange Implemented Without Outcome Monitoring

A therapeutic interchange is implemented to capture savings, and the savings are reported, but no one measures whether clinical outcomes (efficacy, adverse events, readmissions, switching rates) changed. The mechanism is that the financial metric is easy to report and the clinical outcome is hard to measure. The false signal is that the budget impact was as projected. The harm is that a clinically inferior interchange may be silently harming patients, and the program continues because the adverse signal was never collected. The corrective is to build outcome monitoring into every interchange protocol and to review it on a defined schedule.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The standard adult formulary comparison is done well, but the pediatric indication, the pregnancy category, the renal-adjustment requirement, or the rare disease population is handled with the same defaults. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary of indication, population, or special handling.

## Self-Check

- Did I base the cost comparison on the institution's actual acquisition cost (WAC, AWP, ASP, 340B, or GPO), state the basis, and avoid cross-basis comparisons?
- Did I include total cost of care — acquisition, administration, monitoring, waste, adverse events, and downstream cost of failure — rather than acquisition cost alone?
- Did I source every CEA input (efficacy, adverse events, utility weights, time horizon) to a citable reference, run sensitivity analysis, and report the ICER range rather than a single point estimate?
- Did I build the budget impact model on the institution's actual patient volumes and a realistic range of market-share and uptake assumptions, rather than manufacturer defaults?
- Did I structure the formulary monograph to present clinical evidence and financial impact in a balanced way, with the recommendation following from the evidence?
- Did I design any therapeutic interchange on evidence of clinical equivalence with defined exclusions and a built-in outcome monitoring plan, not on cost alone?
- Did I follow the P&T committee process, disclose any conflicts of interest, and accept the committee's decision as the governance outcome?
- Does the output stay within my scope, deferring the final formulary and therapeutic decisions to the P&T committee and the medical staff, and escalating to pharmacy administration and compliance where financial conflict or contract obligation is unclear?
