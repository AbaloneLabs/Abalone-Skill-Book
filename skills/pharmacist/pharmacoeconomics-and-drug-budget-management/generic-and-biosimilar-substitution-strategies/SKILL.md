# Generic and Biosimilar Substitution Strategies

Generic and biosimilar substitution is one of the highest-value, lowest-risk interventions in pharmacy, but it is governed by rules that differ sharply between small-molecule generics and biologic biosimilars, and the distinction determines what the pharmacist may do without prescriber contact. The judgment problem is that substitution looks like a simple cost decision but is actually a regulatory and clinical decision: a generic that is AB-rated may be automatically substituted under state law, but a biosimilar that is not designated interchangeable may not, and a narrow therapeutic index drug may require tighter control even when AB-rated. A pharmacist who substitutes incorrectly — substituting a non-interchangeable biosimilar without prescriber approval, or substituting a generic that the patient has previously reacted to — causes regulatory violation, clinical harm, or both. The pharmacist's role is to apply the correct substitution rule for the product class, to consult the FDA Orange Book and Purple Book, to respect state law and patient-specific factors, and to capture the cost savings through formulary design and measurement.

## Core Rules

### Apply the Generic Substitution Rule: AB-Rating and the Orange Book

For small-molecule drugs, the FDA's Approved Drug Products with Therapeutic Equivalence Evaluations (the Orange Book) is the authoritative reference. A generic product that is rated "A" (therapeutically equivalent) with the same active ingredient, dosage form, route, and strength, and a specific "AB" code (indicating bioequivalence to the reference listed drug) is considered therapeutically equivalent and may be substituted for the brand product under state law without additional prescriber approval, subject to state-specific notification requirements. Verify the specific product's equivalence code, because a product rated "B" is not considered therapeutically equivalent and must not be automatically substituted. Recognize that bioequivalence means the generic's rate and extent of absorption fall within accepted confidence intervals of the reference, not that the products are identical in inactive ingredients — which matters for patients with specific excipient sensitivities. Document the substitution per state law, and notify the prescriber where required.

### Distinguish Biosimilar from Interchangeable Biosimilar Using the Purple Book

Biologics are large, complex molecules manufactured in living systems, and a biosimilar is a product that the FDA has determined is highly similar to the reference biologic with no clinically meaningful differences in safety, purity, and potency. The critical regulatory distinction is between a biosimilar and an interchangeable biosimilar. A biosimilar may be dispensed when prescribed by its own name, but it may not automatically be substituted for the reference biologic without prescriber approval unless it has been specifically licensed by the FDA as interchangeable. An interchangeable biosimilar may be substituted for the reference product at the pharmacy without consulting the prescriber, subject to state law, because the FDA has determined that switching between the reference and the interchangeable is safe without supervision. The FDA's Purple Book (Lists of Licensed Biological Products with Reference Product Exclusivity and Biosimilarity or Interchangeability Evaluations) lists each biologic's status, and the pharmacist must consult it before any substitution. Substituting a non-interchangeable biosimilar for a reference biologic without prescriber approval is a regulatory violation regardless of the products' clinical similarity.

### Respect State Law on Automatic Substitution and Notification

Substitution law is state law, and it varies in the specifics: which products may be automatically substituted, whether the pharmacist must notify the prescriber and within what timeframe, whether the patient must be notified, and whether the patient may refuse the substitution. Most states permit automatic substitution of AB-rated generics and of interchangeable biosimilars, but the notification and documentation requirements differ. Some states have specific rules for biologics that may restrict even interchangeable biosimilar substitution. The pharmacist must know and follow the state's current requirements, and must not assume that because a product is federally rated for substitution the state permits it without conditions. When in doubt, consult the board of pharmacy's current guidance rather than relying on habit.

### Manage Narrow Therapeutic Index Drugs with Extra Care

Narrow therapeutic index (NTI) drugs — warfarin, levothyroxine, phenytoin, carbamazepine, digoxin, tacrolimus, and others — have a small difference between the effective dose and the toxic dose, and even small bioavailability differences between products can cause loss of efficacy or toxicity. The FDA applies tighter bioequivalence standards to NTI drugs, and AB-rated NTI generics are considered therapeutically equivalent, but many clinicians and pharmacists still prefer to keep the patient on the same specific product (brand or a single generic manufacturer) to avoid variability. The practical rule is: when initiating or switching an NTI drug, or when the patient's condition is tightly controlled on a specific product, prefer to maintain the same manufacturer's product and avoid uncontrolled switching at each refill, and monitor the patient's levels or clinical status closely after any unavoidable switch. Communicate with the prescriber about any product change for an NTI drug, and document the monitoring plan.

### Capture Cost Savings Through Formulary Design and Measurement

The financial value of generic and biosimilar substitution is realized only if it is actually implemented and measured. Design the formulary to default to the lowest-cost therapeutically equivalent option: require generics where available, set the biosimilar (or interchangeable biosimilar) as the preferred product where the reference biologic is not contractually required, and build the substitution into the default order sets and dispensing cabinet overrides. Measure the generic fill rate (the percentage of prescriptions for which a generic is dispensed when available) and the biosimilar adoption rate, and report these to pharmacy administration and the P&T committee. Track the cost avoidance achieved by substitution against the brand alternative, and use the data to identify gaps (a low biosimilar adoption rate may indicate prescriber resistance, prior authorization design, or a contract structure that does not reward the switch). Savings that are not measured are not captured, and a formulary that permits but does not drive substitution leaves money on the table.

### Communicate Substitutions to Patients and Address Concerns

Patients may notice a change in pill color, shape, or packaging after a generic or biosimilar substitution, and an unexplained change can cause confusion, non-adherence, or alarm (especially with "this looks different — is it the right drug?"). When dispensing a substituted product, counsel the patient that the product is therapeutically equivalent (for an AB-rated generic) or has been determined by the FDA to have no clinically meaningful differences (for a biosimilar), explain why the change occurred (formulary, supply, cost), and tell them what to expect and what to report. For biologics, address the specific concern about whether the biosimilar is "the same" as the reference, and document the education. A patient who stops taking a substituted product because it looks different has lost the therapeutic benefit that the substitution was meant to preserve.

## Common Traps

### Substituting a Non-Interchangeable Biosimilar Automatically

A biosimilar is available for a reference biologic, and the pharmacist substitutes it automatically, assuming that because it is a biosimilar it may be substituted like a generic. The mechanism is that "biosimilar" sounds like "generic equivalent." The false signal is that the products are clinically similar, so substitution is safe. The harm is a regulatory violation (only interchangeable biosimilars may be substituted without prescriber approval), potential clinical concern if the prescriber intended the specific reference product, and erosion of trust if the prescriber learns of the substitution after the fact. The corrective is to check the Purple Book for the interchangeability designation before any biosimilar substitution, and to obtain prescriber approval for non-interchangeable biosimilars.

### Assuming All Products with the Same Active Ingredient Are Equivalent

The pharmacist sees two products with the same active ingredient, strength, and dosage form and substitutes them, without verifying the specific AB code or equivalence rating. The mechanism is that the active ingredient and strength feel sufficient for equivalence. The false signal is that the products look interchangeable. The harm is that a product rated "B" (not therapeutically equivalent) is dispensed in place of an "AB"-rated product, exposing the patient to a potentially different bioavailability and the pharmacy to a substitution violation. The corrective is to verify the specific Orange Book equivalence code, not just the active ingredient.

### Uncontrolled Switching of NTI Drug Manufacturers

A patient on levothyroxine or tacrolimus receives a different generic manufacturer at each refill as the pharmacy's stock rotates, and the small bioavailability differences accumulate into a clinically significant change in thyroid function or immunosuppression level. The mechanism is that each individual generic is AB-rated, so each substitution seems permissible. The false signal is that each product is individually equivalent, so switching is harmless. The harm is loss of therapeutic control, the need for dose re-titration, and potential adverse events from over- or under-dosing. The corrective is to maintain the patient on a single manufacturer for NTI drugs where feasible, to communicate with the prescriber about any change, and to monitor levels after an unavoidable switch.

### Reporting Savings Without Measuring Adoption

A generic or biosimilar is added to the formulary, and the projected savings are reported as achieved, but no one measures the actual substitution rate, so the projected savings never materialize because prescribers continue ordering the brand or the substitution is not enforced at dispensing. The mechanism is that the formulary change feels like the outcome. The false signal is that the formulary lists the cheaper product, so it is being used. The harm is that the budget target is missed and the gap is invisible until the financial review. The corrective is to measure the generic fill rate and biosimilar adoption rate continuously and to act on the gaps.

### Patient Non-Adherence After an Unexplained Product Change

A patient receives a generic that looks different from the brand and, without counseling, stops taking it or doubles up because they think it is wrong or additional. The mechanism is that the substitution was made without patient communication. The false signal is that the dispensing was completed, so the patient received the therapy. The harm is non-adherence, therapeutic failure, or accidental double-dosing, none of which are captured in the substitution rate metric. The corrective is to counsel on every substitution, explain the change, and document the education.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The standard oral solid generic substitution is handled correctly, but the injectable biosimilar, the ophthalmic product with a different preservative, the pediatric oral suspension with a different bioavailability, or the pregnancy-related NTI drug is handled with the same defaults. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary of product type, patient population, or clinical condition.

## Self-Check

- Did I verify the specific Orange Book AB code (not just the active ingredient) before substituting a generic, and document and notify per state law?
- Did I consult the Purple Book to determine whether a biosimilar is designated interchangeable before any automatic substitution, and obtain prescriber approval for non-interchangeable biosimilars?
- Did I confirm that state law permits the automatic substitution and that I have met the notification and documentation requirements for this jurisdiction?
- For narrow therapeutic index drugs, did I prefer to maintain the same manufacturer's product, communicate any change to the prescriber, and arrange monitoring after an unavoidable switch?
- Did I design the formulary to default to the lowest-cost therapeutically equivalent option, and did I measure the generic fill rate and biosimilar adoption rate rather than assuming the formulary change achieved the savings?
- Did I counsel the patient on the substitution, explain the change and its rationale, and document the education?
- Did I avoid cross-basis comparisons and confirm that the substituted product is genuinely therapeutically equivalent, not merely the same active ingredient?
- Does the output stay within my scope, deferring clinical decisions about whether a specific patient should remain on a reference product to the prescriber, and escalating to the board of pharmacy where substitution law is ambiguous?
