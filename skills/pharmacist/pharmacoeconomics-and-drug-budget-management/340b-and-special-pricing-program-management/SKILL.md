# 340B and Special Pricing Program Management

The 340B Drug Pricing Program is a federal program that lets eligible covered entities purchase outpatient drugs from manufacturers at a discounted ceiling price, so that the savings can stretch scarce resources to serve more eligible patients and expand services. The judgment problem is that 340B is governed by strict eligibility and compliance rules whose violation — dispensing a 340B-purchased drug to an ineligible patient (diversion), billing Medicaid for a 340B drug without the proper rebate exclusion (duplicate discount), or failing to maintain auditable records — can trigger repayment demands, program termination, and exclusion from future participation, and the violations are easy to commit silently because the discounted product looks identical to the non-discounted one. The pharmacist's role is to verify eligibility at every step, to maintain the physical and software separation that prevents diversion and duplicate discounts, to keep auditable records, and to distinguish 340B from the separate Medicaid Drug Rebate Program.

## Core Rules

### Confirm Covered Entity Status and the Eligible Patient Definition

Only a covered entity — a defined category including federally qualified health centers, Ryan White HIV/AIDS clinics, critical access hospitals, disproportionate share hospitals, hemophilia treatment centers, and others — may purchase at 340B prices, and only for an eligible patient. An eligible patient is one who has an established relationship with the covered entity (a record demonstrating the individual is a patient of the facility), who receives health care services from a provider employed by or contracted to the covered entity, and for whom the covered entity assumes responsibility for the care. A drug purchased at 340B price may be dispensed only to a patient who meets this definition at the time of dispensing; dispensing to anyone else is diversion. The pharmacist must verify covered entity status (the entity must be enrolled and listed on the 340B database) and must apply the patient definition rigorously, because a patient who is "associated with" the entity but does not meet the three-part test is not eligible and a 340B drug dispensed to them is diversion.

### Manage Contract Pharmacy Arrangements Within the Rules

Because many covered entities do not have an in-house pharmacy, they contract with one or more retail pharmacies (contract pharmacies) to dispense 340B-purchased drugs to eligible patients. The contract pharmacy arrangement must be documented in a written agreement, the contract pharmacy must be registered with HRSA, and the arrangement must include mechanisms to prevent diversion and duplicate discounts. The covered entity remains responsible for compliance even when the dispensing occurs at the contract pharmacy, which means the entity must be able to audit the contract pharmacy's 340B transactions and reconcile them to eligible patient encounters. The rules around contract pharmacy have evolved (including HRSA guidance and enforcement actions regarding limitations on contract pharmacy arrangements), so the pharmacist must follow current HRSA guidance and the entity's compliance plan, and must not assume that a prior arrangement remains compliant without review.

### Prevent Diversion and Duplicate Discounts Through Split Billing and Auditing

The two core compliance risks are diversion (a 340B drug dispensed to an ineligible patient) and duplicate discounts (a 340B drug for which Medicaid is billed, causing both the 340B discount and the Medicaid rebate to apply to the same drug). Prevention requires both software and process controls. Split billing software (also called 340B software) screens each prescription at the point of dispensing against the eligibility rules and the Medicaid billing carve-out, and routes the cost to the 340B account only for eligible, non-duplicate-discount transactions. The software must be correctly configured (the eligible provider roster, the Medicaid carve-out file, the eligible patient definition), and its output must be audited periodically because the software's accuracy depends entirely on its configuration. Maintain a process for transactions the software cannot resolve (cash, third-party, complex eligibility) and for the prevention of diversion to inpatients, employees, and non-eligible outpatients. Duplicate discount prevention requires accurate identification of Medicaid patients (the Medicaid billing file or the state's Medicaid exclusion declaration), because billing Medicaid for a 340B drug without the proper exclusion creates a duplicate discount.

### Calculate and Verify the Ceiling Price and Watch for Penny Pricing

The 340B ceiling price is the maximum price a manufacturer can charge a covered entity for a covered outpatient drug, calculated as the average manufacturer price (AMP) minus the unit rebate amount (URA), with a floor of one cent (the "penny pricing" rule) where the calculation would otherwise produce zero or a negative amount. The pharmacist who purchases or reconciles 340B drugs should understand that the ceiling price is set by HRSA and published, and that the actual 340B price the entity pays should not exceed the ceiling price. Penny-priced items (drugs whose ceiling price is $0.0001 per unit) are a known category and must be purchased at the penny price, not at a higher price, and must be tracked separately. Verify invoices against the ceiling price, and dispute any overcharge through the manufacturer and, if unresolved, through HRSA. The ceiling price calculation is not something the dispensing pharmacist performs at the point of care, but the purchasing pharmacist must understand it to verify invoices and to recognize overcharges.

### Maintain Audit Readiness and Records

HRSA audits covered entities on a routine and for-cause basis, and the entity must be able to demonstrate, for any sampled 340B prescription, that the patient was eligible, that the drug was purchased and dispensed at 340B price, that Medicaid was not billed in a way that created a duplicate discount, and that the records reconcile. Maintain auditable records: the covered entity registration, the contract pharmacy agreements, the patient eligibility determination process, the split billing software configuration and audit logs, the Medicaid carve-out file, the purchase and dispensing records, and the periodic self-audits. Conduct internal compliance audits at a defined frequency, identify and remediate findings, and report and repay any identified diversion or duplicate discount. An entity that cannot produce the records for a sampled transaction will be found non-compliant regardless of the underlying intent.

### Distinguish 340B from the Medicaid Drug Rebate Program

The 340B Drug Pricing Program and the Medicaid Drug Rebate Program are separate federal programs with opposite mechanics, and confusing them causes compliance failure. The Medicaid Drug Rebate Program requires drug manufacturers to pay rebates to state Medicaid programs for drugs dispensed to Medicaid patients, as a condition of Medicaid coverage of the manufacturer's drugs. The 340B program requires manufacturers to sell drugs to covered entities at a discount. The duplicate discount risk arises specifically because both programs can apply to the same drug: if a 340B-purchased drug is dispensed to a Medicaid patient and billed to Medicaid, the manufacturer both gave the 340B discount and owes the Medicaid rebate on the same unit. The prevention is the Medicaid billing carve-out: the entity declares (per state-specific rules) which patients are Medicaid and whether 340B drugs dispensed to them will be billed to Medicaid, so that the duplicate is avoided. The pharmacist must understand which program applies to each transaction and must not conflate the rebate mechanics with the discount mechanics.

## Common Traps

### Dispensing 340B Drugs to Ineligible Patients (Diversion)

A 340B drug is dispensed to an inpatient, to an employee who is not an eligible patient, to a patient of a provider not contracted to the covered entity, or to a patient who has no established record with the entity, and the transaction is recorded as a 340B purchase. The mechanism is that the eligibility check is skipped or the patient "feels" eligible. The false signal is that the prescription was filled and the system accepted it, so it must be compliant. The harm is diversion, which on HRSA audit triggers repayment, potential program termination, and exposure for the entity and the individuals involved. The corrective is to apply the three-part patient definition at every dispensing and to audit the split billing software's eligibility determinations.

### The Duplicate Discount on a Medicaid-Billed 340B Drug

A 340B drug is dispensed to a Medicaid patient and billed to Medicaid, and the manufacturer both gave the 340B discount and owes the Medicaid rebate on the same unit. The mechanism is that the Medicaid carve-out file was misconfigured or the patient's Medicaid status was not flagged. The false signal is that the claim was paid, so the transaction is complete. The harm is a duplicate discount, which is a core compliance violation and triggers repayment and audit findings. The corrective is to maintain an accurate Medicaid billing file, to configure the split billing software to exclude Medicaid-billed claims from 340B (or to follow the state's specific Medicaid carve-out rules), and to audit for duplicates.

### Trusting the Split Billing Software Without Auditing

The split billing software flags a transaction as 340B-eligible and the cost is routed to the 340B account, and no one audits whether the eligibility determination was correct. The mechanism is that the software feels authoritative. The false signal is that the software approved the transaction, so it must be compliant. The harm is that the software's accuracy depends entirely on its configuration (provider roster, Medicaid file, patient definition), and a configuration error silently misroutes hundreds or thousands of transactions, creating systemic diversion or duplicate discounts that surface only at audit. The corrective is to audit a sample of 340B and non-340B transactions periodically, to verify the configuration against current rosters and files, and to investigate any discrepancy.

### Inadequate Records at Audit

The entity is audited and cannot produce, for a sampled prescription, the documentation that the patient was eligible, that the drug was 340B-purchased, and that Medicaid was not billed in a duplicate manner. The mechanism is that the records were not maintained or were not reconciled. The false signal is that the entity was compliant in practice, so the records will confirm it. The harm is that HRSA treats the inability to produce records as non-compliance, and the entity faces repayment and findings regardless of intent. The corrective is to maintain auditable records continuously and to conduct internal audits so that the documentation is tested before the external audit.

### Confusing 340B with the Medicaid Drug Rebate Program

The pharmacist conflates the 340B discount with the Medicaid rebate, assuming that because the manufacturer "already gave a discount" no rebate is owed, or that Medicaid billing rules apply to 340B the same way they apply to non-340B drugs. The mechanism is that both programs involve manufacturer payments and feel related. The false signal is that the programs sound similar, so the rules are the same. The harm is a duplicate discount or a misrouted rebate that creates compliance failure. The corrective is to understand the distinct mechanics of each program and to apply the Medicaid carve-out correctly for 340B transactions.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The standard outpatient retail transaction is handled correctly, but the inpatient discharge prescription, the refill at a contract pharmacy, the drug purchased through a group purchasing organization that is also 340B-eligible, or the patient whose eligibility changes mid-episode is handled with the same defaults. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary of eligibility, site of care, or payer.

## Self-Check

- Did I confirm the entity is a registered covered entity and that each patient meets the three-part eligible patient definition (established record, care by an eligible provider, entity responsibility) at the time of dispensing?
- Did I document any contract pharmacy arrangement in a written agreement, register it with HRSA, and build in audit and reconciliation of the contract pharmacy's 340B transactions?
- Did I configure and periodically audit the split billing software — eligible provider roster, Medicaid carve-out file, patient definition — and investigate any discrepancy between software output and actual eligibility?
- Did I prevent duplicate discounts by accurately identifying Medicaid patients and applying the state-specific Medicaid billing carve-out to 340B transactions?
- Did I verify 340B purchase invoices against the published ceiling price, recognize and track penny-priced items, and dispute any overcharge?
- Did I maintain auditable records (registration, agreements, eligibility process, software logs, Medicaid file, purchase and dispensing records, internal audits) and conduct periodic self-audits with remediation of findings?
- Did I distinguish the 340B discount mechanics from the Medicaid Drug Rebate Program rebate mechanics, and apply the correct rule to each transaction?
- Does the output stay within my scope, escalating complex eligibility, contract pharmacy, and audit-response questions to the 340B compliance officer, pharmacy administration, legal counsel, and HRSA where the question exceeds my competence or authority?
