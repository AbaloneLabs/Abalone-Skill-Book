# Sedation, Analgesia, and Delirium Management in ICU

ICU sedation and analgesia look like drug selection but are actually a daily exercise in balancing three competing harms: untreated pain and agitation cause self-extubation, line removal, and sympathetic surges; oversedation causes prolonged ventilation, delirium, weakness, and death; and the drugs used to manage both carry their own toxicities (propofol infusion syndrome, dexmedetomidine bradycardia, benzodiazepine delirium, opioid hyperalgesia and ileus). The pharmacist's judgment problem is that sedation is dynamic, the targets shift as the patient's condition changes, and the easy path of deep continuous sedation is the wrong default. Modern ICU practice is analgesia-first, light sedation, daily awakening, early mobility, and delirium prevention, all of which require active management and tolerate none of the autopilot patterns that historically dominated the unit.

## Core Rules

### Treat Pain First and Use Analgosedation as the Default

Pain is the most common driver of agitation in the ICU, and treating pain before adding sedation (analgosedation) reduces total sedation exposure, ventilation time, and delirium. Use a validated pain scale (Critical-Care Pain Observation Tool for patients unable to report) and treat pain with an opioid (fentanyl for rapid effect and in renal impairment, hydromorphone for longer duration) before adding a sedative. Avoid relying on sedatives to mask pain, because benzodiazepines and propofol do not treat pain and can mask the signs while the patient remains in pain. Reassess pain at regular intervals and titrate analgesia to comfort before escalating sedation.

### Target Light Sedation With Validated Monitoring and Daily Awakening

The default target is light sedation (Richmond Agitation-Sedation Scale score of 0 to -2 for most mechanically ventilated patients), because deep sedation is associated with longer ventilation, more delirium, and higher mortality. Monitor sedation depth with the RASS at regular intervals and titrate to the lightest level compatible with safety and ventilator synchrony. Implement a protocol of daily sedation interruption or spontaneous awakening trials (paired with spontaneous breathing trials) to assess the underlying neurologic status and to reduce accumulation, with safeguards against self-extubation. Deep sedation should be an active decision for specific indications (intracranial pressure control, severe ARDS, status epilepticus, neuromuscular blockade), not the default.

### Prefer Propofol or Dexmedetomidine Over Benzodiazepines for Maintenance Sedation

Benzodiazepines (midazolam, lorazepam) are associated with more delirium and longer ventilation than propofol or dexmedetomidine and should not be the maintenance sedative in most ICU patients. Propofol is preferred for short-term sedation because of rapid onset and offset, but it causes hypotension, hypertriglyceridemia, and the rare but fatal propofol infusion syndrome at high doses or prolonged use (monitor triglycerides and creatine kinase, cap dose around 50 to 65 mcg/kg/min, and reassess beyond 48 hours). Dexmedetomidine is an alpha-2 agonist that provides sedation and analgesia with less respiratory depression and may reduce delirium, but it causes bradycardia and hypotension and is costly. Reserve benzodiazepines for alcohol or benzodiazepine withdrawal, status epilepticus, and specific indications.

### Prevent and Screen for Delirium as a Core Activity, Not an Afterthought

ICU delirium is common, associated with longer stay, higher mortality, and long-term cognitive impairment, and it is largely preventable. Screen every patient with a validated tool (Confusion Assessment Method for the ICU) at least once per shift. Address the modifiable contributors: treat pain, reduce sedation depth, minimize benzodiazepines, correct sleep disruption (light, noise, clustering care), promote orientation and communication, remove unnecessary lines and catheters, treat infection and metabolic disturbance, and provide early mobility. Nonpharmacologic delirium prevention (mobilization, sleep hygiene, sensory aids, family presence) is more effective than pharmacologic prevention, and routine antipsychotics do not prevent delirium and should not be used for that purpose.

### Use Pharmacologic Delirium Treatment Selectively, Not Reflexively

When delirium occurs despite prevention, treat the underlying contributors first. Pharmacologic treatment with antipsychotics (haloperidol, quetiapine, olanzapine) is reserved for patients with severe agitation that risks self-harm or treatment disruption and is not supported for routine delirium treatment or for delirium without agitation. Correct QTc before and during antipsychotic use, avoid in Parkinson disease and Lewy body dementia, and use the lowest dose for the shortest time. Do not use benzodiazepines for delirium except in alcohol or benzodiazepine withdrawal, because they worsen delirium. Dexmedetomidine may help delirious patients wean from ventilation and is an option in selected cases.

### Anticipate Withdrawal, Tolerance, and the Drugs' Own Toxicities

Prolonged opioid and sedative infusions produce tolerance and physical dependence, and abrupt discontinuation causes withdrawal (agitation, tachycardia, hypertension, seizures for benzodiazepines). Plan scheduled down-titration rather than abrupt cessation for patients on sustained infusions, and consider transition to longer-acting enteral agents (methadone for opioid-sparing, clonidine or dexmedetomidine bridge for sedation). Monitor for the specific toxicities: propofol infusion syndrome (watch for metabolic acidosis, rhabdomyolysis, heart failure with high or prolonged doses), dexmedetomidine bradycardia and hypotension, opioid-induced ileus and constipation, and benzodiazepine accumulation in renal and hepatic failure. The pharmacist must lead toxicity surveillance for these high-risk infusions.

### Coordinate With Ventilation, Mobility, and the Daily Plan

Sedation is not managed in isolation; it is tied to the ventilator weaning plan, the mobility goal, and the daily awakening. Coordinate sedation targets with the spontaneous breathing trial schedule so that awakening and breathing trials occur together, coordinate with physical and occupational therapy so that sedation is lightened for mobility windows, and adjust sedation for procedures then return to baseline. A sedation plan that is not coordinated with the daily ICU plan produces conflicting orders (deep sedation ordered on a day planned for mobilization) and poor outcomes.

## Common Traps

### Deep Sedation as the Default

The pharmacist and team keep the patient deeply sedated to prevent agitation and self-extubation. The mechanism is that deep sedation feels safe and quiet. The false signal is that a still patient is a well-managed patient. The harm is prolonged ventilation, ventilator-associated pneumonia, delirium, ICU-acquired weakness, and higher mortality, all of which are associated with deep sedation. Light sedation with analgesia-first is the default, and deep sedation requires a specific indication.

### Masking Pain With Sedatives

The patient is agitated and the pharmacist increases the propofol or midazolam without assessing pain. The mechanism is that agitation is treated as a sedation problem. The false signal is that the patient is calm after the sedative increase, so the problem is solved. The harm is that the underlying pain is untreated, the sedative accumulates, and the patient is oversedated; when sedation is lightened, the pain re-emerges as agitation. Pain must be assessed and treated before escalating sedation.

### Benzodiazepine Maintenance Sedation

The pharmacist continues a midazolam infusion for maintenance sedation because it is effective. The mechanism is that benzodiazepines are familiar and reliable. The false signal is that the patient is sedated, so the drug is working. The harm is that benzodiazepines are associated with more delirium and longer ventilation than propofol or dexmedetomidine. Propofol or dexmedetomidine should be the maintenance sedative, with benzodiazepines reserved for withdrawal, seizures, and specific indications.

### Missing Propofol Infusion Syndrome

The pharmacist runs high-dose propofol for days without monitoring triglycerides, creatine kinase, lactate, or pH. The mechanism is that propofol is common and usually safe. The false signal is that the infusion is tolerated, so it is safe. The harm is that propofol infusion syndrome is rare but fatal, presenting as metabolic acidosis, rhabdomyolysis, and cardiac failure, and it is dose- and duration-dependent. Triglycerides, creatine kinase, and acid-base status must be monitored with high or prolonged doses, and the dose capped.

### Routine Antipsychotics for Delirium Prevention or Nonagitated Delirium

The pharmacist orders haloperidol or quetiapine routinely to prevent or treat delirium in patients without agitation. The mechanism is that delirium is bad, so preventing it with a drug seems reasonable. The false signal is that an antipsychotic is a delirium treatment. The harm is that routine antipsychotics do not prevent delirium, prolong QTc, cause extrapyramidal effects, and are not supported for nonagitated delirium. Antipsychotics are reserved for severe agitation risking harm, with QTc monitoring and avoidance in Parkinson disease.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical ventilated patient is managed well, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the common path is handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the typical case and never tested the boundary such as alcohol withdrawal, neuromuscular blockade requiring deep sedation, or severe hepatic encephalopathy.

## Self-Check

- Did I assess and treat pain first with a validated scale before adding or escalating sedation, using analgosedation as the default?
- Did I target light sedation (RASS 0 to -2) with regular monitoring and daily spontaneous awakening trials, reserving deep sedation for specific indications?
- Did I prefer propofol or dexmedetomidine over benzodiazepines for maintenance sedation, reserving benzodiazepines for withdrawal, seizures, and specific indications?
- Did I screen for delirium with CAM-ICU each shift and address modifiable contributors (pain, sedation depth, benzodiazepines, sleep, lines, infection, mobility) before pharmacologic treatment?
- Did I reserve antipsychotics for severe agitation risking harm, check QTc, avoid them in Parkinson disease and Lewy body dementia, and use the lowest dose for the shortest time?
- Did I monitor for propofol infusion syndrome (triglycerides, creatine kinase, acid-base) at high or prolonged doses and cap the infusion rate?
- Did I plan down-titration or bridging for patients on sustained opioid or sedative infusions to prevent withdrawal, and monitor for drug-specific toxicities?
- Did I coordinate sedation targets with the ventilator weaning plan, mobility goals, and the daily ICU plan?
- Are assumptions, uncertainties, and confidence levels stated explicitly rather than buried in a confident-sounding recommendation?
