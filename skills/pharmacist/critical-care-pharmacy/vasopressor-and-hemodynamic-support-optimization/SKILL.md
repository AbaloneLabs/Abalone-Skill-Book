# Vasopressor and Hemodynamic Support Optimization

Vasopressor and inotrope management is the most pharmacologically intense activity in the ICU, because these drugs are given in titrated continuous infusions to patients whose hemodynamics change minute to minute, and every dose change has immediate, visible, and sometimes irreversible consequences. The pharmacist's judgment problem is that hemodynamic support is not a matter of matching a number to a drug; it is a matter of understanding which physiology is failing (vasodilation, low cardiac output, hypovolemia, distributive shock with mixed features), choosing the agent that addresses that physiology, titrating to a perfusion target rather than a blood pressure number, and preventing the complications of high-dose catecholamines (arrhythmia, ischemia, extravasation, gut and digital necrosis). The danger is treating the mean arterial pressure as the goal rather than as a proxy for perfusion, and escalating pressors into a patient who needs fluid, source control, or an inotrope instead.

## Core Rules

### Diagnose the Shock Physiology Before Selecting the Pressor

Before choosing a vasopressor or inotrope, determine the dominant shock physiology: distributive (vasodilatory, as in septic or neurogenic shock), cardiogenic (low cardiac output with high filling pressures, as in myocardial infarction or decompensated heart failure), hypovolemic (low preload), obstructive (tension pneumothorax, massive pulmonary embolism, tamponade), or mixed. Distributive shock responds to vasoconstrictors (norepinephrine first-line); cardiogenic shock may need inotropes (dobutamine, milrinone) and vasopressors to support pressure; hypovolemic and obstructive shock require volume and source correction, not pressors, and escalating pressors into these states masks the problem and worsens outcomes. The pressor must match the physiology, and the physiology must be reassessed as it evolves.

### Use Norepinephrine as First-Line for Distributive and Most Mixed Shock

Norepinephrine is the first-line vasopressor for septic and most distributive shock because it provides potent alpha-mediated vasoconstriction with modest beta-1 activity, raising mean arterial pressure with less tachyarrhythmia than dopamine or epinephrine. Start when fluid resuscitation has not restored adequate perfusion, titrate to the target mean arterial pressure (typically 65 mmHg in septic shock, individualized higher for chronic hypertension and lower for bleeding or specific contexts), and reassess perfusion (skin temperature, capillary refill, urine output, mental status, lactate trend), not just the pressure number. Avoid dopamine as a first-line pressor because it is associated with higher arrhythmia and mortality in septic shock compared to norepinephrine.

### Add Vasopressin and Consider Second Agents at Escalating Doses

When norepinephrine doses rise (commonly above 0.25 to 0.5 mcg/kg/min), add vasopressin as a catecholamine-sparing second agent at a fixed dose (typically 0.03 units/min), because vasopressin acts via a different receptor and reduces catecholamine requirements, arrhythmia risk, and possibly atrial fibrillation. Do not titrate vasopressin; it is used at a fixed dose as adjunctive therapy. For refractory septic shock, consider adding epinephrine or, in selected cases, corticosteroids (stress-dose hydrocortisone for vasopressor-dependent septic shock). Recognize that escalating pressor requirements without improvement should prompt reassessment of source control, volume status, adrenal insufficiency, and alternative diagnoses rather than simply more pressors.

### Select the Inotrope by the Cardiac Physiology and the Afterload Context

When low cardiac output is the problem, choose the inotrope by the physiology. Dobutamine provides beta-1 mediated inotropy with some beta-2 vasodilation, useful for low-output states with adequate or high pressure, but it can worsen hypotension and tachyarrhythmia. Milrinone is a phosphodiesterase inhibitor that provides inotropy and vasodilation independent of beta receptors, useful in beta-blocked patients or right heart failure, but its long half-life and renal clearance complicate titration and accumulation. Epinephrine provides inotropy and vasoconstriction and is used in cardiogenic shock and anaphylaxis. Avoid inappropriately adding an inotrope to a vasodilated patient without pressure support, because the vasodilation will worsen hypotension.

### Titrate to Perfusion, Not to a Pressure Number Alone

The mean arterial pressure target is a surrogate for perfusion, and a patient can have an adequate pressure with poor perfusion (cold, oliguric, confused, rising lactate) or an apparently low pressure with adequate perfusion. Titrate pressors to a perfusion goal: warm skin, brisk capillary refill, adequate urine output, clearing mental status, and falling lactate. In patients with chronic hypertension, a higher target may be needed to perfuse vital organs; in those with bleeding or specific contexts, a lower target may be acceptable to limit hemorrhage. Do not chase a single blood pressure reading without assessing the perfusion it produces, because over-pressurization causes ischemia (myocardial, mesenteric, digital) without benefit.

### Manage Central Access and Extravasation Risk Proactively

Vasopressors cause severe tissue necrosis on extravasation, and access management is a core safety responsibility. Confirm central access early for sustained pressor therapy, but recognize that peripheral initiation is acceptable for short durations with close monitoring and a large proximal vein. Establish a protocol for peripheral pressor monitoring (site checks every one to two hours, immediate stop at any sign of infiltration) and for extravasation management (stop the infusion, aspirate, and administer phentolamine antidote per protocol for alpha-agonist extravasation). The pharmacist must ensure phentolamine is available and that the team knows the extravasation protocol, because delayed recognition causes irreversible digital or limb necrosis.

### Integrate Pressor Therapy With Fluid, Source Control, and Adjuncts

Vasopressors are one component of shock resuscitation. Ensure adequate (but not excessive) fluid resuscitation guided by dynamic measures (passive leg raise, fluid responsiveness, dynamic arterial elastance), because giving pressors to an underfilled patient worsens vasoconstriction without improving perfusion, and over-resuscitation causes edema and worsens outcomes. Address source control (drainage, debridement, antimicrobial optimization) in parallel, because pressor escalation without source control is futile. Consider stress-dose corticosteroids in vasopressor-dependent septic shock, and reassess the shock physiology as it evolves, because a patient who starts distributive may develop cardiogenic or mixed features.

## Common Traps

### Escalating Pressors Into Hypovolemia or Obstructive Shock

The pharmacist and team increase norepinephrine for a falling pressure without assessing volume status or ruling out obstruction. The mechanism is that the pressure is the visible problem. The false signal is that low pressure means the pressor dose is insufficient. The harm is worsening vasoconstriction, ischemia, and delayed correction of the actual cause (bleeding, tamponade, pneumothorax, pulmonary embolism), because pressors cannot compensate for empty or obstructed vasculature. Fluid responsiveness and obstruction must be assessed before pressor escalation.

### Using Dopamine as a First-Line Pressor

The pharmacist or team selects dopamine for septic shock because of familiarity or its renal-dose myth. The mechanism is that dopamine is well known. The false signal is that dopamine supports pressure and renal perfusion. The harm is that dopamine is associated with more arrhythmia and higher mortality than norepinephrine in septic shock, and the renal-dose concept is unsupported. Norepinephrine is the first-line agent for distributive shock.

### Chasing a Pressure Number Without Assessing Perfusion

The pharmacist titrates pressors to reach a mean arterial pressure of 65 while the patient remains cold, oliguric, and confused with rising lactate. The mechanism is that the number is the documented target. The false signal is that the target pressure means adequate resuscitation. The harm is that over-pressurization causes ischemia (myocardial, mesenteric, digital) while the underlying poor perfusion persists, because the pressure is a surrogate, not the goal. Perfusion signs must guide titration.

### Missing Extravasation on Peripheral Pressors

A pressor runs peripherally and the site is not checked, leading to unrecognized infiltration. The mechanism is that peripheral initiation is allowed and the team becomes inattentive. The false signal is that the infusion is running, so the access is fine. The harm is severe tissue and digital necrosis that may require debridement or amputation, because alpha-agonist extravasation causes profound vasoconstriction in the tissue. Sites must be checked hourly and phentolamine must be available.

### Adding an Inotrope to a Vasodilated Patient Without Pressure Support

The pharmacist adds dobutamine or milrinone for low cardiac output in a hypotensive patient without ensuring adequate pressure support. The mechanism is that the cardiac output is the identified problem. The false signal is that improving contractility will fix the hemodynamics. The harm is that these agents vasodilate and worsen hypotension, causing ischemia and arrhythmia. Inotropes in vasodilated patients must be paired with adequate vasopressor support.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical septic shock patient is supported well, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the common path is handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the typical case and never tested the boundary such as pheochromocytoma crisis, anaphylactic shock, or pulmonary hypertension with right heart failure.

## Self-Check

- Did I diagnose the dominant shock physiology (distributive, cardiogenic, hypovolemic, obstructive, mixed) before selecting the vasopressor or inotrope?
- Did I use norepinephrine as first-line for distributive and most mixed shock, and avoid dopamine as a first-line agent?
- Did I add vasopressin at a fixed dose when norepinephrine requirements escalated, and consider corticosteroids for vasopressor-dependent septic shock?
- Did I select the inotrope by the cardiac physiology and ensure adequate pressure support when adding a vasodilating inotrope?
- Did I titrate to perfusion signs (skin temperature, capillary refill, urine output, mental status, lactate trend), not to the pressure number alone, with an individualized target?
- Did I ensure central access for sustained therapy, monitor peripheral sites hourly, and confirm that phentolamine and an extravasation protocol are available?
- Did I integrate pressor therapy with fluid responsiveness assessment, source control, and adjuncts rather than escalating pressors without addressing the cause?
- Are assumptions, uncertainties, and confidence levels stated explicitly rather than buried in a confident-sounding recommendation?
