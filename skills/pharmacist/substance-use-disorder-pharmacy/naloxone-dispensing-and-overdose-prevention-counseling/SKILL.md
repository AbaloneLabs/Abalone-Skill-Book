# Naloxone Dispensing and Overdose Prevention Counseling

Naloxone is one of the few medications that can stop a death in progress, and the pharmacist's job is not only to dispense it but to ensure it is in the right hands, with the right knowledge, before the moment it is needed. The judgment problem is that the overdose happens when the pharmacist is not present, so the counseling must transfer the entire response sequence (recognize overdose, call for help, give naloxone, do rescue breathing, repeat if needed, stay until help arrives) to a layperson who may be panicked, may be using substances themselves, and may have only seconds. The easy mistake is to hand over the box with a brief verbal instruction, when the real risks are that no one in the household knows where it is, that the first dose is given too late, that rescue breathing is skipped, or that the person leaves after the naloxone wears off and the opioid re-accumulates. Effective naloxone dispensing is a counseling and harm-reduction intervention, not a transaction.

## Core Rules

### Offer Naloxone Proactively to Every Patient at Elevated Overdose Risk

Naloxone should be offered, not waited for. Patients at elevated risk include anyone prescribed an opioid (especially at higher doses, with overlapping benzodiazepines or other sedatives, with a history of overdose or substance use disorder, after a period of abstinence with lost tolerance such as post-release or post-discharge, or with sleep-disordered breathing), anyone using illicit opioids or stimulants (which may be contaminated with fentanyl), and their household members and close contacts. Use standing orders or pharmacist-initiated protocols where available to dispense without an individual prescription. Frame the offer as routine safety, like a fire extinguisher, to reduce stigma and refusal.

### Teach the Recognition of Overdose Accurately and Repeatedly

The counseling must distinguish overdose from other states so the responder acts correctly. Teach the signs: unresponsiveness or inability to wake with a loud voice and sternal rub, slow or absent breathing, cyanosis of the lips or nails, pinpoint pupils (though this is not always present, especially with fentanyl or polysubstance exposure), and limp body. Emphasize that snoring or gurgling may indicate a compromised airway and is not normal sleep. Correct the misconception that someone can sleep off an overdose, because the difference between deep sleep and fatal respiratory depression is whether they can be roused and are breathing adequately.

### Train on the Specific Product and the Full Response Sequence

Different naloxone products (intranasal prefilled devices, intranasal spray, intramuscular or subcutaneous auto-injector) have different administration steps, and the counseling must match the dispensed product. Teach the full sequence: check responsiveness and breathing, call for emergency help, give the first naloxone dose, begin rescue breathing or CPR if trained, and if there is no response in two to three minutes, give a second dose if available. Emphasize that naloxone's effect is shorter than many opioids (especially long-acting agents and fentanyl), so the person can slip back into overdose after it wears off, which is why they must not be left alone and why emergency help must be called. Reassure the responder that naloxone will not harm someone who is not opioid-overdosed, so the risk of giving it unnecessarily is low.

### Address Fentanyl and Polysubstance Realities

Fentanyl and its analogues are potent, fast-acting, and increasingly present in heroin, counterfeit pills, and stimulants, which means overdose can occur rapidly and may require higher or repeated naloxone doses. Counsel that a single dose may not fully reverse a fentanyl overdose, that additional doses may be needed, and that because the response window is short, calling for help and starting rescue breathing immediately is critical. Polysubstance overdose (opioids with benzodiazepines, alcohol, xylazine) complicates the picture: naloxone reverses the opioid component but not the others, so the person may remain sedated or have complicated airway and cardiovascular issues even after naloxone, and ongoing emergency care is essential.

### Counsel on Post-Reversal Care and Avoid Retaliation and Re-overdose

After naloxone reverses the overdose, the person will often go into precipitated withdrawal, which is uncomfortable (nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, agitation, body aches) and may make them want to use more opioid to feel better, which is dangerous because the naloxone is still active and the next dose could re-overdose when naloxone wears off. Counsel the responder to keep the person calm, in the recovery position if they must be left momentarily, to discourage further use, and to ensure they are evaluated by emergency responders even if they feel better. Address the fear of police involvement that deters people from calling for help, and share any applicable Good Samaritan protections.

### Reduce Stigma and Treat the Encounter as Harm Reduction

The way naloxone is offered and dispensed signals whether the pharmacy is a safe place. Use non-stigmatizing language (person who uses drugs, not addict or abuser), do not require the patient to disclose illicit use to receive naloxone, and treat the dispensing as routine and dignified. For patients who use drugs, the pharmacy may be a trusted access point, and a respectful encounter can open the door to further care including referral to treatment, syringe services, and infectious disease screening. Document the dispensing and any counseling per regulatory requirements without creating barriers.

## Common Traps

### Dispensing Naloxone Without Hands-On Counseling on Administration

The naloxone box is handed over with a verbal "you know how to use this," and no one in the household has practiced or even opened the box, so when the overdose happens the responder fumbles with the device and loses critical seconds. The mechanism is the assumption that the product is intuitive, and the false signal is that the medication was dispensed so the safety intervention is complete. The harm is delayed or failed reversal because the responder could not administer the dose correctly under stress. The counseling must include a demonstration and a verbal walk-through of the steps.

### Assuming a Single Dose Will Reverse Every Overdose

The responder gives one dose, the person does not immediately wake, and the responder assumes the naloxone failed or the person is dead, when in fact fentanyl and polysubstance overdoses often require a second or third dose after the two to three minute interval. The mechanism is the expectation of an instant, single-dose reversal, and the false signal is that one dose is the maximum. The harm is abandonment of a reversible patient and failure to start rescue breathing during the wait. Counsel clearly that multiple doses may be needed and that rescue breathing bridges the gap.

### Letting the Person Leave or Re-Use After Reversal

The person wakes up, feels terrible from precipitated withdrawal, and leaves against advice or uses more opioid to relieve the withdrawal, then re-overdoses when the short-acting naloxone wears off and the longer-acting opioid re-accumulates. The mechanism is that reversal feels like the crisis is over, and the false signal is that wakefulness means safety. The harm is a fatal re-overdose within the next hour. Counsel that the person must be observed until emergency help arrives and must not use more opioid.

### Failing to Call for Emergency Help Because of Fear of Consequences

The responder reverses the overdose and does not call emergency services because they fear arrest, child welfare involvement, or immigration consequences, so the person is not evaluated for the polysubstance or medical complications and may re-overdose. The mechanism is the rational fear of consequences outweighing the perceived need for help once the person is awake, and the false signal is that the crisis is resolved. The harm is untreated complications and re-overdose. Counsel on Good Samaritan protections and the medical necessity of evaluation.

### Stigmatizing the Offer So the Patient Refuses

The naloxone offer is made in a way that implies suspicion of addiction ("Do you need this? Are you sure?"), and the patient refuses out of embarrassment or denial, leaving the household without a reversal agent. The mechanism is that the framing makes naloxone feel like an accusation, and the false signal is that refusal is an informed choice. The harm is a household without a life-saving tool. Frame naloxone as routine safety equipment offered to everyone at risk, like a smoke detector.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical adult intranasal naloxone dispensing is handled well, but the exception (a child with accidental exposure, a patient on a long-acting opioid patch, a household with a previous non-fatal overdose, a patient who is homeless or unstably housed, or a responder with limited English proficiency) is skipped. The trap is that the standard counseling is well-delivered while the exception silently fails because the boundary condition was never addressed.

## Self-Check

- Did I offer naloxone proactively to every patient at elevated overdose risk (high-dose opioids, overlapping sedatives, history of overdose or substance use disorder, recent abstinence with lost tolerance, illicit drug use) and to their household contacts?
- Did I teach the accurate recognition of overdose (unresponsiveness, slow or absent breathing, cyanosis, airway compromise) and correct the misconception that someone can sleep it off?
- Did I demonstrate administration of the specific dispensed product and walk through the full response sequence (check responsiveness, call for help, give naloxone, rescue breathing, repeat dose if no response in two to three minutes, stay until help arrives)?
- Did I counsel on fentanyl and polysubstance realities, including that multiple doses may be needed and that naloxone does not reverse non-opioid components?
- Did I address post-reversal care, including the risk of precipitated withdrawal, the danger of re-using opioid, the need for observation, and the importance of emergency evaluation?
- Did I share applicable Good Samaritan protections and address the fear of consequences that deters calling for help?
- Did I use non-stigmatizing language, treat the encounter as dignified harm reduction, and avoid requiring disclosure of illicit use to dispense?
- Did I stay within scope, providing education and dispensing per protocol, and refer to medical and treatment resources where the situation exceeds the counseling role?
