# Alcohol Use Disorder Pharmacotherapy

Alcohol use disorder (AUD) pharmacotherapy is underused relative to its evidence base, largely because the three first-line agents (naltrexone, acamprosate, disulfiram) have different mechanisms, different timing requirements, and different risk surfaces that reward careful matching to the patient rather than a one-size-fits-all default. Naltrexone reduces craving and heavy drinking and can be started while the patient is still drinking, but it requires opioid-free status (it will precipitate withdrawal in a patient on opioids) and attention to hepatotoxicity. Acamprosate supports abstinence maintenance and is best started after detoxification, is renally cleared, and demands adherence three times daily. Disulfiram creates an aversive reaction with any alcohol and works only when the patient is committed to taking it and avoiding alcohol, and it carries its own hepatic and psychiatric risks. The judgment problem is that the choice, timing, and monitoring of these agents are not interchangeable, and the wrong default (usually "start naltrexone on everyone" or "these do not work") leads to either avoidable harm or to denying the patient an effective treatment.

## Core Rules

### Match the Agent to the Patient's Drinking Pattern, Liver Function, and Adherence Capacity

Select the pharmacotherapy based on the clinical context rather than habit. For a patient who is still drinking and aiming to reduce heavy use, oral or long-acting injectable naltrexone is usually first-line because it reduces craving and the reward of drinking and does not require pre-existing abstinence. For a patient who has achieved abstinence after detoxification and wants to maintain it, acamprosate is a reasonable choice, though its thrice-daily dosing challenges adherence and it is best for patients with structured routines or support. For a highly motivated patient who wants an aversive deterrent and can commit to strict abstinence and supervised dosing, disulfiram has a role. Adjust the choice for hepatic function (naltrexone and disulfiram need caution in liver disease; acamprosate is hepatically safe but renally cleared), renal function, and adherence capacity.

### Confirm Opioid-Free Status Before Any Naltrexone Dose

Naltrexone is an opioid antagonist, and giving it to a patient who has used opioids in the past week to ten days will precipitate severe acute opioid withdrawal. Before the first dose, take a careful history of prescription opioid, illicit opioid, and opioid agonist therapy use, and for the long-acting injectable, a naloxone challenge test may be used to confirm opioid receptor clearance. Patients must be counseled that naltrexone will block the effect of opioids and that they should inform every clinician of their naltrexone use, because pain management in a naltrexone-treated patient requires non-opioid strategies or higher-potency opioids under specialist supervision. Missing this step turns a safe medication into an emergency.

### Assess Liver Function Before and During Naltrexone and Disulfiram Therapy

Both naltrexone and disulfiram carry hepatotoxicity risk and require baseline and periodic liver function monitoring. Check transaminases before starting, and monitor at intervals during therapy, especially in the first months. Naltrexone can be used cautiously in stable, mild to moderate liver disease (it is not absolutely contraindicated in cirrhosis as once thought, but acute hepatitis and severe hepatic failure are contraindications), and the decision should weigh the substantial benefit of AUD treatment against the hepatic risk. Disulfiram is more hepatotoxic and is generally avoided in significant hepatic disease. Counsel patients on signs of hepatotoxicity (jaundice, dark urine, fatigue, abdominal pain) and instruct them to stop and seek care if these occur.

### Time Acamprosate to the Post-Detoxification Window and Address Adherence

Acamprosate's mechanism (modulating glutamatergic tone after chronic alcohol exposure) is best suited to maintaining abstinence after detoxification, and it is typically initiated once the patient has achieved some alcohol-free days. It is renally cleared, so dose-adjust for renal impairment, and its thrice-daily dosing is a major adherence barrier that must be addressed with counseling, pill organizers, or support systems. Acamprosate can be combined with naltrexone in selected patients, and the pharmacist should reinforce adherence because missed doses simply do not work. The drug is generally well tolerated, with diarrhea being the most common adverse effect.

### Counsel Disulfiram Patients Thoroughly on Hidden Alcohol Sources

Disulfiram works by inhibiting aldehyde dehydrogenase, so any alcohol intake produces an accumulation of acetaldehyde that causes flushing, nausea, vomiting, palpitations, headache, hypotension, and in severe cases cardiovascular collapse. The effectiveness depends on the patient taking it consistently and avoiding all alcohol, including hidden sources: mouthwashes, cough syrups, vinegars, certain sauces, aftershaves and colognes absorbed through skin, and communion wine. Counsel the patient and family on these sources, on carrying a medical alert, on the need to inform every clinician and dentist, and on the reaction timeline. Supervised dosing (a family member or clinic observes each dose) dramatically improves effectiveness.

### Coordinate Pharmacotherapy With Psychosocial Treatment and Withdrawal Management

AUD pharmacotherapy is most effective when paired with psychosocial support (counseling, mutual-help groups, behavioral therapy), and the pharmacist should reinforce engagement rather than treat the medication as a standalone solution. For patients in active withdrawal, benzodiazepine-based withdrawal management (using a symptom-triggered or fixed-schedule protocol, with chlordiazepoxide, diazepam, or lorazepam depending on liver function) comes first; AUD maintenance pharmacotherapy is layered on as withdrawal resolves. Thiamine must be given to any patient at risk of Wernicke encephalopathy, before or alongside any glucose, because glucose can precipitate Wernicke in a thiamine-deficient patient. Folate and multivitamin support are common adjuncts.

## Common Traps

### Giving Naltrexone to a Patient Still Using Opioids and Precipitating Withdrawal

A patient is started on naltrexone without a careful opioid history, and within hours they are in severe acute opioid withdrawal requiring emergency care. The mechanism is that naltrexone abruptly displaces opioids from the receptor, and the false signal is that naltrexone is a safe AUD drug so the history can be brief. The harm is severe, trust-destroying withdrawal and a risk that the patient abandons AUD treatment. A thorough opioid history and, for injectable naltrexone, opioid-receptor clearance confirmation are mandatory before any dose.

### Defaulting to Naltrexone in Significant Hepatic Disease Without Monitoring

A patient with active hepatitis or decompensated liver disease is started on naltrexone because it is the default first-line agent, and transaminases rise further or hepatic failure worsens. The mechanism is the reflex to use the most common agent without filtering for hepatic status, and the false signal is that naltrexone is generally safe so the exception does not matter. The harm is hepatic decompensation in a patient already at risk. Acamprosate is the hepatically safer alternative in significant liver disease, and naltrexone requires baseline and periodic monitoring with a clear plan to stop if transaminases exceed threshold.

### Expecting Acamprosate to Work Without Adherence Support

Acamprosate is dispensed with a thrice-daily schedule and no adherence strategy, and the patient takes it inconsistently, so it has no effect and AUD treatment is declared a failure. The mechanism is that the drug's effect depends on consistent dosing that the schedule undermines, and the false signal is that the medication was given so the treatment was provided. The harm is a preventable failure attributed to pharmacology rather than adherence, and the patient is discouraged from further pharmacotherapy. Adherence counseling and practical supports are part of the prescription.

### Ignoring Hidden Alcohol in a Disulfiram Patient

A disulfiram patient has a severe reaction after using an alcohol-based mouthwash, cough syrup, or cologne, or after eating food cooked with alcohol, and the reaction is misattributed to non-adherence or to the drug itself. The mechanism is that alcohol is present in many non-beverage sources that the patient did not think to avoid, and the false signal is that the patient "must have drunk." The harm is a frightening reaction, erosion of trust, and possible discontinuation of an effective deterrent. Thorough counseling on hidden alcohol sources is essential at initiation and at refills.

### Withholding Thiamine Before Glucose in a Withdrawing Patient

A withdrawing or malnourished patient with possible Wernicke risk receives intravenous glucose without thiamine, precipitating acute Wernicke encephalopathy with confusion, ataxia, and ophthalmoplegia. The mechanism is that glucose metabolism consumes thiamine and unmasks deficiency, and the false signal is that glucose is the urgent treatment for the patient's distress. The harm is irreversible neurologic injury. Thiamine must be given before or alongside any glucose in any patient at risk of alcohol-related thiamine deficiency.

### Treating AUD Pharmacotherapy as a Standalone Intervention

The patient is given the medication with no counseling, no mutual-help referral, and no follow-up, and the inevitable relapse is blamed on the drug. The mechanism is the assumption that pharmacotherapy alone should suffice, and the false signal is that a prescription equals treatment. The harm is that the patient is denied the combination of medication and psychosocial support that the evidence supports, and they disengage. Pharmacotherapy must be paired with psychosocial treatment and structured follow-up.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical adult outpatient with uncomplicated AUD is managed well, but the exception (severe hepatic impairment, significant renal impairment for acamprosate, co-occurring severe psychiatric illness, pregnancy, the elderly, or the patient on opioid agonist therapy for whom naltrexone is contraindicated) is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces harm because the boundary condition was never tested.

## Self-Check

- Did I match the agent (naltrexone, acamprosate, disulfiram) to the patient's drinking pattern, liver and renal function, and adherence capacity rather than defaulting to a single agent?
- Did I confirm opioid-free status (careful history, and naloxone challenge or clearance confirmation for injectable naltrexone) before any naltrexone dose to avoid precipitated opioid withdrawal?
- Did I check baseline liver function and set a monitoring plan for naltrexone or disulfiram, with a clear threshold to stop, and consider acamprosate in significant hepatic disease?
- Did I time acamprosate to the post-detoxification abstinence window and address the thrice-daily adherence barrier with counseling and practical supports?
- Did I counsel the disulfiram patient and family on all hidden alcohol sources (mouthwash, cough syrup, cologne, vinegar, communion wine), medical alert use, and the reaction timeline?
- Did I ensure thiamine was given before or alongside any glucose in a withdrawing or malnourished patient to prevent Wernicke encephalopathy?
- Did I coordinate pharmacotherapy with psychosocial treatment, mutual-help referral, and structured follow-up rather than treating the medication as standalone?
- Did I stay within scope, presenting recommendations as advisory and deferring final prescribing decisions and licensed clinical judgment to the treating clinician?
