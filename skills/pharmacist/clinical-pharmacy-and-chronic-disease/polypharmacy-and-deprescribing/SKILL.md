# Polypharmacy and Deprescribing

Polypharmacy — the use of many medications, commonly defined as five or more — is not itself a disease, but it is the most powerful predictor of medication-related harm in older adults and complex patients, because each additional drug multiplies the risk of interactions, additive adverse effects, falls, cognitive impairment, and non-adherence. The pharmacist's traditional orientation is to add and optimize drugs; deprescribing — the planned, supervised reduction or discontinuation of inappropriate or unnecessary medications — requires a different mindset and is harder than prescribing, because the patient and the prescriber often believe every drug is necessary and because stopping a drug that the body has adapted to can cause withdrawal or rebound. The danger is that the medication list grows over years through accretion (each prescriber adds, no one subtracts), and the cumulative burden — the anticholinergic load, the sedative load, the total pill count — reaches a level where the harms reliably exceed the benefits, but no single drug is obviously "wrong," so nothing is stopped and the burden continues to grow.

## Core Rules

### Identify Medications With No Current Indication or Exceeded Benefit Horizon

The first category of deprescribing candidates is medications that no longer have a valid reason to be taken. The pharmacist must review each drug against the patient's current conditions and ask: does this drug still have an active indication? Indications resolve (the ulcer healed, the depression remitted, the acute event passed), circumstances change (the patient is now near end of life and the prevention benefit will never be realized), and the original rationale may never have been strong. A particular subcategory is preventive medications in patients with limited life expectancy: a statin for primary prevention in a patient with months to live provides no benefit within their lifespan and adds burden and risk. The pharmacist must identify drugs whose indication has resolved or whose benefit horizon exceeds the patient's expected lifespan, and must raise these as deprescribing candidates with the prescriber, because the default of continuation assumes an indication that may no longer exist.

### Quantify and Reduce Cumulative Drug Burden

Beyond individual drugs, the pharmacist must assess the cumulative burden of the regimen, particularly in domains where additive effects cause harm. Anticholinergic burden — the cumulative effect of drugs with anticholinergic properties (many antihistamines, tricyclics, antispasmodics, sleep aids, some antidepressants) — is strongly associated with cognitive impairment, delirium, falls, and constipation in older adults, and the burden can be quantified using validated scales (the Anticholinergic Burden Scale, the ACB) to prioritize reduction. Sedative burden — the cumulative effect of benzodiazepines, Z-drugs, antipsychotics, opioids, and other central nervous system depressants — drives falls and cognitive impairment. The pharmacist must identify patients with high cumulative burden in these domains and prioritize the reduction of the highest-burden, lowest-benefit drugs, recognizing that the harm comes from the total load, not from any single drug, and that reducing one drug may not be enough if the burden remains high.

### Use an Evidence-Based Deprescribing Framework for Each Drug Class

Deprescribing is not simply stopping a drug; each class has a specific evidence-based approach that minimizes withdrawal and rebound, and the pharmacist must use the framework appropriate to the drug. Proton pump inhibitors can often be tapered by dose reduction then stepped down, with monitoring for rebound reflux. Benzodiazepines and Z-drugs require slow tapers (10 to 25 percent dose reduction every two to four weeks) to prevent withdrawal seizures, anxiety, and insomnia rebound, often over months. Statins in end-of-life care can often be stopped directly. Antipsychotics in dementia require slow tapering with monitoring for behavioral symptoms. Antihypertensives may need dose reduction rather than discontinuation to avoid rebound hypertension. The pharmacist must apply the class-specific taper and must set up monitoring for the symptoms or parameters the drug was controlling, because deprescribing without monitoring can produce a withdrawal effect or a disease flare that is then misattributed and leads to restarting the drug at a higher dose.

### Engage the Patient and the Prescriber as Partners in the Decision

Deprescribing fails when it is imposed. The patient who has taken a drug for years often believes it is essential and fears stopping; the prescriber may feel that stopping another clinician's drug is an intrusion. The pharmacist must engage both as partners: explain to the patient why the drug is a candidate for deprescribing (the indication resolved, the burden exceeds the benefit, the prevention will not be realized in their lifespan), what the taper will involve, what to expect, and what the monitoring plan is; and coordinate with the prescriber to ensure the deprescribing fits the overall plan and to obtain the order changes. Patient acceptance is much higher when the rationale is framed positively (reducing burden, improving function, lowering fall risk) rather than as taking something away. A deprescribing decision that is shared is sustained; one that is imposed is reversed at the next refill.

### Prioritize, Sequence, and Monitor the Deprescribing Plan

Deprescribing one drug at a time, in a planned sequence, with monitoring, is far more successful than attempting to stop multiple drugs at once. The pharmacist must prioritize the highest-burden, lowest-benefit drugs first, must sequence the changes so that the effect of each can be observed before the next is made (stopping two drugs at once makes it impossible to attribute a subsequent symptom), and must establish a monitoring plan for each deprescribing event: what parameter or symptom to watch, over what timeframe, and what action to take if the drug was actually needed (restart, alternative, or accept the symptom). The deprescribing plan must be documented and communicated to the patient, the prescriber, and the pharmacy, so that the plan is sustained across refills and care transitions rather than reversed by a refill that assumes continuation.

## Common Traps

### The Accretion Default

The medication list grows over years as each prescriber adds drugs for new conditions and acute events, and no one reviews the list to subtract, because subtraction is no one's explicit job. The mechanism is that addition is the default response to a new problem and continuation is the default response at each refill. The false signal is that each drug was added for a reason, so each must still be needed. The harm is that the cumulative burden grows silently — the patient is now on ten drugs whose combined anticholinergic and sedative load is causing the falls and confusion that brought them to care — and no single drug is the obvious culprit, so the burden is never addressed. Deprescribing requires an explicit subtraction step that the default workflow does not provide.

### Stopping Without Tapering

The pharmacist or prescriber identifies an inappropriate drug and stops it abruptly. The mechanism is that the drug is inappropriate, so stopping it is the correct action. The false signal is that the drug is discontinued, so the problem is resolved. The harm is that drugs the body has adapted to (benzodiazepines, opioids, proton pump inhibitors, beta-blockers, some antidepressants) produce withdrawal or rebound when stopped abruptly — benzodiazepine withdrawal can seize, proton pump inhibitor rebound can produce severe reflux, beta-blocker cessation can cause tachycardia and hypertension — and the withdrawal is then misattributed to the underlying condition, leading to restarting the drug at a higher dose. Deprescribing must use the class-appropriate taper.

### Deprescribing Without Monitoring

The drug is tapered and stopped, and the patient is not monitored for the symptom or parameter the drug was controlling. The mechanism is that the deprescribing is complete when the drug is stopped. The false signal is that the drug is gone, so the task is done. The harm is that the underlying condition may flare (reflux returns, blood pressure rises, behavior re-emerges), and the flare is discovered weeks later as a new problem requiring a new drug, when it was actually the deprescribing revealing the original condition. Monitoring after deprescribing is essential to distinguish a successful discontinuation from one that revealed the drug was still needed.

### The "Don't Touch Another Prescriber's Drug" Norm

The pharmacist identifies a deprescribing candidate but does not raise it because the drug was prescribed by a specialist and "isn't ours to change." The mechanism is a professional norm of deference to the prescribing clinician. The false signal is that deference preserves the care relationship. The harm is that the burden continues because the only person who sees the full medication list — the pharmacist — declines to act on the cross-prescriber view that is their unique contribution. The pharmacist must raise deprescribing candidates with the relevant prescriber respectfully and with evidence, not unilaterally stop the drug, but also not remain silent out of deference.

### Patient Fear Override

The pharmacist proposes deprescribing and the patient expresses fear of stopping a long-term drug, so the pharmacist abandons the proposal. The mechanism is that the patient's fear feels like informed refusal. The false signal is that the patient declined, so the decision is theirs to make. The harm is that the patient's fear is often based on a misunderstanding (they believe the drug is essential, they fear the condition will return catastrophically) that accurate information and a slow taper would address, and abandoning the proposal leaves the burden in place. The pharmacist should explore the fear, provide the rationale and the monitoring plan, and propose a cautious trial of tapering — often, a patient who would not accept outright discontinuation will accept a trial reduction with the option to restart.

## Self-Check

- Did I review each drug against the patient's current conditions to identify those with no active indication, with an exceeded benefit horizon (especially preventive drugs in limited life expectancy), or with a resolved original rationale?
- Did I quantify the cumulative anticholinergic and sedative burden using a validated scale, and prioritize reduction of the highest-burden drugs recognizing that the harm is in the total load?
- For each deprescribing candidate, did I use the class-specific evidence-based taper (slow for benzodiazepines and Z-drugs, stepped for proton pump inhibitors, direct for some end-of-life preventives) rather than abrupt cessation?
- Did I engage the patient and the prescriber as partners, framing the rationale positively and coordinating the order changes, rather than imposing or remaining silent out of deference?
- Did I prioritize, sequence (one drug at a time), and monitor each deprescribing event, with a defined parameter or symptom to watch and an action if the drug was needed?
- Did I document and communicate the deprescribing plan so it is sustained across refills and care transitions, rather than reversed by a refill that assumes continuation?
