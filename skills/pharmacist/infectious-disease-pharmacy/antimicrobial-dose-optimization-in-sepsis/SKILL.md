# Antimicrobial Dose Optimization in Sepsis

In sepsis and septic shock, antimicrobial dosing is a time-critical pharmacokinetic problem layered onto rapidly changing physiology. The danger is twofold and opposite: in the first hours, under-dosing from failure to give a loading dose or from delayed infusion increases mortality, because each hour of inadequate exposure in septic shock worsens outcome; over the subsequent days, the patient's volume status, renal function, and clearance shift dramatically, and a dose that was correct at hour one becomes toxic or subtherapeutic. The pharmacist must apply aggressive loading, then track the moving target of organ function and volume of distribution, and use advanced administration strategies such as extended or continuous infusion and therapeutic drug monitoring to keep exposure adequate without accumulating toxicity. Static standard dosing is inadequate in this population.

## Core Rules

### Administer a Loading Dose Immediately and Prioritize Time to First Dose

In septic shock, the first adequate dose must be delivered as early as possible, ideally within one hour of recognition, because delayed or inadequate initial antimicrobial exposure is associated with increased mortality. Use a loading dose for beta-lactams, vancomycin, and other agents with significant distribution, because the goal in the first hours is to achieve adequate concentration at the site of infection immediately, not to wait for accumulation. For vancomycin, give a weight-based loading dose of 20 to 25 mg/kg of actual body weight for serious MRSA infection. Do not delay the first dose to wait for levels, renal function results, or line placement if a working access exists; the cost of delay exceeds the cost of imperfect initial dosing.

### Anticipate and Detect Augmented Renal Clearance

In the early phase of sepsis, younger patients, those with trauma, burns, pancreatitis, or high cardiac output states, and those receiving aggressive fluid resuscitation often develop augmented renal clearance (creatinine clearance above 120 to 130 mL/min), which clears hydrophilic antibiotics (beta-lactams, vancomycin, aminoglycosides, linezolid) so rapidly that standard doses produce subtherapeutic exposure and treatment failure. The pharmacist must estimate creatinine clearance using a timed urine collection or a validated equation, recognize augmented renal clearance as a risk, and increase the dose or frequency or switch to extended or continuous infusion for beta-lactams. Failing to detect augmented renal clearance is a leading cause of unrecognized under-dosing in the critically ill.

### Adjust for Dynamic Renal Function and Renal Replacement Therapy

Renal function in sepsis is not stable. A patient may have normal clearance on day one, develop acute kidney injury by day two, and require renal replacement therapy by day three, then recover. Each transition changes antimicrobial dosing dramatically. Check creatinine clearance daily and adjust doses for beta-lactams, vancomycin, aminoglycosides, levofloxacin, and other renally cleared agents. For patients on intermittent hemodialysis, continuous renal replacement therapy, or sustained low-efficiency dialysis, dosing differs by modality, filter, and flow rates; use modality-specific recommendations and therapeutic drug monitoring where available. Do not carry a dose forward without reassessing the changing clearance.

### Use Extended or Continuous Infusion for Beta-Lactams in Severe Infection

Beta-lactam efficacy is driven by the time the free drug concentration remains above the minimum inhibitory concentration, and in severe infection with resistant organisms or high inoculum, standard intermittent dosing may not achieve adequate time above MIC, especially with augmented renal clearance. Extended infusion (over 3 to 4 hours) or continuous infusion (over 24 hours) of beta-lactams such as piperacillin-tazobactam, cefepime, meropenem, and ceftazidime improves the probability of target attainment in critically ill patients. After the loading dose, transition to extended or continuous infusion for severe infection, high-MIC organisms, or patients with augmented renal clearance, and ensure the infusion is compatible and stable for the intended duration.

### Apply AUC-Guided Vancomycin Dosing With Monitoring

Vancomycin dosing in sepsis should target an area under the curve over 24 hours to MIC ratio of 400 to 600 for serious MRSA infection, using a loading dose followed by maintenance dosing guided by trough or Bayesian monitoring. Fixed trough-only targets (15 to 20 mg/L) are no longer the standard because they correlate imperfectly with AUC and increase nephrotoxicity risk. Monitor levels early (within 24 to 48 hours for unstable patients), adjust based on actual body weight and renal function, and watch for concurrent nephrotoxins (piperacillin-tazobactam, contrast, amphotericin) that increase vancomycin-associated kidney injury. In acute kidney injury, hold or reduce vancomycin and reconsider the need, because accumulation is rapid.

### Match the Drug to the Site and Use Protein Binding and Volume of Distribution Logic

Sepsis increases volume of distribution due to capillary leak and fluid resuscitation, which lowers the peak concentration of hydrophilic drugs and increases the loading dose requirement. Protein binding changes with critical illness (low albumin increases the free fraction of highly bound drugs like ceftriaxone and ertapenem, which can both increase toxicity and increase clearance of the free drug). For source-specific coverage, ensure the chosen drug reaches the site: fluoroquinolones and linezolid penetrate lung and skin well; aminoglycosides and beta-lactams penetrate poorly into abscesses, bone, and cerebrospinal fluid and may require higher doses or combination. Daptomycin is inactivated by surfactant and must not be used for pneumonia.

### Reassess Daily and Integrate With Source Control and De-escalation

Each day, reassess the dose against the current organ function, the clinical response, and the culture results. Narrow and de-escalate as soon as the pathogen and susceptibility are known, and convert to oral therapy when the patient is improving and absorbing. Ensure source control (drainage, device removal) is addressed in parallel, because ongoing bacteremia or fever often reflects an uncontrolled source rather than a dosing problem. Daily pharmacist review of antimicrobial dosing in the ICU is associated with improved outcomes and reduced toxicity.

## Common Traps

### Standard Dosing Without a Loading Dose in Septic Shock

The pharmacist orders a standard maintenance dose without a loading dose because the patient's renal function is borderline. The mechanism is fear of overdose. The false signal is that conservative dosing is safe. The harm is that in septic shock, the first hours require adequate concentration, and a loading dose is needed to achieve it; withholding the load to avoid toxicity delays adequate exposure and worsens outcome. The loading dose should be given immediately, then maintenance adjusted to renal function.

### Missing Augmented Renal Clearance

The pharmacist doses a beta-lactam for a young trauma patient based on a normal serum creatinine and assumes the dose is adequate. The mechanism is that serum creatinine looks normal. The false signal is that normal creatinine means normal clearance. The harm is that augmented renal clearance in hyperdynamic critically ill patients clears hydrophilic antibiotics rapidly, producing subtherapeutic exposure and treatment failure that is misattributed to resistance or source control. Creatinine clearance must be estimated and augmented clearance anticipated.

### Carrying a Dose Forward Across Changing Renal Function

The pharmacist continues the day-one dose as the patient develops acute kidney injury or starts renal replacement therapy. The mechanism is that the dose was correct and no one reassessed. The false signal is that stability of the order means stability of the dose. The harm is accumulation and toxicity (vancomycin nephrotoxicity, beta-lactam neurotoxicity, aminoglycoside ototoxicity) when clearance falls, or under-dosing when clearance rises or renal replacement therapy is started. Renal function must be checked daily.

### Trough-Only Vancomycin Management

The pharmacist targets a trough of 15 to 20 mg/L without AUC estimation. The mechanism is that trough monitoring is familiar. The false signal is that the trough reflects exposure. The harm is that trough correlates imperfectly with AUC, and trough-only targeting increases nephrotoxicity while sometimes failing to achieve adequate AUC. AUC-guided dosing with Bayesian or two-level monitoring is the current standard for serious MRSA infection.

### Treating Ongoing Fever as a Dosing Problem

The patient remains febrile and the pharmacist increases the antibiotic dose or adds agents. The mechanism is that fever feels like treatment failure. The false signal is that persistent fever means inadequate antibiotic exposure. The harm is that ongoing fever often reflects an undrained source (abscess, infected line), drug fever, or a noninfectious process, and escalating antibiotics without addressing the cause delays source control and adds toxicity. Persistent fever warrants source control review and reassessment, not reflexive dose escalation.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The standard septic patient is dosed well, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the common path is handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the typical case and never tested the boundary such as extracorporeal membrane oxygenation, pediatric septic shock, or morbid obesity.

## Self-Check

- Did I administer a loading dose immediately and prioritize time to first adequate dose within one hour of recognition of septic shock?
- Did I estimate creatinine clearance and actively screen for augmented renal clearance, increasing dose or frequency or switching to extended or continuous infusion for beta-lactams where indicated?
- Did I reassess renal function daily and adjust doses for beta-lactams, vancomycin, aminoglycosides, and other renally cleared agents across transitions and renal replacement therapy?
- Did I use extended or continuous infusion for beta-lactams in severe infection, high-MIC organisms, or augmented renal clearance, after an appropriate loading dose?
- For vancomycin, did I apply AUC-guided dosing (400 to 600 target) with early monitoring, weight-based loading, and attention to concurrent nephrotoxins?
- Did I account for volume of distribution changes, protein binding shifts, and site-of-infection penetration in the dose and drug choice?
- Did I integrate daily dosing review with source control assessment and de-escalation rather than treating persistent fever with reflexive dose escalation?
- Are assumptions, uncertainties, and confidence levels stated explicitly rather than buried in a confident-sounding dose recommendation?
