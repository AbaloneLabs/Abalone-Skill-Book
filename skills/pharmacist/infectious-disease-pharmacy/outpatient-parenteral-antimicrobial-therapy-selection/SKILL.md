# Outpatient Parenteral Antimicrobial Therapy Selection

Outpatient parenteral antimicrobial therapy (OPAT) moves a therapy designed for the monitored inpatient environment into the home, the infusion center, or the skilled nursing facility, and every assumption that held in the hospital must be re-examined. The pharmacist's judgment problem is that OPAT succeeds only when the drug, the device, the access, the patient, and the monitoring plan are all aligned; a failure in any one of these produces a readmission, a line infection, a stability failure, or a missed dose. The danger is that OPAT is often planned as a discharge convenience rather than as a clinical decision, with the regimen chosen for inpatient continuity rather than for outpatient feasibility, and with insufficient attention to whether the patient can actually manage the device, recognize complications, and return for monitoring. The pharmacist must treat OPAT selection as a distinct prescribing decision, not a continuation of the inpatient order.

## Core Rules

### Confirm That Parenteral Therapy Is Truly Required Before Arranging OPAT

Before committing to OPAT, confirm that intravenous therapy is genuinely necessary. Many infections managed with IV therapy in the hospital can be completed with highly bioavailable oral agents (fluoroquinolones, linezolid, trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole, metronidazole, fluconazole), and oral completion therapy is safer (no line, no line infection), cheaper, and often equally effective when the patient is improving and absorbing. Apply criteria for oral switch (improving clinical status, tolerating oral intake, available oral agent with adequate bioavailability, no malabsorption) before arranging OPAT. Reserving OPAT for infections that truly require it (endocarditis, complex osteomyelitis with resistant organisms, central nervous system infection, deep-seated abscess with ongoing drainage) reduces harm and cost.

### Select an Agent With Outpatient Stability and Compatibility as a First Filter

The inpatient agent may not be suitable for OPAT because of stability, dosing frequency, or administration requirements. The agent selected for OPAT must be stable in the chosen delivery vehicle for the intended period (often 24 hours at room temperature, or longer for refrigerated elastomeric pumps), compatible with the access and any co-infusions, and feasible at a dosing frequency the patient can manage. Prefer agents that allow once-daily dosing (ertapenem, ceftriaxone, daptomycin, once-daily aminoglycoside) when appropriate, because once-daily regimens reduce line manipulations, nursing visits, and disruption. Verify stability data for the specific concentration, diluent, container, and temperature, and do not extrapolate from a different formulation.

### Match the Device to the Drug and the Patient

The delivery device (electronic ambulatory pump, elastomeric pump, or bolus administration) must match the drug's administration requirements and the patient's ability. Elastomeric pumps are silent, portable, and simple but deliver at a fixed rate and have limited stability windows; they suit continuous or extended infusion of stable agents. Electronic pumps allow programmable rates and alarms but require power, patient or caregiver training, and troubleshooting. Once-daily agents may be given as a slow IV push or short infusion via a saline lock, which is simplest. The choice depends on the drug, the infusion duration, the patient's cognition and dexterity, and the home support. Do not default to the most complex device when a simpler option suffices.

### Assess Vascular Access and Plan for Line Care

Vascular access is the single most common source of OPAT complications. Select the appropriate access: a peripherally inserted central catheter is common but carries thrombosis and infection risk; a midline may suffice for non-irritant, non-vesicant agents given for shorter courses but is not central and is unsuitable for agents requiring central administration; tunneled lines are used for longer courses. Plan line care (dressing changes, flush protocols, exit-site monitoring), counsel the patient and caregiver on signs of line infection (erythema, drainage, fever), and define who performs line care and how problems are reported. Line selection and care are as important as drug selection.

### Assess Patient and Caregiver Suitability Honestly

OPAT depends on the patient's and caregiver's ability to manage the device, recognize complications, adhere to the schedule, maintain a clean environment, and return for monitoring. Assess cognition, dexterity, vision, language, home environment, refrigeration, telephone access, transportation, and caregiver availability before discharge. A patient who cannot manage the device safely, who has no reliable caregiver, or who cannot return for monitoring is not a candidate for home OPAT and may be better served by a skilled nursing facility or an infusion center. Overstating patient capability to enable discharge is a common and dangerous error.

### Define a Monitoring Plan and Clear Triggers for Re-evaluation

OPAT requires structured monitoring: weekly or more frequent clinical and laboratory monitoring for toxicity and efficacy (complete blood count, renal and hepatic function, therapeutic drug monitoring for vancomycin or aminoglycosides), monitoring of the access site, and a defined point of contact for problems. Define the expected duration, the criteria for response, the criteria for escalation or readmission, and the follow-up schedule before discharge. Without a monitoring plan, toxicity (vancomycin nephrotoxicity, linezolid thrombocytopenia, beta-lactam neutropenia) and line infection go undetected until the patient is readmitted. Ensure the patient knows whom to call and what symptoms warrant urgent evaluation.

### Coordinate the Transition and the Documentation

OPAT spans the inpatient team, the home infusion pharmacy, the visiting nurse, the outpatient prescriber, and often the skilled nursing facility. The pharmacist must ensure the orders, the drug, the device, the access care, the monitoring, and the follow-up are communicated clearly to all parties, that the home infusion pharmacy has the prescription and the stability information, and that the outpatient provider accepts the monitoring responsibility. Incomplete handoff is a leading cause of OPAT failure, because no one owns the patient once they leave the building. Document the plan, the contacts, and the contingency for problems.

## Common Traps

### Defaulting to OPAT When Oral Completion Therapy Is Feasible

The pharmacist continues the inpatient IV agent into OPAT because the patient was on it, without evaluating oral switch. The mechanism is continuity and the assumption that IV is more complete. The false signal is that the patient was on IV, so IV should continue. The harm is exposure to line risk, infection, cost, and disruption when a bioavailable oral agent would have been equally effective. Oral switch criteria should be applied before OPAT is arranged.

### Selecting an Agent Without Verifying Outpatient Stability

The pharmacist orders the inpatient agent for elastomeric infusion without checking stability at the concentration, diluent, container, temperature, and duration. The mechanism is that the drug worked inpatient. The false signal is that an IV drug is an IV drug. The harm is precipitation, degradation, or subpotent delivery that causes treatment failure or line occlusion, because stability differs dramatically by formulation and container. Stability must be verified for the specific outpatient setup.

### Overestimating Patient or Caregiver Capability

The pharmacist and team arrange home OPAT for a patient with limited cognition, no caregiver, or an unstable home, assuming the visiting nurse will compensate. The mechanism is the desire to discharge. The false signal is that nursing visits cover the gaps. The harm is missed doses, line infections, unrecognized toxicity, and readmission, because the patient cannot manage between visits. Capability must be assessed honestly, and the setting (home, infusion center, skilled nursing facility) must match the patient.

### Ignoring Line Selection and Care

The pharmacist focuses on the drug and treats the access as a detail. The mechanism is that the access is procedural, not pharmacologic. The false signal is that any line works. The harm is that central line infections, thrombosis, and occlusions are among the most common OPAT complications and are largely preventable by correct line selection, care, and monitoring. Access choice and care must be planned as deliberately as the drug.

### No Monitoring Plan or Defined Point of Contact

The patient is discharged on OPAT with no scheduled labs and no clear contact for problems. The mechanism is that the discharge is treated as the endpoint. The false signal is that the prescription is complete. The harm is that toxicity and line infection develop silently and present as readmissions, often late and severe. A monitoring plan and a defined contact must be established before discharge.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The straightforward OPAT patient is managed well, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the common path is handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the typical case and never tested the boundary such as OPAT in pregnancy, in intravenous drug users, or for agents requiring cold chain integrity in transit.

## Self-Check

- Did I confirm that intravenous therapy is genuinely required and apply oral switch criteria before arranging OPAT?
- Did I select an agent with verified stability, compatibility, and dosing frequency appropriate for the outpatient setting, preferring once-daily agents where suitable?
- Did I match the delivery device (electronic pump, elastomeric pump, bolus) to the drug's requirements and the patient's ability?
- Did I select appropriate vascular access and plan line care, with patient and caregiver counseling on line infection signs?
- Did I assess patient and caregiver cognition, dexterity, environment, and support honestly, and choose the setting (home, infusion center, skilled nursing facility) to match capability?
- Did I define a monitoring plan with laboratory and clinical monitoring frequency, therapeutic drug monitoring where indicated, and clear triggers for re-evaluation and readmission?
- Did I coordinate the handoff across the inpatient team, home infusion pharmacy, visiting nurse, and outpatient prescriber, with documented contacts and contingencies?
- Are assumptions, uncertainties, and confidence levels stated explicitly rather than buried in a confident-sounding recommendation?
