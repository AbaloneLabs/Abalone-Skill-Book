# Renal and Hepatic Dose Adjustment

Organ function is the single most important patient-specific factor in dose selection, because the kidneys and the liver are the two organs that eliminate nearly every drug, and when their function declines, the standard dose — derived from studies in patients with normal function — accumulates to toxic levels. The danger is twofold: the impairment is often invisible (an older adult with normal-appearing serum creatinine but reduced clearance; a patient with compensated cirrhosis and normal liver enzymes but markedly reduced metabolic capacity), and the adjustment is frequently omitted (the prescriber did not have the current labs, assumed the pharmacy would adjust, or the adjustment did not occur to them). The pharmacist is the last check before a standard dose reaches a patient whose organs cannot clear it, and the discipline is to estimate organ function for every patient on renally or hepatically cleared drugs, to adjust proactively rather than waiting for the prescriber or the alert system, and to recognize the limitations of the estimates and the references.

## Core Rules

### Estimate Renal Function for Every Patient on Renally Cleared Drugs

Renal dose adjustment begins with an accurate estimate of renal function, and the pharmacist must generate this estimate for every patient on renally cleared drugs rather than assuming it has been done. The serum creatinine alone is an inadequate measure, particularly in older adults (reduced muscle mass), amputees, patients with low body weight, and patients with rapidly changing function. The pharmacist must estimate creatinine clearance using the Cockcroft-Gault equation (the equation most drug dosing references are based on) or the estimated glomerular filtration rate from CKD-EPI (increasingly used, but the pharmacist must recognize that dosing recommendations were generally developed using Cockcroft-Gault, and there can be discrepancies). The estimate must use the patient's actual body weight (or adjusted body weight in obesity, where the actual weight overestimates clearance), age, and sex, and the pharmacist must be alert to situations where the equation is unreliable (rapidly changing renal function, dialysis, extremes of muscle mass). The estimate is the input to every renal dose decision, and a wrong estimate produces wrong doses across the regimen.

### Apply Renal Dose Adjustments From Authoritative References and Verify the Threshold

Renal dose adjustment recommendations specify the creatinine clearance thresholds below which the dose or interval must change, and the adjusted regimen (often a reduced dose, a longer interval, or both). The pharmacist must consult an authoritative, current reference (the drug label, Lexicomp, UpToDate, or a renal dosing handbook) for each renally cleared drug and must apply the recommendation for the patient's estimated clearance. The pharmacist must verify the threshold precisely — many drugs have different recommendations at different clearance ranges (50-80, 30-50, below 30, dialysis), and an estimate near a threshold requires a deliberate decision about which range applies. The pharmacist must be alert to drugs that should be avoided entirely below a threshold (nitrofurantoin below 30, metformin in significant impairment with risk factors), to drugs that require supplemental or post-dialysis dosing, and to drugs whose active metabolites accumulate in renal failure (morphine-6-glucuronide, meperidine's neurotoxic metabolite). The adjustment must be communicated to the prescriber as a recommendation within scope or applied under a collaborative practice agreement, with documentation.

### Assess Hepatic Function and Adjust for Drugs Cleared by the Liver

Hepatic dose adjustment is less precise than renal because hepatic function is harder to quantify — there is no single equivalent of creatinine clearance, and the liver's drug metabolism involves multiple enzyme systems with differential susceptibility to disease. The pharmacist must assess hepatic function using available tools: the Child-Pugh score (combining bilirubin, albumin, INR, ascites, and encephalopathy to classify impairment as mild, moderate, or severe) is the most commonly used framework for drugs with hepatic dosing recommendations. The pharmacist must consult the reference for each hepatically cleared drug (many statins, certain benzodiazepines, many antipsychotics, beta-blockers, and numerous others metabolized by the liver) and apply the recommended adjustment or avoid the drug in significant impairment. The pharmacist must be particularly alert to drugs with narrow therapeutic indices that are hepatically cleared (warfarin, phenytoin, theophylline), where impaired metabolism produces toxicity, and to drugs that are hepatotoxic themselves (acetaminophen at high doses, certain antibiotics, statins), where the impaired liver is also the target of harm. When hepatic function is hard to estimate or the reference is silent, the pharmacist must use lower starting doses and slower titration with close monitoring.

### Reassess Organ Function When the Patient's State Changes

Organ function is not static, and a dose that was correctly adjusted at one point becomes incorrect when the patient's function changes. The pharmacist must reassess organ function — and the corresponding dose adjustments — at every care transition, after any acute illness (especially acute kidney injury, sepsis, dehydration, heart failure exacerbation), with the addition of nephrotoxic or hepatotoxic drugs (NSAIDs, contrast, certain antibiotics), and with the progression of chronic disease. A patient whose creatinine clearance was 50 at discharge may have a clearance of 30 after a hospitalization for dehydration, and the renally adjusted dose from discharge is now excessive. The pharmacist must treat organ function as a moving parameter and must request current labs before assuming the prior adjustment still applies, because applying a stale adjustment to a changed function produces the same toxicity as applying no adjustment at all.

### Recognize the Limitations of Estimates and References

The creatinine clearance estimate and the hepatic function score are approximations with limitations, and the dose adjustment references are generalizations that may not fit the individual patient. The pharmacist must recognize that the Cockcroft-Gault equation is unreliable in rapidly changing function, in extremes of body size, and in advanced renal failure; that the eGFR may differ from the Cockcroft-Gault creatinine clearance in ways that matter for dosing; that the Child-Pugh score is a coarse classification; and that the dosing recommendations are often based on pharmacokinetic studies in small populations, not outcome trials. When the estimate is uncertain, when the patient is at a threshold, or when the reference is silent or conflicting, the pharmacist must use clinical judgment: lower starting doses, slower titration, closer monitoring of drug effect and adverse effects (drug levels where available, INR for warfarin, symptom assessment), and consultation with the prescriber or a specialist. The adjustment is a clinical decision informed by the reference, not a mechanical application of it.

## Common Traps

### The Normal Serum Creatinine Assumption

The pharmacist sees a normal-range serum creatinine and concludes renal function is normal, applying standard doses to renally cleared drugs. The mechanism is that the lab value is within the reference range, so it reads as normal. The false signal is that the value is normal, so the clearance must be adequate. The harm is that the serum creatinine is determined by muscle mass and creatinine production as well as clearance, so an older, frail, or low-muscle-mass patient can have a normal serum creatinine with significantly reduced clearance, and the standard dose accumulates to toxicity. The pharmacist must estimate clearance with an equation, not rely on the serum creatinine.

### Stale Renal Function Used for Current Dosing

The pharmacist applies a renal dose adjustment based on a creatinine clearance estimated from labs drawn months ago, without checking whether the function has changed. The mechanism is that the prior estimate is in the record and feels current. The false signal is that the adjustment was made, so it remains correct. The harm is that renal function declines with acute illness, dehydration, nephrotoxic drugs, and disease progression, and a dose correctly adjusted to last year's clearance is now excessive for this year's reduced clearance, producing accumulation and toxicity. The pharmacist must request current labs and re-estimate before relying on a prior adjustment.

### Threshold Misapplication

The patient's estimated clearance is near a dosing threshold (say, 31 mL/min, with a threshold at 30), and the pharmacist applies the higher-clearance recommendation without recognizing the proximity to the threshold. The mechanism is that the number is just above the threshold, so the higher range seems to apply. The false signal is that the estimate is 31, so the over-30 recommendation is correct. The harm is that the estimate is an approximation with uncertainty, and a true clearance near 30 may be below it; applying the higher-clearance dose near a threshold risks accumulation, particularly for narrow-therapeutic-index drugs. The pharmacist must be conservative near thresholds, especially for high-risk drugs, choosing the lower-clearance recommendation or monitoring closely.

### Hepatic Impairment Missed Because Enzymes Are Normal

A patient with cirrhosis has normal or near-normal liver enzymes (because the liver is scarred and not actively inflamed) and the pharmacist concludes hepatic function is adequate, dosing hepatically cleared drugs at standard doses. The mechanism is that the enzymes are the familiar liver "function" tests, and they are normal. The false signal is that the liver tests are normal, so hepatic drug handling must be normal. The harm is that enzymes reflect injury, not function; a patient with compensated cirrhosis may have markedly reduced metabolic capacity with normal enzymes, and standard doses of hepatically cleared drugs (statins, sedatives, certain analgesics) accumulate or cause excessive effect. The pharmacist must assess hepatic function with the Child-Pugh score (which includes bilirubin, albumin, INR) or its equivalent, not the enzymes alone.

### Adjustment Made but Not Communicated or Monitored

The pharmacist identifies the need for a renal or hepatic adjustment and documents it in the pharmacy record, but does not communicate it to the prescriber, does not obtain the order change, and does not set up monitoring. The mechanism is that documenting the recommendation feels like completing the task. The false signal is that the adjustment is identified, so it will be applied. The harm is that without the prescriber's order change, the standard dose continues to be dispensed, and the patient accumulates the drug; and without monitoring, the toxicity is detected only when it causes harm. The adjustment must be communicated, the order changed, and the effect monitored (drug levels, organ function, adverse effects), or the identification provides no protection.

## Self-Check

- Did I estimate creatinine clearance (using Cockcroft-Gault with actual or adjusted body weight, age, and sex) for every patient on renally cleared drugs, rather than relying on the serum creatinine alone?
- For each renally cleared drug, did I consult an authoritative reference, verify the precise threshold, and apply the adjusted dose or interval (or recommend avoidance below the threshold) for the patient's estimated clearance?
- For hepatically cleared drugs, did I assess hepatic function with the Child-Pugh score or equivalent (not relying on liver enzymes alone) and apply the reference's hepatic adjustment or avoidance?
- Did I reassess organ function at care transitions, after acute illness or nephrotoxic drug exposure, and with chronic disease progression, requesting current labs rather than relying on a stale estimate?
- Near dosing thresholds, did I make a conservative choice (lower-clearance recommendation or closer monitoring), especially for narrow-therapeutic-index drugs where estimate uncertainty matters most?
- Did I communicate the adjustment to the prescriber, obtain the order change, document the rationale, and set up monitoring of drug effect, organ function, and adverse effects, rather than treating identification as completion?
