# Pediatric Weight-Based Dosing

Pediatric dosing is fundamentally different from adult dosing, and the difference is the source of both its safety and its danger. Adults are dosed with standard amounts because adult physiology is roughly uniform; children are dosed by weight (or body surface area) because a child's size, organ maturity, and drug disposition change dramatically from a premature neonate to a teenager, and a dose that is correct for one child can be ten times too much or too little for another of a different weight or age. The danger is that pediatric dosing errors are among the most lethal in pharmacy, because the gap between a therapeutic and a toxic dose is narrow in small bodies, because decimal point and unit errors multiply into large absolute errors in small patients, and because the child cannot report early adverse effects the way an adult can. The pharmacist's discipline is to treat every pediatric dose as a calculation that must be independently verified, to use the child's actual current weight (not a remembered or estimated value), and to apply age-specific considerations that change the answer at every developmental stage.

## Core Rules

### Calculate Every Pediatric Dose From the Child's Actual Current Weight

A pediatric dose is never a standard amount; it is a calculation: the dose per kilogram (from the authoritative pediatric reference) multiplied by the child's weight in kilograms, capped at the maximum adult dose for older or heavier children. The pharmacist must obtain the child's actual current weight — measured, not estimated, and not a weight from months ago — and must convert it to kilograms if recorded in pounds (a unit conversion error here is a 2.2-fold dosing error, which is catastrophic in a child). The pharmacist must verify the recommended mg/kg dose and the dosing interval against a current authoritative pediatric reference (Lexicomp, the Harriet Lane Handbook, pediatric prescribing guidelines), because pediatric dosing recommendations change and adult references are not reliable for children. The calculated dose must be checked against the maximum single dose and the maximum daily dose, because a large child dosed purely by weight could receive more than the adult maximum. The calculation must be independently verified by a second qualified person before dispensing, because a single calculation error in a small child is a major overdose or underdose.

### Apply Age-Specific and Developmental Considerations

Pediatric dosing is not uniform across childhood; the child's developmental stage changes the pharmacokinetics and the appropriate dose. Neonates (especially premature) have immature hepatic metabolism and reduced renal clearance, requiring lower doses and longer intervals for many drugs — chloramphenicol causes gray baby syndrome in neonates due to immature conjugation, and many renally cleared drugs accumulate. Infants have rapidly maturing clearance and may require higher mg/kg doses than older children or adults for some drugs (phenytoin, antiepileptics) due to their high metabolic rate. Children over about two years generally have clearance that meets or exceeds adults per kilogram, and may need higher mg/kg dosing. Adolescents approaching adult weight should be dosed with adult maximums in mind. The pharmacist must apply the developmental context, because a mg/kg dose that is correct for a school-age child may be excessive for a neonate or insufficient for an infant, and the reference must specify the age range for which the dose applies.

### Select the Appropriate Dosage Form, Concentration, and Strength

Children often cannot use adult dosage forms — they cannot swallow tablets, they need liquid formulations, they may need a different concentration to deliver a small dose accurately. The pharmacist must select a dosage form the child can take (a liquid suspension or solution for young children, a chewable or orally disintegrating tablet for older children, a suppository or other route when oral is impossible) and must select a concentration that allows accurate measurement of the small dose. A concentration that is too strong requires measuring a fraction of a milliliter, which caregivers measure inaccurately; a concentration that is too weak requires a large volume the child will not tolerate. The pharmacist should provide an oral dosing syringe or dropper marked in the relevant units (milliliters, not teaspoons, because "teaspoon" is interpreted variably and kitchen spoons vary in volume), and must counsel the caregiver to use the provided device, not a household spoon. For compounded pediatric liquids, the concentration must be clearly labeled and the caregiver must understand the strength, because a different concentration at refill delivers a different dose from the same volume.

### Verify Against Age-Specific Contraindications and Safety Concerns

Many drugs that are safe in adults are contraindicated or carry specific warnings in children of certain ages, and the pharmacist must verify the prescription against these. Codeine and tramadol are contraindicated in children under 12 (and in adolescents up to 18 for certain situations) due to the risk of fatal respiratory depression in ultra-rapid metabolizers. Tetracyclines are avoided in children under 8 due to tooth discoloration. Fluoroquinolones are generally avoided in children due to musculoskeletal concerns, with specific approved uses. Aspirin is avoided in children due to Reye syndrome, except for specific indications like Kawasaki disease under specialist supervision. Cough and cold preparations are not recommended in young children (under 2, and caution up to 4-6) due to lack of efficacy and serious adverse effects. The pharmacist must know these age-specific prohibitions and must refuse or query a prescription that violates them, because the harm is severe and the prohibition is evidence-based.

### Counsel Caregivers on Safe Administration and Measurement

The caregiver is the actual administrator of the medication, and the counseling must equip them to do it correctly. The pharmacist must demonstrate the measurement using the provided device, must specify the dose in the units the device uses (milliliters, not a translated "half teaspoon" that invites a household spoon), must explain the timing and the relationship to meals, must explain what to do if the child spits up or vomits a dose (generally, if vomiting occurs within 15-30 minutes, the dose may be repeated; later, do not), and must explain the expected effect, the common adverse effects, and the danger signs. The pharmacist must address adherence barriers specific to children: palatability (a flavor may be added or an alternative formulation chosen), refusal (never force a struggling child, which causes aspiration; use a syringe to the inside of the cheek in small amounts), and the importance of completing a course (antibiotics) even when the child improves. The counseling must verify, by teach-back, that the caregiver can state the dose, the timing, and the danger signs.

### Document the Basis and the Reasoning

Every conclusion should be traceable to its evidence, assumptions, and alternatives considered. Record not only the outcome but the reasoning path: what was checked, what was ruled out, what uncertainty remains, and what would change the conclusion. Documentation that captures the basis allows another practitioner to review, reproduce, or challenge the work, and it prevents confident conclusions from becoming unrepeatable assertions. A decision made without a recorded basis cannot be audited, improved, or safely handed off.

## Common Traps

### The Pound-Kilogram Conversion Error

The child's weight is recorded or reported in pounds and the pharmacist uses it as kilograms (or vice versa) without conversion, producing a 2.2-fold overdose or underdose. The mechanism is that the unit is implicit and the number is used directly. The false signal is that the number came from a record or a caregiver, so it must be in the expected unit. The harm is that a 2.2-fold error in a small child is a massive overdose (toxicity, sometimes fatal, as in pediatric acetaminophen or opioid overdoses) or a massive underdose (treatment failure). The pharmacist must always confirm the unit and perform the conversion explicitly, documenting the weight in kilograms used for the calculation.

### The Decimal Point Error

A decimal point is misplaced or dropped in a pediatric calculation — 0.5 written as .5 (misread as 5), or 5 mg written as 5.0 mg (the .0 ignored, but the risk is the reverse), or a ten-fold error in either direction. The mechanism is that decimal points are small visual elements and pediatric doses are small numbers where a misplaced decimal is a ten-fold error. The false signal is that the number looks plausible at a glance. The harm is that a ten-fold overdose in a child is frequently fatal (the classic case is pediatric morphine or other high-alert drugs), and a ten-fold underdose produces treatment failure. The pharmacist must use leading zeros (0.5, never .5), must avoid trailing zeros (5 mg, never 5.0 mg), and must have the calculation independently verified, because decimal errors are the most lethal pediatric dosing error.

### Adult Formulation or Concentration for a Child

The pharmacist dispenses an adult-strength tablet or a concentrated liquid to a child because it is what is in stock, assuming the caregiver can split or measure appropriately. The mechanism is that the adult product is available and the dose can theoretically be derived from it. The false signal is that the product contains the right drug, so it must be usable. The harm is that splitting an unscored tablet produces inaccurate doses, a concentrated liquid requires measuring a fraction of a milliliter that caregivers cannot measure accurately, and the resulting dose is wrong in a child where the margin is narrow. The pharmacist must select a pediatric-appropriate formulation and concentration, or compound one, rather than dispensing an adult product that invites measurement error.

### The "Teaspoon" Instruction

The prescription or label says "give one teaspoon" and the caregiver uses a household teaspoon, which varies from 3 to 7 milliliters across households. The mechanism is that "teaspoon" is a familiar household measure. The false signal is that the instruction is in plain language, so it must be clear. The harm is that the actual dose varies by a factor of two or more depending on the spoon, and in a child this variability produces either underdosing (treatment failure) or overdosing (toxicity). The pharmacist must convert all doses to milliliters, provide a dosing syringe or cup marked in milliliters, and counsel the caregiver to use only the provided device.

### Age-Specific Contraindication Missed

The pharmacist verifies the dose calculation but does not check whether the drug is appropriate for the child's age, dispensing codeine to a young child or a cough and cold preparation to an infant. The mechanism is that the dose calculation feels like the complete verification. The false signal is that the dose is correct, so the prescription is appropriate. The harm is that the drug is contraindicated regardless of dose — codeine can kill a child through respiratory depression at any dose in an ultra-rapid metabolizer, and infant cough and cold preparations have caused serious adverse events and deaths. The pharmacist must verify the drug against age-specific contraindications in addition to verifying the dose, because a correct dose of a contraindicated drug is still dangerous.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- Did I calculate the dose from the child's actual current measured weight in kilograms (confirming the unit and converting from pounds explicitly), using an authoritative pediatric reference for the mg/kg dose and interval, and capping at the adult maximum?
- Did I have the calculation independently verified by a second qualified person, using leading zeros and avoiding trailing zeros, to catch decimal and unit errors?
- Did I apply the developmental context (neonate, infant, child, adolescent), recognizing that the mg/kg dose and the clearance change with age and organ maturity?
- Did I select a pediatric-appropriate dosage form and a concentration that allows accurate measurement, and did I provide a milliliter-marked dosing syringe rather than relying on household spoons?
- Did I verify the drug against age-specific contraindications (codeine and tramadol under 12, tetracyclines under 8, fluoroquinolones, aspirin and Reye syndrome, cough and cold preparations in young children)?
- Did I counsel the caregiver with a demonstration of measurement using the provided device, instructions in milliliters, guidance on vomiting a dose, expected and adverse effects, danger signs, and adherence strategies, verified by teach-back?
- Does the output stay within the agent's scope, deferring final authority, licensed judgment, or specialist sign-off to the qualified person where the question exceeds the agent's competence?
- Is the reasoning documented clearly enough that another practitioner could review the basis and reproduce the conclusion?
