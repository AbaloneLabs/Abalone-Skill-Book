# Geriatric Dosing and Beers Criteria

Older adults experience medications differently than younger adults, and the difference is not captured by the standard adult dose. With age comes a decline in renal clearance (often invisible because the serum creatinine appears normal due to reduced muscle mass), a change in body composition (more fat, less water, altering the volume of distribution for water-soluble and fat-soluble drugs), increased sensitivity to central nervous system effects (sedatives, anticholinergics cause falls and delirium at doses that younger adults tolerate), and a high burden of comorbidities and concurrent medications that multiply interaction risk. The danger is that the standard adult dose — which was studied in younger, healthier populations — is excessive and inappropriate for many older adults, and the drugs that are routine in younger patients (benzodiazepines for sleep, anticholinergics for overactive bladder, first-generation antihistamines for allergies) cause falls, fractures, delirium, and cognitive decline in the elderly. The pharmacist's discipline is to apply geriatric-specific tools — the Beers Criteria, renal dose adjustment, and a heightened suspicion for central nervous system and anticholinergic effects — to every older adult's regimen, because the standard approach systematically overmedicates this population.

## Core Rules

### Apply the Beers Criteria to Every Older Adult Regimen

The AGS Beers Criteria catalog potentially inappropriate medications (PIMs) for older adults based on extensive evidence of harm exceeding benefit in this population. The pharmacist must apply the Beers Criteria to every older adult's regimen as a routine screen, not only when a problem is suspected. The criteria cover: medications to avoid in most older adults (long-acting benzodiazepines, first-generation antihistamines, certain muscle relaxants, long-acting sulfonylureas like glyburide, anticholinergic antispasmodics); medications to avoid in specific conditions (NSAIDs in heart failure or chronic kidney disease, anticholinergics in dementia or delirium); medications to use with caution (diuretics, tricyclics, antipsychotics); and medications requiring renal dose adjustment. A Beers-flagged drug is not an absolute prohibition — there are situations where the drug is the best choice and the risk is accepted with monitoring — but it is a signal that the pharmacist must verify the drug is genuinely necessary, that no safer alternative exists, and that the risk is acknowledged and monitored. The pharmacist must raise Beers-flagged drugs with the prescriber and document the rationale for continuation or the recommendation for substitution.

### Estimate Renal Function Accurately and Adjust Doses Accordingly

Renal function declines with age, and the decline is systematically underestimated by the serum creatinine alone, because older adults have less muscle mass and therefore produce less creatinine, so a "normal" serum creatinine in an 85-year-old may represent significantly reduced clearance. The pharmacist must estimate renal function using a validated equation (the Cockcroft-Gault equation for drug dosing, or the CKD-EPI equation, recognizing that the choice depends on the reference used for the dosing recommendation) and must adjust the doses of renally cleared drugs accordingly. Many drugs require renal dose adjustment or avoidance below specific creatinine clearance thresholds (gabapentin, pregabalin, certain antibiotics, apixaban dose adjustment, metformin thresholds, dabigatran avoidance in significant impairment), and the standard adult dose in a patient with unrecognized renal impairment accumulates and causes toxicity (gabapentin ataxia and somnolence, hypoglycemia from renally cleared sulfonylureas, bleeding from renally cleared anticoagulants). The pharmacist must not assume the prescriber has adjusted for renal function — the adjustment is frequently omitted — and must verify or recommend the adjustment.

### Minimize Central Nervous System and Anticholinergic Burden

The single most actionable geriatric pharmacotherapy principle is to minimize drugs that affect the central nervous system, because these drugs cause the falls, fractures, delirium, and cognitive decline that devastate older adults. The pharmacist must identify and seek to reduce: benzodiazepines and Z-drugs (falls, fractures, dependence, no long-term benefit for insomnia), antipsychotics (falls, stroke, mortality when used for dementia behavioral symptoms), opioids (falls, delirium, constipation, especially in combination with other sedatives), first-generation antihistamines (anticholinergic delirium), tricyclic antidepressants (anticholinergic, orthostatic), and skeletal muscle relaxants (sedation, falls, limited efficacy). The pharmacist must also quantify the cumulative anticholinergic burden, because the harm comes from the total load across multiple drugs each with modest anticholinergic effect, and a patient on three mildly anticholinergic drugs may have the cognitive and fall risk of one strongly anticholinergic drug. The pharmacist must prioritize reduction of CNS and anticholinergic burden as the highest-value intervention in geriatric medication review.

### Use Lower Starting Doses and Slower Titration ("Start Low, Go Slow")

Older adults are more sensitive to drug effects, have reduced clearance, and are more vulnerable to adverse effects, so the principle "start low, go slow" applies: begin with a lower than standard adult starting dose, titrate in smaller increments over longer intervals, and monitor for effect and adverse effects at each step. This is particularly important for psychoactive drugs (antidepressants, antipsychotics, sedatives), cardiovascular drugs (antihypertensives, where aggressive dosing causes orthostatic hypotension and falls), anticoagulants, and opioids. The pharmacist must verify that the prescribed starting dose is geriatric-appropriate (often half the standard adult dose) and must counsel the patient and caregiver on what to expect and what to report during titration. The corollary is "stop when the drug is no longer needed" — the regimen should be reviewed at each visit for drugs that can be reduced or discontinued, because accumulation over years is the source of much geriatric polypharmacy.

### Assess and Counsel on Fall Risk and Functional Impact

The functional impact of medications — particularly on falls, cognition, and continence — is the outcome that matters most to older adults, more than any lab value. The pharmacist must assess each regimen for fall risk (sedatives, antihypertensives causing orthostasis, anticholinergics, opioids, drugs causing dizziness or hypoglycemia) and must counsel the patient and caregiver on fall prevention (stand slowly, report dizziness, ensure adequate lighting and footwear, consider physical therapy). The pharmacist must counsel on the cognitive effects to watch for (confusion, sedation, delirium — which may be attributed to "aging" or "dementia" but is drug-induced and reversible) and on continence effects (diuretics timing, anticholinergic urinary retention). A medication that controls a lab value but causes a fall that breaks a hip has harmed the patient far more than the lab value ever would have, and the pharmacist must weigh the functional impact alongside the disease control in every geriatric regimen.

### Respect Scope and Escalation Boundaries

Know where the agent's authority and competence end. When the question requires a license, a specialist's judgment, a final approval, or expertise the agent does not hold, the correct action is to escalate rather than to produce a confident answer that overreaches. Scope discipline protects the recipient from harm caused by an unqualified conclusion and protects the agent from liability. State explicitly when the output is advisory and must be confirmed by the qualified person.

## Common Traps

### The "Normal" Serum Creatinine in an Older Adult

The pharmacist sees a normal-range serum creatinine in an 85-year-old and concludes renal function is normal, dosing renally cleared drugs at standard doses. The mechanism is that the serum creatinine is within the reference range, so it reads as normal. The false signal is that the lab value is normal, so the clearance must be normal. The harm is that the older adult's reduced muscle mass produces less creatinine, so a normal serum creatinine represents a significantly reduced glomerular filtration rate, and the standard dose accumulates, causing toxicity (gabapentin somnolence, hypoglycemia, anticoagulant bleeding). The pharmacist must estimate clearance with an equation that accounts for age and weight, not rely on the serum creatinine.

### The Benzodiazepine Continuation Default

An older adult has been on a benzodiazepine for years for sleep or anxiety, and the pharmacist refills it indefinitely because it is "chronic" and "working." The mechanism is that chronicity reads as established and stable. The false signal is that the drug has been tolerated, so it must be safe. The harm is that benzodiazepines increase fall and fracture risk, cognitive impairment, and dependence in older adults, and the risk grows with age and with the addition of other sedatives — the patient who "tolerated" the drug at 70 falls and breaks a hip at 85 because the cumulative CNS burden has crossed their threshold. The pharmacist must raise benzodiazepine deprescribing with the prescriber and patient, with a slow taper, rather than defaulting to continuation.

### Treating a Drug-Induced Symptom With Another Drug

An older adult on an anticholinergic drug develops constipation, and a laxative is added; the patient on a diuretic develops urinary frequency and incontinence, and an anticholinergic for overactive bladder is added (worsening constipation and cognition). The mechanism is that each new symptom is treated as a new problem rather than as a side effect of an existing drug. The false signal is that the symptom is a new condition requiring treatment. The harm is the prescribing cascade — each added drug adds its own adverse effects, which are then treated with more drugs, and the regimen spirals into polypharmacy with the original cause never addressed. The pharmacist must suspect a drug cause for every new symptom in an older adult and must recommend reducing or changing the offending drug before adding another.

### Beers Criteria Applied as Absolute Prohibition Without Context

The pharmacist refuses to fill or aggressively pushes to stop every Beers-flagged drug regardless of context. The mechanism is that the Beers Criteria is an authoritative list, so compliance feels like avoiding every flagged drug. The false signal is that the drug is flagged, so it must never be used. The harm is that some flagged drugs are genuinely the best choice in specific situations (a benzodiazepine for severe alcohol withdrawal, an antipsychotic for a patient with a psychotic disorder, an NSAID short-term when alternatives have failed), and reflexive refusal abandons the patient's legitimate need. The Beers Criteria signal that the drug is potentially inappropriate and that the decision must be deliberate, documented, and monitored — not that the drug is never acceptable.

### Disease-Centric Optimization Ignoring Function

The pharmacist optimizes the regimen for disease control (blood pressure at target, A1c at target) without considering the functional cost (orthostatic hypotension causing a fall, hypoglycemia causing confusion, polypharmacy burden reducing adherence). The mechanism is that the disease targets are the explicit goals. The false signal is that the numbers are at target, so the regimen is successful. The harm is that the older adult's quality of life and function decline — a hip fracture from a fall is far worse for an 85-year-old than a blood pressure of 145 — and the "successful" regimen has harmed the patient. The pharmacist must weigh functional impact alongside disease control, often accepting less aggressive targets in older adults to preserve function and avoid harm.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- Did I apply the Beers Criteria to the older adult's regimen as a routine screen, raising flagged drugs with the prescriber with a documented rationale for continuation or substitution?
- Did I estimate renal function with an equation accounting for age and weight (not relying on the serum creatinine alone), and adjust or recommend adjustment of renally cleared drugs?
- Did I identify and prioritize reduction of central nervous system and anticholinergic burden (benzodiazepines, Z-drugs, antipsychotics, opioids, first-generation antihistamines, tricyclics, antispasmodics), quantifying the cumulative load?
- Did I verify the starting dose is geriatric-appropriate (lower than standard adult, "start low, go slow") for psychoactive, cardiovascular, anticoagulant, and opioid drugs, and review for deprescribing at each visit?
- For every new symptom in an older adult, did I suspect a drug cause and recommend addressing the offending drug before adding another, to avoid the prescribing cascade?
- Did I weigh the functional impact (fall risk, cognition, continence) alongside disease control, accepting less aggressive targets where the functional cost exceeds the disease benefit, and counsel on fall prevention?
- Does the output stay within the agent's scope, deferring final authority, licensed judgment, or specialist sign-off to the qualified person where the question exceeds the agent's competence?
- Are assumptions, uncertainties, and confidence levels stated explicitly rather than buried in a confident-sounding conclusion?
