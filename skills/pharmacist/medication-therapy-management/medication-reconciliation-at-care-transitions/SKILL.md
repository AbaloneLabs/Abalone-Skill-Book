# Medication Reconciliation at Care Transitions

Care transitions — admission to hospital, transfer between units or facilities, discharge to home or skilled nursing — are the single most dangerous moments in a patient's medication journey, because that is when the medication list is rebuilt by people who did not build it the first time. A drug the patient took at home is omitted from the admission orders; a drug started in the hospital is unintentionally continued at discharge; a dose is changed in transit and no one notices; an anticoagulant is held for a procedure and never restarted. The pharmacist's role at the transition is to be the one person who compares the old list against the new list, drug by drug, and forces every discrepancy to a deliberate decision rather than an accidental drift. The danger is that reconciliation becomes a clerical exercise — copying the home list into the admission system, or copying the hospital list onto the discharge summary — without ever asking whether each difference is intentional, safe, and communicated.

## Core Rules

### Collect the Best Possible Medication History Before Comparing Anything

Reconciliation fails at the first step if the source list is wrong. The "home medication list" captured at admission is often incomplete or inaccurate because it is taken from the patient's memory, a single pharmacy, or an outdated chart. The pharmacist must collect the best possible medication history (BPMH) using at least two sources: a structured patient or caregiver interview, plus verification against the prescription refill record (ideally from a prescription drug monitoring program or the patient's community pharmacy), recent discharge summaries, and any clinic medication lists. Each drug must be captured with name, dose, route, frequency, indication, last dose taken, and adherence pattern. A reconciliation built on a flawed source list will faithfully reproduce the flaw — every comparison downstream will be wrong because the starting point was wrong. The investment in an accurate BPMH is the single highest-yield step in the entire process.

### Reconcile Drug by Drug and Classify Every Discrepancy Deliberately

Once the BPMH and the current orders exist, the pharmacist must compare them drug by drug and classify every discrepancy into one of five categories: (1) intentional addition — a new drug deliberately started for a new indication; (2) intentional discontinuation — a drug deliberately stopped; (3) unintentional omission — a home drug missing from the orders with no documented reason; (4) unintended duplication — two drugs in the same therapeutic class without intent; (5) dose, route, or frequency change — a difference that may or may not be intentional. Every discrepancy in categories three through five must be resolved to a deliberate decision: either the prescriber confirms the change is intended (and documents why), or the order is corrected. The pharmacist must not let a discrepancy pass simply because it is small or because the prescriber "probably meant it." Unresolved discrepancies are the direct cause of transition-related adverse events, and each one is a latent error waiting to reach the patient.

### Pay Special Attention to High-Risk Drug Classes at Every Transition

Certain drug classes cause disproportionate harm when mishandled at transitions, and the pharmacist must apply heightened scrutiny to each: anticoagulants (omitted, duplicated, or continued across a procedure without a bridge plan), insulin (basal-bolus regimens collapsed or sliding scales continued at discharge), opioids (duplicated across prescribers or continued long after the acute event), corticosteroids (abruptly stopped without a taper after a prolonged course, precipitating adrenal crisis), antiepileptics (held for nothing-by-mouth status without an IV conversion), immunosuppressants (transplant regimens disrupted by formulary substitution), and antibiotics (IV-to-oral conversions missed or duplicated). For each of these classes, the pharmacist must verify not just that the drug is present on the new list but that the dose, the duration, and the plan for continuation or discontinuation are deliberate and documented.

### Counsel the Patient on What Changed and What to Do at Home

Discharge reconciliation is incomplete without patient counseling, because the patient is the one who will execute the regimen at home. The pharmacist must provide a reconciled discharge medication list that clearly marks, for each drug, whether it is continued, changed, started, or stopped — and must verbally walk the patient through every change. The counseling must answer: which of my old medicines am I still taking, and at what dose; which new medicines am I starting, and why; which medicines am I stopping, and do I need to throw them away; what do I do about the medicine I was told to hold in the hospital. Patients who do not understand the changes frequently continue stopped drugs, double up on duplicated drugs, or abandon new drugs — each of which reproduces the very discrepancy the reconciliation was supposed to prevent.

### Close the Loop With the Receiving Care Team and Community Pharmacy

The reconciliation does not end at discharge. The pharmacist must ensure the reconciled list reaches the next care setting — the primary care provider, the skilled nursing facility, the home health agency — and, where possible, the community pharmacy that will fill the discharge prescriptions. The common failure is that the hospital reconciles internally, discharges the patient with a paper list, and the community pharmacy and primary care provider never receive the updated regimen, so the next refill cycle reproduces the pre-admission list and the in-hospital changes are lost. The pharmacist must transmit the reconciled list and the rationale for key changes to the receiving team, and must confirm receipt rather than assume transmission equals delivery.

## Common Traps

### The Copy-Forward Trap

At admission, the pharmacist or clerk copies the home medication list from the electronic record directly into the admission orders, and at discharge the hospital list is copied onto the discharge summary. The mechanism is that copying is fast and feels like reconciliation because the lists now match the source. The false signal is that matching documents mean a reconciled regimen. The harm is that copying reproduces every error in the source list — the drug the patient stopped taking months ago, the duplicate the patient never actually took — and introduces new errors when a held drug is copied forward as active or an active drug is dropped in the copy. Reconciliation requires comparison and deliberate decision-making, not transcription.

### The Held-Medication Amnesia Trap

During a hospitalization, medications are routinely held — anticoagulants before surgery, antihypertensives for low blood pressure, oral hypoglycemics for nothing-by-mouth status. The mechanism of harm is that "held" is documented as a temporary status, but at discharge the held drug is not restarted because no one distinguishes "held for the procedure" from "discontinued." The false signal is that the drug's absence from the active order set reads as intentional. The harm is that the patient goes home without their anticoagulant, their antiepileptic, or their immunosuppressant, and the consequence — a stroke, a seizure, a graft rejection — appears days later with no obvious link to the transition. The pharmacist must explicitly resolve every held medication at discharge: restart, discontinue with rationale, or change.

### The IV-to-Oral Conversion Gap

A patient is on IV antibiotics or IV proton pump inhibitors in the hospital, and at discharge the oral equivalent is either omitted, duplicated (both IV and oral ordered), or dosed incorrectly. The mechanism is that the route change is treated as a formality rather than a clinical decision requiring dose recalculation and explicit ordering. The false signal is that "same drug, different route" feels equivalent. The harm is that bioavailability differs dramatically by route (oral absorption of some drugs is a fraction of IV), so a direct copy produces a subtherapeutic oral dose, or the IV drug is continued at discharge when an oral equivalent was intended, or the patient is sent home with no antibiotic at all because the IV was stopped and no oral was started.

### Discharge Counseling That Lists Without Explaining

The pharmacist hands the patient a printed discharge medication list, reads the drug names aloud, and considers the counseling complete. The mechanism is that handing over the document feels like communicating the information. The false signal is that the patient received the list, so they must understand it. The harm is that a list of fifteen drugs with no indication of what changed is incomprehensible to most patients, who then default to their pre-admission routine — continuing stopped drugs, missing new drugs, or doubling up. Effective counseling requires explicitly marking and verbally explaining each change, not delivering a static inventory.

### Assuming the Community Pharmacy Will Figure It Out

The hospital pharmacist reconciles, discharges the patient, and assumes the community pharmacy will sort out any discrepancies when the discharge prescriptions arrive. The mechanism is that the downstream pharmacy is treated as a safety net. The false signal is that the prescriptions are "in the system." The harm is that the community pharmacy sees only the new prescriptions, not the reconciled list or the rationale, so it cannot distinguish an intentional discontinuation from an omission — and a conscientious pharmacist may call the prescriber to "fix" a deliberate stop, or worse, fill a duplicate alongside the patient's remaining home supply. The reconciliation must be transmitted with context, not just as a stream of new prescriptions.

## Self-Check

- Did I collect the best possible medication history from at least two sources, including a structured patient interview, before attempting any comparison?
- Did I compare the lists drug by drug and classify every discrepancy (intentional add, intentional stop, unintentional omission, duplication, dose/route/frequency change), resolving each category-three-through-five discrepancy to a deliberate decision?
- For each high-risk class (anticoagulants, insulin, opioids, corticosteroids, antiepileptics, immunosuppressants, antibiotics), did I verify the dose, duration, and continuation plan are deliberate and documented?
- Did I provide the patient with a reconciled discharge list that explicitly marks each drug as continued, changed, started, or stopped, and did I verbally walk through every change?
- Did I transmit the reconciled list and the rationale for key changes to the receiving care team (primary care, SNF, home health) and the community pharmacy, and did I confirm receipt?
- For every medication held during the stay, did I explicitly resolve its status at discharge (restart, discontinue with rationale, or change) rather than letting its absence pass as intentional?
