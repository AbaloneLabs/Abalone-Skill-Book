# Comprehensive Medication Review

A comprehensive medication review (CMR) is the centerpiece of medication therapy management, and it is fundamentally different from dispensing verification. Dispensing asks "is this one prescription correct?" A CMR asks "is this patient's entire medication regimen — every prescription drug, every over-the-counter product, every supplement, every as-needed medicine — appropriate, effective, safe, and adhered to, taken together?" The danger is that a pharmacist under time pressure reduces the CMR to a refill check or a box-ticking exercise: confirm the drugs are on file, note no new interactions, and close the case. When that happens, the medication-related problems the CMR exists to find — untreated conditions, redundant therapy, doses wrong for the patient's current organ function, drugs continued years past their indication, adherence failures invisible to the refill record — remain undiscovered, and the patient leaves with the same unsafe regimen they arrived with.

## Core Rules

### Build the Personal Medication Record From the Patient, Not the Pharmacy System

The single most important step in a CMR is obtaining an accurate, complete medication list, and the pharmacy dispensing record is never sufficient. The dispensing record captures what was filled at this pharmacy; it misses drugs filled at other pharmacies, samples from clinics, over-the-counter products, herbal and dietary supplements, and as-needed medications the patient takes irregularly. The pharmacist must ask the patient (or caregiver) to bring every bottle, blister pack, and box to the review, and must conduct a structured interview that probes each medication by name, dose, frequency, indication, and the patient's actual taking behavior. The gap between "what is prescribed" and "what the patient actually takes" is often enormous, and that gap is precisely where most medication-related problems live. A reconciliation that trusts the record over the patient's report will systematically miss adherence problems, double-therapy from multiple prescribers, and abandoned treatments.

### Identify Medication-Related Problems Using a Structured Taxonomy

A CMR is only as good as the framework used to find problems. The pharmacist should work through a structured taxonomy of medication-related problems rather than scanning the list impressionistically: (1) untreated conditions that have no drug but need one; (2) unnecessary therapy where a drug has no current indication or duplicates another; (3) wrong drug where a safer or more effective agent exists for the indication; (4) subtherapeutic dose where the dose is too low to achieve the goal; (5) supratherapeutic dose where the dose is too high for the patient's organ function or body size; (6) adverse drug reactions the patient is experiencing but has not reported; (7) interactions — drug-drug, drug-disease, drug-food — that compromise safety or efficacy; and (8) non-adherence, intentional or unintentional. Walking the list against this taxonomy forces the pharmacist to consider failure modes they would otherwise skip. An impressionistic review finds the obvious duplication; a structured review finds the untreated condition and the silent adherence failure.

### Contextualize Every Drug Against the Patient's Clinical Goals and Parameters

Each medication must be judged not in isolation but against the patient's current clinical state: renal function (creatinine clearance), hepatic function, current labs relevant to the drug (INR for warfarin, A1c for diabetes, potassium for diuretics and ACE inhibitors), current vitals, and the documented therapeutic goal. A drug that was appropriate when prescribed may be inappropriate now because the patient's function has changed. A statin started five years ago may still be at its starting dose despite an LDL that never reached goal. A diuretic may still be at full dose in a patient whose blood pressure is now normal and whose potassium is low. The pharmacist must request and review the relevant labs and parameters — contacting the prescriber or patient to obtain them — rather than reviewing drugs against stale or absent data. A CMR performed without current clinical data is a documentation exercise, not a clinical service.

### Distinguish Problems the Pharmacist Can Resolve From Those Requiring Prescriber Action

The pharmacist must sort identified problems into two categories: those within scope to resolve directly (counseling to improve adherence, recommending an over-the-counter change, adjusting a dose under a collaborative practice agreement, discontinuing a clearly duplicate as-needed product the patient no longer uses) and those requiring prescriber involvement (changing a prescription drug, adding therapy for an untreated condition, ordering labs). For the latter, the pharmacist must communicate the recommendation to the prescriber in writing with the specific problem, the evidence, the recommended change, and a request for a response — not a vague "please review." The pharmacist must not unilaterally change prescription therapy outside their authorized scope; doing so is a scope-of-practice violation and a patient-safety risk. The CMR's value comes from the recommendations that reach the prescriber and result in change, not from the documentation alone.

### Document the Review, the Findings, and the Follow-Up Plan

A CMR that is not documented did not happen, from both a clinical and a billing standpoint. The pharmacist must produce a personal medication record (the reconciled list with indication for each drug), a medication action plan (the prioritized list of problems and recommended actions, in patient-friendly language), and a record of the recommendations communicated to each prescriber. The documentation must also specify the follow-up: what will be checked, by whom, and when. A CMR without a defined follow-up is an event, not a service; the chronic-disease medications the review is meant to optimize require ongoing monitoring, and the value of the review is realized only when the follow-up confirms the recommended changes were made and achieved their goal.

## Common Traps

### The Record-Equals-Reality Trap

The pharmacist opens the patient's profile, sees a clean list of refilled prescriptions, and treats the dispensing record as the patient's actual regimen. The mechanism is that the record feels authoritative — it has dates, doses, and prescriber names — so the pharmacist does not probe beyond it. The false signal is completeness: a list of ten refilled drugs looks like a complete picture. The harm is that the record silently omits the second pharmacy filling the patient's opioids, the over-the-counter NSAID the patient takes daily for back pain (creating a bleeding risk with the prescribed anticoagulant), and the herbal supplement that induces the metabolism of the patient's antiepileptic. The CMR that trusts the record will never find these, and the patient's adverse event will be attributed to bad luck rather than to a review that never actually happened.

### Impressionistic Scanning Instead of Structured Problem-Finding

Under time pressure, the pharmacist scans the medication list looking for anything that "stands out," relying on pattern recognition to surface problems. The mechanism is that pattern recognition is fast and feels sufficient because it does surface some problems — an obvious duplicate, a known interaction. The false signal is that finding one or two problems feels like the review worked. The harm is that the impressionistic scan systematically misses the failure modes that do not look familiar: the untreated condition (no drug to flag), the subtherapeutic dose (the drug is present and "correct" but ineffective), the adherence failure invisible to a refill record. A structured taxonomy forces the pharmacist to ask, for every drug and every gap, "is there a problem here?" — and that is the only reliable way to find the problems that do not announce themselves.

### The "Continue Because It's Chronic" Assumption

When a drug has been on the list for years, the pharmacist assumes it must still be needed. The mechanism is that chronicity feels like evidence of appropriateness — if the drug were wrong, someone would have stopped it by now. The false signal is that the drug's long presence reads as endorsement. The harm is that indications resolve and circumstances change: the patient's gastric ulcer healed years ago but the proton pump inhibitor continues, causing magnesium deficiency and fracture risk; the patient's gout is controlled but the colchicine continues at full dose; the patient's depression remitted but the SSRI continues and now causes hyponatremia in an older adult. The CMR must treat every chronic drug as a candidate for reassessment, asking whether the indication still exists and whether the dose still matches the patient's current state.

### Recommending Without Communicating to the Prescriber

The pharmacist identifies a problem and documents the recommendation in the pharmacy record or the patient's action plan, then considers the problem addressed. The mechanism is that writing the recommendation down feels like completing the task. The false signal is that the documented recommendation reads as action taken. The harm is that the recommendation never reaches the prescriber who has the authority to act on it, so the regimen does not change, and the patient's problem persists. A recommendation that is not transmitted to and acknowledged by the prescriber has zero clinical effect; the CMR's value is realized only when the recommendation changes the prescription.

### Ignoring Non-Adherence Because Refills Look Current

The refill record shows the patient picking up medications on schedule, and the pharmacist concludes the patient is adherent. The mechanism is that refill timing is the easiest adherence proxy and it usually correlates with taking behavior. The false signal is that timely refills equal actual ingestion. The harm is that many patients refill faithfully and do not take the medication — they stockpile it, they take it inconsistently, they take it wrong (crushing an extended-release tablet, taking two when they miss a dose). The CMR must ask the patient directly how they take each medication, because the refill record cannot distinguish a full bottle in the cabinet from a drug that is controlling the disease.

## Self-Check

- Did I build the medication list from the patient's actual bottles and a structured interview, or did I rely on the dispensing record and assume it was complete?
- Did I work through a structured taxonomy of medication-related problems (untreated, unnecessary, wrong drug, subtherapeutic, supratherapeutic, adverse reaction, interaction, non-adherence), or did I scan the list impressionistically?
- Did I obtain and review the current clinical data (renal function, relevant labs, vitals, documented goals) needed to judge each drug, or did I review drugs against stale or absent data?
- Did I separate problems I can resolve within scope from those requiring prescriber action, and did I transmit the latter to the prescriber with a specific, evidence-based recommendation and a request for response?
- Did I produce a personal medication record, a patient-friendly medication action plan, and a documented follow-up plan specifying what is checked, by whom, and when?
- For every chronic drug on the list, did I ask whether the indication still exists and whether the dose still matches the patient's current organ function and clinical state?
