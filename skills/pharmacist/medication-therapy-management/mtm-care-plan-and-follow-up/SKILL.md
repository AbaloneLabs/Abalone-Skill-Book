# MTM Care Plan and Follow-Up

Identifying medication-related problems is only the first half of medication therapy management; the second half — the half that actually changes patient outcomes — is converting those findings into a prioritized care plan with measurable goals and a follow-up that confirms the plan worked. The danger is that the pharmacist treats the care plan as paperwork produced at the end of the review and filed away, rather than as a living instrument that drives action by the patient, the prescriber, and the pharmacist over time. A care plan with no goals cannot be evaluated, a care plan with no prioritization overwhelms everyone and accomplishes nothing, and a care plan with no follow-up is a one-time event that decays back to the status quo within weeks. The pharmacist's discipline is to make the plan specific, prioritized, owned, and followed up — because that is what separates an MTM service from a chart note.

## Core Rules

### Prioritize Problems by Clinical Risk and Likelihood of Action

A typical comprehensive review surfaces five to fifteen medication-related problems, and attempting to address all of them simultaneously guarantees that none are addressed well. The pharmacist must prioritize using two axes: clinical risk (the probability and severity of harm if the problem is not addressed) and actionability (the likelihood that an intervention will actually change the outcome). High-risk, high-actionability problems come first — a supratherapeutic anticoagulant dose in a patient with falling renal function, or a duplicated opioid regimen across two prescribers. Low-risk problems with low actionability — a minor interaction manageable with monitoring, or a supplement of uncertain value the patient is attached to — may be noted but deferred. The pharmacist must communicate the prioritization to the patient and the prescriber, so that the limited attention available is directed at the problems most likely to cause harm and most likely to be resolved. A plan that lists every problem with equal weight invites the prescriber to address none of them.

### Set Goals That Are Specific, Measurable, and Patient-Centered

Each problem in the care plan must have a goal that is specific enough to evaluate at follow-up. "Improve blood pressure control" is not a goal; "reduce systolic blood pressure to below 130 mmHg within eight weeks, measured at home twice weekly" is. Goals must be measurable using a parameter the team can actually obtain — a lab value (A1c, INR, LDL), a vital sign (blood pressure, weight), a patient-reported outcome (pain score, symptom frequency), or a behavior (adherence measured by refill synchronization or dose-tracking). Goals must also be patient-centered: the patient must understand why the goal matters and agree it is worth pursuing, because a goal the patient does not own will not be achieved. The pharmacist should negotiate goals with the patient when the prescriber-set target conflicts with the patient's priorities or capacity — an elderly patient may reasonably prioritize fall-risk reduction over aggressive blood-pressure targets, and the care plan must reflect that tradeoff explicitly rather than imposing a goal the patient will not pursue.

### Assign Each Action an Owner and a Deadline

Every action in the care plan must have a named owner — the patient, the prescriber, or the pharmacist — and a deadline by which the action should be completed or re-evaluated. Ambiguity about ownership is the most common reason care plans fail: the recommendation is documented, everyone assumes someone else will act, and nothing happens. The pharmacist must specify, for each item, who will do what and by when: the prescriber will adjust the dose and reorder labs within one week; the patient will begin home blood-pressure monitoring and bring readings to the next visit; the pharmacist will follow up by phone in two weeks to confirm the change was made and check for adverse effects. The pharmacist must not assign actions to the prescriber without communicating them — a care plan that lists "prescriber to discontinue duplicate" without the prescriber's knowledge is a wish, not a plan.

### Schedule Follow-Up Before the Patient Leaves the Review

Follow-up must be scheduled at the time of the review, not deferred to "when something comes up." The interval depends on the risk and the parameter being monitored: a dose change in a narrow-therapeutic-index drug (warfarin, digoxin) requires follow-up in days to a week; a chronic-disease titration (antihypertensive, oral hypoglycemic) requires follow-up in four to eight weeks to allow the dose to reach effect; an adherence intervention requires earlier follow-up (two to four weeks) to catch relapse. The follow-up must specify what will be checked (the lab, the vital, the adherence metric, the symptom), who will check it, and what action will be taken if the goal is not met. A care plan without a scheduled follow-up is an open loop, and open loops in chronic disease management close themselves by reverting to the prior state.

### Document and Close the Loop on Each Recommendation

For every recommendation transmitted to a prescriber, the pharmacist must document the recommendation, the date sent, the prescriber's response (or non-response), and the resulting change (or lack of change). Recommendations that go unanswered must be escalated — a second contact, a different channel, or escalation to the patient — rather than abandoned. A recommendation that is sent but never confirmed acted upon is an unresolved problem, and the pharmacist's responsibility does not end at transmission. At follow-up, the pharmacist must close the loop: confirm whether the change was made, whether the goal was achieved, whether adverse effects emerged, and whether further action is needed. The care plan is a cycle (assess, plan, act, follow up, reassess), not a line that ends at the recommendation.

### Respect Scope and Escalation Boundaries

Know where the agent's authority and competence end. When the question requires a license, a specialist's judgment, a final approval, or expertise the agent does not hold, the correct action is to escalate rather than to produce a confident answer that overreaches. Scope discipline protects the recipient from harm caused by an unqualified conclusion and protects the agent from liability. State explicitly when the output is advisory and must be confirmed by the qualified person.

## Common Traps

### The Equal-Weight Plan That Addresses Nothing

The pharmacist lists every identified problem with equal emphasis in the care plan and sends it to the prescriber. The mechanism is that comprehensive listing feels thorough and complete. The false signal is that a complete list reads as a complete plan. The harm is that a prescriber receiving a list of twelve unprioritized recommendations cannot triage them under time pressure, so they address the easiest one or two and defer the rest, and the highest-risk problems — which required the most attention — receive none. Prioritization is not optional formatting; it is the mechanism by which the limited attention of the care team is directed at the problems most likely to cause harm.

### Vague Goals That Cannot Be Evaluated

The care plan sets goals like "optimize therapy," "improve adherence," or "monitor for side effects." The mechanism is that vague goals feel aspirational and are easy to write. The false signal is that the goal exists on paper, so the plan must be complete. The harm is that a goal that cannot be measured cannot be evaluated at follow-up, so the pharmacist has no way to determine whether the intervention worked — and the follow-up becomes a subjective impression rather than a clinical assessment. Every goal must specify a parameter, a target, and a timeframe, or it is decoration rather than a plan.

### Ownership Diffusion

The care plan states that "the team will address the duplicate therapy," with no named individual responsible. The mechanism is that collective language feels collaborative and avoids singling anyone out. The false signal is that assigning responsibility to "the team" feels like assigning it to everyone. The harm is the well-documented bystander effect: when everyone is responsible, no one is, and the action falls through the gap between the pharmacist, the prescriber, and the patient. Every action must have a single named owner, or it will not happen.

### Follow-Up Deferred Indefinitely

The pharmacist completes the review, transmits the recommendations, and intends to follow up "soon." The mechanism is that the immediate task (the review) is done, so the follow-up feels like a future task that can be scheduled later. The false signal is that the intent to follow up is recorded, so it will happen. The harm is that without a concrete scheduled date, the follow-up is displaced by the next urgent task and never occurs, so the pharmacist never learns whether the recommendation was adopted or whether it achieved the goal — and the care plan's effect on the patient's outcome is never known. Follow-up must be scheduled at the time of the review, with a specific date and a specific parameter to check.

### Treating Transmission as Completion

The pharmacist sends the recommendation to the prescriber and marks the problem as addressed in the care plan. The mechanism is that sending the message feels like completing the action. The false signal is that the recommendation is "in the system," so it must be on its way to resolution. The harm is that transmission is not adoption — the prescriber may not read it, may disagree, may intend to act and forget — and a recommendation that is sent but not confirmed acted upon leaves the problem unresolved while creating the illusion that it is handled. The pharmacist must close the loop by confirming the prescriber's response and the resulting change, not by treating the send as the finish line.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- Did I prioritize the identified problems by clinical risk and actionability, and did I communicate the prioritization so that attention is directed at the highest-value problems first?
- Is every goal in the care plan specific, measurable (with a defined parameter, target, and timeframe), and patient-centered, or did I write vague aspirations that cannot be evaluated?
- Does every action in the plan have a single named owner (patient, prescriber, or pharmacist) and a deadline, or did I assign actions to "the team" with no individual accountability?
- Did I schedule a concrete follow-up (with a date, a parameter to check, and an action if the goal is unmet) before the patient left the review, or did I defer follow-up to an unspecified "later"?
- For every recommendation sent to a prescriber, did I document the send, seek the response, and close the loop at follow-up — or did I treat transmission as completion?
- At follow-up, did I confirm whether the change was made, whether the goal was achieved, whether adverse effects emerged, and whether further action is needed, treating the care plan as a cycle rather than a one-time event?
- Is the reasoning documented clearly enough that another practitioner could review the basis and reproduce the conclusion?
- Are assumptions, uncertainties, and confidence levels stated explicitly rather than buried in a confident-sounding conclusion?
