# Dose Adjustment in Renal and Hepatic Impairment

Dose adjustment for organ impairment is the point where pharmacokinetics meets the individual patient. A drug that is safe and effective at its standard dose in a healthy adult can accumulate to toxicity or fail to reach efficacy in a patient whose kidneys or liver cannot clear or metabolize it. The danger is that the pharmacist treats dose adjustment as a lookup exercise — find the creatinine clearance, find the drug in a renal dosing table, apply the reduction — without engaging the factors that make the table's recommendation wrong for this patient: the lag between serum creatinine and actual renal function, the difference between clearance modalities, the unpredictable pharmacokinetics of hepatic impairment, and the clinical consequence of underdosing a critical drug. When dose adjustment becomes mechanical, the patient is either poisoned by accumulation or undertreated by excessive caution.

## Core Rules

### Calculate Renal Function From the Right Equation and the Right Input

Renal dose adjustment begins with an estimate of creatinine clearance or estimated glomerular filtration rate, and the choice of equation and input values determines whether the estimate is usable. Cockcroft-Gault remains the standard for drug dosing because most drug studies and FDA labels used it, but it requires actual body weight, and the choice between actual, ideal, and adjusted body weight matters enormously for obese patients — using actual weight overestimates clearance in the obese, using ideal weight underestimates it. For patients with very low muscle mass — frail elderly, amputees, paraplegics, cirrhosis with sarcopenia — serum creatinine underestimates renal impairment because less creatinine is produced, and the calculated clearance looks better than the actual kidney function. The pharmacist must recognize when the calculated clearance is falsely reassuring and seek additional evidence: a rising creatinine trend, urine output, cystatin C, or the clinical picture of uremia.

### Treat Rapidly Changing Renal Function as a Moving Target

A single creatinine clearance value is a snapshot. In acute kidney injury, the patient's clearance is falling; in recovering AKI, it is rising. A dose adjusted for today's clearance will be wrong tomorrow. The pharmacist must distinguish stable chronic kidney disease (where a calculated clearance supports a durable regimen) from acute or fluctuating renal function (where the regimen must be reviewed daily and the dose must track the trend). The specific trap is the patient whose creatinine is rising but still within the "normal" range — the absolute value looks acceptable, but the trend shows a kidney that is failing, and a dose calculated from the current value will accumulate as clearance falls. The pharmacist must plan for the trajectory, not just the current value, and must set triggers for reassessment: the next creatinine, the next urine output, the next dialysis decision.

### Dialysis and Renal Replacement Therapy Change the Dosing Logic Entirely

The pharmacist must not apply chronic kidney disease dosing to a patient on dialysis or continuous renal replacement therapy (CRRT). Hemodialysis removes some drugs efficiently and others not at all; the pharmacist must know the drug's dialysis clearance, whether supplemental post-dialysis dosing is needed, and whether the drug is removed by the specific dialyzer membrane in use. CRRT (CVVH, CVVHD, CVVHDF) provides continuous clearance that often exceeds the clearance of intermittent hemodialysis, and standard "dialysis" dosing underdoses the patient. The CRRT prescription — the effluent rate, the filter type, whether the circuit is running or paused — determines the drug clearance, and the pharmacist must dose to the actual delivered clearance, which changes when the circuit clots and is paused. Sustained low-efficiency dialysis (SLED) has its own clearance profile that falls between intermittent and continuous. The pharmacist who applies a single "renal impairment" dose to all of these modalities will get the dose wrong for most of them.

### Hepatic Impairment Requires Judgment, Not a Table

Unlike renal impairment, there is no single quantitative measure of hepatic function that drives dose adjustment. The Child-Pugh score is a crude categorization that does not predict the pharmacokinetics of individual drugs, and many hepatically metabolized drugs have no specific dosing guidance for cirrhosis. The pharmacist must reason from the drug's metabolism pathway: a high-extraction drug (morphine, propranolol) is affected by reduced liver blood flow, so the dose must be lower in conditions that reduce flow (heart failure, portal hypertension); a low-extraction drug (diazepam, theophylline) is affected by reduced enzyme capacity, so the dose must be lower in parenchymal disease. Albumin and coagulation reflect synthetic function but not necessarily metabolic capacity. The pharmacist must combine the score, the pathway, the clinical picture (encephalopathy, ascites, coagulopathy), and the drug's therapeutic index to arrive at a dose, and must monitor clinically for accumulation because no level will predict it reliably.

### Distinguish "Reduce the Dose" From "Extend the Interval"

Renal dose adjustment can be achieved by reducing each dose, extending the interval between doses, or both — and the choice matters for efficacy and toxicity. Beta-lactam antibiotics require time above the minimum inhibitory concentration (T greater than MIC), so reducing the dose while maintaining the interval may drop the concentration below the efficacy threshold; extending the interval while maintaining the dose (or using a prolonged infusion) preserves the time-above-MIC. Aminoglycosides require a high peak, so reducing the dose sacrifices efficacy while extending the interval preserves the peak and reduces toxicity. Vancomycin requires total AUC, so the adjustment must preserve area, not just hit a trough. The pharmacist must understand the pharmacodynamic target of the drug and adjust in the direction that preserves that target, not apply a uniform "give half the dose" reduction that works for some drugs and fails for others.

## Common Traps

### The Falsely Normal Creatinine in a Frail or Cirrhotic Patient

An 82-year-old woman with sarcopenia has a serum creatinine of 0.9 mg/dL, and the Cockcroft-Gault equation returns a clearance of 55 mL/min — a dose reduction may not be triggered. The mechanism of the trap is that the equation assumes a normal muscle mass producing creatinine, and the patient's low muscle mass produces less creatinine, so the same serum concentration represents worse clearance than the equation reports. The harm is that a renally cleared drug is dosed at near-standard levels in a patient whose actual clearance is 25 mL/min, the drug accumulates, and toxicity develops in a patient too frail to tolerate it. The false signal is that a creatinine within the lab's reference range means the kidneys are fine, when in fact the reference range assumes a muscle mass the patient does not have. Cirrhosis with sarcopenia produces the same trap, and cystatin C may reveal the true clearance that creatinine conceals.

### Applying Chronic Kidney Disease Dosinging to a CRRT Patient

A patient in the ICU on CVVHDF is prescribed an antibiotic. The pharmacist looks up the renal dosing recommendation, finds "CrCl less than 30: give every 48 hours," and applies it. The mechanism of the trap is that the dosing reference assumes intermittent hemodialysis three times a week, not continuous clearance at 25 to 35 mL/min. CRRT often delivers more clearance than the reference anticipated, so the every-48-hour dose produces subtherapeutic levels and treatment failure in a critically ill patient whose outcome depends on adequate exposure. The harm is that the infection worsens, the patient deteriorates, and the cause — a dosing reference applied to the wrong modality — is identified only when the team finally checks a level. The false signal is that "renal impairment" is a single category with a single dose, when in fact the clearance delivered by CRRT, intermittent dialysis, and SLED differ enough to change the regimen for most drugs.

### Hepatic Dosing Treated as Renal Dosing With a Different Table

A patient with Child-Pugh C cirrhosis is prescribed a hepatically metabolized drug. The pharmacist looks for a Child-Pugh-based dosing recommendation, finds none, and either applies the standard dose (on the assumption that no guidance means no adjustment) or applies an arbitrary reduction. The mechanism of the trap is that the pharmacist expects the same structure as renal dosing — a score, a table, a recommendation — and when that structure does not exist, the pharmacist defaults to no adjustment rather than to clinical judgment. The harm is that a high-extraction drug at standard dose accumulates in a patient with portosystemic shunting and reduced first-pass metabolism, producing excessive sedation, hypotension, or respiratory depression in a patient who is already encephalopathic. The false signal is that the absence of a published adjustment means no adjustment is needed, when in fact the absence reflects the difficulty of quantifying hepatic function, not the safety of standard dosing.

### Reducing the Dose When Extending the Interval Would Preserve Efficacy

A patient with a creatinine clearance of 30 mL/min is prescribed piperacillin-tazobactam. The pharmacist reduces the dose from 4.5 g every 8 hours to 2.25 g every 8 hours, preserving the interval. The mechanism of the trap is that the dose reduction lowers the concentration, and because beta-lactams require time above the MIC, the reduced dose may spend too little time above the threshold, producing treatment failure in a serious infection. The correct adjustment is often to extend the interval (every 12 hours) or to use a prolonged infusion that maximizes time above MIC at a lower peak, preserving the pharmacodynamic target. The harm is that the patient receives a dose that is both less toxic and less effective, and the infection progresses because the adjustment optimized for safety in a way that sacrificed the efficacy that was the reason for the drug. The false signal is that any dose reduction is a safe adjustment, when in fact the direction of the adjustment must follow the drug's pharmacodynamic target.

## Self-Check

- Did I use the correct weight (actual, ideal, or adjusted) in the Cockcroft-Gault equation, and did I consider whether the patient's muscle mass makes the calculated clearance falsely reassuring?
- Did I distinguish stable chronic kidney disease from acute or fluctuating renal function, and did I set triggers for reassessment based on the trend rather than a single value?
- Did I dose to the actual delivered clearance of the patient's renal replacement therapy modality (intermittent hemodialysis, CRRT, SLED), or did I apply a generic "renal impairment" dose?
- For hepatically metabolized drugs, did I reason from the extraction ratio and metabolic pathway, or did I default to no adjustment because no Child-Pugh-based table exists?
- Did I adjust in the direction (dose reduction versus interval extension) that preserves the drug's pharmacodynamic target — time above MIC, peak/MIC, or AUC — or did I apply a uniform reduction that may sacrifice efficacy?
- Did I consider whether the drug requires a loading dose despite renal or hepatic impairment, so that the patient reaches effective exposure quickly rather than waiting for the reduced maintenance dose to accumulate?
