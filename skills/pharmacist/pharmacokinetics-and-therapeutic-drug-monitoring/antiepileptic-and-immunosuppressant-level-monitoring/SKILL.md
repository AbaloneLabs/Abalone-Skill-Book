# Antiepileptic and Immunosuppressant Level Monitoring

Antiepileptic drugs (AEDs) and immunosuppressants are monitored because their efficacy and toxicity both track closely with blood concentration, but the relationship between dose and concentration is distorted by factors that are easy to overlook: protein binding that shifts dramatically in critical illness, drug interactions that double or halve levels overnight, and genetic polymorphisms that make two patients on the same dose have wildly different exposure. The danger is that the pharmacist treats the therapeutic range as a fixed window and the level as a clean number, when in fact the range is a population average and the number is a snapshot distorted by the patient's binding proteins, their interacting medications, and the time of the draw. When monitoring becomes mechanical, the pharmacist either over-treats a falsely low level or under-treats a falsely normal one, and the patient either seizes or rejects the organ.

## Core Rules

### Interpret Phenytoin Through Free Concentration, Not Total Concentration

Phenytoin is highly protein-bound, and only the free (unbound) fraction is pharmacologically active. In patients with low albumin — common in critical illness, malnutrition, hepatic failure, and pregnancy — the total phenytoin level underestimates the free concentration, and a "therapeutic" total level may represent a toxic free level. The pharmacist must correct the total level for albumin using a validated equation (Sheiner-Tozer) or, preferably, order a free phenytoin level directly when the patient's albumin is low or their clinical picture does not match the total level. Uremia further displaces phenytoin from binding proteins, so a patient with renal failure needs a free level even if their albumin is normal. The pharmacist who adjusts a phenytoin dose based on an uncorrected total level in a hypoalbuminemic patient is, in effect, treating a number that does not represent the drug the patient's brain is actually exposed to.

### Re-evaluate the Regimen Every Time an Interaction Is Introduced or Removed

AEDs and immunosuppressants are among the most interaction-prone drugs in pharmacotherapy. Tacrolimus levels are doubled or halved by the addition or removal of a CYP3A4 inhibitor or inducer — a single dose of clarithromycin, a course of fluconazole, the addition of phenytoin, or the patient starting St. John's wort can move a transplant patient from therapeutic to toxic or subtherapeutic within days. The pharmacist must treat every new medication added to or removed from a patient on TDM as a trigger for rechecking the level, not as a routine interaction alert to be dismissed. The same applies in reverse: when an interacting drug is stopped, the level that was therapeutic on the combination will change, and the patient may suddenly be underexposed. The pharmacist must anticipate the direction and magnitude of the change and schedule the recheck before toxicity or graft rejection becomes clinically apparent.

### Time the Draw to the Drug's Pharmacokinetic Profile

Each monitored drug has a specific sampling requirement that reflects its half-life and absorption. Tacrolimus troughs must be drawn immediately before the next dose; a level drawn even an hour after the dose reflects a different point on the curve and cannot be interpreted against the reference range. Carbamazepine induces its own metabolism, so a level drawn in the first week of therapy will be higher than a level drawn at steady state three weeks later, even on the same dose — the pharmacist must not interpret an early level as the final steady-state value. Valproate's nonlinear protein binding means that as the dose increases, the free fraction increases disproportionately, so a total level in the "therapeutic" range at a high dose may represent a toxic free concentration. The pharmacist must know which drug requires a trough, which requires a peak, which requires a level at a specific post-dose interval, and which requires free-level monitoring — and must verify that the draw met that requirement before interpreting the result.

### Connect the Level to the Clinical Endpoint, Not Just the Reference Range

The therapeutic range is a population-derived window, not a guarantee of efficacy or safety for the individual patient. A seizure patient whose seizures are controlled at a phenytoin level below the reference range does not necessarily need a dose increase; a transplant patient whose graft is stable at a tacrolimus level at the low end of the range does not necessarily need escalation. The pharmacist must integrate the level with the clinical picture: seizure frequency, graft function, signs of toxicity (ataxia, nystagmus, tremor, nephrotoxicity). When the level is in range but the clinical endpoint is not met, the pharmacist must question whether the level is valid, whether the assay is measuring the right thing, or whether the patient's individual therapeutic window differs from the population average. Conversely, when the clinical endpoint is met but the level is outside the range, the pharmacist must consider whether the dose can be maintained with closer monitoring rather than reflexively adjusting toward the range.

### Recognize the Special Demands of the Transplant and Pregnancy Contexts

Transplant immunosuppression operates under constraints that change the meaning of the level. In the immediate post-transplant period, the target range is higher (to prevent acute rejection) and narrows over months (to reduce chronic nephrotoxicity and infection risk). The pharmacist must know where the patient is in the transplant timeline and apply the range appropriate to that phase, not a single static target. Pregnancy dramatically alters AED pharmacokinetics: increased volume of distribution, increased clearance, and decreased protein binding all lower the total level, and a pregnant patient whose seizures were controlled may break through on the same dose because the free concentration has fallen. The pharmacist must monitor free levels in pregnancy and anticipate the need for dose increases as pregnancy progresses, then reductions postpartum as clearance normalizes — failure to reduce postpartum produces toxicity.

### Document the Basis and the Reasoning

Every conclusion should be traceable to its evidence, assumptions, and alternatives considered. Record not only the outcome but the reasoning path: what was checked, what was ruled out, what uncertainty remains, and what would change the conclusion. Documentation that captures the basis allows another practitioner to review, reproduce, or challenge the work, and it prevents confident conclusions from becoming unrepeatable assertions. A decision made without a recorded basis cannot be audited, improved, or safely handed off.

## Common Traps

### Treating the Total Level as the Active Drug in a Hypoalbuminemic Patient

A patient in the ICU has an albumin of 2.0 g/dL and a total phenytoin level of 12 mg/L — within the reference range. The pharmacist, seeing a number in range, recommends no change. The mechanism of the trap is that the total level is the number reported, the number is in range, and the cognitive effort of correcting for albumin feels like unnecessary complication. The harm is that the corrected free concentration is in the toxic range, the patient develops nystagmus and ataxia, and the cause — a falsely reassuring total level — is never identified until the free level is finally drawn. The false signal is that a value reported with a reference range is already adjusted for the patient, when in fact the reference range assumes normal protein binding that the patient does not have.

### Missing the Interaction Because It Was Documented Days Ago

A transplant patient on stable tacrolimus develops pneumonia and is started on clarithromycin. The interaction is flagged in the electronic medical record, the pharmacist acknowledges it, and the team proceeds. Three days later the patient's creatinine rises. The trap is that the pharmacist treated the interaction acknowledgment as the completion of the task, rather than as the trigger for closer monitoring. The mechanism is that documentation feels like action — the interaction was noted, so it was handled — when in fact the acknowledgment did not include a level recheck, a dose adjustment, or a plan to monitor for the rising level that clarithromycin's CYP3A4 inhibition inevitably produces. The harm is that the tacrolimus level climbs into the nephrotoxic range, compounding the pneumonia with acute kidney injury, and the connection to the interaction documented days earlier is made only in retrospect.

### Carbamazepine Auto-Induction Misread as Non-Adherence or Worsening Disease

A patient starts carbamazepine and has a level of 8 mg/L at week one. At week three, on the same dose, the level is 4 mg/L. The prescriber suspects non-adherence or disease progression. The trap is that the pharmacist fails to recognize carbamazepine's auto-induction — the drug induces its own metabolism, and the level falls over the first several weeks even without a dose change. The mechanism is that the pharmacist interprets the falling level as a patient problem rather than a pharmacokinetic phenomenon, and the response is to confront the patient about adherence or to escalate the dose based on a level that has not yet reached steady state. The harm is that the patient is wrongly accused of non-adherence, or receives a dose increase that will produce toxicity once auto-induction completes and the true steady state is reached.

### The "In Range" Level That Ignores the Clinical Endpoint

A patient's tacrolimus level is within the reference range, but their creatinine is climbing and a biopsy shows acute rejection. The trap is that the pharmacist and team treat the in-range level as evidence that the immunosuppression is adequate, and they look elsewhere for the cause of the graft dysfunction. The mechanism is that the reference range feels authoritative — if the number is in range, the drug must be working — and the cognitive effort of questioning whether this patient's individual therapeutic window is lower than the population average is avoided. The harm is that the rejection progresses while the team chases other explanations, and the window for salvage therapy narrows. The false signal is that a population-derived range guarantees individual efficacy, when in fact some patients reject at levels that are therapeutic for the population.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

### Treating a confident conclusion as a substitute for evidence

A well-written, confident-sounding conclusion is accepted as proof. The trap is that the tone of certainty masks unstated assumptions, missing verification, or unresolved uncertainty, and the confident framing prevents anyone from asking the question that would expose the gap.

## Self-Check

- For phenytoin, did I correct the total level for the patient's albumin or order a free level, or did I interpret an uncorrected total level in a hypoalbuminemic or uremic patient?
- Did I treat the addition or removal of a CYP3A4 inhibitor, inducer, or other interacting drug as a trigger to recheck the level, or did I acknowledge the interaction without scheduling a recheck?
- Did I verify that the level was drawn at the correct time relative to the dose (trough, peak, or specific post-dose interval) before interpreting it against the reference range?
- For carbamazepine, did I consider auto-induction when interpreting a level drawn before steady state, or did I treat the falling level as non-adherence?
- Did I integrate the level with the clinical endpoint — seizure control, graft function, signs of toxicity — or did I treat the reference range as the goal independent of whether the patient's clinical condition was actually controlled?
- For a pregnant patient on an AED, did I monitor free levels and anticipate dose changes across trimesters, or did I apply the non-pregnant reference range to a patient whose pharmacokinetics have fundamentally changed?
- Does the output stay within the agent's scope, deferring final authority, licensed judgment, or specialist sign-off to the qualified person where the question exceeds the agent's competence?
- Is the reasoning documented clearly enough that another practitioner could review the basis and reproduce the conclusion?
