# Therapeutic Versus Toxic Range Interpretation and Dose Adjustment

A drug level is not a number to be acted on; it is a measurement taken at a specific time relative to a dose, in a patient with specific physiology, by an assay with specific limitations. Interpreting it correctly requires knowing when the level was drawn, what the target range means, how the patient's organ function and protein binding affect the number, and whether the assay can be trusted. The danger is that a level is treated as a standalone verdict — a high number triggers a dose cut, a low number triggers an increase — without regard to timing, to the difference between peak and trough, to assay interference, or to the clinical picture that may contradict the number. This skill covers the judgment used to interpret therapeutic drug levels and adjust dosing so that the patient is in range because the dose is right, not because the number was chased.

## Core Rules

### Interpret the Level Against the Time of Draw and the Dosing Interval

A drug level is meaningless without the draw time relative to the last dose. A trough drawn just before a dose and a level drawn at random mean entirely different things, and a "high" level drawn an hour after a dose is expected, while the same number at trough is alarming. Confirm the draw time, the time of the last dose, and the dosing interval before interpreting. For peak-dependent drugs (aminoglycosides), the peak reflects efficacy and the trough reflects accumulation; for trough-monitored drugs (vancomycin), the trough is the primary target. A level drawn at the wrong time produces a number that looks actionable but is not.

### Distinguish Subtherapeutic, Therapeutic, and Toxic Against the Right Target

Therapeutic ranges are context-dependent. The vancomycin trough target shifted from 15–20 to AUC-guided dosing for serious infections; the aminoglycoside target depends on extended-interval versus traditional dosing; the phenytoin range is affected by hypoalbuminemia and the free fraction; the digoxin range is lower in heart failure than in atrial fibrillation. Apply the target that matches the indication and the patient's biochemistry (corrected phenytoin for albumin, corrected calcium or free levels where relevant). A "therapeutic" number against the wrong target is misleading, and a "toxic" number in a patient with altered protein binding may be a free-drug artifact.

### Adjust Dosing Based on the Pattern, Not a Single Level

Dose adjustment should respond to the pattern of levels and the patient's clearance, not to a single number. For aminoglycosides, use the peak and trough together to compute the half-life and the volume of distribution, and adjust the dose and the interval to keep both in range. For vancomycin, use the trough or AUC with the patient's renal function to adjust. For antiepileptics, consider whether a low level reflects non-adherence, rapid metabolism, or a drug interaction before increasing the dose. A single level that prompts a dose change without understanding the pharmacokinetics produces oscillation — up, then down, then up — without ever achieving stable control.

### Recognize Assay Interference and Falsely Elevated or Low Levels

Assays can be interfered with, and a level that contradicts the clinical picture must be questioned before it is acted on. Hemolysis, hyperbilirubinemia, lipemia, and cross-reacting metabolites can distort immunoassays (notably digoxin, where endogenous digoxin-like immunoreactive factors and certain antibodies falsely elevate or lower the result; and vancomycin in some assays). Heterophile antibodies and monoclonal antibody therapies can interfere with specific assays. When the level does not fit the clinical state, suspect the assay: check for sample issues, consider a different method (LC-MS/MS versus immunoassay), and re-draw. Acting on a falsely elevated digoxin level by withholding a needed dose, or on a falsely low level by giving more, harms the patient.

### Account for Protein Binding and the Free Fraction

Total drug levels reflect bound plus free drug, and only the free drug is active. In hypoalbuminemia (common in critical illness, liver disease, nephrotic syndrome), highly protein-bound drugs (phenytoin, valproate, digoxin to a lesser extent) have a higher free fraction at the same total level, so a "therapeutic" total level may be toxic in the free fraction. Correct the phenytoin level for albumin (or measure free phenytoin), interpret valproate total levels with caution at the high end in hypoalbuminemia, and recognize that the active concentration is what matters. A normal total level in a hypoalbuminemic patient can still produce toxicity.

### Correlate the Level With the Clinical Picture

The drug level is one data point alongside the clinical response. A vancomycin trough in range does not guarantee source control; a subtherapeutic aminoglycoside peak may still produce clinical improvement; a "toxic" digoxin level in an asymptomatic patient with normal potassium may be assay error. Use the level to confirm or challenge the clinical impression, not to replace it. When the level and the clinical picture disagree, investigate both before changing the dose — the discrepancy is usually more informative than either alone.

### Use Population and Bayesian Methods With Judgment

Bayesian and population pharmacokinetic dosing tools integrate population parameters with the patient's levels to recommend doses, and they are valuable, but they are only as good as their inputs. Confirm that the levels entered are correctly timed, that the patient's weight and organ function are current, and that the model's assumptions fit the patient (it may not for obesity, ECMO, burns, or pediatrics). Treat the tool's output as a recommendation to be checked against pharmacokinetic reasoning and the clinical picture, not as an instruction. A model fed wrong timing or stale weight produces a confident wrong answer.

## Common Traps

### The Level Acted On Without the Draw Time

A high or low number triggers a dose change, and the draw time relative to the dose is never confirmed. The mechanism is that the number is concrete and the timing is administrative detail, so the number dominates. The false signal is an actionable level. The harm is that a peak read as a trough causes a dose cut that under-treats, or a trough read as a peak causes a dose increase that accumulates to toxicity. The draw time must be confirmed before any interpretation; a level without a draw time is uninterpretable.

### Single-Level Chasing Producing Oscillation

Each level prompts a dose change, and the patient's levels oscillate above and below target without achieving stability. The mechanism is that single-level feedback ignores the pharmacokinetics — the half-life, the volume, the clearance — and reacts to noise. The false signal is responsive dosing. The harm is alternating toxicity and subtherapeutic periods, never stable control, and the erosion of confidence in monitoring. Dose adjustment must be based on the pattern and the pharmacokinetic parameters, with anticipation of where the next level will land.

### Total Level Interpreted Without Protein Binding in Hypoalbuminemia

A "therapeutic" total phenytoin or valproate level in a hypoalbuminemic patient is treated as in range, when the free fraction is toxic. The mechanism is that total levels are the routine result and the free fraction requires a correction or a separate test that is easy to omit. The false signal is a total level in range. The harm is toxicity (ataxia, nystagmus, sedation for phenytoin; confusion and other effects for valproate) attributed to other causes, while the dose is continued or increased. Total levels of highly bound drugs must be corrected for albumin or supplemented with free levels in hypoalbuminemia.

### Assay Interference Trusted Over the Clinical Picture

A digoxin or vancomycin level that contradicts the patient's clinical state is acted on literally — a falsely high digoxin leads to dose withholding, a falsely low level to overdose. The mechanism is that the number is treated as ground truth and the clinical picture as secondary. The false signal is a precise-looking result. The harm is dose changes in the wrong direction, driven by interference from hemolysis, digoxin-like factors, monoclonal antibodies, or cross-reacting metabolites. A level that does not fit the picture must be questioned and re-checked before it changes the dose.

### AUC or Bayesian Output Treated as Ground Truth

A pharmacokinetic tool's recommended dose is implemented without checking the inputs (timing, weight, organ function) or the model fit. The mechanism is that the tool produces a confident, specific output that feels authoritative. The false signal is a precise recommendation. The harm is that wrong inputs (a mistimed level, a stale weight, a model that does not fit ECMO or obesity) yield a confidently wrong dose. The tool's output must be checked against the inputs and the clinical reasoning, not implemented uncritically.

### Toxic Level in an Asymptomatic Patient Over-Treated

A level above the toxic range in a patient with no clinical signs of toxicity drives aggressive dose reduction or cessation, when the level may reflect assay interference, a draw timing artifact, or a patient who tolerates a higher concentration. The mechanism is that the word "toxic" on the report dominates the absence of symptoms. The false signal is a flagged result. The harm is under-treatment of the underlying condition (seizures, arrhythmia, infection) because of a number. The level must be correlated with the clinical picture, and discrepancies investigated, before the dose is slashed.

## Self-Check

- Did I confirm the draw time relative to the last dose and the dosing interval before interpreting the level?
- Did I apply the target range that matches the indication and the patient's biochemistry (AUC versus trough for vancomycin, extended-interval versus traditional for aminoglycosides, corrected for albumin where relevant)?
- Did I base dose adjustment on the pattern of levels and the pharmacokinetic parameters (half-life, volume, clearance), not a single number?
- Where the level contradicted the clinical picture, did I suspect and investigate assay interference (hemolysis, digoxin-like factors, monoclonal antibodies, cross-reactivity) before acting?
- For highly protein-bound drugs in hypoalbuminemia, did I correct the total level or measure the free fraction?
- Did I correlate the level with the clinical response, using each to check the other rather than treating the number as the verdict?
- For Bayesian or population dosing tools, did I verify the inputs (timing, weight, organ function, model fit) and check the output against pharmacokinetic reasoning before implementing?
