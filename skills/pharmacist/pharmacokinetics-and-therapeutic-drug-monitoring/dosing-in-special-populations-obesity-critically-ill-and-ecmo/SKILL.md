# Dosing in Special Populations: Obesity, Critically Ill, and ECMO

Standard dosing assumes a patient of typical weight, with stable organ function, normal volume of distribution, and predictable clearance. None of those assumptions hold in the obese, the critically ill, or the patient on ECMO or CRRT, and applying standard doses in these patients produces predictable failure: underdosing of hydrophilic drugs in edema and sepsis, overdosing of lipophilic drugs dosed on total body weight, loss of drug to the ECMO circuit, and clearance that changes hour to hour as organ function fluctuates. The pharmacist's job is to reason from pharmacokinetic principles — volume of distribution, protein binding, clearance, and the physicochemical properties of the drug — rather than from a default dose, and to anticipate how the non-standard physiology will distort each parameter. This skill covers the judgment used to dose medications when the patient's physiology does not match the population on which the dose was derived.

## Core Rules

### Reason From the Drug's Physicochemical Properties, Not From a Default Dose

Every dosing decision in non-standard physiology starts with the drug's properties: hydrophilic (water-soluble, low volume of distribution, influenced by renal clearance and by changes in extracellular fluid) versus lipophilic (fat-soluble, large volume of distribution, influenced by adipose tissue and by protein binding), and the degree of protein binding (highly bound drugs are affected by changes in albumin and alpha-1-acid glycoprotein in critical illness). Hydrophilic drugs (beta-lactams, aminoglycosides, vancomycin) distribute into extracellular fluid, so edema, sepsis, and burns increase their volume of distribution and require a larger loading dose; lipophilic drugs (fentanyl, propofol, amiodarone, macrolides) distribute into fat, so dosing on total body weight in obesity can overdose. Start from the drug's properties and predict how the patient's physiology will distort them.

### Use the Correct Body Size Descriptor for Each Drug

In obesity, the choice of weight descriptor — total body weight, ideal body weight, adjusted body weight — depends on the drug. Lipophilic drugs may require total body weight (with caps), hydrophilic drugs usually use ideal or adjusted body weight, and many drugs have specific obesity dosing recommendations. Using total body weight for a hydrophilic drug overdoses; using ideal body weight for a lipophilic drug underdoses. Confirm the recommended descriptor for the specific drug, apply it consistently, and use obesity-specific guidance where it exists rather than extrapolating. For underweight patients, the same logic applies in reverse: verify that the dose is not excessive for the actual lean mass.

### Anticipate Increased Volume of Distribution in Sepsis, Edema, and Burns

Critically ill patients with sepsis, capillary leak, aggressive fluid resuscitation, burns, or post-operative third-spacing have an expanded extracellular fluid volume that increases the volume of distribution of hydrophilic drugs. Standard doses of beta-lactams, aminoglycosides, and vancomycin produce subtherapeutic concentrations in these patients, which for antibiotics drives failure and resistance. Use a larger loading dose to achieve the target concentration quickly, and recognize that maintenance dosing may need to be higher or more frequent, with therapeutic drug monitoring where available. The volume of distribution normalizes as the patient recovers and mobilizes fluid, so doses that were right in peak sepsis become excessive later — re-evaluate as physiology changes.

### Account for ECMO Circuit Sequestration

ECCMO adds a pharmacokinetic compartment: the circuit itself, whose components (tubing, oxygenator membrane) sequester drugs, particularly lipophilic and highly protein-bound drugs. This increases the apparent volume of distribution and reduces the circulating concentration, so standard doses may under-treat. The effect is greatest for lipophilic drugs (sedatives, analgesics, certain antibiotics) and less for hydrophilic drugs. There is no simple formula; the response is to anticipate increased dosing requirements for affected drugs, to use loading doses, and to rely on therapeutic drug monitoring and clinical response rather than standard maintenance doses. Coordinate with the perfusion and critical care team, because circuit changes (oxygenator exchange) acutely alter drug levels.

### Model CRRT Clearance Against the Effluent Rate and the Drug Properties

Continuous renal replacement therapy (CRRT) clears drugs that are water-soluble and have low protein binding, with the clearance proportional to the effluent rate and the sieving coefficient. The dosing must match the CRRT prescription (CVVH, CVVHD, CVVHDF), the effluent rate, and the filter — and it must be revisited when the prescription changes, because a dose that was right at one effluent rate is wrong at another. Many antibiotics require higher or more frequent dosing on CRRT than on intermittent hemodialysis, because CRRT provides continuous clearance. Use CRRT-specific dosing references, confirm the current prescription, and adjust when the prescription changes. For drugs that are highly protein-bound or lipophilic, CRRT clears little, and accumulation depends on residual non-renal clearance.

### Re-dose Dynamically as Organ Function Changes

Critically ill patients have dynamic organ function: renal function falls and recovers, hepatic function fluctuates, cardiac output changes with vasopressors and volume status, and the net effect is that clearance changes over hours to days. A dose that was correct at the start may be excessive by the next day (recovering kidneys clearing more) or subtherapeutic (worsening function clearing less). Build in dynamic reassessment: recheck renal and hepatic function frequently, use therapeutic drug monitoring for narrow-index drugs (vancomycin, aminoglycosides, antiepileptics, digoxin), and adjust dosing as the physiology moves. Static dosing in a dynamic patient is a leading cause of both toxicity and failure.

### Use Loading Doses to Reach Target Quickly, Then Monitor

In patients with altered volume of distribution, the time to reach therapeutic concentration with maintenance dosing alone may be dangerously long. Use a loading dose (calculated from the volume of distribution and the target concentration) for drugs where early therapeutic levels matter (antibiotics in sepsis, antiepileptics, antiarrhythmics, antifungals), then follow with maintenance dosing adjusted to the patient's clearance. Confirm that the loading dose uses the correct weight descriptor and that maintenance is not simply continued at the loaded rate. Loading without monitoring, or maintenance dosing that ignores the load, produces overshoot or undershoot.

## Common Traps

### Total Body Weight Used for a Hydrophilic Drug in Obesity

A hydrophilic drug (beta-lactam, aminoglycoside) is dosed on total body weight in an obese patient, producing excessive peak levels and toxicity. The mechanism is that hydrophilic drugs distribute poorly into adipose tissue, so total-body-weight dosing overestimates the relevant distribution volume. The false signal is a weight-based dose that "looks right." The harm is nephrotoxicity (aminoglycosides), ototoxicity, or excess beta-lactam exposure, particularly in patients whose renal function is already at risk. Hydrophilic drugs require ideal or adjusted body weight in obesity, per drug-specific guidance.

### Standard Maintenance Dose in Expanded-Volume Sepsis

A patient in septic shock with capillary leak receives standard maintenance doses of a hydrophilic antibiotic, and the concentration is subtherapeutic because the volume of distribution is expanded. The mechanism is that edema and resuscitation dilute the drug into a larger volume, and standard dosing was derived in patients without that expansion. The false signal is a "standard" dose being given. The harm is antibiotic failure and resistance selection in a patient who needs effective levels most, with mortality consequences. Expanded-volume patients need loading doses and possibly augmented maintenance, with monitoring.

### ECMO Sequestration Ignored for Lipophilic Drugs

A sedative, analgesic, or lipophilic antibiotic is dosed at standard levels on ECMO, and the patient is under-sedated or under-treated because the circuit sequesters the drug. The mechanism is that the ECMO circuit adds a compartment that binds lipophilic and protein-bound drugs, increasing the apparent volume of distribution. The false signal is a dose that would be adequate off ECMO. The harm is inadequate effect (awareness, pain, subtherapeutic antibiotic) and the false conclusion that the patient is "resistant" to the drug. ECMO patients need anticipated dose increases for affected drugs, with clinical and level-based monitoring.

### CRRT Dose Not Adjusted to Effluent Rate

An antibiotic is dosed per a generic "renal replacement" recommendation, without matching the dose to the actual effluent rate or the CRRT modality, and the patient is over- or under-dosed. The mechanism is that CRRT clearance is proportional to effluent rate, so a dose correct at one rate is wrong at another, and generic recommendations obscure this. The false signal is a "CRRT dose." The harm is subtherapeutic levels (under-dosing at high effluent rates, driving antibiotic failure) or accumulation (over-dosing at low rates, causing toxicity). CRRT dosing must match the current prescription and be revisited when it changes.

### Static Dosing in a Patient With Recovering or Worsening Function

A dose adjusted for acute kidney injury is continued unchanged as the kidneys recover, and the patient is now under-dosed, or a dose established in good function is continued as function worsens, and the patient is toxic. The mechanism is that critical illness is dynamic, but dosing is often set once and not revisited. The false signal is a dose that was once correct. The harm is failure or toxicity that tracks the unmonitored change in clearance. Dosing in critical illness requires scheduled reassessment of organ function and TDM-guided adjustment.

### Loading Dose Followed by Unadjusted Maintenance

A loading dose is given to reach target quickly, and then maintenance dosing is continued at a standard rate that ignores the loading logic, producing overshoot or a slow drift off target. The mechanism is that the loading dose and the maintenance dose are treated as separate decisions rather than a coordinated plan. The false signal is a loading dose given. The harm is that the early advantage of the load is lost, or toxicity accrues, because the maintenance dose does not match the patient's clearance and volume. Loading and maintenance must be planned together, with monitoring to confirm the transition.

## Self-Check

- Did I reason from the drug's physicochemical properties (hydrophilic versus lipophilic, protein binding) to predict how the patient's physiology will distort distribution and clearance?
- Did I use the correct body size descriptor (total, ideal, adjusted) for the specific drug in obesity, applying obesity-specific guidance rather than extrapolating?
- For sepsis, edema, or burns, did I account for the expanded volume of distribution with a loading dose and possibly augmented maintenance for hydrophilic drugs?
- For ECMO patients, did I anticipate circuit sequestration of lipophilic and highly protein-bound drugs and plan increased dosing with monitoring?
- For CRRT, did I match the dose to the current effluent rate and modality, using CRRT-specific references, and plan to adjust when the prescription changes?
- Did I build in dynamic reassessment of organ function and therapeutic drug monitoring for narrow-index drugs, rather than static dosing?
- Did I plan the loading and maintenance doses together, with monitoring to confirm the target is reached and maintained as physiology changes?
