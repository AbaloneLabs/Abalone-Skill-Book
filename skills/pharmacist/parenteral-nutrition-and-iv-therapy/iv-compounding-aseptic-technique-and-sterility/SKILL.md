# IV Compounding Aseptic Technique and Sterility

Intravenous and other sterile compounding carries a failure mode that ordinary dispensing does not: a preparation contaminated with bacteria or fungus looks identical to a sterile one, passes every visual check, and reaches the patient with no signal that anything is wrong until the febrile patient, the positive blood culture, or the fatal infection appears days or weeks later, by which point the contamination event is difficult to trace. The judgment problem is that sterility is invisible, so the pharmacist must treat the environment, the garbing, the aseptic manipulation, the beyond-use dating, and the environmental monitoring as independent controls whose individual failures are silent, and must perform each control correctly every time, because the absence of visible contamination is never evidence of sterility. The pharmacist's discipline is to compound only within a certified environment, to apply first-air and no-touch technique without shortcuts, to assign beyond-use dates that respect the sterility window as well as the chemical stability window, and to escalate any deviation to the compounding supervisor and, where patient harm may have occurred, to the medical team and infection prevention.

## Core Rules

### Match the CSP Category and Compounding Environment to the Preparation

USP <797> classifies compounded sterile preparations (CSPs) into categories that dictate the required environment, the maximum beyond-use date (BUD), and the verification requirements. The category depends on the source of the components (commercially sterile sterile commercial products versus non-sterile powders that must be sterilized), the manipulation involved, and the intended BUD. Immediate-use CSPs have the shortest BUD (typically 4 hours) and the fewest environmental requirements but are reserved for urgent direct patient care, not routine batch preparation. Category 1 CSPs are prepared in a segregated compounding area (SCA) with an ISO Class 5 primary engineering control (PEC) and have short BUDs (up to 12 hours at controlled room temperature or 24 hours refrigerated). Category 2 CSPs are prepared in a classified cleanroom suite — an ISO Class 7 buffer area with an ISO Class 8 ante area, or an engineered negative-pressure segregated compounding area for hazardous drugs — and qualify for longer BUDs based on stability data and sterility assumptions. The pharmacist must classify each CSP correctly and compound it only in an environment certified to that level; an order that requires an ISO Class 5 PEC must never be prepared on an open dispensing bench regardless of urgency.

### Verify Cleanroom Certification and Airflow Before Compounding

The cleanroom and the PEC (laminar airflow workbench, biological safety cabinet, or compounding aseptic isolator) are the primary engineering controls, and their function depends on certification that is invisible in daily operation. Before compounding, confirm that the PEC and the cleanroom are within their current certification cycle (HEPA filter integrity, airflow velocity and unidirectionality, air pressure differentials between the buffer and ante areas, and total air changes per hour). A HEPA filter can tear, clog, or lose its seal without any visible signal, and an air pressure differential can drift out of spec, delivering contaminated air over the preparation while the hood appears to function normally. Certification stickers and logs must be checked, not assumed, and any out-of-certification hood or cleanroom must be taken out of service until recertified. Do not compound in a space whose certification status is unknown.

### Perform Garbing and Hand Hygiene in the Correct Sequence

Garbing — the donning of shoe covers, hair and beard covers, face masks, gowns, and sterile gloves — and the hand hygiene that precedes it are the primary defenses against the personnel, who are the largest microbial source in any cleanroom. Garb in the correct sequence (shoe covers, hair and beard covers, face mask, hand hygiene, gown, sterile gloves) and in the correct location (progressing from the dirtiest to the cleanest area, finishing sterile gloving at the edge of or within the buffer area). Perform hand hygiene with an antiseptic hand rub or surgical scrub before gloving, and don sterile gloves aseptically without contaminating the outside surface. Sanitize the gloved hands repeatedly with sterile 70 percent isopropyl alcohol during compounding, because contamination accumulates with every manipulation and the alcohol's effect decays over minutes. Every skipped step, every shortcut in sequence, introduces contamination that is invisible at the time and untraceable later.

### Apply First-Air and No-Touch Aseptic Technique

Aseptic technique rests on two principles: nothing sterile touches anything non-sterile, and critical sites (the needle, the vial septum, the needle hub, the bag port) are exposed only to first air — the unidirectional HEPA-filtered air that has not passed over any obstacle. Work at least six inches inside the front edge of the PEC, away from the airflow disruption of the hood edges and any items that obstruct airflow, and arrange materials so that first air reaches every critical site. Pierce vial septa and bag ports with the needle oriented so that first air sweeps the site immediately before and during the puncture, and never let a sanitized gloved hand pass between the HEPA filter and an open critical site. Avoid talking, coughing, or rapid movement that disrupts the unidirectional airflow. Each of these principles exists because a single breach can introduce a low inoculum that proliferates during storage and harms the patient.

### Assign BUDs as the More Conservative of Sterility and Stability

For sterile CSPs, the beyond-use date must account for both chemical stability (the drug remains potent and compatible) and microbial sterility (the preparation remains free of contamination that could grow during storage). The sterility BUD is dictated by the CSP category, the storage temperature, and whether the preparation was terminally sterilized or aseptically compounded, and it is often shorter than the stability BUD. Assign the more conservative of the two dates, and do not extend a BUD based on stability data alone if the sterility BUD is shorter. Refrigeration and freezing can extend some BUDs, but verify that the drug remains stable at the lower temperature (some drugs precipitate or degrade when refrigerated) and that the container and closure are rated for frozen storage. Extending a BUD beyond the sterility window allows a contaminant introduced at compounding to grow to dangerous levels before administration.

### Conduct Environmental Monitoring and Personnel Competency on Schedule

Environmental controls are only credible if they are verified continuously. Viable air sampling and surface sampling of the PEC and cleanroom surfaces must be performed at the frequency required by the CSP category (for example, monthly or quarterly for Category 2), and viable and non-viable particle counts must trend within action levels. Personnel who compound must pass gloved fingertip sampling (verifying hand hygiene and gloving technique) and media-fill simulations (compounding a CSP with sterile culture media in place of drug, then incubating to detect contamination) initially and at defined intervals (annually for low- and medium-risk, semiannually for high-risk compounding). A person who has not passed current competency testing must not compound sterile preparations. Investigate any out-of-specification environmental result or media-fill failure, identify the root cause, and retrain and retest before resuming compounding.

### Distinguish Sterile from Hazardous Compounding and Apply USP 800 Where Required

Sterile compounding of hazardous drugs — antineoplastics, certain hormones, certain antivirals, and other agents identified by the National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health (NIOSH) — must additionally comply with USP <800>, which exists to protect personnel and the environment from the hazardous drug, not to protect the product. Hazardous sterile compounding must be performed in an ISO Class 5 PEC located within a containment primary engineering control (a Class II or III biological safety cabinet or a compounding aseptic containment isolator) housed within an ISO Class 7 negative-pressure containment buffer area, with a dedicated ante area, and personnel must use double chemotherapy-rated gloves, chemotherapy gowns, eye and face protection when appropriate, and closed-system drug-transfer devices (CSTDs) where feasible. Spills must be managed with a hazardous drug spill kit, and waste must be segregated and disposed of as hazardous. Do not confuse the two standards: USP 797 protects the product from contamination; USP 800 protects the personnel and environment from the drug. A CSP that is both sterile and hazardous must meet both.

## Common Traps

### The Urgency Override of the Environment

A stat antibiotic or a critical-care vasopressor arrives, and the technician or pharmacist prepares it on an open bench or in an uncertified hood to save time. The mechanism is that the urgency feels like a justification for bypassing the environmental control. The false signal is that the preparation was made quickly and looks fine, so the patient was served safely. The harm is that a CSP compounded outside the certified environment carries a contamination risk that is invisible at administration and may manifest as a bloodstream infection days later, when the link to the compounding event is obscure. Urgency never justifies bypassing the environmental controls; the controls exist precisely because the consequences of contamination are severe and delayed. If an order is truly immediate-use, apply the immediate-use criteria strictly rather than stretching a longer-BUD preparation into an open-bench shortcut.

### The Clean-Looking Hood Assumption

The hood looks clean, the airflow feels normal, and a certification sticker is somewhere in the recent past, so the pharmacist compounds without verifying current certification. The mechanism is that the hood's appearance and feel are treated as evidence of function. The false signal is that the hood looks operational, so it must be protecting the preparation. The harm is that a HEPA filter can fail without any visible signal, the air pressure differential can drift out of spec, and an uncertified hood delivers contaminated air over the preparation while appearing to function normally. Certification must be verified against the log, not assumed, because the hood's failure mode is silent.

### Glove Sanitization Decay

The compounder sanitizes the gloves at the start and then performs a long series of manipulations without re-sanitizing, trusting that the initial sanitization persists. The mechanism is that the initial sanitization felt sufficient and the gloves look clean. The false signal is that the gloves were sanitized, so they remain sterile. The harm is that sterile gloves accumulate contamination with each manipulation — touching a vial, adjusting a bag, brushing a sleeve — and the alcohol's antimicrobial effect decays over minutes, so the contaminated glove transfers organisms into the preparation. Re-sanitization between manipulations, especially after any non-critical-site contact, is required to maintain the glove surface's effectiveness.

### Media-Fill as a One-Time Formality

The media-fill test is performed once during initial training and then treated as a checked box, with periodic re-testing skipped or delayed. The mechanism is that passing once feels like proof of competency. The false signal is that the person passed the test, so their technique is verified. The harm is that aseptic technique decays without practice and feedback — bad habits creep in, new manipulations are attempted without validation — and the once-verified compounding is now producing contaminated output that no one is detecting because the periodic verification has lapsed. The media-fill must be repeated at the defined interval, and any failure must trigger retraining and re-testing before the person resumes compounding.

### Stability BUD Mistaken for Sterility BUD

The pharmacist finds a published stability study supporting a 30-day refrigerated BUD and assigns 30 days without checking that the sterility BUD for the CSP category is shorter. The mechanism is that the stability study feels like authoritative evidence. The false signal is that the literature supports the date, so the date is defensible. The harm is that a CSP stored for 30 days may remain chemically stable but may not remain sterile, and a contaminant introduced at compounding can grow to dangerous levels over the extended storage. The assigned BUD must be the more conservative of the stability and sterility dates, not the more convenient.

### Mixing Hazardous and Non-Hazardous Practices

A hazardous drug is compounded in a standard laminar airflow workbench in a positive-pressure buffer area, or non-hazardous technique (single gloves, no chemo gown) is used for a hazardous CSP, because the difference between USP 797 and USP 800 is not understood. The mechanism is that both are sterile compounding, so the same environment and technique feel adequate. The false signal is that the CSP looks sterile and the workflow feels familiar. The harm is twofold: the hazardous drug contaminates the positive-pressure environment and the personnel, and the personnel are exposed to occupational risk that USP 800 exists to prevent. The corrective is to route every hazardous CSP to the containment area with USP 800 controls, and to never let familiarity with sterile technique override the containment requirement.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The routine adult IV admixture is compounded correctly, but the immediate-use CSP is batched beyond the 4-hour limit, or the high-risk CSP (from non-sterile components) is assigned a low-risk BUD. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary of risk category, BUD, or hazardous status.

## Self-Check

- Did I classify the CSP to the correct USP <797> category (immediate-use, Category 1, Category 2) and compound it only in an environment certified to that level, with current PEC and cleanroom certification verified against the log?
- Did I confirm the PEC and cleanroom are within their certification cycle (HEPA integrity, airflow, pressure differentials, air changes) before compounding, and did I refuse to compound in a space of unknown certification status?
- Did I perform garbing in the correct sequence and location, with hand hygiene before gloving, and did I maintain first-air and no-touch aseptic technique with repeated glove sanitization throughout compounding?
- Did I assign the beyond-use date as the more conservative of the stability date and the sterility date for the CSP category and storage condition, rather than extending it based on stability data alone?
- Did I confirm that viable and non-viable environmental monitoring is current and within action levels, and that every compounder has passed current gloved fingertip and media-fill testing at the required interval?
- Did I distinguish sterile from hazardous compounding and route every hazardous CSP to the containment area with USP <800> controls (BSC or CACI, negative-pressure buffer, double chemo gloves, chemo gown, CSTD)?
- Did I refuse to bypass environmental or technique controls for an urgent order, recognizing that the contamination consequence is severe, invisible, and delayed?
- Does the output stay within my scope, escalating to the compounding supervisor for environmental deviations, to infection prevention and the medical team where patient exposure may have occurred, and to the board of pharmacy where regulatory non-compliance is suspected?
