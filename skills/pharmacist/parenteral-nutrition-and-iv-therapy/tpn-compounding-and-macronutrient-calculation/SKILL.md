# TPN Compounding and Macronutrient Calculation

Parenteral nutrition (PN) is one of the most complex sterile admixtures a pharmacist compounds, because the formulation is not a single drug in solution but a multi-component metabolic prescription that must simultaneously meet the patient's entire caloric, nitrogen, electrolyte, vitamin, trace element, and fluid needs while remaining physically and chemically stable in the bag. The judgment problem is that every decision interacts with every other decision: the dextrose concentration sets the osmolarity and the glucose infusion rate, the amino acid concentration sets the nitrogen load and the calcium-phosphate solubility window, the lipid sets the caloric density and the total nutrient admixture stability, and the electrolyte additions can precipitate with each other or destabilize the lipid emulsion. An error in any one variable — a miscalculated kilocalorie, a calcium-phosphate pair that precipitates, a glucose infusion rate that exceeds hepatic capacity — can cause refeeding syndrome, hyperglycemia, embolic precipitation, or sepsis, and the error is invisible until the patient deteriorates. The pharmacist's role is to verify the formulation against the patient's metabolic needs, to check the physical and chemical compatibility of every component, and to defer the underlying nutritional prescription decisions to the prescriber and the nutrition support team.

## Core Rules

### Calculate Caloric and Protein Needs Using a Validated Method, Not a Guess

The foundation of PN is the energy and protein target, and it must be derived from a validated method appropriate to the patient. Use predictive equations such as Harris-Benedict (with stress and activity factors) or a simple weight-based estimate (for example, 20 to 30 kcal/kg/day for general adult maintenance, adjusted upward for critical illness or catabolic states and downward for obesity using adjusted or ideal body weight). Protein needs are typically 0.8 to 1.5 g/kg/day for most adults, higher (up to 2.0 to 2.5 g/kg/day) for critically ill, burns, or wound-healing patients, and lower with advanced renal or hepatic failure depending on the clinical context. Never accept a PN order that lacks a stated weight and a stated caloric and protein target, because without these the formulation cannot be checked for adequacy. Where the prescribed target seems inconsistent with the patient's clinical state (excessive calories in an obese patient risking overfeeding, or inadequate protein in a catabolic patient), flag the concern to the prescriber and the nutrition support team rather than silently compounding what was ordered. The pharmacist verifies the arithmetic and the appropriateness; the prescriber owns the therapeutic decision.

### Balance Macronutrient Ratios and Verify the Glucose Infusion Rate

Once the caloric and protein targets are set, distribute the calories across the three macronutrients and verify that the dextrose load is tolerable. Dextrose provides 3.4 kcal/g, amino acids provide 4 kcal/g, and lipid emulsion provides 10 kcal/g for a 20 percent emulsion (11 kcal/g for 30 percent diluted). A typical distribution provides roughly 50 to 60 percent of non-protein calories as carbohydrate and 40 to 50 percent as lipid, though ratios are individualized. The critical safety check is the glucose infusion rate (GIR), calculated as milligrams of dextrose per kilogram per minute, which must not exceed the liver's capacity to metabolize glucose (generally keeping GIR below 4 to 5 mg/kg/min in adults, with even tighter limits on initiation to avoid hyperglycemia). For peripheral PN, the osmolarity should be kept below approximately 900 mOsm/L, which limits dextrose and amino acid concentrations and usually requires central access for higher-concentration formulations. Recalculate the GIR whenever the rate or the dextrose concentration changes, because a rate increase that seems minor can push the GIR into the hyperglycemia range.

### Check Calcium-Phosphate Solubility Before Every Addition

Calcium and phosphate can precipitate as calcium phosphate in the PN bag, and the precipitate can embolize to the lungs and kill the patient. Solubility depends on the calcium concentration, the phosphate concentration, the amino acid concentration (amino acids complex calcium and increase solubility), the temperature, the pH, and the order of mixing, and it is not intuitive. Use published solubility curves or validated software, and recognize that organic phosphates (sodium glycerophosphate) have substantially higher solubility than inorganic phosphates (sodium or potassium phosphate). The rule is to keep the total calcium and phosphate below the solubility limit at the final concentration and final temperature, to add phosphate before calcium during compounding, and to never exceed the curve's limit even when the order demands it — instead, contact the prescriber to redistribute the electrolytes or split the calcium into a separate infusion. A precipitate that forms in a three-in-one (TNA) admixture may be obscured by the lipid's opacity, so the final visual inspection of a TNA must be performed against a light with the bag inverted and gently squeezed, and any haze, crystals, or layering must halt release.

### Distinguish Two-in-One from Three-in-One and Evaluate Lipid Stability

A two-in-one admixture (dextrose and amino acids, with lipids infused separately or piggybacked) and a three-in-one total nutrient admixture (TNA, with lipids included in the bag) have different stability profiles. In a TNA, the lipid emulsion is destabilized by low pH, high divalent cation concentration (calcium, magnesium), and high trace element concentration, and destabilization can cause lipid droplet coalescence (cracking) or free-oil formation that can embolize. The cation load matters: monovalent cations (sodium, potassium) have a higher tolerance, while divalent cations must be limited (typically total divalent cations below approximately 20 mEq per liter of TNA, with lower limits when lipid is present). Verify the formulation against the manufacturer's stability data and institutional validated stability studies, and do not assume that a stable two-in-one remains stable when lipid is added. If stability is uncertain, the safer default is a two-in-one with separate lipid infusion, which also allows earlier visual detection of precipitation.

### Add Electrolytes, Vitamins, and Trace Elements According to Patient Needs and Stability Limits

Electrolyte additions (sodium, potassium, magnesium, phosphate, calcium, chloride, acetate) must be individualized to the patient's serum levels and ongoing losses, and must respect the compatibility limits above. Standard adult maintenance ranges provide a starting point, but repletion of a deficit requires additional supplementation, often beyond what can be safely added to the PN (severe hypokalemia or hypophosphatemia requires separate infusions, not just PN adjustment). Add a daily multivitamin (per ASPEN recommendations to prevent deficiency) and trace elements; omit iron from PN unless stability is confirmed, because iron can interact with other components, and consider separate parenteral iron. Acetate is preferred over chloride as the anion when metabolic acidosis is present or to avoid hyperchloremic metabolic acidosis from excessive chloride. Every electrolyte change must be rechecked against the calcium-phosphate solubility and the cation stability limits, because adjusting one electrolyte can push another over its limit.

### Verify Compounding Accuracy Through Gravimetric and Automated Methods

The compounding of PN is a multi-step process with multiple additions, and each addition is a potential error point. Automated compounders (gravimetric and volumetric) reduce error, but they must be correctly programmed, the correct source solutions must be loaded, and the compounder must be calibrated and maintained. Gravimetric verification — weighing each component addition against the expected weight — provides an objective check that the correct volume of the correct solution was added, and it should be used for every PN. Verify the final weight of the bag against the calculated expected weight, and investigate any deviation beyond the tolerance (commonly plus or minus 5 percent). The pharmacist's final verification must confirm the label against the order, the components against the compounding record, and the physical appearance (no precipitate, no separation, no discoloration) before release.

### Initiate PN Cautiously to Prevent Refeeding Syndrome

Patients who are malnourished, have had minimal intake for an extended period, or are at the extremes of nutrition status are at risk for refeeding syndrome — the potentially fatal electrolyte and fluid shifts (hypophosphatemia, hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia, thiamine depletion, fluid retention) that occur when carbohydrate is reintroduced. Initiate PN at a low rate (commonly 10 to 20 kcal/kg/day, or even lower in severe malnutrition), give thiamine before and during initiation, monitor electrolytes closely (phosphorus, potassium, magnesium daily or more often), and titrate upward only as tolerated. The pharmacist should flag any patient at risk for refeeding syndrome and confirm that the initiation rate and monitoring plan are appropriate; this is a clinical judgment that belongs to the prescriber and nutrition support team, but the pharmacist is a key checkpoint.

## Common Traps

### Exceeding the Glucose Infusion Rate in a Critically Ill Patient

A critically ill patient is started on PN at full caloric target on day one, and the dextrose load produces a GIR that exceeds the liver's metabolic capacity. The mechanism is that the full target feels like the correct endpoint and the stress of critical illness feels like a reason to feed aggressively. The false signal is that the ordered calories match the calculated need, so the formulation is adequate. The harm is hyperglycemia, which in critical illness is associated with infection, osmotic diuresis, and worse outcomes, and in extreme cases hyperosmolar hyperglycemic state. The corrective is to calculate the GIR on every PN, to initiate at a reduced rate and titrate, and to remember that the calculated caloric need is a target to approach over days, not a starting point.

### Assuming Calcium-Phosphate Solubility from the Total Volume

The pharmacist checks the total calcium and phosphate against a solubility curve but uses the total bag volume rather than the concentration, or assumes that because the bag is large the concentrations are safe. The mechanism is that the total amounts feel modest relative to a large bag. The false signal is that the total milliequivalents and millimoles are within published limits. The harm is that solubility is a function of concentration, not total amount, and a bag that is large but dilute in amino acids can still precipitate if the local concentration of calcium and phosphate is too high, especially after the lipid is added or the bag cools. The corrective is to always use the final concentration, to consult the correct curve for the amino acid product and temperature, and to prefer organic phosphate when solubility is borderline.

### Missing a Precipitate in a Three-in-One Admixture

A TNA is compounded and the lipid's opacity obscures a fine calcium-phosphate precipitate that has formed in the bag. The mechanism is that the lipid makes the admixture milky and opaque, and a visual check through the bag cannot detect a small precipitate. The false signal is that the bag looks like every other TNA, so it must be fine. The harm is that the precipitate is infused and can embolize, with consequences that appear days later as pulmonary or other vascular complications that are difficult to link back to the PN. The corrective is to inspect every TNA against a strong light with the bag inverted, to check for any crystals or haze, and to hold any bag whose appearance is uncertain, and to favor two-in-one with separate lipid when solubility is borderline so that precipitation is visible.

### Adding Iron or Incompatible Trace Elements Without Stability Confirmation

Iron is added to a PN to correct deficiency, but iron can interact with lipid, with other trace elements, and with amino acids, and the stability of iron-containing PN is limited and product-specific. The mechanism is that iron deficiency is real and adding it to the PN feels efficient. The false signal is that the iron was added to the bag without immediate visible change, so it must be compatible. The harm is that iron can catalyze oxidation of the lipid, can form complexes that precipitate over the BUD, and can reduce the stability of other components, and the instability may develop over days rather than immediately. The corrective is to exclude iron from PN unless a validated stability study supports it for the specific formulation, and to give parenteral iron separately.

### Treating the Automated Compounder as Infallible

The compounder is programmed and run, and the pharmacist trusts the machine's output without independent verification, assuming that automation eliminates error. The mechanism is that the machine feels authoritative and the workflow rewards speed. The false signal is that the compounder completed the cycle without an alarm, so the bag must be correct. The harm is that a compounder can be loaded with the wrong source solution, can be miscalibrated, or can have a programming error, and the resulting bag is wrong in a way that no alarm detected. The corrective is to verify the source solutions loaded, to use gravimetric confirmation of each addition, and to treat the compounder as a tool that reduces but does not eliminate the need for pharmacist verification.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical adult standard PN is verified correctly, but the neonatal, pediatric, hepatic-failure, or renal-failure formulation is handled with the same defaults. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary of altered amino acid products, fluid-restricted volumes, or pediatric trace element dosing.

## Self-Check

- Did I calculate or verify the caloric and protein targets using a validated method and the correct patient weight, and flag any target that seems inconsistent with the patient's clinical state to the prescriber and nutrition support team?
- Did I distribute the macronutrients correctly, calculate the glucose infusion rate, and confirm it is within tolerable limits, especially at initiation and after any rate change?
- Did I check calcium-phosphate solubility using the correct final concentration, amino acid product, and temperature, and did I prefer organic phosphate or split the calcium when solubility was borderline?
- Did I distinguish two-in-one from three-in-one, evaluate lipid stability against the divalent cation and trace element load, and inspect the TNA against a strong light for precipitate or cracking?
- Did I individualize electrolyte, vitamin, and trace element additions to the patient's levels and needs, and recheck the compatibility limits after every change?
- Did I verify the compounding accuracy through gravimetric confirmation and source-solution checks, and confirm the final bag weight and appearance before release?
- Did I identify patients at risk for refeeding syndrome and confirm the initiation rate, thiamine supplementation, and electrolyte monitoring plan are appropriate?
- Does the output stay within my scope, deferring nutritional prescription decisions to the prescriber and nutrition support team, and escalating to a clinical pharmacist specialist or nutrition support pharmacist where the formulation exceeds my competence?
