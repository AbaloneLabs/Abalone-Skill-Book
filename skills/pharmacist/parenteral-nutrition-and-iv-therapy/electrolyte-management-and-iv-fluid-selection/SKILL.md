# Electrolyte Management and IV Fluid Selection

Electrolyte and fluid management looks deceptively simple because the orders are common and the agents are familiar, but each electrolyte carries a narrow therapeutic window and a specific, potentially fatal complication when infused incorrectly: potassium can cause cardiac arrest when infused too rapidly or in renal failure, sodium correction that is too rapid causes osmotic demyelination, phosphate and calcium can precipitate or cause hypocalcemia, and magnesium infusion causes respiratory depression in overdose. The judgment problem is that the safe rate, the safe concentration, and the safe route depend on the patient's serum level, renal function, cardiac status, and the urgency of repletion, and the wrong combination — a peripheral potassium concentration too high, a sodium correction too aggressive, a phosphate dose given with calcium — can kill the patient in minutes to hours. The pharmacist must verify every electrolyte order against the patient's current labs and renal function, enforce the infusion-rate and concentration limits, check compatibility, and defer the underlying diagnosis and the target correction rate to the prescriber while acting as the safety gatekeeper for the mechanics of administration.

## Core Rules

### Correct Sodium Slowly and Define the Correction Rate Upfront

Hyponatremia and hypernatremia are disorders of water, not just sodium, and the danger is not the abnormal value alone but the rate of correction. Rapid correction of chronic hyponatremia (more than 8 to 12 mEq/L in any 24 hours) risks osmotic demyelination syndrome, a devastating and often irreversible neurologic injury, while inadequate correction of symptomatic severe hyponatremia risks seizure and herniation. Before dispensing a hypertonic saline (3 percent) or a free-water restriction, confirm the prescriber has set an explicit correction target and rate, that the sodium is being measured frequently (every 2 to 4 hours for hypertonic saline), and that the patient is not at elevated risk for osmotic demyelination (malnutrition, alcohol use disorder, hypokalemia, liver disease). Hypertonic saline must be infused via a central line at a controlled rate, never as an unmonitored peripheral bolus. For hypernatremia, correction is similarly rate-limited (no more than approximately 10 to 12 mEq/L per 24 hours), and the fluid choice (free water, hypotonic saline) depends on the volume status and the underlying cause. The pharmacist enforces the rate; the prescriber owns the diagnosis and the target.

### Enforce Potassium Infusion Rate and Concentration Limits

Potassium is the electrolyte most likely to cause immediate, witnessed cardiac arrest when infused incorrectly, because rapid elevation of serum potassium depolarizes the myocardium. Peripheral infusion is generally limited to 10 mEq/hour at a concentration of 10 mEq per 100 mL (or per institutional policy), and central infusion with cardiac monitoring may allow higher rates (up to 20 mEq/hour) for severe symptomatic hypokalemia. Never allow a potassium-containing solution to be infused as an IV push or an uncontrolled gravity infusion; a potassium bolus can arrest the heart before the infusion completes. Verify the patient's renal function and current potassium before dispensing, because a dose that is appropriate repletion for a hypokalemic patient with normal kidneys is a dangerous load for a patient with acute kidney injury. Recheck the serum potassium after significant repletion, because the deficit is often larger than the serum value suggests (total body deficit of 100 to 200 mEq may correspond to a modestly low serum level).

### Give Phosphate and Magnesium with Attention to Calcium Interaction and Renal Function

Phosphate repletion must account for the calcium-phosphate interaction: intravenous phosphate binds ionized calcium and can cause symptomatic hypocalcemia (perioral numbness, tetany, arrhythmia), and phosphate and calcium should not be infused simultaneously through the same line because of precipitation risk. Severe hypophosphatemia can cause respiratory muscle weakness, hemolysis, and cardiac dysfunction, and repletion is weight-based (for example, 0.08 to 0.24 mmol/kg) with dose reduction in renal impairment. Magnesium repletion (typically 1 to 4 grams of magnesium sulfate IV over several hours) must be slowed or reduced in renal impairment, because magnesium is renally cleared and accumulates rapidly, causing hypotension, areflexia, respiratory depression, and cardiac arrest in overdose. For both electrolytes, verify renal function and the concurrent calcium status before dispensing, and separate phosphate and calcium infusions in time or line.

### Select the IV Fluid by Matching Tonicity, Volume Status, and Electrolyte Content

IV fluid selection is a clinical decision driven by the patient's volume status, electrolyte needs, and acid-base status, and the pharmacist must verify that the chosen fluid matches the intent. Normal saline (0.9 percent sodium chloride) is isotonic and useful for volume resuscitation, but large volumes cause hyperchloremic metabolic acidosis and may worsen renal outcomes compared with balanced crystalloids. Balanced crystalloids (lactated Ringer's, Plasma-Lyte) contain a chloride-reducing buffer (lactate, acetate, gluconate) and are preferred for large-volume resuscitation in many settings, though they contain potassium and calcium that must be considered in renal failure and with certain drug compatibilities. Dextrose-containing fluids (D5W, D5NS) provide free water and calories but are not resuscitation fluids; they distribute throughout total body water and are inappropriate for volume expansion. Hypertonic saline (3 percent or higher) is reserved for symptomatic hyponatremia or traumatic brain injury with elevated intracranial pressure and requires central access and frequent monitoring. Maintenance fluid (typically a hypotonic solution with dextrose, sodium, and potassium) differs from replacement fluid (isotonic) and from resuscitation fluid (isotonic, given in boluses); using the wrong category for the clinical situation causes fluid overload, electrolyte derangement, or inadequate resuscitation.

### Calculate Osmolarity and Confirm Vascular Access Is Appropriate

The osmolarity of a peripheral infusion matters because peripheral veins tolerate only a limited osmolarity (commonly cited as below approximately 900 mOsm/L), and higher osmolarity causes phlebitis, pain, extravasation injury, and thrombosis. Calculate the estimated osmolarity of the admixture (each component contributes: dextrose approximately 5 mOsm/g, sodium chloride approximately 308 mOsm/L for 0.9 percent, potassium chloride approximately 40 mOsm per 30 mEq, and amino acids contribute significantly). If the calculated osmolarity exceeds the peripheral limit, the infusion requires central venous access, and the pharmacist must verify that central access is in place before dispensing. Parenteral nutrition, concentrated potassium, and concentrated dextrose routinely exceed peripheral tolerance and must be routed to central access. Do not assume that because the order says "peripheral" the access can tolerate it; calculate and confirm.

### Check Y-Site Compatibility for Every Concurrent Infusion

When two or more infusions meet at a Y-site, they mix in the line before reaching the patient, and the mixture may be physically or chemically incompatible even though each drug is stable alone. Phosphate and calcium precipitate; ceftriaxone and calcium precipitate (and can be fatal, especially in neonates); certain drugs adsorb to plastic or react with the other drug's vehicle. Consult a compatibility reference (Trissel's or institutional data) for every Y-site combination, and recognize that compatibility depends on concentration, contact time, and vehicle, so a reference entry for one concentration does not generalize to another. When compatibility is unknown or borderline, separate the infusions in time or use a dedicated line. For multiple incompatible drugs in a patient with limited access, sequence the infusions and flush between them rather than risking an in-line precipitate.

## Common Traps

### Correcting Sodium Too Rapidly in Chronic Hyponatremia

A patient with chronic hyponatremia (for example, sodium 108 mEq/L) receives hypertonic saline and the sodium rises 18 mEq/L in 24 hours, and the patient develops osmotic demyelination. The mechanism is that the severe value feels like an emergency demanding aggressive correction, and the first improvement feels like success. The false signal is that the sodium is rising toward normal, so the treatment is working. The harm is that rapid correction of chronic hyponatremia causes osmotic demyelination syndrome, a devastating neurologic injury that appears days later as dysarthria, dysphagia, paralysis, and often death, and is largely irreversible. The corrective is to set an explicit correction-rate limit at the outset, to measure sodium every 2 to 4 hours during hypertonic saline, and to stop or switch to hypotonic fluids if the rate exceeds the target.

### Peripheral Potassium at a Dangerous Concentration

A potassium repletion is ordered as 40 mEq in 100 mL to run over 4 hours peripherally, and the concentration (40 mEq per 100 mL) exceeds the peripheral limit and risks phlebitis and pain, or the rate is miscalculated and runs faster than intended. The mechanism is that the total dose feels standard and the volume feels convenient. The false signal is that 40 mEq is a normal repletion dose, so the order must be safe. The harm is phlebitis and extravasation injury from the high concentration, or, if the rate exceeds the cardiac-safe limit, arrhythmia and arrest. The corrective is to check both the concentration and the rate against the peripheral limits, to dilute or centralize as needed, and to never allow an unmonitored rapid potassium infusion.

### Giving Phosphate and Calcium Together

A patient is hypocalcemic and hypophosphatemic, and both are repleted through the same line or back-to-back without adequate flushing, and the phosphate and calcium precipitate in the line or bind in the serum. The mechanism is that both deficits are real and the orders feel independent. The false signal is that each order is appropriate on its own. The harm is in-line precipitation that can embolize, or symptomatic hypocalcemia induced by the phosphate binding, which can cause tetany or arrhythmia. The corrective is to never co-infuse phosphate and calcium, to separate them in time or line, to recheck ionized calcium after phosphate, and to reduce the phosphate dose in renal impairment.

### Defaulting to Normal Saline for All Resuscitation

Normal saline is chosen for every resuscitation because it is familiar and isotonic, and the patient receives large volumes that cause hyperchloremic metabolic acidosis and, in some populations, worsened renal function. The mechanism is that normal saline feels like the safe default. The false signal is that the patient's blood pressure improved, so the fluid was correct. The harm is that the hyperchloremic acidosis confuses the clinical picture, may contribute to acute kidney injury, and complicates subsequent electrolyte management. The corrective is to consider balanced crystalloids for large-volume resuscitation and to reserve normal saline for situations where its chloride load is tolerable or where its specific content is needed (hyponatremia, metabolic alkalosis).

### Assuming Y-Site Compatibility from a Different Concentration

The pharmacist looks up compatibility for drug A and drug B and finds them compatible, but the reference entry was for a different concentration than the patient's infusion, and the actual combination precipitates. The mechanism is that the compatibility check feels complete. The false signal is that the reference said compatible, so the combination is safe. The harm is an in-line precipitate that occludes the line or embolizes to the patient. The corrective is to match the concentration, vehicle, and contact time in the reference to the actual infusion, and to separate the infusions when the specific combination is not confirmed.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The standard adult repletion is dosed correctly, but the pediatric weight-based dose, the renal-failure reduced dose, or the heart-failure fluid-restricted patient is handled with the same defaults. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary of weight, renal function, or fluid restriction.

## Self-Check

- Did I confirm the sodium correction has an explicit target rate, that sodium is being measured frequently during hypertonic saline, and that the rate stays within the limit to avoid osmotic demyelination?
- Did I check the potassium infusion concentration and rate against the peripheral and central limits, confirm cardiac monitoring for high-rate infusions, and verify renal function and current potassium before dispensing?
- Did I separate phosphate and calcium infusions in time or line, reduce phosphate and magnesium doses for renal impairment, and recheck ionized calcium and magnesium after repletion?
- Did I match the IV fluid to the clinical intent (resuscitation, replacement, maintenance), choose balanced crystalloids for large-volume resuscitation where appropriate, and avoid dextrose fluids for volume expansion?
- Did I calculate the estimated osmolarity of the admixture and confirm central access is in place for any infusion that exceeds the peripheral tolerance?
- Did I check Y-site compatibility for every concurrent infusion, matching the concentration and vehicle to the reference, and separate infusions where compatibility is unconfirmed?
- Did I verify the patient's renal function, current electrolyte values, and volume status before dispensing, and flag any order inconsistent with these to the prescriber?
- Does the output stay within my scope, deferring diagnosis and target correction rates to the prescriber, and escalating to a clinical pharmacist specialist or nephrology where the electrolyte disorder is complex or refractory?
