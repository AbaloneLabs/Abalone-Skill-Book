# IV-to-Oral Conversion and Duration of Therapy Optimization

Antibiotic duration is one of the most modifiable drivers of antimicrobial resistance, toxicity, and C. difficile infection, yet therapy is routinely continued longer than necessary. The pharmacist's role in duration optimization and IV-to-oral conversion is to actively shorten courses to evidence-based endpoints, convert to oral therapy as soon as the patient can absorb it, and challenge the default of "finish the course" when the evidence supports fewer days. The judgment problem is that stopping antibiotics feels riskier than continuing them, and the clinician's defensive reflex runs directly against the patient's interest and the institution's ecology.

## Core Rules

### Convert IV to Oral as Soon as Criteria Are Met

Intravenous antibiotics carry higher cost, higher line-infection risk, and longer hospital stays, and they offer no therapeutic advantage over oral therapy once the patient can absorb oral medications. Convert to oral when the patient is hemodynamically stable, tolerating oral intake or receiving enteral nutrition, and has a functioning gastrointestinal tract. Certain antibiotics — fluoroquinolones, linezolid, fluconazole, metronidazole, doxycycline, and trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole — have oral bioavailability approaching one hundred percent, making them ideal conversion targets. Identify conversion candidates daily and intervene proactively, because the clinical team often does not prioritize conversion once the patient is improving. For patients who do not meet standard conversion criteria but are otherwise improving, consider sequential therapy with a highly bioavailable oral agent.

### Apply Evidence-Based Durations Rather Than Default Rounds

Antibiotic durations should be guided by evidence and clinical response, not by convention or the arbitrary concept of a "complete course." Many common infections require shorter durations than historically taught: community-acquired pneumonia typically requires five days, uncomplicated cystitis three to five days, uncomplicated pyelonephritis seven days, intra-abdominal infection four to seven days after source control, and cellulitis five to six days. Longer is not safer — it increases resistance selection, toxicity, and C. difficile risk without improving outcomes. Use evidence-based duration guidelines, individualize based on clinical response, and document the planned duration at the time of prescribing so that the endpoint is explicit.

### Conduct an Antibiotic Timeout at 48 to 72 Hours

At 48 to 72 hours after antibiotic initiation — the point at which culture results typically return and the patient's clinical trajectory is evident — conduct a formal antibiotic timeout. Reassess every antibiotic: is the infection still suspected, has the diagnosis been confirmed or revised, are culture results available for de-escalation, is the duration still appropriate, can therapy be narrowed, converted, or stopped? The timeout is a structured pause that forces a decision at the moment of maximum information, counteracting the inertia that keeps antibiotics running once started. Document the timeout decision and communicate it to the team.

### Stop Antibiotics When the Diagnosis Is Not Infection

A significant proportion of empiric antibiotics are started for suspected infection that is never confirmed — fever turns out to be drug fever, leukocytosis is steroid-induced, the infiltrate on chest imaging is atelectasis or edema, the positive blood culture was contamination. The trap is continuing antibiotics because "we already started them" or "just to be safe," when the correct action is to stop them once the infectious diagnosis is no longer supported. Review every patient on antibiotics at 48 to 72 hours and ask whether the original indication still holds; if it does not, stop the antibiotics rather than completing an arbitrary course for an infection the patient does not have.

### Shorten Duration Based on Clinical Response

Clinical improvement — defervescence, hemodynamic stability, resolving leukocytosis, improving imaging — should trigger reassessment of whether the full planned duration is still necessary. For many infections, patients who improve rapidly can be treated with shorter courses than those who improve slowly. Use dynamic duration assessment: set an initial target, reassess at intervals, and stop when the patient has achieved the clinical endpoint rather than mechanically completing the original number of days. The evidence increasingly supports shorter durations for responders, and the pharmacist should advocate for stopping at the evidence-based endpoint rather than extending for subjective caution.

### Manage Prophylactic Antibiotics Separately From Therapeutic Courses

Surgical prophylaxis and other prophylactic antibiotic use have specific, short duration recommendations — typically a single preoperative dose with redosing for long procedures, and discontinuation within 24 hours after surgery. Prolonged prophylactic antibiotics do not prevent surgical site infection and select for resistance. Manage prophylaxis separately from therapeutic courses, enforce discontinuation at the recommended endpoint, and do not allow prophylactic antibiotics to drift into de facto therapeutic courses without a new infectious indication.

## Common Traps

### Continuing IV Therapy Because the Patient Is "Still in the Hospital"

The hospital location is not an indication for intravenous therapy. The trap is leaving patients on IV antibiotics throughout their hospital stay because no one evaluates oral conversion, or because the team plans to convert "closer to discharge," or because IV feels more definitive. The harm is prolonged line exposure, increased cost, delayed mobility and discharge readiness, and unnecessary days of higher-concentration antibiotic exposure. The mechanism is that IV-to-oral conversion requires an active decision and no one owns it, while continuing IV requires no decision at all. The corrective is daily review of every patient on IV antibiotics for conversion eligibility and active intervention when criteria are met.

### Extending Duration for Subjective Caution

The defensive reflex to extend antibiotic duration — "let's give it a few more days to be safe," "the patient was pretty sick, let's not stop too early" — feels responsible but is often harmful. The trap is that extending duration does not reduce recurrence risk for most infections once the evidence-based endpoint is reached, but it does increase C. difficile risk, resistance selection, toxicity, and cost. The mechanism is that the clinician perceives the risk of stopping (possible relapse, visible and attributable) but not the risk of continuing (resistance, C. difficile, invisible and diffuse), and so systematically overweighs the former. The corrective is to anchor to evidence-based durations, to treat the endpoint as a clinical decision based on response rather than a feeling, and to recognize that shorter is safer when the evidence supports it.

### Completing an Arbitrary Course for a Non-Existent Infection

When empiric antibiotics are started for suspected infection and the workup reveals a non-infectious cause, the correct action is to stop the antibiotics. The trap is completing the originally planned course — seven days, ten days, fourteen days — for an infection the patient never had, because the antibiotics are already running and stopping feels like giving up on treatment. The harm is full toxicity, resistance, and C. difficile exposure for zero therapeutic benefit. The mechanism is that stopping requires acknowledging the original diagnosis was wrong, which feels like error, while continuing feels like thoroughness. The corrective is to treat the diagnosis, not the prescription, and to stop antibiotics when the indication resolves.

### Treating Inflammatory Markers as Duration Endpoints

C-reactive protein and procalcitonin can support antibiotic decisions, but they are not standalone duration endpoints. The trap is continuing antibiotics until inflammatory markers normalize, which extends therapy beyond the evidence-based duration in patients whose markers lag behind clinical recovery, or who have baseline elevation from non-infectious inflammation. The harm is unnecessarily prolonged therapy driven by a number rather than by clinical judgment. The corrective is to use inflammatory markers as one input alongside clinical signs, to recognize their lag and their non-infectious drivers, and to anchor duration to clinical response and evidence-based endpoints rather than to a laboratory threshold.

### Failing to Enforce Surgical Prophylaxis Discontinuation

Surgical prophylaxis is among the most overused and over-extended antibiotic categories, and the trap is allowing prophylactic antibiotics to continue for 24, 48, or 72 hours postoperatively — or until discharge — despite evidence that continuation beyond the recommended endpoint does not reduce infection and increases resistance. The mechanism is that surgeons may extend prophylaxis out of caution or habit, the pharmacy may not flag prophylactic orders for discontinuation, and no one owns the endpoint. The corrective is to identify prophylactic antibiotic orders, enforce the recommended discontinuation time, and distinguish prophylactic continuation from legitimate therapeutic initiation for a confirmed postoperative infection.

## Self-Check

- Did I evaluate every patient on IV antibiotics daily for oral conversion eligibility, and intervene proactively when criteria are met?
- Did I anchor antibiotic duration to evidence-based endpoints rather than conventional "rounds" or subjective caution?
- Did I conduct a formal antibiotic timeout at 48 to 72 hours, reassessing every antibiotic for continued indication, de-escalation, conversion, and duration?
- Did I stop antibiotics when the original infectious diagnosis was not confirmed, rather than completing an arbitrary course for a non-existent infection?
- Did I reassess duration based on clinical response and shorten courses for rapid responders where evidence supports it?
- Did I manage surgical and other prophylactic antibiotics separately, enforcing discontinuation at the recommended endpoint?
- Did I use inflammatory markers as one input among several rather than as standalone duration endpoints?
- Did I document the planned duration at prescribing so the endpoint is explicit and accountable?
