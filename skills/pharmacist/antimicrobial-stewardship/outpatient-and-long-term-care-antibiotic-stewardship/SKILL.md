# Outpatient and Long-Term Care Antibiotic Stewardship

Most antibiotic use happens outside the hospital — in outpatient clinics, urgent care, and long-term care facilities (LTCFs) — and it is in these settings that stewardship is weakest and overuse is greatest. The pressures are different from inpatient care: visits are short, patients expect a prescription, diagnostic uncertainty is high, and follow-up is uncertain, so antibiotics are prescribed "to be safe" for conditions that are viral, self-limited, or not bacterial at all. In LTCFs, the pressure inverts toward overtreatment of asymptomatic bacteriuria and nonspecific changes in condition. The pharmacist's role is to apply evidence-based prescribing criteria, to resist the default-to-prescribe, to manage the specific overuse syndromes (asymptomatic bacteriuria, viral upper respiratory infections, colonized wounds), and to build the metrics and protocols that make the right choice the easy choice. This skill covers the judgment used to practice and promote stewardship in non-acute settings, where the largest volume of unnecessary antibiotics is generated.

## Core Rules

### Apply Syndrome-Specific Evidence and Duration Limits to Every Prescription

Outpatient antibiotic prescribing should be guided by syndrome-specific evidence, not by habit. For each common syndrome — uncomplicated UTI, acute bacterial rhinosinusitis, streptococcal pharyngitis, community-acquired pneumonia, uncomplicated skin and soft tissue infection — apply the first-line agent, the criteria that justify antibiotics (and the criteria that do not), and the shortest evidence-based duration. Current guidance has shortened durations for many syndromes (for example, shorter courses for uncomplicated cystitis and pneumonia) that prescribers still exceed out of habit. Verify that the prescribed drug, dose, and duration match the syndrome guideline, and intervene when they do not. The duration is as much a stewardship target as the drug choice.

### Do Not Treat Asymptomatic Bacteriuria

Asymptomatic bacteriuria is common, especially in older adults, catheterized patients, and LTCF residents, and it does not require treatment except in pregnancy and before urologic procedures with mucosal bleeding. Treating it does not prevent symptomatic infection, does not improve outcomes, and causes harm: adverse drug effects, C. difficile, selection of resistant organisms, and the false reassurance that something is being done. The trap in LTCFs is that a positive urine culture is obtained for a nonspecific change in condition (confusion, falls, decreased appetite) and treated as a UTI without localizing urinary symptoms. Require localizing genitourinary symptoms (or, in catheterized patients, systemic signs) before treating, and resist culturing stable patients without symptoms.

### Avoid Antibiotics for Viral and Self-Limited Respiratory Infections

Acute bronchitis, viral upper respiratory infection, nonspecific sore throat, and viral rhinosinusitis account for a large share of unnecessary outpatient antibiotics. Apply the criteria that distinguish bacterial from viral (for sinusitis, duration beyond 10 days or worsening after initial improvement; for pharyngitis, a positive rapid strep test or Centor-based testing, not empirical treatment). Use delayed or watchful-waiting prescriptions where appropriate, and provide symptomatic guidance. The expectation that an infection requires an antibiotic is the driver, and addressing that expectation directly — with explanation, a plan, and a safety net — is more effective than silently withholding the prescription.

### Use the Right Diagnostic Test Before the Antibiotic

Stewardship depends on the right test, used correctly. A positive strep test justifies antibiotics for pharyngitis; a negative test does not. A urine culture without symptoms is not actionable. A procalcitonin or inflammatory marker may help where available. Confirm that the diagnostic pathway supports the antibiotic: was the test done, was it the right test, and does the result support a bacterial diagnosis? Avoid treating a positive culture in the absence of compatible symptoms, and avoid treating empirically when a rapid test is available and negative.

### Tailor Stewardship to the Long-Term Care Setting

LTCF stewardship has specific features: a captive population with high colonization rates, frequent transfers that import resistant organisms, diagnostic uncertainty in non-verbal residents, and pressure to "do something" for nonspecific decline. Establish minimum criteria for antibiotic initiation (localizing signs, vital signs, systemic signs), require review of every started antibiotic at 48 to 72 hours for continuation or stop, and build a facility antibiogram to guide empiric choices. The "change in condition" workup should look beyond the urine — evaluate for dehydration, constipation, pain, medication effects, and infection at other sites — before defaulting to a UTI diagnosis and antibiotic.

### Track Stewardship Metrics and Feed Them Back

What is not measured is not improved. Track antibiotic starts per 1000 resident-days (LTCF) or per 1000 visits (outpatient), the proportion of prescriptions that meet criteria, the duration distribution for key syndromes, and the rates of C. difficile and adverse effects. Feed these back to prescribers in peer-comparison format, which is one of the most effective outpatient stewardship interventions. An antibiogram, syndrome-specific order sets, and peer feedback together shift prescribing more than education alone.

### Provide Patient Education and Set Expectations

Much overuse is driven by patient expectation, and expectation is modifiable. Explain the viral or self-limited nature of the illness, the expected course, the symptomatic measures that help, the specific reasons to return (worsening, specific red-flag symptoms), and the harms of unnecessary antibiotics (adverse effects, resistance, C. difficile). A delayed prescription — fill only if symptoms persist or worsen — gives patients a safety net while reducing use. Setting expectations is a clinical intervention, not a courtesy.

## Common Traps

### Asymptomatic Bacteriuria Treated Because the Culture Is Positive

An LTCF resident has a positive urine culture obtained for confusion or a fall, has no localizing urinary symptoms, and is treated for a UTI. The mechanism is that the positive culture is concrete and the nonspecific symptom is hard to leave untreated, so the antibiotic is started to be safe. The false signal is a "treated infection." The harm is that the underlying cause of the decline (dehydration, constipation, medication effect, another infection) goes unaddressed, while the antibiotic causes adverse effects, resistance, and C. difficile risk, and the resident is labeled a "frequent UTI" patient who is repeatedly re-treated. Asymptomatic bacteriuria requires localizing symptoms or systemic signs, not a positive culture alone.

### Antibiotics for Acute Bronchitis or Viral URI

A patient with a cough and clear viral presentation receives an antibiotic because the visit feels incomplete without a prescription. The mechanism is that acute bronchitis produces a prolonged cough that feels like it "needs treatment," and prescribers yield to time pressure and expectation. The false signal is a satisfied patient. The harm is unnecessary exposure, resistance selection, adverse effects, and the reinforcement of the expectation that coughs require antibiotics, which drives future visits and future prescriptions. Acute bronchitis in a healthy adult is viral and does not require antibiotics.

### Duration Driven by Habit Rather Than Evidence

The prescriber defaults to 7, 10, or 14 days because that is the habit, when the evidence supports a shorter course for the syndrome. The mechanism is that round-number durations are memorable and feel safe, and the newer shorter-duration evidence has not displaced them. The false signal is a "standard course." The harm is excess exposure that increases resistance, adverse effects, and C. difficile without improving cure rates. Duration must match the syndrome-specific evidence, which has shortened for many common infections.

### Empiric Treatment When a Rapid Test Is Available

Pharyngitis is treated empirically without a strep test, or sinusitis is treated without meeting diagnostic criteria, because the test or the wait feels like an obstacle. The mechanism is that empiric treatment is faster and avoids a return visit. The false signal is a decision made. The harm is that viral illness is treated with antibiotics, the patient is exposed unnecessarily, and the diagnostic standard is eroded. Where a rapid, actionable test exists, it should guide the decision rather than be bypassed.

### Broad-Spectrum Default in Settings With Limited Diagnostics

In outpatient or LTCF settings with limited diagnostics, prescribers default to broad-spectrum agents "to cover everything," escalating resistance pressure across the population. The mechanism is that uncertainty feels safer with broader coverage, and the local antibiogram that would support narrower choices is absent. The false signal is a "safe" broad choice. The harm is population-level selection of resistance and the displacement of first-line agents that would suffice. First-line narrow agents guided by a facility antibiogram should be the default; broad-spectrum use should be justified and reviewed.

### Stewardship Treated as Education Alone Without Metrics or Feedback

The program distributes guidelines and expects prescribing to improve, without tracking starts, criteria compliance, or peer comparison. The mechanism is that education is easy to deliver and feels sufficient, while measurement and feedback are harder. The false signal is a program that "exists." The harm is that prescribing does not change, because the social and habitual drivers of overuse are unaffected by passive education. Peer-comparison feedback and order-set defaults change prescribing; guidelines alone usually do not.

## Self-Check

- For each outpatient antibiotic, did I verify the drug, dose, and duration match the syndrome-specific guideline, including the shortest evidence-based duration?
- Did I require localizing urinary symptoms (or systemic signs in catheterized patients) before accepting a UTI treatment, and did I avoid treating asymptomatic bacteriuria?
- Did I apply the criteria that distinguish bacterial from viral for respiratory infections, and avoid antibiotics for acute bronchitis and uncomplicated viral URI?
- Did I confirm the right diagnostic test was used and that the result supports a bacterial diagnosis before the antibiotic was continued?
- In an LTCF setting, did I apply minimum criteria for initiation, require 48–72 hour review of every started antibiotic, and use a facility antibiogram for empiric choices?
- Are stewardship metrics tracked and fed back to prescribers in peer-comparison format, alongside an antibiogram and syndrome-specific order sets?
- Did I set patient expectations with explanation of the illness course, symptomatic measures, return precautions, and the harms of unnecessary antibiotics, using delayed prescriptions where appropriate?
