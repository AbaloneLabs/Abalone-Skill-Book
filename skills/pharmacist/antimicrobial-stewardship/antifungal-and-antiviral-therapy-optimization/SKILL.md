# Antifungal and Antiviral Therapy Optimization

Antifungal and antiviral therapy is a domain where the right drug depends on the organism, the host, the syndrome, and a dense web of drug interactions, and where the default or the "one good agent" mindset produces both failure and toxicity. Antifungal choice turns on whether the target is a yeast or a mold, on whether the use is prophylaxis, empiric, or targeted treatment, and on the organ function and interaction profile of the patient. Antiviral therapy turns on the virus, the resistance history, the immune status of the host, and the overlapping toxicities of combination regimens. The pharmacist's role is to match the agent to the organism and the host, to manage the interactions that these drug classes generate in abundance, and to optimize dosing across changing organ function. This skill covers the judgment used to select and optimize antifungal and antiviral therapy so that the organism is covered, the host is protected, and the regimen is deliverable.

## Core Rules

### Match the Antifungal Spectrum to the Organism and the Syndrome

Antifungal coverage is not interchangeable. The echinocandins cover Candida (including most resistant species) but not Cryptococcus, endemic fungi, or molds, and they are first-line for candidemia and invasive candidiasis in most patients. The triazoles vary: fluconazole covers many Candida species but not molds (and resistance is rising, especially in C. glabrata and C. krusei); voriconazole covers Aspergillus and is first-line for invasive aspergillosis; posaconazole and isavuconazole extend mold coverage. Amphotericin B has the broadest coverage but significant toxicity. Confirm the suspected or proven organism and the syndrome, and select an agent whose spectrum matches — empiric Candida therapy in a stable patient is an echinocandin, not a mold-active azole, while suspected aspergillosis requires a mold-active agent. Do not default to fluconazole for empiric coverage when resistant species or molds are plausible.

### Distinguish Prophylaxis, Empiric, and Targeted Treatment

The antifungal role defines the agent, the duration, and the threshold to stop. Prophylaxis (in neutropenic or transplant patients) uses specific agents (often posaconazole or fluconazole) for the duration of risk and stops when risk resolves. Empiric therapy in a febrile neutropenic or critically ill patient covers the likely organisms until diagnostics return, then narrows or stops. Targeted therapy treats a documented infection for a defined duration. Conflating these roles produces overuse (continuing empiric coverage after a negative workup) or underuse (stopping prophylaxis prematurely in a still-immunosuppressed patient). Define the role explicitly at the start and re-evaluate at each milestone.

### Manage the Interaction Profile of Azoles Proactively

The triazole antifungals are among the most prolific interaction generators in medicine, because they inhibit CYP3A4 (strongly, for voriconazole, itraconazole, posaconazole, ketoconazole) and many also require gastric acidity for absorption (itraconazole capsules, posaconazole suspension). Screen the entire medication list for CYP3A4 substrates that will accumulate (statins, benzodiazepines, calcineurin inhibitors, many chemotherapeutics, certain antiarrhythmics that prolong QT), for QT prolongation that the azole will worsen, and for acid-suppressive therapy that will reduce absorption. Therapeutic drug monitoring applies to voriconazole, itraconazole, and posaconazole, with both subtherapeutic and toxic ranges, and levels should be checked because absorption and metabolism are highly variable. Do not start an azole without a full interaction review and a monitoring plan.

### Select and Dose Antiviral Therapy for the Specific Virus and Host

Antiviral therapy is virus-specific. HSV and VZV are treated with acyclovir, valacyclovir, or famciclovir, with dosing adjusted for renal function and for the severity (mucocutaneous versus CNS or disseminated disease, which requires intravenous acyclovir). CMV uses ganciclovir, valganciclovir, foscarnet, or cidofovir, with resistance and toxicity (bone marrow, renal) shaping the choice, and letermovir for prophylaxis in transplant. HIV therapy is combination antiretroviral treatment selected for resistance, baseline regimen, interactions, and tolerability, with the goal of durable suppression. Viral hepatitis therapy (HCV direct-acting agents, HBV nucleos(t)ides) is regimen-specific and curative for HCV. Confirm the virus, the resistance history, the host's immune status, and the organ function before selecting and dosing.

### Adjust Dosing for Renal and Hepatic Function and for Body Weight

Many antifungals and antivirals require renal or hepatic dose adjustment. Acyclovir, ganciclovir, and several antiretrovirals are renally cleared and accumulate to toxicity (neurotoxicity, bone marrow suppression) if not adjusted. Amphotericin B requires renal monitoring and electrolyte replacement. Voriconazole and certain antiretrovirals have hepatic considerations and weight-based or genotypic dosing variability. Confirm the current organ function and adjust; recheck during therapy because function changes. For weight-based dosing in obese patients, apply the agent-specific guidance (some use adjusted body weight, some cap doses), because underdosing in obesity drives failure and resistance.

### Monitor for Efficacy, Toxicity, and Resistance

Therapeutic monitoring in this domain includes both drug levels (voriconazole, posaconazole, itraconazole, some antiretrovirals) and clinical markers (cultures, antigen tests, viral loads, radiographic response, inflammatory markers). Define the monitoring plan at the start: when levels will be drawn, what toxicity markers will be tracked (renal function with amphotericin and foscarnet, bone marrow with ganciclovir and flucytosine, liver function with azoles and certain antiretrovirals), and what defines response and failure. For failing therapy, consider resistance testing (HIV genotype, CMV resistance, Aspergillus azole resistance) rather than empiric escalation, because failure is often resistance, not underdosing.

### Coordinate Prophylaxis With the Immunosuppression Timeline

In transplant and oncology patients, antifungal and antiviral prophylaxis must be coordinated with the immunosuppression timeline: the highest-risk periods (engraftment, graft-versus-host disease, high-dose corticosteroids) dictate when prophylaxis is needed and when it can stop. Prophylaxis continued past the risk period wastes resources and selects resistance; prophylaxis stopped during an ongoing risk period invites breakthrough infection. Align prophylaxis start and stop with the documented immune risk, and adjust when the immune state changes (steroid burst, rejection treatment, engraftment).

## Common Traps

### Fluconazole Defaulted for Empiric Coverage When Molds or Resistant Yeast Are Plausible

A critically ill or neutropenic patient receives fluconazole empirically when the plausible organisms include molds (Aspergillus) or fluconazole-resistant Candida. The mechanism is that fluconazole is familiar, cheap, and well-tolerated, so it is chosen by default. The false signal is "antifungal coverage provided." The harm is that the organism is not covered, therapy fails, and the delay to an effective agent increases mortality in invasive fungal infection. Empiric coverage must match the plausible organisms, which often requires an echinocandin for Candida or a mold-active azole in the right host.

### Azole Started Without an Interaction or QT Review

A triazole is started without reviewing the full medication list, and a CYP3A4 substrate accumulates or the QT prolongs with a co-prescribed antiarrhythmic. The mechanism is that the azole's interaction profile is extensive and easy to overlook when the focus is the infection. The false signal is a single drug added to the list. The harm is statin rhabdomyolysis, calcineurin-inhibitor nephrotoxicity, sedative oversedation, or torsade from QT prolongation. Every azole start requires a full interaction review, QT assessment, and a monitoring plan, with TDM where applicable.

### Antiviral Renal Dose Adjustment Omitted

Acyclovir, ganciclovir, or a renally cleared antiretroviral is dosed at standard levels in a patient with reduced renal function, because the adjustment was not checked or the function changed during therapy. The mechanism is that these drugs are commonly used and the standard dose is memorable, while the renal adjustment requires a current creatinine clearance and re-checking. The false signal is a standard dose. The harm is neurotoxicity (acyclovir, ganciclovir), bone marrow suppression, or nephrotoxicity, which can mimic the underlying disease and delay recognition. Renal function must be confirmed and the dose adjusted, with re-checking during therapy.

### Prophylaxis Continued Past the Risk Period

Antifungal or antiviral prophylaxis is continued after the immunosuppression risk has resolved, because no one revisited the stop date. The mechanism is that prophylaxis is started for a defined risk window but the stop is never scheduled or executed. The false signal is a patient "covered." The harm is unnecessary exposure, cost, toxicity, and resistance selection, and the displacement of attention from active problems. Prophylaxis needs a documented stop tied to the immune risk timeline, with adjustment when the risk changes.

### Therapeutic Drug Monitoring Omitted for Variable-Absorption Azoles

Voriconazole, posaconazole, or itraconazole is started without levels, on the assumption that the prescribed dose yields a therapeutic concentration. The mechanism is that absorption and metabolism of these azoles are highly variable (genotype for voriconazole, formulation and acidity for posaconazole and itraconazole), and the prescribed dose does not predict the level. The false signal is a dose that "should work." The harm is subtherapeutic levels that drive failure and resistance, or supratherapeutic levels that cause neurotoxicity and hepatotoxicity. TDM is indicated for these agents and must be scheduled.

### Failing Therapy Escalated Without Resistance Testing

A patient on antifungal or antiviral therapy who is not responding has the regimen broadened empirically, without resistance testing. The mechanism is that escalation feels active and resistance testing takes time. The false signal is "doing more." The harm is that the true cause of failure (resistance, undrained source, inadequate source control, immune failure) is not addressed, and the patient is exposed to broader, more toxic therapy that still fails. Failing therapy warrants a diagnostic pause for resistance testing and source control reassessment, not reflex escalation.

## Self-Check

- Did I match the antifungal spectrum to the organism and syndrome (yeast versus mold, Candida species, Aspergillus), rather than defaulting to fluconazole?
- Did I define the role as prophylaxis, empiric, or targeted treatment, with a corresponding duration and stop or reassessment point?
- For any azole, did I complete a full CYP3A4 and QT interaction review, address acid-suppression effects on absorption, and schedule therapeutic drug monitoring where indicated?
- Did I select and dose the antiviral for the specific virus (HSV, VZV, CMV, HIV, HCV, HBV), the host's immune status, and the resistance history?
- Did I confirm current renal and hepatic function, adjust dosing accordingly, and plan to recheck during therapy, including weight-based considerations in obesity?
- Did I define the efficacy, toxicity, and resistance monitoring plan, including drug levels, cultures or viral loads, and organ-function markers?
- Is prophylaxis coordinated with the immunosuppression timeline, with a documented stop tied to the risk period rather than continued indefinitely?
