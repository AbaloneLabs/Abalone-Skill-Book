# Empiric Antibiotic Selection and De-escalation

Empiric antibiotic therapy is prescribed before definitive pathogen identification, which means the pharmacist must make a high-stakes bet: cover the likely pathogens adequately enough to treat the infection, while using the narrowest spectrum that does so, while accounting for local resistance patterns, patient-specific risk factors, and the downstream consequence of every unnecessary broad-spectrum day on the hospital's ecology. The judgment problem is that the safe direction (broad coverage) and the responsible direction (narrow coverage) point in opposite directions, and the window for de-escalation — the moment culture results arrive and the bet can be settled — is routinely missed because no one owns it.

## Core Rules

### Anchor Empiric Selection to the Syndrome, the Site, and the Antibiogram

Empiric antibiotic selection is not a matter of personal preference or "what we usually use." It follows from three inputs: the suspected infection syndrome (community-acquired pneumonia, complicated intra-abdominal infection, catheter-associated UTI), the likely pathogens at the infection site, and the local antibiogram — the facility-specific resistance data that tells you what your hospital's organisms actually resist. Use institutional guidelines, which are built from the antibiogram and should reflect current local resistance. Where guidelines do not exist or the patient does not fit the guideline population, reason from first principles: what organisms cause this infection in this patient, what is the local resistance probability for each, and what agent covers them at adequate concentration at the site?

### Escalate Coverage for Sepsis, Immunocompromise, and Resistant-Risk

Default empiric coverage must be broadened when the stakes of inadequate therapy are high. Sepsis or septic shock demands immediate broad-spectrum coverage within the first hour, because each hour of delay in adequate therapy increases mortality. Neutropenic fever, severe immunocompromise, and known colonization with resistant organisms require expanded coverage targeting the resistant threat. The rule is to escalate aggressively at the front end when the risk of under-treatment is high, and then narrow aggressively once data returns — because the harm of inadequate empiric therapy in these populations is immediate and irreversible, while the harm of over-broad therapy is cumulative and manageable.

### De-escalate the Moment Culture and Sensitivity Data Return

De-escalation — narrowing therapy based on culture and sensitivity results — is the single most effective antimicrobial stewardship intervention, and it is the most commonly missed. The moment final culture and sensitivity results return, reassess every antibiotic the patient is receiving: stop unnecessary agents, narrow broad-spectrum agents to targeted therapy, convert intravenous to oral where the patient meets criteria, and discontinue therapy that is no longer indicated. Do not wait for the clinical team to initiate de-escalation, because they often will not — own this transition actively. Track culture results daily for every patient on broad-spectrum therapy, and intervene when results return.

### Apply PK/PD Principles to Optimize Dosing

Antibiotic efficacy depends not only on choosing the right drug but on achieving the right exposure at the infection site, and exposure depends on pharmacokinetic and pharmacodynamic (PK/PD) principles that vary by drug class. Beta-lactams require time above the minimum inhibitory concentration (T>MIC), which means more frequent dosing, extended infusion, or continuous infusion for severe infections. Aminoglycosides and fluoroquinolones require peak concentration to MIC ratios (Cmax/MIC). Vancomycin requires area-under-the-curve to MIC ratios (AUC/MIC). Apply these principles to dosing, particularly in severe infection, obese patients, renal impairment, and critically ill patients whose volumes of distribution and clearance are unpredictable. Standard doses achieve standard exposures in standard patients, and critically ill patients are not standard.

### Account for Allergy, Renal Function, and Drug Interactions in Every Adjustment

Every antibiotic adjustment — initial selection, de-escalation, dose change — must be filtered through patient-specific constraints. Reassess the allergy history: a reported penicillin allergy is often not a true IgE-mediated allergy, and delabeling or cross-reactivity assessment can unlock first-line beta-lactam therapy that is more effective and less toxic than alternatives. Adjust for renal function at every change, because renal function fluctuates in acute illness and a dose correct at admission may become toxic or subtherapeutic days later. Screen for drug interactions, because antibiotics interact with warfarin, oral contraceptives, calcineurin inhibitors, and many others, and the interaction profile changes with every adjustment.

### Document the Rationale and Set an Automatic Stop Date

Every antibiotic decision should be documented with its rationale — the suspected infection, the empiric choice and why, the plan for de-escalation, and the expected duration. Set an automatic stop date or reassessment date at the time of prescribing, because antibiotics without a defined endpoint tend to continue indefinitely. Documentation supports continuity across handoffs, enables prospective audit by the stewardship team, and creates accountability for the de-escalation decision when culture data return.

## Common Traps

### Continuing Broad-Spectrum Therapy After Culture Results Return

The most common and most harmful stewardship failure is continuing empiric broad-spectrum therapy — vancomycin plus piperacillin-tazobactam, the ubiquitous "vanc-zosyn" default — after culture results identify the organism and demonstrate susceptibility to a much narrower agent. The mechanism of the trap is diffusion of responsibility: the prescriber assumes the pharmacist will flag it, the pharmacist assumes the team will narrow at rounds, the team assumes someone else ordered the de-escalation, and the patient accumulates unnecessary broad-spectrum days that drive resistance, C. difficile risk, and cost. The harm is ecological and individual — the patient on unnecessary broad-spectrum therapy disrupts their own microbiome, risks nephrotoxicity and secondary infections, and contributes to the selection pressure that creates the next resistant organism. The corrective is for the pharmacist to own de-escalation actively, track pending cultures daily, and intervene the moment results return rather than waiting for the clinical team to notice.

### Treating Colonization Instead of Infection

Not every positive culture represents infection. Positive urine cultures in catheterized patients without symptoms represent asymptomatic bacteriuria, which should not be treated except in pregnancy and before urologic procedures. Positive sputum cultures may represent colonization rather than pneumonia. Positive blood cultures with skin flora may represent contamination rather than true bacteremia. The trap is treating the culture result rather than the clinical syndrome, which exposes the patient to unnecessary antibiotics, drives resistance, and delays the search for the actual cause of the patient's symptoms. The mechanism is that a positive culture feels like actionable data, and the path of least resistance is to treat it, while the disciplined path — correlating the culture with clinical signs, inflammatory markers, and the patient's trajectory — requires more effort. The corrective is to always correlate culture results with clinical presentation before continuing or adjusting therapy.

### Defaulting to the Broadest Coverage "Just in Case"

The defensive reflex to prescribe the broadest available coverage — "to be safe," "just in case," "we don't want to miss anything" — is understandable but counterproductive. The trap is that broad coverage that is unnecessary still carries full toxicity, resistance, and C. difficile risk, and the "safety" it provides is illusory because it covers organisms the patient almost certainly does not have while creating the conditions for the patient to acquire organisms that no agent covers. The harm is that the cumulative effect of thousands of "just in case" decisions across a hospital is the selection of multidrug-resistant organisms that then spread to other patients. The corrective is to match coverage to the actual risk: adequate coverage for the likely pathogens, escalation only where sepsis or resistant-risk justifies it, and trust that de-escalation will narrow once data returns.

### Ignoring the Antibiogram in Favor of Personal Experience

Pharmacists and prescribers who have practiced at an institution for years often rely on their experience of "what works here," but local resistance patterns shift over time, and experience accumulated five years ago may no longer reflect current reality. The trap is selecting empiric therapy based on memory and habit rather than the current antibiogram, particularly when the antibiogram shows resistance rates that have crept upward unnoticed. The harm is inadequate empiric therapy — the patient receives an agent to which their organism is resistant, losing the critical early window of adequate treatment. The corrective is to consult the current antibiogram for every empiric decision, to participate in maintaining and updating the antibiogram, and to treat personal experience as a hypothesis to be checked against data rather than a substitute for it.

### Failing to Adjust Dosing as Renal Function Changes

Acute illness causes renal function to fluctuate, and a dose that was appropriate at admission may become nephrotoxic or subtherapeutic days later as renal function declines or improves. The trap is setting a renally-adjusted dose at admission and not reassessing it, or setting a standard dose and not noticing when the patient's creatinine begins to rise. The harm is concentration-dependent toxicity — vancomycin nephrotoxicity, aminoglycoside ototoxicity and nephrotoxicity — or subtherapeutic exposure that fails to treat the infection and selects for resistance. The corrective is to reassess renal function at every antibiotic adjustment and at least every few days for patients on renally-cleared agents, and to adjust doses proactively rather than reactively.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- Did I anchor empiric selection to the infection syndrome, the likely pathogens at the site, and the current local antibiogram rather than personal preference or habit?
- Did I escalate coverage appropriately for sepsis, immunocompromise, neutropenic fever, or known resistant colonization, while planning to narrow once data returns?
- Did I track culture results daily and de-escalate immediately when final results returned — stopping unnecessary agents, narrowing spectrum, converting IV to oral where appropriate?
- Did I apply PK/PD principles to dosing, particularly for severe infection, critical illness, obesity, and renal impairment, rather than defaulting to standard doses?
- Did I correlate every positive culture with the clinical syndrome before treating, and avoid treating colonization or contamination as infection?
- Did I reassess allergy history, renal function, and drug interactions at every antibiotic adjustment, not just at initial selection?
- Did I document the rationale for empiric selection and set an automatic stop or reassessment date?
- Did I consult the current antibiogram rather than relying on accumulated personal experience?
