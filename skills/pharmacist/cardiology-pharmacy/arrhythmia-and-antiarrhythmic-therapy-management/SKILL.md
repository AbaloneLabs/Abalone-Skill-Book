# Arrhythmia and Antiarrhythmic Therapy Management

Antiarrhythmic therapy is the most paradoxical area of cardiovascular pharmacy, because the very drugs used to suppress arrhythmias can themselves generate new and more dangerous arrhythmias, and the line between the therapeutic and the toxic effect is narrow and patient-specific. The judgment problem is that the antiarrhythmic selection is never just about suppressing the rhythm: it is about matching the drug's efficacy and its proarrhythmic profile to the patient's structural heart disease, their QT interval, their electrolyte status, and their concurrent medications, and a drug that is safe and effective in one patient is lethal in another. The harm is twofold: the drug that prolongs the QT in the patient with the predisposition produces the torsades de pointes and the sudden death, and the drug that is contraindicated in the structural heart disease (the flecainide in the coronary disease) produces the fatal ventricular arrhythmia. The pharmacist's discipline is to screen for the structural heart disease and the QT risk before the selection, to manage the drug interactions and the electrolytes that amplify the proarrhythmia, to monitor the electrocardiogram and the drug levels where required, and to coordinate the rhythm control with the anticoagulation that prevents the stroke.

## Core Rules

### Screen for the Structural Heart Disease Before Selecting the Antiarrhythmic

The single most important determinant of the antiarrhythmic safety is the presence or the absence of the structural heart disease, because several antiarrhythmics are contraindicated in the structural disease and others are preferred. The pharmacist must determine whether the patient has the structural heart disease (the coronary artery disease, the prior myocardial infarction, the heart failure with the reduced ejection fraction, the significant valvular disease, the left ventricular hypertrophy), because the sodium channel blockers (the flecainide, the propafenone) are contraindicated in the coronary artery disease and the structural disease due to the increased mortality from the fatal ventricular arrhythmias. The pharmacist must know that the amiodarone and the sotalol are the options for the structural heart disease, that the dofetilide is the option for the heart failure with the reduced ejection fraction (with the rigorous inpatient initiation), and that the flecainide and the propafenone are reserved for the patients with the normal hearts. The pharmacist must not recommend the sodium channel blocker without confirming the absence of the structural disease, because the fatal ventricular arrhythmia in the diseased heart is the consequence.

### Manage the QT Prolongation and the Torsades Risk for Every QT-Active Drug

Several antiarrhythmics prolong the QT interval (the sotalol, the dofetilide, the amiodarone, the quinidine, the procainamide, the ibutilide), and the QT prolongation carries the risk of the torsades de pointes, the polymorphic ventricular tachycardia that can degenerate into the ventricular fibrillation and the sudden death. The pharmacist must assess the baseline QT interval before the initiation of any QT-active drug, must correct it for the heart rate (the QTc), and must recognize that the baseline QTc above the threshold (commonly above 450 milliseconds in the men and 470 in the women, and certainly above 500) is the contraindication or the caution. The pharmacist must manage the factors that amplify the QT risk during the therapy: the hypokalemia and the hypomagnesemia (the electrolyte abnormalities that must be corrected and maintained), the bradycardia (the slow heart rate that lengthens the QT), the drug interactions with the other QT-prolonging drugs, and the renal dysfunction that accumulates the renally cleared antiarrhythmics (the sotalol, the dofetilide). The pharmacist must monitor the QTc during the therapy, must hold or reduce the drug when the QTc exceeds the threshold, and must recognize that the amiodarone, despite the substantial QT prolongation, has the lower torsades risk than the other QT-active drugs due to its additional electrophysiologic effects.

### Manage the Drug Interactions That Amplify the Proarrhythmia

The antiarrhythmic proarrhythmia is often the result of the drug interactions, and the pharmacist must screen for the interactions at the initiation and whenever a new drug is added. The pharmacist must screen for the additive QT prolongation (the combination of the antiarrhythmic with the other QT-prolonging drugs: the certain antibiotics, the antipsychotics, the antidepressants, the antiemetics, the methadone), and must avoid or minimize these combinations. The pharmacist must screen for the interactions that raise the antiarrhythmic levels (the amiodarone is the potent inhibitor of the cytochrome P450 and the P-glycoprotein, and it raises the levels of the warfarin, the digoxin, the statins, and the other antiarrhythmics, requiring the dose reductions), and the interactions that alter the metabolism (the rifampin and the phenytoin lower the amiodarone levels). The pharmacist must screen for the additive atrioventricular node suppression (the beta-blockers, the non-dihydropyridine calcium channel blockers, and the digoxin combined with the antiarrhythmics produce the bradycardia and the heart block). The pharmacist must manage the interactions proactively, because the proarrhythmia from the interaction is the preventable harm.

### Choose Between the Rate Control and the Rhythm Control for the Atrial Fibrillation

The atrial fibrillation management offers the two strategies: the rate control (the beta-blocker, the non-dihydropyridine calcium channel blocker, the digoxin) that controls the ventricular rate without restoring the sinus rhythm, and the rhythm control (the antiarrhythmic, the cardioversion, the ablation) that restores and maintains the sinus rhythm. The pharmacist must know that the choice depends on the patient's symptoms, the duration, the comorbidities, and the goals, and that the rate control is the adequate strategy for many patients (particularly the older and the asymptomatic), while the rhythm control is favored for the symptomatic, the younger, the heart failure, and the new-onset patients. The pharmacist must recognize that the rhythm control does not eliminate the need for the anticoagulation (the stroke risk persists even in the apparent sinus rhythm due to the recurrences), and that the rate control must achieve the adequate rate (the resting and the exertional) to control the symptoms and to prevent the tachycardia-induced cardiomyopathy. The pharmacist must manage the rate control agents with the attention to the hypotension, the bradycardia, and the decompensation in the heart failure (the non-dihydropyridine calcium channel blockers are contraindicated in the reduced ejection fraction).

### Coordinate the Anticoagulation With the Rhythm Management

The atrial fibrillation carries the stroke risk, and the anticoagulation is the therapy that prevents the stroke, and it must be coordinated with the rhythm management. The pharmacist must assess the stroke risk (the CHA2DS2-VASc score) and the bleeding risk (the bleeding score), and must ensure the appropriate anticoagulation for the stroke risk, independent of the rate or the rhythm strategy. The pharmacist must coordinate the anticoagulation around the cardioversion (the adequate anticoagulation for the three weeks before and the four weeks after, or the transesophageal echocardiogram-guided approach), because the cardioversion of the un-anticoagulated patient produces the thromboembolic stroke. The pharmacist must recognize that the amiodarone interacts with the warfarin (the marked increase in the international normalized ratio, requiring the warfarin dose reduction) and the dabigatran and the apixaban (the P-glycoprotein interaction, with the caution), and must manage these interactions. The pharmacist must ensure the continuity of the anticoagulation across the transitions of the care, because the gap in the anticoagulation is the stroke.

### Respect Scope and Escalation Boundaries

Know where the agent's authority and competence end. The antiarrhythmic selection, the cardioversion, the ablation, and the management of the device therapy require the cardiologist and the electrophysiologist. When the question requires a license, a specialist's judgment, a final approval, or expertise the agent does not hold, the correct action is to escalate rather than to produce a confident answer that overreaches. Scope discipline protects the recipient from harm caused by an unqualified conclusion and protects the agent from liability. State explicitly when the output is advisory and must be confirmed by the qualified person.

## Common Traps

### Prescribing the Flecainide or the Propafenone in the Structural Heart Disease

The patient with the coronary artery disease or the reduced ejection fraction receives the flecainide or the propafenone for the atrial fibrillation, and the fatal ventricular arrhythmia occurs. The mechanism is that the drug suppresses the atrial arrhythmia and feels effective. The false signal is that the rhythm is controlled. The harm is that the sodium channel blockers increase the mortality in the structural heart disease due to the proarrhythmic ventricular effect, and the contraindication is the hard safety boundary. The pharmacist must confirm the absence of the structural disease before the sodium channel blocker.

### Initiating the Sotalol or the Dofetilide Without the QT and the Electrolyte Assessment

The patient receives the sotalol or the dofetilide without the baseline QTc check or the electrolyte correction, and the torsades de pointes occurs. The mechanism is that the drug is initiated as the routine antiarrhythmic. The false signal is that the drug is the standard therapy. The harm is that the sotalol and the dofetilide prolong the QT and cause the torsades, particularly in the hypokalemia, the bradycardia, and the renal dysfunction, and the unmonitored initiation produces the fatal arrhythmia. The pharmacist must assess the QTc and the electrolytes before and during the therapy, and the dofetilide requires the inpatient initiation.

### Missing the Amiodarone Drug Interactions

The patient on the warfarin or the digoxin is started on the amiodarone, and the international normalized ratio rises dangerously or the digoxin reaches the toxicity, because the amiodarone inhibits the metabolism and the transport. The mechanism is that the amiodarone is the potent inhibitor of the cytochrome P450 and the P-glycoprotein. The false signal is that the amiodarone is the safe and the effective antiarrhythmic. The harm is that the amiodarone raises the levels of the warfarin (the bleeding), the digoxin (the toxicity), the statins (the myopathy), and the other drugs, and the unmanaged interaction produces the toxicity that is attributed to the other drug rather than to the amiodarone. The pharmacist must reduce the warfarin, the digoxin, and the statin doses when the amiodarone is initiated.

### Combining the QT-Active Antiarrhythmic With the Other QT-Prolonging Drug

The patient on the sotalol is prescribed the fluoroquinolone or the antipsychotic that also prolongs the QT, and the torsades occurs. The mechanism is that the additive QT prolongation is not screened. The false signal is that the individual drugs are tolerable. The harm is that the combination of the QT-prolonging drugs multiplies the torsades risk, and the antibiotic or the antipsychotic that is benign alone becomes dangerous with the antiarrhythmic. The pharmacist must screen for the additive QT prolongation and must avoid or minimize the combination.

### Discontinuing the Anticoagulation After the Successful Rhythm Control

The patient whose atrial fibrillation is converted to the sinus rhythm with the cardioversion or the ablation has the anticoagulation discontinued, and the stroke occurs. The mechanism is that the sinus rhythm is restored, so the stroke risk is assumed gone. The false signal is that the normal rhythm means the normal stroke risk. The harm is that the atrial fibrillation recurs silently in many patients, and the stroke risk persists even in the apparent sinus rhythm, so the anticoagulation must continue based on the stroke risk score, not on the rhythm. The pharmacist must ensure the anticoagulation continues based on the risk score.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- Did I confirm the presence or the absence of the structural heart disease (the coronary disease, the reduced ejection fraction, the valvular disease) before selecting the antiarrhythmic, and did I avoid the sodium channel blockers (the flecainide, the propafenone) in the structural disease?
- For every QT-active drug (the sotalol, the dofetilide, the amiodarone), did I assess the baseline QTc, correct and maintain the potassium and the magnesium, account for the renal function, and monitor the QTc during the therapy?
- Did I screen for the additive QT prolongation (the antibiotics, the antipsychotics, the antidepressants, the antiemetics, the methadone) and avoid or minimize the combinations?
- Did I screen for the amiodarone interactions (the warfarin, the digoxin, the statins, the other antiarrhythmics) and reduce the doses of the affected drugs when the amiodarone was initiated?
- Did I ensure the rate control achieves the adequate resting and the exertional rate, and did I avoid the non-dihydropyridine calcium channel blockers in the reduced ejection fraction?
- Did I coordinate the anticoagulation with the rhythm management, ensuring the adequate anticoagulation around the cardioversion and the continuation of the anticoagulation based on the stroke risk score rather than on the rhythm?
- Does the output stay within the agent's scope, deferring the antiarrhythmic selection, the cardioversion, the ablation, and the device therapy to the cardiologist and the electrophysiologist where the question exceeds the agent's competence?
- Is the antiarrhythmic regimen documented clearly enough that the prescriber, the patient, and the monitoring team can follow the drug, the QTc, the electrolytes, and the interactions?
