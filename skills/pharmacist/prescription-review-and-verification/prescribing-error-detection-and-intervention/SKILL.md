# Prescribing Error Detection and Intervention

Detecting a prescribing error is only the first half of the pharmacist's safety role. The second half — what to do about it — is where the judgment lies. An error that is detected but not acted upon is functionally identical to an error that was never detected: the patient receives the wrong medication either way. The danger is that pharmacists identify problems but then defer to the prescriber's authority, release the medication with a note, or delay the intervention until it is too late. The pharmacist's professional obligation is not merely to notice errors but to ensure they are corrected before the medication reaches the patient, and this requires a willingness to interrupt prescribers, hold medications, and escalate when a prescriber is unreachable or unresponsive.

## Core Rules

### Classify the Error by Severity Before Choosing an Intervention

Not all prescribing errors require the same response. The pharmacist must first classify the error by its potential clinical consequence: is this a life-threatening error (tenfold overdose of a narrow-therapeutic-index drug, a severe allergy contraindication, a look-alike drug-name confusion involving a high-alert medication), a harmful error (therapeutic duplication, moderate interaction, incorrect but not dangerous dose), or a minor error (non-standard frequency, incomplete directions)? The intervention must match the severity. A life-threatening error requires holding the medication immediately and contacting the prescriber before any dispensing occurs. A harmful error requires clarification before dispensing but may allow a brief window for prescriber contact. A minor error may be resolved with pharmacist-level adjustment and documentation. Misclassifying a severe error as minor — because the drug is common or the dose "looks normal" — is the most dangerous classification failure.

### Hold the Medication Before Investigating, Not After

When a potential error is detected, the default action is to stop the dispensing process, not to continue while investigating. The medication should be quarantined — physically or electronically removed from the workflow — until the question is resolved. The reason is that the investigation takes time, and during that time the medication can be dispensed, administered, or consumed if it remains in the active workflow. The pharmacist must resist the pressure to "keep the line moving" and deal with the problem later. A held medication can always be released; a released medication cannot be un-administered.

### Contact the Right Person Through the Right Channel

When a prescribing error requires clarification, the pharmacist must contact the prescriber who wrote the order, not a covering provider, a nurse, or a medical assistant who may relay the message incorrectly. The contact must be direct — a phone call for urgent issues, a secure message for non-urgent clarifications — and the conversation must be documented. Leaving a message with a third party and assuming it will be relayed is not an intervention; it is a deferral. If the prescriber is unreachable, the pharmacist must contact the covering prescriber or the on-call provider, and must escalate to a supervisor or medical director if no prescriber can be reached and the medication is urgent.

### Document the Error, the Intervention, and the Resolution

Every prescribing error that the pharmacist detects and intervenes on must be documented with three elements: what the error was (the specific problem with the prescription), what the pharmacist did (the intervention, including who was contacted and when), and what the resolution was (the corrected prescription, the alternative therapy, or the decision to hold). This documentation serves three purposes: it creates a record that protects the patient and the pharmacist if an adverse event occurs, it enables the organization to track error patterns and improve systems, and it fulfills the pharmacist's professional and legal obligation to document clinical interventions. A pharmacist who detects an error, corrects it verbally, and documents nothing has no evidence that the intervention occurred.

### Escalate When the Prescriber Disagrees or Is Unreachable

There are situations where the prescriber confirms the order despite the pharmacist's concern, or where the prescriber cannot be reached at all. In these cases, the pharmacist must exercise independent professional judgment. If the pharmacist believes the medication will cause harm, they have both the right and the obligation to refuse to dispense. This refusal must be communicated to the prescriber with the clinical reasoning, documented thoroughly, and escalated to the pharmacist-in-charge or pharmacy manager. The pharmacist must not dispense a medication they believe is dangerous simply because the prescriber insists — the pharmacist's license and the patient's safety both depend on the willingness to hold the line.

## Common Traps

### The Deference Trap — Noticing the Error but Trusting the Prescriber

The pharmacist detects a potential error — a dose that seems high, an interaction that seems significant — and contacts the prescriber. The prescriber says "I know, I intended that." The pharmacist, reassured by the prescriber's confidence, releases the medication. The trap mechanism is that the prescriber's confidence is not evidence of correctness — the prescriber may not have reviewed the patient's current labs, may not be aware of a new medication added by another provider, or may be relying on outdated information. The harm is that the pharmacist's independent clinical judgment is overridden by social authority, and the safety net collapses at the moment it was most needed. The pharmacist must evaluate the prescriber's reasoning, not just accept their conclusion — if the prescriber cannot explain why the dose is safe for this patient, the pharmacist's concern is not resolved.

### The "I'll Note It and Move On" Deferral

The pharmacist detects a minor issue — a slightly non-standard frequency, a potential but low-severity interaction — and rather than holding the medication and contacting the prescriber, adds a note to the profile and dispenses the medication. The trap is that the note creates an illusion of intervention: the pharmacist feels they have "addressed" the issue because they documented it. But the patient still receives the medication, the prescriber is never informed, and the note sits unread in the profile. The harm is that a problem that could have been corrected at the pharmacy level becomes an adverse event that the note does nothing to prevent. Documentation is not intervention — it is the record of an intervention that should have occurred.

### The Relay Message Assumption

The pharmacist calls the prescriber's office, reaches a medical assistant, leaves a message, and considers the intervention complete. The trap mechanism is that relayed messages are frequently distorted, delayed, or lost. The medical assistant may not convey the clinical urgency, the prescriber may not return the call, and the pharmacist assumes the message was delivered and acted upon. The harm is that a clarification that was never received by the prescriber is treated as a resolved intervention, and the medication — which was never actually clarified — is dispensed on the assumption that silence means consent. The pharmacist must confirm that the prescriber received and understood the message, not merely that a message was left.

### The Urgency Compression — Treating All Clarifications as Equally Non-Urgent

Under workflow pressure, the pharmacist begins to treat all clarifications the same way: leave a message, continue dispensing other orders, and wait for a callback. The trap is that this uniform approach fails to distinguish between a non-urgent clarification (incomplete directions on a maintenance medication) and an urgent one (a suspected tenfold overdose of a pediatric chemotherapy agent). The harm is that the urgent clarification is handled at the same pace as the non-urgent one, and the window for preventing harm closes while the pharmacist waits for a callback that may not come for hours. The pharmacist must triage clarifications by clinical urgency and use direct, real-time communication for any error with potential for serious harm.

## Self-Check

- Did I classify the detected error by severity before choosing my intervention, or did I apply the same response regardless of clinical consequence?
- Did I hold the medication before investigating, or did I leave it in the active workflow while I looked into the problem?
- Did I contact the prescriber directly (not through a relay), and did I document who I spoke with, what was discussed, and what changed?
- If the prescriber confirmed the order despite my concern, did I evaluate their clinical reasoning, or did I defer to their authority without verifying the basis for their decision?
- Did I treat documentation as the intervention itself, or did I document an actual intervention (hold, contact, resolution)?
- For urgent errors, did I use real-time communication (phone call), or did I leave a message and assume it would be returned in time?
