# Dose Calculation and Unit Verification

Dose errors are among the most common and most dangerous medication errors because they survive every downstream check. A wrong dose that is clearly written, properly labeled, and correctly dispensed will still harm the patient — the clarity of the error makes it invisible to form-based verification. The pharmacist's role is to treat every dose as a calculation to be verified, not a number to be transcribed. The danger is that dose checking becomes a visual match — "does the prescription match the label?" — rather than a clinical verification — "is this number correct for this patient?" When the pharmacist checks only whether the numbers agree across documents, the dose that was wrong at the point of prescribing remains wrong at the point of dispensing, and the safety net has failed without anyone noticing.

## Core Rules

### Treat Every Dose as a Calculation, Not a Transcription

A dose on a prescription is a number that was produced by a process — a prescriber's mental calculation, a reference to a dosing guideline, or a copy from a previous order. That process can be wrong. The pharmacist must independently verify that the dose is correct for the patient, not merely that it matches what was written. This means checking the dose against the recommended range for the indication, confirming the calculation for weight-based dosing (mg/kg), and verifying that the total daily dose, not just the per-dose amount, falls within safe limits. A dose that "looks right" because it is a round number or a common dose may still be wrong if the patient's weight, renal function, or indication requires a different amount.

### Verify Units at Every Step

Medication doses are expressed in multiple units — mg, mcg, mL, units, milliunits, mEq — and the most catastrophic errors occur at unit boundaries. A prescription for "500 mcg" that is dispensed as "500 mg" represents a thousand-fold overdose. The pharmacist must confirm the unit at every transfer point: from prescription to label, from label to product selection, and from product to final check. High-alert units include insulin (measured in units, not mg), heparin (units, with multiple strengths in identical vials), and electrolytes (mEq, where concentrated vials are easily confused with premixed bags). When the unit is ambiguous or could be misread, the pharmacist must resolve the ambiguity before dispensing — never assume the unit based on the drug or the dose magnitude.

### Confirm Weight-Based Dosing Against Actual Body Weight

Weight-based dosing (mg/kg) requires an accurate, current patient weight. The pharmacist must verify that the weight used in the calculation is current — weights in the electronic record may be months old, estimated, or entered in the wrong unit (kg vs lb). For pediatric patients, the weight must be confirmed at every visit, as children grow and a dose that was correct three months ago may now be subtherapeutic. For adults, the pharmacist must distinguish between actual body weight and ideal body weight, as certain drugs (digoxin, vancomycin, aminoglycosides) require dosing based on ideal or adjusted body weight to avoid toxicity in obese patients. A weight-based dose calculated on a stale or incorrect weight is a calculation error disguised as a correct calculation.

### Check the Total Daily Dose, Not Just the Per-Dose Amount

A single dose may fall within the safe range while the total daily dose exceeds it. This happens when the frequency is increased, when multiple orders for the same drug exist, or when the patient is taking the same drug under different brand names. The pharmacist must calculate the total daily dose by multiplying the per-dose amount by the frequency and comparing the result to the maximum daily limit. This check is especially important for drugs with narrow therapeutic indices (warfarin, phenytoin, lithium) and for over-the-counter medications containing the same active ingredient (acetaminophen in combination products, where the total daily dose from all sources must not exceed 4 grams).

### Apply Pediatric and Geriatric Dose Adjustments Proactively

Standard adult dosing does not apply to the extremes of age. Pediatric patients require weight-based dosing with age-specific maximums, and certain drugs are contraindicated entirely in specific pediatric age groups (tetracyclines in children under 8, fluoroquinolones generally avoided in children). Geriatric patients often require reduced doses due to decreased renal clearance, increased sensitivity (lower starting doses for antipsychotics, benzodiazepines, opioids), and higher risk of adverse effects. The pharmacist must apply these adjustments proactively — not wait for the prescriber to specify a reduced dose, but recommend the adjustment when the standard dose is prescribed for a patient whose age requires modification.

## Common Traps

### The Round Number Illusion

Doses that are round numbers — 500 mg, 1 tablet, 10 mL — feel correct because they are familiar and clean. The pharmacist's brain recognizes the number as a "normal dose" and skips the calculation. The trap mechanism is that familiarity produces a false sense of verification: the number looks right, so it must be right. But a round number can be a miscalculation that happened to land on a familiar figure, or a dose that is standard for one indication but excessive for another (prednisone 40 mg daily is standard for an acute exacerbation but excessive for chronic maintenance). The harm is that the dose that feels most routine receives the least scrutiny, and the calculation that should have been performed is skipped because the answer "looks obvious."

### The Leading Zero and Trailing Zero Trap

Decimal errors are among the most dangerous dose errors because they change the dose by a factor of ten. A dose written as ".5 mg" instead of "0.5 mg" can be misread as 5 mg — a tenfold overdose. A dose written as "1.0 mg" can have the trailing zero missed, resulting in 10 mg. The trap is that these notations look normal to a pharmacist who is reading quickly — the brain fills in the intended meaning and does not register the ambiguity. The harm is that a tenfold dose error, if it reaches the patient, produces toxicity or treatment failure that is dramatic and immediate. The pharmacist must enforce safe decimal notation (always use a leading zero, never use a trailing zero) and must treat any ambiguous decimal as a clarification-required prescription, not a dispensable order.

### The Stale Weight Assumption

Weight-based dosing requires a current weight, but the weight in the electronic health record may be days, weeks, or months old. In pediatric patients, a weight from three months ago may no longer be accurate — a child who has grown 2 kg receives a subtherapeutic dose if the old weight is used. The trap is that the system displays a weight, the pharmacist uses it without questioning its currency, and the calculation is "correct" relative to the weight used but wrong for the patient's actual current weight. The harm is a dose that is silently subtherapeutic (treatment failure, resistance development for antibiotics) or silently toxic (if the patient has lost weight due to illness). The pharmacist must verify weight currency, especially for pediatric and oncology patients, and must not treat a displayed weight as automatically current.

### Maximum Daily Dose Invisibility

The per-dose amount is prominent on every prescription; the maximum daily dose is not. The pharmacist verifies that the individual dose is within range but does not always calculate the total daily exposure. The trap mechanism is that the safety check focuses on the visible number (the per-dose amount) and skips the invisible calculation (per-dose times frequency). When a prescriber increases the frequency without reducing the per-dose amount — or when a patient is taking the same drug through multiple prescriptions — the total daily dose can exceed the safe maximum while every individual dose appears correct. The harm is cumulative toxicity from a drug that was "verified" at every step, because no step checked the aggregate.

## Self-Check

- Did I independently verify the dose calculation against the patient's actual current weight, or did I accept the prescribed number as a transcription?
- Did I confirm the unit at every transfer point (mg vs mcg, units vs mg, mL vs mg), especially for high-alert drugs like insulin and heparin?
- Did I calculate the total daily dose (per-dose times frequency) and compare it to the maximum daily limit, or did I check only the per-dose amount?
- Did I verify that the weight used for weight-based dosing is current and in the correct unit (kg vs lb)?
- Did I check for decimal notation errors (missing leading zero, trailing zero) and treat any ambiguity as a clarification requirement?
- Did I apply pediatric or geriatric dose adjustments proactively, or did I wait for the prescriber to specify a modified dose?
