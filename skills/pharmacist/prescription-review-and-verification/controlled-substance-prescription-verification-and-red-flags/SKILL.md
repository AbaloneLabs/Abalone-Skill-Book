# Controlled Substance Prescription Verification and Red Flags

Verifying a controlled substance prescription is unlike verifying an ordinary prescription, because the drug itself is the hazard as well as the treatment. A legitimate opioid, stimulant, or benzodiazepine order relieves suffering; the same order, written or filled in service of misuse, diversion, or dependence, causes harm that the pharmacist is legally and professionally accountable for catching. The difficulty is that almost no single red flag is, by itself, proof of illegitimacy — pain patients cash-pay, travelers lose medications, and dose escalations occur legitimately — so the pharmacist must weigh patterns rather than single signals, must consult the prescription drug monitoring program (PDMP) as a routine rather than an exception, and must refuse to dispense when the totality points to a prescription that is not for a legitimate medical purpose. The opposite error is equally dangerous: reflexively refusing or stigmatizing legitimate chronic-pain and ADHD patients, denying therapy to people who need it. This skill covers the judgment used to separate a legitimate controlled-substance prescription from one that should be held, clarified, or refused.

## Core Rules

### Confirm the Prescription Is for a Legitimate Medical Purpose in the Usual Course of Practice

The legal and clinical foundation of every controlled-substance dispensing decision is that the prescription must be issued for a legitimate medical purpose by a practitioner acting in the usual course of professional practice. This is not satisfied merely because a prescription is written, electronic, or bears a valid DEA number. The pharmacist must consider whether the drug, dose, quantity, and frequency are consistent with a recognized treatment of a documented condition, whether the prescriber has a plausible treating relationship with the patient, and whether the pattern fits the standard of care. A prescription that fails this test must not be dispensed regardless of how it was transmitted; the corresponding responsibility of the pharmacist is equal to that of the prescriber.

### Run the PDMP as a Routine Step, Not a Suspicion-Based Exception

The PDMP query is a core verification step for controlled substances, not an investigation reserved for suspicious patients. Query the PDMP before dispensing any new controlled substance and at refill intervals, and review the full controlled-substance history across prescribers, pharmacies, and drugs. The PDMP reveals the patterns that no single prescription or single pharmacy can show: multiple prescribers in different systems, multiple pharmacies, overlapping prescriptions, early refills, dose escalation, and combinations that increase risk (opioid plus benzodiazepine, opioid plus muscle relaxant). Document the query and its findings. Where the PDMP is unavailable or down, document that and apply heightened clinical scrutiny.

### Evaluate the Totality of Red Flags Rather Than Any Single Signal

No single red flag proves diversion or doctor-shopping, and treating any one flag as dispositive leads both to wrongful refusal and to wrongful dispensing. Evaluate the pattern: multiple prescribers and pharmacies, cash payment, geographic distance between prescriber and patient, early refill requests, lost or stolen medication reports, quantity or dose outside expected ranges, specific drug or formulation preferences (immediate-release, high-potency), requests to bypass insurance, and combinations with other controlled substances. Weigh the flags together and against the patient's clinical context. A chronic-pain patient with one specialist and one pharmacy who cash-pays is very different from a patient seeing four prescribers across three counties filling at five pharmacies.

### Clarify With the Prescriber Before Refusing or Dispensing

When red flags are present but not conclusive, the correct action is usually to contact the prescriber and clarify — confirm the diagnosis, the treatment plan, awareness of other prescriptions, and the rationale for the current order. A prescriber who is genuinely treating the patient can explain the pattern; a prescriber who cannot, who is evasive, or who cannot be reached supports the decision to refuse. Document the clarification attempt, the substance of the conversation, and the prescriber's response. Do not dispense "pending clarification" in a way that releases the drug before the question is resolved.

### Refuse When the Evidence Supports Refusal, and Document the Refusal

When the totality of evidence indicates the prescription is not for a legitimate medical purpose — confirmed doctor-shopping, a known pill-mill prescriber, forged or altered prescriptions, or a prescriber who confirms no treating relationship — the pharmacist must refuse to dispense. Refusal is a professional act, not an accusation, and it must be documented with the specific reasons, the PDMP findings, the clarification attempts, and the actions taken (notification of the prescriber, reporting per state law). Do not refuse on the basis of stigma, the patient's appearance, or the dose alone; refuse on the documented totality of evidence.

### Apply Quantity, Refill, and Partial-Fill Rules Exactly

Controld substances carry strict federal and state limits on days' supply, refills, and partial fills that vary by schedule and drug. Apply these exactly: Schedule II rules (no refills, partial-fill and emergency-dispensing constraints, electronic prescribing requirements), Schedule III–IV refill limits, and state-specific limits on days' supply for initial opioid prescriptions. A partial fill must be documented and the remaining balance handled per the rules; some states allow partial fills to limit initial opioid exposure, and the pharmacist should offer these where appropriate. Do not let convenience override the regulatory limits.

### Balance Diversion Prevention Against Legitimate Access

The pharmacist's duty is not to eliminate controlled-substance dispensing but to ensure it is legitimate and safe. Stigmatizing or reflexively denying legitimate chronic-pain, palliative, oncology, or ADHD patients causes real harm — untreated pain, abandoned treatment, and erosion of trust. Apply the same evidentiary standard to all patients, recognize that high doses and long durations can be legitimate, and use risk-mitigation tools (PDMP, naloxone co-dispensing, treatment agreements) to support safe access rather than to justify denial. When in doubt, clarify and mitigate rather than refuse outright.

## Common Traps

### Treating Any Single Red Flag as Dispositive

A patient pays cash, or visits a new pharmacy, or requests an early refill, and the pharmacist treats that single signal as proof of diversion and refuses. The mechanism is that red flags are highly visible and emotionally charged, while the legitimate explanations (high deductible, pharmacy closure, vacation) are invisible unless asked. The false signal is "this looks suspicious." The harm is twofold: legitimate patients are denied needed medication and stigmatized, and the pharmacist's judgment is reduced to a checklist that both over-flags innocent behavior and misses the patient whose single visible signal is part of a larger hidden pattern. Red flags must be weighed in combination and against context, not treated individually as proof.

### PDMP Queried Only When Suspicious

The pharmacist queries the PDMP only when something looks wrong, and the routine controlled-substance verification proceeds without it. The mechanism is that the query takes time and the default verification feels complete without it, so it is skipped for "normal-looking" patients. The false signal is a prescription that appears routine. The harm is that the multi-prescriber, multi-pharmacy pattern — which is invisible without the PDMP — goes undetected in exactly the patients who look most ordinary, because experienced diverters deliberately present as unremarkable. The PDMP must be a routine step for every controlled substance, not an investigation triggered by suspicion.

### Corresponding Responsibility Misunderstood as Prescriber's Problem Alone

The pharmacist believes that if the prescriber wrote the prescription, the responsibility for its legitimacy rests with the prescriber, and dispensing is a clerical act. The mechanism is that the pharmacist's role is conflated with fulfillment, and the legal doctrine of corresponding responsibility — which holds the dispenser equally accountable — is not internalized. The false signal is "the doctor ordered it." The harm is that prescriptions lacking a legitimate medical purpose are dispensed, the pharmacist becomes complicit in diversion, and in enforcement actions the pharmacist's license and the pharmacy's registration are at risk alongside the prescriber's. Corresponding responsibility requires independent pharmacist judgment, not deference.

### Clarification Used as a Rubber Stamp

The pharmacist calls the prescriber, the prescriber confirms "yes, I wrote it," and the pharmacist dispenses on that confirmation alone, treating the call as having resolved the red flags. The mechanism is that a yes/no confirmation is easy to obtain and feels like due diligence, while a substantive clinical discussion is harder and may be unwelcome. The false signal is "I checked with the prescriber." The harm is that the underlying questions — diagnosis, treatment plan, awareness of overlapping prescriptions — remain unanswered, and the prescription that should have been held is dispensed with the appearance of verification. Clarification must address the specific red flags, not merely confirm authorship.

### Refusal Without Documentation or Notification

The pharmacist refuses to dispense based on suspicion but documents nothing, does not notify the prescriber, and does not follow state reporting requirements. The mechanism is that refusal feels safer than dispensing and documentation feels like extra exposure, so the refusal is handled informally. The false signal is a problem avoided. The harm is that the decision is unreviewable, the patient has no record to appeal or explain, patterns across pharmacies cannot be assembled, and the pharmacist has no protection if the refusal is later challenged. Refusal must be documented with reasons, PDMP findings, and the actions taken.

### Stigma-Driven Denial of Legitimate Chronic-Pain or ADHD Patients

The pharmacist refuses or delays dispensing to patients on chronic opioids or stimulants based on the dose, the duration, or assumptions about the patient, rather than on evidence of illegitimacy. The mechanism is that high-dose and long-term controlled-substance therapy carry known risks, and discomfort with those risks is projected onto the patient as suspicion. The false signal is "this dose is high." The harm is untreated or abandoned pain and psychiatric care, patient stigma, and erosion of the therapeutic relationship, with no corresponding reduction in diversion. Access decisions must rest on evidence and risk mitigation, not on the patient's appearance or the dose alone.

## Self-Check

- Did I confirm the prescription is for a legitimate medical purpose by a practitioner in the usual course of practice, beyond confirming it was written?
- Did I query the PDMP as a routine step and review the full cross-prescriber, cross-pharmacy history, documenting the findings?
- Did I evaluate the totality of red flags in combination and against clinical context, rather than treating any single flag as dispositive?
- Where red flags were present, did I contact the prescriber and clarify the specific concerns (diagnosis, plan, overlapping prescriptions), not merely confirm authorship?
- Where I refused, did I document the specific reasons, PDMP findings, clarification attempts, and notifications or reports made?
- Did I apply quantity, refill, and partial-fill rules exactly for the schedule and drug, including state-specific initial-opioid limits?
- Did I balance diversion prevention against legitimate access, offering risk mitigation (naloxone, monitoring) rather than denying based on stigma, dose, or duration alone?
