# Pediatric and Weight-Based Dose Verification

Pediatric dosing is one of the highest-risk verification tasks in pharmacy, because children are dosed by weight and a single misplaced decimal, a kilogram-versus-pound confusion, or a missed maximum can deliver a tenfold overdose to a body that has little reserve. Unlike adult dosing, where a standard milligram dose covers most patients, every pediatric prescription is a patient-specific calculation, and the pharmacist is often the last person who will independently recompute that calculation before the drug reaches a child. The danger is that the calculation looks simple — milligrams per kilogram — so it is trusted rather than re-derived, that weight-based ranges are applied without checking the daily-maximum or the age band, and that adult formulations and concentrations are dispensed to infants whose weight and metabolism make them unsafe. This skill covers the judgment used to verify pediatric prescriptions so that the dose is correct for the child in front of the order, not merely arithmetically plausible.

## Core Rules

### Independently Recompute Every Weight-Based Dose From the Weight

Do not trust the prescriber's arithmetic, and do not trust the printed dose. Obtain the child's current weight, confirm it is in kilograms (convert from pounds and verify the conversion), and independently recompute the dose: mg/kg per dose, mg/kg/day, and the resulting milligram amount and frequency. Confirm the prescriber's dose against your calculation; a discrepancy means one of you used the wrong weight, the wrong unit, or the wrong frequency. Document the weight used and the calculation. The most catastrophic pediatric errors are decimal errors that produce tenfold doses, and independent recomputation is the only reliable catch, because the wrong dose looks plausible on its face.

### Check the Total Daily Dose Against the Maximum and Against the Frequency

A mg/kg per-dose figure can be correct while the daily total is toxic, because the frequency multiplies it. Always compute the total daily dose (mg/kg/day) and compare it to the recommended maximum, and check that the prescribed frequency matches the intended regimen. A common error is prescribing a per-dose amount appropriate for twice-daily dosing but writing it four times daily, doubling the daily exposure. Conversely, confirm that a once-daily pediatric dose is not a misplaced twice-daily figure. The daily-maximum check catches the errors that the per-dose check misses, because toxicity tracks cumulative exposure, not any single dose.

### Verify the Dose Against the Correct Age and Developmental Band

Pediatric dosing ranges shift with age, and a dose correct for a school-age child may be unsafe for a neonate or infant. Confirm the child's age and apply the age-appropriate range, paying particular attention to neonates (first 28 days) and infants, whose hepatic metabolism, renal clearance, and blood-brain barrier immaturity make many adult-safe drugs toxic. Some drugs are contraindicated below specific ages (for example, codeine in children, certain antitussives in young children, quinolones and tetracyclines by age). Do not apply a general pediatric range to a neonate; use neonatal-specific references and dosing.

### Confirm the Formulation, Concentration, and Volume Are Appropriate for the Child

Pediatric liquid formulations carry their own error surface: concentrations vary by product, the dispensed volume for an infant may be fractions of a milliliter, and an adult concentration dispensed to a small child yields an unmeasurable or dangerous volume. Confirm the concentration matches the prescribed dose and that the resulting volume is measurable with the available dosing device (oral syringe, not a household spoon). Where the concentration is wrong, contact the prescriber rather than dispensing a volume the caregiver cannot measure accurately. Verify that the formulation is age-appropriate — no honey-containing products in infants, alcohol-free where required, palatable where adherence depends on it.

### Screen for Drugs That Are Unsafe or Require Special Caution in Children

Maintain active vigilance for drugs that are contraindicated, black-boxed, or require special caution in pediatric patients: codeine and tramadol in children, cough and cold preparations in young children, tetracyclines and dental staining, quinolones and the developing skeleton, aspirin and Reye syndrome, certain antihistamines and paradoxical excitation in young children. When such a drug is prescribed, confirm the indication and age are appropriate and that the risk is justified; if not, contact the prescriber and recommend an alternative. Do not dispense a contraindicated pediatric drug on the assumption that the prescriber has weighed the risk.

### Confirm the Weight Is Current and the Dose Is Not Stale

Children grow, and a weight-based dose established months ago may now be subtherapeutic or, for weight-based maximums, may have been correct at a lower weight and is now being exceeded. Confirm the weight on the prescription is current; where it is old or absent, obtain a current weight and recheck the dose. For chronic therapies, recheck the dose at expected growth intervals. A dose that was correct at the last visit is not guaranteed correct now, and a child who has outgrown a weight-based maximum is being under-protected by an outdated calculation.

### Counsel the Caregiver on Dosing Device, Measurement, and Storage

The correct dose is only correct if the caregiver administers it accurately. Counsel on the use of the dosing device provided (oral syringe or dosing cup, not a household spoon), demonstrate the measurement of the prescribed volume, and confirm the caregiver can reproduce it. Address storage (refrigeration where required, child-resistant closures, safe storage out of reach), because pediatric accidental ingestions are a leading cause of poisoning. Document the counseling. A verified dose delivered with the wrong device or stored accessibly still harms the child.

## Common Traps

### The Tenfold Decimal Error Trusted at Face Value

The prescriber writes a dose with a misplaced decimal — 5.0 mg read as 50, or 0.5 written without the leading zero as .5 — and the pharmacist dispenses it because the number looks like a plausible adult-style dose. The mechanism is that decimal errors produce numbers that are individually plausible, and the brain accepts a plausible number without recomputing from the weight. The false signal is a dose that "looks like a real dose." The harm is a tenfold (or greater) overdose delivered to a child, which for narrow-therapeutic-index drugs is fatal or near-fatal. Independent recomputation from the weight, and avoidance of trailing zeros and enforcement of leading zeros, are the only reliable defenses.

### Pound-to-Kilogram Confusion Doubling the Dose

The weight is recorded or communicated in pounds but treated as kilograms, or vice versa, and the resulting dose is off by a factor of 2.2. The mechanism is that weights arrive in mixed units across systems and conversations, and the unit is assumed rather than confirmed. The false signal is a calculated dose that fits a plausible range. The harm is a dose roughly doubled (if pounds were used as kg) or roughly halved (if kg were used as pounds), producing toxicity or therapeutic failure. The unit must be explicitly confirmed and the conversion verified, not assumed from context.

### Per-Dose Range Applied Without the Daily-Maximum Check

The pharmacist confirms the mg/kg per dose is within range and dispenses, without computing the total daily dose or checking it against the maximum. The mechanism is that the per-dose check is the obvious one and the daily-total check is an extra step that is easy to omit. The false signal is a per-dose amount within range. The harm is that a high frequency multiplies a correct per-dose figure into a toxic daily total, and the child accumulates exposure that no single dose would predict. The daily-maximum check is mandatory, because toxicity tracks cumulative dose.

### Adult Concentration Dispensed for an Infant

A liquid is dispensed in the adult concentration, and the resulting volume for an infant is a fraction of a milliliter that cannot be measured accurately, or a caregiver rounds up and overdoses. The mechanism is that the same drug exists in multiple concentrations, and the default stock or the prescribed product is the adult one. The false signal is the right drug in the right form. The harm is either an unmeasurable dose that caregivers approximate (overdosing) or, if a higher concentration is used to hit a larger volume, an inadvertent overdose. The concentration must be confirmed against the dose and the resulting volume checked for measurability.

### Stale Weight on a Chronic Prescription

A child's weight-based dose was last checked months ago, the child has grown, and the refill is dispensed on the original dose without re-verification. The mechanism is that refills feel routine and the original verification feels sufficient, so the weight is not re-checked. The false signal is a dose that was once correct. The harm is that a child who has outgrown the dose is now underdosed (subtherapeutic, failing therapy), or a child who has grown into a weight where the dose now exceeds a per-kg maximum is overexposed. Chronic pediatric therapy requires periodic weight re-verification.

### Age-Inappropriate Drug Accepted Because the Dose Is Correct

The pharmacist confirms the mg/kg is correct and dispenses, without checking that the drug itself is appropriate for the child's age. The mechanism is that the dose check consumes the attention and the age-contraindication check is a separate, easily skipped step. The false signal is an arithmetically correct dose. The harm is that an age-contraindicated drug (codeine in a child, an antitussive in an infant, aspirin in a febrile child) is delivered despite the dose being "right," causing the specific harm the contraindication exists to prevent. The drug must be verified age-appropriate independently of the dose.

## Self-Check

- Did I obtain the child's current weight in kilograms (converting and verifying from pounds), and independently recompute the mg/kg per dose and mg/kg/day?
- Did I compute the total daily dose and check it against the recommended maximum, and confirm the prescribed frequency matches the intended regimen?
- Did I apply the age-appropriate dosing range, with neonate- and infant-specific references where the child is in those bands?
- Did I confirm the formulation, concentration, and resulting volume are appropriate and measurable with the provided dosing device, not a household spoon?
- Did I screen for drugs contraindicated or requiring special caution in children of this age, and confirm the indication justifies the risk?
- Did I confirm the weight is current rather than stale, and for chronic therapy, is the dose re-verified at growth intervals?
- Did I counsel the caregiver on the dosing device, accurate measurement, and safe storage out of the child's reach?
