# Non-Sterile Compounding Quality and Beyond-Use Dating

Non-sterile compounding produces preparations that are not required to be sterile — creams, ointments, gels, oral liquids, suspensions, capsules, troches — but that still must be of correct strength, stable for the period claimed, and safe for the route of administration. The central judgment problem is the beyond-use date (BUD): how long the preparation remains potent, physically stable, and free of microbial growth. Assigning a BUD that is too long risks degradation, loss of potency, and contamination; assigning one that is too short wastes preparation and inconveniences the patient. USP 795 provides the framework, default BUDs, and the conditions under which longer BUDs may be assigned, and the pharmacist must apply it with knowledge of the active ingredients, the vehicle, the container, and the available stability evidence. The danger is that the BUD is assigned by habit or by what is convenient, that quality is assumed rather than verified, and that preparations are dispensed in containers or storage conditions that accelerate degradation. This skill covers the judgment used to compound non-sterile preparations that are correctly formulated, properly dated, and quality-assured.

## Core Rules

### Assign the Beyond-Use Date by USP 795 Criteria, Not by Convenience

The BUD must be assigned per USP 795, which provides default BUDs based on the dosage form and the presence of water, and which allows longer BUDs only when supported by valid stability information or when criteria for an extended BUD are met. For non-aqueous formulations, the default is shorter; for aqueous formulations, shorter still; for solid dosage forms, longer. Do not assign a BUD longer than the default without specific, applicable stability evidence for the same formulation, concentration, vehicle, and container — stability is formulation-specific, and evidence for one concentration or vehicle does not transfer to another. Document the basis for every BUD, including the USP category applied and any stability reference used.

### Base the Formulation on a Credible Master Formula Record

Every preparation should be made from a documented master formula record that specifies the ingredients, quantities, equipment, procedure, container, storage, and BUD, and that is based on a credible reference (USP monograph, peer-reviewed stability study, manufacturer information). Do not compound from memory or from an unverified recipe, because errors in concentration, vehicle selection, or procedure produce preparations that are wrong in strength or unstable. The master formula record ensures consistency across batches and across compounders, and it is the reference against which deviations are detected. Verify the calculation of the active ingredient quantity, accounting for the form of the drug (salt, base, potency of a powder) and for any loss in compounding.

### Match the Vehicle and the Container to the Drug and the Route

The vehicle (the base or solvent) and the container affect stability, release, and compatibility. A drug unstable in an aqueous vehicle must be compounded in a non-aqueous base or as a solid form; a drug sensitive to light requires an opaque container; a preparation prone to sorption requires a container that does not interact (glass versus certain plastics). Confirm that the vehicle is compatible with the active ingredient, that it provides the needed release for the route (percutaneous absorption for a topical, dissolution for an oral liquid), and that the container protects the preparation from the degradation factors (light, air, moisture, temperature). A correct active ingredient in the wrong vehicle or container degrades or fails to deliver.

### Apply Quality Verification Appropriate to the Preparation

Quality verification for non-sterile compounding includes weighing and measurement accuracy, visual inspection of the finished preparation (uniformity, absence of separation or discoloration), and, where applicable, potency testing and microbial screening for higher-risk preparations. For batches, weigh the active ingredient on a calibrated balance, verify the final weight or volume, and inspect for homogeneity. For preparations where potency or contamination risk is significant (certain oral liquids, preparations for vulnerable patients), arrange potency assay and microbial testing. Do not dispense a preparation that has separated, discolored, or otherwise appears compromised; investigate and recompound.

### Account for the Active Ingredient's Stability Characteristics

Each active ingredient has known degradation pathways — hydrolysis, oxidation, photolysis — and the compounding must protect against them. Hydrolysis-prone drugs must avoid water and be compounded as solids or in non-aqueous vehicles; oxidation-prone drugs require antioxidants, minimal headspace, or opaque containers; light-sensitive drugs require light protection. Confirm the known stability of the active ingredient in the chosen vehicle and adjust the BUD and the storage accordingly. Where the active ingredient is a powder with a potency less than 100 percent (common with some antibiotics and hormones), account for the potency in the calculation, because ignoring it produces an under-strength preparation.

### Control Microbial Risk in Aqueous and Multi-Dose Preparations

Non-sterile preparations are not sterile, but aqueous and multi-dose preparations can support microbial growth, and the BUD and preservative choice must reflect this. USP 795 limits aqueous BUDs more tightly than non-aqueous for this reason. For oral liquids and other aqueous preparations, use a preservative where appropriate, store under refrigeration where that extends the BUD, and counsel the patient on storage and discard. Multi-dose containers require a preservative and a BUD that accounts for in-use contamination. A preservative-free aqueous preparation with a long BUD is a microbial growth medium.

### Document the Preparation, the BUD Basis, and the Counseling

Document the preparation: the master formula record used, the ingredients and lot numbers, the quantities, the compounder, the BUD and its basis, the container, and the storage. Documentation is the record that supports traceability, that allows investigation of any adverse event, and that demonstrates compliance. Counsel the patient on the preparation's use, storage, and the discard date, because a correctly compounded preparation stored wrong or used past its BUD still harms the patient. Documentation and counseling close the loop on quality.

## Common Traps

### BUD Assigned by Habit or Convenience

The pharmacist assigns a 30- or 90-day BUD because that is the habit, regardless of the formulation's actual stability or the USP 795 default. The mechanism is that the default BUDs require looking up and the habit BUD is effortless, and the difference is invisible until the preparation degrades. The false signal is a BUD that "looks reasonable." The harm is that a preparation used to the end of an unsupported BUD may be sub-potent (treatment failure) or microbiologically compromised, with the patient none the wiser. The BUD must be assigned per USP 795 and documented with its basis.

### Stability Evidence From a Different Formulation Applied

A stability study for a different concentration, vehicle, or container is used to justify a longer BUD for the current preparation. The mechanism is that stability data feels transferable and finding formulation-specific data is hard, so the closest available reference is used. The false signal is a stability citation. The harm is that stability is formulation-specific — a different pH, vehicle, or container can halve the shelf life — and the borrowed BUD overstates the real stability, producing degradation. Stability evidence must match the formulation, concentration, vehicle, and container, or the default BUD applies.

### Powder Potency Ignored in the Calculation

An active ingredient powder with a potency of less than 100 percent (or a salt form) is weighed as if it were pure, producing an under-strength preparation. The mechanism is that the potency correction is a small step that is easy to omit, and the certificate of analysis is not always at hand. The false signal is a weighed quantity that matches the formula. The harm is a preparation below labeled strength, which for critical doses (hormones, potent pediatric liquids) is a treatment failure. The potency and the salt form must be accounted for in every calculation.

### Wrong Vehicle or Container Accelerating Degradation

A drug is compounded in an aqueous vehicle when it is hydrolytically unstable, or in a clear container when it is light-sensitive, and the preparation degrades well before the BUD. The mechanism is that the vehicle and container are chosen for convenience or availability rather than compatibility. The false signal is a preparation that looks acceptable at dispensing. The harm is that degradation proceeds silently, and by the time the patient uses the later doses, the potency is lost. The vehicle and container must match the drug's stability characteristics.

### Aqueous Preparation With a Long BUD and No Preservative

An oral liquid is compounded without a preservative and assigned a BUD that assumes stability but not microbial growth, and the multi-dose bottle grows organisms over use. The mechanism is that aqueous preparations support microbes and the BUD for such preparations is tighter, but the preservative and refrigeration steps are easy to omit. The false signal is a stable-looking liquid. The harm is microbial contamination of a preparation taken orally, potentially by a vulnerable patient. Aqueous and multi-dose preparations require preservative consideration, refrigeration where indicated, and a BUD that reflects microbial risk.

### Quality Assumed Without Verification

A preparation is dispensed without visual inspection, final weight or volume check, or any potency verification, because the compounding "followed the recipe." The mechanism is that the procedure feels sufficient and verification is an extra step. The false signal is a completed preparation. The harm is that errors in weighing, mixing, or procedure — a non-uniform ointment, a miscalculated liquid, a separated emulsion — reach the patient undetected. Quality verification appropriate to the preparation is part of compounding, not optional.

## Self-Check

- Is the BUD assigned per USP 795 criteria for the dosage form and water content, with any extension supported by formulation-specific stability evidence and the basis documented?
- Is the preparation made from a documented master formula record based on a credible reference, with the active ingredient quantity calculated accounting for salt form and powder potency?
- Do the vehicle and container match the drug's stability characteristics (hydrolysis, oxidation, photolysis) and provide the needed release for the route of administration?
- Is quality verification applied — calibrated weighing, final weight or volume, visual inspection for uniformity, and potency or microbial testing where the risk warrants?
- Did I confirm the active ingredient's degradation pathways and protect against them in the formulation, container, and storage?
- For aqueous or multi-dose preparations, did I address microbial risk with a preservative, refrigeration, and a BUD that reflects microbial growth potential?
- Did I document the master formula, ingredients and lots, quantities, compounder, BUD and basis, container, and storage, and counsel the patient on use, storage, and the discard date?
