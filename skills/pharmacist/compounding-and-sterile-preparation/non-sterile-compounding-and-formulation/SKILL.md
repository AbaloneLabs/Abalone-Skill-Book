# Non-Sterile Compounding and Formulation

Compounding is the art of transforming bulk ingredients into a customized medication tailored to a patient whose needs cannot be met by a manufactured product — a dose strength that is not commercially available, a dosage form a patient cannot use (a suspension for a patient who cannot swallow tablets), an allergen-free formulation, or a flavor for pediatric acceptance. The danger in compounding is that the pharmacist is now the manufacturer, and with that role comes manufacturer-level responsibility for strength, quality, stability, and labeling — responsibilities that cannot be delegated to a label check. A compounding error is invisible: the preparation looks identical whether it contains the correct potency or ten times the intended dose, whether it is stable for thirty days or three, and whether the base was appropriate or incompatible. The pharmacist's discipline is to treat every compounding calculation, every ingredient selection, and every beyond-use date assignment as a high-stakes decision verified against authoritative references, because there is no downstream manufacturer's quality control to catch what the pharmacist misses.

## Core Rules

### Verify the Master Formulation Record and Calculate Ingredients Independently

Every compounded preparation must be prepared from a verified master formulation record (a "recipe" document) that specifies each ingredient, its quantity, the dosage form, the preparation procedure, the container, the storage conditions, and the beyond-use date. The pharmacist must not compound from memory or improvised recipes. Before preparation, the pharmacist must independently recalculate the quantity of each active ingredient — particularly for high-potency or narrow-therapeutic-index drugs where a calculation error is clinically catastrophic — and must have a second qualified person verify the calculation and the ingredient selection before any measuring begins. The verification must check that the correct salt form of the active ingredient is being used (the sulfate salt is not interchangeable with the hydrochloride), that the correct potency or concentration of the bulk powder is accounted for (a powder labeled as 90 percent potency requires a quantity adjustment), and that the units are consistent (milligrams versus grams, weight versus volume). Calculation errors are the most common and most dangerous compounding error, and they survive every visual check because the preparation looks normal.

### Assign Beyond-Use Dates From Stability Evidence, Not Convenience

The beyond-use date (BUD) — the date after which the preparation must not be used — is a stability and safety determination, not a marketing or convenience decision. The pharmacist must assign the BUD based on the best available evidence, in this order of preference: a validated stability study for the specific formulation (the gold standard, often published in peer-reviewed compounding literature); a USP general chapter default for the dosage form when no formulation-specific data exist (for non-sterile preparations, USP <795> provides conservative default BUDs by category); or, least defensibly, an estimate. The pharmacist must never assign a BUD longer than the evidence supports, because a preparation that degrades after the BUD may deliver a subtherapeutic dose (the patient fails therapy) or a toxic degradation product. The BUD must also account for the specific vehicle and preservative used, the concentration, the storage temperature, and the container — a suspension in a non-preserved vehicle at room temperature has a much shorter BUD than a preserved formulation refrigerated in a tight container. When in doubt, the pharmacist must assign the shorter, more conservative BUD.

### Select the Vehicle and Excipients for Compatibility and Patient Suitability

The choice of base, suspending agent, preservative, flavoring, and buffer is not cosmetic — it determines whether the active ingredient remains stable, whether the preparation is usable by the patient, and whether the excipients are safe for this patient. The pharmacist must verify that each excipient is compatible with the active ingredient (some drugs are unstable in aqueous vehicles, others adsorb to certain suspending agents), that the pH of the vehicle is appropriate for the drug's stability, and that the excipients do not introduce an allergen or intolerance for this patient (sugar alcohols in pediatric suspensions, gluten in some bases, dyes, preservatives like parabens). For pediatric preparations, palatability and sugar content matter; for dermatologic preparations, the base (ointment, cream, gel, lotion) determines penetration and efficacy. The pharmacist must document the rationale for excipient selection and must not substitute excipients without re-evaluating compatibility and stability.

### Maintain Documentation That Reconstructs the Entire Batch

The compounding record must allow a complete reconstruction of what was made, from whom, with what, and when — for every individual batch. The record must include: the date; the prescription or order number; the name and quantity of every ingredient and its lot number; the manufacturer and expiration date of each component; the master formulation record reference; the total quantity prepared; the assigned BUD; the container and labeling; the name of the person who prepared and the person who verified; and any deviations from the standard procedure. This documentation is not bureaucratic — it is the mechanism by which a recall, an adverse event report, or a quality investigation can trace a problem back to its source. If a patient has a reaction, the record must show whether the preservative was the likely cause; if a batch is found subpotent, the record must show which component lot was used. A compounding record that cannot reconstruct the batch is functionally absent.

### Apply Quality Assurance Across Personnel, Environment, and Equipment

Non-sterile compounding quality depends on conditions that extend beyond the single preparation. The pharmacist must ensure that compounding personnel are trained and competency-assessed for the dosage forms they prepare, that the compounding area is clean and organized and separated from dispensing activity, that equipment (balances, graduates, mortars and pestles) is calibrated and cleaned appropriately between preparations to avoid cross-contamination, and that high-potency or hazardous drugs (hormones, cytotoxics) are compounded with dedicated equipment and additional containment to protect both the patient and the personnel. The pharmacist must implement a quality assurance program that includes periodic checks of balance accuracy, verification of graduated cylinder and syringe accuracy, and investigation of any out-of-specification result. A compounding operation without quality assurance is a craft operation, not a pharmaceutical operation, and craft operations produce variable and sometimes dangerous products.

## Common Traps

### The Memory-Recipe Trap

The pharmacist compounds a familiar preparation from memory rather than retrieving the master formulation record. The mechanism is that the preparation is well-known and the pharmacist has made it many times, so memory feels reliable. The false signal is that the familiar preparation feels automatic, so the steps must be correct. The harm is that memory drifts — the quantity recalled is the one from a slightly different formulation, the BUD recalled is from a different concentration, the salt form recalled is from a different supplier — and the error is invisible because the preparation looks identical to the correct one. Memory is not a verified reference; the master formulation record exists precisely because memory is unreliable for high-stakes quantitative work.

### The Convenient BUD

The pharmacist assigns a beyond-use date that is longer than the evidence supports because the patient wants fewer refills, the clinic wants less frequent preparation, or the default seems too short. The mechanism is that a longer BUD is more convenient for everyone and the preparation "probably" remains stable. The false signal is that the date is a number on a label, so it can be set by preference. The harm is that a preparation used past its true stability date may deliver a degraded or subtherapeutic dose — the patient's hormone replacement fails, the antibiotic suspension loses potency, the ophthalmic preparation grows contaminant — and the link to the over-long BUD is never made because the degradation is invisible. The BUD must be set by evidence, and when evidence is uncertain, the conservative shorter date must prevail.

### The Salt-Form or Potency Omission

The pharmacist calculates the quantity of active ingredient without accounting for the salt form or the potency of the bulk powder. The mechanism is that the drug name on the label matches the prescription, so the calculation feels complete. The false signal is that the ingredient name matches, so the quantity must be correct. The harm is that the salt form changes the molecular weight and therefore the amount of active moiety per milligram (a drug dosed as the base requires more of the salt to deliver the same active), and a bulk powder with 90 percent potency delivers 10 percent less active than a calculation assuming 100 percent. The cumulative error can produce a clinically significant underdose or overdose that looks correct on the label and is discovered only when the patient fails therapy or develops toxicity.

### Cross-Contamination From Shared Equipment

The pharmacist prepares multiple preparations with the same mortar, pestle, or spatula without adequate cleaning, particularly when handling potent drugs (hormones, beta-agonists, cytotoxics). The mechanism is that the equipment looks clean after a wipe, so it feels ready for the next preparation. The false signal is that visible cleanliness equals pharmaceutical cleanliness. The harm is that residual potent drug on shared equipment contaminates the next preparation — a trace of estrogen in a pediatric preparation, a trace of a cytotoxic in a non-hazardous compound — and the contamination is clinically significant at microgram levels for high-potency drugs. Dedicated equipment or validated cleaning procedures are required for hazardous and high-potency compounds, and visual inspection is insufficient.

### Documentation Treated as Afterthought

The pharmacist completes the preparation, labels it, dispenses it, and fills in the compounding record later from memory or general recollection. The mechanism is that the preparation itself feels like the work, and the record feels like paperwork. The false signal is that the record is a formality, so it can be completed approximately. The harm is that a record completed after the fact is unreliable — the lot numbers are guessed, the quantities are rounded, the deviations are omitted — and when an adverse event or recall requires tracing the batch, the record cannot support the investigation. The compounding record must be completed contemporaneously, at the time of preparation, because that is the only way it accurately reflects what was done.

## Self-Check

- Did I prepare from a verified master formulation record and have a second qualified person verify the calculation and ingredient selection before measuring, or did I compound from memory?
- Did I account for the correct salt form and the labeled potency of the bulk powder in my ingredient calculations, or did I assume the drug name alone was sufficient?
- Did I assign the beyond-use date from formulation-specific stability evidence or the applicable USP default, choosing the shorter conservative date when evidence was uncertain, or did I assign a convenient date?
- Did I select each vehicle and excipient for compatibility with the active ingredient and safety for this patient (allergens, pediatric sugar content, dermatologic base), documenting the rationale?
- Did I complete the compounding record contemporaneously with all lot numbers, quantities, personnel, deviations, and the BUD, so the batch can be fully reconstructed?
- For hazardous or high-potency drugs, did I use dedicated or validated-cleaned equipment and appropriate containment to prevent cross-contamination of subsequent preparations?
