# Hazardous Drug Compounding and USP 800 Compliance

Hazardous drug compounding is the manipulation of drugs that are carcinogenic, teratogenic, reproductively toxic, or organ-toxic — chiefly antineoplastics, but also certain hormones, antivirals, and immunosuppressants — and the central fact is that there is no safe level of occupational exposure to these agents. The harm is cumulative, often delayed, and frequently invisible in the individual worker, which is exactly why the protections must be procedural and engineered rather than left to personal judgment in the moment. USP 800 codifies the containment requirements: the facility (containment primary engineering control, containment secondary engineering control, negative pressure, air exchange), the personal protective equipment, the handling at every stage from receipt to disposal, and the medical surveillance and spill response. The danger is that the requirements feel bureaucratic and are quietly degraded — a hood used past certification, gloves chosen for cost, a spill wiped up without respiratory protection — and each degradation is invisible until the cumulative harm surfaces years later. This skill covers the judgment used to compound hazardous drugs in a way that protects the compounder, the environment, and the patient, with the overriding principle that containment is engineered and procedural, not discretionary.

## Core Rules

### Identify Hazardous Drugs by the Current NIOSH List, Not by Habit

The NIOSH List of Antineoplastic and Other Hazardous Drugs defines what is hazardous, and it is updated; a drug not previously considered hazardous may be added, and the hazard categories (Group 1 through 3, with handling distinctions) shape the requirements. Maintain a facility hazardous drug list based on the current NIOSH list and the dosage forms handled, and review it when the list is updated or when new agents are added to formulary. Do not rely on memory or on "we've always handled it this way," because the list changes and the handling requirements differ by group and by dosage form (a final pharmaceutical manufacturer's intact package is handled differently from a compounded or crushed preparation).

### Apply the Containment Engineering Controls Required for Each Task

Hazardous drug compounding requires specific engineering controls, and the control must match the task. Compounding of sterile hazardous preparations requires a containment primary engineering control (C-PEC, typically a Class II Type B2 biological safety cabinet or a compounding aseptic containment isolator) located within a containment secondary engineering control (C-SEC, a negative-pressure room with the required air exchanges). Non-sterile hazardous compounding has its own C-PEC requirements. Confirm the hood is certified and within current certification, that the room pressure and air exchanges meet the standard, and that the workflow keeps the C-PEC dedicated to hazardous compounding. A hood past certification or a room at the wrong pressure is not a valid control, and compounding in it is non-compliant and unsafe.

### Wear and Dispose of Personal Protective Equipment Correctly

PPE is the last line of defense after engineering controls, and it must be donned, worn, and doffed correctly. Two pairs of chemotherapy-rated gloves (tested to ASTM standards, not exam gloves), a chemotherapy gown with closed front and cuffs and back-closing, eye and face protection where splash is possible, and respiratory protection appropriate to the task (N95 or higher for activities outside a ventilated enclosure, such as spill cleanup). PPE is single-use for a compounding session, is disposed of as hazardous waste after use, and is never reused. The doffing sequence matters: remove PPE in a way that does not transfer contamination to skin or clothing, and dispose of it in the hazardous waste stream. Incorrect PPE — exam gloves instead of chemo gloves, a gown worn past one session — defeats the protection.

### Control Containment at Every Stage: Receipt, Storage, Compounding, Administration, Disposal

USP 800 applies to the full lifecycle, not just compounding. Receipt: hazardous drugs are received in a manner that protects the receiver (PPE, inspection for damage, segregation). Storage: hazardous drugs are stored separately from non-hazardous in a negative-pressure area. Compounding: in the C-PEC within the C-SEC with full PPE. Administration: closed-system transfer devices (CSTDs) for administration where required, PPE for the administering nurse, priming done with the hazardous drug or a CSTD. Disposal: all contaminated materials (vials, tubing, syringes, PPE, spill materials) are hazardous waste, segregated and disposed of per regulation. A gap at any stage — an unsegregated storage shelf, priming without a CSTD, regular trash for contaminated materials — exposes workers and violates the standard.

### Use Closed-System Transfer Devices for Compounding and Administration

CSTDs mechanically contain the drug during transfer, preventing aerosol and vapor escape during compounding and administration, and they are required for administration and recommended for compounding of hazardous drugs. Confirm the CSTD is used for every hazardous transfer, that it is the correct device for the drug and the container, and that staff are trained on its use (a CSTD used incorrectly provides less protection than claimed). CSTDs complement, not replace, the C-PEC and PPE; they are one layer of a layered containment strategy.

### Plan and Equip for Spills Before They Happen

Spill response must be planned, equipped, and rehearsed, not improvised. Maintain spill kits accessible to all areas where hazardous drugs are handled, with the required contents (absorbent pads, chemotherapy-rated gloves, gowns, eye and face protection, N95 or higher respirator, scoop, hazardous waste bags, warning signs). A spill requires immediate containment with PPE and respiratory protection, restriction of the area, and disposal of all contaminated materials as hazardous waste. Spills outside a ventilated enclosure require respiratory protection; a worker who cleans a spill without it inhales the drug. Document spills and exposures, and use them to improve prevention.

### Participate in Medical Surveillance and Document Exposures

Medical surveillance (baseline and periodic) detects the cumulative effects of exposure that the individual cannot perceive, and exposure incident reporting captures acute events that must be learned from. Participate in the surveillance program, report every exposure (skin contact, spill, eye splash, sharps injury with a contaminated device), and ensure the reports drive corrective action. A facility without active surveillance and exposure reporting has no way to detect whether its containment is working, and the absence of reported exposures may reflect under-reporting rather than safety.

## Common Traps

### Engineering Control Used Past Certification or Outside Specification

A biological safety cabinet or isolator is used past its certification date, or the C-SEC room is at the wrong pressure or air exchange, and compounding proceeds because the equipment "looks fine." The mechanism is that certification is periodic and the equipment appears to function between checks, so the lapse is invisible. The false signal is a hood that is running. The harm is that a hood past certification or a room at positive pressure no longer contains, and the compounder is exposed through a control that is assumed to be working. Certification status and room parameters must be verified before each use.

### Exam Gloves or Reused Gowns Substituted for Chemotherapy-Rated PPE

Exam gloves are used because they are cheaper and at hand, or a gown is reused across sessions because it "looks clean," defeating the protection. The mechanism is that the difference between exam and chemotherapy-rated gloves is invisible to the eye, and the gown's contamination is not visible. The false signal is PPE that appears adequate. The harm is dermal absorption of the hazardous drug through gloves that were never rated for it, and cumulative gown contamination transferred to the worker. PPE must be chemotherapy-rated, double-gloved, and single-use per session.

### Spill Cleaned Without Respiratory Protection

A spill outside the hood is wiped up with gloves and a gown but no respirator, because the spill seems small and the respirator is in another room. The mechanism is that the immediate impulse is to clean, and respiratory protection feels excessive for a small spill. The false signal is a quickly cleaned spill. The harm is inhalation of aerosolized drug during cleanup, which for some agents is a significant exposure. Spill response requires the full kit including respiratory protection, regardless of spill size, and the area must be restricted until cleanup is complete.

### Contaminated Materials Disposed of as Regular or Biohazard Waste

Tubing, syringes, vials, and PPE from hazardous compounding are placed in regular trash or yellow biohazard waste rather than the hazardous (chemotherapy) waste stream. The mechanism is that the waste looks identical to non-hazardous waste and the segregation step is easy to skip. The false signal is waste that "is disposed of." The harm is environmental release of hazardous drug, exposure to waste handlers, and regulatory violation. All materials contaminated with hazardous drugs must be segregated into the hazardous waste stream.

### CSTD Omitted During Administration or Priming

A hazardous drug is administered or a line is primed without a CSTD, exposing the administering nurse to aerosol and drip. The mechanism is that administration happens outside the pharmacy and the CSTD is seen as a pharmacy tool, or priming is done quickly without it. The false signal is a line that is running. The harm is repeated low-dose exposure to nursing staff over years, the exact cumulative exposure the standard exists to prevent. CSTDs must be used for administration and priming of hazardous drugs, with PPE for the administrator.

### No Surveillance or Exposure Reporting, Interpreted as Safety

A facility has no medical surveillance program and no reported exposures, and concludes its containment is effective. The mechanism is that the absence of surveillance and reporting produces the absence of data, which is read as the absence of harm. The false signal is "no exposures reported." The harm is that cumulative exposure goes undetected, the workers' delayed effects surface years later with no link to the workplace, and the facility has no basis to improve. Surveillance and exposure reporting must be active, and their absence is a red flag, not reassurance.

## Self-Check

- Is the facility hazardous drug list based on the current NIOSH list, with handling distinctions by group and dosage form, and is it reviewed on updates?
- For each compounding task, is the correct C-PEC used within a C-SEC that meets pressure and air-exchange requirements, with current certification verified before use?
- Is chemotherapy-rated PPE (double gloves, back-closing gown, eye/face protection, respiratory protection where required) donned, worn for a single session, doffed in correct sequence, and disposed of as hazardous waste?
- Is containment applied across the full lifecycle — receipt, segregated negative-pressure storage, compounding, CSTD-based administration, and hazardous-waste disposal — with no stage left uncontrolled?
- Are closed-system transfer devices used for every hazardous transfer in compounding and administration, with staff trained on correct use?
- Are spill kits accessible and complete, and is spill response (including respiratory protection and area restriction) planned, rehearsed, and documented?
- Is there active medical surveillance and exposure incident reporting, with reports driving corrective action, rather than an absence of reporting read as safety?
