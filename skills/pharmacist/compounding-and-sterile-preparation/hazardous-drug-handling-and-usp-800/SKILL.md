# Hazardous Drug Handling and USP 800

Hazardous drugs — antineoplastics, certain hormones, some antivirals, immunosuppressants, and other agents that are carcinogenic, teratogenic, reproductive-toxic, or organ-toxic at low doses — pose a threat not only to the patient but to every person who handles them: the pharmacist who compounds, the technician who transports, the nurse who administers, the environmental services worker who cleans, and the family member who cares for the patient at home. The danger is that the hazard is invisible and cumulative. A single handling event produces no immediate symptom; the harm — cancer, reproductive loss, organ damage — appears years later, statistically, in the exposed workforce, with no traceable link to any single event. USP <800> exists because the historical evidence showed measurable hazardous drug contamination on work surfaces, in the air, and in the urine of handling personnel, even when standard precautions were followed. The pharmacist's discipline is to treat every hazardous drug encounter as a contamination-control problem, because the absence of visible exposure is never evidence of safety, and because the consequences of complacency are borne by people who never chose the exposure.

## Core Rules

### Identify Hazardous Drugs Systematically Using the NIOSH List

The first control is correct identification: a drug cannot be handled as hazardous if it is not recognized as hazardous. The pharmacist must maintain a current facility list of hazardous drugs based on the NIOSH List of Antineoplastic and Other Hazardous Drugs, reviewed at least annually and whenever a new drug is added to formulary. The list must distinguish drugs requiring full containment (antineoplastics and other high-hazard agents) from those requiring a risk-based assessment (certain hormones, antivirals, and others where the handling risk depends on the dosage form and manipulation). The facility list must be accessible at every point of handling — receiving, storage, compounding, dispensing, administration — so that any worker can confirm a drug's status before handling it. A drug that is hazardous but not on the list, or that is handled without recognizing its status, exposes personnel to undocumented contamination, and the exposure continues until the identification failure is corrected.

### Contain Hazardous Drugs Through Engineering Controls Matched to the Manipulation

The primary defense against hazardous drug exposure is containment at the source, through engineering controls matched to the manipulation being performed. Compounding of hazardous drugs must occur in a containment primary engineering control (C-PEC) — a biological safety cabinet or compounding aseptic containment isolator — located within a containment secondary engineering control (C-SEC), which is a negatively pressurized room with adequate air exchange. Non-sterile compounding of hazardous drugs requires a containment ventilated device. The pharmacist must verify that the engineering controls are certified, functioning, and matched to the task: a Class II biological safety cabinet is required for sterile hazardous compounding, and the room must maintain negative pressure relative to the surrounding area. Compounding a hazardous drug in a standard laminar airflow workbench (which blows contaminated air toward the operator) or in a non-contained area is a serious exposure event. The engineering control is the primary barrier; if it is wrong or absent, the personal protective equipment that follows cannot compensate.

### Wear Personal Protective Equipment Appropriate to the Task

Personal protective equipment (PPE) is the secondary defense, required for all hazardous drug handling tasks including receiving, storage, compounding, dispensing, administration, spill response, and waste handling. The pharmacist must specify and enforce the PPE for each task: two pairs of chemotherapy-tested gloves, a chemotherapy gown (closed front, long sleeves, cuffed, changed every two to three hours or immediately after a spill), respiratory protection (a NIOSH-certified respirator appropriate to the task; surgical masks are insufficient for hazardous drug powder), eye and face protection when splash risk exists, and hair and shoe covers as indicated. The PPE must be donned before the task and doffed and disposed of as hazardous waste immediately after, with hand hygiene before and after. The pharmacist must not allow PPE to be downgraded for convenience (a single glove, a non-chemo gown, a surgical mask for a powder manipulation) because each downgrade increases the exposure that the engineering controls do not fully eliminate.

### Manage Receiving, Storage, Transport, and Waste as Contamination Points

Contamination control extends beyond compounding. Hazardous drugs must be received with PPE and inspected for damage (a cracked vial releases powder or vapor into the receiving area). They must be stored separately from non-hazardous drugs, in a negative-pressure room with sufficient ventilation, on shelves that can be cleaned and that contain spills. Transport must use impervious containers labeled as hazardous, with secondary containment to capture a breakage. Waste — including used vials, tubing, syringes, gowns, gloves, and spill clean-up materials — must be collected in hazardous waste containers labeled and disposed of per hazardous waste regulations, never in standard medical waste or trash. The pharmacist must trace the entire lifecycle of the hazardous drug in the facility and ensure each point has a defined contamination-control procedure, because contamination at receiving or in storage exposes personnel who never touch the compounding process.

### Plan and Equip Spill Response Before a Spill Occurs

A hazardous drug spill is a foreseeable event, and the response must be planned, equipped, and rehearsed before it happens. Spill kits appropriate to the volume and type of hazardous drug must be available at every handling location, with contents including chemotherapy-rated gloves and gowns, respiratory protection, absorbent materials, a scoop and puncture-resistant sharps container, and hazardous waste bags. The spill procedure must be written, posted, and known: isolate the area, don full PPE including respiratory protection, contain the spill from the outside in, absorb liquids and collect solids, place all materials in hazardous waste, clean the area three times with appropriate detergent and then a deactivating agent where applicable, document the spill, and assess any personnel exposure. The pharmacist must not allow a spill to be cleaned with standard supplies by an unprepared worker, because that converts a contained event into a workforce exposure.

## Common Traps

### The "Just This Once" PPE Bypass

A quick task — counting tablets of an oral hazardous drug, retrieving a vial from storage — is performed without the full PPE because the task is small and the worker is in a hurry. The mechanism is that the small task feels low-risk and the PPE feels disproportionate. The false signal is that a brief handling without PPE produces no immediate symptom, so it must be safe. The harm is that hazardous drug exposure is cumulative and asymptomatic in the short term; each bypass adds to the body burden, and the cancer or reproductive harm that appears years later is the statistical sum of many small exposures that each felt negligible at the time. There is no safe level of "just this once" for a cumulative carcinogen.

### Surgical Mask Substitution for Respiratory Protection

A worker handles a hazardous drug powder or a vial reconstitution wearing a surgical mask instead of a NIOSH-certified respirator. The mechanism is that the surgical mask looks like respiratory protection and is readily available. The false signal is that the face is covered, so the airway must be protected. The harm is that a surgical mask does not seal against the face and is not rated to filter the small particles and vapors released during hazardous drug manipulation; the contaminant flows around the mask and is inhaled. Respiratory protection for hazardous drug handling must be a properly fitted respirator with the appropriate rating, and substituting a surgical mask is an exposure dressed as protection.

### Engineering Control Assumed From the Workbench Type

A worker compounds a hazardous drug in a standard laminar airflow workbench because it is a "hood" and looks like the right equipment. The mechanism is that the equipment looks similar and the worker has not been trained on the distinction. The false signal is that the workbench is enclosed and has airflow, so it must contain the hazard. The harm is that a laminar airflow workbench blows air from the filter toward the worker, sweeping hazardous drug particles directly into the worker's breathing zone — the opposite of containment. Only a biological safety cabinet or compounding aseptic containment isolator contains hazardous drug aerosols, and using the wrong hood is a guaranteed exposure event.

### Surface Contamination Ignored Because It Is Invisible

Work surfaces in the compounding area, storage area, and administration area accumulate hazardous drug residue that is invisible to the eye, and cleaning is deferred or performed with standard supplies. The mechanism is that the surfaces look clean, so they are assumed to be clean. The false signal is that visible cleanliness equals decontamination. The harm is that the invisible residue is transferred to gloves, gowns, skin, and eventually to personnel who never directly handled the drug — a receptionist who touches a contaminated counter, a pharmacist who answers a phone with a contaminated glove. Routine surface decontamination with appropriate agents, on a defined schedule, is required to control this reservoir, and the absence of visible residue is never evidence of a clean surface.

### Spill Response Improvised in the Moment

A hazardous drug spill occurs and the responder uses whatever supplies are at hand — paper towels, standard gloves, a trash bag — because no spill kit is accessible or no one remembers the procedure. The mechanism is that the spill is unexpected and the responder acts on instinct with available materials. The false signal is that the spill was cleaned up, so it must be handled. The harm is that standard supplies do not contain the hazardous drug — they spread it, contaminate the responder's skin and airway, and place the residue in standard waste where it continues to expose workers downstream. Spill response must be pre-planned, with kits accessible and the procedure rehearsed, because the moment of the spill is the worst time to improvise contamination control.

## Self-Check

- Did I confirm the drug's hazardous status against the current facility NIOSH-based list before any handling, and is the list reviewed at least annually?
- For compounding, did I use a certified containment primary engineering control (biological safety cabinet or compounding aseptic containment isolator) within a negatively pressurized containment secondary engineering control, never a standard laminar airflow workbench?
- Did I wear and enforce PPE appropriate to the task (two pairs of chemo gloves, chemo gown, NIOSH respirator for powder or aerosol, eye protection for splash risk), with no downgrade for "quick" tasks?
- Did I manage receiving, storage (separate, negative pressure), transport (labeled, secondary containment), and waste (hazardous waste stream) as distinct contamination-control points with defined procedures?
- Are spill kits accessible at every handling location, is the spill procedure written and known, and has it been rehearsed so that response is not improvised in the moment?
- Did I perform routine surface decontamination of compounding, storage, and administration areas with appropriate agents on a defined schedule, recognizing that visible cleanliness is not evidence of decontamination?
