# Opioid Stewardship and Risk Mitigation

Opioid stewardship is the discipline of ensuring that opioids are used only when the benefits outweigh the risks, at the lowest effective dose and shortest effective duration, with the monitoring and safeguards that the risk requires. The pharmacist is central to this discipline because the pharmacy is the checkpoint where every opioid prescription is reviewed before it reaches the patient, and where the cumulative pattern of opioid use becomes visible across prescribers and time. The danger is two opposite failures: over-prescribing that fuels dependence, overdose, and diversion, and under-prescribing that abandons patients with genuine pain to suffering. The skilled pharmacist navigates between these by assessing each patient's opioid regimen against evidence-based risk thresholds, ensuring the safeguards (PDMP review, naloxone, treatment agreements, monitoring) are in place, and — when the risks have come to outweigh the benefits — coordinating a safe, humane taper in partnership with the prescriber and the patient.

## Core Rules

### Quantify Opioid Exposure in Morphine Milligram Equivalents

Opioid risk scales with dose, and dose cannot be compared across opioids without conversion to a common unit. The pharmacist must calculate the morphine milligram equivalent (MME) daily dose for every patient on opioids, summing all opioids the patient is taking. The calculation must use current conversion factors (which are approximate and vary by source, so the pharmacist must use a consistent authoritative reference) and must account for the actual daily intake, including long-acting and short-acting components. The MME supports risk stratification: guidelines generally identify 50 MME/day as a threshold above which overdose risk increases meaningfully and at which additional safeguards (naloxone, more frequent monitoring, consideration of specialist referral) are warranted, and 90 MME/day as a high-risk threshold at which the risks often outweigh the benefits and tapering or specialist consultation should be strongly considered. The pharmacist must not rely on the prescribed dose as written without converting to MME, because a "low" dose of one opioid may be a high-MME regimen, and the risk is invisible without the conversion.

### Assess Overdose Risk Factors and Ensure Naloxone Availability

Opioid overdose risk is determined by dose and by co-existing factors, and the pharmacist must assess both. Beyond the MME, the major overdose risk factors are: concurrent benzodiazepine or other central nervous system depressant use (which multiplies overdose risk and is a frequent contributor to fatal overdose); history of overdose or substance use disorder; sleep-disordered breathing (sleep apnea) or other respiratory vulnerability; hepatic or renal impairment that alters opioid clearance; age (elderly patients have higher risk); and mental health conditions associated with overdose risk. When any of these factors are present, or when the MME reaches the threshold for heightened concern, the pharmacist must ensure naloxone is available to the patient and the household — co-prescribed or offered, with counseling on overdose recognition, naloxone administration, and the importance of calling emergency services. Naloxone is a low-risk, high-benefit intervention that reverses fatal overdose, and the pharmacist should normalize its availability rather than treating the offer as an accusation.

### Verify the Treatment Framework for Chronic Opioid Therapy

Chronic opioid therapy (generally beyond three months) requires a treatment framework that acute prescribing does not, and the pharmacist must verify its presence. The framework typically includes: a documented treatment agreement that sets expectations and rules (single prescriber, single pharmacy, no early refills, no replacement for lost or stolen medication); periodic urine drug screening to confirm adherence and detect undisclosed substances; periodic review of the treatment goals and continued benefit (pain and function assessment); and documented periodic reassessment of whether the benefits continue to outweigh the risks. When a patient on chronic opioids has none of these in place, the pharmacist must raise the gap with the prescriber — not unilaterally stop the opioid, which can precipitate withdrawal and is outside pharmacy scope, but ensure the prescriber is aware and the framework is established or the regimen reconsidered. Chronic opioid therapy without the framework is unmanaged risk.

### Coordinate Tapering When Risks Exceed Benefits

When the opioid risks have come to exceed the benefits — high MME without functional improvement, adverse events, signs of misuse, or the patient's own desire to reduce — the pharmacist's role is to support a safe, humane taper coordinated with the prescriber. Abrupt opioid discontinuation causes withdrawal (which is severe and drives illicit use) and is rarely appropriate; a slow, individualized taper is the standard. The pharmacist must understand the principles of tapering: reduce by a small percentage (often 5 to 20 percent) every one to four weeks, slower as the dose decreases; convert long-acting and short-acting opioids into a manageable regimen; address breakthrough symptoms with non-opioid strategies; and integrate behavioral support, because tapering is psychologically demanding. The pharmacist must not initiate or dictate a taper independently of the prescriber, but must support the prescriber's plan, counsel the patient on what to expect, monitor adherence and symptoms, and flag complications that warrant slowing the taper or re-evaluating.

### Integrate Non-Opioid and Multimodal Pain Management

Opioid stewardship is not only about reducing opioids; it is about ensuring effective pain management through safer means. The pharmacist should be familiar with non-opioid pharmacologic options (acetaminophen, NSAIDs, certain anticonvulsants and antidepressants for neuropathic pain, topical agents, muscle relaxants for specific indications) and their appropriate use, contraindications, and interactions. The pharmacist should also support non-pharmacologic approaches (physical therapy, exercise, cognitive behavioral therapy for pain, interventional procedures) by understanding their role and encouraging their integration. When a patient's pain is not controlled by opioids or when the opioid risks are high, the pharmacist should raise with the prescriber the option of a multimodal plan that reduces opioid reliance — not as a substitute for clinical judgment, but as a contribution of drug expertise to a shared plan.

## Common Traps

### The Dose-as-Written Without MME Conversion

The pharmacist sees a prescription for a "low" dose of a potent opioid (a modest microgram-per-hour fentanyl patch, a few tablets of hydromorphone) and treats it as low-risk without converting to MME. The mechanism is that the dose number looks small in the units it is written in. The false signal is that the number is small, so the risk must be low. The harm is that the same number represents very different MME depending on the opioid, and a "low" dose of a potent opioid may be a high-MME, high-risk regimen. The risk is invisible without the conversion, and the safeguards that should accompany high-MME regimens (naloxone, monitoring, taper consideration) are not deployed.

### Concurrent Benzodiazepine Normalization

A patient on chronic opioids is also prescribed a benzodiazepine, and the combination has persisted for so long that it feels routine and the pharmacist refills both without comment. The mechanism is that the chronic co-prescribing feels established and stable. The false signal is that the combination has been tolerated, so it must be safe. The harm is that the opioid-benzodiazepine combination is one of the most consistent and powerful predictors of fatal overdose, and the apparent stability masks a risk that has not caused harm yet but is the leading contributor to overdose death. The pharmacist must raise the combination with the prescriber at every opportunity, advocate for a plan to address it (taper one or both, document the rationale if continuation is deemed essential), and ensure naloxone is available — not normalize it through familiarity.

### Naloxone Offered as Accusation

The pharmacist avoids offering naloxone because it feels like telling the patient they are at risk of overdose, which feels judgmental and may offend. The mechanism is that the offer feels stigmatizing. The false signal is that withholding the offer protects the relationship. The harm is that the patient most at risk of overdose is exactly the patient who needs naloxone, and the offer framed as a routine safety measure (like a smoke detector) is well-received by most patients and saves lives. The pharmacist should normalize naloxone as a standard safety tool for anyone on opioids, particularly at higher MME or with risk factors, rather than gating the offer on a judgment of who is "at risk."

### Abrupt Discontinuation as Stewardship

The pharmacist or prescriber identifies excessive opioid risk and stops the opioid abruptly. The mechanism is that stopping the dangerous drug feels like the responsible action. The false signal is that the opioid is discontinued, so the risk is removed. The harm is that abrupt discontinuation precipitates withdrawal, which is severe and which drives patients to illicit opioids (often more dangerous than the prescribed opioid), overdose (with lost tolerance), or crisis. Stewardship is the management of risk over time through a planned, supported taper, not the sudden removal of a dependence-producing drug. The pharmacist must advocate for and support a taper, not for abrupt cessation.

### Treating the Opioid in Isolation From the Pain

The pharmacist focuses on reducing the opioid without engaging the underlying pain that the opioid was treating. The mechanism is that the opioid is the visible risk, so reducing it is the visible task. The false signal is that the opioid dose is decreasing, so stewardship is succeeding. The harm is that unmanaged pain drives both non-adherence to the taper and the substitution of illicit opioids or alcohol, and the patient whose pain is untreated is not improved by the opioid reduction. Effective stewardship integrates the opioid reduction with a plan for the pain — non-opioid medications, non-pharmacologic therapies, and the prescriber's clinical reassessment — or the taper fails and the patient is worse off.

## Self-Check

- Did I calculate the total daily MME across all opioids the patient is taking, and did I apply risk thresholds (50 MME/day, 90 MME/day) to determine the required safeguards?
- Did I assess overdose risk factors (concurrent benzodiazepines, prior overdose, sleep apnea, organ impairment, mental health) and ensure naloxone is available and the patient and household are counseled on its use?
- For chronic opioid therapy, did I verify the treatment framework (single prescriber and pharmacy, treatment agreement, urine drug screening, periodic goal and benefit reassessment) is in place, and did I raise gaps with the prescriber?
- When risks exceed benefits, did I support a coordinated, slow, individualized taper with the prescriber — addressing breakthrough symptoms and integrating behavioral support — rather than advocating abrupt discontinuation?
- Did I integrate non-opioid and multimodal pain management options into the discussion with the prescriber and the patient, rather than treating the opioid in isolation from the underlying pain?
- For any patient on opioids plus benzodiazepines, did I raise the combination with the prescriber and advocate for a plan, rather than normalizing the combination through familiarity?
