# Controlled Substance Recordkeeping and Inventory

The recordkeeping and inventory requirements for controlled substances exist for one reason: to make diversion detectable. Every requirement — the initial and biennial inventories, the perpetual tracking, the dispensing logs, the receipt and transfer records, the destruction documentation, the discrepancy reporting — is a control whose purpose is to ensure that every dose can be accounted for from receipt to dispensing or destruction, and that any dose that disappears is identified promptly and investigated. The danger is that these controls are treated as bureaucratic paperwork rather than as the diversion-detection system they are. When the pharmacist treats the records as a compliance formality, the reconciliation as a periodic nuisance, and the discrepancy as a rounding error to be written off, the entire detection system goes silent — and diversion, whether by an external actor or by staff with access, continues undetected until the loss becomes catastrophic or a regulator arrives.

## Core Rules

### Maintain a Complete and Accurate Inventory System

The foundation of controlled-substance accountability is a complete inventory system that tracks every dose from receipt to final disposition. The pharmacist must ensure the pharmacy maintains: an initial inventory taken on the date the pharmacy first handles controlled substances (or on a change of pharmacist in charge); a biennial inventory taken every two years on a specific date; and, for Schedule II substances, a perpetual inventory that is updated with every receipt, dispensing, and adjustment. The inventory must record, for each controlled substance, the name, dosage form, strength, and quantity on hand, counted in a manner appropriate to the dosage form (opened containers counted by content, sealed commercial containers that may be counted by content or, where permitted, recorded as received). The inventory must be signed and dated by the person taking it. The system must be designed so that, at any moment, the pharmacy can produce an accurate count of every controlled substance on hand and reconcile it against what the records say should be on hand.

### Reconcile Regularly and Investigate Every Discrepancy

Inventory records are useful only if they are reconciled against actual counts and only if discrepancies are investigated. The pharmacist must establish a reconciliation schedule — at minimum the biennial inventory, but more frequently (monthly, weekly, or per-shift for high-volume or high-risk substances) for tighter control — and must perform the reconciliation by physically counting the on-hand quantity and comparing it to the perpetual record. Every discrepancy — a short count, an over count, an unexplained adjustment — must be investigated promptly. The investigation must trace the relevant transactions (receipts, dispensing, transfers, returns, destructions) to identify the source of the discrepancy: a data entry error, a dispensing miscount, a receipt that was not logged, or — most seriously — a diversion. A discrepancy that is not investigated is a diverted dose that is allowed to continue disappearing. The pharmacist must document the investigation and its conclusion, and must escalate to the DEA and state authorities when the discrepancy is significant or unexplained after investigation.

### Document Receipts, Transfers, and Destructions Through the Required Forms

Every movement of a controlled substance into, out of, or within the pharmacy's custody must be documented through the form the regulation requires. Receipts of Schedule II substances must be documented on a DEA Form 222 (or the electronic equivalent), with the form retained and the transaction logged; receipts of all controlled substances must be logged with date, supplier, drug, and quantity. Transfers between pharmacies or to reverse distributors must be documented with the receiving party, the drug, the quantity, and the date, and Schedule II transfers require a Form 222. Destructions of controlled substances must be documented with the drug, quantity, date, method of destruction, and the witnesses, and must be performed through a reverse distributor or via a method authorized by the DEA, with the records retained. The pharmacist must understand which form is required for which transaction and must ensure the form is completed correctly and retained for the required period (generally a minimum of two years, with some records retained longer). A movement that is not documented through the correct form is a break in the chain of custody that diverts the dose from accountability.

### Secure Storage and Limit Access

Physical security is a control that prevents diversion at the source, and the pharmacist must maintain it. Controlled substances must be stored in a substantially constructed, securely locked cabinet or safe, dispersed throughout the stock (so that a theft is not concentrated in one location) or stored as a class, with access limited to the minimum number of authorized personnel. The pharmacist in charge is responsible for the security of the controlled-substance storage, including the keys and combinations, and must control and document who has access. Open access — a controlled-substance cabinet left unlocked, a combination shared widely, a stock room accessible to non-pharmacy staff — defeats every downstream recordkeeping control, because diversion becomes trivial and the records cannot distinguish legitimate from illegitimate dispensing. Access must be limited, controlled, and revocable when personnel change.

### Report Losses and Thefts Promptly

A theft, significant loss, or unexplained discrepancy of controlled substances must be reported to the DEA and, where required, to state authorities, promptly upon discovery, using the DEA Form 106. The pharmacist must not delay reporting while completing the investigation, must not write off the loss as a routine adjustment, and must not omit the report because the loss seems small or embarrassing. The report is the mechanism by which diversion patterns are detected across pharmacies and regions — a single pharmacy's loss may be one data point in a pattern of a diverter targeting multiple pharmacies — and the failure to report conceals the pattern. The pharmacist must cooperate with any subsequent investigation and must take corrective action to address the vulnerability the loss revealed.

## Common Traps

### The Biennial Inventory as a Compliance Ritual

The biennial inventory is performed on schedule, signed, and filed, but the perpetual tracking between inventories is incomplete and no reconciliation occurs in the interim. The mechanism is that the biennial inventory fulfills the explicit regulatory requirement, so the obligation feels met. The false signal is that the inventory was taken and documented, so accountability is maintained. The harm is that two years of unaudited perpetual records can conceal substantial diversion — a technician diverting small quantities accumulates a large loss over two years — and the biennial inventory discovers it only at the end, long after the diversion could have been detected and stopped. Reconciliation must occur far more frequently than biennially for meaningful detection.

### Discrepancy Written Off as Rounding

A reconciliation reveals a small shortage, and the pharmacist records it as an adjustment — a rounding error, a miscount, a data entry mistake — without investigation. The mechanism is that small discrepancies are common and usually benign, so writing them off feels efficient. The false signal is that the discrepancy was small, so it must be insignificant. The harm is that diversion often begins as small, frequent discrepancies that look like rounding errors, and each write-off confirms to the diverter that the discrepancy will not be investigated. A pattern of small unexplained shortages is a stronger diversion signal than a single large one, and writing them off as adjustments conceals the pattern until the loss is large.

### Chain-of-Custody Break on Transfers and Destructions

A controlled substance is transferred to another pharmacy or sent for destruction, and the transaction is logged without the receiving party's confirmation, or the destruction is performed without the required witness documentation. The mechanism is that the transaction was initiated and recorded, so the chain feels intact. The false signal is that the record shows the drug left the pharmacy, so it is accounted for. The harm is that without the receiving confirmation or the witness documentation, the chain of custody is broken — the drug may not have arrived, may have been diverted in transit, or may not have been destroyed — and the record cannot distinguish a legitimate transfer from a diversion. Every transfer and destruction must be documented with the receiving party's confirmation and the required witnesses, or the chain is not intact.

### Open Access Defeating the Records

The controlled-substance cabinet is left unlocked during business hours, or the combination is known to all staff including non-pharmacy personnel, because locking and unlocking for every transaction is inconvenient. The mechanism is that open access is efficient and the staff are trusted. The false signal is that no loss has been detected, so access must be controlled. The harm is that open access makes every recordkeeping control unreliable — any dose can be removed without a corresponding dispensing record, and the discrepancy, when discovered, cannot be attributed because everyone had access. Physical security is the prerequisite for record integrity; without it, the records are theater.

### Loss Report Delayed for Investigation

A significant discrepancy is discovered, and the pharmacist delays the DEA Form 106 report while completing an internal investigation to "be sure" before reporting. The mechanism is that the pharmacist wants certainty before making a report that has consequences. The false signal is that a thorough investigation will produce a clearer report. The harm is that the regulation requires prompt reporting upon discovery, and delay conceals the loss from the cross-pharmacy pattern detection that the report supports; moreover, a delayed report looks like concealment and exposes the pharmacy to enforcement action. The report must be filed upon discovery, with the investigation continuing and supplemental information provided as it develops.

## Self-Check

- Do I maintain a complete inventory system — initial, biennial, and perpetual for Schedule II — that can produce an accurate count of every controlled substance on hand at any time?
- Do I reconcile on a schedule frequent enough to detect diversion promptly (not just biennially), and do I investigate and document every discrepancy rather than writing it off as rounding?
- For every receipt, transfer, and destruction, do I use the correct DEA form (Form 222, dispensing log, Form 106 for losses), completed correctly and retained for the required period?
- Is controlled-substance storage substantially constructed, locked, access-limited to authorized personnel, with the pharmacist in charge controlling keys and combinations and documenting who has access?
- Upon discovery of a theft, significant loss, or unexplained discrepancy, do I file the DEA Form 106 promptly without delaying for investigation, and do I cooperate with subsequent inquiry and take corrective action?
- Do I treat the records as a diversion-detection system rather than a compliance formality, recognizing that every control exists to make a missing dose identifiable and investigatable?
