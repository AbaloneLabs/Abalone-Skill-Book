# Antipsychotic Selection and Metabolic Monitoring

Antipsychotic selection is a decision in which the same drug that controls psychosis can silently produce weight gain, dyslipidemia, hyperglycemia, and accelerated cardiovascular disease, so the pharmacist is choosing not only an agent for the current episode but a trajectory of metabolic risk that may outlast the psychiatric illness itself. The judgment problem is that efficacy differences between agents are modest while metabolic and sedative side effects differ by orders of magnitude, and the patient most vulnerable to psychosis (young, first-episode, often with limited insight and follow-up) is the patient most vulnerable to untreated metabolic harm. Selection and monitoring are inseparable: choosing an agent commits the patient to a monitoring plan, and a monitoring plan that is never executed converts a manageable risk into a silent disease.

## Core Rules

### Select by Side-Effect Profile and Patient Vulnerability, Not by Habit

Within the atypical antipsychotic class, efficacy is broadly similar, but metabolic burden is not. Olanzapine and clozapine carry the highest risk of weight gain, dyslipidemia, and hyperglycemia; quetiapine, risperidone, and paliperidone are intermediate; aripiprazole, ziprasidone, lurasidone, and cariprazine are comparatively metabolically sparing. Match the agent to the patient's baseline risk: a young, first-episode patient with a normal metabolic profile should not begin on olanzapine when a metabolically neutral agent would control symptoms, because the weight and glucose changes can be rapid and difficult to reverse. For a patient with established obesity, diabetes, or cardiovascular disease, prefer the metabolically sparing agents from the outset. Document the reasoning that links the choice to the patient's baseline weight, lipids, glucose, and cardiovascular risk.

### Capture Baseline Metabolic Parameters Before or at the Start of Therapy

Metabolic monitoring is only interpretable against a baseline. Obtain weight and body mass index, waist circumference, fasting glucose or hemoglobin A1c, and a fasting lipid panel before or shortly after starting any second-generation antipsychotic, and repeat them at defined intervals. Without baseline values, a later abnormal glucose cannot be attributed to the drug, to the patient's pre-existing risk, or to progression, and the opportunity to intervene early is lost. The pharmacist should confirm baseline labs exist or are ordered, and should treat a prescription for a metabolically burdensome agent without baseline data as a gap to close, not a complete order.

### Follow a Structured Monitoring Schedule, Not Ad Hoc Checking

Metabolic adverse effects have characteristic time courses: weight gain often appears within weeks, glucose and lipid changes within months, and cardiovascular consequences over years. Follow a defined schedule such as baseline plus four to twelve weeks for weight, then quarterly for weight and annually or more often for fasting glucose, hemoglobin A1c, and lipids, with more frequent monitoring for high-risk agents and patients. The pharmacist should track these intervals, prompt the team when they are missed, and treat an unmonitored patient on a high-risk agent as a patient at risk of undetected harm. Ad hoc monitoring that occurs only at clinic visits reliably misses the patients who do not return.

### Address Prolactin, Extrapyramidal, and Sedative Burden Alongside Metabolic Risk

Risperidone and paliperidone are notable for prolactin elevation, which can cause gynecomastia, galactorrhea, menstrual irregularity, and sexual dysfunction, and which should be assessed clinically with laboratory confirmation when symptoms arise. Extrapyramidal symptoms, akathisia, and tardive dyskinesia are risks that vary by agent and dose, with higher-potency first-generation agents and some atypicals carrying greater motor risk. Sedation, orthostatic hypotension, and QT prolongation further differentiate agents and must be matched to the patient (for example, avoiding a sedating, hypotensive agent in an older patient at fall risk). The pharmacist should keep the full side-effect profile in view, not only the metabolic axis, and should select and dose accordingly.

### Engage the Patient in Self-Monitoring and Lifestyle Intervention

Pharmacologic monitoring is necessary but not sufficient; the patient's own observations of weight, appetite, energy, and motor symptoms are early signals that precede laboratory change. Counsel the patient and family to track weight at home, to report rapid weight gain, increased thirst or urination, tremor, restlessness, and involuntary movements, and to maintain diet and activity as feasible. For patients on high-risk agents, a structured weight-management and dietary intervention should be offered proactively rather than after weight gain has occurred. The patient who understands why monitoring matters is more likely to return for it and to act on early signals.

### Define a Response and Reassessment Plan, Including Switching for Metabolic Harm

Antipsychotic therapy should have defined response criteria (target symptoms, timing of reassessment) and a plan for what to do if the agent is effective but metabolically harmful. If a patient gains significant weight or develops dysglycemia on olanzapine or quetiapine, the options include dose reduction, addition of metformin under direction, behavioral intervention, or a switch to a metabolically sparing agent, balancing the risk of relapse against the harm of continued metabolic progression. The pharmacist should ensure the plan is explicit, because the default in the absence of a plan is to continue an effective but harmful agent indefinitely while the metabolic disease advances.

## Common Traps

### Defaulting to a Familiar Agent Without Considering Metabolic Burden

A prescriber or pharmacist reaches for olanzapine or quetiapine because they are familiar and broadly effective, and applies them to a young first-episode patient whose baseline metabolic risk is low. The mechanism is cognitive economy and the comfort of a known drug. The false signal is that an effective, familiar agent is a safe default. The harm is rapid weight gain, dyslipidemia, and sometimes new-onset diabetes within months, converting a psychiatric treatment into a cardiovascular risk trajectory that may persist even if the drug is later stopped. The corrective is to select by side-effect profile and baseline vulnerability, reserving metabolically burdensome agents for patients in whom they are clearly justified.

### Ordering the Drug Without Baseline Metabolic Data

The prescription is verified and dispensed, but no baseline weight, glucose, or lipid values are obtained, so later changes cannot be interpreted. The mechanism is that the prescription looks complete and the labs are treated as someone else's responsibility. The false signal is that the order is actionable as written. The harm is that early metabolic change goes undetected, attribution becomes impossible, and the window for switching or intervening closes before harm is recognized. The corrective is to confirm baseline parameters exist or are ordered before or at the start of any second-generation antipsychotic.

### Monitoring Only When the Patient Returns to Clinic

Metabolic monitoring is deferred to whatever clinic visits happen to occur, so patients who miss visits or are lost to follow-up accumulate unmonitored risk. The mechanism is passive reliance on visits rather than active tracking. The false signal is that monitoring happens because the patient is in care. The harm is that the patients at highest metabolic risk, who are often the least stable in follow-up, are precisely the ones who go unmonitored, and their diabetes or dyslipidemia presents only at complication. The corrective is to track monitoring intervals actively and to prompt the team and patient when they lapse.

### Treating Weight Gain as an Expected, Untreatable Consequence

The patient gains weight on an antipsychotic and the team accepts it as the unavoidable cost of treatment, without considering dose reduction, behavioral intervention, metformin, or a switch to a metabolically neutral agent. The mechanism is therapeutic fatalism: the drug works, so its side effects must be endured. The false signal is that weight gain is intrinsic and immutable. The harm is that preventable metabolic disease progresses to diabetes and cardiovascular events, shortening life in a population already at elevated risk. The corrective is to treat significant weight gain as an actionable signal requiring a documented response, not an accepted outcome.

### Focusing on Metabolic Risk While Ignoring Prolactin, Motor, and Sedative Effects

The team monitors weight and glucose diligently but never asks about sexual dysfunction, gynecomastia, akathisia, or tardive symptoms, so these burdens accumulate unrecognized. The mechanism is that metabolic monitoring has become protocolized while the other axes have not. The false signal is that the monitored parameters represent the full risk. The harm is that prolactin-related and motor side effects drive non-adherence and distress, and tardive dyskinesia may become irreversible before it is detected. The corrective is to keep the complete side-effect profile in view and to assess each axis on a defined schedule.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The standard adult outpatient with regular follow-up is handled well, but the unusual patient such as a child or adolescent, a pregnant patient, an older adult at fall risk, a first-episode patient with no prior exposure, or a patient with pre-existing diabetes is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is safe while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- Did I select the antipsychotic by side-effect profile and the patient's baseline metabolic, motor, prolactin, and sedative vulnerability, rather than by habit?
- Were baseline weight, body mass index, fasting glucose or hemoglobin A1c, and fasting lipids obtained or ordered before or shortly after starting therapy?
- Is there a defined monitoring schedule (early weight check, then quarterly weight and periodic glucose and lipids) appropriate to the agent's metabolic risk?
- Did I assess and plan to monitor prolactin-related symptoms, extrapyramidal symptoms, akathisia, and tardive dyskinesia, not only metabolic parameters?
- Did I counsel the patient and family on self-monitoring of weight, appetite, thirst, motor symptoms, and the importance of returning for scheduled labs?
- For a patient with significant weight gain or dysglycemia, is there an explicit documented plan (dose reduction, behavioral intervention, metformin, or switch) rather than passive acceptance?
- Did I confirm the choice and monitoring plan are appropriate for special populations such as first-episode, pediatric, pregnant, or older adult patients?
- Is the selection rationale documented so another practitioner can review the basis and the monitoring commitment?
