# Heparin and LMWH Dosing and Reversal

Heparin therapy, whether unfractionated heparin by intravenous infusion or low-molecular-weight heparin by subcutaneous injection, is among the most error-prone areas of inpatient pharmacy because the same drug is used across a wide range of indications, weights, and renal functions, and because the consequences of error are immediate in both directions, bleeding from overdose and thrombosis from underdose. The judgment problem is that heparin dosing is governed by nomograms and weight bands that look mechanical but require continuous clinical judgment: the nomogram assumes a typical patient, and the patient in front of you may be obese, renally impaired, bleeding, hypercoagulable, or developing heparin-induced thrombocytopenia. The pharmacist must apply the nomogram while watching for the situations where the nomogram is wrong, and must treat protamine reversal as a precise calculation, not a guess.

## Core Rules

### Dose Unfractionated Heparin by Weight-Based Nomogram and Adjust to the aPTT or Anti-Xa

Intravenous unfractionated heparin for acute thrombosis is dosed by a weight-based nomogram, with a bolus and a maintenance infusion titrated to a target activated partial thromboplastin time or, increasingly, an anti-Xa level, because the aPTT can be unreliable in heparin resistance, liver disease, and other states. The pharmacist should verify the bolus and initial infusion rate are weight-based, that the target range is defined, and that the nomogram's dose adjustments are being followed at each check. Deviations from the nomogram should be intentional and documented, because ad hoc adjustments produce both subtherapeutic and supratherapeutic results. For patients with heparin resistance or confounded aPTT values, advocate for anti-Xa monitoring to guide the infusion accurately.

### Adjust Low-Molecular-Weight Heparin for Renal Function and Body Weight

Low-molecular-weight heparins such as enoxaparin are renally cleared, so they accumulate in renal impairment and require dose reduction or avoidance below defined creatinine clearance thresholds, and some agents are contraindicated in severe renal failure. Body weight also matters: standard doses may be inadequate in obesity, and many centers use weight-based or capped dosing with anti-Xa monitoring for pregnant, obese, or renally impaired patients. The pharmacist must confirm a current creatinine clearance before dispensing, must adjust the dose to renal function, and must consider weight-based dosing and monitoring in obese or pregnant patients. A standard prophylactic or treatment dose applied without these adjustments is a common source of both bleeding in renal failure and thrombosis in obesity.

### Recognize and Respond to Heparin-Induced Thrombocytopenia

Heparin-induced thrombocytopenia is a paradoxical prothrombotic complication of heparin in which antibodies form against platelet factor 4 complexes, causing platelet activation, thrombocytopenia, and a high risk of new thrombosis. The pharmacist must monitor platelet counts in any patient on heparin or low-molecular-weight heparin, must recognize the typical pattern of a platelet fall beginning days after exposure (or rapidly on re-exposure), and must understand that when HIT is suspected, all heparin exposure including line flushes must stop and a non-heparin anticoagulant must be started, because stopping anticoagulation entirely in HIT invites thrombosis. Warfarin must not be started until the platelet count recovers, because early warfarin in HIT can cause skin necrosis and venous limb gangrene. Low-molecular-weight heparin is not a safe substitute for unfractionated heparin when HIT is suspected.

### Manage Bridging Therapy With Clear Start, Stop, and Overlap Criteria

Bridging with heparin or low-molecular-weight heparin around warfarin initiation or interruption is used when a patient's thrombotic risk is high enough that a period without anticoagulation is unsafe. Bridging requires clear criteria: when to start the heparin relative to the warfarin or the procedure, when to stop it, and what overlap is required. The pharmacist should confirm the bridging plan is explicit and appropriate to the patient's thrombotic and bleeding risk, because unnecessary bridging adds bleeding risk and inadequate bridging adds thrombotic risk. For perioperative bridging, plan the last dose of low-molecular-weight heparin based on its half-life and the procedure's bleeding risk, and plan resumption based on hemostasis.

### Calculate Protamine Reversal Precisely and Understand Its Limits

Protamine reverses unfractionated heparin and partially reverses low-molecular-weight heparin, but the dose depends on how much heparin remains active, which depends on the time since the last dose and the route. For unfractionated heparin, protamine is dosed to neutralize the circulating heparin based on the time since the infusion stopped. For low-molecular-weight heparin, protamine only partially reverses the anti-Xa effect, with diminishing effect the longer the interval, and complete reversal is not achievable, so additional supportive measures may be needed in major bleeding. The pharmacist must calculate the protamine dose from the residual heparin, must avoid excessive protamine (which itself has anticoagulant effect), and must understand the limits of reversal for low-molecular-weight heparin. Protamine has its own risks, including hypotension and hypersensitivity, and should be administered slowly with monitoring.

### Reconcile All Heparin Exposure, Including Flushes and Locked Lines

A meaningful fraction of heparin errors and of HIT cases comes from uncounted heparin exposure in line flushes, heparin locks, and heparin-coated catheters, which are easy to overlook because they are not written as medication orders. The pharmacist must identify all sources of heparin exposure when calculating total dose, when assessing bleeding or thrombocytopenia, and when HIT is suspected and all heparin must be eliminated. A patient with apparent heparin resistance, unexpected bleeding, or a falling platelet count may be receiving heparin from a source that was never reconciled into the medication review.

## Common Traps

### Using Standard Heparin Doses Without Weight or Renal Adjustment

A fixed heparin dose or a standard enoxaparin dose is applied without adjusting for body weight or renal function. The mechanism is that the default dose is fast and the adjustments feel like extra work. The false signal is that the standard dose is a safe default. The harm is subtherapeutic anticoagulation and thrombosis in obese patients who need more, and accumulation with bleeding in renally impaired patients who need less. The corrective is to verify weight-based dosing for unfractionated heparin and renal and weight adjustment for low-molecular-weight heparin every time.

### Following the Nomogram Mechanically Without Clinical Context

The nomogram is applied without recognizing that the patient has heparin resistance, a confounded aPTT, active bleeding, or evolving thrombosis. The mechanism is that the nomogram feels objective and complete. The false signal is that following the algorithm is sufficient. The harm is that the algorithm cannot detect the patient for whom the aPTT is misleading, and the heparin is titrated to a number that does not reflect the patient's actual anticoagulation, producing either bleeding or thrombosis. The corrective is to interpret the nomogram in context and to use anti-Xa monitoring where the aPTT is unreliable.

### Missing Heparin-Induced Thrombocytopenia Because the Platelet Fall Is Attributed Elsewhere

A falling platelet count in a heparinized patient is attributed to infection, sepsis, or other causes without considering HIT, and heparin is continued. The mechanism is that thrombocytopenia is common in sick inpatients and has many causes. The false signal is that another plausible cause explains the platelet fall. The harm is that HIT progresses to limb- or life-threatening thrombosis while the precipitant continues to be given, and the opportunity to switch to a non-heparin anticoagulant is missed. The corrective is to monitor platelet trends on heparin and to consider HIT whenever the count falls in the typical window, stopping all heparin exposure and starting an alternative anticoagulant.

### Substituting LMWH for UFH When HIT Is Suspected

When HIT is suspected, the patient is switched from unfractionated heparin to low-molecular-weight heparin on the assumption that it is safer. The mechanism is the belief that low-molecular-weight heparin is a gentler heparin. The false signal is that low-molecular-weight heparin does not cause HIT. The harm is that low-molecular-weight heparin can perpetuate and worsen HIT, because it cross-reacts with the pathogenic antibodies, and the patient develops further thrombosis. The corrective is to switch to a non-heparin anticoagulant when HIT is suspected, never to another heparin product.

### Giving Protamine Without Calculating the Residual Heparin or Recognizing LMWH Limits

Protamine is given as a fixed large dose to reverse heparin or low-molecular-weight heparin without calculating the residual circulating heparin. The mechanism is the reflex to reverse fully and quickly. The false signal is that more protamine means more complete reversal. The harm is that excessive protamine has its own anticoagulant and hypotensive effect, worsening bleeding, and that for low-molecular-weight heparin the reversal is only partial regardless of protamine dose, giving false reassurance. The corrective is to calculate protamine from residual heparin based on timing and route and to recognize the limits of low-molecular-weight heparin reversal.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The standard adult with a normal weight and renal function is handled well, but the unusual case such as a morbidly obese patient, a patient in renal failure, a pregnant patient needing anti-Xa monitoring, a patient with heparin resistance, someone with suspected HIT, or a patient with heparin exposure hidden in line flushes is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is safe while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- Did I verify that unfractionated heparin is dosed by a weight-based nomogram with a defined target aPTT or anti-Xa range, and that adjustments follow the nomogram?
- Did I adjust the low-molecular-weight heparin dose for current renal function and consider weight-based dosing and anti-Xa monitoring in obesity, pregnancy, or renal impairment?
- Did I monitor the platelet count for heparin-induced thrombocytopenia, and do I know the plan if the count falls in the typical window?
- If HIT is suspected, did I ensure all heparin exposure, including line flushes and locks, is stopped and a non-heparin anticoagulant is started, with warfarin deferred until platelet recovery?
- For bridging therapy, is the start, stop, and overlap plan explicit and matched to the patient's thrombotic and bleeding risk?
- Did I calculate protamine reversal from the residual circulating heparin based on time and route, and recognize the partial and time-dependent limits of low-molecular-weight heparin reversal?
- Did I reconcile all sources of heparin exposure, including flushes, locks, and heparin-coated lines, into the total dose and the HIT assessment?
- For special situations such as heparin resistance, a confounded aPTT, pregnancy, obesity, or renal failure, did I use appropriate monitoring and dosing rather than the standard path?
