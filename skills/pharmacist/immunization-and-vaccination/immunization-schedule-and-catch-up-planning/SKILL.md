# Immunization Schedule and Catch-Up Planning

Determining the right vaccine at the right time for the right patient is a scheduling problem that depends on age, history, risk factors, and the complex spacing rules that govern valid dosing. The danger is that the schedule is treated as a simple age-based lookup (a 65-year-old needs the shingles vaccine) rather than a patient-specific calculation that accounts for what the patient has already received, what they are missing, the minimum intervals between doses, and the conditions (pregnancy, immunocompromise, travel, occupation) that change the recommendation. A patient who is "due" for a dose but receives it before the minimum interval (invalid dose), a patient who is missing doses but is told they are "up to date" (under-vaccinated), a patient whose pregnancy or immunocompromise changes the recommended vaccines (wrong vaccine or missed indication) — each represents a planning failure that leaves the patient unprotected or inappropriately vaccinated. The pharmacist's discipline is to assess the patient's full immunization history, to calculate the valid schedule (routine or catch-up) based on current ACIP recommendations, and to adjust for the conditions that change the plan.

## Core Rules

### Build the Vaccination Plan From the Patient's Documented History, Not From Age Alone

The routine immunization schedule is age-based, but the patient's actual need is determined by their documented history — what they have received, what they are missing, and what is currently due based on the intervals. The pharmacist must obtain the patient's immunization history from the immunization registry, the patient's records, and the patient's self-report (with verification where possible), and must use the history to determine the gaps. A patient who is 65 and "due" for shingles vaccine may also be missing a pneumococcal series, a Tdap booster, an updated COVID-19 dose, and a hepatitis series if they are at risk — and the age-based lookup misses all of these. The pharmacist must assess the full schedule for the patient's age and risk, must identify every vaccine that is due, overdue, or missing, and must build a plan that addresses all of them, not only the vaccine the patient requested. The history is the foundation, and a plan built without it is a plan built on assumption.

### Apply the Minimum Intervals and Minimum Ages to Every Dose Decision

Every dose in a vaccine series has a minimum age (the youngest age at which the dose is valid) and a minimum interval (the shortest time from the previous dose at which the next dose is valid), and a dose given before the minimum age or interval is invalid and must be repeated. The pharmacist must know the minimum intervals for each vaccine series (which vary by the dose number — the interval between dose 1 and 2 differs from the interval between 2 and 3) and must apply them to every dose decision. For a patient on a routine schedule, the intervals are met by the standard timing; for a patient on a catch-up schedule, the intervals must be calculated from the doses already received, and the next dose must be scheduled no earlier than the minimum interval from the last valid dose. The pharmacist must not administer a dose before its minimum interval (even if the patient is "due" or "overdue" by the calendar), because the invalid dose does not count toward the series and must be repeated, wasting the dose and delaying protection. The "grace period" (a few days before the minimum interval) is a narrow exception, not a license to dose early, and the pharmacist must apply it conservatively.

### Construct Catch-Up Schedules Using the Current ACIP Catch-Up Tables

For a patient who is behind on immunizations (an adult who never completed a childhood series, an immigrant whose vaccination record is incomplete, a patient who deferred during a pandemic), the pharmacist must construct a catch-up schedule using the current ACIP catch-up tables, which specify the minimum intervals for each dose in a series that is being accelerated. The catch-up schedule is not a guess; it is a defined calculation that produces the earliest valid dates for each remaining dose, and the pharmacist must follow it precisely. For multi-dose series (HPV, hepatitis B, the pneumococcal series), the catch-up schedule may compress the intervals to the minimum, allowing the series to be completed faster than the routine schedule. For series with complex rules (the pneumococcal series with PCV and PPSV sequencing, the hepatitis B series with the accelerated and four-dose options), the pharmacist must reference the specific catch-up guidance and must not improvise. The catch-up plan must be documented (which doses are needed, on what dates, and the rationale), so the patient and the next provider can continue the series correctly.

### Adjust the Schedule for Pregnancy, Immunocompromise, Travel, and Occupation

The routine schedule is modified by conditions that change the vaccine indications, the vaccine choice, or the timing, and the pharmacist must adjust the plan for each. Pregnancy modifies the plan: influenza (inactivated) and Tdap (in each pregnancy, ideally 27-36 weeks) are recommended and prioritized; live vaccines (MMR, varicella, LAIV) are contraindicated and must be deferred until after delivery; RSV vaccination may be recommended depending on timing and season; COVID-19 vaccination is recommended. Immunocompromise modifies the plan: live vaccines are contraindicated; additional doses of certain vaccines may be recommended (extra pneumococcal doses, meningococcal coverage for complement deficiency); the response to inactivated vaccines may be reduced, requiring post-vaccination serology for some (hepatitis B in dialysis patients). Travel modifies the plan: travel vaccines (yellow fever, typhoid, Japanese encephalitis, rabies pre-exposure) may be indicated based on destination, and the timing (some require a series started weeks before travel) must be planned. Occupation and risk modify the plan: healthcare workers need hepatitis B and influenza; meningococcal vaccine for dormitory or military settings; hepatitis A for certain occupations. The pharmacist must assess these factors and must adjust the plan, because the routine schedule applied to a patient with modifying conditions leaves specific risks unaddressed.

### Coordinate Multiple Vaccines at the Same Visit Using Co-Administration Rules

When a patient is due for multiple vaccines, the pharmacist should co-administer them at the same visit (to minimize visits and improve completion), and must apply the co-administration rules to ensure all doses are valid. Most inactivated vaccines can be co-administered at the same visit, given at different sites (separated by at least one inch, in different anatomical sites if possible, and documented by site). Live vaccines can be co-administered on the same day; if not given on the same day, they must be separated by at least 28 days (the second live vaccine given before 28 days is invalid and must be repeated). COVID-19 vaccines can be co-administered with other vaccines (current guidance). The pharmacist must document each vaccine by site (so a local reaction can be attributed to the correct vaccine), must use separate syringes and separate sites, and must apply the spacing rules to avoid invalid doses. Co-administration increases the chance that the patient completes the needed vaccines, but it requires the pharmacist to manage the complexity of multiple vaccines, sites, and documentation at one visit.

### Respect Scope and Escalation Boundaries

Know where the agent's authority and competence end. When the question requires a license, a specialist's judgment, a final approval, or expertise the agent does not hold, the correct action is to escalate rather than to produce a confident answer that overreaches. Scope discipline protects the recipient from harm caused by an unqualified conclusion and protects the agent from liability. State explicitly when the output is advisory and must be confirmed by the qualified person.

## Common Traps

### Treating "Due" or "Overdue" in the Record as Sufficient Without Verifying the Interval

The immunization record or the registry may flag a patient as "due" or "overdue" for a dose based on a calendar calculation, but the flag may not account for the actual date of the previous dose or the minimum interval (especially if the previous dose was given early or if the interval was calculated incorrectly). The trap is that the pharmacist trusts the flag and administers the dose, which may be before the minimum interval from the previous dose, rendering it invalid. The harm is a wasted dose, a patient recorded as vaccinated but not protected, and a series that must be restarted or repeated. The fix is to verify the date of the previous dose and the minimum interval before administering, to calculate whether the dose is valid, and to defer if the interval is not met, even if the record flags the patient as due.

### Planning Only the Requested Vaccine and Missing the Other Indicated Vaccines

A patient who requests a specific vaccine (the flu shot, the shingles vaccine) may also be due for or missing other vaccines, but the pharmacist who focuses only on the requested vaccine misses the opportunity to address the full schedule. The trap is that the requested vaccine is the salient one, so the pharmacist administers it and stops, and the other indicated vaccines (the overdue Tdap, the missing pneumococcal series, the indicated hepatitis vaccine for a risk factor) are not addressed. The harm is a patient who leaves partially vaccinated, believing they are "up to date" because they received the requested vaccine, while remaining unprotected against other preventable diseases. The fix is to assess the full schedule for the patient's age and risk at every immunization encounter, to identify every indicated vaccine, and to offer the additional vaccines (or to document the recommendation and the plan) rather than stopping at the requested vaccine.

### Applying the Routine Schedule to a Patient Who Needs a Catch-Up or Modified Schedule

The routine schedule assumes a patient who has followed the schedule from the start, and applying it to a patient who is behind, who has a modifying condition, or who has an incomplete record produces a plan that does not address the patient's actual need. The trap is that the routine schedule is the default and the easiest to apply, so the pharmacist uses it without recognizing that the patient's history or condition requires a different calculation. The harm is a patient who remains under-vaccinated (the catch-up doses are not given) or who receives a contraindicated or suboptimal vaccine (a live vaccine in pregnancy, a routine dose where an additional dose is indicated for immunocompromise). The fix is to assess the patient's history and conditions before selecting the schedule, to use the catch-up tables for patients behind, and to adjust for pregnancy, immunocompromise, travel, and occupation.

### Failing to Account for a Blood Product or a Recent Live Vaccine Before Scheduling the Next Live Vaccine

A recent blood product extends the interval before a live vaccine can be effective (the antibodies in the product neutralize the vaccine), and a recent live vaccine requires a 28-day interval before the next live vaccine (if not given the same day). The trap is that these constraints are not part of the routine schedule calculation, so the pharmacist schedules the next live vaccine without accounting for them, and the vaccine is neutralized or invalid. The harm is a patient who receives a live vaccine that does not produce immunity (neutralized by a recent blood product) or that must be repeated (given within 28 days of another live vaccine), and the patient is left unprotected or over-vaccinated. The fix is to ask about recent blood products and live vaccines before scheduling any live vaccine, to apply the deferral intervals, and to document the constraint and the rescheduled date.

### Documenting the Dose Without the Plan for the Remaining Series

A multi-dose series requires the patient to return for subsequent doses, and the pharmacist who documents the current dose without documenting the plan for the series (the next dose date, the remaining doses, the completion target) leaves the series to chance. The trap is that the current dose is the immediate task, so it is documented, while the series plan (which requires a calculation and a schedule) is deferred or omitted, and the patient does not return for the next dose because the plan was not communicated or documented. The harm is an incomplete series (the patient is partially protected) and a record that does not reflect the plan, so the next provider cannot continue the series correctly. The fix is to document the series plan (the next dose date based on the minimum interval, the remaining doses, the completion target) at every dose, to communicate the plan to the patient, and to enter the next dose in the recall or reminder system.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- Did I obtain the patient's documented immunization history (registry, records, verified self-report) and use it to identify every vaccine that is due, overdue, or missing — not only the requested vaccine?
- For each dose I planned or administered, did I verify the minimum age and minimum interval against the previous dose, and did I defer any dose that would be invalid if given now?
- For a patient behind on immunizations, did I construct the catch-up schedule using the current ACIP catch-up tables, calculating the earliest valid dates for each remaining dose?
- Did I adjust the schedule for the patient's conditions — pregnancy (prioritize influenza and Tdap, defer live vaccines), immunocompromise (no live vaccines, additional doses where indicated), travel (indicated travel vaccines with adequate lead time), occupation and risk?
- For multiple vaccines at the same visit, did I apply the co-administration rules (separate sites, separate syringes, 28-day separation for live vaccines not given the same day) and document each by site?
- Did I account for recent blood products (deferral interval before a live vaccine) and recent live vaccines (28-day separation) before scheduling the next live vaccine?
- Did I document not only the current dose but the plan for the remaining series (next dose date, remaining doses, completion target), communicate the plan to the patient, and enter the next dose in the recall system?
- Does the output stay within the agent's scope, deferring final authority, licensed judgment, or specialist sign-off to the qualified person where the question exceeds the agent's competence?
