# Vaccine Screening and Contraindication Assessment

Vaccination is one of the few clinical interventions in which a pharmacist independently screens, decides, and administers, and the safety of that decision depends entirely on the quality of the pre-vaccination assessment. The danger is that screening is treated as a checkbox formality — a quick "any allergies?" and "feeling okay?" — rather than a structured assessment that identifies the contraindications and precautions that would make the vaccine dangerous or ineffective for this patient at this time. A patient with a severe egg allergy given influenza vaccine without graded assessment, an immunocompromised patient given a live vaccine, a pregnant patient given a contraindicated live vaccine, a patient who received a blood product and whose live vaccine must be deferred — each represents a screening failure that can cause anaphylaxis, vaccine failure, or fetal harm. The pharmacist's discipline is to conduct the screening as a clinical assessment, to know the specific contraindications and precautions for each vaccine, and to defer or refer when the screening reveals a condition that exceeds the pharmacist's independent authority to manage.

## Core Rules

### Conduct a Structured Pre-Vaccination Assessment for Every Patient Every Time

Every immunization encounter, including a returning patient receiving a different vaccine, must begin with a structured assessment that covers the elements that determine whether the vaccine is safe and appropriate for this patient at this time. The assessment must cover: the patient's current health (acute illness, fever — a moderate or severe acute illness is a precaution, not a contraindication, but warrants deferral until recovered); the patient's allergy history (specifically severe allergic reactions to a previous dose of the vaccine or to a vaccine component, which are contraindications); the patient's immune status (immunocompromising conditions or therapies, which contraindicate live vaccines and alter the response to inactivated vaccines); the patient's pregnancy status (which contraindicates certain live vaccines and is a precaution for others); the patient's vaccination history (what they have received, what is due, what is overdue, and the spacing rules that apply); and the patient's recent blood product or transfusion history (which extends the interval before a live vaccine can be effective). The pharmacist must not abbreviate this assessment under time pressure, because each element identifies a specific risk, and the absence of the assessment is the absence of the safety check.

### Distinguish Contraindications From Precautions and Apply the Correct Response

Contraindications and precautions are distinct categories that require different responses, and the pharmacist must know the distinction for each vaccine. A contraindication means the vaccine must not be administered (a severe allergic reaction to a previous dose or component, severe immunodeficiency for live vaccines, pregnancy for certain live vaccines) — administering despite a contraindication risks serious harm. A precaution means the vaccine should be deferred unless the benefit outweighs the risk in the clinician's judgment (a moderate or severe acute illness, a history of Guillain-Barré syndrome within six weeks of a prior tetanus-toxoid or influenza vaccine, a pregnancy-specific precaution for certain vaccines) — the default is to defer, but the decision may be made to proceed with clinical justification and documentation. The pharmacist must apply contraindications absolutely (defer and refer) and must apply precautions as a default deferral with documented justification for proceeding. The pharmacist must not treat a precaution as a contraindication (which leaves the patient unprotected unnecessarily) or a contraindication as a precaution (which exposes the patient to harm), because the distinction is the safety boundary.

### Know and Apply the Specific Contraindications for Each Vaccine

The contraindications and precautions are vaccine-specific, and the pharmacist must know them for each vaccine administered, not apply a generic "allergy or illness" screen. Live vaccines (MMR, varicella, live attenuated influenza [LAIV], rotavirus) are contraindicated in severe immunodeficiency (primary, acquired, or iatrogenic) and in pregnancy, and LAIV has additional contraindications (young age, immunocompromised close contacts of severely immunocompromised patients requiring protective isolation, certain chronic conditions). Inactivated influenza vaccine requires egg-allergy assessment (a severe egg allergy is a precaution requiring observation and possibly a graded approach or referral, though current guidance has relaxed this for many patients — the pharmacist must follow current ACIP guidance). Pertussis-containing vaccines (DTaP, Tdap) have precautions for progressive neurologic disorders and for a history of specific adverse events following a prior dose. The pharmacist must reference current ACIP recommendations for each vaccine's contraindications and precautions, must not rely on memorized or outdated lists, and must update their knowledge when guidance changes (as it did for egg allergy and for COVID-19 vaccines).

### Evaluate Vaccine Spacing, Catch-Up Schedules, and Co-Administration

Vaccine spacing — the minimum intervals between doses in a series, between different vaccines, and between a vaccine and a blood product — determines whether a dose is valid (counts toward the series) or invalid (must be repeated), and the pharmacist must apply the spacing rules precisely. For a patient on a catch-up schedule, the pharmacist must calculate the valid intervals for each dose based on the doses already received and the minimum intervals, and must not administer a dose before its minimum interval (which would be invalid and require repetition). For co-administration, the pharmacist must know which vaccines can be given at the same visit (most inactivated vaccines can be co-administered; live vaccines given at the same visit are fine, but if not given the same day, must be separated by at least 28 days) and must apply the spacing to avoid an invalid dose. For patients who received a blood product (which contains antibodies that can neutralize a live vaccine), the pharmacist must apply the deferral interval (which varies by the type and dose of blood product, from weeks to months) before administering a live vaccine. The pharmacist must document the spacing rationale, because an invalid dose administered too early is a wasted dose and a patient left unprotected.

### Defer and Refer When the Screening Exceeds Independent Authority

Some screening findings exceed the pharmacist's independent authority to manage and require deferral and referral to the prescriber. A complex allergy history (anaphylaxis to multiple vaccines or components, a history of vaccine-related adverse events), an uncertain immune status (a patient on immunosuppressive therapy whose live-vaccine eligibility is unclear), a pregnancy with a vaccination question outside the pharmacist's protocol, a patient with a history of severe adverse events following vaccination — these require the pharmacist to defer the vaccination, document the finding, communicate with the prescriber, and refer the patient for the prescriber's decision. The pharmacist must recognize the boundary of independent immunization authority (which is defined by state law, protocol, and training) and must not exceed it, because the complexity of these cases requires the integrated clinical judgment of the prescriber who knows the patient's full history. Deferral and referral are not failures; they are the appropriate response to a screening finding that exceeds the pharmacist's scope, and they protect the patient from a vaccination decision made without the full clinical context.

### Respect Scope and Escalation Boundaries

Know where the agent's authority and competence end. When the question requires a license, a specialist's judgment, a final approval, or expertise the agent does not hold, the correct action is to escalate rather than to produce a confident answer that overreaches. Scope discipline protects the recipient from harm caused by an unqualified conclusion and protects the agent from liability. State explicitly when the output is advisory and must be confirmed by the qualified person.

## Common Traps

### Treating the Screening Form as a Checkbox Rather Than a Clinical Assessment

The pre-vaccination questionnaire (often completed by the patient) covers the right elements, but a pharmacist who accepts the completed form without clinical follow-up misses the findings that matter — the patient who checked "no allergies" but has a severe egg allergy they did not associate with the vaccine, the patient who checked "not pregnant" but is uncertain, the patient whose "feeling okay" conceals a moderate acute illness. The trap is that the form feels sufficient, so the pharmacist does not probe, and the finding that would have changed the decision is buried in the form or absent from it. The harm is that the contraindication or precaution is missed, and the vaccine is administered to a patient for whom it is unsafe or ineffective. The fix is to use the form as the starting point for a clinical conversation, probing each element with specific questions (what kind of allergic reaction, how severe, to what; when was your last menstrual period, is pregnancy possible; what are your symptoms and how long) rather than accepting the form as the assessment.

### Applying a Generic Allergy Screen Instead of Vaccine-Specific Component Assessment

"Allergies?" is the standard screening question, but it is insufficient for vaccines because the relevant allergens are the specific vaccine components (egg protein, gelatin, latex in the vial stopper, specific preservatives and adjuvants, the antibiotic trace residues), and a patient may have a severe reaction to a component without identifying it as an "allergy" in the generic question. The trap is that the generic screen identifies the patient's known allergies (penicillin, peanuts) but misses the component-specific risk (the egg allergy that matters for influenza, the gelatin allergy that matters for MMR and varicella, the latex allergy that matters for vial stoppers), because the pharmacist did not connect the component to the patient. The harm is an anaphylactic reaction to a component that was identifiable through a component-specific assessment. The fix is to screen for the specific components of each vaccine being administered, referencing the vaccine's components against the patient's allergy and reaction history, and to defer or observe when a component-specific risk is identified.

### Administering a Live Vaccine Without Checking Immune Status or Pregnancy

Live vaccines are safe for the general population but dangerous for the immunocompromised (the live organism can cause uncontrolled infection) and for the pregnant patient (the live organism can infect the fetus), and the risk is invisible without the assessment. The trap is that the live vaccine is administered routinely (MMR, varicella, LAIV) without the immune-status or pregnancy assessment, because the patient "looks healthy" and "is not obviously pregnant," and the contraindicated condition is not surfaced. The harm is severe: a live vaccine in a severely immunocompromised patient can cause the disease it is meant to prevent; a live vaccine in early pregnancy can cause fetal infection. The fix is to assess immune status (current immunosuppressive therapy, recent chemotherapy, primary immunodeficiency, HIV status) and pregnancy status (last menstrual period, possibility of pregnancy, contraception) before every live vaccine, and to defer or refer when either is uncertain.

### Giving a Dose Before the Minimum Interval Because the Patient Is "Due"

The catch-up schedule and the routine schedule both define minimum intervals between doses, and a patient who presents "due" for a dose may actually be presenting before the minimum interval (because the previous dose was given early, or because the interval is longer than the patient or the system assumed). The trap is that the pharmacist sees "due" or "overdue" in the record and administers the dose without verifying the interval from the previous dose, and the dose is invalid (administered before the minimum interval) and must be repeated. The harm is a wasted dose, a patient who believes they are protected but is not, and a record that appears complete but is invalid. The fix is to verify the date of the previous dose and the minimum interval before administering, to calculate whether the dose is valid, and to defer if the interval is not met, documenting the deferral and the rescheduled date.

### Failing to Account for a Recent Blood Product Before a Live Vaccine

Blood products (transfusion, immune globulin, IVIG) contain antibodies that can neutralize a live vaccine, rendering it ineffective, and the deferral interval after a blood product (before a live vaccine can be administered) varies from weeks to months depending on the product and dose. The trap is that the pharmacist does not ask about recent blood products (the question is not on the standard form, or the patient does not mention it), so the live vaccine is administered within the deferral interval and is neutralized, and the patient is left unprotected without knowing it. The harm is a vaccine failure that is silent (no adverse event, just no protection) and that is discovered only when the patient contracts the disease. The fix is to ask about recent blood products and transfusions before any live vaccine, to reference the deferral interval for the specific product and dose, and to defer the live vaccine until the interval has passed, documenting the reason for deferral.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- For every immunization encounter, did I conduct a structured assessment covering current health, allergy history (component-specific), immune status, pregnancy status, vaccination history, and recent blood products?
- Did I distinguish contraindications (defer absolutely and refer) from precautions (defer by default, proceed only with documented clinical justification), and apply each correctly?
- Did I reference current ACIP recommendations for the specific contraindications and precautions of each vaccine administered, rather than relying on a generic or memorized screen?
- For a catch-up or co-administration scenario, did I verify the minimum intervals between doses and the spacing between live vaccines or between a blood product and a live vaccine, and did I defer any dose that would be invalid?
- Did I screen for the specific vaccine components (egg, gelatin, latex, preservatives, antibiotics) against the patient's allergy and reaction history, not just a generic "allergies?"
- Before any live vaccine, did I assess immune status (immunosuppressive therapy, immunodeficiency, HIV) and pregnancy status (last menstrual period, possibility of pregnancy)?
- When the screening revealed a condition exceeding my independent authority (complex allergy, uncertain immune status, pregnancy outside protocol, severe adverse event history), did I defer, document, communicate with the prescriber, and refer rather than proceeding?
- Did I document the screening findings, the decision rationale, and any deferral or referral, so the record demonstrates the assessment that justified the vaccination or the deferral?
