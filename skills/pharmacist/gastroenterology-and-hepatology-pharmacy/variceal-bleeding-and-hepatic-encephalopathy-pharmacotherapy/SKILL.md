# Variceal Bleeding and Hepatic Encephalopathy Pharmacotherapy

Variceal bleeding and hepatic encephalopathy are the two decompensation events where pharmacotherapy is time-critical, endpoint-driven, and easy to get wrong because the drugs feel familiar. In acute variceal hemorrhage, vasoactive agents (octreotide, terlipressin, somatostatin) must be started early and continued for several days to reduce portal pressure while endoscopy is arranged, prophylactic antibiotics must be given because they reduce mortality (not just infection), and the coagulation picture is misleading because standard measures like INR do not capture the rebalanced hemostasis of cirrhosis. In hepatic encephalopathy, lactulose is titrated to a specific clinical endpoint (two to three soft bowel movements per day), rifaximin is added for recurrence prevention, and the real work is identifying and reversing precipitants rather than escalating drugs. The judgment problem is that both conditions are treated with agents that work slowly or indirectly, the endpoints are clinical rather than numeric, and the easy reflex (more drug, indefinite duration) causes electrolyte chaos, volume depletion, and masking of the underlying precipitant.

## Core Rules

### Start Vasoactive Therapy Early and Sustain It Through the Bleeding Window

In suspected or confirmed acute variceal bleeding, vasoactive therapy should begin at first contact, before endoscopy, because these agents (octreotide bolus then infusion, terlipressin boluses then infusion, somatostatin) reduce splanchnic blood flow and portal pressure and have been shown to improve outcomes when started early. Continue the infusion for the recommended duration (commonly three to five days), because rebleeding risk persists beyond the initial hemostasis. The pharmacist must verify the bolus was given (the most commonly omitted step), confirm the infusion concentration and rate are correct, and watch for adverse effects: octreotide can cause hyperglycemia and abdominal cramping, terlipressin can cause ischemia and hyponatremia and requires careful use in older or cardiac patients. Do not stop therapy prematurely because the bleeding appears controlled.

### Give Prophylactic Antibiotics in Every Acute Variceal Bleed

Antibiotic prophylaxis in acute variceal bleeding is not optional comfort care; it reduces infection, early rebleeding, and mortality. Cirrhosis patients who bleed are highly prone to bacterial infections (often spontaneous bacterial peritonitis or bacteremia), and infection drives rebleeding and death. Give a short course (typically three to seven days) of an appropriate agent (commonly a third-generation cephalosporin such as ceftriaxone, adjusted to local resistance and renal function) at presentation. The pharmacist must ensure the antibiotic is started promptly, dose-adjusted for the coexisting renal impairment, and de-escalated or stopped at the defined endpoint rather than continued indefinitely.

### Titrate Lactulose to a Clinical Endpoint, Not a Fixed Dose

Lactulose is the first-line agent for hepatic encephalopathy, and its effect comes from acidifying the colon, trapping ammonia as ammonium, and producing an osmotic cathartic effect that removes it. The dose is not fixed; it is titrated to the clinical endpoint of two to three soft bowel movements per day. Underdosing leaves encephalopathy undertreated, and overdosing causes diarrhea, dehydration, hypernatremia, and worsening renal function, which can paradoxically worsen encephalopathy. The pharmacist must counsel patients and caregivers to adjust the dose based on stool output, must monitor volume and electrolyte status, and must recognize that a patient with severe diarrhea and worsening confusion may be over-treated, not under-treated.

### Add Rifaximin for Recurrence Prevention, Not as Monotherapy

Rifaximin is added to lactulose (not substituted for it) in patients who have a second episode of hepatic encephalopathy or who have not responded to lactulose alone, and it reduces recurrence and hospitalization. It is minimally absorbed, alters gut flora to reduce ammonia production, and is generally well tolerated. The pharmacist must confirm the indication (recurrence prevention, adjunct to lactulose), counsel on adherence because it is expensive and often subject to prior authorization, and ensure that lactulose is continued alongside it. Rifaximin monotherapy for acute encephalopathy is not the standard; lactulose remains the backbone.

### Identify and Reverse the Precipitant Before Escalating Drug Therapy

Hepatic encephalopathy is almost always triggered, and the triggers are commonly iatrogenic or reversible: infection (spontaneous bacterial peritonitis, pneumonia, urinary tract infection), gastrointestinal bleeding (which delivers a protein load and generates ammonia), constipation, dehydration, electrolyte disturbance (especially hypokalemia), sedatives and opioids, and non-adherence to lactulose. The pharmacist must drive a precipitant review at every encephalopathy episode: check for a new sedative or constipating drug, check for infection, confirm lactulose adherence and bowel movement frequency, and correct electrolytes. Escalating encephalopathy therapy without addressing the precipitant treats the symptom while the underlying cause continues, and the patient becomes refractory.

### Use Secondary Prophylaxis and Avoid Rebound After a Bleed

After a variceal bleed, secondary prophylaxis combines non-pharmacologic measures with pharmacotherapy: a non-selective beta-blocker (propranolol, nadolol, or carvedilol) titrated to the maximally tolerated dose to reduce portal pressure, often alongside endoscopic band ligation. The pharmacist must ensure the beta-blocker is titrated rather than fixed-dosed, must monitor for hypotension and bradycardia (especially with carvedilol, which also reduces portal pressure through alpha-blockade), and must avoid abrupt discontinuation which can trigger rebound bleeding. In encephalopathy, secondary prophylaxis is continued lactulose with rifaximin for patients with recurrent episodes, with ongoing precipitant vigilance.

## Common Traps

### Omitting the Octreotide Bolus and Starting Only the Infusion

The order specifies an octreotide infusion but the bolus is missed or never ordered, so the infusion reaches effective levels slowly while the varix continues to bleed. The mechanism is that the infusion alone eventually works, so the omission feels harmless, and the false signal is that the drug is running. The harm is a delay in achieving portal pressure reduction during the most dangerous window, increasing transfusion requirements, rebleeding, and mortality. The pharmacist must verify the bolus was given before or as the infusion starts.

### Treating a Cirrhosis Patient's INR as Evidence of Bleeding Risk and Over-Correcting

The INR is elevated in cirrhosis because of reduced clotting factor synthesis, but it does not reflect the rebalanced hemostasis (reduced procoagulants are matched by reduced anticoagulants), so it overstates bleeding risk and understates thrombosis risk. The mechanism is the reflex to correct an abnormal number, and the false signal is a high INR that looks dangerous. The harm is unnecessary plasma transfusion, volume overload, and a false sense of correction, while the patient may still bleed from portal pressure. The pharmacist must avoid reflexive correction based on INR alone and support transfusion guided by the actual bleeding and viscoelastic testing where available.

### Fixed-Dose Lactulose Without Endpoint Titration

Lactulose is prescribed at a fixed dose and never adjusted, so the patient has either too few stools (undertreated encephalopathy) or severe diarrhea (volume depletion and hypernatremia that worsens encephalopathy). The mechanism is treating lactulose like a standard drug with a standard dose, and the false signal is that the drug is being given so the treatment is complete. The harm is that the clinical endpoint (two to three soft stools daily) is never assessed, and the dose drifts away from effective. Lactulose must be titrated to stool output at every encounter.

### Giving Rifaximin Without Continuing Lactulose

Rifaximin is added and lactulose is discontinued because the patient dislikes the diarrhea, leaving the patient on monotherapy that is less effective for acute and recurrent encephalopathy. The mechanism is that rifaximin is better tolerated, so it feels like a reasonable substitute, and the false signal is that the patient is on an encephalopathy drug. The harm is loss of the cathartic ammonia-removal mechanism and higher recurrence rates. Rifaximin is an adjunct to lactulose, not a replacement, and adherence to both must be reinforced.

### Escalating Encephalopathy Therapy Without Searching for a Precipitant

The patient's encephalopathy worsens, more lactulose is given, rifaximin is added, but no one looks for the new infection, the constipating drug, the electrolyte disturbance, or the occult gastrointestinal bleed. The mechanism is that encephalopathy is the expected complication so escalation feels correct, and the false signal is that more drug is the answer. The harm is that the precipitant continues to drive ammonia production or neurotoxicity, the patient becomes refractory, and a reversible cause (a urinary tract infection, a new opioid) is missed. A precipitant search must precede or accompany every escalation.

### Stopping the Non-Selective Beta-Blocker Abruptly After a Bleed

The patient becomes hypotensive or bradycardic and the non-selective beta-blocker is stopped abruptly, triggering a rebound increase in portal pressure and rebleeding. The mechanism is treating the vital sign in isolation, and the false signal is that the abnormal vital sign is corrected. The harm is that portal pressure rebounds and the patient rebleeds, often catastrophically. Beta-blocker changes in secondary variceal prophylaxis must be gradual and coordinated with the treating team, weighing hemodynamic tolerance against rebleeding risk.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical acute variceal bleed or overt encephalopathy episode is managed well, but the exception (refractory ascites with recurrent encephalopathy, hepatorenal syndrome requiring terlipressin with fluid restriction, the pregnant cirrhosis patient, or minimal hepatic encephalopathy affecting driving safety) is skipped. The trap is that the standard pathway is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome because the boundary condition was never tested.

## Self-Check

- Did I confirm the vasoactive agent was started early, that the bolus was given before or alongside the infusion, and that the infusion will continue for the recommended three to five day window?
- Did I ensure prophylactic antibiotics were given promptly for the acute variceal bleed, dose-adjusted for renal function, and scheduled to stop at the defined endpoint?
- Did I titrate lactulose to the clinical endpoint of two to three soft bowel movements per day, and did I counsel the patient or caregiver to adjust the dose based on stool output?
- Did I confirm rifaximin is added to (not substituted for) lactulose for recurrence prevention, with adherence reinforced given cost and prior authorization?
- Did I drive a precipitant search (infection, bleeding, constipation, electrolytes, new sedatives or opioids, lactulose non-adherence) before or alongside escalating encephalopathy therapy?
- Did I avoid reflexive INR correction with plasma and support transfusion guided by actual bleeding and viscoelastic testing where available?
- Did I ensure non-selective beta-blocker secondary prophylaxis is titrated to maximal tolerated dose and changed only gradually, weighing hemodynamic tolerance against rebleeding risk?
- Did I stay within scope, presenting recommendations as advisory and deferring final prescribing decisions and licensed clinical judgment to the treating team?
