# Drug-Drug Interaction Screening and Assessment

Drug-drug interactions are pervasive in patients on multiple medications, and the pharmacist's challenge is not detecting them — the software does that — but distinguishing the interactions that matter from the hundreds that do not. A patient on fifteen medications may generate dozens of interaction alerts, of which perhaps two are clinically significant. The danger is binary thinking: either the pharmacist overrides every alert reflexively (alert fatigue), or they treat every interaction as absolute and refuse to dispense medications that are safely co-administrable with monitoring. The skilled pharmacist triages interactions by mechanism, severity, and clinical context, intervening on the few that pose real risk while allowing the many that are manageable to proceed with appropriate guidance.

## Core Rules

### Classify Interactions by Mechanism Before Assessing Severity

Drug interactions operate through two primary mechanisms: pharmacokinetic (one drug alters the absorption, distribution, metabolism, or excretion of another) and pharmacodynamic (two drugs produce additive or antagonistic effects at the same receptor or physiological pathway). The mechanism determines the management strategy. Pharmacokinetic interactions often require dose adjustment — if Drug A inhibits the CYP3A4 enzyme that metabolizes Drug B, Drug B's levels will rise, and the dose must be reduced. Pharmacodynamic interactions often require spacing or monitoring — if two drugs both prolong the QT interval, the additive effect requires ECG monitoring or replacement of one agent. The pharmacist must identify the mechanism before deciding on the intervention, because the wrong mechanism leads to the wrong management: adjusting a dose for a pharmacodynamic interaction (where the issue is additive toxicity, not altered levels) will not prevent the harm.

### Assess Severity in the Context of the Specific Patient

Interaction severity is not a universal property of the drug pair — it depends on the patient. A moderate CYP3A4 interaction may be insignificant in a healthy young adult but dangerous in an elderly patient with hepatic impairment. A QT-prolongation interaction is more dangerous in a patient with existing cardiac disease, electrolyte abnormalities, or a baseline prolonged QT. The pharmacist must contextualize the interaction severity using patient-specific factors: age, organ function, comorbidities, concurrent conditions (bradycardia, hypokalemia), and the presence of other interacting medications that may compound the effect. An interaction rated "moderate" in a drug database may be "severe" in a specific patient, and the pharmacist's judgment — not the database rating — must guide the intervention.

### Determine Whether the Interaction Is Avoidable or Manageable

Many significant interactions do not require discontinuing either drug. The pharmacist must determine whether the interaction can be managed through one of four strategies: dose adjustment (reducing the affected drug's dose to account for altered levels), temporal separation (spacing the administration of two drugs that chelate or interfere with absorption), enhanced monitoring (checking drug levels, INR, electrolytes, or ECG at defined intervals), or therapeutic substitution (replacing one interacting drug with a non-interacting alternative in the same class). The decision depends on whether both drugs are necessary, whether alternatives exist, and whether the monitoring required to manage the interaction is feasible in the patient's setting. An interaction that requires weekly blood draws is manageable in an inpatient setting but may be impractical for a community-dwelling elderly patient.

### Communicate the Interaction and the Management Plan

When a significant interaction is identified and a management decision is made, the pharmacist must communicate the plan to three parties: the prescriber (who needs to know about the interaction and approve any dose adjustment or substitution), the patient (who needs to know about timing, symptoms to watch for, and the importance of monitoring), and the pharmacy record (which must document the interaction, the intervention, and the monitoring plan). Patient counseling is especially important for interactions managed through temporal separation — the patient must understand not just "take with food" but "take Drug A at least two hours before Drug B, because Drug B prevents Drug A from being absorbed." Vague instructions like "space your medications" are insufficient; the patient needs specific times and a clear explanation of why spacing matters.

### Re-Screen When the Medication List Changes

Interactions are dynamic. A patient's interaction profile changes every time a medication is added, removed, or dose-adjusted. A patient who was safely co-managed on two interacting drugs may develop a new risk when a third drug is added that compounds the effect. The pharmacist must re-screen the interaction profile at every prescription event — not just the new prescription, but the new prescription in the context of the full active list. This is especially important at care transitions (hospital discharge, transfer to a skilled nursing facility) where the medication list often changes substantially and where interaction-related adverse events are most common.

## Common Traps

### The Database Rating as a Proxy for Clinical Judgment

Drug interaction databases assign severity ratings — "minor," "moderate," "severe" — based on general population data. The trap is that the pharmacist treats the rating as the final assessment rather than the starting point. A "moderate" interaction in a patient with severe renal impairment and five other medications may be more dangerous than a "severe" interaction in an otherwise healthy patient on no other therapy. The harm is that the pharmacist intervenes on the "severe" interaction (because the database says so) while overlooking the "moderate" interaction that is actually more dangerous in this specific patient. The database rating is a flag, not a verdict — the pharmacist must apply clinical judgment to determine the real severity in context.

### The CYP Enzyme Oversimplification

Cytochrome P450 interactions are the most commonly discussed drug interactions, and pharmacists learn to check for CYP inhibitors and inducers. The trap is that this focus becomes tunnel vision: the pharmacist checks for CYP interactions but overlooks interactions mediated through other mechanisms — P-glycoprotein transport (digoxin and amiodarone), renal tubular secretion (methotrexate and NSAIDs), or pharmacodynamic additivity (two sedatives, two anticholinergics). The harm is that a clinically significant non-CYP interaction passes undetected because the pharmacist's mental model of "drug interaction" is limited to the CYP system. The pharmacist must screen for all interaction mechanisms, not just the one they remember best.

### The "It's Only Moderate" Override Pattern

Under alert fatigue, pharmacists develop a pattern of overriding any interaction rated "moderate" or below without reading the details. The trap mechanism is that most moderate interactions are indeed manageable, so the override is usually correct — which reinforces the behavior. But the harm occurs when a moderate interaction is not manageable in this patient (because of organ impairment, polypharmacy, or inability to monitor), and the pharmacist overrides it with the same reflex used for the manageable cases. The false signal is that "moderate" always means "safe to override," when in fact it means "assess in context." The pharmacist must read at least the mechanism and the management recommendation for every moderate interaction before overriding, not just the severity rating.

### Monitoring Plans That Exist Only in the Documentation

The pharmacist identifies a significant interaction, develops a monitoring plan (check INR in three days, check renal function weekly), documents the plan, and considers the interaction managed. The trap is that the monitoring plan exists in the pharmacy documentation but is never communicated to the person who will actually perform the monitoring — the prescriber who orders the lab, the nurse who draws it, or the patient who keeps the appointment. The harm is that a well-designed monitoring plan that is never executed provides zero protection — the interaction proceeds unmonitored, and the adverse event that the monitoring was supposed to catch occurs on schedule. The pharmacist must ensure the monitoring plan is communicated to and accepted by the party responsible for executing it.

## Self-Check

- Did I classify the interaction by mechanism (pharmacokinetic vs pharmacodynamic) before deciding on management, or did I apply a generic "hold and notify" response?
- Did I assess the severity in the context of this specific patient's organ function, comorbidities, and full medication list, or did I rely solely on the database severity rating?
- Did I determine whether the interaction is avoidable (discontinue) or manageable (dose adjust, space, monitor, substitute), and did I choose the least disruptive effective strategy?
- Did I communicate the management plan — including specific timing, monitoring parameters, and follow-up intervals — to the prescriber, the patient, and the pharmacy record?
- Did I re-screen the interaction profile at this care transition, considering the new prescription in the context of the full active medication list?
- For every "moderate" interaction I overrode, did I read the mechanism and management recommendation before deciding, or did I override based on the rating alone?
