# Polypharmacy and Deprescribing in Older Adults

Polypharmacy in older adults is not simply the count of medications; it is the accumulation of drugs beyond what evidence supports, often layered over years by different prescribers addressing different problems, none of whom revisited the whole list. Older adults are physiologically vulnerable — reduced renal clearance, altered volume of distribution, increased blood-brain barrier permeability — so a drug that was appropriate at age 60 may be harmful at 80, and a new symptom is more likely a drug effect than a new disease. Deprescribing is the deliberate, planned reduction of inappropriate or unnecessary medications, and it is one of the highest-value interventions a pharmacist can make. The danger is that the list is treated as fixed and legitimate, that every drug is assumed to be "still needed," and that deprescribing is avoided because stopping feels riskier than continuing. This skill covers the judgment used to identify inappropriate polypharmacy and to plan deprescribing safely, with the overriding principle that in older adults, less is often more, and every continuation is a decision that should be justified.

## Core Rules

### Apply Explicit Inappropriate-Medication Criteria to the Entire List

Use structured tools — the Beers Criteria, STOPP/START, and the Medication Appropriateness Index — to screen the complete medication list for drugs that are potentially inappropriate in older adults. These tools flag specific drugs and classes (long-acting benzodiazepines, first-generation antihistamines, certain muscle relaxants, long-acting sulfonylureas, NSAIDs in chronic use, anticholinergic burden) where the harms in older adults consistently exceed the benefits. Apply the criteria to the whole list, not just the newest drug, because the inappropriate medications are usually the old ones that no one has revisited. The criteria are a screen, not a verdict — each flagged drug still requires individualized assessment.

### Distinguish a True Indication From a Prescribing Cascade

A prescribing cascade occurs when a drug's adverse effect is misinterpreted as a new condition and a second drug is prescribed to treat it, which then causes its own effects, and so on. Classic cascades include a calcium-channel-blocker causing edema treated with a diuretic, a non-dihydropyridine causing constipation treated with a laxative cascade, a dopaminergic drug causing hallucinations treated with an antipsychotic, or a cholinesterase inhibitor causing incontinence treated with an anticholinergic (which then worsens cognition). For every drug on the list, ask whether the symptom it treats might itself be an adverse effect of another drug. Identifying and reversing a cascade can remove several drugs at once and resolve the originating symptom.

### Quantify and Reduce Anticholinergic and Sedative Burden

Cumulative anticholinergic and sedative burden is strongly associated with cognitive impairment, falls, and delirium in older adults, and the risk is a function of the total load across drugs, not any single agent. Use an anticholinergic burden scale to quantify the cumulative score across the list, and prioritize reduction of high-burden drugs and combinations (an antihistamine plus a tricyclic plus an overactive-bladder agent plus a sleep aid, for example). Similarly, cumulative sedative load from benzodiazepines, Z-drugs, antipsychotics, and opioids raises fall and fracture risk. Burden is a property of the regimen, and reducing it often means targeting drugs that each look modest alone.

### Reassess Benefit Versus Harm at the Current Age and Function

A drug's benefit-harm balance shifts as a patient ages, loses function, or nears the end of life. Statins for primary prevention, intensive glycemic control (tight A1c targets), and aggressive antihypertension in frail elderly patients carry harms (myopathy, hypoglycemia, falls and orthostasis) that may exceed benefits measured in years-to-benefit that the patient will not live to realize. For each preventive drug, ask: what is the time-to-benefit, what is the patient's life expectancy and current function, and do the benefits in this individual still exceed the harms? Preventive therapies with long time-to-benefit are frequently appropriate to stop in frail or limited-life-expectancy patients.

### Plan Deprescribing as a Tapered, Monitored Process, Not a Sudden Stop

Deprescribing is not deletion. Many drugs require tapered withdrawal to avoid rebound or withdrawal syndromes (benzodiazepines, Z-drugs, opioids, certain antihypertensives, SSRIs), and others require monitoring for return of the underlying condition (reflux, pain, psychosis, seizures). Plan each deprescribing action with a taper schedule, monitoring parameters, a timeframe, and a plan to restart if the original condition recurs. Engage the patient and family, because the most common reason deprescribing fails is that the patient restarts the drug on their own when a symptom returns. One drug at a time, with observation, allows attribution of any change to the right cause.

### Prioritize the Highest-Risk, Lowest-Benefit Drugs First

When the list is long, prioritize deprescribing by risk and by likelihood of success. Target first the drugs with the highest current harm (anticholinergic burden, fall risk, hypoglycemia, bleeding), the weakest indication (no current diagnosis, duplicate therapy, preventive drugs past time-to-benefit), and those most likely to be successfully stopped (symptomatic drugs where the symptom has resolved, prophylactic drugs in end-of-life care). Deprescribing the highest-risk, lowest-benefit drugs first produces the greatest safety gain and builds confidence for further reduction.

### Engage the Patient and the Prescriber, and Document the Plan

Deprescribing is a shared decision. Patients may be attached to drugs they have taken for years and fear stopping them; prescribers may be protective of drugs they started. Frame the conversation around benefit and harm in the patient's current state, set clear expectations for the taper and monitoring, and document the plan and rationale. Coordinate with all prescribers so that one does not inadvertently restart what another has stopped. Deprescribing without communication produces confusion, duplication, and restarts.

## Common Traps

### Treating the Medication List as Fixed and Legitimate

The pharmacist reviews the list for interactions and doses but never asks whether each drug is still needed, treating the existing regimen as the legitimate baseline. The mechanism is that an existing prescription carries the presumption of intent, and questioning it feels like overstepping. The false signal is a list that "all has reasons." The harm is that inappropriate drugs accumulate indefinitely, each one adding risk, because no one ever revisits the original decision. Every drug on an older adult's list should be periodically re-justified, not assumed necessary.

### The Prescribing Cascade Mistaken for Comorbid Disease

A drug's adverse effect is diagnosed as a new condition and a new drug is added, and the cascade is never recognized because each addition is clinically plausible in isolation. The mechanism is that adverse effects mimic common conditions (edema, constipation, hallucinations, incontinence), and the temporal link to the causative drug is not made. The false signal is a plausible new diagnosis. The harm is regimen growth that worsens the original problem while adding new drugs and new risks. For every new symptom or drug, the question must be asked: could this be an adverse effect of something already on the list?

### Preventive Therapy Continued Past Time-to-Benefit in Frail Patients

A statin, a tight glycemic target, or aggressive antihypertension is continued in a frail 90-year-old because "it was working," when the time-to-benefit exceeds the life expectancy and the harms (falls, hypoglycemia, myopathy) are immediate. The mechanism is that preventive drugs are started for good reasons and then never revisited as the patient's function and prognosis change. The false signal is a "controlled" risk factor. The harm is active harm in the present to prevent events the patient will not live to experience. Benefit-harm balance must be reassessed at the current age and function, not frozen at the time of initiation.

### Anticholinergic Burden Assessed Drug by Drug, Not Cumulatively

Each drug's anticholinergic load is low, so each is judged acceptable, and the cumulative burden across five drugs is never computed. The mechanism is that burden scales are applied per drug rather than summed, and the cumulative cognitive and fall risk is invisible at the single-drug level. The false signal is that no single drug is "high anticholinergic." The harm is that the regimen as a whole produces cognitive impairment, delirium, and falls that no individual drug would predict. Cumulative burden must be quantified across the whole list.

### Sudden Discontinuation Causing Rebound or Withdrawal

A benzodiazepine, Z-drug, SSRI, or antihypertensive is stopped abruptly, and the patient suffers withdrawal, rebound hypertension, rebound insomnia, or relapse, which is then interpreted as proof the drug was needed. The mechanism is that discontinuation syndromes mimic the original condition, and abrupt cessation maximizes them. The false signal is a returning symptom that "proves" the drug was necessary. The harm is that the patient is restarted on the drug, deprescribing is abandoned, and the lesson learned is that stopping is dangerous. Deprescribing must be tapered and monitored, with rebound distinguished from true recurrence.

### Deprescribing Without Patient and Prescriber Engagement

The pharmacist recommends stopping a drug but does not engage the patient or coordinate with the prescriber, and the patient restarts it or another prescriber re-adds it. The mechanism is that the recommendation is treated as a one-way instruction rather than a shared plan. The false signal is a documented recommendation. The harm is that the deprescribing fails silently, the regimen regrows, and the pharmacist's effort is wasted. Deprescribing requires engagement, coordination, and documented agreement to succeed.

## Self-Check

- Did I apply Beers, STOPP/START, or a medication appropriateness screen to the entire list, not just the newest drug?
- For each drug, did I ask whether the symptom it treats could be an adverse effect of another drug on the list (prescribing cascade)?
- Did I quantify cumulative anticholinergic and sedative burden across the regimen, not just per drug, and prioritize reduction of high-burden combinations?
- For each preventive drug, did I reassess benefit versus harm at the patient's current age, function, and life expectancy, including time-to-benefit?
- Did I plan deprescribing as a tapered, monitored process with a schedule, monitoring parameters, and a restart plan, rather than abrupt cessation?
- Did I prioritize the highest-risk, lowest-benefit drugs first, and sequence one change at a time so effects can be attributed?
- Did I engage the patient and family and coordinate with all prescribers, documenting the deprescribing plan and rationale to prevent silent restarts?
