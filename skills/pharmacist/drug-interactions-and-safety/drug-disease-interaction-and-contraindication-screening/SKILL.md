# Drug-Disease Interaction and Contraindication Screening

Drug-disease interactions are more dangerous than drug-drug interactions because they are harder to detect and more likely to be overlooked. A drug that is safe in the general population can be catastrophic in a patient with the wrong comorbidity — NSAIDs in heart failure, metformin in severe renal impairment, beta-blockers in severe asthma. The pharmacist's challenge is that the disease context is not always visible on the prescription or even in the medication profile. The patient's conditions live in the medical record, in the problem list, in lab values, and sometimes only in the patient's own report. A pharmacist who verifies the drug against the drug list but not against the disease list will miss the contraindication that matters most, because the drug is correct for the indication but wrong for the patient.

## Core Rules

### Check Every New Medication Against the Active Problem List

Every prescription must be screened against the patient's documented medical conditions, not just their medication list. The pharmacist must access the patient's problem list, active diagnoses, and relevant clinical data before confirming that a medication is appropriate. This check must go beyond the indication for which the drug is prescribed — a drug may be correctly chosen for one condition while being contraindicated by another. A patient prescribed an NSAID for osteoarthritis who also has chronic kidney disease and heart failure faces two independent contraindications that have nothing to do with the arthritis being treated. The pharmacist must look at the whole patient, not just the prescribing question in front of them.

### Flag Absolute Contraindications Immediately and Hold

Some drug-disease combinations are absolute contraindications — the drug must not be used in patients with the condition, period. Examples include metformin in severe renal impairment (risk of lactic acidosis), warfarin in active bleeding, NSAIDs in the third trimester of pregnancy (premature closure of the ductus arteriosus), and ACE inhibitors in pregnancy (fetal renal damage). When the pharmacist identifies an absolute contraindication, the medication must be held immediately, and the prescriber must be contacted before any dispensing occurs. There is no management strategy, no monitoring plan, and no "low dose" exception for an absolute contraindication — the risk is not dose-dependent, and the harm can be irreversible.

### Assess Relative Contraindications With Risk-Benefit Analysis

Many drug-disease interactions are relative contraindications — the drug may be used with caution, at a reduced dose, or with enhanced monitoring, depending on the severity of the condition and the availability of alternatives. Examples include beta-blockers in mild asthma (may be acceptable with cardioselective agents), NSAIDs in well-controlled heart failure (may be acceptable at the lowest effective dose for the shortest duration), and anticholinergics in patients with benign prostatic hyperplasia (may cause urinary retention). The pharmacist must perform a risk-benefit analysis: how severe is the condition, how necessary is the drug, what alternatives exist, and what monitoring would make the combination acceptable? The decision must involve the prescriber, but the pharmacist's role is to identify the issue, present the analysis, and recommend the safest course.

### Screen for Pregnancy and Lactation Status Proactively

Pregnancy and lactation status changes the safety profile of nearly every medication, yet it is frequently not documented on the prescription and not checked by the pharmacist. For any female patient of childbearing age, the pharmacist must consider pregnancy status before dispensing medications with teratogenic potential (isotretinoin, valproic acid, warfarin, methotrexate, ACE inhibitors, tetracyclines). When pregnancy status is unknown, the pharmacist must ask — not assume. A single dose of a teratogen in early pregnancy, before the patient knows she is pregnant, can cause irreversible fetal harm. The pharmacist must also consider lactation: many medications pass into breast milk, and some (radioactive iodine, cytotoxic chemotherapy, high-dose sedatives) are absolute contraindications to breastfeeding.

### Use Lab Values to Identify Undocumented Disease States

Lab values often reveal disease states that are not in the problem list. A creatinine clearance below 30 mL/min indicates significant renal impairment that requires dose adjustment for renally cleared drugs, even if "chronic kidney disease" is not in the diagnosis list. Elevated liver enzymes indicate hepatic impairment that affects drugs metabolized by the liver. A low platelet count or elevated INR indicates a bleeding risk that contraindicates anticoagulants and NSAIDs. The pharmacist must review available lab values as part of the screening process, using them to detect disease states that the prescriber may not have documented but that nonetheless change the safety profile of the prescribed medication.

## Common Traps

### The Indication Tunnel — Verifying the Drug Against the Diagnosis, Not the Patient

The pharmacist confirms that the drug matches the indication — amoxicillin for an ear infection, correct — and considers the verification complete. The trap is that the indication-based check answers only one question ("is this the right drug for this condition?") while ignoring the more dangerous question ("is this drug safe for this patient?"). The patient with the ear infection also has a documented penicillin allergy that the pharmacist did not check because the drug-indication match felt sufficient. The harm is that the most common verification pattern — drug against diagnosis — systematically misses drug-disease and drug-allergy contraindications, because those require checking the drug against the patient's other conditions, not against the prescribing diagnosis.

### The Missing Problem List

In community pharmacy, the patient's problem list is often not available. The pharmacist has the medication list but not the diagnoses. The trap is that the pharmacist proceeds with verification using only the medication information, assuming that the absence of disease data means the absence of disease. The harm is that contraindications are invisible — the patient on furosemide and lisinopril may have undiagnosed or undocumented renal impairment, and the NSAID prescription that the pharmacist verifies without access to renal function data may precipitate acute kidney injury. The pharmacist must use every available signal — lab values, the medication list itself (furosemide suggests heart failure or kidney disease; insulin suggests diabetes), and direct patient questioning — to infer disease states when the problem list is absent.

### The "Low Dose" Rationalization for Absolute Contraindications

The pharmacist identifies an absolute contraindication — metformin in a patient with an eGFR of 20 — but rationalizes dispensing it because "the dose is low" or "the prescriber must have considered this." The trap mechanism is that absolute contraindications are not dose-dependent. The risk of lactic acidosis with metformin in severe renal impairment exists at any dose; the risk of fetal harm with ACE inhibitors in pregnancy exists at any dose. The harm is that the pharmacist converts an absolute contraindication into a relative one through rationalization, and the patient receives a medication that should never have been dispensed. The pharmacist must hold absolute contraindications regardless of dose, duration, or prescriber intent, and must contact the prescriber to recommend an alternative.

### Pregnancy Status Assumption

For female patients of childbearing age, the pharmacist assumes the patient is not pregnant unless told otherwise. The trap is that early pregnancy is asymptomatic — the patient may not know she is pregnant — and the assumption leads to dispensing teratogenic medications without any screening. The harm is that the window of greatest teratogenic risk (the first trimester, before pregnancy is recognized) is exactly the window where the assumption "not pregnant" is most likely to be wrong. The pharmacist must ask about pregnancy status and last menstrual period for any female patient of childbearing age receiving a potentially teratogenic medication, and must document the response. The question takes five seconds; the harm it prevents is irreversible.

## Self-Check

- Did I check the prescribed medication against the patient's active problem list and documented comorbidities, not just against the indication?
- Did I identify any absolute contraindications, and if so, did I hold the medication immediately and contact the prescriber — without rationalizing based on dose or prescriber intent?
- For relative contraindications, did I perform a risk-benefit analysis and communicate the recommendation to the prescriber?
- Did I screen pregnancy and lactation status for any female patient of childbearing age receiving a medication with reproductive risk?
- Did I review available lab values (renal function, liver enzymes, platelets, INR) to detect undocumented disease states that change the safety profile?
- When the problem list was unavailable, did I use the medication list and lab values to infer disease states, or did I assume the absence of disease data meant the absence of disease?
