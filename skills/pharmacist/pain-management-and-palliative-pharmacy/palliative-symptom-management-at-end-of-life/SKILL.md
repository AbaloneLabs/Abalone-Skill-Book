# Palliative Symptom Management at End of Life

The end-of-life phase produces a predictable cluster of symptoms (pain, dyspnea, nausea, agitation, delirium, noisy secretions, fever), and the management of these symptoms is the work that determines whether the patient dies in comfort or in distress. The danger is that the symptom management is treated as optional or as a last resort, that the oral route is lost (the patient can no longer swallow) and no alternative route is planned, that the non-comfort medications are continued (the statins, the antihypertensives, the disease-modifying drugs) adding burden without benefit, and that the caregivers are not prepared for the medications they will administer. The pharmacist's discipline is to anticipate the symptoms before they become crises, to plan the routes before the oral route is lost, to discontinue the non-comfort medications, and to prepare the caregivers to manage the comfort medications with confidence.

## Core Rules

### Anticipate and Pre-empt the Common Terminal Symptoms

The terminal symptoms are predictable, and the proactive management prevents the crises that the reactive management cannot catch up to. The pharmacist must ensure the common comfort medications are available before the symptoms become severe: an opioid for the pain and the dyspnea (morphine is the standard, with hydromorphone for the renal-impaired patient), an anxiolytic for the agitation and the anxiety (lorazepam, diazepam, midazolam), an anticholinergic for the noisy secretions (glycopyrrolate, scopolamine, atropine), an antiemetic for the nausea (haloperidol, metoclopramide, olanzapine), and sometimes a corticosteroid for the inflammation or the appetite. The pharmacist must ensure these are available in the home or at the bedside, with the orders in place to administer them when the symptoms appear, because the symptom that appears at 2 a.m. without the medication available is the symptom that is suffered. The anticipation is the deliberate step that prevents the crisis.

### Plan the Route Before the Oral Route Is Lost

The dying patient loses the ability to swallow, and the oral route becomes unavailable, and the comfort medications must continue or be initiated by an alternative route. The pharmacist must plan the alternative routes in advance: the subcutaneous route (the preferred route for the terminal phase, well-tolerated, easy for the caregiver, suitable for the opioids, the benzodiazepines, the anticholinergics, the antiemetics), the transdermal route (suitable for some medications but with the delayed onset that limits the acute use), the sublingual or buccal route (suitable for some medications), the rectal route (suitable for some medications), and the intravenous route (when access exists). The pharmacist must know which medications are available in which formulations, must ensure the alternative-route formulations are available before the oral route is lost, and must convert the doses appropriately when switching routes (the subcutaneous morphine is roughly equivalent to the oral morphine at half to a third the dose). The route planning in advance is the step that prevents the patient from being without comfort medication when the swallowing fails.

### Discontinue the Non-Comfort Medications

The dying patient is often on a long list of medications for the chronic diseases and the prevention (the statins for the cholesterol, the antihypertensives for the blood pressure, the oral antidiabetics for the glucose, the bisphosphonates, the anticoagulants, the disease-modifying drugs), and these medications add burden (the pills to swallow, the adverse effects, the monitoring, the cost) without benefit in the terminal phase, where the goals have shifted from prevention and longevity to comfort. The pharmacist must review the medication list against the new goals, must recommend discontinuing the medications whose benefit is in the long term (the statins, the bisphosphonates, the preventive therapies), must weigh the continuation versus the discontinuation for the medications whose abrupt cessation causes harm (the beta-blockers, the corticosteroids, the opioids, the benzodiazepines, some anticonvulsants), and must simplify the regimen to the comfort medications. The deprescribing is a deliberate clinical act, communicated to the team and the family, and documented.

### Manage the Specific Terminal Symptoms with the Evidence-Based Approach

Each terminal symptom has an evidence-based approach, and the pharmacist must apply it rather than improvising. The dyspnea is managed with the opioid (the morphine, at a dose appropriate to the opioid tolerance, which relieves the air hunger without causing the harmful respiratory depression at the appropriate dose), the anxiolytic for the anxiety component, the oxygen if hypoxic, and the non-pharmacologic measures (the upright position, the cool air, the fan). The terminal agitation and delirium are managed with the neuroleptic (haloperidol, olanzapine, quetiapine) for the delirium, the benzodiazepine for the anxiety and the restlessness (especially in the benzodiazepine-tolerant patient or the alcohol-withdrawing patient), and the search for the reversible causes (the pain, the urinary retention, the constipation, the hypoxia, the medication adverse effect). The noisy secretions (the death rattle) are managed with the anticholinergic (the glycopyrrolate, the scopolamine patch, the atropine drops) to dry the secretions, and the counseling of the family that the noise is more distressing to the observer than to the patient (who is typically unconscious). The nausea is managed based on the cause (the gastric stasis with the metoclopramide, the chemical or the opioid-induced with the haloperidol or the olanzapine, the bowel obstruction with the anticholinergic and the corticosteroid).

### Prepare the Caregivers to Administer the Comfort Medications

The comfort medications in the terminal phase are often administered by the family caregivers at home, in the moment of the symptom, and the preparation of the caregivers determines whether the medications are given correctly and confidently. The pharmacist must counsel the caregivers on each medication (the indication, the dose, the route, the timing, the minimum interval, the maximum doses, the adverse effects, the storage), must provide the written instructions and the emergency contact, and must verify the understanding with the teach-back. The pharmacist must address the common caregiver fears (the fear of causing death with the morphine, the fear of addiction, the fear of giving too much), because the fears cause the under-dosing and the under-treatment of the symptoms. The pharmacist must explain that the appropriate dosing of the comfort medications relieves the symptoms and does not hasten death (the principle of double effect, where the intended effect is the comfort and the foreseen but unintended effect, at appropriate doses, is not the hastening). The prepared caregiver is the caregiver who can keep the patient comfortable.

### Respect Scope and Escalation Boundaries

Know where the agent's authority and competence end. End-of-life care often involves the hospice team, the palliative specialist, and the prescriber, and the pharmacist works within the collaborative plan. When the question requires a license, a specialist's judgment, a final approval, or expertise the agent does not hold, the correct action is to escalate rather than to produce a confident answer that overreaches. Scope discipline protects the recipient from harm caused by an unqualified conclusion and protects the agent from liability. State explicitly when the output is advisory and must be confirmed by the qualified person.

## Common Traps

### Withholding the Opioid for the Terminal Dyspnea Out of the Respiratory Depression Fear

The patient is dyspneic and distressed in the terminal phase, and the opioid is withheld or under-dosed because of the fear of causing respiratory depression or hastening death. The mechanism is that the opioids can depress the respiration, so the fear feels justified. The false signal is that the opioid is dangerous in the dying patient. The harm is that the appropriately dosed opioid relieves the air hunger and the distress of the dyspnea without causing the clinically significant respiratory depression in the opioid-tolerant or the carefully titrated patient, and the withholding leaves the patient suffering the terrifying symptom of air hunger through the dying process. The pharmacist must recommend the opioid at the appropriate dose for the terminal dyspnea and must address the fear with the evidence.

### Continuing the Non-Comfort Medications Through the Terminal Phase

The patient is dying and the medication list continues to include the statins, the antihypertensives, the oral antidiabetics, the bisphosphonates, and the disease-modifying drugs, adding the burden of the swallowing and the adverse effects without the benefit. The mechanism is that the medications are on the list, so they are continued. The false signal is that the chronic disease management continues to matter. The harm is that the goals have shifted to the comfort, the long-term prevention is no longer relevant, the medications add the burden and the adverse effects (the hypotension, the hypoglycemia, the myalgia, the GI upset), and the pill load competes with the comfort medications and the limited swallowing. The pharmacist must review the list and recommend the deprescribing of the non-comfort medications.

### Losing the Comfort Medications When the Oral Route Fails

The patient can no longer swallow, and the comfort medications were only available in the oral formulation, so the symptoms go untreated while the alternative formulations are sought. The mechanism is that the oral route was the current route, so the alternatives were not planned. The false signal is that the oral route will continue to be available. The harm is that the patient loses the comfort medications at the moment they are most needed, and the symptoms (the pain, the dyspnea, the agitation) escalate while the alternative-route formulations are ordered and obtained, sometimes over hours or days. The pharmacist must plan the alternative routes (the subcutaneous, the transdermal, the sublingual) and ensure the formulations are available before the oral route is lost.

### Under-dosing the Terminal Agitation or Delirium

The patient is agitated and delirious in the terminal phase, and the medication is under-dosed or given too infrequently, so the agitation recurs and the patient remains distressed. The mechanism is that the sedation is feared, so the dose is kept low. The false signal is that the low dose is the safe dose. The harm is that the under-dosed regimen does not control the agitation, the patient suffers the restlessness and the distress, the family witnesses the suffering, and the recurrent need for the medication produces the erratic control that is worse than the steady control. The pharmacist must recommend the adequate dose and the adequate frequency for the terminal agitation, with the titration to the comfort.

### Failing to Prepare the Caregivers for the Comfort Medications

The comfort medications are sent to the home, but the caregivers are not counseled on the indication, the dose, the timing, and the fears, so the medications are under-used or used incorrectly. The mechanism is that the medications are available, so the counseling feels complete. The false signal is that the availability is the preparation. The harm is that the caregivers, driven by the fear of causing harm or the addiction, under-dose or delay the medications, and the patient suffers the symptoms that the available medications would have relieved. The pharmacist must counsel the caregivers on each medication and must address the fears with the teach-back.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- Did I ensure the common comfort medications (the opioid, the anxiolytic, the anticholinergic, the antiemetic) are available before the symptoms become severe, with the orders in place to administer them?
- Did I plan the alternative routes (the subcutaneous, the transdermal, the sublingual, the rectal) and ensure the alternative-route formulations are available before the oral route is lost, with the doses converted appropriately?
- Did I review the medication list against the comfort goals and recommend the deprescribing of the non-comfort medications (the statins, the antihypertensives, the preventive therapies), while managing the medications whose abrupt cessation causes harm?
- For the dyspnea, did I recommend the opioid at the appropriate dose (and address the respiratory depression fear), and for the agitation and delirium, did I recommend the neuroleptic and the benzodiazepine at the adequate dose with the titration to comfort?
- For the noisy secretions, did I recommend the anticholinergic and counsel the family that the noise is more distressing to the observer than to the typically unconscious patient?
- Did I counsel the caregivers on each comfort medication (the indication, the dose, the route, the timing, the minimum interval, the maximum doses, the adverse effects, the storage) and address the fears (the respiratory depression, the addiction, the hastening of death) with the teach-back?
- Does the output stay within the agent's scope, deferring final authority, licensed judgment, or specialist sign-off to the hospice or palliative team where the question exceeds the agent's competence?
- Is the comfort plan documented clearly enough that the team and the caregivers can carry it out consistently?
