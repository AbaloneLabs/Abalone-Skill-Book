# Breakthrough and Procedural Pain Management

Breakthrough pain is the pain that breaks through the around-the-clock analgesic regimen, and procedural pain is the pain produced by a planned intervention (a dressing change, a wound care, a procedure, a turning). Both are predictable in pattern but unpredictable in timing, and both are consistently undertreated because the rescue medication is selected without matching the onset and duration to the pain's time course, the dose is set without reference to the baseline regimen, and the procedural pain is not pre-medicated at all. The danger is that the patient with good baseline control still suffers severe episodic pain because the rescue strategy was an afterthought, and the patient undergoing a procedure experiences preventable pain because no one planned the analgesia ahead of the intervention. The pharmacist's discipline is to design the breakthrough regimen as deliberately as the baseline regimen, to match the drug and the formulation to the pain's onset and duration, to dose the rescue relative to the baseline, and to pre-medicate the procedural pain before it begins.

## Core Rules

### Match the Rescue Formulation to the Onset and Duration of the Breakthrough Pain

Breakthrough pain varies in onset and duration, and the rescue formulation must match the time course or the patient is either waiting in pain for a slow drug to work or overmedicated long after the pain has passed. The rapid-onset breakthrough pain (the incident pain of movement, the volitional pain of coughing or wound care, peaking within minutes) requires a rapid-onset formulation (the oral transmucosal fentanyl citrate, the buccal tablets, the sublingual tablets, the nasal spray) that works within minutes, because the traditional oral opioid takes 30 to 60 minutes to peak and the pain has resolved or the patient has suffered by then. The slower breakthrough pain (the predictable afternoon pain, the pain that builds over an hour) can be managed with the traditional short-acting oral opioid (morphine, oxycodone, hydromorphone), which peaks in 30 to 60 minutes and lasts 3 to 4 hours. The pharmacist must assess the time course of the patient's breakthrough pain, must select the formulation whose pharmacokinetics match, and must recognize that the rapid-onset transmucosal fentanyl products are not interchangeable (they have different bioavailabilities and dosing) and require opioid tolerance.

### Dose the Rescue Relative to the Baseline Regimen

The breakthrough opioid dose must be calculated relative to the baseline around-the-clock dose, not chosen as a fixed standard dose, because the patient's tolerance determines the effective rescue dose. The commonly used starting point is 10 to 20 percent of the total daily long-acting opioid dose, given every 2 to 4 hours as needed, with a maximum of 4 to 6 doses per day (beyond which the baseline regimen should be increased). The pharmacist must calculate this from the actual total daily dose, must adjust for incomplete cross-tolerance when the rescue opioid differs from the baseline opioid, and must recognize that the rapid-onset transmucosal fentanyl products are titrated independently (they do not correlate predictably with the baseline dose, and must be started at the lowest dose and titrated). The pharmacist must monitor the number of rescue doses per day, because the frequent rescue use signals that the baseline regimen is inadequate and must be increased, and the rare use signals that the baseline may be excessive.

### Pre-Medicate the Procedural and Incident Pain Before It Begins

The procedural pain (the wound care, the dressing change, the turning, the minor procedure) and the predictable incident pain (the pain of standing, of swallowing, of a bowel movement) are best managed by pre-medicating before the trigger, not by treating the pain after it has peaked. The pharmacist must identify the predictable pain triggers in the patient's day, must ensure a rescue dose is scheduled 20 to 60 minutes before the trigger (the timing matched to the formulation's onset), and must coordinate the timing with the nursing or the caregiver so the dose is given before the activity. The pharmacist must also consider the non-opioid adjuncts for the procedural pain (the topical lidocaine or the topical anesthetic for the wound care, the nitrous oxide for the procedure, the regional block where appropriate), because the multimodal approach reduces the opioid requirement and the opioid adverse effects. The procedural pain that is not pre-medicated is the procedural pain that is suffered, and the pre-medication is the deliberate step that prevents it.

### Counsel on the Safe and Effective Use of the Rescue Medication

The rescue medication is used by the patient or the caregiver in the moment of pain, often at home and without immediate professional supervision, and the counseling determines whether the dose is taken correctly, taken at the right time, and not overused. The pharmacist must counsel on the indication (the breakthrough pain, not the baseline pain, not the pain that the baseline should control), the dose, the timing (when to take it relative to the pain onset), the minimum interval between doses (to prevent the overuse and the accumulation), the maximum number of doses per day (beyond which the prescriber must be called), and the adverse effects to watch (the sedation, the respiratory depression, the constipation). The pharmacist must counsel on the storage and the security of the rescue opioid (the lockbox, the keeping away from children and others), and on the naloxone availability and use. The pharmacist must verify the understanding with the teach-back, because the rescue counseling that is delivered but not understood produces no benefit and may produce harm.

### Monitor the Rescue Use and Adjust the Baseline Regimen

The rescue use is a signal, and the pharmacist must monitor the number of doses per day and adjust the baseline regimen accordingly. The persistent need for more than 3 to 4 rescue doses per day signals that the baseline regimen is inadequate and must be increased (the long-acting dose raised by the total rescue dose used, in divided increments). The persistent need for less than 1 to 2 doses per day signals that the baseline regimen may be excessive and can be reduced. The rescue use that escalates without a corresponding change in the baseline pain signals tolerance, disease progression, or a new pain problem, and requires reassessment rather than simply more rescue. The pharmacist must track the rescue use over time, must communicate the pattern to the prescriber, and must adjust the regimen in response, because the rescue strategy that is not monitored and adjusted becomes either inadequate or excessive.

### Respect Scope and Escalation Boundaries

Know where the agent's authority and competence end. When the question requires a license, a specialist's judgment, a final approval, or expertise the agent does not hold, the correct action is to escalate rather than to produce a confident answer that overreaches. Scope discipline protects the recipient from harm caused by an unqualified conclusion and protects the agent from liability. State explicitly when the output is advisory and must be confirmed by the qualified person.

## Common Traps

### Using a Fixed Standard Rescue Dose Regardless of the Baseline Regimen

The pharmacist dispenses a fixed short-acting opioid dose (for example, 5 mg oxycodone) as the rescue for every patient, regardless of the baseline around-the-clock dose. The mechanism is that the fixed dose is the familiar default. The false signal is that the standard dose is the rescue dose. The harm is that the fixed dose is inadequate for the highly tolerant patient (who needs a dose proportional to the baseline, and who suffers uncontrolled breakthrough pain) and excessive for the low-baseline patient (who is overmedicated and at risk of sedation and respiratory depression). The pharmacist must calculate the rescue as 10 to 20 percent of the total daily baseline dose and must adjust for the specific patient.

### Mismatching the Formulation to the Pain's Time Course

The pharmacist selects an oral short-acting opioid for a patient whose breakthrough pain peaks within minutes and resolves within 30 minutes. The mechanism is that the oral tablet is the familiar and the cheaper formulation. The false signal is that the oral opioid is the breakthrough medication. The harm is that the oral opioid takes 30 to 60 minutes to peak, so by the time it works, the pain has resolved or the patient has suffered for the duration, and the patient is reluctant to use the rescue for the next episode because it did not help. The pharmacist must match the formulation to the time course, using the rapid-onset transmucosal products for the rapid-onset pain.

### Treating the Rapid-Onset Transmucosal Fentanyl Products as Interchangeable

The pharmacist substitutes one rapid-onset transmucosal fentanyl product for another at the same dose, or starts a patient at a dose based on the baseline opioid. The mechanism is that the products all contain fentanyl, so they feel equivalent. The false signal is that the fentanyl dose is the dose. The harm is that the products have different bioavailabilities, different absorption, and different titration schedules, and they do not correlate predictably with the baseline opioid dose, so substituting at the same dose or starting at a high dose produces overdose, and the products are not interchangeable. The pharmacist must use the specific product as prescribed, start at the lowest dose, and titrate per the product-specific schedule.

### Failing to Pre-Medicate the Procedural Pain

The patient undergoes a painful procedure (the wound care, the dressing change, the turning) without pre-medication, and the pain is treated only after it has peaked. The mechanism is that the procedure is scheduled, but the analgesia is not scheduled with it. The false signal is that the procedure is the task, and the pain is the aftermath. The harm is that the patient suffers severe and preventable pain during the procedure, develops anticipatory fear and reluctance to cooperate with future care, and may experience the destabilization of the pain control that a severe episode produces. The pharmacist must identify the procedural triggers and ensure a rescue dose is scheduled before the procedure.

### Overusing the Rescue Without Adjusting the Baseline

The patient uses 6 to 8 rescue doses per day for several days, and the regimen is continued unchanged because the rescue is available. The mechanism is that the rescue is working, so the regimen feels adequate. The false signal is that the pain is controlled because the patient has the rescue. The harm is that the persistent high rescue use signals that the baseline is inadequate, and the patient is experiencing recurrent pain and is at risk of the adverse effects of the high total opioid dose, while the underlying inadequacy is not addressed. The pharmacist must monitor the rescue use and must recommend increasing the baseline regimen when the rescue use exceeds the threshold.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- Did I assess the onset and duration of the patient's breakthrough pain and select a rescue formulation whose pharmacokinetics match (rapid-onset transmucosal products for the minute-scale pain, traditional oral opioids for the hour-scale pain)?
- Did I calculate the rescue opioid dose as 10 to 20 percent of the total daily baseline opioid dose, adjust for incomplete cross-tolerance when the rescue opioid differs from the baseline, and set a maximum number of doses per day?
- For the rapid-onset transmucosal fentanyl products, did I confirm opioid tolerance, use the specific product as prescribed (not substituting), start at the lowest dose, and titrate per the product-specific schedule?
- Did I identify the predictable procedural and incident pain triggers and ensure a rescue dose (and non-opioid adjuncts where appropriate) is scheduled 20 to 60 minutes before the trigger, coordinated with the nursing or caregiver?
- Did I counsel the patient or caregiver on the indication, the dose, the timing, the minimum interval, the maximum daily doses, the adverse effects, the storage and security, and the naloxone, and verify the understanding with the teach-back?
- Did I monitor the rescue use over time and recommend adjusting the baseline regimen upward when the use exceeds 3 to 4 doses per day, or downward when the use is rare, and reassess when the use escalates without a change in the baseline pain?
- Does the output stay within the agent's scope, deferring final authority, licensed judgment, or specialist sign-off to the qualified person where the question exceeds the agent's competence?
- Is the rescue strategy documented clearly enough that another practitioner could review the basis and reproduce the plan?
