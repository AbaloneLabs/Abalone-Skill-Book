# Biologic Therapy for Severe Asthma and Chronic Urticaria

Biologic therapy for severe asthma and chronic spontaneous urticaria (CSU) is reserved for patients who have failed optimized standard therapy, and the decision to start a biologic is consequential in cost, commitment, and safety. The judgment problem is twofold: first, many patients referred for biologics have not actually failed standard therapy, they have failed to take it correctly, and starting a biologic on a patient whose real problem is adherence or inhaler technique is both wasteful and harmful; second, among the several available biologics with overlapping but distinct indications, the choice depends on the patient's specific inflammatory phenotype, biomarkers, comorbidities, and practical factors that are easy to overlook. The disciplined approach confirms true severe disease, characterizes the phenotype precisely, matches the biologic to the phenotype and the patient, and manages the initiation and monitoring obligations that biologics require.

## Core Rules

### Confirm True Severe, Refractory Disease Before Initiating a Biologic

A biologic is indicated for severe asthma that remains uncontrolled despite optimized high-dose inhaled corticosteroid-long-acting beta-agonist therapy, with adherence and inhaler technique confirmed, triggers addressed, and comorbidities treated. The pharmacist must verify that each of these has been genuinely addressed, because a large fraction of "biologic candidates" have difficult-to-treat asthma (driven by adherence, technique, or comorbidities) rather than severe refractory asthma (driven by disease biology). Objective adherence data (refill records), observed technique, and documented trigger and comorbidity management must precede the biologic decision. Initiating a biologic on a patient whose real driver is poor adherence simply layers an expensive injectable on top of an unsolved behavioral problem, and the underlying inflammation remains uncontrolled.

### Characterize the Type 2 Phenotype and Biomarkers to Match the Biologic

The available asthma biologics have distinct targets and eligibility profiles: omalizumab (anti-IgE, for allergic asthma with elevated IgE and sensitization), mepolizumab and reslizumab (anti-IL-5, for eosinophilic asthma with elevated eosinophils), benralizumab (anti-IL-5 receptor, eosinophilic), dupilumab (anti-IL-4 receptor alpha, for type 2 asthma with elevated eosinophils or FeNO, and the only one with data across a range including oral corticosteroid sparing), and tezepelumab (thymic stromal lymphopoietin blocker, broadly active including non-eosinophilic). The pharmacist must obtain the relevant biomarkers (blood eosinophil count, total IgE with weight-based dosing for omalizumab, FeNO, sensitization panel) and match the biologic to the patient's phenotype. Choosing a biologic without biomarker characterization, or defaulting to one agent for all patients, produces suboptimal or non-responsive therapy.

### Screen for Pretreatment Considerations Specific to Each Biologic

Each biologic carries pretreatment obligations that the pharmacist must verify. Omalizumab requires anaphylaxis risk acknowledgment and a defined observation period after injection due to rare but serious hypersensitivity. The anti-IL-5 agents and dupilumab require consideration of parasitic (helminth) infection, because type 2 immunity participates in helminth defense, and patients at risk should be screened and treated before initiation. Live vaccines should be timed appropriately around immunomodulatory therapy. Dupilumab and tezepelumab have specific monitoring for eosinophilia and other parameters. The pharmacist must run the biologic-specific pretreatment checklist before the first dose, because a missed screen (an untreated helminth infection, an unaddressed anaphylaxis plan) can cause a preventable harm.

### Select the Biologic on Phenotype, Comorbidities, and Practical Factors Together

Beyond the inflammatory match, the choice should incorporate the patient's comorbidities and practical realities. A patient with comorbid chronic spontaneous urticaria or atopic dermatitis may benefit from an agent with overlapping indications (dupilumab or omalizumab). A patient with comorbid nasal polyposis may benefit from dupilumab or mepolizumab, both approved for that condition. Practical factors matter: dosing frequency (ranging from every two weeks to every eight weeks), route (subcutaneous self-injection versus in-office), storage, the patient's ability to self-inject or attend visits, and the payer's formulary preference. A biologic that is biologically perfect but operationally impossible for the patient will fail in adherence. The selection integrates biology, comorbidity, and feasibility.

### Define Response Criteria and a Stopping Rule Before Starting

Biologics are expensive and commitment is long, so the decision to continue must be based on objective response, not on the mere fact of ongoing prescription. Before starting, the pharmacist and prescriber should define the response criteria (reduction in exacerbations, improvement in symptom control score, reduction in oral corticosteroid requirement, improvement in lung function) and the timeframe for assessment (commonly three to six months), and a stopping rule for non-response. Continuing a biologic for a year without a documented response assessment is poor stewardship and exposes the patient to ongoing cost and risk without benefit. The pharmacist must schedule the response review and act on it.

### Manage the Chronic Urticaria Indication With the Same Rigor

For chronic spontaneous urticaria, omalizumab is the biologic with established efficacy, used after failure of optimized antihistamine therapy (up-dosed second-generation H1 antihistamines up to fourfold, with or without H2 blockers or leukotriene antagonists). The pharmacist must confirm that the antihistamine steps were genuinely optimized before the biologic, must define the urticaria response criteria (weekly urticaria activity score, rescue medication use), and must apply the same pretreatment and monitoring rigor as for asthma. CSU biologics are not a casual escalation, and the same discipline of confirming step failure, characterizing the patient, and defining response applies.

## Common Traps

### Initiating a Biologic on Difficult-to-Treat, Not Severe Refractory, Asthma

The patient is referred for a biologic because standard therapy "failed," but the failure was adherence, technique, untreated comorbidity (rhinitis, GERD, obesity), or unaddressed triggers, not disease biology. The mechanism is that "uncontrolled on standard therapy" feels like a clear indication, and the false signal is that persistent symptoms despite prescriptions mean refractory disease. The harm is an expensive biologic layered on an unsolved problem, with continued poor control, ongoing cost, and a patient labeled refractory who was actually undertreated. Adherence, technique, and comorbidities must be confirmed before the biologic.

### Biologic Selection Without Biomarker Characterization

The clinician chooses a biologic by habit or formulary preference without measuring eosinophils, IgE, or FeNO. The mechanism is that the biologics feel interchangeable, and the false signal is that any type 2 biologic will work for any severe asthmatic. The harm is a non-response or suboptimal response in a patient whose phenotype did not match the chosen agent, with months of ineffective therapy and cost before the mismatch is recognized. Biomarker-driven matching is the standard of care.

### Missing the Helminth Screen on an IL-5 or IL-4 Pathway Biologic

The patient at epidemiologic risk of parasitic infection starts an anti-IL-5 or anti-IL-4 receptor biologic without screening or pretreatment. The mechanism is that helminth risk feels rare and the screen feels optional, and the false signal is that the biologic's safety profile is clean. The harm is a patient in whom an existing or acquired helminth infection is poorly controlled because type 2 immunity is blocked, leading to clinical parasitic disease. The pretreatment helminth consideration must be addressed for the relevant agents.

### Omalizumab Without an Anaphylaxis Plan

The patient starts omalizumab without acknowledgment of the rare anaphylaxis risk or a defined post-injection observation period. The mechanism is that anaphylaxis is rare and feels theoretical, and the false signal is that the injection is routine. The harm is a delayed or mishandled anaphylactic reaction, because no observation or emergency plan was in place. Omalizumab requires a defined observation window and an anaphylaxis-capable setting, especially for early doses.

### Continuing the Biologic Without a Response Assessment

The patient stays on the biologic for a year or more without a documented response review against pre-defined criteria. The mechanism is that ongoing prescription feels like ongoing benefit, and the false signal is that continuation equals response. The harm is ongoing cost and risk for a patient who is a non-responder, and a missed opportunity to stop or switch. A scheduled response review with a stopping rule must be built into the initiation.

### CSU Biologic Without Antihistamine Optimization

The patient with chronic spontaneous urticaria starts omalizumab without confirming that antihistamine therapy was genuinely optimized (up-dosing to fourfold, adding H2 blockers or leukotriene modifiers). The mechanism is that standard-dose antihistamine failure feels like step failure, and the false signal is that persistent urticaria on standard dosing means refractory disease. The harm is an unnecessary biologic escalation for a patient who would have responded to optimized antihistamine dosing. The antihistamine steps must be exhausted first.

## Self-Check

- Did I confirm the patient has true severe refractory asthma (optimized ICS-LABA, confirmed adherence and technique, addressed triggers and comorbidities) rather than difficult-to-treat asthma, before initiating a biologic?
- Did I obtain the relevant biomarkers (blood eosinophils, total IgE, FeNO, sensitization) and match the biologic to the patient's specific type 2 phenotype, rather than choosing by habit?
- Did I complete the biologic-specific pretreatment checklist (helminth consideration for IL-5 and IL-4 agents, anaphylaxis plan and observation for omalizumab, vaccine timing)?
- Does the selected biologic account for comorbidities (nasal polyposis, atopic dermatitis, CSU) and practical factors (dosing frequency, route, self-injection capability, storage, formulary)?
- Are written response criteria (exacerbation reduction, control score, oral corticosteroid sparing, lung function) and a stopping rule defined before initiation, with a scheduled response review at three to six months?
- For chronic spontaneous urticaria, did I confirm that antihistamine therapy was optimized (up-dosed to fourfold, with adjuncts considered) before initiating omalizumab?
- Is the patient enrolled in any required registry or REMS, and is the administration setting prepared for the relevant risks (anaphylaxis for omalizumab)?
- Does the output defer final biologic selection, phenotyping interpretation, and prescribing to the qualified specialist where the question exceeds the agent's competence?
