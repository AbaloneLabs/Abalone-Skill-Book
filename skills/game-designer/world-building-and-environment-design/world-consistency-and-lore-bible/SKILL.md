# World Consistency and Lore Bible

A world's credibility is a function of its internal consistency, and consistency is a property that is easy to lose and nearly impossible to recover. The judgment problem is that every detail a team fixes — a date, a name, a rule of magic, a political structure — becomes a constraint on every future detail, and the constraints accumulate until they conflict, at which point the world contains contradictions that writers must work around and players will discover. Agents tend to miss this because each detail is added to serve an immediate need and feels harmless, and because the contradiction only appears when two independently-authored details collide, often late or post-launch. The harm is a world that savvy players dismantle in forums, writers who are paralyzed by conflicting canon, and adaptations (sequels, transmedia) that are boxed in by careless early decisions. This skill covers how to maintain a lore bible, decide what to fix versus leave flexible, and manage the constraint accumulation that destroys worlds. The agent has latitude in the world's content, but the obligation to maintain internal consistency is not optional.

## Core Rules

### Maintain a Single Source of Truth and Enforce It

A lore bible only works if it is the single canonical source that every writer, designer, and artist checks before adding detail, and if deviations are corrected rather than tolerated. The decision rule: designate one living document as the canon, require every world detail to be checked against it before commitment, and update it whenever canon changes. Multiple conflicting sources of truth guarantee contradictions, because each author resolves ambiguities differently and the resolutions diverge.

### Fix Only What Must Be Fixed and Leave the Rest Flexible

Every fixed detail is a future constraint, so the discipline is to fix only the details that the core experience depends on and leave everything else deliberately ambiguous. The decision rule: for each proposed canon detail, ask whether the game's core loop, fantasy, or key narrative requires it to be fixed; if not, leave it open. Over-specifying the world creates a brittle canon that constrains future work; under-specifying the load-bearing details creates a world that feels arbitrary. The art is fixing the load-bearing few and leaving the rest free.

### Resolve Contradictions Immediately, Not Later

When a contradiction appears — two sources state different facts about the same thing — it must be resolved at the moment of discovery, because unresolved contradictions compound as more content builds on each branch. The decision rule: treat every discovered contradiction as a blocking issue, pick the canonical resolution, update the bible, and audit dependent content. Teams that defer contradiction resolution ship worlds where the contradictions have propagated into dozens of dependent details, making the fix expensive and the audit painful.

### Distinguish Hard Canon From Soft Canon and Communicate the Distinction

Hard canon is detail the player can observe and that must never contradict (the king's name, the magic system's rules); soft canon is background detail that can flex (the king's childhood, the history of a minor house). The decision rule: classify each canon element as hard or soft, document the classification, and allow soft canon to evolve while protecting hard canon absolutely. Conflating the two either freezes the world (treating soft canon as immutable) or breaks it (treating hard canon as flexible).

### Audit Cross-Media and Sequel Content Against Canon Before Commitment

Sequels, DLC, transmedia, and marketing content are the most common sources of canon violation, because they are authored by different teams under different pressures and often without full bible access. The decision rule: route every piece of cross-team or cross-media content through a canon check before commitment, and treat marketing copy and tie-in material as canon-bound as the game itself. The trailer line that contradicts the game's timeline becomes canon to millions of viewers and must be reconciled.

### Plan for the World to Grow Beyond the Original Bible

A world that succeeds will be extended — sequels, expansions, transmedia — and the bible must be structured to accommodate growth without breaking. The decision rule: design the bible with extension in mind (room for new regions, eras, factions), avoid over-constraining the future by fixing details no current content needs, and revisit the bible at each extension to confirm new content fits. Worlds built only for the first game cannot absorb their own sequels and must be retconned, which damages credibility.

## Common Traps

### Over-Specifying Canon Until the World Is Brittle

The team, enthusiastic about the world, fixes every detail — every date, every name, every historical event — creating a canon so dense that any new content risks contradiction, and writers become paralyzed. The trap is that a detailed bible feels like a rich world. The false signal is that the lore document is comprehensive. The harm is that the over-specified canon constrains every future decision, sequels and expansions must navigate a minefield of fixed facts, writers spend their effort avoiding contradiction rather than creating, and the world that was meant to be a foundation becomes a cage, because the team fixed what should have been left flexible.

### The Unresolved Contradiction That Compounds

A contradiction is discovered — two sources disagree on a fact — and the team defers resolution because the immediate content ships either way, and the contradiction propagates as more content builds on each branch. The trap is that deferral feels pragmatic. The false signal is that the contradiction is minor and unnoticed. The harm is that each branch accumulates dependent detail, the eventual resolution requires retconning one branch's dependents, the audit becomes expensive, and the world that could have been fixed with one decision now requires a cascade of corrections, because the contradiction was allowed to compound instead of being resolved at discovery.

### Treating Soft Canon as Immutable

Background detail that was never meant to be load-bearing is treated as fixed canon, freezing the world and preventing evolution that would serve the experience better. The trap is that all written detail feels canonical. The false signal is that consistency is being maintained. The harm is that the world cannot adapt to better ideas, writers are constrained by throwaway details no player remembers, and the rigidity that was meant to protect consistency instead prevents the world from improving, because the team failed to distinguish the load-bearing canon from the flexible background.

### Cross-Media Content That Violates Game Canon

A trailer, a tie-in novel, or a marketing site introduces detail that contradicts the game's canon, and because the cross-media reaches a wide audience, the contradiction becomes the version millions believe. The trap is that cross-media teams operate under separate pressures and timelines. The false signal is that the cross-media content is approved and shipped. The harm is that the game's canon is now in conflict with a widely-distributed version, players encounter the contradiction, the world's credibility suffers, and the team must either retcon the game or retcon the cross-media, both of which damage trust, because the canon check was not enforced across teams.

### The Single-Game Bible That Cannot Absorb Its Sequel

The world is built and documented only for the first game, with no room for extension, and when the sequel requires new regions, eras, or factions, the canon cannot accommodate them without contradiction. The trap is that the first game's scope felt complete. The false signal is that the bible covers the shipped game. The harm is that the sequel must either retcon the original (damaging credibility) or contort itself to fit a canon that was never designed to grow, the world that succeeded cannot extend its own success, and the franchise is boxed in by a bible that served one game and strangled the next, because the world was not planned to grow beyond its origin.

## Self-Check

- Is there a single designated source of truth for canon, and is every world detail checked against it before commitment?
- For each fixed canon detail, have I confirmed it is load-bearing for the core experience, leaving non-essential detail flexible?
- When contradictions are discovered, are they resolved immediately and dependent content audited, rather than deferred?
- Is each canon element classified as hard or soft, with soft canon allowed to evolve and hard canon protected absolutely?
- Is all cross-team, cross-media, and marketing content routed through a canon check before commitment?
- Is the bible structured to accommodate sequels, expansions, and transmedia without breaking?
- Did I avoid over-specifying canon to the point that the world becomes brittle and writers are paralyzed by constraint?
