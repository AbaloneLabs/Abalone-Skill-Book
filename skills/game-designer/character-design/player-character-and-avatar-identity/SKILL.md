# Player Character and Avatar Identity

The player character is the player's presence in the game, and the design of that character — whether a defined protagonist with their own identity or a blank-slate avatar the player projects onto — shapes player agency, immersion, and identification in fundamental ways. The judgment problem is that the choice between defined and blank-slate carries tradeoffs (defined characters enable authored arcs but restrict agency; blank-slate avatars enable projection but cannot carry authored arcs), and that mismatches between the character's identity and the player's actions produce dissonance that breaks immersion. Agents tend to miss this because the choice is often made by convention (the genre's default) rather than by analysis of what the game needs, and because the dissonance between a defined character's personality and the player's sandbox freedom is invisible until played. The harm is a player character who fights the player's agency, who the player cannot identify with, or whose authored identity contradicts the player's expression. This skill covers how to choose and design the player character's identity to serve agency and immersion, and how to avoid the dissonance and identification-failure traps. The agent has latitude in the character, but the obligation to align identity with agency and immersion is not optional.

## Core Rules

### Choose Defined or Blank-Slate Based on the Game's Narrative and Agency Needs

The choice between a defined protagonist (with authored personality, history, arc) and a blank-slate avatar (the player projects onto) must be based on the game's needs: defined protagonists serve authored narrative arcs; blank-slate avatars serve player expression and agency. The decision rule: determine whether the game's core value is the authored story (choose defined) or the player's expressed story (choose blank-slate), and avoid hybrid approaches that dilute both. Hybrid characters — a protagonist with a defined personality the player is forced to express against — produce dissonance, because the choice did not serve the game's primary value.

### Ensure the Character's Identity Does Not Contradict Player Agency

If the player character has a defined identity, that identity must not contradict the agency the gameplay offers — a defined pacifist should not be forced into violent gameplay, a defined coward should not be forced into brave actions — or the dissonance breaks immersion. The decision rule: for each defined character trait, check whether the gameplay forces actions that contradict it, and either adjust the trait or adjust the gameplay to align. Defined identity that contradicts gameplay agency tells the player the character is incoherent, because the actions did not follow from the identity.

### For Blank-Slate Avatars, Minimize Authored Identity That Restricts Projection

A blank-slate avatar should have minimal authored identity — the player projects their own personality, decisions, and expression onto the character — and adding authored identity (voiced dialogue, defined history, forced personality) restricts the projection and defeats the blank-slate purpose. The decision rule: for blank-slate avatars, minimize authored identity, avoid voicing the character's personality, and let the player's choices define who the character is. Authored identity on a blank-slate avatar contradicts the projection, because the character was defined when they should have been open.

### Make the Character's Capabilities Match Their Established Identity

The character's gameplay capabilities — their skills, abilities, combat style — should match their established identity, so a defined soldier fights like a soldier and a defined scholar researches like a scholar, and the capabilities reinforce rather than contradict the identity. The decision rule: align the character's capabilities with their identity, ensure the gameplay expresses who the character is established to be, and avoid capabilities that contradict the identity. Capabilities that contradict identity break immersion, because the character plays as someone they are not established to be.

### Provide Customization That Serves Identification Without Undermining Identity

Customization (appearance, name, build) serves player identification, but for defined characters it must not undermine the authored identity — the player can customize within the bounds of who the character is, not redefine them. The decision rule: calibrate customization to the character type (extensive for blank-slate, bounded for defined), and ensure customization serves identification without overriding identity. Unbounded customization on a defined character undermines the authored identity, because the player redefined who the character was established to be.

### Ensure the Character's Arc Respects Player Investment

If the player character has an arc, that arc must respect the player's investment in the character — the arc should build on the player's experience, not override it, and the resolution should honor the player's engagement — or the arc feels imposed and betrays the player's investment. The decision rule: ensure the character's arc engages the player's experience rather than overriding it, and that the resolution honors the investment the player built. Arcs that override player investment feel like betrayals, because the player's engagement was discarded rather than respected.

## Common Traps

### Hybrid Character That Dilutes Both Defined and Blank-Slate

The team designs a character who is partly defined (authored personality, voice) and partly blank-slate (player choices), and the hybrid dilutes both: the authored personality restricts projection while the player choices contradict the authored identity. The trap is that the hybrid seems to offer the best of both. The false signal is that the character has both identity and agency. The harm is that the defined elements prevent projection and the agency elements contradict the definition, the player cannot fully identify with or fully author the character, and the character serves neither the authored-story nor the expressed-story value, because the choice between the two was not made cleanly.

### Defined Identity Contradicting Gameplay Agency

The team gives the player character a defined identity — a pacifist, a coward, a kind soul — but the gameplay forces actions that contradict that identity — violence, bravery, cruelty — and the dissonance breaks immersion. The trap is that the identity reads well in the story. The false signal is that the character is well-defined. The harm is that the gameplay forces the character to act against their established identity, the player perceives the character as incoherent, the immersion that depends on the character's consistency is broken, and the identity that was authored is contradicted by the play, because the identity and the gameplay were not aligned.

### Authored Identity on a Blank-Slate Avatar

The team designs a blank-slate avatar for player projection but adds authored identity — voiced personality, defined history, forced traits — and the authored identity restricts the projection the blank-slate was meant to enable. The trap is that some authored identity adds depth. The false signal is that the character has personality. The harm is that the authored elements define the character the player was meant to project onto, the projection is restricted, the player cannot fully express through the character, and the blank-slate purpose is defeated, because the character was defined when they should have been open.

### Capabilities That Contradict Established Identity

The team establishes a character's identity — a scholar, a diplomat — but gives them gameplay capabilities that contradict that identity — combat prowess, violent solutions — and the character plays as someone they are not established to be. The trap is that the capabilities serve the gameplay. The false signal is that the character is fun to play. The harm is that the capabilities contradict the identity, the player perceives the character as incoherent, the immersion is broken, and the character who was established as one thing plays as another, because the capabilities were not aligned with the identity.

### Unbounded Customization Undermining Defined Identity

The team allows extensive customization on a defined character — appearance, personality, build — that lets the player redefine the authored identity, and the customization undermines the character the story depends on. The trap is that customization serves player expression. The false signal is that the player can make the character their own. The harm is that the customization overrides the authored identity, the character the story depends on is redefined by the player, the narrative that assumes the defined character no longer fits, and the authored story is undermined, because the customization was not bounded to respect the identity.

### Arcs That Override Player Investment

The team writes a character arc that resolves in a way the player's experience did not build toward — a betrayal, a sacrifice, a transformation the player did not choose or earn — and the arc feels imposed and betrays the player's investment. The trap is that the arc is dramatic. The false signal is that the character has a resolution. The harm is that the arc overrides the player's engagement, the resolution feels imposed rather than earned, the investment the player built is discarded, and the player feels betrayed by the character they invested in, because the arc did not respect the player's experience.

## Self-Check

- Did I choose defined protagonist or blank-slate avatar based on whether the game's value is authored story or expressed story?
- If the character is defined, does their identity avoid contradicting the gameplay agency the player is offered?
- If the character is blank-slate, have I minimized authored identity that would restrict player projection?
- Do the character's gameplay capabilities match and reinforce their established identity?
- Is customization calibrated to the character type, serving identification without undermining authored identity?
- Does the character's arc respect and build on the player's investment, rather than overriding it?
- Did I avoid the hybrid character that dilutes both defined identity and blank-slate agency?
