# Vehicle Combat and Integration

Vehicles and mounts in combat — whether as the player's platform, as enemies, or as integrated tools — add variety and spectacle, but they are also a common source of segregation (vehicle combat separate from core combat), imbalance (vehicles overpowered or underpowered), and gimmickry (vehicle sections that feel bolted on). The judgment problem is that vehicle combat must integrate with the core loop (not feel like a different game), must be balanced against on-foot play (not trivialize or be trivialized), and must serve the game's fantasy (not exist as a checkbox feature). Agents tend to miss this because vehicle sections that are fun in isolation can disrupt the core game's pacing and balance, and because the temptation to make vehicles powerful (for fantasy) can unbalance the encounter design. The harm is vehicle combat that feels like a different game, that trivializes on-foot play, or that exists as a gimmick the player endures rather than enjoys. This skill covers how to integrate vehicles into combat coherently, balance them against core play, and avoid the segregation and gimmick traps. The agent has latitude in the vehicles, but the obligation to integrate and balance them is not optional.

## Core Rules

### Integrate Vehicle Combat With the Core Loop, Not as a Separate Mode

Vehicle combat should integrate with the core gameplay loop — sharing mechanics, progression, and feel with on-foot play — rather than existing as a separate mode with different rules, because segregated vehicle combat feels like a different game bolted onto the core, disrupting coherence. The decision rule: share mechanics and feel between vehicle and on-foot combat, ensure the vehicle combat reinforces the core loop, and avoid vehicle sections that abandon the core rules. Segregated vehicle combat feels incoherent, because the player switched games rather than switched tools.

### Balance Vehicles Against On-Foot Play So Neither Is Trivialized

Vehicles must be balanced against on-foot play — vehicles should offer advantages (power, speed, protection) with tradeoffs (cost, vulnerability, situational limits) — so neither mode trivializes the other, and the choice between vehicle and on-foot is meaningful. The decision rule: define vehicle advantages and tradeoffs, ensure on-foot play remains viable and valuable, and avoid vehicles that are universally superior or universally inferior. Universally superior vehicles trivialize on-foot play; universally inferior vehicles are never used, because the balance did not make the choice meaningful.

### Ensure Vehicle Combat Serves the Game's Fantasy and Loop

Vehicle combat should serve the game's core fantasy and loop — reinforcing what the game is about — rather than existing as a checkbox feature added because vehicles are expected, because vehicle combat disconnected from the core fantasy feels like a gimmick. The decision rule: confirm vehicle combat reinforces the core fantasy and loop, and cut vehicle content that does not serve the game's identity. Vehicle combat that does not serve the fantasy is a gimmick, because it exists without connection to what the game is.

### Design Vehicle Encounters to Use the Vehicle's Strengths

Vehicle encounters should be designed to use the vehicle's strengths — large-scale battles, speed-based challenges, environments suited to the vehicle — rather than forcing the vehicle into encounters that do not suit it, because vehicle combat in unsuited encounters feels awkward and constrained. The decision rule: design vehicle encounters around the vehicle's strengths, avoid forcing vehicles into on-foot-scaled encounters, and ensure the encounter leverages what the vehicle does well. Vehicles in unsuited encounters feel constrained, because the encounter did not use the vehicle's strengths.

### Provide Counterplay So Vehicle Combat Is Not One-Sided

Vehicle combat must provide counterplay — enemies that can threaten the vehicle, situations that create risk, weaknesses the player must manage — so the vehicle combat is engaging rather than one-sided stomps where the vehicle is invincible. The decision rule: design enemies and situations that threaten the vehicle, give the vehicle weaknesses to manage, and avoid vehicle combat where the player is invincible. Invincible vehicle combat is boring, because the absence of threat removes the engagement.

### Transition Smoothly Between Vehicle and On-Foot Play

Transitions between vehicle and on-foot play should be smooth — quick, intuitive, without disrupting flow — so the player can move between modes as the situation demands, rather than the transition being a friction point that discourages mixing. The decision rule: design smooth vehicle-on-foot transitions, minimize friction, and avoid transitions that disrupt flow or discourage mixing. Frictionful transitions discourage mixing, because the cost of switching exceeded the benefit of using the right tool.

## Common Traps

### Segregated Vehicle Combat as a Separate Mode

The team designs vehicle combat as a separate mode with different rules, mechanics, and feel from the core game, and the vehicle sections feel like a different game bolted on. The trap is that separate vehicle combat is simpler to build. The false signal is that the game has variety. The harm is that the vehicle combat feels incoherent with the core, the player experiences a disjointed product, the core loop's coherence is disrupted, and the vehicle sections feel like a gimmick rather than integration, because the combat was segregated rather than integrated.

### Vehicles That Trivialize On-Foot Play

The team makes vehicles universally superior — more powerful, better protected, no tradeoffs — and the player uses the vehicle for everything, trivializing the on-foot play the core loop depends on. The trap is that powerful vehicles serve the fantasy. The false signal is that the vehicles feel rewarding. The harm is that the on-foot combat is bypassed, the core loop that the game depends on is undermined, the encounter design that assumed mixed play is trivialized, and the game collapses to vehicle-only, because the vehicles had no tradeoffs to make on-foot viable.

### Vehicle Combat as a Checkbox Gimmick

The team adds vehicle combat because vehicles are expected in the genre, without connecting it to the game's fantasy or loop, and the vehicle sections feel like a gimmick. The trap is that vehicles are a genre expectation. The false signal is that the game has vehicle content. The harm is that the vehicle combat does not serve the core fantasy, the player experiences it as a bolted-on distraction, the coherence of the game's identity is diluted, and the vehicle content wastes development on a feature disconnected from what the game is, because it was added for expectation rather than integration.

### Vehicles in Unsuitable Encounters

The team forces vehicles into encounters scaled for on-foot play — tight spaces, small enemies, on-foot objectives — and the vehicle feels awkward and constrained rather than powerful. The trap is that the vehicle section needed content. The false signal is that the vehicle is present. The harm is that the vehicle cannot use its strengths in the unsuited encounter, the player experiences the vehicle as clumsy rather than empowering, the vehicle fantasy is betrayed, and the vehicle combat is frustrating rather than fun, because the encounter did not suit the vehicle.

### Invincible Vehicle Combat With No Counterplay

The team designs vehicle combat where the vehicle is invincible or overwhelming, with no enemies that can threaten it, and the combat is one-sided stomps without engagement. The trap is that powerful vehicles feel good. The false signal is that the vehicle is strong. The harm is that the absence of threat removes the engagement, the vehicle combat is boring rather than challenging, the player goes through the motions without risk, and the vehicle section is an empty power trip rather than a combat challenge, because the counterplay was absent.

### Frictionful Vehicle-On-Foot Transitions

The team designs vehicle-on-foot transitions with friction — long animations, awkward controls, flow disruption — and the player avoids mixing the modes because the transition cost is too high. The trap is that the transition was not prioritized. The false signal is that both modes are available. The harm is that the player sticks to one mode to avoid the transition friction, the mixing that would serve the encounter is absent, the player uses the wrong tool because switching is too costly, and the integration of vehicle and on-foot is undermined, because the transition was not smooth.

## Self-Check

- Is vehicle combat integrated with the core loop, sharing mechanics and feel, rather than segregated as a separate mode?
- Are vehicles balanced against on-foot play, with advantages and tradeoffs, so neither trivializes the other?
- Does vehicle combat serve the game's core fantasy and loop, rather than existing as a checkbox gimmick?
- Are vehicle encounters designed around the vehicle's strengths, not forced into unsuitable on-foot-scaled situations?
- Is there counterplay — enemies and situations that threaten the vehicle — so combat is engaging, not one-sided?
- Are vehicle-on-foot transitions smooth and low-friction, so the player mixes modes as the situation demands?
- Did I confirm the vehicle combat reinforces rather than disrupts the core game's coherence?
