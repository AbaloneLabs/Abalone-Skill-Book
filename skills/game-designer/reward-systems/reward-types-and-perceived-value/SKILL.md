# Reward Types and Perceived Value

Not all rewards are equal, and the type of reward chosen — functional versus cosmetic, intrinsic versus extrinsic, earned versus granted — shapes both how valuable the reward feels and what it does to the player's motivation. The judgment problem is that rewards can undermine the very intrinsic motivation they appear to support (the overjustification effect), that functional rewards can unbalance the game, and that perceived value depends on scarcity, effort, and meaning in ways that are easy to get wrong. Agents tend to miss this because a reward that is generous on paper (high quantity, frequent) can feel cheap (inflationary, unearned), and because the motivation research on how extrinsic rewards crowd out intrinsic motivation is counterintuitive. The harm is rewards that devalue themselves through inflation, that unbalance the game, or that replace the player's intrinsic enjoyment with a hollow extrinsic drip that collapses when the drip stops. This skill covers how to choose reward types, manage perceived value, and avoid the motivation and balance traps. The agent has latitude in the specific rewards, but the obligation to weigh perceived value and motivation effects is not optional.

## Core Rules

### Match Reward Type to What the Player Values and the Game Needs

Reward types — functional (gear, abilities, resources), cosmetic (skins, titles, visual flair), social (status, leaderboards, recognition), and narrative (story, lore, resolution) — serve different motivations and must be matched to what the player values and what the game's loop needs. The decision rule: identify what the player population values and what the core loop needs to reinforce, and select reward types that serve both, rather than defaulting to one type. Mismatched rewards (functional rewards in a game that values expression, cosmetic rewards in a game that values power) feel irrelevant and fail to reinforce engagement.

### Manage Scarcity to Preserve Perceived Value

Perceived value depends on scarcity: a reward that is common feels cheap regardless of its objective quality, and a reward that is scarce feels valuable. The decision rule: manage the scarcity of each reward tier so that common rewards feel like satisfying baseline gains and rare rewards feel like meaningful achievements, and avoid flooding the player with rewards that inflate away the value of all of them. Reward inflation — too many rewards, too frequent — makes each reward feel cheap and undermines the value of the entire reward economy, because scarcity is the substrate of perceived value.

### Prefer Cosmetic Rewards for Expression, Functional for Progression

Cosmetic rewards (which do not affect gameplay power) are ideal for expression and status without unbalancing the game, while functional rewards (which affect power) must be carefully balanced against the game's economy and difficulty. The decision rule: use cosmetic rewards for expression, status, and long-term collection (they cannot unbalance), and use functional rewards for progression and power (they must be balanced), and avoid using functional rewards where cosmetic would serve, because functional rewards carry balance risk that cosmetics do not. Functional rewards used carelessly unbalance the game; cosmetic rewards used for progression feel insufficient.

### Ensure Rewards Feel Earned, Not Granted

A reward feels valuable in proportion to the effort or achievement required to earn it, and rewards granted without effort feel cheap regardless of their objective value. The decision rule: calibrate each reward to the effort required, ensure the effort is meaningful (a real challenge or decision, not busywork), and avoid granting valuable rewards for trivial actions. Rewards granted for trivial effort devalue themselves and the achievements they were meant to celebrate, because the player senses the reward was not earned and it carries no satisfaction.

### Beware Extrinsic Rewards Crowding Out Intrinsic Motivation

Extrinsic rewards (points, items, achievements) can crowd out the intrinsic motivation (enjoyment of the activity itself) that sustains long-term engagement, a well-documented effect where adding rewards to an intrinsically enjoyable activity reduces the player's intrinsic interest. The decision rule: for activities the player already finds intrinsically enjoyable, use extrinsic rewards sparingly or not at all, and reserve extrinsic rewards for activities that are not intrinsically motivating but are necessary. Extrinsifying an intrinsically enjoyable activity teaches the player to do it for the reward, and when the reward is removed, the intrinsic enjoyment is diminished.

### Avoid Power Creep and Reward Inflation Over the Game's Life

Over the game's life, the temptation to add more and better rewards produces power creep (each new reward outclasses the last) and inflation (rewards become so common that value erodes), both of which undermine the reward economy. The decision rule: plan the reward economy's long-term trajectory, establish power and scarcity ceilings, and resist the temptation to outclass existing rewards with each addition. Power creep and inflation, left unchecked, make earlier rewards worthless and require ever-escalating rewards to feel valuable, collapsing the economy that sustains engagement.

## Common Traps

### Mismatched Reward Types That Feel Irrelevant

The team defaults to one reward type — functional gear, say — without considering what the player population values, and the rewards feel irrelevant to players who value expression or narrative. The trap is that one reward type is simpler to produce. The false signal is that rewards are being delivered. The harm is that the rewards do not reinforce the motivations the player population actually has, they feel like generic drips rather than meaningful recognition, and the engagement the rewards were meant to drive does not occur, because the reward type was chosen for production ease rather than player value.

### Reward Inflation That Devalues Everything

The team, wanting the player to feel rewarded, delivers rewards frequently and generously, and the abundance inflates away the perceived value of each reward and of the entire economy. The trap is that generous rewards feel generous. The false signal is that players receive many rewards. The harm is that each reward feels cheap because it is common, the rare rewards that should feel special are devalued by the surrounding abundance, and the player stops feeling rewarded because everything is rewarded, because scarcity — the substrate of perceived value — was spent on generosity.

### Functional Rewards That Unbalance the Game

The team uses functional rewards — powerful gear, abilities — without balancing them against the game's economy and difficulty, and the rewards unbalance the game, trivializing content or invalidating other rewards. The trap is that functional rewards feel impactful. The false signal is that rewards feel powerful. The harm is that the reward's power breaks the game's balance, the content the reward was meant to enhance is trivialized, other rewards are invalidated by the overpowered one, and the engagement the reward was meant to drive is undermined by the broken balance it caused, because functional rewards carry balance risk that was not managed.

### Rewards Granted Without Earned Effort

The team grants valuable rewards for trivial actions — a login, a click, a tutorial completion — to make the player feel rewarded early, and the rewards feel cheap because they were not earned. The trap is that easy rewards feel welcoming. The false signal is that players receive valuable things. The harm is that the rewards carry no satisfaction because they required no achievement, the player senses the reward was granted not earned, and the achievements that should feel meaningful are devalued by the surrounding unearned abundance, because the effort-reward link that creates perceived value was severed.

### Extrinsic Rewards Killing Intrinsic Motivation

The team adds extrinsic rewards — points, achievements, items — to an activity the player already finds intrinsically enjoyable, and the extrinsic rewards crowd out the intrinsic motivation, so the player now does the activity for the reward rather than for the enjoyment. The trap is that adding rewards feels like adding value. The false signal is that the activity now has rewards. The harm is that the player's intrinsic enjoyment is diminished by the extrinsic frame, when the rewards are removed or exhausted the player stops the activity they previously enjoyed, and the long-term engagement that intrinsic motivation sustained is replaced by brittle extrinsic dependence, because the overjustification effect was not considered.

### Power Creep and Inflation Over Time

The team, over the game's life, adds rewards that outclass existing ones to make each addition feel exciting, and the result is power creep (old rewards become worthless) and inflation (value erodes), undermining the economy. The trap is that escalating rewards feel exciting each time. The false signal is that each new reward is better than the last. The harm is that earlier rewards become worthless, players who earned them feel their achievement devalued, the game requires ever-escalating rewards to feel valuable, and the economy that sustains engagement collapses under the escalation, because the long-term trajectory was not planned and each addition outclassed the last.

## Self-Check

- Did I match reward types (functional, cosmetic, social, narrative) to what the player population values and what the core loop needs?
- Am I managing scarcity so common rewards feel like baseline gains and rare rewards feel like achievements, without inflation?
- Am I using cosmetic rewards for expression and functional rewards for progression, with functional rewards balanced against the economy?
- Does each reward require meaningful effort or achievement, so it feels earned rather than granted?
- For intrinsically enjoyable activities, am I using extrinsic rewards sparingly to avoid crowding out intrinsic motivation?
- Have I planned the reward economy's long-term trajectory with power and scarcity ceilings to prevent creep and inflation?
- Did I avoid the overjustification effect — adding rewards to activities that were already intrinsically motivating?
