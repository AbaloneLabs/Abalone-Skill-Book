# Representation and Stereotype Avoidance

Representation in games is not a cosmetic concern layered on top of "the real design"; it is a load-bearing design decision because games are one of the most widely consumed narrative and visual media, and what they depict shapes how millions of players understand real groups of people, including members of those groups understanding themselves. The judgment problem is that stereotype is the path of least resistance: it is fast, it is legible to a broad audience, and it borrows pre-existing cultural shorthand that requires no research or imagination, which is why even well-intentioned designers default to it under deadline pressure. Agents tend to miss the harm because stereotypes often feel like "accurate" or "efficient" characterization to someone outside the group depicted, and the people harmed are usually not in the room to object. The result is a recurring pattern where marginalized characters are reduced to a single trait, cast in limited roles (sidekick, villain, victim, comic relief, magical guide), or made to carry the weight of representing their entire group rather than being a person who happens to belong to it. This skill covers how to design characters and worlds that represent real identities with specificity and dignity, how to audit for stereotype, and how to do the work without tokenism, exoticization, or the paralysis of fear. The agent has broad creative latitude, but the obligations around research, consultation, and avoiding predictable harms are binding.

## Core Rules

### Treat Characters as People First, Identities Second

A well-written character from a marginalized group is, first and foremost, a person with their own goals, flaws, arc, and interiority, whose identity is one facet of who they are rather than the entirety of their function in the story. The failure mode is the "representative character" whose every scene is about their demographic category and who exists to deliver the group's perspective rather than to live their own life. The decision rule: ask whether the character would still be interesting and active if their demographic identity were changed; if the answer is no, the character is a stereotype wearing a face. Identity should inform the character's history, perspective, and stakes, but it should not be the only thing the character is for.

### Replace Stereotype with Specificity

Stereotypes are broad cultural shorthand; specificity is the antidote. A stereotype says "this character is [group], therefore they are [trait]"; specificity says "this character is a specific person from a specific place, family, and history, and here is how that shaped them." The decision rule: when depicting a real-world identity, research the actual diversity within the group — regional, generational, class, religious, and individual variation — and draw from that specificity rather than from the media shorthand that already circulates. The more specific the character, the harder it is for them to function as a stereotype, because stereotypes require generality to operate. Specificity is also what makes characters memorable and human to players of all backgrounds.

### Audit the Cast for Role Distribution, Not Just Presence

Tokenism is not solved by adding more tokens; it is solved by examining what roles the characters from each group are allowed to occupy. The decision rule: audit the cast by mapping which groups are represented as protagonists, antagonists, mentors, comic relief, victims, and background, and look for patterns where a group is consistently confined to a narrow set of roles. A cast with demographic diversity but consistent role confinement (one group always the villain, another always the sidekick, another always the victim) is not inclusive; it is stereotype with a diversity veneer. The goal is not statistical balance in every title but the absence of confining patterns across the work.

### Consult Members of the Depicted Group, and Compensate Them

The single most reliable safeguard against harmful depiction is consultation with people who belong to the group being depicted, conducted early enough to shape the design rather than only to vet a finished product. The decision rule: budget for and engage cultural consultants, sensitivity readers, or community advisors from the relevant group, involve them at concept stage rather than only at review, treat their feedback as authoritative on questions of harm, and compensate them professionally rather than asking for free labor. Consultation is not a guarantee of perfection, but its absence is a reliable predictor of harm, and the excuse that "we could not find anyone" almost always means the search was not resourced.

### Examine the Intersection of Identity and Power in the Fiction

Representation harm is not only about whether a group appears but about what the fiction says about them through structure: who has agency, who is saved, who is powerful, who is degraded, who is the subject versus the object of the gaze. The decision rule: when a marginalized character is included, examine whether the narrative grants them agency or uses them as an instrument of another character's arc, whether their suffering is framed as spectacle, and whether the game's mechanics and camera position them with dignity. Structural harm — the damsel, the sacrifice, the exotic backdrop — persists even when individual dialogue is respectful, because it operates at the level of role and framing.

### Distinguish Representation from Appropriation and Exoticization

Drawing on a culture's aesthetics, symbols, or traditions without understanding or respect is appropriation; framing a culture as mysterious, primitive, or exotic for the entertainment of outsiders is exoticization. Both can occur even with positive intent. The decision rule: when incorporating elements from a culture not your own, ask whether you understand the meaning and context of what you are using, whether you are using it in a way the culture would recognize and accept, and whether the depiction centers the culture's own perspective or an outsider's fantasy of it. When in doubt, simplify toward original creation rather than borrowing what you do not understand, and credit and consult where borrowing is appropriate.

### Avoid the Paralysis of Fear and the Reflex of Avoidance

Fear of getting representation wrong can produce a counterproductive response: avoiding marginalized characters entirely, stripping them of any cultural specificity to be "safe," or writing them so blandly that they cannot offend because they have no content. The decision rule: recognize that avoidance is itself a form of harm, because a medium that only depicts the dominant group tells marginalized players they do not belong in the story. The goal is not risk-free depiction, which is impossible, but informed, consulted, specific depiction with the humility to revise when feedback reveals harm. Done representation is better than perfect intention never shipped.

## Common Traps

### The Single-Defining-Trait Character

A character from a marginalized group is written so that their demographic identity is their entire personality, with no goals, flaws, or interiority beyond it. The trap is that this feels like "representation" because the group is visible. The false signal is that the character is present and named. The harm is that the character functions as a stereotype with a positive valence, reducing real people to a single dimension and signaling to players from that group that they are not imagined as full human beings.

### The Magical Marginalized Person

A character from a marginalized group is cast as the wise guide, the spiritual mentor, or the magical helper whose function is to develop the protagonist, often from a dominant group, at the cost of their own arc. The trap is that the role feels respectful because the character is powerful and revered. The false signal is that the character is dignified and important to the plot. The harm is that the character exists to serve another's growth and has no interiority of their own, recapitulating a long media history of marginalized people as instruments rather than subjects.

### Tokenism Confused with Diversity

One character from a marginalized group is added to an otherwise homogeneous cast, often in a minor role, and the team celebrates the diversity. The trap is that the addition feels like progress. The false signal is that the cast now "includes" the group. The harm is that the single character carries impossible representational weight, any flaw in their writing is read as a statement about the whole group, and the underlying homogeneity of the cast and creative team is unchanged, so the next project repeats the same pattern.

### Villain Coding and Demographic Monopoly

Antagonists are consistently drawn from a particular real-world group through accent, appearance, name, or cultural markers, while protagonists are drawn from another, so the fiction teaches players an association between the marginalized group and villainy. The trap is that the choices feel like neutral aesthetic or genre convention. The false signal is that each individual villain is "just a character." The harm is cumulative and well-documented: media patterns of villain coding shape real-world prejudice, and players from the coded group internalize the association or face it from others.

### Exoticization as Aesthetic

A culture's dress, architecture, religion, or symbols are used as visual flavor without engaging with their meaning, framing the culture as a mysterious or picturesque backdrop for a story about outsiders. The trap is that the aesthetic is genuinely beautiful and the team admires the culture. The false signal is that the depiction is visually reverent. The harm is that the culture is reduced to scenery, its people are positioned as exotic objects for outsider consumption, and the depiction reinforces a worldview in which the culture exists to be looked at rather than understood.

### Research-Free Borrowing Under Deadline

Under time pressure, the team uses cultural elements from a quick web search or from other media rather than from genuine research or consultation, on the assumption that close enough is fine. The trap is that the elements look plausible to outsiders. The false signal is that no one on the team objects. The harm is that inaccuracies and insensitivities ship to a global audience that includes members of the culture, who recognize the carelessness immediately, and the resulting harm to trust and reputation far exceeds the time the research would have cost.

## Self-Check

- Is each character a person with their own goals, flaws, and interiority first, whose identity informs but does not exhaust who they are, and would they remain interesting if their demographic identity changed?
- Have I replaced stereotype with specificity by researching the real diversity within the group and drawing from regional, generational, and individual variation rather than media shorthand?
- Have I audited the cast for role distribution, mapping which groups appear as protagonists, villains, sidekicks, and victims, and identified any confining patterns rather than only checking for presence?
- Did I engage and compensate cultural consultants or sensitivity readers from the depicted group at concept stage, treating their feedback as authoritative on questions of harm?
- Have I examined the structural position of marginalized characters — their agency, framing, the camera's gaze, and whether they are subjects or instruments of another character's arc?
- Where I have drawn on a culture not my own, have I distinguished respectful engagement from appropriation and exoticization, and simplified toward original creation where my understanding is insufficient?
- Have I avoided both harmful depiction and the paralysis of avoidance, shipping informed and consulted representation rather than omitting marginalized characters entirely out of fear?
