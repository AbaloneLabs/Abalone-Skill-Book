# Mobile Session and Engagement Design

Mobile games are played in a fundamentally different context than PC or console games — short, interrupted sessions, on the go, in environments full of distraction — and the session and engagement design must fit this context or fight it, and fighting mobile context churns players. The judgment problem is that mobile sessions must be short and interruptible (fitting the player's context), engagement must drive frequent return (daily habits) without exploitative compulsion, and the design must respect the mobile player's fragmented attention rather than demanding the sustained focus of a PC session. Agents tend to miss this because engagement patterns that work on PC (long sessions, deep focus) fight the mobile context, and because the metrics that look good (session length) may reflect players trapped in long sessions they resent rather than engaged players. The harm is mobile games that demand sessions the context cannot support, that churn players through context-mismatch, or that extract engagement through compulsion rather than earning it. This skill covers how to design mobile sessions and engagement that fit the context, drive healthy retention, and avoid the mismatch and compulsion traps. The agent has latitude in the design, but the obligation to fit the mobile context is not optional.

## Core Rules

### Design Sessions for Short, Interruptible Mobile Play

Mobile sessions must be short (fitting the available minutes, not demanding hours) and interruptible (the player can stop at any point without severe penalty, because mobile play is frequently interrupted), because the mobile context provides fragmented time that long, uninterruptible sessions cannot use. The decision rule: design sessions to be short and completable in available minutes, ensure interruption does not severely penalize, and avoid long mandatory sessions. Long, uninterruptible sessions fight the context, because the mobile time could not support them.

### Drive Frequent Return Through Daily Habits, Not Compulsion

Mobile engagement should drive frequent return through daily habits — rewarding daily play, providing daily goals — without exploitative compulsion (energy systems that demand return, mechanics that punish absence), because habit-driven retention is sustainable while compulsion-driven retention churns when the compulsion exceeds tolerance. The decision rule: drive return through desirable daily habits, avoid compulsion that punishes absence, and ensure the return is earned through desire not extracted through pressure. Compulsion-driven retention churns, because the pressure exceeded the willingness.

### Respect Fragmented Attention With Clear Session Goals

Mobile players have fragmented attention (distractions, interruptions), and each session should have clear, graspable goals (what to do, what to achieve) that the player can engage with despite the fragmentation, because sessions without clear goals lose the player in the distraction. The decision rule: design each session with clear, graspable goals, ensure the player can engage despite fragmentation, and avoid sessions that require sustained focus. Focus-demanding sessions lose mobile players, because the fragmented attention could not sustain the focus.

### Use Push and Notifications Responsibly to Support Habits

Push notifications and reminders can support daily habits (reminding the player of goals, events), but they must be used responsibly (relevant, not excessive, respectful of the player's time) because notification spam produces annoyance and uninstalls rather than engagement. The decision rule: use notifications to support habits responsibly (relevant, limited, opt-out-able), and avoid notification spam. Notification spam produces uninstalls, because the annoyance exceeded the engagement value.

### Provide Quick, Satisfying Core Loops for Micro-Sessions

The core loop should be quick and satisfying — providing meaningful engagement and reward in a short session — so that even a two-minute micro-session is worthwhile, because mobile players often have only minutes and a loop that requires longer sessions excludes micro-session play. The decision rule: ensure the core loop provides satisfaction in short sessions, and avoid loops that require long sessions to be worthwhile. Long-session loops exclude micro-session play, because the loop could not satisfy in minutes.

### Avoid Energy and Gate Systems That Frustrate More Than Engage

Energy systems and gates (which limit play and require waiting or payment to continue) can drive retention (returning when energy refills) but can also frustrate (stopping play the player wants to continue), and their use must be weighed, because frustration-driven churn may exceed the retention benefit. The decision rule: if using energy or gates, weigh the retention benefit against the frustration cost, and avoid gates that frustrate more than they engage. Frustration-exceeding gates churn, because the frustration exceeded the retention value.

## Common Traps

### Long, Uninterruptible Sessions Fighting Mobile Context

The team designs sessions that are long and uninterruptible (demanding sustained focus, penalizing interruption), and the sessions fight the mobile context. The trap is that deep sessions are engaging. The false signal is that the game has depth. The harm is that the mobile player cannot engage with long sessions in their fragmented time, the sessions are interrupted with penalty, the player cannot play in the available minutes, and the game churns players whose context cannot support the session design, because the sessions fought rather than fit the context.

### Compulsion-Driven Retention That Churns

The team drives retention through compulsion — energy systems that demand return, mechanics that punish absence — rather than desirable habits, and the compulsion churns when it exceeds tolerance. The trap is that compulsion drives metrics. The false signal is that daily return is high. The harm is that the compulsion extracts return through pressure rather than desire, the players who tolerate the compulsion dwindle, the churn accelerates as the pressure exceeds willingness, and the retention is unsustainable, because the return was extracted rather than earned.

### Focus-Demanding Sessions Losing Mobile Players

The team designs sessions that require sustained focus (complex states, long arcs), and the mobile player with fragmented attention loses the thread. The trap is that the sessions are deep. The false signal is that the game is engaging. The harm is that the mobile player cannot sustain the focus the session demands, the distractions and interruptions break the engagement, the player loses the thread and disengages, and the game churns players whose attention is fragmented, because the sessions demanded focus the context could not provide.

### Notification Spam Producing Uninstalls

The team uses notifications aggressively to drive return, and the notification spam produces annoyance and uninstalls rather than engagement. The trap is that notifications drive return. The false signal is that notifications are sent. The harm is that the excessive, irrelevant notifications annoy the player, the annoyance exceeds the engagement value, the player disables notifications or uninstalls to escape the spam, and the retention mechanism produces the opposite of retention, because the notifications were not responsible.

### Long-Session Loops Excluding Micro-Session Play

The team designs a core loop that requires long sessions to be worthwhile, and the mobile player with only minutes cannot engage. The trap is that the loop is deep. The false signal is that the game is satisfying. The harm is that the loop cannot satisfy in a short session, the player with only minutes finds nothing worthwhile to do, the micro-session play that is the mobile context is excluded, and the game churns players who cannot provide long sessions, because the loop could not satisfy in minutes.

### Frustrating Energy Gates Exceeding Retention Value

The team uses energy gates that frustrate more than they engage, and the frustration churn exceeds the retention benefit. The trap is that gates drive return. The false signal is that players return when energy refills. The harm is that the gates stop play the player wants to continue, the frustration accumulates, the players who would continue instead leave (to avoid the frustration), the churn from frustration exceeds the retention from the gate, and the game loses more players than the gate retains, because the frustration exceeded the value.

## Self-Check

- Are sessions short and interruptible, fitting the mobile player's fragmented time without penalizing interruption?
- Is frequent return driven through desirable daily habits rather than exploitative compulsion that punishes absence?
- Do sessions have clear, graspable goals that the player can engage with despite fragmented attention?
- Are push notifications used responsibly (relevant, limited, opt-out-able) to support habits without spamming?
- Does the core loop provide satisfaction in short micro-sessions, not just long sessions?
- If energy or gates are used, is the retention benefit weighed against the frustration cost, avoiding frustration-exceeding churn?
- Did I confirm the session and engagement design fits the mobile context rather than fighting it?
