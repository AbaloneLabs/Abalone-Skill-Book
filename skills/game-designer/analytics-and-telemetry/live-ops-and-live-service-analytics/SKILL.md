# Live-Ops and Live-Service Analytics

Live-service games require continuous operation — events, content updates, balance changes, community engagement — and the analytics that monitor and drive live-ops determine whether the game sustains a healthy player base or degrades through content treadmill burnout, whale-dependence, and metric-chasing. The judgment problem is that live-ops must sustain engagement without exhausting the team or the players, must monetize sustainably without depending on a vulnerable whale minority, and must balance the content cadence against production capacity, and agents tend to miss this because the live metrics (daily revenue, engagement) can look healthy while the foundation (player sentiment, team sustainability, monetization concentration) erodes. The harm is live games that burn out their teams, that depend on a few whales whose departure collapses revenue, and that chase metrics into degradation. This skill covers how to run live-ops analytics that monitor true health, sustain engagement and monetization, and avoid the treadmill and dependence traps. The agent has latitude in the live-ops, but the obligation to monitor and sustain true health is not optional.

## Core Rules

### Monitor Leading Indicators of Health, Not Just Lagging Revenue

Live-game health must be monitored through leading indicators (retention cohorts, sentiment, engagement quality) not just lagging revenue (which reflects past health and masks current decline), because by the time revenue declines, the underlying erosion is advanced. The decision rule: track leading indicators of health (cohort retention, sentiment, engagement depth) alongside revenue, and act on leading indicators before they become lagging decline. Revenue-only monitoring detects decline too late, because the lagging metric masked the leading erosion.

### Diversify Monetization to Avoid Whale-Dependence

Live-service monetization must be diversified across the player base, not concentrated in a small whale minority, because whale-dependence makes revenue fragile (a few whale departures collapse it) and pressures the design toward whale-exploiting mechanics that harm the broader base. The decision rule: monitor monetization concentration (what percentage of revenue from the top spenders), diversify across the base, and avoid dependence on a vulnerable minority. Whale-dependent monetization is fragile and exploitative, because the revenue rested on a few players whose exploitation or departure would collapse it.

### Balance Content Cadence Against Sustainable Production

The content cadence — how often new content releases — must be balanced against sustainable production capacity, because a cadence that exceeds sustainable capacity burns out the team and produces a treadmill that cannot be maintained, while a cadence too slow loses the audience. The decision rule: set the content cadence to what the team can sustainably produce, avoid cadence creep (faster than sustainable), and plan for long-term sustainability. Unsustainable cadence burns out the team, because the production exceeded the sustainable capacity.

### Use Live Events to Re-engage Without Exploiting FOMO

Live events (limited-time content, seasonal events) re-engage lapsed players and sustain active ones, but they must do so without exploiting FOMO (fear of missing out) in ways that produce anxiety and resentment, because FOMO-driven engagement is brittle and damages sentiment. The decision rule: use live events to re-engage with desirable content, avoid FOMO-exploiting pressure (excessive scarcity, punishing miss consequences), and frame events as opportunities not obligations. FOMO-exploiting events damage sentiment, because the engagement was extracted through anxiety rather than earned through desire.

### Monitor Cohort Retention to Understand Long-Term Sustainability

Cohort retention — how each player cohort (by acquisition date) retains over time — reveals the game's long-term sustainability, because if newer cohorts retain worse than older ones, the game is declining even if total numbers look stable, and cohort analysis must be part of live monitoring. The decision rule: track cohort retention over time, compare cohorts, and act on declining cohort retention as a leading indicator of decline. Aggregate-only retention hides cohort decline, because the stable total masked the worsening cohorts.

### Avoid the Content Treadmill That Exhausts Players and Team

The content treadmill — the relentless demand for new content to sustain engagement — can exhaust both players (who cannot keep up) and team (who cannot produce sustainably), and live-ops must find a sustainable rhythm rather than an escalating treadmill that burns out both. The decision rule: find a sustainable content rhythm that engages without exhausting, avoid escalation that produces treadmill burnout, and prioritize evergreen systems over disposable content. Treadmill live-ops burns out players and team, because the cadence escalated beyond sustainability.

## Common Traps

### Revenue-Only Monitoring Detecting Decline Too Late

The team monitors revenue as the primary health metric, and when revenue declines, the underlying erosion is advanced, because revenue is a lagging indicator. The trap is that revenue is the visible metric. The false signal is that revenue is stable. The harm is that the leading indicators (cohort retention, sentiment) declined before revenue, the decline is advanced by the time revenue reflects it, the team cannot intervene in time, and the game's health erodes undetected, because the leading indicators were not monitored.

### Whale-Dependence Making Revenue Fragile and Exploitative

The team allows monetization to concentrate in a small whale minority, and the revenue becomes fragile and the design pressures toward whale-exploiting mechanics. The trap is that whale revenue is high. The false signal is that monetization is strong. The harm is that the revenue rests on a few players, the departure of key whales collapses revenue, the design is pressured toward mechanics that exploit the whales (and harm the broader base), and the monetization is both fragile and ethically compromised, because the concentration was not diversified.

### Unsustainable Content Cadence Burning Out the Team

The team sets a content cadence faster than sustainable production, and the team burns out trying to maintain it, and the cadence cannot be sustained. The trap is that a fast cadence satisfies the audience. The false signal is that content is releasing. The harm is that the production exceeds sustainable capacity, the team burns out, the quality declines under the pressure, the cadence eventually collapses when the team cannot sustain it, and the audience that expected the cadence is alienated by the slowdown, because the cadence was not set to sustainable capacity.

### FOMO-Exploiting Events Damaging Sentiment

The team uses live events with FOMO-exploiting pressure — excessive scarcity, punishing miss consequences — to drive engagement, and the events produce anxiety and resentment. The trap is that FOMO drives engagement. The false signal is that event participation is high. The harm is that the engagement is extracted through anxiety, the players who miss events feel punished, the sentiment toward the game degrades, the engagement is brittle (it lasts only as long as the FOMO pressure), and the events damage the long-term relationship, because the engagement was not earned through desire.

### Aggregate Retention Hiding Cohort Decline

The team monitors aggregate retention (total players over time) without cohort analysis, and declining newer cohorts are hidden by stable or declining older cohorts, and the decline is missed. The trap is that aggregate retention is simple. The false signal is that retention is stable. The harm is that the newer cohorts retain worse than older ones, the aggregate stability masks the cohort decline, the game is declining even as the total looks stable, and the team does not intervene because the cohort erosion was invisible, because cohort analysis was not used.

### The Content Treadmill Exhausting Players and Team

The team escalates the content cadence and scope to sustain engagement, and the treadmill exhausts both players (who cannot keep up) and team (who cannot produce sustainably). The trap is that escalation sustains engagement. The false signal is that content is growing. The harm is that the players cannot keep up with the content pace (falling behind, churning), the team cannot produce the escalating scope sustainably (burning out), the treadmill that was meant to sustain engagement instead exhausts both sides, and the live-ops becomes unsustainable, because the rhythm escalated rather than stabilized.

## Self-Check

- Am I monitoring leading indicators of health (cohort retention, sentiment, engagement quality), not just lagging revenue?
- Is monetization diversified across the base, avoiding fragile and exploitative whale-dependence?
- Is the content cadence balanced against sustainable production capacity, avoiding cadence creep and team burnout?
- Do live events re-engage through desirable content without exploiting FOMO in ways that damage sentiment?
- Am I tracking cohort retention to detect declining newer cohorts before they collapse the aggregate?
- Have I found a sustainable content rhythm that engages without exhausting players and team on a treadmill?
- Did I confirm the live-ops strategy sustains true long-term health rather than chasing metrics into degradation?
