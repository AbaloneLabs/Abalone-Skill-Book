# Voice and Audio Localization

Voice acting localization — recording dialogue in multiple languages — is one of the most expensive and logistically complex parts of localization, and it is also where localized versions most commonly diverge in quality from the original, through miscast voices, mismatched performance, and cheap production that undermines the experience. The judgment problem is that localized voice must match the original's quality and emotional intent (so the localized experience is equivalent), must cast appropriately for each language (so characters sound right in each localization), and must be scoped and budgeted realistically (so the localization is not abandoned mid-production), and agents tend to miss this because the original-language voice sounds right (it was carefully cast and directed) while the localized voice is treated as a checkbox, and because the cost of full voice localization often leads to partial or cheap localization that harms the experience. The harm is localized versions that feel inferior, characters who sound wrong in translation, and experiences undermined by audio that does not match the original's care. This skill covers how to localize voice and audio with quality parity, cast and direct appropriately, and scope realistically. The agent has latitude in the production, but the obligation to deliver equivalent quality is not optional.

## Core Rules

### Aim for Quality Parity Between Original and Localized Audio

Localized voice acting should aim for quality parity with the original — equivalent performance quality, equivalent emotional intent, equivalent production value — so the localized experience is equivalent to the original, not a lesser version. The decision rule: set quality parity as the goal for localized audio, allocate budget and time to achieve it, and avoid treating localized audio as a cheaper afterthought. Lesser localized audio produces an inferior experience, because the localization did not match the original's care.

### Cast Voice Talent Appropriate to Each Language and Character

Voice casting for each language must be appropriate — the voice must fit the character in that language, considering the character's age, personality, and the language's voice acting conventions — because a voice that fits in the original may not fit in another language's conventions, and miscasting undermines the character. The decision rule: for each language, cast voices that fit the character within that language's conventions, and avoid assuming the original's casting translates. Miscast localized voices undermine characters, because the voice did not fit the localized context.

### Direct Localized Performance to Match Emotional Intent

Localized voice direction must match the original's emotional intent — the same tone, intensity, and pacing — so the localized performance conveys the same emotion, and direction must account for how emotion is expressed differently across languages. The decision rule: direct localized performances to match the original's emotional intent, provide directors with the original's context and intent, and avoid letting localized performances diverge in emotion. Emotion-divergent localization undermines scenes, because the performance did not match the intent.

### Scope Voice Localization Realistically Against Budget

Voice localization is expensive (talent, studio, direction, editing for each language), and the scope — how many lines, how many languages, how much of the game is voiced — must be realistic against the budget, or the localization is partial (some lines unvoiced) or abandoned (some languages dropped). The decision rule: scope voice localization against the realistic budget, decide what is voiced based on budget, and avoid over-scoping that produces partial or dropped localization. Over-scoped voice localization is partial or abandoned, because the budget could not support the scope.

### Ensure Lip-Sync and Timing Accommodate Localization

Lip-sync and line timing are tied to the original language's phonetics and length, and localized audio (different phonetics, different length) must be accommodated — through flexible lip-sync systems, adjusted timing, or acceptable divergence — or the localized audio visibly mismatches the visuals. The decision rule: design lip-sync and timing systems to accommodate localization (flexible animation, length-tolerant scenes), and avoid hard-tied original-language lip-sync. Hard-tied lip-sync mismatches in localization, because the system assumed the original language.

### Maintain Consistency Across the Localized Audio Production

Across the localized audio production — multiple characters, multiple sessions, multiple languages — consistency must be maintained (consistent character voices, consistent quality, consistent direction), so the localized audio is coherent rather than varying in quality or characterization. The decision rule: maintain consistency across the localized production (character voice references, consistent direction, quality standards), and avoid drift that produces inconsistent audio. Inconsistent localized audio undermines coherence, because the production did not maintain standards.

## Common Traps

### Localized Audio as a Cheaper Afterthought

The team treats localized voice as a checkbox, allocating less budget and care than the original, and the localized audio is inferior. The trap is that the original was the priority. The false signal is that the game is localized. The harm is that the localized audio lacks the original's quality, the localized experience is inferior, players in localized regions experience a lesser version, and the localization undermines the game's reception in those markets, because quality parity was not the goal.

### Miscast Localized Voices

The team casts localized voices without considering the language's conventions, assuming the original casting translates, and the voices do not fit the characters in the localized context. The trap is that the casting matches the original. The false signal is that the characters are voiced. The harm is that the voices do not fit the language's conventions or the character's localization, the characters sound wrong, the player's connection to the characters is undermined, and the localized experience feels off, because the casting did not account for the localized context.

### Emotion-Divergent Localized Performance

The team records localized performance without matching the original's emotional intent, and the localized scenes convey different emotion than intended. The trap is that the lines are translated. The false signal is that the dialogue is recorded. The harm is that the localized performance diverges in tone, intensity, or pacing, the scenes convey different emotion, the dramatic or comedic intent is lost, and the localized experience does not match the original's impact, because the direction did not match the emotional intent.

### Over-Scoped Voice Localization That Is Partial or Abandoned

The team scopes voice localization beyond the budget, and the localization is partial (some lines unvoiced) or abandoned (some languages dropped). The trap is that full voice localization is the goal. The false signal is that the game will be fully voiced in all languages. The harm is that the budget cannot support the scope, some lines are unvoiced (breaking immersion) or some languages are dropped (losing markets), the partial localization undermines the experience, and the over-scoping produces a worse outcome than realistic scoping, because the scope was not matched to the budget.

### Hard-Tied Lip-Sync Mismatching in Localization

The team ties lip-sync and timing to the original language's phonetics and length, and the localized audio (different phonetics, different length) visibly mismatches the visuals. The trap is that the lip-sync matches the original. The false signal is that the visuals sync. The harm is that the localized audio does not match the lip movement or timing, the mismatch is visible and distracting, the immersion is broken, and the localized experience feels cheap, because the system assumed the original language.

### Inconsistent Localized Audio Production

The team produces localized audio across multiple sessions and languages without maintaining consistency, and the audio varies in quality and characterization. The trap is that the production is parallel. The false signal is that the audio is recorded. The harm is that the character voices vary across lines, the quality varies across sessions, the localized audio is incoherent, the player experiences inconsistency that breaks immersion, and the localization feels unprofessional, because consistency was not maintained.

## Self-Check

- Is quality parity with the original the goal for localized audio, with adequate budget and care?
- Are localized voices cast to fit the characters within each language's conventions, not assumed to translate?
- Is localized performance directed to match the original's emotional intent, tone, and pacing?
- Is voice localization scoped realistically against the budget, avoiding partial or abandoned localization?
- Do lip-sync and timing systems accommodate localization, rather than being hard-tied to the original language?
- Is consistency maintained across the localized audio production (character voices, quality, direction)?
- Did I confirm the localized audio delivers an equivalent experience rather than a lesser afterthought?
