# Community Feedback and Communication

The community is both the game's most valuable feedback source and its most volatile stakeholder, and how the team communicates with and listens to the community determines whether the relationship builds trust and informs design or erodes into hostility and misinformed development. The judgment problem is that feedback must be heard (so the community feels respected and the design benefits) without the team becoming reactive (chasing every complaint), communication must be honest (so trust is built) without over-promising (which destroys trust when unmet), and the vocal minority must be distinguished from the silent majority. Agents tend to miss this because the feedback that is loudest (forums, social media) is not necessarily representative, and because the temptation to appease the loudest voices produces reactive design that serves the minority at the majority's expense. The harm is communities that feel ignored (feedback vacuum) or misled (over-promising), design that chases complaints rather than serving the player base, and the erosion of the goodwill that sustains a live game. This skill covers how to process community feedback productively, communicate honestly, and avoid the reactive and vacuum traps. The agent has latitude in the communication style, but the obligation to build trust and inform design through the community is not optional.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish the Vocal Minority From the Silent Majority

Community feedback is skewed toward the vocal minority (the players who post on forums and social media), while the silent majority (who do not post) may have different needs and preferences, and design decisions must account for both, not just the vocal. The decision rule: supplement vocal feedback with broader data (telemetry, surveys) that captures the silent majority, and avoid designing for the vocal minority at the majority's expense. Vocal-only feedback misinforms design, because the minority was mistaken for the majority.

### Communicate Honestly Without Over-Promising

Developer communication must be honest — transparent about plans, challenges, and limitations — without over-promising (committing to features or timelines that cannot be met), because honest communication builds trust while over-promising destroys it when the promises are broken. The decision rule: communicate honestly about what is planned and what is uncertain, avoid committing to specifics that may not be delivered, and under-promise and over-deliver. Over-promising destroys trust, because the unmet promises revealed the communication as unreliable.

### Process Feedback Systematically, Not Reactively

Community feedback must be processed systematically — aggregated, categorized, assessed against design goals — rather than reacted to impulsively (changing design in response to the loudest complaint), because reactive design chases complaints and produces incoherent, whipsawing changes. The decision rule: aggregate and categorize feedback, assess it against design goals and broader data, and make deliberate decisions rather than reactive changes. Reactive design whipsaws, because each change chased the loudest complaint rather than a coherent vision.

### Acknowledge Feedback Even When Not Acting on It

Players who provide feedback need to know it was heard, even if the team decides not to act on it, because unacknowledged feedback produces a vacuum that erodes goodwill, and acknowledgment (even of disagreement) maintains the relationship. The decision rule: acknowledge feedback broadly (even when not acting), explain decisions when feasible, and avoid the silence that produces a feedback vacuum. Unacknowledged feedback erodes goodwill, because the community felt ignored.

### Close the Loop Between Feedback, Decision, and Communication

When feedback leads to a decision, the team should close the loop — communicate that the feedback was heard, what was decided, and why — so the community sees that feedback informs design, reinforcing the value of providing it. The decision rule: when feedback informs a decision, communicate the connection (the feedback, the decision, the reasoning), and close the loop visibly. Unclosed loops discourage feedback, because the community saw no evidence it mattered.

### Maintain Communication Through Good and Bad Periods

Community trust is built through consistent communication across both good periods (celebrating successes) and bad periods (addressing problems, delays, controversies), and going silent during bad periods destroys the trust built during good ones, because silence in difficulty reads as abandonment. The decision rule: maintain communication through both good and bad periods, address problems honestly rather than going silent, and avoid the silence-in-difficulty that reads as abandonment. Silent-in-bad-periods communication abandons the community, because the difficulty was met with silence.

## Common Traps

### Designing for the Vocal Minority

The team reacts to the vocal minority's feedback — the loudest forum posts, the most visible complaints — and designs for them, while the silent majority with different needs is underserved. The trap is that the vocal feedback is visible. The false signal is that the community wants this. The harm is that the vocal minority is mistaken for the majority, the design serves a minority at the majority's expense, the silent players whose needs differ are underserved, and the game drifts toward the vocal minority's preferences, because the feedback was not supplemented with broader data.

### Over-Promising That Destroys Trust

The team, to satisfy the community, over-promises features or timelines, and when the promises cannot be met, the trust is destroyed. The trap is that promises satisfy the community in the moment. The false signal is that the community is happy. The harm is that the unmet promises reveal the communication as unreliable, the trust built by the promises is destroyed by their failure, the community becomes cynical about all future communication, and the relationship is worse than if the team had been honest from the start, because the promises exceeded what could be delivered.

### Reactive Design That Whipsaws

The team reacts impulsively to complaints — changing design in response to each loud complaint — and the design whipsaws between contradictory changes, producing incoherence. The trap is that reacting feels responsive. The false signal is that the team listens. The harm is that each change chases the loudest complaint, the design whipsaws between contradictory directions, the coherence of the game's vision is lost, the players who preferred the previous state are alienated, and the game becomes incoherent, because the design was reactive rather than systematic.

### The Feedback Vacuum of Unacknowledged Input

The team reads feedback but does not acknowledge it, and the community feels ignored, and the feedback vacuum erodes goodwill. The trap is that the team is processing feedback internally. The false signal is that the feedback is being read. The harm is that the community sees no acknowledgment, the feedback feels wasted, the goodwill that motivates providing feedback erodes, the community becomes hostile (feeling ignored), and the valuable feedback channel dries up, because the acknowledgment was absent.

### Unclosed Loops Discouraging Feedback

The team acts on feedback but does not communicate the connection, and the community sees no evidence that feedback informs design, and future feedback is discouraged. The trap is that the action speaks for itself. The false signal is that the change was made. The harm is that the community does not connect the change to the feedback, the value of providing feedback is not reinforced, the community sees no reason to continue providing feedback, and the feedback channel that should inform design dries up, because the loop was not closed.

### Silence in Bad Periods Reading as Abandonment

The team communicates during good periods but goes silent during bad periods (problems, delays, controversies), and the silence reads as abandonment. The trap is that silence avoids saying the wrong thing. The false signal is that the team is working on the problem. The harm is that the silence in difficulty reads as abandonment, the trust built during good periods is destroyed, the community fills the silence with speculation and hostility, and the relationship is worse than honest communication of the difficulty would have produced, because the bad period was met with silence.

## Self-Check

- Am I distinguishing the vocal minority from the silent majority, supplementing forum feedback with broader data?
- Is communication honest without over-promising, under-promising and over-delivering rather than committing to uncertain specifics?
- Is feedback processed systematically (aggregated, categorized, assessed) rather than reacted to impulsively?
- Is feedback acknowledged even when not acted on, avoiding the vacuum that erodes goodwill?
- When feedback informs decisions, is the loop closed visibly so the community sees feedback matters?
- Is communication maintained through both good and bad periods, addressing problems rather than going silent?
- Did I confirm community communication builds trust and informs design rather than eroding goodwill and misinforming development?
