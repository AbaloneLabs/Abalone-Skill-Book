# Content Creator and Modding Support

Content creators (streamers, video makers) and modders (user-generated content) can extend a game's life, reach, and community far beyond what the team alone could achieve, and supporting them is one of the highest-leverage community investments — but it is also where games incur legal risk (IP, content liability), quality dilution (poor mods reflecting on the game), and the ethical question of depending on unpaid labor. The judgment problem is that creator and mod support must extend the game (through tools, partnership, visibility) without exposing the game to liability (for mod content), without letting poor-quality UGC dilute the brand, and without exploiting the creators whose labor generates the value. Agents tend to miss this because the benefit (extended life, free content) is visible while the risks (liability, quality, exploitation) are diffuse, and because the temptation to benefit from unpaid creator labor without fair support raises ethical questions that are easy to defer. The harm is games that expose themselves to mod-content liability, that are diluted by poor UGC, or that exploit the creator economy they depend on. This skill covers how to support creators and modders productively, manage the risks, and build sustainable creator relationships. The agent has latitude in the support, but the obligation to manage risk and treat creators fairly is not optional.

## Core Rules

### Provide Tools and Support That Enable Quality Creation

Creators and modders need tools, documentation, and support to produce quality content, and the team should provide robust creation tools (modding APIs, level editors, asset pipelines) and documentation, because quality creation requires quality tools, and poor tooling produces poor content. The decision rule: provide robust, documented creation tools, support the creator community, and avoid expecting quality content without quality tools. Poor-tooling expectation produces poor content, because the creators lacked the tools to create well.

### Manage Legal and IP Risk in Mod and UGC Content

Mod and UGC content carries legal risk — IP infringement (mods using copyrighted assets), liability for harmful content, brand association with problematic mods — and the team must manage this through clear terms, content policies, takedown processes, and disclaimers. The decision rule: establish clear mod and UGC terms, implement content policies and takedown processes, disclaim liability for mod content, and manage IP and harm risk. Unmanaged mod risk incurs liability, because the harmful or infringing content was not addressed.

### Curate or Signal Quality to Prevent Brand Dilution

If poor-quality mods or UGC are associated with the game, they can dilute the brand (players encountering poor content attribute it to the game), and the team should curate, highlight quality, or signal quality tiers so the game's association with UGC reflects well. The decision rule: curate or highlight quality UGC, signal quality (featured, verified), and avoid letting poor UGC dilute the brand undifferentiated. Undifferentiated UGC dilutes the brand, because the poor content was associated with the game without quality signaling.

### Treat Creators and Modders Fairly, Avoiding Exploitation

Creators and modders generate value for the game, and the team should treat them fairly — through revenue sharing, recognition, support, and respect for their labor — rather than benefiting from unpaid labor without fair treatment, because exploitation is both unethical and unsustainable (creators who feel exploited stop creating). The decision rule: treat creators fairly (revenue share where appropriate, recognition, support), avoid exploiting unpaid labor, and build sustainable creator relationships. Exploitative creator relationships are unsustainable, because the creators who felt exploited stopped contributing.

### Foster a Creator Community That Sustains Itself

Beyond individual creator support, the team should foster a creator community — where creators share, collaborate, and support each other — because a self-sustaining creator community produces more content and resilience than isolated creators dependent on the team. The decision rule: foster creator community (forums, events, collaboration tools), and avoid relying on isolated creators. Isolated-creator dependence is fragile, because the creators lacked the community to sustain them.

### Integrate Creator Content Without Breaking the Core Experience

Creator and mod content should integrate with the game without breaking the core experience — compatibility, balance, performance — through standards, testing, or sandboxing, because poorly-integrated content (incompatible, unbalanced, performance-breaking) harms the experience for players who encounter it. The decision rule: establish integration standards (compatibility, performance), test or sandbox creator content, and avoid content that breaks the core experience. Unintegrated content breaks the experience, because the compatibility and performance were not ensured.

## Common Traps

### Expecting Quality Content Without Quality Tools

The team expects creators to produce quality content but does not provide quality tools, and the content is poor because the tools were inadequate. The trap is that creation is the creators' job. The false signal is that the tools exist. The harm is that the creators lack the tools to create well, the content is poor, the UGC that should extend the game instead dilutes it, and the creator community cannot produce the quality that would benefit the game, because the tooling was inadequate.

### Unmanaged Legal and IP Risk in Mod Content

The team allows modding without managing the legal and IP risk, and infringing or harmful mod content exposes the game to liability. The trap is that modding extends the game. The false signal is that the community is creating. The harm is that the infringing mods (using copyrighted assets) incur IP liability, the harmful mods incur content liability, the brand is associated with problematic content, and the game incurs legal risk that modding policies would have managed, because the risk was not addressed.

### Undifferentiated UGC Diluting the Brand

The team allows UGC without quality signaling, and poor-quality content is associated with the game undifferentiated, diluting the brand. The trap is that all UGC is content. The false signal is that the game has lots of content. The harm is that players encounter poor UGC, attribute the poor quality to the game, the brand is diluted by the association, the quality UGC is buried among the poor, and the game's reputation suffers from UGC it did not curate, because the quality was not signaled.

### Exploitative Creator Relationships That Are Unsustainable

The team benefits from creator labor without fair treatment — no revenue share, no recognition, no support — and the creators feel exploited and stop contributing. The trap is that creator content is free. The false signal is that the community is contributing. The harm is that the creators generate value without fair treatment, the exploitation is both unethical and unsustainable, the creators who feel exploited stop creating, the creator community that sustained the game erodes, and the game loses the content it depended on, because the creators were not treated fairly.

### Isolated-Creator Dependence That Is Fragile

The team relies on isolated creators without fostering community, and the creators are fragile (lacking support, collaboration, resilience) and the content pipeline is brittle. The trap is that individual creators produce content. The false signal is that content is being created. The harm is that the isolated creators lack community support, they are more likely to burn out or leave, the content pipeline depends on individuals whose departure disrupts it, and the creator ecosystem is fragile, because the community was not fostered.

### Unintegrated Content Breaking the Experience

The team allows creator content without integration standards, and incompatible, unbalanced, or performance-breaking content harms the player experience. The trap is that the content is optional. The false signal is that players have more content. The harm is that the poorly-integrated content breaks compatibility, balance, or performance, the players who encounter it have a harmed experience, the content reflects poorly on the game, and the UGC that should extend the game instead degrades it, because the integration was not ensured.

## Self-Check

- Are creators provided robust, documented tools to produce quality content?
- Is legal and IP risk in mod and UGC content managed through terms, policies, takedowns, and disclaimers?
- Is UGC quality curated or signaled (featured, verified) to prevent brand dilution?
- Are creators treated fairly (revenue share, recognition, support), avoiding exploitation of unpaid labor?
- Is a self-sustaining creator community fostered, rather than relying on isolated creators?
- Does creator content integrate with standards (compatibility, performance) without breaking the core experience?
- Did I confirm creator and mod support extends the game sustainably without legal risk, dilution, or exploitation?
