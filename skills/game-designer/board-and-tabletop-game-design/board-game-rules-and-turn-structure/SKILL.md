# Board Game Rules and Turn Structure

Board game rules are the entire game — unlike a video game, there is no engine to enforce them, only the players' reading, memory, and good faith — and this makes rule design a judgment problem unlike digital design, where elegance, completeness, and teachability must all hold simultaneously. The judgment problem is that rules must be complete (covering every case) without being overcomplicated (so players can learn and remember them), must be unambiguous (so disputes do not arise) without being verbose, and must be elegant (so the system feels clean) without being shallow. Agents tend to miss this because the rules that are complete in the designer's head are often ambiguous or incomplete on the page, and because the elegance that feels right in solo design fails when taught to a real group with varying skill and attention. The harm is rulebooks players cannot parse, turns that stall in edge-case disputes, and systems burdened with exception memory that kills the joy of play. This skill covers how to design board game rules and turns that are elegant, complete, and teachable, and avoid the complexity, ambiguity, and edge-case traps. The agent has latitude in the rules, but the obligation to make rules learnable and complete is not optional.

## Core Rules

### Prioritize Elegance and Minimal Exception Memory

Board game rules should be elegant — achieving their depth with the fewest rules and exceptions — because every exception is memory the player must carry, and exception burden kills the joy of play as players stall to recall whether the exception applies. The decision rule: prioritize elegance, minimize exceptions, and consolidate similar rules into general principles. Overcomplicated rules burden players, because the exception memory exceeded the play capacity.

### Write Rules for Teachability and First-Read Comprehension

The rulebook is the only teacher most players will have, and it must be written for first-read comprehension — clear structure, examples for every rule, a teachable sequence — because a rulebook that cannot be parsed on first read stalls the game before it begins. The decision rule: write rules for teachability (clear structure, examples, teachable sequence), and avoid dense or assumed-knowledge prose. Dense rulebooks stall games, because the players could not parse the teaching.

### Resolve Timing, Priority, and Stack Interactions Explicitly

Board games with simultaneous or reactive effects create timing and priority questions (what resolves first, what interrupts), and these must be resolved explicitly — a clear timing structure, a priority system — because ambiguous timing creates disputes that stall the game and sour the experience. The decision rule: define timing and priority explicitly (a structure, a system), and avoid leaving timing to interpretation. Ambiguous timing creates disputes, because the resolution was not defined.

### Define Win Conditions and End States Unambiguously

The win condition and end state must be defined unambiguously — exactly when and how the game ends, exactly who has won — because ambiguous end conditions create disputes at the highest-stakes moment (the ending), and a disputed ending sours the entire experience. The decision rule: define win and end conditions unambiguously (exact triggers, exact comparison), and avoid loose end language. Ambiguous endings create disputes, because the conclusion was not exact.

### Playtest Rules for Edge Cases and Real-Group Comprehension

Rules must be playtested for edge cases (the interactions the designer did not foresee) and real-group comprehension (whether actual players, not the designer, can parse and apply them), because solo design cannot reveal the edge cases and comprehension gaps that real groups hit. The decision rule: playtest rules with fresh groups for edge cases and comprehension, and avoid assuming the designer's clarity transfers. Unplaytested rules fail at the table, because the edge cases and gaps were not found.

### Match Turn Structure to Desired Pacing and Downtime

Turn structure determines pacing and downtime, and it must match the desired experience — simultaneous turns for low downtime, structured turns for control, action-point systems for flexibility — because a turn structure that creates long downtime (one player acting while others wait) harms the experience for the waiting players. The decision rule: match turn structure to desired pacing and downtime, and avoid structures that create excessive downtime. High-downtime structures bore waiting players, because the turn design made them wait.

## Common Traps

### Overcomplicated Rules Burdening Players

The team designs rules with many exceptions and special cases, and the complexity burdens the players. The trap is that the exceptions add depth. The false signal is that the rules are complete. The harm is that the exception memory exceeds the players' capacity, the play stalls as players recall whether exceptions apply, the cognitive load crowds out the strategy, the joy of play is killed by rule lookup, and the game is remembered as a chore rather than a pleasure, because the rules were not elegant.

### Dense Rulebooks Stalling Games

The team writes a dense rulebook without teachable structure or examples, and the rulebook stalls the game. The trap is that the rules are complete. The false signal is that the rules are written. The harm is that the dense rulebook cannot be parsed on first read, the players stall before the game begins, the teaching fails, the group gives up or plays wrong, and the game is abandoned before it is experienced, because the rulebook was not teachable.

### Ambiguous Timing Creating Disputes

The team leaves timing and priority to interpretation, and the ambiguity creates disputes. The trap is that the timing is intuitive. The false signal is that the effects work. The harm is that the ambiguous timing creates disputes when effects interact, the players argue over what resolves first, the game stalls in the dispute, the experience is soured by the argument, and the game is remembered for the rules fight rather than the play, because the timing was not explicit.

### Ambiguous End Conditions Disputing Conclusions

The team defines win and end conditions loosely, and the ambiguity disputes the conclusion. The trap is that the end is clear. The false signal is that the game ends. The harm is that the ambiguous end condition creates a dispute at the highest-stakes moment, the players argue over who won or whether the game ended, the entire experience is soured by the disputed conclusion, the game's outcome is undermined, and the play is remembered for the ending dispute rather than the journey, because the end was not unambiguous.

### Unplaytested Rules Failing at the Table

The team ships rules without playtesting for edge cases and real-group comprehension, and the rules fail at the table. The trap is that the rules are clear to the designer. The false signal is that the rules work in solo design. The harm is that the unplaytested rules hit edge cases the designer did not foresee, the real-group comprehension gaps emerge, the players encounter broken interactions and unparsable rules, the game fails in the hands of real players, and the design that worked in the designer's head fails at the table, because the playtesting was absent.

### High-Downtime Structures Boring Waiting Players

The team designs a turn structure with long downtime, and the waiting players are bored. The trap is that the turns are deep. The false signal is that the turns are engaging. The harm is that the high-downtime structure makes players wait while one acts, the waiting players disengage, the pacing drags, the experience is boring for the majority at any moment, the game is remembered as slow and dull, and the players avoid the game because of the downtime, because the turn structure was not matched to pacing.

## Self-Check

- Are rules elegant with minimal exception memory, consolidating similar rules into general principles?
- Is the rulebook written for teachability and first-read comprehension with clear structure and examples?
- Are timing, priority, and stack interactions resolved explicitly rather than left to interpretation?
- Are win conditions and end states defined unambiguously with exact triggers and comparison?
- Have rules been playtested with fresh groups for edge cases and real comprehension?
- Does the turn structure match the desired pacing, avoiding excessive downtime for waiting players?
- Did I confirm the rules are elegant, complete, and teachable rather than overcomplicated and ambiguous?
