# Virtual Goods Design and Perceived Value

A virtual good's value is not its stats or its art; it is what the player believes it is worth, and that belief is constructed by scarcity, utility, expression, and social context. The judgment problem is that agents tend to design goods from the inside out (what does this item do) rather than from the player's perspective (why would anyone want this), producing inventories full of goods that are technically functional but emotionally worthless, and a few goods that are so desirable they distort the entire economy around themselves. Agents miss this because perceived value is contextual and emergent — the same item is priceless in one game's context and trash in another's — and because the team's own valuation is contaminated by their knowledge of the design intent. The harm is an economy where most goods are ignored, where the few desirable goods become the only thing anyone pursues, and where the reward loop fails because the rewards are not perceived as rewards. This skill covers how to construct perceived value, manage scarcity and rarity, and design goods the player wants for distinct reasons. The agent has latitude in the specific goods, but the obligation to design for perceived value rather than mere functionality is not optional.

## Core Rules

### Design Each Good to Satisfy a Distinct Player Motivation

Players acquire goods for different reasons — power (it makes me stronger), expression (it lets me look or play how I want), completion (it fills a set), status (it signals achievement), or utility (it saves time). The decision rule: for each good, name the primary motivation it serves and confirm it serves that motivation better than existing goods, rather than overlapping. Goods that serve no distinct motivation are filler; goods that serve a motivation already saturated by other goods are redundant. A portfolio where each good has a clear motivational role produces an economy where every acquisition feels purposeful.

### Use Scarcity as the Primary Lever of Perceived Value

Perceived value is governed more by scarcity than by intrinsic properties: an item available to everyone is worth less than an identical item held by few. The decision rule: calibrate each good's availability to the value you want it to carry — common goods are abundant and low-value, rare goods are scarce and high-value, prestige goods are extremely scarce and signal status — and resist the urge to make desirable goods widely available, because availability destroys the value. The most common error is democratizing a desirable good until it is worthless.

### Separate Cosmetic Value From Power Value to Avoid Pay-to-Win

Goods that grant power and goods that grant expression must be separated, because when a single good does both, the player who wants the expression is forced to engage with the power (and vice versa), and the economy distorts around the combined good. The decision rule: design power goods and cosmetic goods as distinct categories with distinct acquisition paths, so a player can pursue expression without altering balance and pursue power without being funneled through cosmetics. Conflating the two produces goods that are simultaneously mandatory (for power) and aspirational (for looks), which breaks both the economy and the balance.

### Make Rarity Tiers Meaningful, Not Just Labels

Rarity tiers (common, rare, epic, legendary) are only useful if they communicate real differences in scarcity, power, and desirability, so the player can read a good's value from its tier. The decision rule: ensure each rarity tier has a distinct scarcity rate, a distinct power band, and a distinct acquisition path, and that the tiers are consistent across the game. Inflated rarity (everything is "legendary") makes the label meaningless; inconsistent rarity (a common item stronger than a legendary) breaks the player's ability to value goods at a glance.

### Design Sets and Collections to Create Long-Term Acquisition Goals

Players value goods more highly when they contribute to a set or collection whose completion is itself a goal, because the set transforms individual acquisitions into progress toward a larger aspiration. The decision rule: group desirable goods into sets with completion bonuses or cosmetic unlocks, so acquiring one good increases the value of acquiring the others. Sets create retention pull that individual goods cannot, because the player is no longer acquiring items but completing an aspiration.

### Avoid the Single Must-Have Good That Distorts the Economy

When one good is so desirable that it overshadows all others, the economy distorts around it: every player pursues only that good, all other goods become worthless by comparison, and the reward loop collapses to a single target. The decision rule: scan the goods portfolio for any single good whose desirability dwarfs the others, and either rebalance it down, raise alternatives, or split its appeal across multiple goods. The must-have good converts a rich economy into a single-minded grind and trivializes every other reward.

## Common Traps

### Designing Goods for Function and Ignoring Perception

The team designs goods by their stats and mechanics — this sword does more damage, this armor has better defense — and ships an inventory of functionally-distinct but emotionally-flat goods that no one is excited to acquire. The trap is that functional design is concrete and reviewable. The false signal is that each good is balanced and distinct numerically. The harm is that players acquire goods as upgrades, not as desires, the acquisition carries no emotional weight, the reward loop feels like a stat treadmill, and the game whose economy was full of goods has an economy full of things no one wants, because value is perceived, not specified.

### Democratizing a Desirable Good Until It Is Worthless

A good is desirable because it is scarce, the team sees the desire and decides to make it more available (to be generous, to reduce frustration, to broaden appeal), and the good's perceived value collapses as it becomes common. The trap is that making a desired good available feels player-friendly. The false signal is positive feedback from players who wanted the good. The harm is that the good's value was its scarcity, availability destroys that value, the players who acquired it when scarce feel their achievement devalued, and the good that drove engagement becomes just another common item, because the team traded long-term value for short-term gratitude.

### Conflating Power and Cosmetics in a Single Good

A good is both the best-in-slot for power and the most desirable cosmetic, so every player must acquire it for mechanical reasons and wants it for expressive reasons, and the economy distorts around the combined good. The trap is that combining power and cosmetics into one desirable item feels efficient. The false signal is that the good is exciting. The harm is that the good becomes mandatory, players who lack it are both weaker and less fashionable, the acquisition path becomes a bottleneck and a source of resentment, and the economy that should offer varied goals collapses to a single pursuit, because one good was asked to serve two value systems that should have been separate.

### Rarity Inflation That Makes Tiers Meaningless

The team, wanting rewards to feel exciting, labels most rewards as high rarity — everything is epic or legendary — until the rarity tiers carry no information and the player cannot value a good from its label. The trap is that high-rarity labels feel rewarding in the moment. The false signal is that players react positively to "legendary" drops. The harm is that when everything is legendary, nothing is, the rarity system stops communicating scarcity, players cannot assess a good's value at a glance, and the reward that was meant to feel special feels routine, because the team spent the rarity signal on everything and it now signals nothing.

### The Must-Have Good That Collapses the Economy to One Goal

One good — a weapon, a mount, a cosmetic — is so far above the others in desirability that every player pursues only it, all other goods become stepping stones or vendor trash, and the rich economy the team built is experienced by players as a single-item grind. The trap is that a standout good feels like a strong aspirational anchor. The false signal is concentrated player desire and clear engagement metrics around the good. The harm is that the economy's variety is wasted, players disengage from every reward that is not the must-have, the reward loop narrows to one target, and when players finally acquire it they often quit, because there was nothing else they wanted and the single goal that organized their play is now complete.

## Self-Check

- For each good, have I named the distinct player motivation (power, expression, completion, status, utility) it serves, and confirmed it is not redundant with existing goods?
- Is each good's scarcity calibrated to the value I want it to carry, and have I resisted democratizing desirable goods until they are worthless?
- Are power goods and cosmetic goods designed as distinct categories with distinct acquisition paths, avoiding single goods that conflate both?
- Do my rarity tiers communicate real, consistent differences in scarcity, power, and desirability, so a player can value a good from its tier?
- Have I grouped desirable goods into sets or collections that create long-term completion goals beyond individual acquisitions?
- Did I scan the portfolio for any single must-have good whose desirability dwarfs the others, and rebalance to avoid collapsing the economy to one goal?
- Did I design goods for perceived value (scarcity, motivation, context), not merely for functional distinction?
