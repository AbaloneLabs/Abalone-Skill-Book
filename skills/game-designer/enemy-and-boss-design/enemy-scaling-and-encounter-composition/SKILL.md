# Enemy Scaling and Encounter Composition

Enemy scaling (tuning enemy stats for difficulty and progression) and encounter composition (mixing enemies into encounters) are the levers that determine whether combat stays varied and fair across the game's length or degrades into bullet-sponge padding, trivial encounters, and compositions that collapse to a single tactic. The judgment problem is that scaling must keep enemies relevant as the player grows in power (without inflating them into sponges or deflating them into triviality), and composition must combine enemies for varied tactics (without overwhelming the player or collapsing to one response). Agents tend to miss this because scaling that looks right on a spreadsheet (numbers increase steadily) produces sponges in play (enemies take too many hits), and because compositions that look varied (many enemy types) can collapse if the types do not force varied responses. The harm is combat that pads playtime with sponges, that trivializes through under-scaling, or that collapses to spamming one tactic regardless of composition. This skill covers how to scale enemies for fair progression and compose encounters for varied tactics. The agent has latitude in the tuning, but the obligation to keep combat varied and fair across the game is not optional.

## Core Rules

### Scale Enemy Stats to Maintain Time-to-Kill, Not to Inflate Numbers

Enemy scaling should maintain a consistent feel for time-to-kill (how long an enemy takes to defeat) relative to the player's growing power, rather than simply inflating enemy stats, because inflation produces bullet sponges that pad playtime without adding engagement. The decision rule: scale enemy stats to maintain the intended time-to-kill as the player's power grows, and avoid inflating stats beyond what maintains the feel. Stat inflation produces sponges, because the numbers increased without regard for the time-to-kill feel they produce.

### Compose Encounters to Force Varied Tactical Responses

Encounter composition should mix enemies that force different responses — so the player must vary tactics across the encounter — rather than mixing enemies that all succumb to the same response, which collapses the encounter to that one tactic. The decision rule: for each encounter, select enemies that force varied responses (a shield, a sniper, a swarm), confirm the mix requires tactical variety, and avoid compositions that collapse to a single approach. Compositions that collapse to one tactic waste the variety the roster could provide, because the mix did not force varied responses.

### Scale Encounter Difficulty With Player Progression, Not Just Enemy Stats

Encounter difficulty should scale with the player's progression through composition and complexity (more enemies, more complex mixes, more demanding tactics), not only through enemy stat inflation, because composition scaling produces varied challenge while stat scaling produces sponges. The decision rule: scale encounter difficulty primarily through composition and complexity as the player progresses, and use stat scaling only to fine-tune within a composition. Stat-only scaling produces sponges, because the challenge increased through numbers rather than through tactical variety.

### Avoid Overwhelming Composition That Is Unfair

Encounter composition must avoid overwhelming the player — too many enemies, too many simultaneous demands, no space to respond — because overwhelming encounters are unfair and produce frustration rather than challenge. The decision rule: for each encounter, confirm the composition is fair (the player can perceive and respond to the demands), and avoid compositions that exceed the player's capacity to handle. Overwhelming compositions are unfair, because the demands exceeded the player's ability to respond.

### Ensure Trivial Encounters Are Brief, Not Padded

Some encounters will be trivial relative to the player's power (early enemies revisited, weak filler), and these should be brief — quick, satisfying demonstrations of the player's growth — rather than padded into long trivial encounters that waste time. The decision rule: keep trivial encounters brief, use them to demonstrate player growth, and avoid padding them into long trivial slogs. Padded trivial encounters waste time, because the brevity that would make them satisfying was extended into tedium.

### Validate Scaling and Composition Through Play, Not Spreadsheets

Enemy scaling and encounter composition must be validated through play, because the feel of combat (time-to-kill, fairness, variety) is not captured by spreadsheets and only emerges in the actual play experience. The decision rule: playtest encounters across the progression, confirm scaling feels right and compositions are varied and fair, and adjust based on play rather than on numbers. Spreadsheet-validated scaling ships sponges and collapsed compositions, because the feel the spreadsheet cannot capture was not checked.

## Common Traps

### Stat Inflation Producing Bullet Sponges

The team scales enemy difficulty by inflating stats — more health, more damage — to match the player's growing power, and the enemies become bullet sponges that pad playtime without adding engagement. The trap is that stat inflation is simple to tune. The false signal is that enemies scale with the player. The harm is that the enemies take too many hits to defeat, the combat feels like padding rather than challenge, the engagement that varied combat would sustain is replaced by tedious sponge-grinding, and the playtime is extended without enjoyment, because the scaling inflated numbers rather than maintaining time-to-kill feel.

### Compositions That Collapse to a Single Tactic

The team composes encounters from enemy mixes, but the enemies in each mix all succumb to the same response, and the encounter collapses to spamming that tactic. The trap is that the mix has many enemies. The false signal is that the encounter has variety. The harm is that the composition does not force varied responses, the player applies one tactic to the whole mix, the variety the roster could provide is wasted, and the encounter is monotonous rather than tactically varied, because the composition did not combine enemies that force different responses.

### Stat-Only Scaling Without Composition Variety

The team scales encounter difficulty only through enemy stats, without varying composition or complexity, and the encounters increase in sponge-density without increasing in tactical variety. The trap is that stat scaling is simpler than composition design. The false signal is that encounters get harder. The harm is that the challenge increases through numbers rather than tactics, the enemies become sponges, the variety that composition scaling would provide is absent, and the combat becomes progressively more tedious rather than progressively more varied, because the difficulty scaled through stats alone.

### Overwhelming Composition That Is Unfair

The team composes encounters with too many enemies or too many simultaneous demands, exceeding the player's capacity to respond, and the encounter is unfair. The trap is that many enemies feel challenging. The false signal is that the encounter is hard. The harm is that the player cannot perceive or respond to all the demands, the encounter is unfair rather than challenging, the frustration of unanswerable threats accumulates, and the player churns at encounters that exceeded their capacity, because the composition was not checked for fairness.

### Padded Trivial Encounters That Waste Time

The team extends trivial encounters — early enemies, weak filler — into long slogs, rather than keeping them brief, and the padding wastes the player's time. The trap is that longer encounters feel substantial. The false signal is that the encounter has content. The harm is that the trivial encounter is padded beyond its satisfying brevity, the player's time is wasted on non-engaging combat, the demonstration of player growth that a brief trivial encounter would provide is lost in tedium, and the pacing is damaged, because the trivial encounter was not kept brief.

### Spreadsheet-Validated Scaling That Ships Sponges

The team validates enemy scaling on spreadsheets — numbers increase steadily, stats look balanced — without playtesting the feel, and ships sponges and collapsed compositions that the spreadsheet said were fine. The trap is that spreadsheets are precise. The false signal is that the scaling is tuned. The harm is that the feel of combat — time-to-kill, fairness, variety — is not captured by the spreadsheet, the sponges and collapsed compositions that emerge in play were invisible on paper, and the scaling that looked right ships wrong, because the validation did not include play.

## Self-Check

- Am I scaling enemy stats to maintain consistent time-to-kill feel, not inflating numbers into bullet sponges?
- Do encounter compositions mix enemies that force varied tactical responses, rather than collapsing to one tactic?
- Am I scaling difficulty through composition and complexity as the player progresses, not just through stat inflation?
- Are compositions checked for fairness, avoiding overwhelming the player with too many simultaneous demands?
- Are trivial encounters kept brief to demonstrate player growth, rather than padded into long slogs?
- Have I validated scaling and composition through play, not just spreadsheets, checking the feel of combat?
- Did I confirm combat stays varied and fair across the game's progression, without sponge-padding or collapse?
