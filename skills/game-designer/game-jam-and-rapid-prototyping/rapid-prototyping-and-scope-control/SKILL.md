# Rapid Prototyping and Scope Control

Rapid prototyping — building a playable test of a core idea under severe time constraints (game jams, internal prototypes) — is a discipline of ruthless prioritization, because the time is so short that every hour spent on non-essential systems, content, or polish is an hour stolen from reaching and validating the core fun, and most prototypes fail by over-scoping into unfinished demos that prove nothing. The judgment problem is that the prototype must reach the core fun (the thing worth testing) before time runs out, must scope ruthlessly to serve that core (cutting everything that does not serve it), and must be playable and complete enough to validate the idea (not an unfinished mess that proves nothing). Agents tend to miss this because the scope that feels ambitious (many systems, rich content) is exactly the scope that prevents reaching the core, and because the team's excitement drives scope expansion that the time cannot support. The harm is prototypes that run out of time before the core fun, that ship as unfinished demos, or that fail to validate the idea they were built to test. This skill covers how to prototype rapidly with ruthless scope control, and avoid the over-scoping, core-missing, and unfinished traps. The agent has latitude in the prototype, but the obligation to reach and validate the core is not optional.

## Core Rules

### Identify the Core Hypothesis and Prototype Only That

The first task is to identify the core hypothesis — the one thing the prototype exists to test (is this mechanic fun? does this loop engage?) — and prototype only that, because the prototype's purpose is to validate the hypothesis, and everything that does not serve the hypothesis is scope that steals time from it. The decision rule: identify the core hypothesis, prototype only what tests it, and cut everything else. Hypothesis-drift prototypes waste time, because the scope left the hypothesis.

### Scope Ruthlessly to Reach the Core Fun in Time

Scope must be controlled ruthlessly — cutting systems, content, and polish that do not serve the core — so the team reaches the core fun before time runs out, because the time is so short that any non-essential scope prevents reaching the core, and an unreached core proves nothing. The decision rule: scope ruthlessly to the core, cut aggressively, and avoid scope creep. Scope creep prevents reaching the core, because the time was spent on non-essential scope.

### Use Shortcuts, Placeholders, and Tools Aggressively

Rapid prototyping should use shortcuts, placeholders, and tools aggressively — greybox art, placeholder audio, existing engines and frameworks — because building custom assets and systems from scratch steals time from the core, and placeholders let the team reach playable fast. The decision rule: use shortcuts, placeholders, and tools aggressively, and avoid building from scratch what a tool provides. From-scratch building wastes time, because the custom work did not serve the core.

### Reach Playable Fast and Iterate From There

The prototype should reach playable (the core loop functioning, even rough) as fast as possible, then iterate from there, because a playable prototype can be tested and improved while an unplayable one cannot, and the iteration from playable is where the core fun is found and refined. The decision rule: reach playable fast, iterate from there, and avoid long pre-playable development. Long pre-playable development delays testing, because the prototype could not be played.

### Ensure the Prototype Is Complete Enough to Validate

The prototype must be complete enough to validate the hypothesis — the core fun must be reachable and testable, the experience must be sufficient to judge — because an incomplete prototype (core not reached, experience too rough) proves nothing, and the validation fails for reasons other than the idea's quality. The decision rule: ensure the prototype reaches and tests the core, and avoid incomplete prototypes. Incomplete prototypes prove nothing, because the core was not testable.

### Cut Features Relentlessly When Time Is Short

When time is short (and it always is), features must be cut relentlessly — anything not essential to the core is cut — because shipping a small complete prototype that validates the core is far better than shipping a large incomplete one that proves nothing, and the courage to cut is what enables completion. The decision rule: cut features relentlessly when time is short, prioritize completion of the core, and avoid feature commitment beyond time. Feature commitment beyond time prevents completion, because the scope exceeded the time.

## Common Traps

### Hypothesis-Drift Prototypes Wasting Time

The team lets the prototype drift from the core hypothesis, and the time is wasted. The trap is that the additional scope is interesting. The false signal is that the prototype is richer. The harm is that the hypothesis-drift prototype adds scope that does not serve the core, the time is spent on systems and content that do not test the hypothesis, the core is reached late or never, the validation fails because the hypothesis was not tested, and the prototype proves nothing, because the scope left the hypothesis.

### Scope Creep Preventing Reaching the Core

The team allows scope to creep, and the core is never reached. The trap is that the scope is ambitious. The false signal is that the prototype is full-featured. The harm is that the scope creep adds systems and content beyond the time, the time is consumed by non-essential scope, the core fun is reached late or never, the prototype runs out of time before the core, the validation fails, and the ambitious prototype ships as an unfinished demo, because the scope was not controlled.

### From-Scratch Building Wasting Time

The team builds assets and systems from scratch instead of using shortcuts, and the time is wasted. The trap is that the custom work is better. The false signal is that the assets are original. The harm is that the from-scratch building steals time from the core, the custom assets and systems do not serve the hypothesis, the playable is delayed by unnecessary production, the core fun is reached late, and the prototype suffers for the production pride, because the shortcuts were not used.

### Long Pre-Playable Development Delaying Testing

The team spends too long before reaching playable, and the testing is delayed. The trap is that the systems need to be ready. The false signal is that the development is thorough. The harm is that the long pre-playable development delays the playable, the prototype cannot be tested until late, the iteration from playable (where the core fun is found) is compressed or absent, the core problems are discovered too late to fix, and the prototype ships without the iteration it needed, because the playable was delayed.

### Incomplete Prototypes Proving Nothing

The team ships an incomplete prototype, and the validation proves nothing. The trap is that the prototype is mostly done. The false signal is that the core is implemented. The harm is that the incomplete prototype does not reach the core fun, the experience is too rough to judge, the validation fails for reasons other than the idea's quality (was the idea bad, or was the prototype just incomplete?), the team cannot distinguish a bad idea from an unfinished prototype, and the prototype fails its purpose, because the core was not testable.

### Feature Commitment Beyond Time Preventing Completion

The team commits to features beyond the time, and the completion fails. The trap is that the features are planned. The false signal is that the scope is defined. The harm is that the feature commitment exceeds the time, the team cannot cut the committed features, the prototype runs out of time with features unfinished, the core is buried under incomplete scope, the prototype ships as a mess, and the courage to cut (which would have enabled completion) was absent, because the commitment was beyond time.

## Self-Check

- Is the core hypothesis identified, and is the prototype scoped to test only that?
- Is scope controlled ruthlessly, cutting systems, content, and polish that do not serve the core?
- Are shortcuts, placeholders, and tools used aggressively instead of building from scratch?
- Does the prototype reach playable fast, then iterate from the playable state?
- Is the prototype complete enough to validate the hypothesis (core reachable and testable)?
- Are features cut relentlessly when time is short, prioritizing completion of the core over scope?
- Did I confirm the prototype reaches and validates the core fun rather than over-scoping into an unfinished demo?
