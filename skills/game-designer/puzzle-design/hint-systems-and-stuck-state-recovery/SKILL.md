# Hint Systems and Stuck State Recovery

Every puzzle game will eventually confront a player who is stuck — who has exhausted their current understanding and cannot find the next step — and the hint system is the design's response to this inevitability. The judgment problem is that help exists on a knife edge: too little and the player quits in frustration, too much and the puzzle is solved for them, destroying the satisfaction they came for. Designers tend to miss this because they treat hints as a feature to be added late — a signpost here, a skip button there — rather than as a core system that must be designed with the same care as the puzzles themselves. The harm of getting it wrong runs in both directions and both are severe. A hint system that is too aggressive or too visible trains the player to reach for help at the first friction, so they never sit with the productive struggle that produces insight, and they complete the game feeling they were carried rather than clever. A hint system that is too sparse or too punishing to access leaves stuck players to churn, convinced the game is unfair, and they never see the content they paid for. This skill covers the design of progressive, agency-preserving hint systems, the detection of stuck states, and the recovery paths that return a player to productive play without stealing their victory. The agent has latitude in the flavor and delivery of hints, but the obligation is to ensure help is available when genuinely needed, calibrated to give the smallest useful nudge, and structured so that the player who uses it still feels they solved the puzzle.

## Core Rules

### Deliver Hints Progressively, From Smallest Nudge to Largest

The cardinal principle of hint design is that the first hint should be the smallest possible nudge that could unstick the player, and each subsequent hint should escalate only if the previous one was insufficient. The progression typically runs: a general redirect (you are looking in the wrong area), a specific redirect (examine the object you overlooked), a mechanical reminder (remember that water conducts electricity), a partial solution (the first step is to redirect the flow), and only as a last resort the full solution. The reason for this escalation is that each hint the player does not need is a piece of satisfaction stolen from them, and the player who would have solved the puzzle from a gentle nudge but receives the full answer has been robbed of their insight. The discipline is to design at least three tiers of hint for every puzzle, each independently useful, and to gate them so the player must request or wait for each tier rather than receiving them all at once. The decision criterion: for each hint tier, could a player who receives only that tier still solve the puzzle through their own reasoning? If a tier hands them the answer, it is not a hint; it is a solution, and it belongs only at the final tier.

### Make Hints Opt-In, Not Pushed, Unless the Player Is Genuinely Stuck

A hint that appears unprompted — a glowing object, an NPC interjection, a UI popup — interrupts the player who is still reasoning productively and steals their struggle before it bears fruit. The strong default is that hints are player-requested: the player must take an action (examine a hint object, open a hint menu, consult a journal) to receive help, which ensures help is given only to those who want it. The exception is the stuck-state detection system that, after evidence of genuine stuckness (long idle time, repeated failed interactions, revisiting the same area), offers a gentle prompt — not a hint itself, but an offer of a hint. The discipline is to separate the offer ("You seem stuck. Would you like a hint?") from the hint content, so the player can decline and continue struggling without the actual hint being revealed. The decision criterion: is the player ever shown hint content without explicitly requesting it or accepting an offer? If so, the system is pushing hints and is stealing struggle from players who did not want help.

### Detect Stuck States Through Behavior, Not Time Alone

A stuck-state detection system infers that a player needs help from their behavior, and it must be calibrated carefully to avoid both false positives (offering help to a player who is thinking, not stuck) and false negatives (failing to help a player who has given up). Time alone is a weak signal: a player may be thinking, taking notes, or away from the keyboard. Stronger signals include repeated identical failed interactions (trying the same wrong approach many times), cycling between areas without progress, long periods with no input combined with camera movement suggesting searching, and abandoning the puzzle area entirely. The discipline is to combine multiple weak signals into a confident stuck inference before intervening, and to make the intervention an offer rather than an imposition. The decision criterion: what behaviors indicate genuine stuckness versus productive thinking or absence, and does my system require multiple converging signals before it acts? A system that triggers on a single timer will harass thinking players.

### Preserve the Player's Sense of Authorship Even When Helped

The deepest goal of hint design is that the player who used a hint still feels they solved the puzzle, because the hint pointed them toward the reasoning rather than doing the reasoning for them. This requires that hints illuminate the path (here is what you overlooked, here is the rule you forgot) rather than narrate the destination (do this, then this, then this). A hint that tells the player what to do produces compliance; a hint that reframes the problem produces insight. The discipline is to write hints as questions or perspective shifts — "Have you considered why that door is locked from this side?" — rather than instructions. The decision criterion: after receiving this hint, does the player still have to reason to reach the solution, or has the hint done the reasoning for them? If the latter, the hint is too strong and should be weakened to a nudge that requires the player to complete the logic.

### Provide a Dignified Skip Path for the Player Who Has Exhausted Patience

Some players will reach a puzzle they cannot or will not solve, and the design must offer a path forward that does not punish them for their limitation. A skip mechanic — allowing the player to bypass the puzzle and continue the game — is not a failure of design; it is an accessibility feature that respects the diversity of player skill, patience, and circumstance. The discipline is to make the skip available but not frictionless: require the player to attempt the puzzle, to request the skip explicitly, and to acknowledge the consequence (the puzzle is marked as skipped, the solution may be shown). This friction ensures the skip is used by players who need it, not by players who could solve the puzzle with a little more effort. The decision criterion: is there a point beyond which a stuck player can continue the game without solving this puzzle, and does that path require deliberate choice rather than being the path of least resistance?

### Avoid the Hint Death Spiral Where Hints Become the Default Strategy

When hints are too easy to access or too generous, players learn to request them at the first sign of friction, and the game devolves into a sequence of hint-reading rather than puzzle-solving. This is the hint death spiral: the player stops struggling, stops developing the reasoning skills the puzzles are meant to build, and becomes dependent on hints for every subsequent puzzle, because they never built the mastery that would make hints unnecessary. The discipline is to build friction into hint access (a cooldown, a cost in currency or score, a requirement to demonstrate effort first) and to make early hints weak enough that the player is encouraged to try reasoning before escalating. The decision criterion: is there any cost or friction to requesting a hint that discourages premature use, and are the early tiers weak enough that struggling remains more rewarding than hint-seeking for the player who can eventually solve it?

### Ensure Hints Reference the Player's Current State, Not a Generic Solution

A hint that assumes the player is at the beginning of the puzzle is useless to a player who is stuck at step four, and a hint that assumes the player has made a particular wrong inference may confuse a player who made a different one. The strongest hint systems are state-aware: they track what the player has already done, what they have examined, and where they appear to be in the solution, and they deliver a hint targeted to the player's actual stuck point. The discipline is to map each puzzle's solution into states and to write hints for each state, so that the hint a stuck player receives addresses their specific confusion rather than restating the puzzle's opening. The decision criterion: does the hint system know where in the puzzle the player is, and does the hint it delivers address that location in the solution space? If hints are generic regardless of player state, they will be unhelpful to players stuck past the opening and will be ignored.

## Common Traps

### The Hint That Solves the Puzzle for the Player

A hint is written to be helpful, and in the effort to be clear, it ends up narrating the entire solution: do this, then this, then this, and the puzzle is done. The trap is that the hint's clarity is its failure, because a clear solution is not a hint; it is an answer, and the player who receives it has not solved the puzzle — they have followed instructions. The false signal is that "the player progressed," which looks like success. The harm is that the player feels no accomplishment, the puzzle's intended insight is bypassed, and the player is trained to expect solutions rather than nudges, eroding their willingness to struggle on subsequent puzzles.

### Pushed Hints That Interrupt Productive Struggle

The game detects that the player has been on a puzzle for a while and pushes a hint — a popup, an NPC comment, a highlighted object — without the player requesting help. The trap is that the player may have been on the verge of the insight, and the pushed hint arrives seconds before they would have reached it themselves, stealing the satisfaction of the discovery and replacing it with the mild shame of having needed help. The false signal is that "the player was stuck and we helped." The harm is that productive struggle is punished, the player learns that thinking hard triggers unwanted intervention, and they begin to avoid deep engagement to prevent the interruptions.

### The Stuck-State Detector That Harasses Thinking Players

The stuck-state detection triggers on a timer or a single weak signal, and offers help to a player who is thinking, taking notes, or simply distracted. The trap is that the false positive irritates the player and breaks their concentration, and repeated false positives train the player to dismiss or disable the hint system entirely, so that when they are genuinely stuck, the system they have learned to ignore cannot help them. The false signal is that "we are being attentive to player needs." The harm is a hint system that is tuned out by the players who might need it, because it cried wolf too many times.

### The Skip With No Friction That Becomes the Path of Least Resistance

A skip button is available at all times with no cost, no requirement to attempt the puzzle, and no consequence. The trap is that the path of least resistance is to skip every puzzle that requires more than momentary thought, and players will take that path not because they cannot solve the puzzles but because skipping is easier than struggling. The false signal is that "players are progressing through the game." The harm is that the player bypasses the core content, experiences the game as a shallow walk-through, and concludes the game has no depth — because they never engaged with the depth that existed behind the skips they took reflexively.

### Generic Hints That Ignore Where the Player Actually Is

The hint system delivers the same hint regardless of the player's progress, so a player stuck at the final step receives the hint for the first step, which they already completed. The trap is that the hint is technically correct but useless, because it does not address the player's actual confusion, and the player concludes the hint system is broken and stops using it. The false signal is that "we provided a hint." The harm is that the hint system fails precisely when it is needed most — for the player deep in a puzzle who needs targeted help — and those players churn because no help is available for their specific stuck point.

### Hints That Reveal Information the Player Had Not Discovered

A hint references a clue, object, or mechanic the player has not yet found, prematurely revealing content that was meant to be discovered through exploration. The trap is that the hint, in trying to be helpful, spoils the discovery and collapses the exploration the game was built around. The false signal is that "the hint is accurate." The harm is that the player is robbed of the discovery moment, the game's pacing is disrupted, and the player learns that hints will spoil content, making them reluctant to use hints even when genuinely stuck for fear of further spoilers.

## Self-Check

- For each puzzle, are there at least three tiers of hint escalating from the smallest useful nudge to the full solution, and is each tier independently useful so a player receiving only an early tier can still reason to the answer?
- Are hints opt-in by default, with hint content shown only after explicit player request or acceptance of an offer, so productive struggle is never interrupted by pushed help?
- Does the stuck-state detector require multiple converging behavioral signals (not time alone) before intervening, and is the intervention an offer rather than an imposition of hint content?
- Do hints illuminate the reasoning path through questions and perspective shifts rather than narrating the solution, so the player who uses a hint still feels they solved the puzzle through their own logic?
- Is there a dignified skip path with deliberate friction (attempt requirement, explicit choice, acknowledged consequence) for players who have exhausted patience, so no one is permanently walled off from the game's content?
- Is there friction or cost to hint access that prevents the hint death spiral, where players reach for help at the first friction and never develop the reasoning mastery that would make hints unnecessary?
- Are hints state-aware, referencing the player's actual progress and stuck point rather than restating the puzzle's opening, and do they avoid revealing content the player has not yet discovered?
