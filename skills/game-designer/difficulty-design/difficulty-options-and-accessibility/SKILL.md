# Difficulty Options and Accessibility

Difficulty options are the most direct way to broaden a game's audience, letting each player find the challenge that fits their skill, time, and ability — but they are also easy to implement poorly in ways that defeat their purpose. The judgment problem is that difficulty options must serve a wide range of needs (skill variance, disability, time scarcity, mood) without stigmatizing the players who use them, without fragmenting the experience into "real" and "easy" tracks, and without being so coarse that they cannot fit individual needs. Agents tend to miss this because the options look sufficient in a menu (Easy, Normal, Hard) while failing the players whose needs do not map to those buckets, and because the framing of options (story mode, casual, git gud) carries social meaning that affects who feels permitted to use them. The harm is options that exist but are not used by the players who need them, or that carry stigma, or that are too coarse to help. This skill covers how to design difficulty options that genuinely broaden access, structure them for individual fit, and avoid stigma and fragmentation. The agent has latitude in the options offered, but the obligation to make them serve the players who need them is not optional.

## Core Rules

### Offer Granular Options, Not Just Coarse Tiers

Coarse tiers (Easy, Normal, Hard) assume the player's needs are uniform across all dimensions, but a player may need easier combat while wanting full puzzle challenge, or more time on reactions while wanting full enemy aggression. The decision rule: offer granular options that let the player adjust individual dimensions (enemy damage, reaction windows, resource costs, fail-state severity) rather than only packaged tiers, so each player can fit the game to their specific need. Coarse tiers serve the player whose needs happen to match a package; granular options serve everyone.

### Frame Options Without Stigma So Players Feel Permitted to Use Them

The language and framing of difficulty options affects whether players feel permitted to use them: "Story Mode" invites; "Baby Mode" stigmatizes; "Casual" may feel dismissive. The decision rule: frame every option as a valid choice that serves a legitimate play preference, use neutral or positive language, and avoid framing lower difficulty as a lesser or shameful choice. Stigmatizing framing prevents the players who need the options from using them, because the social cost of selecting the stigmatized option exceeds the benefit, and the option exists but is not used.

### Ensure Each Option Genuinely Fits Its Intended Audience

A difficulty option must actually produce the experience its audience needs — the "accessibility" setting must genuinely make the game accessible to the player who needs it, not just nominally easier. The decision rule: for each option, define the audience it serves, test with that audience, and confirm the option produces a playable experience for them. Options that are labeled for an audience but do not fit that audience's needs (an "accessibility" mode that is still too hard for the disabled players it was named for) betray the players who trusted the label.

### Avoid Fragmenting the Experience Into "Real" and "Easy" Tracks

When difficulty options produce materially different experiences, the game fragments into a "real" version (the intended hard path) and a "lesser" version (the easy path), and players on the easy path feel they are not really playing the game. The decision rule: ensure all difficulty paths deliver the core experience and fantasy, differing in challenge demand but not in content, narrative, or legitimacy, so no path is the "real" one. Fragmented tracks tell players on the easier path that their experience is lesser, which undermines the engagement the option was meant to enable.

### Let Players Change Difficulty Mid-Game Without Penalty

Players' needs change — a hard section frustrates, a session's energy is low, a disability flares — and the option to change difficulty must be available mid-game without penalty (locked achievements, flagged saves, condescending prompts) so the player can adapt the game to their current need. The decision rule: allow mid-game difficulty changes without mechanical or social penalty, and avoid prompts that shame the player for choosing easier. Penalized difficulty changes trap players in a challenge that no longer fits, because the cost of changing exceeds the cost of struggling.

### Validate Options With the Players Who Need Them

Difficulty and accessibility options must be tested with the players they are meant to serve — disabled players for accessibility options, time-constrained players for story modes, lower-skill players for easy tiers — because the team cannot evaluate whether the option fits an audience they are not part of. The decision rule: test each option with representative players from its target audience, and revise based on whether the option genuinely served them. Options validated only by the team ship without serving the audience they were built for, because the team's needs and abilities do not match the audience's.

## Common Traps

### Coarse Tiers That Cannot Fit Individual Needs

The team offers only Easy, Normal, and Hard, and a player who needs easier combat but full puzzle challenge, or more reaction time but full aggression, cannot find a fit, because the tiers bundle all dimensions together. The trap is that coarse tiers are simple to implement and communicate. The false signal is that three options cover the spectrum. The harm is that the player whose needs cut across the tiers has no good option, they pick the least-bad tier and suffer the dimensions it mismatches, and the option that would have served them was never offered, because the team assumed uniform needs that the player population does not have.

### Stigmatizing Framing That Prevents Use

The team frames the lower difficulty with stigmatizing language — "Casual," "Story Mode," or worse — and players who need the option avoid it to escape the stigma, suffering through a difficulty that does not fit. The trap is that the framing reflects the team's hierarchy of play. The false signal is that the options are available. The harm is that the players who need the easier option will not select it because the social cost exceeds the benefit, the option exists but is underused by exactly the audience it was meant to serve, and the retention the option was meant to enable does not occur, because the framing gated the option behind a stigma.

### The Accessibility Option That Does Not Actually Fit

The team adds an "accessibility" or "assist" mode labeled for disabled players, but the mode is only nominally easier and does not actually make the game playable for the disabled players it was named for, because it was not tested with them. The trap is that the option exists and is marketed. The false signal is that the game has accessibility features. The harm is that disabled players, trusting the label, find the game still unplayable, the option that was meant to include them instead betrays them, and the game earns credit for accessibility it did not actually provide, because the option was not validated against the audience it claimed to serve.

### Fragmented Tracks Where Easy Is the "Lesser" Game

The difficulty options produce materially different experiences — the easy path has less content, simpler encounters, a reduced narrative — and players on the easy path feel they are playing a lesser version, not the real game. The trap is that the easy path was built with less investment. The false signal is that the options offer variety. The harm is that players on the easier path are told through the content itself that their experience is lesser, their engagement is undermined by the knowledge they are not playing the "real" game, and the option that was meant to broaden access instead creates a hierarchy that devalues the players it included.

### Penalized Mid-Game Changes That Trap Players

The team penalizes mid-game difficulty changes — locked achievements, flagged saves, condescending "are you sure?" prompts — to preserve the integrity of the default difficulty, and players who need to change are trapped by the penalty. The trap is that penalties protect the default difficulty's meaning. The false signal is that the default difficulty retains its prestige. The harm is that players whose needs change — frustration, fatigue, a disability flare — cannot adapt without cost, they struggle through a difficulty that no longer fits, and they churn rather than pay the penalty to change, because the option to adapt was gated behind a punishment.

### Validating Options Only With the Team

The team evaluates the difficulty and accessibility options themselves, concludes they are sufficient, and ships, but the team's skills and abilities do not match the audiences the options are meant to serve. The trap is that the team is the available test pool. The false signal is that the options pass internal review. The harm is that the options do not fit the audiences they were built for, those players find the game unplayable despite the options, and the accessibility that was claimed is not delivered, because the options were never tested by the players who needed them.

## Self-Check

- Do I offer granular options for individual dimensions (damage, reaction windows, fail-state severity), not just coarse packaged tiers?
- Is every option framed with neutral or positive language that avoids stigmatizing the players who use it?
- Has each option been tested with its target audience and confirmed to genuinely fit their needs?
- Do all difficulty paths deliver the core experience and fantasy, without fragmenting into "real" and "lesser" tracks?
- Can players change difficulty mid-game without mechanical or social penalty?
- Did I test options with representative players from each option's target audience, not only with the team?
- Did I avoid stigmatizing framing that prevents the players who need the options from using them?
