# Item Rarity and Stat Scaling

Item rarity systems (common, rare, epic, legendary) and stat scaling (how item power grows across progression) are the backbone of loot-driven games, and they are also where the item economy most commonly breaks — through power creep, stat inflation, loot fatigue, and devalued economies. The judgment problem is that rarity must convey meaning (a legendary should feel legendary), scaling must sustain excitement across the game (each tier should feel like an upgrade), and the loot economy must avoid the inflation that devalues all items. Agents tend to miss this because a rarity system that looks clear (color tiers) can be meaningless in practice (legendaries drop constantly), and because stat scaling that looks steady on a curve can produce power creep that devalues earlier items. The harm is items that do not feel special (because rarity is inflated), progression that loses excitement (because upgrades are trivial), and economies that collapse (because inflation devalues everything). This skill covers how to design rarity and scaling that sustain meaning and excitement, and avoid the creep and fatigue traps. The agent has latitude in the system, but the obligation to preserve item value across the game is not optional.

## Core Rules

### Make Rarity Tiers Meaningful Through Scarcity and Distinction

Rarity tiers must be meaningful — each tier scarcer than the last, each tier qualitatively distinct (not just higher stats but unique effects or properties) — so a higher-rarity item feels special, and the meaning is preserved through scarcity and distinction rather than inflated away. The decision rule: for each rarity tier, define the scarcity (drop rate) and the distinction (unique properties beyond stats), and avoid inflating drop rates or making higher tiers merely higher-stat versions. Inflated rarity is meaningless, because the scarcity and distinction that would make it special were spent.

### Scale Item Stats to Maintain Upgrade Excitement Across Progression

Item stat scaling must maintain upgrade excitement across the progression — each new tier should feel like a meaningful upgrade, the power curve should make upgrades satisfying — without inflating so fast that earlier items become instantly worthless or so slow that upgrades feel trivial. The decision rule: calibrate the stat scaling curve so each tier is a satisfying upgrade while earlier items retain residual value, and avoid curves that inflate (devaluing earlier items) or flatten (trivializing upgrades). Mis-calibrated scaling either devalues earlier items or trivializes upgrades, because the curve did not sustain excitement.

### Avoid Power Creep That Devalues Existing Items

Over the game's life or across content updates, the temptation to add more powerful items produces power creep — each new item outclasses the last — and power creep devalues existing items, making the player's collection worthless and requiring constant escalation. The decision rule: establish power ceilings, resist adding items that outclass existing ones without reason, and plan the long-term power trajectory to avoid creep. Power creep devalues the economy, because each addition made the previous worthless.

### Design Loot Tables to Sustain Excitement, Not Fatigue

Loot tables and drop rates must sustain excitement — the player should feel that drops are rewarding, that sought items are achievable — without producing fatigue (too many unwanted drops) or frustration (sought items too rare). The decision rule: calibrate drop rates to sustain excitement (sought items achievable, filler drops not overwhelming), and avoid tables that flood unwanted items or gate sought items behind excessive rarity. Mis-calibrated tables produce fatigue or frustration, because the drops did not sustain excitement.

### Ensure Item Stats Are Legible and Comparable

Item stats must be legible — the player understands what an item does, how it compares to alternatives — through clear stat display, consistent formatting, and meaningful comparison, so item decisions are informed rather than guesswork. The decision rule: display item stats clearly and consistently, provide comparison against alternatives, and avoid opaque or inconsistent stat presentation. Illegible stats produce uninformed decisions, because the player could not assess the item.

### Balance Item Power Against Other Progression Systems

Item power must be balanced against other progression systems — skills, abilities, character levels — so items are one contributor to power among several, not the sole determinant, and no single system dominates. The decision rule: balance item power against other systems, ensure each contributes to overall power, and avoid items that dominate or are dominated by other systems. Item-dominant progression makes other systems pointless; item-irrelevant progression makes items pointless, because the balance was not struck.

## Common Traps

### Inflated Rarity That Is Meaningless

The team inflates the drop rates of higher rarity tiers — legendaries drop frequently — to make the player feel rewarded, and the rarity becomes meaningless because the scarce is no longer scarce. The trap is that frequent high-rarity drops feel rewarding. The false signal is that players get good loot. The harm is that the rarity tiers lose meaning, the legendary that should feel special is common, the excitement that scarcity would sustain is absent, and the item economy is devalued, because the scarcity was inflated away.

### Stat Scaling That Devalues Earlier Items or Trivializes Upgrades

The team calibrates stat scaling that either inflates too fast (earlier items become instantly worthless) or flattens too much (upgrades feel trivial), and the progression loses excitement. The trap is that the scaling curve looks steady. The false signal is that items scale with progression. The harm is that the player either finds their collection devalued by inflation or finds upgrades unsatisfying, the excitement that well-calibrated scaling would sustain is absent, and the progression loses its drive, because the curve did not maintain upgrade excitement.

### Power Creep Devaluing the Economy

The team, over updates or late-game content, adds items that outclass existing ones to make each addition exciting, and the power creep devalues the player's collection and the economy. The trap is that escalating items feel exciting each time. The false signal is that each new item is better. The harm is that earlier items become worthless, players who collected them feel their effort devalued, the economy requires constant escalation to feel valuable, and the item system collapses under the creep, because the long-term trajectory was not controlled.

### Loot Tables That Produce Fatigue or Frustration

The team calibrates loot tables that either flood unwanted filler drops (fatigue) or gate sought items behind excessive rarity (frustration), and the loot stops being exciting. The trap is that the tables provide drops. The false signal is that the player receives loot. The harm is that the player either drowns in unwanted drops (fatigue) or cannot obtain sought items (frustration), the excitement that well-calibrated tables would sustain is absent, and the loot system is resented, because the drop rates did not balance excitement.

### Illegible Stats Producing Uninformed Decisions

The team designs item stats that are opaque or inconsistently presented, and the player cannot assess or compare items, making decisions guesswork. The trap is that the stats are displayed. The false signal is that the information is present. The harm is that the player cannot make informed decisions, they may use inferior items or miss upgrades, the item system fails to support the decisions it exists to enable, and the player is frustrated, because the stats were not legible.

### Item-Dominant or Item-Irrelevant Progression

The team balances items such that they dominate progression (other systems pointless) or are irrelevant (items pointless), and the progression is unbalanced. The trap is that items are the visible progression. The false signal is that items drive power. The harm is that either other systems are bypassed (item-dominant) or items are ignored (item-irrelevant), the variety that balanced progression would provide is absent, and the system that is bypassed or ignored is wasted, because the balance was not struck across systems.

## Self-Check

- Are rarity tiers meaningful through both scarcity (drop rates) and distinction (unique properties beyond stats)?
- Does stat scaling maintain upgrade excitement across progression, without inflating or flattening?
- Have I established power ceilings and a long-term trajectory to avoid power creep devaluing existing items?
- Are loot tables calibrated to sustain excitement, avoiding filler-drop fatigue and excessive-rarity frustration?
- Are item stats legible, consistent, and comparable, supporting informed decisions?
- Is item power balanced against other progression systems (skills, abilities, levels), with no system dominating?
- Did I confirm the item economy preserves value and excitement across the game's progression?
