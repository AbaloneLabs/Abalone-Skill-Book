# Character and World Bible Consistency

A character and world bible is the canonical reference that holds the setting's rules, history, geography, factions, and each character's voice, motivations, and established behavior — the single source of truth that lets a distributed writing team produce content that feels authored by one mind. The judgment problem is that consistency is the invisible foundation of believable worlds and the first thing to erode under production pressure, because every individual inconsistency seems minor in isolation (one character uses the wrong term, one location is misplaced) while the cumulative effect is a world players stop trusting and characters who feel like different people from scene to scene. Agents tend to miss this because they treat the bible as a document to write once rather than a living constraint to enforce, and because the cost of inconsistency is deferred — it surfaces not in the scene containing the error but in the player's growing sense that the narrative cannot be trusted, appearing as vague dissatisfaction the team cannot trace to its source. The harm is continuity errors that dedicated fans catalog to damage reputation, characters whose arc collapses because they behaved inconsistently to serve a scene, and a world whose rules are violated often enough that players stop engaging with it as coherent. This skill covers how to build a bible that functions as an enforceable constraint and resolve conflicts between new content and canon.

## Core Rules

### Treat the Bible as an Enforceable Constraint, Not a Reference Document

A bible that writers consult for inspiration but are not required to obey is not a bible; it is a suggestion box, and under deadline pressure every writer will prioritize their scene's immediate needs over a suggestion. The decision rule: the bible's canonical facts — names, dates, geography, character voice, established motivations, world rules — are constraints that new content must satisfy, and any deviation requires an explicit, documented exception with a stated reason, not a silent drift. When the bible is advisory, consistency erodes within the first content drop, because each writer resolves ambiguity in the direction their scene needs, and the contradictions accumulate until the canon is irrecoverable. Enforcement, not existence, is what makes a bible work.

### Define Character Voice With Testable Criteria, Not Adjectives

A character bible entry that describes a character as "wry," "guarded," or "passionate" provides no constraint, because those adjectives can be stretched to justify almost any line. The decision rule: define each major character's voice with testable criteria — specific verbal tics, sentence structures they use or avoid, topics they will and will not discuss, emotional registers they default to under stress, and example lines that anchor the voice — so that a writer or reviewer can test whether a given line is in-character against concrete standards rather than subjective impression. Adjective-based voice entries produce characters who sound like whatever writer last touched them; criteria-based entries produce characters who sound like themselves across an entire team.

### Resolve Canon Conflicts by Establishing Which Fact Is Load-Bearing

When new content contradicts established canon, the resolution is not always "the new content is wrong" — sometimes the new content is better and the canon should be revised, and sometimes the contradiction reveals that the canon was underspecified. The decision rule: when a conflict arises, determine which fact is load-bearing (referenced by multiple downstream scenes, central to the world's logic, or already experienced by players in shipped content) and which is peripheral, then revise the peripheral fact and update the bible to reflect the resolution, documenting the change so the team knows the canon moved. Silent resolution — fixing one scene without updating the reference — guarantees the contradiction recurs when the next writer consults the outdated canon.

### Track Continuity State Across All Narrative Touchpoints

Continuity is not only the writer's concern; it spans dialogue, environmental text, item descriptions, NPC barks, loading screen tips, and marketing copy, and a fact established in an item description can contradict a fact in a main-scene dialogue without either writer knowing the other exists. The decision rule: maintain the canon as a shared state that every narrative-producing discipline checks against, and assign ownership of continuity review to a specific role who audits all touchpoints, because distributed authoring without centralized review is the primary source of contradictions that reach players. The bible is only as strong as the breadth of content it is checked against, and the touchpoints writers forget are the ones that ship errors.

### Distinguish Hard Canon From Flexible Canon Explicitly

Not every detail in a bible carries the same weight: some facts are hard canon (the king is dead, magic requires sacrifice, the war lasted ten years) and violating them breaks the world; others are flexible canon (the exact layout of a city, the specific wording of a historical event) and can be revised when a better idea emerges. The decision rule: label each canonical element as hard or flexible in the bible, so that writers know which constraints are inviolable and which can be negotiated, preventing both the rigidity that stifles good new ideas and the laxity that breaks the world. Ambiguous-weight canon produces either paralysis (writers fear to touch anything) or chaos (writers change anything), and neither serves the narrative.

### Make the Bible Searchable and Versioned, Not a Static Document

A bible that exists as a single long document becomes unmaintainable as the setting grows, because no writer can find the relevant entry, and unversioned bibles cannot track when and why canon changed, so the team loses the ability to audit contradictions to their source. The decision rule: structure the bible so entries are individually addressable and searchable (a wiki, a database, or a well-indexed document), and maintain a changelog that records every canonical revision with date, reason, and author, so that any writer can find the current truth and any reviewer can trace why it is the current truth. A bible that cannot be searched is not consulted; a bible that cannot be audited cannot be trusted.

## Common Traps

### The Bible Written Once and Never Maintained

The team invests in a thorough bible during preproduction, then never updates it as the narrative evolves through production, so the reference diverges from the shipped canon and writers who consult it produce content consistent with the outdated bible and inconsistent with the actual game. The trap is that the bible exists and feels authoritative. The false signal is that writers have a reference document. The harm is that the reference is wrong, every writer who trusts it introduces contradictions, and the bible becomes a source of errors rather than a guard against them — an unmaintained bible is worse than no bible, because it lends false confidence to inconsistent content.

### Characterization Drift in Service of Scene Needs

A writer needs a character to perform a dramatic action in a scene, and the action contradicts the character's established motivations or values, but the writer proceeds because the scene works better with it, and the character behaves inconsistently to serve the plot. The trap is that the scene is genuinely improved by the inconsistency. The false signal is that the scene reads well in isolation. The harm is that the character's arc loses credibility, players who tracked the character's values feel the betrayal, and the character becomes a puppet who does whatever the current scene requires — characterization that bends to plot is the most common reason players stop investing in characters, because they sense the characters have no stable self.

### Voice Defined by Adjectives That Justify Anything

The bible describes a character's voice with evocative adjectives — "sardonic," "world-weary," "fiercely loyal" — and every writer interprets the adjectives differently, producing a character whose dialogue varies wildly in tone, vocabulary, and personality across scenes. The trap is that the adjectives feel descriptive and sufficient. The false signal is that the bible entry reads well. The harm is that the character sounds like a different person depending on who wrote the scene, players cannot form a stable sense of the character, and the characterization reads as inconsistent even when no factual canon is violated — voice inconsistency is invisible in fact-checking and devastating in experience.

### Continuity Errors From Distributed Authoring Without Central Review

Multiple writers, environmental designers, and marketers produce narrative content independently, each consulting the bible but no one checking the others' output against each other, so contradictions ship that no individual author could have caught. The trap is that each author was consistent with the bible as they understood it. The false signal is that individual contributions pass review. The harm is that the shipped product contains contradictions between touchpoints — an item description and a dialogue line that disagree, a loading screen and a cutscene that conflict — and dedicated players, who cross-reference these touchpoints, catalog the errors and circulate them as evidence the team did not care about the world.

### Revised Canon That Does Not Propagate

The team decides to change a canonical fact — a character's backstory, a historical event, a world rule — and updates the bible, but content already written against the old canon is not audited and revised, so the shipped game contains both the old and new versions of the fact. The trap is that the bible was updated, so the team believes the canon is consistent. The false signal is that the reference document is current. The harm is that the shipped content contradicts the current canon, players encounter both versions, and the contradiction is attributed to carelessness, when the real failure was the absence of a propagation step that audited existing content against the revised canon.

## Self-Check

- Is the bible treated as an enforceable constraint, with deviations requiring documented exceptions rather than silent drift under deadline pressure?
- For each major character, is voice defined by testable criteria — verbal tics, sentence structures, topic boundaries, stress registers, example lines — rather than adjectives that can justify any line?
- When canon conflicts arise, do I identify which fact is load-bearing, revise the peripheral fact, and update the bible with a documented change rather than silently fixing one scene?
- Is continuity reviewed across all narrative touchpoints — dialogue, environmental text, item descriptions, NPC barks, loading tips, marketing — by a role with explicit ownership, not only by the writers of each piece?
- Are canonical elements explicitly labeled hard or flexible, so writers know which constraints are inviolable and which can be negotiated?
- Is the bible structured to be searchable and individually addressable, with a versioned changelog recording every revision's date, reason, and author?
- Have I checked for the specific failures: an unmaintained bible diverging from shipped canon, characterization drift serving scene needs, voice inconsistency from adjective-based definitions, and revised canon that did not propagate to existing content?
