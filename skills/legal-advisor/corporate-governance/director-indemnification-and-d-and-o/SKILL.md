# Director Indemnification and D&O

Director indemnification and D&O insurance are the mechanisms that determine who bears the personal financial risk of serving as a director or officer. The defining judgment problem is that indemnification, advancement, and insurance are three overlapping but distinct protections, each with limits, conditions, and exclusions that interact in non-obvious ways — and the moment a director most needs protection (an adverse judgment, insolvency, or a derivative claim) is often the moment one or more protections falls away. An agent must map all three layers, identify the gaps, and never present indemnification as a complete answer.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish indemnification, advancement, and insurance

- **Indemnification** is the corporate obligation to pay covered expenses, judgments, settlements, and fines on behalf of a director or officer, governed by state corporate law and the company's charter, bylaws, and indemnification agreements.
- **Advancement** is the obligation to pay expenses (typically defense costs) as they are incurred, before final disposition, subject to an undertaking to repay if ultimately determined not entitled to indemnification. Advancement is procedurally critical; without it, a director may be unable to defend a claim even if ultimately indemnifiable.
- **Insurance** is a contract under which the insurer, not the company, pays covered losses, subject to limits, retentions, exclusions, and conditions. Insurance is essential where the company cannot or will not indemnify (e.g., insolvency, derivative claims, or uninsured conduct).

### Statutory limits on indemnification

- State corporate law (e.g., Delaware) permits indemnification where the director acted in good faith and in a manner reasonably believed to be in, or not opposed to, the company's best interests, with different standards for criminal and civil matters.
- Indemnification is generally **prohibited** for certain outcomes: a derivative action in which the director is found liable to the company, and a third-party action in which the director is found liable on the basis of improper personal benefit. These are structural limits that insurance may or may not cover.
- Some statutes permit **limitation of director liability** in the charter for certain breaches of the duty of care (but not loyalty, bad faith, unlawful distributions, or improper personal benefit), which reduces the surface for personal liability but does not eliminate it.

### D&O insurance structure and exclusions

- **Side A / Side B / Side C** structure matters. Side A responds when the company cannot indemnify (most important for personal protection); Side B reimburses the company for indemnification payments; Side C protects the company for its own liability (typically for securities claims). Side A is the critical layer when the company is insolvent or prohibited from indemnifying.
- Common exclusions include prior acts, pending litigation, insured-vs-insured (often with carve-backs for derivative claims and shareholder derivative suits), personal profit/advantage, fraud/dishonesty (often final-adjudication styled), and pollution/professional services. Exclusions frequently determine whether coverage exists in the cases that matter most.
- Policy limits, retentions, and sub-limits (e.g., for regulatory investigations, entity coverage, or sub-side-A limits) should be evaluated against realistic exposure, including defense costs, which can exhaust limits before resolution.
- Notice and consent obligations are strict; late notice can void coverage. Counsel must understand the policy's notice triggers and comply promptly.

### Exclusive forum and charter/bylaw provisions

- Exclusive forum provisions (for intracorporate disputes, and for securities claims under federal authority) can consolidate litigation and reduce multiplicity, but their enforceability and scope vary by jurisdiction and must be drafted carefully.
- Fee-shifting and loss-advancement provisions in governing documents affect the economics of derivative claims and should be evaluated for enforceability in the governing jurisdiction.

### Corporate opportunity and fiduciary duties interact with indemnification

- The duty of loyalty, corporate opportunity, and fair dealing are generally outside the scope of indemnification and limitation of liability. A director who usurps a corporate opportunity or receives an improper benefit cannot rely on indemnification for the resulting liability.
- Waiver and disclosure mechanisms (informed consent, disinterested approval, committee approval) can cleanse certain conflicts prospectively, but only if documented and compliant with the governing statute and documents.

### Insolvency and bankruptcy

- Indemnification obligations are unsecured claims in bankruptcy and may be disallowed if the company cannot pay. This is the central reason Side A insurance exists.
- The bankruptcy code can subrogate or recover certain payments, and a debtor-in-possession may decline to advance expenses, making insurance the only realistic protection.

## Common Traps

- **Treating indemnification as a complete answer.** Indemnification does not cover derivative liability, improper personal benefit, or insolvency scenarios; insurance is the backstop for those.
- **Ignoring advancement.** A right to indemnification without advancement leaves a director personally funding a defense, which can be ruinous regardless of the ultimate outcome.
- **Overlooking Side A.** When the company cannot indemnify (insolvency, derivative prohibition), only Side A insurance protects the individual; entity coverage and Side B are irrelevant to that risk.
- **Assuming the policy covers the claim.** Exclusions (insured-vs-insured, personal benefit, fraud) often align with the claims that matter most; a coverage analysis must precede reassurance.
- **Late notice.** Failing to provide timely notice can forfeit coverage entirely; the trigger is often broader than "service of a lawsuit."
- **Relying on limitation of liability for loyalty breaches.** Charter limitations cannot eliminate liability for loyalty, bad faith, unlawful distributions, or improper personal benefit.
- **Ignoring exclusive-forum and fee-shifting enforceability.** These provisions vary by jurisdiction and must be evaluated for the governing law, not assumed.
- **Stale indemnification agreements.** Agreements that predate changes in law or the company's financial condition may not provide the protection assumed.

## Self-Check

- Have I separately analyzed indemnification, advancement, and insurance as three distinct layers, rather than treating indemnification as the complete answer?
- Are the statutory limits on indemnification (derivative liability, improper personal benefit, criminal matters) identified, and is insurance evaluated as the backstop?
- Is Side A coverage present and adequate for the insolvency / cannot-indemnify scenario, with limits and sub-limits assessed against realistic defense-cost exposure?
- Have I reviewed the policy exclusions (insured-vs-insured, personal benefit, fraud, prior acts) against the specific claim or risk, rather than relying on the policy summary?
- Are notice and consent obligations understood and being met promptly, with the notice trigger mapped to events (not only lawsuits)?
- If relying on charter limitation of liability, have I confirmed it does not reach loyalty, bad faith, unlawful distributions, or improper personal benefit?
- Are exclusive-forum and fee-shifting provisions evaluated for enforceability under the governing law?
- Have I recommended qualified corporate counsel admitted in the state of incorporation for indemnification structure, charter/bylaw drafting, and coverage disputes?
- Have I stated the jurisdictional limits of general guidance and recommended insurance-coverage counsel for policy interpretation and claims decisions?
