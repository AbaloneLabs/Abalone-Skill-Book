# Fiduciary Duties

Fiduciary duties are the legal obligations that constrain how directors, officers, managers, and controlling shareholders make decisions on behalf of the company and its owners. They are not aspirational ethics; they are enforceable duties that can produce personal liability, rescission of transactions, and removal from office. Agents often describe fiduciary duty in vague moral terms and miss the operational reality: a director who votes on a transaction without reading the materials, who approves a deal with a family member without disclosure, or who takes a corporate opportunity for themselves has breached a duty regardless of good intentions. The business judgment rule protects informed, disinterested, good-faith decisions, but it does not protect laziness, self-dealing, or bad faith.

Use this skill before advising a board on a transaction, before a director faces a conflict, before approving related-party deals, before designing indemnification or D&O insurance, or when reviewing whether a past decision created duty breach exposure. The goal is to force the agent to separate the duties, identify conflicts, document process, and recognize when the business judgment rule will and will not protect the decision-maker. Fiduciary duty standards vary by jurisdiction and entity type; final analysis must be confirmed with qualified counsel.

## Core Rules

### Separate The Two Core Duties And Their Whole-Fairness Standard

Fiduciary duty is usually analyzed as two components: the duty of care and the duty of loyalty, with some jurisdictions adding a duty of good faith or oversight as part of loyalty or as a separate duty.

The duty of care requires informed, deliberate decision-making. A fiduciary must inform themselves of all material information reasonably available before acting. Voting without reading materials, skipping meetings, or relying on management without independent inquiry where warranted can breach care.

The duty of loyalty requires that the fiduciary act in the best interests of the company and its owners, not in their own interest. Self-dealing, usurping corporate opportunities, competing with the company, and undisclosed conflicts breach loyalty.

When loyalty is implicated, such as in a controlling shareholder transaction or a director self-dealing, the entire fairness standard may apply, requiring fair process and fair price, with the burden on the fiduciary. Agents must recognize when the heightened standard is triggered.

### Identify And Manage Conflicts Of Interest Proactively

Conflicts are common and not inherently wrongful, but undisclosed or unmanaged conflicts are. The duty is to disclose, recuse, and where required obtain independent approval.

For any potential conflict, capture:

- the nature of the conflict (financial, familial, positional, competitive);
- the parties and the transaction at issue;
- whether the conflicted person disclosed it fully;
- whether they recused from deliberation and voting;
- whether an independent committee or disinterested body approved;
- whether fair market value was established;
- whether the process was documented in minutes.

Common conflicts include loans to executives, transactions with family members, purchases from entities the director controls, employment of relatives, simultaneous service on competing boards, and investments in companies in the same industry.

### Build A Record That Supports The Business Judgment Rule

The business judgment rule is a presumption that a fiduciary acting on an informed basis, in good faith, and in the honest belief the action is in the company's interest has not breached duty. To earn the presumption, the fiduciary must build a defensible process.

Support the rule by documenting:

- board materials distributed and reviewed in advance;
- attendance and participation;
- questions asked and answers received;
- reliance on qualified advisors (legal, financial, valuation);
- time spent deliberating;
- independent advisors where the transaction is complex or conflicted;
- minutes reflecting the discussion, not only the vote;
- disclosure of conflicts and recusal of conflicted persons;
- a coherent rationale for the decision.

The presumption is lost when the fiduciary is interested, lacks independence, fails to inform themselves, or acts in bad faith. Once lost, the burden shifts and the transaction must withstand entire fairness review.

### Recognize Corporate Opportunity And Non-Compete Exposure

Fiduciaries cannot take for themselves a business opportunity that belongs to the company, and they cannot compete with the company while serving it. These are among the most litigated loyalty breaches.

Address:

- the line of business the company is in or plans to enter;
- opportunities presented to the fiduciary in their corporate capacity;
- opportunities the company cannot or will not pursue, and whether it formally rejected them;
- board disclosure and consent before a fiduciary pursues an opportunity;
- post-termination non-compete obligations and their enforceability;
- investments in competitors and disclosure duties.

A director who learns of an acquisition target through board service and invests personally has usurped a corporate opportunity unless the board was informed and consented.

### Distinguish Controlling Shareholder Duties

Controlling shareholders owe fiduciary duties to minority shareholders in many jurisdictions, especially in transactions where the controller is on both sides, such as a freeze-out merger. Controllers cannot extract value at the expense of minorities.

Watch for:

- self-dealing transactions with the controller;
- freeze-out or squeeze-out mergers;
- dilutive issuances to the controller;
- preferential dividends or distributions;
- controller-led financing rounds that disadvantage minorities;
- sales of control with disparate treatment.

Controller transactions often trigger entire fairness review and may require an independent special committee and a majority-of-the-minority vote to shift the burden or obtain deferential review.

### Provide Oversight And Avoid The Duty To Monitor

Directors cannot turn a blind eye to risk. The duty of oversight, sometimes called the Caremark duty, requires implementing reporting systems and acting on red flags. Ignoring known risks can breach loyalty, not just care.

Implement:

- board oversight of mission-critical risks;
- reporting systems for compliance, financial controls, safety, and cybersecurity;
- escalation paths for red flags;
- regular review of audit, compliance, and risk reports;
- documentation of board attention to key risks;
- response to whistleblower and audit findings.

A board that ignores repeated warnings about a compliance failure faces personal liability even if no one intended harm.

### Use Indemnification, Advancement, And D&O Insurance Correctly

Fiduciaries face personal liability, and companies use indemnification, advancement of defense costs, and D&O insurance to attract qualified directors. These protections have limits and conditions.

Confirm:

- charter or operating agreement provisions authorizing indemnification to the fullest extent permitted by law;
- indemnification agreements with advancement rights;
- statutory limits on indemnification for acts in bad faith or derived benefits;
- D&O insurance coverage, retentions, exclusions, and consent provisions;
- coverage for entity and securities claims, including Side A coverage for non-indemnifiable claims;
- notice obligations to preserve coverage;
- coordination between indemnification and insurance to avoid gaps.

Indemnification generally does not protect a fiduciary from liability to the company for bad-faith acts or from derivative claims, and insurance has exclusions for fraud and personal profit.

## Common Traps

### Treating Fiduciary Duty As Mere Ethics

Duties of care and loyalty are enforceable legal obligations, not aspirations. Breaches produce personal liability and rescission.

### Skipping Materials And Relying On Management

Voting without reading materials or without independent inquiry on major decisions forfeits the business judgment presumption.

### Undisclosed Or Unmanaged Conflicts

A conflict is not fatal, but failing to disclose, recuse, or obtain independent approval is. Document the process.

### Usurping Corporate Opportunities

Taking a deal learned of through board service, without disclosure and consent, is a loyalty breach even if the company might not have pursued it.

### Assuming The Business Judgment Rule Always Applies

The rule is a presumption that is lost when a fiduciary is interested, uninformed, or acting in bad faith. Entire fairness then applies.

### Ignoring Oversight Red Flags

A board that fails to act on repeated compliance or safety warnings breaches the duty to monitor, often analyzed as loyalty.

### Overreliance On Indemnification

Indemnification has statutory limits and may be unavailable for bad faith or derivative claims. D&O insurance has exclusions and retentions. Do not assume full protection.

### Controller Self-Dealing Without Protections

Controller transactions without an independent committee and minority vote invite entire fairness review and liability.

## Self-Check

- Are the duties of care and loyalty distinguished, with entire fairness identified where loyalty is implicated?
- Are conflicts of interest captured with disclosure, recusal, independent approval, fair value, and minute documentation?
- Does the decision record support the business judgment rule through advance materials, attendance, reliance on advisors, deliberation, and coherent rationale?
- Are corporate opportunity and non-compete exposures addressed, including disclosure and consent before a fiduciary pursues an opportunity?
- Are controlling shareholder transactions flagged for entire fairness, special committee, and majority-of-minority protections?
- Is the duty of oversight operationalized through risk reporting, escalation, and documented board attention to red flags?
- Are indemnification, advancement, and D&O insurance structured with statutory limits, exclusions, Side A coverage, and notice obligations understood?
- Does the output distinguish protected, informed, disinterested decisions from those vulnerable to entire fairness or bad-faith challenge?
- Are jurisdictional and entity-type variations flagged for counsel confirmation?
- Does the analysis avoid asserting that a decision is protected or enforceable without qualified counsel review of the governing law?
