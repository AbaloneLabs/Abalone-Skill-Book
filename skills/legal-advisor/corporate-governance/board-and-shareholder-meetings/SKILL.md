# Board And Shareholder Meetings

Corporate decisions are only valid if the meeting that produced them was properly called, noticed, attended by a quorum, voted on by the right majority, and documented in minutes that survive scrutiny. Agents often focus on the substance of a resolution and ignore the procedural machinery that gives it legal force. A board approval with no notice, a shareholder vote without quorum, a special committee meeting without a charter, or a written consent missing a signature can void a financing, a merger, an equity grant, or a director election. Years later, in litigation or due diligence, the absence of valid minutes can unravel a transaction or block a closing.

Use this skill before calling a meeting, before drafting notice, before holding a special meeting, before relying on written consent, before forming a committee, or when auditing corporate minutes for validity. The goal is to force the agent to treat meeting procedure as a compliance discipline where each step, notice, waiver, quorum, vote, and minute entry, must be defensible. Meeting rules are jurisdiction- and charter-specific; final procedures must be confirmed against the governing statute and governing documents.

## Core Rules

### Follow The Governing Documents And Statute Hierarchy

Meeting rules come from multiple sources that must be read together: the statute of the formation state, the charter, the bylaws or operating agreement, shareholder agreements, and any committee charters. When they conflict, identify the order of precedence.

Confirm:

- the state corporation or LLC statute defaults;
- charter provisions that cannot be overridden;
- bylaws or operating agreement meeting rules;
- shareholder or voting agreement restrictions;
- committee charters;
- any court orders or regulatory requirements.

Do not assume default statutory rules apply if the bylaws override them. Many governance disputes turn on a bylaw provision the drafter ignored.

### Provide Proper Notice With Required Content And Timing

Notice is the most common procedural defect. A meeting held without proper notice can have its actions challenged and set aside, even if everyone attended.

For each meeting, verify:

- who must receive notice (directors, shareholders, members);
- the required advance notice period;
- the permitted notice methods (mail, email, electronic, personal delivery);
- the required content (date, time, place or remote access, purpose, agenda for special meetings);
- whether the purpose must be stated and whether action is limited to noticed purposes;
- notice waivers and whether attendance cures defective notice;
- record date for shareholder meetings;
- record-keeping of notice sent and received.

Special meetings are particularly strict: business transacted is generally limited to the purpose stated in the notice. Annual meetings have broader agendas but still require notice.

### Establish Quorum And Voting Thresholds

A meeting without quorum cannot transact business validly. Voting thresholds vary by action and governing document.

Confirm:

- quorum requirements for directors and shareholders;
- whether a quorum is present throughout or only at the start;
- voting standard (majority, supermajority, unanimous) for each action;
- classes or series with separate voting rights;
- voting agreements and proxies;
- remote participation and electronic voting rules;
- abstentions and conflicted director treatment;
- cumulative voting where applicable.

For major actions such as mergers, charter amendments, asset sales, or dissolution, supermajority or separate class voting may be required. Verify the threshold before the vote, not after.

### Document Meetings With Defensible Minutes

Minutes are the primary evidence of valid corporate action. Sparse or missing minutes invite challenges, while overly detailed minutes can create unintended admissions. The goal is a clear, accurate record of what was approved and the basis for the decision.

Capture in minutes:

- meeting type, date, time, and location or remote platform;
- notice given and waivers of notice;
- attendees, including directors, officers, guests, and counsel;
- presence of quorum;
- agenda items and the substance of deliberation;
- materials presented and advisors relied upon;
- conflicts disclosed and recusals;
- motions made, seconded, and vote results;
- resolutions adopted with exact text;
- actions ratified or approved;
- adjournment and next meeting.

Minutes need not be verbatim transcripts, but they should reflect informed deliberation. For conflicted or major transactions, document the process that supports the business judgment rule: materials reviewed, advisors consulted, questions asked, and rationale.

### Use Written Consents Correctly

Written consents allow action without a meeting but have strict requirements. A defective consent is void.

Verify:

- who must sign (all directors or shareholders, or the required majority depending on statute);
- whether unanimous consent is required for some actions;
- the form and content of the consent;
- delivery and effective date;
- record date for shareholder consents;
- notice to non-consenting shareholders where required;
- filing in the minute book;
- electronic signature validity.

For shareholder written consents, statutes often require notice to non-consenting shareholders, and the consent must satisfy the same voting thresholds as a meeting.

### Form And Charter Committees Properly

Committees such as audit, compensation, nominating, and special transaction committees delegate board authority but must be properly constituted to act validly.

Confirm:

- the board resolution authorizing the committee;
- the committee charter defining scope and limits;
- committee composition, especially independence requirements;
- quorum and voting rules within the committee;
- authority to retain independent advisors;
- reporting back to the full board;
- limits on delegable functions (some actions cannot be delegated).

A special committee for a conflicted transaction must be independent, fully informed, and empowered to say no, or it will not earn deferential review.

### Handle Remote And Electronic Meetings

Virtual and hybrid meetings are now common and permitted in most jurisdictions, but they must allow participation and voting consistent with statute.

Verify:

- the governing documents permit remote meetings;
- the platform allows all participants to hear or read proceedings concurrently;
- participants can vote and be verified;
- record-keeping of attendance and votes;
- notice includes access instructions;
- technical failures and adjournment procedures.

### Maintain The Annual Meeting And Filing Calendar

Annual meetings and recurring filings are easy to miss and create compliance gaps. Calendar them.

Track:

- annual shareholder meeting date and notice window;
- regular board meeting schedule;
- franchise tax and annual report deadlines;
- committee meeting cadence;
- consent expiration and renewal dates;
- record dates for distributions and votes.

## Common Traps

### Treating Notice As A Formality

Defective notice can void meeting actions even when all parties attended. Verify timing, content, method, and waivers.

### Sparse Minutes That Reveal Nothing

Minutes that record only the vote, with no deliberation, undermine the business judgment rule and invite challenges. Document informed process.

### Overly Detailed Minutes That Admit Too Much

Verbatim transcripts or speculative language can be used against the company. Record decisions and rationale, not casual remarks.

### Wrong Quorum Or Voting Threshold

Acting without quorum or under the wrong supermajority voids the action. Check thresholds before the vote.

### Misusing Written Consent

Consents that lack required signatures, notice, or proper form are void. Follow statutory consent rules precisely.

### Special Committee Without Independence Or Authority

A committee that lacks independence or the power to reject a transaction earns no deference and fails its purpose.

### Ignoring Purpose Limits At Special Meetings

Business at special meetings is limited to noticed purposes. Surprise actions are vulnerable.

### Forgetting Remote Meeting Compliance

Virtual meetings must allow real-time participation and verifiable voting, or actions may be challenged.

## Self-Check

- Is the hierarchy of statute, charter, bylaws, operating agreement, shareholder agreements, and committee charters identified and reconciled?
- Is notice verified for each meeting by recipient, timing, method, content, purpose statement, waivers, and record date?
- Are quorum and voting thresholds confirmed for each action, including class voting, supermajority, proxies, and abstentions?
- Do minutes capture attendance, quorum, notice, deliberation, materials, advisors, conflicts, recusals, motions, votes, and exact resolution text?
- Are written consents checked for required signatories, form, delivery, effective date, notice to non-consenters, and minute book filing?
- Are committees properly chartered with defined scope, independence, quorum, advisor authority, and reporting duties?
- Are remote and electronic meetings compliant with platform, participation, voting, verification, and technical-failure procedures?
- Is an annual calendar maintained for shareholder meetings, board meetings, franchise tax, annual reports, committee cadence, and consent renewals?
- Are special meeting purpose limits respected so that actions stay within noticed business?
- Does the output avoid asserting that meeting actions are valid without confirming the governing statute and documents with qualified counsel?
