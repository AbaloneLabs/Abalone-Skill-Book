# Related-Party Transactions and Conflicts

Related-party transactions sit at the intersection of fiduciary duty, corporate disclosure, and substantive deal terms. The defining judgment problem is that a transaction can be entirely fair on the merits and still be set aside if the process fails to manage the conflict, while a conflicted controller can obtain business-judgment-rule protection only by satisfying demanding procedural cleansing requirements. An agent must reason about who is conflicted, what process can cleanse the conflict, what disclosure is required, and what standard of review the chosen process will invoke.

## Core Rules

### Identify the conflict before structuring the transaction

- A conflict arises when a director, officer, or controlling stockholder stands on both sides of a transaction, or has a personal interest that may diverge from the company's interest. Common contexts include executive compensation, loans to insiders, mergers with a controlling stockholder, purchases or sales of assets between related parties, and joint ventures.
- The conflict must be identified and disclosed before the process is designed. A transaction that proceeds without recognizing the conflict cannot be cleansed retroactively.

### The standard of review follows the process

- **Business judgment rule** applies where directors are disinterested and independent, act in good faith, and exercise informed judgment. The default is deferential.
- **Entire fairness** applies where a controlling stockholder stands on both sides, or where directors are conflicted and uncleanised. Entire fairness requires fair dealing and fair price, with the burden on the conflicted party. It is the most demanding standard and the one a well-advised conflicted controller tries to avoid.
- **Cleansing mechanisms** can shift a conflicted transaction from entire fairness to business judgment review if they satisfy the controlling precedent for the governing jurisdiction.

### Cleansing mechanisms and their requirements

- For controlling-stockholder transactions, a common cleansing structure (following the framework associated with landmark Delaware precedent) requires approval by both (1) an independent, empowered special committee and (2) a fully informed, uncoerced vote of a majority of the minority stock. Both conditions must be satisfied up front; partial cleansing does not move the standard.
- **Special committee** members must be independent of the controlling stockholder and the transaction, and the committee must have real authority (to say no, to negotiate, to retain advisors). A committee that is not independent, or that lacks authority, does not cleanse.
- **Minority vote** must be fully informed (adequate disclosure), uncoerced (no threats or structurally coercive features), and free of majority-of-the-minority mechanics that exclude conflicted parties.
- These mechanisms are jurisdiction-specific. The framework and its requirements differ across states; what cleanses in one jurisdiction may not in another.

### Director conflict disclosure and abstention

- Directors with a personal interest in a matter must disclose the interest fully and generally abstain from deliberation and vote, subject to the governing statute and documents.
- Disclosure must be timely, complete, and specific enough to allow disinterested directors or stockholders to evaluate the conflict. Incomplete or misleading disclosure can itself be a breach of duty.
- Where a quorum of disinterested directors cannot be achieved, stockholder approval may be required, and the standard of review may shift.

### Corporate opportunity and fair dealing

- The duty of loyalty prohibits usurping a corporate opportunity — a business opportunity that the company has an interest or expectancy in, or that is within the company's line of business — unless it is first offered to the company and properly rejected.
- Related-party transactions should be evaluated for fair terms (price, process, and protections) independent of the cleansing mechanism. Cleansing changes the standard of review; it does not make an unfair deal fair.

### Disclosure, audit committee, and securities obligations

- Public companies face related-person transaction policies (typically requiring audit-committee or comparable approval) and disclosure obligations under securities laws for material related-party transactions, including in proxy and annual reports.
- Disclosure must be accurate and complete; omitting a material related-party relationship or misstating its terms can create securities-liability exposure independent of the fiduciary analysis.
- Tax rules (e.g., excess benefit, intermediate sanctions, transfer pricing, and below-market loans) may impose separate consequences on related-party terms.

### Controlling stockholders and minority protection

- Controlling stockholders owe fiduciary duties to minority stockholders. A controller can extract value through freeze-out mergers, self-interested deals, or diversion of opportunities; the entire-fairness standard and cleansing mechanisms exist to discipline this risk.
- Defensive measures and control-protective transactions involving a controller receive heightened scrutiny.

## Common Traps

- **Failing to identify the conflict before structuring.** A transaction built without recognizing the conflict cannot be cleansed retroactively, and the process will be judged as if unconflicted.
- **Partial cleansing.** Approving a controlling-stockholder deal by committee alone, without the minority vote (or vice versa), does not shift the standard of review and leaves entire fairness exposed.
- **Committee that is not independent or empowered.** A committee with ties to the controller, or that lacks authority to reject the deal, does not cleanse.
- **Inadequate disclosure.** Disclosure that omits material facts, uses boilerplate, or is timed to influence the vote can defeat cleansing and create separate liability.
- **Treating cleansing as a substitute for fairness.** Cleansing changes the standard of review; an unfair deal can still face claims under the cleansed standard or under other theories.
- **Ignoring securities and tax dimensions.** Related-party terms can trigger separate disclosure obligations and tax consequences independent of fiduciary duties.
- **Assuming one jurisdiction's cleansing works everywhere.** The framework and requirements differ across states and countries; analyze under the governing law.
- **Conflicted directors voting.** Allowing a conflicted director to participate in deliberation or vote taints the process and can shift the standard of review.

## Self-Check

- Has the conflict been identified and disclosed before the process was designed, with the conflicted party's interest fully described?
- Is the applicable standard of review (business judgment, entire fairness, or cleansed) correctly identified for the governing jurisdiction?
- If relying on a cleansing mechanism, are both the special committee (independent and empowered) and the majority-of-the-minority vote satisfied up front, with full disclosure and no coercion?
- Are special-committee members demonstrably independent of the controller and the transaction, with real authority and independent advisors?
- Is the minority vote fully informed, uncoerced, and properly scoped to exclude conflicted parties?
- Have conflicted directors disclosed and abstained, and is there a quorum of disinterested directors (or stockholder approval where required)?
- Has the substantive fairness of the terms (price and process) been evaluated independent of the cleansing mechanism?
- Have securities disclosure and audit-committee approval obligations for material related-party transactions been addressed?
- Have tax consequences (excess benefit, intermediate sanctions, transfer pricing, below-market loans) been considered separately?
- Have I recommended qualified corporate counsel admitted in the state of incorporation for conflict structuring, cleansing, and disclosure, and stated the jurisdictional limits of general guidance?
