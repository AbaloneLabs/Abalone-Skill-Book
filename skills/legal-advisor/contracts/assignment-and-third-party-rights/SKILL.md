# Assignment And Third-Party Rights

Whether a party can transfer a contract, delegate its duties, or rely on someone else to perform is rarely a single clause question. It sits at the intersection of assignment language, anti-assignment restrictions, change-of-control triggers, consent regimes, delegation limits, and the separate doctrine of third-party beneficiary rights. Agents often treat the "Assignment" section as boilerplate, but it controls who the real counterparty is after a sale, restructuring, financing, insolvency, or subcontracting decision. A contract that cannot be assigned can block a transaction, destroy collateral value, or strand a party in a relationship it cannot exit. Conversely, a clause drafted too loosely can let a counterparty hand performance to an unknown, uncreditworthy, or hostile substitute.

Use this skill before signing a contract that may later need to be transferred, before acquiring or selling an entity whose contracts must move with it, before granting a security interest in contract rights, before subcontracting duties, before relying on a third party's promise, or before advising whether a proposed transfer is permitted. The goal is to force the agent to read the assignment regime as a whole, distinguish assignment of rights from delegation of duties, identify consent and deemed-consent traps, and recognize when third-party beneficiary doctrine creates enforceable rights the drafter did not intend. Assignment analysis is jurisdiction-specific and often turns on common-law default rules that vary by state and country; material questions should be reviewed by qualified counsel.

## Core Rules

### Separate Assignment Of Rights From Delegation Of Duties

Assignment and delegation are different operations with different rules. Assigning rights transfers the benefit of the contract (the right to receive payment, goods, or performance). Delegating duties transfers the burden (the obligation to perform). Many contracts permit one but restrict the other, and the common law treats them differently.

Identify for the contract:

- whether the party may assign its rights, and on what conditions;
- whether the party may delegate its duties, and to whom;
- whether delegation releases the delegating party from liability or only adds the delegate;
- whether the counterparty must consent, and whether consent can be unreasonably withheld;
- whether there is a deemed-consent or notice-only mechanism.

Do not assume that a broad "assignment" clause authorizes delegation of core obligations, or that delegation extinguishes the original party's duty. Under most regimes, delegation does not discharge the delegator unless the obligee agrees to a novation.

### Read Anti-Assignment And Consent Clauses For Scope And Effect

Anti-assignment clauses come in several strengths, and the wording controls enforceability.

Recognize:

- a clause prohibiting assignment of rights may or may not be enforceable depending on jurisdiction; some courts read such clauses as making assignment a breach rather than voiding it;
- a clause requiring consent to assignment, with consent not to be unreasonably withheld, implies an objective reasonableness standard;
- a clause allowing assignment only with prior written consent, with no reasonableness qualifier, may permit arbitrary withholding;
- a deemed-consent clause (consent deemed given if no response within X days) shifts leverage and must be operationally workable;
- a clause distinguishing assignments to affiliates from assignments to third parties is common in commercial deals and must define "affiliate" precisely.

Confirm whether the clause restricts assignment by operation of law (such as a merger or change of control) as well as voluntary assignment. Many disputes arise because the anti-assignment clause was triggered by a corporate transaction the parties did not think of as an "assignment."

### Map Change-Of-Control And Merger Triggers

In many jurisdictions, a stock merger or change of control does not technically "assign" the contract because the contracting entity survives, but the contract may still treat it as one. Drafter intent controls.

Check:

- whether the clause expressly captures mergers, consolidations, or change-of-control transactions;
- whether it captures a sale of all or substantially all assets;
- whether it captures transfers of equity or control by founders or key shareholders;
- whether affiliate carve-outs apply only to internal reorganizations or also to a sale of the affiliate to an outside party;
- whether a permitted assignment to a successor in a financing or securitization is contemplated.

A target company whose material contracts terminate on change of control can lose most of its value at closing. This is a frequent and expensive diligence finding in mergers and acquisitions.

### Evaluate Novation Versus Assignment

A novation substitutes a new party and releases the original party; an assignment generally does not. The distinction matters when a party wants a clean exit.

Determine:

- whether the transaction requires a novation agreement signed by all three parties;
- whether the contract contemplates a streamlined novation process;
- whether the original party remains secondarily liable after the transfer;
- whether the transferee's creditworthiness and performance capability have been assessed;
- whether the transferor has any continuing indemnity or guarantee obligation.

Insist on a novation, not merely an assignment, when the goal is to be released from future liability. An assignment that leaves the assignor on the hook for performance is not an exit.

### Identify Non-Assignable Rights By Nature

Some rights cannot be assigned even when the contract is silent, and some duties cannot be delegated.

Common non-assignable or non-delegable items include:

- personal services contracts where performance depends on the specific party;
- rights whose assignment would materially change the risk or burden on the obligor;
- rights barred by statute, such as certain claims for personal injury or statutory benefits;
- duties requiring a specific license, qualification, or security clearance;
- rights under insurance policies, which often require insurer consent.

Do not assume silence in the contract means free assignability. Default common-law limits may still apply and vary by jurisdiction.

### Analyze Third-Party Beneficiary Status

A third party may enforce a contract it did not sign if the contracting parties intended to confer a benefit on it. This doctrine can create liability the drafter did not anticipate.

Assess:

- whether the contract names or identifies a class of third-party beneficiaries;
- whether the intent to benefit is direct and unequivocal, or merely incidental;
- whether the contract disclaims third-party beneficiary rights;
- whether governing law distinguishes intended from incidental beneficiaries and what test it applies;
- whether the third party's rights vest and cannot later be modified by the contracting parties.

Drafters who wish to prevent third-party enforcement should include an express no-third-party-beneficiaries clause. Drafters who intend to benefit a third party should say so clearly and consider whether the beneficiary should be named.

### Consider Assignment As Collateral And In Insolvency

Contract rights are often pledged as collateral in financing and are treated as assets in bankruptcy. The assignment regime affects both.

Check:

- whether the clause permits assignment to a secured party or lender as collateral;
- whether a security interest in contract rights is perfected and enforceable;
- whether an anti-assignment clause would be triggered by enforcement of the collateral;
- how the contract is treated in the assignor's insolvency, including executory contract assumptions and rejections;
- whether ipso facto clauses (termination on insolvency) are enforceable in the relevant jurisdiction, since many bankruptcy regimes invalidate them.

## Common Traps

### Treating Assignment As Boilerplate

The assignment clause often determines whether a transaction can close. Reading it last, or not at all, is a frequent and serious error.

### Confusing Assignment With Novation

An assignment that leaves the assignor liable is not a release. Parties who think they have exited a contract often have not.

### Ignoring Change-Of-Control Triggers

A merger or equity sale can trip an anti-assignment clause even when no contract is technically assigned. Map these triggers during diligence.

### Assuming Consent Cannot Be Unreasonably Withheld

Without a reasonableness qualifier, a consent requirement may allow arbitrary refusal. Negotiate the standard or build in a deemed-consent fallback.

### Overlooking Delegation Of Core Duties

A broad assignment clause may not authorize delegating duties that are personal or central to the bargain, and the delegator usually remains liable regardless.

### Missing Third-Party Beneficiary Exposure

A contract that confers an intended benefit can be enforced by a non-party. If that is not desired, disclaim it expressly.

### Forgetting Perfection And Insolvency Treatment

Contract rights pledged as collateral must be perfected, and ipso facto clauses may be unenforceable in bankruptcy. Verify under the relevant insolvency regime.

## Self-Check

- Does the analysis separate assignment of rights from delegation of duties, and identify which the contract permits, restricts, or prohibits?
- Are anti-assignment and consent clauses read for scope, reasonableness standards, deemed-consent mechanisms, and operation-of-law triggers?
- Are change-of-control, merger, asset-sale, and affiliate carve-out provisions mapped so that transaction risk is visible before signing?
- Is the distinction between assignment and novation applied, with a novation obtained where a true release of liability is required?
- Are rights that are non-assignable or duties that are non-delegable by nature or statute identified under the governing law?
- Is third-party beneficiary exposure assessed, with an express disclaimer or clear intended-benefit language as appropriate?
- Are collateral assignment, perfection, and insolvency treatment (including ipso facto enforceability) checked for financing and bankruptcy scenarios?
- Does the output avoid asserting that an assignment is valid or enforceable without qualified counsel review of the governing jurisdiction's default rules?
