# Contract Risk And Obligation Review

Contract review is not just finding unfavorable clauses. A contract creates obligations, rights, remedies, deadlines, dependencies, evidence requirements, and operational work. A clause can look harmless until it interacts with payment timing, data security, insurance, renewal, termination, indemnity, limitation of liability, service levels, intellectual property, compliance warranties, or dispute forum. Agents often summarize a contract accurately but fail to surface what the organization must do after signing.

Use this skill before reviewing a contract, drafting terms for counsel, preparing a negotiation checklist, summarizing obligations for operations, comparing vendor terms, or deciding what legal issues need attorney review. The goal is to turn contract text into clear risk, obligation, and decision points without pretending that generic language is enough.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Contract Type And Business Context

Contract risk depends on what the agreement is trying to accomplish. A vendor agreement, SaaS subscription, NDA, lease, employment agreement, operating agreement, loan document, purchase order, distribution agreement, data processing agreement, and statement of work each creates different risks.

Capture:

- contract type;
- parties and entity names;
- role of each party;
- effective date;
- term;
- transaction value;
- goods or services;
- regulated subject matter;
- business owner;
- signer authority;
- related documents;
- current negotiation stage;
- renewal or replacement status.

Do not review the document as a standalone artifact if it references order forms, exhibits, policies, data processing addenda, service descriptions, schedules, purchase orders, or incorporated online terms.

### Verify Authority, Formation, And Version

Before analyzing terms, confirm that the right document is being reviewed and that the parties have authority to sign or perform.

Check:

- legal names and entity types;
- signature blocks;
- signatory authority;
- effective date and execution date;
- version control;
- redlines and comments;
- incorporated documents;
- amendments and addenda;
- order of precedence;
- governing language if translated;
- whether the contract is already signed.

If the agreement is already signed, the task is risk management and performance review, not negotiation. If unsigned, identify negotiation positions and fallback terms.

### Build An Obligation Map

A contract summary should tell the business what it must do, by when, and what happens if it fails. Extract obligations from the whole document, not only obvious action clauses.

Map:

- deliverables;
- payment amounts and timing;
- invoicing requirements;
- acceptance criteria;
- service levels;
- reporting duties;
- confidentiality duties;
- data handling duties;
- insurance requirements;
- audit rights;
- notice obligations;
- renewal deadlines;
- termination rights;
- cure periods;
- recordkeeping duties;
- compliance certifications;
- post-termination duties.

For each obligation, identify owner, deadline, evidence, and operational dependency. A term nobody can operationalize is a risk.

### Review Risk Allocation Clauses Together

Risk allocation clauses interact. Indemnity may be limited by liability caps, excluded damages, insurance, defense control, notice rules, and carveouts. Do not review each clause in isolation.

Review together:

- indemnification;
- limitation of liability;
- exclusion of damages;
- warranty disclaimers;
- remedies;
- insurance;
- defense and settlement control;
- third-party claims;
- direct claims;
- consequential damages;
- confidentiality breaches;
- data breaches;
- IP infringement;
- regulatory violations;
- payment disputes.

Ask whether the party taking the risk can prevent, insure, price, or control it. If not, flag for negotiation or counsel review.

### Check Payment, Revenue, And Cash Terms

Many contract failures come from payment mechanics rather than legal theory. Payment terms should match cash planning, accounting, tax, and service delivery.

Review:

- price and currency;
- taxes;
- invoicing requirements;
- payment due date;
- late fees;
- setoff rights;
- refunds;
- chargebacks;
- expense reimbursement;
- milestone payments;
- acceptance dependencies;
- renewal price increases;
- minimum commitments;
- termination fees;
- suspension rights;
- collection costs.

Make sure finance can comply with invoicing details and that revenue or expense recognition issues are flagged for accounting review.

### Review Data, Confidentiality, And Intellectual Property

Contracts often move data, confidential information, software, content, inventions, models, customer lists, credentials, and regulated records. These clauses must match actual operations.

Check:

- definition of confidential information;
- permitted uses;
- disclosure exceptions;
- security requirements;
- personal data processing;
- cross-border transfers;
- breach notice;
- return or destruction;
- ownership of pre-existing IP;
- ownership of deliverables;
- licenses granted;
- open-source or third-party materials;
- publicity rights;
- feedback rights;
- residual knowledge clauses.

Do not assume a general confidentiality clause covers privacy, data security, export, health information, financial data, or regulated records.

### Review Termination, Renewal, And Exit

Exit terms often matter more than entry terms. The business should know how it can leave, how the other party can leave, what continues, and what transition support exists.

Review:

- initial term;
- auto-renewal;
- renewal notice deadline;
- termination for convenience;
- termination for cause;
- cure period;
- immediate termination triggers;
- suspension rights;
- post-termination payment;
- data return;
- transition services;
- survival clauses;
- non-solicit or non-compete restrictions;
- return of property;
- wind-down obligations.

Calendar notice windows and renewal deadlines. Auto-renewal risk is easy to miss.

### Identify Governing Law, Forum, And Dispute Process

Dispute clauses determine where and how conflicts are resolved. They affect cost, leverage, confidentiality, discovery, appeal rights, and timing.

Check:

- governing law;
- venue;
- court jurisdiction;
- arbitration clause;
- class action waiver;
- jury waiver;
- mediation requirement;
- escalation process;
- notice method;
- injunctive relief carveout;
- fee shifting;
- limitation period;
- language of proceedings.

If the forum is inconvenient, foreign, costly, or inconsistent with the business risk, flag it before signing.

### Create A Decision-Oriented Output

A contract review should help someone decide. Do not merely summarize clauses.

Provide:

- high-risk issues;
- missing documents;
- operational obligations;
- negotiation asks;
- fallback positions;
- owner for each obligation;
- deadlines to calendar;
- clauses requiring attorney review;
- assumptions;
- unresolved questions;
- whether the summary is pre-signature or post-signature.

Avoid stating that a clause is enforceable unless qualified counsel has reviewed the governing law.

## Common Traps

### Summarizing Without Operationalizing

A summary that says "party must comply with law" is weak. Identify which team must do what, what evidence is needed, and what deadline applies.

### Ignoring Incorporated Terms

Online terms, policies, exhibits, order forms, data addenda, service descriptions, and purchase orders may override or add obligations. Review the full contract package.

### Reviewing Indemnity Without Liability Caps

Indemnity, caps, exclusions, insurance, and remedies should be reviewed together. A favorable indemnity can be hollow if capped too low or excluded.

### Missing Auto-Renewal And Notice Windows

Renewal and termination deadlines are operational risks. Calendar them.

### Assuming Boilerplate Is Harmless

Governing law, venue, assignment, notices, waiver, severability, integration, force majeure, audit rights, and order of precedence can change outcomes.

### Treating Templates As Attorney Approval

A template may be outdated, jurisdiction-specific, or mismatched to the deal. Material changes, regulated data, unusual risk, and high value should trigger counsel review.

### Forgetting Signer Authority

A good contract signed by someone without authority may create disputes. Confirm authority and entity names.

## Self-Check

- Is the contract type, business context, transaction value, parties, term, and negotiation stage clear?
- Are entity names, signatory authority, effective dates, versions, amendments, incorporated terms, and order of precedence checked?
- Are all obligations mapped with owner, deadline, evidence, and operational dependency?
- Are indemnity, limitation of liability, damages exclusions, warranties, remedies, insurance, and carveouts reviewed together?
- Are payment, tax, invoice, renewal pricing, refund, suspension, and termination fee terms reviewed for finance impact?
- Are confidentiality, data protection, security, privacy, IP ownership, licenses, deliverables, and breach notice obligations checked?
- Are termination, renewal, cure, survival, transition, data return, and exit obligations calendared?
- Are governing law, venue, arbitration, mediation, class waiver, fee shifting, and notice provisions identified?
- Does the output distinguish summary, risk, negotiation ask, fallback, and attorney-review item?
- Does the review avoid claiming enforceability or legal sufficiency without qualified counsel and governing-law analysis?
