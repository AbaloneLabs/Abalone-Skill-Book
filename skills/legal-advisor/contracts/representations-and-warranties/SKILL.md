# Representations And Warranties

Representations and warranties are the mechanism by which one party certifies facts about itself, its business, or the subject matter of a deal, and allocates the risk that those facts are wrong. The judgment problem is that reps and warranties look like boilerplate assertions, but they are carefully constructed risk-shifting devices where a single word, like "material," a knowledge qualifier, or a disclosure schedule, can change who bears a multi-million dollar loss. Agents often summarize reps as "the seller warrants it owns the assets" while missing that the rep is qualified by knowledge, limited by disclosures, subject to a materiality scrape, capped by indemnity, and conditioned by a bring-down at closing. A rep that looks protective can be hollow, and a rep that looks routine can create broad liability.

Use this skill before reviewing, drafting, or negotiating representations, warranties, disclosure schedules, bring-down certificates, or related indemnity and accuracy conditions in any transaction or commercial agreement. The goal is to make the agent distinguish reps, warranties, and covenants, analyze qualifiers and disclosures, assess breach consequences and caps, and check accuracy conditions. This is contract support; final rep packages, indemnity structures, and disclosure decisions require qualified counsel.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Representations, Warranties, And Covenants

These three create different obligations and consequences, and confusing them weakens drafting and review.

- A representation is a statement of past or present fact made to induce the other party to enter the deal. Falsity can support rescission, indemnity, or fraud claims.
- A warranty is a promise that a fact is true, often with a remedy for breach. Warranties can overlap with reps but may carry different limitation periods and remedies.
- A covenant is a promise to do or not do something in the future. Breach sounds in contract damages, not in the falsity-based remedies attached to reps.

A strong analysis labels each provision correctly. A rep mislabeled as a covenant, or a future promise drafted as a rep, can change the available remedies and the limitation analysis.

### Analyze Qualifiers, Materiality, And Knowledge Scopes

Reps are rarely absolute. Identify qualifiers that narrow them: "material" contracts, "to the knowledge of" the company, "except as disclosed" in schedules, and defined thresholds. Materiality qualifiers reduce the rep's scope but can complicate indemnity if the standard is not scraped. Knowledge qualifiers limit liability to what specified individuals actually know, often defined by a knowledge standard that includes what they would have learned through inquiry. Map each qualifier to understand what is actually certified and what is carved out.

### Read Disclosure Schedules As Part Of The Reps

Disclosure schedules are where the disclosing party lists exceptions to the reps, and they are as important as the reps themselves. A rep that says "no litigation" is qualified by a schedule disclosing pending suits. Review the schedules against the reps carefully, confirm they actually disclose the intended exceptions, and check that disclosures in one schedule are not deemed repeated across all reps unless intended. Over-broad or ambiguous disclosures can unintentionally expand exceptions, and missing disclosures create breach risk. The schedules and reps must be read as one integrated package.

### Assess Bring-Down And Accuracy Conditions

In transactions with a gap between signing and closing, reps are typically repeated as of closing through a bring-down certificate, and closing is conditioned on the reps being true. Identify the accuracy standard, whether it is "true and correct in all respects" or "true and correct in all material respects," and whether materiality is scraped for this purpose. A bring-down with a materiality qualifier can let a party walk or claim breach for changes between signing and closing. Understand who holds the closing condition and what remedies flow from inaccuracy.

### Connect Breach Consequences To Indemnity And Caps

A breached rep only matters if the consequences are enforceable and meaningful. Trace breach to the indemnity, survival period, deductibles, baskets, caps, and exclusions. Identify whether indemnity is the sole remedy or whether fraud and fundamental reps carve out of the cap. A favorable rep can be hollow if the indemnity cap is low, the survival period short, or the basket high. Conversely, a narrow rep can be costly if it is a fundamental rep with an uncapped indemnity. Always read the rep, the indemnity, and the limitation of liability together.

### Check Fundamental Versus Non-Fundamental Treatment

Transactions often distinguish fundamental reps, such as organization, authority, and capitalization, from business reps. Fundamental reps may carry longer survival, higher or uncapped indemnity, and stricter accuracy standards, because they go to the validity of the deal itself. Identify which reps are treated as fundamental and how that treatment differs, because the allocation of risk concentrates there. Misclassifying a rep can shift significant liability.

### Evaluate Accuracy Of Reps As A Closing Condition

Beyond indemnity, the accuracy of reps often operates as a closing condition that lets a party refuse to close or terminate if reps are inaccurate. Identify the standard, the consequences of inaccuracy, and whether minor inaccuracies are excused. A strict accuracy condition gives the buyer leverage to renegotiate or walk between signing and closing. Understand this leverage, because it operates independently of post-closing indemnity.

### Coordinate Reps With Due Diligence And Disclosures

Reps should align with what due diligence revealed. If diligence surfaced an issue, the rep should disclose it in the schedule rather than assert it away, because an undisclosed known issue can support fraud and indemnity claims. Confirm that the disclosing party can stand behind each rep as of the relevant date, and that the schedules capture the exceptions. Reps that conflict with diligence findings are a red flag for both sides.

### Watch For Anti-Reliance And Non-Reliance Clauses

Many agreements include anti-reliance clauses stating that the buyer is not relying on any statements outside the agreement, which can defeat fraud claims based on extra-contractual representations. Identify these clauses, because they change the consequences of a false rep and the value of pre-signing statements. Coordinate the reps, disclosures, and anti-reliance language so the risk allocation is internally consistent.

## Common Traps

### Confusing Reps, Warranties, And Covenants

Each carries different remedies and limitation rules. Label and draft each correctly.

### Ignoring Materiality And Knowledge Qualifiers

A qualified rep certifies far less than it appears. Map every qualifier.

### Treating Disclosure Schedules As Boilerplate

Schedules define the real scope of the reps. Read them as an integrated package.

### Missing The Bring-Down Standard

A materiality-qualified bring-down changes closing leverage. Identify the accuracy standard.

### Reviewing Reps Without The Indemnity And Caps

A strong rep is hollow under a low cap or short survival. Read them together.

### Misclassifying Fundamental Versus Business Reps

Risk concentrates in fundamental reps. Confirm the classification and treatment.

### Reps That Conflict With Due Diligence

An undisclosed known issue supports fraud. Disclose in the schedule instead.

### Overlooking Anti-Reliance Clauses

Anti-reliance changes the fraud consequences of a false rep. Coordinate it with the reps.

## Self-Check

- Are representations, warranties, and covenants distinguished, with each provision correctly labeled for its remedies and limitation treatment?
- Are materiality, knowledge, and disclosure qualifiers mapped to understand what each rep actually certifies?
- Are disclosure schedules read as an integrated part of the reps, with exceptions checked against the reps and cross-disclosure rules understood?
- Is the bring-down and accuracy standard identified, including whether materiality is scraped for closing condition purposes?
- Are breach consequences traced through indemnity, survival, deductibles, baskets, caps, and fraud carve-outs?
- Are fundamental reps distinguished from business reps, with the differing survival, cap, and accuracy treatment confirmed?
- Is the accuracy of reps analyzed as a closing condition independent of indemnity, including the consequences of inaccuracy?
- Are reps coordinated with due diligence findings, with known issues disclosed in schedules rather than asserted away?
- Are anti-reliance and non-reliance clauses identified and coordinated with the reps and disclosures?
- Does the output flag that final rep packages, indemnity structures, and disclosure decisions require qualified counsel?
