# Force Majeure And Material Adverse Change

Contracts allocate the risk of events the parties did not foresee and could not control. Force majeure, hardship, frustration, impossibility, and material adverse change clauses are the devices that do this work, and they interact in ways that are easy to get wrong. Agents often assume a force majeure clause is a standard list of disasters that excuses any non-performance, but enforceability turns on foreseeability, causation, notice, mitigation, and the specific wording of the clause and governing law. A party that invokes force majeure too casually may admit its own breach, forfeit defenses, or trigger termination rights. A party that ignores a material adverse change clause may miss a lawful exit from a deteriorating deal.

Use this skill before drafting risk-allocation language for disruptive events, before invoking or responding to a force majeure notice, before deciding whether to suspend or terminate performance, before walking away from a deal on a MAC assertion, or before advising on supply chain, sanctions, pandemic, or regulatory disruption. The goal is to force the agent to read the clause against the governing law's default doctrines, demand evidence of causation and mitigation, follow notice and procedural requirements strictly, and avoid waiving rights by silence or continued performance. These doctrines are intensely jurisdiction-specific; invocation and walk-away decisions require qualified counsel review.

## Core Rules

### Read Force Majeure As A Limitation, Not A General Excuse

A force majeure clause excuses non-performance caused by defined events, but it is not a blanket release from the contract. The scope is set by the clause's enumerated events, any catch-all, and the governing law's interpretation.

Identify:

- the enumerated force majeure events (act of God, war, terrorism, epidemic or pandemic, government action, strikes, natural disaster, fire, flood);
- whether there is a catch-all and what standard it applies (events beyond reasonable control, unforeseeable events, similar kind to enumerated events);
- whether the listed events are interpreted narrowly or broadly under the governing law;
- whether the clause requires the event to be unforeseeable, unavoidable, or insurmountable;
- whether performance must be impossible or merely more difficult or expensive.

In many civil-law jurisdictions force majeure is partly statutory; in common-law jurisdictions it is almost entirely contractual. Do not assume a U.S.-style clause works the same way under a civil code.

### Distinguish Force Majeure From Common-Law Doctrines

The contract's force majeure clause may sit alongside, or displace, default doctrines whose thresholds differ.

Map the relationship:

- impossibility or impracticability excuses performance that is objectively impossible or radically different;
- frustration of purpose applies when the principal purpose of the contract is destroyed by a supervening event;
- force majeure is contractual and its scope is whatever the parties wrote;
- hardship doctrines in some jurisdictions require renegotiation when performance becomes excessively onerous but not impossible;
- commercial impracticability under the UCC or equivalents has a high bar and rarely excuses mere price increases.

A party may have a force majeure argument but no impossibility defense, or vice versa. Analyze both and choose the stronger path.

### Establish Causation And Foreseeability

Invocation fails most often on causation and foreseeability, not on whether the event occurred.

Demand evidence:

- a direct causal link between the force majeure event and the specific non-performance;
- that the event, not ordinary commercial risk or the party's own failure, caused the delay or inability;
- that the event was beyond the party's reasonable control;
- that the event was not foreseeable at the time of contracting, where the clause or law requires foreseeability;
- that no alternative means of performance was reasonably available.

A pandemic, war, or sanction does not automatically excuse every affected contract. A party whose supplier chose a cheaper single-source route, or who failed to plan for a known risk, may not satisfy causation.

### Follow Notice, Mitigation, And Procedural Requirements

Force majeure rights are usually conditional on procedural steps, and missing them can waive the defense.

Confirm:

- the required notice content, timing, and method (often a short window after the party knew or should have known);
- the obligation to mitigate and continue mitigation throughout;
- the duty to resume performance promptly when the event ends;
- the requirement to update notice as circumstances change;
- any obligation to negotiate an alternative means of performance or to renegotiate in good faith;
- the consequences of continued partial performance during the event.

A late or defective notice, or continued performance without reservation, can be treated as a waiver. Document mitigation efforts contemporaneously.

### Define The Consequences: Suspension, Termination, Or Renegotiation

Force majeure clauses differ on what relief they provide. Read the consequence provisions carefully.

Determine:

- whether performance is merely suspended during the event;
- whether the clause allows termination after a defined duration of the event;
- whether extended force majeure converts into a termination right for the other party;
- whether the clause requires renegotiation or good-faith adjustment;
- how payment obligations are treated during suspension (accrual, deferral, or forgiveness);
- whether deposits, prepayments, or milestone payments are refunded or retained.

Do not assume suspension excuses payment; many clauses require continued payment for obligations unrelated to the disrupted performance.

### Analyze Material Adverse Change And Material Adverse Effect Clauses

MAC and MAE clauses appear in financing, acquisition, and commitment documents and function as conditions to closing or funding. They are interpreted narrowly and are difficult to invoke.

Assess:

- the defined scope of material adverse effect (financial condition, operations, business, prospects, assets);
- whether industry-wide or macroeconomic downturns are carved out;
- whether the effect must be durationally significant (often a material, long-lasting, non-temporary effect);
- whether the clause captures regulatory changes, litigation, or specific identified risks;
- whether the target's representations and disclosure schedules interact with the MAE definition;
- whether the consequence is a right to refuse to close, to reprice, or to terminate.

Courts in major jurisdictions rarely find an MAE has occurred based on ordinary volatility or short-term disruption. A walk-away based on MAC must be supported by a strong, well-documented record.

### Address Sanctions, Export Controls, And Regulatory Disruption

Modern contracts increasingly treat sanctions and regulatory change as force majeure or MAC events, but the wording matters.

Check:

- whether sanctions, export controls, or changes in law are enumerated events or caught by a catch-all;
- whether compliance with new sanctions excuses performance to sanctioned parties;
- whether the clause distinguishes events caused by a party's own non-compliance;
- whether a change in law that merely makes performance uneconomic is covered;
- whether regulatory approval delays or denials are addressed separately.

Sanctions regimes (OFAC, EU, UN) can make performance unlawful and may excuse it even absent a force majeure clause, but the analysis depends on the governing law and the specific sanctions program.

## Common Traps

### Invoking Force Majeure For Ordinary Commercial Hardship

Price increases, currency moves, or a lost customer are rarely force majeure. Invoking the clause for commercial reasons can backfire as an anticipatory breach.

### Missing The Notice Window

Many force majeure rights require prompt notice. A party that waits to see if things improve may waive the defense.

### Ignoring Mitigation

Failure to mitigate, or to document mitigation, can defeat an otherwise valid force majeure claim.

### Assuming A Pandemic Or War Automatically Excuses Performance

Causation and foreseeability still must be shown. The event must cause the specific non-performance, not merely create a difficult environment.

### Treating MAC Clauses As A Pricing Escape

MAC clauses are interpreted narrowly and rarely excuse ordinary commercial risk or short-term disruption. Invoking them lightly risks a breach claim and specific performance.

### Confusing Civil-Law And Common-Law Force Majeure

Civil codes may provide statutory force majeure or hardship; common-law regimes rely almost entirely on the contract. Transplanting one system's assumptions into the other is dangerous.

### Continuing To Perform Without Reservation

Accepting performance or continuing to perform after an event can waive force majeure rights or estop later invocation.

## Self-Check

- Is the force majeure clause read for enumerated events, catch-all standard, foreseeability requirement, and governing-law interpretation, rather than treated as a general excuse?
- Are force majeure, impossibility, impracticability, frustration, and hardship doctrines distinguished and the strongest path chosen?
- Is there evidence of direct causation, lack of control, unforeseeability, and the absence of alternative means of performance?
- Are notice timing, content, method, mitigation, update, and resumption obligations satisfied and documented?
- Are the consequences of the clause (suspension, termination, renegotiation, payment treatment, refund) clearly identified?
- Is the MAC or MAE clause analyzed for scope, carve-outs, durationality, industry-wide effects, and the strength of the record needed to invoke it?
- Are sanctions, export controls, and regulatory change provisions read for whether they excuse performance and whether own-non-compliance is excluded?
- Does the output avoid advising invocation or walk-away without qualified counsel review of the governing law and the specific facts?
