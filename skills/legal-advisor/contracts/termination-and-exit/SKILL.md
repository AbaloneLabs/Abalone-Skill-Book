# Termination And Exit

Exit terms often matter more than entry terms, because the moment a relationship sours is when the contract's real risk surfaces. The judgment problem is that termination is treated as a single right, when it is actually a chain of triggers, notice, cure, effects, wind-down, survival, and unwind obligations that determine whether a party can leave cleanly or is trapped. Agents often summarize a termination clause as "either party can terminate for convenience" while missing the notice window, the cure period that protects the breaching party, the data return and transition obligations that bind the exiting party, the early termination fee, and the post-termination clauses that survive. A party that cannot exit cleanly may be stuck with a bad vendor, a leaking data pipeline, or an obligation to keep paying.

Use this skill before reviewing, drafting, or negotiating termination, renewal, exit, transition, or unwind terms in any commercial agreement. The goal is to make the agent map every way a party can leave, the notice and cure required, the effects on payment and data, the wind-down obligations, the surviving duties, and the early-exit costs. This is contract support; final exit strategy and enforceability of termination fees and restrictions require qualified counsel.

## Core Rules

### Map Every Path To Termination

Termination is not one right. Identify every path: termination for convenience by either party, termination for cause or material breach, termination for insolvency or change of control, termination for regulatory or legal prohibition, and automatic termination on expiry or external event. For each path, identify who holds the right, what triggers it, what notice and cure are required, and what the effects are. A strong exit review lays out the matrix; a weak review quotes one clause.

### Scrutinize Notice And Cure Periods

Notice and cure periods determine how fast a party can actually leave and whether a breaching party can repair. Identify the required notice method, recipient, and period for each termination type. For cause termination, check whether a cure period is required, how long it is, whether it applies to curable breaches only, and whether repeated breaches shorten it. Notice that must be sent by registered mail to an obscure address, or cure periods that run for months, can make a termination right practically useless. Calendar the notice windows and renewal deadlines that gate exit.

### Define The Effects Of Termination Clearly

Termination changes obligations, and the contract should state exactly how. Identify what payments are due on termination, including work performed to date, non-refundable advances, true-ups, and termination fees. Determine what licenses, access, and rights end, and what continues for wind-down. Check whether the customer can continue using deliverables already paid for, and whether the supplier must stop all work. Ambiguous termination effects are a common source of post-exit disputes.

### Plan Wind-Down And Transition Obligations

Few relationships end instantly; most require a transition period. Identify transition services, data return or migration, knowledge transfer, assistance to a successor, and the duration and pricing of these obligations. Check whether transition is mandatory or optional, whether the supplier can charge for it, and whether the customer must cooperate. A contract without transition support can leave a customer stranded and a supplier exposed to business disruption claims. Wind-down obligations should be realistic and priced.

### Identify Surviving Obligations And Post-Termination Duties

Some obligations survive termination and continue to bind the parties. Identify which clauses survive, typically confidentiality, intellectual property, indemnity, limitation of liability, dispute resolution, governing law, audit, non-solicit, non-compete, and any accrued rights. Check the survival period, because obligations that survive "indefinitely" differ from those surviving two years. Confirm that survival is explicit; clauses presumed to survive can be disputed. Post-termination non-solicit and non-compete restrictions are particularly high-stakes and must be reasonable to be enforceable.

### Analyze Restitution, Unwind, And Reversion

Some terminations require unwinding the relationship, not just stopping it. Identify restitution or reimbursement obligations, reversion of property or data, destruction or return of confidential information, and license revocation. For advance payments, determine what is refundable and what is earned. For goods, determine what must be returned and in what condition. Unwind obligations can be operationally complex and should be specified, because vague "reasonable cooperation" language invites dispute.

### Evaluate Early Termination Fees And Liquidated Damages

Early exit often carries a cost. Identify early termination fees, liquidated damages, buy-out amounts, and remaining minimum commitments, and assess whether they are enforceable as liquidated damages or vulnerable as penalties. Enforceability depends on whether the amount reasonably estimates actual harm and is not a penalty, and the analysis is jurisdiction-specific. Check whether the fee is a fixed sum, a formula, or the remaining contract value, and whether it scales down over time. A termination fee that is actually a penalty may be unenforceable, leaving the parties to litigate real damages.

### Coordinate Exit With Data, IP, And Regulatory Obligations

Exit interacts with data protection, intellectual property, and regulatory duties. Confirm that data return or deletion complies with privacy law and retention requirements, that IP licenses end or continue as intended, and that regulatory notice or transition obligations are met. A clean commercial exit that leaves data stranded or IP licensed incorrectly creates compliance and liability risk. Coordinate exit planning with privacy, security, and compliance functions.

### Calendar Renewal And Notice Windows To Avoid Unwanted Continuation

Auto-renewal and renewal notice deadlines are exit risks. Identify whether the contract auto-renews, the notice window to prevent renewal, and the method of notice. Missing a renewal notice window can lock a party into another term. Calendar these dates operationally and assign an owner, because renewal risk is easy to miss and expensive when missed.

## Common Traps

### Treating Termination As A Single Right

There are multiple paths with different triggers, notice, and effects. Map all of them.

### Notice Or Cure Periods That Make Termination Impractical

Long cure periods or rigid notice methods can gut a termination right. Check that exit is actually usable.

### Silent Or Ambiguous Termination Effects

Vague effects invite dispute over payment, access, and continued use. Specify them.

### No Transition Or Data Return Plan

An exit without transition support strands the customer and exposes the supplier. Plan wind-down.

### Assuming Survival Without Checking

Clauses presumed to survive may not. Confirm survival explicitly and note the period.

### Early Termination Fees That Are Unenforceable Penalties

A fee that does not estimate real harm may be struck down as a penalty. Assess enforceability.

### Missing Auto-Renewal And Notice Windows

An overlooked renewal notice window locks a party into another term. Calendar it.

## Self-Check

- Is every termination path mapped, including convenience, cause, insolvency, change of control, and expiry, with trigger, holder, notice, cure, and effects for each?
- Are notice methods, recipients, periods, and cure terms checked for practical usability, with renewal and exit deadlines calendared?
- Are termination effects on payment, access, licenses, and continued use specified and unambiguous?
- Are wind-down, transition, data return, knowledge transfer, and successor assistance obligations defined, realistic, and priced?
- Are surviving obligations identified explicitly, with survival periods, especially for confidentiality, IP, indemnity, non-solicit, and non-compete?
- Are restitution, unwind, reversion, data destruction, and refund obligations specified?
- Are early termination fees, liquidated damages, and remaining commitments assessed for enforceability as estimates rather than penalties?
- Is exit coordinated with data protection, IP licensing, retention, and regulatory notice obligations?
- Are auto-renewal and renewal notice windows identified, calendared, and assigned an owner?
- Does the output flag that final exit strategy, enforceability of fees and restrictions, and regulatory unwind require qualified counsel?
