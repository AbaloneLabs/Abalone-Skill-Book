# Tax Treaties And Withholding

Cross-border payments of dividends, interest, royalties, and service fees are usually subject to withholding tax at the source country's domestic rate, which can be reduced or eliminated under a tax treaty. But treaty benefits are not automatic. They depend on residency, beneficial ownership, the nature of the income, limitation on benefits (LOB), principal purpose test (PPT), and proper claim procedures. Agents often apply a reduced treaty rate from a table without checking whether the recipient is the beneficial owner, whether a conduit structure defeats the claim, or whether the income falls in the right treaty article. A wrong claim creates withholding exposure for the payer, assessment risk for the recipient, and potential denial with interest.

Use this skill before advising on withholding rates, treaty benefit claims, conduit or back-to-back structures, residency certificates, double taxation relief, or mutual agreement procedure. The goal is to force the agent to test each treaty condition against the facts before concluding the rate. Treaty interpretation is jurisdiction-specific and depends on the exact treaty text (OECD Model, UN Model, US Model, and bilateral modifications) and any MLI modifications; any conclusion must be confirmed against the applicable treaty and current competent authority practice.

## Core Rules

### Start With Domestic Withholding, Then Apply The Treaty

Withholding is imposed by domestic law first. A treaty can only reduce or eliminate it; it rarely increases it.

Identify:

- the domestic withholding rate on the payment type (dividend, interest, royalty, service, technical service, rental);
- whether the payment is within the domestic withholding base at all;
- any domestic exemptions or reverse-charge mechanisms;
- the treaty rate that would apply if all conditions are met;
- the procedural claim required (form, residency certificate, timing).

Never quote a treaty rate without confirming the higher domestic rate and the reduction mechanics. The payer often remains liable for the difference if the claim fails.

### Confirm Residence Of The Recipient

Treaty benefits flow only to persons resident in a treaty country under that treaty's residency article. Residence is not incorporation alone.

Verify:

- tax residency of the recipient under the source country's domestic rules and the treaty country's rules;
- dual residency and the treaty tie-breaker (place of effective management for entities, center of vital interests for individuals);
- residency certificate issued by the competent authority, with current validity;
- whether a partnership, hybrid entity, or transparent vehicle qualifies and how benefits flow through;
- treaty residence vs. domestic residence mismatches.

A recipient that is not treaty-resident cannot claim benefits, even if incorporated in a treaty country with no substance.

### Establish Beneficial Ownership

Most modern treaties and domestic anti-conduit rules require the recipient to be the beneficial owner of the income to claim reduced rates on dividends, interest, and royalties.

Assess:

- whether the recipient has the right to use and enjoy the income;
- whether the recipient is contractually or legally obligated to pass the income on (conduit, back-to-back);
- whether the recipient bears real risk and has discretion;
- substance of the recipient (directors, office, employees, decision-making);
- the OECD beneficial ownership guidance and local anti-conduit rules;
- reporting under DAC6-type or mandatory disclosure where relevant.

A conduit entity inserted to access a treaty is vulnerable to denial even where each step is facially compliant.

### Apply Limitation On Benefits And Principal Purpose Test

Even a resident beneficial owner can be denied benefits under anti-treaty-shopping rules.

Check:

- LOB tests where the treaty includes them (ownership and base erosion, active trade or business, derivative benefits, headquarters company, competent authority discretion);
- PPT under the MLI or treaty protocol, which denies benefits if obtaining the benefit was one of the principal purposes of the arrangement;
- whether the arrangement has a genuine commercial purpose beyond treaty access;
- holding structures, intermediate jurisdictions, and ultimate parent residency;
- timing relative to MLI entry into force for each treaty.

PPT is broad and fact-based. A structure designed primarily to access a treaty rate is at high risk even if it passes formal LOB tests.

### Classify Income Under The Correct Treaty Article

The rate depends on the article. Misclassification is a common denial ground.

Classify:

- dividends (Article 10), including deemed dividends and dividend equivalents;
- interest (Article 11), including contingent, profit-participating, and hybrid instruments;
- royalties (Article 12), including software, know-how, and streaming;
- technical or consultancy fees where the treaty includes a separate article;
- capital gains (Article 13), by asset type;
- business profits (Article 7) requiring permanent establishment;
- independent personal services and employment income.

Hybrid instruments can be debt in one country and equity in another, producing different article classification and double taxation or double non-taxation. Resolve classification in both countries.

### Resolve Double Taxation Through Relief Mechanisms

Where both countries tax the same income, relief comes through credit, exemption, or deduction, depending on the treaty and domestic law.

Determine:

- exemption method vs. credit method under the treaty;
- underlying tax credit for dividends from subsidiaries;
- indirect credit tiers and ownership thresholds;
- source-country tax creditable in the residence country;
- timing of credit (year of payment vs. year of accrual);
- carryforward and carryback of excess credits;
- whether the income is taxed at all in the residence country (participation exemption, territorial systems).

A structure that achieves double non-taxation is often a target for anti-avoidance. Distinguish legitimate relief from aggressive positioning.

### Follow The Correct Claim And Documentation Procedure

A valid claim requires procedure, not just entitlement.

Confirm:

- claim form filed with the payer or the source-country authority;
- residency certificate valid for the relevant period;
- timing (relief at source vs. refund procedure);
- payer obligations to retain evidence and report;
- periodic refresh of certificates;
- consequences if the recipient's status changes mid-year.

Procedural failures can deny the benefit even where entitlement is clear. Build the claim into the payment workflow.

### Use Mutual Agreement Procedure For Disputes

Where two countries disagree on treaty application, the mutual agreement procedure (MAP) is the treaty remedy.

Assess:

- MAP availability under the treaty and any arbitration clause;
- the MAP deadline (often three years from first notification);
- whether to suspend collection or litigation during MAP;
- competent authority cooperation and likely outcome;
- effect of a MAP agreement on the taxpayer and related parties;
- cost, time, and disclosure tradeoffs.

MAP does not guarantee relief and can take years. Initiate early and in parallel with domestic remedies where permitted.

## Common Traps

### Quoting A Treaty Rate Without Beneficial Ownership

The table rate is not the answer. If the recipient is a conduit, the benefit is denied.

### Ignoring The Principal Purpose Test

PPT catches structures whose main purpose is treaty access. A formal LOB pass does not cure a PPT failure.

### Assuming Incorporation Equals Residence

A company incorporated in a treaty country may not be resident there, or may be dual-resident. Confirm residency under the treaty tie-breaker.

### Misclassifying Hybrid Instruments

An instrument treated as debt in one country and equity in another produces mismatched articles and potential double taxation. Resolve classification in both.

### Forgetting Payer Withholding Liability

The payer often remains liable for the domestic rate if the claim fails. Contract gross-up clauses do not cure penalties.

### Stale Residency Certificates

Certificates expire. A claim based on an expired certificate can be denied at audit.

### Skipping The MAP Deadline

MAP deadlines are short and jurisdictional. Missing them forecloses the treaty remedy.

### Treating Double Non-Taxation As Settled

Structures producing double non-taxation attract anti-avoidance scrutiny. Flag the risk rather than presenting it as safe.

## Self-Check

- Is the domestic withholding rate identified before applying any treaty reduction?
- Is the recipient's treaty residency confirmed, including dual residency and tie-breaker?
- Is beneficial ownership established, with conduit and back-to-back risks assessed?
- Are LOB and PPT tests applied, with commercial purpose documented?
- Is the income classified under the correct treaty article in both countries, with hybrid mismatches resolved?
- Is double taxation relief (credit, exemption, participation) determined with method, timing, and carryforward?
- Is the claim procedure followed with valid residency certificate, form, timing, and payer retention duties?
- Is MAP availability, deadline, and suspension of collection considered for disputes?
- Are conduit structures flagged for denial risk rather than presented as safe?
- Does the output reference the specific treaty text, MLI status, and competent authority practice, and recommend confirmation by a qualified international tax advisor?
