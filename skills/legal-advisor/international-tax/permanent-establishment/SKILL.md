# Permanent Establishment

Permanent establishment (PE) is the threshold that gives a source country the right to tax the business profits of a non-resident. Below the threshold, the non-resident generally has no corporate income tax exposure in that country (though it may still face VAT, payroll, or regulatory duties). Above it, the non-resident must register, file, allocate profits, and often face withholding and compliance burdens. Agents often conclude "no PE" because there is no office or no local subsidiary, ignoring dependent agents, construction duration, service days, digital nexus, warehousing, or employee travel. A missed PE creates back-tax, interest, penalties, and sometimes permanent establishment exposure for multiple years.

Use this skill before advising on cross-border sales, remote services, construction projects, employee secondments, commissionaire or agency arrangements, digital business models, warehousing, or any activity that could create a taxable presence. The goal is to force the agent to test every PE category against the facts before concluding no exposure. PE analysis is treaty- and jurisdiction-specific; the OECD Model, UN Model, US Model, MLI, and bilateral modifications all matter, and the conclusion must be confirmed against the applicable treaty and domestic significant-economic-presence rules.

## Core Rules

### Start With The Treaty Definition, Then Domestic Law

PE is primarily a treaty concept. Domestic law may assert tax on a broader basis (significant economic presence), but a treaty can cap that exposure for residents of the treaty partner.

Identify:

- whether a treaty applies between the source and residence country;
- the treaty's PE definition (OECD, UN, or US Model base, plus bilateral modifications);
- MLI modifications, including anti-fragmentation and independent agent exceptions;
- domestic significant economic presence or digital nexus rules that operate outside the treaty;
- the interaction where domestic law is broader than the treaty.

If a treaty applies, the source country can only tax business profits where a PE exists and only to the profits attributable to it.

### Test Fixed Place Of Business PE

A fixed place of business PE exists where there is a fixed facility through which the business is wholly or partly carried on.

Check:

- place of management, branch, office, factory, workshop, mine, warehouse (depending on treaty);
- permanence (duration and frequency of use);
- disposal of the facility (right to use, not necessarily ownership);
- whether business is carried on through it (not merely storage, display, or delivery under exceptions);
- preparatory or auxiliary exception and whether it survives the anti-fragmentation rule.

The preparatory or auxiliary exception is narrow. A warehouse that is integral to sales, or an office that negotiates and concludes contracts, generally fails the exception.

### Test Construction And Project PE

Construction, installation, assembly, and related supervisory activities create a PE above a duration threshold, often 6 or 12 months.

Compute:

- total duration including preparatory work, mobilization, and punch list;
- whether related projects are aggregated (single project) or separate;
- interruptions and their effect on the count;
- supervisory activities tied to the construction;
- the treaty's specific threshold (months vs. days).

Fragmenting a long project into short contracts does not avoid PE if the work is part of one project. The MLI anti-fragmentation rule reinforces aggregation.

### Test Service PE

Many treaties (especially UN Model) create a PE for services performed in a country above a threshold, even without a fixed place.

Track:

- aggregate days of employees or personnel performing services in the country;
- the threshold (often 183 days within any 12-month period, but varies);
- whether services are furnished to the same or related project;
- whether the services are of the same or connected nature;
- seconded personnel and who bears risk and direction.

Short business trips accumulate. A services business that sends staff repeatedly can cross the threshold even with no office.

### Test Dependent Agent PE

A dependent agent that habitually concludes contracts, or plays the principal role leading to routine conclusion, creates a PE for the principal.

Assess:

- whether the agent concludes contracts in the principal's name;
- whether the agent plays the principal role in contract conclusion without material modification (post-MLI);
- the agent's independence (commercial independence, acting in ordinary course);
- commissionaire arrangements and their post-MLI treatment;
- employees of the principal vs. third-party agents;
- exclusivity and authority.

Independent agents acting in the ordinary course of their business generally do not create a PE. Employees and dependent agents with contract authority usually do.

### Address Digital And Significant Economic Presence

Digital business models can create tax exposure without physical presence under unilateral digital services taxes, significant economic presence rules, or Amount A (if adopted).

Check:

- domestic significant economic presence thresholds (revenue, users, contracts);
- digital services tax applicability and rate;
- Amount A allocation (Pillar One) where in force;
- whether a treaty caps the exposure;
- interaction with VAT on digital services.

A digital business may face no income tax PE but still owe DST or VAT. Treat each tax separately.

### Evaluate Insurance, Off-Shore, And Special PE Rules

Some jurisdictions have special PE rules for insurance, off-shore activities, and natural resources.

Consider:

- insurance PE through collection of premiums or risk assumption;
- off-shore PE for activities related to the country performed abroad;
- natural resource PE (oil, gas, mining) and rig or vessel day counts;
- satellite, cable, and telecommunications PE rules where applicable.

These special rules can create PE where the general test would not.

### Determine The Consequences Of PE Creation

If a PE exists, the consequences go beyond income tax.

Map:

- corporate income tax registration and filing;
- profit attribution to the PE (separate and independent enterprise, OECD Authorized Approach);
- branch profits tax or remittance tax;
- withholding on branch remittances;
- VAT/GST registration and invoicing;
- payroll and social security for local and seconded employees;
- permanent establishment reporting and documentation;
- transfer pricing between the PE and head office.

Attribution of profits to a PE follows a separate-and-independent-enterprise method and requires functional and risk analysis. Do not assume a nominal attribution will hold.

## Common Traps

### Concluding No PE Because There Is No Office

PE can arise from agents, services, construction, warehousing, or digital nexus without a fixed office.

### Fragmenting A Long Project

Splitting a single construction or services project into short contracts does not avoid PE. Aggregation rules apply.

### Ignoring Employee Travel Days

Service PE counts accumulate across trips. A remote services business can cross the threshold without a local presence.

### Treating A Commissionaire As Independent

Commissionaires that conclude contracts or play the principal role create a dependent agent PE post-MLI.

### Assuming The Preparatory Exception Applies

Storage, display, delivery, and auxiliary offices only qualify if genuinely preparatory or auxiliary. Integral functions fail.

### Forgetting Digital Services Tax And VAT

No income tax PE does not mean no tax. DST and VAT can apply independently.

### Nominal Profit Attribution

Attributing minimal profits to a PE without functional analysis is a frequent audit adjustment. Use the separate-and-independent-enterprise method.

### Stale Treaty Text And MLI Status

PE definitions change with MLI and bilateral protocols. Confirm the current treaty text and reservations.

## Self-Check

- Is the applicable treaty identified, including model base, bilateral modifications, and MLI status?
- Is fixed place of business PE tested, including permanence, disposal, business carried on, and preparatory exception?
- Is construction and project PE tested with correct duration, aggregation, and interruptions?
- Is service PE tested with accurate day counts, related project rules, and secondment analysis?
- Is dependent agent PE tested, including contract conclusion, principal role, and independence?
- Are digital services tax, significant economic presence, and Amount A exposure assessed separately from treaty PE?
- Are insurance, off-shore, natural resource, and special PE rules considered where relevant?
- If a PE exists, are registration, profit attribution, branch tax, VAT, payroll, and reporting consequences mapped?
- Is profit attribution based on the separate-and-independent-enterprise method, not a nominal figure?
- Does the output flag PE as jurisdiction-specific and recommend confirmation against the applicable treaty and current rules by a qualified advisor?
