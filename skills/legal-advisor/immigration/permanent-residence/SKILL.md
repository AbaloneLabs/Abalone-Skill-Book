# Permanent Residence

Permanent residence (green card or equivalent) is a multi-year, high-stakes process that ties a worker to a sponsoring employer, depends on visa availability that fluctuates by category and country, and carries conditions, deadlines, and dependency risks for the whole family. Agents often treat it as an HR benefit and miss the preference-category strategy, the labor-certification rigor, the priority-date and visa-availability timing, the conditional-residence compliance, and the portability and dependency consequences that determine success or failure.

Use this skill before sponsoring an employee for permanent residence, selecting a preference category, preparing labor certification, managing conditional residence, advising on portability, or coordinating family and citizenship planning. The goal is to force the agent to connect category, eligibility, visa availability, sponsorship duties, conditions, and downstream consequences, and to escalate to immigration counsel for high-stakes matters. This skill is decision support, not legal advice.

## Core Rules

### Select The Preference Category Strategically

Category selection drives timelines, requirements, and outcomes.

Identify employment-based categories:

- priority workers (extraordinary ability, outstanding professors, multinational executives);
- advanced-degree or exceptional-ability professionals;
- skilled workers, professionals, and other workers (often requiring labor certification);
- special categories (investors, religious workers, certain physicians, national-interest waivers);
- the applicable preference system and caps.

And family-based categories:

- immediate relatives (spouses, parents, minor children of citizens);
- family-preference categories with annual caps;
- fiancé and derivative categories.

Compare viable categories by timeline, labor-certification requirement, country retrogression, and the candidate's profile. A category that is faster today may retrogress; a labor-certification route may be unavoidable for most workers.

### Assess Eligibility And Supporting Evidence

Each category has specific eligibility and evidence standards.

Address:

- qualifying degree, experience, and credentials (with equivalency evaluation where foreign);
- employer sponsorship and ability to pay;
- job offer and prevailing-wage requirements;
- national-interest or extraordinary-ability documentation standards;
- health, financial, and character admissibility;
- prior immigration history and violations;
- dependent eligibility.

Weak evidence is the leading cause of denials. Build the evidentiary record before filing, and address admissibility issues early.

### Navigate Labor Certification And Sponsorship Rigor

Labor certification (or equivalent) is required for most employment-based categories.

Address:

- prevailing-wage determination;
- recruitment and test-of-the-labor-market;
- minimum requirements and business necessity;
- layoff and notification effects;
- filing and processing timelines;
- the employer's ongoing ability-to-pay obligation;
- the bona fide job offer and intent to employ.

Labor certification is scrutinized. Tailor requirements to genuine business need, document recruitment, and be prepared to justify the business necessity of any restrictive requirement.

### Manage Priority Dates, Visa Availability, And Retrogression

Visa availability is the gating constraint for capped categories.

Track:

- the candidate's priority date and country of chargeability;
- the monthly visa bulletin and retrogression;
- the difference between final-action and filing dates;
- downgrade or upgrade strategies;
- portability after adjustment is pending;
- dependent derivative status and aging-out protection.

A viable case can wait years for a priority date to become current. Plan around retrogression and protect dependents from aging out.

### Handle Adjustment, Consular Processing, And Status Maintenance

The final stage has two paths with different implications.

Address:

- adjustment of status for those in the U.S. with lawful status;
- consular processing for those abroad or ineligible to adjust;
- maintaining lawful nonimmigrant status during the wait;
- travel and work authorization during pending adjustment;
- medical examinations and interviews;
- processing timelines and priority-date control.

Falling out of status during the multi-year wait can foreclose adjustment. Maintain status and use advance parole and work authorization carefully.

### Manage Conditional Residence And Waivers

Certain categories grant conditional residence with removal conditions.

Address:

- investor and marriage-based conditional residence;
- the deadline to petition for removal of conditions;
- evidence of ongoing investment or bona fide marriage;
- waivers for good-faith, extreme hardship, or battery;
- consequences of missing the removal-of-conditions deadline.

Missing the removal-of-conditions deadline can terminate residence and trigger removal. Calendar it years in advance and prepare evidence early.

### Coordinate Dependents And Family Planning

Permanent residence affects the whole family.

Address:

- derivative eligibility for spouses and children;
- aging-out protection and timing strategies;
- dependents' work authorization;
- family members' separate or follow-to-join processing;
- effect of the principal's status loss on dependents;
- birth and adoption during the process.

Dependent timing can make or break a family's immigration plan. Coordinate the whole family's strategy, not just the principal's.

### Plan The Path To Citizenship And Long-Term Status

Permanent residence is often a stepping stone to citizenship.

Address:

- continuous residence and physical-presence requirements;
- good moral character standards;
- tax-filing and child-support obligations;
- selective-service registration;
- time outside the country and its effect;
- dual-citizenship considerations;
- effect of extended travel or relocation on residence.

Extended absences or certain conduct can jeopardize both residence and naturalization. Advise on maintenance and naturalization eligibility.

### Manage Employer Sponsorship Obligations And Portability

Sponsorship creates duties and the worker gains portability rights.

Address:

- the employer's good-faith intent to employ;
- ability-to-pay obligations throughout the process;
- effect of layoffs, restructurings, or role changes;
- portability to a new employer after adjustment is pending for a required period;
- the new role's same-or-similar occupational classification;
- revocation risk if the worker leaves too early.

Sponsoring and then restructuring the role, or the worker porting to a non-similar role, can create revocation and compliance risk. Align sponsorship with genuine, stable employment.

## Common Traps

### Category Selection For Convenience

Suboptimal categories cause years of delay. Compare viable options by timeline and retrogression.

### Weak Labor-Certification Evidence

Scrutinized applications fail on recruitment and business-necessity documentation. Build the record before filing.

### Ignoring Priority-Date Retrogression

A viable case can wait years. Plan around retrogression and protect dependents.

### Falling Out Of Status During The Wait

Status lapse can foreclose adjustment. Maintain status and manage travel.

### Missing Removal-Of-Conditions Deadlines

Missing the deadline can terminate residence. Calendar years ahead.

### Neglecting Dependent And Aging-Out Planning

Dependent timing can break a family plan. Coordinate the whole family.

### Restructuring The Sponsored Role

Role changes can trigger revocation and portability classification disputes. Align sponsorship with stable employment.

## Self-Check

- Is the preference category selected by comparing employment-based and family-based options on timeline, labor-certification requirement, caps, and retrogression?
- Is eligibility assessed with qualifying credentials, equivalency, sponsorship, ability to pay, prevailing wage, admissibility, and dependent eligibility?
- Is labor certification handled with prevailing wage, recruitment, business necessity, layoff effects, timelines, and ongoing ability-to-pay?
- Are priority dates, visa bulletin, retrogression, downgrade or upgrade, portability, and dependent aging-out tracked and planned?
- Are adjustment versus consular processing, status maintenance, travel and work authorization, medical exams, and interviews managed?
- Are conditional residence removal-of-conditions deadlines, evidence, waivers, and consequences handled with advance calendaring?
- Are dependents coordinated for derivative eligibility, aging-out protection, work authorization, follow-to-join, and status-loss effects?
- Is the path to citizenship planned with continuous residence, physical presence, good moral character, tax and support obligations, travel effects, and dual-citizenship considerations?
- Are employer sponsorship duties (good-faith intent, ability to pay, restructuring effects, portability classification, revocation risk) managed?
- Does the output escalate category strategy, labor certification, retrogression, conditional residence, and portability disputes to qualified immigration counsel?
