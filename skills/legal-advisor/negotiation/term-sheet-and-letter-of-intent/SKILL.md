# Term Sheet And Letter Of Intent

A term sheet or letter of intent is the bridge between a commercial handshake and a binding contract, and it is one of the most litigation-prone documents in transactional practice. The central judgment problem is that the parties often treat the term sheet as a non-binding summary of intent while behaving as if the deal is done, and the law may treat some provisions as binding even when the parties did not intend that. Agents often mark a term sheet "non-binding" without distinguishing which provisions (confidentiality, exclusivity, expenses, governing law) are intended to bind, omit the language that prevents reliance, or allow the commercial terms to drift between the term sheet and the definitive agreement. The result is a preliminary document that creates binding obligations, estoppel, or a duty to negotiate in good faith that the parties did not anticipate.

Use this skill before drafting or signing a term sheet, letter of intent, or heads of terms, before granting or accepting exclusivity, before relying on preliminary terms, or before deciding whether to walk away from a deal after a term sheet is signed. The goal is to force the agent to separate binding from non-binding provisions, manage exclusivity and good faith exposure, prevent reliance and estoppel, and align the term sheet with the intended definitive agreement. The binding effect of preliminary documents is jurisdiction-specific; term sheets in significant transactions should be reviewed by qualified counsel.

## Core Rules

### Separate Binding From Non-Binding Provisions Explicitly

The most important drafting decision is which provisions bind the parties and which are merely an agreement to agree. A blanket "non-binding" label is often insufficient and can be contradicted by the parties' conduct or by specific provisions that look binding.

Classify each provision:

- binding provisions typically include confidentiality, exclusivity or no-shop, expenses, governing law, dispute resolution, and sometimes a duty to negotiate in good faith;
- non-binding provisions typically include the commercial terms (price, structure, valuation, conditions);
- mixed provisions, such as a commitment to negotiate in good faith toward a definitive agreement, may be binding as to the process but not as to the outcome.

State expressly which provisions are binding and which are not, and avoid language that implies a binding commitment to close on the commercial terms.

### Draft The Binding Provisions Carefully

The binding provisions of a term sheet are real contracts and must be drafted with the same care as a definitive agreement.

Address for binding provisions:

- confidentiality: scope, duration, return or destruction, and permitted disclosures;
- exclusivity or no-shop: duration, scope (parties, assets, jurisdictions), permitted carve-outs (fiduciary duties, unsolicited bids), and consequences of breach;
- expenses: who bears transaction costs regardless of closing;
- governing law and dispute resolution;
- any good faith or obligation to negotiate clause, with defined scope;
- public announcements and regulatory notification.

Exclusivity is the most consequential binding provision: it can prevent a party from pursuing a better offer for months. Define its duration and scope tightly and include fiduciary or unsolicited-bid carve-outs where appropriate.

### Manage Good Faith And The Duty To Negotiate

Some jurisdictions recognize a duty to negotiate in good faith, and even where they do not, the parties may create one by contract. This duty can constrain the ability to walk away.

Consider:

- whether the governing law recognizes a pre-contractual duty of good faith;
- whether the term sheet creates a contractual duty to negotiate in good faith and what that requires;
- the line between good faith negotiation and an obligation to reach an agreement;
- the consequences of bad faith termination, including liability for wasted costs in some jurisdictions;
- the risk that prolonged negotiation creates reliance.

A duty to negotiate in good faith is not the same as a duty to close, but breaching it can expose a party to damages for negotiation costs. Decide deliberately whether to include such a clause.

### Prevent Reliance, Estoppel, And Promissory Estoppel

Preliminary terms can create reliance that supports an estoppel or promissory estoppel claim, even when labeled non-binding.

Manage:

- include an express no-reliance or non-reliance clause covering pre-definitive statements;
- state that no binding obligation arises except for the enumerated binding provisions;
- avoid conduct that induces reliance, such as repeated assurances or substantial preparatory expenditures by the other side;
- use subject-to-contract or subject-to-definitive-agreement language consistently;
- clarify that the parties do not intend to be bound until a definitive agreement is executed.

Promissory estoppel can enforce a non-binding promise where the other party reasonably relied on it to its detriment. Clear non-reliance language and consistent conduct reduce this risk.

### Align Commercial Terms With The Intended Definitive Agreement

The term sheet should accurately reflect the commercial deal, because it becomes the template for the definitive agreement and the reference point for any later dispute about intent.

Confirm:

- the structure (asset, stock, merger), price or valuation methodology, and form of consideration;
- the conditions to closing, including regulatory and diligence;
- the key reps, indemnities, and risk allocation principles;
- the treatment of employees, contracts, and IP;
- any earnout, escrow, or post-closing adjustment in principle;
- the timeline and any long-stop date.

Vague or inconsistent commercial terms invite disputes during definitive drafting. Capture the key terms with enough specificity to anchor the negotiation without becoming inadvertently binding.

### Control The Transition To The Definitive Agreement

The path from term sheet to definitive agreement is where deals often stall or break. Manage the transition deliberately.

Address:

- a timeline and milestones for definitive drafting;
- the allocation of drafting responsibility;
- the scope of remaining diligence and its effect on terms;
- the conditions under which a party may walk away without breach;
- the survival of the binding provisions if the deal fails;
- the long-stop date after which exclusivity and the negotiation commitment end.

A term sheet without a clear path to the definitive agreement can trap a party in exclusivity with no leverage. Build the transition mechanics and the exit.

### Address Regulatory And Approval Considerations Early

Some deals require approvals that affect timing and feasibility, and the term sheet should flag them.

Identify:

- antitrust or merger notification requirements;
- sectoral approvals (banking, telecom, media, defense, foreign investment);
- shareholder, board, or works council approvals;
- regulatory conditions that could block or condition the deal.

Flagging these early prevents surprises that delay closing and supports the reasonableness of the conditions and timeline.

## Common Traps

### A Blanket "Non-Binding" Label

Courts may find specific provisions binding based on intent and conduct. State expressly which provisions bind.

### Overbroad Or Open-Ended Exclusivity

Exclusivity prevents better offers and can trap a party. Define duration, scope, and carve-outs.

### Inadvertently Creating A Duty To Negotiate In Good Faith

A good faith clause can constrain walk-away rights and expose a party to wasted-cost damages. Include it deliberately or omit it.

### Reliance And Promissory Estoppel From Conduct

Repeated assurances and preparatory expenditures can create reliance despite a non-binding label. Use non-reliance language and consistent conduct.

### Vague Commercial Terms

Imprecise price, structure, or conditions invite disputes during definitive drafting and may reveal unintended commitments.

### No Transition Or Exit Mechanics

A term sheet without a path to definitive agreement or a long-stop date traps parties in exclusivity.

### Missing Regulatory And Approval Flags

Approvals discovered late delay closing and can break the deal. Identify them in the term sheet.

## Self-Check

- Are binding and non-binding provisions separated explicitly, with confidentiality, exclusivity, expenses, governing law, and good faith identified as binding where intended?
- Are the binding provisions (especially exclusivity) drafted with defined duration, scope, carve-outs, and consequences of breach?
- Is the duty to negotiate in good faith managed, recognizing whether the governing law imposes it and whether the term sheet creates it?
- Are reliance, estoppel, and promissory estoppel risks addressed through no-reliance clauses, subject-to-contract language, and consistent conduct?
- Are the commercial terms aligned with the intended definitive agreement, capturing structure, price, conditions, reps, indemnities, and timeline?
- Is the transition to the definitive agreement managed with milestones, drafting responsibility, walk-away conditions, and a long-stop date?
- Are regulatory and approval requirements (antitrust, sectoral, shareholder, foreign investment) flagged early?
- Does the output recommend qualified counsel review the binding effect of the term sheet under the governing jurisdiction?
