# Negotiation Ethics And Good Faith

Negotiation sits in an uncomfortable space between adversarial advocacy and the duty not to deceive. The law permits a wide zone of puffing, posturing, and strategic silence, but it draws lines at fraudulent misrepresentation, breaches of a duty to disclose, and conduct that violates good faith obligations or an officer of the court's duty of candor. Agents often assume that anything said in negotiation is inadmissible or non-binding, that "buyer beware" excuses misrepresentation, or that a general duty of good faith governs every deal. None of these assumptions is reliable. The judgment problem is to know where the lines are drawn in the relevant jurisdiction and context, to stay on the defensible side of them, and to recognize when a tactic that feels routine creates fraud, estoppel, or ethics exposure.

Use this skill before negotiating a settlement, contract, or transaction, before deciding what must be disclosed and what may be withheld, before making statements that could be relied on, or before advising on the ethical limits of a tactic. The goal is to force the agent to distinguish puffing from misrepresentation, identify any duty to disclose, apply good faith and candor requirements honestly, and avoid creating reliance or estoppel through negotiation conduct. Negotiation ethics are jurisdiction- and role-specific; high-stakes negotiations and any conduct near the fraud line should be reviewed by qualified counsel.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Puffing From Misrepresentation

The law allows sellers and negotiators a zone of opinion, estimate, and puffery that is not actionable. Crossing into false statements of present or past fact creates misrepresentation exposure.

Classify statements:

- opinions, predictions, and estimates ("this is a great opportunity," "we expect strong growth") are generally puffing and not actionable;
- statements of present or past fact ("the revenue was X last year," "there is no pending litigation") must be true when made;
- statements of intention or future conduct may be misrepresentation if the speaker had no intention of following through, or if accompanied by a present false fact;
- half-truths can be misrepresentation when they create a misleading impression by omitting material facts the speaker had a duty to disclose.

The line turns on whether a reasonable listener would understand the statement as verifiable fact or as opinion, and on whether the speaker knew it was false. When in doubt, phrase statements as opinion and avoid specific factual claims that cannot be supported.

### Identify Any Duty To Disclose

Silence is generally permissible in arm's-length negotiation, but a duty to disclose arises in specific circumstances and changes the analysis.

Recognize duties to disclose arising from:

- a fiduciary or confidential relationship (lawyer-client, director-shareholder, partner-partner);
- partial or ambiguous statements that become misleading without clarification;
- active concealment of material facts;
- specific statutory or regulatory disclosure duties (securities, consumer protection, real estate);
- a contractual duty to disclose or a representation that triggers update obligations;
- correction of a prior statement that later becomes false.

A negotiator with a fiduciary duty to the other side, or who has made a partial statement, may have to correct or supplement it even absent a direct question. Identify any such duty before relying on silence.

### Apply Good Faith Obligations Accurately

Good faith is widely misunderstood. It does not require parties to be generous or to disclose everything, but it does constrain certain conduct depending on the context and jurisdiction.

Distinguish:

- the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing in contract performance, which prevents evasive or opportunistic performance but rarely requires disclosure in negotiation;
- statutory or codified good faith duties in some jurisdictions, which may be broader;
- pre-contractual good faith duties recognized in some civil law jurisdictions, which can impose liability for breaking off negotiations abruptly;
- good faith in settlement negotiations, which may require honesty about material facts;
- the distinction between good faith in formation and good faith in performance.

Do not assume a universal duty of good faith governs all negotiation, and do not assume it is toothless where it does apply. Match the analysis to the governing law and the relationship.

### Respect The Duty Of Candor For Officers Of The Court

Lawyers negotiating on behalf of clients owe duties beyond ordinary commercial candor, because they are officers of the court.

Apply:

- the prohibition on knowingly making false statements of material fact or law to a tribunal;
- limits on false statements of fact to third parties in negotiation, which vary by jurisdiction but are generally narrower than the duty to the court;
- the duty of candor in settlement discussions with the court or in court-connected mediation;
- the prohibition on offering evidence known to be false.

A lawyer's false statement of material fact in negotiation may be an ethics violation in some jurisdictions even if a non-lawyer's would be permissible puffing. Know the local rule.

### Avoid Creating Reliance And Estoppel

Statements and conduct in negotiation can create reliance or estoppel even without a formal representation.

Manage:

- disclaimers that statements are preliminary, subject to contract, or not to be relied on;
- the risk that repeated assurances create a pattern of reliance;
- the line between negotiation positions and representations that survive into the contract;
- integration and no-reliance clauses that limit pre-contractual reliance claims;
- the treatment of side communications, emails, and term sheets that may be construed as binding or relied on.

A party that induces reliance through negotiation statements, then disclaims them in the final contract, may still face estoppel or misrepresentation claims. Use clear subject-to-contract and no-reliance language.

### Handle Settlement Negotiation Ethics Specially

Settlement negotiations carry their own rules and protections.

Address:

- the general inadmissibility of settlement offers and concessions, and the limits of that protection;
- the duty of good faith in some mandatory or court-connected mediation contexts;
- the prohibition on fraud in settlement, including lying about material facts such as insurance coverage or assets;
- authority to settle, confirming that the negotiator has actual authority or disclosing the need for ratification;
- the binding effect of a settlement agreement once reached, even if oral in some jurisdictions.

Lying about material facts in settlement can void the agreement and create liability, even though settlement positions are generally inadmissible at trial.

### Document The Basis For Positions

A defensible negotiation record shows that positions were supportable and that disclosures were appropriate.

Maintain:

- the factual basis for material statements made;
- the scope of any disclosures and the disclaimers used;
- the authority under which the negotiator acted;
- the evolution of positions and the reasons for concessions;
- the subject-to-contract and no-reliance language relied on.

Documentation protects against later claims of misrepresentation or bad faith and supports the enforceability of the resulting agreement.

## Common Traps

### Treating All Negotiation Statements As Inadmissible

Settlement positions are generally inadmissible, but false statements of material fact may create liability and may be admissible on fraud.

### Crossing From Puffing Into False Fact

Specific, verifiable statements of fact must be true. Phrase uncertain claims as opinion.

### Relying On Silence When A Duty To Disclose Exists

Fiduciary relationships, partial statements, and active concealment can create a duty to disclose. Identify any such duty.

### Assuming Good Faith Requires Generosity

Good faith constrains opportunism but rarely requires disclosure in arm's-length negotiation. Match the doctrine to the governing law.

### Ignoring The Lawyer's Heightened Candor Duties

Officers of the court face limits on false statements that may not bind non-lawyers. Know the local rule.

### Creating Reliance Through Repeated Assurances

A pattern of assurances can create estoppel despite a final no-reliance clause. Use clear disclaimers throughout.

### Lying About Authority Or Material Facts In Settlement

Misrepresenting authority, insurance, or assets can void a settlement and create liability.

## Self-Check

- Are statements classified as puffing (opinion, prediction, estimate) versus misrepresentation (false present or past fact), and are uncertain claims phrased as opinion?
- Is any duty to disclose identified, including fiduciary relationships, partial statements, active concealment, and statutory duties?
- Are good faith obligations applied accurately, distinguishing performance duties from pre-contractual duties and matching the governing law?
- Are the heightened candor duties for lawyers as officers of the court respected, including limits on false statements of fact?
- Are reliance and estoppel risks managed through subject-to-contract, no-reliance clauses, and consistent disclaimers?
- Are settlement negotiation ethics handled, including inadmissibility limits, good faith in mediation, fraud prohibitions, and authority to settle?
- Is the negotiation record documented with the factual basis for statements, disclosures, disclaimers, and authority?
- Does the output recommend qualified counsel review of any tactic near the fraud, estoppel, or ethics line?
