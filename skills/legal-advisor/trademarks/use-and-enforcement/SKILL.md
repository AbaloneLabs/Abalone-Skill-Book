# Use And Enforcement

Trademark rights depend on use. A registration that is not supported by continuous use can be canceled for abandonment, and rights that are not enforced against infringers can be weakened or lost. Agents often focus on obtaining registrations and treat enforcement as optional, when in fact the value of a trademark is maintained only through proper use documentation, active monitoring, and timely action against infringers. Conversely, aggressive enforcement against non-confusing uses can waste resources, invite counterclaims, and create reputational harm. Enforcement must be targeted, proportionate, and grounded in a real likelihood of confusion analysis, not reflexive letter-sending.

Use this skill before documenting trademark use, before sending a cease and desist letter, before filing an opposition or cancellation, before initiating infringement litigation, or when responding to an accusation of infringement. The goal is to force the agent to maintain use evidence, monitor strategically, evaluate infringement before acting, and choose the right enforcement tool. Trademark enforcement is jurisdiction-specific and litigation-heavy; it requires qualified trademark counsel.

## Core Rules

### Maintain Continuous Use And Document Specimens

Trademark rights arise from use and are maintained by continuous use. Gaps in use can lead to abandonment, and enforcement depends on proof of use.

Maintain:

- continuous use of the mark in commerce on the identified goods or services;
- specimens showing the mark as used, such as packaging, labels, website screenshots, advertising, and point-of-sale displays;
- records of dates of first use anywhere and in commerce;
- evidence of use for each class and each good or service in the registration;
- proper use guidelines to prevent the mark from becoming generic;
- policing of licensees and partners to ensure quality control, since naked licensing can void rights.

A registration without supporting use is vulnerable to cancellation. Document use continuously to support maintenance filings and enforcement.

### Monitor For Infringing And Conflicting Uses

Rights not policed can be lost or weakened. Active monitoring catches infringers early.

Monitor:

- new trademark applications through watch services;
- domain registrations and websites;
- social media, app stores, and marketplaces;
- trade press and advertising;
- competitor product launches;
- counterfeits on e-commerce platforms;
- geographic expansion of known competitors.

Monitoring should prioritize high-risk channels and marks most likely to confuse. Not every similar use warrants action, but ignoring systematic infringement weakens the portfolio and invites genericide claims.

### Evaluate Infringement Before Acting

Before sending a demand, evaluate whether there is a real likelihood of confusion and whether enforcement is wise. Reflexive enforcement wastes resources and can backfire.

Evaluate:

- likelihood of confusion across mark similarity, goods or services, channels, consumers, mark strength, intent, and actual confusion;
- the strength of the senior mark and any weaknesses such as descriptiveness;
- the defendant's defenses, including fair use, descriptive use, parody, first amendment, and prior rights;
- the risk of a counterclaim for cancellation or declaratory judgment;
- the cost and likelihood of success of litigation;
- the reputational risk of aggressive enforcement, especially against small users, critics, or parodies;
- alternative remedies such as coexistence, license, or rebrand assistance.

Sending a demand to a non-infringer can trigger a declaratory judgment action in the defendant's home court. Evaluate before acting.

### Choose The Right Enforcement Tool

Enforcement ranges from informal contact to litigation. Match the tool to the situation.

Tools include:

- a cease and desist letter setting out rights and demands;
- negotiated coexistence or consent agreements;
- takedown notices to platforms, marketplaces, and social media;
- opposition during the publication period;
- cancellation of the infringer's registration;
- administrative proceedings such as UDRP for domains;
- customs recordation and border enforcement against counterfeits;
- litigation for injunction, damages, and fees.

A cease and desist is often the first step, but it should be calibrated. For clear counterfeits, litigation and customs action may be appropriate immediately. For marginal uses, a softer approach may preserve relationships and avoid escalation.

### Draft Cease And Desist Letters Carefully

A poorly drafted demand can create liability, admissions, or escalation. Draft with precision.

Include:

- identification of the senior mark and registration;
- the specific infringing use and why it confuses;
- a clear demand and deadline;
- a request for information on use, dates, and scope;
- reservation of rights;
- avoidance of overbroad or threatening language;
- consideration of whether to offer a license or transition period.

Avoid making unsupported claims, demanding destruction of materials without basis, or threatening criminal action you cannot pursue. Overreaching demands invite adverse publicity and counterclaims.

### Pursue Opposition And Cancellation Strategically

Administrative proceedings before the trademark board can resolve conflicts without full litigation.

Pursue:

- opposition during the 30-day publication window, extendable;
- cancellation of registrations on grounds of likelihood of confusion, priority, descriptiveness, abandonment, fraud, or genericness;
- discovery, testimony, and briefing;
- settlement through consent, coexistence, or assignment;
- coordination with parallel litigation and the effect of board decisions.

Opposition and cancellation are less expensive than federal litigation but still adversarial and require counsel. They are effective against applicants and registrants but not against common law users without registrations.

### Litigate Infringement With Clear Goals

Litigation is the strongest remedy but the most expensive. Enter it with clear goals.

Define:

- the relief sought, injunction, damages, destruction, and fees;
- the likelihood of confusion evidence and actual confusion;
- the validity and strength of the senior mark, anticipating challenges;
- the defendant's defenses and counterclaims;
- the forum, including where the defendant resides and where confusion occurs;
- the discovery burden and cost;
- settlement leverage and alternative outcomes.

Litigation can produce broad injunctions and significant damages, including enhanced damages and fees for willful infringement and counterfeiting. But it can also expose the senior mark to cancellation.

### Address Counterfeiting And Online Infringement

Counterfeits and online infringement require specialized tools.

Address:

- customs recordation to seize counterfeit imports;
- platform takedown programs for marketplaces and social media;
- UDRP and URS proceedings for infringing domains;
- investigations and supply chain enforcement;
- criminal referrals for large-scale counterfeiting;
- enhanced damages and statutory remedies for counterfeiting.

Counterfeiting often warrants aggressive, multi-channel enforcement because the harm is direct and ongoing.

## Common Traps

### Refluxive Enforcement Without Confusion Analysis

Sending demands to non-infringers wastes resources and risks declaratory judgment actions. Evaluate first.

### Failing To Maintain Use Evidence

A registration without continuous use is vulnerable to cancellation. Document specimens.

### Ignoring Infringement

Systematic infringement weakens the mark and invites genericide. Monitor and act.

### Overbroad Or Threatening Demand Letters

Overreaching demands invite adverse publicity and counterclaims. Calibrate language.

### Choosing Litigation Without Goals

Litigation without clear goals and settlement leverage wastes budget. Define outcomes first.

### Exposing The Mark To Cancellation

Aggressive litigation can prompt counterclaims to cancel the senior mark. Assess validity before suing.

### Naked Licensing

Failing to police licensee quality control can void trademark rights. Maintain quality control.

### Treating Opposition As Cheap

Opposition and cancellation are litigation. They require counsel and budget.

## Self-Check

- Is continuous use maintained and documented with specimens, dates of first use, evidence for each class and good or service, proper use guidelines, and licensee quality control?
- Is monitoring conducted across applications, domains, social media, marketplaces, trade press, competitors, and counterfeits, prioritized by risk?
- Is infringement evaluated for likelihood of confusion, mark strength, defendant defenses, counterclaim risk, cost, success likelihood, reputational risk, and alternatives before acting?
- Is the enforcement tool matched to the situation, including cease and desist, coexistence, takedown, opposition, cancellation, UDRP, customs, and litigation?
- Are cease and desist letters drafted with identification, specific demands, deadlines, information requests, reserved rights, and without overbroad threats?
- Are opposition and cancellation pursued strategically with grounds, discovery, settlement, and coordination with litigation?
- Is litigation entered with clear relief goals, confusion evidence, validity assessment, defense and counterclaim analysis, forum, discovery burden, and settlement leverage?
- is counterfeiting and online infringement addressed through customs, platform takedowns, UDRP, investigations, criminal referrals, and enhanced remedies?
- Are naked licensing and genericide risks managed through quality control and proper use?
- Does the output avoid asserting infringement or validity and instead recommend qualified trademark counsel for the specific marks, uses, and jurisdiction?
