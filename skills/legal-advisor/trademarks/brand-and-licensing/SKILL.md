# Brand And Licensing

A brand's value can be multiplied through licensing, co-branding, and franchising, but each arrangement risks diluting the brand if not structured and controlled. Agents often draft a trademark license as a simple permission to use the mark, when trademark licensing requires quality control to preserve rights, clear scope to prevent overuse, audit rights to capture royalties, and termination mechanics to reclaim the brand. A license without quality control is naked licensing that can void the trademark. A co-branding deal without control over the partner's product can taint the brand. A franchise without compliance with franchise disclosure laws can create liability and rescission. Brand monetization is a contract and compliance discipline as much as a marketing exercise.

Use this skill before licensing a trademark, before entering a co-branding or franchise arrangement, before assigning a brand, or when auditing existing brand deals for quality control and compliance. The goal is to force the agent to structure the deal to preserve trademark rights, control brand use, capture value, and comply with franchise and consumer protection law. Brand and licensing agreements are jurisdiction-specific; they require qualified trademark and franchise counsel.

## Core Rules

### Require Quality Control In Every License

Trademark licensing requires the licensor to exercise quality control over the licensed goods or services. A license without quality control is naked licensing, which can void the trademark.

Include:

- the licensor's right to approve products, services, materials, and marketing;
- quality standards and specifications;
- inspection and audit rights;
- approval timelines and procedures;
- remedies for non-compliance, including cure and termination;
- ongoing oversight obligations, not just a one-time approval.

Quality control must be real, not nominal. A contract that grants approval rights that the licensor never exercises is still naked licensing. Document oversight to preserve rights.

### Define License Scope Precisely

The scope defines what the licensee may do. Vague scope invites overuse and disputes.

Define:

- the licensed marks and any logos or variations;
- the licensed goods or services;
- the licensed territory, including online and cross-border reach;
- exclusivity versus non-exclusivity;
- field of use restrictions;
- sublicense rights and conditions;
- channels of distribution;
- co-marketing and co-branding permissions;
- duration and renewal terms.

A license that omits territory or field of use can be read broadly. A license that grants sublicensing without conditions can let the brand spread to uncontrollable users.

### Structure Royalties, Minimums, And Audit Rights

The economics of a license depend on the royalty structure and the ability to verify it.

Structure:

- the royalty base and rate, whether percentage of revenue, unit fee, or lump sum;
- minimum guaranteed royalties to ensure commitment;
- reporting frequency, format, and content;
- audit rights, notice periods, and cost allocation;
- payment terms, currency, and taxes;
- late payment interest and remedies;
- the effect of underpayment and the threshold for audit cost shifting.

Audit rights are meaningless without recordkeeping obligations and a meaningful threshold. Require the licensee to retain records for a defined period and to make them available on notice.

### Manage Co-Branding And Joint Marketing

Co-branding combines two brands on a product or campaign. It can build equity or taint both brands.

Control:

- which party approves the co-branded product and marketing;
- the relative prominence of each brand;
- quality standards for the co-branded offering;
- allocation of liability for defects, recalls, and consumer claims;
- ownership of co-created materials and trademarks;
- termination rights and post-termination use of co-branded assets;
- exclusivity and restrictions on co-branding with competitors.

A co-branding deal where the partner's product fails can damage both brands. Allocate liability and build in quality and exit controls.

### Comply With Franchise Laws When Structure Triggers Them

Franchising is heavily regulated. A license that includes marketing support, a system, and a fee can be a franchise requiring disclosure and registration.

Determine whether the arrangement is a franchise by checking:

- whether the licensee operates under the licensor's mark;
- whether the licensor provides significant marketing support or a system;
- whether the licensee pays a fee;
- whether the relationship meets the jurisdiction's franchise definition.

If it is a franchise, comply with:

- pre-sale disclosure document requirements;
- registration in franchise registration states;
- relationship laws governing termination and renewal;
- advertising and earnings claim restrictions;
- cooling-off and rescission rights.

Misclassifying a franchise as a license can trigger rescission rights, penalties, and personal liability for principals. When in doubt, structure to avoid franchise triggers or comply fully.

### Handle Assignments And Recordation Correctly

Trademarks can be assigned, but an assignment in gross, without the associated goodwill, is invalid in many jurisdictions. Record the assignment to protect against subsequent claimants.

Ensure:

- the assignment includes the associated goodwill and business;
- the assignment covers the relevant registrations and applications;
- the assignment is in writing and signed;
- recordation with the trademark office in each jurisdiction;
- notification to licensees of the change of ownership;
- coordination with any change of name or merger.

An assignment without goodwill can void the trademark. Always transfer the associated business or goodwill.

### Protect Brand Integrity And Prevent Dilution

Licensing can dilute a brand if the licensed products diverge from the brand's positioning. Control brand integrity.

Control:

- consistency of licensed products with brand positioning;
- restrictions on discounting and channel placement;
- guidelines for logo use, color, typography, and placement;
- approval of marketing materials;
- restrictions on alterations and derivative works;
- monitoring for brand erosion across the portfolio.

A premium brand licensed to discount channels can erode equity permanently. Maintain positioning discipline.

### Plan For Termination And Brand Reclamation

Every license ends. Plan for reclamation to avoid brand loss.

Address:

- termination rights for breach, insolvency, and change of control;
- post-termination sell-off periods and obligations;
- return or destruction of branded materials;
- transition of customers and inventory;
- non-compete and non-solicit post-termination where enforceable;
- survival of quality, audit, and indemnity obligations;
- recordation of termination with the trademark office where applicable.

A licensee that continues using the mark after termination is an infringer. But without clear termination and transition mechanics, reclaiming the brand is difficult.

## Common Traps

### Licensing Without Quality Control

Naked licensing voids trademark rights. Exercise and document real oversight.

### Vague License Scope

Omitted territory, field, or sublicensing terms invite overuse. Define scope precisely.

### Weak Audit And Reporting

Audit rights without recordkeeping and thresholds are unenforceable. Require records and meaningful audits.

### Co-Branding Without Liability Allocation

A partner's defective product damages both brands. Allocate liability and control quality.

### Misclassifying A Franchise As A License

Franchise triggers create disclosure and registration obligations. Comply or restructure.

### Assignment In Gross

Transferring a mark without goodwill can void it. Transfer the associated business.

### Brand Dilution Through Discount Channels

Licensing to off-brand channels erodes equity. Maintain positioning discipline.

### No Termination And Reclamation Plan

Without clear termination mechanics, reclaiming the brand is difficult. Plan the exit.

## Self-Check

- Does every license include real, documented quality control with approval rights, standards, inspections, audit, non-compliance remedies, and ongoing oversight?
- Is license scope defined for marks, goods or services, territory, exclusivity, field, sublicensing, channels, co-marketing, and duration?
- Are royalties, minimum guarantees, reporting, audit rights, recordkeeping, payment terms, taxes, and remedies structured to capture and verify value?
- Are co-branding and joint marketing controlled for approval, prominence, quality, liability allocation, ownership, termination, and competitor restrictions?
- Is franchise law compliance assessed for mark use, marketing support, fee, and jurisdictional definition, with disclosure, registration, relationship, advertising, and rescission obligations met?
- Are assignments structured with goodwill, written and signed, covering all registrations and applications, recorded, and coordinated with name changes and mergers?
- Is brand integrity protected through consistency, channel and discount restrictions, logo guidelines, marketing approval, derivative work restrictions, and portfolio monitoring?
- Is termination and reclamation planned for breach, insolvency, change of control, sell-off, material return, transition, non-compete, survival, and recordation?
- Are international licensing and recordation requirements addressed for each jurisdiction?
- Does the output avoid asserting that a license preserves trademark rights or complies with franchise law and instead recommend qualified trademark and franchise counsel for the specific deal and jurisdictions?
