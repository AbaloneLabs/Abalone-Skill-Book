# OFAC, UN, and EU Sanctions

Sanctions are prohibitions on dealing with designated persons, entities, sectors, or entire jurisdictions, imposed by national authorities and supranational bodies (UN Security Council, EU, UK, US OFAC and State Department, and others). Unlike many regulatory regimes, sanctions compliance is strict-liability in key jurisdictions (notably US OFAC): a prohibited transaction violates the law even if the violator did not know the counterparty was designated. The lists change frequently, designations can be sudden, and enforcement can produce very large penalties and reputational harm.

The judgment problem is that sanctions are layered (multiple regimes can apply to one transaction), extraterritorial in reach (notably US sanctions tied to US persons, US dollars, and US-origin items), and often turn on ownership and control rather than direct listing (the 50-percent rule, indirect control, and secondary sanctions). Agents frequently assume that because a counterparty is not on a list the transaction is fine, that a non-US company is outside US sanctions reach, or that a general license covers the activity when it does not.

This skill is advisory only. Sanctions regimes are complex, change frequently, and vary by jurisdiction. Confirm any sanctions conclusion with qualified sanctions counsel before acting.

## Core Rules

### Identify every regime whose sanctions may apply

A single cross-border transaction can be subject to multiple sanctions regimes simultaneously: UN (globally binding on member states), EU (binding on EU persons and transactions with EU nexus), UK, US (with broad extraterritorial reach), and the regimes of other involved jurisdictions. Determine nexus to each.

- US sanctions reach US persons (citizens, permanent residents, US-incorporated entities, and persons physically in the US) and, for many programs, non-US persons acting in ways that trigger secondary sanctions or using US-dollar clearing or US-origin items.
- A strong analysis lists each regime, the nexus facts, and the resulting prohibitions and licensing options.
- A weak analysis checks only the home-jurisdiction list.

### Treat asset freezes and dealing prohibitions as absolute

Designated persons and entities are subject to asset freeze (no funds or economic resources made available, directly or indirectly) and dealing prohibition. "Directly or indirectly" captures transactions through intermediaries, front companies, and non-designated parties acting for or on behalf of designated persons.

- The obligation is not merely to avoid listed parties; it is to avoid making any economic resource available, directly or indirectly, to a designated person.
- Funds must be frozen immediately and without prior authority authorization, and reported to the competent authority.

### Apply ownership-and-control rules, not just direct listings

Sanctions reach entities owned (commonly 50 percent or more, individually or aggregated) by one or more designated persons, even if the entity itself is not listed. Control (a designated person controlling an entity by other means) can also bring the entity within scope.

- Aggregate ownership across multiple designated persons; two designated persons each owning 25 percent bring the entity within the 50-percent rule in US OFAC terms.
- Conduct ownership and control analysis on counterparties, not just list screening, and update as ownership changes.

### Distinguish comprehensive, sectoral, and list-based programs

Comprehensive embargoes prohibit broad categories of dealings with an entire jurisdiction. Sectoral sanctions restrict specific types of transactions (e.g., certain debt, equity, or energy transactions) with named entities or sectors. List-based programs target designated individuals and entities. The applicable prohibitions differ sharply by program type.

- Identify the program type before assessing the transaction; a comprehensive embargo requires a different analysis than a list-based designation.
- Sectoral sanctions have specific directives with specific prohibitions; read the directive, not just the listing.

### Use general and specific licenses correctly

General licenses authorize categories of transactions without individual application, subject to conditions and reporting. Specific licenses are issued to named parties for specific transactions. Both are conditional; exceeding the scope voids the authorization.

- Document the specific general-license citation and confirm all conditions are met before relying on it.
- When no general license applies, assess whether a specific license is available and apply with accurate facts; misrepresentation in an application is itself a violation.

### Address secondary sanctions and caution

Secondary sanctions expose non-US persons to sanctions risk for certain dealings with designated persons or sectors, even without US nexus, and can result in loss of access to US financial systems or designation. Some regimes also issue "caution" or advisory notes.

- Non-US companies are not automatically safe from US sanctions; assess secondary-sanctions exposure for dealings with sensitive jurisdictions or sectors.
- Factor secondary-sanctions risk into commercial decisions, including the risk of being cut off from US-dollar clearing.

### Coordinate sanctions with export-control and AML analysis

Sanctions interact with export controls (many designated parties are also export-control end-users) and AML (sanctions evasion is a predicate for suspicious-activity reporting). A transaction that passes one filter may fail another.

- Run sanctions, export-control, and AML checks together, not in silos.

## Common Traps

### "The counterparty is not on the list, so we are fine"

The most dangerous trap. List screening is necessary but not sufficient. Ownership-and-control rules, indirect dealing, and front companies mean an unlisted counterparty can still be within scope. Combine screening with ownership analysis and red-flag review.

### Assuming a non-US company is outside US sanctions

US sanctions reach non-US persons through US-dollar clearing, US-origin items, US-person involvement, and secondary sanctions. A non-US company using US-dollar clearing for a prohibited transaction has a US nexus. Assess nexus facts, not just corporate location.

### Ignoring the 50-percent aggregation rule

An entity owned 50 percent or more in aggregate by designated persons is blocked even if unlisted. Failing to aggregate ownership across multiple designated persons misses blocked entities. Conduct and document ownership analysis.

### Treating a general license as a blanket permission

General licenses have conditions (purpose, parties, time, reporting, recordkeeping). Exceeding any condition voids the authorization. Read the conditions and document compliance, do not rely on the license title.

### Indirect provision through intermediaries

Routing a transaction through an intermediary to reach a designated person is itself a violation (directly or indirectly). Structuring around a designation does not cure it; it often aggravates it.

### Stale screening

A counterparty clear today may be designated tomorrow, and designations can be sudden. One-time screening at onboarding misses subsequent designations. Screen continuously or at each transaction, and have a process to freeze newly designated parties' funds.

### Sectoral sanctions read as list-based

Sectoral sanctions directives impose specific transaction-type restrictions (e.g., maturity limits on debt), not a blanket dealing prohibition. Treating a sectoral-sanctioned entity as fully blocked over-complies; treating it as unlisted under-complies. Read the directive.

### Secondary sanctions treated as optional

Secondary sanctions can designate non-US persons or cut them off from US finance. Treating secondary-sanctions risk as "not our law" ignores the commercial and enforcement consequences. Factor it into decisions.

## Self-Check

- For the transaction, have I identified every sanctions regime with potential nexus (UN, EU, UK, US, and other involved jurisdictions), and the facts establishing or negating nexus?
- For any designated or potentially blocked party, have I confirmed the asset-freeze and dealing-prohibition obligations, including the duty to freeze immediately and report?
- Have I conducted ownership-and-control analysis (including 50-percent aggregation across designated persons) on the counterparty, not just list screening?
- Have I classified the relevant program (comprehensive, sectoral, list-based) and read the specific directive or prohibition that applies?
- Where relying on a general license, have I documented the citation and confirmed all conditions, reporting, and recordkeeping are met?
- For non-US parties, have I assessed secondary-sanctions exposure and US-dollar-clearing or US-origin-item nexus?
- Is screening performed continuously or at each transaction, with a process to freeze funds of newly designated parties?
- Have I coordinated sanctions analysis with export-control and AML checks, and flagged that conclusions must be confirmed with qualified sanctions counsel?
