# Violation and Penalty

Despite a well-designed compliance program, violations occur: a designated party transacts before the list update is processed, controlled technology is released to a foreign national without a license, a re-export is made without authorization. How the organization responds determines whether the matter is a manageable civil event or escalates into a major enforcement action with criminal exposure, debarment, and loss of export or operating privileges. The response window is short, and the early decisions (preserve, investigate, disclose) are the most consequential.

The judgment problem is that disclosure is strategically powerful but not always advisable, that internal investigations must preserve privilege and evidence while meeting regulatory expectations, and that penalty frameworks weigh aggravating and mitigating factors that the organization can influence through its response. Agents often under-appreciate the strict-liability nature of key sanctions regimes, the value of a voluntary self-disclosure in penalty mitigation, the personal exposure of individuals, and the role of insurance and settlement structures in managing the outcome.

This skill is advisory only. Violations can carry civil and criminal liability for entities and individuals. Engage qualified sanctions/export-control counsel immediately on discovery of a potential violation; do not act on this skill alone.

## Core Rules

### Preserve evidence and stabilize immediately on discovery

On learning of a potential violation, the first steps are to preserve relevant records (email, system logs, screening data, transaction records, communications), prevent further potentially prohibited conduct, and escalate to the compliance officer and counsel. Do not alter, delete, or "fix" records or transactions.

- Issue a litigation-hold/preservation notice promptly and confirm compliance.
- Stabilize the relationship or transaction at issue to prevent compounding violations while the facts are established.

### Conduct a privileged internal investigation proportionate to the issue

Investigate under attorney direction to protect privilege, scope the investigation to the facts needed (what happened, when, who was involved, what controls failed, the legal analysis), and document findings. The depth should match the severity and the potential disclosure.

- Privilege is fragile; structure communications and work product carefully and limit distribution.
- The investigation should also identify root cause and remediation, not just whether a violation occurred.

### Make the voluntary self-disclosure decision deliberately

Voluntary self-disclosure (VSD) to the relevant authority can be a significant mitigating factor in penalty determination and, in some regimes, a prerequisite for leniency or non-prosecution. But disclosure is a strategic decision with consequences: it commits the organization to a narrative, may waive privilege, and triggers a formal process.

- Assess VSD early, with counsel, against the specific regime's mitigation framework (e.g., OFAC's framework, export-control VSD guidelines, EU/national equivalents).
- Timing matters; delayed disclosure after the authority has begun its own inquiry may lose the voluntary character.
- Disclosure of individual conduct can create personal exposure; balance organizational and individual interests with separate counsel where needed.

### Understand the penalty framework and how to influence it

Penalty frameworks weigh aggravating factors (willfulness, harm to sanctions objectives, prior violations, concealment, senior management involvement) and mitigating factors (voluntary disclosure, remediation, cooperation, effective compliance program, limited harm). The organization can influence the outcome through its response.

- A genuine, resourced compliance program is a mitigating factor; a paper program is not, and can be aggravating.
- Remediation (fixing the root cause, disciplining where appropriate, enhancing controls) is distinct from and complementary to cooperation.

### Address individual exposure

Enforcement increasingly targets individuals (executives, compliance officers, traders) as well as entities. Individuals may face civil fines, debarment, and criminal charges. Their interests can diverge from the entity's.

- Provide separate counsel for individuals whose conduct is under scrutiny; do not assume aligned interests.
- Be cautious about employee interviews and statements that could create individual exposure.

### Manage cooperation and the authority relationship

Cooperation (responsive document production, witness availability, factual presentations) is generally rewarded, but the scope of cooperation and any privilege waiver should be deliberate and limited. Broad privilege waivers can be avoided through targeted factual presentations.

- Cooperate substantively while protecting privilege; document what is produced and when.
- Do not make factual concessions without full investigation; premature admissions are hard to retract.

### Structure settlements and remediation for durable resolution

Settlements often include a base penalty, mitigation credit, compliance-commitment undertakings, monitors or self-reporting, and suspension of penalties conditioned on compliance. The undertakings can shape the business for years.

- Negotiate undertakings that the organization can realistically meet; over-promising leads to breach and re-opening.
- Integrate remediation into the compliance program so the same failure does not recur; a repeat violation is severely aggravating.

### Consider insurance and financial provisioning

Sanctions and export-control fines are often excluded from insurance coverage, but defense costs, investigation costs, and some civil matters may be covered. D&O coverage may respond to individual exposure. Assess coverage early and preserve notice obligations.

- Do not assume fines are insured; review policies and provide timely notice.
- Consider financial provisioning for penalties in light of disclosure and the framework's maximum exposure.

## Common Traps

### Self-help after discovering a violation

The most damaging trap. Altering records, re-classifying after the fact, quietly re-shipping, or deleting communications converts a potentially civil matter into an obstruction or false-statement case with criminal exposure. Preserve and escalate.

### Treating strict liability as a reason not to investigate

In strict-liability regimes, the absence of intent is not a defense to liability, but it profoundly affects penalty. Investigation establishes the facts (intent, harm, root cause) that drive mitigation. Do not skip investigation because liability is strict.

### Delayed or non-voluntary disclosure

Disclosure after the authority has opened its own inquiry, or prompted by a subpoena, may lose the voluntary-disclosure mitigation. Decide on VSD promptly with counsel; delay is often itself a decision against disclosure.

### Premature factual concessions

Admitting a violation before the investigation is complete, to appear cooperative, can lock in an inaccurate narrative and forfeit defenses. Cooperate through factual presentations based on completed investigation, not rushed admissions.

### Paper compliance program offered as mitigation

Authorities examine whether the program was effective in operation, not just on paper. Offering a non-operational program as mitigation, when the violation shows it failed, can be aggravating. Focus on genuine remediation.

### Ignoring individual exposure

Focusing only on the entity's penalty leaves individuals exposed and can create conflicts that undermine the defense. Identify individuals at risk early and address separate representation.

### Over-promising in settlement undertakings

Agreeing to compliance commitments the organization cannot meet to secure a settlement leads to breach, re-opening, and worse outcomes. Negotiate realistic undertakings and resource them.

### Assuming fines are insured

Many policies exclude sanctions and export-control fines, and late notice can forfeit defense-cost coverage. Review policies and give notice early.

## Self-Check

- On discovery of a potential violation, have records been preserved (litigation hold), further potentially prohibited conduct stabilized, and counsel engaged?
- Is there a privileged, scoped internal investigation establishing what happened, when, who, the controls that failed, the legal analysis, and the root cause?
- Has the voluntary self-disclosure decision been made deliberately and promptly with counsel, against the specific regime's mitigation framework, before the authority independently inquires?
- Have aggravating and mitigating factors been identified, and is the response (cooperation, remediation, compliance-program enhancement) designed to maximize mitigation?
- Have individuals whose conduct is under scrutiny been identified, and has separate counsel been considered where interests may diverge?
- Is cooperation substantive while protecting privilege, with factual presentations based on completed investigation rather than premature admissions?
- Are settlement undertakings realistic and resourced, and is remediation integrated into the compliance program to prevent recurrence?
- Has insurance coverage been reviewed and timely notice given, recognizing that fines may be excluded but defense and investigation costs may be covered?
- Have I flagged that violations carry civil and criminal exposure and that qualified sanctions/export-control counsel must be engaged immediately?
