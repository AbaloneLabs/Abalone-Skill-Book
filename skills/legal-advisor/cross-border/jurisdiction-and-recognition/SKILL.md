# Jurisdiction And Recognition

Winning a case is only half the battle; the judgment must be obtained in a court with jurisdiction and then recognized and enforced where the assets or counterparty are. Agents often assume that a court with jurisdiction over the dispute will produce an enforceable judgment, ignoring that the forum may lack personal jurisdiction, that a foreign court may refuse recognition on public policy or due process grounds, and that parallel proceedings in another country can produce an inconsistent or preclusive judgment. The web of treaties (Hague Choice of Court, Hague Judgments, New York Convention), regional regimes (Brussels I, Lugano), and domestic reciprocity rules determines what is enforceable where.

Use this skill before suing, defending, drafting or challenging forum selection and arbitration clauses, or seeking recognition of a foreign judgment. The goal is to force the agent to map jurisdiction, recognition, and enforcement as a single strategy rather than three separate steps. Recognition and enforcement rules are jurisdiction-specific and treaty-dependent; any conclusion must be confirmed against the applicable regime and the law of the enforcing court.

## Core Rules

### Establish Subject Matter And Personal Jurisdiction

Jurisdiction has two faces, and both must be satisfied.

Subject matter jurisdiction:

- the court's power to hear the type of case (commercial, family, administrative, criminal, IP);
- exclusive jurisdiction rules for certain matters (immovable property, company validity, IP registration, public registers);
- specialized tribunals and their scope.

Personal jurisdiction:

- the defendant's domicile, residence, or presence;
- submission by appearing and defending on the merits;
- consent through a forum selection clause;
- statutory long-arm jurisdiction over the claim;
- assets-based jurisdiction and its limits (freezing vs. merits);
- jurisdiction over third parties, guarantors, and affiliates.

A court without personal jurisdiction can issue a judgment that is unenforceable abroad. Do not assume presence of assets confers merits jurisdiction.

### Apply The Applicable Jurisdiction Regime

Different regimes apply different jurisdiction rules to cross-border disputes.

Identify:

- Brussels I (recast) and Lugano for EU/EFTA and the domicile-based rules, special jurisdiction, and exclusive jurisdiction;
- Hague Choice of Court Convention where a valid exclusive choice of court agreement exists;
- Hague Judgments Convention for recognition of judgments in civil and commercial matters;
- bilateral treaties on recognition and enforcement;
- domestic rules based on reciprocity, comity, or statute;
- the forum's rules for provisional measures and antisuit relief.

The regime determines the available jurisdiction bases and the grounds for refusing recognition. Do not mix regimes.

### Draft And Challenge Forum Selection Clauses Carefully

Forum selection clauses are powerful but fragile if drafted poorly.

For each clause:

- exclusive vs. non-exclusive;
- scope (disputes covered, parties bound, third-party beneficiaries);
- chosen court's jurisdiction and whether it is exclusive for Hague purposes;
- asymmetrical clauses and their enforceability;
- consistency with the governing law and arbitration clauses;
- compliance with consumer, employment, and insurance protective rules;
- clarity of language and the consequence of ambiguity.

An ambiguous or overbroad clause can be unenforceable or can be read narrowly. Draft for the chosen regime and challenge strategically.

### Distinguish Litigation, Arbitration, And Mediation Clauses

The choice of dispute resolution mechanism changes the entire enforcement path.

Map:

- court litigation under a forum selection clause and the applicable recognition regime;
- arbitration under a valid arbitration agreement and the New York Convention;
- mediation and conciliation and the Singapore Convention on Mediation;
- expert determination and its contractual but limited enforceability;
- hybrid and multi-tier clauses and their enforceability.

Arbitration awards are generally more widely enforceable than court judgments because of the New York Convention. Choose the mechanism with enforcement in mind.

### Plan Recognition And Enforcement Where The Assets Are

A judgment is enforced where the debtor's assets are, not where it was obtained.

Check:

- the recognition regime between the rendering and enforcing states;
- grounds for refusal (public policy, lack of jurisdiction, fraud, conflicting judgment, due process, natural justice);
- exequatur or direct registration requirements;
- time limits and interest recoverability;
- the effect of sovereign and state immunity;
- the need for a local power of attorney and local counsel.

Design the claim and the forum so the judgment is recognizable in the likely enforcement jurisdictions. A strong judgment in a non-recognizing forum is weak.

### Coordinate Parallel And Related Proceedings

Parallel proceedings create cost, inconsistency, and preclusion risk.

Manage:

- lis pendens and related-action rules (Brussels I, domestic);
- anti-suit injunctions and their extraterritorial reach;
- stays in favor of arbitration or a chosen court;
- coordination of multi-jurisdictional group litigation;
- the effect of a first-in-time judgment on later proceedings;
- recognition of foreign judgments as preclusive.

Aggressive anti-suit injunctions can themselves be unenforceable or sanctionable abroad. Coordinate strategically and consider the political cost.

### Raise And Defend Against Refusal Grounds

Recognition can be refused on specific grounds. Know them when seeking or resisting enforcement.

Common grounds:

- the foreign court lacked jurisdiction under the enforcing state's rules;
- the judgment is final and conclusive;
- the defendant was not given due notice and a fair opportunity to be heard;
- the judgment was obtained by fraud;
- the judgment is contrary to public policy;
- there is a conflicting judgment between the same parties;
- the subject matter is within exclusive jurisdiction of the enforcing state.

Build the recognition record (translations, authentication, certificates) to defeat refusal grounds, and raise them precisely when resisting.

### Handle Sovereign, State, And Head Of State Immunity

Enforcement against states and state entities raises immunity issues.

Assess:

- sovereign immunity from jurisdiction and from execution;
- commercial activity exception;
- waiver of immunity in contracts and treaties;
- execution against state assets and the distinction between commercial and sovereign assets;
- head of state and diplomatic immunity.

A judgment against a state is often hard to enforce. Structure transactions and dispute clauses with immunity in mind.

## Common Traps

### Suing Where You Cannot Enforce

A judgment in a forum unrecognized where the assets are is nearly worthless. Plan enforcement first.

### Confusing Presence With Merits Jurisdiction

Asset presence may support freezing but not merits jurisdiction. Confirm the basis.

### Drafting Forum Clauses Without The Regime In Mind

A clause must satisfy the chosen regime (Hague, Brussels) to be exclusive and enforceable. Ambiguity defeats it.

### Mixing Litigation And Arbitration Clauses

Inconsistent or hybrid clauses can be unenforceable and spawn jurisdictional disputes. Keep them clear and exclusive.

### Ignoring Refusal Grounds Until Enforcement

Public policy, due process, and jurisdiction grounds can block recognition. Address them when designing the claim.

### Aggressive Anti-Suit Injunctions

These can be unenforceable or sanctionable abroad and can worsen the position. Use carefully.

### Forgetting Sovereign Immunity

Enforcement against states is restricted. Structure transactions with immunity and waiver in mind.

### Assuming Reciprocity Exists

Reciprocity is not automatic and varies by subject matter. Confirm the regime for each enforcing state.

## Self-Check

- Are subject matter and personal jurisdiction both established on a recognized basis?
- Is the applicable jurisdiction regime identified (Brussels I, Lugano, Hague, bilateral, domestic) and applied consistently?
- Is the forum selection clause exclusive, scoped, and compliant with the chosen regime and protective rules?
- Is the dispute resolution mechanism (court, arbitration, mediation, expert) chosen with enforcement in mind?
- Is recognition and enforcement planned against the regime and refusal grounds of the likely enforcing states?
- Are parallel and related proceedings coordinated, with lis pendens, stays, and anti-suit relief handled strategically?
- Are refusal grounds (jurisdiction, due process, fraud, public policy, conflicting judgment, exclusive jurisdiction) raised or defended precisely?
- Is the recognition record (translations, authentication, certificates, powers of attorney) prepared?
- Are sovereign, state, and diplomatic immunity assessed for enforcement against states and officials?
- Does the output recommend confirmation against the applicable regime by qualified counsel in the rendering and enforcing jurisdictions?
