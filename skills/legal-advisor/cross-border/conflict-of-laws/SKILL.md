# Conflict Of Laws

When a matter crosses borders, the first question is rarely who wins on the merits; it is which law applies and which court decides. Conflict of laws (private international law) determines the governing law, the forum, and the recognition of foreign acts. Agents often skip this layer and argue the merits under whichever law feels familiar, missing that the choice of law can change the entire outcome, that mandatory rules and public policy can override a chosen law, and that the forum controls procedure, evidence, and remedies. A wrong assumption about governing law can void a contract, defeat a claim, or expose a party to liability it never anticipated.

Use this skill before advising on any cross-border contract, dispute, tort, property, family, succession, or corporate matter where more than one legal system could apply. The goal is to force the agent to identify the connecting factors, characterize the issue, apply the relevant choice-of-law rules, and test for mandatory rules and public policy before reaching the merits. Conflict of laws is jurisdiction-specific and depends on treaties (Hague, regional), regulations (such as Rome I and Rome II in the EU), and domestic rules; any conclusion must be confirmed against the forum's actual choice-of-law rules.

## Core Rules

### Characterize The Issue Before Choosing The Law

Choice-of-law rules attach to categories of issues. Mischaracterization routes the matter to the wrong law.

Characterize:

- contract (formation, validity, performance, interpretation);
- tort or non-contractual obligation (place of injury, place of conduct, common habitual residence);
- property (movable, immovable, tangible, intangible);
- family and succession (marriage, divorce, maintenance, succession, matrimonial property);
- corporate status, capacity, and internal affairs;
- trusts and estates;
- intellectual property rights.

The same facts can raise multiple characterizations, each with its own connecting factor. Characterize each issue separately.

### Identify The Forum And Its Choice-Of-Law Rules

The forum's choice-of-law rules determine which law applies. Different forums apply different rules to the same issue.

Determine:

- which courts have jurisdiction (see jurisdiction rules, treaties, and forum selection clauses);
- whether the forum applies its own choice-of-law rules or a supranational regime (Rome I, Rome II, Hague Conventions);
- whether the forum applies the law designated as a whole (including its conflict rules, raising renvoi) or only its internal law;
- whether the forum's public policy or mandatory rules override the designated law.

Do not assume the forum applies the law of the place with the closest connection. The forum applies its own choice-of-law methodology.

### Determine The Governing Law For Each Issue

Governing law is issue-by-issue, not all-or-nothing.

For contracts:

- party autonomy and the scope of the choice (formation, validity, performance, third parties);
- absent an express choice, the closest connection or characteristic performer rules;
- mandatory rules of the forum and of third countries with a close connection;
- overriding mandatory provisions (lois de police) that apply regardless of choice;
- formal validity and capacity rules.

For torts:

- party autonomy where allowed (Rome II allows choice);
- place of injury, place of conduct, common habitual residence;
- safety and conduct rules of the place of injury;
- exceptions for unfair competition, environmental damage, IP infringement, and restrictive practices.

For property, family, and corporate matters, specific connecting factors apply. Do not collapse distinct issues into one governing law.

### Test For Mandatory Rules And Public Policy

A chosen or designated law can be overridden by mandatory rules and public policy.

Check:

- overriding mandatory provisions of the forum that apply regardless of choice (consumer, employment, insurance, competition, securities, sanctions);
- mandatory provisions of a third country with a close connection where the forum's rules permit;
- public policy (ordre public) exclusion of foreign law that manifestly conflicts with fundamental principles;
- mandatory EU and international rules where applicable;
- sanctions and export control that override any choice of law.

Public policy is a narrow but powerful exception. Use it deliberately, not as a general escape hatch.

### Handle Renvoi And The Scope Of The Designated Law

Renvoi occurs when the designated law's conflict rules point back to the forum or to a third law.

Determine:

- whether the forum accepts renvoi (some do for succession and family, rarely for contract);
- whether the chosen law is the internal law or the whole law including conflict rules;
- the effect on the outcome where renvoi changes the applicable internal law.

For express contractual choice of law, renvoi is generally excluded; the choice means the internal law. For default connecting factors, renvoi may apply depending on the forum.

### Assess Jurisdiction, Forum Non Conveniens, And Lis Pendens

Which court decides is as important as which law applies.

Map:

- jurisdiction bases (domicile, presence, defendant's place, place of performance, place of injury, assets, submission);
- forum selection and arbitration clauses and their scope and enforceability;
- lis pendens and related actions in multiple courts (Brussels I, Hague Choice of Court, domestic rules);
- forum non conveniens and discretionary stays;
- anti-suit and anti-enforcement injunctions.

Parallel proceedings can produce inconsistent judgments. Address jurisdiction strategically before litigating the merits.

### Plan For Recognition And Enforcement

A judgment won is worthless if it cannot be enforced where the assets are.

Check:

- recognition and enforcement regimes (Hague Choice of Court, Hague Judgments Convention, Brussels I, bilateral treaties, domestic reciprocity);
- grounds for refusal (public policy, lack of jurisdiction, fraud, conflicting judgment, due process);
- the need for an exequatur or direct registration;
- the effect of arbitration awards under the New York Convention.

Design the forum and the claim with enforcement in mind. A strong judgment in an unenforceable forum is a weak position.

### Preserve Evidence, Privilege, And Procedure Across Systems

Procedure is governed by the forum (lex fori), and procedural rules differ sharply.

Consider:

- burden and standard of proof;
- discovery and disclosure (common law vs. civil law);
- expert evidence and the role of the judge;
- privilege and confidentiality (which communications are protected, and where);
- language, translation, and legalization of documents;
- statutes of limitation and their characterization as procedural or substantive.

Privilege in one country may not protect communications in another. Route sensitive communications through counsel and structure cross-border discovery carefully.

## Common Traps

### Arguing The Merits Without Choosing The Law

The governing law can change the outcome entirely. Resolve choice of law before the merits.

### Collapsing All Issues Into One Law

Contract, tort, property, capacity, and procedure can each follow different laws. Characterize each issue.

### Ignoring Mandatory Rules And Public Policy

A chosen law can be overridden by overriding mandatory provisions and public policy. Test both.

### Assuming Renvoi Never Applies

For default connecting factors, renvoi can change the applicable internal law. Confirm the forum's approach.

### Overlooking Forum Selection And Arbitration Clauses

These clauses can control the forum and the law. Read their scope and enforceability first.

### Parallel Proceedings Without Coordination

Lis pendens and inconsistent judgments create cost and risk. Address jurisdiction strategically.

### Privilege Assumed Universal

Privilege differs by country. Communications protected at home may be discoverable abroad.

### Forgetting Enforcement At The Outset

A judgment is only as good as its enforceability where the assets are. Plan enforcement before suing.

## Self-Check

- Is each issue characterized (contract, tort, property, family, corporate, trust, IP) before choosing the law?
- Is the forum identified, and are its choice-of-law rules and any supranational regime (Rome I/II, Hague) applied?
- Is the governing law determined issue-by-issue, with party autonomy, closest connection, and characteristic performer considered?
- Are overriding mandatory provisions, third-country mandatory rules, and public policy tested against the designated law?
- Is renvoi addressed, including whether the forum accepts it and the effect on the applicable internal law?
- Are jurisdiction bases, forum selection, arbitration, lis pendens, and forum non conveniens mapped?
- Is recognition and enforcement planned against the relevant regime and grounds for refusal?
- Are procedure, burden of proof, discovery, privilege, language, and limitation handled under the lex fori?
- Are sanctions and export control considered as potential overrides of any choice of law?
- Does the output flag the analysis as jurisdiction-specific and recommend confirmation against the forum's actual rules by qualified counsel?
