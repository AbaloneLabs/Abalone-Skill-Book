# Cross-Border Insolvency

A single corporate group can have assets, creditors, operations, and proceedings in many countries. Each country's insolvency law applies to the assets and parties within its reach, and without coordination the estate is fragmented, creditors race to local assets, and value is destroyed. Agents often treat a foreign insolvency as if it were domestic, ignoring recognition of foreign proceedings, the distinction between main and non-main proceedings, the automatic stay and its extraterritorial limits, and the need for cooperation among officeholders. A wrong step can expose the applicant to liability, defeat the stay, or trigger competing administrations that litigate for years.

Use this skill before seeking recognition of a foreign proceeding, coordinating group insolvency across borders, enforcing a stay on foreign assets, or distributing assets to international creditors. The goal is to force the agent to identify the center of main interests (COMI), choose the recognition vehicle, and plan cooperation before acting. Cross-border insolvency law is treaty- and statute-specific (UNCITRAL Model Law, EU Insolvency Regulation, chapter 15, bilateral treaties); any conclusion must be confirmed against the applicable instrument and the law of each relevant jurisdiction.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Center Of Main Interests And Proceedings

Recognition and jurisdiction turn on where the debtor has its center of main interests (COMI) and where proceedings are pending.

Determine:

- the debtor's COMI (presumptively the place of the registered office, rebuttable by creditor perception);
- establishments (place of operations where the debtor carries out non-transitory economic activity with human means and goods);
- the location of assets and operations;
- where proceedings have been opened or are contemplated;
- group structure and the COMI of each group member.

COMI is the key concept. A debtor with a registered office in one country and operations and creditor relationships in another may have its COMI rebutted. Document the factors creditors would perceive.

### Choose The Recognition Vehicle

Different instruments govern recognition depending on the jurisdictions involved.

Identify:

- UNCITRAL Model Law (adopted as chapter 15 in the US and elsewhere) for recognition of foreign main and non-main proceedings;
- EU Insolvency Regulation (recast) for proceedings within EU member states, with main, secondary, and territorial proceedings;
- bilateral treaties on cross-border insolvency;
- domestic common law or statutory recognition where no instrument applies;
- the distinction between main, non-main, and territorial proceedings and their effects.

The vehicle determines the recognition standard, the relief available, and the cooperation duties. Do not assume chapter 15 or the Model Law applies everywhere.

### Seek Recognition Of The Foreign Proceeding

Recognition produces legal effects, including the automatic stay (in main proceedings) and access to relief.

Prepare:

- the petition and required evidence (certified copy of the foreign commencement decision, confirmation of the foreign representative's appointment, foreign representative's statement);
- evidence of COMI or establishment;
- the list of creditors and the debtor's assets;
- translations and authentication;
- the relief sought (automatic stay, discretionary relief, turnover of assets).

Recognition of a foreign main proceeding triggers the automatic stay and the suspension of the right to transfer or encumber assets. Non-main proceeding recognition gives access to discretionary relief without the automatic stay.

### Manage The Automatic Stay And Discretionary Relief

The stay and relief are the core protections, but their scope and limits matter.

Assess:

- the scope of the automatic stay (acts against the debtor, assets, and enforcement);
- extraterritorial reach and its limits (the stay generally operates within the recognizing jurisdiction);
- discretionary relief (turnover, injunctive relief, examination, discovery, examination of witnesses);
- the effect on pending litigation and enforcement;
- carve-outs for regulated entities, employment, and IP licenses;
- the risk of stay violation and sanctions.

The stay is powerful but not universal. Coordinate with local counsel in each jurisdiction where assets or creditors are located.

### Coordinate Assets, Officeholders, And Creditors Across Borders

Cross-border insolvency requires coordination to preserve and distribute value.

Map:

- the location and control of assets;
- the foreign representative and local officeholder roles;
- turnover and transfer of assets, subject to local priority rules;
- the treatment of creditors (priority, set-off, ipso facto clauses, contracts);
- avoidance and recovery actions and their extraterritorial reach;
- group coordination and procedural consolidation (joint administration, protocol).

Avoidance actions (preferences, fraudulent transfers) may not be recognized extraterritorially. Plan recovery through local proceedings where needed.

### Handle Creditor Rights, Priorities, And Set-Off

Creditor treatment differs by jurisdiction and can drive forum choice.

Consider:

- priority rules (secured, preferential, unsecured, subordinated) in each jurisdiction;
- the effect of recognition on set-off and netting;
- ipso facto clauses and their enforceability or suspension;
- contracts (executory contracts, licenses, leases) and assignment;
- employee, tax, and consumer claims and their special treatment;
- the creditor voting and confirmation process.

A distribution plan must respect local priorities or coordinate them through a protocol or scheme of arrangement.

### Use Protocols, Cross-Class Deals, And Schemes

Where no single regime covers the group, protocols and structures coordinate the administration.

Options:

- cross-border protocols among officeholders and courts;
- schemes of arrangement and restructuring plans with cross-border recognition;
- chapter 15 recognition of compromises and arrangements;
- EU group coordination proceedings;
- voluntary cooperation and information sharing.

A protocol can reduce litigation and align distributions, but it requires consent and court approval.

### Address Sovereign, Regulatory, And Public Interest Issues

Some assets and entities raise special issues.

Check:

- regulated entities (banks, insurers) and special resolution regimes;
- sovereign and state-owned assets and immunity;
- public interest and employment considerations;
- sanctions and export control on asset transfers;
- data protection and confidentiality of insolvency information.

Special resolution regimes can override general insolvency law and exclude recognition. Confirm the status of each entity and asset.

## Common Traps

### Assuming Domestic Insolvency Law Applies Extraterritorially

A domestic stay and avoidance rules generally do not reach foreign assets without recognition. Seek recognition.

### Misjudging COMI

COMI is rebuttable based on creditor perception. A registered office with no real operations may not be the COMI.

### Confusing Main, Non-Main, And Territorial Proceedings

Each has different recognition effects and relief. Match the proceeding type to the goal.

### Overstaying The Automatic Stay

The stay operates within the recognizing jurisdiction. Coordinate stays in each relevant country.

### Ignoring Local Priority Rules

Distribution must respect local priorities or coordinate them through a protocol or scheme.

### Assuming Avoidance Actions Travel

Preferences and fraudulent transfer recoveries may need local proceedings. Plan recovery accordingly.

### Forgetting Regulated Entity Exclusions

Banks, insurers, and other regulated entities may be outside general cross-border regimes. Confirm status.

### Inadequate Recognition Evidence

Missing certified copies, translations, or statements delay or defeat recognition. Prepare the record carefully.

## Self-Check

- Is the debtor's COMI determined, with establishments, assets, operations, and group member COMIs mapped?
- Is the recognition vehicle identified (Model Law/chapter 15, EU Insolvency Regulation, bilateral treaty, domestic law)?
- Is the recognition petition supported by certified decisions, representative confirmation, statements, and translations?
- Is the automatic stay scope and its extraterritorial limits assessed, with discretionary relief sought where needed?
- Are assets, officeholders, creditors, avoidance actions, and contracts coordinated across jurisdictions?
- Are creditor priorities, set-off, netting, ipso facto clauses, and special claims handled under local rules or a protocol?
- Are cross-border protocols, schemes of arrangement, and group coordination used where no single regime applies?
- Are regulated entities, sovereign assets, sanctions, and data protection issues addressed?
- Is the distinction between main, non-main, and territorial proceedings respected in seeking relief?
- Does the output recommend confirmation against the applicable instrument and local counsel in each jurisdiction?
