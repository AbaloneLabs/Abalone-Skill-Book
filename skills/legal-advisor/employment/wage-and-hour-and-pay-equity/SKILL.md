# Wage-and-Hour and Pay Equity

Wage-and-hour and pay equity law is deceptively simple in concept and punishing in practice. A single misclassification, a rounding rule applied inconsistently, or an unexplained pay gap can convert years of routine payroll into a collective or class action with multi-year look-back, liquidated damages, and personal liability for owners and managers in some jurisdictions. Because these rules are enforced by both government agencies and private plaintiffs' counsel, and because they vary sharply by state, county, and city, an agent must reason from the specific job, the specific location, and the specific pay practice rather than from a generic federal default.

## Core Rules

### Classification drives everything else

Exempt versus non-exempt, and employee versus independent contractor, are the two classification questions that determine nearly every downstream obligation. Both are fact-intensive and jurisdiction-specific.

- **Exempt/non-exempt.** A job title or a salary does not create an exemption. Federal Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA) exemptions (executive, administrative, professional, computer, outside sales) each require a salary basis, a salary level threshold, and specific job duties. State exemptions frequently differ, and some impose stricter salary thresholds or additional duties tests. The duties test, not the job description, controls.
- **Employee/contractor.** The common-law "right to control" test, the FLSA "economic reality" test, and state-specific tests (such as the ABC test used in some states for wage and unemployment purposes) can reach opposite conclusions on the same facts. A worker may be a contractor under one statute and an employee under another. Classify per statute, not globally.

### Work time and pay practices are where exposure accumulates

- **"Hours worked" is broader than scheduled time.** It can include pre-shift donning of protective gear, mandatory security screenings, travel between worksites, on-call time depending on restrictions, and certain training. Determine whether the activity is integral and indispensable to principal work and whether it is controlled by the employer.
- **Overtime is usually time-and-one-half of the regular rate**, not the base rate. The regular rate must include most non-discretionary bonuses, shift differentials, and certain incentive pay. A bonus paid after the fact can retroactively increase overtime owed for prior periods, requiring recalculated payments.
- **Meal and rest breaks** are not federally required but are mandated, with specific requirements, in many states. A meal break that is interrupted, short, or not fully relieved of duty may be compensable and may trigger premium pay under state law.
- **Rounding and timekeeping** are permissible only if neutral on average and applied in both directions. Rounding that consistently favors the employer is unlawful, and policies that auto-deduct meal breaks without a mechanism to report missed breaks create liability.
- **Off-the-clock work** — checking email after hours, pre-shift setup, post-shift cleanup — is a frequent source of collective actions, especially for non-exempt remote and salaried-non-exempt workers.

### Pay equity and transparency

- **Equal pay laws** have expanded beyond "equal work" to "substantially similar work" and now commonly prohibit pay differences based on sex, race, and other protected classes, with limited affirmative defenses (seniority, merit, productivity, and a bona fide factor other than a protected characteristic that is job-related and consistent with business necessity).
- **Salary history bans** in many jurisdictions prohibit asking about or relying on prior pay, and some require an employer to provide a pay scale or range upon request or in postings.
- **Pay transparency** posting requirements and applicant/employee pay-range disclosure rules vary by state and city and often apply extraterritorially to remote workers.

### Damages and enforcement shape risk

- The FLSA provides a two-year look-back (three years for willful violations), plus liquidated damages doubling the unpaid amount and attorney's fees. State wage laws often provide longer look-backs (up to four years or more), daily penalties, waiting-time penalties for final pay, and itemized wage statement penalties that can exceed the underlying wages.
- **Collective and class procedures** allow one plaintiff to represent similarly situated workers, multiplying exposure. Conditional certification under the FLSA is a low bar.
- **Personal liability** for owners, officers, and managers who control pay decisions exists under some state wage laws.

## Common Traps

- **Treating "salaried" as synonymous with "exempt."** Salary is necessary but not sufficient; the duties test is the dispositive question. A low-level administrator paid a salary is often non-exempt.
- **Applying the federal default and ignoring state law.** Many states have higher minimum wages, stricter exemption thresholds, daily overtime (after 8 hours), seventh-consecutive-day overtime, and broader break requirements.
- **Forgetting the regular rate for overtime.** Paying overtime on base hourly rate only, while paying non-discretionary bonuses, underpays every overtime hour and compounds across the workforce.
- **Auto-deduct meal breaks with no reclaim mechanism.** The policy is permissible only if employees can easily report missed breaks and get paid for them.
- **Classifying by written contract.** An "independent contractor agreement" does not control; the actual working relationship does. Reliance on the agreement alone is a classic trap.
- **Remote workers retain their work-location protections.** A worker in a high-protection state is generally covered by that state's law even if the employer is headquartered elsewhere.
- **Posting pay ranges inconsistently.** Some transparency laws apply to job postings even on third-party boards; failing to include ranges on external postings can trigger penalties.
- **Ignoring final-pay and wage-statement rules.** These are separate, technical obligations with standalone penalties; a correct total wage paid late can still incur waiting-time and itemized-statement penalties.

## Self-Check

- For every classification, have I stated the duties test and salary threshold under both federal and the applicable state law, rather than relying on title or salary alone?
- Have I identified the regular rate components (bonuses, differentials, incentives) and confirmed overtime is calculated on the regular rate, not the base rate?
- If meal breaks are auto-deducted, is there a mechanism for employees to report and be paid for missed or interrupted breaks?
- Have I checked the specific state, county, and city rules for minimum wage, daily/seventh-day overtime, and meal/rest break requirements, including for remote workers?
- For any pay gap, have I considered whether the "bona fide factor other than" defense is job-related and consistent with business necessity, and documented the rationale?
- Have I confirmed salary-history-ban and pay-transparency obligations for each relevant jurisdiction, including third-party job boards?
- Have I flagged final-pay timing and itemized wage-statement requirements as separate obligations with standalone penalties?
- Where exposure is plausible across a workforce, have I recommended counsel and, where appropriate, a privileged wage audit rather than a conclusory answer?
- Have I noted the limits of general guidance and recommended a qualified employment lawyer admitted in the relevant jurisdiction for classification and audit decisions?
