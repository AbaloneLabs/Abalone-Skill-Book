# Termination And Severance

Termination is where employment law does its heaviest lifting. A dismissal that looks clean on paper can be wrongful, discriminatory, retaliatory, or procedurally defective, and the cost of getting it wrong includes back pay, reinstatement, statutory penalties, punitive damages, reputational harm, and regulator scrutiny. Agents often focus on the stated reason for termination while ignoring the procedural steps, documentation, protected-activity overlap, notice obligations, and severance-release formalities that actually determine whether the termination holds.

Use this skill before terminating an employee, conducting a redundancy or layoff, drafting a separation agreement, responding to a wrongful-termination allegation, calculating severance, or planning a performance-based dismissal. The goal is to force the agent to evaluate reason, procedure, documentation, protected characteristics, retaliation exposure, notice and severance math, and release validity, and to escalate to employment counsel when rights or significant exposure are at stake. This skill is decision support, not legal advice.

## Core Rules

### Classify The Termination Type And Its Legal Regime

The right process depends on why and how the employment is ending.

Identify:

- termination by employer for cause (misconduct, performance, attendance);
- termination by employer without cause (redundancy, restructuring, role elimination);
- termination by employee (resignation, constructive dismissal);
- mutual agreement or settlement;
- fixed-term contract expiry;
- retirement;
- death or permanent incapacity;
- probationary termination.

Each type triggers different notice, severance, procedure, and documentation rules. Do not relabel a performance dismissal as "restructuring" or vice versa to dodge procedure; the actual driver controls.

### Verify A Lawful And Substantiated Termination Reason

In most jurisdictions outside at-will U.S. employment, the employer must have a valid reason.

Assess:

- misconduct with documented evidence and prior warnings where required;
- capability or qualifications with fair performance process;
- redundancy with genuine business reason and fair selection;
- statutory illegality (e.g., loss of work authorization);
- some substantial reason of a kind in the relevant category.

Vague reasons ("not a good fit," "culture mismatch") are weak. A strong reason is specific, evidenced, and tied to a recognized category, with the evidence gathered before the decision.

### Run The Protected-Class And Retaliation Filter

Even a valid reason becomes unlawful if it masks discrimination or retaliation.

Check overlap with:

- protected characteristics (race, sex, age, disability, religion, national origin, pregnancy, sexual orientation, gender identity, veteran status, and others by jurisdiction);
- recent protected activity (complaints of discrimination or harassment, whistleblowing, union activity, leave or accommodation requests, safety complaints, wage complaints);
- family, medical, military, or caregiving leave usage;
- accommodation requests and the interactive process;
- recent return from protected leave.

If a protected activity or characteristic is anywhere near the termination timeline, document the independent, legitimate, non-discriminatory reason with particularity and escalate to counsel.

### Follow Required Procedure Before The Decision

Procedural fairness is often independently protected. A substantively justified dismissal can fail for process defects.

Confirm:

- investigation proportionate to the allegation;
- notice of the concerns and evidence to the employee;
- opportunity to respond, with representation or companion where required;
- impartial decision-maker;
- right of appeal where applicable;
- consideration of alternatives (improvement plan, transfer, warning);
- consistency with how similar cases were handled;
- works-council, union, or collective consultation where required;
- advance notification obligations for mass or collective redundancies.

For collective redundunds, consultation and notice obligations often begin before individual notices and can require weeks or months. Late consultation is a common and serious defect.

### Calculate Notice, Severance, And Final Pay Correctly

Money owed at termination is heavily regulated and miscalculation is a frequent dispute source.

Compute:

- statutory and contractual notice, or pay in lieu;
- accrued and unused leave payout (and whether carry-over is capped);
- severance or redundancy pay per statute, contract, or policy;
- commissions, bonuses, and incentives earned or vested;
- overtime owed;
- expense reimbursements;
- deductions permitted by law (overpayments, loans, advances) and their limits;
- final-pay deadline, which is often shorter than a normal pay cycle;
- tax withholding and reporting (including severance-specific treatment);
- benefits continuation or conversion rights;
- pension and equity treatment on termination.

Do not offset disputed amounts or withhold final pay as leverage; many jurisdictions penalize this directly.

### Structure Severance And Release Agreements To Be Enforceable

A severance agreement only releases what it validly covers, and formal defects can void it.

Address:

- consideration beyond what the employee already earned (often required);
- scope of release (claims, parties, known and unknown);
- carve-outs that cannot be waived (statutory whistleblower rights, accrued pension, workers' compensation, unemployment);
- non-disparagement, confidentiality, and cooperation clauses;
- references and neutral-verification language;
- return of property and data;
- non-compete or non-solicitation survival or new restrictions;
- tax treatment and Section 409A or equivalent compliance;
- revocation period where required (e.g., age-discrimination releases);
- language and formatting requirements;
- governing law and forum;
- timing relative to the employee's last day and any waiting periods.

Releases of age-discrimination claims in the U.S. often require specific periods, references to the statute, and revocation windows. Generic release language can fail to waive these claims.

### Handle Documentation, Communication, And Records

The termination file is what the employer will rely on if challenged.

Maintain:

- investigation notes and evidence;
- warnings, performance reviews, and improvement plans;
- the termination letter with reason, date, and final terms;
- consultation or meeting notes;
- acknowledgement of policies and handbook;
- final pay calculation;
- return-of-property receipts;
- benefit and continuation notices;
- reference or verification policy applied.

Communicate respectfully and consistently. Avoid casual explanations, blame, or admissions in exit conversations.

### Manage Post-Termination Obligations And Risks

Termination is not the end of obligations.

Check:

- benefits continuation notices (e.g., COBRA) and deadlines;
- unemployment insurance response and contest strategy;
- references and verification-of-employment requests;
- data retention and deletion for personnel records;
- return or secure company devices, accounts, and access;
- post-termination restrictive covenant enforcement;
- potential whistleblower, retaliation, or discrimination charges;
- regulator or works-council notifications;
- non-disparagement and confidentiality monitoring.

## Common Traps

### Deciding First, Documenting Later

Backdated or thin documentation is obvious and often fatal. Build the record before the decision.

### Skipping Consultation For Redundancy

Collective redundancy consultation has strict timing and scope. Starting individual notices first can void the process and trigger protective awards.

### Ignoring Protected Activity In The Timeline

A termination weeks after a harassment complaint or leave request invites retaliation claims. Map the timeline explicitly.

### Withholding Final Pay As Leverage

Holding final pay to force signature on a release is unlawful in many jurisdictions and can invalidate the release.

### Overbroad Or Defective Releases

A release that omits required language, consideration, revocation period, or statutory references may not waive the claims it targets.

### Inconsistent Treatment

Terminating one employee for conduct that others kept their jobs for undermines the stated reason and suggests pretext.

### Relabeling To Dodge Procedure

Calling a performance dismissal a "restructuring" (or the reverse) to avoid procedure rarely survives scrutiny and damages credibility.

## Self-Check

- Is the termination type classified (cause, no-cause, redundancy, resignation, fixed-term expiry, retirement) and matched to the correct legal regime?
- Is the reason lawful, substantiated, specific, evidenced, and documented before the decision?
- Has the protected-class and retaliation filter been run against the timeline of complaints, leave, accommodation, and protected activity?
- Are investigation, notice, opportunity to respond, impartial decision-maker, appeal, consultation, and collective-redundancy procedures satisfied?
- Are notice, pay in lieu, accrued leave, severance, commissions, overtime, reimbursements, deductions, final-pay deadline, tax, benefits continuation, pension, and equity computed correctly?
- Does the severance or release agreement include valid consideration, proper scope, non-waivable carve-outs, revocation periods, required statutory references, and tax compliance?
- Is the termination file complete with investigation notes, warnings, reviews, the termination letter, consultation notes, final pay, and return-of-property receipts?
- Are post-termination obligations (benefits continuation, unemployment, references, data retention, access revocation, restrictive covenants) addressed with deadlines?
- Does the output flag retaliation, discrimination, collective redundancy, and cross-border exposure and escalate to qualified employment counsel?
