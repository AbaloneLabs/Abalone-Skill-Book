# Accommodation and Leave Management

Accommodation and leave management is the area where well-intentioned employers most often create liability through process failures rather than overt discrimination. The law generally does not require the "right" accommodation; it requires a good-faith interactive process, individualized assessment, and a decision based on evidence rather than assumptions. An agent advising here must treat the process as the substance: documenting the dialogue, avoiding premature conclusions about what an employee can do, and navigating overlapping federal, state, and local regimes that rarely align cleanly.

## Core Rules

### The interactive process is the obligation, not just the outcome

- **Trigger the process early and broadly.** The duty to engage in the interactive process can arise from an oral request, a pattern of absences, or a manager's observation — not only from a formal written request or medical certification. Train managers to recognize triggers and route them to the right owner.
- **Individualized assessment is mandatory.** The core error is deciding what a person with a given condition "can't do" based on the condition rather than the specific employee's actual limitations and the specific job's essential functions. A current, well-drafted job description identifying essential functions is the anchor for the analysis.
- **Consider accommodation options in good faith.** Reasonable accommodation can include modified schedules, remote work, equipment, reassignment to a vacant position, modified duties, or leave. The employer need not eliminate essential functions, lower production standards, or displace other employees, but it must consider options it would not spontaneously choose.
- **Undue hardship is a defense the employer must support.** It is not a vague inconvenience; it requires evidence of significant difficulty or expense relative to the employer's size, structure, and resources. Undue hardship analyses differ between disability accommodation (ADA standard) and religious accommodation (Title VII standard, revised by Supreme Court precedent to a "substantial increased costs" standard).

### Leave is layered and overlapping

- **FMLA eligibility is narrow and technical.** The employee must work for a covered employer, have worked 1,250 hours in the prior 12 months, and work at a site with 50 employees within 75 miles. Confirm each element; do not assume.
- **Serious health condition categories** have specific definitions (e.g., continuing treatment, inpatient care, incapacity plus treatment). Medical certification should be requested timely and may be clarified, but the employer generally cannot demand an exact diagnosis.
- **State and local paid family and medical leave (PFML) programs** increasingly run parallel to FMLA, often with broader coverage, paid benefits, and different job-protection rules. Leaves may run concurrently or sequentially depending on the statute.
- **Pregnancy accommodation** is required in many jurisdictions on a lower threshold than the ADA — for example, a duty to accommodate known limitations related to pregnancy, childbirth, or related conditions without requiring the employee to prove a disability.
- **ADA leave as accommodation** may extend beyond FMLA entitlement when leave is the reasonable accommodation for a disability; terminating at FMLA exhaustion can be a violation if additional leave would be a reasonable accommodation.

### Return to work and fitness for duty

- **Return-to-work demands must be job-related and consistent with business necessity.** Medical inquiries and exams must be narrowly tailored; blanket "100% healed" or full-release demands can violate the ADA.
- **Light duty and transitional work** should be offered consistently and not used to screen out individuals with disabilities who can perform essential functions with accommodation.
- **Direct threat** determinations require an individualized, evidence-based assessment of significant risk of substantial harm that cannot be eliminated by reasonable accommodation, not stereotypes about a condition.

### Documentation and confidentiality

- Medical information obtained through accommodation and leave processes must be maintained confidentially and separate from personnel files, with access limited to those with a need to know.
- Document the interactive process steps, options considered, and the rationale for the outcome. Process documentation is often decisive in litigation.

## Common Traps

- **Asking for a diagnosis.** Certification should focus on functional limitations, duration, and the nature of restrictions, not the underlying diagnosis, except where a specific diagnosis is legally relevant.
- **Assuming an essential function.** Deciding an employee "must lift 50 pounds" without a validated, current job analysis that reflects how the job is actually performed.
- **Ending the process after one denial.** The interactive process is ongoing; if the first proposed accommodation fails, the duty to consider alternatives continues.
- **Treating FMLA exhaustion as a hard stop.** ADA leave-as-accommodation may require additional leave beyond FMLA, and state leave laws may extend job protection further.
- **Applying a single national policy to all workers.** State PFML, pregnancy, and sick leave laws can impose broader obligations; a uniform policy that satisfies federal law may underperform in high-protection states.
- **Confusing disability and religious accommodation standards.** The undue hardship thresholds differ; applying the ADA standard to a religious request (or vice versa) produces wrong answers.
- **Failing to train managers.** Most process failures originate with a line manager who denied a request, demanded too much medical detail, or treated a leave conversation as performance management.
- **Retaliation and interference.** Adverse actions taken close in time to protected leave or accommodation requests invite interference and retaliation claims, even when the underlying reason is legitimate.

## Self-Check

- Have I confirmed whether the employer is covered and the employee is eligible under FMLA, and checked parallel state PFML and pregnancy-accommodation regimes?
- Is the analysis based on a current, essential-functions job description and the specific employee's documented limitations, rather than assumptions about the condition?
- Have I documented the interactive process: requests, information exchanged, options considered, and the evidence-based rationale for the outcome?
- If denying an accommodation, have I supported undue hardship with concrete evidence (cost, disruption, staffing, structure) appropriate to the correct legal standard (disability vs. religious)?
- Have I avoided blanket medical inquiries and confirmed that return-to-work demands are job-related and consistent with business necessity?
- If the employee has exhausted FMLA, have I separately analyzed whether additional leave or reassignment is a reasonable accommodation under the ADA and state law?
- Have I flagged retaliation and interference risk for any adverse action near protected activity, and recommended a documented, non-pretextual rationale?
- Have I recommended qualified employment counsel admitted in the relevant jurisdiction for individualized accommodation decisions, medical certification disputes, and termination decisions involving protected leave?
