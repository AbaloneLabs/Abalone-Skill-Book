# Exclusivity And Non-Compete

Exclusivity and non-compete clauses restrain what a party can do, and they sit at the intersection of contract freedom and competition law. The judgment problem is that these clauses are powerful commercial tools but legally fragile: a restraint that is too broad in scope, geography, duration, or subject matter can be struck down as unenforceable, and in some jurisdictions restraints are presumptively invalid or regulated by statute. Agents often draft or review non-competes by copying a template, producing a clause that is either too weak to protect the interest or too broad to enforce. The recurring failure is failing to ask whether the restraint is reasonable, necessary, and lawful in the specific jurisdiction and context.

Use this skill before reviewing, drafting, or negotiating exclusivity, non-compete, non-solicit, employee non-raiding, or customer restriction clauses in any commercial, employment, or sale-of-business context. The goal is to make the agent define the restraint precisely, tailor it to a legitimate interest, assess reasonableness and enforceability, and account for the competition and employment law limits that may invalidate it. This is contract support; enforceability of restraints is highly jurisdiction-specific and requires qualified counsel.

## Core Rules

### Define Scope, Territory, Duration, And Subject Matter Precisely

A restraint must be defined along four dimensions, and each must be reasonable. Scope defines what is prohibited, such as competing business, soliciting customers, or hiring employees. Territory defines where the restraint applies, which can be geographic or market-based. Duration defines how long it lasts. Subject matter defines the products, services, or activities covered. Vague or over-broad definitions, like "any competing business anywhere for five years," are vulnerable to challenge. Define each dimension specifically and tie it to the legitimate interest being protected.

### Tie The Restraint To A Legitimate Protectable Interest

Courts enforce restraints only to protect a legitimate interest, not simply to suppress competition. Recognized interests typically include protecting confidential information and trade secrets, protecting customer relationships and goodwill, and preventing the misuse of specialized training. Identify the specific interest the restraint protects, because a restraint that protects no recognized interest is likely unenforceable. A non-compete that merely prevents ordinary competition, without protecting a recognized interest, will usually fail. State the interest and connect the restraint's scope to it.

### Distinguish Contexts, Since Enforceability Differs Sharply

The enforceability of non-competes depends heavily on context. Restraints in a sale of business, where the buyer protects the goodwill it paid for, are generally more enforceable. Restraints in employment are far more restricted, and many jurisdictions limit or ban employee non-competes, especially for lower-paid workers. Restraints in commercial contracts, like distributor or franchise exclusivity, fall in between and are judged on reasonableness and competition law. Identify the context and apply the right standard, because an enforceable sale-of-business non-compete may be void as an employment non-compete.

### Assess Reasonableness On Scope, Geography, And Time

Even where restraints are permitted, they must be reasonable. The reasonableness test typically asks whether the restraint is no wider than necessary to protect the legitimate interest, whether it is reasonable as between the parties, and whether it is consistent with the public interest. A restraint broader than necessary is vulnerable, even if the underlying interest is legitimate. Assess each dimension and be prepared to narrow, because courts in many jurisdictions can blue-pencil or refuse to reform over-broad clauses. Draft to the minimum necessary scope.

### Account For Competition And Antitrust Law

Restraints can violate competition or antitrust law independently of contract enforceability. Horizontal restraints between competitors, market allocation, price-fixing, and abuses of dominance can attract regulatory action and fines even if the contract clause looks reasonable between the parties. Exclusivity that forecloses a substantial share of a market can raise competition concerns. Assess the market position of the parties and the foreclosure effect, and where exclusivity is significant, consider whether notification or clearance is needed. Competition law can invalidate a clause and create liability beyond the contract.

### Handle Non-Solicit, Customer, And Employee Restrictions Carefully

Non-solicit and customer restrictions are often treated more leniently than full non-competes, but they still require reasonableness. Define solicitation, since passive response to an approach or general advertising may not count. For customer restrictions, identify whether they cover all customers or only those the restrained party dealt with, and for how long. For employee non-raiding, define which employees are covered and the interest protected, since blanket bans on hiring are vulnerable. Tailor each restriction to the interest and the relationship.

### Consider Consideration And Procedural Fairness

In some jurisdictions, enforceability depends on consideration and procedural fairness, especially in employment. A non-compete signed after employment begins may require fresh consideration, and a non-compete imposed without genuine choice may be challenged as unconscionable or against public policy. Confirm the restraint is supported by adequate consideration and disclosed at the right time. In sale-of-business contexts, the purchase price typically supplies consideration.

### Build In Severability And Reasonableness Mechanics

Because restraints are vulnerable to partial invalidity, build in severability and, where the jurisdiction permits, a mechanism for a court to reform an over-broad restraint to the maximum enforceable scope. Note that some jurisdictions refuse to blue-pencil and will strike an over-broad clause entirely, so drafting to the minimum necessary is safer than relying on reformation. Include a cascading or fallback scope so that if one dimension fails, a narrower version may survive, but only where the governing law permits this approach.

### Document The Legitimate Interest And Negotiation

Where enforceability may be challenged, document the legitimate interest being protected and the negotiation that produced the restraint. Evidence of the interest, the consideration, and the parties' intent supports enforcement. For sophisticated parties, the negotiation record can show the restraint was bargained for and reasonable. Keep this documentation consistent with the final clause, because inconsistencies undermine the case for enforceability.

## Common Traps

### Over-Broad Scope, Territory, Or Duration

A restraint broader than necessary is vulnerable. Draft to the minimum that protects the interest.

### No Legitimate Protectable Interest

A non-compete that merely suppresses competition will fail. Tie it to a recognized interest.

### Treating Employment Like Sale Of Business

Employee non-competes are far more restricted and often banned. Apply the right standard for the context.

### Ignoring Competition And Antitrust Law

Significant exclusivity can violate competition law and attract regulatory action. Assess market position and foreclosure.

### Vague Non-Solicit Definitions

Undefined solicitation leads to disputes over passive response and general advertising. Define it.

### Blanket Employee Non-Raiding

Broad hiring bans are vulnerable. Limit to specific employees and a defined interest.

### Relying On Blue-Pencil In A Non-Reformation Jurisdiction

Some courts strike over-broad clauses entirely rather than reforming. Draft narrowly.

## Self-Check

- Are scope, territory, duration, and subject matter defined precisely and tied to the legitimate interest?
- Is the restraint connected to a recognized protectable interest such as confidential information, customer goodwill, or specialized training?
- Is the context, sale of business, employment, or commercial, identified and the corresponding enforceability standard applied?
- Is the restraint reasonable in scope, geography, and time, and no wider than necessary to protect the interest?
- Are competition and antitrust risks assessed, including market position, foreclosure, and any notification or clearance needs?
- Are non-solicit, customer, and employee restrictions tailored with clear definitions and limited scope?
- Is the restraint supported by adequate consideration and procedurally fair, especially in employment?
- Are severability and reasonableness mechanics included, with awareness of whether the jurisdiction permits reformation?
- Is the legitimate interest and negotiation documented to support enforceability?
- Does the output flag that enforceability of restraints is highly jurisdiction-specific and requires qualified counsel?
