# Delivery And Acceptance

Delivery and acceptance terms determine when risk and title pass, when payment is earned, and when a party is stuck with non-conforming goods or services. The judgment problem is that these clauses are treated as logistics boilerplate, when in fact they allocate some of the most contested risks in commerce: who bears loss if goods are damaged in transit, who decides whether deliverables are acceptable, how long the buyer has to inspect, what counts as a defect, and what happens to payment if acceptance is withheld. Agents often summarize "delivery within 30 days, acceptance within 10" while missing the Incoterm that places transit risk on the buyer, an acceptance process the seller can game, an inspection period too short to test, or a defect notice rule that waives claims if missed.

Use this skill before reviewing, drafting, or negotiating delivery, acceptance, inspection, risk of loss, or defect notice terms in any supply, services, software, or distribution agreement. The goal is to make the agent map the delivery and acceptance chain, allocate risk of loss consciously, design a fair and usable acceptance process, set realistic inspection periods, and align defect notice with the parties' operational reality. This is contract support; final commercial terms, especially for international or regulated supply, require qualified counsel.

## Core Rules

### Map The Delivery Chain And Where Risk Transfers

Delivery is a chain, not an event, and risk transfers at a defined point. Identify who is responsible for manufacturing, packing, transport, insurance, customs, unloading, and installation, and at which point title and risk of loss pass from seller to buyer. Where Incoterms are used, confirm the chosen term matches the commercial intent, because EXW, FOB, CIF, DDP, and similar terms place very different obligations and risks on each party. A buyer who agrees to FOB origin bears transit risk and must arrange insurance; a buyer who expects DDP may be surprised by customs duties. State the delivery point, the risk transfer point, and the responsibilities on each side explicitly.

### Design Acceptance Criteria That Are Objective And Testable

Acceptance determines when deliverables are deemed satisfactory and payment is earned. Define acceptance criteria that are objective, measurable, and tied to specifications, because subjective acceptance lets the buyer reject for any reason and subjective rejection is a common source of dispute. For software and services, specify functional, performance, and documentation criteria, the test environment, the test period, and the procedure for retesting after fixes. Distinguish acceptance, which certifies conformity, from warranty, which governs later defects. A strong acceptance process is repeatable; a weak one relies on the buyer's satisfaction.

### Balance Acceptance With Anti-Withholding And Deemed Acceptance

A buyer can delay payment indefinitely by withholding acceptance, so balance the process with protections for the seller. Include a deemed acceptance rule, where deliverables are deemed accepted if not rejected within the test period with specific defects, and an anti-withholding clause preventing the buyer from rejecting for minor or unrelated issues. Require that rejection state the specific non-conformities, so the seller can cure. These protections make acceptance a real process rather than an open-ended veto. Without them, the seller bears the risk of indefinite delay.

### Set Realistic Inspection Periods And Defect Notice Rules

Inspection and defect notice periods determine how long a buyer has to find and report problems, and missing them can waive claims. Set periods that match the nature of the goods or services: obvious defects need short periods, latent defects need longer periods or separate latent defect rules. Define what constitutes a defect, the notice required, and the cure obligations, such as repair, replacement, refund, or credit. Coordinate the defect notice rule with the warranty and limitations periods so they do not contradict. A defect notice period too short to detect latent defects can leave the buyer without recourse.

### Align Acceptance With Payment And Milestones

Acceptance and payment are linked, and the link shapes cash flow and leverage. Determine whether payment is due on delivery, on acceptance, or on milestone completion, and what happens if acceptance is delayed or disputed. Check whether milestones have dependencies that can defer acceptance indefinitely, and whether partial acceptance is allowed for partial deliverables. Align the payment triggers with the acceptance process so that neither party can manipulate timing to the other's disadvantage. A payment-on-acceptance structure with no deemed acceptance leaves the seller exposed.

### Allocate Risk Of Loss For Non-Conforming And In-Transit Goods

Risk of loss rules differ depending on whether goods conform. Under many regimes, risk passes on delivery for conforming goods, but if the buyer rightfully rejects non-conforming goods, the risk may revert to the seller. State the rule explicitly, especially for goods damaged during a rejection or return. For in-transit loss, confirm who bears the risk based on the delivery term and who must insure. Ambiguity here creates disputes when goods are lost or damaged between delivery and final disposition.

### Coordinate With Warranties, Remedies, And Limitations

Delivery and acceptance interact with warranties, remedies, and limitation of liability. A defect found after acceptance typically falls under warranty rather than acceptance, with different cure and remedy rules. Confirm the warranty period starts at acceptance or delivery as intended, and that remedies for non-conformity, such as repair, replacement, refund, or credit, are consistent across the acceptance, warranty, and limitation clauses. Inconsistent remedy language creates gaps the other side can exploit.

### Account For International, Customs, And Regulatory Delivery Issues

Cross-border delivery adds customs duties, export controls, sanctions, import licensing, and regulatory compliance. Identify who is responsible for each, based on the Incoterm and the contract, because a DDP term puts customs and duties on the seller while an EXW term puts nearly everything on the buyer. Check export control and sanctions compliance for the goods, destinations, and end users, and confirm regulatory certifications required at the delivery point. International delivery clauses that ignore these allocate risk no one intended to bear.

## Common Traps

### Silent Or Mismatched Incoterms

A missing or wrong Incoterm leaves transit risk and duties on an unintended party. State the term and responsibilities.

### Subjective Acceptance Criteria

"Acceptable to buyer" lets the buyer reject arbitrarily. Use objective, testable criteria.

### No Deemed Acceptance Or Anti-Withholding

Without these, the buyer can delay payment indefinitely. Add them to balance the process.

### Inspection Period Too Short For Latent Defects

A short period can waive latent defect claims. Match the period to the defect type.

### Payment Triggers That Let One Party Manipulate Timing

Payment on acceptance with no deemed acceptance exposes the seller. Align triggers with the process.

### Inconsistent Risk Of Loss And Remedy Language

Contradictions between delivery, warranty, and limitation clauses create gaps. Reconcile them.

### Ignoring Customs, Export, And Regulatory Delivery Issues

Cross-border delivery without addressing duties, export controls, and certifications allocates unintended risk.

## Self-Check

- Is the delivery chain mapped, with responsibilities for manufacture, packing, transport, insurance, customs, and installation, and the point of title and risk transfer stated?
- Does the chosen Incoterm match the commercial intent, with obligations and risks allocated as the parties intend?
- Are acceptance criteria objective, measurable, and tied to specifications, with test environment, period, and retest procedure defined?
- Are deemed acceptance and anti-withholding clauses present, with rejection required to state specific non-conformities?
- Are inspection and defect notice periods realistic for the defect type, with latent defect rules where needed, and coordinated with warranty and limitations?
- Are payment triggers aligned with the acceptance process, with partial acceptance and milestone dependencies addressed?
- Is risk of loss for conforming, non-conforming, in-transit, and rejected goods allocated explicitly?
- Are acceptance, warranty, remedy, and limitation clauses consistent across the agreement?
- Are customs duties, export controls, sanctions, import licensing, and regulatory certifications allocated for cross-border delivery?
- Does the output flag that final commercial terms, especially international or regulated supply, require qualified counsel?
