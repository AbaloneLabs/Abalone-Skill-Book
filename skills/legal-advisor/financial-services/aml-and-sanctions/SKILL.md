# AML And Sanctions

AML and sanctions are the areas where financial institutions face the largest penalties and where the rules impose obligations that run against commercial convenience. Agents often treat AML as a paperwork exercise (collect the KYC documents) and miss that the obligation is ongoing monitoring and reporting, or treat sanctions as a list-checking exercise and miss that the rules prohibit dealing with sanctioned parties regardless of whether they appear on a list. The strict liability nature of sanctions and the extraterritorial reach of major sanctions programs (notably US sanctions) make this an area where good intentions are not a defense.

Use this skill before advising on AML programs, customer onboarding, transaction monitoring, sanctions screening, correspondent relationships, or enforcement response. The goal is to force the agent to build a risk-based program, understand the scope and reach of sanctions, and identify the reporting and blocking obligations. AML and sanctions law is jurisdiction-specific and enforcement-intensive; conclusions must be confirmed against the applicable statutes, regulations, and guidance by qualified financial services and sanctions counsel.

## Core Rules

### Build A Risk-Based AML Program

AML is a program, not a form.

Address:

- the risk assessment (customers, products, geographies, channels);
- customer due diligence (CDD) proportionate to risk;
- enhanced due diligence (EDD) for higher risk;
- ongoing monitoring and the obligation to update customer information;
- suspicious activity reporting (the obligation, timing, and confidentiality);
- the AML compliance officer, training, and independent audit;
- governance and senior management responsibility.

A risk-based program is the expected standard. Document the risk assessment and tailor the controls to the risk.

### Conduct Customer Due Diligence And Identify Beneficial Ownership

CDD is the foundation.

Address:

- the identification and verification of the customer (identity, address, legal form);
- the identification and verification of beneficial owners (the threshold, typically 25 percent, and the control test);
- the understanding of the purpose and nature of the relationship;
- ongoing due diligence and the refresh of information;
- the treatment of politically exposed persons (PEPs);
- the reliance on third parties and the limits.

Beneficial ownership identification is a core obligation and a frequent enforcement focus. Identify the beneficial owners through ownership and control, and verify them.

### Monitor Transactions And Report Suspicious Activity

Monitoring and reporting are ongoing.

Address:

- the transaction monitoring system and the alert investigation;
- the red flags for money laundering, terrorist financing, and sanctions evasion;
- the decision to file a suspicious activity report (SAR) or suspicious transaction report (STR);
- the timing of the report (promptly after the decision);
- the confidentiality of the report (no tipping off the customer);
- the ongoing monitoring after a report and the decision to maintain or exit the relationship.

Filing a SAR is required when there is a suspicion, regardless of the transaction size. Maintain confidentiality and do not tip off.

### Understand The Scope And Reach Of Sanctions

Sanctions are broad and strict liability.

Address:

- the scope of each sanctions program (comprehensive, targeted, sectoral);
- the lists (SDN, consolidated, sectoral) and the 50 percent rule (an entity owned 50 percent or more by a listed party is blocked even if not listed);
- the strict liability nature (knowledge or intent is not required);
- the extraterritorial reach (US sanctions reach US persons, US dollar transactions, and US-origin goods, and can reach non-US persons under secondary sanctions);
- the prohibition on dealing with sanctioned parties and the blocking (freezing) obligation;
- the licensing process for authorized transactions.

Sanctions are not just list-checking. The 50 percent rule and the broad prohibitions mean that a party can be sanctioned without appearing on a list. Screen against the lists and analyze ownership.

### Screen And Manage Sanctions Risk Across Products And Channels

Sanctions risk varies by product and channel.

Address:

- the screening of customers, counterparties, and beneficial owners;
- the screening of transactions (payments, trade finance, correspondent banking);
- the screening of vessels, aircraft, and goods in trade finance;
- the screening of crypto wallets and VASP counterparties;
- the management of false positives and the investigation of alerts;
- the handling of potential matches (blocking, rejecting, reporting).

Trade finance, correspondent banking, and crypto have elevated sanctions risk. Build screening for vessels, goods, and counterparties, and handle matches correctly.

### Manage Correspondent Banking And Trade Finance Controls

Correspondent banking is high-risk.

Address:

- the due diligence on respondent banks (including their AML controls and sanctions risk);
- the prohibition on payable-through accounts and nested relationships without controls;
- the assessment of the respondent's customer base and geographies;
- the trade finance controls (vessel tracking, goods screening, dual-use and controlled goods, destination and end-user).

Correspondent and trade finance are frequent enforcement areas. Conduct the respondent due diligence and the trade controls.

### Respond To Investigations And Enforcement

Enforcement is severe and reputational.

Prepare for:

- regulator examinations and requests for information;
- the obligation to preserve documents and the privilege issues;
- internal investigations and the self-disclosure decision;
- the settlement and penalty negotiation;
- the monitorship and remediation requirements;
- the reputational and business consequences (loss of correspondent relationships, de-risking).

Penalties for AML and sanctions violations can be enormous and can trigger de-risking and loss of access. Treat investigations seriously and consider self-disclosure.

### Address The Extraterritorial And Secondary Sanctions Risk

US and other major sanctions reach beyond their borders.

Assess:

- the US nexus (US persons, US dollars, US-origin goods, US correspondents);
- the secondary sanctions risk for non-US persons dealing with sanctioned parties;
- the conflict of laws (complying with US sanctions may breach blocking statutes or local law);
- the blocking statute risk (EU Blocking Statute and equivalents).

A non-US institution can face US sanctions exposure through US dollar clearing or US correspondents, and conflict-of-laws risk from blocking statutes. Assess the nexus and the conflict.

## Common Traps

### Treating AML As Paperwork

AML is an ongoing program of monitoring and reporting. Build the program.

### Treating Sanctions As List-Checking

The 50 percent rule and broad prohibitions mean parties can be sanctioned without being listed. Analyze ownership.

### Filing A SAR Too Late Or Tipping Off

The report must be prompt and confidential. Do not delay or disclose.

### Assuming Strict Liability Has A Knowledge Defense

Sanctions are strict liability. Good faith is not a defense to the violation.

### Ignoring The 50 Percent Rule

An entity owned 50 percent or more by a listed party is blocked. Screen ownership.

### Overlooking Correspondent And Trade Finance Risk

These are high-risk channels. Conduct respondent due diligence and trade controls.

### Underestimating Extraterritorial Reach

US sanctions reach non-US persons through US dollar clearing and correspondents. Assess the nexus.

### Ignoring Conflict Of Laws

Complying with sanctions may breach blocking statutes. Assess the conflict.

## Self-Check

- Is the AML program risk-based with assessment, CDD/EDD, monitoring, SAR, governance, training, and audit?
- Is customer due diligence (identification, beneficial ownership, purpose, refresh, PEPs, third-party reliance) conducted?
- Is transaction monitoring (system, red flags, SAR decision, timing, confidentiality, ongoing monitoring) in place?
- Is the scope and reach of sanctions (programs, lists, 50 percent rule, strict liability, extraterritorial, blocking, licensing) understood?
- Is sanctions screening (customers, counterparties, beneficial owners, payments, trade finance, vessels, crypto) managed with match handling?
- Are correspondent banking (respondent due diligence, payable-through, nested) and trade finance (vessels, goods, dual-use, end-user) controls in place?
- Is the enforcement response (examinations, preservation, investigation, self-disclosure, settlement, monitorship, de-risking) prepared?
- Is the extraterritorial and secondary sanctions risk (US nexus, secondary, conflict of laws, blocking statutes) assessed?
- Does the output recommend confirmation against the applicable statutes, regulations, and guidance by qualified financial services and sanctions counsel?
