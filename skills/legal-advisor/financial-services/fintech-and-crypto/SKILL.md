# Fintech And Crypto

Fintech and crypto regulation is the hardest area to advise on because the same activity can be regulated differently depending on how it is characterized, and the characterization is contested and changing. Agents often import one jurisdiction's classification (a token is a utility token, a smart contract is just code) into another where the same token is a security and the same activity is a regulated financial service. The cross-border nature of crypto compounds this: a token sale, an exchange, or a custody service can reach users and regulators in many jurisdictions simultaneously.

Use this skill before advising on fintech market entry, token classification, exchange or custody licensing, AML for digital assets, token sales, or cross-border crypto services. The goal is to force the agent to classify the token or activity under each relevant jurisdiction's framework, identify the licensing and AML triggers, and avoid assuming that one jurisdiction's treatment governs globally. Fintech and crypto law is rapidly evolving and jurisdiction-specific; conclusions must be confirmed against the applicable statutes, regulations, and guidance by qualified financial services and technology counsel.

## Core Rules

### Classify The Token Or Activity Under Each Jurisdiction

Classification is the central question.

For tokens, distinguish:

- payment tokens or virtual currencies;
- utility tokens (access to a product or service);
- security tokens (investment contracts, equity, debt);
- stablecoins (asset-referenced, fiat-referenced, algorithmic);
- non-fungible tokens (and whether they are truly unique or fractionalized investment products);
- governance tokens.

For activities, distinguish:

- exchange and trading platforms;
- custody and wallet services (custodial vs. non-custodial);
- lending, staking, and yield products;
- payment and remittance services;
- mining and validation.

The same token can be a security in one jurisdiction and a utility token in another. Classify under each relevant framework, and do not assume global uniformity.

### Identify The Licensing Triggers

Regulated activities require licenses.

Address:

- the definition of regulated activities (dealing, advising, arranging, managing, custody, exchange);
- the licensing requirements for each activity (capital, governance, systems, personnel);
- the treatment of decentralized and non-custodial models (and whether they are regulated);
- the cross-border reach (marketing to users, solicitation, and the need for local licensing);
- the sandbox and innovation hub pathways (and their limits).

A crypto exchange or custody service is usually a regulated financial service. Confirm the licensing model in each user jurisdiction.

### Apply AML And KYC To Digital Asset Activities

Crypto AML is a major enforcement focus.

Address:

- whether the activity is a virtual asset service provider (VASP) subject to AML obligations;
- customer due diligence (KYC) and ongoing monitoring;
- transaction monitoring and suspicious activity reporting;
- the travel rule for virtual asset transfers;
- sanctions screening of wallets and counterparties;
- the risk of privacy coins, mixers, and unhosted wallets.

VASP AML obligations mirror traditional financial services. Build KYC, monitoring, and travel rule compliance, and address privacy coin and mixer exposure.

### Structure Token Sales And Offerings

Token sales have securities and AML exposure.

Address:

- the securities law analysis (the Howey-type investment contract test and equivalents);
- the registration or exemption pathway for security tokens;
- the disclosure and marketing rules;
- the AML/KYC of purchasers;
- the resale and secondary trading restrictions;
- the cross-border reach of the offering.

A token sale that funds a common enterprise with expectation of profit from the efforts of others is likely a security. Analyze under each jurisdiction and structure exemptions and disclosures.

### Address Stablecoins And E-Money

Stablecoins are under increasing regulation.

Address:

- the classification (e-money, payment instrument, security, commodity);
- the reserve, redemption, and transparency requirements;
- the licensing of issuance and custody;
- the interaction with monetary policy and central bank digital currencies.

Stablecoins are not unregulated. Address the reserve, redemption, and licensing requirements.

### Manage Custody, Client Assets, And Segregation

Custody is a regulated activity with asset protection rules.

Address:

- the licensing of custody;
- the segregation of client assets from proprietary assets;
- the use, lending, or rehypothecation of client assets (and consent requirements);
- the security and key management;
- the insolvency treatment of client assets (and the risk of commingling).

Commingling or misuse of client assets in insolvency can result in loss to users. Segregate and document the trust or client asset arrangements.

### Handle Cross-Border And Jurisdictional Reach

Crypto is inherently cross-border.

Address:

- the jurisdictional reach of marketing and solicitation;
- the blocking of users in restricted jurisdictions (geoblocking and its limits);
- the choice of law and dispute resolution;
- the enforcement risk from foreign regulators;
- the data transfer and privacy issues.

Marketing or soliciting users in a jurisdiction can trigger its regulation even without a local presence. Assess the cross-border reach and the geoblocking strategy.

### Monitor Decentralized And Smart Contract Models

Decentralization does not automatically avoid regulation.

Address:

- whether the model is truly decentralized (or whether there is a controlling party);
- the responsibility for protocol operation, upgrades, and governance;
- the treatment of DAOs and their legal personality;
- the AML and consumer protection obligations of decentralized protocols;
- the smart contract audit, security, and upgrade risk.

Regulators are increasingly asserting jurisdiction over decentralized models that have a controlling party or that solicit users. Assess the decentralization and the residual responsibilities.

## Common Traps

### Assuming One Jurisdiction's Token Classification Governs Globally

The same token can be a security in one jurisdiction and a utility token in another. Classify under each.

### Treating Crypto Activities As Unregulated

Exchanges, custody, and token sales are usually regulated. Confirm the licensing model.

### Skipping AML For Crypto

VASP AML obligations mirror traditional finance. Build KYC, monitoring, and the travel rule.

### Marketing Globally Without Assessing Cross-Border Reach

Soliciting users triggers regulation. Assess the reach and geoblocking.

### Assuming Decentralization Avoids Regulation

Regulators assert jurisdiction over models with controlling parties. Assess the decentralization.

### Commingling Client Assets

Commingling in insolvency causes user losses. Segregate and document.

### Treating Stablecoins As Unregulated

Stablecoins have reserve, redemption, and licensing requirements. Address them.

### Ignoring Secondary Trading Restrictions

Security tokens have resale restrictions. Structure the exemptions.

## Self-Check

- Is the token or activity classified (payment, utility, security, stablecoin, NFT, governance; exchange, custody, lending, payment) under each relevant jurisdiction?
- Are the licensing triggers (regulated activities, requirements, decentralized models, cross-border reach, sandbox) identified?
- Is AML/KYC (VASP status, due diligence, monitoring, travel rule, sanctions, privacy coins) applied?
- Are token sales (securities analysis, registration or exemption, disclosure, AML, resale restrictions, cross-border) structured?
- Are stablecoins and e-money (classification, reserves, redemption, licensing) addressed?
- Are custody, client asset segregation, rehypothecation, key management, and insolvency treatment managed?
- Is the cross-border reach (marketing, geoblocking, choice of law, enforcement, data) assessed?
- Are decentralized and smart contract models (decentralization, protocol responsibility, DAO personality, AML, audit) addressed?
- Does the output recommend confirmation against the applicable statutes, regulations, and guidance by qualified financial services and technology counsel?
