# En Banc And Supreme Court

Beyond the initial appellate panel lies further review: en banc review by the full court, and review by a supreme court through a petition for certiorari or its equivalent. These are not ordinary appeals. They are discretionary, granted rarely, and the criteria for grant differ fundamentally from the criteria for reversal on the merits. Agents often file en banc or cert petitions as if they were second appellate briefs, re-arguing the merits of the panel decision, when in fact these courts grant review to resolve circuit splits, correct egregious errors of exceptional importance, or settle questions of national significance. A petition that does not frame the case in those terms will almost certainly be denied.

The statistical reality is sobering: en banc review and certiorari are granted in a small fraction of cases. The decision to seek further review must be realistic about the odds and strategic about the goals, which may include delay, preserving issues, or influencing the development of law even if review is denied. Use this skill before deciding whether to seek en banc or supreme court review, before drafting a petition, before coordinating amicus support, or before advising a client on the prospects. The goal is to help the agent evaluate and pursue further review strategically, not to provide legal advice, which requires qualified appellate counsel experienced in the specific court.

## Core Rules

### Understand That Further Review Is Discretionary, Not Of Right

En banc and supreme court review are discretionary. The court chooses which cases to hear, and the criteria for grant are about significance, not merely correctness.

Understand:

- en banc review is granted for questions of exceptional importance or to maintain uniformity of the court's decisions;
- certiorari is granted for questions of national significance, circuit splits, or egregious errors;
- the court is not correcting most errors, even clear ones;
- the denial of review is not a ruling on the merits;
- the odds of grant are low, often single-digit percentages;
- the criteria differ from the merits-reversal criteria of a panel appeal.

A petition must explain why this case, among thousands, warrants the court's limited attention. Merits correctness alone is rarely enough.

### Identify The Criteria That Actually Drive Grants

To predict and frame a petition, identify the criteria the specific court uses to grant review. These are the questions the petition must answer.

Identify:

- a circuit split, where courts disagree on the same legal question;
- a question of exceptional public or national importance;
- a conflict with a supreme court precedent;
- an egregious error that undermines the integrity of the process;
- a recurring question that needs definitive resolution;
- the urgency or ripeness of the question;
- the suitability of this case as a vehicle, with a clean record and preserved issue.

A clean vehicle with a circuit split on a recurring question is the strongest case for certiorari. A messy record or an idiosyncratic fact pattern weakens the petition even if the legal question is interesting.

### Assess The Vehicle Quality Realistically

The supreme court and en banc courts care about the vehicle, the specific case and record, as much as the legal question. A great legal question in a bad vehicle is often denied.

Assess vehicle:

- whether the issue was preserved below;
- whether the record is clean and adequate;
- whether there are threshold jurisdictional or standing problems;
- whether the facts are sympathetic or problematic;
- whether there are alternative grounds that could decide the case without reaching the question;
- whether the case is interlocutory or final;
- whether there are competing petitions on the same question.

A court may deny certiorari because the vehicle is poor even if the question is important. Be honest about vehicle quality before investing.

### Frame The Question Presented For Significance

The question presented is the most important sentence in a cert petition. It must frame a question of broad significance, not the specific dispute.

Frame the question:

- to highlight the circuit split or national importance;
- in terms of legal principle, not party-specific facts;
- to be answerable with a clear rule;
- to avoid being too narrow or too broad;
- to invite the court's interest in settling the law;
- consistently with how the issue was preserved below.

A question framed as "whether the panel erred in this case" will be denied. A question framed as "whether [statute] requires X, in light of the circuit split" has a chance.

### Build Support Through Amicus Curiae Briefs

Amicus briefs can signal the broader significance of a question and the interest of important stakeholders. They are part of a serious cert strategy.

Build support:

- identify stakeholders affected by the legal question;
- coordinate amicus briefs from industry, government, academics, or public-interest groups;
- time amicus filings to support the petition or opposition;
- ensure amici add distinct perspectives, not repetitive arguments;
- use amicus interest to demonstrate national significance;
- avoid overloading the court with duplicative briefs.

Amicus briefs do not substitute for a strong petition, but they can reinforce significance. Coordinate quality over quantity.

### Evaluate The Strategic Value Beyond Winning

Even when the odds of grant are low, seeking further review can have strategic value. The decision should weigh these alongside the odds.

Evaluate strategic value:

- delay of an adverse judgment during the petition;
- preservation of issues for later or collateral review;
- influence on the development of law through the question framing;
- settlement leverage created by pending review;
- the cost and distraction of the petition;
- the risk of an adverse ruling that worsens the law;
- the effect on parallel or related cases.

A petition can create value even if denied, but a grant followed by an adverse ruling can make bad law. Weigh the downside risk.

### Coordinate With The Merits And Remedy Strategy

If review is granted, the case proceeds to merits briefing on the granted question. The petition strategy must anticipate the merits.

Coordinate:

- whether the question framed in the petition is winnable on the merits;
- the record and authority available for merits briefing;
- the remedy sought, reversal, vacatur, or remand;
- the effect on the parties and related cases;
- the timeline and cost of merits briefing and argument;
- the possibility of a narrow ruling that does not fully resolve the dispute.

A petition that wins review but loses on the merits can be worse than no petition. Assess the merits posture before seeking review.

### Manage Deadlines And Procedural Requirements

En banc and cert petitions have strict deadlines and procedural requirements. Missing them forecloses further review.

Manage:

- the deadline to petition for rehearing or en banc review, often short;
- the deadline to file a cert petition, typically 90 days from the judgment or denial of rehearing;
- the content and form requirements of the petition;
- the certification and service requirements;
- the timing of amicus briefs;
- the effect of pending post-judgment motions on the clock;
- the rules on rehearing and renewed petitions.

Calendar the deadlines immediately upon the panel decision. The cert clock runs from the denial of rehearing in many courts, so rehearing may be a prerequisite.

## Common Traps

### Treating Further Review As A Second Appeal

En banc and cert are discretionary and significance-driven. Re-arguing the panel merits fails.

### Ignoring The Vehicle

A great question in a bad vehicle is denied. Assess vehicle quality honestly.

### Framing The Question Too Narrowly

A party-specific question signals no broader significance. Frame for national importance.

### Overestimating The Odds

Grant rates are low. Be realistic and weigh strategic value.

### Forgetting The Downside Risk

A grant followed by an adverse merits ruling makes bad law. Assess the merits posture.

### Missing The Petition Deadline

Deadlines are strict. Calendar rehearing and cert deadlines immediately.

### Duplicative Amicus Briefs

Repetitive amicus filings clutter the record. Coordinate distinct perspectives.

## Self-Check

- Is the discretionary nature of en banc and cert understood, with criteria of exceptional importance, uniformity, national significance, circuit splits, and egregious error distinguished from merits reversal?
- Are the grant criteria identified, including circuit split, public importance, supreme court conflict, egregious error, recurring question, urgency, and vehicle suitability?
- Is vehicle quality assessed for preservation, clean record, jurisdictional soundness, sympathetic facts, absence of alternative grounds, finality, and competing petitions?
- Is the question presented framed for significance, highlighting the split or importance, in legal principle terms, answerable with a clear rule, properly scoped, and consistent with preservation?
- Is amicus support built through stakeholder identification, coordinated filings, timing, distinct perspectives, significance demonstration, and quality over quantity?
- Is strategic value beyond winning evaluated, including delay, issue preservation, law-development influence, settlement leverage, cost, downside risk, and effect on related cases?
- Is the petition coordinated with merits and remedy strategy, considering winnability, record and authority, remedy sought, effect on parties, timeline and cost, and narrow-ruling risk?
- Are deadlines and procedural requirements managed for rehearing or en banc deadlines, cert deadline, content and form, certification and service, amicus timing, post-judgment motion effects, and rehearing rules?
- Does the output escalate to qualified appellate counsel experienced in en banc and supreme court practice for petition strategy, question framing, amicus coordination, and merits assessment?
