# Liquidation And Reorganization

Bankruptcy is a federal legal process that restructures the relationship between an insolvent debtor and its creditors. It is not simply "going out of business." The choice among liquidation, reorganization, or an out-of-court workout determines whether the company survives, who controls its assets, which contracts continue, how creditors are paid, and what liabilities survive. Agents often describe bankruptcy as a single event and miss that each chapter has distinct rules: the automatic stay freezes collection, the trustee or debtor-in-possession controls the estate, priority and security govern who gets paid, the plan allocates losses across classes, and the discharge defines what survives. A company that files the wrong chapter, or that files without preparing the estate, can convert a recoverable situation into a liquidation, or can trigger preference and fraudulent transfer clawbacks against its recent counterparties.

Use this skill before recommending a bankruptcy filing, before advising a debtor on chapter choice, before a creditor assesses its recovery, before planning a sale of assets under bankruptcy, or when negotiating a plan of reorganization. The goal is to force the agent to map the chapter, the estate, the stay, the priority scheme, and the plan structure, and to coordinate with the out-of-court alternatives. Bankruptcy is a specialized federal practice with local rules; all bankruptcy work requires qualified bankruptcy counsel.

## Core Rules

### Choose The Right Chapter For The Situation

The bankruptcy chapters serve different purposes and suit different debtors.

Compare:

- Chapter 7 liquidation, where a trustee collects and sells non-exempt assets and distributes proceeds to creditors, used when the business cannot continue;
- Chapter 11 reorganization, where the debtor usually remains in possession, continues operations, and proposes a plan to restructure debts and emerge;
- Chapter 13 wage-earner reorganization, for individuals with regular income, with debt limits;
- Chapter 12 for family farmers and fishermen;
- Subchapter V of Chapter 11, a streamlined small-business reorganization with debt limits;
- out-of-court workouts and assignments for the benefit of creditors as alternatives.

The chapter choice depends on whether the business should continue, the debtor type, debt levels, the desire for control, and the complexity of the estate. A business that could reorganize under Chapter 11 but files Chapter 7 loses the going-concern value.

### Understand The Automatic Stay And Its Scope

Filing bankruptcy triggers an automatic stay that freezes most collection actions against the debtor and the estate. The stay is broad but not absolute.

Map the stay:

- it stops lawsuits, foreclosures, repossessions, garnishments, and collection calls;
- it does not stop criminal proceedings, certain family law actions, or regulatory police-power actions;
- secured creditors can seek relief from the stay to pursue collateral;
- lessors can seek relief to reclaim leased property subject to time limits;
- the stay does not apply to non-debtor parties unless extended by court order;
- repeat filings face stay limitations.

The stay is the debtor's primary breathing spell, but creditors can and do obtain relief, especially where collateral is not adequately protected or the debtor has no equity in the asset.

### Identify The Estate And Who Controls It

The bankruptcy estate includes the debtor's legal and equitable interests at filing, plus certain after-acquired property and recoveries. Control depends on the chapter.

Determine:

- in Chapter 7, a trustee takes control and liquidates;
- in Chapter 11, the debtor typically remains as debtor-in-possession with trustee-like powers and duties;
- the appointment of a trustee in Chapter 11 is exceptional, for cause such as fraud or gross mismanagement;
- the US trustee oversees administration and appoints committees;
- the official committee of unsecured creditors represents creditor interests;
- professionals must be retained with court approval and disclose conflicts.

The debtor-in-possession owes fiduciary duties to the estate and creditors, not only to shareholders. Mismanagement can lead to trustee appointment or plan denial.

### Classify And Prioritize Claims And Interests

Bankruptcy pays claims in a statutory priority order. Understanding priority is essential to predicting recovery.

The priority scheme generally provides:

- secured claims, paid from collateral up to its value;
- priority unsecured claims, including certain domestic support, administrative expenses, wage and benefit claims, tax claims, and deposits;
- general unsecured claims, paid pro rata from remaining assets;
- equity interests, paid last if at all.

Within secured claims, determine whether the lien is fully secured, undersecured, or unsecured, since an undersecured creditor has a secured claim up to collateral value and an unsecured claim for the deficiency. Priority claims can consume estate value and leave general unsecured creditors with little.

### Treat Executory Contracts And Leases Correctly

Executory contracts and unexpired leases can be assumed or rejected, which can add or remove value from the estate.

Address:

- the debtor's option to assume or reject, subject to cure of defaults and adequate assurance of future performance;
- the time limits for deciding, generally shorter for leases;
- assignment of contracts to third parties, subject to adequate assurance and prohibitions on assignment of personal-service contracts;
- contracts with ip licensing, non-assignment, and anti-assignment clauses, which bankruptcy can override subject to statutory limits;
- the treatment of default and cure amounts;
- the effect of rejection as a breach giving rise to a damage claim.

Assuming favorable contracts and rejecting burdensome ones is a core value-creation tool in Chapter 11.

### Structure And Confirm A Plan Of Reorganization

A Chapter 11 plan classifies claims and interests and proposes their treatment. Confirmation requires meeting statutory requirements.

Plan elements:

- classification of claims and interests;
- treatment of each class, including payment, modification, or impairment;
- the means of execution, including financing, asset sales, or new equity;
- the best interests of creditors test, requiring creditors to receive at least what they would in Chapter 7;
- feasibility, requiring the plan to be likely to succeed;
- acceptance by impaired classes, with the cramdown alternative if a class rejects;
- the absolute priority rule, barring junior interests from receiving value unless senior classes are paid in full or consent.

Cramdown is powerful but constrained by the absolute priority rule. A plan that pays equity while impairing a class of creditors without that class's consent faces strict scrutiny.

### Use Sales Of Assets Under Bankruptcy Properly

Bankruptcy sales can transfer assets free and clear of liens and interests, which is valuable but constrained.

Confirm:

- the sale is for fair value, with notice and an auction where appropriate;
- liens attach to sale proceeds in priority order;
- successor liability is addressed, since some liabilities may follow the assets;
- the sale is in the ordinary course or requires notice and a hearing;
- stalking-horse bids and break-up fees are reasonable;
- the buyer obtains free-and-clear treatment only for interests the court can extinguish.

Bankruptcy sales can unlock value but must follow procedures to withstand challenge.

### Coordinate With Out-Of-Court Alternatives

Bankruptcy is not the only path. Out-of-court workouts, assignments for the benefit of creditors, and state-law receiverships may be faster, cheaper, or less damaging.

Compare:

- speed, cost, and publicity;
- the ability to bind dissenting creditors;
- the automatic stay and injunction powers;
- tax and litigation consequences;
- the impact on guarantors and affiliates;
- the availability of asset sales free and clear.

## Common Traps

### Filing The Wrong Chapter

A business that could reorganize under Chapter 11 but files Chapter 7 loses going-concern value. Match the chapter to the situation.

### Misunderstanding The Automatic Stay

The stay is broad but creditors obtain relief, especially for underprotected collateral. Do not assume it stops everything permanently.

### Confusing Debtor-In-Possession With Shareholder Control

The debtor-in-possession owes duties to the estate and creditors. Shareholders are last in priority.

### Ignoring Priority And Security

Priority claims and secured collateral can consume the estate, leaving general unsecured creditors with little. Model the waterfall.

### Mishandling Executory Contracts

Failing to assume valuable contracts or reject burdensome ones leaves value on the table or bloats the estate.

### Underestimating Plan Confirmation Requirements

Best interests, feasibility, acceptance, and absolute priority are strict. A plan that ignores them will be denied.

### Assuming Free-And-Clear Sales Avoid All Liability

Some liabilities follow the assets. Address successor liability in the sale order.

### Overlooking Out-Of-Court Alternatives

Workouts and assignments may be faster and cheaper. Compare before filing.

## Self-Check

- Are Chapter 7, Chapter 11, Chapter 13, Chapter 12, Subchapter V, and out-of-court alternatives compared for the debtor type, debt levels, business continuity, control, and complexity?
- Is the automatic stay mapped for scope, exceptions, relief from stay, adequate protection, time limits, and non-debtor parties?
- Is the estate identified and control determined, including trustee versus debtor-in-possession, fiduciary duties, US trustee, creditors committee, and professional retention?
- Are claims and interests classified and prioritized, including secured, priority unsecured, general unsecured, and equity, with undersecured treatment?
- Are executory contracts and leases addressed for assumption, rejection, assignment, cure, ip and anti-assignment clauses, and rejection damages?
- Is the plan of reorganization structured with classification, treatment, means, best interests, feasibility, acceptance, cramdown, and absolute priority?
- are asset sales structured for fair value, notice, auction, liens on proceeds, successor liability, ordinary course, and stalking horse?
- Are out-of-court workouts, assignments, and receiverships compared for speed, cost, binding effect, stay, tax, and guarantor impact?
- Are preference and fraudulent transfer clawback risks to recent counterparties considered?
- Does the output avoid asserting recovery estimates or plan confirmability and instead recommend qualified bankruptcy counsel for the specific case and jurisdiction?
