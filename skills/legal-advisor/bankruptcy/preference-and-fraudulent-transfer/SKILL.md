# Preference And Fraudulent Transfer

Avoidance law lets a bankruptcy trustee or debtor-in-possession claw back transfers made before bankruptcy, on the theory that the debtor gave away value it should have preserved for creditors. Preference law targets payments to creditors in the run-up to bankruptcy that favored them over others. Fraudulent transfer law targets transfers made to hinder, delay, or defraud creditors, or made for less than reasonably equivalent value while insolvent. Agents often tell clients that a paid invoice is final, when in fact a payment received within the preference period can be clawed back years later, forcing the recipient to return money it already booked as revenue. Conversely, agents analyzing a debtor's pre-bankruptcy conduct often miss transfers to insiders, asset spins to affiliates, or leveraged buyouts that stripped the company of assets, all of which may be avoidable.

Use this skill before advising a creditor that received payments from a now-bankrupt customer, before structuring asset transfers near insolvency, before a leveraged transaction that strips a company, or when defending or pursuing an avoidance claim. The goal is to force the agent to identify the transfer, the lookback period, the avoidance theory, the available defenses, and the realistic exposure. Avoidance litigation is technical and fact-intensive; all matters require qualified bankruptcy counsel.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Transfer And The Applicable Lookback

Avoidance begins with identifying the transfer and the period the trustee can reach back. Different theories have different periods.

Identify:

- the transfer, whether a payment, lien grant, asset conveyance, or guarantee;
- the date of the transfer;
- the relationship between the debtor and the transferee, insider or non-insider;
- the preference lookback, typically 90 days for non-insiders and one year for insiders;
- the fraudulent transfer lookback, which can be longer and may extend under state uniform fraudulent transfer acts;
- the relation-back doctrine and when a transfer is deemed complete.

Insiders face longer lookbacks and heightened scrutiny. A payment to a founder's family member or affiliate within a year of bankruptcy is presumptively suspect.

### Analyze Preference Elements And Defenses

A preference is a transfer to a creditor on an antecedent debt, made while the debtor was insolvent, within the lookback, that allows the creditor to receive more than it would in a Chapter 7 liquidation. Each element must be met, but several defenses can defeat the claim.

Establish the elements:

- a transfer to or for the benefit of a creditor;
- on account of an antecedent debt;
- made while insolvent (insolvency is presumed during the 90-day window);
- made within the lookback period;
- enabling the creditor to receive more than in a Chapter 7.

Evaluate the defenses:

- contemporaneous exchange for new value, where the transfer was intended as a substantially contemporaneous exchange;
- ordinary course of business, where the payment was made in the ordinary course of the parties' dealings or according to ordinary business terms;
- subsequent new value, where the creditor extended new value after the transfer that was not replaced;
- security interest in new value, where the transfer created a perfected security interest;
- net result and aggregate analysis across multiple transfers.

The ordinary course defense turns on the historical billing and payment pattern between the parties. Document the payment history to support or challenge the defense.

### Analyze Fraudulent Transfer Elements For Actual And Constructive Fraud

Fraudulent transfer has two theories: actual fraud, where the debtor intended to hinder, delay, or defraud creditors, and constructive fraud, where the debtor received less than reasonably equivalent value while insolvent or with inadequate capital.

Actual fraud badges:

- transfer to an insider;
- retention of possession or control after the transfer;
- concealment of the transfer;
- transfer of substantially all assets;
- the debtor removing assets or fleeing;
- receipt of no or nominal consideration;
- insolvency or imminent insolvency at the time;
- a pattern of transfers after a large debt was incurred;
- capitalization of the transferee entity.

Constructive fraud elements:

- less than reasonably equivalent value received;
- insolvency at the time, on a balance sheet or cash flow basis;
- unreasonably small capital for the business;
- intent to incur debts beyond the ability to pay.

A leveraged transaction that pays out shareholders while leaving the company insolvent is a classic constructive fraud target. The intent to defraud is not required for constructive fraud.

### Distinguish The Transferee Categories And Their Defenses

Different transferees have different defenses. A good-faith transferee for value without knowledge of the debtor's fraud has stronger protection.

Classify:

- initial transferee, who received the transfer directly;
- immediate or mediate transferee, who received from the initial transferee;
- insider or affiliate transferee;
- good-faith transferee for value, who may have a defense;
- conduit or nominee transferees.

A good-faith transferee who gave value and lacked knowledge of the fraud may keep the transfer to the extent of the value given. The defense is fact-specific and must be developed with evidence.

### Evaluate Insolvency Indicators

Insolvency is central to both preference and constructive fraud. Analyze the debtor's financial condition at the time of the transfer.

Test:

- balance sheet insolvency, where liabilities exceed assets at fair valuation;
- cash flow insolvency, where the debtor cannot pay debts as they mature;
- unreasonably small capital for the business;
- intent to incur debts beyond the ability to pay;
- the use of going-concern versus liquidation values;
- contingent and disputed liabilities.

A debtor that was solvent on a balance sheet basis but cash-flow insolvent may still be treated as insolvent. Document the valuation method and assumptions.

### Assess Clawback Exposure And Settlement Strategy

Avoidance claims are often settled rather than litigated. Assess the realistic exposure and settlement value.

Weigh:

- the strength of each element and defense;
- the amount at stake and the cost of litigation;
- the risk of reciprocal new value offsets;
- the availability of contribution from other transferees;
- the reputational and relationship costs;
- the trustee's litigation budget and willingness to settle.

A creditor with a strong ordinary course defense may settle for a fraction of the transfer, while one with weak defenses faces full repayment.

### Coordinate With Related Transactions And Parties

Avoidance does not occur in isolation. A transfer may be part of a larger pattern.

Consider:

- a series of payments to the same creditor, analyzed in aggregate;
- transfers to multiple insiders around the same time;
- a leveraged buyout or dividend recapitalization that stripped the company;
- asset spins to affiliates before bankruptcy;
- guarantees granted for the benefit of affiliates;
- the effect of releases and bar orders in related settlements.

### Document The Ordinary Course And New Value Defenses Proactively

Creditors can reduce preference exposure by maintaining clean records during the relationship, before any bankruptcy looms.

Maintain:

- consistent billing and payment terms;
- records of any deviations and their business reasons;
- new value extended after each payment, such as continued shipments on credit;
- communications showing ordinary course dealing;
- security agreements and perfection records.

## Common Traps

### Treating A Paid Invoice As Final

A payment received in the preference period can be clawed back. Book it as at-risk until the period closes.

### Missing The Insider Lookback

Insiders face a one-year preference lookback and heightened scrutiny. Do not assume the 90-day rule applies.

### Ignoring Constructive Fraud

Actual intent is not required for constructive fraud. A transfer for less than value while insolvent is avoidable regardless of intent.

### Failing To Document Ordinary Course

The ordinary course defense turns on payment history. Without records, the defense fails.

### Overlooking Leveraged Transactions

LBOs and dividend recaps that leave the company insolvent are classic fraudulent transfer targets.

### Assuming Good Faith Always Protects

The good-faith defense requires value given and lack of knowledge. It is fact-specific, not automatic.

### Forgetting New Value Offsets

New value extended after a preference payment reduces clawback exposure. Track it.

### Misjudging Insolvency

Balance sheet solvency does not defeat cash-flow or equitable insolvency tests. Analyze all tests.

## Self-Check

- Is the transfer identified with date, parties, insider status, and the applicable preference and fraudulent transfer lookback periods?
- Are preference elements established and defenses evaluated, including contemporaneous exchange, ordinary course, subsequent new value, security interest, and aggregate net result?
- Are actual and constructive fraudulent transfer elements analyzed, with badges of fraud, reasonably equivalent value, insolvency tests, and unreasonably small capital?
- Are transferees classified as initial, mediate, insider, conduit, or good-faith-for-value, with applicable defenses?
- Is insolvency assessed on balance sheet, cash flow, and equitable tests with valuation method and assumptions?
- Is clawback exposure weighed against settlement value, considering element strength, defense strength, cost, offsets, contribution, and reputational impact?
- Are related transactions and patterns considered, including series of payments, insider transfers, LBOs, dividend recaps, affiliate spins, and guarantees?
- Are ordinary course and new value defenses documented proactively through consistent billing, payment records, deviations, new value tracking, and communications?
- Are insider lookback and constructive fraud risks flagged for affiliated and leveraged transactions?
- Does the output avoid asserting that a transfer is or is not avoidable and instead recommend qualified bankruptcy counsel for the specific facts and jurisdiction?
