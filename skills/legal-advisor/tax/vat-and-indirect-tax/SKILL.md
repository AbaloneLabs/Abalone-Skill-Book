# VAT And Indirect Tax

Value-added tax (VAT), goods and services tax (GST), sales tax, and digital services taxes are transaction taxes that look simple but turn on place of supply, status of supplier and customer, nature of the supply, and registration thresholds. A single sale can be taxed, zero-rated, exempt, or outside the scope depending on tiny factual differences. Agents often assume "B2B is reverse charge" or "exports are zero-rated" without checking the destination rule, the customer's VAT status, the digital services threshold, or whether an exemption removes the right to recover input tax. Indirect tax mistakes are especially costly because they recur on every transaction and are often collected from customers, creating customer disputes and gross-up exposure.

Use this skill before advising on cross-border sales, digital services, e-commerce, marketplace liability, invoicing systems, place-of-supply questions, refund claims, or indirect tax registration. The goal is to force the agent to map the supply, the parties, the place, and the recovery chain before concluding the tax treatment. Indirect tax rules are highly jurisdiction-specific and change frequently; any rate, threshold, or rule must be confirmed for the relevant country and period.

## Core Rules

### Determine Whether There Is A Taxable Supply

Not every payment is a supply of goods or services. Classify the transaction first.

Identify:

- supply of goods (transfer of right to dispose, deemed supply on self-supply or free of charge);
- supply of services (everything else, including digital, licensing, construction, financial);
- deemed supply (self-supply, transfer of business assets, private use);
- exempt supplies (financial, insurance, medical, education, real estate in some systems);
- supplies outside the scope (statutory compensation, certain government transfers);
- transactions that are not supplies at all (pure equity contributions, dividends in some systems).

Exempt is not the same as zero-rated. Exemption usually blocks input tax recovery; zero-rating usually preserves it.

### Establish Place Of Supply

Place of supply determines which country's VAT applies. Getting this wrong produces double taxation or unintended non-taxation.

For goods:

- cross-border: import and export rules, incoterms, customs value, place of dispatch or arrival;
- domestic: where goods are located when dispatched or made available.

For services, the default and overrides differ by jurisdiction and supply type:

- B2B general rule: where the customer is established (often with reverse charge);
- B2C general rule: where the supplier is established;
- real estate-related services: where the property is located;
- passenger transport: where transport takes place;
- admission to events: where the event occurs;
- restaurant and catering: where supplied;
- short-term hiring of transport: where the vehicle is put at disposal.

Digital services to consumers have destination-based rules with registration or one-stop-shop (OSS/ MOSS) options and thresholds. Confirm the customer location using two non-contradictory pieces of evidence in many systems.

### Check Registration And Collection Duties

Registration thresholds, distance-selling thresholds, and digital services thresholds create collection duties even without a local presence.

Map:

- domestic registration threshold and whether it applies to taxable person status;
- cross-border distance selling thresholds for goods (OSS schemes);
- digital services thresholds for B2C electronic services;
- marketplace and platform deemed-supplier rules (marketplace collects on behalf of sellers);
- reverse charge shifting the obligation to the customer;
- voluntary registration for input tax recovery;
- registration triggers from holding stock, warehousing, installation, or personnel.

Failing to register when over a threshold creates back-tax and penalty exposure, often gross of the tax that should have been collected.

### Apply Reverse Charge And Self-Assessment Correctly

Reverse charge shifts accounting from supplier to customer. It is common in cross-border B2B services, digital services, certain domestic high-fraud sectors, and supplies by non-established suppliers.

Confirm:

- whether reverse charge applies to this supply in this jurisdiction;
- whether the customer is registered and able to self-assess;
- the rate applied on the reverse charge;
- whether input recovery on the self-assessed tax is immediate or restricted;
- cash-flow and gross-up effects in contracts.

A wrong reverse charge assumption can leave the supply untaxed or double-taxed, and can trigger customer disputes over who bears the shortfall.

### Resolve Exemption, Zero-Rating, And Input Recovery

The right to recover input tax depends on the nature of outputs.

Check:

- zero-rated outputs (often exports, international transport) preserve full input recovery;
- exempt outputs block input recovery directly attributable to them, and require partial exemption apportionment for mixed suppliers;
- blocked input tax (entertainment, certain cars, business gifts) regardless of output;
- input recovery on cross-border services through reverse charge;
- capital goods scheme adjustments over the adjustment period;
- changes in use that trigger input adjustment.

Partial exemption calculations are a frequent audit area. Document the method and the apportionment basis.

### Handle Invoicing And Evidence Requirements

A valid VAT invoice and supporting evidence are conditions for recovery and zero-rating in many systems.

Verify:

- mandatory invoice content (supplier and customer identity, VAT numbers, description, rate, amount, date, sequential number);
- e-invoicing and real-time reporting regimes (SAF-T, e-invoices, live reporting);
- evidence of export or intra-community supply (customs, transport, customer VAT number, recapitulative statement);
- place-of-supply evidence for digital services (two non-contradictory pieces);
- credit notes and corrections;
- retention periods.

Invoices that do not meet content rules can invalidate input recovery even where the underlying supply is real.

### Manage Refunds, Corrections, And Cross-Border Recovery

Refund mechanisms differ for established and non-established businesses.

Identify:

- domestic refund or credit mechanisms and time limits;
- cross-border refund under the 13th Directive or equivalent (non-established businesses);
- one-stop-shop refund for EU and treaty-country businesses;
- corrections through credit notes and amended returns;
- statute of limitations for refund claims;
- offset against other tax debts.

Refund claims often fail on evidence, deadlines, or status of the claimant. Confirm eligibility before filing.

### Monitor Digital, Platform, And Indirect Tax Reform

Indirect tax is changing fast, especially for digital and cross-border commerce.

Track:

- digital services tax and its interaction with VAT;
- platform and marketplace deemed-supplier rules;
- real-time reporting and e-invoicing rollout;
- carbon and environmental levies layered on transactions;
- customs valuation and origin interaction with VAT on imports;
- OECD Pillar One Amount B and indirect tax simplification.

Treat any rate, threshold, or rule as version-controlled and dated.

## Common Traps

### Confusing Exempt With Zero-Rated

Exempt supplies usually block input recovery; zero-rated supplies usually allow it. Treating them as the same distorts pricing and recovery.

### Assuming B2B Means Reverse Charge Everywhere

Reverse charge rules differ by jurisdiction, supply type, and customer status. Confirm before relying on it.

### Ignoring Place Of Supply For Digital Services

Digital services to consumers follow destination rules with evidence requirements. Assuming supplier-location taxation creates under-collection.

### Forgetting Marketplace Deemed-Supplier Rules

Platforms can be liable to collect on behalf of sellers above thresholds, even where the seller itself is below the threshold.

### Treating VAT As The Customer's Problem

Contract gross-up clauses do not cure non-registration penalties. The supplier can still face back-tax, interest, and fines.

### Missing Real-Time Reporting And E-Invoicing

Many jurisdictions now require near-real-time reporting. Legacy invoicing systems can create compliance gaps.

### Overlooking Partial Exemption Apportionment

Mixed suppliers must apportion input tax. Using full recovery on exempt-attributable inputs is a common audit finding.

### Stale Rates And Thresholds

Indirect tax rates, thresholds, and schemes change often. Confirm the rule for the relevant period, not the last known version.

## Self-Check

- Is the transaction classified as a supply of goods, services, deemed supply, exempt, zero-rated, or outside scope?
- Is place of supply determined under the correct general rule and overrides for this jurisdiction and supply type?
- Are registration, distance-selling, digital services, and marketplace thresholds checked with collection duties identified?
- Is reverse charge application confirmed, including customer status, rate, and input recovery?
- Are exemption, zero-rating, and partial exemption input recovery rules applied with apportionment documented?
- Are invoice content, e-invoicing, real-time reporting, and export evidence requirements satisfied?
- Are refund mechanisms, deadlines, and claimant eligibility confirmed for cross-border recovery?
- Are digital services tax, platform rules, and recent reforms tracked with version and date?
- Are contract gross-up clauses checked against actual registration penalties, not just customer reimbursement?
- Does the output flag jurisdiction-specific uncertainty and recommend confirmation by a qualified indirect tax advisor?
