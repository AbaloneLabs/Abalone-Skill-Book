# Strikes And Unfair Labor Practices

Labor disputes escalate quickly and the legality of each side's conduct depends on technical distinctions: economic strikes versus unfair labor practice strikes, primary versus secondary picketing, lawful lockouts, and the scope of protected concerted activity. Agents often apply general litigation instincts and miss the specialized doctrines, tight timelines, and agency-driven remedies that govern this field. A wrong call on strike status or picketing scope can expose the employer to reinstatement duties, back pay, or injunctions.

Use this skill before responding to a strike threat or actual strike, considering a lockout, assessing picketing or secondary pressure, defending or filing an unfair labor practice charge, or seeking interim relief. The goal is to force the agent to classify the dispute, assess conduct legality, identify remedies and timelines, and escalate to labor counsel. This skill is decision support, not legal advice.

## Core Rules

### Classify The Strike And Its Legal Consequences

Strike type determines the rights of strikers and the employer's responses.

Distinguish:

- economic strikes (over wages, hours, and terms) — strikers retain certain reinstatement rights but may be replaced;
- unfair labor practice strikes (protesting employer unlawful conduct) — strikers have stronger reinstatement rights;
- sit-down strikes or occupations (often unprotected and may permit discipline);
- sympathy strikes (legality varies by jurisdiction and agreement);
- political strikes (treatment varies widely);
- partial or intermittent strikes (often unprotected).

Misclassifying an unfair labor practice strike as an economic strike can lead to wrongful discipline and back-pay liability. Analyze the strike's object and the employer's recent conduct.

### Assess The Lawfulness Of Employer Responses

Employers have rights during disputes, but they are bounded.

Evaluate:

- lockouts (defensive versus offensive, and their lawfulness);
- permanent replacement of economic strikers and its limits;
- hiring of temporary or permanent replacement workers;
- discontinuation of benefits during a strike and reinstatement on return;
- discipline or discharge for unprotected conduct;
- injunctions against unlawful picketing or violence;
- continuation of operations and protection of non-striking employees;
- unfair labor practice exposure from each response.

Offensive lockouts, or permanent replacement of unfair labor practice strikers, are common violations. Confirm the strike type before acting.

### Distinguish Primary From Secondary Activity

Secondary pressure is heavily restricted in some regimes, particularly the U.S.

Address:

- primary picketing at the employer's premises;
- common-situs picketing and reserved-gate requirements;
- prohibitions on secondary boycotts and pressure on neutral employers;
- product picketing and its limits;
- publicity proviso and truthful appeals to the public;
- recognitional, organizational, and area standards picketing limits.

Picketing that pressures neutral customers or suppliers can be an unfair labor practice even when the underlying dispute is legitimate. Structure picketing to stay primary.

### Identify Unfair Labor Practices By Category

Both sides can commit unfair labor practices, and the categories are specific.

Common employer violations:

- interference, restraint, or coercion of employee rights;
- domination of or contribution to a labor organization;
- discrimination in hiring or tenure to encourage or discourage union membership;
- discharge or discrimination for filing charges or testifying;
- refusal to bargain collectively in good faith.

Common union violations (where recognized):

- restraint or coercion of employees in the exercise of rights;
- causing or attempting to cause employer discrimination;
- refusal to bargain in good faith;
- certain secondary activities and featherbedding.

Map the conduct to the specific statutory category before filing or defending a charge.

### Meet Timelines For Charges And Remedies

Labor-relations proceedings have short, jurisdiction-specific limitations periods and procedural steps.

Track:

- charge-filing limitations periods;
- investigation and complaint issuance by the agency;
- hearing and decision timelines;
- remedies (cease-and-desist, reinstatement, back pay, bargaining orders, notice posting);
- interim or preliminary injunctions and their standards;
- judicial enforcement and review;
- settlement opportunities and their effect.

Missing a limitations period or procedural step can forfeit rights. Calendar deadlines immediately upon notice.

### Preserve Status Quo And Bargaining Obligations During Disputes

Disputes do not suspend all obligations.

Address:

- continuing duty to bargain (subject to impasse rules);
- maintenance of the status quo for mandatory subjects;
- no unilateral changes during bargaining or a lawful strike;
- grievance and arbitration under an existing agreement;
- effects bargaining for decisions affecting unit employees.

Unilateral changes made under the pressure of a dispute are frequent violations. Maintain the status quo unless impasse is clearly reached and documented.

### Manage Violence, Property, And Public Safety

Labor disputes can involve conduct that crosses into criminal or tort liability.

Address:

- lawful picketing versus obstruction, trespass, or violence;
- injunction standards for unlawful mass picketing or violence;
- protection of non-striking employees and their access;
- property and security measures;
- coordination with law enforcement within lawful limits;
- avoidance of provocation that escalates or creates liability.

Injunctions for lawful picketing are often unavailable, while injunctions for violence or obstruction may be granted. Do not conflate the two, and avoid overreach that itself becomes a violation.

### Coordinate With Arbitration And Contract Remedies

Many disputes under an existing agreement are governed by grievance and arbitration.

Address:

- grievance steps and timelines under the agreement;
- arbitrability of the dispute;
- arbitration standards and deference;
- enforcement and vacatur of awards;
- interaction with unfair labor practice charges;
- no-strike and no-lockout clauses and their scope.

Bypassing the agreed grievance-arbitration process, or striking in violation of a no-strike clause, can itself be unlawful. Follow the contract machinery.

## Common Traps

### Misclassifying Strike Type

Treating an unfair labor practice strike as economic can lead to wrongful replacement and back pay. Analyze the object and recent employer conduct.

### Offensive Lockouts Or Unlawful Replacements

Lockouts and replacements are bounded. Confirm lawfulness before acting.

### Secondary Pressure On Neutrals

Picketeting that pressures customers or suppliers can be a violation even with a legitimate dispute. Stay primary.

### Unilateral Changes During Disputes

Status-quo violations under dispute pressure are frequent and avoidable.

### Missing Limitations Periods

Labor-relations timelines are short. Calendar immediately.

### Overbroad Injunctions Against Lawful Picketing

Courts often will not enjoin lawful picketing, and seeking one can backfire. Target only unlawful conduct.

### Bypassing Grievance-Arbitration

Striking or acting outside the contract machinery can itself be unlawful under a no-strike clause.

## Self-Check

- Is the strike classified (economic, unfair labor practice, sit-down, sympathy, political, intermittent) with the consequences for reinstatement and replacement analyzed?
- Are employer responses (lockout, replacement, benefit discontinuation, discipline, injunction, operations) assessed for lawfulness based on strike type?
- Is picketing structured to stay primary, with common-situs, secondary-boycott, product-picketing, and publicity limits respected?
- Is the conduct mapped to specific unfair labor practice categories for both employer and union?
- Are charge-filing limitations, investigation, complaint, hearing, remedies, injunctions, and judicial review timelines calendared?
- Is the status quo maintained for mandatory subjects, with no unilateral changes during bargaining or strike?
- Are violence, obstruction, trespass, and public-safety responses targeted at unlawful conduct only, avoiding overbroad injunctions?
- Is the grievance-arbitration machinery under an existing agreement followed, with no-strike and no-lockout clauses respected?
- Does the output escalate strikes, lockouts, picketing disputes, unfair labor practice charges, and injunctions to qualified labor counsel?
