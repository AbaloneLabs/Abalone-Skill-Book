# Union And Collective Bargaining

Labor relations is a field where good intentions and ordinary management reflexes can become unfair labor practices. Employer conduct during organizing, the scope of bargaining units, the duty to bargain in good faith, and the limits on employer speech are heavily regulated and enforced by dedicated agencies. Agents often apply general employment-law instincts and miss the specific prohibitions on interference, domination, discrimination, and refusal to bargain that define this area.

Use this skill before responding to organizing activity, defining or contesting a bargaining unit, entering or defending a representation process, beginning or refusing negotiations, changing terms affecting represented employees, or preparing a labor-relations risk assessment. The goal is to force the agent to connect organizing status, bargaining-unit scope, good-faith duties, permissible versus prohibited employer conduct, and contract negotiation strategy, and to escalate to labor counsel for high-stakes matters. This skill is decision support, not legal advice.

## Core Rules

### Recognize Organizing Status And Its Triggers

The employer's obligations shift the moment organizing or representation becomes a live issue.

Identify:

- whether organizing activity is underway or reasonably anticipated;
- whether a petition for representation has been or may be filed;
- the appropriate labor-relations statute and agency (e.g., NLRA and NLRB in the U.S., or the relevant national regime);
- whether a union is already certified or recognized;
- whether an existing collective agreement is in force;
- card-check, election, or recognition procedures applicable in the jurisdiction.

Do not assume obligations begin only after certification. Some duties (non-interference, no retaliation) attach during organizing, and some jurisdictions impose duties even earlier.

### Respect The Limits On Employer Speech And Conduct

Employers may communicate views, but coercion, threats, interrogation, surveillance, and promises cross the line.

Permissible versus prohibited conduct:

- stating opinions on unionization (generally permitted if non-coercive);
- predicting objective consequences of unionization (permitted) versus threatening retaliation (prohibited);
- interrogating employees about union views or activity (prohibited);
- surveilling or creating the appearance of surveilling organizing (prohibited);
- granting or promising benefits to discourage union support (prohibited);
- disciplining or discharging for protected concerted activity (prohibited);
- holding captive-audience meetings within regulated limits;
- soliciting grievances or making unilateral changes to undermine support (prohibited).

The line between prediction and threat is fact-specific and jurisdiction-dependent. Err on the side of caution and route communications through labor counsel.

### Define And Contest Bargaining-Unit Scope Deliberately

The bargaining unit determines who is represented and who can be included or excluded.

Address:

- community of interest (skills, duties, supervision, interchange, location);
- inclusion or exclusion of supervisors, managers, guards, and confidential employees;
- professional versus non-professional units;
- fragmentation and appropriateness of the unit;
- accretion of new roles into an existing unit;
- jurisdiction-specific rules (e.g., micro-units or broader units).

Unit composition affects bargaining leverage and outcomes. Do not concede scope without analysis, and do not manipulate job titles to exclude employees.

### Fulfill The Duty To Bargain In Good Faith

Good-faith bargaining is a continuing obligation with both procedural and substantive dimensions.

Ensure:

- meeting at reasonable times and places;
- providing relevant information needed for bargaining;
- sending representatives with authority;
- avoiding surface bargaining or delay tactics;
- considering proposals and offering counterproposals;
- signing a written agreement reached;
- not insisting on a permissive subject to the point of impasse improperly;
- not implementing unilateral changes to mandatory subjects without bargaining.

Good faith does not require reaching agreement, but it does require genuine engagement. Patterned refusal, surface bargaining, or unilateral implementation of mandatory subjects are classic violations.

### Distinguish Mandatory, Permissive, And Illegal Subjects

Not everything is bargainable, and some subjects are off-limits.

Classify:

- mandatory subjects (wages, hours, and other terms and conditions of employment) — both sides must bargain;
- permissive or voluntary subjects (e.g., retiree benefits in some regimes, decisions about capital investment) — bargaining is optional;
- illegal subjects (proposals that would violate law) — cannot be bargained.

Misclassifying a subject leads to bad-faith findings or unlawful concessions. Analyze each proposal's category before the table.

### Manage Mid-Contract Changes And Management Rights

Even with a collective agreement in place, certain changes require bargaining.

Address:

- management-rights clauses and their limits;
- zipper clauses and their effect on mid-term bargaining;
- status-quo obligations for changes not clearly reserved to management;
- effects bargaining for decisions that affect unit employees;
- subcontracting, relocation, and automation decisions and their bargaining triggers;
- discipline and grievance procedures under the agreement.

Unilateral changes to mandatory subjects during the contract term, even if management believes it has the right, can be violations unless clearly reserved.

### Handle Information Requests And Disclosure Duties

Bargaining and grievance administration require information, and refusing to provide it can be a violation.

Address:

- relevance of requested information;
- formats and timelines for provision;
- confidentiality and privacy of individual employee data;
- cost allocation for compiling information;
- continuing duty to update.

Stonewalling information requests is a frequent and avoidable violation. Provide relevant, non-privileged information promptly.

### Coordinate With Works Councils And Co-Determination Regimes

Outside the U.S., works councils and co-determination add layers of consultation and consent.

Address:

- works-council establishment thresholds;
- information, consultation, and co-determination rights;
- timing relative to decisions;
- election and composition rules;
- interaction with unions and collective agreements;
- remedies for failure to consult.

Skipping works-council consultation can void decisions and trigger penalties. Build consultation into the timeline before acting.

## Common Traps

### Treating Organizing As A General HR Matter

Labor-relations statutes impose specific prohibitions during organizing. Apply the specialized framework, not general employment instinct.

### Predictions That Function As Threats

The line is fact-specific. Route communications through labor counsel and avoid implying retaliation.

### Unilateral Changes To Mandatory Subjects

Implementing wage, hour, or policy changes affecting represented employees without bargaining is a classic violation.

### Surface Bargaining And Delay

Going through the motions without genuine engagement is bad faith. Engage substantively.

### Stonewalling Information Requests

Refusing relevant information is an avoidable violation. Provide it promptly.

### Manipulating Job Titles To Affect Unit Scope

Reclassification to exclude employees invites challenges and undermines credibility.

### Skipping Works-Council Consultation

Outside the U.S., consultation timing can be outcome-determinative. Build it into the timeline.

## Self-Check

- Is the organizing or representation status identified, with the applicable labor-relations statute, agency, and trigger points for obligations recognized?
- Are employer speech and conduct kept within permissible bounds, with threats, interrogation, surveillance, promises, and retaliation avoided?
- Is bargaining-unit scope analyzed for community of interest, exclusions, and appropriateness before conceding or contesting?
- Is the duty to bargain in good faith fulfilled through reasonable meetings, information provision, authorized representatives, substantive engagement, and written agreements?
- Are subjects classified as mandatory, permissive, or illegal before proposals are made or refused?
- Are mid-contract changes, management rights, effects bargaining, subcontracting, relocation, and automation handled with status-quo and consultation duties respected?
- Are information requests met with relevant, timely, privacy-protective disclosure?
- Are works councils and co-determination regimes coordinated with correct timing and remedies?
- Does the output escalate organizing responses, unit disputes, bargaining strategy, and unfair-labor-practice exposure to qualified labor counsel?
