# Conflicts And Disqualification

A conflict of interest exists when a lawyer's or advisor's duty to one client, former client, or personal interest materially limits, or appears to limit, the ability to represent another. Conflicts are not a courtesy check; they can disqualify counsel, void agreements, expose lawyers to discipline and malpractice, require restitution of fees, and taint the outcome of a matter. Agents often treat conflict screening as a box to tick, but the real judgment problem is that the most dangerous conflicts are not obvious. They hide in common ownership, joint representations, former firm lawyers, adverse affiliates, confidential information from a prior matter, and the structural pressure of a firm's "walled" practice groups. A conflict that is discovered late can derail a deal, force a party to find new counsel mid-litigation, and hand the other side a strategic weapon.

Use this skill before a firm or advisor accepts a new client, before adding a party to an existing matter, before a lateral lawyer joins, before a joint representation is proposed, before an internal investigation crosses director and company interests, and before any matter where the same advisor might touch both sides. The goal is to make the agent map all current and former client relationships, identify where duties collide, and decide whether consent, screening, declination, or escalation is required. This skill supports conflict identification and process; actual conflict waivers and disqualification decisions require a licensed attorney applying the rules of the relevant jurisdiction.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Current Client, Former Client, And Personal Conflicts

Conflict analysis changes depending on the relationship.

- Current client conflicts arise when representing one client against another current client, or when representation of one current client is directly adverse to another. Direct adversity is usually a bright-line prohibition absent consent.
- Former client conflicts arise under the substantial relationship test: a lawyer may not represent a new client in the same or a substantially related matter in which the former client's interests are materially adverse, unless the former client gives informed consent. Confidential information learned in the prior matter is the core concern.
- Personal conflicts arise when the lawyer's own financial, business, or personal interests could limit representation, such as a proprietary interest in the subject matter.

A strong screen asks all three questions for every new matter. A weak screen only checks whether the new party is already a client.

### Apply The Substantial Relationship Test Carefully

For former clients, the question is whether the current matter is the same or substantially related to the prior representation. Substantial relationship can be found where the matters involve the same transaction, the same legal issue, or where confidential information from the prior matter could materially advance the new representation. Courts differ on how strictly to infer a relationship and how to treat access to confidential information. Do not assume that different legal theories or different parties mean no relationship; the underlying facts and access to secrets can be enough.

### Map The Entire Firm, Not Just The Lead Lawyer

Conflicts are imputed. Under the general rule, a conflict of one lawyer in a firm is imputed to all lawyers in the firm, because of the presumption that lawyers within a firm share confidences. This means a lateral hire can import a conflict that disqualifies the entire receiving firm, and a single lawyer's prior government service can bar the firm from matters. Identify all lawyers who will work on the matter, all lawyers who joined recently, and any government or judicial employment in their backgrounds.

### Evaluate Screening And Ethical Walls

Imputed conflicts can sometimes be cured by screening the conflicted lawyer off the matter, with timely notice, no participation, no access to files, and periodic certifications. The acceptability of screening varies sharply by jurisdiction and by whether the conflict involves a former government lawyer, a lateral move, or a current client. Some jurisdictions do not permit screening to cure current-client conflicts at all. A screen that is not documented, enforced, and noticed is not a defense.

### Assess Confidentiality Beyond The Conflict Rules

Even where a formal conflict does not exist, the duty of confidentiality continues. A lawyer may not use a current or former client's confidential information to that client's disadvantage, regardless of whether the matters are related. Confidential information learned in a pro bono matter, a consultation that did not result in engagement, or a casual inquiry can all be protected. Treat "we never formally represented them" as a warning, not an answer.

### Handle Joint And Common Representation Explicitly

Joint representation of multiple parties with aligned interests is common in deals, family matters, and entity formations, but it carries built-in risk. If interests later diverge, the lawyer may have to withdraw from all parties. Confidentiality between jointly represented clients is reduced, and a conflict with one can become a conflict with all. A strong engagement documents the scope of the joint representation, the information that will be shared, and the consequences of divergence. A weak engagement lets the joint relationship develop by implication.

### Identify Organizational Client And Director Conflicts

When the client is an organization, the lawyer represents the entity, not its individual directors, officers, or employees. An internal investigation, a derivative claim, a transaction, or a regulatory inquiry can create a situation where the company's interests diverge from those of its managers. The lawyer must clarify who is represented, advise individuals when their interests may diverge so they can seek separate counsel, and avoid taking direction from someone whose interests conflict with the entity.

### Check Government And Judicial Switching Rules

Lawyers leaving government face temporary or permanent restrictions on matters they worked on personally and substantially. The receiving firm may also be affected, sometimes curable by screening, sometimes not. Judges and judicial staff face restrictions on appearing before the courts where they served. These rules are strict and jurisdiction-specific and must be checked before a lateral is staffed on any matter.

### Document Consent And Waivers Properly

Where a conflict is consentable, the consent must be informed, usually in writing, and must address the risks of the representation, the alternatives, and the implications. Some conflicts are non-consentable, such as direct adversity between current clients in many jurisdictions. A generic waiver buried in an engagement letter is weak; a meaningful waiver explains the conflict in terms the client can evaluate.

## Common Traps

### Screening Only By Party Name

Affiliates, parent and subsidiary entities, predecessors, and individuals under common control can create conflicts that a name-only search misses. Search by related entities, principals, and matter facts.

### Assuming "Substantially Related" Means Identical

Courts have found substantial relationships between matters that look different on the surface but share facts, witnesses, documents, or legal issues. Overconfidence here is a common source of disqualification motions.

### Treating A Consultation As A Non-Engagement

A prospective client who discloses confidential information during a consultation may be treated as a former client for conflict purposes, even if no engagement resulted. Intake must capture and screen these contacts.

### Letting A Lateral Start Work Before Screening

The most expensive conflicts are often imported by laterals. No lateral should touch a matter until prior representations and government service are fully screened.

### Relying On An Undocumented Ethical Wall

A wall that exists only in conversation, without notice, access controls, and certifications, will not protect the firm if challenged. Documentation is the defense.

### Joint Representation Without Explaining Divergence Risk

Clients who agreed to joint representation without understanding that a future conflict forces withdrawal often feel betrayed and file complaints when it happens. Set expectations at the start.

### Ignoring The Organizational Client Boundary

Representing "the company" while secretly taking direction from a founder whose interests diverge from the entity is a classic ethical failure. Clarify the client in writing.

## Self-Check

- Have current client, former client, and personal conflicts each been evaluated for the new matter?
- Has the substantial relationship test been applied to any former client connection, considering shared facts and confidential information?
- Has the entire firm, including recent laterals and former government lawyers, been screened, recognizing imputation?
- If screening is proposed as a cure, is it documented, noticed, access-controlled, and jurisdictionally permitted for this conflict type?
- Has confidentiality been considered independently of the formal conflict rules, including consultations and non-engagements?
- For joint or common representation, is the scope, shared information, and divergence risk documented in the engagement?
- For organizational clients, is it clear who is represented, and have individuals whose interests may diverge been advised to consider separate counsel?
- Are government and judicial switching rules checked for any lawyer with prior public service?
- For any consentable conflict, is the waiver informed, written, and specific to the risks, or is it a generic clause?
- Does the output escalate non-consentable conflicts and final waiver decisions to qualified counsel rather than resolving them informally?
