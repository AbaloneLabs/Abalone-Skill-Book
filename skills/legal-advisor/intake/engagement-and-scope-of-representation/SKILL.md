# Engagement And Scope Of Representation

The engagement letter is the legal and ethical foundation of the representation. It defines who the client is, what the lawyer will and will not do, how and when the lawyer will be paid, what conflicts exist, and how the relationship ends. Agents often treat engagement as an administrative step and focus on the fee, while the scope, authority, communication, and termination terms are where disputes, malpractice claims, and fee collections actually arise. A scope that is too broad invites unpaid work and blurred authority; a scope that is too narrow can leave the client exposed and the lawyer liable for tasks the client assumed were covered. Limited-scope and unbundled representations add a sharper layer of risk because the boundary between covered and uncovered work must be explicit and constantly enforced.

Use this skill before opening a new matter, before signing or sending an engagement letter, before converting a prospect into a client, before expanding an existing engagement to new matters, or before withdrawing from a representation. The goal is to force the agent to define the client entity precisely, set a clear and defensible scope, address conflicts and authority, build communication and termination mechanics, and resist scope creep. Engagement terms are regulated by jurisdiction-specific ethics rules; engagement letters should be reviewed by qualified counsel familiar with the relevant bar rules.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Client Entity Precisely

The most common source of engagement disputes is ambiguity about who the client is. The engagement letter must name the client with specificity.

Confirm:

- the exact legal entity or individual that is the client, including entity type and jurisdiction of formation;
- whether affiliates, parent, subsidiaries, principals, officers, or employees are also clients or are third parties;
- whether the representation is of the entity or of an individual within the entity (the entity representation rules differ from individual representation);
- whether a new matter creates a joint representation that requires conflicts analysis and informed consent;
- whether the client has authority to engage counsel and bind the entity.

Ambiguity here is dangerous. Representing a company while advising its founder creates conflicts when the founder's interests diverge from the entity's. The engagement letter must resolve who is the client and who is not.

### Define The Scope Of Representation Explicitly

The scope determines what the lawyer must do and, equally important, what the lawyer is not required to do. Vague scope is the root of malpractice and fee disputes.

Specify:

- the matter or matters covered (a specific transaction, litigation, investigation, or advice);
- the tasks included (drafting, negotiation, filing, court appearance, opinion);
- the tasks expressly excluded (for example, tax advice, IP prosecution, employment matters, regulatory filings not listed);
- whether the scope is full representation or limited-scope (unbundled) representation;
- any deadlines, courts, jurisdictions, or regulators involved;
- whether the scope includes appeals, ancillary proceedings, or enforcement.

For limited-scope representation, state with particularity what is included (for example, document review only, coaching, or a single court appearance) and what the client must handle alone. The narrower the scope, the more explicit the boundaries must be.

### Address Conflicts And Waivers Before Work Begins

Conflicts analysis must precede or accompany engagement, not follow it.

Confirm:

- a conflicts check has been run against the firm's existing and former clients;
- any actual or potential conflicts are disclosed in writing;
- where a conflict is waivable, informed written consent is obtained from all affected clients;
- the engagement letter addresses whether new conflicts may arise and how they will be handled;
- the firm's advance conflict waiver, if any, is clearly disclosed and enforceable under the jurisdiction's rules.

Starting work before clearing conflicts can disqualify the firm, breach duties to existing clients, and forfeit fees.

### Set Fee, Billing, And Advance Cost Terms

Fee disputes are the most common client complaint. The engagement letter must make the economics clear and comply with ethics rules.

Address:

- the fee structure (hourly, fixed, contingency, hybrid, retainer) and whether it is permitted for the matter type;
- hourly rates, timekeepers, and any rate changes;
- the scope of work the fee covers and what triggers additional charges;
- retainer or advance payment requirements and how they are replenished;
- costs and disbursements (filing fees, experts, travel) and who bears them;
- billing frequency, format, and detail;
- consequences of non-payment, including withdrawal rights;
- for contingency matters, the percentage, the definition of recovery, and how costs are deducted.

Contingency fees are regulated and capped in many jurisdictions for certain matter types (for example, domestic relations or criminal defense). Verify permissibility and limits.

### Establish Communication, Authority, And Decision Rights

The engagement letter should allocate decisions between lawyer and client consistent with ethics rules.

Address:

- decisions reserved to the client (settlement, whether to testify, whether to appeal, goals of the representation);
- decisions delegated to the lawyer (tactics, legal strategy, discovery scope);
- the primary client contact and communication frequency;
- how the client will be kept informed of significant developments;
- the handling of client property, documents, and funds;
- the client's obligations to provide information and cooperate.

Ethics rules generally reserve substantive decisions to the client and tactical decisions to the lawyer, but the boundary should be made explicit to avoid disputes.

### Build Termination And Withdrawal Mechanics

Every engagement should contemplate its end. Withdrawal rules are jurisdiction-specific and can trap a lawyer in a matter.

Address:

- the grounds on which either party may terminate;
- the effect of termination on pending deadlines, filings, and court proceedings;
- the lawyer's right to withdraw for non-payment, loss of confidence, or scope completion;
- court approval requirements where litigation is pending;
- the handling of the client file, work product, and funds on termination;
- final billing and the return of unearned retainer.

Withdrawal from active litigation often requires court approval and cannot prejudice the client. Plan the exit path at engagement.

### Resist Scope Creep Actively

Scope expands through informal requests: a quick question, a related matter, a new jurisdiction. Each expansion changes the economics and the risk.

Manage:

- a process for documenting scope changes in writing;
- whether a new matter requires a new engagement letter or an amendment;
- conflicts checks for any new matter or party;
- fee adjustments for expanded scope;
- clear communication when a request falls outside the existing scope.

Silent scope creep is how lawyers end up doing unpaid work and how clients end up surprised by bills. Document every change.

## Common Traps

### Ambiguous Client Identity

Representing an entity while advising its principals creates conflicts. Name the client precisely and exclude non-clients.

### Vague Or Overbroad Scope

"General corporate representation" invites unpaid work and blurred responsibility. Define the matter and the tasks.

### Skipping Conflicts Checks

Starting work before clearing conflicts can disqualify the firm and forfeit fees. Run and document the check.

### Fee Terms That Violate Ethics Rules

Contingency in prohibited matter types, or fees that a court finds unreasonable, can be voided and sanctioned.

### No Withdrawal Plan

A lawyer stuck in a matter with no clean exit faces ethics and malpractice exposure. Build termination mechanics at engagement.

### Silent Scope Creep

Informal expansions of work without documentation lead to fee disputes and unmet client expectations.

### Confusing Limited-Scope With Full Representation

In unbundled representation, the client must understand exactly what is and is not covered. Ambiguity creates gaps that harm the client and expose the lawyer.

## Self-Check

- Is the client entity named precisely, with affiliates, principals, and officers clearly identified as clients or third parties, and entity representation rules applied?
- Is the scope of representation explicit, including covered matters and tasks, excluded tasks, jurisdictions, deadlines, and whether the engagement is full or limited-scope?
- Have conflicts been checked, actual or potential conflicts disclosed, and informed written consent obtained for any waivable conflict?
- Are fee structure, rates, retainer, costs, billing frequency, non-payment consequences, and contingency permissibility and limits clearly stated and ethics-compliant?
- Are decision rights allocated between client and lawyer, with communication frequency, primary contact, and client obligations defined?
- Are termination grounds, withdrawal rights, court approval needs, file handling, and final billing addressed so the exit path is clear?
- Is scope creep managed through written documentation, conflicts checks, and fee adjustments for new matters?
- Does the engagement letter get reviewed by qualified counsel familiar with the jurisdiction's ethics rules before signature?
