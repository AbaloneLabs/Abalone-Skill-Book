# Legal Issue Spotting And Authority

Legal work starts by asking what legal question is actually being answered, who is affected, which jurisdiction governs, what authority controls, and what facts are still missing. Agents often jump to a rule that sounds plausible while ignoring venue, timing, state law, contract terms, regulated status, procedural posture, or whether the matter requires a licensed attorney. That is dangerous because the same facts can produce different legal outcomes depending on place, date, party status, industry, forum, and procedural deadline.

Use this skill before researching a legal question, summarizing legal obligations, drafting a legal-risk memo, advising an agent what to check next, preparing an intake for counsel, or deciding whether a request is legal information, operational guidance, or legal advice. The goal is to make the agent slow down before turning a generic rule into a case-specific conclusion.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Information, Analysis, And Advice

Do not present a case-specific legal conclusion as if it were neutral information. General legal information describes rules, processes, and common considerations. Legal analysis applies authority to facts. Legal advice recommends what a specific person or entity should do based on the applicable law and the user's situation.

Classify the task:

- general explanation of legal concepts;
- legal issue spotting;
- jurisdiction and authority mapping;
- document review for risks;
- factual summary for counsel;
- procedural checklist;
- draft language for attorney review;
- case-specific recommendation;
- representation or negotiation strategy.

When the output affects rights, obligations, liability, filing deadlines, litigation strategy, immigration status, criminal exposure, employment rights, family status, housing, health, financial position, or regulatory penalties, route the matter to a qualified lawyer or authorized professional. The agent can organize questions and risks but should not pretend to be counsel.

### Identify Parties, Roles, And Interests

Legal duties depend on who the parties are and what role each party plays. A person may be consumer, employee, contractor, officer, director, shareholder, landlord, tenant, patient, creditor, debtor, parent, fiduciary, vendor, platform operator, data controller, or regulated professional.

Capture:

- names or anonymized party labels;
- entity type;
- location;
- relationship between parties;
- who is asking;
- who the client or beneficiary is;
- who may be adverse;
- who has authority to act;
- who has signed or approved documents;
- who controls relevant records;
- who may owe duties to whom.

Do not assume the user is the party with authority. A manager, agent, employee, family member, contractor, or shareholder may not have legal authority to bind the entity or make decisions.

### Determine Jurisdiction And Forum

Jurisdiction is not a background detail. Federal, state, local, tribal, foreign, administrative, arbitration, and contract forum rules can change the answer.

Ask:

- Where did the relevant conduct occur?
- Where do the parties live, work, or operate?
- Where is the property located?
- Where was the contract formed or performed?
- What governing law clause applies?
- What forum selection, arbitration, or venue clause applies?
- Is the matter in court, agency review, mediation, arbitration, or pre-dispute negotiation?
- Is there federal law, state law, local ordinance, or international law involved?
- Are multiple jurisdictions implicated?

If jurisdiction is unknown, do not finalize the rule. Provide a jurisdiction-dependent checklist and identify which facts are needed.

### Use Current Authoritative Sources

Legal rules change. Use current primary or authoritative sources whenever the output depends on law. Secondary summaries are useful for orientation, but they are not a substitute for binding authority.

Prioritize:

- statutes;
- regulations;
- court rules;
- agency rules and guidance;
- official forms and instructions;
- binding contract terms;
- court orders;
- administrative decisions where relevant;
- local rules;
- attorney-reviewed internal policies.

For U.S. federal law, official routes include Congress.gov, the U.S. Code, the Code of Federal Regulations, agency websites, and federal court resources. For state law, use state legislature, state code, state court, attorney general, or regulator websites. Record the source date and effective period when available.

### Separate Facts From Assumptions

Legal conclusions depend on facts. Treat missing or uncertain facts as risk, not as details to fill in casually.

Separate:

- confirmed facts;
- disputed facts;
- user assumptions;
- document text;
- oral statements;
- dates and deadlines;
- missing evidence;
- facts requiring verification;
- facts requiring privileged attorney-client handling.

Do not silently assume facts that determine the outcome. State conditional reasoning: "If the contract is governed by California law and the clause is enforceable, then the review should focus on X; if another state governs, counsel should confirm."

### Check Deadlines And Procedural Posture Early

Deadlines can destroy rights. The first legal risk in many matters is not the merits but the clock.

Identify:

- filing deadlines;
- response deadlines;
- notice periods;
- appeal deadlines;
- statute of limitations;
- contractual cure periods;
- termination notice periods;
- agency charge deadlines;
- preservation duties;
- discovery deadlines;
- hearing or trial dates;
- payment or performance deadlines.

When any deadline may exist, tell the agent to escalate quickly and avoid giving a relaxed timeline. Calendar all dates with source and uncertainty.

### Preserve Privilege, Confidentiality, And Evidence

Legal work often involves sensitive information. The agent should not ask for or reveal unnecessary details. Privilege can be waived and evidence can be spoiled.

Protect:

- attorney-client communications;
- attorney work product;
- settlement communications;
- trade secrets;
- personal data;
- health information;
- payroll and personnel records;
- financial account details;
- criminal allegations;
- immigration status;
- minors' information;
- litigation strategy.

Recommend collecting facts in a way counsel can use, but avoid broad dissemination. If litigation or investigation is reasonably anticipated, preserve relevant records and avoid routine deletion.

### Identify Who Must Review Or Approve

Legal issues often require more than a legal memo. Internal approval, board approval, regulator notice, insurance notice, lender consent, counterparty consent, or court filing may be required.

Check whether the matter requires:

- licensed attorney;
- compliance officer;
- board or manager approval;
- insurer notice;
- regulator filing;
- HR review;
- finance review;
- privacy officer;
- security team;
- union or works council consultation;
- guardian or fiduciary approval;
- court or agency approval.

If the task crosses into regulated practice, unauthorized practice of law risk, fiduciary duty, or representation of another person, escalate.

## Common Traps

### Giving A Rule Without Jurisdiction

Generic legal rules are often wrong when applied to a specific place. State law, local ordinance, administrative rules, and contract terms can change the answer.

### Treating A Blog Summary As Authority

Secondary sources can explain, but primary authority controls. If action depends on the rule, verify the statute, regulation, court rule, agency guidance, or contract text.

### Ignoring Procedural Posture

The right answer before a lawsuit may differ from the answer after service, after default, during discovery, after judgment, or on appeal. Procedure changes options.

### Missing The Client Or Decision-Maker

The person asking may not be the legal client or the person with authority. Avoid advice that assumes authority without confirming it.

### Overlooking Conflicts

One agent cannot safely advise both sides of a dispute. Even in internal business work, interests of company, founders, employees, investors, and customers can diverge.

### Losing Privilege Through Over-Sharing

Forwarding legal analysis broadly, mixing legal advice with business chatter, or putting privileged content in public systems can create waiver risk. Keep sensitive legal material controlled.

### Treating Missing Facts As Harmless

Missing dates, locations, governing law, signatures, notices, or payment history can change the outcome. Flag them explicitly.

## Self-Check

- Is the task classified as legal information, issue spotting, analysis, document review, or case-specific advice?
- Are parties, roles, authority, adverse interests, and decision-makers identified?
- Is jurisdiction, governing law, forum, venue, arbitration, and procedural posture checked?
- Are current primary or authoritative sources identified instead of relying on memory or summaries?
- Are confirmed facts, assumptions, disputed facts, missing facts, and documents separated?
- Are deadlines, statutes of limitation, notice periods, response dates, and preservation duties checked early?
- Is sensitive legal, personal, financial, employment, health, criminal, immigration, or trade-secret information protected?
- Are privilege and work-product risks considered before sharing or storing legal analysis?
- Are required reviewers such as attorney, compliance, board, insurer, regulator, HR, privacy, or security identified?
- Does the output avoid pretending to give licensed legal advice where qualified counsel is needed?
