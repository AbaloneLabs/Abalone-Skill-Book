# Fee Arrangements And Billing Ethics

The fee arrangement is both a commercial deal and an ethical obligation, and the two can conflict in ways that are easy to miss. A fee that looks reasonable at engagement can become unreasonable or unenforceable if the scope balloons, if the contingency exceeds statutory caps, if the retainer is mismanaged in a trust account, or if the billing records cannot support the charges. Agents often focus on the headline rate or percentage and overlook the ethics rules that govern fee reasonableness, the form and timing of fee disclosures, the handling of client funds, the limits on non-refundable retainers, and the procedures for collecting unpaid fees. Fee disputes are among the most common sources of bar complaints and malpractice counterclaims.

Use this skill before proposing or agreeing to a fee arrangement, before drafting fee terms in an engagement letter, before accepting an advance payment or retainer, before billing contingent or hybrid matters, or before pursuing collection of unpaid fees. The goal is to force the agent to select a fee structure suited to the matter and client, comply with disclosure and reasonableness rules, handle client funds correctly, and anticipate the disputes that arise at the end of a representation. Fee ethics are jurisdiction-specific and enforced by bar authorities; fee terms should be reviewed by qualified counsel familiar with the relevant rules.

## Core Rules

### Match The Fee Structure To The Matter And Client

Different fee structures suit different matters, and some are prohibited or restricted for certain case types.

Evaluate:

- hourly billing suits matters with uncertain scope (litigation, complex transactions) but requires disciplined timekeeping;
- fixed or flat fees suit predictable, repeatable work but require careful scope definition;
- contingency fees suit plaintiff-side matters where recovery is uncertain but potentially large, but are restricted or capped in many jurisdictions for certain matter types;
- hybrid fees (reduced hourly plus contingency) share risk but add complexity;
- subscription or retainer arrangements suit ongoing client relationships but must distinguish fees for services from advance payments held in trust;
- capped or collared fees set ceilings and floors but require change-of-scope discipline.

Contingency fees are commonly prohibited or capped in criminal defense and domestic relations matters in many jurisdictions. Verify permissibility before proposing.

### Satisfy Fee Reasonableness Requirements

Ethics rules require that fees be reasonable. Reasonableness is judged by factors that go beyond the hourly rate.

Assess against the governing rule:

- the time and labor required, the novelty and difficulty of the questions, and the skill needed;
- the likelihood that the engagement precludes other employment;
- the fee customarily charged in the locality for similar services;
- the amount involved and the results obtained;
- the time limitations imposed by the client or circumstances;
- the nature and length of the professional relationship;
- the experience, reputation, and ability of the lawyer.

A fee that is reasonable for a sophisticated client may be unreasonable for a vulnerable one. Document the basis for the fee, especially for contingency and flat fees, to defend reasonableness if challenged.

### Comply With Fee Disclosure And Writing Requirements

Many jurisdictions require fee agreements to be in writing, and contingency fees almost universally require a written agreement signed by the client.

Confirm:

- the fee agreement is in writing where required;
- contingency agreements state the percentage, the method of calculating the fee, and whether costs are deducted before or after the fee;
- the client is advised in writing of the right to seek independent advice on the fee in some jurisdictions;
- any changes to the fee are documented in writing;
- the basis or rate of the fee is communicated to the client, preferably in writing, before or within a reasonable time after commencing the representation.

Oral fee agreements are enforceable in some jurisdictions but are a major source of disputes. Reduce every fee term to writing.

### Handle Retainers And Client Funds Correctly

Advance payments and retainers are subject to trust account rules, and mishandling them is a common and serious ethics violation.

Distinguish:

- an advance fee payment for services not yet earned is generally client property and must be held in a trust or escrow account until earned;
- a security retainer remains the client's property until the lawyer earns it through billing;
- an evergreen or replenishing retainer requires periodic top-up;
- a non-refundable retainer may be unenforceable in jurisdictions that treat unearned fees as client property;
- earned fees must be transferred from trust to operating account promptly;
- client funds must be kept separate from the lawyer's own funds, with accurate records.

Commingling client funds with the firm's operating funds, or failing to maintain trust account records, is among the most severely sanctioned ethics violations. Never treat an unearned advance as the firm's money unless the jurisdiction clearly permits a true non-refundable retainer.

### Manage Billing Records And Fee Disputes

Billing records are the evidence that supports the fee. Poor records undermine collection and invite challenges.

Maintain:

- contemporaneous time entries describing the work, the timekeeper, and the time spent;
- entries that are sufficiently detailed to support the charge and to comply with any audit rights;
- separate tracking of costs and disbursements;
- records of scope changes and client approvals;
- documentation of the results obtained and the value delivered.

Fee disputes often arise at the end of the matter. Strong billing records support collection, defend against reasonableness challenges, and demonstrate compliance with the engagement terms.

### Address Fee Sharing, Referrals, And Division

Sharing fees with other lawyers or firms is restricted and often requires disclosure and client consent.

Confirm:

- fee divisions between lawyers in different firms are generally permitted only if the division is in proportion to the work done, or if each lawyer assumes joint responsibility, and the client agrees in writing;
- the client must be informed of the division and the basis;
- paying a referral fee to a non-lawyer is generally prohibited;
- receiving compensation from a third party for recommending a service is restricted and requires disclosure.

Undisclosed fee sharing can void the fee agreement and result in discipline.

### Plan Fee Collection And Withdrawal

Collecting unpaid fees requires care, because aggressive collection can trigger malpractice counterclaims and ethics complaints.

Address:

- the right to withdraw for non-payment, subject to court approval in pending litigation;
- the procedure for turning over the client file and returning unearned funds;
- the ethics limits on retaining client files or property as security for unpaid fees (a lien may be available but is restricted);
- the risk that a fee collection suit invites a malpractice counterclaim;
- alternative dispute resolution for fee disputes, which some jurisdictions require or encourage.

Withdrawing or suing for fees without following the rules can convert a collection matter into a discipline matter. Plan the collection path carefully.

## Common Traps

### Proposing A Prohibited Or Capped Fee Structure

Contingency in criminal or domestic matters, or a percentage above the statutory cap, can void the fee and trigger sanctions.

### Treating Unearned Retainers As Earned

Advance payments for future services are usually client property. Withdrawing them to operating funds before earning them risks commingling and conversion.

### Oral Or Vague Fee Agreements

Disputes over scope, rate, or contingency percentage are hard to resolve without a written agreement. Reduce all terms to writing.

### Inadequate Billing Records

Contemporaneous, detailed time entries are essential to support fees and defend challenges. Reconstructed records are weak.

### Undisclosed Fee Sharing

Dividing fees with another firm without client consent and proportionality or joint responsibility violates ethics rules.

### Aggressive Collection Without Following Rules

Suing for fees without returning the file, or retaining client property improperly, invites counterclaims and discipline.

### Ignoring Reasonableness For Vulnerable Clients

A fee that survives reasonableness review for a sophisticated client may not for a vulnerable one. Document the basis and consider the client's sophistication.

## Self-Check

- Is the fee structure matched to the matter type and client, with contingency permissibility and caps verified for the jurisdiction?
- Is fee reasonableness assessed against the governing factors (time, novelty, locality, amount, results, relationship, skill) and documented?
- Are fee agreements in writing where required, with contingency terms (percentage, calculation, cost deduction) and changes documented?
- Are advance payments and retainers handled per trust account rules, distinguishing earned from unearned funds and avoiding commingling?
- Are billing records contemporaneous, detailed, cost-separated, and supportive of the fee and any audit rights?
- Are fee sharing, referrals, and divisions disclosed, proportionate or joint-responsibility-based, and consented to in writing?
- Is fee collection planned with withdrawal rights, file return, lien limits, malpractice counterclaim risk, and ADR considered?
- Are fee terms reviewed by qualified counsel familiar with the jurisdiction's bar rules before the engagement begins?
