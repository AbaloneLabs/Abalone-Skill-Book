# Legal Hold And Preservation

The duty to preserve relevant evidence arises when litigation is reasonably anticipated, and it is one of the most consequential and most mishandled obligations in modern disputes. Once the duty attaches, routine deletion must stop, custodians must be identified and instructed, and the preservation effort must be documented. Agents often treat the legal hold as a one-time email notice, when in fact preservation is an ongoing, scoped, monitored, and audited process whose failures lead to adverse inference, monetary sanctions, default judgment, and separate spoliation tort liability. The cost of a botched hold frequently exceeds the cost of the underlying dispute.

Electronically stored information is the core preservation challenge. Auto-deletion, backup rotation, device replacement, and cloud retention policies silently destroy evidence unless the hold reaches them. Use this skill before issuing a legal hold, before defining preservation scope, before suspending deletion, before monitoring compliance, or before responding to spoliation allegations. The goal is to help the agent implement a defensible preservation process, not to provide legal advice, which requires qualified counsel familiar with the governing jurisdiction's spoliation standards.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Trigger For The Preservation Duty

The duty to preserve begins when litigation is reasonably anticipated, which can be earlier than most assume. Identifying the trigger date frames every later decision.

Recognize triggers:

- receipt of a demand letter or threat of suit;
- a dispute that has escalated beyond ordinary course;
- a regulatory inquiry or investigation;
- an accident, injury, or incident likely to produce a claim;
- a decision to file suit;
- receipt of a subpoena;
- a contract breach that is heading to formal dispute;
- knowledge of an employee departure likely to produce a claim.

The trigger is fact-specific. When in doubt, preservation should begin and counsel consulted. Document the rationale for the trigger date.

### Define The Scope Of Preservation Proportionately

Over-preservation is costly and under-preservation is sanctionable. Scope must be tied to the issues, custodians, and time period of the dispute.

Define scope:

- the claims, defenses, and factual issues that drive relevance;
- the custodians likely to have relevant information;
- the data sources, email, chat, file shares, cloud, devices, applications;
- the time period relevant to the dispute;
- the data types, documents, messages, logs, recordings, databases;
- the proportionality to the dispute's value and burden;
- the distinction between preservation and collection, preserve first, collect later.

A hold that captures everything is expensive and hard to defend; one that captures too little invites spoliation. Scope deliberately and document the reasoning.

### Identify Custodians And Data Sources Comprehensively

Missing a custodian or data source is a common preservation failure. Map the organization's information landscape to the dispute.

Identify:

- key decision-makers and communicators;
- employees in relevant roles or departments;
- departed employees whose data may still exist;
- third-party platforms, SaaS, and cloud providers;
- personal devices used for business under BYOD policies;
- shared drives, repositories, and databases;
- backup systems and archival storage;
- ephemeral or auto-deleting messaging platforms.

Ephemeral messaging platforms that auto-delete require special attention. Disable auto-deletion for relevant custodians or preserve proactively.

### Issue Clear And Actionable Hold Notices

The hold notice is the primary tool for instructing custodians. Vague or one-time notices fail to preserve and to demonstrate compliance.

Issue notices that:

- state the purpose and the duty to preserve in plain language;
- describe the scope, time period, and data sources;
- instruct custodians not to delete, edit, or alter relevant material;
- explain the suspension of auto-deletion and routine cleanup;
- identify a contact for questions;
- require acknowledgment of receipt;
- are re-issued periodically as reminders;
- are supplemented when scope changes, such as new custodians or claims.

A notice that is sent once and never followed up is weak. Periodic reminders and scope updates maintain the hold's effectiveness.

### Suspend Routine Deletion And Auto-Purging

Routine deletion is the silent destroyer of evidence. The hold must reach the systems that automatically purge data.

Suspend:

- email retention and deletion policies;
- chat and messaging retention settings;
- voicemail and call log retention;
- backup rotation and overwriting;
- file-share cleanup scripts;
- cloud application retention rules;
- device wipe or replacement procedures;
- ephemeral messaging auto-deletion.

Confirm with IT that suspensions are actually implemented, not merely requested. Document the technical steps taken.

### Monitor, Audit, And Document Compliance

A hold that is issued but not monitored cannot be defended. Documentation is the evidence of compliance if spoliation is later alleged.

Monitor:

- custodian acknowledgments and follow-up on non-responses;
- periodic reminders at appropriate intervals;
- IT confirmation of suspension implementation;
- audits of preservation status for key custodians and sources;
- tracking of departing employees and their data;
- updates to scope as the case develops;
- logs of all hold actions, notices, and confirmations.

A defensible hold is a documented hold. Maintain records that show what was preserved, when, and how.

### Handle Departing Custodians And System Changes

Employee departures and system migrations are high-risk moments for preservation. Data can be lost in transitions if not actively managed.

Handle:

- preservation of departing employees' data before offboarding;
- imaging or collection of departing custodian devices;
- preservation during email migrations or platform changes;
- preservation during hardware replacement or wipes;
- preservation of data during corporate transactions or reorganizations;
- the retention of departed employee data beyond normal policies while the hold is active.

A custodian who departs with their laptop wiped loses evidence. Preserve before offboarding completes.

### Respond To Spoliation Allegations And Sanctions Risk

If evidence is lost despite efforts, the response determines the sanctions exposure. Good-faith, documented preservation reduces consequences.

Respond by:

- investigating the loss promptly and thoroughly;
- documenting the cause and the preservation efforts made;
- assessing the materiality of the lost evidence;
- evaluating the applicable spoliation standard in the jurisdiction;
- considering remediation such as collection from alternate sources;
- preparing a defense based on reasonableness and proportionality;
- disclosing the loss appropriately to counsel and the court.

Sanctions turn on whether the preservation effort was reasonable and whether the loss caused prejudice. Document the good-faith effort throughout.

## Common Traps

### Treating The Hold As A One-Time Notice

A single email is not a defensible hold. Issue reminders, track acknowledgments, and update scope.

### Missing A Custodian Or Data Source

Overlooking a key custodian, departed employee, or ephemeral platform causes silent loss. Map the information landscape comprehensively.

### Failing To Suspend Auto-Deletion

Routine deletion destroys evidence despite the hold. Confirm IT implementation, not just requests.

### Over-Preserving Indefensibly

Capturing everything is costly and hard to justify. Scope to relevance and proportionality.

### No Documentation Of Compliance

An undocumented hold cannot be defended. Log all notices, acknowledgments, and technical steps.

### Ignoring Departing Custodians

Offboarding without preservation loses data. Image devices before wipe.

### Late Trigger Recognition

Waiting until suit is filed to preserve is often too late. The duty arises when litigation is reasonably anticipated.

## Self-Check

- Is the preservation trigger identified and documented, recognizing demand letters, escalated disputes, regulatory inquiries, incidents, suit decisions, subpoenas, and likely claims?
- Is the preservation scope defined proportionately, tied to claims, defenses, custodians, data sources, time period, data types, and the distinction between preservation and collection?
- Are custodians and data sources identified comprehensively, including decision-makers, departed employees, third-party platforms, personal devices, shared drives, backups, and ephemeral messaging?
- Are hold notices clear and actionable, stating purpose, scope, time period, data sources, suspension instructions, contact, acknowledgment, periodic reminders, and scope updates?
- Is routine deletion suspended for email, chat, voicemail, backups, file shares, cloud applications, device procedures, and ephemeral messaging, with IT confirmation documented?
- Is compliance monitored and audited through acknowledgments, reminders, IT confirmations, audits, departing-employee tracking, scope updates, and action logs?
- Are departing custodians and system changes handled with pre-offboarding preservation, device imaging, migration preservation, hardware replacement, corporate transactions, and extended retention?
- Is spoliation response prepared with prompt investigation, cause documentation, materiality assessment, jurisdictional standard, remediation, reasonableness defense, and appropriate disclosure?
- Does the output escalate to qualified counsel for trigger determination, scope, sanctions exposure, and spoliation response?
