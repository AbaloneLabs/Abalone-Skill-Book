# Offerings And Exemptions

Issuing stock, notes, tokens, or partnership interests is a securities transaction regulated at the federal and state level, and often abroad. Agents often treat fundraising as a business event and miss that every sale of a security must either be registered or fit within an exemption, and that the wrong exemption, missing filing, ineligible investor, or general solicitation without verification can turn a successful raise into a rescission right, enforcement action, or personal liability for founders. The choice among public offering, private placement, mini-IPO, crowdfunding, and offshore offering is not cosmetic; it determines who can invest, how much, what disclosures are required, what filings are due, and what liability attaches to the issuer and its officers.

Use this skill before launching a fundraising round, before issuing equity or convertible instruments, before marketing to investors, before tokenizing an asset, or before accepting investment from non-accredited or foreign investors. The goal is to force the agent to identify the security, select the right registration or exemption route, map the investor eligibility and solicitation rules, and calendar the filings. Securities law is jurisdiction-specific and high-risk; all offerings require qualified securities counsel.

## Core Rules

### Identify Whether The Instrument Is A Security

The first question is whether what is being sold is a security. The definition is broad and includes stocks, bonds, notes, investment contracts, partnership and LLC interests, options, and many tokens. The Howey investment contract test asks whether there is an investment of money in a common enterprise with an expectation of profits derived from the efforts of others.

Identify:

- the instrument and its economic rights;
- whether investors are passive or active;
- whether profits come from promoter efforts;
- whether the instrument is marketed as an investment;
- whether token or digital asset features create security status;
- whether notes fall under the Reves commercial presumption.

If it is a security, registration or an exemption is required. Assuming an instrument is not a security because it is labeled a token, a note, or a membership interest is a common and costly error.

### Choose Between Registration And Exemption

A security must be sold under a registration statement or an available exemption. Registration is costly and time-consuming and is used for public offerings. Exemptions allow private sales with narrower requirements.

Compare the main routes:

- public offering with an effective registration statement, for broad retail or institutional access;
- Regulation D Rule 506(b), no general solicitation, up to 35 non-accredited but sophisticated investors plus unlimited accredited investors;
- Regulation D Rule 506(c), general solicitation permitted but all purchasers must be verified accredited;
- Regulation A and A+, mini-IPOs with streamlined disclosure and caps on raise size;
- Regulation Crowdfunding, small raises from retail investors through portals with caps;
- Regulation S, offshore offerings exempt from US registration if no directed selling efforts to US persons;
- intrastate exemptions for offerings entirely within one state;
- private resales under Rule 144 for affiliates and controlled securities.

Match the route to the investor base, raise size, timeline, cost tolerance, and disclosure capacity. A route that allows general solicitation but requires verification shifts cost to the verification process.

### Map Investor Eligibility And Verification

Each route restricts who can invest and how verification must be documented. Errors here invalidate the exemption.

Verify:

- accredited investor status and the documentation required for 506(c), including income, net worth, or third-party verification;
- non-accredited sophistication and disclosure requirements for 506(b);
- investment limits for Regulation Crowdfunding based on investor income and net worth;
- eligibility for Regulation A tiers and testing-the-waters;
- US person status and offshore compliance for Regulation S;
- bad actor disqualification under Rule 506 for issuers and covered persons;
- affiliate and control status for resales.

Document verification before accepting funds. Reliance on a bare investor representation of accredited status is insufficient for 506(c).

### Control Solicitation And Marketing Activity

General solicitation rules differ by route. A 506(b) offering that uses general solicitation loses its exemption; a 506(c) offering may use general solicitation but must verify accredited status.

Control:

- who is contacted and how;
- whether pitches, demos, websites, or social media constitute general solicitation;
- whether a demo day or pitch event is limited to pre-existing substantive relationships;
- whether a finder or placement agent is used and whether they are registered broker-dealers;
- testing-the-waters communications under permitted rules;
- the use of solicitation materials and their consistency with disclosure documents.

Using an unregistered finder or broker-dealer can create rescission rights and regulatory liability. Paying commissions to unregistered persons in a securities transaction is a recurring violation.

### Prepare Required Disclosures And Filings

Each route requires specific disclosure and filings, both federal and state. Missing filings can suspend the exemption.

Prepare:

- Form D filing with the SEC within 15 days of first sale for Regulation D;
- state notice filings and fees where the investors reside;
- offering memorandum or PPM for private placements with non-accredited investors;
- offering circular and financial statements for Regulation A;
- crowdfunding platform filings and annual reports;
- blue sky compliance and review of state exemptions;
- bad actor questionnaires and covered persons review;
- books and records of the offering.

Calendar filing deadlines at the outset. Late Form D filings are common and create compliance gaps.

### Address Disclosure Liability And Anti-Fraud Rules

Even exempt offerings are subject to anti-fraud rules. Material misstatements or omissions create liability under federal and state law, regardless of exemption.

Ensure:

- disclosures are accurate and not misleading;
- material risks are disclosed;
- financial statements are prepared appropriately;
- forward-looking statements are identified and accompanied by cautionary language;
- conflicts and related-party interests are disclosed;
- use of proceeds is specific;
- no selective disclosure to favored investors;
- updates if material changes occur before closing.

Anti-fraud liability applies to issuers, officers, directors, and control persons. An exemption from registration is not an exemption from telling the truth.

### Coordinate With Existing Capital Structure And Governance

An offering changes the cap table and may trigger rights of existing investors. Coordinate before launching.

Check:

- preemptive rights of existing shareholders;
- information and consent rights of existing investors;
- rights of first refusal and co-sale;
- board and shareholder approvals required;
- authorized share sufficiency and any charter amendment;
- protective provisions that may veto the terms;
- impact on existing option pool and 409A valuations;
- tax and accounting consequences.

Selling a round that violates existing investor rights can be unwound or trigger damages.

### Consider International And Cross-Border Issues

Offerings to investors outside the home jurisdiction trigger foreign securities laws. A US offering to foreign investors, or a foreign offering to US persons, requires careful structuring.

Address:

- Regulation S compliance for offshore sales;
- local prospectus and licensing requirements;
- sanctions and anti-money-laundering screening of investors;
- tax withholding and reporting on distributions;
- data transfer rules for investor information;
- coordination of disclosure across jurisdictions.

## Common Traps

### Assuming The Instrument Is Not A Security

Tokens, notes, and membership interests are often securities. Apply the investment contract test before assuming an exemption is unnecessary.

### Using General Solicitation In A 506(b) Offering

General solicitation destroys the 506(b) exemption. Limit outreach or switch to 506(c) with verification.

### Paying Unregistered Finders Or Brokers

Commissions to unregistered persons create rescission rights and regulatory liability. Use registered broker-dealers.

### Missing Form D And State Filings

Late or missing filings create compliance gaps and can suspend the exemption. Calendar them before first sale.

### Inadequate Accredited Investor Verification For 506(c)

A bare investor self-certification is insufficient. Obtain documented verification.

### Ignoring Bad Actor Disqualification

A covered person with a disqualifying event can void the Rule 506 exemption. Screen covered persons before each offering.

### Selective Or Inconsistent Disclosure

Telling favored investors more than others, or marketing materials inconsistent with the PPM, creates fraud and liability exposure.

### Overlooking Existing Investor Rights

Preemptive rights, consent rights, and protective provisions can block or unwind a round.

## Self-Check

- Is the instrument analyzed for security status under the investment contract test and applicable note and token rules?
- Are registration and exemption routes compared, including public offering, Regulation D 506(b) and 506(c), Regulation A, crowdfunding, Regulation S, and intrastate?
- Is investor eligibility mapped, with accredited verification, non-accredited sophistication and disclosure, crowdfunding limits, offshore status, and bad actor screening?
- Are solicitation and marketing controls in place, including general solicitation limits, broker-dealer registration, testing-the-waters, and material consistency?
- Are required disclosures and filings prepared and calendared, including Form D, state notices, PPM, offering circular, crowdfunding reports, and blue sky compliance?
- Are anti-fraud disclosure obligations met, with accurate, non-misleading, risk-disclosing, forward-looking, conflict-aware, and updated materials?
- Are existing investor rights, preemptive rights, consents, charter sufficiency, and option pool impacts coordinated before launch?
- Are international and cross-border issues addressed, including Regulation S, local prospectus rules, sanctions, tax withholding, and data transfer?
- Are bad actor disqualification, covered persons, and ongoing reporting obligations reviewed?
- Does the output avoid asserting that an offering is compliant and instead recommend qualified securities counsel to structure, document, and file the specific offering?
