# Disclosure And Reporting

Public companies and reporting companies owe the market continuous, accurate, and timely disclosure. Disclosure is not marketing; it is a legal obligation enforced by regulators and private plaintiffs. Agents often draft disclosure that reads like promotion, omits known material risks, buries bad news in boilerplate, or fails to update when circumstances change. The result is regulatory enforcement, shareholder class actions, restatements, and personal liability for officers and directors. A single material misstatement in a registration statement, annual report, or earnings release can trigger liability that dwarfs any benefit the optimistic disclosure produced.

Use this skill before drafting or reviewing a registration statement, prospectus, annual or quarterly report, earnings release, investor presentation, or insider filing, before establishing disclosure controls, or when responding to a material event. The goal is to force the agent to apply materiality discipline, distinguish required from voluntary disclosure, integrate financial and non-financial information, and avoid the patterns that produce fraud claims. Disclosure rules are detailed and jurisdiction-specific; reporting companies must use qualified securities counsel and disclosure committees.

## Core Rules

### Apply A Rigorous Materiality Standard

Materiality is the threshold for most disclosure obligations. Information is material if there is a substantial likelihood a reasonable investor would consider it important, or if it would alter the total mix of information. Materiality is not what management wishes investors to focus on.

Assess materiality for:

- financial results and trends;
- known trends, demands, commitments, and uncertainties expected to affect results;
- liquidity and capital resources;
- off-balance-sheet arrangements;
- contractual obligations;
- risk factors specific to the company;
- legal proceedings above thresholds;
- changes in business, products, or strategy;
- management and governance changes;
- cybersecurity incidents;
- restatements and material weaknesses;
- related-party transactions;
- subsequent events.

When in doubt, lean toward disclosure and document the materiality analysis. Undisclosed material information is the core of most fraud cases.

### Build And Follow A Disclosure Controls Framework

Disclosure quality depends on process, not heroics. A disclosure committee and controls framework ensure that material information reaches the right people and is evaluated for disclosure.

Establish:

- a disclosure committee with finance, legal, operations, and business representation;
- procedures to identify, escalate, and evaluate material information;
- integration with internal controls over financial reporting;
- a calendar of periodic reports and event-driven filings;
- review and certification by principal officers under applicable requirements;
- recordkeeping of disclosure decisions and their basis;
- training on insider trading, Regulation FD, and selective disclosure;
- escalation for whistleblower and audit committee matters.

Without a controls framework, disclosure becomes ad hoc and inconsistent, and certifications become personal liability traps.

### Draft Risk Factors That Are Specific And Decision-Useful

Risk factors are mandatory in registration statements and annual reports, but generic risk factors invite regulator comment and plaintiff scrutiny. Risk factors should be company-specific and explain how the risk could materially affect the company.

For each risk factor:

- describe the risk specifically, not generically;
- explain why it matters to this company;
- connect it to business strategy, financial condition, or results;
- avoid hypothetical risks disconnected from the business;
- update risk factors as circumstances change;
- do not omit known material risks to avoid alarming investors;
- ensure consistency with the rest of the document.

Boilerplate risks such as "we may fail to compete" add little and signal weak disclosure. Tailored risks such as "we depend on a single supplier for 60 percent of our components and have no qualified alternative" are useful and required.

### Prepare Accurate Financial Statements And MD&A

Financial statements and the management discussion and analysis are the heart of periodic reports. Errors here trigger restatements and liability.

Ensure:

- financial statements are prepared under the applicable accounting standards;
- accounting policies are consistently applied and disclosed;
- estimates and judgments are explained;
- MD&A discusses liquidity, capital resources, and results with year-over-year analysis;
- known trends and uncertainties are disclosed;
- off-balance-sheet arrangements are disclosed;
- critical accounting estimates are explained;
- subsequent events are evaluated;
- internal control deficiencies and material weaknesses are disclosed;
- auditor opinions and changes in auditor are disclosed;
- non-GAAP measures, if used, reconcile to GAAP and comply with rules.

Non-GAAP measures are a frequent source of comment and enforcement. Follow reconciliation, prominence, and consistency rules strictly.

### File Section 16 And Insider Trading Reports On Time

Insiders and major shareholders must report transactions promptly. Late filings are common and create liability and reputational harm.

Track:

- Section 16 reporting for officers, directors, and 10 percent shareholders;
- Forms 3, 4, and 5 deadlines and transaction codes;
- Rule 144 and control stock sales;
- 10b5-1 trading plans and their adoption, amendment, and good-faith requirements;
- blackout periods and trading windows;
- short-swing profit recovery under Section 16(b);
- Section 13D and 13G for major shareholders;
- beneficial ownership calculations.

Implement pre-clearance procedures and automate filing to avoid late filings, which are visible to the market and regulators.

### Manage Material Nonpublic Information And Regulation FD

Selective disclosure of material nonpublic information to favored analysts or investors violates Regulation FD and creates liability. Disclosure must be broad or not at all.

Control:

- who speaks to analysts and investors and what they may say;
- pre-clearance of investor communications;
- use of broadly disseminated methods for intentional disclosures;
- prompt public disclosure of unintentional selective disclosure;
- earnings releases and calls with simultaneous webcasting;
- fair disclosure of material developments;
- internal walls around material nonpublic information;
- training for executives who interact with the market.

A CEO who tells a favored analyst about a shortfall before public release creates an FD violation and a securities fraud risk.

### Update Disclosure When Circumstances Change

Disclosure is not a one-time event. Material changes require updates through periodic reports, current reports, and prospectus supplements.

Trigger updates for:

- material agreements and amendments;
- acquisitions and dispositions;
- financings and defaults;
- litigation and settlements;
- management and auditor changes;
- cybersecurity incidents;
- restatements and control deficiencies;
- bankruptcy or insolvency events;
- regulatory inquiries and enforcement;
- going concern doubts.

Failure to update stale disclosure can be as misleading as an initial misstatement.

### Coordinate With Liability And Insurance Protection

Disclosure creates liability exposure for the company and its officers and directors. Coordinate protection.

Confirm:

- D&O insurance coverage for securities claims, including Side A;
- indemnification and advancement rights;
- disclosure counsel review of registration statements;
- underwriting due diligence and reliance on experts;
- separation of company and individual counsel where conflicts exist;
- recordkeeping supporting due diligence defenses.

## Common Traps

### Drafting Disclosure As Marketing

Promotional tone, unsupported claims, and omission of risks create fraud exposure. Disclosure must be balanced and accurate.

### Omitting Known Material Risks

Hiding bad news to avoid alarming investors is the core of fraud claims. Disclose material risks specifically.

### Generic Boilerplate Risk Factors

Generic risks invite comment letters and signal weak disclosure. Tailor risks to the company.

### Late Or Inaccurate Section 16 Filings

Late insider filings are visible and create short-swing liability. Automate and pre-clear trades.

### Selective Disclosure To Analysts

Telling favored analysts first violates Regulation FD and creates fraud risk. Disclose broadly or not at all.

### Non-GAAP Measure Misuse

Non-GAAP measures without proper reconciliation or prominence trigger enforcement. Follow the rules strictly.

### Stale Disclosure

Failing to update for material changes can be as misleading as an initial misstatement. Update promptly.

### Weak Disclosure Controls

Without a disclosure committee and escalation, certifications become personal liability traps and material information may be missed.

## Self-Check

- Is materiality assessed rigorously for financial results, trends, liquidity, risk factors, legal proceedings, governance, cybersecurity, restatements, related-party transactions, and subsequent events?
- Is a disclosure controls framework in place with a committee, escalation, certifications, recordkeeping, training, and integration with internal controls?
- Are risk factors company-specific, decision-useful, updated, and consistent with the rest of the document?
- Are financial statements and MD&A accurate, with consistent policies, disclosed estimates, liquidity and results analysis, off-balance-sheet items, critical estimates, subsequent events, control deficiencies, and compliant non-GAAP measures?
- Are Section 16 Forms 3, 4, and 5, Rule 144, 10b5-1 plans, blackout periods, short-swing recovery, and Section 13D/G filings tracked and filed on time?
- Are material nonpublic information and Regulation FD controlled through pre-clearance, broad dissemination, prompt correction of selective disclosure, and executive training?
- Is disclosure updated for material agreements, acquisitions, financings, litigation, management changes, cybersecurity, restatements, bankruptcy, and regulatory matters?
- Are D&O insurance, indemnification, disclosure counsel review, underwriter due diligence, and recordkeeping coordinated for liability protection?
- Are restatements, material weaknesses, and going concern matters escalated and disclosed appropriately?
- Does the output avoid asserting that disclosure is sufficient or non-actionable and instead recommend qualified securities counsel and disclosure committee review?
