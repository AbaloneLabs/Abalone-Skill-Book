# Pre-Litigation And Demand

The pre-litigation phase is where most disputes are won, lost, or settled before any court is involved. A demand letter is not a formality; it is a strategic instrument that frames the claims, sets the legal and factual narrative, starts or stops the limitation clock, preserves evidence duties, and often determines whether the matter resolves quietly or escalates into costly litigation. Agents frequently treat the demand stage as a simple letter-writing exercise, when in fact it is the moment to lock in facts, secure evidence, assess the other side's position, and create a clean record that a court or arbitrator may later rely on.

Conversely, responding to a demand is equally delicate. A poorly worded reply can create admissions, waive defenses, restart a limitation period, or trigger regulatory notice duties. The same letter can be the foundation of a strong case or the source of a damaging one. Use this skill before drafting a demand, before responding to a demand, before sending a content-certified notice, before preserving evidence for a foreseeable claim, or before deciding whether to file suit or negotiate. The goal is to help the agent use the pre-litigation stage deliberately, not to provide legal advice, which requires qualified counsel in the governing jurisdiction.

## Core Rules

### Determine The Purpose And Posture Of The Demand

A demand can serve several distinct purposes, and the purpose shapes the tone, content, and legal effect of the letter. Confusing them produces weak letters and missed deadlines.

Classify the purpose:

- notice of breach required by contract before termination or suit;
- demand for payment on a liquidated debt;
- cease and desist for ongoing harm such as IP infringement or defamation;
- preservation notice to lock in evidence duties;
- tolling agreement proposal to extend the limitation period;
- final pre-suit settlement offer to trigger fee-shifting or costs;
- regulatory or statutory notice that is a condition precedent to suit;
- invitation to negotiate or mediate.

A statutory notice that is a condition precedent must meet exact form, timing, and content requirements. A settlement offer may be designed to trigger cost or fee consequences if rejected. Match the letter to its legal function.

### Verify The Limitation Period And Tolling Position

The single most important pre-litigation question is whether the claim is time-barred or about to be. Limitation periods vary by claim type, jurisdiction, and contract, and missing them extinguishes the right to sue.

Identify:

- the governing limitation statute for each claim;
- the accrual date, when the cause of action arose;
- the limitation period length;
- tolling events such as minority, incapacity, fraudulent concealment, or statutory tolling;
- whether a written demand is required to start or stop a period;
- whether the contract shortens or lengthens the period;
- whether a tolling agreement is needed or in place;
- whether a demand resets or pauses the clock.

If the limitation period is close to expiring, the priority is filing suit or executing a tolling agreement, not negotiating. A demand letter does not by itself toll the period unless a tolling agreement is signed.

### Frame Claims And Facts With Precision

A demand letter often becomes an exhibit in later litigation. Its factual and legal framing should be deliberate, complete enough to be credible, but not so detailed that it over-commits the sender or waives later arguments.

Include:

- the parties and their relationship;
- the key facts in chronological order;
- the contract clauses, statutes, or rights invoked;
- the specific breach or wrongful conduct;
- the damages or harm suffered, with a basis for the amount;
- the relief demanded, monetary and nonmonetary;
- a deadline for response;
- the consequences of non-compliance, such as suit, termination, or escalation;
- reservation of rights language.

Avoid speculation, insults, threats of criminal action to coerce civil payment, or admissions that could bind the sender. Do not assert facts the sender cannot support.

### Preserve Evidence Before And During The Demand

The duty to preserve relevant evidence arises when litigation is reasonably anticipated, which is often at the demand stage. A demand letter can trigger the recipient's preservation duty, and sending one should prompt the sender's own.

Preserve:

- contracts, amendments, and incorporated documents;
- emails, chats, and messages;
- invoices, payments, and financial records;
- system logs and metadata;
- recordings and meeting notes;
- physical evidence and samples;
- third-party platform data;
- personnel and customer records as relevant.

Issue or confirm a litigation hold. Do not delete, edit, or "clean up" records after the demand. Spoliation can lead to adverse inference, sanctions, or separate tort liability.

### Calibrate Tone And Escalation

The tone of a demand affects whether the matter resolves. An overly aggressive letter can harden positions, provoke a counter-suit, or create bad publicity, while an overly soft letter can signal weakness and invite delay.

Calibrate based on:

- the strength of the legal position;
- the value of the ongoing relationship;
- the likelihood of settlement;
- the other side's likely sophistication and counsel;
- regulatory or reputational sensitivity;
- whether the goal is payment, cessation, or negotiation;
- whether a court will later read the letter.

A strong legal position with a damaged relationship may warrant a firm ultimatum. A valuable ongoing relationship may warrant a cooperative notice with a cure period. Strong does not mean abusive.

### Manage Admissions And Privilege In The Response

Responding to a demand is as risky as sending one. Every word can become evidence. The response should preserve defenses, avoid unnecessary admissions, and protect privilege.

In the response:

- deny specifically what is disputed, rather than making blanket denials;
- admit only what is verifiable and necessary;
- preserve all defenses, counterclaims, and setoffs;
- avoid explanations that create new admissions;
- reserve rights explicitly;
- route legal analysis through counsel on privileged channels;
- avoid making settlement offers that could be characterized as admissions of liability;
- consider whether a response is required at all, or whether silence plus preservation is safer.

A response that admits the underlying relationship or receipt of goods may concede elements of a claim. Verify each statement against the evidence before sending.

### Use Tolling And Standstill Agreements Deliberately

When the limitation period is short but negotiation is promising, a tolling or standstill agreement lets the parties negotiate without losing the right to sue. But these agreements have traps.

Address:

- which claims and parties are covered;
- the length of the tolling period and any extensions;
- the tolling mechanism, suspension versus extension;
- whether the agreement itself tolls or merely pauses;
- waiver of limitation defenses during the period;
- preservation and confidentiality obligations;
- what happens if negotiation fails;
- governing law and forum for disputes over the agreement.

Do not let a tolling agreement expire without either filing suit or extending it. A poorly drafted agreement can itself create disputes.

### Document The Settlement Attempt

Pre-litigation settlement efforts should be documented in a way that supports later fee-shifting, cost, or good-faith arguments, without creating damaging admissions.

Capture:

- the offers exchanged;
- the dates and channels;
- the authority of each side to settle;
- whether offers were made under Rule 408 or equivalent settlement-privilege protection;
- the reasons for rejection or failure;
- any conditional or time-limited terms.

In many jurisdictions, settlement negotiations are not admissible to prove liability, but they may be admissible for other purposes. Use settlement-privilege framing where available.

## Common Traps

### Sending A Demand Without Checking The Limitation Period

A demand does not stop the clock. If the period expires during negotiation, the claim is lost. File or toll first.

### Overstating Claims Or Threatening Criminal Action

Exaggerated damages, threats of criminal referral to coerce civil payment, or abusive language can backfire, create counterclaims, or violate extortion or debt-collection laws.

### Making Admissions In The Response

Explaining, apologizing, or justifying in a response can concede elements of a claim. Deny specifically, admit only what is necessary, and reserve rights.

### Confusing A Contractual Notice With A Settlement Demand

A contractual notice of breach may need exact form, method, and content to be effective. A generic demand letter may not satisfy it, leaving the right to terminate or sue unperfected.

### Letting A Tolling Agreement Expire

A standstill agreement that lapses without suit or extension extinguishes the claim. Calendar the expiration and act before it.

### Forgetting The Sender's Own Preservation Duty

Sending a demand triggers anticipation of litigation. The sender must preserve its own evidence too, not only demand the recipient do so.

### Sending The Demand Through The Wrong Channel

If the contract specifies notice by certified mail to a registered agent, an email demand may not satisfy the condition precedent. Match the required method.

## Self-Check

- Is the purpose of the demand classified as breach notice, payment demand, cease and desist, preservation notice, tolling proposal, settlement offer, or statutory condition precedent?
- Is the limitation period for each claim identified with accrual date, length, tolling events, and whether the demand affects the clock?
- Does the letter frame parties, facts, contract clauses, statutes, breach, damages, relief, deadline, and consequences with reservation of rights, without over-committing or threatening improper action?
- Are relevant records preserved on both sides, with a litigation hold issued or confirmed, and no deletion or editing after the demand?
- Is the tone calibrated to legal strength, relationship value, settlement likelihood, sophistication, and reputational sensitivity?
- Does any response deny specifically, admit only what is verifiable, preserve defenses and counterclaims, reserve rights, and route legal analysis through privileged channels?
- If a tolling or standstill agreement is used, are covered claims, period, mechanism, waiver, preservation, failure consequences, and governing law addressed, with expiration calendared?
- Are settlement efforts documented under settlement-privilege framing to support fee or cost arguments without creating admissions?
- Does the output escalate to qualified counsel for statutory notices, limitation analysis, tolling agreements, and any matter with material exposure?
