# Early Case Assessment

Early case assessment is the disciplined process of evaluating a dispute before significant resources are committed. The goal is not to predict the outcome with false precision, but to surface the legal, factual, financial, temporal, and reputational variables that should drive the decision to litigate, defend, settle, or walk away. Agents often produce case assessments that read like advocacy memos, overstating strengths and burying weaknesses, or that reduce complex uncertainty to a single misleading number. Both errors lead to bad decisions.

A useful early case assessment separates what is known from what is assumed, attaches realistic ranges to damages and probability, models the cost of litigation against the value of the claim, weighs non-monetary consequences such as reputation and precedent, and produces a defensible recommendation with clear conditions. Use this skill before recommending suit, before advising a client to defend rather than settle, before setting a settlement reserve, before reporting exposure to leadership or auditors, or before committing to a litigation budget. The assessment supports the decision; it does not replace qualified counsel for the governing jurisdiction.

## Core Rules

### Map The Legal And Factual Theory First

Probability and value cannot be assessed without a clear theory of the case. Start by identifying the elements that must be proven and the facts that support or undermine each element.

For each claim or defense, capture:

- the legal elements or contract requirements;
- the supporting facts and evidence;
- the adverse facts and missing evidence;
- the witnesses and their credibility;
- the documentary record and gaps;
- the governing law and uncertain legal questions;
- the burden of proof and who bears it;
- the likely defenses and counterclaims.

A claim with strong facts but a weak legal theory, or a strong theory but missing evidence, must be discounted accordingly. Do not assess probability before the theory is mapped.

### Attach Probability As A Range, Not A Point

Litigation outcomes are inherently uncertain. A single percentage such as "70 percent likely to win" invites false confidence and cannot be defended. Use ranges and identify the drivers of uncertainty.

Express probability as:

- a range, such as 55 to 75 percent;
- broken down by element or phase, such as liability versus damages;
- with the key uncertainties identified, such as a disputed fact or a novel legal question;
- with the scenarios that move the range up or down;
- with a confidence level in the assessment itself.

Be explicit about what would have to be true for the high end versus the low end. Distinguish cases that turn on one dispositive issue from those with many independent variables.

### Model Damages And Exposure Realistically

Damages modeling is where assessments most often go wrong. Plaintiffs overstate recoverable damages; defendants understate exposure. Both ignore components that a court or arbitrator may actually award.

Model:

- compensatory damages with a defensible method;
- consequential or incidental damages and their foreseeability limits;
- statutory or liquidated damages if applicable;
- interest, including pre-judgment and post-judgment rates;
- attorneys' fees and cost-shifting rules;
- multiple or treble damages for statutory claims;
- injunctive or non-monetary relief and its business value;
- the probability-adjusted expected value, not the headline number.

Present a range from conservative to aggressive, with the method for each. A single headline damages figure without a method is not an assessment.

### Compare Litigation Cost Against Expected Value

The decision to litigate is a financial decision under uncertainty. A meritorious claim can be uneconomic to pursue, and a weak defense can be cheaper to settle than to fight.

Compare:

- the expected recovery or savings, probability-adjusted;
- the estimated fees and costs through trial and appeal;
- the cost of discovery, experts, and e-discovery;
- the opportunity cost of management and employee time;
- the cost of settlement at various stages;
- the cost of doing nothing, including continued harm or accruing liability.

Identify the settlement value at which litigation becomes uneconomic. A case worth litigating at one damages level may not be worth litigating at half that level.

### Weigh Non-Monetary Consequences

Money is rarely the only variable. Litigation affects reputation, customer relationships, employee morale, regulatory exposure, precedent, competitors, and future disputes.

Assess:

- reputational harm from public filings, press, or disclosure;
- precedent risk if the outcome affects other claims or contracts;
- regulatory attention that litigation may attract;
- impact on customers, partners, and employees;
- distraction and loss of key personnel time;
- counterclaims or related suits that may follow;
- disclosure obligations to investors, auditors, or insurers.

A win on the merits can still be a loss if it sets bad precedent or triggers regulation. A settlement with no admission can still be costly if it invites copycat claims.

### Set A Defensible Settlement Range And Walk-Away

The assessment should produce a settlement range that the client can defend internally and use in negotiation. A range without a walk-away point is not actionable.

Define:

- the floor, the minimum acceptable outcome to settle;
- the target, the realistic negotiated outcome;
- the ceiling, the maximum acceptable outcome to settle;
- the walk-away, the point at which litigation is preferred;
- the BATNA, the best alternative to a negotiated agreement;
- the authority required to approve each level;
- the timing windows where settlement value changes, such as before filing, after discovery, or before trial.

Update the range as facts develop. A settlement range set at the start of a case and never revised is a sign of a stale assessment.

### Sequence The Decision And Identify Information Gates

A go/no-go decision is rarely final at the outset. Most cases have information gates where the assessment should be revisited.

Identify:

- what is known now versus what will be known after discovery;
- the cost and feasibility of obtaining key evidence;
- the dispositive motions available and their likelihood;
- the points at which the assessment should be revisited;
- the decisions that are reversible versus irreversible;
- the cost of delay, including limitation periods and accruing harm.

Recommend a staged approach where the case is reassessed after key milestones, rather than a single binary decision.

### Document Assumptions And Confidence

An assessment is only as good as its transparency about assumptions. Document what was assumed, what was verified, and what was guessed, so the decision-maker can weigh the recommendation.

Document:

- the facts relied upon and their source;
- the legal assumptions and governing law;
- the probability and damages methods;
- the confidence level in each component;
- the open questions that could change the recommendation;
- the date of the assessment and what has changed since.

An assessment that cannot be revisited because its assumptions were not recorded is a liability.

## Common Traps

### Producing An Advocacy Memo Instead Of An Assessment

A one-sided memo that maximizes strengths and minimizes weaknesses leads to over-litigation and surprise. Include the weaknesses explicitly.

### Reducing Uncertainty To A Single Number

A single probability figure is not defensible and hides the drivers of risk. Use ranges and identify what moves them.

### Using Headline Damages Without A Method

A large damages number without a method, probability adjustment, or range is advocacy, not assessment. Show the work.

### Ignoring Non-Monetary Consequences

Reputation, precedent, regulation, and distraction often outweigh money. A purely financial assessment misses the real decision.

### Setting A Settlement Range Without A Walk-Away

A range without a walk-away point and a BATNA does not support negotiation. Define when litigation is preferred.

### Never Revisiting The Assessment

Cases change after discovery, motions, and rulings. An assessment frozen at the outset becomes wrong. Reassess at information gates.

### Forgetting Cost-Shifting And Fee Exposure

In jurisdictions or contracts with fee-shifting, losing can mean paying the other side's fees. This can double or triple the real exposure.

## Self-Check

- Is each claim or defense mapped with elements, supporting and adverse facts, evidence, witnesses, governing law, burden of proof, and likely defenses and counterclaims?
- Is probability expressed as a range, broken down by element or phase, with key uncertainties and scenarios that move the range identified?
- Are damages modeled as a range with a defensible method, including compensatory, consequential, statutory, interest, fees, and non-monetary relief, with a probability-adjusted expected value?
- Is the expected value compared against litigation cost, including fees, discovery, experts, e-discovery, management time, and settlement at various stages?
- Are non-monetary consequences assessed, including reputation, precedent, regulation, customers, employees, distraction, counterclaims, and disclosure duties?
- Is there a settlement range with floor, target, ceiling, walk-away, BATNA, required authority, and timing windows?
- Are information gates identified where the assessment should be revisited, with reversible and irreversible decisions distinguished?
- Are assumptions, sources, legal basis, probability and damages methods, confidence levels, open questions, and assessment date documented?
- Does the recommendation escalate to qualified counsel and avoid asserting a guaranteed outcome in a specific jurisdiction?
