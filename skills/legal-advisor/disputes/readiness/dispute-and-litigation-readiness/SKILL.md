# Dispute And Litigation Readiness

Dispute readiness begins before a complaint is filed. The most important early tasks are understanding the dispute, preserving evidence, identifying deadlines, avoiding harmful admissions, protecting privilege, notifying the right stakeholders, and escalating to counsel when rights are at risk. Agents often focus on argument quality while missing the procedural steps that determine whether a party can defend itself later.

Use this skill before responding to a demand letter, organizing facts for counsel, preparing a litigation risk memo, handling a customer or vendor dispute, preserving records, reviewing a complaint, considering settlement, or planning arbitration, mediation, investigation, or civil litigation support. The goal is to help the agent organize and protect the matter without acting as litigation counsel.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Dispute Posture

The right action depends on where the matter sits in the dispute lifecycle.

Classify:

- early complaint or grievance;
- informal business dispute;
- demand letter;
- cease and desist letter;
- notice of breach;
- insurance claim;
- regulator inquiry;
- subpoena or civil investigative demand;
- arbitration notice;
- mediation;
- filed lawsuit;
- served complaint;
- discovery phase;
- settlement discussion;
- judgment or enforcement.

Do not treat all disputes like negotiations. A served lawsuit, subpoena, regulator inquiry, or court order may have immediate legal deadlines and consequences.

### Capture Parties, Claims, And Desired Relief

A dispute map should make clear who is alleging what, against whom, and what outcome they seek.

Document:

- claimant or plaintiff;
- respondent or defendant;
- related parties;
- counsel contacts;
- claims or allegations;
- contract clauses or laws cited;
- factual events;
- amount demanded;
- nonmonetary relief requested;
- deadlines stated;
- forum;
- governing law;
- insurance or indemnity possibilities;
- business relationship status.

Separate what the other side alleges from what is known, disputed, or unknown.

### Preserve Evidence Immediately

Once litigation, investigation, or formal dispute is reasonably anticipated, relevant records should be preserved. Routine deletion can become a major legal problem.

Preserve:

- contracts and amendments;
- emails;
- chat messages;
- tickets;
- invoices and payments;
- meeting notes;
- call recordings;
- logs;
- source files;
- system records;
- customer communications;
- marketing materials;
- security records;
- personnel records;
- physical evidence;
- mobile messages;
- third-party platform data.

Work with counsel or authorized leaders on a litigation hold when appropriate. Do not edit, delete, backdate, or "clean up" records after a dispute emerges.

### Protect Privilege And Communications

Disputes create communication risk. Casual messages can become evidence. Over-sharing legal analysis can waive privilege.

Use controlled channels for:

- attorney communications;
- legal analysis;
- settlement strategy;
- witness interviews;
- investigation notes;
- expert work;
- insurance communications;
- board reporting.

Limit distribution to people who need to know. Marking something privileged does not make it privileged by itself; the communication must fit the legal requirements. Avoid mixing legal advice with broad business commentary in public channels.

### Check Deadlines And Required Responses

Deadlines should be identified before merits analysis. Missing a response deadline can cause default, waiver, sanctions, lost rights, or adverse inference.

Check:

- service date;
- answer deadline;
- motion deadline;
- cure period;
- contractual notice deadline;
- preservation deadline;
- subpoena response date;
- agency response date;
- arbitration response date;
- mediation date;
- discovery deadline;
- appeal deadline;
- insurance notice deadline.

If the deadline is uncertain, treat it as urgent and escalate to counsel. Calendar the earliest plausible deadline until confirmed.

### Assess Forum And Procedure

Civil disputes may proceed through court, arbitration, mediation, administrative agency, small claims, regulatory enforcement, or contractual escalation. Procedure shapes strategy, cost, evidence, and timing.

Identify:

- court or agency;
- arbitration provider;
- mediation requirement;
- contract escalation process;
- venue and jurisdiction;
- judge or administrator if known;
- procedural rules;
- local rules;
- filing requirements;
- discovery availability;
- confidentiality;
- fee shifting;
- appeal rights.

For U.S. federal civil cases, the process commonly includes complaint, service, case preparation, discovery, motions, settlement efforts, and possible trial. State and arbitration procedures can differ.

### Build A Merits And Risk Matrix

A useful dispute memo separates legal theory, facts, evidence, damages, defenses, counterclaims, and business risk.

For each issue, capture:

- claim element or contract requirement;
- supporting facts;
- adverse facts;
- evidence available;
- missing evidence;
- witness;
- damages exposure;
- injunction or nonmonetary risk;
- defenses;
- counterclaims;
- settlement leverage;
- business impact;
- uncertainty.

Do not write a one-sided advocacy memo if the purpose is risk assessment. Include weaknesses.

### Consider Insurance, Indemnity, And Notice

Disputes may trigger insurance coverage, indemnity rights, defense obligations, customer notice, vendor notice, regulator notice, or board reporting.

Review:

- insurance policies;
- tender deadlines;
- notice requirements;
- defense control;
- consent before settlement;
- indemnity clauses;
- limitation of liability;
- contractual notice method;
- third-party claim procedure;
- board or investor reporting;
- regulator or agency notification.

Late notice can affect coverage or rights. Escalate quickly.

### Approach Settlement Deliberately

Settlement can reduce cost and risk, but it can also create admissions, confidentiality issues, tax consequences, regulatory obligations, precedent, or insurance problems.

Before settlement discussions, identify:

- authority to settle;
- minimum and target terms;
- payment timing;
- release scope;
- confidentiality;
- non-disparagement;
- no admission language;
- tax reporting;
- ongoing relationship;
- compliance commitments;
- dismissal procedure;
- enforcement mechanism;
- insurance consent;
- board or stakeholder approval.

Do not promise settlement terms without authority.

## Common Traps

### Responding Before Preserving Evidence

The first move should often be preservation and escalation, not argument drafting. Evidence loss can harm the case more than a weak first draft.

### Missing Service Or Response Deadlines

A demand letter and a served complaint are different. Confirm whether formal service occurred and what deadline it creates.

### Making Casual Admissions

Apologies, explanations, edits, or internal blame messages can become evidence. Communicate carefully and route sensitive messages through counsel.

### Over-Sharing Legal Strategy

Broad internal distribution can create waiver and discovery risk. Keep legal strategy on need-to-know channels.

### Ignoring Insurance And Indemnity

Coverage and defense rights can be lost through late notice, unauthorized settlement, or failure to tender.

### Treating Settlement As Purely Financial

Settlement terms can affect confidentiality, licenses, product changes, tax reporting, future claims, customer communications, and compliance obligations.

### Relying Only On The Other Side's Timeline

The other side may omit or misstate deadlines. Verify through rules, contract, court docket, agency notice, or counsel.

## Self-Check

- Is the dispute posture identified as informal complaint, demand, notice, investigation, subpoena, arbitration, mediation, filed lawsuit, discovery, settlement, or judgment?
- Are parties, claims, allegations, forum, law cited, demanded relief, deadlines, counsel, insurance, and business relationship captured?
- Are relevant records preserved across email, chat, systems, contracts, invoices, logs, physical evidence, and third-party platforms?
- Are privilege, work product, legal analysis, witness interviews, settlement strategy, and sensitive communications restricted appropriately?
- Are service, response, cure, subpoena, agency, arbitration, discovery, appeal, and insurance notice deadlines checked and calendared?
- Is forum, venue, procedure, local rule, arbitration, mediation, discovery, confidentiality, fee shifting, and appeal posture understood?
- Does the merits matrix include supporting facts, adverse facts, evidence, missing evidence, damages, defenses, counterclaims, and uncertainty?
- Are insurance, indemnity, defense control, notice method, limitation of liability, and consent-before-settlement obligations reviewed?
- Are settlement authority, release scope, confidentiality, no-admission language, payment, tax, dismissal, and enforcement terms considered?
- Does the output escalate to qualified counsel when rights, deadlines, litigation, regulator inquiry, subpoena, or material exposure is present?
