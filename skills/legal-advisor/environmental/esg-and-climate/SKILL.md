# ESG And Climate

ESG (environmental, social, and governance) and climate have moved from voluntary aspiration to a regulated disclosure and liability landscape. Companies face mandatory climate disclosure, carbon pricing and trading, greenwashing enforcement by regulators and competitors, and investor and stakeholder claims tied to sustainability commitments. Agents often treat ESG statements as marketing, missing that a net-zero target, a carbon-neutral claim, or a sustainability report can create securities, consumer protection, competition, and misrepresentation exposure if it is not substantiated and aligned with the company's actual performance. The landscape is also fragmented across jurisdictions and changing rapidly.

Use this skill before advising on ESG disclosure, climate commitments, green claims, sustainability reporting, or investor and stakeholder ESG communications. The goal is to force the agent to identify the disclosure duties, substantiate the claims, and manage the misrepresentation and regulatory risk before publishing. ESG and climate law is jurisdiction-specific and evolving; any conclusion must be confirmed against the applicable disclosure regime, regulator guidance, and the company's actual data.

## Core Rules

### Identify Mandatory ESG And Climate Disclosure Duties

Disclosure is increasingly mandatory.

Map:

- mandatory climate and sustainability reporting (EU CSRD, CSDDD, taxonomy; SEC climate rules where in force; UK and other national regimes);
- scope 1, 2, and 3 greenhouse gas reporting and assurance;
- financial and material climate risk disclosure in securities filings;
- taxonomy and classification of sustainable activities;
- supply chain due diligence (forced labor, deforestation, conflict minerals);
- board and governance disclosure on climate oversight.

A statement in a sustainability report can be a material representation in a securities filing. Align the two and ensure consistency.

### Substantiate Green And Sustainability Claims

Greenwashing is a top enforcement priority.

For each claim:

- the exact claim text (express and implied);
- the substantiation required (lifecycle analysis, third-party certification, science-based targets);
- the scope and limitations (which product, geography, timeframe);
- the clarity and prominence of qualifications;
- comparatives ("greener," "cleanest") and their basis;
- the treatment of future commitments and net-zero targets;
- carbon offset and credit claims and their integrity.

A claim must be substantiated for the specific product and benefit. Vague terms ("eco-friendly," "sustainable," "green") without substantiation are high-risk.

### Manage Net-Zero, Targets, And Transition Plans

Commitments create forward-looking exposure.

Assess:

- the basis for the target (science-based, absolute, intensity);
- the pathway and milestones;
- reliance on offsets, carbon capture, and unproven technologies;
- the disclosure of assumptions and risks;
- the governance and accountability for delivery;
- the effect of missing targets on securities, consumer, and competition claims.

A net-zero target presented as achievable when the company has no credible pathway is a misrepresentation risk. Disclose assumptions and uncertainties.

### Address Carbon Pricing, Trading, And Border Measures

Carbon regulation affects operations and trade.

Consider:

- emissions trading systems (EU ETS, UK ETS, California, China, and others) and allowance obligations;
- carbon taxes and their interaction with trading systems;
- carbon border adjustment mechanisms (CBAM) and reporting on imports;
- voluntary carbon markets and the integrity of credits;
- the treatment of internal carbon pricing in decisions and disclosure.

Carbon costs are rising and border measures extend them to imports. Build carbon into operational and supply chain decisions.

### Handle Investor, Securities, And Fiduciary Exposure

ESG statements reach investors.

Map:

- material misrepresentation in offering documents and periodic reports;
- the duty to disclose material climate risk;
- green securities and ESG fund labeling (EU SFDR, fund names rules);
- fiduciary duties of directors and investment managers on climate;
- shareholder proposals and derivative claims;
- the effect of inconsistent statements across audiences.

Inconsistent ESG statements across sustainability reports, securities filings, and marketing are a frequent enforcement and litigation target. Ensure consistency.

### Manage Competition And Consumer Risk

Greenwashing is enforced by multiple routes.

Assess:

- consumer protection enforcement (FTC Green Guides, EU empowers consumer directive, UK CMA, national regulators);
- competition law claims by competitors (false advertising, unfair competition);
- class action exposure for consumers;
- the role of advertising standards bodies;
- cross-border enforcement cooperation.

A claim lawful in one jurisdiction may be challenged in another. Assess exposure in all markets where the claim is published.

### Coordinate Supply Chain And Human Rights Due Diligence

ESG extends through the supply chain.

Address:

- forced labor and modern slavery due diligence and reporting;
- deforestation-free supply chain rules;
- conflict minerals and responsible sourcing;
- supply chain emissions (scope 3) reporting;
- the effect on import and customs (forced labor import bans).

Supply chain ESG duties are expanding and carry import and customs consequences. Build due diligence into procurement.

### Align Governance, Assurance, And Internal Controls

ESG reporting needs the rigor of financial reporting.

Build:

- board and committee oversight of ESG and climate;
- internal controls and data systems for ESG metrics;
- assurance (limited and reasonable) by independent providers;
- the role of the audit committee and external auditor;
- consistency between ESG and financial reporting;
- document retention and audit trails.

Investors and regulators increasingly expect assured ESG data. Build the controls and assurance before publishing.

## Common Traps

### Treating ESG As Marketing

ESG statements can create securities, consumer, and competition exposure. Substantiate and align them.

### Vague Green Claims Without Substantiation

"Eco-friendly" and "sustainable" without substantiation are high-risk. Specify the benefit and evidence.

### Net-Zero Without A Credible Pathway

A target without a pathway is a misrepresentation risk. Disclose assumptions and uncertainties.

### Inconsistent Statements Across Audiences

Inconsistent ESG statements are an enforcement and litigation target. Ensure consistency.

### Ignoring Border Measures

CBAM and similar measures extend carbon costs to imports. Build them into supply chain decisions.

### Overstating Offset Integrity

Offsets of questionable integrity undermine the claim. Verify the credits.

### Missing Mandatory Disclosure

CSRD, CSDDD, SFDR, SEC, and national regimes impose mandatory disclosure. Map the duties.

### ESG Data Without Controls

Unassured ESG data invites challenge. Build controls and assurance.

## Self-Check

- Are mandatory ESG and climate disclosure duties (CSRD, CSDDD, SFDR, SEC, taxonomy, scope 1-3) mapped and aligned with securities filings?
- Is each green and sustainability claim substantiated for the specific product, benefit, scope, and timeframe?
- Are net-zero and transition targets backed by a credible pathway, with assumptions and uncertainties disclosed?
- Are carbon pricing, trading, border measures (CBAM), and internal carbon pricing built into decisions and disclosure?
- Are investor, securities, fiduciary, fund labeling, and shareholder exposure assessed for inconsistent statements?
- Are consumer protection, competition, class action, and advertising standards risks assessed across markets?
- Are supply chain due diligence (forced labor, deforestation, conflict minerals, scope 3) and import consequences addressed?
- Are board oversight, internal controls, data systems, and assurance built to financial reporting rigor?
- Are offsets and credits verified for integrity before reliance?
- Does the output recommend confirmation against the applicable disclosure regime and regulator guidance by qualified counsel?
