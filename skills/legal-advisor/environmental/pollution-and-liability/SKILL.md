# Pollution And Liability

Environmental liability is distinctive because it can attach without fault, reach parties who did not cause the contamination, and persist across decades and ownership changes. A company that buys contaminated land, a successor to a long-closed plant, or a transporter of waste can all face cleanup costs that dwarf the transaction value. Agents often assume that liability follows causation or that a seller's indemnity solves the problem, missing the strict, joint and several, and retroactive nature of many regimes, the reach to current owners and operators, and the natural resource and personal injury exposures that indemnities do not cover. Environmental liability is also heavily site- and substance-specific.

Use this skill before advising on contaminated property, a transaction involving industrial or waste sites, a cleanup obligation, or an environmental claim. The goal is to force the agent to identify the regime, the potentially responsible parties, the site conditions, and the insurance and indemnity landscape before concluding. Environmental liability law is jurisdiction- and site-specific; any conclusion must be confirmed against the applicable statute, regulator, and site data.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Applicable Liability Regime

Different regimes impose different liability structures.

Map:

- comprehensive strict liability regimes for contaminated sites (CERCLA/Superfund in the US, equivalent regimes elsewhere);
- strict liability for hazardous activities and waste under civil law and EU directives;
- fault-based pollution and nuisance regimes;
- sector-specific regimes (oil spills, mining, chemicals, waste);
- natural resource damages and public trust doctrines;
- criminal environmental liability for knowing violations.

A site can be subject to multiple regimes simultaneously. Identify each and its liability structure.

### Determine The Potentially Responsible Parties

Environmental liability reaches a wide set of parties.

Identify:

- current owners and operators, regardless of fault;
- former owners and operators at the time of disposal or contamination;
- generators and arrangers of waste;
- transporters and selected disposal facilities;
- successors and asset purchasers (with exceptions like the bona fide prospective purchaser);
- lenders and fiduciaries (with exemptions);
- tenants and subtenants.

Current owners can be liable for contamination they did not cause. Trace the chain of ownership and operation.

### Apply Strict, Joint And Several, And Retroactive Liability

Many regimes impose liability without fault and across multiple parties.

Assess:

- strict liability (no fault required);
- joint and several liability (any responsible party can be held for the full cleanup);
- retroactive liability for contamination that occurred before the statute;
- the ability to seek contribution from other responsible parties;
- the effect of settlements and covenants not to sue;
- defenses and exemptions (innocent landowner, bona fide prospective purchaser, contiguous property owner, third-party defense, act of God).

Joint and several liability means a party with deep pockets can pay the entire cleanup and then pursue contribution. Assess exposure on a worst-case basis.

### Characterize The Site And The Contamination

Liability turns on site conditions.

Investigate:

- the history of the site and its uses;
- the contaminants present and their sources;
- the extent and migration of contamination (soil, groundwater, vapor, sediment);
- pathways to receptors (drinking water, ecological, human);
- the applicable cleanup standards and the required remedy;
- the estimated cost and timeline of remediation.

A Phase I and Phase II environmental site assessment is the baseline. Do not rely on representations without investigation.

### Address Natural Resource Damages And Personal Injury

Cleanup is not the only exposure.

Consider:

- natural resource damages for injury to public resources (wildlife, habitat, water, air);
- trustee claims and the assessment of restoration, diminution, and lost use;
- personal injury and property damage tort claims by exposed individuals;
- medical monitoring and class action exposure;
- punitive damages and criminal liability for knowing violations.

Natural resource damages can be substantial and are often separate from cleanup costs. Assess them distinctly.

### Manage Contribution, Indemnity, And Settlement

Responsible parties pursue each other for contribution.

Build:

- contribution claims against other responsible parties;
- contractual indemnities from sellers, predecessors, and waste generators;
- insurance coverage (historic general liability, environmental impairment liability, pollution legal liability);
- the effect of prior settlements and covenants not to sue;
- the allocation of cleanup costs among parties.

An indemnity is only as good as the indemnitor's solvency. Combine indemnity with insurance and contribution claims.

### Evaluate Insurance And Risk Transfer

Environmental insurance can fill gaps.

Assess:

- historic comprehensive general liability policies and the pollution exclusion;
- environmental impairment liability and pollution legal liability policies;
- cleanup cost caps and stop-loss policies;
- the scope of coverage (on-site, off-site, third-party, transportation);
- the effect of notice and the duty to defend;
- lender and transactional insurance.

Historic policies may predate the absolute pollution exclusion and can be valuable assets. Identify and preserve them.

### Coordinate Cleanup With The Regulator And Community

Cleanup is a regulatory and community process.

Manage:

- the selection of the remedy and regulator approval;
- public comment and community engagement;
- long-term monitoring and operation and maintenance;
- institutional and engineering controls (deed restrictions, caps, monitoring);
- five-year reviews and reopener risk;
- the effect of property transfer on cleanup obligations.

A cleanup is not necessarily final. Reopeners, reviews, and changing standards can extend obligations for decades.

## Common Traps

### Assuming Liability Follows Causation

Current owners and operators can be liable without fault. Trace the chain.

### Relying On A Seller Indemnity

An indemnity is only as good as the indemnitor's solvency. Combine with insurance and contribution.

### Skipping Site Investigation

Representations without a Phase I and II are insufficient. Investigate.

### Forgetting Natural Resource Damages

These are separate from cleanup and can be substantial. Assess distinctly.

### Joint And Several Exposure Underestimated

A deep-pocket party can pay the full cleanup. Assess worst-case.

### Missing Historic Insurance

Pre-exclusion policies can be valuable assets. Identify and preserve them.

### Treating Cleanup As Final

Reopeners, reviews, and changing standards extend obligations. Plan for the long term.

### Overlooking Criminal Exposure

Knowing violations carry criminal liability for individuals. Build compliance.

## Self-Check

- Is the applicable liability regime (strict, fault-based, sector-specific, natural resource, criminal) identified?
- Are all potentially responsible parties (current and former owners, operators, generators, transporters, successors) mapped?
- Is strict, joint and several, and retroactive liability applied, with defenses and exemptions assessed?
- Is the site characterized (history, contaminants, extent, pathways, standards, remedy, cost)?
- Are natural resource damages, personal injury, medical monitoring, and punitive and criminal exposure assessed?
- Are contribution, indemnity, settlement, and cost allocation structured?
- Is insurance (historic general liability, environmental, cost cap) identified and preserved?
- Is cleanup coordinated with the regulator, community, monitoring, controls, and reviews?
- Is the worst-case joint and several exposure assessed, not just proportional share?
- Does the output recommend confirmation against the applicable statute, regulator, and site data by qualified counsel?
