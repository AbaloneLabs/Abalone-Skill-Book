# Conditions Precedent And Closing

A condition precedent is a promise about the future that determines whether obligations ever arise. It is not a covenant to perform, and confusing the two is one of the most expensive drafting errors in transactional law. A party that treats a condition as a covenant may sue for damages when the right remedy was termination; a party that treats a covenant as a condition may lose a deal for a curable breach. Closing conditions in acquisitions, financings, and complex commercial contracts are where this distinction does its heaviest work, because they decide whether billions of dollars move, whether representations survive, and whether a party can walk away without liability after months of work. Agents often draft conditions vaguely, omit material ones, or fail to build the operational machinery (long-stop dates, bring-downs, escrows) that makes conditions enforceable and the closing manageable.

Use this skill before drafting closing conditions, before agreeing to a condition in a term sheet or commitment letter, before preparing closing checklists and signature pages, or before advising a party on whether an unmet condition permits walk-away or triggers damages. The goal is to force the agent to distinguish conditions from covenants, draft conditions that are objective and verifiable, build the closing machinery that supports them, and identify when a failed condition creates a right to terminate versus a claim for breach. Condition analysis is jurisdiction-specific and often turns on subtle common-law distinctions; material conditions in high-value transactions require qualified counsel review.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Conditions Precedent From Covenants And Promises

The single most important distinction is between a condition (which must occur before a duty arises) and a covenant (which is a promise to do something, breach of which gives a damages claim).

Classify each operative provision:

- condition precedent: the duty to close or perform arises only if the condition is satisfied;
- condition subsequent: an existing duty terminates if the condition occurs;
- covenant: an independent promise to act, breach of which is a claim for damages, not a failure of the condition;
- representation: a statement of present or past fact, breach of which may give rise to misrepresentation claims and, if repeated at closing, failure of condition.

Mislabeling a covenant as a condition can let a party walk away from a curable obligation; mislabeling a condition as a covenant can force a party to perform when the precondition was never met. Use language that makes the classification unmistakable.

### Draft Conditions To Be Objective And Verifiable

A condition that depends on subjective satisfaction or vague standards invites dispute at closing.

For each condition, ensure:

- it states a factual event that can be confirmed (regulatory approval obtained, representations true in all material respects, no material adverse effect, consents received);
- subjective conditions, where used, specify whose satisfaction controls and whether it must be reasonable;
- "material" qualifiers are defined or used consistently with the definitions section;
- the evidence of satisfaction is specified (certificate, opinion, filing receipt, officer certificate);
- the party bearing the risk of non-occurrence is clear.

Avoid conditions like "buyer is satisfied with the business," which are unenforceable in many jurisdictions unless tied to an objective standard or reasonable-satisfaction test.

### Build The Standard Closing Condition Set For Transactions

Acquisition, financing, and investment closings share a recognizable condition architecture. Confirm the standard set is present and complete.

Typical closing conditions include:

- accuracy of representations and warranties when made and as of closing (bring-down);
- performance and compliance with covenants and required pre-closing actions;
- absence of material adverse change or effect since a reference date;
- receipt of required regulatory and third-party consents and approvals;
- absence of legal restraints, injunctions, or laws prohibiting the transaction;
- delivery of officer certificates, legal opinions, and closing documents;
- no material litigation or governmental proceeding threatening the business;
- completion of any required pre-closing reorganization or restructuring.

Omitting a condition that a party needs (for example, a key regulatory approval) can force a closing the party cannot legally complete.

### Establish Bring-Down, Opinions, And Closing Certificates

Conditions often require that representations remain true at closing. The operational device is the bring-down certificate and legal opinion.

Confirm:

- representations are repeated as of closing or a specified date, with the appropriate materiality scrape or qualifiers;
- an officer's certificate certifies compliance with covenants and the truth of representations;
- legal opinions cover enforceability, authority, no conflict, and required consents, with assumptions and qualifications clearly scoped;
- the scope of opinions matches the negotiated term sheet and the assumptions are acceptable;
- the timing of opinion delivery and reliance is workable.

A bring-down that is missing or qualified at closing can itself fail a condition and justify walk-away.

### Set Long-Stop Dates And Termination Rights

Conditions that may never be satisfied need an outside date so the parties are not trapped indefinitely.

Address:

- a long-stop or outside date by which closing must occur;
- extensions, who can exercise them, and for how long;
- the consequences of missing the long-stop date (automatic termination, termination by either party, termination by one party only);
- whether regulatory conditions permit unilateral extension if the parties are pursuing approvals in good faith;
- break fees, expense reimbursement, or reverse break fees tied to a failed closing.

A deal without a workable long-stop date can leave a party committed to a transaction that never closes, with no clean exit.

### Manage Escrow, Holdback, And Post-Closing Conditions

Not all conditions resolve at signing or closing. Some risk is deferred through escrows, holdbacks, and post-closing true-ups.

Consider:

- escrow or holdback of part of the purchase price pending resolution of identified risks;
- earnouts tied to post-closing performance, with clear measurement and dispute mechanics;
- post-closing purchase price adjustments based on working capital, net debt, or closing accounts;
- milestone payments tied to regulatory or commercial events;
- the escrow agent agreement, release conditions, and dispute resolution;
- tax treatment of escrows and holdbacks.

These mechanisms convert uncertain conditions into manageable risk allocation, but they create their own disputes if measurement and release are not precisely defined.

### Identify When A Failed Condition Permits Walk-Away Versus Breach

The remedy for an unmet condition depends on why it failed.

Determine:

- if a condition fails through no fault of either party, typically either party may terminate without liability;
- if a condition fails because a party breached a covenant to use efforts to satisfy it, the other party may have a damages claim and, in some jurisdictions, specific performance;
- if a party intentionally frustrated a condition, courts may excuse the condition or deem it satisfied (the prevention doctrine);
- if a party waived a condition, it cannot later rely on its failure;
- whether the agreement's termination provisions govern or default law applies.

The prevention and waiver doctrines can defeat a party that tries to engineer a condition failure to escape the deal.

## Common Traps

### Confusing Conditions With Covenants

The most common and most expensive error. A condition permits walk-away; a covenant gives damages. Label and draft each clearly.

### Drafting Subjective Conditions Without A Reasonableness Standard

"Buyer satisfaction" without an objective or reasonable-satisfaction test is often unenforceable or construed narrowly.

### Omitting A Required Regulatory Or Consent Condition

Forgetting a necessary approval can force a closing the parties cannot legally complete and create regulatory liability.

### Missing Or Unrealistic Long-Stop Dates

A deal with no outside date, or one too short for regulatory review, traps parties or collapses prematurely.

### Bring-Down Gaps At Closing

Representations not repeated as of closing, or a qualified officer certificate, can fail a condition unexpectedly.

### Materiality Scrape Inconsistency

Applying or omitting a materiality scrape inconsistently between representations, conditions, and indemnities changes the risk allocation the parties intended.

### Ignoring Prevention And Waiver Doctrines

A party that blocks or waives a condition cannot later rely on its failure. Conduct at closing matters as much as the text.

## Self-Check

- Is each operative provision classified as condition precedent, condition subsequent, covenant, or representation, and drafted with unmistakable classification language?
- Are conditions objective and verifiable, with subjective conditions tied to a reasonable-satisfaction or objective standard and specified evidence of satisfaction?
- Does the transaction include the standard closing condition set (bring-down, covenant compliance, no MAC, regulatory and third-party consents, no legal restraint, opinions, no material litigation)?
- Are bring-down certificates, legal opinions, and officer certificates scoped to match the negotiated terms, with acceptable assumptions and workable delivery timing?
- Is there a long-stop date with clear extension, termination, break-fee, and expense provisions so parties are not trapped indefinitely?
- Are escrow, holdback, earnout, working capital adjustment, and milestone mechanisms defined with precise measurement, release, dispute, and tax treatment?
- Is the remedy for a failed condition mapped, including walk-away, damages, specific performance, prevention, and waiver, under the governing law?
- Does the output flag material condition questions for qualified counsel review rather than asserting enforceability of the closing structure?
