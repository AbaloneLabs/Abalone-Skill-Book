# Clause Design And Structure

Contract drafting is engineering with language. The judgment problem is that a clause is not just a paragraph of words; it is a logical structure where definitions, conditions, obligations, exceptions, and cross-references must fit together without ambiguity or contradiction. Agents often produce clauses that read smoothly in isolation but fail structurally: a defined term used before it is defined, a condition that contradicts an obligation, a cross-reference that points to the wrong section, an exception that swallows the rule, or a nested proviso so deep that no one can parse it. These failures surface only when the contract is disputed, at which point ambiguity is interpreted against the drafter and a single structural mistake can reverse the outcome.

Use this skill before drafting, restructuring, or revising the structure of any contract clause or section. The goal is to make the agent design clauses as logical structures, order sections for coherence, define terms precisely, express conditions and obligations cleanly, prevent contradictions, manage cross-references, and prioritize readability. This is drafting support; final contracts, especially for high-value or regulated transactions, require review and approval by qualified counsel.

## Core Rules

### Design Each Clause As A Logical Structure

Before writing prose, identify the structural role of the clause: is it a definition, a representation, a covenant, a condition, a permission, a prohibition, a remedy, or a procedural rule. Each role has a standard internal structure. A covenant states who must do what, by when, and with what consequence. A condition states what must be true for an obligation to arise. A representation states a fact as of a point in time. Drafting a covenant as if it were a rep, or a condition as if it were a promise, changes the remedies and the meaning. Know the role, then apply the matching structure.

### Order Sections For Coherent Reading

Contract order is not arbitrary. A reader needs context before detail. The conventional flow moves from parties and recitals, to definitions, to operative obligations, to conditions, to reps and warranties, to term and termination, to risk allocation, to boilerplate, to signatures. Definitions should precede the sections that use them. Conditions should appear before or alongside the obligations they gate. Reordering for the reader's logic prevents the confusion that breeds disputes. A contract that defines a term in section 12 and uses it in section 3 forces the reader to jump.

### Define Terms Precisely And Use Them Consistently

Definitions are the load-bearing structure of a contract. Define every term that carries legal or commercial weight, define it once, and use it consistently throughout. Watch for terms that are defined but then used with a different meaning, and for ordinary words used in a special sense without definition. Check that defined terms are neither too broad nor too narrow for their use, because an over-broad definition can sweep in unintended conduct and an over-narrow one can exclude intended conduct. Use a definitions section and capitalize defined terms consistently to signal their special meaning.

### Express Conditions And Obligations Cleanly

Conditions and obligations look similar but operate differently, and confusing them changes the contract. A condition uses words like "if," "provided that," "unless," and "subject to," and determines whether an obligation arises. An obligation uses "shall," "must," or "will" to impose a duty. A condition phrased as an obligation, or vice versa, can change who bears the risk of non-occurrence. Use "shall" for obligations, "may" for permissions, "will" for future facts, and condition language only for conditions. Mixing "shall" for permissions or conditions is a classic source of ambiguity.

### Prevent Internal Contradictions

A contract must not say two inconsistent things. As you add or revise clauses, check them against every other clause that touches the same subject. Common contradictions arise between the body and a schedule, between a general clause and a specific exception, between an obligation and a force majeure or excuse clause, and between the order of precedence and the actual text. Resolve contradictions explicitly, either by narrowing one clause or by stating which controls. A contract that contradicts itself invites litigation over which provision wins.

### Manage Cross-References Accurately

Cross-references are efficient but fragile. Every cross-reference should point to the correct section, use a stable reference such as a section number and title rather than "above" or "below," and be updated whenever sections are renumbered. Check that referenced sections actually exist and say what is claimed. Circular references, where two sections point to each other without resolving the question, create interpretive dead ends. Maintain a reference map during drafting and verify it before finalizing.

### Avoid Over-Nesting And Dense Provisos

Nested exceptions, multiple "provided that" clauses, and long sentences with embedded conditions become unreadable and ambiguous. When a clause accumulates exceptions, break it into numbered subsections or a list, each stating a single rule. Prefer short, declarative sentences. If a sentence runs beyond a few lines or contains more than one conditional, restructure it. Readability is not cosmetic; a clause a court cannot parse will be interpreted against the drafter.

### Use Numbering, Headings, And Formatting To Aid Navigation

Numbering, section headings, and consistent formatting let the reader navigate and let other documents reference specific clauses. Use a hierarchical numbering scheme, descriptive headings that state the section's purpose, and parallel structure across similar sections. Avoid unnumbered paragraphs floating in a section, because they are hard to reference and easy to overlook. Treat the contract as a machine whose parts must be locatable and addressable.

### Reconcile Plain Language With Precision

Plain language aids understanding, but precision must not be sacrificed. Avoid archaic redundancy like "null and void" or "cease and desist" where a single word suffices, but do not strip out terms of art that carry established legal meaning. The goal is clarity without loss of precision. Test a clause by asking whether a non-lawyer reader could understand what they must do, and whether a court could apply it without guessing.

## Common Traps

### Using A Defined Term Before Or Without Definition

Undefined or forward-referenced terms create ambiguity. Define first, use consistently.

### Phrasing A Condition As An Obligation

"If" versus "shall" changes who bears the risk. Use condition language for conditions and "shall" for duties.

### Internal Contradictions Between Body And Schedule

Schedules often override or supplement the body. Reconcile them explicitly.

### Broken Or Circular Cross-References

Renumbering breaks references, and circular references create dead ends. Maintain a reference map.

### Over-Nested Provisos

Deep "provided that" chains become unparseable. Break them into subsections.

### Headings That Do Not Match Content

Misleading headings misdirect the reader and the court. Keep headings descriptive and current.

### Sacrificing Precision For Plain Language

Removing terms of art can change meaning. Keep clarity without losing legal precision.

## Self-Check

- Is each clause designed for its structural role as definition, rep, covenant, condition, permission, prohibition, or remedy?
- Are sections ordered so definitions precede use, conditions precede or accompany obligations, and the reader gains context before detail?
- Are all defined terms defined once, used consistently, and neither too broad nor too narrow for their purpose?
- Are conditions expressed with "if," "provided that," "unless," and "subject to," and obligations with "shall" or "must," without mixing the functions?
- Are internal contradictions between body, schedules, general clauses, and exceptions identified and resolved?
- Are cross-references accurate, stable, non-circular, and verified against existing sections?
- Are nested exceptions and dense provisos broken into readable subsections with short declarative sentences?
- Does numbering, headings, and formatting support navigation and referenceability?
- Is plain language balanced with preservation of legal terms of art and precision?
- Does the output flag that final contracts, especially high-value or regulated ones, require review and approval by qualified counsel?
