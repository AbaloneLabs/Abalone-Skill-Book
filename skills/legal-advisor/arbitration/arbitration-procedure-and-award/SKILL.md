# Arbitration Procedure And Award

Arbitration procedure is more flexible than court litigation, which is both its strength and its danger. The tribunal, with the parties' input, shapes the procedure, and the choices made at the outset, on document production, witness evidence, hearing format, and the issues to be decided, drive cost, speed, and outcome. Agents often assume arbitration is simpler than litigation, when in fact poorly managed procedure can make arbitration as expensive and slow as court, with the added disadvantage of limited appeal rights. The award is the culmination, and its quality, reasoning, and enforceability determine whether the process delivers value.

Judicial intervention in arbitration is limited but consequential. Courts may assist with interim measures, compel arbitration, appoint arbitrators, and ultimately enforce or set aside the award, but only within defined boundaries. Use this skill before participating in an arbitration, before structuring submissions and evidence, before preparing for the hearing, before drafting or reviewing an award, or before seeking enforcement or challenge. The goal is to help the agent manage arbitration procedure and the award strategically, not to provide legal advice, which requires qualified counsel familiar with the institution, seat, and governing law.

## Core Rules

### Confirm Jurisdiction And Resolve Gateway Disputes Early

The tribunal's jurisdiction is often the first battleground. Challenges to the existence, validity, or scope of the arbitration agreement must be resolved before the merits.

Address:

- whether a valid arbitration agreement exists;
- whether the tribunal has jurisdiction over the parties and the dispute;
- whether the dispute falls within the clause scope;
- the principle of competence-competence, allowing the tribunal to rule on its own jurisdiction;
- the interaction with court proceedings on the same gateway issue;
- the timing of jurisdiction challenges, usually at the outset;
- the consequences of participating without reserving jurisdiction objections.

A party that participates in the merits without reserving a jurisdiction objection may waive it. Raise gateway issues at the first opportunity.

### Structure The Statement Of Claim And Defense Around Issues

Arbitration submissions should be focused and issue-driven, not sprawling. The statement of claim and statement of defense define the dispute for the tribunal.

Structure:

- the factual background necessary for context;
- the legal claims or defenses with elements;
- the relief sought, monetary and non-monetary;
- the key documents relied upon;
- the witnesses and experts to be called;
- the quantum and its method;
- the issues that require tribunal decision.

Overbroad submissions increase cost and dilute focus. Identify the dispositive issues and concentrate the case there.

### Manage Document Production Proportionately

Document production in arbitration is typically narrower than U.S.-style discovery but broader than some civil law systems. The tribunal controls scope based on relevance and proportionality.

Manage production:

- the requests tied to specific issues and documents;
- the standard of relevance and materiality;
- the proportionality to the amount in dispute;
- the handling of privilege and confidentiality;
- redactions and protective orders;
- the production format and metadata;
- the consequences of non-production.

Document production is often the largest cost in arbitration. Target requests to the issues and resist overbroad demands with proportionality arguments.

### Prepare Witness And Expert Evidence Effectively

Witness evidence in arbitration is often presented through written statements followed by examination at the hearing. Experts address technical or valuation issues.

Prepare:

- the fact witnesses with direct knowledge of key events;
- written witness statements that address the elements;
- the examination strategy for the hearing;
- expert reports on liability and quantum;
- the independence and qualifications of experts;
- the tribunal's procedures for hot-tubbing or concurrent expert evidence;
- impeachment material for opposing witnesses.

Witness statements should be the witness's own account, not counsel's argument. Experts must be independent and transparent about methodology.

### Plan The Hearing Format And Issues To Be Decided

The hearing is the tribunal's main opportunity to assess evidence and argument. Its format should serve efficient decision-making.

Plan:

- the issues to be addressed and their sequence;
- the witnesses and experts to be heard;
- the time estimates for each party;
- the documentary evidence and demonstratives;
- the procedure for examination and cross-examination;
- the post-hearing briefs and submissions;
- the recording and transcript arrangements.

A focused hearing on the dispositive issues is more persuasive and cost-effective than an exhaustive traversal of every fact.

### Address Interim And Conservatory Measures When Needed

Parties may need interim relief before or during the arbitration. The tribunal and the courts have roles.

Address:

- interim measures from the tribunal, such as preservation of assets or evidence;
- emergency arbitrator relief before the tribunal is constituted;
- court interim measures in aid of arbitration;
- the standard for interim relief, usually a serious question and risk of harm;
- the enforceability of interim measures across jurisdictions;
- security for costs where appropriate.

Interim measures can preserve the status quo but require showing urgency and a risk that the award will be frustrated.

### Scrutinize The Award For Reasoning, Form, And Finality

The award must resolve the issues submitted, state reasons where required, and be in the form prescribed by the institution and the seat. Defects can ground setting-aside or resistance to enforcement.

Scrutinize:

- whether all submitted claims and issues are decided;
- whether the award states reasons sufficient for review and enforcement;
- whether the award is within the tribunal's jurisdiction and the relief requested;
- whether the award is final and binding;
- whether the form complies with the institution's scrutiny process;
- whether the award addresses costs and interest;
- whether dissenting or separate opinions are handled correctly.

An award that exceeds the tribunal's jurisdiction, fails to state reasons, or omits decided issues is vulnerable to challenge.

### Understand The Limited Grounds For Setting Aside And Intervention

Judicial intervention to set aside an award is limited to defined grounds, such as lack of jurisdiction, serious procedural irregularity, public policy, or inability to present one's case. Merits review is generally unavailable.

Know the grounds:

- lack of a valid arbitration agreement;
- the tribunal exceeded its jurisdiction;
- serious procedural irregularity or breach of due process;
- the award conflicts with public policy;
- failure to state reasons or address submitted issues;
- corruption, fraud, or bias affecting the award;
- the time limits and forum for challenge at the seat.

Arbitration is not a vehicle for re-arguing the merits. Challenges must fit the defined grounds and meet strict deadlines.

### Plan Enforcement Of The Award Across Jurisdictions

An award is valuable only if enforceable. The New York Convention and domestic arbitration acts govern recognition and enforcement.

Plan enforcement:

- the jurisdictions where the debtor has assets;
- the recognition procedure under the New York Convention or local law;
- the grounds for refusal of enforcement, which mirror setting-aside grounds;
- the effect of a setting-aside action at the seat on enforcement elsewhere;
- the need for authenticated copies and translations;
- the time and cost of enforcement proceedings;
- the coordination with parallel bankruptcy or insolvency.

An award set aside at the seat may still be enforceable in some jurisdictions, but the risk increases. Plan enforcement before the award is issued.

## Common Traps

### Participating Without Reserving Jurisdiction

A party that argues the merits without reserving a jurisdiction objection may waive it. Raise gateway issues early.

### Overbroad Document Production

Treating arbitration like full discovery drives cost and delay. Target production to issues and proportionality.

### Witness Statements That Are Counsel's Argument

Witness statements must be the witness's own account. Argument disguised as testimony loses credibility.

### Overlooking Expert Independence

Experts must be independent and transparent. Biased or advocacy experts are discounted or excluded.

### An Award That Exceeds Jurisdiction Or Omits Reasons

Awards vulnerable to challenge waste the process. Scrutinize the draft for jurisdiction, reasons, and completeness.

### Treating Setting-Aside As A Merits Appeal

Setting-aside grounds are narrow. Re-arguing the merits is not a valid challenge.

### Failing To Plan Enforcement Before The Award

Enforcement across jurisdictions takes time and may face refusal grounds. Plan enforcement strategy early.

## Self-Check

- Are jurisdiction and gateway disputes addressed at the outset, with competence-competence, court interaction, timing, and waiver of objections considered?
- Are the statement of claim and defense structured around issues, with facts, legal elements, relief, key documents, witnesses, experts, quantum, and tribunal issues identified?
- Is document production managed proportionately, tied to issues, with relevance, privilege, redactions, format, and non-production consequences addressed?
- Are fact witnesses and experts prepared with written statements, examination strategy, independent and qualified experts, hot-tubbing procedures, and impeachment material?
- Is the hearing format planned around issues, witnesses, time estimates, evidence, examination, post-hearing briefs, and recording?
- Are interim and conservatory measures addressed, including tribunal measures, emergency arbitrator, court aid, standards, cross-border enforceability, and security for costs?
- Is the award scrutinized for resolution of all claims, sufficient reasons, jurisdiction and relief limits, finality, institutional form, costs and interest, and handling of opinions?
- Are the limited grounds for setting aside understood, including jurisdiction, excess of jurisdiction, procedural irregularity, public policy, reasons, fraud or bias, time limits, and forum?
- Is award enforcement planned across jurisdictions, with New York Convention procedure, refusal grounds, effect of seat challenges, authenticated copies, time and cost, and bankruptcy coordination?
- Does the output escalate to qualified counsel for arbitration procedure, award challenges, and enforcement?
