# Arbitration Clauses And Forum

An arbitration clause is a forward-looking decision about how every future dispute will be resolved. Once embedded in a contract, it removes the parties from the courts, defines the arbitral institution and rules, fixes the seat and language, and determines who decides the dispute. Agents often treat arbitration clauses as boilerplate, copying a template without considering that the choice of institution, seat, scope, number of arbitrators, and carve-outs fundamentally change cost, confidentiality, enforceability, and outcome. A poorly drafted clause can itself become the subject of years of litigation over whether arbitration is even available.

The enforceability of an arbitration agreement depends on its formation, scope, and conformity with the governing arbitration law and any international conventions. Use this skill before drafting an arbitration clause, before advising a client to accept one, before choosing an institution or seat, or before litigating the existence or scope of an arbitration agreement. The goal is to surface the decisions that determine whether arbitration delivers its promised efficiency and finality, not to provide legal advice, which requires qualified counsel familiar with the governing arbitration regime.

## Core Rules

### Decide Whether Arbitration Fits The Dispute Profile

Arbitration is not universally better than litigation. Its suitability depends on the type of dispute, the parties, the desired confidentiality, the need for specialized expertise, and the enforceability of the eventual award.

Assess fit:

- confidentiality, which arbitration provides and courts often do not;
- specialized expertise, where technical or industry disputes benefit from expert arbitrators;
- enforceability across borders, where the New York Convention aids arbitration awards;
- finality, since appeals are limited compared to court judgments;
- cost and speed, which can favor or disfavor arbitration depending on complexity;
- the need for interim or emergency relief;
- the desire to avoid a specific jurisdiction's courts or juries.

Arbitration's limited appeal rights are a feature for finality but a risk if the arbitrator errs. Match the choice to the dispute profile.

### Draft The Scope Of Disputes Deliberately

The scope clause determines which disputes go to arbitration. Narrow scopes leave many disputes in court; broad scopes capture most but may be challenged.

Define scope:

- all disputes arising out of or relating to the contract, the broad formulation;
- disputes arising under the contract, a narrower formulation;
- specific claim types included or excluded;
- whether tort, statutory, and IP claims are covered;
- whether disputes with non-signatories are covered;
- the interaction with carve-outs for injunctive relief or small claims.

An ambiguous scope invites gateway litigation over arbitrability. Choose the formulation intentionally and consistently with the rest of the dispute clause.

### Choose The Institution And Rules With Purpose

The institution and its rules shape procedure, costs, arbitrator appointment, and administration. Different institutions suit different dispute types.

Consider:

- major international institutions such as ICC, LCIA, SIAC, HKIAC;
- domestic institutions and their rules;
- ad hoc arbitration under rules such as UNCITRAL;
- the institution's experience with the industry or claim type;
- appointment procedures for arbitrators;
- emergency and interim relief mechanisms;
- cost structure and administrative fees;
- the institution's neutrality relative to the parties.

A clause that names a non-existent or unsuitable institution creates appointment problems. Verify the institution exists and that its current rules are referenced.

### Fix The Seat, Language, And Governing Law

The seat is the legal place of arbitration, distinct from the venue of hearings, and determines the supervisory courts and the law governing the arbitration procedure. These choices are independent and must each be deliberate.

Fix:

- the seat, which determines judicial supervision and award-challenge jurisdiction;
- the venue of hearings, which can differ from the seat;
- the language of the arbitration;
- the governing law of the contract;
- the governing law of the arbitration agreement, which can differ from the contract;
- the number of arbitrators, typically one or three based on complexity and value;
- the method of arbitrator selection and the default if the parties cannot agree.

The seat is often the most consequential choice, because the courts of the seat can annul an award. Choose a seat with a supportive arbitration law and neutral courts.

### Specify Arbitrator Qualifications When Expertise Matters

For technical or specialized disputes, the arbitrator's qualifications affect quality and credibility. Generic clauses leave selection to availability.

Specify:

- required expertise, industry, or legal background;
- the number of arbitrators;
- nationality or neutrality requirements;
- language proficiency;
- the procedure for challenging an arbitrator for bias or conflict;
- the institution's role in appointment.

Overly specific qualification requirements can make appointment difficult. Balance specificity with feasibility.

### Address Interim, Emergency, And Injunctive Relief

Parties may need relief before the tribunal is constituted. The clause should address how interim measures are obtained.

Address:

- the right to seek interim measures from the tribunal once constituted;
- emergency arbitrator or pre-arbitral relief under the institution's rules;
- the right to seek court interim relief without waiving arbitration;
- carve-outs for specific performance or injunctive relief in court;
- the interaction with the governing law on interim relief.

A clause that purports to bar all court relief may leave parties without protection before the tribunal forms. Preserve emergency and interim options.

### Define Carve-Outs And Court Access Clearly

Some parties want certain issues resolved in court regardless of arbitration. Carve-outs must be explicit to avoid gateway disputes.

Common carve-outs:

- injunctive or equitable relief for IP, confidentiality, or non-compete;
- small claims court below a threshold;
- emergency court action to preserve assets or evidence;
- recognition and enforcement of the award;
- bankruptcy or insolvency proceedings.

Ambiguous carve-outs create litigation over whether a dispute belongs in court or arbitration. Draft carve-outs as narrowly and clearly as possible.

### Ensure Enforceability Under The Governing Regime

An arbitration agreement is only useful if enforceable. Enforceability depends on formation, writing, capacity, and conformity with the applicable law and conventions.

Ensure:

- the agreement is in writing where required by the New York Convention or local law;
- the parties have capacity to arbitrate;
- the subject matter is arbitrable under the governing law;
- the clause is not unconscionable or procedurally unfair;
- consumer, employment, and statutory claims are handled per mandatory law;
- the clause survives termination of the main contract;
- the signature or incorporation method satisfies the writing requirement.

Some jurisdictions restrict arbitration of consumer, employment, or certain statutory claims. Mandatory non-waivable rights can limit enforceability.

## Common Traps

### Copying A Boilerplate Clause Without Adaptation

A template clause may name the wrong institution, omit the seat, or use a scope inconsistent with the deal. Adapt each clause to the contract.

### Confusing Seat And Venue

The seat is the legal place controlling supervision and annulment; the venue is where hearings occur. Conflating them causes errors.

### Ambiguous Scope Inviting Gateway Litigation

Unclear scope leads to disputes over arbitrability. Choose the formulation deliberately.

### Naming A Non-Existent Or Unsuitable Institution

A clause referencing a defunct or mismatched institution creates appointment failures. Verify the institution and current rules.

### Overbroad Carve-Outs That Swallow Arbitration

Carve-outs for injunctive relief that capture the substance of the dispute effectively nullify arbitration. Draft carve-outs narrowly.

### Ignoring Arbitrability Restrictions

Consumer, employment, and certain statutory claims may not be arbitrable or may require specific procedures. Check mandatory law.

### Forgetting The Clause Survives Termination

Dispute clauses typically survive contract termination. Confirm survival is stated or implied.

## Self-Check

- Is the fit of arbitration assessed against confidentiality, expertise, cross-border enforceability, finality, cost, interim relief, and avoidance of specific courts?
- Is the dispute scope defined deliberately as broad or narrow, with tort, statutory, IP, and non-signatory coverage and carve-outs considered?
- Is the institution chosen with purpose, verifying existence, current rules, appointment procedures, emergency relief, cost, and neutrality?
- Are the seat, hearing venue, language, governing law of the contract, governing law of the arbitration agreement, number of arbitrators, and selection method each fixed deliberately?
- Are arbitrator qualifications specified where expertise matters, balanced with feasibility, with challenge procedures and institution role addressed?
- Are interim, emergency, and injunctive relief addressed, including tribunal measures, emergency arbitrator, court relief without waiver, and carve-outs?
- Are carve-outs and court access defined narrowly and clearly for injunctive relief, small claims, emergency action, award enforcement, and bankruptcy?
- Is enforceability ensured under the New York Convention or local law, with writing, capacity, arbitrability, unconscionability, mandatory consumer and employment rules, and survival addressed?
- Does the output escalate to qualified counsel for clause drafting, seat and institution selection, and enforceability analysis?
