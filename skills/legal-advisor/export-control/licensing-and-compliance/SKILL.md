# Licensing and Compliance

Classification tells you an item is controlled; licensing and compliance determine whether, how, and with what conditions you may move it across borders, and how you evidence that the movement was lawful. Export enforcement has intensified, with significant civil and criminal penalties, denial of export privileges, and debarment from government contracting. The compliance burden falls not only on the exporter but on freight forwarders, technology providers, and any party that causes a controlled export.

The judgment problem is that a license is a conditional permission, not a blanket approval. Agents often treat the license as the end of the work and neglect the conditions (end-use, end-user, re-export, diversion, reporting), the recordkeeping that evidences compliance, and the internal controls that prevent unlicensed exports by engineering, sales, and support teams who do not realize they are exporting. A compliance program that exists on paper but does not catch a rogue shipment or an intangible technology release is the failure mode.

This skill is advisory only. License conditions and program expectations are jurisdiction-specific. Confirm with the exporter's empowered-official or qualified trade-compliance counsel before acting.

## Core Rules

### Choose the right license type and application strategy

License types differ by purpose: individual/export licenses for specific transactions, global or general licenses for recurring transactions to defined destinations, special comprehensive licenses, and temporary-import/export licenses. The application requires accurate item classification, end-use and end-user information, consignee details, and often end-user statements or import certificates.

- Match the license type to the business pattern; a series of individual licenses for recurring shipments is inefficient and error-prone.
- Applications with incomplete or inaccurate end-use information are a red flag and can themselves be a violation; invest in end-use due diligence before applying.

### Honor license conditions as binding obligations

A license carries conditions: named consignee, authorized end-use, prohibition on re-export or diversion, reporting of changes, and validity period. Breaching a condition is a violation even if the underlying export would otherwise have been lawful under a different authorization.

- Communicate conditions to all parties in the chain (freight forwarder, consignee, intermediate consignee, end-user) and obtain end-use assurances.
- Monitor for and report changes in end-use, end-user, or transaction structure that fall outside the license.

### Design a risk-based internal compliance program

Authorities expect a structured, risk-based export-compliance program proportionate to the business. Core elements typically include senior-management commitment, risk assessment, written policies and procedures, classification and license management, screening of parties and transactions, training, recordkeeping, audit, and escalation.

- Tailor the program to the business's actual risk profile (products, destinations, end-users, channels). A software company's intangible-export risks differ from a manufacturer's physical-shipment risks.
- Assign clear responsibility (an empowered official or compliance officer) with authority and resources.

### Control all export channels, including intangible transfers

Exports happen through physical shipment, electronic transmission, cloud access, oral disclosure, and visual inspection. Each channel must be controlled. Technology-release controls are the most frequently missed.

- Implement access controls on repositories, design documents, and production systems based on nationality and license status.
- Train engineering, support, and sales teams that sharing technical information with foreign nationals can be an export.

### Screen parties and transactions continuously

Screen all parties (customers, end-users, consignees, intermediate parties, suppliers) against denied-party, entity, military-end-user, unverified, and sanctions lists, at onboarding and on an ongoing basis, because lists change.

- Screening is not a one-time check; a clear party today may be listed tomorrow. Continuous or periodic re-screening is expected.
- Maintain evidence of screening with dates and results.

### Maintain records for the required period

Export records (classification rationale, license and application, end-use statements, screening results, shipping documents, communications) must be retained for the statutory period (commonly five years, but verify by jurisdiction). Records are the primary defense in an audit or investigation.

- Centralize records so they can be produced quickly; inability to produce records is itself a violation.

### Handle suspected violations through escalation and self-disclosure

When a potential violation is identified, preserve records, escalate internally, and assess whether voluntary self-disclosure to the authority is appropriate. Self-disclosure can be a significant mitigating factor in penalty determination, but it is a strategic decision requiring counsel.

- Do not "fix" records or shipments after discovering a potential violation; that compounds the problem.
- Establish a clear escalation path so employees know how to report concerns.

## Common Traps

### Treating the license as the finish line

Obtaining a license is the start, not the end. The conditions, end-use monitoring, re-export prohibitions, and recordkeeping continue for the license's life and beyond. Non-compliance with conditions is a standalone violation.

### Incomplete end-use due diligence

Applications built on assumptions about end-use, without verification, are vulnerable to diversion and to authority rejection. Reluctance by the customer to provide end-use information is itself a red flag requiring further inquiry.

### Compliance program on paper only

A program that is not operationalized (training delivered, screening run, access controls enforced, audits performed) provides no defense and can be cited as an aggravating factor. The test is whether the program caught or would have caught the violation.

### Missing intangible exports by non-trade teams

Engineering, support, and sales teams routinely release controlled technology by email, chat, shared drives, and calls without recognizing an export. Without targeted training and access controls, these channels are unmonitored.

### One-time screening

Screening a customer at onboarding and never again misses subsequent listings. Lists update frequently; re-screen periodically and on trigger events.

### Records that cannot be found

Retaining records in scattered inboxes or local drives that cannot be produced on request is functionally equivalent to not having them. Centralize and index.

### Self-help after a suspected violation

Altering records, re-shipping to "fix" classification, or quietly correcting without escalation destroys the record and can convert a civil matter into a criminal one. Preserve and escalate.

### Ignoring freight forwarder and third-party conduct

The exporter remains responsible even when a forwarder or agent handles the shipment. Forwarder errors, rerouting, or misclassification still expose the exporter. Select and oversee third parties.

## Self-Check

- Have I selected the license type that matches the business pattern, with accurate classification and verified end-use and end-user information in the application?
- Are all license conditions (consignee, end-use, re-export, diversion, reporting, validity) communicated to the chain and monitored for compliance?
- Is there a risk-based internal compliance program with senior-management commitment, an empowered official, classification and license management, screening, training, recordkeeping, and audit?
- Are all export channels controlled, including intangible technology releases through repositories, cloud access, and technical support, with access controls by nationality and license status?
- Is party screening performed at onboarding and on an ongoing basis, with evidence retained?
- Are export records centralized, indexed, and retained for the required statutory period?
- Is there a defined escalation and voluntary-self-disclosure process, with records preserved on discovery of a potential violation?
- Have I flagged that license conditions and program expectations are jurisdiction-specific and must be confirmed with qualified trade-compliance counsel?
