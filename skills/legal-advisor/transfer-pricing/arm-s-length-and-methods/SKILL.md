# Arm's Length And Methods

Transfer pricing applies the arm's length principle: related parties should price their dealings as independent parties would. The principle sounds simple, but method selection, comparability analysis, and functional analysis determine whether a price holds up. Agents often pick a method that produces a desired result, use internal comparables without testing them, or apply a one-size margin across diverse transactions. A transfer price that does not reflect functions performed, assets used, and risks assumed is vulnerable to adjustment, double taxation, and penalties. Intangibles, financing, and services carry their own pitfalls because independent comparables are scarce and assumptions are easy to challenge.

Use this skill before setting intercompany prices, drafting intercompany agreements, defending a transfer pricing position, or restructuring related-party flows. The goal is to force the agent to choose the most appropriate method based on functional analysis and reliable comparables, not the most convenient one. Transfer pricing follows the OECD Guidelines, UN Manual, and local rules, all of which evolve; any position must be confirmed against current guidance and local documentation requirements.

## Core Rules

### Begin With A Controlled Transaction And Comparability Analysis

Transfer pricing is transaction-based. Define the controlled transaction precisely before choosing a method.

Identify:

- the parties and their relationship;
- the transaction type (sale of goods, services, intangible license, financing, guarantee, cost contribution);
- the contract terms and their consistency with conduct;
- the functions performed, assets used, and risks assumed (FAR analysis);
- the economic circumstances, market, and geography;
- business strategies and restructurings.

Comparability factors determine whether an uncontrolled transaction can benchmark the controlled one. A comparable must match the controlled transaction on the factors that matter, or be adjusted reliably.

### Perform A Real Functional Analysis

Functional analysis is not boilerplate. It determines who earns what.

Map:

- manufacturing, procurement, logistics, marketing, sales, R&D, financing, management functions;
- tangible and intangible assets owned and used;
- risks assumed and the capability to control them (market, credit, foreign exchange, inventory, R&D failure);
- decision-making authority and who performs DEMPE functions for intangibles;
- contractual allocation vs. actual conduct.

Contractual risk allocation that is not matched by control and financial capacity is disregarded. Realign profits to where DEMPE functions and risk control actually occur.

### Select The Most Appropriate Method

No single method is universally correct. The OECD Guidelines require the most appropriate method based on the specifics.

Methods and when they fit:

- comparable uncontrolled price (CUP): works when reliable internal or external comparables exist, especially for commodities and financial instruments;
- resale price method (RPM): fits distributors that add limited value, where gross margin is the reliable indicator;
- cost plus method (CPM): fits manufacturers and service providers that perform routine functions, where cost plus markup is reliable;
- transactional net margin method (TNMM): widely used where reliable net margin comparables exist and functional differences are reflected in net margins;
- transactional profit split method (TPSM): fits highly integrated operations or unique intangibles where both parties make unique and valuable contributions.

Justify the selection by reference to comparability, reliability, and the nature of the transaction. Do not default to TNMM without explaining why others are less appropriate.

### Build And Test Comparables

A method is only as strong as its comparables.

For each comparable set:

- source (internal, external, commercial databases);
- independence from the controlled parties;
- matching of comparability factors;
- adjustments for differences (working capital, capacity, contract terms, geography);
- financial years covered and multi-year averaging;
- loss-making comparables and their treatment;
- interquartile range and the point used (median, mean, negotiated range).

External comparables often need significant adjustment. Document the search strategy, the rejection criteria, and the adjustments. A comparables set built to produce a result is vulnerable to challenge.

### Handle Intangibles Carefully

Intangibles are the highest-risk transfer pricing area because independent comparables are scarce and value is hard to measure.

Address:

- legal ownership vs. economic ownership (DEMPE);
- unique and valuable contributions and profit split;
- routine returns to funding or legal ownership;
- hard-to-value intangibles (HTVI) and the ex-post approach;
- valuation techniques and their assumptions;
- IP migration and exit charges;
- cost contribution arrangements (CCAs).

A legal owner that does not perform DEMPE functions earns only a routine return. Do not allocate residual profit to a mere legal owner.

### Price Financing And Guarantees Realistically

Intra-group loans, guarantees, cash pools, and guarantees are frequently challenged.

Determine:

- arm's length interest rate using credit rating, term, currency, security, and options;
- implied support and the benefit of guarantees;
- capacity to borrow and the borrower's standalone credit rating;
- guarantee fee the beneficiary would pay an independent party;
- cash pool leader's return and the spread to members;
- hybrid instruments and their classification.

A rate based on the group's strong rating, ignoring the borrower's standalone position, is a common adjustment.

### Document And Defend The Position

A defensible position is contemporaneously documented.

Maintain:

- functional analysis and comparability analysis;
- method selection rationale;
- comparables search and adjustments;
- intercompany agreements consistent with conduct;
- economic analysis and benchmarking;
- alignment with the master file and local file;
- assumptions, limitations, and sensitivity.

Documentation created after an audit is weak and often penalized. Build it for the year of the transaction.

### Apply The Arm's Length Range And Adjustment Mechanics

The result should fall within an arm's length range.

Determine:

- the range from reliable comparables;
- the point used within the range;
- whether the actual result falls inside or outside;
- the adjustment needed if outside;
- the effect on the other jurisdiction and double taxation risk;
- correlative and consequential adjustments.

A position outside the range requires adjustment or strong justification. Document why the point chosen is arm's length.

## Common Traps

### Choosing The Method To Hit A Target

Method selection driven by the desired result is both non-compliant and discoverable. Choose based on comparability and reliability.

### Boilerplate Functional Analysis

Generic FAR descriptions do not support a price. Map actual functions, assets, risks, and DEMPE.

### Untested Internal Comparables

Internal CUPs must be genuinely comparable and independent. Related-party or non-comparable internal data is not a CUP.

### One-Size Margin Across Diverse Transactions

A single TNMM margin applied to different transactions ignores functional differences. Benchmark each controlled transaction or group of similar ones.

### Legal Owner Earns The Residual

Without DEMPE, a legal owner earns a routine return. Residual profit follows the people functions.

### Group-Rating Financing

Intra-group loans priced on the group's rating ignore the borrower's standalone position and implied support.

### Post-Hoc Documentation

Documentation built during an audit is weak and penalized. Build contemporaneously.

### Ignoring The Other Jurisdiction

An adjustment in one country creates double taxation unless the other accepts a correlative relief. Consider MAP exposure.

## Self-Check

- Is the controlled transaction precisely defined, with parties, type, terms, and consistency with conduct?
- Does the functional analysis map actual functions, assets, risks, DEMPE, and decision-making?
- Is the most appropriate method selected with rationale based on comparability and reliability?
- Are comparables sourced, tested for independence, adjusted, and presented with search strategy and rejection criteria?
- For intangibles, are legal vs. economic ownership, DEMPE, unique contributions, and HTVI addressed?
- For financing and guarantees, are credit rating, standalone capacity, implied support, and arm's length rates determined?
- Is the position contemporaneously documented with method, comparables, agreements, and economic analysis?
- Is the result placed within an arm's length range, with the chosen point justified and adjustments mapped?
- Are correlative and consequential adjustments and double taxation risk considered?
- Does the output reference OECD Guidelines, UN Manual, and local rules, and recommend confirmation by a qualified transfer pricing advisor?
