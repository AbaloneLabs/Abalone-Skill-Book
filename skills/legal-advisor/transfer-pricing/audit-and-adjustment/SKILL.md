# Audit And Adjustment

A transfer pricing audit is not a routine review. The tax authority can reallocate profits, impose adjustments, and trigger double taxation in another jurisdiction unless correlative relief follows. Agents often treat the audit as bilateral negotiation when it is a formal procedure with deadlines, documentation defenses, and penalty exposure. A proposed adjustment in one country automatically creates double taxation in the other unless that country grants correlative relief, and the mechanics of relief (spontaneous, on request, or through MAP) are strict. Settlements and advance pricing arrangements can resolve years of risk but also bind future years and create disclosure duties.

Use this skill before responding to a transfer pricing audit, defending against a proposed adjustment, requesting correlative relief, pursuing MAP or an APA, or negotiating a settlement. The goal is to force the agent to manage the procedural vehicle, the documentation defense, the double taxation mechanics, and the multi-year consequences before responding. Audit and MAP rules are jurisdiction-specific and time-critical; deadlines and procedures must be confirmed for each relevant tax authority, and qualified transfer pricing controversy counsel should be engaged.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Audit Stage And Procedural Vehicle

A transfer pricing audit has distinct stages, each with different rights and deadlines.

Classify:

- risk review or pre-audit inquiry;
- formal examination with information requests;
- proposed adjustment and the right to protest;
- final assessment and the right to administrative or judicial appeal;
- collection and enforcement;
- criminal or fraud referral where willfulness is suspected.

Do not assume a discussion is informal. A proposed adjustment usually starts a protest deadline even if the authority is open to negotiation.

### Build The Defense From Contemporaneous Documentation

The strongest defense is documentation prepared in the year of the transaction.

Assemble:

- master file, local file, and CbCR;
- functional analysis and comparables for the year under audit;
- intercompany agreements consistent with conduct;
- contemporaneous business rationale and substance evidence;
- method selection rationale and arm's length range;
- prior-year positions and any APAs.

Documentation created after the audit starts is weak and may be penalized. Where contemporaneous documentation is incomplete, build the defense from conduct, industry data, and functional reality, and be honest about the gap.

### Respond To Information Requests Strategically

Information requests are not unlimited. Scope, privilege, and proportionality matter.

Manage:

- the deadline and any extension;
- scope and relevance, with narrowing where overbroad;
- privilege and confidentiality review before production;
- consistency with positions taken;
- effect on the audit strategy and other jurisdictions;
- voluntary production vs. compelled production.

Over-production can create contradictions and expand the audit. Under-production can trigger summons and penalties. Coordinate through counsel.

### Evaluate The Proposed Adjustment On The Merits

A proposed adjustment should be tested, not assumed correct.

Assess:

- the authority's method and comparables;
- the authority's functional analysis vs. the taxpayer's;
- whether the adjustment is within the arm's length range;
- the authority's use of secret comparables and the right to challenge them;
- the financial impact and the years affected;
- consistency with the taxpayer's documentation and conduct.

Identify the strongest contrary points and the strongest taxpayer points. A purely defensive posture without engaging the merits is weak.

### Manage Double Taxation And Correlative Relief

A primary adjustment in one country creates double taxation in the other unless correlative relief follows.

Map:

- the primary adjustment amount and years;
- the corresponding income in the other jurisdiction;
- correlative relief mechanisms (spontaneous, on request, or through MAP);
- the deadline to request relief;
- the effect on withholding, credits, and interest;
- secondary adjustments and deemed dividend treatment.

Correlative relief is not automatic. The taxpayer often must request it and pursue MAP. Failing to do so leaves double taxation in place.

### Use The Mutual Agreement Procedure Effectively

MAP is the treaty remedy for transfer pricing and treaty disputes.

Assess:

- MAP availability under the treaty and any arbitration clause;
- the MAP deadline (often three years from first notification);
- whether to suspend collection or litigation during MAP;
- competent authority cooperation and likely outcome;
- the effect of a MAP agreement on the taxpayer and related parties;
- cost, time, and disclosure tradeoffs.

MAP does not guarantee relief and can take years. Initiate early and in parallel with domestic remedies where permitted, and preserve the MAP deadline.

### Consider Advance Pricing Arrangements For Future Certainty

An APA resolves transfer pricing for future years through agreement with one or more tax authorities.

Evaluate:

- unilateral, bilateral, or multilateral APA scope;
- covered transactions, methods, and comparables;
- term, rollback, and renewal;
- annual reporting and compliance obligations;
- cost, time, and disclosure of sensitive data;
- binding effect and the consequence of deviation.

An APA provides certainty but binds future years and requires annual compliance. Weigh certainty against flexibility and disclosure.

### Weigh Settlement, Concession, And Litigation

Most transfer pricing disputes settle. The decision should be deliberate.

Weigh:

- strength of the substantive position;
- penalty exposure and whether settlement reduces it;
- interest, collection costs, and cash flow;
- precedential risk of an adverse ruling;
- impact on other years, entities, and jurisdictions;
- effect on APAs and future positions;
- time, cost, and management distraction.

A settlement that resolves one year can bind positions in others. Read the scope, preclusive effect, and disclosure duties before agreeing.

## Common Traps

### Treating A Proposed Adjustment As A Discussion

A proposed adjustment usually starts a protest deadline. Protect the appeal right while negotiating.

### Defending Without Contemporaneous Documentation

Post-hoc documentation is weak and penalized. Build the defense from the year-of-transaction record.

### Over-Producing Without Strategy

Broad production can create contradictions and expand the audit. Scope, privilege, and consistency matter.

### Ignoring Correlative Relief

A primary adjustment creates double taxation unless relief is requested. Pursue correlative relief and MAP.

### Missing The MAP Deadline

MAP deadlines are short and jurisdictional. Missing them forecloses the treaty remedy.

### Secret Comparables Left Unchallenged

Authorities sometimes rely on undisclosed comparables. The taxpayer has the right to challenge them.

### Settling Without Reading Scope

Settlements can preclude arguments in later years and jurisdictions. Read scope and preclusive effect.

### Assuming An APA Is Always Beneficial

APAs bind future years and require annual compliance. Weigh certainty against flexibility and disclosure.

## Self-Check

- Is the audit stage classified and are protest, appeal, and collection deadlines calendared?
- Is the defense built from contemporaneous documentation, functional analysis, comparables, and consistent agreements?
- Are information requests managed for deadline, scope, privilege, consistency, and strategic effect?
- Is the proposed adjustment tested on the merits, including method, comparables, range, and secret comparables?
- Are primary and correlative adjustments, double taxation, and relief mechanisms mapped with deadlines?
- Is MAP availability, deadline, suspension of collection, and arbitration considered and initiated early?
- Are unilateral, bilateral, or multilateral APAs evaluated for scope, term, rollback, reporting, and binding effect?
- Are settlement, concession, and litigation tradeoffs weighed against penalty, interest, precedential, and cross-year effects?
- Are positions consistent with APAs, prior years, and other jurisdictions?
- Does the output recommend qualified transfer pricing controversy counsel and avoid relaxed timelines on jurisdictional deadlines?
