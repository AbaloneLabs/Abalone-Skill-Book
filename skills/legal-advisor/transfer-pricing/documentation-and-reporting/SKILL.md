# Documentation And Reporting

Transfer pricing documentation is not paperwork; it is the primary defense in an audit and a legal obligation with penalties for non-compliance. The BEPS Action 13 three-tier approach (master file, local file, country-by-country report) has been adopted in most jurisdictions with local variations, thresholds, and deadlines. Agents often treat documentation as a template exercise, copy prior-year files without updating the comparables, or allow intercompany agreements to drift out of sync with conduct. Late, incomplete, or low-quality documentation triggers penalties, can shift the burden of proof, and in some jurisdictions is itself an offense independent of the underlying price.

Use this skill before preparing transfer pricing documentation, filing CbCR, responding to a documentation request, or restructuring intercompany arrangements that affect reporting. The goal is to force the agent to produce timely, accurate, and consistent documentation that aligns with the actual conduct and the master file. Documentation rules are jurisdiction-specific and change; any filing must be confirmed against the local rules, thresholds, and deadlines for each relevant country.

## Core Rules

### Apply The Three-Tier Approach Correctly

Most jurisdictions require a master file, local file, and country-by-country report, but the scope and thresholds differ.

Master file:

- high-level overview of the group's global operations and transfer pricing policies;
- important intangibles, intercompany financial activities, and financial and tax positions;
- threshold (often consolidated group revenue above a set amount);
- language and filing country (usually the ultimate parent's country, with surrogacy where needed).

Local file:

- detailed information on specific material controlled transactions for each country;
- functional analysis, method, comparables, and financial data for each transaction or category;
- threshold for materiality by transaction type and amount;
- intercompany agreements and organizational structure.

Country-by-country report:

- allocated revenue, profit, tax, employees, and tangible assets by jurisdiction;
- identification of each constituent entity and its main activities;
- threshold (often consolidated group revenue above a set amount);
- filing by the ultimate parent, with surrogate filing and local filing in some jurisdictions.

Confirm which tier applies to which entity based on group size, materiality, and local rules.

### Meet Timeliness And Notification Requirements

Documentation obligations include notification as well as filing.

Calendar:

- CbCR notification deadline (often the ultimate parent's year-end or filing date);
- master file and local file deadlines (often tied to the tax return or a fixed date);
- language requirements and translations;
- filing method (file and hold, submit on request, or automatic filing);
- penalty triggers for late or missing documentation.

Some jurisdictions require local file to be available by the tax return deadline; others require submission only on request but with short response windows. Treat notification and availability as hard deadlines.

### Keep Comparables Current

Prior-year comparables are not automatically valid. Documentation must reflect the current year's facts and data.

Update:

- comparables sets with current-year financial data;
- multi-year averaging and the years covered;
- loss-making comparables and their treatment;
- the interquartile range and the point used;
- new comparables and rejections;
- changes in the controlled transaction, functions, or risks.

A documentation file with stale comparables is low-quality and vulnerable. Update the economic analysis each year, even if the method is unchanged.

### Align Intercompany Agreements With Conduct

Documentation must be consistent with the actual conduct and the legal agreements.

Verify:

- intercompany agreements exist for each material controlled transaction;
- terms match the functions performed, assets used, and risks assumed;
- pricing mechanism matches the transfer pricing method;
- agreement is dated, signed, and effective for the relevant year;
- conduct during the year matches the agreement (substance over form);
- restructurings and changes are documented and priced.

An agreement that contradicts conduct, or a conduct pattern with no agreement, is a common audit adjustment and can defeat the documentation defense.

### Ensure Consistency Across Tiers And Years

The master file, local files, CbCR, and prior-year positions should be consistent.

Check:

- organizational chart and ownership across tiers;
- description of controlled transactions and policies;
- financial data consistency (master file, local file, CbCR, statutory accounts, tax returns);
- method consistency for recurring transactions, with documented reasons for change;
- alignment with positions taken in other jurisdictions and any APAs.

Inconsistencies across tiers or years are red flags and can trigger coordinated audits.

### Manage Confidentiality And Voluntary Disclosure Tradeoffs

Documentation contains sensitive commercial information. Filing and sharing must balance compliance with confidentiality.

Assess:

- what the local file and CbCR reveal about strategy, margins, and structures;
- confidentiality protections in the local filing regime;
- risk of public disclosure (some jurisdictions move toward public CbCR);
- voluntary disclosure vs. holding documentation until requested;
- effect on other jurisdictions through information exchange.

Public CbCR is expanding. Assume documentation may become visible and draft accordingly.

### Respond To Documentation Requests Promptly And Accurately

When a tax authority requests documentation, the response itself is a compliance event.

Manage:

- the response deadline (often short, e.g., 30 days);
- scope of the request and any narrowing;
- privilege and confidentiality review before production;
- consistency with positions taken;
- effect on the audit strategy;
- correlative and consequential considerations.

A late or incomplete response can trigger penalties and adverse inferences. Coordinate the response through counsel.

### Track Quality And Penalty Risk

Documentation quality affects penalty exposure and burden of proof.

Maintain:

- contemporaneity (prepared in the year of the transaction or by the deadline);
- completeness (all material transactions and required elements);
- consistency (across tiers, years, and jurisdictions);
- accuracy (correct data, comparables, and analysis);
- defensibility (method rationale, comparables, and conclusions).

Many jurisdictions impose penalties for missing, late, or low-quality documentation independent of the underlying price. Some shift the burden of proof to the taxpayer where documentation is inadequate.

## Common Traps

### Template-Only Documentation

Copying a prior template without updating facts, comparables, and analysis produces low-quality, non-defensible files.

### Stale Comparables

Prior-year comparables are not automatically valid. Update the economic analysis each year.

### Agreements Out Of Sync With Conduct

Intercompany agreements that contradict actual conduct defeat the documentation defense and invite substance challenges.

### Missing CbCR Notification

CbCR notification deadlines are short and easy to miss. Treat them as hard deadlines.

### Inconsistency Across Tiers

Master file, local file, CbCR, and tax returns must be consistent. Inconsistencies trigger coordinated audits.

### Ignoring Public CbCR Risk

Public CbCR is expanding. Draft documentation assuming it may become visible.

### Late Response To Requests

Short response windows and adverse inferences make late responses costly. Coordinate early.

### Treating Penalties As Minor

Documentation penalties can be substantial and independent of the underlying price. Some shift the burden of proof.

## Self-Check

- Are master file, local file, and CbCR obligations confirmed against thresholds, scope, and local rules for each jurisdiction?
- Are CbCR notification, master file, and local file deadlines calendared with filing method, language, and penalty triggers?
- Are comparables updated with current-year data, multi-year averaging, range, and rejections documented?
- Are intercompany agreements in place, signed, dated, and consistent with conduct and the transfer pricing method?
- Is the documentation consistent across master file, local file, CbCR, statutory accounts, tax returns, and prior years?
- Are confidentiality, public CbCR risk, and information exchange effects assessed before filing?
- Are documentation requests met with timely, scoped, privilege-reviewed, and consistent responses?
- Is documentation quality assessed for contemporaneity, completeness, consistency, accuracy, and defensibility?
- Are penalties for missing, late, or low-quality documentation and burden-of-proof shifts identified?
- Does the output recommend confirmation against local rules and thresholds by a qualified transfer pricing advisor?
