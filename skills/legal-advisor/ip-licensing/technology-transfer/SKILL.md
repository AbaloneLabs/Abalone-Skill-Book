# Technology Transfer

Technology transfer is the conveyance of not just the legal right to use intellectual property, but the practical capability to practice it. Unlike a pure patent or copyright license, technology transfer includes know-how, technical data, training, documentation, and ongoing technical assistance. The judgment problem is that the value of the deal depends on tacit knowledge that is hard to define, hard to price, and hard to verify has actually been delivered. Agents who draft technology transfer as if it were a standard license, listing IP rights and a royalty, systematically under-protect the licensor and under-deliver to the licensee.

The second judgment problem is regulatory overlay. Technology transfer frequently crosses borders and industries that implicate export controls, sanctions, and competition law. A deal that is commercially attractive can be illegal to execute, or can be restructured by competition authorities, if these regimes are ignored. Agents must also navigate the distinctive dynamics of university and institutional technology transfer, where the licensor's mission, tax status, and obligation to the public create constraints absent in commercial deals. The skill is structuring the transfer to actually convey the capability, allocating the risks of improvements and feedback, and clearing the regulatory gates before the deal is signed.

This skill addresses technology transfer under US law with reference to international export control and competition regimes. Export control, sanctions, antitrust, and (for university deals) Bayh-Dole and tax-exempt rules are specialized and jurisdiction-specific. A licensed attorney and often regulatory and export counsel must review material technology transfer arrangements.

## Core Rules

### Define the technology and know-how being transferred with specificity

Technology transfer must enumerate what is being conveyed. Identify the patents, copyrights, software, technical data, specifications, designs, processes, formulas, and know-how. For know-how, which is inherently intangible, describe the categories and the form of transfer (documentation, on-site training, access to personnel, consulting hours). A vague reference to "the technology" or "all related know-how" creates disputes over whether delivery is complete and what the licensee actually acquired. Strong agreements include a technology schedule or exhibit detailing deliverables and acceptance criteria.

### Structure technical assistance and training as enforceable obligations

The capability transfer often lives in training and technical assistance, not documents. Define the scope: how many personnel, how many hours or days, at what location, on what topics, with what deliverables. Specify whether assistance is included in the license fee or billed separately, and the rate for additional assistance beyond the included amount. Address who bears travel and expenses. Tie training to acceptance criteria where the licensee must demonstrate capability. Vague "reasonable assistance" obligations are unenforceable and leave the licensee without recourse if the tacit knowledge is never conveyed.

### Define deliverables, documentation, and acceptance

Enumerate the tangible deliverables: source code, object code, documentation, specifications, test protocols, manufacturing instructions, and updates. Specify the form, format, and language of documentation. Where the technology must meet performance criteria, define acceptance tests, who conducts them, the consequences of passing or failing, and the remedy for failure (rework, refund, additional assistance). Acceptance criteria protect the licensee from paying for technology that does not work and protect the licensor from open-ended rework obligations.

### Allocate improvements and feedback deliberately

Technology transfer deals generate improvements on both sides. Define ownership of improvements developed by each party and the grant-back terms. Common structures reserve licensor improvements to the licensor with an option to license them to the licensee (often at a discount or for a defined term), and grant the licensor a license to licensee improvements necessary to support the transferred technology. Address feedback: whether the licensee must provide bug reports and field data, and whether that feedback is licensed back to the licensor for use in improving the technology for all licensees. Avoid structures that chill either party's incentive to improve.

### Address background and foreground IP

Distinguish background IP (what each party brought to the deal) from foreground IP (what is created during the collaboration). Confirm that each party retains its background IP and that the license grants only the rights necessary to practice the transferred technology. For jointly developed foreground IP, define ownership (often joint with specified rights to exploit) and the obligations of each party. Ambiguity here produces post-deal ownership disputes that can paralyze commercialization.

### Clear export control and sanctions before committing

Technology transfer, especially cross-border, is subject to export controls (US EAR, ITAR for defense-related, and counterparts such as EU dual-use rules) and economic sanctions. The transfer of technical data, source code, or know-how to foreign persons, even within the US, can constitute a deemed export requiring authorization. Restricted end-uses, end-users, and destinations can block the deal entirely. Conduct export classification of the technology, screen the parties and end-users against denied-party lists, and determine license requirements before signing. Building a deal that cannot be legally executed wastes both parties' resources and creates liability. Include representations about end-use and end-user, and audit rights for compliance.

### Evaluate competition law limits on licensing terms

Competition and antitrust law (US antitrust, EU Technology Transfer Block Exemption, and counterparts) constrain licensing terms that restrict competition. Field-of-use and territory restrictions are generally permissible when they protect the IP, but resale price maintenance, market allocation among licensees, mandatory grant-backs of separately patentable improvements, and no-challenge clauses can violate competition law, particularly in the EU and for deals with market power. Structure restrictions to serve legitimate IP-protection purposes, avoid horizontal restraints, and consider safe harbors or block exemptions where available. Terms that would be lawful in a pure license can be problematic in a technology transfer with market power.

### Navigate university and institutional technology transfer distinctly

University and research institution technology transfer operates under different constraints. Under US Bayh-Dole rules, federally funded inventions have march-in rights, US manufacturing preference, and reporting obligations. Universities, as tax-exempt entities with public-benefit missions, typically prefer non-exclusive or field-limited exclusive licenses, require diligent commercialization milestones, and reserve rights for research and educational use. Pricing and terms reflect the mission, not just the market. Engage the institution's technology transfer office early, understand its standard terms and constraints, and structure the deal to satisfy both commercial and institutional requirements.

### Manage confidentiality and trade secret protection throughout

Technology transfer necessarily exposes the licensor's trade secrets. Robust confidentiality terms are essential, including defined confidential information, permitted-use limits, return or destruction obligations, survival of obligations post-term, and restrictions on reverse engineering beyond the license. For the licensee, ensure that improvements it develops using the technology are not swept into the licensor's trade secret claims. Coordinate confidentiality with the trade secret protection program.

## Common Traps

### Treating technology transfer as a standard license

A license grant without know-how, training, and deliverables leaves the licensee unable to practice the technology and the licensor exposed to claims of non-delivery. Structure the capability transfer, not just the legal right.

### Vague "reasonable assistance" obligations

Undefined assistance is unenforceable and leaves the tacit knowledge unconveyed. Specify scope, hours, deliverables, and acceptance.

### Ignoring export controls until execution

Discovering export control issues after signing can block the deal and create liability. Classify, screen, and clear before committing.

### Ambiguous improvement and feedback ownership

Vague improvement terms produce disputes over who owns follow-on inventions and whether the licensor can use field feedback. Define ownership and grant-backs explicitly.

### Overreaching grant-backs and no-challenge clauses

Mandatory grant-backs of separately patentable improvements and no-challenge clauses can violate competition law, particularly in the EU and with market power. Structure restrictions carefully.

### Conflating background and foreground IP

Failing to distinguish what each party brought from what was jointly created produces ownership paralysis. Define background and foreground IP and their treatment.

### Assuming university terms mirror commercial terms

University technology transfer carries Bayh-Dole, tax-exempt, and mission constraints absent in commercial deals. Imposing commercial-style terms can fail and damage the relationship.

### Weak confidentiality that exposes trade secrets

Technology transfer exposes the licensor's most sensitive know-how. Confidentiality that is not robust invites misappropriation and undermines the trade secret protection program.

## Self-Check

- Does the agreement enumerate the patents, copyrights, software, technical data, specifications, and know-how being transferred, with a detailed technology schedule?
- Are training and technical assistance defined as enforceable obligations with scope, hours, deliverables, location, and cost allocation?
- Are tangible deliverables, documentation form and language, and acceptance criteria with pass/fail remedies specified?
- Are improvements, feedback, background IP, and foreground IP allocated with explicit ownership and grant-back terms?
- Has export classification, party and end-user screening, and license-requirement analysis been completed before signing, with end-use representations and audit rights?
- Have licensing restrictions been evaluated against competition law, avoiding horizontal restraints, overbroad grant-backs, and no-challenge clauses?
- For university deals, have Bayh-Dole, tax-exempt, and institutional mission constraints been identified and accommodated?
- Is confidentiality robust, with defined confidential information, permitted use, return or destruction, survival, and reverse-engineering limits?
- Have I distinguished trade secret protection obligations from the licensee's right to its own improvements?
- Have I flagged that export control, sanctions, competition law, and institutional rules are specialized and jurisdiction-specific, and that licensed attorneys and regulatory counsel must review material technology transfer arrangements?
