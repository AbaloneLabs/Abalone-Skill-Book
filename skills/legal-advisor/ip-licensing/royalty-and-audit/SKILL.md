# Royalty and Audit

The royalty provisions are where the economic value of a license is actually realized, and where the most common and most expensive disputes arise. Agents frequently treat royalty drafting as a matter of agreeing on a percentage and a payment date, when the real risk is in the definition of the royalty base, the exclusions, the calculation methodology, and the audit rights that determine whether underreporting can be detected and remedied. A well-negotiated rate is worthless if the base against which it is calculated can be eroded by creative accounting.

The judgment problem is information asymmetry. The licensee knows its sales, costs, and channel structure; the licensor does not. The licensor depends on the licensee's self-reported royalties, and the audit right is the only mechanism to verify them. But audit rights that are too narrow, too expensive to exercise, or too short in limitation period are effectively unenforceable. The skill is designing a royalty structure that is commercially sensible, a base definition that resists manipulation, reporting that is specific enough to audit, and an audit mechanism that is economically feasible to use. Agents must also resist the temptation to over-engineer royalties into complexity that no one can administer, because unadministrable terms are routinely ignored and impossible to audit.

This skill addresses royalty and audit provisions under US law with reference to common international practices. Tax treatment of royalties, withholding obligations, and transfer pricing interact with these clauses. Royalty and audit drafting is specialized; a licensed attorney and often a financial advisor should review material arrangements.

## Core Rules

### Define the royalty base with precision and exclusions

The royalty base is the number against which the rate is applied. Common bases include net sales, gross sales, unit count, or a defined profit measure. Each has manipulation risk. Gross sales is simplest but may over-reward the licensor where the licensee bears significant downstream costs; net sales allows deductions that can be inflated. If using net sales, enumerate the permitted deductions exhaustively (trade discounts, returns, taxes, freight, packaging) and prohibit all others. Define how bundled and discounted products are valued, since bundling licensed IP with unlicensed products is the classic base-erosion tactic. Strong clauses require reasonable allocation methods for bundles and prohibit allocations that systematically understate the licensed component.

### Choose the rate structure to match the deal

Rate structures include flat percentage of sales, tiered rates (declining or increasing with volume), per-unit rates, minimum annual royalties, and milestone payments. Each creates different incentives. Flat percentages are simple but insensitive to volume leverage; tiered rates can reward volume or protect against erosion; per-unit rates avoid base-manipulation but may misalign with value in cost-reducing technology. Match the structure to the value driver: a technology that reduces cost may warrant a share of cost savings; a brand license may warrant a percentage of revenue. Avoid structures that are impossible to calculate from available data.

### Use minimum guarantees to protect downside and motivate

A minimum guaranteed royalty sets a floor that the licensee must pay regardless of sales, protecting the licensor's downside and signaling the licensee's commitment. Minimums can be structured as annual floors, with earned royalties above the floor. They are especially valuable in exclusive licenses, where the licensor has foregone other opportunities. Define whether the minimum is creditable against earned royalties in the same period, whether shortfalls carry forward, and the consequences of repeated shortfall (often a right to terminate or convert exclusivity to non-exclusive). Beware of minimums set so high they are commercially unrealistic and become a deal-breaker or a litigation target.

### Include most-favored-licensee protection where appropriate

A most-favored-licensee (MFL) clause entitles a licensee to the benefit of more favorable terms granted to a later licensee. MFLs protect a licensee from being undercut but create administrative burden and antitrust risk if they facilitate price coordination. If used, define the comparison scope precisely (rate only, or rate plus other economic terms), the notice and claim mechanics, and the effective period. MFLs are uncommon and should be used judiciously; they are more typical in large, strategic licenses.

### Design reporting that is specific enough to audit

Royalty reports must contain enough detail to be auditable. Require periodic reports (monthly or quarterly) showing units sold or revenue by product and territory, the calculation of the royalty base with deductions itemized, the royalty amount, and currency conversion methodology for international sales. Require reports even in periods with no sales (negative reporting), which prevents silent lapses. Specify the reporting format and the supporting documentation to be retained (sales records, invoices, allocation worksheets). Vague reporting obligations make audits fruitless and disputes unresolvable.

### Make audit rights economically feasible

An audit right that is too expensive or limited to use is no right at all. Specify the scope (all records relevant to royalty calculation), the frequency (typically annually, with reasonable notice), who may conduct it (independent accountant, bound by confidentiality), and who pays. A common structure: the licensor pays unless a specified underpayment threshold is exceeded (e.g., 5% or 10%), in which case the licensee pays the audit cost plus interest and possibly penalties. Set a limitation period for audits long enough to be meaningful (often two to four years), since underreporting may not surface quickly. Address electronic records, data formats, and access to systems, not just paper documents.

### Address interest, penalties, and overpayment carryforwards

Specify interest on late or underpaid royalties (often a defined rate or a statutory rate plus a margin). Consider penalties for material underreporting (sometimes a percentage of the underpayment above a threshold). Symmetrically, address overpayments: whether they carry forward as credits, are refundable, or expire. Symmetry reduces disputes and signals good faith.

### Handle currency, tax, and withholding

For international licenses, specify the reporting and payment currency, the exchange rate methodology and date, and which party bears currency risk. Address withholding taxes on royalty payments, which many jurisdictions impose: typically the licensee must gross up the payment or the parties allocate withholding under a tax treaty, and the licensor must cooperate to obtain treaty benefits. Clarify that taxes are not deductible from the royalty base unless specified. Coordinate with transfer pricing for related-party licenses.

### Reserve termination and conversion rights for chronic underpayment

Repeated or material underpayment should trigger remedies beyond interest. Reserve the right to terminate the license, convert exclusivity to non-exclusive, or require prepayment or escrow if audits reveal a pattern of underreporting. Define the threshold (e.g., underpayment exceeding a percentage in two consecutive periods) to avoid arbitrary enforcement.

## Common Traps

### Vague "net sales" with open-ended deductions

Net sales defined as "gross sales less customary deductions" invites the licensee to characterize a wide range of costs as customary, eroding the base. Enumerate deductions exhaustively.

### Bundling that understates the licensed component

When licensed IP is sold in a bundle with unlicensed products or services, a royalty on only the licensed component can be manipulated downward. Require a defined, reasonable allocation method and prohibit allocations that understate the licensed value.

### Audit rights too narrow to be useful

An audit right limited to once every three years, with a six-month limitation period, or excluding electronic records, is effectively unenforceable. Design audits to be feasible.

### No negative reporting

Without a requirement to report zero-sales periods, silent lapses in payment are hard to distinguish from low sales and disputes fester. Require reports in every period.

### Ignoring international withholding and currency

Failure to address withholding taxes and currency conversion leads to the licensor receiving less than expected and disputes over who bears the gap. Address both expressly.

### Minimums set too high

An unrealistically high minimum can become a termination trigger the licensee never intended to meet, or a litigation target. Set minimums at commercially supportable levels.

### Overly complex royalty structures

Structures requiring data the licensee does not track, or calculations no one can verify, are routinely miscalculated and impossible to audit. Favor administrable simplicity.

## Self-Check

- Is the royalty base precisely defined, with permitted deductions enumerated exhaustively and bundle allocation methodology specified?
- Does the rate structure match the value driver of the deal, and is it calculable from data the licensee actually maintains?
- Are minimum guarantees set at commercially realistic levels, with clear crediting, carryforward, and shortfall-consequence provisions?
- If a most-favored-licensee clause is included, is its scope, notice mechanics, and antitrust risk acceptable?
- Do royalty reporting requirements specify enough detail (units, revenue, deductions, currency, format) and negative reporting to be auditable?
- Is the audit right economically feasible, with scope, frequency, cost allocation, limitation period, and electronic records access defined?
- Are interest, penalties for material underreporting, and overpayment carryforwards addressed symmetrically?
- Are currency conversion, withholding taxes, and treaty cooperation addressed for international licenses?
- Are termination, conversion, or escrow rights reserved for chronic or material underpayment, with defined thresholds?
- Have I flagged that royalty and audit terms interact with tax, transfer pricing, and accounting rules that vary by jurisdiction, and that a licensed attorney and financial advisor should review material arrangements?
