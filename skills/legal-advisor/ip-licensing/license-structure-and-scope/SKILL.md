# License Structure and Scope

A license is a grant of permission to do something that would otherwise be IP infringement. The structure of that grant, the scope clauses, is where almost all the commercial and legal value of the deal lives. Agents often treat the grant clause as boilerplate to be filled in, when in fact it is the most litigation-prone and most expensive-to-fix part of the agreement. Every word in the grant defines what the licensee may do, what it may not do, what happens at the boundary, and what remedies exist when the lines are crossed.

The judgment problem is that license scope is bidirectional risk. Draft the grant too narrowly and the licensor leaves value on the table or the licensee cannot operate; draft it too broadly and the licensor has effectively given away rights it intended to reserve, including the ability to control future products, markets, or competitive positioning. Ambiguity in scope does not protect the licensor by default; courts often construe ambiguous grants against the drafter and may read in implied licenses that erode exclusivity. The skill is defining the grant with surgical precision across the dimensions of exclusivity, field, territory, sublicensing, duration, and the interaction between them, while anticipating how the licensee's business may evolve in ways that test the boundaries.

This skill addresses IP licensing under US law with reference to common international principles. Specific rules on exhaustion, implied licenses, and antitrust limits on field restrictions vary by jurisdiction. License drafting is a specialized practice; a licensed attorney should review material license agreements.

## Core Rules

### Draft the grant clause as the center of the deal

The grant clause states what IP is licensed, to whom, and with what permissions and reservations. It should expressly identify the licensed IP (which patents by number, which trademarks by registration, which copyrights and software, which know-how), the licensed activities (make, use, sell, offer to sell, import, modify, distribute, display), and what is expressly reserved to the licensor. Do not rely on the licensee to infer limits from context. Every reserved right should be stated. The grant clause is where implied-license disputes originate; clarity defeats them.

### Choose exclusivity deliberately and define what it means

Exclusivity is not binary. Options include sole (only the licensor and the licensee may practice), exclusive (only the licensee may practice, not even the licensor), and non-exclusive (the licensor may grant to others and practice itself). Define whether exclusivity is against the world or limited to a field or territory. Address whether the licensor retains the right to use the IP internally for research, internal operations, or non-commercial purposes. Exclusivity carries antitrust implications when it restricts competition in a relevant market; field-limited or territory-limited exclusivity is generally more defensible than blanket exclusivity. State the exclusivity level explicitly; silence is often construed as non-exclusive.

### Define field of use with precision

Field of use restricts the licensee to practicing the IP in a defined application or industry (e.g., automotive, medical devices, research only). Field restrictions are valuable because they let the licensor monetize the same IP across multiple non-competing licensees. Define the field by objective criteria, not vague labels. "Consumer electronics" is ambiguous at the boundary; "devices sold to end consumers for personal use, excluding enterprise and industrial applications" is more workable. Anticipate convergence: if a technology spans fields that later merge (e.g., phones and cameras), a field restriction can become a dispute. Address cross-field use and improvements.

### Define territory with enforceability in mind

Territory restricts where the licensee may practice (e.g., manufacture in, sell into, or use in specified countries). Territory restrictions must respect the interplay with patent exhaustion and the territorial nature of IP rights. A patent license territory is meaningful because patents are national; a copyright or trademark territory interacts with first-sale and parallel import rules. Define whether territory restricts manufacture, sale, use, or all three. Beware of territory restrictions that function as market allocation among licensees, which can raise antitrust concerns, particularly price or customer allocation.

### Control sublicensing explicitly

The right to sublicense is not implied; it must be granted. Decide whether sublicensing is permitted at all, whether it requires licensor consent, whether the licensor must approve the sublicensee, and whether sublicense terms must flow down (audit rights, confidentiality, territory and field limits, insurance). Require the licensee to remain responsible for sublicensee compliance. Unrestricted sublicensing can destroy the licensor's control and revenue, as sublicensees may operate outside the field or territory. A common strong structure permits sublicensing only to affiliates and approved subcontractors with mandatory flow-down terms.

### Set duration and renewal tied to the IP and the deal

License duration options include perpetual, term-limited, and co-extensive with the IP (e.g., for the life of the patent). A perpetual license eliminates ongoing leverage but may be appropriate for a paid-up technology transfer. Term-limited licenses preserve renewal leverage but create cliff risks at expiration (what happens to products already made, inventory in the channel, and ongoing services). Address the post-term position expressly: sell-off periods for existing inventory, ongoing support and maintenance rights, and whether the license survives for products already distributed. For software, distinguish the license term from support and update terms.

### Address improvements and feedback

Define who owns improvements developed by each party and what rights the other has. Common structures grant the licensor a license to licensee improvements, or reserve all improvements to the developer with optional grant-backs. Feedback and bug-fix provisions should specify whether feedback is licensed back to the licensor and on what terms. Avoid ambiguous "all improvements belong to the licensor" clauses that may be unenforceable or that chill the licensee's incentive to improve. Distinguish patentable improvements from copyrightable derivative works and know-how.

### Reserve enforcement and quality control appropriately

For trademarks, the licensor must maintain quality control or risk losing the mark; include quality standards, inspection rights, and approval procedures. For patents and trade secrets, reserve the right to enforce the IP and clarify whether the licensee may participate in enforcement or recover for its damages. Address whether the licensee must notify the licensor of suspected infringement. For exclusive licensees, consider whether the licensee has standing to sue, which often depends on whether the license is effectively an assignment of all substantial rights.

## Common Traps

### Granting "all rights necessary" without enumerating them

A grant of "all rights necessary to commercialize the technology" is dangerously broad and may be construed to include rights the licensor intended to reserve. Enumerate the specific licensed activities and reserve the rest.

### Leaving exclusivity undefined

Silence on exclusivity is often construed as non-exclusive, which can defeat a licensee's expectation of exclusivity or a licensor's expectation of retained freedom. State the exclusivity level expressly.

### Overbroad field or territory that reads as a market allocation

Field and territory restrictions that, in combination with other terms, allocate markets among licensees can raise antitrust scrutiny. Ensure restrictions serve a legitimate IP-protection purpose and are not disguised horizontal restraints.

### Permitting unrestricted sublicensing

Unrestricted sublicensing hands the licensee the ability to propagate the IP to parties the licensor never vetted, often outside the intended field or territory. Control sublicensing with consent and flow-down requirements.

### Ignoring the post-expiration position

A license that ends abruptly can strand inventory, ongoing services, and customer relationships. Define sell-off, transition, and survival for products already distributed.

### Conflating IP types in a single grant clause

A grant clause that treats patent, copyright, trademark, and trade secret rights identically will misstate the law for each. Each IP type has distinct exhaustion, territoriality, and duration rules. Draft separate grant provisions for each IP type in a mixed license.

### Ambiguous "improvements" ownership

Vague improvement clauses produce disputes over who owns follow-on inventions. Define the scope of improvements, ownership, and grant-back terms explicitly.

## Self-Check

- Does the grant clause expressly identify the licensed IP and the specific licensed activities, and reserve all other rights to the licensor?
- Is the exclusivity level (sole, exclusive, non-exclusive) stated explicitly, and is it consistent with antitrust limits on market restriction?
- Are field of use and territory defined by objective, workable criteria, and have I anticipated convergence or boundary disputes?
- Is sublicensing controlled with consent, flow-down terms, and licensee responsibility for sublicensee compliance?
- Does the duration and renewal structure match the deal economics, with clear post-term provisions for inventory, services, and distributed products?
- Are improvements, feedback, and derivative works addressed with explicit ownership and grant-back terms?
- For trademark licenses, are quality control, inspection, and approval provisions sufficient to avoid naked licensing and protect the mark?
- Are enforcement rights and standing to sue addressed, particularly for exclusive licensees?
- Have I drafted separate provisions for each IP type rather than a single blended grant clause?
- Have I flagged that license scope interacts with exhaustion, territoriality, and antitrust rules that vary by jurisdiction, and that a licensed attorney should review the agreement?
