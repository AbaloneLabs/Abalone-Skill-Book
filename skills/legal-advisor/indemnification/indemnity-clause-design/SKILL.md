# Indemnity Clause Design

Indemnification is one of the most heavily negotiated and most frequently mis-drafted provisions in commercial contracts. It is the mechanism by which one party contractually assumes loss that would otherwise fall on the other party, and it often does so for losses the indemnifying party would not bear under ordinary tort or contract law. Because indemnity reallocates risk by agreement, the wording controls almost everything: who pays, for what, on whose behalf, with what duties, up to what limit, and subject to what conditions.

Agents tend to treat indemnity as a boilerplate risk-shifting sentence and miss the load-bearing distinctions. A "defend" obligation is not the same as an "indemnify" obligation. A third-party claim indemnity is not the same as a direct (first-party) indemnity. A broad "any and all claims" phrase can sweep in consequences the drafter never intended, while an overly narrow "to the extent caused by" qualifier can gut the protection the indemnitee thought it had. Indemnity also interacts with insurance, limitation of liability, survival, and statutes of limitation in ways that determine whether the clause is actually worth anything when a claim arrives years later.

This skill is jurisdictional guidance, not legal advice. Indemnity enforceability, anti-indemnity statutes, and workers' compensation exclusivity vary by jurisdiction. Always confirm governing law and recommend qualified counsel review for high-value or atypical allocations.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish the three indemnity functions and draft each deliberately

Indemnity clauses commonly bundle three distinct obligations that have different triggers and remedies. Draft each one consciously:

- **Indemnify** — reimburse the indemnitee for loss it actually suffers (a back-end money obligation after loss crystallizes).
- **Defend** — assume and conduct the defense of a claim against the indemnitee, including paying counsel (a front-end obligation triggered when a claim is made, before liability is established).
- **Hold harmless** — release the indemnitee from liability, functionally a promise not to assert claims (often redundant with indemnify but with different historical roots).

A clause that says only "indemnify" may impose no duty to defend, leaving the indemnitee to front defense costs and seek reimbursement. A clause that says "defend, indemnify, and hold harmless" is broader but should still be read with the scope language to determine what claims trigger the defense duty. Strong drafting separates the defense obligation and states when it attaches (e.g., "upon written notice of a covered third-party claim") and who controls counsel.

### Decide third-party vs. direct claim scope explicitly

The single most important scope decision is whether indemnity covers only claims by third parties, or also direct losses between the parties.

- **Third-party claim indemnity** — the classic form: Party A indemnifies Party B against claims brought by outsiders (e.g., customers, regulators, IP holders) arising from A's breach, IP infringement, or negligence. This is the default expectation in most commercial contracts.
- **Direct (first-party) indemnity** — A reimburses B for B's own direct losses on defined triggers (e.g., environmental contamination, data breach, title defects) without any third-party claim. This is common in M&A, real estate, and certain regulatory contexts.

Drafting that says "indemnify against any and all claims, losses, damages, and liabilities" without specifying third-party vs. direct can be read either way. If the intent is third-party only, say so ("claims brought by third parties"). If direct losses are intended, enumerate the triggering facts so the scope is predictable. Mixing the two without intention is the most common source of indemnity disputes.

### Tie scope to fault allocation and avoid over-broad "any claim" language

A well-drafted indemnity links the obligation to a defined triggering event and, where appropriate, to fault. Common patterns:

- **Strict trigger** — indemnity arises on the occurrence regardless of fault (e.g., IP infringement, breach of confidentiality, violation of law by the indemnitor). Strong protection for indemnitee; high risk for indemnitor.
- **Fault-based trigger** — indemnity arises only for losses "caused by" or "arising out of" the indemnitor's breach, negligence, or misconduct. More balanced; common in mutual indemnities.
- **Comparative / several** — each party indemnifies only to the extent of its own fault; joint fault allocated proportionally. Common in construction and joint venture contexts.

Avoid bare "indemnify against any and all claims" without a trigger. It creates unpredictable exposure and invites arguments about scope. Prefer a closed list of triggering categories with a clear causal connector ("to the extent arising out of or resulting from").

### Specify the defense obligation in operational detail

If the clause includes a duty to defend, address:

- **Trigger** — when does the duty attach? On notice of claim, on tender of defense, or only on a finding of liability? Requiring a tender gives the indemnitor control but can delay; automatic attachment protects the indemnitee.
- **Control of defense** — who selects counsel, controls strategy, and approves settlements? Indemnitor control is standard for third-party claims, but the indemnitee may demand co-counsel or veto rights over conflicts, admission of its fault, or settlements that impose non-monetary obligations on it.
- **Reasonableness of costs** — defense costs are typically subject to reasonableness; state the standard and who decides.
- **Cooperation duty** — the indemnitee must reasonably cooperate; define what that means (document preservation, witness availability) and whether costs are reimbursed.
- **Settlement consent** — address whether the indemnitor can settle without the indemnitee's consent, and carve out settlements that impose non-monetary obligations, admissions of fault, or injunctive relief.

A defense duty without these operational terms invites disputes at the worst time — when a claim is live and money is flowing.

### Set caps, baskets, and survival that reflect the risk

Indemnity economics are governed by limitations layered on top of the clause:

- **Caps** — an overall liability cap, and possibly a separate (often higher or unlimited) cap for specific high-stakes categories (IP infringement, fraud, confidentiality breach, willful misconduct).
- **Baskets / deductibles** — a threshold the indemnitee must exceed before recovery (a "basket"), often structured as a true deductible (first dollars lost) or a tipping basket (once exceeded, recovery from dollar one). The difference is large.
- **Survival** — how long the indemnity survives contract termination or closing. General reps often survive to the contractual limitations period; fundamental reps, IP, tax, and fraud often survive longer or indefinitely.
- **Time bars** — separate limitations periods for different categories (e.g., 2 years general, 6 years tax, unlimited fraud).

These must be internally consistent. An unlimited indemnity subject to a short survival period may be worthless for latent claims; a long survival subject to a low cap may undercompensate catastrophic loss.

### Address notice and cooperation conditions

Indemnity is almost always conditioned on timely notice and cooperation. Specify:

- **Notice form and timing** — written notice "promptly" or within a defined period; whether late notice bars recovery entirely or only to the extent of prejudice.
- **Cooperation** — what the indemnitee must do and at whose expense.
- **Reservation of rights** — whether the indemnitor can defend under a reservation of rights and what happens if coverage is later denied.

Courts in many jurisdictions hold that late notice bars indemnity only if the indemnitor was prejudiced; drafters who want a strict bar must say so explicitly and confirm enforceability under governing law.

### Handle mutual indemnities and anti-indemnity limits

For mutual indemnities, ensure symmetry is intentional. Mirror-image language can create unintended asymmetry if one party's triggers are broader. Also confirm governing law does not impose anti-indemnity limits:

- **Construction** — many U.S. states bar or limit indemnity for the indemnitee's own negligence in construction contracts.
- **Environmental / regulatory** — some statutes prohibit indemnity for regulatory violations or require specific wording.
- **Consumer / employment** — indemnity may be unenforceable or regulated where one party is a consumer or employee.

### Coordinate with insurance and limitation of liability

Indemnity does not operate in isolation. Confirm:

- **Insurance backstop** — does the indemnitor maintain insurance that responds to the indemnity? Consider an additional insured endorsement so the indemnitee can claim directly.
- **LoI / limitation of liability interaction** — does the cap apply to indemnity? Carve out fundamental breaches, fraud, and IP if intended.
- **Waiver of subrogation** — prevent insurers from pursuing the other party after paying a loss.
- **Other insurance / primary language** — clarify which policy responds first when both parties are insured.

## Common Traps

- **Treating "defend" and "indemnify" as synonyms.** They are not. A defense obligation is triggered earlier and requires active conduct; an indemnify-only clause may leave the indemnitee fronting all defense costs. Read the verbs.
- **"Any and all claims" without a trigger.** This sweeps unpredictably and invites scope litigation. Tie indemnity to defined, enumerated triggering events with a causal connector.
- **Drafting third-party language when direct loss was intended.** A clause that indemnifies only "against third-party claims" provides nothing for the indemnitee's own direct losses (e.g., remediation, regulatory fines). Confirm the intended category and state it.
- **Forgetting that a duty to defend can exceed ultimate liability.** Defense costs can be incurred even where no liability is found, and they can exhaust a cap before any indemnity is owed. Decide whether defense costs are inside or outside the cap.
- **Ignoring anti-indemnity statutes.** In construction and certain regulated industries, indemnity for the indemnitee's own sole negligence is void. Drafting it anyway wastes the clause and may void the entire indemnity under some statutes.
- **Setting a basket that swallows recovery.** A high tipping basket that must be exceeded before any recovery can make the indemnity illusory for the realistic claim size. Model the economics against expected loss.
- **Mismatched survival and limitations.** A long survival paired with a short contractual limitations period, or vice versa, creates internal inconsistency. Reconcile them; **Assuming "hold harmless" adds anything.** In many jurisdictions it is redundant with "indemnify." Relying on it to expand scope is risky; use explicit scope language instead
- **Ignoring the indemnitor's right to control vs. the indemnitee's conflict concerns.** Granting the indemnitor sole control of a defense that may involve admitting the indemnitee's fault creates a conflict. Reserve consent rights over settlements and admissions; **Overlooking consequential damages.** Indemnity often covers losses that a consequential damages exclusion in the contract would otherwise bar. Confirm the indemnity expressly survives any general exclusion of consequential or indirect loss if that is intended

## Self-Check

- Does the clause distinguish indemnify, defend, and hold harmless, and does it state when the defense duty attaches?
- Is the scope explicitly third-party, direct, or both, and is the triggering event enumerated and tied to a causal connector?
- Are caps, baskets (true deductible vs. tipping), and survival periods stated, internally consistent, and appropriately carved out for fundamental breaches, IP, tax, and fraud?
- Is the notice condition specified (form, timing, and whether late notice is a strict bar or prejudice-based)?
- Are defense counsel selection, control of defense, settlement consent (especially for non-monetary terms or admissions), and cooperation duties addressed?
- Has the drafter confirmed governing law does not impose an anti-indemnity limit that voids the clause (construction, environmental, consumer, employment)?
- Does the indemnity coordinate with insurance (additional insured, waiver of subrogation, primary/non-contributing) and with the limitation of liability clause?
- Are defense costs expressly inside or outside the cap, and is the consequence of a finding of no liability addressed?
- For mutual indemnities, is the symmetry intentional, or has one party inadvertently assumed broader triggers?
- Has the agent flagged that enforceability is jurisdiction-dependent and recommended qualified counsel review for high-value or atypical allocations?
