# Deal Structure

Deal structure is the architecture that determines who buys what, who keeps which liabilities, how the deal is taxed, what approvals are needed, and what happens after closing. Agents often default to a familiar structure, usually a stock purchase or a merger, without tracing how the choice affects liability transfer, tax efficiency, contract assignability, employee treatment, regulatory approvals, and post-closing integration. The wrong structure can turn a good deal into a bad one: a stock deal that inherits hidden tax liabilities, an asset deal that fails to assign key contracts, a merger that triggers unexpected change-of-control payments, or a structure that requires antitrust clearance the parties did not anticipate.

Use this skill before issuing a term sheet or letter of intent, before choosing stock versus asset versus merger, before planning a spin-off or carve-out, or when evaluating whether a proposed structure achieves the parties' goals. The goal is to force the agent to compare structures across liability, tax, approvals, speed, and post-closing obligations, and to recognize when a hybrid or alternative structure serves the deal better. Structure is jurisdiction- and fact-specific; final decisions require tax, corporate, and regulatory counsel.

## Core Rules

### Compare Stock, Asset, And Merger Structures Across All Axes

The three primary acquisition structures differ fundamentally in what transfers, what liabilities are inherited, and what consents are required.

Stock purchase: the buyer acquires the target's equity and steps into its shoes, inheriting all assets, contracts, and liabilities, known and unknown. Pros include continuity, no need to retitle assets, and no third-party consents for asset transfer. Cons include inherited liabilities and the need for 100 percent shareholder approval or a squeeze-out mechanism.

Asset purchase: the buyer acquires specified assets and assumes specified liabilities. Pros include cherry-picking assets and avoiding unwanted liabilities, and tax basis step-up. Cons include the need to assign each contract, retitle assets, transfer employees, and obtain consents, and the risk of successor liability.

Merger: a statutory combination where one entity absorbs another. Pros include speed, no need for individual asset transfers, and the ability to squeeze out minorities with a statutory merger. Cons include inherited liabilities, shareholder approval requirements, and appraisal rights.

A strong analysis names the structure and explains the trade-offs. A weak analysis picks one without comparison.

### Map Liability Transfer And Successor Liability

The core question is which liabilities the buyer inherits. This drives both price and risk allocation.

Analyze:

- for stock and merger, all liabilities transfer by default;
- for asset deals, only assumed liabilities transfer, but watch for successor liability exceptions;
- successor liability theories: de facto merger, mere continuation, fraud, express assumption, product line;
- tax liabilities that follow the business regardless of structure;
- environmental liabilities that can attach to property and operators;
- employment and benefit obligations, including WARN and benefit plan transfer rules;
- product liability for products sold before closing;
- litigation and regulatory matters.

Use indemnities, escrows, holdbacks, and reps to allocate residual liability risk, but recognize that contractual allocation does not bind third parties who may sue the buyer directly.

### Evaluate Tax Consequences For Buyer And Seller

Tax is often the decisive factor in structure choice, and the parties' interests diverge. Sellers generally prefer stock sales for capital gains treatment and single-level tax. Buyers generally prefer asset sales for basis step-up and future depreciation deductions.

Evaluate:

- seller-level tax (corporate-level tax on asset sales for C corporations, double tax);
- seller shareholder-level tax;
- buyer basis in assets or stock;
- depreciation and amortization benefits;
- net operating loss and credit utilization and limitations (section 382);
- transfer taxes on real property and assets;
- sales and use tax on asset transfers;
- employment taxes and benefit plan continuation;
- international tax, withholding, and treaty issues;
- tax opinions and reporting requirements.

A structure that is tax-efficient for one party is often inefficient for the other. Negotiate the tax price into the deal economics.

### Identify And Plan For Required Approvals And Consents

Every structure requires approvals, and the approval matrix affects timing and deal certainty. Missing a consent can delay or kill a deal.

Identify:

- board and shareholder approvals for both parties;
- appraisal or dissenters' rights for minority shareholders;
- third-party consents for contract assignment or change of control;
- regulatory approvals: antitrust, foreign investment, sector-specific;
- landlord consents for lease assignment;
- lender consents and debt payoff or assumption;
- license and permit transfers;
- works council or labor consultations;
- court or bankruptcy court approvals for distressed deals.

Build a consent roadmap into the signing-to-closing timeline and condition closing on key consents. Antitrust and foreign investment reviews can take months and require early planning.

### Consider Alternative And Hybrid Structures

Not every deal fits stock, asset, or merger. Alternative structures can solve specific problems.

Consider:

- reverse triangular merger to avoid third-party consents while preserving the target;
- forward merger for simplicity;
- carve-out or spin-off for divestitures, with tax-free versus taxable structures;
- joint venture for partial combination without full merger;
- earnout to bridge valuation gaps;
- roll-over equity to align seller incentives post-closing;
- option or staggered acquisition for staged control;
- pre-packaged bankruptcy for distressed acquisitions;
- holding company or umbrella reorganizations.

Each alternative has its own tax, liability, and approval profile. Do not default to the simplest structure when a tailored one serves the deal.

### Address Employees, Benefits, And Culture Integration

People are central to most deals, and structure affects how they transfer.

Address:

- asset deal treatment: employees typically terminate with the seller and are rehired by the buyer, raising WARN, benefits, and severance issues;
- stock and merger: employees continue, but change-of-control triggers may accelerate severance or equity;
- benefit plan continuation, spin-offs, and termination;
- collective bargaining and works council obligations;
- retention agreements and key employee terms;
- equity award treatment: cash-out, assumption, or replacement;
- immigration and visa transfers;
- compensation harmonization and integration.

Failure to plan employee transitions can trigger liability and lose the talent the buyer paid for.

### Plan Post-Closing Obligations And Integration

Structure does not end at closing. Post-closing obligations define the real risk.

Plan:

- transition services for functions not yet integrated;
- earnout metrics and dispute resolution;
- escrow and holdback release conditions;
- indemnity survival periods and baskets;
- working capital adjustments and true-ups;
- tax matters and cooperation agreements;
- integration milestones and governance;
- non-compete and non-solicit enforceability;
- data migration and system integration;
- regulatory commitments and consent orders.

A clean signing with a messy post-closing can destroy deal value. Structure the integration into the deal documents.

### Balance Speed, Certainty, And Risk

Structure is a tradeoff among closing speed, deal certainty, and risk transfer. A two-step merger closes fast but may inherit more risk. An asset deal transfers less risk but requires more consents and time.

Explicitly weigh:

- time to signing and closing;
- risk of regulatory delay or block;
- risk of consent denial;
- risk of minority dissent;
- risk of post-closing liabilities;
- integration complexity;
- tax efficiency loss or gain.

Document the tradeoffs so the parties choose with eyes open.

## Common Traps

### Defaulting To A Familiar Structure

Stock deals and mergers are common but not always right. Compare structures before choosing.

### Ignoring Inherited Liabilities In Stock And Merger Deals

These structures inherit all liabilities. Diligence and indemnity must cover the unknowns.

### Assuming Asset Deals Avoid All Liability

Successor liability, tax, environmental, employment, and product liability can follow assets. Structure does not fully shield the buyer.

### Overlooking Contract Assignment And Change-Of-Control Consents

Consents can delay, reprice, or kill a deal. Map them early.

### Misjudging Tax Efficiency

A structure good for the seller is often bad for the buyer. Negotiate the tax cost into price.

### Missing Antitrust And Foreign Investment Filings

These reviews can take months and block deals. Plan filings before signing.

### Underestimating Employee And Benefit Complexity

Asset deals create termination and rehire issues; stock deals trigger change-of-control payments. Cost and plan the transition.

### Ignoring Post-Closing Integration

Structure that ignores transition services, earnouts, escrows, and indemnity survival creates post-closing disputes and value loss.

## Self-Check

- Are stock, asset, and merger structures compared across liability transfer, tax, approvals, speed, and post-closing obligations?
- Is liability transfer mapped for the chosen structure, including successor liability, tax, environmental, employment, product, litigation, and regulatory liabilities?
- Are tax consequences analyzed for both buyer and seller, including entity-level tax, shareholder tax, basis, depreciation, NOLs, transfer taxes, and international issues?
- Is an approval and consent roadmap built, covering boards, shareholders, dissenters, third-party contracts, regulators, landlords, lenders, licenses, and labor?
- Are alternative and hybrid structures considered, such as reverse triangular merger, carve-out, spin-off, joint venture, earnout, roll-over equity, or pre-packaged bankruptcy?
- Are employee transitions, benefit plans, change-of-control triggers, retention, equity awards, collective bargaining, and immigration addressed and costed?
- Are post-closing obligations structured, including transition services, earnouts, escrows, indemnity survival, working capital adjustments, tax cooperation, and integration milestones?
- Are speed, certainty, and risk tradeoffs documented and consciously chosen?
- Does the output avoid asserting that a structure is tax-efficient or liability-protective without confirmation by tax and corporate counsel for the specific facts and jurisdictions?
