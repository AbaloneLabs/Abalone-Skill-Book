# Earnouts And Post-Closing Adjustments

Deferred and contingent purchase price mechanisms are where sellers and buyers most often end up in post-closing litigation. An earnout or working capital adjustment looks like a pricing device, but it is really a governance arrangement: it requires the buyer to operate the business in a way that affects the seller's payout, and it requires the parties to measure, dispute, and pay based on definitions that were drafted before the future was known. Agents often focus on the headline earnout percentage or the working capital target and under-specify the operating covenants, the accounting definitions, the information and audit rights, the dispute resolution mechanism, and the protections against buyer conduct that suppresses the earnout. The judgment problem is to recognize that any deferred payment creates a relationship of dependency and conflict, and to draft the machinery that makes measurement fair and disputes resolvable.

Use this skill before structuring an earnout or post-closing adjustment, before drafting the relevant provisions in a purchase agreement, before negotiating the operating covenants and dispute mechanics, or before advising a party on a post-closing true-up dispute. The goal is to force the agent to define the metrics precisely, allocate operational control and risk, build information and audit rights, and design a dispute mechanism that is workable when the parties are already in conflict. Earnouts and adjustments are heavily litigated and jurisdiction-specific; structuring and disputes should be reviewed by qualified M&A counsel.

## Core Rules

### Define The Metric And Its Components With Precision

The earned amount turns entirely on how the metric is defined. Vague definitions guarantee disputes.

Specify:

- the exact financial measure (revenue, gross profit, EBITDA, net income, free cash flow) and its components;
- whether the measure is GAAP, IFRS, or a custom definition, and which version applies;
- the deductions, exclusions, and add-backs, with examples;
- the treatment of one-time, non-recurring, extraordinary, and related-party items;
- the treatment of items arising from the buyer's integration or restructuring;
- the measurement period and whether it is calendar, fiscal, or trailing;
- multiple periods, thresholds, caps, and acceleration triggers.

"EBITDA as determined by the buyer" is a dispute waiting to happen. Define the accounting policy, the baseline, and the treatment of integration costs explicitly.

### Allocate Operational Control And Risk

During an earnout, the buyer controls the business but the seller's payout depends on performance. This creates inherent conflict.

Address:

- whether the buyer covenant to operate the business in the ordinary course, consistent with past practice, or with specific commercial reasonableness;
- whether the buyer must use commercially reasonable efforts to achieve the earnout targets;
- the buyer's freedom to make strategic decisions (acquisitions, restructuring, product changes) that affect the metric;
- capital expenditure commitments and required investment levels;
- staffing, key employee retention, and customer relationship obligations;
- the treatment of actions taken in the buyer's broader interest that suppress the earnout.

Operating covenants are the heart of an earnout. A buyer with unfettered discretion can restructure or starve the business and defeat the earnout; a seller with too much control undermines the buyer's ownership. Balance the covenants to the deal's risk allocation.

### Build Information, Reporting, And Audit Rights

The seller cannot verify the earnout without access to the books. Information rights must be robust.

Provide:

- regular financial statements and earnout calculations delivered on a set schedule;
- access to the general ledger, supporting schedules, and underlying records;
- the right to audit or engage accountants to review the calculations;
- the cost allocation for audits and the consequences of material discrepancies;
- notice of, and consultation on, significant operational or accounting changes;
- protection of competitively sensitive information through clean teams or aggregation.

Without strong information and audit rights, the seller is dependent on the buyer's self-reported numbers. Build the rights to make verification real, not theoretical.

### Design A Workable Dispute Resolution Mechanism

Earnout and adjustment disputes are common, and the mechanism must function when the parties are already adversarial.

Structure:

- a short cure and negotiation period for disputed amounts;
- escalation to a neutral accountant or expert for accounting disputes, with defined scope and binding effect;
- arbitration or expert determination for broader disputes, with limits on discovery and timing;
- the treatment of good faith disagreements versus errors;
- payment under protest and escrow release pending resolution;
- the allocation of dispute costs.

A mechanism that requires full litigation to resolve a working capital dispute defeats the purpose of a quick true-up. Expert or accountant determination is standard for accounting-defined disputes.

### Structure Working Capital And Net Debt Adjustments Carefully

Working capital and net debt adjustments are the most common post-closing true-up, and they turn on definitions and mechanics.

Address:

- the target working capital and how the closing estimate is determined;
- the items included and excluded (cash, debt, prepaid expenses, accrued liabilities);
- the treatment of transaction-related expenses and closing-date items;
- the post-closing true-up based on final measured working capital;
- a collar or threshold below which no adjustment is made;
- the interaction with escrow or holdback to fund potential adjustments.

Inconsistent definitions between the working capital target, the closing estimate, and the final measurement create disputes. Use identical definitions and worked examples.

### Manage Escrow, Holdback, And Indemnity Interaction

Deferred payments interact with escrows and indemnities, and the interaction must be coherent.

Coordinate:

- whether the earnout or adjustment is held in escrow or paid at closing and trued up;
- whether escrow funds are available for indemnity claims and earnout shortfalls, and the priority;
- the release schedule for escrow and the conditions;
- whether indemnity caps and baskets apply to earnout-related claims;
- the survival of earnout and operating covenants beyond general reps and warranties.

An escrow that is drained by indemnity claims before the earnout is measured leaves the seller unpaid. Define the priority and allocation clearly.

### Anticipate Integration And Changed Circumstances

Earnouts span future periods during which the business and market will change. Draft for the changes that are foreseeable.

Consider:

- the effect of acquisitions, divestitures, or reorganizations on the metric;
- the treatment of new products, customers, or geographies;
- currency effects for cross-border metrics;
- the effect of changes in accounting standards or tax law;
- force majeure, regulatory change, and macroeconomic disruption;
- a catch-up or true-up if the buyer sells the business before the earnout period ends.

A buyer's sale of the business mid-earnout is a common trap. Address acceleration, deemed achievement, or carry-over of the earnout to the buyer.

## Common Traps

### Vague Metric Definitions

"EBITDA as determined by the buyer" guarantees disputes. Define components, accounting policy, and add-backs explicitly.

### Weak Or Missing Operating Covenants

A buyer with unfettered discretion can suppress the earnout. Covenant commercial reasonableness or ordinary course operation.

### Inadequate Information And Audit Rights

The seller cannot verify the payout without access to books and audit rights. Build them robustly.

### No Workable Dispute Mechanism

A dispute that requires full litigation defeats the true-up. Use expert or accountant determination for accounting disputes.

### Inconsistent Working Capital Definitions

Target, closing estimate, and final measurement must use identical definitions. Add worked examples.

### Escrow Drained By Indemnity Before Earnout

Define priority between indemnity claims and earnout shortfalls so the seller is not left unpaid.

### Ignoring Integration And Changed Circumstances

Acquisitions, reorganizations, currency, and a mid-earnout sale change the metric. Draft for foreseeable changes.

## Self-Check

- Is the earnout or adjustment metric defined with exact components, accounting basis, deductions, add-backs, measurement period, thresholds, and caps?
- Are operating covenants allocating control and risk, including ordinary course operation, commercially reasonable efforts, capital expenditure, and key employee retention?
- Are information, reporting, and audit rights robust enough for the seller to verify the calculation, with cost allocation and discrepancy consequences?
- Is the dispute resolution mechanism workable when parties are adversarial, with expert or accountant determination for accounting disputes?
- Are working capital and net debt adjustments defined consistently across target, closing estimate, and final measurement, with collars and worked examples?
- Is the escrow, holdback, and indemnity interaction coordinated, with priority, release schedule, and survival defined?
- Are integration, acquisitions, divestitures, currency, accounting changes, force majeure, and mid-earnout sale addressed?
- Does the output recommend qualified M&A counsel review of the earnout and adjustment structure and the dispute mechanics?
