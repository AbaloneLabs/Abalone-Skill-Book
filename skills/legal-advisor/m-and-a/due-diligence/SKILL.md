# Due Diligence

Due diligence is the investigation that turns a deal story into verified facts. A seller presents the business in its best light; diligence tests whether the reality matches. Agents often treat diligence as a document collection exercise, gathering contracts and financials into a data room without analyzing what they reveal. The danger is that the buyer pays for assets the seller does not own, assumes liabilities that are not on the balance sheet, inherits litigation or regulatory exposure, or discovers after closing that key contracts terminate on change of control, that IP is owned by founders, or that revenue is concentrated in one customer who is leaving. Diligence that does not change the deal price, structure, reps, escrow, or walk-away decision has not done its job.

Use this skill before signing a letter of intent, before opening a data room, before issuing a diligence report, before relying on a third-party diligence provider, or before recommending a deal proceed. The goal is to force the agent to scope diligence to the deal's risk surface, read documents for what they actually say, distinguish verified facts from representations, and report findings with their financial and legal significance. Diligence is fact- and jurisdiction-specific; material findings must be reviewed by qualified counsel and advisors.

## Core Rules

### Scope Diligence To The Deal's Risk Surface

Not every deal needs the same diligence. Scope is driven by deal type, industry, size, assets, and risk tolerance. A stock purchase that inherits all liabilities needs deeper diligence than an asset purchase that cherry-picks assets.

Scope across:

- corporate existence, authority, and capitalization;
- financial statements, controls, and projections;
- material contracts and change-of-control clauses;
- customers, concentration, and pipeline;
- employees, benefits, and labor matters;
- intellectual property ownership and licenses;
- litigation, investigations, and claims;
- regulatory compliance and licenses;
- real estate and personal property;
- tax filings, positions, and exposures;
- data privacy and cybersecurity;
- environmental, health, and safety;
- insurance coverage and claims history;
- related-party transactions;
- product liability and warranties.

For each area, define what is in scope, who reviews it, what the key questions are, and what would constitute a red flag. A scoping memo prevents both over-diligence that kills the deal timeline and under-diligence that misses material risk.

### Distinguish Asset, Stock, And Merger Diligence

The deal structure determines which liabilities transfer and therefore what diligence must cover.

- Stock purchase or merger: the buyer generally inherits the company and all its liabilities, known and unknown. Diligence must be comprehensive, including contingent and off-balance-sheet liabilities.
- Asset purchase: the buyer typically acquires only specified assets and assumed liabilities. Diligence focuses on title to assets, assignability of contracts, and any liabilities that follow the assets by law.
- Merger of equals: both sides diligence each other, and integration risk is as important as liability risk.

Match diligence depth to structure. A stock deal with shallow diligence is the most dangerous combination.

### Read Contracts For Change-Of-Control, Assignment, And Termination Triggers

The single most overlooked diligence finding is a contract that terminates or allows termination on change of control or assignment. A target whose revenue depends on contracts that evaporate at closing is worth far less than its financials suggest.

For each material contract, extract:

- change-of-control termination rights;
- assignment restrictions and consent requirements;
- term, renewal, and notice deadlines;
- exclusivity, non-compete, and most-favored-nation clauses;
- minimum commitments and take-or-pay terms;
- pricing changes and volume tiers;
- indemnities, liability caps, and exclusions;
- IP ownership and license scope;
- data processing and security obligations;
- governing law, forum, and dispute clauses;
- defaults and cure periods;
- side letters and amendments.

Flag every contract that requires consent to assign or that terminates on the deal. These drive the closing conditions and the purchase price adjustment.

### Verify Ownership Of Core Assets, Especially IP

Many deals discover that the target does not own what it sells. IP ownership is the classic problem: founders who did not assign IP, contractors who retain rights, open-source components that taint the product, and licenses that are non-transferable.

Verify:

- chain of title for patents, trademarks, copyrights, and domains;
- assignments from founders, employees, and contractors;
- work-for-hire and present-tense assignment language;
- open-source usage and copyleft risk;
- trademark registrations and clearance;
- trade secret protection measures;
- license agreements and their transferability;
- encumbrances, liens, and security interests;
- joint ownership and third-party claims.

A target whose core product is built on IP it does not own is a fundamentally different valuation.

### Quantify Liabilities, Including Contingent And Off-Balance-Sheet

The balance sheet shows recorded liabilities; diligence must find the rest. Contingent liabilities are where deals go wrong.

Investigate:

- pending and threatened litigation;
- government investigations and inquiries;
- product liability and warranty claims;
- environmental remediation obligations;
- tax positions and uncertain tax positions;
- underfunded pension and benefit obligations;
- employment claims and severance exposure;
- lease and contract termination penalties;
- indemnification obligations to customers or partners;
- data breach notification and regulatory exposure;
- guarantees and contingent obligations;
- related-party debts.

For each, estimate the range of outcomes and the probability, and decide whether to seek a specific indemnity, escrow, or price adjustment.

### Check Employees, Benefits, And Change-Of-Control Triggers

People are often the real asset in a deal, and employee issues are a frequent source of post-closing surprises.

Review:

- employment agreements, offer letters, and policies;
- change-of-control and severance triggers and acceleration;
- equity and bonus obligations at closing;
- benefit plans, including 401(k), health, and equity;
- collective bargaining and works council obligations;
- pending employment claims and investigations;
- worker classification (employee versus contractor);
- immigration sponsorship obligations;
- retention and key employee risk;
- benefit plan funding and compliance.

Quantify the cost of change-of-control payments and retention, because they affect the deal economics.

### Manage The Data Room, Confidentiality, And Clean Teams

Diligence involves exchanging sensitive information. The process itself creates confidentiality, antitrust, and operational risk.

Establish:

- a confidentiality or NDA before any disclosure;
- a structured data room with version control and access logs;
- clean team arrangements for competitively sensitive information;
- restrictions on downloading and forwarding;
- antitrust counsel review where competitors are involved;
- escalation for missing or inconsistent documents;
- a Q&A process with the seller;
- tracking of every document reviewed and finding raised.

Document what was reviewed and what was not. A documented diligence trail supports the reliance defense if a problem surfaces later.

### Report Findings With Significance And Limitations

A diligence report that lists documents without explaining their significance is useless to decision-makers. Translate findings into deal impact.

For each material finding, report:

- the fact and its source;
- the legal, financial, or operational significance;
- the range of potential outcomes;
- whether it is confirmed, inferred, or unresolved;
- the recommended response (price adjustment, indemnity, escrow, condition, walk-away);
- the residual risk if the recommendation is accepted;
- any limitation on the diligence scope or reliance.

State clearly what diligence did not cover and that no diligence can eliminate all risk. Reliance on diligence is not a guarantee.

## Common Traps

### Collecting Documents Without Analyzing Them

A full data room that nobody reads provides false comfort. Diligence is analysis, not collection.

### Ignoring Change-Of-Control Clauses

Contracts that terminate at closing can destroy the value the buyer thought it was acquiring. Read every material contract for these triggers.

### Assuming The Target Owns Its IP

Founder, contractor, and open-source gaps are common. Verify chain of title before relying on IP value.

### Missing Contingent And Off-Balance-Sheet Liabilities

Litigation, tax positions, environmental, and benefit obligations often sit off the balance sheet. Quantify them.

### Overreliance On Seller Representations

A representation is not a substitute for verification. If it matters, confirm it independently and back it with indemnity or escrow.

### Confusing Scope With Depth

A long checklist with shallow review misses more than a short checklist with deep review. Prioritize by risk.

### Ignoring Integration And Consent Requirements

Contracts requiring consent to assign can delay or block closing and shift leverage to customers and partners.

### Reporting Without Significance

A finding that does not translate into price, structure, or risk allocation does not help the decision-maker.

## Self-Check

- Is diligence scoped to the deal type, industry, size, and risk surface, with a scoping memo defining areas, reviewers, key questions, and red flags?
- Does diligence depth match the structure, with comprehensive review for stock and merger deals and focused title and assignability review for asset deals?
- Are material contracts read for change-of-control, assignment, term, exclusivity, IP, data, and dispute clauses, with consent requirements flagged?
- Is IP ownership verified through chain of title, assignments, work-for-hire, open-source, trademarks, trade secrets, and encumbrances?
- Are contingent and off-balance-sheet liabilities identified and quantified, including litigation, investigations, tax, environmental, benefits, and guarantees?
- Are employee, benefit, change-of-control acceleration, retention, and labor obligations reviewed and costed?
- Are the data room, NDAs, clean team, antitrust, access controls, Q&A, and document trail managed and documented?
- Does the report translate findings into significance, range of outcomes, recommended response, and residual risk, with limitations stated?
- Are seller representations backed by independent verification, indemnity, or escrow where material?
- Does the output avoid asserting that diligence eliminated all risk and instead recommend qualified counsel review of material findings?
