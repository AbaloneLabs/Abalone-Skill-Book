# Purchase Agreement And Closing

The purchase agreement is where the deal's risk is actually allocated. A term sheet captures intent; the definitive agreement captures liability. Agents often focus on price and structure and underweight the provisions that determine who bears loss when the deal goes wrong: the representations and warranties, the closing conditions, the pre-closing covenants, the termination rights, and the indemnification mechanics. A buyer that accepts broad reps with a high basket and short survival discovers after closing that it has no effective remedy for a breach. A seller that grants open-ended indemnity discovers it has effectively guaranteed the business. Closing itself is where small drafting errors, missing consents, broken conditions, or misallocated payments can delay or unwind a signed deal.

Use this skill before drafting a definitive agreement, before negotiating reps and indemnity, before preparing a closing checklist, or when diagnosing why a signed deal is stuck before closing. The goal is to force the agent to read the agreement as a risk allocation system where every provision interacts, and to treat closing as a managed process, not a single event. Definitive agreements are jurisdiction-specific and high-stakes; they must be prepared and negotiated by qualified M&A counsel.

## Core Rules

### Read The Agreement As An Integrated Risk Allocation System

The purchase agreement's provisions interact. A favorable rep is worthless if the indemnity basket is too high, the survival period too short, or the cap too low. A strong indemnity is undermined by a broad knowledge qualifier that strips the rep of meaning. Review the agreement as a whole.

Map the interaction of:

- representations and warranties;
- knowledge qualifiers and bring-downs;
- closing conditions and the gap between signing and closing;
- pre-closing covenants and conduct of business;
- termination rights and break fees;
- indemnification, baskets, caps, and survival;
- escrows, holdbacks, and earnouts;
- working capital adjustments and purchase price mechanics;
- restrictive covenants and non-competes;
- dispute resolution and governing law.

A change in one provision should trigger review of the others.

### Scrutinize Representations And Warranties By Category

Reps are the seller's statements of fact about the business. They are the primary risk-shifting device and the basis for indemnity claims. Read each rep for scope, qualifiers, and disclosure.

Evaluate by category:

- corporate authority and capitalization;
- financial statements and no undisclosed liabilities;
- material contracts and change-of-control;
- title to assets and IP ownership;
- litigation and compliance;
- employees and benefits;
- tax matters;
- customers and suppliers;
- insurance;
- data privacy and cybersecurity;
- environmental matters;
- related-party transactions;
- full and fair disclosure.

For each, ask: is the scope appropriate to the deal size and risk? Is there a knowledge qualifier that narrows it? Is there a materiality scrape that broadens recovery? Are the disclosures consistent with diligence findings? Reps that conflict with diligence are a red flag.

### Manage Closing Conditions And The Signing-To-Closing Gap

Between signing and closing, conditions must be satisfied. The gap is where deals die or get renegotiated. Define conditions precisely.

Cover:

- accuracy of reps at signing and bring-down at closing, with materiality qualifiers;
- performance of pre-closing covenants;
- absence of litigation or injunctions;
- regulatory approvals and antitrust clearance;
- third-party consents;
- no material adverse effect or change;
- financing conditions, if any;
- officer's certificates and legal opinions;
- closing deliverables and funds flow.

A loosely drafted MAE clause can be either a trap for the buyer or unenforceable. Define material adverse effect carefully and consider carveouts for industry-wide and macroeconomic conditions.

### Define Pre-Closing Covenants And Conduct Of Business

Between signing and closing, the seller usually covenants to operate the business in the ordinary course. This protects the buyer from pre-closing value destruction.

Specify:

- ordinary course operation;
- restrictions on major actions (acquisitions, asset sales, debt, equity issuance);
- restrictions on hiring, compensation, and benefits changes;
- restrictions on capital expenditures;
- customer and supplier relationship maintenance;
- required buyer consent for specified actions;
- access to information and cooperation;
- integration planning;
- no solicitation of employees or customers;
- public announcement coordination.

The buyer wants tight covenants; the seller wants flexibility. The negotiated balance should reflect the expected gap length and integration plan.

### Allocate Indemnification Risk With Baskets, Caps, And Survival

Indemnification is the post-closing remedy for breaches. Its mechanics determine whether the remedy is real or illusory.

Define:

- indemnifying and indemnified parties;
- scope of general indemnity for rep breaches;
- special indemnities for specific known risks;
- baskets: deductible versus tipping basket;
- caps and super-caps for fundamental reps and fraud;
- survival periods, with longer periods for tax and fundamental reps;
- exclusions for losses recovered from insurance or third parties;
- defense and settlement control for third-party claims;
- notice and cooperation obligations;
- sole remedy provisions and fraud carveouts;
- escrow or holdback as the funding source.

A high basket with a short survival period effectively bars most claims. Match the indemnity package to the deal risk and the diligence findings.

### Handle Termination Rights And Break Fees

Not every signed deal closes. Termination rights allocate the cost of failure.

Address:

- termination for failure of closing conditions by a date certain;
- termination for breach uncured after notice;
- termination for regulatory block;
- termination fees and expense reimbursement;
- reverse break fees for regulatory failure;
- effect of termination on surviving obligations;
- non-circumvention and standstill post-termination;
- specific performance as an alternative remedy.

Termination fees must be reasonable to be enforceable and should reflect the deal's lost opportunity cost.

### Manage Working Capital, Purchase Price, And Earnouts

Price is not a single number. Working capital adjustments, earnouts, and holdbacks can shift significant value.

Define:

- closing date balance sheet and working capital target;
- post-closing true-up mechanics and dispute resolution;
- escrow or holdback for adjustments;
- earnout metrics, measurement, and access to information;
- earnout dispute resolution and good faith obligations;
- seller's post-closing obligations to support earnout;
- anti-manipulation protections;
- treatment of contingent payments for tax.

Earnouts are heavily litigated. Draft metrics, defaults, and dispute procedures with precision.

### Prepare And Execute The Closing Checklist

Closing is a process with many moving deliverables. A missed deliverable delays closing and can breach the agreement.

Build a closing checklist:

- conditions precedent satisfied or waived;
- officer's certificates and incumbency;
- legal opinions, with assumptions and qualifications;
- consents and waivers collected;
- equity and asset transfer documents;
- bills of sale and assignments;
- employee documents and benefit transfers;
- IP assignments and recordations;
- lease and contract assumptions;
- funds flow and wire instructions, verified against fraud;
- escrow agreements;
- press releases and notifications;
- post-closing obligations and transition agreements.

Verify wire instructions through a known channel to avoid business email compromise fraud, which has caused massive losses at closing.

### Plan Post-Closing Integration And Dispute Resolution

The agreement governs post-closing conduct. Plan for it.

Address:

- integration milestones and governance;
- transition services and their duration;
- tax cooperation and filings;
- indemnity claim procedures;
- dispute resolution and arbitration;
- restrictive covenants and their enforceability;
- retention and earnout support;
- data migration and system access.

## Common Traps

### Overlooking Rep-Qualifier Interactions

A broad rep with a knowledge qualifier or materiality qualifier can be far narrower than it appears. Read qualifiers carefully.

### Accepting A High Basket And Short Survival

These can make indemnity illusory. Match baskets and survival to deal risk.

### Drafting A Vague MAE Clause

An unclear material adverse effect clause invites litigation. Define it with carveouts and thresholds.

### Ignoring The Signing-To-Closing Gap

Conditions and covenants must bridge the gap. A long gap without tight covenants invites value erosion.

### Underestimating Earnout Litigation

Earnouts are heavily disputed. Draft metrics, access, defaults, and dispute resolution with care.

### Missing Or Fraudulent Wire Instructions

Business email compromise at closing causes huge losses. Verify wire instructions through a known channel.

### Letting Consents Slip

Third-party consents condition closing. Track them from signing.

### Assuming The Agreement Allocates Risk To Third Parties

Indemnity binds the parties, not third-party creditors or regulators who may pursue the buyer directly.

## Self-Check

- Is the agreement read as an integrated risk allocation system where reps, qualifiers, conditions, covenants, indemnity, and price mechanics interact?
- Are representations evaluated by category for scope, knowledge qualifiers, materiality scrapes, and consistency with diligence?
- Are closing conditions defined precisely, including rep bring-downs, covenant performance, MAE, regulatory approvals, consents, financing, opinions, and deliverables?
- Are pre-closing covenants scoped to ordinary course with enumerated restrictions and consent rights?
- Is indemnification structured with baskets, caps, survival, special indemnities, escrow funding, defense control, and sole remedy subject to fraud carveouts?
- Are termination rights, break fees, regulatory reverse break fees, and specific performance addressed?
- Are working capital targets, true-ups, escrows, and earnout metrics, access, and dispute resolution drafted precisely?
- Is a closing checklist prepared covering conditions, certificates, opinions, consents, transfer documents, funds flow, and fraud-verified wire instructions?
- Are post-closing integration, transition services, tax cooperation, indemnity claims, and dispute resolution planned?
- Does the output avoid asserting that the agreement is enforceable or sufficient without qualified M&A counsel review of the governing law and specific terms?
