# Incorporation Process

Incorporation is not filing one form and getting a certificate. A validly formed and capitalized entity requires a charter, an organizational agreement, ownership issuance, governance appointments, records, tax registrations, licenses, and ongoing formalities. Many entities are formed but never properly organized, leaving them undercapitalized, missing bylaws, with shares issued without board approval, or operating without required licenses. Agents often treat the certificate of incorporation as the finish line, when it is only the starting line. The result is an entity that looks legitimate but can be pierced, voided, or penalized because the formation package is incomplete.

Use this skill before forming an entity, before organizing a freshly formed entity, before issuing equity, before registering for taxes or licenses, or when auditing whether an existing entity was properly set up. The goal is to force the agent to treat incorporation as a sequence of interdependent steps, each of which can break the entity's validity, liability protection, or tax status. This is jurisdiction-specific; final documents and filings must be prepared or reviewed by qualified corporate counsel in the formation jurisdiction.

## Core Rules

### Treat Formation As A Multi-Step Organizational Sequence

Do not stop at the charter. A complete formation package typically includes formation filings, internal governance documents, ownership issuance, tax and regulatory registrations, and records. Skipping a step creates latent defects.

The sequence usually includes:

- name availability search and reservation;
- articles of incorporation or certificate of formation;
- registered agent appointment;
- filing fee and franchise tax setup;
- organizational consent or meeting;
- bylaws or operating agreement;
- shareholder or member consents;
- board or manager organizational actions;
- issuance of shares or units and acceptance of consideration;
- officers or managers appointed;
- employer identification number;
- tax registrations (income, sales, payroll, franchise);
- business licenses and permits;
- bank account and capitalization records;
- stock ledger or cap table;
- annual report and future filing calendar.

A strong checklist confirms each step is complete and dated. A weak checklist assumes the certificate means the entity is ready to operate.

### Draft The Charter To Match The Business Plan

The charter or certificate is the public formation document and constrains what the entity can do. Generic charters can create problems for financing, class structure, and purpose limitations.

Review:

- corporate purpose (broad versus narrow);
- authorized shares or units and classes;
- par value and authorized but unissued reserves;
- preferred stock authorization if equity financing is planned;
- preemption or anti-dilution provisions where permitted by statute;
- director liability limitation and indemnification provisions;
- name, registered agent, and registered office;
- incorporator and initial directors;
- effective date and delayed effective date if needed;
- special provisions for professional, benefit, or close corporations.

Authorize enough shares and classes to support planned fundraising and equity compensation, but avoid excessive authorized shares that trigger high franchise taxes in some states.

### Complete The Internal Governance Documents

The charter is public; the bylaws or operating agreement govern day-to-day internal rules. An entity without governing documents is hard to administer and vulnerable to disputes.

Ensure execution of:

- bylaws for corporations or operating agreement for LLCs;
- shareholder or voting agreement if multiple owners;
- buy-sell agreement where ownership transfer must be restricted;
- founder vesting and IP assignment agreements;
- indemnification agreements for directors and officers;
- conflict of interest and related-party transaction policy;
- equity incentive plan and related grants;
- recordkeeping and inspection procedures.

For LLCs, the operating agreement can override many statutory defaults, so a missing or generic agreement leaves default rules in place that may not match owner intent.

### Properly Issue Equity And Document Capitalization

Equity issuance is where formation defects most often hide. Shares or units issued without consideration, without board approval, or in violation of securities law can be void or create rescission rights.

For each issuance, document:

- board or manager approval;
- consideration received (cash, property, services, promissory note, cancellation of debt);
- valuation or fair market value for tax purposes;
- securities law exemption relied upon;
- subscription or purchase agreement;
- restrictive legends and transfer restrictions;
- entry in the stock ledger or cap table;
- tax filings such as 83(b) elections where applicable;
- vesting schedule and acceleration terms;
- rights of first refusal and co-sale.

Verify that property or service contributions are properly valued and that services performed before incorporation are treated correctly for tax and ownership purposes.

### Register For Taxes And Obtain Required Licenses

An entity can be validly formed yet illegal to operate because it lacks tax registrations or licenses. These obligations often arise at the federal, state, and local levels simultaneously.

Register for:

- federal employer identification number;
- state income and franchise tax accounts;
- sales and use tax permits where applicable;
- payroll tax withholding and unemployment accounts;
- local business licenses and permits;
- professional or industry-specific licenses;
- alcohol, food, transportation, or other regulated activity permits;
- fictitious or doing-business-as names if operating under another name;
- foreign qualification in states where business is conducted.

Confirm whether the entity has nexus in states beyond its formation state and whether economic nexus rules require sales tax registration.

### Set Up Records, Bank Accounts, And The Annual Calendar

An entity that cannot prove its formalities is vulnerable to veil piercing and disputes. Records and separate finances are part of liability protection.

Establish:

- corporate minute book or electronic equivalent;
- stock ledger or cap table;
- separate bank account with no commingling;
- accounting system and books;
- annual meeting and consent calendar;
- franchise tax and annual report deadlines;
- registered agent renewal;
- license renewals;
- securities law periodic filings for outstanding exemptions.

Commingling funds or failing to hold required meetings are among the most common reasons courts pierce the corporate veil.

### Confirm Foreign And Cross-Border Considerations

If owners, operations, or markets are outside the formation jurisdiction, additional steps may be required.

Check:

- foreign qualification in each operating state or country;
- treaty and tax residency analysis for foreign owners;
- withholding obligations on distributions to nonresidents;
- export control and sanctions screening;
- foreign investment notification or review;
- data localization and transfer rules;
- recognition of the entity form in relevant jurisdictions.

### Verify The Complete Formation Package

Before declaring an entity ready, audit the package end to end. A single missing document can invalidate governance or capitalization.

Confirm:

- filed charter with state stamp or confirmation;
- registered agent acceptance on file;
- bylaws or operating agreement executed;
- organizational consents signed;
- equity issued with consideration and ledger entries;
- EIN obtained;
- tax and license registrations complete;
- bank account opened and separate;
- annual filing calendar set;
- IP assigned to the entity;
- founder and employee agreements signed.

## Common Traps

### Stopping At The Certificate Of Incorporation

Filing the charter alone does not organize the entity. Bylaws, consents, equity issuance, and registrations must follow.

### Issuing Shares Without Board Approval Or Consideration

Unapproved or naked issuances can be void and create securities and tax problems. Document every issuance.

### Using Generic Bylaws Or Operating Agreements

Defaults may contradict owner intent, especially in LLCs where the operating agreement can override statutory defaults. Tailor governance to the business.

### Forgetting 83(b) Elections On Restricted Stock

A missed 83(b) election can convert future appreciation into ordinary income and create a tax trap for founders and employees.

### Ignoring Securities Law Exemptions For Founder Stock

Even founder issuances are securities transactions. Document the exemption and any required filings.

### Commingling Funds

Using the entity account for personal expenses, or vice versa, undermines limited liability and invites veil piercing.

### Missing Foreign Qualification And Licenses

Operating in a state without qualifying, or operating a regulated activity without a license, creates penalties and can bar enforcement of contracts.

### Authorizing Too Few Or Too Many Shares

Too few shares blocks fundraising and equity grants; too many can trigger high franchise taxes and awkward split decisions.

## Self-Check

- Is the formation treated as a full sequence including charter, governance documents, equity issuance, registrations, licenses, and records?
- Does the charter authorize the share classes, par value, purpose, indemnification, and registered agent needed for the business plan?
- Are bylaws or operating agreement, shareholder consents, founder agreements, IP assignment, and equity plan executed?
- Is every equity issuance documented with board approval, consideration, valuation, securities exemption, ledger entry, and any 83(b) election?
- Are EIN, income tax, franchise tax, sales tax, payroll, business license, and any industry-specific registrations complete?
- Are foreign qualification, registered agent, and nexus-based tax registrations addressed for all operating jurisdictions?
- Is a separate bank account established with no commingling, and are books and a stock ledger maintained?
- Is an annual calendar set for franchise tax, annual reports, registered agent renewal, license renewals, and securities filings?
- Are cross-border owner, treaty, withholding, export control, and investment review issues flagged where relevant?
- Does the output avoid asserting final legal sufficiency and instead recommend qualified corporate counsel to prepare or review jurisdiction-specific documents?
