# Founder Agreements

Founder relationships are not held together by goodwill alone. A founder agreement defines who owns what, who does what, what happens when someone leaves, who controls decisions, who owns the intellectual property, and how disputes are resolved. Most startup failures are not market failures; they are founder failures caused by ambiguous equity, missing vesting, no IP assignment, and no exit mechanism. Agents often draft a founder agreement that captures the friendly status quo but ignores the hard scenarios: a founder quits after six months holding 50 percent of fully vested stock, a founder is fired for cause but keeps their shares, a founder starts a competing company, or two founders deadlock and paralyze the company.

Use this skill before incorporating, before issuing founder equity, before a new co-founder joins, or when auditing an existing founder arrangement. The goal is to force the agent to treat the founder agreement as a risk allocation document for the worst-case scenarios, not a record of current enthusiasm. Founder agreements are jurisdiction-specific and involve securities, tax, and employment law; final documents must be prepared or reviewed by qualified counsel.

## Core Rules

### Allocate Equity Deliberately, Not Equally By Default

Equal splits feel fair but are rarely proportional to contribution, risk, or ongoing commitment. Equity should reflect past contribution, future commitment, role, opportunity cost, and cash investment.

Decide:

- total founder equity and the split rationale;
- whether equity is fixed or subject to reallocation;
- treatment of pre-formation contributions (cash, IP, services, relationships);
- treatment of ongoing roles and time commitment (full-time, part-time, advisory);
- treatment of future co-founders and an option pool for employees;
- voting versus economic rights;
- anti-dilution expectations among founders.

Document the rationale. A split that looks arbitrary invites later claims of unfairness or breach of an oral understanding. Consider dynamic equity or milestone-based vesting where contributions are uncertain.

### Impose Vesting On All Founder Equity

Vesting is the single most important protection against a founder departing with a large unearned stake. Without vesting, a founder who leaves early keeps their full share, diluting the remaining founders and discouraging replacement talent.

Define:

- vesting schedule (typically four years with a one-year cliff);
- commencement date and credit for pre-formation time;
- cliff mechanics and what happens if someone leaves before the cliff;
- acceleration on termination without cause, constructive termination, or change of control;
- treatment on death, disability, or involuntary departure;
- repurchase rights and mechanics at fair market value or cost;
- tax treatment and 83(b) elections on restricted stock;
- whether vesting continues after a financing or acquisition.

All founders, including the CEO, should vest. A common error is vesting the junior founder but not the senior one.

### Define Roles, Authority, And Decision-Making

Ambiguous roles create deadlock and resentment. The agreement should allocate responsibilities and decision authority clearly.

Address:

- titles, roles, and primary responsibilities;
- who is CEO and how that can change;
- board composition and founder board seats;
- day-to-day management authority;
- major decision thresholds (financing, sale, IP licensing, hiring executives, compensation, relocation);
- tie-breaking or deadlock resolution;
- time commitment and exclusivity expectations;
- outside activities, board seats, and other ventures;
- relocation or remote work expectations;
- performance expectations and grounds for removal.

Exclusivity matters: a founder who splits time across ventures can starve the company while retaining full equity.

### Build A Departure And Buyout Mechanism

Every founder eventually leaves, by choice, cause, death, disability, or termination. The agreement must define what happens to equity and roles in each case.

Cover:

- voluntary resignation and treatment of vested and unvested equity;
- termination for cause and for-cause definitions;
- termination without cause and severance;
- death, disability, and retirement;
- repurchase rights on departure (cost, fair market value, formula);
- payout terms and security for unpaid balances;
- post-departure consulting or transition duties;
- continued vesting or accelerated vesting on certain departures;
- effect on board seats and officer roles.

Without a buyout mechanism, a departing founder can retain equity indefinitely, blocking financing and diluting active founders.

### Assign All Intellectual Property To The Company

IP assignment is critical and frequently botched. If founders do not assign pre-formation and ongoing IP to the company, the company may not own its own product, and investors will refuse to fund it.

Require:

- assignment of all pre-formation relevant IP to the company;
- automatic assignment or present assignment of future inventions;
- work made for hire language where applicable;
- moral rights and authorship waivers;
- cooperation obligations for registration and enforcement;
- disclosure of prior inventions and excluded IP;
- obligations surviving termination;
- spousal consent where required to protect against community property claims.

Use a present-tense assignment of future IP, not a promise to assign, to avoid disputes over whether a duty to assign was breached.

### Add Restrictive Covenants Appropriate To The Role

Founders have access to confidential information, trade secrets, customers, and strategy. Reasonable restrictive covenants protect the company after departure, subject to jurisdictional enforceability limits.

Consider:

- confidentiality obligations;
- non-solicitation of employees, customers, and partners;
- non-compete during employment and for a limited post-departure period where enforceable;
- non-disparagement;
- return of property and data;
- notice of subsequent employment.

Recognize that non-competes are limited or unenforceable in some jurisdictions, especially for employees and in certain states. Tailor scope to what is likely enforceable and rely on non-solicitation and confidentiality where non-competes are barred.

### Define Voting Rights And Dispute Resolution

Deadlock between co-founders can paralyze and kill a company. The agreement should provide voting structure and a path to break deadlocks.

Address:

- voting rights attached to founder equity;
- protective provisions or veto rights on major decisions;
- supermajority or unanimous consent items;
- drag-along rights to compel sale participation;
- tag-along rights to join a sale;
- right of first refusal on transfers;
- permitted transfers (estate planning, affiliates) and lockups;
- dispute resolution: negotiation, mediation, arbitration, or buy-sell shootout;
- governing law and forum.

A buy-sell or shotgun clause can resolve intractable deadlock but must be drafted carefully to avoid abuse.

### Handle Securities, Tax, And Disclosure Formalities

Founder equity is a securities transaction and has tax consequences. Skipping formalities creates rescission rights and tax traps.

Confirm:

- securities law exemption for founder issuances and any required notices;
- 83(b) elections filed within 30 days for restricted stock;
- valuation support for option grants and 409A compliance;
- written consents and board approvals for all issuances;
- disclosure of all agreements to co-founders and investors;
- tax treatment of property or service contributions;
- independent legal counsel for each founder or waivers documented.

Each founder should ideally have separate counsel, or at least acknowledge the conflict of joint representation, because their interests diverge on vesting, departure, and control.

## Common Traps

### Equal Splits Without Vesting

Equal, fully vested splits feel fair at the start and become toxic when a founder leaves early with a large permanent stake.

### No IP Assignment

Without a present assignment of pre-formation and future IP, the company may not own its core assets, and investors will require remediation that can be expensive or impossible.

### Vesting Only The Junior Founder

Senior founders who do not vest can leave with full equity while junior founders are penalized, creating resentment and litigation.

### Missing 83(b) Elections

A missed 83(b) election on restricted stock converts future appreciation into ordinary income and can create tax liability without cash to pay it.

### No Deadlock Mechanism

Two 50/50 founders with no tie-breaker can deadlock the company into insolvency. Build in escalation, mediation, or a buy-sell clause.

### Overbroad Non-Competes

Non-competes that exceed jurisdictional limits are unenforceable and can taint the whole agreement. Tailor scope and rely on non-solicitation where needed.

### Assuming Goodwill Survives Stress

Friendships do not substitute for written terms. The agreement must work when the founders are in conflict, not only when they agree.

### Joint Counsel For All Founders

Using one lawyer for all founders creates conflicts on vesting, control, and departure. Recommend separate counsel or documented waivers.

## Self-Check

- Is the equity split documented with a rationale reflecting contribution, commitment, role, and investment rather than default equality?
- Does all founder equity vest on a defined schedule with a cliff, and are acceleration, death, disability, and departure scenarios addressed?
- Are roles, titles, CEO designation, decision thresholds, exclusivity, and time commitment clearly allocated?
- Is there a complete departure and buyout mechanism covering voluntary, for-cause, without-cause, death, disability, and retirement scenarios?
- Is all pre-formation and future IP assigned to the company in present tense, with moral rights waivers, cooperation duties, and excluded IP disclosed?
- Are confidentiality, non-solicitation, non-compete, non-disparagement, and property return provisions tailored to enforceable scope?
- Are voting rights, protective provisions, drag-along, tag-along, transfer restrictions, and deadlock or dispute resolution mechanisms defined?
- Are securities exemptions, 83(b) elections, 409A valuations, board consents, and tax treatment of contributions addressed?
- Is separate counsel recommended for each founder, or are conflicts and waivers documented?
- Does the output avoid asserting final enforceability and instead recommend qualified counsel for jurisdiction-specific drafting and review?
