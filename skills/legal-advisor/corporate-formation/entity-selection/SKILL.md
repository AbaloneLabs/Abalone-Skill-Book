# Entity Selection

Entity selection is not picking the most popular company type. The choice of legal form defines who is personally liable for business debts, how profits are taxed, who can make decisions, how ownership is transferred, how money is raised, how the business ends, and which regulator has authority. A choice that works for a solo consultant can be catastrophic for a venture-backed startup, and a choice that suits a real-estate holding vehicle can be wrong for a professional services firm. Agents often recommend an entity based on a single factor such as "limited liability" or "tax pass-through" without tracing how the form interacts with financing plans, exit strategy, owner disputes, industry licensing, and cross-border operations.

Use this skill before a founder files formation documents, before an owner changes entity type, before a foreign business sets up a local subsidiary, or before recommending a structure to a new venture. The goal is to force the agent to compare entity forms across liability, tax, governance, capital, transferability, dissolution, and jurisdiction, and to mark the limits of general guidance. This is jurisdiction-dependent legal and tax advice territory; final selection must be confirmed with qualified corporate counsel and tax advisors admitted in the relevant jurisdiction.

## Core Rules

### Compare Entity Forms Across All Decision Axes

Do not reduce entity selection to liability alone. Each form has a different profile across liability, taxation, management, capital raising, ownership transfer, and dissolution. Compare the realistic candidates for the specific business.

Typical candidates include sole proprietorship, general partnership, limited partnership (LP), limited liability partnership (LLP), limited liability company (LLC), S corporation, C corporation, and in some jurisdictions benefit corporation, professional corporation, or close corporation.

For each candidate, capture:

- personal liability of owners for business debts and torts;
- taxation of profits (pass-through versus entity-level, plus owner-level);
- self-employment or payroll tax exposure;
- management structure and decision authority;
- ability to issue different classes of ownership;
- eligibility for outside investment;
- restrictions on number or type of owners;
- ownership transfer and admission of new owners;
- continuity upon death or withdrawal of an owner;
- formality and recordkeeping burden;
- dissolution and winding-up process;
- state or country of formation and foreign qualification needs.

A strong analysis names two or three realistic candidates and explains why each fits or fails the business. A weak analysis names one form and asserts it is best.

### Match The Form To The Funding And Exit Plan

Capital strategy should drive entity choice as much as tax. Investors, lenders, grantors, and acquirers often have form expectations.

Ask:

- Will the company raise venture capital or institutional equity? C corporations are typically required for preferred stock and standard VC terms.
- Will the company rely on debt, grants, or founder capital? LLCs or S corporations may be more tax-efficient.
- Will owners want to reinvest profits or distribute them? C corporations face double taxation on distributions; pass-through entities avoid entity-level tax but may create owner-level tax on profits not distributed.
- Is an IPO or acquisition a realistic exit? Acquirers and underwriters prefer clean corporate structures.
- Will the company offer equity compensation broadly? Corporations handle options and restricted stock more predictably than LLCs.

A common error is choosing an LLC for a startup that plans to raise VC, then converting later at tax and legal cost. Another is choosing a C corporation for a lifestyle business that will distribute profits, creating avoidable double tax.

### Evaluate Tax Treatment For The Specific Owners

Tax is not a single number. The same entity form can produce very different outcomes depending on owner residency, profit level, loss expectations, distribution policy, and interaction with other income.

Examine:

- pass-through taxation and the QBI deduction where applicable;
- entity-level tax for C corporations and any accumulated earnings concerns;
- owner-level tax on distributions versus allocations;
- self-employment tax for LLC members versus salary plus dividends for S corporation owners;
- treatment of losses and basis limitations;
- passive activity and at-risk rules;
- state franchise, gross receipts, or capital taxes;
- international or expat owner complications;
- conversion or restructuring tax cost.

State clearly that tax outcomes depend on facts that change, and that a tax advisor must run the actual numbers. Do not assert a tax advantage without identifying the assumptions behind it.

### Map Liability Protection And Its Limits

Limited liability is the main reason owners incorporate, but it is not absolute. Agents often treat "limited liability" as a guarantee and ignore the conditions that pierce it.

Identify the limits:

- personal guarantees on loans or leases;
- personal liability for unpaid trust fund taxes, wages, or certain statutory obligations;
- piercing the corporate veil for undercapitalization, commingling, or failure to observe formalities;
- professional malpractice liability for licensed owners;
- fraud or tortious conduct by an owner;
- environmental and regulatory strict liability;
- parent-subsidiary liability in group structures.

Liability protection requires ongoing compliance, not merely formation. Note that a failure to maintain corporate formalities can erase the protection the entity was formed to provide.

### Consider Governance And Control Tradeoffs

Entity form determines default management rules, which can be modified by agreement but only within statutory limits. Control expectations should be explicit.

Review:

- who appoints and removes managers or directors;
- voting thresholds for major decisions;
- deadlocks and tie-breakers;
- fiduciary duties of managers and controlling owners;
- ability to create classes with different voting or economic rights;
- buy-sell and transfer restrictions;
- information and inspection rights;
- dissolution and amendment thresholds.

LLCs offer the most contractual flexibility; corporations impose more statutory defaults. Flexibility is useful but can create ambiguity if the operating agreement is poorly drafted.

### Check Jurisdiction And Foreign Qualification

Where the entity is formed and where it operates both matter. An entity formed in one state or country may need to qualify as foreign in every jurisdiction where it does business.

Check:

- formation jurisdiction and its fees, taxes, and privacy rules;
- states or countries where the entity has employees, property, offices, or substantial sales;
- foreign qualification and registered agent requirements;
- naming availability and trademark conflicts in each jurisdiction;
- nexus for income, sales, and franchise taxes;
- treaties or trade agreements affecting cross-border structures;
- sanctions, export control, and foreign investment review where relevant.

Choosing a formation jurisdiction for low fees while ignoring qualification and tax nexus in operating states can create back taxes and penalties.

### Document The Recommendation And Its Assumptions

A recommendation that omits assumptions is dangerous because the business may change and invalidate the choice. Record the basis for the recommendation.

Capture:

- the business description and ownership;
- the funding and exit assumptions;
- the tax assumptions and which advisor verified them;
- the jurisdictions considered;
- the alternatives rejected and why;
- the follow-up actions required after formation;
- the triggers that should prompt reconsideration, such as raising institutional capital, adding owners, or expanding geographically.

## Common Traps

### Choosing Based On A Single Factor

Selecting an LLC because it is "simple" or a C corporation because it is "what startups do" ignores liability, tax, governance, and capital interactions. Compare candidates across all axes.

### Ignoring Conversion Cost

Forming the wrong entity now to fix later can trigger taxable events, re-documentation, contract consents, and asset transfers. Anticipate the likely trajectory, not only the current state.

### Treating Limited Liability As Absolute

Personal guarantees, trust fund taxes, malpractice, fraud, and veil-piercing can defeat limited liability. The entity must be funded and operated properly to preserve protection.

### Overlooking State-Level Taxes And Formalities

Franchise taxes, annual reports, registered agents, and qualification filings create ongoing obligations. A low-fee formation state can still impose operating-state taxes.

### Assuming Foreign Owners Can Use Any Form

S corporations bar nonresident alien shareholders. LLCs with foreign members can create complex US tax filings for the members. Cross-border structures need specialist review.

### Recommending Without Funding Reality

A C corporation for a bootstrapped lifestyle business creates double tax on distributions. An LLC for a VC-backed startup creates conversion friction. Match form to realistic capital path.

### Forgetting Industry Licensing

Professional corporations, healthcare entities, financial firms, and licensed trades may require specific entity forms or ownership restrictions. General entity rules may not apply.

## Self-Check

- Are at least two or three realistic entity candidates compared across liability, tax, governance, capital, transferability, continuity, and dissolution?
- Does the recommendation match the realistic funding and exit path, including VC, debt, grants, distributions, IPO, or acquisition?
- Are tax outcomes described with explicit assumptions and a recommendation to confirm numbers with a tax advisor?
- Are the limits of limited liability identified, including personal guarantees, trust fund taxes, veil piercing, malpractice, and fraud?
- Are governance and control tradeoffs addressed, including voting thresholds, deadlocks, fiduciary duties, and transfer restrictions?
- Is the formation jurisdiction stated, with foreign qualification, registered agent, naming, and tax nexus considered for operating jurisdictions?
- Are cross-border owner complications, sanctions, and investment review flagged where relevant?
- Are industry licensing restrictions checked before recommending a generic form?
- Does the output document assumptions, rejected alternatives, follow-up actions, and triggers for reconsideration?
- Does the analysis avoid asserting final legal or tax sufficiency and instead recommend qualified counsel and tax advisors in the relevant jurisdiction?
