# Causes Of Action And Defenses

A cause of action is only as strong as its weakest element, and a defense is only as strong as its fit to the elements it attacks. The judgment problem is that most people, including agents, reason about legal claims at the level of labels: "this is breach of contract" or "this is negligence." But a court does not decide labels; it decides whether each required element is met, proven by the party bearing the burden, and not defeated by an applicable defense. A claim that sounds strong in narrative form can collapse when its elements are listed, because one element has no facts, no evidence, or a fatal defense. Conversely, a defense that sounds fair can be legally irrelevant because it does not negate any element or trigger any recognized excuse.

Use this skill before assessing whether a party has a viable claim or defense, before drafting a complaint or answer, before evaluating litigation risk, or before advising on settlement leverage. The goal is to make the agent decompose every claim into elements, allocate the burden of proof, identify every category of defense, check limitations and accrual, and test the whole structure against the available facts. This is analytical support; pleading decisions, litigation strategy, and final risk calls require licensed counsel.

## Core Rules

### Decompose Every Claim Into Its Elements

Every cause of action has required elements, and the plaintiff must prove each one. For breach of contract the classic elements are a valid contract, performance or excuse by the plaintiff, breach by the defendant, and damages. For negligence they are duty, breach, causation, and damages. List the elements for the specific claim under the governing law, because jurisdictions vary in how they state elements and what they require. A strong analysis tests each element against the facts and flags the weakest one. A weak analysis asserts the claim exists without ever naming the elements.

### Allocate The Burden Of Proof For Each Element

The party asserting a claim generally bears the burden of proof, but burdens can shift for specific elements or defenses, and the standard differs between civil and criminal matters. In civil cases the typical standard is a preponderance of the evidence, but some elements or defenses require clear and convincing evidence. Identify who must prove what and to what standard, because an element the plaintiff cannot prove defeats the claim even if the facts feel sympathetic. Burdens on affirmative defenses usually fall on the defendant.

### Distinguish Admissions, Denials, And Affirmative Defenses

Responses to claims fall into functionally different categories, and confusing them weakens both pleading and analysis.

- An admission concedes a fact and removes it from dispute.
- A denial puts a fact in issue and forces the other side to prove it.
- An affirmative defense admits the underlying conduct but asserts a legal reason the defendant should nonetheless prevail, such as statute of limitations, waiver, estoppel, consent, self-defense, or contributory negligence. The defendant typically bears the burden of pleading and proving an affirmative defense.

A strong defense map identifies which elements are denied, which are admitted, and which affirmative defenses apply. A weak map lumps everything into a general denial and misses defenses that must be specifically pleaded or are waived.

### Evaluate Counterclaims And Crossclaims Strategically

A defendant may have claims back against the plaintiff, against co-defendants, or against third parties. Counterclaims can be compulsory, meaning they arise from the same transaction and must be brought in the same action or be lost, or permissive, meaning they can be brought separately. Evaluate whether a counterclaim offsets the plaintiff's demand, creates leverage, or introduces new risk. Compulsory counterclaims are a particularly high-stakes decision because failing to raise them can bar them forever.

### Check Statutes Of Limitations And Accrual Carefully

Limitations periods can be shorter than expected and can run from a date earlier than the injury was discovered. Determine the limitations period for each claim under the governing law, the accrual rule, and any tolling or discovery rule that delays accrual. Some claims accrue on the date of injury, others on the date of discovery, and others on the date of a later event. Fraudulent concealment, minority, disability, and statutory tolling can extend the period. A claim that is otherwise strong is worthless if it is time-barred, so check limitations early and explicitly.

### Identify Jurisdiction-Specific Element Variations

Elements are not uniform. A tort claim may require an additional element in one state that it lacks in another. Some jurisdictions recognize claims others do not, such as certain privacy torts, economic loss rule variations, or fiduciary duty flavors. Always source the elements to the governing jurisdiction rather than assuming a generic formulation. Economic loss rules, in particular, can bar tort recovery for purely economic losses in commercial settings.

### Map Defenses To The Specific Elements They Attack

A defense is only useful if it negates an element, excuses liability, or limits recovery. Map each defense to the element or principle it targets. For example, contributory or comparative negligence attacks causation or damages; assumption of risk attacks duty or breach; waiver and estoppel attack the plaintiff's right to enforce; the statute of frauds attacks contract formation; and the statute of limitations attacks the right to sue at all. A defense that does not map to any element is probably irrelevant and should be discarded or reframed.

### Test The Whole Structure Against The Facts

After listing elements, burdens, and defenses, test the complete structure against the confirmed and disputed facts. Identify which elements are well-supported, which are thin, which defenses are strong, and where the outcome genuinely turns. This element-by-element test is more reliable than a narrative judgment about who is right. It also surfaces the factual disputes that will drive discovery and trial.

## Common Traps

### Asserting A Claim Without Naming Its Elements

A claim label without elements is untestable. Decompose first, then evaluate.

### Assuming The Burden Is Always On The Plaintiff

Burdens shift for affirmative defenses and for some elements. Identify the burden for each element and defense specifically.

### Missing An Affirmative Defense That Must Be Pleading

Some defenses are waived if not raised in the answer. Identify and plead them or risk losing them.

### Forgetting Compulsory Counterclaims

A strong counterclaim that arises from the same transaction must be raised now or may be barred forever. Do not defer it.

### Applying The Wrong Limitations Period Or Accrual Rule

A few months' difference in accrual can bar a claim. Check the period, the accrual rule, and tolling for every claim.

### Using Generic Elements For A Specific Jurisdiction

Elements vary by state. Source the elements to the governing law, not a generic textbook formulation.

### Keeping A Defense That Maps To No Element

A defense that sounds fair but negates nothing wastes effort and can annoy the court. Map each defense to an element or principle.

## Self-Check

- Is each cause of action decomposed into its elements as stated by the governing jurisdiction?
- Is the burden of proof and standard allocated for each element and each affirmative defense?
- Are admissions, denials, and affirmative defenses distinguished, with affirmative defenses identified that must be specifically pleaded?
- Have compulsory and permissive counterclaims been evaluated, with compulsory claims flagged for immediate raising?
- Is the statute of limitations, accrual rule, and any tolling or discovery rule checked for every claim?
- Are jurisdiction-specific element variations and doctrines like the economic loss rule considered?
- Does each defense map to a specific element or principle it negates, excuses, or limits?
- Has the complete elements-burdens-defenses structure been tested against confirmed and disputed facts to find the decisive disputes?
- Does the output escalate pleading, strategy, and final risk decisions to qualified counsel rather than asserting viability conclusively?
