# Statute Of Limitations And Tolling

The limitations period is often the single most decisive issue in a matter, and it is frequently mishandled because it looks simple. Agents often grab the headline number from a statute, apply it to the date of the underlying event, and declare the claim barred or viable, when the real analysis turns on accrual, discovery, tolling, estoppel, the distinction between limitations and repose, and the interaction of multiple limitations regimes when claims, parties, or forums overlap. A limitations defense can dispose of an entire case, but asserting it wrongly, or missing an available tolling doctrine, changes the outcome. The judgment problem is to identify the correct limitations regime for each claim, determine the accrual date and any tolling, and recognize when the analysis is genuinely uncertain.

Use this skill before assessing whether a claim is time-barred, before pleading or moving on limitations grounds, before advising a client on the viability of stale claims, or before coordinating limitations analysis across multiple jurisdictions. The goal is to force the agent to identify the correct period and regime for each claim, determine accrual under the governing rule, evaluate every applicable tolling and estoppel doctrine, and distinguish limitations from repose. Limitations analysis is intensely jurisdiction- and claim-specific; a limitations ruling can end a case and should be confirmed by qualified counsel.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Correct Limitations Regime For Each Claim

Different claims carry different periods, and the wrong regime produces the wrong answer.

Determine for each claim:

- the statutory limitations period that applies to that cause of action in that jurisdiction;
- whether the claim is governed by a special or shorter period (for example, defamation, libel, claims against government entities with notice-of-claim requirements);
- whether a choice-of-law rule points to a different jurisdiction's limitations period;
- whether the claim is subject to a federal or uniform statute with its own period;
- whether the claim is subject to a statute of repose with an absolute outside date.

Do not assume one period covers all claims. A single dispute may involve claims with periods ranging from one year to over a decade, and the shortest period often controls strategy.

### Distinguish Statute Of Limitations From Statute Of Repose

Limitations and repose look similar but operate differently, and the distinction matters.

Recognize:

- a statute of limitations runs from accrual, typically when the claimant could first sue, and is subject to tolling and equitable doctrines;
- a statute of repose runs from a defined event (often the date of an act, delivery, or completion) and sets an absolute outside bar, often not subject to tolling;
- repose periods are common in construction, products liability, and professional malpractice;
- the two may run concurrently, with repose cutting off claims that limitations would otherwise allow after tolling.

A claim that survives limitations analysis may still be barred by repose. Analyze both when both apply.

### Determine Accrual Under The Governing Rule

When the clock starts is often the contested question, and accrual rules differ by claim type and jurisdiction.

Apply the correct accrual rule:

- the injury or harm rule, where the claim accrues when injury occurs, even if the plaintiff does not know the cause;
- the discovery rule, where the claim accrues when the plaintiff knew or should have known of the injury and its cause, common in professional malpractice, latent injury, and fraud;
- continuous treatment or continuous representation tolling, which delays accrual while a professional relationship relevant to the injury continues;
- fraudulent concealment, which delays accrual while the defendant actively conceals the injury;
- breach of contract accrual, typically at breach, not at discovery of damages, unless the contract involves latent defects;
- claims for contribution or indemnity, which often accrue when the underlying liability is established.

Identify the accrual rule for the specific claim and jurisdiction, and gather the facts needed to fix the accrual date.

### Evaluate Every Applicable Tolling Doctrine

Tolling pauses or extends the limitations period, and missing an available doctrine is a common and costly error.

Consider:

- equitable tolling, where the claimant diligently pursued rights but was prevented by circumstances beyond their control;
- equitable estoppel, where the defendant's conduct (such as promises or concealment) induced the claimant to delay;
- statutory tolling for infancy, incapacity, or imprisonment;
- tolling during the pendency of certain prior proceedings;
- class action tolling, which tolls limitations for putative class members during the pendency of a class action;
- tolling agreements, which the parties can execute to extend the period by contract;
- bankruptcy automatic stay, which tolls claims against the debtor;
- government claims with notice-of-claim periods that have their own timing rules.

For each potentially applicable doctrine, confirm the elements, the jurisdiction's acceptance of it, and the factual support. A viable tolling doctrine can rescue an otherwise stale claim.

### Account For Minority, Incapacity, And Government Claim Rules

Special rules apply to certain claimants and defendants and can dramatically change the period.

Check:

- infancy or minority tolling, which often extends the period until the claimant reaches majority, sometimes with a repose cap;
- incapacity tolling for mental incapacity, where accepted;
- notice-of-claim and presentment requirements for claims against government entities, which can be far shorter than ordinary limitations periods and are often jurisdictional;
- anti-slapp or special motion rules that may shorten the effective period to challenge;
- criminal restitution or victim compensation rules that may affect civil timing.

Government claims are a frequent trap: a short notice-of-claim window, sometimes as little as 60 or 90 days, can bar a claim before the ordinary limitations period even becomes relevant.

### Coordinate Across Multiple Claims, Parties, And Forums

When a dispute spans multiple claims or jurisdictions, the limitations analysis must be done claim by claim and party by party.

Map:

- the limitations period and accrual date for each claim;
- the applicable tolling for each claim and party;
- whether a claim asserted against one party relates back to the filing date for later-added parties under the relation-back doctrine;
- whether cross-claims, counterclaims, or third-party claims have their own limitations treatment;
- whether a foreign forum's longer or shorter period applies under choice-of-law rules.

A single complaint may have some claims timely and others barred. Do not treat the limitations analysis as uniform across the pleading.

### Preserve Limitations Defenses And Avoid Waiver

Limitations is an affirmative defense that can be waived if not raised properly.

Confirm:

- the defense is pleaded in the answer or pre-answer motion where required;
- a limitations defense raised in a motion to dismiss is based only on the face of the complaint unless extrinsic evidence is offered, which may convert the motion;
- the defense is preserved through amendment and not inadvertently waived;
- the statute of repose defense is raised distinctly where applicable.

Failing to plead limitations, or pleading it inconsistently, can forfeit a meritorious defense.

## Common Traps

### Applying One Limitations Period To All Claims

Different claims have different periods. A single period rarely governs an entire multi-count complaint.

### Confusing Limitations With Repose

Repose sets an absolute bar often not subject to tolling. A claim surviving limitations may still be barred by repose.

### Assuming Accrual At The Date Of Injury

Discovery, continuous treatment, and fraudulent concealment rules can delay accrual. Identify the governing accrual rule.

### Missing An Available Tolling Doctrine

Equitable tolling, estoppel, infancy, class action tolling, and tolling agreements can rescue stale claims. Evaluate each.

### Overlooking Short Government Notice-Of-Claim Windows

Claims against government entities often have very short, jurisdictional notice periods that bar claims before ordinary limitations apply.

### Ignoring Choice-Of-Law Effects On The Period

A different forum's law may apply a longer or shorter period. Run the choice-of-law analysis.

### Waiving The Defense By Failure To Plead

Limitations is an affirmative defense. Failing to plead or preserve it can forfeit a meritorious defense.

## Self-Check

- Is the correct limitations regime identified for each claim, including special or shorter periods, choice-of-law effects, and any applicable statute of repose?
- Are limitations and repose distinguished, with both analyzed where both apply, recognizing that repose is often not subject to tolling?
- Is the accrual date determined under the governing rule (injury, discovery, continuous treatment, fraudulent concealment, breach), with supporting facts?
- Is every applicable tolling doctrine evaluated (equitable tolling, estoppel, infancy, incapacity, class action tolling, tolling agreements, bankruptcy stay)?
- Are minority, incapacity, and government notice-of-claim rules checked, including short jurisdictional presentment windows?
- Is the analysis done claim by claim and party by party, with relation-back and cross-claim treatment considered?
- Is the limitations defense preserved through proper pleading and not waived by inconsistent positions?
- Does the output avoid asserting that a claim is definitively barred or viable and instead recommend qualified counsel confirm the analysis for the specific jurisdiction?
