# Government Contracts And Public Procurement

Contracting with the government is fundamentally different from contracting with a private party, because the government is not free to contract on whatever terms it chooses: it must spend public money through a regulated procurement process designed to ensure competition, fairness, and accountability, and the resulting contract is governed by a dense overlay of statutes, regulations, and clauses that allocate risk, mandate compliance, and create rights and remedies that private contracts do not. The defining risk in advising on government contracts is treating them like commercial contracts, when in fact the government's power to contract is constrained by procurement rules that can be challenged (bid protests), the contract terms include mandatory clauses (labor, audit, termination, intellectual property) that private contracts lack, and disputes are resolved through specialized claims processes (the Contract Disputes Act and equivalents) rather than ordinary commercial litigation. A contractor that bids without understanding the procurement rules, performs without complying with the mandatory clauses, or disputes without using the required claims process can lose awards, incur liability, or forfeit valid claims. The discipline is to engage procurement as a regulated process, to comply with the contract's mandatory terms, and to pursue rights through the specialized mechanisms the framework provides.

Use this skill when advising on government contracting, public procurement, bid protests, contract performance and compliance, or claims and disputes with government buyers. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating government contracts as commercial contracts, and from overlooking the procurement, compliance, and dispute mechanisms unique to public contracting.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Procurement Process And Competition Rules

Government contracting begins with a regulated procurement designed to ensure fair competition:

- Procurement methods: distinguish sealed bidding (lowest responsible responsive bidder), negotiated procurement (best value, tradeoff), and sole source; each has different rules and challenge points.
- Solicitation requirements: read the solicitation carefully for mandatory requirements, evaluation criteria, and submission rules; failure to meet a material requirement renders a bid non-responsive and ineligible.
- Competition requirements: full and open competition is the default, with set-asides for small businesses and socioeconomic programs (small, disadvantaged, women-owned, veteran-owned, HUBZone) under specific rules.
- Debriefing and protest: unsuccessful offerors may seek a debriefing and, where the procurement was flawed, file a bid protest challenging the award.

A contractor that bids without understanding the procurement rules submits non-compliant bids, misses set-aside opportunities, or fails to challenge flawed awards. Engage the procurement process on its own terms.

### Pursue Or Defend Bid Protests On The Correct Grounds

Bid protests challenge flaws in the procurement process, and the grounds and forums are specific:

- Protest grounds: common grounds include arbitrary evaluation, unequal treatment, misapplication of evaluation criteria, and inclusion of unfair requirements; the protest must identify a specific violation of the procurement rules.
- Timing: protests have strict deadlines, often days after the award or after the basis was known; missing the deadline forfeits the protest.
- Forums: protests may go to the agency, the Government Accountability Office (GAO) or equivalent, or the Court of Federal Claims or equivalent court; each has different procedures, standards, and timelines.
- Stay of performance: a timely protest may stay contract performance, affecting strategy and the agency's response.

Bid protests are powerful but procedurally demanding. Identify the specific flaw, the correct forum, and the deadline, because procedural missteps forfeit the challenge.

### Comply With Mandatory Contract Clauses

Government contracts include mandatory clauses that private contracts do not, and compliance is not optional:

- Labor and employment clauses: prevailing wage (Davis-Bacon, Service Contract Act), equal employment opportunity, affirmative action, and other labor requirements carry significant liability for noncompliance.
- Cost and pricing clauses: contracts with cost-reimbursement elements are subject to cost accounting standards, allowability rules, and audit rights; unallowable costs must be excluded.
- Termination clauses: the government may terminate for convenience (with compensation of costs, not lost profit) or for default; the distinction has major financial consequences.
- Intellectual property and data rights: clauses governing the government's rights in technical data, computer software, and inventions differ from commercial IP and must be managed.
- Compliance and certification: certifications (small business size, ethics, trafficking, supply chain) carry liability for false statements; compliance programs must support the certifications.

A contractor that performs a government contract as if it were commercial, ignoring the mandatory clauses, incurs liability and forfeits rights. Read and comply with the contract's government-specific terms.

### Manage Government Audits And Investigations

Government contracts are subject to audit and investigation, with rights and obligations distinct from commercial relationships:

- Audit rights: the government and its auditors have broad rights to inspect records, including cost and pricing data, for years after performance; maintain compliant records.
- False claims exposure: submission of false claims or statements (overbilling, false certifications, kickbacks) exposes the contractor to treble damages, exclusion, and criminal liability under the False Claims Act and equivalents.
- Internal investigations: where noncompliance is suspected, internal investigation and, where appropriate, self-disclosure can mitigate liability; coordinate with counsel.
- Suspension and debarment: misconduct can lead to suspension or debarment from government contracting, which for many contractors is an existential consequence.

Government audits and investigations carry consequences, treble damages, exclusion, criminal exposure, that far exceed commercial disputes. Maintain compliant records and respond strategically.

### Pursue Claims And Disputes Through The Required Process

Disputes with the government are resolved through specialized processes, not ordinary litigation:

- Contract Disputes Act (CDA) and equivalents: claims must be submitted to the contracting officer, with certification for larger claims, and denied (or deemed denied) before appeal to a board or court.
- Claims process: a claim is a written demand seeking payment or other relief; it must be specific, documented, and certified where required; failure to follow the process forfeits the claim.
- Forums: appeals go to an agency board of contract appeals or the Court of Federal Claims, with their own procedures and expertise.
- Limitations: claims have specific limitations periods (often six years); calendar them, because missing the period forfeits the claim.

A contractor that disputes informally, or that files suit without exhausting the claims process, forfeits its rights. Use the required process, with documentation and certification, within the deadlines.

### Address Socioeconomic Programs And Set-Aside Compliance

Set-aside and socioeconomic programs offer opportunities but carry specific compliance requirements:

- Eligibility: size standards, ownership, control, and other eligibility criteria must be met; representing eligibility falsely creates False Claims Act exposure.
- Affiliation: affiliation with other entities can affect size status; manage affiliation through the rules.
- Performance of work: set-aside contracts require the small business to perform a specified percentage of the work (limitations on subcontracting); comply to maintain eligibility.
- Mentor-protégé and joint ventures: these structures can enhance eligibility but have specific rules; structure them carefully.

Socioeconomic programs are valuable but compliance-heavy. Meet the eligibility and performance requirements, and document them, because the government actively polices program abuse.

## Common Traps

### Treating Government Contracts As Commercial

Ignoring procurement rules and mandatory clauses. The trap is that the contract feels standard, but government terms and processes differ fundamentally.

### Non-Compliant Bids

Missing mandatory requirements or submission rules. The trap is that the bid feels competitive, but non-responsiveness disqualifies it.

### Missing Protest Deadlines And Forums

Foreclosing challenges through procedural errors. The trap is that protests feel available, but deadlines and forum rules are strict.

### Ignoring Mandatory Clauses During Performance

Treating labor, cost, termination, and IP clauses as boilerplate. The trap is that clauses feel standard, but noncompliance carries significant liability.

### Informal Dispute Resolution

Skipping the claims process and filing suit. The trap is that litigation feels like the remedy, but the CDA process is a prerequisite.

### Set-Aside Compliance Failure

Misrepresenting eligibility or violating performance requirements. The trap is that set-asides feel like opportunity, but abuse creates False Claims Act exposure.

## Self-Check

- Is the procurement method (sealed bid, negotiated, sole source) understood, with solicitation requirements met and set-aside eligibility assessed?
- Are bid protests pursued on specific grounds, in the correct forum, and within strict deadlines, where the procurement was flawed?
- Are mandatory contract clauses (labor, cost and pricing, termination, IP, certifications) read and complied with during performance?
- Are government audit rights respected, with compliant records maintained, and false claims exposure managed through internal investigation and self-disclosure?
- Are claims and disputes pursued through the Contract Disputes Act process, with specific, documented, certified claims and exhaustion before appeal?
- Are socioeconomic program eligibility and performance requirements met and documented, with affiliation and joint venture rules managed?
- Are suspension and debarment risks understood and managed, given their existential consequences for government contractors?
- Has qualified government contracts counsel reviewed the procurement, performance, and dispute strategy, given the highly regulated and specialized nature of public contracting?
