# Non-Compete and Restrictive Covenants

Restrictive covenants are contractual limits on what an employee or seller may do after a relationship ends. They are disfavored in law because they restrain trade and labor mobility, but they are enforceable when carefully tailored to protect a legitimate business interest. The central judgment problem is that enforceability is intensely jurisdiction-dependent and turns on reasonableness across multiple dimensions simultaneously. An agent who drafts a single template non-compete and applies it across roles and states is building covenants that are partly or wholly unenforceable, and in some jurisdictions, illegal to impose at all.

The landscape has shifted dramatically. Several jurisdictions ban or sharply limit non-competes for most employees (notably California, and an increasing number of states, plus the FTC's rulemaking activity). Others enforce them subject to reasonableness. The trap for agents is relying on outdated assumptions or a "one strong covenant" approach: courts often refuse to blue-pencil or reform overbroad covenants, so a covenant drafted too broadly may be void entirely rather than narrowed. The skill is matching the covenant type, scope, and consideration to the jurisdiction, the role, and the actual interest being protected, and never using a non-compete as a lazy substitute for genuine trade secret protection.

This skill addresses US employment and sale-of-business restrictive covenants. Standards vary by state and are changing rapidly. Some jurisdictions are essentially per se unenforceable for employees. Restrictive covenant advice is jurisdiction-specific; a licensed attorney must confirm current enforceability before relying on any covenant.

## Core Rules

### Choose the right covenant type for the actual interest

Restrictive covenants are a family, not a single tool. Match the restriction to the interest. Non-disclosure protects specific confidential information and trade secrets; it is the most broadly enforceable and should be the foundation. Non-solicitation of customers protects customer relationships and goodwill; it is more enforceable than a full non-compete in many jurisdictions. Non-solicitation of employees protects against raiding of the workforce. Non-compete, the broadest, restricts working for competitors or in the field and is the most disfavored and most often struck down. Use the narrowest covenant that protects the legitimate interest. A non-compete is rarely the right first choice.

### Establish a legitimate business interest

Enforceability generally requires the covenant to protect more than mere competition. Recognized interests typically include trade secrets and confidential information, customer relationships and goodwill (especially where the employee was the face of the company to clients), and specialized training. Mere desire to avoid competition, or to retain staff, is not a legitimate interest. Articulate the specific interest before drafting scope. If the interest is trade secrets, a non-disclosure plus narrow non-solicitation may suffice and be far more enforceable.

### Calibrate reasonableness across all dimensions

Where enforceable, a covenant must be reasonable in duration, geographic scope, and line of business, and no greater than necessary to protect the interest. These dimensions are judged together; a shorter duration can support broader geography and vice versa. Duration commonly ranges from six months to two years post-employment; longer is increasingly hard to justify. Geographic scope should reflect where the employee actually worked and where the protected interest exists, not the company's entire footprint. Industry scope should be the actual competitive field, not "any business." Document the reasoning behind each choice.

### Secure adequate consideration

Consideration is a frequent failure point. In many jurisdictions, a non-compete must be supported by adequate consideration at the time it is signed. For a new hire, the offer of employment may suffice. For an existing employee required to sign a new or broader covenant, continued employment alone may be insufficient; additional consideration (a bonus, promotion, severance, or payment) is often required. Sale-of-business covenants are treated more favorably because they are ancillary to the sale of goodwill and are typically supported by the purchase price. Do not assume continued employment is enough; check the jurisdiction's rule.

### Distinguish employment covenants from sale-of-business covenants

Covenants ancillary to the sale of a business are generally enforced more liberally than employment covenants because the seller is paid for goodwill and the restriction protects the asset purchased. Sale covenants may permit longer durations and broader scope, though they remain subject to reasonableness. Do not import employment-covenant drafting into a sale context or vice versa; the standards and expectations differ.

### Address the jurisdiction question explicitly

Enforceability varies by state and is changing. Some jurisdictions prohibit or sharply limit employee non-competes for all or most workers. Others enforce them subject to reasonableness. Some distinguish by income level or profession. Choice-of-law and forum clauses can matter but are not a cure: courts may refuse to apply a chosen law that conflicts with a strong public policy of the employee's work state. For multi-state employers, a single nationwide covenant is usually wrong. Tailor by employee location or use a carefully constructed choice-of-law provision reviewed by counsel.

### Include remedies and notice provisions

A covenant without a remedy is decorative. Include provisions for injunctive relief (the primary remedy, since damages for competitive activity are hard to prove), attorney fees for enforcement, tolling of the restriction period during violation, and return of materials. Provide written notice of the restriction at hire and again at termination, including the specific scope and duration that will apply. Notice strengthens the employee's awareness and supports enforcement.

### Build in severability and reformation, but do not rely on them

A severability clause allows a court to strike an unenforceable provision while preserving the rest, and a reformation clause invites the court to narrow an overbroad covenant. Include them. But do not rely on them: many jurisdictions refuse to blue-pencil, and an overbroad covenant may be void in its entirety. Draft to enforceable scope from the start.

### Consider alternatives that are more likely enforceable

Where a non-compete is doubtful, consider alternatives: garden leave (paid restriction period), a well-drafted non-solicitation, a strong non-disclosure tied to specific trade secrets, clawbacks or forfeiture provisions tied to competition, and robust onboarding and offboarding confidentiality controls. These often protect the real interest more reliably than a non-compete that will be challenged.

## Common Traps

### Using a one-size non-compete for all roles

A covenant appropriate for a senior executive with trade secret access is almost certainly overbroad for a mid-level employee and unenforceable for entry-level staff. Tailoring is not optional; uniform covenants invite wholesale invalidation.

### Drafting too broadly on the assumption courts will narrow

Many jurisdictions do not blue-pencil. An overbroad covenant may be void entirely, leaving no protection. Draft to the enforceable scope rather than relying on judicial reformation.

### Ignoring the jurisdiction's current law

Non-compete law is in flux. A covenant enforceable a few years ago may be banned today in a given state. Relying on a stale template without checking current law produces unenforceable or illegal covenants and potential liability for imposing them.

### Treating continued employment as sufficient consideration everywhere

In several jurisdictions, continued employment without more is inadequate consideration for a new or expanded covenant. The covenant fails for lack of consideration even if scope is reasonable.

### Conflating customer non-solicitation with a non-compete

A broadly drafted "non-solicitation" that effectively bars working with any customer the employee ever contacted is often treated and struck down as a de facto non-compete. Scope the non-solicitation to customers the employee actually serviced or had material contact with, and limit the duration.

### Failing to protect trade secrets directly

Using a non-compete as a proxy for trade secret protection is fragile. If the non-compete is struck, the trade secret protection may be inadequate. Build trade secret protection (non-disclosure, access controls) as the foundation, with restrictive covenants layered on top.

### Ignoring no-poach and no-recruit risks

No-poach agreements between companies (as opposed to non-solicitation in an employment contract) can constitute antitrust violations and carry criminal and civil exposure. Distinguish a covenant in an employment contract from an agreement among competitors not to hire each other's employees; the latter is dangerous.

## Self-Check

- Is the restrictive covenant the narrowest type that protects the legitimate business interest, and have I articulated that interest specifically?
- Are duration, geographic scope, and line of business each reasonable and no greater than necessary, and is the reasoning documented?
- Is there adequate consideration for the covenant at the time of signing, particularly for an existing employee rather than a new hire?
- Have I confirmed the current enforceability rules in the employee's work jurisdiction, including any ban or income-based limits?
- Have I distinguished employment covenants from sale-of-business covenants and applied the appropriate standard?
- Does the covenant include injunctive relief, tolling, fee, and notice provisions, with written notice at hire and termination?
- Is there a severability and reformation clause, while recognizing some jurisdictions will not blue-pencil and the covenant must be enforceable as drafted?
- Have I considered enforceable alternatives (garden leave, non-solicitation, non-disclosure, clawbacks) where a non-compete is doubtful?
- Have I avoided no-poach agreements among competitors that could raise antitrust exposure?
- Have I flagged that restrictive covenant law is jurisdiction-specific and rapidly changing, and that a licensed attorney must confirm enforceability before reliance?
