# Trade Secret Misappropriation and Remedies

Misappropriation is not the same as breach of a confidentiality agreement, and it is not the same as an employee leaving for a competitor. The legal claim requires proof that a trade secret existed, that it was acquired by improper means or disclosed or used without consent, and that the defendant knew or had reason to know the information came through improper means. Agents frequently collapse these elements into a general sense of betrayal, which produces claims that fail at the motion-to-dismiss stage. The discipline of misappropriation analysis is separating genuine improper acquisition from lawful competition, general skill use, and independent development.

The judgment problem is asymmetric speed. Information moves faster than litigation. A competitor that has begun using stolen source code or a customer list can inflict irreversible market harm in weeks, while a court case takes years. The remedies that matter most, injunctions and seizure orders, are available only at the outset and only on a strong, specific evidentiary showing. An agent who waits for a complete picture before acting may lose the only effective remedy. Conversely, an agent who rushes to court on vague allegations of "they stole our stuff" faces sanctions, fee shifting, and the loss of credibility for any later, better-supported claim. The skill is moving fast on specifics while resisting the urge to litigate on generalities.

This skill addresses US law under the Defend Trade Secrets Act (DTSA, federal) and state Uniform Trade Secrets Act variants, plus comparable regimes elsewhere. Remedies, statutes of limitation, and standards for extraordinary relief differ by jurisdiction. Misappropriation litigation is high-stakes and fact-specific; a licensed attorney must direct any enforcement action.

## Core Rules

### Establish the elements before asserting a claim

A misappropriation claim requires three things. First, the existence of a protectable trade secret, identified with reasonable particularity. Second, acquisition by improper means (theft, bribery, misrepresentation, breach or inducement of breach of a duty to maintain secrecy, espionage) or use or disclosure without consent. Third, that the defendant knew or had reason to know the information was acquired improperly, often shown by direct notice or by circumstances. Map the facts to each element before acting. A strong suspicion of wrongdoing is not a claim.

### Identify the trade secrets with particularity early

Vague claims fail. Before filing or sending a demand, articulate the specific information alleged misappropriated: which source files, which process parameters, which customer data fields, which financial model. The identification can evolve in litigation, but the initial pleading must give fair notice. Overbroad identification ("our entire platform") invites a motion to dismiss and undermines the genuine secrets buried within.

### Distinguish improper means from lawful competition

Not every departure to a competitor is misappropriation. Employees may use general skills, knowledge, and experience. Competitors may independently develop the same information. They may reverse-engineer a lawfully acquired product. They may use information that became public. The line is improper acquisition or use of specific protected information. A claim premised on "they could not have built this without our know-how" must be supported by evidence of access plus substantial similarity or other indicia of use, not mere inference.

### Act quickly to preserve injunctive relief

Injunctive relief is the primary remedy because damages for information misuse are hard to prove and often inadequate. A preliminary injunction requires a likelihood of success on the merits, irreparable harm, balance of equities favoring the plaintiff, and public interest. Irreparable harm is often presumed in trade secret cases but can be rebutted, especially if the plaintiff delayed. Move promptly upon discovery. Under the DTSA, an ex parte seizure order is available in extraordinary circumstances to prevent propagation, but the standard is demanding and improper requests carry damages exposure.

### Build the access-and-use evidentiary chain

Misappropriation is often proved circumstantially. The strongest cases establish a chain: the secret existed and was protected, the defendant had access (the departing employee downloaded files, the contractor received the data), the defendant had motive and opportunity, and the defendant's subsequent product or conduct bears indicia of use (identical code structure, unusual design choices, suspicious performance gains). Forensic evidence from devices and systems is often decisive. Preserve logs, images, and metadata before they are overwritten.

### Choose remedies strategically

Available remedies typically include injunctive relief (prohibiting use or disclosure, sometimes for a limited period to eliminate commercial advantage), actual damages (the plaintiff's loss or the defendant's unjust enrichment, whichever is greater), and in cases of willful and malicious misappropriation, exemplary damages (often up to twice the actual damages) and attorney fees. The DTSA also permits exemplary damages and fees for willful conduct, and attorney fees for bad-faith claims or unreasonable demands. Match the remedy request to the evidence of willfulness and the provable loss. Overclaiming exemplary damages on thin willfulness evidence invites fee shifting against the plaintiff.

### Handle the inevitable confidentiality problem in litigation

Trade secret litigation creates a paradox: the plaintiff must prove the secret in a public courtroom, which risks further disclosure. Courts routinely enter protective orders limiting access to sensitive materials, sealing filings, and restricting who may view designated confidential information. Plan the protective order strategy early. Over-designation of documents as highly confidential can be challenged and sanctions can follow; under-designation risks waiving secrecy.

### Evaluate counterclaims and defenses

Defendants commonly assert that the information is not a trade secret (publicly known, readily ascertainable, independently developed), that there was no improper means, that the plaintiff did not take reasonable efforts, or that the statute of limitations has run. Anticipate these defenses and prepare rebuttals. The statute of limitations is typically short (often two to three years from discovery or when reasonable diligence would have revealed misappropriation); delay is dangerous.

### Consider criminal referrals for egregious conduct

In cases involving clear theft, espionage, or coordinated misappropriation (particularly crossing borders or involving government interests), criminal prosecution under the Economic Espionage Act or state statutes may be available. Criminal referrals carry trade-offs: loss of control over timing and outcome, but the prospect of search warrants and restitution. Evaluate whether criminal referral supports or undermines the civil strategy.

## Common Traps

### Suing on "they must have used it"

A claim built entirely on inference without evidence of access or indicia of use is vulnerable to dismissal and fee shifting. The plaintiff bears the burden of showing misappropriation, not merely opportunity.

### Delaying after discovery

Courts deny preliminary injunctions when the plaintiff sat on knowledge of misappropriation. The presumption of irreparable harm erodes quickly. Document the discovery date and act within days, not months, for injunctive relief.

### Conflating NDA breach with misappropriation

A breach of a confidentiality clause is a contract claim. Misappropriation is a separate tort with different elements and remedies. Both may be pled, but the trade secret claim must stand on its own elements; the NDA does not create the trade secret right.

### Overbroad injunction requests

Requesting an injunction that bars a former employee from working in the industry, or that sweeps in non-secret information, is often denied in part and signals overreaching. Tailor the injunction to the specific secrets and the demonstrated risk.

### Failing to preserve forensic evidence

If the company suspects misappropriation, it must preserve device images, email, access logs, and cloud activity immediately. Routine deletion policies can destroy the decisive evidence. Issue a litigation hold promptly.

### Ignoring the bad-faith risk

Under the DTSA and many state laws, bringing a misappropriation claim in bad faith, or making an unreasonable demand, can trigger exemplary damages and attorney fees against the plaintiff. Assess the strength of the claim realistically before threatening litigation.

### Assuming the trade secret survives the lawsuit

Aggressive public filings, overly broad discovery responses, or failure to obtain a protective order can publish the very secret the plaintiff sought to protect. Litigation strategy must include secrecy preservation throughout.

## Self-Check

- Have I identified the specific trade secrets alleged misappropriated with reasonable particularity, and can I show each is protectable?
- Can I articulate the improper means or unauthorized use, and the defendant's knowledge or reason to know of impropriety?
- Have I distinguished genuine misappropriation from lawful competition, general skill use, independent development, and reverse engineering?
- Is there an evidentiary chain showing access and indicia of use, supported by preserved forensic evidence and a documented litigation hold?
- If seeking injunctive relief, have I acted promptly upon discovery and can I show likelihood of success and irreparable harm?
- Are the requested remedies (injunction, actual damages, exemplary damages, fees) matched to the evidence of willfulness and provable loss?
- Have I anticipated the principal defenses (not a secret, no improper means, no reasonable efforts, statute of limitations) and prepared rebuttals?
- Is there a protective order and sealing strategy to prevent the litigation from publishing the trade secret?
- Have I evaluated whether exemplary damages or a criminal referral is warranted and whether bad-faith risk cuts against the plaintiff?
- Have I confirmed the governing jurisdiction's elements, statute of limitations, and remedy standards, and flagged that a licensed attorney must direct the litigation?
