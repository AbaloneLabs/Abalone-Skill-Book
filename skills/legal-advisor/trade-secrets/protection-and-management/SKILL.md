# Trade Secret Protection and Management

Trade secrets are the only major intellectual property right that exists solely because of how the owner behaves. Unlike a patent or trademark, there is no registration certificate that proves ownership. The right exists only so long as the information is actually secret, commercially valuable because it is secret, and protected by reasonable efforts. This means the protection program itself is the asset. Agents advising on trade secrets often treat the legal definition as a box to check, when in fact the day-to-day management controls are what determine whether a claim survives in litigation.

The judgment problem is that secrecy and usability are in tension. A formula locked in a safe that no employee can access is well protected but commercially useless. A customer list emailed to the entire sales team is useful but may have lost its secret status. The skill here is calibrating reasonable efforts to the value and risk of each category of information, and doing so consistently enough that a court later accepts the program as genuine rather than theatrical. Agents must also resist the common error of labeling everything "confidential," because over-classification destroys the credibility of the classification system and makes it impossible to show that truly sensitive material received differentiated treatment.

This skill applies to US state law (principally the Uniform Trade Secrets Act as adopted, and the Defend Trade Secrets Act of 2016) and comparable regimes in other jurisdictions. Specific thresholds, statutes of limitation, and definitions of reasonable efforts vary. Trade secret advice is jurisdiction-specific and fact-intensive; a licensed attorney should review any protection program or enforcement plan.

## Core Rules

### Identify protectable subject matter with precision

Trade secret protection is narrower than "anything we do not want competitors to know." To qualify, information must derive independent economic value from not being generally known or readily ascertainable by proper means, and must be the subject of reasonable efforts to maintain secrecy. Strong subject matter categories include formulas, source code and algorithms, manufacturing processes, supplier and pricing terms, customer lists with curated detail, financial models, unreleased product roadmaps, and negative know-how (what does not work). Weak or non-protectable categories include general industry knowledge, employee general skills, information readily reverse-engineerable from public products, and single-customer relationships that could be independently developed.

Before designing controls, identify the specific assets and articulate why each is secret and valuable. A vague inventory ("our know-how") cannot be protected or enforced.

### Apply the three-part test consistently

Every claimed trade secret should be evaluated against the three statutory elements. First, is the information actually not generally known to the public or competitors? A technique published in a paper, shown at a conference, or embedded in a publicly sold product without restriction may have lost secrecy. Second, does it confer commercial advantage precisely because competitors lack it? Third, have reasonable efforts been applied? Document the analysis. Courts look for evidence that the company itself treated the information as secret, not merely asserted it after a departure.

### Calibrate reasonable efforts to value and risk

Reasonable efforts is a sliding scale, not a fixed checklist. The required measures depend on the value of the secret, the ease of misappropriation, industry custom, and the size and resources of the company. A multi-billion-dollar process formula warrants biometric access, need-to-know segmentation, and logging. A modest customer list may be adequately protected by password-protected CRM, confidentiality clauses, and exit reminders. The trap is applying the same low bar everywhere, or the same expensive bar everywhere. Differentiated controls are more credible than uniform ones.

Reasonable efforts typically include some combination of: written confidentiality agreements with employees, contractors, vendors, and business partners; physical and electronic access restrictions; marking documents and systems as confidential; limiting access on a need-to-know basis; exit interviews that collect materials and reiterate obligations; and periodic review of who has access. The absence of any one of these is often fatal to a claim.

### Segment access on a need-to-know basis

Broad access is the single most common reason trade secret claims fail. If every employee can download the source repository or export the full customer database, the company cannot later claim the information was closely held. Implement role-based access, audit trails for sensitive exports, and watermarks or logging where feasible. Review access lists periodically and revoke promptly on role change or departure. The goal is to be able to show, for any given secret, who had access and when.

### Use written agreements as the backbone, not the whole program

NDAs and confidentiality clauses are necessary but not sufficient. They establish notice and contractual duty, but a signed NDA does not cure the absence of physical and technical controls. Employment agreements should include confidentiality obligations that survive termination, assignment of inventions clauses where appropriate, and return-of-materials provisions. Contractor and vendor agreements should define what is confidential, restrict use to the engagement purpose, and require similar controls downstream. Ensure the agreements are actually signed before access is granted, not collected as an afterthought.

### Manage the information lifecycle, not just creation

Trade secrets can be destroyed inadvertently. Publishing a paper, presenting at a trade show, filing a patent that publishes the technique, launching a product that reveals the method, or failing to mark confidential materials can each extinguish secrecy. Coordinate patent and trade secret strategy: some subject matter is better kept as a trade secret (long-lived, not detectable in products, such as a manufacturing process), while other subject matter is better patented (detectable, shorter-lived, such as a consumer feature). Once a patent application publishes, the disclosed subject matter is no longer secret.

### Conduct disciplined offboarding

The highest-risk moment for trade secrets is employee departure. A credible offboarding process includes: a written reminder of continuing confidentiality and any post-employment obligations; collection of company devices and materials; revocation of system access concurrent with or before the exit interview; review of recent data access logs for anomalies; and confirmation of the employee's next employer when relevant. Document the process. The exit record is often the central exhibit in later misappropriation litigation.

### Distinguish trade secrets from employee general skills

Employees legitimately retain general knowledge, skills, and experience gained on the job. Attempting to claim everything an employee learned as a trade secret overreaches and undermines the credibility of legitimate claims. The line is between specific protected information (the customer list, the formula, the process parameters) and general capability (how to do the job). Post-employment restrictions must be carefully scoped to avoid unenforceable overbreadth.

## Common Traps

### Labeling everything confidential

Stamping every document "CONFIDENTIAL" without differentiation trains employees to ignore the marking and tells a court the company made no real effort to distinguish sensitive material. Reserve marking for genuinely secret information and tier the marking (e.g., internal, confidential, restricted) so the most sensitive material stands out.

### Relying on the NDA alone

A signed agreement without corresponding access controls, logging, and offboarding discipline looks like a company that wanted legal cover but did not actually protect the secret. Courts and juries notice the gap. The program must match the paperwork.

### Failing to identify the secret before litigation

Companies often sue claiming "our entire business method" was stolen, only to discover under discovery that most of it was public, independently developed, or general skill. A claim must identify the specific trade secrets with reasonable particularity before or early in litigation. Vague claims are dismissed.

### Ignoring the reverse-engineering and publication risk

If the secret can be discovered by inspecting a sold product, protection may vanish the moment the product ships. If a team member publishes the technique, secrecy ends. Build disclosure review into publication, conference, and patent processes.

### Overbroad non-competes as a substitute

Some companies use sweeping non-compete agreements as a backdoor trade secret protection. In jurisdictions that limit or ban non-competes, this strategy fails and can render the entire restrictive covenant unenforceable, leaving the genuine trade secret claim weaker. Use targeted confidentiality and, where lawful and narrow, limited non-solicitation instead.

### Assuming customer lists are always protectable

A list of company names discoverable from public sources is often not a trade secret. The protectable value usually lies in the curated detail: contact names, pricing history, buying patterns, notes. Articulate what specifically took effort to compile and is not readily ascertainable.

### Neglecting joint development and contractor arrangements

When work is done with partners or contractors without clear confidentiality and ownership terms, the resulting know-how may be jointly held or effectively unprotected. Agree on treatment of jointly developed information before collaboration begins.

## Self-Check

- Have I identified the specific trade secret assets, and for each, can I articulate why it is not generally known and why it has economic value?
- Does the protection program include both written agreements and corresponding technical and physical controls, and do they match in rigor?
- Is access segmented on a need-to-know basis with audit trails for sensitive exports, and can I show who had access to a given secret?
- Are confidentiality markings differentiated rather than applied uniformly to everything?
- Does the offboarding process include access revocation, device and material return, written reminder of obligations, and logging review, all documented?
- Have I distinguished protectable trade secrets from employee general skills and general industry knowledge?
- Is there a process to review publications, patent filings, product launches, and conference materials for inadvertent disclosure before secrecy is lost?
- Are contractor, vendor, and joint-development arrangements covered by confidentiality and ownership terms agreed before access is granted?
- Have I confirmed the governing jurisdiction's definition of trade secret, reasonable-efforts standard, and statute of limitations, and flagged that a licensed attorney should review the program?
- If litigation were filed tomorrow, could the company identify the trade secrets with reasonable particularity and produce evidence of reasonable efforts?
