# Defamation And Reputation

Defamation law protects reputation against false statements of fact, but the elements, defenses, and remedies differ sharply by jurisdiction and by whether the speaker is a public figure, a media outlet, or an ordinary user. Agents often assume that a statement is protected because it is an opinion, or that it is defamatory because it is negative, missing the fact-opinion distinction, the falsity requirement, the fault standard, and the privileges that can defeat a claim. Online and cross-border publication multiplies the complexity: a single post can be published in dozens of jurisdictions with different standards, and intermediary liability shields vary. A wrong call can expose a publisher to damages or, conversely, leave a defamed party without a remedy.

Use this skill before publishing, reviewing, or responding to statements that could affect reputation, drafting takedown or retraction requests, or assessing a defamation claim. The goal is to force the agent to analyze the statement, the audience, the fault standard, and the available defenses before acting. Defamation law is jurisdiction-specific and constitutionally constrained (especially in the US by the First Amendment); any conclusion must be confirmed against the applicable law and the speaker's status.

## Core Rules

### Determine Whether There Is A Defamatory Statement Of Fact

Not every harmful statement is actionable. Separate fact from opinion and from rhetorical hyperbole.

Assess:

- whether the statement is provably true or false (fact) or is subjective assessment (opinion);
- whether the statement implies undisclosed defamatory facts;
- whether the context (news, satire, review, social media) signals fact or opinion;
- whether the statement is loose, figurative, or hyperbolic language protected as opinion;
- whether the statement is a mixed opinion that relies on false facts.

An opinion that implies false underlying facts can be actionable. A statement that is purely opinion or rhetorical hyperbole generally is not.

### Establish Publication To A Third Party

Defamation requires publication to someone other than the plaintiff.

Identify:

- who heard, read, or saw the statement;
- the size and composition of the audience;
- single publication vs. multiple publication rules for online content;
- republication and aggregation by others;
- whether the defendant published or merely distributed (intermediary).

Online republication can refresh the limitations period under some rules. Each jurisdiction treats online publication differently.

### Analyze Falsity And The Substantial Truth Doctrine

The plaintiff must generally prove falsity, and a statement is not defamatory if substantially true.

Check:

- the precise defamatory meaning (sting) of the statement;
- whether the statement is substantially true even if details are inaccurate;
- whether the statement conveys a true gist;
- the burden of proving falsity (on the plaintiff in the US after Philadelphia Newspapers; varies elsewhere).

A statement that is technically wrong in details but true in gist is not defamatory. Focus on the defamatory sting, not the literal wording.

### Apply The Correct Fault Standard

Fault depends on the plaintiff's status and the jurisdiction.

Determine:

- private figure on a matter of private concern (often negligence);
- private figure on a matter of public concern (often negligence, with constitutional limits in the US);
- public official or public figure (actual malice under New York Times v. Sullivan in the US: knowledge of falsity or reckless disregard);
- the distinction between all-purpose and limited-purpose public figures;
- the treatment of corporations as public or private figures;
- stricter standards in other jurisdictions (e.g., strict liability or reversed burdens in some common law systems).

Misapplying the fault standard is a common error. Confirm the plaintiff's status and the applicable standard before assessing liability.

### Identify And Weigh Privileges And Defenses

Privileges can defeat an otherwise defamatory statement.

Consider:

- truth and substantial truth;
- opinion and fair comment;
- fair report privilege (accurate and disinterested report of official proceedings);
- neutral reportage of an official or public dispute;
- qualified privilege for statements made in a duty, interest, or common interest context;
- absolute privilege for statements in judicial, legislative, and certain official contexts;
- consent and statutory privileges (retraction statutes, single-publication);
- intermediary liability shields (Section 230 in the US, e-commerce directives elsewhere).

Qualified privilege can be defeated by malice. Intermediary shields vary and are under legal pressure globally. Do not assume a shield applies without checking the platform's role and the jurisdiction.

### Manage Takedown, Retraction, And Correction

Procedural steps affect remedies and sometimes liability.

Address:

- retraction statutes that cap or condition damages if a retraction is requested and refused;
- notice-and-takedown procedures for online content;
- the right to be forgotten and delisting in some jurisdictions;
- the difference between correction, apology, and retraction;
- the effect of refusal to retract on punitive damages.

Some jurisdictions require a retraction request before suit or cap damages if one is made. Follow the procedural path to preserve remedies.

### Assess Damages, Injunctions, And Remedies

Remedies differ and some are constitutionally constrained.

Evaluate:

- presumed, general, and special damages;
- punitive damages and their constitutional limits;
- the availability of injunctive relief (often disfavored as prior restraint in the US, more available elsewhere);
- declaratory relief and correction orders;
- the effect of anti-SLAPP statutes on early dismissal and fees.

Injunctions against future speech face strong constitutional barriers in the US. Anti-SLAPP laws can shift fees and dismiss weak claims early. Consider these strategically.

### Handle Cross-Border And Online Publication

A single online statement can be published worldwide, exposing the speaker to multiple regimes.

Map:

- the publisher's jurisdiction and the audience's jurisdictions;
- forum shopping and libel tourism (and reforms to curb it);
- the effect of US SPEECH Act on enforcement of foreign defamation judgments;
- intermediary liability and cross-border takedown;
- the right to be forgotten and its territorial scope;
- enforcement of foreign judgments and awards.

A statement lawful in the publisher's country may be actionable elsewhere. Assess exposure in the likely enforcement jurisdictions.

## Common Traps

### Treating Opinion As Automatically Protected

Opinion that implies false facts is actionable. Analyze the implied facts.

### Ignoring The Fault Standard

Public figures require actual malice; private figures may require only negligence. Confirm the plaintiff's status.

### Focusing On Literal Wording Over Gist

Substantial truth defeats a claim if the gist is true. Analyze the defamatory sting.

### Assuming Intermediary Shields Are Universal

Section 230 and similar shields vary and are under pressure. Check the platform's role and jurisdiction.

### Overlooking Anti-SLAPP Exposure

Anti-SLAPP laws can dismiss weak claims and shift fees early. Assess before filing.

### Seeking Injunctions Against Future Speech

Prior restraints face strong constitutional barriers. Prefer damages and correction.

### Forgetting Retraction Statutes

Some jurisdictions require or reward retraction requests. Follow the procedural path.

### Ignoring Cross-Border Exposure

A statement lawful at home may be actionable elsewhere. Map the enforcement jurisdictions.

## Self-Check

- Is the statement classified as fact, opinion, mixed opinion, or hyperbole, with implied facts identified?
- Is publication to third parties established, with audience and online republication considered?
- Is falsity assessed against the defamatory sting, with substantial truth analyzed?
- Is the correct fault standard applied based on the plaintiff's status (private, public, official, corporation)?
- Are privileges and defenses (truth, opinion, fair report, qualified, absolute, consent, intermediary shield) weighed?
- Are retraction, takedown, and correction procedures followed to preserve remedies?
- Are damages, injunctions, declaratory relief, and anti-SLAPP exposure assessed?
- Is cross-border publication mapped, with forum shopping, libel tourism, and enforcement considered?
- Is the distinction between compensable and non-compensable speech applied?
- Does the output recommend confirmation against the applicable law and the speaker's status by qualified counsel?
