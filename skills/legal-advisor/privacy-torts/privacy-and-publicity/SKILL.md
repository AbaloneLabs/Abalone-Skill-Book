# Privacy And Publicity

Privacy and publicity are related but distinct. Privacy torts protect against intrusion, disclosure, and false light; publicity (personality) rights protect the commercial value of a person's identity. Agents often conflate the two, assume that a public figure has no privacy rights, or believe that any commercial use of a likeness requires consent while missing the newsworthiness and public interest defenses. The right of publicity is recognized very differently across jurisdictions (a robust common law and statutory right in much of the US, a narrower personality right in civil law countries, and varying post-mortem durations), and consent, scope, and revocation drive most disputes.

Use this skill before using a person's name, image, likeness, voice, or personal story commercially or editorially, drafting a release or consent, or assessing a privacy or publicity claim. The goal is to force the agent to identify which right is implicated, whether consent or a defense applies, and how the jurisdiction treats the right. Privacy and publicity law is jurisdiction-specific; any conclusion must be confirmed against the applicable law, especially for cross-border use.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish The Privacy Torts From The Right Of Publicity

The four privacy torts and the right of publicity protect different interests.

Classify:

- intrusion upon seclusion (offensive intrusion into private matters);
- public disclosure of private facts (highly offensive disclosure of true private facts not of legitimate public concern);
- false light (highly offensive false implication);
- appropriation or right of publicity (commercial use of name, image, likeness, voice, or identity).

Intrusion is about the act of gathering; disclosure and false light are about publication; appropriation is about commercial use. Analyze which interest is actually implicated before choosing a framework.

### Establish The Commercial Use Element For Publicity

The right of publicity generally requires commercial use, not merely publication.

Assess:

- whether the use is in advertising, merchandising, or product endorsement;
- whether the use is incidental or central to the commercial message;
- whether the use is editorial, informational, or expressive (often protected);
- the treatment of transformative and artistic works (First Amendment defenses in the US);
- the distinction between commercial and non-commercial use in the applicable jurisdiction.

Editorial, news, parody, and many expressive uses are protected. Commercial endorsement and merchandising generally require consent.

### Determine Whether Consent Or A Release Applies

Consent is the primary defense to publicity and many privacy claims.

Verify:

- the existence, scope, and duration of a written release;
- whether the release covers the specific use, medium, territory, and term;
- whether the subject was a minor and the effect on revocation;
- whether consent was informed and voluntary;
- revocation rights and their limits;
- the effect of payment and consideration.

A release for one use does not cover another. Read the scope carefully and confirm it covers the actual use.

### Apply The Newsworthiness And Public Interest Defenses

Disclosure of private facts and some publicity claims are defeated by legitimate public concern.

Assess:

- whether the matter is newsworthy or of legitimate public concern;
- the newsworthiness standard (not limited to current headlines; includes human interest);
- the relationship between the disclosure and the public concern;
- whether the disclosure goes beyond what is newsworthy;
- the treatment of public figures and officials (reduced privacy expectation, but not eliminated).

Newsworthiness is broad but not unlimited. Highly offensive disclosure of intimate facts may exceed newsworthiness even for public figures.

### Analyze False Light Distinctly From Defamation

False light and defamation overlap but differ.

Determine:

- whether the implication is highly offensive to a reasonable person;
- whether the implication is false;
- the fault standard (often actual malice for public figures);
- whether the jurisdiction recognizes false light at all (some US states do not);
- the difference from defamation (reputation vs. emotional dignity).

False light protects dignity, not reputation. Some jurisdictions reject the tort to avoid overlap with defamation.

### Handle Minors, Deceased Persons, And Post-Mortem Rights

Status affects the right.

Consider:

- parental consent for minors and revocation on majority;
- the privacy and publicity rights of deceased persons (no privacy right after death in most jurisdictions; post-mortem publicity varies);
- the duration of post-mortem publicity rights (none in some states, 10-100 years in others);
- the treatment of heirs and estate assignees;
- group rights and the rights of crowds and background figures.

Post-mortem publicity is highly variable. Confirm whether the jurisdiction recognizes it and for how long.

### Address Digital, AI, And Synthetic Media

New technologies create new exposure.

Map:

- deepfakes, face swaps, and voice cloning;
- AI-generated likenesses and the question of whose likeness is used;
- platform terms and takedown for synthetic media;
- the interaction with copyright (the image) and publicity (the likeness);
- disclosure requirements for synthetic media in some jurisdictions.

A synthetic likeness can infringe publicity rights even without a real photograph. Assess the source and the resemblance.

### Coordinate With Data Protection And Publicity Across Borders

Cross-border use implicates multiple regimes.

Check:

- GDPR and equivalent privacy regimes for personal data, including images and voiceprints;
- the right of publicity or personality in each relevant country;
- consent requirements under data protection law (often stricter than publicity consent);
- cross-border data transfer rules;
- the effect of differing post-mortem and publicity durations.

Data protection consent and publicity consent are not the same. Obtain both where applicable.

## Common Traps

### Conflating Privacy And Publicity

They protect different interests and have different defenses. Classify the right first.

### Assuming Public Figures Have No Privacy Rights

Public figures retain privacy in intimate matters. Newsworthiness is broad but not unlimited.

### Treating Any Commercial Use As Infringement

Editorial, expressive, and transformative uses are often protected. Distinguish commercial from non-commercial.

### Relying On A Narrow Release

A release for one use does not cover another. Read the scope carefully.

### Ignoring Post-Mortem Variation

Post-mortem publicity durations vary widely. Confirm the jurisdiction's rule.

### Overlooking False Light Non-Recognition

Some jurisdictions reject false light. Confirm the tort exists.

### Missing Data Protection Consent

GDPR and equivalent regimes require consent for personal data, including images. Obtain both consents.

### Synthetic Media Without Source Analysis

AI-generated likenesses can infringe publicity rights. Assess the source and resemblance.

## Self-Check

- Is the right classified (intrusion, disclosure, false light, publicity) before analysis?
- Is the commercial use element assessed, distinguishing commercial from editorial and expressive use?
- Is consent or a release verified for scope, duration, medium, territory, minority, and revocation?
- Is the newsworthiness or public interest defense applied to disclosure and publicity claims?
- Is false light analyzed distinctly from defamation, including jurisdictional recognition?
- Are minors, deceased persons, post-mortem duration, and group rights handled under the applicable law?
- Are digital, AI, and synthetic media uses assessed for publicity and data protection exposure?
- Are data protection consent and publicity consent both obtained for cross-border use?
- Is the difference between commercial endorsement and incidental or transformative use applied?
- Does the output recommend confirmation against the applicable law and post-mortem rules by qualified counsel?
