# Protection And Ownership

Copyright protects original works of authorship, but the protection is only valuable to someone who actually owns it. Agents often assume that because a company paid for a website, a logo, software, or content, it owns the copyright. Frequently it does not. Without a written work-for-hire clause that fits the narrow statutory categories, or a written assignment, the independent contractor who created the work owns the copyright, and the company has at most an implied license that can be revoked or disputed. The same confusion infects joint works, employee contributions, and pre-existing materials incorporated into deliverables. Companies discover the ownership gap at the worst moment: during financing, acquisition, or a dispute with the creator.

Use this skill before commissioning creative work, before relying on employee or contractor output, before acquiring a content business, before registering copyrights, or when auditing ownership of creative assets. The goal is to force the agent to verify copyrightability, trace the chain of ownership through employment, commission, joint authorship, and assignment, and identify gaps before they become disputes. Copyright ownership is jurisdiction-specific; ownership analysis and transfers require qualified copyright counsel.

## Core Rules

### Verify Copyrightability: Originality, Fixation, And Subject Matter

Copyright protects original works of authorship fixed in a tangible medium. Not everything qualifies.

Verify:

- originality, requiring a modicum of creativity and independent creation, not novelty;
- fixation, requiring the work to be captured in a sufficiently permanent medium;
- eligible subject matter, including literary, musical, dramatic, pictorial, sculptural, audiovisual, architectural, and software works;
- exclusions, including ideas, procedures, processes, systems, methods of operation, concepts, principles, discoveries, facts, and useful articles apart from separable expressive features;
- the idea-expression distinction, protecting expression but not the underlying idea;
- compilation and database originality in selection, coordination, and arrangement.

Functional features of software and useful articles are not protected by copyright, though their expressive elements may be. Verify that the protectable elements exist before claiming copyright.

### Resolve Work Made For Hire And Employee Authorship

Ownership depends on the author's status. Employees and contractors create different ownership defaults.

Determine:

- whether the creator is an employee under agency law, in which case the employer owns the work as a work made for hire;
- whether the work qualifies as a work made for hire for an independent contractor, which requires both a signed written agreement and that the work falls into one of the narrow statutory categories, such as a contribution to a collective work, a translation, a supplementary work, a compilation, an instructional text, a test, answer material, or an atlas;
- the consequences if the work does not qualify as a work made for hire, in which case the contractor owns the copyright absent a written assignment.

Most contractor-created software, websites, and marketing content do not fit the work-for-hire categories and require a written assignment to transfer ownership. Relying on a work-for-hire clause for non-qualifying work is a common and fatal error.

### Secure Written Assignments From Contractors

When work-for-hire does not apply, ownership transfers only by a written assignment signed by the author. The assignment should also include a fallback work-for-hire clause and a present-tense assignment of future rights.

Include in contractor agreements:

- a present-tense assignment of all rights, title, and interest in the work;
- a work-for-hire clause as a fallback if the work qualifies;
- a waiver of moral rights where permissible;
- a promise to execute further documents to perfect ownership;
- a warranty of originality and non-infringement;
- disclosure of pre-existing materials incorporated into the work;
- a license to any pre-existing materials necessary to use the deliverable;
- cooperation in registration and enforcement.

A present-tense assignment of future creations is more robust than a promise to assign, which may require a later signed writing. Use present-tense assignment language.

### Handle Joint Authorship And Contributions

Joint works are created by two or more authors intending their contributions to be merged. Joint authors share ownership with complex consequences.

Address:

- whether the contributors intended a joint work at the time of creation;
- the rights of each joint author to use, license, and account for the work;
- the inability of one joint author to grant exclusive rights without the others;
- the duty to account to co-authors for profits;
- the effect of joint authorship on termination rights;
- the desirability of a joint authorship or contribution agreement to allocate rights.

Unintended joint authorship can give a contributor unexpected ownership rights. Use written agreements to define contributions and ownership.

### Trace Pre-Existing Materials And Third-Party Content

Deliverables often incorporate pre-existing materials owned by the creator or third parties. The client may own the new work but not the incorporated elements.

Identify:

- pre-existing code, libraries, fonts, images, music, and templates incorporated into the deliverable;
- open-source components and their license obligations;
- third-party content requiring licenses;
- the scope of any license granted to use pre-existing materials;
- whether the license is exclusive, perpetual, or revocable;
- the effect on the client's ability to use, modify, and distribute the deliverable.

A website built on a contractor's proprietary framework may belong to the client but depend on a license to the framework that could be revoked. Require disclosure and broad licenses to pre-existing materials.

### Manage Transfers, Assignments, And Termination Rights

Copyright transfers require writing. Authors also have termination rights that can claw back transfers after decades.

Ensure:

- transfers are in writing and signed by the transferor;
- the transfer covers all rights or specifies the granted rights;
- recordation of transfers with the copyright office;
- awareness of termination rights that allow authors or heirs to terminate grants after 35 to 40 years in the US, with strict notice windows;
- the effect of termination on licenses and assignments;
- work-for-hire works are not subject to termination, making proper classification important.

Termination rights can unwind licenses decades later. Factor them into long-term content deals, especially for music and books.

### Address Moral Rights And Attribution

Moral rights protect the author's personal connection to the work, including attribution and integrity. They vary widely by jurisdiction.

Address:

- the right of attribution and the right of integrity in jurisdictions that recognize them;
- the narrower US visual artists rights for certain works of visual art;
- waivers of moral rights where permissible;
- the effect of moral rights on modifications and destruction of works;
- international moral rights that survive transfer.

Moral rights can restrict how a work is modified even after assignment. Address them in contracts, especially for visual art and in jurisdictions with strong moral rights.

### Register Copyrights For Key Works

Registration provides significant benefits, including the right to sue, statutory damages, and attorney fees for timely registrations.

Register:

- key software, content, and creative assets;
- before publication or within the grace period for statutory damages and fees;
- in jurisdictions where registration is a precondition to suit;
- with accurate authorship, ownership, and deposit information;
- updates and new versions as material changes occur.

Registration is relatively inexpensive and provides powerful remedies. Register key works promptly.

## Common Traps

### Assuming Payment Means Ownership

Paying a contractor does not transfer copyright without a written assignment or qualifying work-for-hire. Secure rights in writing.

### Misapplying Work-For-Hire To Non-Qualifying Works

Software, websites, and marketing content rarely fit work-for-hire categories. Use assignments.

### Ignoring Pre-Existing Materials

A deliverable built on a contractor's framework depends on a license to that framework. Require disclosure and broad licenses.

### Unintended Joint Authorship

Without a written agreement, a contributor may gain joint ownership. Define contributions and ownership.

### Overlooking Termination Rights

Long-term licenses can be terminated decades later. Factor termination into content deals.

### Forgetting Moral Rights Internationally

Moral rights can restrict modifications abroad. Address them in cross-border deals.

### Skipping Registration

Without registration, statutory damages and fees are unavailable, and suit may be barred. Register key works.

### Using A Promise To Assign Instead Of Present Assignment

A promise to assign may require a later signed writing. Use present-tense assignment of future rights.

## Self-Check

- Is copyrightability verified for originality, fixation, eligible subject matter, exclusions, idea-expression distinction, and useful article separability?
- Is work made for hire and employee authorship resolved under agency law and the statutory contractor categories, with assignments used where work-for-hire does not apply?
- Do contractor agreements include present-tense assignment, fallback work-for-hire, moral rights waiver, further assurances, originality warranty, pre-existing materials disclosure and license, and cooperation?
- Is joint authorship and contribution addressed through written agreements defining ownership, licensing, accounting, and termination effects?
- Are pre-existing materials, third-party content, open-source components, and the scope of necessary licenses identified and documented?
- Are transfers in writing, signed, recorded, and analyzed for termination rights and their effect on long-term deals?
- Are moral rights, attribution, integrity, waivers, and international variations addressed?
- Are key works registered before or within the grace period of publication for statutory damages and fees, with accurate information?
- Is the chain of ownership traced through employment, commission, joint authorship, assignment, and corporate transactions?
- Does the output avoid asserting ownership or validity and instead recommend qualified copyright counsel for the specific works and jurisdiction?
