# Infringement And DMCA

Copyright infringement turns on whether the accused work is substantially similar to the protected work and whether the accused had access to it, but online infringement has its own procedural regime under the DMCA. Agents often conflate these regimes, sending a takedown notice where a lawsuit is needed, or suing where a takedown would suffice. On the enforcement side, agents assert infringement based on superficial similarity without analyzing access or protectable expression, wasting effort on unprotectable ideas. On the platform side, agents misunderstand the safe harbor, failing to maintain a repeat infringer policy, ignoring the designated agent registration, or sending non-compliant notices that platforms reject. The DMCA's notice-and-counter-notice system is a precise statutory mechanism; deviations defeat the safe harbor or the takedown.

Use this skill before asserting copyright infringement, before sending a DMCA takedown, before responding to a takedown as a user, before advising a platform on safe harbor, or when litigating online infringement. The goal is to force the agent to analyze infringement properly, to use the DMCA mechanism correctly, and to preserve safe harbor or user rights. Infringement and DMCA practice is jurisdiction-specific; enforcement and defense require qualified copyright counsel.

## Core Rules

### Analyze Infringement Through Access And Substantial Similarity

Copyright infringement of a work requires that the accused copied it, proven by access plus substantial similarity, or by striking similarity that permits an inference of access.

Establish:

- ownership of a valid copyright, including originality and registration where required;
- access, the defendant's reasonable opportunity to encounter the work;
- substantial similarity of protectable expression, not just ideas;
- the extrinsic test for objective similarity of protectable elements;
- the intrinsic test for subjective similarity as perceived by an ordinary observer;
- the filtration of unprotectable elements such as ideas, scenes a faire, and merger;
- striking similarity that permits an inference of access when the works are nearly identical.

Infringement requires similarity of protectable expression, not of ideas or standard elements. Filter out unprotectable components before comparing.

### Separate Protectable Expression From Ideas And Standard Elements

Copyright protects expression, not ideas, and many elements are unprotectable.

Filter:

- ideas and concepts underlying the work;
- procedures, processes, and methods;
- scenes a faire, standard or obligatory features of a genre;
- merger doctrine, where an idea can only be expressed in a few ways;
- historical and factual elements;
- standard interfaces and interoperability features;
- public domain elements.

A work can resemble another because both draw on the same unprotectable elements. Infringement requires similarity in the protectable expression that remains after filtration.

### Send A Compliant DMCA Takedown Notice

The DMCA provides a safe harbor to online service providers that respond to compliant takedown notices. A non-compliant notice may be ignored and can create liability for misrepresentation.

Include in a takedown notice:

- a physical or electronic signature of the owner or authorized agent;
- identification of the copyrighted work claimed to be infringed;
- identification of the infringing material to be removed, with specific URLs;
- contact information for the complaining party;
- a statement of good-faith belief that the use is unauthorized;
- a statement, under penalty of perjury, of accuracy and authority;
- the statutory elements exactly as required.

Sending a notice without a good-faith belief, or with knowledge of material misrepresentation, creates liability for damages, including fees and costs. Do not use takedowns to suppress non-infringing speech or criticism.

### Maintain Safe Harbor Protection As A Platform

Platforms that host user content rely on the DMCA safe harbor, which requires specific compliance.

Maintain:

- a designated agent registered with the copyright office, with current contact information;
- a public policy for terminating repeat infringers in appropriate circumstances;
- accommodation of standard technical measures;
- expeditious removal upon receipt of compliant notices;
- preservation of counter-notifications and restoration within the statutory window;
- no actual knowledge of infringement or awareness of red flags;
- no financial benefit directly attributable to infringing activity with right and ability to control.

Failure to maintain a repeat infringer policy or a current designated agent can forfeit the safe harbor. The repeat infringer policy must be implemented in good faith, not merely written.

### Handle Counter-Notifications And Restoration

When a user disputes a takedown, the counter-notice process determines whether the material is restored.

Process:

- the user submits a counter-notice with the statutory elements, including consent to local jurisdiction;
- the platform provides the counter-notice to the complainant;
- the complainant has 10 to 14 business days to file a court action seeking a restraining order;
- if no action is filed, the platform restores the material;
- the platform maintains protection for complying with the process.

Platforms must restore material if no lawsuit is filed within the window, or they lose safe harbor. The counter-notice process balances the rights of owners and users.

### Avoid Misrepresentation And Abuse

The DMCA imposes liability for knowingly false takedown claims. Agents and owners face damages for misuse.

Avoid:

- claiming infringement without a good-faith belief;
- using takedowns to suppress lawful speech, criticism, or competition;
- claiming rights you do not own;
- ignoring fair use and licenses before sending notices;
- automated takedown systems that send false positives without review.

Misuse can result in damages, fees, and sanctions, and can create adverse publicity. Review notices before sending.

### Evaluate Litigation Versus Takedown

Takedowns are fast and cheap but temporary and limited to the noticed platform. Litigation is slow and expensive but produces binding injunctions and damages.

Choose based on:

- the scale and persistence of infringement;
- the platforms involved;
- the need for damages and fees;
- the strength of the infringement claim;
- the defendant's identity and location;
- the budget and timeline;
- the availability of statutory damages and attorney fees for registered works.

For widespread or commercial infringement, litigation may be necessary. For isolated uploads, takedowns suffice.

### Address Secondary Liability

Platforms and intermediaries can face secondary liability for others' infringement.

Assess:

- contributory infringement, knowing material contribution with knowledge of infringement;
- vicarious infringement, the right and ability to control plus direct financial benefit;
- inducement, actively encouraging infringement;
- the safe harbor defenses that shield qualifying platforms;
- the limits of safe harbor for active involvement.

A platform that actively curates and profits from infringing content may lose safe harbor and face secondary liability.

## Common Traps

### Asserting Infringement On Superficial Similarity

Infringement requires similarity of protectable expression after filtering ideas, scenes a faire, and merger. Analyze before asserting.

### Confusing Access With Coincidence

Independent creation is a defense. Without access or striking similarity, similarity may be coincidence.

### Non-Compliant DMCA Notices

Missing statutory elements cause rejection and forfeit the safe harbor leverage. Follow the statute exactly.

### Takedown Abuse And Misrepresentation

Knowingly false claims create liability and bad publicity. Review before sending.

### Lapsed Designated Agent Or Weak Repeat Infringer Policy

Platforms lose safe harbor without a current agent or good-faith repeat infringer policy. Maintain compliance.

### Failing To Restore After Counter-Notice

Platforms must restore if no lawsuit is filed within the window. Failure forfeits safe harbor.

### Suing Without Registration

Registration is required to sue and, for timely registration, unlocks statutory damages and fees. Register key works.

### Ignoring Secondary Liability

Platforms that actively profit from infringement face contributory or vicarious liability beyond safe harbor.

## Self-Check

- Is infringement analyzed through ownership of a valid copyright, access, substantial similarity of protectable expression, extrinsic and intrinsic tests, filtration of unprotectable elements, and striking similarity?
- Are ideas, procedures, scenes a faire, merger, facts, standard interfaces, and public domain elements filtered before comparison?
- Do DMCA takedown notices include signature, work identification, infringing material identification with URLs, contact information, good-faith belief statement, perjury accuracy and authority statement, and all statutory elements?
- Does the platform maintain a registered designated agent, good-faith repeat infringer policy, standard technical measure accommodation, expeditious removal, counter-notice handling, no actual knowledge or red flags, and no direct financial benefit with control?
- Are counter-notifications processed with statutory elements, complainant notice, 10 to 14 business day lawsuit window, and restoration absent court action?
- Is misrepresentation and abuse avoided through good-faith review, no suppression of lawful speech, ownership verification, fair use and license consideration, and human review of automated notices?
- Is litigation versus takedown chosen based on scale, persistence, platforms, damages and fees need, claim strength, defendant identity, budget, and registration status?
- Is secondary liability assessed for contributory, vicarious, and inducement theories, with safe harbor limits for active involvement?
- Are statutory damages and attorney fees availability confirmed through timely registration?
- Does the output avoid asserting infringement or safe harbor eligibility and instead recommend qualified copyright counsel for the specific work, platform, and jurisdiction?
