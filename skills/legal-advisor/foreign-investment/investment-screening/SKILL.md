# Investment Screening

Foreign direct investment is no longer a purely commercial decision. Many countries operate investment screening regimes that can block, condition, or unwind an acquisition by a foreign investor on national security or public order grounds. The US CFIUS, the EU FDI Screening Regulation and member-state regimes, the UK National Security and Investment Act, and similar regimes in Australia, Canada, Japan, and others each have their own triggers, thresholds, timelines, and remedies. Agents often assume that a deal below a notification threshold is safe, that screening is voluntary, or that closing before clearance carries only a small risk. In fact, closing without required clearance can trigger divestment, civil and criminal penalties, and personal liability for directors.

Use this skill before advising on a cross-border acquisition, greenfield investment, joint venture, asset deal, or lease involving foreign investors, critical infrastructure, critical technology, sensitive data, or defense. The goal is to force the agent to identify every screening regime that could apply, assess mandatory vs. voluntary filing, and plan timing and conditions before signing. Screening regimes are jurisdiction-specific and change rapidly; any conclusion must be confirmed against the current rules, thresholds, and agency practice, with specialized counsel engaged.

## Core Rules

### Identify All Potentially Applicable Screening Regimes

A single transaction can trigger multiple regimes. Map them all.

Check:

- CFIUS (US) for national security, with mandatory declaration triggers for critical technology, critical infrastructure, sensitive data, and foreign government involvement;
- EU FDI Screening Regulation and member-state regimes (France, Germany, Italy, Spain, Netherlands, and others), with cooperation mechanisms;
- UK National Security and Investment Act for 17 sensitive sectors and the call-in power;
- Australia FIRB, Canada ICA, Japan FEFTA, and other national regimes;
- sector-specific regimes (telecom, media, banking, defense, energy, ports, airports, semiconductors);
- state and provincial regimes where relevant.

A deal may need clearance in several jurisdictions simultaneously. Coordinate filings and timing.

### Determine Whether The Investor Is Foreign

Foreign status definitions differ and can be broader than nationality.

Assess:

- the ultimate beneficial owner and control chain, including state-owned enterprises and sovereign wealth funds;
- indirect control through intermediate entities;
- minority investments with governance rights, veto rights, or access to material non-public information;
- the treatment of dual-listed, multi-jurisdiction, and shell structures;
- the treatment of investors from specific countries of concern.

Screening regimes increasingly look through structures to the ultimate controller. Trace ownership to the real decision-maker.

### Assess Whether The Target Is In A Sensitive Sector

Sensitivity drives the analysis.

Map:

- critical infrastructure (energy, water, transport, communications, financial market infrastructure, defense);
- critical technologies (dual-use, advanced computing, semiconductors, AI, quantum, biotech);
- sensitive personal data (large datasets, government data, biometric, health, financial);
- defense and national security supply chains;
- media plurality and strategic assets;
- land and real estate near sensitive sites.

A target that looks commercial can be sensitive because of a single contract, data asset, or technology. Do not screen based on the company's label alone.

### Decide Mandatory Vs. Voluntary Filing

Some filings are mandatory; others are voluntary but carry risk if not made.

Determine:

- mandatory declaration triggers (CFIUS critical technology TID business with certain foreign acquirers; UK notifiable acquisitions; EU member-state mandatory sectors);
- voluntary filing where the transaction may raise national security concerns;
- the consequence of not filing (call-in power, retroactive review, penalties);
- the risk tolerance of the parties and the deal certainty impact.

A voluntary regime with a call-in power means the government can review even without a filing. Assess the call-in window and the likelihood of intervention.

### Plan Timing And Conditions Before Signing

Screening affects deal certainty and timing.

Build into the deal:

- conditions precedent requiring clearance;
- allocation of filing responsibility and cost;
- timing of signing, announcement, and closing;
- risk allocation if clearance is conditioned, delayed, or denied;
- reverse termination fees and break options;
- standstill and conduct-of-business restrictions pending clearance.

Closing before required clearance (gun-jumping) is a serious violation. Build the condition and the timing into the agreement.

### Manage Mitigation, Conditions, And Ongoing Obligations

Clearance may come with conditions that bind the business for years.

Anticipate:

- mitigation agreements (divestiture, board observer limits, information access limits, security clearances, continuity of supply);
- ongoing reporting and compliance obligations;
- the effect on future transactions and corporate governance;
- the cost and operational burden of mitigation;
- the ability to renegotiate or exit mitigation over time.

Mitigation can reshape the economics of the deal. Assess the conditions before accepting clearance.

### Address Divestment, Penalties, And Personal Liability

Closing without required clearance or breaching mitigation has serious consequences.

Understand:

- divestment and unwinding orders;
- civil penalties and fines, sometimes per day of violation;
- criminal liability for willful violations;
- personal liability for directors and officers;
- reputational harm and future scrutiny.

Do not treat screening as a formality. The downside of non-compliance is severe.

### Coordinate With Other Regulatory And Antitrust Reviews

Screening is one of several regulatory gates.

Coordinate:

- merger control (antitrust) filings and timing;
- sector-specific licensing and approvals;
- export control and sanctions on the transferred technology;
- foreign corrupt practices and antibribery due diligence;
- data protection approvals for cross-border data transfers.

Sequencing matters. Closing conditions should reflect all required approvals, not only screening.

## Common Traps

### Assuming Below-Threshold Means Safe

Thresholds are complex and can be triggered by governance rights, data, or technology, not only equity percentage.

### Treating Voluntary Filing As Optional

Voluntary regimes with call-in powers can review non-filed deals. Assess the call-in window.

### Closing Before Clearance

Gun-jumping triggers divestment, penalties, and personal liability. Build conditions into the agreement.

### Looking Only At Direct Acquirer

Screening looks through to the ultimate beneficial owner and controller. Trace the chain.

### Missing Sensitive Data Or Technology

A commercial-looking target can be sensitive because of one data asset or technology. Screen the substance.

### Ignoring Multiple Regimes

A deal can trigger CFIUS, EU, UK, and other regimes simultaneously. Map all of them.

### Underestimating Mitigation Burden

Mitigation can reshape the deal economics and bind the business for years. Assess before accepting.

### Screening Based On Company Label

Sensitivity turns on assets, contracts, and data, not the company's self-description. Investigate the substance.

## Self-Check

- Are all potentially applicable screening regimes (CFIUS, EU, UK, FIRB, ICA, FEFTA, sector-specific) identified?
- Is the investor's foreign status determined, including ultimate beneficial owner, control chain, and state involvement?
- Is the target assessed for sensitive sectors, critical infrastructure, critical technology, sensitive data, and defense links?
- Is mandatory vs. voluntary filing determined, with call-in windows and non-filing risk assessed?
- Are conditions precedent, filing responsibility, timing, and risk allocation built into the deal before signing?
- Are mitigation, ongoing obligations, and their operational and economic burden assessed?
- Are divestment, civil and criminal penalties, and personal liability for gun-jumping understood?
- Are screening, merger control, sector licensing, export control, sanctions, and data protection reviews coordinated?
- Is the risk of closing before clearance explicitly addressed and prevented by contract?
- Does the output recommend specialized screening counsel and confirmation against current rules and agency practice?
