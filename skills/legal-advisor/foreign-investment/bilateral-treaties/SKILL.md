# Bilateral Treaties

Bilateral investment treaties (BITs) and free trade agreements with investment chapters create substantive protections for foreign investors and a mechanism to enforce them directly against host states through investor-state dispute settlement (ISDS). Agents often treat BITs as background diplomacy rather than enforceable rights, missing that a BIT can convert a political risk into an arbitral claim, that most favored nation (MFN) clauses can import better treaty terms, and that the substantive standards (fair and equitable treatment, full protection and security, expropriation) have specific meanings developed in thousands of awards. A wrong assumption about which treaty applies, who qualifies as an investor, or what constitutes a breach can forfeit a multi-million dollar remedy.

Use this skill before advising a foreign investor on host-state risk, structuring an investment to access treaty protection, assessing a regulatory taking or unfair treatment, or pursuing or defending an ISDS claim. The goal is to force the agent to identify the applicable treaty, confirm investor and investment qualification, and analyze the substantive standards before recommending ISDS. Treaty interpretation is jurisdiction- and tribunal-specific; any conclusion must be confirmed against the treaty text, reservations, and recent jurisprudence, with specialized ISDS counsel engaged.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Applicable Treaty Or Investment Chapter

BITs, FTAs, and multilateral treaties differ in scope and protection.

Determine:

- whether a BIT exists between the investor's state and the host state, and its current status (in force, terminated, sunset clause for existing investments);
- whether an FTA investment chapter applies (NAFTA/USMCA, CETA, CPTPP, RCEP, EU agreements);
- whether the Energy Charter Treaty or a sector treaty applies;
- the treaty's date of entry into force and whether it covers the investment;
- the effect of termination and survival clauses on existing investments.

Many older BITs are being terminated or renegotiated. Confirm the treaty is in force and covers the investment at the relevant time.

### Confirm Investor And Investment Qualification

Treaty protection flows only to qualifying investors and investments.

For the investor:

- nationality or incorporation in the treaty state;
- denial of benefits for companies with no real business activities or controlled by non-parties;
- dual nationality and the dominant nationality rule;
- the treatment of natural persons vs. juridical persons.

For the investment:

- the treaty's definition (assets, contributions, duration, risk, regularity);
- whether the asset qualifies as an investment under the treaty and under the ICSID Convention (jurisdictional double test);
- the timing of the investment relative to the treaty's entry into force;
- the legality of the investment (many treaties exclude investments made in violation of law).

Shell companies with no real activities may be denied benefits. Trace the investor's substance and the investment's characteristics.

### Apply The Substantive Protection Standards

Each standard has a developed meaning in arbitral jurisprudence.

Fair and equitable treatment (FET):

- legitimate expectations created by specific representations;
- arbitrariness, bad faith, denial of justice, manifest excess of power;
- procedural fairness and transparency;
- the difference between a regulatory change and a breach of FET.

Full protection and security (FPS):

- physical security and, in some treaties, legal security;
- due diligence in protection against third-party acts.

National treatment and MFN:

- treatment no less favorable than domestic or third-country investors in like circumstances;
- MFN as a route to import more favorable substantive or procedural treaty terms (jurisprudence is split on procedural importation).

Free transfer of funds, umbrella clauses, and observance of obligations clauses may also apply. Analyze each standard against the specific facts.

### Analyze Expropriation And Compensation

Expropriation is direct or indirect.

Distinguish:

- direct expropriation (outright taking of title or possession);
- indirect or creeping expropriation (regulatory or administrative measures that substantially deprive the investor of use, control, or value);
- the police powers doctrine (non-compensable regulation for public purpose);
- the factors (economic impact, interference with reasonable expectations, character of the measure, duration, good faith).

Not every regulatory loss is expropriation. Apply the proportionality and deprivation analysis, and distinguish compensable expropriation from non-compensable regulation.

### Assess The Dispute Resolution Mechanism

ISDS is available under most treaties but with conditions.

Determine:

- the forum (ICSID Convention, ICSID Additional Facility, UNCITRAL, SCC, ad hoc);
- the consent to arbitrate and its scope;
- the cooling-off or negotiation period (often 3-6 months) before arbitration;
- the time limit to bring claims (often 3-5 years from the investor's knowledge);
- the waiver of domestic proceedings requirement;
- the number of arbitrators and the appointment method;
- the language and seat of arbitration.

Failing to observe the cooling-off period or the waiver can defeat jurisdiction. Plan the procedural path before filing.

### Structure Investments To Access Treaty Protection

Treaty planning is legitimate but scrutinized.

Consider:

- routing investment through a treaty state with substance;
- the timing of restructuring relative to the dispute (late restructuring is vulnerable);
- denial of benefits and limitation of benefits clauses;
- the difference between pre-dispute planning and abusive treaty shopping;
- the legitimacy of restructuring under the MFN and applicable jurisprudence.

Restructuring after a dispute has arisen is generally ineffective and can be challenged as abuse. Structure before the dispute crystallizes.

### Weigh ISDS Costs, Risks, And Alternatives

ISDS is expensive, lengthy, and uncertain.

Assess:

- the cost of arbitration (often millions of dollars) and the allocation;
- the time to award (often 4-7 years);
- the enforceability of the award under the ICSID Convention or New York Convention;
- sovereign immunity and the assets available for enforcement;
- the political and reputational consequences;
- alternatives (negotiation, mediation, domestic proceedings, political risk insurance claims).

ISDS is a last resort for high-value disputes. Weigh it against settlement and insurance.

### Monitor Treaty Evolution And Reform

The BIT landscape is changing.

Track:

- treaty terminations and renegotiations;
- the UNCITRAL ISDS reform process;
- the EU's investment court system proposals;
- carve-outs for taxation, financial regulation, and environmental measures;
- the effect of newer model treaties with narrower protections.

A treaty that protected investments a decade ago may have been replaced by a narrower version. Confirm the current text.

## Common Traps

### Treating BITs As Diplomacy Rather Than Enforceable Rights

BITs create direct claims against states. Analyze them as enforceable instruments.

### Missing Investor Or Investment Qualification

Shell companies and non-qualifying assets are denied protection. Trace substance and characteristics.

### Confusing Regulation With Expropriation

Not every regulatory loss is compensable. Apply the deprivation and police powers analysis.

### Ignoring The Cooling-Off And Waiver Requirements

Failing to observe procedural conditions defeats jurisdiction. Plan the path before filing.

### Late Restructuring As Treaty Shopping

Restructuring after a dispute is vulnerable to abuse challenges. Structure before the dispute crystallizes.

### Overlooking MFN Importation

MFN can import better substantive terms, but procedural importation is split. Analyze the jurisprudence.

### Assuming Termination Ends Protection

Survival clauses can protect existing investments for years after termination. Confirm the treaty's status.

### Underestimating ISDS Cost And Time

ISDS is expensive and lengthy. Weigh it against settlement and insurance.

## Self-Check

- Is the applicable treaty or investment chapter identified, with status, entry into force, and survival clauses confirmed?
- Does the investor qualify (nationality, substance, denial of benefits) and does the investment qualify (definition, legality, timing)?
- Are the substantive standards (FET, FPS, national treatment, MFN, transfer, umbrella) analyzed against the specific facts?
- Is expropriation analyzed for direct vs. indirect taking, deprivation, police powers, and compensation?
- Is the dispute resolution mechanism (forum, consent, cooling-off, time limit, waiver, seat) planned before filing?
- Is investment structuring assessed for substance, timing, and abuse risk?
- Are ISDS costs, time, enforceability, sovereign immunity, and alternatives weighed?
- Are treaty terminations, renegotiations, and ISDS reform tracked for the current text?
- Is the difference between compensable expropriation and non-compensable regulation applied?
- Does the output recommend specialized ISDS counsel and confirmation against the treaty text and recent jurisprudence?
