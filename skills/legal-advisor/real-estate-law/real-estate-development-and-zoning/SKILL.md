# Real Estate Development And Zoning

Real estate development is the process of converting land into a use that the law permits, the market will absorb, and the physical and regulatory constraints allow, and it is governed by a regulatory framework, zoning, subdivision, environmental review, historic preservation, building codes, that can approve, condition, delay, or kill a project years before a shovel touches the ground. The defining risk in advising on development is treating the land purchase as the deal, when the real deal is the entitlement: the set of approvals that determine what the owner can actually build, at what density, for what use, and after what review. A developer who closes on land without securing, or at least rigorously underwriting, the entitlements can find that the parcel cannot be developed as planned, that required permits will take years, that environmental review will impose costly mitigation, or that community opposition and political shifts have rendered the project infeasible. The discipline is to underwrite the regulatory path before committing capital, to secure entitlements with conditions the project can bear, and to navigate the political and environmental review processes that determine whether development actually occurs.

Use this skill when advising on real estate development, land use, zoning, entitlements, environmental review, or the regulatory path for a development project. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating land as freely developable, and from neglecting the entitlement, environmental, and political diligence that determines whether a project can proceed.

## Core Rules

### Underwrite The Regulatory Path Before Committing Capital

The most important development work happens before purchase, in underwriting what the law will allow:

- Zoning analysis: confirm the current zoning, what uses and densities it permits, and what the project requires (rezoning, variance, special use permit) to proceed as planned.
- Entitlement timeline: map the approvals required (planning commission, city council, design review, environmental review) and the realistic timeline, which can run years.
- Risk of denial or condition: assess the likelihood of approval and the conditions (affordable housing set-asides, infrastructure contributions, design changes) that may be imposed.
- Exit if entitlement fails: structure the purchase with contingencies or option periods that allow exit if entitlements are not secured on acceptable terms.

A developer who buys land and then discovers the regulatory path is infeasible has made an expensive mistake. Underwrite entitlements as rigorously as the financials.

### Navigate Zoning, Variances, And Special Use Permits

Zoning determines what can be built, and deviations require specific approvals:

- Zoning ordinance analysis: read the ordinance for permitted uses, dimensional standards (setbacks, height, density, lot coverage), parking requirements, and overlay districts that add restrictions.
- Variance: where the project cannot meet a dimensional standard due to parcel-specific hardship, a variance may be available; the standard (practical difficulty, unnecessary hardship) is jurisdiction-specific and demanding.
- Special use permit (conditional use): uses permitted with conditions require a permit often subject to discretionary review and public hearing; prepare for community input.
- Rezoning: changing the zoning district is a legislative act subject to political process; assess the political feasibility and the comprehensive plan consistency.

Match the project to the regulatory tool. A project that needs a rezoning faces a different path and risk profile than one that needs only a variance.

### Manage Environmental Review And Its Consequences

Environmental review can delay, condition, or defeat a project, and must be planned for:

- Statutory environmental review: federal (NEPA), state (e.g., CEQA, SEPA), and local environmental review statutes require assessment of a project's environmental impacts and often mitigation.
- Impact areas: traffic, air quality, water, biological resources, cultural resources, noise, and cumulative impacts are commonly assessed; significant impacts trigger findings, mitigation, or statements of overriding consideration.
- Timeline and litigation risk: environmental review is slow and is a frequent target of project challenge litigation; build timeline and litigation risk into the underwriting.
- Mitigation and conditions: review can impose costly mitigation (traffic improvements, habitat preservation, affordable housing) that affects project feasibility.

Environmental review is not a procedural hurdle; it is a substantive process that shapes what can be built and at what cost. Engage it early and realistically.

### Address Subdivision, Platting, And Infrastructure

Dividing or combining land and providing infrastructure are regulated steps:

- Subdivision and platting: dividing land into lots requires subdivision approval and recording of a plat, with requirements for lot size, access, and infrastructure.
- Infrastructure obligations: development often triggers obligations to provide or fund roads, utilities, schools, parks, and other infrastructure through impact fees or developer agreements.
- Development agreements: a developer agreement with the municipality can vest rights, lock in obligations, and provide predictability over a multi-year project.
- HOA and CC&Rs: residential development may require formation of an owners' association and recorded covenants governing the community.

Subdivision and infrastructure obligations are where project costs accumulate. Quantify them in the underwriting, not after commitment.

### Navigate Community Opposition And Political Risk

Development is political, and community and political dynamics can approve or kill projects:

- Community engagement: early, genuine engagement with neighbors and stakeholders can build support or reduce opposition; late or token engagement invites resistance.
- Political alignment: assess whether the project aligns with the comprehensive plan and the political priorities of the decision-makers; political opposition can defeat an otherwise compliant project.
- Opposition tactics: opponents may use environmental review challenges, referendum, litigation, and political pressure to delay or defeat projects; anticipate and plan for these.
- Benefits and concessions: offering community benefits (affordable housing, public space, local hiring) can build support and secure approval, at a cost to the project.

Treat community and political dynamics as part of the regulatory path, not as external noise. A project that is legally permissible but politically infeasible may not proceed.

### Coordinate Building Codes, Permits, And Construction

After entitlement, the project must comply with building codes and obtain construction permits:

- Building code compliance: the design must comply with the building, fire, accessibility, and energy codes, which affect design and cost.
- Permit sequence: secure permits in the required sequence (demolition, foundation, building) with inspections at each stage; permit delays affect construction timeline.
- Construction and inspections: construction must follow approved plans, with inspections verifying compliance; deviations require change orders and re-inspection.
- Certificate of occupancy: the project cannot be legally occupied until the certificate of occupancy is issued after final inspection.

Entitlement is necessary but not sufficient; the project must also navigate construction permitting and inspection to completion.

## Common Traps

### Buying Land Without Entitlement Underwriting

Committing capital before confirming what can be built. The trap is that the land feels developable, but the regulatory path may be infeasible.

### Mismatching Project And Regulatory Tool

Pursuing a variance when a rezoning is needed, or vice versa. The trap is that the tools seem interchangeable, but they have different standards and risk profiles.

### Underestimating Environmental Review

Treating review as procedural when it is substantive. The trap is that review feels like a formality, but it imposes mitigation and invites litigation.

### Ignoring Subdivision And Infrastructure Costs

Overlooking impact fees and developer obligations. The trap is that entitlement feels like the cost, but infrastructure obligations accumulate.

### Dismissing Community And Political Dynamics

Treating politics as external to the regulatory path. The trap is that legal compliance feels sufficient, but political opposition can defeat a compliant project.

### Neglecting Construction Permitting

Treating entitlement as the finish line. The trap is that entitlement feels like approval, but construction permitting and inspection continue the regulatory process.

## Self-Check

- Is the regulatory path underwritten before capital commitment, including zoning analysis, entitlement timeline, denial/condition risk, and exit contingencies?
- Is the project matched to the correct regulatory tool (variance, special use, rezoning), with the applicable standard and risk profile understood?
- Is environmental review engaged early and realistically, with impact areas, timeline, litigation risk, and mitigation costs planned?
- Are subdivision, platting, infrastructure obligations, development agreements, and HOA/CC&R requirements quantified in the underwriting?
- Are community engagement and political dynamics treated as part of the regulatory path, with opposition tactics anticipated and benefits offered where strategic?
- Are building code compliance, permit sequence, construction inspection, and certificate of occupancy planned through to completion?
- Is the entitlement path secured with conditions the project can bear, before irreversible capital commitment?
- Has qualified land use counsel reviewed the regulatory path, given how jurisdiction-specific and politically variable development approvals are?
