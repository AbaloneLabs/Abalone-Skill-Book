# Easements, Restrictive Covenants, and Title

Easements, restrictive covenants, and title issues determine what an owner can actually do with land that they nominally own. The defining judgment problem is that a clean deed does not guarantee clean title or unencumbered use: recorded and unrecorded servitudes, implied and prescriptive rights, use restrictions that run with the land, and recording-act priority rules can each defeat an owner's intended use or a buyer's expectation of marketable title. An agent must reason from the record, the physical facts, and the statutory framework, and must never confuse "recorded" with "enforceable" or "unrecorded" with "nonexistent."

## Core Rules

### Easements: creation, type, and scope

- **Creation methods** include express grant or reservation (by written, recorded instrument), implication (from prior use or necessity), prescription (open, notorious, continuous, adverse use for the statutory period), estoppel, and condemnation. Each has distinct elements and evidence.
- **Appurtenant vs. in gross.** An easement appurtenant benefits a dominant estate and runs with the land; an easement in gross benefits a person or entity and does not require a dominant estate. Commercial easements in gross (utility, pipeline, telecom) are often assignable and perpetual; personal recreational easements may not be.
- **Scope is fixed by creation.** An easement created for pedestrian access cannot be expanded to vehicle or utility use without consent; overburdening the servient estate is actionable. The grant language, the circumstances of creation, and the physical use all inform scope.
- **Termination** occurs by release, abandonment (often requiring more than non-use), merger (unity of dominant and servient title), expiration, estoppel, or condemnation. Mere non-use rarely terminates an easement; abandonment generally requires affirmative acts plus intent.

### Restrictive covenants and CC&Rs

- **Covenants running with the land** require intent to run, horizontal and vertical privity (jurisdiction-dependent), and that the covenant touch and concern the land. Equitable servitudes can be enforced in equity even where common-law privity requirements fail, typically through notice (actual, record, or inquiry).
- **CC&Rs and HOA restrictions** are generally enforceable if recorded, reasonable, and procedurally fair, but may be subject to reasonableness review, change-of-conditions doctrine, and statutory limits (e.g., solar rights, flag displays, satellite dishes, fair-housing constraints).
- **Amendment procedures** must be followed strictly; amendments that exceed the grant of authority, or that are arbitrary or capricious, may be unenforceable. Selective or discriminatory enforcement can defeat enforcement.

### Marketable title and title examination

- **Marketable title** is title a reasonable buyer would accept without objection — free from reasonable doubt and from encumbrances that materially impair value or use. It does not require perfect title; minor, immaterial encumbrances do not render title unmarketable.
- Title examination reviews the chain of title, recorded instruments, and exceptions to identify defects, encumbrances, and use restrictions. The standard of marketability and the effect of stale or ancient instruments vary by state and by marketable-record-title acts.
- **Marketable Record Title Acts (MRTA)** in some states can extinguish old interests and restrictions if not preserved by a recorded notice within statutory periods, dramatically simplifying (or, if missed, altering) title.

### Recording acts and priority

- **Recording acts** (race, notice, race-notice) determine priority among competing interests. A later bona fide purchaser without notice can defeat an earlier unrecorded interest under notice and race-notice statutes; the specific statute of the property's jurisdiction controls.
- **Constructive notice** from recording protects subsequent purchasers; **inquiry notice** from physical conditions (a visible road, an obvious use) can impute knowledge even without a recorded instrument.
- **Wild deeds** and indexing errors can defeat the chain-of-title analysis and create priority disputes; the effect depends on the jurisdiction's recording statute and search customs.

### Adverse possession and boundary disputes

- **Adverse possession** requires actual, open, notorious, exclusive, hostile, and continuous possession for the statutory period, with state-specific standards (including, in some states, color of title, claim of right, or good-faith belief). Permissive use defeats hostility.
- **Boundary disputes** may be resolved by deed, survey, doctrine of practical location/acquiescence, agreed boundary, or estoppel, depending on the jurisdiction and the facts. Long-maintained fences and lines can establish boundaries even where the deed suggests otherwise.
- Government-owned land, certain protected lands, and property subject to disability tolling often cannot be adversely possessed.

### Title insurance and exceptions

- **Title insurance exceptions** (standard and special) carve coverage and signal enforceable encumbrances. A special exception for a recorded easement, restriction, or agreement means the policy does not insure against loss from that instrument.
- **Endorsements** can add coverage (survey, access, zoning, comprehensive) but cannot cure substantive defects in the underlying title. Distinguish between removing an exception (insuring over it) and eliminating the underlying encumbrance.

### Practical and jurisdictional limits

- Property law is intensely jurisdiction-specific. Statutes of limitation, recording-act type, marketable-title acts, and the elements of adverse possession and prescription differ materially across states and countries.
- Unrecorded interests (implied easements, prescriptive rights, adverse possession) can bind a buyer despite a clean deed and clean title report; physical inspection and inquiry notice are essential.

## Common Traps

- **Equating "recorded" with "enforceable."** A recorded restriction may be unenforceable (unreasonable, amended improperly, or extinguished by MRTA), while an unrecorded interest (prescription, implication) may be fully enforceable.
- **Expanding easement scope.** Using an access easement for utilities, or a residential easement commercially, overburdens the servient estate and invites injunction.
- **Assuming non-use terminates an easement.** Abandonment requires intent plus acts; mere non-use preserves the easement.
- **Ignoring inquiry notice.** Visible roads, paths, and uses can impute notice of unrecorded rights even without a recorded instrument.
- **Treating CC&Rs as automatically enforceable.** Reasonableness, amendment limits, change-of-conditions, and statutory protections can defeat enforcement.
- **Relying on the title report for use rights.** Title insurance addresses marketability and insured risks; it does not guarantee the buyer's intended use is permissible under zoning, restrictions, or easements.
- **Overlooking MRTA and statutory extinguishment.** Old restrictions may have expired unless preserved; assuming perpetual enforceability without checking the statute is a common error.
- **Applying one state's adverse-possession rule everywhere.** Elements, periods, and exceptions (color of title, good faith, disability) vary materially by jurisdiction.

## Self-Check

- Has the easement's creation method, type (appurtenant vs. in gross), and scope been determined from the grant language, circumstances, and physical use?
- For any proposed expansion of use, has the risk of overburdening the servient estate been assessed?
- Has termination been analyzed on the correct standard (release, abandonment requiring intent plus acts, merger, expiration), rather than assuming non-use suffices?
- Are restrictive covenants and CC&Rs evaluated for enforceability (intent to run, touch and concern, notice, reasonableness, amendment compliance, change of conditions)?
- Has marketable title been assessed against the jurisdiction's standard, and have MRTA or statutory extinguishment rules been checked for old interests?
- Has the recording-act analysis (race, notice, race-notice) and inquiry notice from physical conditions been applied to priority disputes?
- For adverse possession or boundary claims, have the jurisdiction-specific elements, statutory period, and exceptions (disability, government land) been confirmed?
- Have title insurance special exceptions been read and understood, with endorsements negotiated where needed, while recognizing insurance does not guarantee intended use?
- Have I recommended qualified real estate counsel admitted in the property's jurisdiction for title opinions, easement and covenant disputes, and adverse-possession determinations, and stated the jurisdictional limits of general guidance?
