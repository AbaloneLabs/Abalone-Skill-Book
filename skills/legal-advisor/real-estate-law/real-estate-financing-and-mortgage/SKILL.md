# Real Estate Financing and Mortgage

Real estate financing is where property law, contract, and credit risk converge. The defining judgment problem is that a mortgage loan is secured by an asset whose value, priority, and enforceability depend on recording acts, title, survey, zoning, environmental condition, and casualty — any of which can defeat the security even when the loan documents are flawless. An agent advising on financing must reason about lien priority, the sufficiency of title and survey, the allocation of property risks through covenants and insurance, and the realistic enforceability of remedies, while recognizing that consumer and usury rules can void terms that are perfectly enforceable in a commercial context.

## Core Rules

### Loan structure follows risk and capital stack

- **Senior debt, mezzanine debt, and equity** occupy different risk and priority positions. Senior mortgage debt is secured by a first-priority lien on the property; mezzanine debt is secured by an interest in the borrower entity (often pledge of ownership interests) and is contractually subordinated to the senior.
- **Loan types** — term/acquisition, construction, bridge, permanent takeout — carry different draw mechanics, covenants, and exit requirements. Construction loans require attention to budgets, completion guarantees, retainage, and inspection; bridge loans to exit timing and extension mechanics.
- **LTV, DSCR, debt yield, and reserves** are the financial covenants that discipline borrower behavior and trigger default; they should be calibrated to the asset's cash flow and the borrower's realistic capacity.

### Priority and lien position are the security

- **First-lien priority** depends on recording order, statutory liens (property tax, mechanic's liens, assessments), and subordination agreements. A first mortgage can be primed by taxes, mechanic's liens (depending on notice and recording timing), and certain statutory super-priority liens.
- **Subordination, non-disturbance, and attornment agreements (SNDAs)** with tenants determine whether a lease survives foreclosure and whether the lender can terminate unfavorable leases. A tenant's non-disturbance right protects its possession; the lender's termination right protects lease economics.
- **Mechanic's lien risk** is time-sensitive; recording, notice, and lien waivers (conditional and unconditional) must be managed through the construction and funding process.

### Title insurance and survey are not formalities

- **Title insurance** insures the priority and enforceability of the lien and the ownership interest, subject to exceptions and exclusions. The agent must read the title commitment, evaluate standard and special exceptions, and negotiate endorsements (e.g., access, contiguity, survey, zoning, environmental, comprehensive) appropriate to the transaction.
- **Special exceptions** — specific recorded easements, restrictions, agreements, or defects — must be understood because they carve coverage and may affect use, value, or enforceability. Generic reliance on "standard exceptions" is insufficient.
- **Survey (ALTA/NSPS where applicable)** confirms boundaries, encroachments, easements, access, and improvements; it is the basis for removing the survey exception and obtaining survey-related endorsements.

### Environmental, casualty, and casualty-risk allocation

- **Environmental risk** (contamination, wetlands, endangered species, asbestos) can impair value, create statutory liability (e.g., under federal superfund-type regimes), and trigger cleanup obligations that survive foreclosure in some circumstances. Phase I (and, where indicated, Phase II) assessments, indemnities, and carve-outs are the tools.
- **Casualty and condemnation** allocation in the loan documents determines who takes property loss or taking proceeds, whether the lender applies them to loan reduction or funds repair, and what happens if repair is infeasible. The "damage and restoration" and "condemnation" covenants are frequently litigated.
- **Insurance requirements** (property, liability, flood where applicable, rental loss/business interruption) must be specified, with lender loss-payee and additional-insured status, and reviewed against realistic replacement cost and code-upgrade exposure.

### Covenants, defaults, and remedies shape behavior

- **Affirmative and negative covenants** (insurance, taxes, maintenance, reporting, no further encumbrance, no transfer/disposition, no distributions except from surplus cash flow) are the ongoing controls. Transfer restrictions and "due-on-sale"/due-on-encumbrance clauses protect against unapproved changes in ownership.
- **Events of default** include payment, covenant, representation, insolvency, and cross-default. Notice-and-cure periods should be calibrated; some defaults (insolvency, transfer) may be non-monetary and non-curable.
- **Remedies** include acceleration, foreclosure (judicial or non-judicial power-of-sale depending on jurisdiction), receivership, and deficiency judgment availability (which varies sharply by state). Some states are non-judicial-foreclosure-only or anti-deficiency; the remedy menu must be confirmed for the property's jurisdiction.

### Guarantees and recourse carve-outs

- **Non-recourse loans** with carve-out guarantees are the commercial norm. "Bad-boy" carve-outs (bankruptcy filing, misapplication of funds, transfer, fraud, environmental misrepresentation) convert non-recourse to recourse.
- The scope of carve-outs is heavily negotiated; broad "springing recourse" for covenant breaches is lender-favorable and borrower-risky. Drafting must distinguish true bad acts from ordinary business-judgment failures.

### Intercreditor and ABL arrangements

- **Intercreditor agreements** between senior and mezzanine lenders govern notice, cure rights, standstill, release, and enforcement during default. They determine whether mezzanine lenders can protect their position during a senior default.
- For operating-business borrowers, **asset-based loan (ABL) intercreditor** arrangements allocate collateral (receivables/inventory vs. real property) and enforcement between lenders.

### Consumer, usury, and anti-flipping limits

- Residential and consumer mortgage loans attract truth-in-lending, RESPA, HOEPA, state usury, anti-predatory-lending, and licensing requirements that can void terms, impose treble damages, and create rescission rights. These do not apply to most commercial loans but must be checked whenever a loan touches a consumer or residential property.
- Usury limits, choice-of-law restrictions, and fee caps vary by state and can invalidate commercially negotiated rates if the borrower is a protected party or the loan is characterized as consumer.

## Common Traps

- **Assuming recording equals priority.** Statutory liens (taxes, mechanic's liens, assessments) and recording-order rules can prime a recorded first mortgage.
- **Treating title insurance as a rubber stamp.** Unread special exceptions and missing endorsements can leave the lender uninsured for the risks that matter (access, zoning, environmental, survey).
- **Overlooking mechanic's lien exposure.** In construction lending, failure to manage waivers, notices, and retainage can allow construction liens to prime the mortgage.
- **Ignoring SNDAs.** Foreclosure that terminates a valuable anchor lease, or fails to terminate an unfavorable one, can destroy asset value.
- **Casualty covenants that under-specify.** Vague restoration obligations or missing condemnation provisions create disputes over proceeds application and repair feasibility.
- **Recourse carve-outs that are too broad.** Springing recourse for ordinary covenant defaults converts a non-recourse loan into full recourse and is a frequent borrower trap.
- **Assuming a deficiency is available.** Many states restrict or prohibit deficiency judgments, especially for residential or non-judicial foreclosures.
- **Applying commercial assumptions to consumer loans.** Usury, truth-in-lending, and licensing rules can void terms that are enforceable in a commercial loan.

## Self-Check

- Is the lien priority confirmed against statutory liens (taxes, mechanic's liens, assessments), recording order, and subordination agreements?
- Has the title commitment been read, with standard and special exceptions understood and endorsements negotiated (access, zoning, survey, environmental)?
- Is an ALTA/NSPS-type survey obtained and reviewed for boundaries, encroachments, easements, and access?
- Are SNDAs with key tenants negotiated to protect both tenant possession and lender lease economics?
- Are environmental (Phase I/II), casualty, and condemnation risks allocated in the loan documents, with insurance requirements and proceeds-application rules specified?
- Are covenants and events of default calibrated to the asset and borrower, with appropriate notice-and-cure and clear identification of non-monetary, non-curable defaults?
- Are foreclosure and deficiency remedies confirmed for the property's jurisdiction (judicial vs. non-judicial, anti-deficiency limits)?
- Are recourse carve-outs limited to genuine bad acts, with springing-recourse provisions flagged for borrower risk?
- For consumer or residential loans, have truth-in-lending, RESPA, HOEPA, usury, and licensing requirements been checked?
- Have I recommended qualified real estate counsel admitted in the property's jurisdiction for lien priority, foreclosure, and consumer-compliance determinations, and stated the jurisdictional limits of general guidance?
