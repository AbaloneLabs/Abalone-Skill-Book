# Real Estate Transactions And Conveyancing

A real estate transaction is the transfer of an interest in land, and land is unlike any other asset because it is immovable, uniquely identifiable, encumbered by a public record of title and interests, and subject to a dense overlay of recording, zoning, tax, and environmental rules that can defeat a deal that looks clean on its face. The defining risk in advising on real estate transactions is treating the purchase agreement as the deal, when the real deal is the title: what the seller can actually convey, free of liens, encumbrances, easements, and defects that would let a third party assert a superior claim after closing. A buyer who closes without rigorous title examination and insurance can discover, years later, that they do not own what they paid for, or that a recorded easement or covenant gutted the property's value. A second risk is treating the transaction as routine when every parcel is unique: the due diligence that protects a buyer, survey, zoning compliance, environmental assessment, lien and judgment searches, must be tailored to the specific property and its intended use, not run from a generic checklist.

Use this skill when advising on, drafting, or analyzing real estate purchase and sale agreements, title and conveyancing, closing mechanics, or transactional due diligence. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating real estate as a standardized transaction, and from neglecting the title, survey, zoning, and environmental diligence that determine whether the buyer actually gets what they are paying for.

## Core Rules

### Structure The Purchase Agreement Around Risk Allocation

The purchase and sale agreement is the framework that allocates risk between buyer and seller before closing:

- Property description: the legal description must be precise and match the title; a vague or incorrect description creates disputes over what was sold.
- Purchase price and adjustments: price, deposit and escrow, financing contingencies, prorations (taxes, rent, utilities), and post-closing adjustments must be specified.
- Conditions and contingencies: financing, inspection, appraisal, sale of buyer's property, and regulatory approvals each create exit rights that must be drafted with clear triggers and deadlines.
- Representations and warranties: distinguish "as is" sales from those with warranties of title, condition, and authority; allocate the risk of unknown defects accordingly.
- Default and remedies: liquidated damages (often the deposit), specific performance, and the right to recover costs must address both buyer and seller default.

Draft the agreement to allocate the risks the parties have actually negotiated, with deadlines and remedies that are operable, because the agreement governs the period of greatest uncertainty before title passes.

### Examine Title Rigorously And Require Title Insurance

Title is what the buyer actually receives, and it must be examined rigorously:

- Title search and examination: search the public record for the chain of title, liens, mortgages, judgments, easements, covenants, restrictions, and leases that affect the property.
- Identify defects: gaps in the chain, undisclosed heirs, forged deeds, unreleased mortgages, and recorded encumbrances can defeat or cloud title; identify them before closing.
- Title insurance: owner's title insurance protects against undiscovered defects; require it, and understand the coverage, exclusions, and exceptions listed in the commitment.
- Curative matters: defects identified before closing must be cured (releases, corrective deeds, probate) or insured over with appropriate endorsements.

Title examination is not a formality. A buyer who closes on defective title may lack recourse against a seller who is gone, insolvent, or judgment-proof; title insurance is the structural protection.

### Conduct Due Diligence Tailored To The Property And Intended Use

Every parcel is unique, and due diligence must match the property and the buyer's intended use:

- Survey: an ALTA or boundary survey locates the improvements, confirms boundaries, and reveals encroachments, setback violations, and access issues not visible in title.
- Zoning and land use: confirm the intended use is permitted, identify variances or special permits needed, and check for pending zoning changes.
- Environmental: Phase I (and where indicated Phase II) environmental site assessment identifies contamination risk; a buyer of commercial or industrial property who skips this may inherit cleanup liability.
- Physical condition: inspection of structure, systems, roof, foundation, and, for commercial property, the roof, HVAC, and compliance with accessibility codes.
- Tenancy and income: for income property, review leases, rent rolls, tenant estoppels, and operating expenses.

Run due diligence for the specific property, not from a generic checklist. A farm, a strip mall, a residential condo, and an industrial site each require different diligence.

### Handle Conveyancing, Deeds, And Recording Correctly

The mechanics of conveyancing determine whether title actually passes and is protected:

- Deed type: warranty deed (full covenants), special warranty deed (covenants only during seller's ownership), and quitclaim deed (no warranties) allocate title risk differently; match the deed to the negotiated allocation.
- Legal description and execution: the deed must contain an accurate legal description, be properly executed, acknowledged (notarized), and delivered to be effective.
- Recording: prompt recording of the deed and mortgage gives priority against later claimants; delay risks a later-recorded interest taking priority.
- Transfer taxes and filings: real estate transfer taxes, capital gains withholding (where applicable), and property tax reassessment must be addressed at closing.

A deed that is defectively executed, inaccurately described, or late-recorded can fail to convey or lose priority. Treat conveyancing mechanics as substantive, not clerical.

### Manage Closing, Escrow, And Funds Securely

Closing is the settlement where funds and title exchange, and it must be handled securely:

- Escrow agent: a neutral escrow or settlement agent holds funds and documents and disburses only when conditions are met; use a reputable agent.
- Closing statement: the closing disclosure or settlement statement details all financial adjustments, prorations, and disbursements; verify its accuracy.
- Funds security: real estate closings are targets for wire fraud; verify wire instructions independently and use secure funds transmission.
- Document execution and recording: ensure all documents are properly executed, notarized, and recorded to perfect the buyer's title and the lender's mortgage.

Closing fraud and errors are common and costly. Verify instructions, secure funds, and confirm recording after closing.

### Address The Interaction With Financing

Most real estate transactions involve financing, which interacts with the transaction:

- Mortgage contingency: the buyer's obligation to close can be contingent on obtaining financing on specified terms; draft the contingency to protect the buyer's deposit.
- Lender requirements: the lender's title insurance, appraisal, survey, and condition requirements must be satisfied and coordinated with the transaction timeline.
- Loan documents: the note and mortgage (or deed of trust) create the lender's security interest; review them for recourse, prepayment, and assignment terms.
- Assumption and subject-to: where the buyer assumes existing financing, the seller may remain secondarily liable; address release and indemnity.

Financing is not separate from the transaction; it shapes contingencies, timing, and the documents that govern the deal. Coordinate the two from the outset.

## Common Traps

### Treating The Agreement As The Deal

Focusing on price and terms while neglecting title. The trap is that the agreement feels like the deal, but title is what the buyer actually receives.

### Skipping Or Superficial Title Examination

Accepting title without identifying liens, easements, and defects. The trap is that title looks clean, but undiscovered defects can defeat ownership.

### Generic Due Diligence

Running a checklist not tailored to the property. The trap is that checklists feel thorough, but a farm and a factory need different diligence.

### Defective Conveyancing

Errors in deed type, legal description, execution, or recording. The trap is that mechanics feel clerical, but they determine whether title passes.

### Closing Fraud And Wire Errors

Falling for fraudulent wire instructions or mishandling funds. The trap is that closings feel routine, but they are prime fraud targets.

### Ignoring Financing Interaction

Treating the loan as separate from the transaction. The trap is that financing feels external, but it shapes contingencies and timing.

## Self-Check

- Does the purchase agreement allocate risk through precise property description, price and adjustments, contingencies with clear triggers and deadlines, representations, and default remedies?
- Is title examined rigorously for chain, liens, easements, covenants, and defects, with owner's title insurance required and exceptions reviewed?
- Is due diligence tailored to the specific property and intended use, including survey, zoning, environmental, physical condition, and tenancy?
- Is conveyancing handled correctly through deed type matching risk allocation, accurate legal description, proper execution, and prompt recording?
- Is closing managed securely with a reputable escrow agent, verified closing statement, secure funds transmission, and confirmed recording?
- Is the financing interaction addressed through mortgage contingency, lender requirements, loan document review, and assumption liability?
- Are transfer taxes, reassessment, and tax withholding addressed at closing?
- Has qualified real estate counsel reviewed the transaction, title, and conveyancing, given the property-specific and recording-driven nature of the work?
