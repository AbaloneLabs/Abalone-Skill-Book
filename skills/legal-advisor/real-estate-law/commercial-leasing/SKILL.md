# Commercial Leasing

A commercial lease is not a standardized rental agreement; it is a complex, long-term contract that allocates between landlord and tenant the risks and costs of occupying real estate for years, often a decade or more, during which market rents, operating costs, the tenant's business, and the property itself will all change. The defining risk in advising on commercial leases is treating the base rent as the deal, when the real economics are buried in the operating expense pass-throughs, the tenant improvement allowance, the renewal and escalation mechanisms, and the assignment and subletting restrictions that determine what the tenant actually pays and what flexibility it retains. A tenant that signs a lease focused only on rent per square foot can discover, years in, that operating expense escalations have raised its effective rent dramatically, that it cannot assign or sublet when its business changes, or that a co-tenancy or exclusivity clause it never noticed has gutted a neighboring tenant's obligations. The discipline is to read the lease as an integrated economic document, to allocate risk deliberately rather than by default to the party with less leverage, and to negotiate the provisions that determine the lease's real cost and flexibility, not just the headline rent.

Use this skill when advising on, drafting, or negotiating commercial leases, including office, retail, and industrial leases, and their rent structures, expense pass-throughs, improvement allowances, and use, assignment, and default provisions. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating the lease as a form, and from neglecting the expense, escalation, and flexibility provisions that determine the lease's true economics.

## Core Rules

### Match The Rent Structure To The Parties' Risk Allocation

The rent structure determines who bears the risk of rising operating costs, and it must be understood and negotiated:

- Triple net (NNN): the tenant pays base rent plus its share of taxes, insurance, and operating expenses; the landlord's rent is relatively stable, and the tenant bears cost escalation risk.
- Gross lease: the landlord pays operating expenses from the base rent; the landlord bears escalation risk and prices it into the rent.
- Modified gross: a hybrid allocating specific expenses (often taxes and insurance to landlord, maintenance to tenant, or vice versa); the allocation must be explicit.
- Base year and stops: in gross or modified gross leases, a base year or expense stop caps the landlord's expense exposure, with the tenant paying increases over the base.

The rent structure is the foundational economic allocation. A tenant comparing leases on base rent alone, without accounting for the expense structure, is comparing apples to oranges.

### Scrutinize Operating Expense Pass-Throughs And Exclusions

In NNN and modified gross leases, the operating expense clause is where significant cost is hidden or saved:

- Controllable vs. uncontrollable: identify which expenses the landlord controls (management fee, administration) and which it does not (taxes, insurance, utilities); negotiate caps on controllable expenses.
- Exclusions: negotiate exclusions from operating expenses, typically capital expenditures (except amortized replacements), leasing commissions, legal fees for landlord matters, and costs of other tenants' defaults.
- Audit rights: the tenant should have the right to audit the landlord's operating expense calculations within a defined window.
- Gross-up provisions: in partially occupied buildings, expenses should be grossed up to full occupancy so the tenant pays its proportionate, not inflated, share.

Operating expense clauses are heavily negotiated because they determine the tenant's real cost over the lease term. Do not accept the landlord's draft pass-throughs without scrutiny.

### Negotiate Tenant Improvements, Build-Out, And Allowance

The physical premises must suit the tenant's use, and the improvement economics are a major lease term:

- Tenant improvement allowance (TI): the landlord's contribution to the tenant's build-out; negotiate the amount, what it covers, and the disbursement mechanics.
- Work letter: the agreement specifying the landlord's delivered condition (vanilla box, warm lit shell) and the tenant's build-out obligations, with plans, permits, and completion responsibilities.
- Timeline and delay: address delays in delivery or build-out, including rent commencement adjustments and liquidated damages.
- Restoration: the tenant's obligation at lease end to restore the premises to its delivered condition can be a major hidden cost; negotiate the scope and limits of restoration.

The TI allowance and work letter determine whether the tenant can afford to build out the space it needs, and the restoration obligation determines what it costs to leave.

### Address Use, Exclusivity, And Co-Tenancy For Retail

For retail tenants, the use provisions and center-wide protections are commercially critical:

- Use clause: the permitted use must be broad enough for the tenant's business and reasonable expansions, and narrow enough to support the tenant's exclusivity rights.
- Exclusive use: the tenant may negotiate that the landlord not lease to competitors in the center, protecting the tenant's draw; define the exclusive narrowly and the remedy (rent reduction, termination).
- Co-tenancy: for anchor-dependent retail, co-tenancy clauses reduce rent or allow termination if anchor tenants leave or occupancy falls below thresholds.
- Radius restrictions: landlords may restrict the tenant from opening nearby competing locations.

Retail leases turn on center dynamics that office and industrial leases do not. Negotiate the use, exclusive, and co-tenancy protections that make the location viable.

### Negotiate Assignment, Subletting, And Change Of Control

The tenant's ability to respond to business change depends on transfer provisions:

- Transfer consent: landlord consent to assignment and subletting should not be unreasonably withheld, conditioned, or delayed; avoid outright prohibition.
- Recapture: the landlord may have the right to recapture the space on a proposed transfer, which can defeat a tenant's deal.
- Transfer profit: the landlord often takes some or all of the profit on a transfer above the tenant's rent; negotiate a reasonable split.
- Change of control: for corporate tenants, a change of ownership should not be deemed an assignment requiring consent, or the lease impedes normal corporate transactions.

A tenant that cannot assign or sublet is locked into a space it may outgrow or need to leave; transfer flexibility is a core business protection.

### Review Default, Remedies, And Termination Provisions

The default and remedy provisions determine what happens when something goes wrong:

- Monetary default: the cure period for nonpayment of rent (often short) and the landlord's remedies (late fees, acceleration, eviction) must be clear.
- Non-monetary default: cure periods for non-monetary defaults (use, insurance, maintenance) should be reasonable, with notice and opportunity to cure.
- Landlord remedies: distinguish between the landlord's self-help, eviction, and damages remedies; understand acceleration and the duty to mitigate.
- Termination rights: identify any tenant termination rights (co-tenancy failure, casualty, condemnation, landlord default) and their mechanics.

Default provisions allocate the risk of breach. A tenant should negotiate reasonable cure periods and avoid provisions that allow termination for minor or curable defaults.

## Common Traps

### Comparing Leases On Base Rent Alone

Ignoring the expense structure when evaluating economics. The trap is that base rent feels like the cost, but pass-throughs and escalations determine the real expense.

### Accepting Landlord Draft Operating Expenses

Uncapped controllable expenses and no exclusions or audit rights. The trap is that the clause feels standard, but it shifts significant cost to the tenant.

### Underestimating Restoration Obligations

Ignoring the cost of restoring the premises at lease end. The trap is that build-out gets attention, but restoration can be equally expensive.

### Narrow Use Clauses Without Exclusivity

A use clause too narrow for the business, with no exclusive protection. The trap is that the use feels sufficient, but it blocks expansion and invites competition.

### Restrictive Transfer Provisions

Outright prohibition or landlord recapture on transfers. The trap is that transfer clauses feel secondary, but they lock the tenant into an inflexible commitment.

### Harsh Default And Remedy Terms

Short cure periods and termination for minor defaults. The trap is that defaults seem unlikely, but they determine the risk of losing the premises.

## Self-Check

- Is the rent structure (NNN, gross, modified gross, base year/stop) understood and matched to the parties' risk allocation?
- Are operating expense pass-throughs scrutinized for controllable expense caps, exclusions, audit rights, and gross-up provisions?
- Are tenant improvement allowance, work letter, timeline, and restoration obligations negotiated to make the build-out affordable and the exit manageable?
- For retail, are use, exclusivity, co-tenancy, and radius provisions negotiated to protect the tenant's draw and viability?
- Are assignment, subletting, and change-of-control provisions flexible enough to allow the tenant to respond to business change?
- Are default, cure, remedy, and termination provisions reviewed for reasonableness and proportionality?
- Is the lease evaluated as an integrated economic document, not just on base rent and term?
- Has qualified real estate counsel reviewed the lease, given the long-term economic and operational consequences of commercial leasing?
