# Coverage And Policy Review

Insurance coverage review is the process of determining whether a policy actually responds to a given claim or risk. It is not enough to know the policy type; the coverage turns on the specific insuring agreement, the definitions, the exclusions, the conditions, and the endorsements, all read together as a whole. Agents often conclude that a risk is covered because it falls within the general policy type, when in fact an exclusion, a condition precedent, or a definition narrows or eliminates the coverage. The financial consequences of a wrong coverage read are severe: an organization that believed it was insured discovers, at the moment of claim, that it bears the loss itself.

Coverage analysis is also forward-looking. A policy review before a claim or before a transaction identifies gaps and allows correction through endorsements, additional policies, or contractual risk transfer. Use this skill before reviewing a policy for a specific claim, before assessing whether a risk is insured, before recommending coverage changes, or before analyzing a coverage denial. The goal is to help the agent read a policy rigorously, not to provide legal advice, which requires qualified coverage counsel for the specific policy and jurisdiction.

## Core Rules

### Read The Insuring Agreement First

The insuring agreement defines what the insurer promises to pay. It is the starting point, and its scope sets the outer boundary of coverage.

Read the agreement:

- the grant of coverage, what triggers the duty to pay or defend;
- whether it is written broadly or narrowly;
- the covered perils, acts, or occurrences;
- who is an insured under the agreement;
- the territory and time period of coverage;
- the relationship to definitions that expand or limit terms;
- the difference between claims-made and occurrence triggers.

A claims-made policy covers claims made during the policy period, regardless of when the underlying event occurred; an occurrence policy covers events occurring during the period, regardless of when the claim is made. Confusing them produces wrong coverage conclusions.

### Apply The Definitions Rigorously

Defined terms control the scope of the insuring agreement and exclusions. A word that seems broad may be narrowly defined.

Apply definitions:

- the definition of occurrence, accident, or claim;
- the definition of bodily injury, property damage, or personal injury;
- the definition of insured, including entities and individuals;
- the definition of professional services, wrongful act, or other key terms;
- whether the definition expands or limits common meaning;
- the interaction of multiple definitions;
- the effect of endorsements modifying definitions.

The definition of "professional services" in a professional liability policy determines whether a given activity is covered. An activity that seems professional may fall outside the definition, or vice versa.

### Analyze Every Exclusion That Could Apply

Exclusions narrow or eliminate coverage that the insuring agreement would otherwise provide. Each exclusion must be read and applied to the facts.

Analyze exclusions:

- the specific exclusions listed, such as expected or intended injury, contractual liability, pollution, or professional services;
- the scope and wording of each;
- whether the exclusion applies to the claim's facts;
- the exceptions to exclusions, which restore coverage;
- the order of analysis, insuring agreement then exclusions then exceptions;
- the anti-concurrent causation or leading cause doctrine in the jurisdiction;
- the effect of endorsements adding or removing exclusions.

An exclusion that seems broad may be narrowed by an exception, and an exception that seems narrow may restore significant coverage. Read the full exclusion-exception structure.

### Check Conditions And Compliance Obligations

Conditions impose obligations on the insured, such as notice, cooperation, and premium payment. Non-compliance can bar coverage.

Check conditions:

- the notice obligations, timing, content, and to whom;
- the cooperation clause and its scope;
- the consent-to-settle clause in professional policies;
- the duty to mitigate;
- the premium and audit obligations;
- the conditions precedent versus subsequent;
- the remedy for breach, bar versus proportionate.

Late notice can bar coverage in some jurisdictions and only in others. Understand the jurisdiction's approach to notice prejudice before assuming a late notice bars coverage.

### Identify Gaps, Inconsistencies, And Ambiguities

A policy review should surface where coverage is missing, where clauses conflict, and where ambiguity may be construed. These drive coverage decisions and reform.

Identify:

- gaps where no policy responds to a material risk;
- inconsistencies between policies in a stack;
- ambiguities that may be construed against the insurer;
- the adequacy of limits for the exposure;
- the adequacy of retentions and deductibles;
- the effect of self-insured retentions versus deductibles;
- the coordination with other policies and indemnities.

An ambiguity is generally construed against the insurer who drafted the policy, but only if the language is genuinely ambiguous. Identify real ambiguities, not wishful readings.

### Evaluate Additional Insured And Endorsement Issues

Endorsements modify the policy, and additional insured status extends coverage to parties who are not named insureds. Both must be analyzed carefully.

Evaluate:

- the endorsements adding, removing, or modifying coverage;
- the additional insured endorsements and their scope;
- the relationship between additional insured coverage and contractual indemnity;
- the priority of coverage among multiple insureds;
- the effect of "other insurance" clauses;
- the consistency of the endorsement with the base policy;
- the requirements to maintain additional insured status.

Additional insured coverage often tracks the indemnity obligation, so its scope depends on both the endorsement and the underlying contract. Read them together.

### Assess Limits, Retentions, And Stacking

The financial structure of the policy determines how much protection it actually provides. Limits, retentions, and stacking rules drive real recovery.

Assess:

- the per-claim and aggregate limits;
- the retention or deductible and who pays it;
- the stacking of limits across policy periods;
- the erosion of limits by defense costs in some policies;
- the effect of self-insured retention on defense duties;
- the excess policy's attachment point and "follow form" obligations;
- the adequacy of the total program for the exposure.

A policy where defense costs erode the limit provides less effective coverage than one where defense is in addition. Model the financial structure realistically.

### Determine The Duty To Defend Versus Indemnify

The duty to defend is broader than the duty to indemnify and is triggered by the potential for coverage. Understanding the distinction is central to coverage strategy.

Distinguish:

- the duty to defend, triggered by allegations potentially within coverage;
- the duty to indemnify, triggered only when liability is actually established for covered claims;
- the standard for the duty to defend, often any potential allegation;
- the insurer's right to control the defense;
- the insured's right to independent counsel in conflicts;
- the allocation of defense for mixed covered and uncovered claims;
- the reservation of rights and its effect.

An insurer may owe a defense even when it ultimately owes no indemnity, because the duty to defend turns on the potential for coverage. Analyze both duties separately.

## Common Traps

### Concluding Coverage From Policy Type Alone

The policy type does not determine coverage. Read the insuring agreement, definitions, and exclusions.

### Ignoring A Controlling Exclusion

An exclusion can eliminate coverage that the insuring agreement grants. Apply every relevant exclusion.

### Confusing Claims-Made And Occurrence

The trigger determines when coverage applies. Do not confuse the two.

### Overlooking Conditions And Notice

Non-compliance with conditions can bar coverage. Check notice, cooperation, and consent.

### Missing Gaps Between Policies

Inconsistencies in a policy stack leave uncovered exposure. Map the full program.

### Assuming Ambiguity Always Favors The Insured

Only genuine ambiguities are construed against the insurer. Identify real ambiguity.

### Conflating Defense And Indemnity Duties

The duty to defend is broader. Analyze each separately.

## Self-Check

- Is the insuring agreement read first for the grant of coverage, covered perils, insureds, territory and period, definitions, and claims-made versus occurrence trigger?
- Are definitions applied rigorously for occurrence, injury, insured, professional services, expansion or limitation, interaction, and endorsement modification?
- Is every relevant exclusion analyzed for scope, application to facts, exceptions, order of analysis, anti-concurrent causation, and endorsement effects?
- Are conditions checked for notice, cooperation, consent to settle, mitigation, premium, precedent versus subsequent, and remedy for breach?
- Are gaps, inconsistencies, ambiguities, limit adequacy, retention adequacy, SIR versus deductible, and coordination with other policies and indemnities identified?
- Are additional insured and endorsement issues evaluated for additions and modifications, additional insured scope, indemnity relationship, priority, other insurance clauses, base policy consistency, and maintenance requirements?
- Are limits, retentions, and stacking assessed for per-claim and aggregate, retention payer, stacking, defense cost erosion, SIR effect, excess attachment and follow form, and program adequacy?
- Is the duty to defend distinguished from indemnify for trigger standard, defense control, independent counsel, allocation, and reservation of rights?
- Does the output escalate to qualified coverage counsel for coverage analysis, denial response, and reform recommendations?
