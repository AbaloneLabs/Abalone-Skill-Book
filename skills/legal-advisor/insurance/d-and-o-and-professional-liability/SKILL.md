# D&O And Professional Liability

Directors and officers liability (D&O) and professional liability, or errors and omissions (E&O), insurance protect individuals and entities against claims arising from their corporate governance and professional services. These policies differ materially from general liability: they are typically claims-made, they define coverage by the wrongful act or professional service, they exclude a range of business risks, and they interact tightly with corporate indemnification. Agents often treat D&O and E&O like ordinary liability policies, when in fact their structure, the distinction between insured persons and entity coverage, the indemnification interplay, and the consent-to-settle provisions create distinct strategic and risk considerations.

These policies also sit at the intersection of corporate governance and personal liability. Directors face personal exposure for their decisions, and the availability of indemnification and insurance affects their willingness to serve and the organization's ability to attract talent. Use this skill before reviewing a D&O or E&O policy, before managing a claim against directors, officers, or professionals, before advising on indemnification and advancement, or before assessing coverage for a governance or professional claim. The goal is to help the agent navigate these specialized coverages, not to provide legal advice, which requires qualified coverage and corporate counsel for the specific policy, jurisdiction, and corporate structure.

## Core Rules

### Understand The D&O Policy Structure And Towers

D&O policies are often structured in towers with sides, and the sides define who and what is covered. Understanding the structure is the foundation of coverage analysis.

Understand the sides:

- Side A, covering directors and officers directly when the company cannot indemnify;
- Side B, reimbursing the company for indemnification of directors and officers;
- Side C, covering the company itself for securities claims;
- the difference between insured persons and entity coverage;
- the priority and exhaustion among sides and layers;
- the role of Side A difference-in-conditions (DIC) coverage;
- the effect of insolvency of the company on indemnification and Side A.

Side A DIC coverage drops down to pay when the company cannot indemnify and other insurance is exhausted, protecting directors in insolvency. Understand which sides respond to a given claim.

### Analyze The Insuring Agreement And Wrongful Act Definition

D&O and E&O coverage turns on the definition of wrongful act or professional service and the insuring agreement's scope. These definitions are narrower than general liability.

Analyze:

- the definition of wrongful act, breach of duty, error, or omission;
- the definition of professional services in E&O;
- whether the act must be in an insured capacity;
- the claims-made trigger and the policy period;
- the covered claim types, demand, suit, regulatory proceeding;
- the territory and the persons covered;
- the relationship between the definition and the claim's facts.

An act outside the insured capacity, or a service outside the professional services definition, falls outside coverage. Match the claim facts to the definition precisely.

### Apply The Key D&O And E&O Exclusions

These policies contain specialized exclusions that frequently determine coverage. Each must be analyzed against the claim.

Apply exclusions:

- the insured-versus-insured exclusion, barring claims by the company against its own directors;
- the prior acts and pending litigation exclusions;
- the bodily injury and property damage exclusions, leaving those to general liability;
- the professional services exclusion in D&O, or the business risk exclusions in E&O;
- the fraud or intentional acts exclusion, often triggered by final adjudication;
- the securities exclusion in non-public company D&O;
- the pollution, employment, and other specific exclusions;
- the effect of endorsements modifying exclusions.

The insured-versus-insured exclusion can bar coverage for derivative suits or claims by a bankruptcy trustee, with important exceptions. The fraud exclusion often requires a final adjudication of fraud before it applies, protecting coverage during the defense.

### Coordinate Corporate Indemnification And Advancement

D&O coverage interacts with the company's indemnification obligations. Indemnification and advancement are often the first line of protection, with insurance as backstop.

Coordinate:

- the company's indemnification obligations under bylaws, charter, and law;
- the advancement of defense costs before final disposition;
- the standards for indemnification, successful defense or good faith;
- the coordination between Side B reimbursement and direct indemnification;
- the effect of the company's inability or unwillingness to indemnify;
- the role of Side A when indemnification is unavailable;
- the director's rights when the company refuses to advance.

Advancement is critical because directors face costs before resolution. Understand the indemnification framework and its interaction with the policy sides.

### Manage Consent To Settle And Hammer Clauses

D&O and E&O policies typically require the insurer's consent to settle, and hammer clauses penalize the insured for settling without consent. These provisions shape settlement strategy.

Manage:

- the consent-to-settle obligation and the reasonableness standard;
- the hammer clause, reducing the insurer's liability if the insured settles without consent;
- the strategic implications for the insured's reputation and future;
- the insurer's incentive to settle to limit defense costs;
- the insured's incentive to fight to clear their name;
- the negotiation of consent and the resolution of disputes;
- the effect on coverage if the insured breaches the consent clause.

Professionals may refuse consent to settle to protect their reputation and license, triggering the hammer clause. Understand the trade-off before acting.

### Handle Notice, Reporting, And Claims-Made Mechanics

D&O and E&O are claims-made policies, so the timing of notice and the reporting of circumstances are critical. Errors here can forfeit coverage.

Handle:

- the notice deadline, often prompt or within a defined period;
- the reporting of circumstances that may give rise to future claims;
- the effect of prior notice on subsequent policy periods;
- the interplay among multiple policy periods and renewal;
- the notice to all potentially responsive layers;
- the effect of late notice on coverage;
- the coordination with the insurer during the claim.

Notice of circumstances in one period can foreclose coverage in later periods for related claims. Manage reporting carefully across renewals.

### Address Entity Coverage And Allocation

When the company itself is sued alongside directors, entity coverage and allocation determine how the policy responds. These issues are central to securities and collective claims.

Address:

- whether the policy covers the entity, Side C or equivalent;
- the allocation between covered insured persons and the entity;
- the allocation among covered and uncovered claims;
- the erosion of limits by entity coverage;
- the effect on Side A availability;
- the insurer's allocation methodology and the insured's rebuttal;
- the coordination with indemnification for the entity's portion.

Entity coverage can erode limits that would otherwise protect directors, a concern in securities class actions. Understand the allocation and its effect on available limits.

### Navigate Regulatory, Criminal, And Investigation Claims

D&O and E&O often respond to regulatory investigations and criminal proceedings, not only civil suits. The definition of claim and the coverage for investigations matter.

Navigate:

- the definition of claim and whether it includes investigations;
- the coverage for subpoenas, civil investigative demands, and regulatory proceedings;
- the coverage for criminal proceedings and defense costs;
- the effect of the fraud or intentional acts exclusion on criminal matters;
- the coordination with government relations and disclosure;
- the advancement of costs in investigations before formal charges;
- the strategic considerations of insurer-funded defense in regulatory matters.

Some policies cover investigations only when a formal proceeding is initiated; others cover subpoenas. Read the claim definition to determine coverage for the regulatory matter.

## Common Traps

### Treating D&O Or E&O Like General Liability

The structure, definitions, and exclusions differ. Analyze the specific policy.

### Confusing Side A, B, And C Coverage

The sides define who and what is covered. Match the claim to the correct side.

### Overlooking The Insured-Versus-Insured Exclusion

This exclusion can bar derivative and trustee claims, with exceptions. Analyze it carefully.

### Ignoring Indemnification And Advancement

Indemnification is often the first protection. Understand its interaction with insurance.

### Breaching The Consent-To-Settle Clause

Settling without consent triggers the hammer clause. Understand the trade-off.

### Late Or Mismanaged Claims-Made Notice

Claims-made notice errors forfeit coverage. Manage reporting across renewals.

### Letting Entity Coverage Erode Director Protection

Entity coverage can exhaust limits. Understand allocation and Side A availability.

## Self-Check

- Is the D&O policy structure understood for Side A, B, and C, insured persons versus entity, priority and exhaustion, Side A DIC, and insolvency effects?
- Is the insuring agreement analyzed for wrongful act or professional service definition, insured capacity, claims-made trigger, claim types, territory, persons, and relationship to claim facts?
- Are key exclusions applied, including insured-versus-insured, prior acts, bodily injury and property damage, professional or business risk, fraud with final adjudication, securities, pollution and employment, and endorsements?
- Is corporate indemnification and advancement coordinated for obligations, advancement standards, defense success and good faith, Side B reimbursement, inability or unwillingness, Side A role, and director rights on refusal?
- Are consent-to-settle and hammer clauses managed for obligation, reasonableness, strategic implications, insurer and insured incentives, negotiation, dispute resolution, and breach effect?
- Are notice, reporting, and claims-made mechanics handled for deadline, circumstances reporting, prior notice effect, multi-period interplay, all-layer notice, late notice effect, and coordination?
- Is entity coverage and allocation addressed for Side C, allocation between persons and entity, covered and uncovered claims, limit erosion, Side A effect, methodology and rebuttal, and indemnification coordination?
- Are regulatory, criminal, and investigation claims navigated for claim definition, subpoena and demand coverage, criminal defense, fraud exclusion effect, government relations, pre-charge advancement, and strategic considerations?
- Does the output escalate to qualified coverage and corporate counsel for policy structure, indemnification, allocation, consent, and regulatory coverage?
