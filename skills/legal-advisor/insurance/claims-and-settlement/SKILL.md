# Claims And Settlement

The insurance claim is the moment the policy's promise is tested. How the claim is noticed, documented, negotiated, and resolved determines whether the insured actually recovers. Agents often treat the claim as a passive process, submitting a notice and waiting, when in fact the claim is an active negotiation whose outcome depends on the quality of the notice, the strength of the evidence, the strategy of the settlement demand, and the disciplined response to denials and reservations of rights. A poorly managed claim can turn a covered loss into an uncovered one through late notice, missed conditions, or unfavorable settlement allocation.

The claim also implicates the insurer's duties of good faith and fair dealing, and the insured's rights when those duties are breached. Bad faith and unfair claims practices provide remedies beyond the policy limits in many jurisdictions. Use this skill before managing a claim, before giving notice, before negotiating settlement, before responding to a denial, or before pursuing coverage action. The goal is to help the agent manage the claim strategically, not to provide legal advice, which requires qualified coverage counsel for the specific policy and jurisdiction.

## Core Rules

### Give Prompt And Complete Notice

Notice is often a condition of coverage, and late or incomplete notice can bar or prejudice the claim. Notice should be prompt, accurate, and comprehensive.

Give notice:

- to all potentially responsive insurers, primary and excess;
- as soon as practicable after the claim or occurrence;
- in the form required by the policy, written, specific information;
- with the facts known at the time, without speculation;
- with reservation of rights against late-notice prejudice;
- with a request for the insurer's position on coverage;
- with documentation of the notice and the insurer's response.

Notice to only the primary insurer may prejudice excess coverage if the claim later escalates. Notice all potentially responsive layers promptly.

### Gather And Organize Claim Evidence

The strength of the claim depends on the evidence of the loss and of coverage. Organize the evidence to support both the loss amount and the coverage trigger.

Gather:

- the facts of the occurrence or claim triggering coverage;
- the damages documentation, invoices, records, expert reports;
- the policy and all endorsements;
- the evidence that the loss falls within the insuring agreement;
- the analysis rebutting applicable exclusions;
- the proof of compliance with conditions;
- the correspondence with claimants and third parties;
- the chronology of the claim and the insured's response.

A claim file that is disorganized or incomplete weakens negotiation. Build the file to support both quantum and coverage.

### Understand The Insurer's Coverage Position

The insurer's response to notice, whether it accepts, denies, or reserves rights, shapes the strategy. Understand and respond to that position precisely.

Understand:

- whether the insurer acknowledged receipt and is investigating;
- whether it accepted coverage, denied it, or reserved rights;
- the specific basis for any denial or reservation;
- whether it is defending under reservation or declining;
- whether it has appointed counsel or agreed to insured's counsel;
- the timeline for the insurer's coverage decision;
- the effect of the position on settlement control.

A reservation of rights preserves the insurer's ability to deny later but may trigger the insured's right to independent counsel where a conflict exists. Analyze the reservation carefully.

### Negotiate Settlement Strategically

Claim settlement is a negotiation. The insured should approach it with a clear valuation, a documented basis, and a strategy for the insurer's likely responses.

Negotiate:

- the documented value of the loss with supporting evidence;
- the coverage analysis supporting recovery;
- the response to the insurer's valuation and coverage positions;
- the allocation between covered and uncovered components;
- the timing of the demand and the counter-offers;
- the authority to settle and the approval process;
- the effect of settlement on coverage for related claims.

An insurer may seek to allocate a mixed loss to uncovered components to reduce payment. Rebut unsupported allocations with evidence and coverage analysis.

### Respond To Denials And Reservations Of Rights

A denial or reservation is not the end of the claim. The response determines whether coverage is preserved and ultimately recovered.

Respond:

- analyze the denial's legal and factual basis;
- identify errors in the insurer's coverage interpretation;
- document the rebuttal with policy analysis and facts;
- preserve the claim and the right to challenge the denial;
- consider tolling or standstill agreements on limitations;
- escalate to coverage counsel for formal response;
- prepare for coverage litigation if the denial persists.

Accepting a denial without analysis forfeits coverage that may be recoverable. Many denials are reversed on proper response or in litigation.

### Manage Defense And Counsel Issues

When the insurer defends, control of the defense and choice of counsel can create conflicts. Manage these issues to protect both the defense and coverage.

Manage:

- whether the insurer appointed counsel or agreed to insured's counsel;
- the independence of defense counsel from the insurer;
- the right to independent counsel where a conflict exists;
- the defense counsel's role in coverage-implicating facts;
- the communication between defense and coverage counsel;
- the allocation of defense costs in mixed claims;
- the consent to settle and the hammer clause implications.

Defense counsel controlled by the insurer may steer the defense toward uncovered outcomes. Assert independent counsel rights where conflicts exist.

### Address Bad Faith And Unfair Claims Practices

Insurers owe a duty of good faith and fair dealing. Breach can expose the insurer to damages beyond policy limits in many jurisdictions.

Address bad faith:

- the standards for bad faith, unreasonable denial or delay;
- the unfair claims practices acts and regulations;
- the documentation of the insurer's conduct;
- the remedy, extra-contractual or punitive damages;
- the threshold and proof requirements;
- the strategic value of a bad faith claim in settlement;
- the coordination with the underlying coverage action.

Bad faith is a powerful lever but has high thresholds in some jurisdictions. Document the insurer's conduct and consult coverage counsel before asserting it.

### Coordinate With Underlying Litigation And Settlement

The claim often arises from underlying litigation against the insured. The claim strategy must coordinate with the underlying defense and settlement.

Coordinate:

- the underlying defense strategy and coverage implications;
- the settlement of underlying claims and insurer consent;
- the allocation of settlement between covered and uncovered claims;
- the covenant-not-to-execute and coverage implications;
- the major participant or Morris-type settlements where insurer denies;
- the effect of underlying admissions on coverage;
- the timing of coverage resolution relative to underlying resolution.

Settling the underlying case without insurer consent or allocation can forfeit coverage. Coordinate the underlying and coverage tracks.

## Common Traps

### Late Or Incomplete Notice

Notice failures can bar coverage. Notice all responsive insurers promptly and completely.

### Passive Claim Management

Waiting for the insurer to act cedes control. Manage the claim actively.

### Accepting A Denial Without Analysis

Many denials are reversible. Analyze and rebut before conceding.

### Unchallenged Allocation To Uncovered Claims

Insurers may allocate mixed losses to reduce payment. Rebut with evidence and coverage analysis.

### Ignoring Independent Counsel Rights

Conflicts may entitle the insured to independent counsel. Assert the right where it exists.

### Settling Underlying Cases Without Insurer Coordination

Unconsented settlements can forfeit coverage. Coordinate the tracks.

### Over-Asserting Bad Faith

Bad faith has high thresholds. Document conduct and consult counsel.

## Self-Check

- Is notice given promptly and completely to all potentially responsive insurers, in required form, with known facts, reservation against late-notice prejudice, coverage position request, and documentation?
- Is claim evidence gathered and organized for occurrence facts, damages documentation, policy and endorsements, insuring agreement fit, exclusion rebuttal, condition compliance, third-party correspondence, and chronology?
- Is the insurer's coverage position understood for acknowledgment, acceptance, denial, or reservation, specific basis, defense under reservation, appointed or agreed counsel, decision timeline, and settlement control effect?
- Is settlement negotiated strategically with documented value, coverage analysis, response to insurer positions, allocation, demand timing, authority, and effect on related claims?
- Are denials and reservations responded to with basis analysis, error identification, documented rebuttal, claim preservation, tolling consideration, coverage counsel escalation, and litigation preparation?
- Are defense and counsel issues managed for appointment, independence, independent counsel rights, coverage-implicating facts, communication, cost allocation, and consent-to-settle or hammer clause?
- Are bad faith and unfair claims practices addressed for standards, regulations, conduct documentation, remedies, thresholds, strategic value, and coordination with coverage action?
- Is the claim coordinated with underlying litigation for defense strategy, settlement consent, allocation, covenant-not-to-execute, major participant settlements, admission effects, and timing?
- Does the output escalate to qualified coverage counsel for denial response, allocation, independent counsel, bad faith, and coverage litigation?
