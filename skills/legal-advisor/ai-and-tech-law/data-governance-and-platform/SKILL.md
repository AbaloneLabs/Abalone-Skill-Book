# Data Governance And Platform

Data and platform regulation has moved from privacy alone to a broader regime covering who controls data, how platforms behave, and how digital markets compete. Agents often advise on data as if it were only a privacy issue and miss the data governance layer (who can access and reuse data, industrial data, and the data held by platforms), or treat platforms as neutral intermediaries and miss the gatekeeper obligations, content moderation duties, and competition rules that now apply. The interaction between data access, interoperability, and competition is where digital market regulation is most active.

Use this skill before advising on data strategy, platform obligations, gatekeeper compliance, content moderation, interoperability, or digital market competition. The goal is to force the agent to separate the privacy, data governance, platform, and competition layers, and to identify the obligations and risks in each. Data and platform law is rapidly evolving and jurisdiction-specific; conclusions must be confirmed against the applicable statutes, regulations, and guidance by qualified technology, competition, and regulatory counsel.

## Core Rules

### Separate The Data, Platform, And Competition Layers

Digital regulation is layered.

Distinguish:

- data protection and privacy (the processing of personal data);
- data governance (the ownership, access, reuse, and sharing of data, including non-personal and industrial data);
- platform regulation (the obligations of online platforms and gatekeepers);
- competition and antitrust (the conduct of dominant firms and the control of mergers).

A single digital business can trigger all four. Map the layers before advising.

### Address Data Governance And Access Rights

Data governance goes beyond privacy.

Address:

- the rights to access and reuse data (the data holder's rights and the user's rights);
- the distinction between personal data, non-personal data, and industrial data;
- the rules on data sharing (B2B, B2C, and B2G);
- the protection of trade secrets in data sharing;
- the sui generis database right and its limits;
- the data portability right (the right to receive and transmit data);
- the rules on AI training data.

Data is not freely appropriable. Identify the governance regime (portability, sharing, trade secret, database right) and the access rights.

### Classify The Platform And Its Obligations

Platform obligations scale with the type.

Determine:

- whether the service is a mere conduit, caching, or hosting (and the conditional liability safe harbor);
- whether it is an online platform with notice-and-action and transparency obligations;
- whether it is a very large online platform (VLOP) with risk assessment and mitigation duties;
- whether it is a marketplace or app store with additional duties;
- whether it is a gatekeeper under digital markets regulation.

The classification determines the content moderation, transparency, and competition obligations. Classify correctly.

### Manage Content Moderation And Platform Liability

Content moderation is regulated.

Address:

- the conditional liability safe harbor and its requirements (notice-and-action, no actual knowledge);
- the duty to remove or disable access to illegal content upon notice;
- the transparency reporting obligations (content removal, algorithmic curation);
- the procedural protections for users (notice, appeal, redress);
- the rules on algorithmic amplification and curation;
- the limits on content moderation (freedom of expression, and the risk of over-removal).

Platforms are not free to moderate as they wish. Follow the notice-and-action, transparency, and procedural rules, and balance moderation with expression rights.

### Apply Gatekeeper And Digital Markets Regulation

Gatekeepers have specific obligations.

Address:

- the gatekeeper thresholds (size, gateway role, entrenched position);
- the prohibitions (self-preferencing, anti-steering, data combination across services without consent);
- the obligations (interoperability, data portability, access to data);
- the enforcement and the penalties (significant percentage of turnover).

A gatekeeper that self-preferences or combines data across services without consent breaches digital markets regulation. Identify gatekeeper status and the obligations.

### Handle Interoperability And Access

Interoperability is a growing obligation.

Address:

- the interoperability obligations for gatekeepers (messaging, operating systems);
- the access to data and APIs;
- the standardization and the technical feasibility;
- the compensation and FRAND-type terms;
- the security and the protection of trade secrets in interoperability.

Interoperability can require opening messaging or operating systems. Assess the technical feasibility, security, and the terms.

### Address Competition And Antitrust In Digital Markets

Digital markets have specific competition issues.

Address:

- the abuse of dominance (self-preferencing, tying, exclusive dealing, predatory pricing);
- the control of digital mergers (killer acquisitions, portfolio effects);
- the hub-and-spoke and algorithmic collusion risks;
- the role of data as a barrier to entry;
- the interaction with gatekeeper regulation.

Dominant digital firms face heightened scrutiny of self-preferencing and data practices. Assess dominance and the conduct.

### Manage Cross-Border Data And Jurisdictional Reach

Digital regulation reaches across borders.

Address:

- the extraterritorial reach of digital regulation (offering services to users, targeting);
- the data localization and transfer rules;
- the choice of law and dispute resolution;
- the enforcement risk from foreign regulators.

Offering services to users in a jurisdiction can trigger its digital regulation. Assess the cross-border reach.

## Common Traps

### Treating Data As Only A Privacy Issue

Data governance (access, reuse, portability, trade secrets, database rights) is a separate layer. Address it.

### Treating Platforms As Neutral Intermediaries

Platforms have content moderation, transparency, and competition obligations. Classify and comply.

### Assuming The Safe Harbor Is Automatic

The conditional liability safe harbor requires notice-and-action and no actual knowledge. Build the process.

### Overlooking Gatekeeper Status

Gatekeepers have specific prohibitions (self-preferencing, data combination). Identify the status.

### Ignoring Interoperability Obligations

Gatekeepers may have to open messaging or operating systems. Assess feasibility and security.

### Treating Digital Competition As Traditional Antitrust

Digital markets have specific issues (self-preferencing, data barriers, killer acquisitions). Apply the digital lens.

### Over-Removing Content

Over-removal breaches expression rights and creates user trust issues. Balance moderation with rights.

### Ignoring Extraterritorial Reach

Offering services to users triggers regulation. Assess the cross-border reach.

## Self-Check

- Are the data protection, data governance, platform, and competition layers separated and mapped?
- Is data governance (access, reuse, personal vs. non-personal, sharing, trade secrets, database right, portability, AI training) addressed?
- Is the platform classified (conduit, hosting, online platform, VLOP, marketplace, gatekeeper) with the corresponding obligations?
- Is content moderation (safe harbor, notice-and-action, transparency, procedural protections, algorithmic curation, expression balance) managed?
- Is gatekeeper regulation (thresholds, prohibitions on self-preferencing and data combination, interoperability, penalties) applied?
- Are interoperability and access (messaging, operating systems, APIs, FRAND, security, trade secrets) addressed?
- Is digital competition (abuse of dominance, mergers, algorithmic collusion, data barriers) assessed?
- Is the cross-border reach (extraterritoriality, localization, choice of law, enforcement) managed?
- Does the output recommend confirmation against the applicable statutes, regulations, and guidance by qualified technology, competition, and regulatory counsel?
