# Worker Classification

Worker classification is one of the highest-frequency and highest-cost errors in employment law. Calling someone a contractor does not make them one, and misclassification can trigger back wages, overtime, payroll taxes, penalties, benefits exposure, and in some regimes personal liability for officers. Agents often rely on the contract label or the worker's preference and miss the multi-factor economic-reality and common-law tests that courts and agencies actually apply, and the divergent tests used by tax, employment, wage-hour, and benefits regulators.

Use this skill before engaging a contractor, converting a contractor to employee, defending a classification, responding to an agency audit, or designing a compliant engagement model. The goal is to force the agent to apply the correct tests per regulator, assess control and economic dependence, document the basis, and escalate to employment and tax counsel for high-stakes matters. This skill is decision support, not legal advice.

## Core Rules

### Apply The Correct Test For Each Regulator

Classification is not uniform. Different regulators use different tests, and a worker can be an employee for one purpose and a contractor for another.

Identify the relevant tests:

- common-law agency test (used by the IRS and some regimes), focusing on behavioral control, financial control, and relationship type;
- economic-reality test (used by the U.S. Department of Labor and many courts), focusing on economic dependence;
- ABC test (used by some states for wage and unemployment), presuming employment unless all three conditions are met;
- common-law control test used by state unemployment and workers' compensation agencies;
- benefits-plan classification under ERISA or equivalent;
- foreign-jurisdiction tests (e.g., U.K. IR35, EU directives, country-specific multi-factor tests).

A classification defensible under one test may fail under another. Analyze under every applicable regulator, not just the most lenient.

### Assess Behavioral Control

Behavioral control is a core factor across tests.

Evaluate:

- instructions on when, where, and how work is done;
- training provided by the business;
- evaluation of methods, not just results;
- supervision and integration into operations;
- required use of the business's tools, systems, or premises;
- sequencing and prioritization of work;
- required hours or schedules.

Detailed direction over how work is performed weighs toward employment. Focusing on results, not methods, weighs toward contractor status.

### Assess Financial Control And Economic Dependence

Financial factors capture whether the worker is in business for themselves.

Evaluate:

- investment in equipment, facilities, or training;
- opportunity for profit or loss;
- whether services are offered to the market generally;
- method of payment (hourly versus project or milestone);
- ability to subcontract or delegate;
- exclusivity and dependence on one client;
- reimbursement of expenses;
- benefits provision.

A worker who cannot realize a profit or loss, has no other clients, and is paid hourly resembles an employee economically.

### Assess Relationship Type And Formal Indicators

Formal terms matter but are not dispositive.

Evaluate:

- written contract and its characterization;
- benefits eligibility or provision;
- permanence versus defined project or term;
- integration into the business's core functions;
- provision of equipment, email, business cards, or titles;
- termination and notice terms;
- whether the relationship is indefinite or project-based.

A contract calling the worker a contractor is evidence but cannot overcome control and economic dependence. Align formal terms with the actual relationship.

### Identify High-Risk Engagement Patterns

Certain patterns strongly indicate employment.

Flag:

- long-term, full-time, exclusive engagements;
- workers integrated into teams with employee-like supervision;
- performance of core business functions;
- hourly pay with set schedules;
- use of company equipment and systems;
- repeated renewal of "project" engagements indefinitely;
- contractors who formerly were employees in the same role;
- single-client contractors with no independent business.

These patterns do not automatically make a worker an employee, but each increases risk and demands stronger countervailing indicators of independence.

### Document The Classification Basis And Review Cadence

Defensibility depends on documentation and periodic review.

Maintain:

- the classification analysis per regulator;
- the written agreement consistent with the actual relationship;
- evidence of the worker's independent business (clients, marketing, business formation);
- records of how the work is performed;
- a review cadence for long-term engagements;
- escalation triggers (e.g., duration thresholds, increased control, role changes).

Stale analyses for long-term contractors are a common audit failure. Re-review periodically and when circumstances change.

### Manage Reclassification And Its Consequences

Moving a contractor to employee status, voluntarily or under pressure, carries consequences.

Address:

- back wages, overtime, and meal-and-rest exposure;
- payroll tax liability and potential personal liability for officers;
- benefits-plan exposure (eligibility, back contributions, penalties);
- workers' compensation and unemployment exposure;
- transition mechanics (onboarding, payroll setup, benefits enrollment);
- communication and relationship management;
- voluntary reclassification programs and their terms.

Reclassification is not always clean. Quantify exposure across regulators before acting, and consider voluntary disclosure programs where available.

### Coordinate Across Jurisdictions And Platforms

Classification varies by jurisdiction and platform context.

Address:

- state-by-state variations (ABC states are stricter);
- foreign-jurisdiction tests and recent gig-economy legislation;
- platform-specific presumptions or carve-outs;
- industry-specific rules (e.g., construction, trucking, healthcare);
- joint-employer and staffing-agency interactions.

A classification valid in one state or country may fail in another. Analyze each jurisdiction where the worker performs services.

## Common Traps

### Relying On The Contract Label Or Worker Preference

Labels and preferences do not control. Apply the multi-factor tests.

### Single-Test Analysis

Different regulators use different tests. Analyze under all applicable regimes.

### Long-Term, Full-Time, Exclusive Engagements

These patterns strongly indicate employment regardless of the contract.

### Contractors Performing Core Business Functions

Integration into core operations weighs toward employment.

### Stale Classification Analyses

Circumstances change. Re-review long-term engagements periodically.

### Ignoring Benefits-Plan Exposure

Misclassification can trigger benefits eligibility and back contributions under ERISA or equivalent.

### Assuming One Jurisdiction's Rule Applies Everywhere

Classification varies by state and country. Analyze each location.

## Self-Check

- Is the classification analyzed under the correct test for each applicable regulator (common-law, economic-reality, ABC, control, benefits, foreign)?
- Is behavioral control assessed for instructions, training, supervision, integration, tools, scheduling, and methods versus results?
- Is financial control and economic dependence assessed for investment, profit-or-loss opportunity, market offerings, payment method, subcontracting, exclusivity, and benefits?
- Is relationship type assessed for contract terms, benefits, permanence, integration, equipment, titles, and termination?
- Are high-risk patterns (long-term, full-time, exclusive, core-function, hourly, former-employee, single-client) flagged and addressed?
- Is the classification basis documented with a written analysis, consistent agreement, evidence of independent business, performance records, review cadence, and escalation triggers?
- Is reclassification exposure quantified across wages, overtime, payroll tax, benefits, workers' compensation, and unemployment, with transition mechanics and voluntary programs considered?
- Are state, foreign, platform, and industry variations, and joint-employer interactions, analyzed for each jurisdiction?
- Does the output escalate classification decisions, audits, reclassifications, and multi-regulator exposure to qualified employment and tax counsel?
