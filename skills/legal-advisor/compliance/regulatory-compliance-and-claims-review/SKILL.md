# Regulatory Compliance And Claims Review

Regulatory compliance is not a single checklist. It depends on industry, jurisdiction, product, customer type, distribution channel, claims made, data collected, licenses held, and enforcement priorities. Agents often say "comply with applicable law" without identifying which law, regulator, evidence, process owner, or control applies. Marketing claims create a separate risk: a statement can be legally risky even if the product works, if the claim is broader than the evidence or missing required disclosures.

Use this skill before launching a product, writing marketing copy, publishing claims, entering a regulated market, reviewing compliance obligations, changing policies, collecting user data, or preparing a legal/compliance memo. The goal is to force the agent to connect activity, jurisdiction, regulator, claim, evidence, and control.

## Core Rules

### Define The Activity And Regulated Surface

Compliance starts with what the organization actually does. Generic business descriptions hide regulated activity.

Identify:

- product or service;
- customer type;
- geography;
- sales channel;
- data collected;
- payment method;
- advertising claims;
- regulated materials;
- professional services;
- health, finance, employment, children, housing, education, transportation, food, alcohol, drugs, securities, insurance, or consumer credit involvement;
- third-party partners;
- platform or marketplace role.

Do not assume a company is unregulated because it is small, online, or software-based. Regulated activity can arise from one feature, claim, data element, or customer segment.

### Map Regulators And Legal Sources

Identify who regulates the activity and which authority applies. A single product may face federal, state, local, foreign, industry, platform, and contract rules.

Map:

- statutes;
- regulations;
- agency rules;
- agency guidance;
- licensing requirements;
- state attorney general guidance;
- self-regulatory standards;
- platform policies;
- contractual compliance obligations;
- consent orders or enforcement trends;
- required notices or disclosures.

Use current official sources where possible. If relying on guidance rather than binding law, label it as guidance and explain why it matters.

### Review Claims Before Publication

Claims include more than ads. Website copy, packaging, labels, sales decks, investor decks, onboarding flows, testimonials, endorsements, product names, FAQs, chat messages, app store text, screenshots, and influencer content can all create legal risk.

For each claim, identify:

- exact claim text;
- express claim;
- implied claim;
- objective or subjective nature;
- target audience;
- medium;
- required substantiation;
- evidence available before publication;
- conditions or limitations;
- required disclosure;
- approval owner;
- date approved.

The FTC baseline for advertising is that claims should be truthful, not deceptive or unfair, and evidence-based. Some categories require specialized substantiation or mandatory disclosures.

### Match Evidence To The Specific Claim

Evidence must support the claim actually made. Evidence for one product, formulation, audience, use case, geography, or time period may not support a broader claim.

Ask:

- Does the evidence cover this exact product or service?
- Does it cover the claimed benefit?
- Does it cover the target audience?
- Does it cover the stated timeframe?
- Does it cover comparative claims?
- Does it cover "best," "guaranteed," "risk-free," "clinically proven," "secure," "private," "free," "Made in USA," or earnings claims?
- Are limitations disclosed clearly?
- Is the evidence current?

Do not turn a narrow study, anecdote, pilot result, or customer testimonial into a broad objective claim.

### Check Disclosures For Clarity And Placement

Disclosures do not cure every claim, and hidden disclosures are weak. The user should see material limitations before being misled.

Review:

- proximity to claim;
- readability;
- timing;
- mobile presentation;
- language;
- font and contrast;
- repeated claims across channels;
- audio or video presentation;
- checkout flow;
- cancellation or renewal terms;
- material connection disclosures for endorsements;
- country-of-origin qualifiers;
- price and fee disclosures.

A disclosure should clarify, not contradict, the main claim. If the main message and disclosure conflict, revise the claim.

### Build Compliance Controls Into Operations

Compliance should be embedded into workflows, not only reviewed at launch.

Define:

- policy owner;
- review process;
- approval workflow;
- evidence repository;
- change-monitoring owner;
- training;
- exception process;
- complaint handling;
- regulator inquiry process;
- incident escalation;
- vendor oversight;
- audit trail;
- retention period.

If no one owns the control after launch, the compliance plan is incomplete.

### Review Third Parties And Vendors

Businesses can face risk from affiliates, agencies, influencers, resellers, processors, lead generators, contractors, and platforms. Contract language alone is not enough if third parties make unsupported claims or mishandle regulated data.

Check:

- role of each third party;
- claims they can make;
- approval rights;
- monitoring process;
- data access;
- licenses and certifications;
- subcontractors;
- audit rights;
- breach notice;
- indemnity;
- termination rights;
- records required.

For endorsers and influencers, check material connection disclosures and whether the endorser can support personal-experience claims.

### Monitor Legal And Policy Changes

Compliance obligations change. A one-time launch review does not protect a business indefinitely.

Set review triggers:

- new jurisdiction;
- new customer segment;
- new product feature;
- new data type;
- new claim;
- regulatory update;
- enforcement action;
- complaint trend;
- vendor change;
- ownership change;
- platform policy change;
- incident or breach;
- renewal or certification deadline.

Track source, owner, date checked, and next review date.

## Common Traps

### Saying "Applicable Law" Without Naming It

This phrase is not a compliance plan. Identify the actual statute, regulation, agency, contract clause, policy, or guidance.

### Treating Marketing As Nonlegal

Marketing claims can create consumer protection, securities, health, privacy, intellectual property, employment, and contract risk. Review claims before publication.

### Relying On Customer Testimonials As Proof

A testimonial is not automatically substantiation for a general claim. It may also require disclosure of material connections or typicality limits.

### Hiding Material Terms In Fine Print

Important limitations, fees, renewal terms, eligibility requirements, and risks should not be buried. Disclosures should be clear and near the claim.

### Ignoring State And Local Regulators

Federal compliance does not end the inquiry. State attorneys general, licensing boards, local agencies, and foreign regulators may also matter.

### Assuming Vendors Carry The Compliance Burden

Vendors and agencies can help, but the business still needs oversight, approvals, contracts, and evidence.

### Forgetting Enforcement Evidence

If challenged, the organization needs records showing claim substantiation, approvals, policy versions, notices, training, complaints, and remediation.

## Self-Check

- Is the activity defined by product, customer, geography, channel, data, claims, partners, and regulated features?
- Are regulators, statutes, regulations, guidance, licenses, platform rules, and contractual obligations identified?
- Are exact express and implied claims captured before publication?
- Does evidence support the specific product, audience, benefit, timeframe, and scope of each claim?
- Are disclosures clear, prominent, timely, mobile-readable, and non-contradictory?
- Are compliance owners, approval workflow, evidence repository, training, monitoring, retention, and escalation defined?
- Are vendors, affiliates, agencies, influencers, processors, resellers, and platforms reviewed for compliance risk?
- Are legal and policy change triggers tracked with owner and next review date?
- Are high-risk sectors, children, health, finance, employment, housing, privacy, security, and earnings claims escalated for counsel review?
- Does the output avoid vague "comply with law" language by naming concrete obligations and unresolved questions?
