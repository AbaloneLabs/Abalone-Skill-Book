# Third-Party and Supplier Compliance

Third-party and supplier compliance is the area where a company's legal exposure is most often created by someone it does not directly employ. Regulators increasingly treat inadequate oversight of distributors, agents, vendors, and sub-processors as the company's own violation, whether under anti-bribery, sanctions, data-protection, export-control, or supply-chain regimes. The central judgment problem is calibrating diligence, contracting, and monitoring to actual risk rather than applying a single uniform process — and recognizing that the contract is only the beginning of the obligation, not the end.

## Core Rules

### Calibrate by risk tier, not by label

- Not every supplier warrants the same scrutiny. Risk-tiering should consider the nature of the relationship (agent vs. vendor vs. distributor), the services performed, geography, government touchpoints, access to data, access to facilities, and whether the third party will interact with officials or handle controlled items.
- A risk-based program applies proportionate diligence, contractual terms, and monitoring to each tier. A flat process either over-spends on low-risk vendors or under-protects against high-risk intermediaries — both are failures, the second more seriously.

### Onboarding due diligence must be verified, not collected

- Questionnaires are inputs, not conclusions. Beneficial-ownership identification, sanctions and watchlist screening, adverse-media checks, and verification of qualifications and reputation should be proportionate to risk and documented.
- Red flags — unusually high commissions, requests to pay third-country accounts, relatives of officials, shell ownership, reluctance to disclose ownership — require escalation, not acceptance with a note.
- For intermediaries who will interact with government officials (agents, sponsors, lobbyists), enhanced diligence should address the relationship to officials, compensation reasonableness, and the legitimate business purpose.

### Contractual flow-downs transfer obligations, not absolution

- The contract is the primary mechanism to extend compliance obligations to the third party and to preserve remedies. Core flow-downs typically include anti-bribery, sanctions, data-protection (with sub-processor and breach-notification terms), export controls, confidentiality, intellectual-property, audit rights, and termination-for-convenience and termination-for-cause rights.
- **Audit rights** should be drafted to be exercisable and meaningful (scope, notice, frequency, cost allocation) but must be reconciled with data-protection limits, especially where the audit would reach personal data or a sub-processor's environment.
- **Indemnification and liability** should reflect the risk allocation; uncapped or poorly scoped indemnities can create uninsurable exposure, while inadequate indemnities leave the company bearing third-party-created liability.
- A flow-down does not eliminate the company's own regulatory obligation. Where the third party is a data processor or a controlled-technology recipient, the company remains the responsible party under the applicable regime.

### Ongoing monitoring, not just onboarding

- Risk is dynamic. Sanctions lists change, ownership changes, adverse events emerge, and business scope creeps. Ongoing screening (including continuous or periodic re-screening), certifications, and periodic re-certification of higher-risk third parties are expected in mature programs.
- Transaction monitoring for unusual payments, geography, or counterparties is a key detection control, particularly for distributors and agents.
- A clear, documented escalation path for red flags, and a willingness to terminate or suspend, are necessary for the program to be credible.

### Specialized regimes create specific diligence obligations

- **Sanctions and export controls.** Screening against consolidated sanctions lists, denied-party lists, and end-use/end-user checks; analysis of jurisdiction and control; and diligence on the ultimate consignee and end use.
- **Data protection.** Where the third party processes personal data, a data-processing agreement with required terms (purpose limitation, sub-processor flow-downs, security, breach notification, transfer mechanisms, deletion on exit) is required under regimes such as the GDPR.
- **Supply-chain and forced labor.** Some jurisdictions impose due-diligence and reporting obligations regarding forced labor and human rights in supply chains, with import bans and enforcement risk. Tier-N visibility may be required for high-risk goods and geographies.
- **Anti-bribery.** Intermediary diligence, compensation reasonableness, and documentation of business purpose, as detailed in the anti-bribery skill.

### Termination and exit

- Termination rights (for cause, for convenience, and for regulatory change) should be preserved and exercisable. Transition assistance terms protect business continuity and data integrity on exit.
- Data return and deletion obligations on exit are especially important where the third party holds personal data, trade secrets, or regulated information.

## Common Traps

- **Treating due diligence as a form.** Collecting a self-attestation without verification, screening, or analysis of red flags does not constitute adequate procedures and provides little protection.
- **Uniform processes for all vendors.** Applying maximal diligence to low-risk vendors wastes resources, while applying minimal diligence to high-risk intermediaries creates the real exposure.
- **Assuming a flow-down clause is sufficient.** The contract allocates risk but does not satisfy the company's own regulatory obligations where the company remains the responsible party.
- **Ignoring sub-processors and tier-N parties.** Many regimes require oversight of the third party's own subcontractors; loss of visibility at tier-N is a frequent source of forced-labor, sanctions, and data-protection failures.
- **Audit rights that cannot be exercised.** Overly broad audit rights that conflict with data-protection law, or that the third party can effectively refuse, create false assurance.
- **No mechanism to act on red flags.** A program that detects but cannot escalate, suspend, or terminate is not a credible compliance program.
- **Stale ownership and sanctions data.** Onboarding-only screening misses changes in beneficial ownership and new designations; periodic or continuous re-screening is expected for higher-risk parties.
- **Ignoring business-purpose and compensation reasonableness.** Paying above-market rates to an intermediary with official ties is a classic corruption red flag regardless of contractual anti-bribery language.

## Self-Check

- Is the third-party program risk-tiered, with proportionate diligence, contracting, and monitoring for each tier, rather than a single uniform process?
- For each high-risk intermediary, is there verified (not merely collected) diligence on beneficial ownership, sanctions status, relationships to officials, and compensation reasonableness?
- Do contracts include appropriate flow-downs (anti-bribery, sanctions, data-protection, export controls, audit, termination) calibrated to the relationship and reconciled with data-protection limits?
- Is there ongoing monitoring — re-screening, transaction monitoring, re-certification — proportionate to risk, with a documented escalation path?
- For data-processing third parties, is there a compliant data-processing agreement with sub-processor flow-downs, breach notification, transfer mechanisms, and exit data-deletion terms?
- Have specialized regimes (sanctions, export controls, forced labor, anti-bribery) been addressed with regime-specific diligence rather than a generic compliance clause?
- Are termination and exit rights preserved and exercicable, with transition assistance and data return/deletion obligations?
- Have I flagged where the company remains the responsible regulatory party despite contractual flow-downs, and recommended qualified counsel for sanctions, export-control, and data-protection decisions?
- Have I stated the jurisdictional limits of general guidance and recommended counsel qualified in the relevant jurisdictions for enforcement-sensitive determinations?
