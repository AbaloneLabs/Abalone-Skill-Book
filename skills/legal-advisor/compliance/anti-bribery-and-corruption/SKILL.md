# Anti-Bribery and Corruption

Anti-bribery and corruption (ABC) law is a high-stakes, cross-border domain where a single improper payment can trigger criminal and civil liability, debarment, loss of government contracts, and successor liability for acquired companies. The defining trap is that the conduct usually happens far from headquarters, through intermediaries, and is often concealed by accurate-looking books. An agent must reason about who is paying whom, through whom, for what purpose, recorded how, and under which statutes' jurisdictional hooks — and must recognize that the absence of a U.S. nexus is rarely the end of the analysis.

## Core Rules

### Identify the controlling regimes and their jurisdictional reach

- **The FCPA** has two prongs: anti-bribery provisions (prohibiting payments to foreign officials to obtain or retain business) and accounting provisions (books-and-records accuracy and internal controls). The accounting prong applies to issuers and reaches conduct that the anti-bribery prong might not independently reach.
- **Jurisdiction is broader than it appears.** The FCPA reaches U.S. issuers, domestic concerns, and certain acts in furtherance taken in U.S. territory (including U.S. dollar clearing), and it can reach foreign companies and nationals acting through U.S. intermediaries or instrumentalities of interstate commerce.
- **The UK Bribery Act** is broader in key respects: it has no facilitating-payment exception, covers bribery of private commercial parties (not only public officials), and has a broad extraterritorial reach for U.K.-incorporated companies and those carrying on business in the U.K. The Section 7 "failure of commercial organizations to prevent bribery" offense creates strict liability subject only to an adequate-procedures defense.
- **Other regimes** — French Sapin II, German, Brazilian, and other national laws, plus multilateral conventions — may apply simultaneously. Identify all potentially applicable regimes before advising.

### Define "foreign official" and "instrumentality" carefully

- The definition of "foreign official" includes officers or employees of foreign government instrumentalities and of public international organizations. State-owned or state-controlled enterprises can be instrumentalities, and their employees can be foreign officials, depending on the entity's control, function, and government support.
- Political parties, party officials, and candidates for political office are also covered. Charitable contributions and sponsorships that benefit officials can be captured.

### Third-party and intermediary risk is the central exposure

- Most enforcement actions involve payments through agents, consultants, distributors, joint-venture partners, or local sponsors. The principal is liable for payments made with knowledge, which includes conscious disregard and willful blindness — not only actual knowledge.
- Risk-based due diligence on third parties should be proportionate to risk and documented: ownership, relationships to officials, qualifications, compensation reasonableness, and reputation. A premium above market or a payment routed to a different party is a red flag.
- Written agreements should include anti-corruption representations, audit rights, termination rights, and training obligations. Ongoing monitoring — not just onboarding — is expected.

### Gifts, travel, and hospitality

- Reasonable and bona fide expenditures directly related to promotion, demonstration, or execution of products or services have a narrow affirmative defense under the FCPA, but it is limited and does not exist under the UK Bribery Act.
- Evaluate each expenditure against the official's role, the business purpose, the amount, the destination, who accompanies the traveler, and whether expenses are paid directly or reimbursed. Entertainment and per diems that exceed modest limits are red flags.
- Many companies impose internal thresholds well below statutory limits because the risk is factual and reputational, not only legal.

### Facilitating payments, extortion, and duress

- The FCPA permits a narrow facilitating (grease) payment to expedite routine, non-discretionary governmental action. The UK Bribery Act does not. Where both regimes apply, the FCPA exception is unusable.
- Payments under true duress or extortion raise safety and documentation issues; they should be escalated, documented contemporaneously, and analyzed for disclosure obligations.

### Accounting provisions and internal controls

- Inaccurate characterization of improper payments (e.g., consulting fees, marketing, travel) violates the books-and-records provision even where the anti-bribery elements are not charged. Internal-controls provisions require reasonable assurance regarding transaction authorization, recording, and safeguarding of assets.
- Weaknesses in approval workflows, segregation of duties, and audit trails are themselves enforcement targets and are frequently the vehicle for resolving cases.

### Voluntary self-disclosure and cooperation

- Voluntary self-disclosure, full cooperation, and remediation can materially affect outcomes under FCPA corporate enforcement policy and analogous foreign regimes, including declination, reduced penalties, and monitorship avoidance.
- Disclosure decisions require analysis of jurisdictional exposure (e.g., SEC issuer obligations, criminal voluntary disclosure programs, foreign mandatory disclosure), privilege and data-protection constraints, and the risk that parallel proceedings (civil, criminal, foreign) will diverge. These decisions should be made with qualified counsel, not unilaterally.

### Successor liability in M&A

- Acquirers can inherit ABC liability for pre-acquisition conduct of the target, and the accounting of that conduct on the acquirer's post-closing books can create new issuer exposure. Pre-closing corruption due diligence, structuring (where lawful), and post-closing remediation and integration are expected and can affect enforcement outcomes.

## Common Traps

- **Treating "no U.S. company involved" as the end of the analysis.** U.S. dollar clearing, U.S. agents, U.S. subsidiaries, and instrumentalities of interstate commerce can create jurisdiction.
- **Reliance on the facilitating-payment exception.** It is narrow under the FCPA and nonexistent under the UK Bribery Act; where both apply, it offers no protection.
- **Due diligence as a checkbox.** A questionnaire with no verification, no beneficial-ownership check, and no risk-based escalation does not constitute adequate procedures and will not support an adequate-procedures defense.
- **Willful blindness.** Ignoring obvious red flags (unusually high commissions, payments to third-country accounts, relatives of officials) can constitute knowledge under the FCPA.
- **Assuming "reasonable" hospitality is always defensible.** The affirmative defense is narrow and fact-specific; lavish or loosely justified travel is high-risk.
- **Overlooking the accounting provisions.** Cases are frequently resolved on books-and-records and internal-controls theories, which do not require proving a corrupt intent.
- **Premature or ill-considered disclosure.** Disclosing before scoping jurisdiction, privilege, data-protection, and parallel-proceeding risks can create unmanageable exposure.
- **Ignoring monitorship and debarment.** Remediation quality and program maturity affect whether a monitor is imposed and whether the company can bid on government contracts.

## Self-Check

- Have I identified every potentially applicable regime (FCPA, UK Bribery Act, Sapin II, local law) and their respective jurisdictional hooks, rather than defaulting to a single statute?
- For each third-party relationship, is there documented, risk-based due diligence and ongoing monitoring proportionate to the risk?
- Have I analyzed whether any counterparty employee is a "foreign official" or employee of an instrumentality, based on control and function rather than label?
- For gifts, travel, and hospitality, have I evaluated business purpose, amount, companions, routing, and the availability (or absence) of an affirmative defense under each applicable regime?
- Have I avoided relying on the facilitating-payment exception where the UK Bribery Act or other no-exception regimes apply?
- Have I flagged books-and-records and internal-controls exposure separately from the anti-bribery analysis?
- For any potential violation, have I recommended qualified counsel for scoping, privilege, disclosure, and cooperation decisions, and avoided advising on disclosure unilaterally?
- In M&A, have I recommended pre-closing corruption diligence and post-closing integration planning to address successor liability?
- Have I stated the jurisdictional limits of general guidance and recommended counsel qualified in each relevant jurisdiction for enforcement-sensitive decisions?
