# Internal Investigations

Internal investigations are where privilege, evidence integrity, fairness, and reporting duties collide. An investigation that is scoped too narrowly, run by conflicted personnel, documented carelessly, or reported to the wrong audience can destroy privilege, taint evidence, fail to find the truth, or create new liability. Agents often treat investigations as fact-finding exercises and miss the legal architecture: trigger thresholds, privilege and work-product protection, Upjohn warnings, evidence preservation, witness protection, and mandatory reporting obligations.

Use this skill before launching an investigation, defining its scope and authority, conducting interviews, handling evidence, preparing findings, or deciding on reporting and remediation. The goal is to force the agent to connect triggers, scope, privilege, evidence, fairness, reporting, and remediation, and to escalate to compliance and litigation counsel for high-stakes matters. This skill is decision support, not legal advice.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Trigger And Determine Whether To Investigate

Not every complaint requires a formal investigation, but ignoring a trigger creates risk.

Assess:

- the source and credibility of the allegation;
- the legal and policy obligations triggered (mandatory reporting, duty to investigate);
- the severity and potential scope of harm;
- whether regulators, litigation, or insurance are reasonably anticipated;
- whether immediate interim action is needed (suspension, access restriction, preservation);
- conflicts of interest that require independent investigation.

A weak choice is launching a full investigation for a trivial matter or, worse, launching none for a serious one. Calibrate the response to the trigger and the duty it creates.

### Define Scope, Objectives, And Authority Before Starting

An investigation without defined scope expands uncontrollably or misses key issues.

Establish:

- the specific allegations and questions to answer;
- in-scope and out-of-scope matters;
- the legal framework and policies implicated;
- who commissioned the investigation and to whom it reports;
- the investigator's authority and independence;
- timelines and milestones;
- resource and expert needs;
- interaction with parallel legal proceedings.

Scope creep wastes resources and broadens privilege risk; too-narrow scope misses connected misconduct. Define scope deliberately and revisit it if findings warrant expansion.

### Protect Privilege And Work Product Where Appropriate

Privilege can shield investigation work, but only if structured correctly.

Address:

- whether counsel is directing the investigation for the purpose of legal advice;
- the role of non-lawyer investigators and their relationship to counsel;
- Upjohn warnings to employee witnesses (the company is the client, not the employee);
- segregation of privileged and non-privileged materials;
- the limits of privilege (it does not cover underlying facts);
- sharing that can waive privilege;
- crime-fraud and other exceptions;
- documenting the privilege basis.

Marking documents privileged does not make them so, and over-sharing waives protection. Structure the investigation to preserve privilege where it serves a legitimate purpose.

### Preserve And Collect Evidence Defensibly

Evidence handling determines admissibility and credibility.

Address:

- issuance of a litigation or preservation hold;
- identification of relevant sources (email, chat, systems, devices, paper);
- collection by qualified personnel with chain of custody;
- forensic preservation where deletion or alteration is a risk;
- documentation of collection scope and methods;
- handling of personal devices and remote data;
- coordination with IT and vendors;
- retention consistent with legal hold.

Sloppy collection, or collection that alters or deletes data, undermines findings and can itself become a violation. Preserve first, then investigate.

### Conduct Fair And Effective Witness Interviews

Interviews are the core of most investigations and carry their own risks.

Ensure:

- preparation with relevant documents and questions;
- Upjohn or equivalent warnings to employees;
- a second interviewer for corroboration;
- neutral, non-leading questioning;
- documentation by contemporaneous notes or statement;
- opportunity for the witness to explain;
- confidentiality instructions;
- protection from retaliation for participants;
- avoidance of coercion or promises that cannot be kept.

Leading or coercive interviews, or interviews without warnings, produce unreliable findings and create their own liability.

### Maintain Confidentiality And Need-To-Know Distribution

Investigation confidentiality protects integrity, privacy, and privilege.

Address:

- limiting distribution to those with a need to know;
- protecting the privacy of complainants, witnesses, and subjects;
- avoiding premature conclusions or public statements;
- managing rumor and reputational risk;
- distinguishing confidentiality from secrecy that hides misconduct.

Over-broad confidentiality (e.g., prohibiting employees from discussing misconduct) can violate whistleblower and labor laws. Tailor confidentiality to legitimate needs.

### Prepare Findings, Conclusions, And Documentation

Findings must be supportable and appropriately documented.

Address:

- separating facts from conclusions and recommendations;
- the evidentiary basis for each finding;
- credibility assessments and their basis;
- application of relevant policies and law;
- the format (oral report, written summary, full report) suited to the audience;
- draft review and accuracy checks;
- retention and privilege treatment.

A report that overstates conclusions or omits contrary evidence is vulnerable. Anchor findings in evidence and acknowledge uncertainty.

### Decide On Reporting, Disclosure, And Remediation

What happens after findings is as important as the investigation itself.

Address:

- reporting to the commissioning authority and governance bodies;
- mandatory regulator, law-enforcement, or insurer disclosures;
- affected-party communications;
- disciplinary or corrective action;
- control and policy remediation;
- monitoring to confirm effectiveness;
- documentation of remediation;
- closure and lessons learned.

Investigating without remediation signals a paper program. Connect findings to concrete corrective action and verify it took effect.

### Manage Parallel Proceedings And External Obligations

Investigations often run alongside litigation, regulator inquiries, or criminal matters.

Address:

- staying interviews or document creation that could prejudice litigation;
- coordinating with counsel in parallel proceedings;
- honoring subpoenas and production duties;
- avoiding obstruction or witness tampering;
- managing insurance and notice obligations;
- considering self-disclosure and its consequences.

An internal investigation that interferes with a regulator or court proceeding can create obstruction liability. Coordinate carefully.

## Common Traps

### Ignoring Or Under-Calibrating A Trigger

Failing to investigate a serious allegation, or over-investigating a trivial one, both create risk. Calibrate to the trigger.

### Scope Creep Or Too-Narrow Scope

Undefined scope expands uncontrollably or misses connected misconduct. Define and revisit deliberately.

### Privilege Lost By Structure Or Over-Sharing

Privilege requires proper structuring. Marking alone does not protect, and sharing waives.

### Sloppy Evidence Collection

Collection that alters or deletes data undermines findings and creates liability. Preserve first.

### Leading Or Coercive Interviews

Poor interview technique produces unreliable findings and liability. Warn, document, and question neutrally.

### Over-Broad Confidentiality

Prohibiting discussion of misconduct can violate whistleblower and labor laws. Tailor confidentiality.

### Investigation Without Remediation

Finding problems without fixing them signals a paper program. Connect findings to action.

### Interference With Parallel Proceedings

An investigation that obstructs a regulator or court creates new liability. Coordinate.

## Self-Check

- Is the trigger assessed for credibility, legal and policy duties, severity, anticipated litigation, interim action, and conflicts requiring independence?
- Are scope, objectives, authority, independence, commissioning and reporting lines, timelines, resources, and parallel-proceeding interaction defined before starting?
- Is privilege and work product structured through counsel direction, investigator roles, Upjohn warnings, material segregation, sharing limits, exceptions, and documentation?
- Is evidence preserved and collected defensibly through holds, source identification, qualified collection, chain of custody, forensic preservation, personal-device handling, and retention?
- Are witness interviews fair and effective with preparation, warnings, corroboration, neutral questioning, contemporaneous documentation, confidentiality, retaliation protection, and non-coercion?
- Is confidentiality maintained on a need-to-know basis, protecting privacy and integrity while avoiding over-broad secrecy that violates whistleblower or labor law?
- Are findings, conclusions, and documentation supportable, evidence-anchored, with credibility assessments, policy and law application, appropriate format, accuracy checks, and retention?
- Are reporting, mandatory disclosure, affected-party communication, discipline, control and policy remediation, monitoring, and closure addressed?
- Are parallel proceedings, subpoenas, insurance notice, obstruction avoidance, and self-disclosure consequences managed?
- Does the output escalate high-stakes investigations, privilege structuring, regulator interaction, and criminal exposure to qualified compliance and litigation counsel?
