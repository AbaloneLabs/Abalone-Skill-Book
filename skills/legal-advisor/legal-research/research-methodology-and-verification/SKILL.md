# Research Methodology And Verification

Legal research fails less often because of a wrong answer and more often because of a missing step. The researcher used the wrong keywords, stopped at the first hit, trusted a secondary summary, cited a case without checking its subsequent history, or never confirmed that the statute was still in force. The judgment problem in legal research is that confidence is easy to manufacture and hard to earn: a fluent summary can hide that the underlying authority was never verified, was superseded, or came from the wrong jurisdiction. Good methodology is what separates research that holds up under scrutiny from research that collapses the moment counsel checks the citations.

Use this skill before beginning any non-trivial legal research task, before reporting conclusions, and before handing research to counsel or a court. The goal is to make the agent plan the research, use systematic keyword and source strategies, evaluate credibility at every step, verify every authority, and state the limits of what was and was not checked. This is research support; conclusions about how the law applies to a specific matter require qualified counsel.

## Core Rules

### Frame The Research Question Before Searching

Imprecise questions produce imprecise research. Before opening a database, state the jurisdiction, the time period, the specific legal issue, the procedural posture, and the known facts that matter. Distinguish a broad background question from a narrow dispositive one. A strong research plan writes the question down, lists the key terms and their synonyms, and identifies the types of authority likely to answer it. A weak plan starts typing keywords into a search box and follows whatever looks relevant.

### Build A Layered Keyword And Source Strategy

Effective legal research moves from secondary sources to primary authority, not the reverse. Use treatises, practice guides, law review articles, and reputable summaries to orient, identify the key statutes and leading cases, and learn the vocabulary of the area. Then move to primary authority: statutes, regulations, court rules, and binding case law. Use both natural language and terms-and-connectors searches, and iterate the keywords based on the language the authorities actually use. Track which databases and searches were run so the work can be reproduced and updated.

### Evaluate Source Credibility At Every Step

Not every source that looks legal is authoritative. Rank sources by weight: binding primary authority first, persuasive primary authority second, official agency guidance next, then reputable secondary sources, then informal sources like blogs and news. Within secondary sources, prefer current, peer-reviewed, or practitioner-respected materials over random web content. For any proposition that affects rights, obligations, or strategy, require primary authority; never rest a material conclusion on a secondary summary alone. Note the publication date and edition, because legal sources go stale.

### Trace Citations Forward And Backward

Authorities cite other authorities, and following those trails is how you find the controlling law. Backward tracing means reading the cases and statutes cited by a useful source to find the foundational authority. Forward tracing, using citator tools, means finding later cases and amendments that cite, follow, distinguish, overrule, or criticize the source. Both directions matter: backward tracing finds the roots, forward tracing finds whether the roots still hold. A case that looks strong in isolation may have been gutted by a later decision you would only discover by tracing forward.

### Update And Verify Every Authority Before Relying On It

A citation collected yesterday can be wrong today. Before relying on any case, statute, or regulation, verify its current validity. For cases, run a citator check for reversal, overruling, vacation, or negative treatment. For statutes, confirm the current version and effective date. For regulations, confirm the rule has not been stayed, vacated, or amended. For guidance, confirm it has not been withdrawn. Record the verification date and tool used. Unverified authority is the most common and most embarrassing research failure.

### Define And Respect Research Limits

No research task is infinite. State explicitly what was searched, what databases were used, what time period was covered, what jurisdictions were included, and what was excluded. If the budget or deadline limited the depth, say so. If a question remains open because the authority is split, ambiguous, or absent, report that honestly rather than forcing a conclusion. A research memo that says "no controlling authority found within the scope searched" is more useful than one that pretends certainty.

### Manage Cost And Efficiency

Legal research can be expensive, especially on commercial databases with per-search or per-document charges. Plan the research to control cost: orient with free or firm-licensed secondary sources, target primary searches, avoid redundant pulls, and use citator runs strategically. For high-stakes matters, deeper and broader research is justified; for routine questions, a focused update may suffice. Match the depth to the stakes and document the tradeoff.

### Preserve The Research Trail

Good research is reproducible. Keep the search terms, databases, dates, citations, and verification results so that counsel can recheck, update, or extend the work. A trail also protects against the silent drift where a researcher remembers a conclusion but cannot recall its basis. When handing research to counsel, include the authorities found, their current status, and the open questions.

### Recognize When To Escalate Or Stop

Some questions exceed the scope of research and require analysis, judgment, or advocacy that belongs to counsel. Recognize when the research has surfaced a genuine legal judgment call, a split of authority, a novel issue, or a high-stakes risk, and escalate rather than overreaching. Stopping at the right point is itself a research skill.

## Common Traps

### Stopping At The First Relevant Hit

The first case or statute found is rarely the best or most current. Iterate the search and verify before concluding.

### Trusting A Secondary Summary As Authority

Secondary sources orient, but they do not bind. Always pull and read the underlying primary authority for any material proposition.

### Forgetting To Update Citations

A case or statute that was good when collected can be reversed, overruled, or amended. Verification is mandatory, not optional.

### Searching The Wrong Jurisdiction

A perfect case from another state is only persuasive, and a federal case interpreting state law may not bind state courts. Confirm the jurisdiction matches the forum.

### Overstating Certainty On A Split Or Novel Issue

When authority is split, ambiguous, or absent, say so. Forcing a confident answer misleads the reader and the client.

### Losing The Research Trail

If the citations and searches are not recorded, the work cannot be updated or defended. Keep the trail.

### Ignoring Cost And Scope

Running exhaustive searches on a low-stakes question wastes budget, while under-researching a high-stakes question creates risk. Match depth to stakes.

## Self-Check

- Is the research question framed with jurisdiction, time period, issue, posture, and key facts before searching begins?
- Does the strategy move from secondary orientation to primary authority, using both natural language and connectors, with searches tracked?
- Is every source ranked by credibility, with primary authority required for any material proposition affecting rights or obligations?
- Have citations been traced both backward to foundational authority and forward to current treatment?
- Has every cited case, statute, and regulation been verified for current validity, with the tool and date recorded?
- Are the research limits stated, including databases, jurisdictions, time period, exclusions, and open questions?
- Is the research depth matched to the stakes, with cost and efficiency considered and documented?
- Is the research trail preserved so counsel can reproduce, update, or extend the work?
- Does the output escalate genuine judgment calls, split authority, and novel or high-stakes issues to qualified counsel rather than forcing a conclusion?
