# Case Law And Precedent

Case law research is the process of finding judicial decisions that bear on a legal question and then judging whether they actually control the outcome. The hard part is not finding cases; it is judging authority. The same reported decision can be mandatory in one court, merely persuasive in another, and irrelevant in a third. A case can be reversed on appeal, overruled by a later decision, limited to its facts, or so heavily criticized that no court will follow it. Agents frequently grab a case whose language looks favorable, quote a sentence out of context, and present it as controlling, while missing that the case is from a different jurisdiction, was decided on other grounds, has been reversed, or is factually distinguishable in a way that destroys the analogy.

Use this skill before relying on any case in a memo, brief, intake analysis, or advice to a user, and before concluding that a legal proposition is settled. The goal is to make the agent evaluate the hierarchy of authority, read the case for its actual holding and reasoning, test the factual analogy, and verify that the case is still good law. This is research support; the final determination of how a court will treat authority belongs to licensed counsel.

## Core Rules

### Establish The Hierarchy Of Authority First

Before reading any case, know what binds your court. Mandatory authority is precedent a court must follow; persuasive authority is precedent a court may consider but is not required to follow.

- Decisions of a higher court in the same jurisdiction are mandatory on lower courts.
- Decisions of the same court or a coordinate court are usually binding or strongly persuasive depending on the jurisdiction.
- Decisions from other jurisdictions, federal courts interpreting state law, or sister states are persuasive only.
- Trial court decisions are rarely binding on anyone and are weak persuasive authority.
- Unpublished or non-precedential decisions may not be cited at all in some jurisdictions, or only in limited circumstances.

A strong analysis labels each case as mandatory, persuasive, or weak and explains why. A weak analysis stacks favorable quotes without regard to source.

### Separate Holding, Dicta, And Reasoning

Not everything in an opinion is law. The holding is the court's ruling on the legal issue necessary to decide the case before it. Obiter dicta are statements not necessary to the decision; they can be illustrative but are not binding. The reasoning is the logical path the court took; it explains the holding and predicts how the court will decide future facts. When citing a case for a proposition, confirm the proposition is part of the holding or necessary reasoning, not a passing comment. Over-reading dicta as holding is a common way a case gets dismissed as inapplicable.

### Test The Factual Analogy Explicitly

Precedent only controls analogous facts. Read the case for the legally significant facts, not the incidental ones, and compare them to your facts point by point. Identify the facts the court said mattered, the facts the court said did not matter, and any facts the court left open. A strong analogy shows that your facts match the legally significant ones; a strong distinction shows that your facts differ on a point the court treated as decisive. Do not claim a case is "on all fours" unless the material facts genuinely align.

### Verify The Case Is Still Good Law

A case can be undermined after it is decided. Before relying on it, check subsequent history for reversal, vacation, overruling, criticism, or limitation. A case that has been reversed is no longer good law for the reversed proposition. A case that has been distinguished into irrelevance by later decisions may technically remain good law but carry little weight. Use citator tools where available, but understand that a green or yellow flag is a signal to read further, not a guarantee. A case can also be legislatively overruled when a statute changes the rule it announced.

### Distinguish Binding Versus Persuasive Within The Same Report

Even within a single jurisdiction, the weight of a case can vary. A plurality opinion, a concurrence, and a dissent carry different weight. A decision by an en banc court binds more than a panel decision. A decision on a certified question from a federal court interprets state law and binds accordingly. Note which opinion you are quoting and whether it commanded a majority.

### Read Beyond The Headnote

Headnotes and summaries are editorial aids, not the court's words, and they can oversimplify, emphasize the wrong point, or omit qualifications. The holding lives in the body of the opinion. Always read the relevant passages in context, including the facts, the legal question framed, the rule stated, the application to the facts, and the disposition. Quoting language without reading the surrounding paragraphs is a reliable way to misstate the case.

### Map The Precedent To The Specific Court And Posture

The value of a case depends on who will decide your matter and at what stage. A case helpful to a plaintiff at the motion-to-dismiss stage may be unhelpful at summary judgment because the factual record differs. A case from a court of appeals may not predict how a trial judge will rule on a discretionary matter. Always frame authority in terms of the court and posture that will actually decide the issue.

### Use Official And Reliable Sources

Prefer official reporters, court websites, and reputable commercial databases. Avoid relying on case summaries from blogs, news articles, or secondary digests as authority. Secondary sources are useful for finding cases, but the case itself must be read and cited. Record the database, reporter, citation, and date accessed so the research can be verified and updated.

## Common Traps

### Quoting A Favorable Sentence Out Of Context

A sentence that supports your point may sit inside reasoning that ultimately reaches the opposite conclusion. Read the full passage before quoting.

### Treating A Persuasive Case As Binding

A well-reasoned case from another state is still only persuasive. Claiming it controls misleads the reader and weakens credibility.

### Ignoring Subsequent History

A case reversed or overruled after the citation was collected is a serious error. Always run a current citator check before filing.

### Overreading Dicta As Holding

Broad statements in an opinion that were not necessary to the decision do not bind later courts. Label them honestly as persuasive reasoning.

### Claiming Identical Facts When They Are Not

Courts read the cases you cite. If the material facts differ, the court will notice, and your credibility drops. Distinguish or limit the claim instead.

### Relying On Unpublished Decisions Where Prohibited

Many jurisdictions prohibit or restrict citation to unpublished or non-precedential decisions. Check the local rule before citing.

### Treating A Trial Court Loss As Precedent Against You

A single trial court ruling is weak authority and may be distinguishable. Do not let one unfavorable lower-court decision discourage a viable argument.

## Self-Check

- Is each cited case labeled as mandatory, persuasive, or weak, with an explanation tied to the deciding court's position in the hierarchy?
- Have you distinguished the holding and necessary reasoning from dicta, and confirmed the cited proposition is part of the holding?
- Is the factual analogy tested point by point against the legally significant facts, with distinctions and similarities stated honestly?
- Has each case been checked for subsequent history, including reversal, overruling, criticism, and legislative supersession?
- Have plurality, concurring, and dissenting statements been correctly identified rather than treated as majority holding?
- Was the relevant passage read in full context, beyond the headnote and editorial summary?
- Is the authority framed for the specific court and procedural posture that will decide the issue?
- Are official reporters, court websites, or reputable databases the source, with citation, reporter, and date recorded?
- Are unpublished or non-precedential decisions checked against the local citation rule before use?
- Does the output avoid presenting a case as controlling without confirming jurisdiction, hierarchy, and current validity through qualified counsel?
