# Legal Memo And Opinion

A legal memo or opinion is supposed to help someone decide. The recurring failure is that the writer produces a long recitation of law that never answers the question, or reaches a confident conclusion that hides the assumptions and the limits of the research. Objective legal writing is harder than persuasive writing because the writer must present the law against their own preferred outcome, acknowledge weaknesses, and tell the reader exactly how much weight the conclusion carries. Agents are prone to two opposite failures: writing a memo that reads like an essay and never reaches a recommendation, or writing a confident opinion that omits the jurisdiction qualifiers, the open questions, and the unresolved authority that would change the answer.

Use this skill before drafting any internal legal memo, opinion letter, research memorandum, or objective analysis for counsel or a client. The goal is to make the agent frame the question precisely, organize the analysis so the reader can follow and verify it, separate confirmed facts from assumptions, state the conclusion with honest limits, and make a usable recommendation. This is drafting support; formal legal opinions relied upon by third parties, securities matters, and regulated transactions require a licensed attorney.

## Core Rules

### Frame The Precise Question And Its Scope

A memo answers a specific question, not a topic. State the question exactly, including the jurisdiction, the time period, the parties, and the decision the reader is trying to make. Distinguish a narrow dispositive question from a broad background survey. If the reader asked one thing but the real risk is another, reframe and address both. A strong memo opens with the question stated so precisely that the reader knows immediately what will and will not be answered.

### Use A Predictable Analytical Structure

Objective legal writing relies on a structure the reader can navigate. The common frameworks are IRAC, CRAC, and CREAC: Issue, Rule, Application, Conclusion, with variations adding Rule Explanation or Counter-analysis. The structure exists so the reader can find the rule, follow the application to the facts, and reach the conclusion independently. Use it consistently for each issue, with one issue per section. A memo that mixes issues, jumps between rule and facts, or buries the conclusion forces the reader to reverse-engineer the analysis.

### Summarize Facts Separately And Honestly

Separate the facts from the analysis. Present the confirmed facts, the disputed facts, the assumptions, and the missing facts in their own section, with sources where possible. Do not let assumptions migrate silently into the analysis as if they were proven. Where a fact is uncertain, say so and explain how the answer changes if the fact is resolved differently. Conditional reasoning, framed explicitly, is a strength in objective writing, not a weakness.

### State The Rule With Authority And Limits

For each issue, state the controlling rule with citation to primary authority, and note whether the authority is binding or persuasive. Identify where the law is settled and where it is split, ambiguous, or absent. Do not present a single case or statute as if it settles a contested question without acknowledging the contrary authority. The reader needs to know not just what the rule says but how secure it is.

### Apply The Law To The Specific Facts

The application is the heart of the memo. Walk through each element or factor and connect it to the specific facts, the evidence, and the inferences. Address the strongest counter-arguments, because an objective memo that ignores the weakness reads as advocacy and loses credibility. Distinguish favorable authority on its facts and acknowledge unfavorable authority rather than pretending it does not exist. The reader should be able to predict how a court would likely rule, including the range of outcomes.

### Lead With The Conclusion For Decision-Makers

Decision-makers need the answer first. Use a bottom-line-up-front approach: state the conclusion and recommendation at or near the top, then support it with the analysis. A memo that makes the reader wade through twenty pages to find the answer fails its purpose. For multi-issue memos, give a short integrated conclusion that resolves how the issues interact, not a list of disconnected answers.

### Mark Limitations, Assumptions, And Open Questions

An honest memo states what it did and did not do. List the jurisdictions checked, the databases searched, the time period covered, the authority not reviewed, and the assumptions made. Identify open questions that would change the conclusion if resolved differently. This is not hedging; it is precision. A reader who knows the limits can weigh the conclusion correctly and decide whether to commission deeper research.

### Make A Usable Recommendation

A memo should end with a recommendation the reader can act on, calibrated to the confidence level. Distinguish a clear recommendation, a recommendation contingent on resolving an open question, and a recommendation to consult counsel or commission further research. Tie the recommendation to the reader's actual decision, whether that is to sign, file, settle, decline, or escalate. A memo that analyzes thoroughly but offers no recommendation leaves the decision-maker to do the hardest work alone.

### Match Tone And Formality To The Audience

An internal research memo, a client-facing opinion letter, and a board memorandum differ in tone, formality, and the degree of certainty appropriate to express. Opinion letters relied on by third parties carry malpractice risk and require careful qualification, reliance limits, and assumptions stated as such. Match the register to the audience and the stakes, and never let a fluent draft imply more certainty than the law supports.

## Common Traps

### Answering A Topic Instead Of A Question

A memo that surveys an area of law without resolving the specific decision is research, not advice. Force a precise question and a precise answer.

### Burying The Conclusion

Decision-makers need the answer first. Lead with the bottom line, then support it.

### Letting Assumptions Become Facts

Assumptions stated in the facts section must stay assumptions in the analysis. Mark them and run the conditional reasoning explicitly.

### Presenting Split Authority As Settled

Ignoring contrary authority makes the memo read as advocacy and undermines credibility. Acknowledge and weigh it.

### Omitting The Limits Of The Research

A memo that does not state what it checked and assumed cannot be weighed by the reader. List the limits honestly.

### Writing Advocacy In An Objective Memo

An objective memo that argues only one side fails its purpose. Address the strongest counter and the range of outcomes.

### Offering A Recommendation With No Confidence Calibration

A recommendation without a stated confidence level and contingencies invites overreliance. Calibrate it to the security of the law and facts.

## Self-Check

- Is the precise question stated with jurisdiction, time period, parties, and the decision to be made?
- Does each issue follow a predictable IRAC, CRAC, or CREAC structure, one issue per section?
- Are confirmed facts, disputed facts, assumptions, and missing facts separated in their own section with sources?
- Is the controlling rule stated with primary authority and labeled as binding or persuasive, with split or ambiguous authority acknowledged?
- Does the application connect each element to specific facts and evidence, and address the strongest counter-arguments?
- Does the memo lead with the conclusion and recommendation, integrating multi-issue answers?
- Are limitations, assumptions, open questions, and the scope of research stated explicitly?
- Is the recommendation actionable and calibrated to the confidence level, with contingencies flagged?
- Do tone, formality, and certainty match the audience and stakes, without implying more than the law supports?
- Does the output flag that formal opinions relied on by third parties require a licensed attorney?
