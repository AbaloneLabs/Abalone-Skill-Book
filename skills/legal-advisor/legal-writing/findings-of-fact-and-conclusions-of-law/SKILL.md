# Findings Of Fact And Conclusions Of Law

In a bench trial, arbitration, or administrative hearing, the written findings of fact and conclusions of law are the decision. Unlike a jury verdict, which is a black box, a court or tribunal's reasoned decision exposes the factual findings and legal reasoning on which the judgment rests, and that exposure is both the source of its legitimacy and the target of any appeal. Agents often draft proposed findings that read like closing arguments, that mix fact and law, that fail to tie each element of a claim or defense to specific credited evidence, or that omit the credibility determinations and legal standards a reviewing court expects. The judgment problem is to write findings and conclusions that are precise, internally consistent, tied to the record, and structured so that a reviewing court can follow the path from evidence to elements to judgment.

Use this skill before drafting proposed findings of fact and conclusions of law, before preparing a tribunal's decision template, before structuring the legal reasoning in an arbitral award, or before reviewing a written decision for appellate error. The goal is to force the agent to separate fact from law, address each element of every claim and defense, tie findings to record evidence with citations, make necessary credibility determinations explicit, and apply the correct legal standards. Findings and conclusions are jurisdiction- and tribunal-specific; proposed findings and appeals should be reviewed by qualified counsel.

## Core Rules

### Separate Findings Of Fact From Conclusions Of Law

The distinction between fact and law is foundational, because findings of fact are usually reviewed for clear error while conclusions of law are reviewed de novo. Mixing them invites the wrong standard of review and obscures the reasoning.

Structure the document so that:

- findings of fact state what happened, based on credited evidence;
- conclusions of law state the legal standards and how they apply to the found facts;
- each conclusion of law references the supporting findings of fact;
- mixed questions (for example, ultimate facts that border on legal conclusions) are placed deliberately and characterized consistently with the desired standard of review.

A party drafting proposed findings should place close calls where the more deferential standard of review helps its position, while recognizing the tribunal ultimately decides.

### Address Every Element Of Every Claim And Defense

A reasoned decision must resolve each element of each claim and each affirmative defense. Omitting an element leaves the judgment vulnerable to reversal.

For each claim:

- list the elements under the governing substantive law;
- make a finding of fact on each element, crediting or discrediting the supporting evidence;
- state whether the element is met and why;
- address each affirmative defense and whether it applies.

A decision that finds liability without addressing an element, or that grants a defense without findings on its elements, is incomplete and reversible. Use a checklist against the pleadings to ensure completeness.

### Tie Each Finding To Record Evidence With Citations

Findings must rest on evidence in the record, not on argument or assertion. Pinpoint citations are essential.

For each finding:

- cite the specific testimony, exhibit, or stipulation that supports it;
- address contrary evidence and explain why it was rejected or given less weight;
- avoid findings based on facts not in evidence or on judicial notice not properly taken;
- distinguish facts proven by a preponderance from those requiring a higher standard, where applicable.

Findings without record citations, or that rely on facts outside the evidence, are vulnerable to reversal for being clearly erroneous or unsupported.

### Make Credibility Determinations Explicit

When findings turn on whom to believe, the credibility determination should be stated, because reviewing courts generally defer to the fact-finder's credibility calls.

Address:

- which witnesses were credited and which were not, and why;
- inconsistencies in testimony and how they were resolved;
- demeanor observations where relevant and permitted;
- documentary evidence that corroborates or undermines testimony;
- the weight given to expert testimony and the basis for crediting one expert over another.

A bare finding that "the court credits the plaintiff" is weaker than one that explains the specific reasons. Explicit credibility findings strengthen the decision and narrow appellate review.

### Apply The Correct Legal Standards

Conclusions of law must apply the correct substantive and procedural standards, and applying the wrong standard is reversible error.

Confirm:

- the elements of each claim and defense reflect current governing law;
- the burden of proof and standard of proof are correctly stated and applied;
- any affirmative defenses with shifting burdens are analyzed under the correct framework;
- damages, if awarded, are calculated under the proper legal measure and supported by evidence;
- any legal preconditions (standing, ripeness, exhaustion) are addressed.

A decision that applies an outdated legal test or misallocates a burden of proof is vulnerable even if the outcome seems right.

### Structure The Reasoning For Reviewability

A well-structured decision lets a reviewing court follow the reasoning and identify any error.

Organize:

- an introduction stating the nature of the case and the disposition;
- a procedural history and statement of claims;
- findings of fact, organized chronologically or by issue;
- credibility determinations where relevant;
- conclusions of law, organized by claim and defense, each tied to findings;
- a damages or remedies section, if applicable;
- a clear statement of the judgment and any conditions.

A decision that jumps between fact and law, or that omits the bridge between findings and conclusions, frustrates review and invites reversal for inadequate findings.

### Anticipate The Standard Of Review

Draft with the appellate standard of review in mind, because it determines how much deference the findings and conclusions will receive.

Consider:

- findings of fact are reviewed for clear error or against the manifest weight, so precise, credited findings are hard to disturb;
- conclusions of law are reviewed de novo, so they must be correct;
- mixed questions may receive varying deference depending on the jurisdiction and the issue;
- evidentiary rulings are usually reviewed for abuse of discretion;
- damages awards are often reviewed for abuse of discretion or against the evidence.

Place close calls strategically and draft the findings and conclusions to survive the applicable standard.

## Common Traps

### Mixing Fact And Law

Conflating findings and conclusions obscures the reasoning and invites the wrong standard of review. Keep them separate.

### Omitting An Element

A decision that fails to address an element of a claim or defense is incomplete and reversible.

### Findings Without Record Citations

Findings must rest on credited evidence with pinpoint citations. Unsupported findings are vulnerable.

### Bare Credibility Findings

"The court credits the plaintiff" without reasons is weak. Explain the specific reasons for credibility calls.

### Applying The Wrong Legal Standard

An outdated test or misallocated burden of proof is reversible error even if the outcome seems right.

### Disorganized Reasoning

A decision that jumps between fact and law frustrates review. Structure it for followability.

### Ignoring The Standard Of Review

Drafting without regard to how findings and conclusions will be reviewed misses the chance to place close calls strategically.

## Self-Check

- Are findings of fact separated from conclusions of law, with mixed questions placed deliberately for the desired standard of review?
- Does the decision address every element of every claim and every affirmative defense, with a finding and conclusion on each?
- Is each finding tied to specific record evidence with pinpoint citations, and is contrary evidence addressed and weighed?
- Are credibility determinations explicit, stating which witnesses were credited and why, including expert testimony?
- Are the correct legal standards applied, including elements, burdens and standards of proof, shifting-burden defenses, and damages measures?
- Is the reasoning structured for reviewability, with introduction, procedural history, findings, credibility, conclusions by claim, and remedies?
- Is the standard of review anticipated, with close calls placed to maximize deference and conclusions drafted to survive de novo review?
- Does the output recommend qualified counsel review of proposed findings and conclusions for the specific tribunal and governing law?
