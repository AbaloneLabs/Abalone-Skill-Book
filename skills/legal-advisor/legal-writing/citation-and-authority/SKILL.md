# Citation And Authority

Citation is how legal writing proves its claims and lets the reader verify them. The recurring failure is treating citation as cosmetic formatting, when it is actually the load-bearing structure of legal authority. A citation does three jobs at once: it identifies the source, it tells the reader how much weight to give it, and it points to the exact page the reader should check. Agents frequently produce citations that name a case but omit the pinpoint, use a signal that overstates the support, fail to mark whether authority is binding, or cite a version of a source that cannot be located or verified. The result is writing that looks professional but collapses under scrutiny, because the reader cannot find the proposition at the page cited or discovers the case does not say what is claimed.

Use this skill before finalizing any citation in a memo, brief, or legal document, and before relying on a citation collected from another source. The goal is to make the agent apply a consistent citation system, use signals accurately, provide pinpoints, mark the weight of authority, and verify every citation against its source. This is drafting support; citation in filed documents remains the responsibility of the licensed attorney of record.

## Core Rules

### Pick A Citation System And Apply It Consistently

Legal citation follows formal systems, most commonly the Bluebook in the United States and ALWD as an alternative, with local court variations and jurisdiction-specific manuals. Pick the system the court or organization requires and apply it consistently throughout the document. Inconsistent citation signals carelessness and can draw the court's ire. Learn the rules for cases, statutes, regulations, secondary sources, and online materials in that system, and do not mix conventions.

### Provide Pinpoint Citations For Every Proposition

A citation to a case or statute without a pinpoint is often useless, because the reader cannot find the specific support. Provide the page or section where the proposition appears. For cases, cite to the exact page of the reported decision; for statutes, cite to the specific subsection. When a proposition spans multiple pages, give the full range. Pinpoints let the reader verify the claim quickly and demonstrate that the writer actually read the source.

### Use Signals Accurately To Show Degree Of Support

Citation signals tell the reader how directly the authority supports the proposition. No signal means the authority directly states the proposition. Signals such as "see," "see also," "cf.," "compare," "accord," and "e.g." indicate varying degrees of indirect support, and signals like "but see" and "contra" indicate contrary authority. Misusing a signal overstates or understates the support and misleads the reader. Use "see" when the authority supports the proposition by inference, "cf." when a comparison is needed, and reserve no-signal citation for direct statements. When in doubt, use a more conservative signal.

### Mark The Weight Of Authority Explicitly

The reader needs to know whether authority is binding or merely persuasive. Signal this in the text or parenthetical, for example by noting the deciding court and its relationship to the forum. A case from a higher court in the same jurisdiction is mandatory; a case from a sister state is persuasive; a trial court decision is weak. Do not let the reader assume a favorable case is controlling when it is only persuasive. Parentheticals can concisely explain the proposition and its weight.

### Verify Every Citation Against The Source

A citation copied from another source, a database snippet, or memory can be wrong. Before relying on it, pull the actual source, confirm the proposition appears at the pinpoint cited, and confirm the citation format is correct. For cases, confirm the reporter, page, and parallel citation; for statutes, confirm the current codified section; for regulations, confirm the title and part. Unverified citations are the most common and most damaging citation error, because they can misstate the law in a filed document.

### Check Subsequent History And Current Validity

A citation must reflect the current status of the authority. A case that was reversed or overruled must carry that subsequent history or be replaced. A statute that was amended must be cited to the current version. A regulation that was vacated or removed must not be cited as current. Run a citator check and update the citation to reflect any negative or superseding history. Citing dead authority as if it were live is a serious error.

### Cite Official And Reliable Sources With Correct Formats

Prefer official reporters and official code citations over commercial alternatives where the system requires. When citing online sources, follow the system's rules for URLs, archives, and access dates, because online content changes and disappears. For unreported or slip opinions, follow the jurisdiction's rules, which may restrict citation or require a specific format. Avoid citing to sources that the reader cannot locate or verify.

### Use Parentheticals To Explain Relevance Concisely

A well-crafted parenthetical tells the reader why the authority is cited and what it holds, in a phrase. Use it to summarize the proposition, note the key facts, or explain the weight. Keep parentheticals concise and consistent in voice, usually beginning with a present participle. Parentheticals turn a bare citation into a useful explanation without forcing the reader to pull the source.

### Handle Quotations And Alterations Correctly

When quoting, reproduce the source exactly, including original emphasis and internal quotation marks. Use brackets for alterations, ellipses for omissions, and the proper signals for added emphasis. Misquoting, even slightly, undermines credibility and can misstate the law. Block-quote longer passages per the system's rules and integrate shorter quotes into the text. Always provide the pinpoint to the quoted material.

## Common Traps

### Omitting The Pinpoint

A citation without a page or section forces the reader to hunt. Always provide the pinpoint.

### Using "See" When The Authority Directly States The Proposition

Over-signaling understates the support. Match the signal to the actual degree of support.

### Citing Persuasive Authority As If Binding

The reader assumes the case controls. Mark the weight, or the court will discount the argument.

### Copying A Citation Without Verifying

Secondhand citations are frequently wrong. Pull the source and confirm the proposition at the pinpoint.

### Citing Overruled Or Superseded Authority

Dead authority cited as live is a serious error. Check subsequent history and current validity.

### Misquoting Or Mishandling Alterations

Even small quotation errors damage credibility. Reproduce exactly and use brackets and ellipses correctly.

### Citing Unreportable Or Online Sources Improperly

Many jurisdictions restrict citation to unreported decisions, and online sources need proper formatting and access dates. Check the rules.

### Mixing Citation Systems

Switching between Bluebook and ALWD mid-document looks careless. Pick one and apply it consistently.

## Self-Check

- Is a single citation system, such as Bluebook or ALWD, selected and applied consistently throughout the document?
- Does every proposition carry a pinpoint citation to the exact page or section where the support appears?
- Are citation signals used accurately to reflect the degree of support, without overstating or understating?
- Is the weight of each authority, binding or persuasive, marked clearly for the reader?
- Has every citation been verified against the actual source, confirming the proposition appears at the pinpoint?
- Has subsequent history and current validity been checked, with overruled or superseded authority updated or replaced?
- Are official reporters, official codes, and properly formatted online sources used, with access dates where required?
- Do parentheticals concisely explain the relevance and weight of cited authority?
- Are quotations reproduced exactly, with brackets, ellipses, and emphasis handled correctly?
- Does the output flag that citation in filed documents remains the responsibility of the licensed attorney of record?
