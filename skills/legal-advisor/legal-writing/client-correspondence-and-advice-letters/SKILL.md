# Client Correspondence And Advice Letters

Written advice to a client is one of the highest-stakes documents a legal advisor produces, because it becomes the record on which the client relies, the evidence in any later malpractice or dispute inquiry, and often the document that defines the scope of the representation. Agents often write advice that is technically correct but operationally useless: it recites the law without translating it into a decision, hedges so much that the client cannot act, or fails to distinguish what the client must do from what is optional. Equally dangerous is advice that overstates certainty, gives a definitive answer on an uncertain question, or slips into practicing law in a jurisdiction where the writer is not licensed. The judgment problem is to write advice that is clear enough to drive a decision, honest about uncertainty, properly scoped, and protective of privilege and the advisor's role limits.

Use this skill before drafting a client advice letter, a status update, a risk communication, a board or executive memo, or any written guidance on which a client will rely. The goal is to force the agent to identify the decision the client must make, present the law and risk in actionable terms, calibrate certainty honestly, manage scope and privilege, and avoid creating negligent misstatement or unauthorized practice exposure. Written legal advice carries malpractice and reliance risk; significant advice should be reviewed by qualified counsel licensed in the relevant jurisdiction.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Decision The Client Must Make

Advice that does not drive a decision wastes the client's time. Before writing, determine what the client needs to decide.

Establish:

- the specific decision pending (sign, file, settle, disclose, terminate, escalate);
- the deadline and what happens if no decision is made;
- the options available and their consequences;
- the information still missing that affects the decision;
- the client's stated goals and risk tolerance.

Structure the letter around the decision. Lead with the recommendation or the options and their tradeoffs, then support with the analysis. A letter that buries the recommendation under pages of law fails the client.

### Translate Law Into Business And Personal Consequences

Clients act on consequences, not doctrine. Translate the legal analysis into what it means for them.

Address:

- the practical outcome of each option (cost, time, exposure, reputational impact, operational burden);
- the probability and magnitude of risk, stated honestly without false precision;
- the dependencies and contingencies that could change the outcome;
- the steps required to implement each option;
- the second-order effects on other matters, contracts, relationships, or obligations.

A statement that "this may be unenforceable" is weak unless it explains what unenforceability means for the client's deal, money, or operations. Make the advice operational.

### Calibrate Certainty Honestly

Overstating certainty creates reliance that can become a malpractice claim; understating it makes the advice useless. Calibrate to the actual state of the law.

Express uncertainty accurately:

- distinguish settled law from open questions;
- distinguish controlling authority from persuasive or absent authority;
- state the range of plausible outcomes and the factors that would determine them;
- avoid false precision in probability estimates;
- flag where the law is unsettled, evolving, or jurisdiction-dependent;
- identify the assumptions on which the advice rests.

A letter that gives a definitive answer on an unsettled question is a liability. A letter that hedges on a settled one is unhelpful. Match the tone to the actual certainty.

### Manage Scope And Avoid Unauthorized Practice

The advice letter defines the scope of the representation and the advisor's role. Drift outside the scope creates risk.

Confirm:

- the letter addresses only the matter and jurisdictions for which the advisor is engaged and licensed;
- advice on other jurisdictions or practice areas is flagged for referral to qualified local counsel;
- the letter does not opine on facts the advisor has not verified;
- the scope of the advice (what was and was not reviewed) is stated;
- the letter distinguishes legal advice from business, tax, accounting, or technical guidance that requires other professionals.

Writing advice on the law of a jurisdiction where the advisor is not licensed can constitute unauthorized practice. Refer cross-jurisdictional questions to local counsel and coordinate.

### Preserve Privilege And Confidentiality

The advice letter may be privileged if it is part of the attorney-client relationship, but privilege can be waived.

Protect privilege:

- address the letter to the client, not to third parties, unless joint representation is intended;
- mark privileged and confidential where appropriate;
- avoid copying non-clients whose inclusion may waive privilege;
- limit distribution to those who need the advice for the representation;
- distinguish legal advice (generally privileged) from business advice (often not);
- avoid embedding legal advice in documents likely to be disclosed (board minutes, public filings).

Privilege analysis is jurisdiction-specific. When in doubt, keep legal advice in a separate, clearly marked communication to the client.

### Structure For Clarity And Usability

A well-structured letter is read and acted upon; a poorly structured one is skimmed and misunderstood.

Use:

- an executive summary stating the recommendation or key conclusions up front;
- clear headings separating background, issue, analysis, options, and recommendation;
- bullet points for options, steps, and deadlines;
- plain language, avoiding unnecessary jargon;
- defined terms used consistently;
- a clear statement of next steps, owners, and deadlines.

Long, undifferentiated prose invites misreading. The client should be able to find the recommendation and the action items in under a minute.

### Document Assumptions, Limitations, And Reliance

The letter's value as a record depends on what it states about its own basis and limits.

State:

- the documents reviewed and the facts assumed;
- the jurisdiction and law on which the advice is based;
- that the advice is based on facts as understood and may change if facts differ;
- that no diligence or verification was done beyond what is stated;
- that the advice is for the client's benefit and not for reliance by third parties, where appropriate;
- the date, as advice can become stale.

These limitations protect the advisor from negligent misstatement claims and from third-party reliance. They also help the client understand the advice's boundaries.

## Common Traps

### Burying The Recommendation Under The Law

Clients need the decision first, the analysis second. Lead with the recommendation or options.

### Overstating Certainty On Unsettled Law

A definitive answer on an open question creates reliance risk and malpractice exposure. Calibrate honestly.

### Drifting Outside Scope Or Jurisdiction

Advice on unengaged matters or unlicensed jurisdictions is unauthorized practice. Refer to local counsel.

### Copying Third Parties And Waiving Privilege

Including non-clients can waive privilege. Limit distribution and mark the letter privileged.

### Advice Without Operational Steps

A recommendation the client cannot implement is useless. Translate law into steps, owners, and deadlines.

### Missing Assumptions And Limitations

Without stated assumptions, scope, and reliance limits, the letter creates negligent misstatement and third-party reliance risk.

### Stale Or Undated Advice

Law changes. Undated advice or advice that ignores pending changes misleads the client over time.

## Self-Check

- Does the letter identify the specific decision pending, the deadline, the options, and the consequences of inaction, and lead with the recommendation?
- Is the law translated into business or personal consequences, with probability, magnitude, dependencies, implementation steps, and second-order effects?
- Is certainty calibrated honestly, distinguishing settled from open law, controlling from persuasive authority, and stating assumptions and plausible outcomes?
- Is the advice scoped to the engaged matter and licensed jurisdiction, with cross-jurisdictional and non-legal questions flagged for referral?
- Is privilege preserved through addressing, marking, distribution, and separation of legal from business advice?
- Is the letter structured for clarity, with an executive summary, headings, bullet points, plain language, and clear next steps?
- Are assumptions, documents reviewed, jurisdiction, scope, reliance limits, and date documented to protect against negligent misstatement?
- Does the output recommend qualified counsel review of significant advice, especially for unsettled law or cross-jurisdictional questions?
