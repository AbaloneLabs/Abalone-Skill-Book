# Defects And Warranty

Construction defects range from patent problems visible at handover to latent problems that emerge years later. The legal treatment differs: patent defects are usually caught by inspection and the defects liability period, while latent defects may survive beyond completion and engage limitation periods, professional negligence, and latent defect insurance. Agents often assume that acceptance or the end of the defects liability period extinguishes all liability, missing that latent defects, design negligence, and misrepresentation can survive, and that the obligation to notify and allow cure affects the right to deduct or replace the contractor.

Use this skill before advising on a construction defect claim, warranty obligation, rectification dispute, or latent defect exposure. The goal is to force the agent to classify the defect, apply the warranty and limitation framework, and manage the notice, cure, and dispute mechanics before acting. Defect and warranty law is jurisdiction-specific and contract-dependent; any conclusion must be confirmed against the contract, the governing law, and the limitation regime.

## Core Rules

### Classify The Defect

Patent and latent defects follow different paths.

Distinguish:

- patent defects (apparent at handover or during the defects liability period);
- latent defects (hidden and emerging later);
- design defects vs. workmanship defects vs. material defects;
- defects in the works vs. defects in the design or specification provided by the owner;
- consequential defects (damage caused by a defect to other work).

The classification determines who bears the defect, the applicable standard, and the limitation period. Do not treat all defects alike.

### Apply The Warranty And Defects Liability Framework

Warranty obligations are contractual and statutory.

Map:

- the contractual defects liability or correction period and its trigger (taking-over, substantial completion);
- the obligation to rectify and the right to have others rectify after notice;
- the standard of performance (fit for purpose, reasonable skill and care, specific criteria);
- statutory implied warranties and their non-excludability;
- the effect of acceptance and taking-over on patent defects;
- the survival of latent defect and design negligence claims beyond the defects liability period.

Acceptance usually does not extinguish latent defect or negligence liability. Confirm what survives and for how long.

### Manage Notice And Opportunity To Cure

The right to cure affects remedies.

Address:

- the obligation to notify the contractor of defects;
- the period allowed for cure;
- the right to cure by repair, replacement, or credit;
- the consequence of denying cure (the owner may lose the right to deduct or claim the cost of replacement);
- emergency and safety exceptions that allow immediate action.

Denying the contractor a reasonable opportunity to cure can defeat a claim for the cost of replacement. Follow the notice and cure procedure unless safety requires otherwise.

### Evaluate Rectification, Rejection, And Deduction

Remedies differ by stage.

Assess:

- rejection of defective work before acceptance;
- the right to require rectification during the defects liability period;
- the right to deduct the cost of rectification or engage others;
- the measure of damages (cost of rectification vs. diminution in value);
- the effect of the owner's own contribution to the defect (interference, specification);
- the treatment of betterment and economic loss.

Courts may award diminution in value rather than the cost of rectification where rectification is unreasonable or disproportionate. Assess the realistic measure.

### Address Design Negligence And Professional Liability

Design defects engage professional liability.

Consider:

- the standard of reasonable skill and care for designers;
- the difference from the contractor's fitness for purpose or reasonable workmanship;
- the designer's professional indemnity insurance and its limits and duration;
- the effect of the owner-provided design on the contractor's responsibility;
- concurrent liability of designer and contractor.

A contractor building to an owner-provided or designer-provided design may not warrant the design. Trace design responsibility carefully.

### Apply Limitation Periods And Longstop

Timing controls defect claims.

Determine:

- the contractual limitation or longstop period;
- the statutory limitation period for contract and tort claims;
- the date of accrual (completion, discovery, damage);
- the discoverability rule and latent damage rules;
- the effect of extension agreements and standstills;
- the difference between the defects liability period and the limitation period.

The defects liability period is short; the limitation period for latent defects can be much longer. Do not assume the end of the defects period ends liability.

### Use Expert Determination And Dispute Resolution

Defect disputes need technical evidence.

Build:

- the dispute resolution clause and its tiers (negotiation, expert determination, mediation, arbitration);
- the role of independent technical experts;
- the scope and binding effect of expert determination;
- the preservation of evidence (access, sampling, testing, photography);
- the coordination of rectification with dispute resolution.

Destroying or altering defective work before evidence is preserved can defeat the claim. Preserve access and evidence before rectifying.

### Coordinate Insurance For Latent Defects

Insurance fills gaps in contractual and statutory liability.

Assess:

- latent defect insurance and its trigger and duration;
- professional indemnity insurance for designers;
- the contractor's liability insurance during and after construction;
- the effect of the policy on subrogation and recovery;
- the coordination with the contractual risk allocation.

Latent defect insurance can provide long-tail protection where contractual liability has expired. Confirm coverage and triggers.

## Common Traps

### Assuming Acceptance Ends Liability

Acceptance usually does not extinguish latent defect or negligence claims. Confirm what survives.

### Denying Cure And Claiming Replacement Cost

Denying a reasonable cure can defeat the claim for replacement cost. Follow the notice and cure procedure.

### Confusing Defects Liability With Limitation

The defects liability period is short; limitation for latent defects can be longer. Do not conflate them.

### Wrong Damage Measure

Courts may award diminution in value rather than rectification cost where rectification is disproportionate. Assess the realistic measure.

### Misallocating Design Responsibility

A contractor building to an owner's design may not warrant the design. Trace responsibility.

### Destroying Evidence Before Preservation

Altering defective work before evidence is preserved can defeat the claim. Preserve access and evidence.

### Missing Professional Indemnity Insurance

Designers' PI insurance has limits and duration. Confirm coverage for latent design defects.

### Ignoring Betterment And Owner Contribution

The owner's own specification or interference can reduce recovery. Account for contribution.

## Self-Check

- Is the defect classified (patent, latent, design, workmanship, material, consequential)?
- Are the contractual defects liability period, standard, cure obligation, and survival of latent claims mapped?
- Are notice and opportunity to cure followed, with exceptions only for safety emergencies?
- Are rejection, rectification, deduction, and damages assessed with the realistic measure (rectification vs. diminution)?
- Is design negligence and professional liability (standard, PI insurance, owner-provided design) addressed?
- Are contractual limitation, statutory limitation, accrual, discoverability, and longstop applied?
- Is dispute resolution (expert determination, arbitration) structured with evidence preservation?
- Is latent defect and professional indemnity insurance coordinated with contractual risk allocation?
- Is the effect of acceptance and taking-over on patent vs. latent defects distinguished?
- Does the output recommend confirmation against the contract, governing law, and limitation regime by qualified construction counsel?
