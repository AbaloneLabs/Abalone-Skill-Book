# Construction Contracts

Construction contracts allocate risk among owners, contractors, designers, subcontractors, and suppliers across a long, uncertain, and interdependent process. The choice of delivery method (design-bid-build, design-build, EPC, construction management), payment structure (lump sum, unit price, cost-plus, target cost), and risk allocation (variations, delay, defects, force majeure, site conditions) determine who bears the cost when something goes wrong, which it almost always does. Agents often copy a standard form without tailoring the risk allocation, or accept onerous terms (unlimited liability, consequential damages, liquidated damages) without understanding their interaction with insurance, bonds, and limitation periods.

Use this skill before drafting, negotiating, or reviewing a construction contract, or advising on a dispute over variations, delay, defects, or payment. The goal is to force the agent to align the delivery method, payment structure, and risk allocation with the project's actual risk profile. Construction law is jurisdiction-specific and standard-form-dependent (FIDIC, AIA, NEC, JCT); any conclusion must be confirmed against the applicable standard form, governing law, and project documents.

## Core Rules

### Match The Delivery Method To The Project

Delivery method drives risk allocation.

Compare:

- design-bid-build (owner holds separate design and construction contracts; owner bears design-construction interface risk);
- design-build and EPC turnkey (single point of responsibility; contractor bears integration risk);
- construction management (at-risk or agency; flexibility but coordination risk);
- PPP and concession (long-term financing, operation, and transfer risk).

Each method shifts interface, design, schedule, and cost risk. Choose deliberately and align the contract to the method.

### Choose The Payment Structure Carefully

Payment structure allocates cost risk.

Assess:

- lump sum (fixed price; contractor bears quantity and cost risk; owner bears scope clarity risk);
- unit price (contractor bears rate risk; owner bears quantity risk);
- cost-plus (owner bears cost risk; contractor has limited risk; needs fee structure and audit);
- target cost (shared risk above and below target; needs gainshare and painshare);
- guaranteed maximum price (cap on owner exposure; needs scope definition).

A lump sum without clear scope exposes the owner to variations. A cost-plus without audit exposes the owner to inflated costs. Align the structure to the project's certainty.

### Allocate Variation And Change Order Risk

Variations are the most common dispute.

Specify:

- the procedure for instructions and change orders (written, priced, time impact);
- the pricing of variations (rates in the contract, fair valuation, daywork);
- the timing of valuation (contemporaneous vs. after the fact);
- the effect on schedule and the requirement to claim extension of time concurrently;
- the consequence of proceeding without agreement on price.

Silent or vague variation clauses produce disputes over scope, price, and time. Define the procedure and the consequence of non-compliance.

### Address Delay, Disruption, And Extension Of Time

Delay claims are complex and evidence-heavy.

Map:

- the completion date and milestones;
- extension of time entitlement for excusable delay (force majeure, variations, client-caused, weather, authorities);
- the notice requirements (often strict and conditional on entitlement);
- liquidated damages for delay and their enforceability (genuine pre-estimate vs. penalty);
- concurrent delay and the prevention principle;
- disruption and loss of productivity claims (measured mile, total cost);
- acceleration and constructive acceleration.

Late notice can defeat an otherwise valid extension of time. Build notice procedures into the contract and the project management.

### Allocate Defect, Warranty, And Performance Risk

Defects liability extends beyond completion.

Specify:

- the defects liability or correction period and its trigger;
- the standard (fit for purpose, reasonable skill and care, specific performance criteria);
- the obligation to rectify and the right to have others rectify;
- the treatment of latent defects and the limitation period;
- performance guarantees and bonds;
- the effect of acceptance and taking-over.

Acceptance may not extinguish latent defect liability. Distinguish patent and latent defects and align the limitation period.

### Manage Bonds, Guarantees, And Security

Security affects cash flow and risk.

Address:

- performance bonds and on-demand vs. default guarantees;
- advance payment guarantees and their reduction;
- retention (percentage, cap, release timing, replacement with bonds);
- parent company guarantees and their enforcement;
- the interaction between security and the right to deduct or set off.

On-demand bonds can be called regardless of the underlying dispute, exposing the contractor to cash flow risk. Confirm the type and the conditions.

### Handle Force Majeure, Site Conditions, And Authority Risk

Construction faces physical and regulatory uncertainty.

Allocate:

- unforeseen ground conditions (differing site conditions) and the risk of adverse conditions;
- force majeure and the procedure for extension and cost;
- authority and permit delays;
- weather conditions beyond the norm;
- archaeological and environmental finds;
- third-party and utility interference.

Silent allocation of ground conditions risk usually leaves it on the contractor, which may be unsupportable. Allocate deliberately.

### Coordinate Insurance, Indemnities, And Liability Caps

Risk transfer tools interact.

Coordinate:

- contractor's all-risk and erection all-risk insurance;
- professional indemnity for designers;
- public and product liability;
- latent defect insurance;
- the liability cap and its carve-outs (gross negligence, willful misconduct, IP, confidentiality);
- the exclusion of consequential and indirect damages;
- the interplay with indemnities and the duty to defend.

An uncapped indemnity can defeat a liability cap. Align the caps, exclusions, indemnities, and insurance.

## Common Traps

### Copying A Standard Form Without Tailoring

Standard forms allocate risk by default. Tailor to the project.

### Lump Sum Without Clear Scope

A lump sum with vague scope produces variation disputes. Define scope.

### Vague Variation Clauses

Silent variation procedures produce disputes over price and time. Define them.

### Late Notice Defeating Extension Of Time

Strict notice conditions can defeat valid delay claims. Build notice into project management.

### Liquidated Damages As Penalty

LDs must be a genuine pre-estimate or they are unenforceable. Assess enforceability.

### On-Demand Bonds Misunderstood

On-demand bonds can be called regardless of dispute. Confirm the type.

### Uncapped Indemnity Defeating The Cap

An uncapped indemnity can defeat a liability cap. Align the risk transfer tools.

### Silent Ground Conditions Risk

Silent allocation usually leaves ground risk on the contractor. Allocate deliberately.

## Self-Check

- Is the delivery method (design-bid-build, design-build, EPC, CM, PPP) matched to the project's interface and risk profile?
- Is the payment structure (lump sum, unit price, cost-plus, target, GMP) aligned to scope certainty and cost risk?
- Are variation and change order procedures specified (instruction, pricing, timing, schedule impact, consequence of non-agreement)?
- Are delay, disruption, extension of time, liquidated damages, concurrent delay, and acceleration addressed with notice procedures?
- Are defect, warranty, performance, latent defect, and limitation periods allocated?
- Are bonds, guarantees, retention, advance payment security, and parent guarantees specified with type and conditions?
- Are force majeure, ground conditions, authority, weather, and third-party risks allocated?
- Are insurance, indemnities, liability caps, consequential damage exclusions, and carve-outs coordinated?
- Are notice procedures built into project management to preserve rights?
- Does the output recommend confirmation against the applicable standard form and governing law by qualified construction counsel?
