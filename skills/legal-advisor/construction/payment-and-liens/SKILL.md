# Payment And Liens

Getting paid is the central risk in construction, and the payment chain from owner through contractor to subcontractors and suppliers is long and fragile. Agents often assume that a contract right to payment is enough, missing that mechanics liens, payment bonds, notice requirements, pay-when-paid clauses, and the contractor's insolvency can defeat or delay payment. Mechanics lien regimes are especially powerful: a subcontractor or supplier unpaid by a remote contractor can encumber the owner's property, even though the owner paid the general contractor. The notice and timing rules are strict and jurisdiction-specific.

Use this skill before advising on construction payment, retention, lien rights, payment bonds, suspension for non-payment, or payment in insolvency. The goal is to force the agent to map the payment chain, secure lien and bond rights, and manage the insolvency risk before non-payment occurs. Payment and lien law is jurisdiction-specific and notice-critical; any conclusion must be confirmed against the applicable lien statute, bond requirements, and the contract.

## Core Rules

### Map The Payment Chain And Each Party's Rights

Payment flows through a chain, and each link has different rights.

Identify:

- owner, lender, and construction lender;
- general contractor and subcontractors (multiple tiers);
- suppliers and materialmen;
- designers and consultants;
- the payment terms at each tier (progress, advance, retention, final);
- the conditions to payment at each tier (certification, approval, pay-when-paid).

A subcontractor's payment can depend on the general contractor receiving payment from the owner. Map the chain and the conditions to understand exposure.

### Structure Progress, Advance, And Retention Payments

Payment structure affects cash flow and risk.

Specify:

- progress payment milestones and the certification process;
- advance payments and their recovery through reduction or guarantees;
- retention (percentage, cap, release timing, first and second half, replacement with bonds);
- the valuation of work in progress and variations;
- the treatment of off-site materials and goods;
- final payment and the effect of taking-over and the defects liability period.

Retention is the owner's security but the contractor's cash flow burden. Balance the two and consider replacement with bonds.

### Secure Mechanics Lien And Bond Rights

Liens and bonds are powerful payment security.

For lien rights:

- the lien statute and who has lien rights (contractors, subcontractors, suppliers, professionals);
- the preliminary notice requirements (often required early and from remote parties);
- the deadline to file or record the lien (often short after last work or supply);
- the deadline to enforce or foreclose the lien (often short after recording);
- the priority of the lien against mortgages and other interests;
- the effect of payment to the general contractor on remote lien rights.

For bond rights:

- payment bonds on public projects (where liens are not available) and private bonds;
- the notice and claim deadlines under the bond (often under the Miller Act or state little Miller Acts);
- the surety's obligations and defenses.

Missing a preliminary notice or a lien deadline extinguishes the right. Calendar them early and strictly.

### Evaluate Pay-When-Paid And Pay-If-Paid Clauses

These clauses allocate the owner's non-payment risk.

Distinguish:

- pay-when-paid (timing; the subcontractor is paid a reasonable time after the contractor is paid, but ultimately must be paid);
- pay-if-paid (condition precedent; the subcontractor is paid only if the contractor is paid);
- the enforceability of pay-if-paid in the jurisdiction (often disfavored or void as against public policy);
- the effect of the clause on lien and bond rights.

Pay-if-paid clauses that shift insolvency risk to the subcontractor are often unenforceable. Confirm the jurisdiction's treatment before relying on them.

### Manage Suspension, Slowdown, And Termination For Non-Payment

Contractors need remedies for non-payment.

Address:

- the right to suspend performance after notice and a cure period;
- the right to terminate for non-payment and the consequence;
- the effect of suspension on the schedule and extension of time;
- the preservation of lien and bond rights during suspension;
- the risk of wrongful termination and the consequence.

Suspension and termination are powerful but risky. Follow the notice and procedure strictly to avoid wrongful termination.

### Handle Certification, Dispute, And Withholding

Payment certification creates disputes.

Manage:

- the role of the engineer, architect, or certifier in payment certification;
- the consequence of withholding certification and the basis required;
- the procedure for disputing certified amounts (pay and argue vs. withhold);
- set-off and deduction rights and their limits;
- the treatment of disputed variations and claims in payment certificates.

Withholding without a contractual basis can be a breach. Follow the pay-and-argue or dispute procedure in the contract.

### Address Insolvency And Bankruptcy Of The Contractor Or Owner

Insolvency disrupts the payment chain.

Assess:

- the owner's insolvency and the contractor's lien and bond rights;
- the general contractor's insolvency and the subcontractor's direct rights against the owner and the surety;
- the effect of automatic stay on liens, set-off, and termination;
- preference and fraudulent transfer exposure for recent payments;
- the role of the bankruptcy trustee and the assumption or rejection of contracts;
- the completion of work and the use of replacement contractors and remaining funds.

Insolvency can defeat payment rights that were not secured before the filing. Secure liens and bonds early and monitor the chain's solvency.

### Coordinate With Lenders And Project Accounts

Construction lenders control the money.

Consider:

- the construction loan agreement and draw procedures;
- the lender's inspection, approval, and retention rights;
- project accounts and escrow of funds;
- the lender's step-in and completion rights on default;
- the effect of lender decisions on payment obligations.

Lender control of draws affects when and whether the contractor is paid. Understand the loan procedures and the lender's role.

## Common Traps

### Assuming A Contract Right Is Enough

Liens, bonds, and notice rules provide security that contract rights alone do not. Secure them.

### Missing Preliminary Notice Or Lien Deadlines

These are short and strict. Missing them extinguishes the right.

### Relying On Unenforceable Pay-If-Paid

Pay-if-paid is often void as against public policy. Confirm enforceability.

### Withholding Without Contractual Basis

Withholding without basis is a breach. Follow the dispute procedure.

### Ignoring Insolvency Risk

Insolvency can defeat unsecured payment rights. Secure liens and bonds early.

### Wrongful Termination For Non-Payment

Termination without strict notice and procedure is wrongful. Follow the procedure.

### Confusing Pay-When-Paid With Pay-If-Paid

They allocate risk differently. Distinguish and confirm enforceability.

### Overlooking Public Project Bond Requirements

Public projects use bonds instead of liens. Follow the Miller Act or little Miller Act deadlines.

## Self-Check

- Is the payment chain mapped with each party's rights, conditions, and payment terms?
- Are progress, advance, retention, off-site materials, and final payment structured with valuation and release?
- Are mechanics lien and bond rights secured with preliminary notice, recording, enforcement, and priority deadlines calendared?
- Are pay-when-paid and pay-if-paid clauses distinguished and their enforceability confirmed?
- Are suspension, slowdown, and termination for non-payment addressed with notice, procedure, and wrongful termination risk?
- Are certification, withholding, set-off, and dispute procedures managed with a contractual basis?
- Is insolvency of owner, contractor, or subcontractor assessed for stay, lien, bond, preference, and completion effects?
- Are construction lender draws, project accounts, and step-in rights understood?
- Are notice and lien deadlines calendared early and strictly?
- Does the output recommend confirmation against the applicable lien statute, bond requirements, and contract by qualified construction counsel?
