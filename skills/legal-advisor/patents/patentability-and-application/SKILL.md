# Patentability And Application

A patent is a government-granted monopoly on an invention in exchange for public disclosure, but only inventions that meet strict requirements qualify. Agents often describe an invention as patentable based on novelty alone, when the real hurdles are non-obviousness over the prior art, eligibility of the subject matter, sufficient written description and enablement, and claims drafted narrowly enough to be allowed but broadly enough to be useful. A poorly drafted application issues as a weak patent that is easy to design around or invalidate, or never issues at all. Worse, a public disclosure or sale before filing can destroy novelty in jurisdictions that require absolute novelty, foreclosing patent rights worldwide.

Use this skill before disclosing an invention publicly, before filing a patent application, before responding to an office action, before deciding between provisional and non-provisional filing, or when assessing whether an existing patent is valid. The goal is to force the agent to evaluate patentability rigorously, conduct and interpret prior art, draft claims strategically, and manage the prosecution timeline. Patent law is highly technical and jurisdiction-specific; patent prosecution requires a registered patent attorney or agent.

## Core Rules

### Evaluate The Three Core Patentability Requirements

Patentability is not a single test. An invention must meet multiple requirements, each of which can defeat the patent.

Assess:

- novelty, the invention must not be entirely disclosed in a single prior art reference;
- non-obviousness or inventive step, the invention must not be obvious to a person having ordinary skill in the art at the time of filing, considering the prior art as a whole;
- industrial applicability or utility, the invention must have a specific, substantial, and credible use;
- patentable subject matter eligibility, excluding abstract ideas, natural phenomena, and laws of nature unless integrated into a practical application;
- sufficiency of disclosure, including written description, enablement, and best mode where required.

A common error is concluding patentability from novelty alone. Most rejections are based on obviousness combining multiple references, not a single anticipatory reference.

### Conduct And Interpret A Prior Art Search

A prior art search tests novelty and obviousness and informs claim scope. A search that misses the most relevant references produces false confidence.

Search across:

- patent databases, granted and published applications, domestic and foreign;
- non-patent literature, including academic papers, conference proceedings, product manuals, and online disclosures;
- the inventors' own prior publications and products;
- public uses, sales, and offers for sale before the filing or priority date;
- the effective filing date and any priority claims that set the prior art cutoff.

Interpret the search results by mapping each reference to the claim elements, identifying anticipatory references and combinable references for obviousness, and noting the gaps that support patentability. Document the search to support a later due diligence or validity challenge.

### Manage The Critical Filing Date And Novelty Bar

The filing or priority date determines what prior art counts against the application. Public disclosures, sales, and offers before that date can destroy novelty.

Control:

- public disclosures, including publications, conference talks, demos, and investor pitches;
- offers for sale and actual sales, which can trigger a statutory bar in some jurisdictions;
- public use, including beta testing without confidentiality;
- the grace period available in some jurisdictions, such as the one-year US grace period, which does not exist in most others;
- the priority date from a first filing and the effect of provisional applications;
- absolute novelty jurisdictions where any pre-filing disclosure defeats the patent.

File before any public disclosure to preserve worldwide rights, because relying on a grace period forecloses protection in absolute novelty jurisdictions.

### Choose Between Provisional And Non-Provisional Filing

A provisional application establishes a priority date with lower formality and cost, but it must be followed by a non-provisional within 12 months. The choice affects cost, timing, and disclosure quality.

Weigh:

- the provisional as a placeholder that buys time to refine the invention and assess commercial value;
- the requirement that the provisional provide written description and enablement support for the later claims, or the priority benefit is lost;
- the risk that a thin provisional fails to support broad claims;
- the benefit of the term running from the non-provisional filing date;
- the cost of maintaining pendency through continuations.

A provisional that omits key aspects of the invention provides no priority support for those aspects and can be worse than no filing if it creates a false sense of security.

### Draft The Specification For Disclosure And Enablement

The specification must describe the invention in enough detail that a skilled person can make and use it. Insufficient disclosure invalidates the patent.

Ensure the specification includes:

- a written description showing possession of the claimed invention;
- enablement, teaching how to make and use the full scope of the claims;
- best mode or at least a useful mode where required;
- detailed description of embodiments and variations;
- examples and experimental data where relevant;
- definitions of terms used in the claims;
- drawings where useful for understanding.

A specification that supports only a narrow embodiment cannot support broad claims. Draft the specification with the broadest claims in mind, or accept narrower claims.

### Draft Claims Strategically For Scope And Defensibility

Claims define the legal monopoly. Drafting claims is the core patent skill, balancing breadth against validity and against the prior art.

Structure claims to:

- include independent claims defining the core invention and dependent claims adding limitations;
- cover the invention from multiple angles, such as apparatus, method, system, and computer-readable medium;
- avoid claiming ineligible subject matter by tying abstract ideas to concrete applications;
- avoid means-plus-function pitfalls by disclosing corresponding structure;
- anticipate design-around attempts by claiming alternative embodiments;
- support each claim limitation in the specification;
- consider claim differentiation, where different claims are presumed to have different scope.

A claim set that covers only one implementation is easy to design around. A layered claim set with independent and dependent claims across product and method provides stronger coverage.

### Navigate Examination And Office Actions

Prosecution is a negotiation with the examiner. Most applications receive rejections that require responses.

Manage prosecution by:

- analyzing each rejection, whether for anticipation, obviousness, eligibility, or formality;
- distinguishing the claims from the cited prior art with arguments and declarations;
- amending claims to overcome rejections while preserving scope where possible;
- interviewing the examiner to clarify positions;
- filing continuations or divisionals to pursue broader claims;
- appealing rejections when appropriate;
- tracking the term adjustment for examiner delays;
- avoiding prosecution history estoppel that limits claim scope in later enforcement.

Arguments made during prosecution become part of the file history and can limit claim interpretation in litigation. Avoid unnecessary narrowing statements.

### Coordinate With Trade Secret And Publication Strategy

Not every invention should be patented. Some are better kept as trade secrets, especially where detection of infringement is difficult.

Decide:

- whether the invention can be detected if practiced by a competitor, favoring patent;
- whether the invention is better protected as a trade secret, such as algorithms or processes difficult to reverse engineer;
- whether publication is strategic to create prior art against competitors;
- the timing of publication relative to filing to preserve rights;
- the effect of patent publication on trade secret protection for related know-how.

## Common Traps

### Concluding Patentability From Novelty Alone

Most rejections are obviousness, not anticipation. Evaluate combinability of references.

### Disclosing Before Filing

A pre-filing disclosure destroys rights in absolute novelty jurisdictions and starts the grace period clock elsewhere. File first.

### Thin Provisional Without Support

A provisional that lacks written description and enablement provides no priority for broad claims. Draft it like a real application.

### Claims Too Narrow To Be Useful

A patent that covers only one embodiment is easy to design around. Layer claims across angles.

### Claiming Ineligible Subject Matter

Abstract ideas and natural phenomena are ineligible unless tied to a concrete application. Integrate practical applications.

### Overlooking Foreign Prior Art

Foreign patents and non-patent literature count as prior art. Search globally.

### Making Narrowing Arguments In Prosecution

Arguments that distinguish the prior art can create estoppel and narrow claim scope in litigation. Choose words carefully.

### Patenting What Should Be A Trade Secret

If infringement is undetectable, a patent publishes the invention for competitors to use secretly. Consider trade secret protection.

## Self-Check

- Are novelty, non-obviousness, industrial applicability, subject matter eligibility, and sufficiency of disclosure each assessed against the prior art?
- Is a prior art search conducted across patents, applications, non-patent literature, the inventors' own work, public uses, and sales, with results mapped to claim elements?
- Is the filing or priority date managed to avoid public disclosures, sales, and offers that trigger novelty bars, with grace period and absolute novelty jurisdiction differences respected?
- Is the provisional versus non-provisional choice weighed for support, cost, term, and the risk of a thin provisional?
- Does the specification provide written description, enablement, best or useful mode, embodiments, examples, definitions, and drawings to support the claims?
- Are claims drafted in layers, covering apparatus, method, and medium, avoiding ineligible subject matter, anticipating design-arounds, and supported by the specification?
- Is prosecution managed by analyzing rejections, distinguishing prior art, amending strategically, interviewing, filing continuations, appealing, and tracking term adjustment?
- Are prosecution arguments reviewed to avoid unnecessary estoppel and narrowing?
- Is the patent versus trade secret decision made based on detectability, with publication timing coordinated?
- Does the output avoid asserting that an invention is patentable or a patent is valid and instead recommend a registered patent attorney for the specific invention and jurisdiction?
