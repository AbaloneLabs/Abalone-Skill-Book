# Infringement And Enforcement

A patent is only as valuable as the owner's ability to prove infringement and enforce it, and only as vulnerable as an accused party's ability to invalidate or design around it. Agents often read a patent claim and a product, conclude infringement, and miss that claim construction is contested, that the doctrine of equivalents is narrowing, that the file history may estop broad readings, and that validity challenges can wipe out the patent entirely. On the defense side, agents often treat a demand letter as a settled obligation, when the asserted patent may be invalid, non-infringed, unenforceable, or exhausted, and when a strong design-around may eliminate liability. Enforcement and defense analysis requires reading the claims, the specification, the file history, and the accused product together.

Use this skill before asserting a patent, before responding to a demand letter, before designing around a patent, before licensing or selling a patent, or when assessing litigation risk. The goal is to force the agent to construe the claims properly, evaluate infringement and validity on both literal and equivalents theories, and weigh the remedies and defenses realistically. Patent litigation is among the most complex and expensive; all enforcement and defense requires experienced patent litigation counsel.

## Core Rules

### Construe The Claims Using The Document And File History

Claim construction determines infringement and validity. Claims are interpreted from the perspective of a skilled person, using the claims, the specification, and the prosecution history.

Construe by:

- reading the claim language as the primary source;
- consulting the specification for definitions and context;
- reviewing the prosecution history for narrowing arguments, amendments, and disclaimers;
- considering the doctrine of prosecution history estoppel, which limits equivalents for claims narrowed during prosecution;
- resolving claim differentiation, where dependent claims presumed different from independent;
- identifying means-plus-function limitations and their corresponding structure;
- avoiding importing limitations from the specification into the claims.

A claim term that seems broad can be narrow if the specification defines it narrowly or if the applicant disclaimed scope during prosecution. Read the file history before asserting broad claims.

### Evaluate Literal Infringement Element By Element

Literal infringement requires that the accused product or process contains every limitation of the claim, literally. Missing one element defeats literal infringement.

Test:

- break the claim into its limitations;
- map each limitation to a feature of the accused product;
- identify any missing or differing element;
- consider the role of doctrine of equivalents for elements that are not literally met;
- account for reverse doctrine of equivalents where the product is outside the claim despite literal reading.

All-elements rule requires every limitation to be present or equivalent. A single missing element defeats infringement.

### Apply The Doctrine Of Equivalents Carefully

The doctrine of equivalents covers insubstantial differences that perform substantially the same function in substantially the same way to achieve substantially the same result. It is narrowing and subject to limits.

Apply limits:

- prosecution history estoppel bars equivalents for subject matter surrendered during prosecution;
- the specific exclusion principle bars equivalents for subject matter explicitly distinguished from the prior art;
- the disclosure-dedication rule dedicates to the public subject matter disclosed but not claimed;
- dedication and estoppel can bar the entire range of equivalents for a narrowed element;
- the all-limitations rule requires equivalents for each limitation, not the claim as a whole.

The doctrine of equivalents is a narrow supplement, not a broad second claim. Overreliance on it is a common error.

### Evaluate Validity Challenges

A defendant can avoid infringement by proving the patent invalid. Validity and infringement are independent analyses.

Assess validity challenges:

- anticipation by a single prior art reference disclosing every element;
- obviousness as a combination of references with motivation to combine and reasonable expectation of success;
- ineligible subject matter for abstract ideas, natural phenomena, and laws of nature;
- insufficient written description, enablement, or best mode;
- indefiniteness of claim terms;
- double patenting and terminal disclaimers;
- inequitable conduct before the patent office;
- statutory bars from pre-filing sales, offers, public use, or description.

A patent that reads on the accused product may still be invalid. Evaluate validity before asserting or before settling a demand.

### Assess Defenses Beyond Invalidity

Several defenses can defeat or reduce infringement liability.

Consider:

- non-infringement on proper claim construction;
- patent exhaustion, where an authorized sale exhausts the patent rights as to that item;
- first sale and repair versus reconstruction;
- experimental use and safe harbors;
- government use and compensation;
- license and implied license;
- laches and estoppel where applicable;
- standing and joinder defects;
- statute of limitations for damages.

### Evaluate Remedies And Damages

Enforcement seeks remedies. Understand what is available and how it is measured.

Remedies include:

- injunctive relief, requiring irreparable harm, inadequate legal remedies, balance of hardships, and public interest, with a higher bar after eBay for ongoing royalties;
- reasonable royalty damages, based on a hypothetical negotiation and the Georgia-Pacific factors;
- lost profits for market-based harm, requiring demand, absence of acceptable non-infringing substitutes, manufacturing and marketing capability, and lost profit calculation;
- willful infringement enhancement and attorney fees in exceptional cases;
- supplemental damages and interest;
- ongoing royalties for post-judgment use.

Injunctions are no longer automatic. Many patentees recover only monetary damages, especially non-practicing entities.

### Design Around Strategically

A design-around modifies the product to avoid the claim limitations. Done well, it eliminates infringement while preserving function.

Design around by:

- identifying the specific claim limitations to avoid;
- modifying the product so no limitation is met literally or by equivalents;
- documenting the design-around analysis with counsel to support non-infringement;
- considering the risk of inducing or contributory infringement by customers;
- obtaining a written opinion of counsel where appropriate to mitigate willfulness;
- evaluating the validity of the patent as an alternative path.

A design-around that still meets a limitation by equivalents is not a defense. Map the modification to each limitation.

### Handle Demand Letters And Pre-Suit Strategy

Demand letters trigger strategy decisions. Responding poorly can create admissions or estoppel.

Manage demands by:

- evaluating the merits of infringement and validity before responding;
- considering the statute of limitations and marking requirements for damages;
- assessing the risk of declaratory judgment jurisdiction;
- preserving evidence and litigation holds;
- evaluating design-around and license alternatives;
- coordinating with counsel to avoid admissions;
- considering joint defense with other accused parties;
- assessing the plaintiff's standing and portfolio.

## Common Traps

### Asserting Broad Claims Without Reading The File History

Narrowing arguments and disclaimers during prosecution can estop broad readings. Read the file history.

### Overreliance On The Doctrine Of Equivalents

Estoppel, dedication, and the all-limitations rule limit equivalents. Treat it as narrow.

### Ignoring Validity In Enforcement

A patent that reads on the product may be invalid. Evaluate validity before asserting.

### Treating A Demand Letter As Settled

The asserted patent may be invalid, non-infringed, exhausted, or unenforceable. Evaluate before paying.

### Missing An Element In Literal Infringement

All elements must be met. One missing limitation defeats infringement.

### Assuming Injunctions Are Automatic

Post-eBay, injunctions require irreparable harm and balance of hardships. Many cases yield only damages.

### Designing Around Without Documenting Non-Infringement

A design-around that still meets a limitation by equivalents is ineffective. Map modifications to each limitation.

### Forgetting Willfulness And Opinions Of Counsel

Continued infringement after notice can enhance damages. Consider opinions of counsel to mitigate willfulness.

## Self-Check

- Are claims construed using claim language, specification definitions, prosecution history, estoppel, claim differentiation, and means-plus-function structure?
- Is literal infringement tested element by element against the accused product, with the all-elements rule applied?
- Is the doctrine of equivalents applied within estoppel, specific exclusion, disclosure dedication, and all-limitations limits?
- Are validity challenges assessed for anticipation, obviousness, eligibility, written description, enablement, indefiniteness, double patenting, inequitable conduct, and statutory bars?
- Are defenses beyond invalidity considered, including exhaustion, repair versus reconstruction, experimental use, government use, license, laches, standing, and limitations?
- Are remedies evaluated for injunctive relief, reasonable royalty with Georgia-Pacific factors, lost profits, willfulness enhancement, fees, supplemental damages, and ongoing royalties?
- Is a design-around mapped to each claim limitation, documented with counsel, and evaluated for equivalents and induced or contributory infringement?
- Are demand letters managed with merits evaluation, declaratory judgment risk, evidence preservation, design-around and license alternatives, and avoidance of admissions?
- Are marking requirements, statute of limitations, standing, and joint defense options assessed?
- Does the output avoid asserting infringement, validity, or non-infringement and instead recommend experienced patent litigation counsel for the specific patent, product, and jurisdiction?
