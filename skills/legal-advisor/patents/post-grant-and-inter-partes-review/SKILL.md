# Post-Grant And Inter Partes Review

Post-grant proceedings let a party challenge the validity of an issued patent at the patent office, often faster and cheaper than district court litigation, and with different evidentiary standards and estoppel consequences. The availability and strategic value of these proceedings have transformed patent litigation strategy, but they are easy to misuse. Agents often assume a post-grant challenge is always preferable, ignore the broad estoppel that bars later invalidity arguments, miss the strict timing windows, or fail to coordinate the post-grant challenge with parallel district court litigation where the patent is being asserted. The judgment problem is to choose the right post-grant forum for the patent and circumstance, manage the estoppel and timing constraints, and integrate the challenge with the overall litigation and licensing strategy.

Use this skill before filing or defending a post-grant challenge, before deciding between inter partes review and district court validity defenses, before coordinating a challenge with parallel litigation, or before advising on the estoppel consequences of a prior challenge. The goal is to force the agent to select the appropriate proceeding, respect the timing and estoppel rules, and coordinate with parallel proceedings. Post-grant proceedings are jurisdiction-specific and procedurally complex; challenges should be handled by qualified patent counsel.

## Core Rules

### Select The Right Post-Grant Forum For The Patent And Circumstance

Different post-grant proceedings have different availability, timing, standards, and scope. Choosing the wrong one wastes time and can forfeit the challenge.

Compare:

- inter partes review (IPR), available for patents under certain statutes, challenging validity on prior art grounds, with a lower burden of proof than district court;
- post-grant review (PGR), available for newer patents within a short window, allowing broader grounds including subject matter eligibility and written description;
- covered business method review, where available, for financial business method patents;
- ex parte reexamination, available at any time, with a lower requester role and no estoppel;
- opposition proceedings before the European Patent Office or other regional offices, with their own timing and scope;
- district court invalidity defenses, which use a higher burden of proof but broader grounds and no filing fee.

Match the proceeding to the patent's age, the available grounds, the desired speed, the budget, and the estoppel tolerance. IPR is the workhorse for prior art challenges on most patents; PGR's broader grounds are available only in a short window for newer patents.

### Respect The Strict Timing Windows

Most post-grant proceedings have tight timing limits that, if missed, foreclose the challenge.

Track:

- the PGR window, typically nine months from grant or issuance of the patent;
- the IPR deadline, typically one year after the petitioner is sued for infringement, which can bar a later IPR;
- the timing for opposition in regional offices, often within months of grant;
- the bar on filing more than one IPR absent good cause;
- the effect of a statutory disclaimer or terminal disclaimer on timing.

Missing the one-year IPR deadline after being sued is a common and fatal error. Calendar these deadlines at the start of any patent dispute.

### Manage The Estoppel Consequences

Post-grant proceedings create estoppel that can bar later challenges, and the scope of estoppel differs by proceeding.

Understand:

- IPR estoppel bars the petitioner, in later proceedings, from asserting invalidity on grounds it raised or reasonably could have raised;
- PGR estoppel is broader, covering more grounds;
- ex parte reexamination creates no requester estoppel;
- estoppel applies in the Patent Trial and Appeal Board, district court, and the International Trade Commission;
- the estoppel for grounds that "reasonably could have been raised" is contested and depends on what prior art was known or discoverable.

A petitioner that brings an IPR and loses may be barred from raising the same invalidity arguments in district court. Assess the strength of the challenge before filing, because a weak IPR can weaken the litigation defense.

### Coordinate With Parallel District Court Litigation

When a patent is asserted in district court, a parallel post-grant challenge can change the litigation dynamics, but coordination is essential.

Coordinate:

- the automatic stay of district court litigation that may accompany an IPR, and the factors courts weigh in granting or denying a stay;
- the effect of the post-grant decision on the litigation, including potential cancellation of claims;
- the timing of claim construction in the PTAB versus the district court, which can diverge;
- settlement dynamics, since a pending post-grant challenge affects leverage;
- the risk that district court discovery reveals prior art that must be included in the IPR.

A well-timed IPR can stall litigation and force settlement; a poorly timed one can proceed while the litigation races ahead. Align the two proceedings strategically.

### Assess The Institution Decision And The Burden

The patent office decides whether to institute a post-grant proceeding, and the institution threshold affects strategy.

Evaluate:

- the reasonable likelihood that at least one claim is unpatentable, the institution standard for IPR;
- the strength of the prior art and the claim construction arguments;
- the patent owner's preliminary response and the likely amendments;
- the higher bar for institution in some proceedings versus others;
- the statistical likelihood of institution and of claim cancellation once instituted.

Filing a petition that is denied institution wastes resources and signals weakness. Assess the institution prospects realistically before filing.

### Prepare For Patent Owner Amendments And Surrogate Claims

Patent owners can amend claims during a post-grant proceeding, and amended or substitute claims can change the landscape.

Anticipate:

- the patent owner's motion to amend and the scope of the substitute claims;
- the burden on the patent owner to show patentability of the amended claims;
- the petitioner's opportunity to oppose amendments and to challenge the substitute claims;
- the effect of amended claims on parallel litigation and pending products;
- the risk that narrower but valid claims still cover the accused product.

A patent owner may narrow claims to survive the challenge while still capturing the accused product. Analyze the amendment strategy and its effect on the dispute.

### Integrate With Overall Licensing And Litigation Strategy

A post-grant challenge is a strategic tool that interacts with licensing, settlement, and parallel disputes.

Consider:

- the effect on licensing negotiations and the patent's perceived value;
- the use of multiple IPRs against a portfolio asserted by a non-practicing entity;
- the coordination with other defendants facing the same patent;
- the settlement leverage created by a pending or instituted challenge;
- the cost-benefit of post-grant challenge versus paying a license.

Post-grant proceedings are most powerful when integrated into a broader strategy, not run in isolation.

## Common Traps

### Choosing The Wrong Proceeding

Filing a PGR outside its window, or an IPR when PGR's broader grounds were available, wastes the opportunity.

### Missing The One-Year IPR Deadline

The deadline to file an IPR after being sued is strict. Calendar it immediately.

### Underestimating Estoppel

A losing IPR can bar later invalidity arguments in district court. Assess strength before filing.

### Filing A Weak Petition

A denied institution wastes resources and signals weakness in parallel litigation.

### Ignoring Parallel Litigation Coordination

A post-grant challenge run without coordinating stays, claim construction, and settlement can be undercut by the litigation.

### Overlooking Patent Owner Amendments

Narrowed substitute claims may still cover the accused product. Analyze the amendment strategy.

### Running The Challenge In Isolation

A post-grant proceeding is most effective when integrated with licensing, settlement, and multi-defendant strategy.

## Self-Check

- Is the post-grant forum selected to match the patent's age, available grounds, desired speed, budget, and estoppel tolerance?
- Are the strict timing windows (PGR window, one-year IPR deadline, regional opposition windows) tracked and calendared?
- Is the estoppel scope understood for the chosen proceeding, including grounds raised and reasonably could have been raised, across the PTAB, district court, and ITC?
- Is the post-grant challenge coordinated with parallel district court litigation, including stays, claim construction, discovery, and settlement?
- Is the institution decision assessed realistically, weighing the prior art strength, claim construction, and statistical likelihood?
- Are patent owner amendments and surrogate claims anticipated and analyzed for their effect on the accused product?
- Is the challenge integrated with licensing, settlement, multi-defendant, and portfolio-level strategy?
- Does the output recommend qualified patent counsel handle the post-grant proceeding and its coordination with litigation?
