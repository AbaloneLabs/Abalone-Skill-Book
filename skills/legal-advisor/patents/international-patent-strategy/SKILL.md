# International Patent Strategy

Patents are territorial. A US patent protects only in the US; protection in other countries requires filing in each country or region under its rules. Agents often advise filing everywhere, burning enormous budgets, when a focused strategy based on markets, manufacturing, competitors, and enforcement would protect the same value at a fraction of the cost. Conversely, agents sometimes file only domestically and discover too late that the company's largest market or manufacturing base is unprotected. International patent strategy is a multi-year, multi-six-figure decision driven by where the invention will be made, used, sold, and challenged, and by the cost, timing, and translation burdens of each jurisdiction.

Use this skill before filing a first application, before the 12-month priority deadline, before national phase entry at 30 months, before abandoning foreign filings, or when auditing a global portfolio for coverage and cost. The goal is to force the agent to select countries by commercial logic, to use the PCT and regional routes efficiently, and to manage translation, annuity, and prosecution costs across the portfolio. International patent prosecution is complex and jurisdiction-specific; it requires coordination among patent attorneys in each country of interest.

## Core Rules

### Use The Priority Year And PCT Strategically

The international system gives applicants time and options. Use them deliberately.

Manage the timeline:

- file a first application establishing the priority date;
- within 12 months of the priority date, file in other Paris Convention countries or file a PCT application claiming priority;
- the PCT application gives a centralized search and optional examination and defers national decisions to 30 or 31 months from priority;
- at national phase entry, choose the specific countries or regions in which to pursue protection;
- regional routes such as the European Patent Office allow a single examination with subsequent validation in member states.

The PCT is a delay and decision tool, not a world patent. It buys time to assess commercial value before committing to the high cost of national filings. Use the international phase to refine claims and evaluate markets.

### Select Countries By Commercial Logic, Not By Habit

Filing in every country is rarely justified. Select jurisdictions based on where protection matters.

Select based on:

- markets where the product is or will be sold;
- manufacturing locations where the product is made;
- competitor locations where enforcement may be needed;
- jurisdictions with strong enforcement and predictable courts;
- jurisdictions where damages are meaningful;
- the cost of filing, translation, prosecution, and maintenance;
- the likelihood of allowance in each jurisdiction;
- the size of the market and the value at risk.

A focused strategy might cover the largest markets, key manufacturing countries, and one or two enforcement-friendly jurisdictions. Filing in low-value jurisdictions consumes budget that could protect more important markets.

### Account For Translation And National Formality Costs

The hidden cost of international patents is translation and local agent fees. These can exceed the official filing fees.

Budget for:

- translation of the application into the local language for certain countries;
- local agent or associate attorney fees in each jurisdiction;
- national examination, response, and grant fees;
- annuities or maintenance fees that accrue annually and escalate;
- validation fees for European patent member states;
- recordation and assignment fees;
- potential opposition and appeal costs.

A portfolio of 20 countries can cost hundreds of thousands of dollars over its life. Model the lifetime cost before committing to broad coverage.

### Coordinate Claim Scope And Prosecution Across Jurisdictions

Claims allowed in one country may be rejected in another. Coordinate prosecution to preserve the strongest scope where it matters.

Coordinate:

- file consistent claims across jurisdictions, allowing for local practice differences;
- use the PCT written opinion and international preliminary examination to strengthen claims before national phase;
- monitor office actions across jurisdictions and harmonize responses where possible;
- accept narrower claims in low-value jurisdictions to save cost while pursuing broader claims in key markets;
- file divisionals and continuations where available to preserve pendency;
- manage the file history to avoid inconsistent claim constructions that could be used against the portfolio.

Inconsistent claim scope across jurisdictions can be exploited in litigation. Keep the core claims aligned where feasible.

### Manage Maintenance And Lifecycle Costs

Patents require ongoing payments. Annuities and maintenance fees escalate over the 20-year term.

Manage:

- annuity schedules and due dates in each jurisdiction;
- the escalating cost of late annuities;
- decisions to abandon low-value patents to save maintenance cost;
- laddering coverage so that core patents are maintained while peripheral ones lapse;
- portfolio reviews against current product and market strategy;
- the interaction with trade secret strategy for inventions no longer worth patenting.

A portfolio that is never pruned becomes a cost sink. Review coverage annually against commercial priorities.

### Evaluate Regional And Unitary Options

Regional offices allow a single examination covering multiple countries, reducing cost and complexity.

Consider:

- the European Patent Office for centralized examination and validation in member states;
- the Unitary Patent and Unified Patent Court for unitary effect across participating EU members, with its enforcement and opt-out considerations;
- regional offices in Eurasia, Africa, and elsewhere for regional coverage;
- the tradeoffs of unitary enforcement, including the risk of central invalidation across all member states.

The Unitary Patent offers cost efficiency but concentrates invalidation risk. Evaluate whether unitary or national validation better suits the portfolio.

### Coordinate With Freedom-To-Operate And Competitor Monitoring

Filing is only half the strategy. Monitor competitors and assess freedom to operate in each market.

Coordinate:

- freedom-to-operate analyses in each market of interest;
- competitor patent monitoring and watch services;
- opposition and observation procedures to challenge problematic competitor applications;
- the timing of publication and its effect on competitors;
- defensive publications to create prior art against competitors.

### Handle Local Nuances And Risks

Each jurisdiction has unique rules and risks that affect strategy.

Account for:

- grace periods, which vary and may not exist;
- first-to-file versus first-to-invent legacy systems;
- utility model and design protection alternatives;
- local working requirements and compulsory licensing;
- sanctions, export control, and foreign filing license requirements;
- data localization and sovereign immunity issues;
- enforcement realities, including local courts, customs, and administrative actions.

A foreign filing license may be required before filing abroad, especially for inventions made in certain countries. Failure to obtain it can create liability and bar domestic rights.

## Common Traps

### Filing Everywhere Out Of Caution

Broad filing burns budget that could protect key markets. Select countries by commercial logic.

### Missing The 12-Month Priority Deadline

Missing the priority deadline forfeits the priority date and can destroy novelty in foreign filings. Calendar the deadline.

### Forgetting Translation And Annuity Costs

Translation and maintenance can exceed filing fees. Model lifetime cost.

### Inconsistent Claims Across Jurisdictions

Inconsistent scope can be exploited in litigation. Harmonize core claims.

### Never Pruning The Portfolio

Unmaintained portfolios become cost sinks. Review annually.

### Overlooking The Unitary Patent Risk

Unitary patents concentrate invalidation risk across member states. Evaluate opt-outs.

### Ignoring Foreign Filing Licenses

Some countries require a license before foreign filing. Noncompliance can bar domestic rights.

### Treating The PCT As A World Patent

The PCT defers national decisions; it does not grant a world patent. Plan national phase entry.

## Self-Check

- Is the priority year used to file a first application and to decide PCT versus direct national filings within 12 months?
- Are countries selected by market, manufacturing, competitor location, enforcement strength, damages, cost, allowance likelihood, and value at risk?
- Are translation, local agent, examination, grant, annuity, validation, recordation, and opposition costs modeled over the portfolio life?
- Are claims coordinated across jurisdictions, using PCT written opinion, harmonized responses, divisionals, and consistent core scope?
- Are maintenance and lifecycle costs managed through annuity schedules, laddering, annual reviews, and pruning of low-value patents?
- Are regional and unitary options evaluated, including EPO, Unitary Patent, UPC opt-out, and other regional routes, with invalidation risk weighed?
- Are freedom-to-operate, competitor monitoring, opposition, defensive publication, and publication timing coordinated with the filing strategy?
- Are local nuances addressed, including grace periods, utility models, local working, compulsory licensing, sanctions, export control, foreign filing licenses, and enforcement realities?
- Is the 12-month priority and 30-month national phase deadline calendared and managed?
- Does the output avoid asserting that filings will issue or protect and instead recommend coordination among registered patent attorneys in each jurisdiction of interest?
