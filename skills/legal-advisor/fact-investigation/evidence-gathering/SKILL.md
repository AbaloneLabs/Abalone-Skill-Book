# Evidence Gathering

Evidence gathering is the process by which raw facts become admissible proof. The judgment problem is that people treat evidence collection as simple accumulation, when in fact every step can make or break whether the material can ever be used. Evidence can be excluded because it was collected unlawfully, because the chain of custody was broken, because it was altered after the fact, because it is privileged, or because it was obtained in violation of procedural rules. Agents often help gather materials without recognizing that the method of collection determines admissibility, that routine deletion can become spoliation, and that a single careless step can turn a winning case into a losing one.

Use this skill before collecting, preserving, or organizing any evidence for a dispute, investigation, regulatory response, or anticipated litigation. The goal is to make the agent identify the types of evidence needed, choose collection methods that preserve admissibility, maintain chain of custody, protect privilege, and avoid unlawful or spoliating conduct. This is investigative support; evidence collection in litigation, especially involving third parties, compelled process, or regulated data, must be conducted under the direction of qualified counsel.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Types Of Evidence Needed For Each Element

Map evidence to the legal elements it must prove, not to a general sense of the story. The major categories are physical or real evidence, documentary evidence, testimonial evidence, demonstrative evidence, and electronic or digital evidence. For each element of each claim or defense, identify what type of evidence proves it, who holds it, and whether it exists at all. A strong evidence plan is element-driven; a weak plan collects everything and hopes something is useful.

### Choose Collection Methods That Preserve Admissibility

The method of collection can determine whether evidence is admissible. Collect originals or forensically sound copies, avoid altering the material, document who collected what and when, and use defensible processes for digital evidence. For electronically stored information, preserve metadata, avoid opening or saving files in ways that change timestamps, and use imaging or collection tools that maintain integrity. For physical evidence, photograph in place before moving, seal and label, and store securely. Sloppy collection invites authenticity and chain-of-custody challenges.

### Maintain Chain Of Custody From The Start

Chain of custody is the documented trail of who handled the evidence, when, and why, from collection through presentation. Breaks or gaps in the chain let the other side argue the evidence was altered, contaminated, or fabricated. Establish the chain at the moment of collection: label, log, seal, and sign, and record every transfer. For digital evidence, hash values provide a mathematical integrity check. A continuous, documented chain is what makes evidence credible; an undocumented gap is what makes it vulnerable.

### Preserve Evidence Before It Is Lost

Once litigation, investigation, or formal dispute is reasonably anticipated, there is a duty to preserve relevant evidence, and routine deletion can become spoliation with sanctions, adverse inferences, or lost claims. Issue a preservation or litigation hold promptly, suspend routine deletion, identify custodians and data sources, and confirm compliance. Preservation is broader than collection; it means stopping destruction even of material you have not yet collected. Do not edit, delete, backdate, or clean up records after a dispute emerges.

### Protect Privilege And Confidentiality During Collection

Evidence collection can sweep up privileged material, such as attorney-client communications, work product, board deliberations, or trade secrets. Collect in a way that separates privileged material, uses privilege review before production, and avoids waiving privilege through over-collection or careless sharing. Logging and clawback agreements can protect against inadvertent waiver. Do not assume a broad collection is harmless; over-collection is a common source of privilege waiver and cost.

### Avoid Unlawful Or Improper Collection Methods

Some collection methods are unlawful regardless of how useful the evidence would be. Examples include recording calls in violation of consent rules, accessing systems or accounts without authorization, obtaining records through deception or pretext, and using illegally seized materials. Unlawfully obtained evidence may be excluded, and the collection itself can create civil or criminal liability. When in doubt about whether a method is lawful, stop and escalate to counsel rather than improvising.

### Account For Authentication And Hearsay From The Outset

Evidence must be authenticated, meaning shown to be what it is claimed to be, and must clear hearsay rules if it is an out-of-court statement offered for its truth. Plan for these at collection: gather witnesses who can authenticate documents, preserve metadata that establishes provenance, and identify the non-hearsay purposes or exceptions you will rely on. Evidence collected without thought to authentication and hearsay may be inadmissible no matter how relevant it is.

### Coordinate With Counsel On Compelled And Third-Party Collection

Some evidence can only be obtained through subpoenas, discovery requests, regulatory demands, or court orders. These are formal processes with strict rules, deadlines, and scope limits, and they belong to counsel. Do not attempt informal collection from third parties in ways that could be construed as coercion, misrepresentation, or interference. Route compelled collection through proper legal process.

### Document The Investigation Itself

Keep a contemporaneous record of what was collected, from whom, when, by what method, and with what chain of custody. Investigation notes, collection logs, and hold confirmations are themselves evidence of a defensible process. A well-documented investigation withstands later challenge; an undocumented one invites accusations of bias, gaps, or spoliation.

## Common Traps

### Collecting Without Mapping To Elements

Gathering everything is expensive and often misses the specific proof a claim needs. Map evidence to elements first.

### Breaking Chain Of Custody Through Careless Handling

Opening, moving, or re-saving files without documentation creates gaps the other side will exploit. Document every transfer.

### Continuing Routine Deletion After A Dispute Arises

Routine destruction after preservation duty attaches is spoliation. Issue a hold and suspend deletion immediately.

### Over-Collecting And Waiving Privilege

Sweeping collection can drag privileged material into production and create waiver risk. Use targeted collection and privilege review.

### Using Unlawful Collection Methods

Recording without consent, unauthorized access, or pretexting can exclude the evidence and create liability. Stay within lawful methods.

### Ignoring Authentication And Hearsay Until Trial

Evidence that cannot be authenticated or falls foul of hearsay is useless. Plan for both at collection.

### Attempting Informal Third-Party Collection

Coercing or misleading third parties for records can backfire and may be unlawful. Use proper legal process through counsel.

## Self-Check

- Is the evidence plan mapped to the legal elements it must prove, with types, holders, and existence confirmed?
- Do collection methods preserve admissibility, with originals or forensic copies, unaltered material, and documented process?
- Is chain of custody established at collection and maintained through every transfer, with hashes for digital material?
- Has a preservation or litigation hold been issued and routine deletion suspended for all relevant custodians and sources?
- Is privileged material separated, reviewed, and protected against inadvertent waiver during collection?
- Are all collection methods lawful, avoiding unauthorized access, illegal recording, pretexting, and improper seizure?
- Are authentication witnesses, metadata, and hearsay exceptions or purposes identified at the outset?
- Is compelled or third-party collection routed through proper legal process under counsel rather than informal methods?
- Is the investigation itself documented with collection logs, hold confirmations, and contemporaneous notes?
- Does the output escalate litigation evidence collection, compelled process, and regulated data handling to qualified counsel?
