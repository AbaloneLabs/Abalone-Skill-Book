# Document Collection And Review

Document collection and review is where most of the cost, risk, and value of modern fact investigation lives. The judgment problem is that documents are not just read; they are preserved, scoped, collected, filtered, reviewed for privilege and relevance, and produced under rules that punish mistakes. Agents often help with document work without recognizing that deleting a file, opening it in the wrong application, collecting too narrowly or too broadly, or producing privileged material can each change the outcome of a matter. The era of electronically stored information means that routine IT operations, auto-deletion, and metadata are now legal evidence, and mishandling them is a fast route to sanctions.

Use this skill before scoping, collecting, reviewing, or producing documents in any dispute, investigation, regulatory response, or anticipated litigation. The goal is to make the agent preserve early, scope defensibly, conduct privilege review, handle metadata and confidential material correctly, use review technology appropriately, and avoid spoliation. This is investigative support; document collection, review, and production in litigation and regulatory matters must be conducted under the direction of qualified counsel.

## Core Rules

### Preserve Before You Collect

The duty to preserve arises when litigation, investigation, or formal dispute is reasonably anticipated, and it is broader than the duty to collect. Issue a written preservation or litigation hold promptly, identify custodians and data sources, suspend routine deletion and auto-purging, and confirm compliance in writing. Preservation failures are the most common source of spoliation sanctions, adverse inferences, and lost claims. Do not wait for collection to begin before preserving; the gap is where evidence disappears.

### Scope Collection Defensibly

Collection scope must be reasonable and defensible, tied to the issues, custodians, time period, and data sources that are relevant. Over-collection drives cost and privilege risk; under-collection misses evidence and invites challenge. Document the scope decisions and rationale, identify the systems and custodians covered, and be prepared to justify what was included and excluded. A defensible scope is one you can explain to a court or regulator under questioning.

### Conduct Privilege Review Before Production

Production of privileged material can waive the privilege, and clawing it back is uncertain and expensive. Before producing, conduct privilege review using a privilege log, screening for attorney-client communications, work product, board deliberations, and other protected categories. Use search terms, technology-assisted review, and attorney review appropriate to the scale. Document the review process so you can defend against privilege disputes. A privilege log that is incomplete or overbroad draws motions and sanctions.

### Handle Metadata And Electronically Stored Information Correctly

Electronically stored information carries metadata that can be evidence of authorship, timing, and alterations. Collect it forensically to preserve metadata, avoid opening or saving files in ways that change dates, and produce in agreed formats that retain metadata where relevant. Native files, load files, and production formats should be negotiated and documented. Mishandling metadata can destroy authentication value and create spoliation issues. Treat the file and its metadata as a single unit.

### Use Review Technology Appropriately And Defensibly

For large collections, manual review is impractical, and technology-assisted review, predictive coding, deduplication, email threading, and clustering are standard tools. Use them defensibly: document the workflow, validate results with sampling, involve counsel in setting protocols, and be prepared to explain the process to a court. Poorly documented or unvalidated technology-assisted review can be challenged as inadequate. Match the technology to the scale and stakes of the matter.

### Manage Confidential, Personal, And Regulated Data

Document collections often contain personal data, health information, financial records, trade secrets, and regulated material. Handle these under applicable privacy law, protective orders, and redaction requirements. Over-collection of personal data can itself create compliance risk. Redact consistently, log redactions, and use secure storage and transmission. Coordinate with privacy, security, and compliance functions where regulated data is involved.

### Prevent Spoliation At Every Step

Spoliation is the destruction or alteration of relevant evidence after the duty to preserve attaches, and it carries sanctions up to adverse inferences and default judgment. Prevent it by issuing holds, suspending deletion, controlling custodian self-collection, using forensic collection, and monitoring compliance. Investigate any gaps or anomalies in the collection, and document remediation. Spoliation is often discovered by the other side, not by the collecting party, so assume the process will be scrutinized.

### Coordinate Production Format, Timing, And Scope With Counsel

Production is governed by rules, protective orders, meet-and-confer conferences, and agreed protocols. Format, load files, metadata, redactions, privilege logs, rolling versus batched production, and clawback agreements must be negotiated and documented. Do not produce unilaterally or informally in litigation. Route production decisions through counsel and follow the agreed protocol exactly.

### Maintain A Defensible Review Trail

Keep records of the hold, scope, collection, processing, review, and production steps, including who did what, when, and with what tools. A defensible trail lets you demonstrate reasonableness if the process is challenged. An undocumented process looks arbitrary and invites accusations of gaps or bias. The trail is itself part of the work product that protects the matter.

## Common Traps

### Collecting Before Preserving

The gap between anticipation and collection is where evidence vanishes. Issue a hold first.

### Over-Collecting Or Under-Collecting

Both create problems: over-collection drives cost and privilege risk, under-collection misses evidence. Scope defensibly and document it.

### Producing Privileged Material Without Review

Inadvertent production can waive privilege. Conduct privilege review and maintain a log before producing.

### Destroying Metadata Through Mishandling

Opening or re-saving files changes timestamps and authentication value. Collect forensically and produce in agreed formats.

### Using Technology-Assisted Review Without Validation

Unvalidated TAR can be challenged as inadequate. Document, sample, and involve counsel in the protocol.

### Ignoring Privacy And Regulated Data Obligations

Personal, health, financial, and trade-secret data carry their own rules. Redact, secure, and coordinate with privacy functions.

### Self-Collection By Custodians Without Oversight

Custodians who select their own documents may miss, alter, or delete material. Use controlled, forensic collection.

### Producing Unilaterally In Litigation

Production format and scope must be negotiated. Do not produce informally without counsel and an agreed protocol.

## Self-Check

- Has a written preservation or litigation hold been issued, with custodians, sources, deletion suspension, and compliance confirmation?
- Is the collection scope reasonable and defensible, tied to issues, custodians, time period, and sources, with the rationale documented?
- Is privilege review conducted before production, with a privilege log and documented workflow?
- Is electronically stored information collected forensically to preserve metadata, with native files and production formats negotiated?
- Is review technology used defensibly, with documented workflow, validation sampling, and counsel involvement?
- Are confidential, personal, health, financial, and trade-secret materials handled under privacy law, protective orders, and redaction?
- Is spoliation prevented through holds, deletion suspension, controlled collection, and anomaly investigation?
- Are production format, timing, scope, redactions, privilege logs, and clawback agreements coordinated with counsel and documented?
- Is a defensible review trail maintained covering hold, scope, collection, processing, review, and production?
- Does the output escalate litigation document collection, review, production, and regulated data handling to qualified counsel?
