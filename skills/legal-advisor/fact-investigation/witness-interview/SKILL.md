# Witness Interview

A witness interview is where facts are either captured accurately or contaminated forever. The judgment problem is that an interview is not a neutral recording; the way questions are asked shapes what the witness remembers and reports, and what the witness says in the interview will constrain or contradict what they can later say under oath. Agents often approach interviews as conversations, using leading questions, telegraphing the desired answer, failing to memorialize the statement, or creating materials that lack privilege and later become discoverable. A poorly conducted interview can lock a witness into a harmful version, taint their memory, or produce a statement that helps the other side.

Use this skill before conducting, preparing, or summarizing any witness interview in a dispute, investigation, or pre-litigation context. The goal is to make the agent plan the interview, use non-leading techniques, memorialize accurately, assess credibility honestly, handle cooperators and adverse witnesses carefully, and protect privilege and confidentiality. This is investigative support; witness interviews in litigation, especially of parties and under oath, must be conducted under the direction of qualified counsel.

## Core Rules

### Prepare With An Element-Driven Question Plan

Do not interview cold. Before the interview, map the legal elements and factual disputes the witness can address, list the topics in order, and prepare open-ended questions for each. Identify what you already know from documents so you can test the witness against the record without leading them. A prepared interviewer controls the flow and catches inconsistencies; an unprepared one follows the witness wherever they wander. Keep the plan flexible enough to follow useful new information.

### Use Open-Ended, Non-Leading Questions

The form of the question determines the quality of the answer. Start broad and open, asking the witness to recount in their own words, then narrow to specifics. Avoid leading questions that suggest the answer, because they contaminate the witness's memory and produce a statement that mirrors your theory rather than the witness's recollection. Use follow-ups like "tell me more about that," "what happened next," and "how do you know that" to develop detail and test the basis of the witness's knowledge. Leading questions have a place in cross-examination, not in investigation interviews.

### Memorialize Accurately And Promptly

Capture what the witness said, not what you wish they said. Options include contemporaneous notes, a recorded interview where lawful, a witness statement or declaration, or a memorandum of interview. Whatever the form, memorialize promptly while memory is fresh, distinguish the witness's words from your characterizations, and record the date, time, location, attendees, and method. A statement the witness reviews and signs carries more weight but also locks them in, so consider whether locking is desirable before seeking a signature.

### Protect Privilege In How The Interview Is Conducted

Whether interview materials are privileged depends on who conducts the interview, for what purpose, and how the materials are handled. Interviews conducted by counsel for the purpose of providing legal advice, with appropriate markings and distribution, may be protected; interviews conducted by business or operational staff, or shared broadly, likely are not. Use Upjohn warnings when interviewing corporate employees to clarify who counsel represents, because employees often wrongly assume counsel represents them personally. Control distribution to preserve any available privilege.

### Assess Credibility Honestly And Record It

An interview is also a credibility assessment. Note the witness's demeanor, certainty, internal consistency, consistency with documents, bias, motive, and opportunity to observe. Distinguish a confident witness from a credible one, and a friendly witness from a reliable one. Record your credibility assessment separately from the substance, because it will matter later when deciding whether to call the witness and how to prepare them. A witness who scores well on substance but poorly on credibility may hurt more than help.

### Handle Cooperators And Adverse Witnesses Differently

Different witnesses require different techniques. A friendly cooperator may need little prompting but may also tell you what they think you want to hear, so test their account against documents and probe for embellishment. A reluctant or adverse witness may require firmer, more structured questioning and careful use of documents to confront inconsistencies. A witness with potential exposure needs an Upjohn or similar warning and may need their own counsel. Match the technique to the witness type and never treat all interviews the same.

### Avoid Contaminating Memory Or Creating Harmful Lock-In

An interview can damage a case by locking a witness into a version that later proves wrong or incomplete. Avoid feeding facts, summarizing in your own words and asking the witness to agree, or pressuring for a desired answer. Let the witness give their own account first, then test it. If the witness's account differs from your theory, record it honestly rather than steering them, because a witness who later changes their story under pressure is easily impeached.

### Coordinate With Counsel On Parties, Opposing Witnesses, And Regulated Interviews

Interviews of parties, opposing witnesses, employees with exposure, government witnesses, and regulated individuals carry special rules. Some jurisdictions prohibit counsel from contacting represented parties directly. Employee interviews may require Upjohn warnings and works council or union consultation. Cooperators may have immunity or cooperation agreements that affect their incentives. Route these interviews through counsel and do not improvise contact with represented or protected individuals.

### Maintain Confidentiality And Need-To-Know Distribution

Interview materials often contain sensitive facts, strategy, and credibility assessments. Restrict distribution to those who need to know, store securely, and avoid discussing interview content in channels that are not privileged. Broad or careless distribution can waive privilege, expose strategy, and create discoverable materials that help the other side.

## Common Traps

### Using Leading Questions That Suggest The Answer

Leading questions contaminate memory and produce mirror statements. Use open-ended questions in investigation interviews.

### Failing To Memorialize While Memory Is Fresh

Delayed notes are less accurate and more vulnerable to challenge. Record promptly with date, time, and attendees.

### Telegrating The Desired Answer

Steering a witness to your theory locks them into a version that may later collapse. Let them give their own account first.

### Skipping The Upjohn Warning With Employees

Employees often wrongly believe counsel represents them. Clarify representation or risk waiver and later disputes.

### Treating A Confident Witness As A Credible One

Confidence is not reliability. Assess demeanor, consistency, bias, and basis of knowledge separately.

### Contacting Represented Parties Directly

Many jurisdictions bar direct contact with represented parties. Route through counsel.

### Over-Broad Distribution That Waives Privilege

Sharing interview materials widely destroys privilege and exposes strategy. Restrict to need-to-know.

## Self-Check

- Is there an element-driven question plan prepared before the interview, with open-ended questions and document-based testing?
- Are questions open-ended and non-leading, with follow-ups used to develop detail and test the basis of knowledge?
- Is the interview memorialized accurately and promptly, distinguishing the witness's words from characterizations, with date, time, location, and attendees recorded?
- Is privilege protected through proper conduct, markings, and distribution, with Upjohn warnings given to corporate employees?
- Is credibility assessed and recorded separately from substance, covering demeanor, consistency, bias, motive, and opportunity to observe?
- Are cooperator, adverse, and exposed-witness interviews handled with appropriate techniques and warnings?
- Does the interview avoid contaminating memory or locking the witness into a harmful version through pressure or leading?
- Are party, opposing, employee, government, and regulated interviews coordinated with counsel and proper warnings or consultations?
- Is confidentiality maintained with need-to-know distribution and secure storage?
- Does the output escalate litigation interviews, party contacts, and regulated witness handling to qualified counsel?
