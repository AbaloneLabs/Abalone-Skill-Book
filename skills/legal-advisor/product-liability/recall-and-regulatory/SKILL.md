# Recall And Regulatory

A product safety issue triggers a regulatory regime that runs in parallel with private liability. Failing to report a known defect, delaying a recall, or conducting a recall poorly can produce regulatory penalties, criminal exposure for individuals, customs holds, and reputational damage that dwarfs the underlying claim. Agents often treat a recall as a logistics exercise, missing the mandatory reporting deadlines, the regulator's expectations on scope and remedy, the customs and market surveillance consequences, and the disclosure duties to other regulators and to investors. A recall is also evidence in private litigation, and how it is conducted affects liability.

Use this skill before advising on a product safety issue, recall decision, regulatory notification, or corrective action. The goal is to force the agent to identify the reporting duties and deadlines, design the recall to meet regulatory expectations, and manage the litigation and disclosure consequences. Recall and product safety regulation is jurisdiction- and sector-specific; any conclusion must be confirmed against the applicable regulator's rules and guidance.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Reporting And Notification Duties

Reporting duties are often mandatory and time-limited.

Map:

- the regulator with jurisdiction (CPSC in the US for consumer products, plus sector regulators for autos, food, drugs, medical devices, aviation, and others);
- the trigger for mandatory reporting (a defect that creates a substantial risk, a violation of a safety standard, an unreasonable risk of serious injury or death);
- the reporting deadline (often 24 hours or a few days after obtaining information);
- the content of the report (product, defect, incidents, volume, remedy);
- reporting to multiple regulators (federal, state, foreign);
- voluntary reporting where mandatory thresholds are not met but risk exists.

Failing to report a known substantial risk is a serious violation with civil and criminal exposure. Calendar the deadline as soon as information is obtained.

### Decide Between Voluntary And Mandatory Recall

Most recalls are voluntary, but under regulator supervision and pressure.

Assess:

- whether the product presents a substantial hazard requiring action;
- the scope of the recall (model, lot, date range, geography);
- the remedy (repair, replacement, refund, retrofit, software update);
- the regulator's expectations and the need for a corrective action plan;
- the risk of a mandatory recall order or injunction if voluntary action is insufficient;
- the cost and feasibility of each remedy.

A voluntary recall conducted with the regulator is generally preferable to a mandatory order. But a voluntary recall still requires reporting and compliance.

### Design The Recall To Meet Regulatory Expectations

A recall must be effective, not merely announced.

Build:

- a corrective action plan agreed with the regulator;
- direct notice to known purchasers and registered users;
- public notice through appropriate channels;
- a clear description of the hazard and remedy;
- an accessible remedy process (free repair, replacement, or refund);
- tracking of reach, response, and completion rates;
- a process for verifying effectiveness and reporting to the regulator.

A recall with low completion rates may require additional action. Regulators increasingly publish recall effectiveness and pursue follow-up.

### Coordinate With Customs And Market Surveillance

Recalls have customs and trade consequences.

Address:

- customs holds and import alerts on the product and related shipments;
- market surveillance authorities in export markets;
- certificates and approvals that may be suspended;
- the effect on pending shipments and inventory;
- coordination with distributors and retailers across jurisdictions.

A recall in one market can trigger customs and surveillance action in others. Coordinate the global response.

### Manage Disclosure To Other Regulators And Stakeholders

A safety issue creates disclosure duties beyond the lead regulator.

Consider:

- sector regulators (FDA, NHTSA, FAA, EMA, and others) for regulated products;
- securities regulators for material safety issues at public companies;
- insurance and contractual notice to customers, suppliers, and insurers;
- board and audit committee oversight;
- foreign regulators through cooperation and mutual recognition.

Disclosure to one regulator can be used by others and in litigation. Coordinate the content and timing across audiences.

### Address Litigation, Document, And Privilege Consequences

A recall is evidence and creates a record.

Manage:

- the litigation hold and preservation of recall-related documents;
- privilege protection for investigations and decisions (with limits);
- the use of recall notices and admissions in private litigation;
- regulatory estoppel and the effect of representations to regulators;
- the coordination of regulatory and litigation strategy;
- public statements that may become admissions.

A recall notice that admits a defect can be powerful evidence in private suits. Draft with both regulatory and litigation consequences in mind.

### Evaluate Penalties, Criminal Exposure, And Individual Liability

Non-compliance carries serious consequences.

Assess:

- civil penalties for reporting and recall violations (often per day or per unit);
- criminal liability for willful violations, false statements, and concealment;
- personal liability for officers, managers, and compliance personnel;
- debarment and exclusion for regulated entities;
- reputational harm and consumer trust effects.

Criminal exposure turns on willfulness and knowledge. Build a compliance and reporting process that documents good-faith decisions.

### Monitor Post-Recall Obligations And Effectiveness

A recall is not over when announced.

Track:

- completion and remedy rates;
- additional incidents and the duty to expand the recall;
- regulator follow-up and reporting;
- the effect on future products and quality systems;
- the retention of recall records.

Low completion or new incidents can require expanded action. Maintain the recall program until the regulator agrees it is complete.

## Common Traps

### Treating A Recall As Logistics

A recall is a regulatory and legal event with reporting, penalty, and litigation consequences. Manage all dimensions.

### Missing The Reporting Deadline

Reporting deadlines are short and triggered by obtaining information, not by confirming the defect. Calendar early.

### Underestimating Regulator Expectations

A recall must be effective, with adequate notice, remedy, and tracking. Low completion invites follow-up.

### Ignoring Customs And Foreign Regulators

A recall in one market triggers customs and surveillance action elsewhere. Coordinate globally.

### Recall Notices As Litigation Admissions

Recall notices can be powerful evidence in private suits. Draft with litigation in mind.

### Inconsistent Disclosures Across Regulators

Disclosure to one regulator can be used by others. Coordinate content and timing.

### Overlooking Criminal And Individual Exposure

Willful violations and false statements carry criminal and personal liability. Document good-faith decisions.

### Declaring Completion Too Early

Low completion or new incidents require expanded action. Maintain the program until the regulator agrees.

## Self-Check

- Are the regulator, mandatory reporting triggers, deadlines, and content identified and calendared?
- Is the decision between voluntary and mandatory recall documented, with scope and remedy justified?
- Does the corrective action plan include direct notice, public notice, remedy, tracking, and effectiveness reporting?
- Are customs, market surveillance, certificates, and foreign regulators coordinated?
- Are disclosures to sector, securities, insurance, contractual, board, and foreign stakeholders coordinated?
- Are litigation hold, privilege, regulatory estoppel, and public statement consequences managed?
- Are civil penalties, criminal exposure, individual liability, debarment, and reputational harm assessed?
- Are post-recall completion rates, additional incidents, expansion duty, and record retention tracked?
- Is the recall designed to meet regulatory expectations, not merely announced?
- Does the output recommend confirmation against the applicable regulator's rules and guidance by qualified counsel?
