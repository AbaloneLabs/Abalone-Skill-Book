# Defect And Causation

Product liability hinges on two linked questions: was the product defective, and did that defect cause the harm? Agents often jump to the injury and assume the product is at fault, or identify a defect but skip the causation chain, missing that the harm may have arisen from misuse, alteration, or an independent cause. The defect categories (design, manufacturing, warning) have distinct tests, and the available theories (strict liability, negligence, breach of warranty, misrepresentation) have different elements and defenses. Causation, especially in complex products with multiple components and intervening acts, is where strong-looking cases often fail.

Use this skill before assessing a product liability claim or defense, analyzing a defect theory, or evaluating causation and damages. The goal is to force the agent to classify the defect, apply the correct test, and trace causation from defect to harm before concluding liability. Product liability law is jurisdiction-specific, with significant differences between US states and between common law and civil law systems; any conclusion must be confirmed against the applicable law.

## Core Rules

### Classify The Defect Theory

Defect categories have distinct tests and evidence.

Identify:

- manufacturing defect (the product departed from its intended design, even if due care was exercised);
- design defect (the product's design itself was unreasonably dangerous);
- warning or instruction defect (inadequate warnings or instructions rendered the product unreasonably dangerous);
- component vs. finished product defect, and the role of suppliers and assemblers.

A manufacturing defect case focuses on the specific unit; a design defect case challenges the whole line; a warning defect case focuses on the adequacy and prominence of warnings. Each requires different evidence.

### Apply The Correct Test For Design Defect

Design defect tests differ by jurisdiction.

Determine:

- the consumer expectation test (did the product perform as safely as an ordinary consumer would expect?);
- the risk-utility or risk-benefit test (are the risks of the design outweighed by the utility, considering feasible alternatives?);
- the hybrid approach used in some jurisdictions;
- the role of feasible safer alternatives and their proof;
- the effect of compliance with industry custom and standards (relevant but not dispositive).

Consumer expectation works for simple products where ordinary expectations are clear; risk-utility dominates for complex products. Identify the test before building the case.

### Assess The Adequacy Of Warnings And Instructions

Warning defect cases turn on adequacy, not mere presence.

Evaluate:

- whether a warning was required for a foreseeable risk;
- whether the warning was clear, specific, and prominent;
- whether the warning reached the end user (intermediate user rule in some jurisdictions);
- whether the warning was timely and updated as risks became known;
- the sophistication of the user and learned intermediary doctrine (especially for prescription products);
- whether the product required instructions for safe use.

A generic or buried warning can be inadequate. A post-sale duty to warn may arise as new risks emerge.

### Establish Causation And Proximate Cause

Causation links the defect to the harm.

Trace:

- actual cause (but-for causation, or substantial factor in some jurisdictions);
- proximate or legal cause (foreseeability and scope of liability);
- intervening and superseding causes (misuse, alteration, third-party conduct);
- the role of comparative fault and assumption of risk;
- specific causation in toxic and complex cases (general causation that the agent can cause the harm, plus specific causation that it did in this case).

Causation is the most common point of failure. A defective product that did not cause the specific harm yields no liability.

### Distinguish Strict Liability, Negligence, And Warranty

The available theories have different elements and defenses.

Map:

- strict liability (defect and causation; no fault required; limited defenses);
- negligence (duty, breach, causation, damages; fault-based; comparative fault available);
- breach of warranty (express and implied warranty of merchantability and fitness; notice and privity issues);
- misrepresentation and fraud (material false representation, reliance, damages).

Strict liability is the backbone of product liability, but negligence and warranty can reach different defendants and damages. Plead and analyze each theory.

### Identify All Potentially Liable Parties

Liability can extend across the supply chain.

Consider:

- the manufacturer of the finished product;
- component and parts manufacturers;
- the seller, distributor, and retailer (strict liability often reaches all in the chain);
- successor corporations and asset purchasers (with exceptions);
- lessors and bailors;
- designers, engineers, and consultants;
- the entity that made representations or marketed the product.

The chain can be long, and some defendants have indemnity and contribution claims against others. Map the full chain.

### Evaluate Defenses And Fault Allocation

Defenses can reduce or eliminate liability.

Assess:

- product misuse (foreseeable vs. unforeseeable misuse);
- alteration and modification;
- assumption of risk;
- comparative and contributory fault;
- compliance with government standards and regulations (relevance, not a complete defense);
- state-of-the-art defense (what was known at the time of manufacture);
- sophisticated user and learned intermediary;
- statutes of limitation and repose (critical timing defenses).

Misuse must be foreseeable to reduce but not eliminate liability in many jurisdictions. Statutes of repose can bar claims after a fixed period regardless of discovery.

### Quantify Damages And Economic Loss

Damages rules differ and can be the largest dispute.

Determine:

- personal injury damages (medical, lost wages, pain and suffering, disability);
- property damage;
- economic loss (often excluded from strict products liability, requiring warranty or negligence);
- punitive damages and their constitutional limits;
- subrogation and lien claims by insurers and providers.

The economic loss rule can bar recovery for purely financial losses under strict liability. Confirm the applicable rule and plead the right theory.

## Common Traps

### Assuming Injury Means Defect

An injury does not prove a defect. Establish the defect category and test.

### Skipping Causation

A defective product that did not cause the harm yields no liability. Trace the chain.

### Using The Wrong Design Defect Test

Consumer expectation and risk-utility differ. Identify the jurisdiction's test.

### Treating A Warning As Adequate Because It Exists

Adequacy turns on clarity, prominence, timing, and reach. A buried warning fails.

### Overlooking Component And Supplier Liability

Liability can reach component makers and the full supply chain. Map the chain.

### Ignoring The Economic Loss Rule

Purely financial losses may be barred under strict liability. Plead warranty or negligence.

### Missing Statutes Of Repose

Repose can bar claims after a fixed period regardless of discovery. Check timing early.

### Treating Compliance As A Complete Defense

Compliance with standards is relevant but not dispositive. State-of-the-art and feasibility still matter.

## Self-Check

- Is the defect classified as manufacturing, design, or warning, with the correct test applied?
- For design defect, is the consumer expectation, risk-utility, or hybrid test identified for the jurisdiction?
- Is the warning assessed for requirement, clarity, prominence, timing, reach, and post-sale duty?
- Is causation traced (actual, proximate, intervening causes) and specific causation established?
- Are strict liability, negligence, warranty, and misrepresentation theories distinguished and analyzed?
- Are all potentially liable parties across the supply chain identified?
- Are defenses (misuse, alteration, assumption of risk, comparative fault, compliance, state of the art, repose) assessed?
- Are damages quantified, including personal injury, property, economic loss, punitive, and subrogation?
- Is the economic loss rule checked to confirm the right theory supports financial loss recovery?
- Does the output recommend confirmation against the applicable jurisdiction's product liability law by qualified counsel?
