# Advertising And Marketing

Advertising law turns on whether a reasonable consumer would be misled, and that standard catches far more than outright lies. A claim can be deceptive because it omits material information, because the fine print contradicts the headline, because the substantiation does not match the claim's breadth, or because an influencer did not disclose a material connection. Agents often focus on whether a claim is literally true and miss the net impression, the implied claims, the substantiation gap, and the disclosure mechanics that regulators actually examine.

Use this skill before publishing ads, marketing copy, social posts, influencer content, endorsements, or promotional offers. The goal is to force the agent to capture express and implied claims, match substantiation, place disclosures, handle endorsements and comparisons, and escalate to marketing counsel for high-risk categories. This skill is decision support, not legal advice.

## Core Rules

### Capture Both Express And Implied Claims

A claim is anything that communicates a message, including what is reasonably implied.

Identify:

- express claims (what is literally stated);
- implied claims (what a reasonable consumer would infer);
- the net impression of the ad as a whole;
- claims conveyed by images, demonstrations, charts, and testimonials;
- claims in headlines, fine print, and calls to action;
- claims across all channels and formats.

The net impression controls. A literally true headline paired with a misleading image or buried qualifier can be deceptive.

### Match Substantiation To The Specific Claim

Before publication, the advertiser must possess competent and reliable evidence for objective claims.

For each claim, assess:

- the exact product, formulation, or service covered;
- the benefit asserted;
- the audience and use case;
- the timeframe;
- the level of proof required (higher for health, safety, environmental, and earnings claims);
- whether evidence is current and from a reliable method;
- whether comparative and superiority claims are supported;
- whether "free," "risk-free," "guaranteed," "clinically proven," or "Made in USA" claims meet their specific standards.

A narrow study does not support a broad claim. Anecdotes and testimonials do not substantiate general efficacy claims. Align evidence to the claim actually made.

### Place Disclosures Clearly And Proximately

Disclosures clarify but do not cure deception, and hidden disclosures are weak.

Ensure:

- proximity to the claim they qualify;
- prominence and readability (font, contrast, size, duration);
- timing so the consumer sees them before acting;
- mobile and small-screen presentation;
- plain language;
- consistency with the main message (a disclosure should not contradict the headline);
- repetition where the claim recurs.

If the disclosure is needed to prevent deception, it must be clear and conspicuous. If it contradicts the headline, revise the claim rather than relying on the disclosure.

### Handle Endorsements And Influencer Content

Endorsements carry their own rules and are an enforcement priority.

Address:

- material-connection disclosures (when there is a paid or free-product relationship);
- the endorser's honest opinion and experience;
- substantiation for objective claims in the endorsement;
- typicality disclosures when results are not typical;
- the advertiser's responsibility for influencer compliance;
- monitoring and correction of non-compliant posts;
- child-directed and minor-influencer considerations.

An influencer's failure to disclose a material connection is the advertiser's problem too. Set expectations, provide disclosure guidance, and monitor.

### Scrutinize Comparative And Superiority Claims

Comparisons invite close regulatory attention.

Address:

- the basis for comparison and its current accuracy;
- identification of the competitor or product compared;
- apples-to-apples methodology;
- survey or testing support for superiority;
- disparagement and false-advertising exposure;
- country-of-origin and "Made in" claims.

A comparative claim without a documented, fair basis is high risk. Substantiate the comparison as rigorously as the underlying claim.

### Watch High-Risk Claim Categories

Certain claims draw heightened scrutiny and require specialized substantiation.

Flag:

- health, safety, and efficacy claims;
- environmental, green, ESG, and sustainability claims (greenwashing);
- earnings and income claims;
- "free," "risk-free," "guarantee," and "money-back" claims;
- "Made in USA," origin, and local claims;
- children-directed advertising;
- dark patterns and manipulative design;
- bait-and-switch and limited-availability claims.

These categories often require specific evidence, disclosures, or procedural safeguards. Treat them as escalation triggers.

### Comply With Pricing, Offer, And Sweepstakes Rules

Promotional mechanics are regulated.

Address:

- reference-price and "sale" claims (was/now pricing) with genuine prior prices;
- automatic renewal and negative-option enrollment with clear disclosure and easy cancellation;
- free-trial and introductory-offer terms;
- shipping and handling transparency;
- sweepstakes versus lottery distinctions (consideration, chance, prize);
- official rules, eligibility, and no-purchase-necessary language;
- testimonials and before/after restrictions.

Hidden recurring charges, deceptive reference prices, and unlawful lotteries are common enforcement targets.

### Manage Digital And Behavioral Advertising

Digital advertising adds privacy and targeting duties.

Address:

- behavioral targeting and its privacy-law implications;
- children's and minors' data restrictions;
- dark patterns in consent and subscription flows;
- ad-tech transparency and data-sharing disclosures;
- retargeting and cross-context advertising rules;
- platform-specific advertising policies.

Behavioral advertising often triggers privacy-law consent and opt-out duties separate from the advertising-law analysis. Coordinate both.

### Localize For Each Market

Advertising rules differ by country and even by state.

Address:

- banned or restricted product advertising (tobacco, alcohol, gambling, pharmaceuticals);
- language and substantiation requirements;
- comparative-advertising permissibility;
- influencer and disclosure rules;
- children's advertising standards;
- self-regulatory and industry codes.

An ad compliant in one market may be unlawful in another. Localize claims, substantiation, and disclosures per jurisdiction.

## Common Traps

### Literal Truth While Net Impression Misleads

A true headline with a misleading image or buried qualifier can be deceptive. Review the net impression.

### Broad Claims On Narrow Evidence

A pilot study or testimonial does not support a general efficacy claim. Match evidence to claim breadth.

### Hidden Or Contradictory Disclosures

Buried fine print does not cure deception, and contradictory disclosures signal a claim to revise.

### Undisclosed Influencer Connections

Material connections must be disclosed, and the advertiser shares responsibility for influencer compliance.

### Unsupported Comparative Claims

Comparisons need a fair, documented basis. Substantiate them rigorously.

### Deceptive Reference Prices And Hidden Recurring Charges

Sale prices and auto-renewals are enforcement targets. Disclose clearly and make cancellation easy.

### Assuming One Market's Rules Apply Everywhere

Advertising rules differ by jurisdiction. Localize claims and disclosures.

## Self-Check

- Are both express and implied claims captured, including net impression, images, demonstrations, and cross-channel consistency?
- Does substantiation match the exact product, benefit, audience, timeframe, and claim breadth, with heightened proof for health, safety, environmental, and earnings claims?
- Are disclosures clear, conspicuous, proximate, mobile-readable, plain-language, timely, non-contradictory, and repeated where claims recur?
- Are endorsements and influencer content handled with material-connection disclosure, honest opinion, substantiation, typicality, advertiser responsibility, monitoring, and minor considerations?
- Are comparative and superiority claims supported by fair, current, documented methodology, with disparagement and origin-claim risks addressed?
- Are high-risk categories (health, green, earnings, free, guarantee, origin, children, dark patterns, bait-and-switch) flagged for specialized substantiation and escalation?
- Are pricing, sale, auto-renewal, free-trial, shipping, and sweepstakes mechanics compliant with reference-price, cancellation, and lottery rules?
- Is digital and behavioral advertising assessed for privacy-law consent, children's data, dark patterns, ad-tech transparency, and platform policies?
- Are claims, substantiation, disclosures, and restricted-product rules localized for each market?
- Does the output escalate high-risk categories, comparative claims, influencer programs, and cross-border campaigns to qualified marketing or regulatory counsel?
