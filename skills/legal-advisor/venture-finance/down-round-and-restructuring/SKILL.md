# Down Round And Restructuring

A down round, recapitalization, or restructuring is a transaction undertaken when the existing capital structure no longer works: the company cannot raise at the prior valuation, the preference stack has become unmanageable, the option pool is underwater, or debt is converting under pressure. These transactions are legally and emotionally fraught because they redistribute value among founders, employees, and investors, often at someone's expense. Agents often focus on getting the deal done and miss the secondary consequences: stockholder litigation risk, option repricing tax traps, fiduciary duty exposure for the board, lost employee morale, and the consent gauntlet required when multiple investor classes have protective provisions. A restructuring that ignores these can close the financing but trigger lawsuits, tax liabilities, and key employee departures that defeat the purpose.

Use this skill before launching a down round, before repricing or exchanging options, before converting debt to equity under stress, before a merger or split to clean up the cap table, or when assembling the consents needed to close. The goal is to force the agent to model who wins and loses, to identify the fiduciary and litigation risks, to assemble the consent matrix, and to handle the tax and securities consequences. Restructurings are high-stakes and jurisdiction-specific; they require experienced corporate, tax, and litigation counsel.

## Core Rules

### Model Who Wins And Loses Before Structuring

A restructuring redistributes value. Before choosing a structure, model the cap table before and after under multiple exit scenarios to see who benefits and who is diluted or wiped out.

Model:

- the preference stack and how restructuring changes seniority and amounts;
- the conversion outcomes for each class of preferred;
- the option pool and the effect of repricing, cancellation, or exchange;
- the founder and employee common outcomes;
- the debt holders if converting debt;
- any new money investor outcomes and the incentive to invest;
- the breakeven exit values at which each class recovers.

A restructuring that wipes out common while preserving investor preference can trigger fiduciary scrutiny and stockholder challenges. Modeling reveals the politics and the litigation risk.

### Identify And Manage Fiduciary Duty Exposure

A down round or restructuring, especially one involving existing investors on both sides, can trigger entire fairness review. The board must build a process that withstands challenge.

Address:

- whether the transaction involves a controlling stockholder or conflicted director;
- whether an independent special committee is required;
- whether majority-of-the-minority approval is available or advisable;
- the fairness of process and price, with valuation support;
- reliance on independent financial advisors and counsel;
- full disclosure to the board and stockholders;
- documentation of alternatives considered;
- the business rationale and urgency.

Courts scrutinize transactions where insiders extract value or where the company is sold under distress. A well-documented process with independent advice is the primary defense.

### Assemble The Consent Matrix Early

Restructurings require consents from multiple constituencies. Missing a consent blocks closing.

Map:

- board approval, including committee approval where required;
- stockholder approval by class, considering protective provisions and class voting;
- preferred stock consents for amendments to rights, preferences, and privileges;
- debt holder consents for conversion, waiver, or amendment;
- option holder consents or cancellations for option exchanges;
- investor preemptive and participation rights;
- regulatory consents where applicable;
- lender consents and debt payoff or assumption.

Build the consent matrix into the timeline. Some consents require notice periods and cannot be rushed. A blocked consent at closing can kill a restructuring that the company needs to survive.

### Handle Option Repricing And Exchange Correctly

Underwater options demoralize employees, but repricing or exchanging them has tax and securities consequences.

Options:

- repricing by amendment, which can trigger variable accounting and tax issues;
- cancellation and regrant, which resets vesting and may require new 409A valuations;
- option exchange for cash or restricted stock, with tax treatment analysis;
- tender offers for option cancellation, with disclosure and timing rules;
- the effect on the option pool and future grants;
- employee communication and retention considerations.

Option repricing can be viewed as a variable award with accounting expense consequences. Cancellation-and-regrant is often cleaner but resets vesting, which can itself cause departures.

### Manage Debt Conversion And Bridge Financings

Restructurings often involve converting convertible notes or SAFs into equity, sometimes at a discount that further dilutes common.

Address:

- the conversion terms, discount, and valuation cap of existing notes;
- whether conversion is automatic, optional, or negotiated;
- the valuation at which debt converts and its effect on the cap table;
- the seniority of converted equity versus new money;
- warrant coverage and bridge fees;
- whether the conversion triggers a change of control or other consent;
- tax treatment of debt forgiveness or conversion.

Aggressive conversion discounts in a down round can hand debt holders a large stake at the expense of founders. Negotiate conversion terms as part of the overall restructuring.

### Use Merger Or Split Structures To Clean The Cap Table

When consents and amendments are impractical, a merger or split can reset the capital structure with a single stockholder vote.

Consider:

- a reverse stock split to reduce share count and eliminate small holdings;
- a merger to create a new capital structure with exchanged equity;
- a Delaware or other state statutory merger to bind dissenting stockholders subject to appraisal rights;
- a split-off or division to separate assets or businesses;
- the appraisal rights triggered by the merger;
- the tax consequences of the merger or split;
- the need for a fairness opinion and disclosure.

Merger structures can accomplish in one vote what would take dozens of consents, but they trigger appraisal rights and require careful disclosure and fairness analysis.

### Address Tax Consequences Of The Restructuring

Restructurings can trigger taxable events for the company or stockholders. Tax analysis must precede execution.

Analyze:

- whether the exchange qualifies for tax-free treatment under reorganization rules;
- the treatment of debt cancellation or forgiveness;
- the tax basis of exchanged securities;
- withholding obligations for foreign stockholders;
- the effect on net operating losses and built-in gains;
- reporting and information returns;
- coordination with the company's tax advisors.

A restructuring intended to save the company can create tax liabilities that defeat the purpose if not modeled in advance.

### Communicate And Retain Key People

Restructurings fail when key employees leave. Communication and retention are part of the legal plan.

Plan:

- timing and content of employee communications;
- retention bonuses or equity refresh grants;
- vesting acceleration or modifications;
- the effect on morale and the narrative;
- coordination with securities law quiet periods;
- confidentiality to prevent leaks that destabilize customers and employees.

## Common Traps

### Failing To Model Winners And Losers

A restructuring that surprises a constituency with dilution invites litigation and blocked consents. Model first.

### Ignoring Fiduciary Duty Exposure

Insider or distressed transactions face entire fairness review. Build an independent process.

### Missing A Consent

A single missing consent blocks closing. Map the matrix early.

### Reckless Option Repricing

Repricing can trigger accounting expense and tax issues, and cancellation-and-regrant can cause departures. Analyze before acting.

### Aggressive Debt Conversion Discounts

Steep conversion discounts hand debt holders a large stake at founders' expense. Negotiate as part of the whole.

### Overlooking Tax Consequences

A tax-free restructuring can become taxable if structured poorly, creating liabilities that defeat the purpose.

### Skipping Fairness And Disclosure

Merger and split structures require fairness opinions and disclosure. Skipping them invites appraisal and fraud claims.

### Neglecting Employee Communication

Silence during restructuring causes departures. Plan retention and communication.

## Self-Check

- Is the cap table modeled before and after the restructuring under multiple exit scenarios, showing who wins and loses across preference, conversion, options, common, debt, and new money?
- Is fiduciary duty exposure assessed for insider or distressed transactions, with independent committee, majority-of-minority, fairness, valuation, disclosure, and documentation considered?
- Is the consent matrix assembled early, covering board, stockholder class votes, preferred consents, debt holders, option holders, preemptive rights, regulators, and lenders?
- Are option repricing and exchange analyzed for amendment versus cancellation-and-regrant, accounting expense, 409A, pool effect, and employee retention?
- Is debt conversion and bridge financing addressed for conversion terms, discount, cap, seniority, warrants, change of control, and tax treatment?
- Are merger, reverse split, and split structures considered for cap table cleanup, with appraisal rights, tax consequences, fairness opinions, and disclosure?
- Are tax consequences analyzed for reorganization qualification, debt forgiveness, basis, withholding, NOLs, and reporting?
- Are employee communication, retention bonuses, equity refresh, and vesting modifications planned to retain key people?
- Are securities law quiet periods and confidentiality managed to prevent destabilizing leaks?
- Does the output avoid asserting that the restructuring is fair, tax-free, or litigation-proof and instead recommend experienced corporate, tax, and litigation counsel for the specific transaction?
