# Anti-Dilution And Protective Provisions

Anti-dilution and protective provisions are the mechanisms by which investors protect their economic stake and control between and across financing rounds. Agents often treat these provisions as standard clauses and copy them from templates without modeling how they actually behave in a down round, a bridge financing, or an acquisition. The result is that founders discover, too late, that a full ratchet wiped out half their ownership, that an investor's preemptive right blocked a critical new investor, or that a protective provision veto paralyzed the company during a pivot. These provisions compound across rounds, and their interaction with pay-to-play, carveouts, and seniority determines who really controls the company and who captures its upside.

Use this skill before drafting or negotiating a financing's anti-dilution and protective provisions, before modeling a down round, before assessing whether existing investor consent is required for a transaction, or when auditing whether accumulated provisions have created governance gridlock. The goal is to force the agent to model each provision under stress scenarios and to recognize that protective provisions are control devices as much as economic ones. These provisions are interdependent and jurisdiction-specific; final documents require experienced venture counsel.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Anti-Dilution Types And Model Their Impact

Anti-dilution adjusts the conversion price of preferred stock when the company issues shares below that price, protecting investors from dilution in a down round. The type chosen dramatically changes the outcome.

Compare:

- full ratchet, which reduces the conversion price to the new lower price regardless of the size of the down round, severely punishing common holders;
- broad-based weighted average, which adjusts the conversion price based on a weighted formula considering the size of the new issuance, the standard market compromise;
- narrow-based weighted average, which uses a narrower share count in the denominator and is more investor-favorable than broad-based;
- no anti-dilution, which leaves investors exposed to down rounds but is founder-favorable.

Model each type under a hypothetical down round. A full ratchet on a small down round can reset the conversion price as if the entire round occurred at the low price, producing a massive shift to investors. Broad-based weighted average dampens the effect proportionally to the round size.

### Define The Conversion Price Formula Precisely

The weighted average formula's variables determine its behavior. Draft the formula with care.

Specify:

- the old conversion price and new conversion price;
- the number of shares outstanding before the new issuance, including all dilutive securities on an as-converted basis for broad-based or only common for narrow-based;
- the number of shares issued in the down round;
- the consideration received;
- any alternative formulas offered by the investor;
- rounding and minimum adjustment thresholds.

A broad-based formula that includes all options, warrants, and convertible securities in the denominator produces a smaller adjustment. A narrow-based formula using only common shares produces a larger adjustment. The difference can be significant in a down round.

### Apply Pay-To-Play To Balance Anti-Dilution

Pay-to-play provisions require investors to participate in future rounds to keep their anti-dilution and other preferred rights. They prevent investors from free-riding on a down round.

Structure:

- the threshold for participation, typically pro rata share of the new round;
- the consequence of failing to participate, typically conversion of non-participating preferred to common or loss of anti-dilution;
- whether the consequence is automatic or requires a vote;
- carveouts for investors unable to participate for regulatory or other reasons;
- coordination with preemptive rights.

Pay-to-play aligns investor incentives to support the company in difficult rounds and protects founders from investors who refuse to fund but keep their preferences.

### Map Preemptive And Participation Rights

Preemptive rights let existing investors buy their pro rata share of new issuances to maintain their ownership. They are economic protections but also control devices.

Define:

- which investors have preemptive rights;
- the pro rata calculation, including or excluding other preemptive rights holders;
- the threshold above which preemptive rights apply;
- carveouts for employee grants, acquisitions, conversions, and bank financings;
- the notice and exercise mechanics and timelines;
- oversubscription rights when some investors do not exercise;
- coordination with new investor demands for large allocations.

Preemptive rights can crowd out new investors and complicate a round. Model the available allocation after preemptive exercises before promising shares to new investors.

### Negotiate Rights Of First Refusal And Co-Sale

ROFR and co-sale rights govern transfers of founder and investor shares. They control who can join the cap table and prevent unwanted transfers.

Define:

- the company's or investors' right to buy shares before a founder transfers them;
- the co-sale right that lets investors sell their proportionate shares alongside a founder;
- the notice and exercise mechanics;
- permitted transfers for estate planning and affiliates;
- lockup periods around an IPO;
- treatment of secondary sales in later rounds.

ROFR and co-sale rights can block or reshape founder secondary sales and affect liquidity planning.

### Allocate Board And Observer Rights

Board composition and observer rights allocate formal control and information access. They are among the most negotiated provisions.

Negotiate:

- the total board size and the allocation among founder common, investor preferred, and independent directors;
- the threshold for each investor to nominate a director;
- observer rights that give investors information and meeting access without a vote;
- removal and vacancy filling mechanics;
- independent director selection process and approval;
- committee composition and independence requirements;
- board actions requiring separate investor director consent.

A board where investors hold multiple seats and separate consent rights can effectively control the company even without a majority of shares.

### Scope Protective Provisions And Veto Rights

Protective provisions give investors veto rights over major corporate actions. Their scope determines how much freedom founders retain.

Identify:

- the matters requiring investor consent, such as charter amendments, new senior equity, asset sales, mergers, dissolution, budgets, executive hiring, related-party deals, debt, and IP licenses;
- the consent threshold, a single major investor, a class majority, or a board majority;
- sunset and standstill provisions that expire the veto after time or milestones;
- exceptions for ordinary course and emergency actions;
- the interaction with board approval and shareholder approval.

Long protective provision lists with low thresholds create governance gridlock. Negotiate which matters require consent and raise thresholds where possible.

### Model The Cumulative Effect Across Rounds

Provisions compound. Three rounds of participating preferred with full ratchet and broad protective provisions can leave founders with minimal control and economics.

Model:

- the cumulative preference stack and its effect on common proceeds across exit values;
- the cumulative anti-dilution adjustments across multiple down rounds;
- the cumulative protective provisions and whether multiple investor classes have vetoes;
- the cumulative board seats and whether founders retain a majority;
- the cumulative preemptive rights and their effect on new investor allocations.

A cap table that looked fine at seed can become unworkable after several rounds. Model the trajectory, not only the current round.

## Common Traps

### Copying Anti-Dilution From A Template Without Modeling

Full ratchet and narrow-based formulas produce very different outcomes than broad-based. Model before agreeing.

### Ignoring Pay-To-Play

Without pay-to-play, investors can refuse to fund a down round but keep their preferences, shifting all dilution to founders.

### Overbroad Protective Provisions

A long veto list with low thresholds paralyzes the company. Limit scope and raise thresholds.

### Missing Preemptive Right Interactions

Preemptive rights can crowd out new investors and complicate a round. Model available allocation first.

### Allowing Multiple Investor Vetoes

When several investor classes each hold vetoes, the company cannot act without unanimous investor consent. Harmonize thresholds.

### Forgetting Cumulative Effects

Provisions compound across rounds. Model the trajectory to avoid an unworkable cap table.

### Vague Conversion Price Formulas

Ambiguous variables produce disputes in down rounds. Define the formula precisely.

### Ignoring Board And Observer Power

Observer rights and separate director consent give investors control without majority ownership. Negotiate information and consent architecture carefully.

## Self-Check

- Are anti-dilution types compared and modeled under a hypothetical down round, with full ratchet, broad-based, narrow-based, and no anti-dilution outcomes calculated?
- Is the conversion price formula defined precisely, including shares outstanding, as-converted treatment, consideration, rounding, and thresholds?
- Is pay-to-play structured with participation thresholds, consequences, carveouts, and coordination with preemptive rights?
- Are preemptive and participation rights mapped for holders, pro rata calculation, thresholds, carveouts, notice and exercise mechanics, and oversubscription?
- Are rights of first refusal and co-sale defined for scope, notice, permitted transfers, lockups, and secondary treatment?
- Are board and observer rights negotiated for size, allocation, nomination thresholds, removal, independents, committees, and separate consent?
- Are protective provisions and veto rights scoped by matter, threshold, sunset, exceptions, and interaction with board and shareholder approval?
- Is the cumulative effect of preferences, anti-dilution, vetoes, board seats, and preemptive rights modeled across multiple rounds?
- Are carveouts for employee grants, acquisitions, conversions, and bank financings included to avoid unintended triggers?
- Does the output avoid asserting that provisions are market or enforceable and instead recommend experienced venture counsel to model, negotiate, and document the specific financing?
