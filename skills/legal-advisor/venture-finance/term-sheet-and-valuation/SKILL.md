# Term Sheet And Valuation

A venture financing term sheet looks short, but it encodes the economic and control relationship between founders and investors for the life of the company. Agents often fixate on valuation and treat the rest as boilerplate, when the liquidation preference, participation, anti-dilution, protective provisions, board composition, and option pool treatment can shift more value than a large valuation difference. A high headline valuation with a participating preferred and a large option pool taken from the pre-money can leave founders with far less than a lower valuation with cleaner terms. Term sheets also set defaults that are hard to renegotiate in definitive documents, so the negotiation moment matters disproportionately.

Use this skill before signing a term sheet, before responding to investor marks, before modeling founder dilution, or when auditing whether a past financing created unfavorable terms. The goal is to force the agent to read the term sheet as an integrated economic and control document, to model the cap table under exit scenarios, and to identify terms that compound across rounds. Term sheets are non-binding except for specified clauses, but they frame the binding documents; final terms require experienced venture counsel.

## Core Rules

### Read Valuation In Context With The Option Pool And Preference

Headline valuation is misleading without the option pool and preference. The effective pre-money can be far lower than advertised.

Adjust for:

- whether the option pool is created pre-money or post-money, which shifts dilution to founders;
- the size of the option pool and whether it covers future hires or is pre-funded;
- the liquidation preference multiple, typically 1x but sometimes higher;
- whether the preference is participating or non-participating;
- the cap on participation if any;
- the conversion ratio and when preferred converts to common;
- any warrants, bridge discounts, or advisor grants that dilute the pool.

Model the effective pre-money by subtracting the option pool and preference-adjusted value. Two term sheets with the same headline valuation can produce very different founder outcomes.

### Model The Liquidation Waterfall Under Multiple Exit Scenarios

The liquidation preference determines who gets paid first and how much in an exit. Modeling the waterfall under low, base, and high exit values reveals who actually benefits.

For each scenario, calculate:

- the preference stack by seniority if multiple rounds exist;
- whether preferred is participating and thus takes its preference plus a share of the remainder;
- whether participation is capped and at what multiple;
- the conversion threshold at which preferred converts to common;
- the founder and employee proceeds after the preference;
- the breakeven exit value at which conversion becomes better than participation.

A participating preferred with no cap can capture most of the upside in a modest exit, leaving founders and employees with little. A 1x non-participating preferred is founder-friendlier.

### Evaluate Dividends And Their Cumulative Effect

Dividends on preferred stock can be current or cumulative, and cumulative dividends accrue even if unpaid, growing the preference over time.

Check:

- whether dividends are cumulative or non-cumulative;
- the dividend rate and compounding;
- whether dividends are paid in cash, stock, or accrued;
- the treatment of accrued dividends at exit or conversion;
- the interaction with the liquidation preference.

Cumulative dividends at 8 percent compounded annually can meaningfully increase the preference over a multi-year hold, eroding common value in an exit.

### Analyze Conversion And Anti-Dilution Mechanics

Conversion rights let preferred convert to common, usually when advantageous. Anti-dilution protections adjust the conversion price in a down round.

Define:

- voluntary conversion terms and when investors convert;
- automatic conversion on an IPO at a threshold price and qualified offering size;
- the conversion price and how anti-dilution adjusts it;
- whether anti-dilution is broad-based weighted average, narrow-based weighted average, or full ratchet;
- the pay-to-play provisions that strip anti-dilution from non-participating investors;
- carveouts for employee grants, acquisitions, and warrant issuances.

Full ratchet anti-dilution is highly investor-favorable and punishes founders severely in a down round. Broad-based weighted average is the market compromise.

### Map Protective Provisions And Control

Protective provisions give investors veto rights over major decisions. They can constrain founders long after the financing.

Identify:

- board composition and investor seats versus common seats versus independent seats;
- matters requiring investor consent, such as charter amendments, new senior equity, asset sales, mergers, budgets, hiring executives, related-party deals, and debt;
- whether consent thresholds are a single investor, a class majority, or a board majority;
- information rights and inspection rights;
- observer rights;
- drag-along rights that compel founders to support a sale;
- co-sale and right of first refusal on founder transfers;
- redemption rights that let investors force a buyback after a period.

A long list of protective provisions with low thresholds effectively transfers control to investors. Negotiate which matters truly require consent and at what threshold.

### Negotiate Founder-Friendly Terms Without Killing The Deal

Not every term is worth fighting over. Prioritize the terms with the largest economic and control impact.

Prioritize:

- effective valuation after option pool and preference;
- liquidation preference multiple and participation;
- anti-dilution type;
- board composition and protective provision thresholds;
- option pool size and placement;
- founder vesting and acceleration;
- drag-along scope and triggers;
- redemption rights.

Concede on lower-impact terms such as expense reimbursement, legal fee caps, and certain information rights to preserve goodwill for the terms that matter.

### Coordinate With Existing And Future Rounds

A term sheet does not exist in isolation. It interacts with existing preferred and sets the precedent for future rounds.

Check:

- rights of existing investors to participate in the new round;
- preemptive rights and how they interact with new investor demands;
- whether the new round is senior, pari passu, or subordinated to existing preferred;
- whether protective provisions require existing investor consent;
- cumulative effect of preferences across rounds;
- impact on the option pool and 409A valuation;
- whether the round triggers pay-to-play for existing investors.

A new round that creates senior preference over existing investors can create conflict and require careful negotiation.

### Address Founder Vesting, Acceleration, And Employment Terms

Investors often require founder vesting refresh or acceleration limits as part of the financing.

Define:

- whether founders re-vest or continue existing vesting;
- cliff and schedule;
- acceleration on termination without cause, constructive termination, or change of control;
- single versus double trigger acceleration;
- treatment of unvested shares on termination;
- non-compete and non-solicit obligations tied to the financing;
- IP assignment confirmations.

## Common Traps

### Fixating On Headline Valuation

The option pool placement, preference, and participation can shift more value than a valuation difference. Model the effective pre-money.

### Accepting Participating Preferred Without A Cap

Participating preferred with no cap captures most upside in modest exits. Negotiate a cap or non-participating terms.

### Ignoring Cumulative Dividends

Accrued cumulative dividends grow the preference over time and erode common value. Read the dividend terms carefully.

### Agreeing To Full Ratchet Anti-Dilution

Full ratchet punishes founders in a down round. Push for broad-based weighted average.

### Overbroad Protective Provisions

A long consent list with low thresholds transfers control to investors. Limit the list and raise thresholds.

### Missing The Option Pool Placement

A pre-money option pool shifts dilution to founders. Negotiate post-money placement or a smaller pool.

### Forgetting Existing Investor Rights

Preemptive and consent rights of existing investors can complicate or block a new round. Coordinate early.

### Treating The Term Sheet As Non-Binding And Unimportant

Most terms are hard to renegotiate in definitive documents. Negotiate at the term sheet stage.

## Self-Check

- Is the effective pre-money modeled after adjusting for option pool placement, size, liquidation preference, participation, warrants, and discounts?
- Is the liquidation waterfall modeled under low, base, and high exit scenarios, with preference seniority, participation caps, conversion thresholds, and founder and employee proceeds?
- Are dividends analyzed for cumulative versus non-cumulative, rate, compounding, and treatment at exit or conversion?
- Are conversion and anti-dilution mechanics defined, including voluntary and automatic conversion, broad-based versus narrow-based weighted average, full ratchet, pay-to-play, and carveouts?
- Are protective provisions and control mapped, including board composition, consent matters, thresholds, information and observer rights, drag-along, co-sale, and redemption?
- Are founder-friendly priorities identified and lower-impact concessions managed to preserve goodwill?
- Are existing investor participation, preemptive, consent, seniority, and pay-to-play interactions coordinated?
- Are founder vesting, acceleration triggers, termination treatment, non-competes, and IP assignment addressed?
- Is the cumulative effect of preferences and terms across rounds considered?
- Does the output avoid asserting that terms are market or enforceable and instead recommend experienced venture counsel to negotiate and document the round?
