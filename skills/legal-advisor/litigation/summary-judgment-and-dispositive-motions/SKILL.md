# Summary Judgment And Dispositive Motions

Dispositive motions decide cases without a trial, and they are where legal strategy and evidentiary discipline meet. A summary judgment motion is not a second amended complaint or a re-argument of the pleadings; it requires admissible evidence that establishes or defeats a material fact such that no genuine dispute remains. Agents often misjudge the motion by treating it as a legal argument alone, by relying on inadmissible evidence, by ignoring the local rule format and page limits, or by failing to develop the separate statement of material facts that many courts require. A weak summary judgment motion does more than lose; it educates the opponent, narrows the issues, and can prejudice the trial record. A strong one ends the case.

Use this skill before filing or opposing a dispositive motion, before deciding whether the record supports summary judgment, before selecting which claims or defenses to move on, or before sequencing dispositive motions with discovery and expert disclosures. The goal is to force the agent to assess the evidentiary record honestly, distinguish legal from factual disputes, comply with procedural and local rules, and weigh the strategic costs of filing. Dispositive motion practice is jurisdiction- and judge-specific; filing decisions and briefing should be reviewed by qualified litigation counsel.

## Core Rules

### Match The Motion To The Standard And Stage

Different dispositive motions apply different standards and are available at different stages.

Distinguish:

- motion to dismiss for failure to state a claim tests the legal sufficiency of the complaint, accepting well-pleaded facts as true and drawing inferences for the plaintiff;
- judgment on the pleadings considers the complaint and answer but generally no extrinsic evidence;
- summary judgment tests whether a genuine dispute of material fact exists, using admissible evidence;
- partial summary judgment addresses specific claims, defenses, or issues, narrowing the case for trial;
- directed verdict or judgment as a matter of law occurs at or after trial and applies a different standard.

Selecting the wrong motion at the wrong stage wastes effort and can waive stronger arguments. A motion to dismiss is often the first and cheapest dispositive option; summary judgment usually follows the close of fact discovery.

### Build The Evidentiary Record Before Moving

Summary judgment lives or dies on the record. The most common reason motions fail is that the movant has no admissible evidence to establish or rebut a fact.

Confirm the record contains:

- authenticated documents and exhibits;
- deposition testimony or affidavits from witnesses with personal knowledge;
- expert reports and testimony where expert opinion is required;
- admissions from requests for admission and responses to interrogatories;
- evidence that satisfies the foundation, hearsay, and authentication requirements;
- evidence that is properly cited to the record with pinpoint citations.

Affidavits must be based on personal knowledge, set out facts that would be admissible in evidence, and show that the affiant is competent to testify. Hearsay in an affidavit is generally disregarded on summary judgment. Build the record through discovery before moving, not after.

### Master The Separate Statement Of Material Facts

Many jurisdictions require a separate, short, and concise statement of material facts, each with record citations, and a corresponding response from the non-movant. Failure to comply can result in the motion being deemed admitted or denied.

Follow the local rule:

- each fact in its own numbered paragraph;
- pinpoint record citations for every fact;
- a matching response that admits, denies, or qualifies, with counter-citations;
- no legal argument embedded in the statement of facts;
- a statement of additional material facts by the non-movant where genuine disputes exist.

Courts often resolve summary judgment by working through the separate statement. A movant whose statement lacks citations, or a non-movant who fails to controvert with evidence, can lose the motion on format alone.

### Distinguish Legal From Factual Disputes

Summary judgment is defeated only by a genuine dispute of material fact, not by a legal argument or a mere denial.

Assess:

- is the disputed fact material, meaning it could affect the outcome under the substantive law?
- is the dispute genuine, meaning a reasonable jury could find for the non-movant?
- is the evidence sufficient to create a dispute, or is it speculative, conclusory, or based on inadmissible hearsay?
- does the dispute turn on credibility, which is usually for the jury?
- is the issue purely legal (statutory interpretation, contract construction) and thus appropriate for decision without a fact dispute?

A party cannot create a factual dispute by mere assertion or by citing inadmissible evidence. Conversely, courts generally do not grant summary judgment where credibility is central.

### Apply The Burden-Shifting Framework

Who bears the burden of production and persuasion shapes the motion.

Determine:

- on a claim where the non-movant bears the burden of proof at trial, the movant can prevail by showing an absence of evidence, or by presenting evidence that negates an essential element;
- on a defense or issue where the movant bears the burden, the movant must affirmatively establish the absence of a genuine dispute;
- once the movant meets its initial burden, the non-movant must come forward with specific facts showing a genuine dispute;
- the non-movant cannot rest on the pleadings or speculation.

A movant without the burden of proof at trial often has the easier path: demonstrate that the opponent lacks evidence on an essential element.

### Sequence Motions With Discovery And Experts

Timing is strategic. Moving too early forfeits the benefit of discovery; moving too late may be barred by scheduling orders.

Plan:

- complete fact discovery needed to support the motion;
- obtain expert reports and depositions where expert testimony is required to establish or defeat an element;
- consider partial summary judgment on liability before damages discovery if liability is the stronger issue;
- coordinate with the discovery cut-off, expert disclosure deadlines, and the dispositive motion deadline in the scheduling order;
- preserve issues for appeal by making the record and rulings clear.

Some claims require expert testimony (for example, medical malpractice, complex damages). Moving for summary judgment without the required expert support is usually futile.

### Weigh The Strategic Costs And Benefits

Filing a dispositive motion is not always wise even when colorable.

Consider:

- the likelihood of success under the specific judge's tendencies;
- the cost of briefing and the impact on the trial schedule;
- the risk of educating the opponent or narrowing issues favorably to them;
- the value of narrowing the case even if the motion is denied;
- whether denial prejudices the trial record or settlement leverage;
- the appealability of the ruling and the effect on timing.

A motion filed mainly to delay, or without a real record, can draw sanctions, adverse inferences, or loss of credibility with the court.

## Common Traps

### Treating Summary Judgment As A Re-Argument Of The Pleadings

Summary judgment requires evidence, not legal assertions. Restating the complaint does not defeat a properly supported motion.

### Relying On Inadmissible Evidence

Hearsay, unauthenticated documents, and opinion outside expert disclosure are disregarded. Evidence must be admissible or reducible to admissible form at trial.

### Ignoring The Separate Statement Requirement

Local rules often make facts deemed admitted or denied based on the statement. Format failures lose motions.

### Moving Before The Record Is Ready

Filing before key discovery or expert reports are in often guarantees denial and wastes resources.

### Confusing Credibility With Material Fact

If the outcome turns on whom to believe, summary judgment is usually inappropriate. Do not move on pure credibility disputes.

### Overlooking The Burden Of Proof

A movant bearing the burden at trial must affirmatively negate any genuine dispute; one without the burden can prevail by showing an absence of evidence.

### Filing Strategically Without Weighing Costs

A weak motion educates the opponent, narrows issues, and can damage credibility with the court. File only with a real record and a real shot at success.

## Self-Check

- Is the motion matched to the correct standard and stage (dismissal, judgment on the pleadings, summary judgment, partial summary judgment, judgment as a matter of law)?
- Does the record contain authenticated documents, deposition testimony or personal-knowledge affidavits, expert reports where required, admissions, and properly cited evidence meeting foundation and hearsay rules?
- Is the separate statement of material facts prepared or controverted per the local rule, with pinpoint citations and no embedded legal argument?
- Are material factual disputes distinguished from legal issues, and is the motion directed at issues appropriate for decision without a jury?
- Is the burden-shifting framework applied correctly, identifying who bears the burden of proof and what each side must produce?
- Is the motion sequenced with fact discovery, expert disclosures, the scheduling order deadlines, and the trial plan?
- Have the strategic costs (briefing expense, issue-narrowing, opponent education, credibility, appealability) been weighed against the likelihood of success?
- Does the output avoid asserting a likely outcome and instead recommend qualified litigation counsel review of the motion, the record, and the specific judge's practice?
