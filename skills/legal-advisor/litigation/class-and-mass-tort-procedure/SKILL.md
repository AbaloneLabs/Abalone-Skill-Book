# Class And Mass Tort Procedure

Aggregate litigation multiplies every ordinary litigation risk. A single certification ruling can expose a defendant to liability across thousands or millions of claims, convert a manageable dispute into an existential one, or, conversely, extinguish claims through a class-wide release. Agents often underestimate how certification standards, adequacy of representation, predominance, and notice requirements drive the entire strategy, and how the ethics of aggregate settlement differ sharply from individual representation. Mass tort and MDL coordination add layers of procedural, evidentiary, and conflicts management problems that do not appear in single-plaintiff cases.

Use this skill before assessing class certification, before advising on MDL or mass tort coordination, before structuring an aggregate settlement, before planning class-wide discovery or damages models, or before evaluating the ethical limits of representing many plaintiffs with divergent interests. The goal is to force the agent to analyze certification requirements rigorously, recognize conflicts and adequacy problems, understand notice and opt-out mechanics, and respect the distinct ethical rules governing aggregate settlements. Class and mass tort practice is highly jurisdiction-specific and often bet-the-company for defendants; certification and settlement decisions require qualified counsel.

## Core Rules

### Analyze The Certification Requirements As A Whole

Certification is not a formality; it is the central battleground. The requirements work together, and failing any one defeats the class.

Confirm against the governing rule (for example, Rule 23 or its equivalents):

- numerosity: the class is so numerous that joinder is impracticable;
- commonality: there are questions of law or fact common to the class;
- typicality: the claims or defenses of the representative are typical of the class;
- adequacy: the representative and class counsel will fairly and adequately protect the class;
- the applicable type: injunctive or declaratory relief class, damages class with common issues predominating, or a settlement class.

Predominance is usually the hardest requirement for damages classes. If individualized issues (causation, damages, defenses, statutes of limitations) overwhelm common ones, certification should fail. Build the predominance analysis around the trial plan.

### Distinguish Class Actions From Mass Tort Aggregation

Not all aggregate litigation is a class action. Mass torts are often coordinated individual actions, and the procedural devices differ.

Recognize:

- class actions bind absent class members through certification and notice;
- mass torts are typically individual cases coordinated for pretrial efficiency, often through MDL or state coordination, without binding absent parties;
- MDL consolidation is for pretrial proceedings and remands cases for trial unless settled;
- bellwether trials are used in mass torts to test value and liability but do not bind non-parties;
- aggregate settlements in mass torts raise distinct ethics issues because each plaintiff is separately represented.

Conflating class and mass tort procedure leads to wrong strategy. A mass tort cannot rely on class-wide release without certification, and a class action cannot proceed case-by-case on individualized issues.

### Scrutinize Adequacy Of Representation And Conflicts

Adequacy protects absent class members who will be bound by the judgment. Conflicts within the class can defeat certification or invalidate a settlement.

Assess:

- whether the named plaintiffs have claims typical of the class or unique defenses that undermine typicality;
- whether class counsel is qualified, resourced, and free of conflicts;
- whether intra-class conflicts exist (for example, current versus former users, severely versus mildly injured, those seeking injunctive relief versus those seeking damages);
- whether a subclass structure or additional representatives are needed to protect divergent interests;
- whether the settlement creates allocation conflicts among class members.

Courts scrutinize adequacy most closely at settlement, because the adversarial check of trial is absent. A settlement that favors some class members at the expense of others can be overturned for inadequate representation.

### Build A Workable Trial Plan And Damages Model

For damages classes, predominance requires a trial plan that can feasibly resolve common issues and then determine individual damages without unmanageable individual hearings.

Address:

- whether liability can be established class-wide through common proof;
- whether a damages model can calculate class-wide damages reliably, or whether damages are inherently individualized;
- how individual issues (exposure, causation, comparative fault, offsets) will be resolved;
- whether experts can opine on class-wide causation and damages under the governing evidentiary standard;
- whether the trial plan complies with the governing rule's manageability requirements.

A damages model that relies on individualized determinations for most elements undermines predominance and certification.

### Manage Notice, Opt-Out, And Objector Dynamics

Class members' due process rights depend on adequate notice and a meaningful opportunity to opt out or object.

Confirm:

- the best notice practicable under the circumstances, including individual notice where identities and addresses are available;
- the content of notice (claims, class definition, opt-out deadline, objector rights, settlement terms);
- the opt-out mechanics and the effect of opting out or remaining in the class;
- the treatment of objectors and the court's obligation to consider objections;
- the effect of the judgment or settlement on class members who did not opt out.

Inadequate notice can void a class settlement. Opt-out rates and objector activity also signal the fairness of the settlement and can affect approval.

### Apply Aggregate Settlement Ethics

Representing multiple clients in aggregate litigation triggers ethical rules that do not apply to single-client representation.

Address:

- the rule against aggregate settlements without each client's informed consent (for example, the aggregate settlement rule that requires disclosure of the total and each client's share);
- conflicts among clients with different injuries, claims, or priorities;
- the allocation methodology and whether it is fair and transparent;
- the need for independent counsel for clients whose interests diverge;
- the prohibition on agreements that restrict a lawyer's ability to disclose conflicts or settlement terms.

Aggregate settlements that allocate recovery opaquely, or that favor some clients over others without informed consent, violate ethics rules and can be set aside.

### Coordinate MDL And Multi-Jurisdiction Strategy

MDL and state coordination create procedural and strategic complexity.

Plan:

- the leadership structure for plaintiffs' and defense counsel;
- the scope of pretrial consolidation (discovery, Daubert, summary judgment) versus remand;
- bellwether selection and the strategic value of early trials;
- coordination with parallel state court proceedings and government enforcement;
- global versus individual settlement strategy;
- the effect of remand on trial strategy and venue.

MDL practice concentrates power in leadership counsel and requires careful management of conflicts, information flow, and settlement authority.

## Common Traps

### Treating Certification As A Formality

Certification is the case. Failing to brief numerosity, commonality, typicality, adequacy, and predominance thoroughly loses the motion.

### Ignoring Intra-Class Conflicts

Current versus former users, severely versus mildly injured, and injunctive versus damages claimants have divergent interests. Conflicts defeat adequacy.

### Assuming A Damages Model Supports Predominance

A model that requires individualized determinations for most elements undermines class-wide proof and certification.

### Inadequate Notice

Notice that omits material terms, opt-out rights, or objector rights can void the settlement and expose parties to collateral attack.

### Violating Aggregate Settlement Ethics

Allocating settlement without informed consent of each client, or favoring some clients over others, breaches ethical rules and can invalidate the deal.

### Conflating Class And Mass Tort Procedure

A mass tort cannot bind absent parties without certification; a class action cannot proceed case-by-case on individualized issues. The procedural devices are distinct.

### Underestimating MDL Leadership And Conflicts

MDL leadership concentrates authority over thousands of clients. Conflicts, allocation disputes, and information asymmetries must be managed from the outset.

## Self-Check

- Are numerosity, commonality, typicality, adequacy, and predominance analyzed against the governing rule, with a focus on the hardest requirement for the claim type?
- Is the matter correctly classified as a class action, mass tort coordination, or MDL, with the procedural consequences of each understood?
- Are intra-class conflicts, typicality defenses, subclass structure, and class counsel qualifications assessed for adequacy, especially at settlement?
- Is there a workable trial plan and damages model that can resolve common issues class-wide and individual damages without unmanageable hearings?
- Does the notice plan provide best-practicable notice, with content, opt-out mechanics, objector rights, and binding effect on absent members?
- Are aggregate settlement ethics (informed consent, disclosure of total and individual shares, allocation fairness, independent counsel for divergent interests) satisfied?
- Is MDL or multi-jurisdiction coordination planned for leadership, pretrial scope, bellwether trials, remand, and global versus individual settlement?
- Does the output avoid predicting certification or settlement approval and instead recommend qualified class action counsel review of the specific jurisdiction's standards?
