# Abuse Of Dominance

Abuse-of-dominance law governs the conduct of firms with substantial market power, and it is treacherous because conduct that is lawful for a small firm can be unlawful for a dominant one. Agents often assess pricing, bundling, or refusal decisions as ordinary commercial strategy and miss that dominance imposes special obligations, that the line between vigorous competition and exclusion is contested, and that authorities in different jurisdictions apply different thresholds and theories. The penalties are large, and the conduct is often everyday business behavior.

Use this skill before setting pricing or discounts, designing bundling or tying, refusing to supply, changing distribution terms, or assessing exposure for a firm with significant market share. The goal is to force the agent to assess dominance, distinguish exclusionary from procompetitive conduct, evaluate specific abuse categories, and escalate to antitrust counsel for any dominant-firm matter. This skill is decision support, not legal advice.

## Core Rules

### Establish Whether The Firm Is Dominant First

Dominance is the threshold, and it turns on market power, not size alone.

Assess:

- market definition (product and geographic) and its contestability;
- market share and its trend (thresholds vary; some regimes presume dominance above certain levels);
- constraints from actual and potential competitors;
- buyer power and countervailing leverage;
- barriers to entry and expansion;
- the firm's ability to behave independently of competitors, customers, and consumers;
- single versus collective dominance.

A firm can be dominant in one market and not another, or dominant after a narrow market definition. Establish dominance market by market before analyzing conduct.

### Distinguish Exclusionary From Exploitative Abuse

Abuse takes two main forms with different theories.

Identify:

- exclusionary abuse (foreclosing or weakening rivals: predatory pricing, exclusive dealing, loyalty rebates, tying, refusal to supply, margin squeeze);
- exploitative abuse (directly harming consumers: excessive pricing, unfair terms);
- discrimination among trading partners;
- the jurisdiction's treatment of each (some focus on exclusion, others on exploitation).

Exclusionary abuse is about protecting competitive structure; exploitative abuse is about direct consumer harm. The theory shapes the evidence and the defense.

### Evaluate Predatory Pricing And Below-Cost Pricing Carefully

Pricing below cost by a dominant firm can be predatory.

Assess:

- the relevant cost standard (average variable cost, average avoidable cost, total cost);
- recoupment feasibility (the ability to recoup losses after excluding rivals);
- legitimate business justifications (promotional pricing, clearing perishables);
- the duration and context of below-cost pricing;
- the distinction between aggressive competition and predation.

Below-cost pricing is not automatically predatory; recoupment and intent matter. But dominant firms pricing below cost face heightened scrutiny and should document justification.

### Assess Exclusive Dealing, Loyalty Rebates, And Tying

Conditional practices can foreclose rivals.

Evaluate:

- exclusivity requirements and their coverage of the market;
- loyalty and fidelity rebates and their conditional nature;
- tying and bundling (pure versus mixed) and their foreclosure effect;
- the share of the market foreclosed;
- the duration and inducement strength;
- objective justifications and efficiencies;
- the distinction between volume and loyalty rebates.

Conditional rebates that reward exclusivity can be abusive even if not strictly exclusive. Dominant firms should prefer unconditional, volume-based incentives where possible.

### Review Refusal To Supply And Essential-Facility Questions

A dominant firm's refusal to deal can be abusive in certain cases.

Assess:

- whether the firm has terminated a prior dealing relationship;
- whether the input or facility is indispensable to competition;
- whether refusal could eliminate all competition;
- the firm's capacity and legitimate reasons;
- intellectual-property and innovation considerations;
- the essential-facilities doctrine and its limits in the jurisdiction.

There is generally no duty to deal with a rival, but once a relationship exists or an input is indispensable, refusal faces scrutiny. Document legitimate business reasons.

### Examine Margin Squeeze And Price-Cost Relationships

A margin squeeze occurs when the spread between wholesale and retail prices is too thin for efficient rivals.

Assess:

- the dominant firm's wholesale (upstream) and retail (downstream) prices;
- whether an equally efficient rival could profitably compete;
- the cost standard applied;
- the role of regulation in setting one of the prices;
- objective justifications.

Margin squeeze is a specific theory that requires both upstream and downstream presence. Test the margin against an equally efficient competitor.

### Consider Price Discrimination And Secondary-Line Effects

Charging different prices to similarly situated customers can raise concerns.

Assess:

- primary-line (harm to the dominant firm's direct competitors);
- secondary-line (harm to disfavored customers' competitors);
- the jurisdiction's treatment of discrimination;
- cost-justification and meeting-competition defenses;
- the role of data and algorithmic pricing.

Price discrimination is treated differently across jurisdictions and is increasingly scrutinized with algorithmic pricing. Document cost-justification where it exists.

### Build Objective Justifications And Efficiencies

Dominance law allows procompetitive justifications, but they must be genuine.

Develop:

- objective business reasons for the conduct;
- efficiencies and consumer benefits;
- less restrictive alternatives considered;
- evidence that the conduct is indispensable to the benefit;
- consistency between stated justification and actual conduct.

A post-hoc justification that does not match the conduct's design will fail. Build the justification contemporaneously and document it.

### Coordinate Multi-Jurisdictional Exposure

Dominance is enforced differently across jurisdictions.

Address:

- differing dominance thresholds and presumptions;
- differing treatment of specific conduct (e.g., loyalty rebates, excessive pricing);
- extraterritorial application;
- the risk of divergent or conflicting obligations;
- concurrent sector regulation.

A practice lawful in one jurisdiction may be abusive in another. Analyze each jurisdiction where the firm is dominant.

## Common Traps

### Assessing Conduct Without Establishing Dominance

Dominance is the threshold. Establish it market by market first.

### Treating Dominant-Firm Strategy As Ordinary Competition

Dominance imposes special obligations. Conduct lawful for small firms can be abusive.

### Below-Cost Pricing Without Justification Documentation

Predatory-pricing scrutiny is heightened. Document legitimate justification.

### Conditional Rebates That Function As Exclusivity

Loyalty rebates can be abusive. Prefer unconditional, volume-based incentives.

### Refusing To Deal Without Documenting Business Reasons

Refusal faces scrutiny once dealing exists or inputs are indispensable. Document reasons.

### Post-Hoc Justifications That Do Not Match Conduct

Justifications must be genuine and contemporaneous. Build them as you go.

### Assuming One Jurisdiction's Rule Applies Everywhere

Dominance thresholds and conduct treatment differ. Analyze each jurisdiction.

## Self-Check

- Is dominance established per relevant market (definition, share, trend, constraints, buyer power, barriers, independence, single versus collective) before conduct analysis?
- Are exclusionary and exploitative abuses distinguished, with the jurisdiction's treatment of each understood?
- Is predatory and below-cost pricing assessed against the relevant cost standard, recoupment, justification, duration, and the competition-versus-predation distinction?
- Are exclusive dealing, loyalty rebates, tying and bundling assessed for foreclosure coverage, duration, inducement, justification, and the volume-versus-loyalty distinction?
- Are refusal to supply and essential-facility questions assessed for prior relationship, indispensability, elimination of competition, capacity, IP, and documented business reasons?
- Is margin squeeze assessed for the upstream-downstream spread, equally-efficient-competitor test, cost standard, regulation, and justification?
- Is price discrimination assessed for primary- and secondary-line harm, jurisdictional treatment, cost-justification, meeting-competition, and algorithmic-pricing considerations?
- Are objective justifications and efficiencies developed contemporaneously with less-restrictive alternatives, indispensability, and conduct-consistency?
- Is multi-jurisdictional exposure (thresholds, conduct treatment, extraterritoriality, divergence, sector regulation) coordinated?
- Does the output escalate dominance assessment, specific conduct review, multi-jurisdiction exposure, and enforcement risk to qualified antitrust counsel?
