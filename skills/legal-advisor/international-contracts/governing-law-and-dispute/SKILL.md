# Governing Law And Dispute

The governing law and dispute resolution clauses are the most consequential clauses in a cross-border contract because they determine what law applies and where and how a dispute is resolved. Agents often treat them as boilerplate, copying a familiar clause without checking whether the chosen law is permissible, whether the arbitration clause is valid and enforceable, or whether the resulting award or judgment can be enforced where the counterparty's assets are. A defective clause can leave parties in limbo, litigating jurisdiction for years before reaching the merits, or holding an unenforceable award against a judgment-proof structure.

Use this skill before drafting or negotiating governing law, arbitration, forum selection, or dispute resolution clauses in a cross-border contract. The goal is to force the agent to choose law and forum deliberately, draft the clause for validity and enforceability, and plan enforcement where the assets are. Choice of law and dispute resolution are jurisdiction-specific and treaty-dependent; any clause must be confirmed against the applicable conventions (Rome I, Hague, New York Convention) and the law of the seat and likely enforcing courts.

## Core Rules

### Choose The Governing Law Deliberately

Party autonomy allows choice of governing law in most commercial contracts, but the choice has limits.

Assess:

- whether the chosen law is permissible (consumer, employment, insurance, and some mandatory regimes restrict choice);
- the scope of the choice (formation, validity, performance, interpretation, third parties);
- whether to choose the law of a country or a sub-unit (state law);
- the interaction with mandatory rules of the forum and third countries with a close connection;
- the effect on matters outside party autonomy (title, capacity, tort, IP, regulatory);
- the consistency with the CISG exclusion and any supranational regime.

A governing law clause that conflicts with mandatory rules or protective regimes can be partially overridden. State the choice clearly and scope it deliberately.

### Decide Between Litigation And Arbitration

The mechanism shapes the entire dispute path.

Compare:

- neutrality and the perceived home-court advantage;
- confidentiality and privacy;
- the ability to choose decision-makers with expertise;
- the scope of discovery and procedural flexibility;
- the enforceability of the result (New York Convention for arbitration; Hague, Brussels I, or reciprocity for court judgments);
- cost, speed, and the ability to appeal;
- interim relief and the role of national courts.

Arbitration generally offers wider enforceability but limited appeal and can be costly. Litigation offers appeal and court powers but narrower recognition. Choose with the counterparty's asset locations in mind.

### Draft A Valid And Enforceable Arbitration Clause

An arbitration clause must satisfy the New York Convention and the law of the seat to be valid.

Include:

- a clear agreement to arbitrate (not merely an aspiration or a clause pointing to non-existent rules);
- the scope of disputes covered;
- the seat (legal place) of arbitration, distinct from the venue;
- the institutional rules (ICC, LCIA, SIAC, HKIAC, AAA/ICDR) or ad hoc rules (UNCITRAL);
- the number and method of appointing arbitrators;
- the language of the arbitration;
- the governing law of the contract (and, if intended, the law of the arbitration clause);
- the waiver of any right to litigate, including class actions where permitted.

A pathologically drafted clause (unclear seat, conflicting rules, missing signature) can spawn jurisdictional disputes that consume years. Use tested model clauses from the chosen institution.

### Draft A Valid And Enforceable Forum Selection Clause

A forum selection clause must be clear and exclusive where intended, and must fit the applicable regime.

Specify:

- exclusive vs. non-exclusive;
- the named court and its jurisdiction;
- the scope of disputes and parties covered;
- consistency with the governing law and any arbitration clause;
- the effect of asymmetric clauses (one party's right to choose) and their enforceability;
- compliance with protective regimes (consumer, employment, insurance);
- the recognition regime for the resulting judgment (Hague Choice of Court, Brussels I).

An ambiguous or overbroad clause can be unenforceable or read narrowly. Draft for the chosen regime.

### Plan Enforcement Where The Assets Are

A clause is only as good as the enforceability of its result.

Map:

- where the counterparty's assets are likely to be;
- the recognition regime between the seat/forum and the enforcing state;
- grounds for refusal (public policy, due process, excess of authority, corruption, procedural irregularity);
- the risk of sovereign immunity and state-owned entity status;
- the need for local counsel and powers of attorney.

Choose the seat and mechanism so the award or judgment is recognizable where enforcement will occur. A well-drafted clause defeated at enforcement is a failed clause.

### Address The Law Of The Clause Separately

The law governing the arbitration clause can differ from the law governing the contract.

Determine:

- whether to specify a separate law for the arbitration clause;
- the default rule (often the law of the seat);
- the separability of the clause from the contract;
- the kompetenz-kompetenz principle and the tribunal's power to rule on its own jurisdiction;
- the consistency between the clause law, the contract law, and the seat.

A separate clause law can avoid surprises where the main contract law would invalidate the arbitration agreement.

### Handle Language, Currency, And Multi-Tier Clauses

Operational details affect cost and enforceability.

Specify:

- the language of the proceedings and translations;
- the currency of claims, awards, and interest;
- pre-award and post-award interest;
- multi-tier clauses (negotiation, mediation, then arbitration) and their enforceability;
- expert determination for technical issues and its limited enforceability;
- the waiver of set-off, consolidation, and class procedures where permitted.

Multi-tier and expert clauses can be unenforceable or create jurisdictional delays if drafted vaguely. Specify triggers and consequences.

### Coordinate With Mandatory And Regulatory Regimes

Some disputes cannot be arbitrated or litigated in the chosen forum.

Check:

- consumer, employment, and insurance protective regimes;
- securities, competition, IP, and insolvency matters that may be non-arbitrable or subject to exclusive jurisdiction;
- sanctions and export control that override forum choice;
- public policy of the seat and enforcing state.

A clause that purports to override mandatory regimes can be partially void. Confirm arbitrability and jurisdiction for the subject matter.

## Common Traps

### Treating Law And Forum As Boilerplate

These clauses determine what law applies and where the dispute is resolved. Draft them deliberately.

### Pathological Arbitration Clauses

Unclear seat, conflicting rules, or missing elements create years of jurisdictional disputes. Use tested model clauses.

### Choosing A Forum That Cannot Enforce

A clause is worthless if the award or judgment is unenforceable where the assets are. Plan enforcement first.

### Ignoring The Law Of The Clause

The arbitration clause can have a different governing law than the contract. Specify it to avoid surprises.

### Asymmetric Clauses Without Checking Enforceability

Asymmetric jurisdiction clauses are valid in some regimes and void in others. Confirm before relying on them.

### Overriding Mandatory Regimes

Consumer, employment, securities, competition, IP, and insolvency matters may be non-arbitrable or subject to exclusive jurisdiction. Check arbitrability.

### Vague Multi-Tier Clauses

Negotiation or mediation conditions can be unenforceable or cause delay if triggers are unclear. Specify them.

### Inconsistent Governing Law And CISG

Choosing a domestic law without addressing the CISG can leave it applicable. Align the clauses.

## Self-Check

- Is the governing law chosen deliberately, with scope, permissibility, mandatory rules, and protective regimes considered?
- Is the dispute mechanism (litigation, arbitration, mediation, expert) chosen with enforceability and asset locations in mind?
- Is the arbitration clause clear on agreement, scope, seat, rules, arbitrators, language, and waiver of litigation?
- Is the forum selection clause exclusive or non-exclusive as intended, scoped, and compliant with the recognition regime?
- Is enforcement planned against the recognition regime and refusal grounds of the likely enforcing states?
- Is the law of the arbitration clause addressed separately from the law of the contract?
- Are language, currency, interest, multi-tier conditions, expert determination, and waivers specified?
- Are mandatory and regulatory regimes (consumer, employment, securities, competition, IP, insolvency, sanctions) checked for arbitrability and jurisdiction?
- Is the clause consistent with the governing law clause and any CISG exclusion?
- Does the output recommend confirmation against the applicable conventions and the law of the seat and enforcing courts by qualified counsel?
