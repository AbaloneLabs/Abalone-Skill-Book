# Currency And Force Majeure

Cross-border contracts are exposed to events that domestic contracts rarely face: currency volatility, exchange controls, sanctions, embargoes, war, expropriation, and abrupt changes in foreign government action. Agents often draft force majeure clauses by copying a generic list, omitting the events that actually matter for the transaction, or assume that a government act or sanction automatically excuses performance. The result is parties locked into contracts they cannot perform, or parties escaping obligations they could have met, with disputes over notice, mitigation, and termination that the contract never addressed.

Use this skill before drafting or reviewing currency, payment, force majeure, hardship, sanctions, or government action provisions in a cross-border contract, or advising on remedies when performance is disrupted. The goal is to force the agent to allocate the risks of currency, control, and government action deliberately, define the trigger and consequence precisely, and address notice, mitigation, and termination. These clauses are interpreted under the governing law and any supranational regime; any conclusion must be confirmed against the applicable law and the parties' actual circumstances.

## Core Rules

### Allocate Currency And Exchange Risk Deliberately

Currency movement can erase a contract's economics. Decide who bears the risk.

Specify:

- the currency of payment and whether alternative currencies are permitted;
- the exchange rate reference (spot, central bank, fixing date) if conversion is needed;
- who bears appreciation and depreciation risk;
- the effect of currency controls and the obligation to obtain approvals;
- the treatment of devaluation, redenomination, and introduction of new currencies;
- the right to renegotiate or terminate on material currency shift (hardship).

A clause silent on currency risk leaves it on the party obliged to pay in a currency it must source. Allocate the risk expressly.

### Address Exchange Controls And Payment Approvals

Many countries restrict cross-border payment, conversion, and repatriation.

Map:

- whether payment requires central bank or regulatory approval;
- the timing and risk of approval delays;
- the obligation to apply diligently and the consequence of refusal;
- the availability of alternative payment routes;
- the effect of a lawful inability to pay on default and termination;
- the interaction with sovereign debt restructuring and standstills.

A party unable to pay due to exchange controls may still be in default unless the clause allocates the risk. Address approval, delay, and refusal expressly.

### Draft Force Majeure Precisely

Force majeure excuses performance only when the clause and governing law are satisfied. Generic lists are weak.

Define:

- the triggering events (war, terrorism, civil unrest, natural disaster, epidemic, government act, sanctions, strike, cyber incident);
- the threshold (beyond reasonable control, unavoidable, unforeseeable);
- causation and the link to non-performance;
- the notice requirement (timing, content, evidence);
- the duty to mitigate and resume performance;
- the consequence (suspension, extension, price adjustment, termination);
- the time limit after which termination is available;
- the carve-outs (events within control, events the party assumed, market or financial difficulty without an external trigger).

Financial difficulty, price changes, and counterparty defaults are generally not force majeure unless expressly included. Do not assume a government act qualifies without checking causation and foreseeability.

### Distinguish Force Majeure, Hardship, And Frustration

These doctrines differ and produce different remedies.

Clarify:

- force majeure (excuse for non-performance during the event, often contractual);
- hardship (fundamental change of circumstances triggering renegotiation and possible termination);
- frustration or impracticability (common law or civil law doctrine that may discharge the contract);
- material adverse change or material adverse effect clauses (often in M&A and finance);
- the governing law's approach, which varies sharply between common law and civil law.

A clause that mixes these doctrines creates ambiguity. Define each trigger and remedy distinctly.

### Handle Sanctions, Embargoes, And Export Controls

Sanctions and export controls can make performance illegal overnight and override any contract.

Address:

- the obligation to comply with all applicable sanctions and export controls;
- the right to suspend or terminate if performance becomes illegal;
- the treatment of designated parties, blocked persons, and controlled items;
- the interaction with secondary sanctions and extraterritorial measures;
- the obligation to screen and the consequence of a designation;
- the governing law's mandatory compliance with UN, EU, UK, US, and other sanctions.

A contract cannot require illegal performance. Sanctions illegality generally excuses performance, but the consequence (suspension vs. termination, cost allocation) should be specified.

### Address Government Acts And Expropriation

Government action can directly disrupt performance.

Consider:

- expropriation, nationalization, and requisition of assets;
- license revocation, regulatory bans, and import or export prohibitions;
- changes in law that make performance illegal or uneconomic;
- war, armed conflict, and sovereign default;
- the distinction between a force majeure event and a political risk allocated to one party.

Political risk can be allocated by contract, insured through political risk insurance, or hedged through BIT protections. Decide deliberately who bears it.

### Define Notice, Mitigation, And Termination Mechanics

The consequence of a triggering event depends on the mechanics.

Specify:

- the notice timeline and content (event, cause, expected duration, mitigation steps);
- the duty to mitigate and resume performance;
- the effect on payment, delivery, and other obligations;
- the right to price adjustment, extension, or suspension;
- the termination right after a defined period or event;
- the treatment of goods in transit and partial performance;
- the allocation of costs during the event.

A clause that excuses performance without addressing these mechanics leaves the parties in limbo.

### Coordinate With Insurance And Risk Transfer

Some risks are better insured than allocated by contract.

Check:

- trade credit insurance for non-payment and political risk;
- political risk insurance for expropriation, currency inconvertibility, and political violence;
- cargo and marine insurance aligned with the Incoterm;
- business interruption and cyber insurance;
- the interaction between insurance proceeds and contractual remedies.

Insurance can complement, but not replace, clear contractual allocation. Coordinate the two.

## Common Traps

### Generic Force Majeure Lists

A copied list omits the events that matter for the transaction. Draft to the actual risk profile.

### Assuming Government Act Equals Force Majeure

Causation, foreseeability, and control matter. Some government acts are assumed risks or allocated to one party.

### Silent Currency Risk

A clause silent on currency leaves the risk on the paying party. Allocate it expressly.

### Ignoring Exchange Controls

Lawful inability to pay can still be default. Address approval, delay, and refusal.

### Mixing Force Majeure, Hardship, And Frustration

These doctrines differ. Define triggers and remedies distinctly.

### Sanctions As An Afterthought

Sanctions override contracts. Address compliance, designation, and illegality expressly.

### Missing Notice And Mitigation Mechanics

Without mechanics, parties litigate the consequence. Specify notice, mitigation, and termination.

### Insurance Replacing Allocation

Insurance complements but does not replace clear contractual risk allocation. Coordinate both.

## Self-Check

- Is currency risk allocated, with payment currency, exchange rate reference, and redenomination addressed?
- Are exchange controls mapped, with approval, delay, refusal, and lawful inability to pay handled?
- Is force majeure drafted with specific triggers, threshold, causation, notice, mitigation, consequence, time limit, and carve-outs?
- Are force majeure, hardship, frustration, and MAC clauses distinguished with separate triggers and remedies?
- Are sanctions, embargoes, and export controls addressed, with compliance, designation, illegality, and termination rights?
- Are government acts, expropriation, and changes in law allocated to a party or insured?
- Are notice, mitigation, suspension, price adjustment, and termination mechanics specified?
- Is insurance (trade credit, political risk, cargo, cyber) coordinated with contractual remedies?
- Are financial difficulty and market changes excluded from force majeure unless expressly intended?
- Does the output recommend confirmation against the governing law's frustration, impracticability, and force majeure doctrines by qualified counsel?
