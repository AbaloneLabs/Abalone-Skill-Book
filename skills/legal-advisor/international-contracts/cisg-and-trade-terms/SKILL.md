# CISG And Trade Terms

The UN Convention on Contracts for the International Sale of Goods (CISG) and Incoterms are the two frameworks that most shape an international sale of goods, and both are routinely mishandled. The CISG applies by default to contracts between parties in different contracting states, yet parties frequently exclude it by accident, exclude it inconsistently, or assume domestic law applies when it does not. Incoterms allocate cost, risk, and delivery duties, but agents often pick a term by familiarity rather than by the actual logistics, leaving gaps on insurance, export clearance, or title that the contract never fills. The result is disputes over conformity, risk of loss, inspection, and remedies that neither party anticipated.

Use this skill before drafting or reviewing an international sales contract, choosing an Incoterm, addressing conformity and inspection, or selecting remedies. The goal is to force the agent to determine CISG applicability and exclusion deliberately, match the Incoterm to the transaction, and fill the gaps the frameworks leave open. CISG interpretation follows its own international principles, and Incoterms versions differ; any conclusion must be confirmed against the applicable version and the parties' actual conduct.

## Core Rules

### Determine Whether The CISG Applies

The CISG applies automatically when the parties have places of business in different contracting states, unless excluded.

Check:

- whether both parties have places of business in different contracting states;
- whether either state has made a reservation (Article 95 on reciprocal application, Article 96 on writing) and its status;
- whether the goods are excluded (consumer goods bought for personal use, auctions, execution, stocks, securities, money, ships, electricity, services predominating);
- whether the contract is for goods, services, or mixed, and which predominates;
- whether the parties' places of business are the relevant ones, not nationality or civil status.

If the CISG applies, it governs formation and the rights and obligations of seller and buyer, but not validity, property rights (title), or tort claims unless the contract relates to the goods.

### Decide Deliberately Whether To Exclude The CISG

Exclusion must be express and clear. Ambiguous exclusion fails.

For each exclusion:

- state the exclusion expressly (not by choosing a domestic law generally);
- exclude both Part I and Part II if intended (formation and obligations);
- consider partial exclusion or opt-back-in for specific issues;
- ensure consistency with the governing law clause;
- avoid the trap of choosing a domestic law that itself would apply the CISG.

Choosing "governed by the law of country X" where country X is a contracting state may still leave the CISG applicable within that country. Exclude expressly if the goal is domestic law.

### Allocate Risk, Cost, And Delivery With The Right Incoterm

Incoterms allocate who bears cost, risk, and delivery duties at each stage. Pick the term that matches the actual logistics.

For the chosen term:

- the delivery point and risk transfer moment;
- who arranges and pays carriage, insurance, and clearance;
- export and import clearance responsibilities;
- packaging, marking, and inspection duties;
- the mode of transport the term fits (maritime-only terms like FOB, CIF vs. any-mode terms like FCA, DAP);
- the Incoterms version (2020 vs. earlier) and the need to specify.

Do not use a maritime term for containerized goods (FCA is generally safer than FOB for containers), and do not assume a term allocates title, insurance beyond the minimum, or breach consequences.

### Address Conformity, Inspection, And Notice

The CISG imposes conformity obligations and notice duties that differ from domestic law.

Map:

- the seller's conformity obligations (quantity, quality, description, packaging, and any agreed or implied requirements);
- the buyer's duty to examine the goods within as short a period as practicable;
- the buyer's duty to give notice of lack of conformity within a reasonable time, with loss of rights if not;
- the effect of a notice that specifies the nature of the lack of conformity;
- the interplay with contractual inspection and rejection clauses.

A buyer that fails to give timely notice loses the right to rely on the lack of conformity, even if the defect exists. Build inspection and notice timelines into the contract.

### Select Remedies Consistent With The CISG

The CISG provides a specific remedies structure that parties should not contradict silently.

Identify:

- buyer remedies (require performance, substitute goods, repair, price reduction, avoidance, damages);
- seller remedies (require performance, cure, repair, substitute goods, damages);
- the fundamental breach threshold for avoidance;
- the right to damages and the duty to mitigate;
- interest on overdue sums;
- the limitation of the foreseeability of damages;
- the exclusion or limitation of damages for personal injury and the permissibility of other exclusions.

Contractual remedies that contradict the CISG may be ineffective or interpreted against the drafter. Align remedy clauses with the CISG framework.

### Fill The Gaps The Frameworks Leave

CISG and Incoterms do not cover everything.

Fill:

- title and retention of title until payment;
- warranties and representations beyond conformity;
- product liability and recalls;
- intellectual property infringement indemnities;
- force majeure and hardship (the CISG has a limited excuse in Article 79);
- dispute resolution, governing law for excluded matters, and jurisdiction;
- confidentiality, export control, and sanctions;
- compliance with import regulations, product standards, and labeling.

A contract that relies only on Incoterms and the CISG leaves major risks unallocated.

### Ensure Consistency Across Contract, Conduct, And Documents

Inconsistencies between the contract, Incoterms, conduct, and trade documents create disputes.

Check:

- consistency between the Incoterm, the bill of lading, insurance, and payment terms;
- consistency between the CISG exclusion and the governing law clause;
- consistency between the contract and the parties' actual performance;
- consistency between standard terms, purchase orders, and order acknowledgments (battle of forms under CISG Article 19);
- consistency between the contract and letters of credit requirements.

The CISG's mirror image rule and battle of forms can produce a contract on terms neither party intended. Manage the exchange of forms deliberately.

### Handle Payment, Letters Of Credit, And Trade Finance

Payment mechanisms interact with delivery and documents.

Address:

- the interaction between the Incoterm and the letter of credit documents required;
- the UCP 600 and the doctrine of strict compliance;
- the independence principle and fraud exception;
- documentary drafts, open account, and escrow;
- currency, exchange controls, and payment timing.

A letter of credit requires documents that match the Incoterm and the contract. Inconsistency can block payment.

## Common Traps

### Assuming Domestic Law Applies

The CISG applies by default between contracting states. Excluding it requires express language.

### Inconsistent Or Ambiguous Exclusion

Choosing a domestic law without expressly excluding the CISG may leave it applicable. Exclude clearly.

### Wrong Incoterm For The Transport Mode

Maritime terms (FOB, CIF) for containers or air freight create gaps. Match the term to the mode.

### Missing The Notice Of Non-Conformity

A buyer that fails timely notice loses remedies, even with a real defect. Build timelines into the contract.

### Contradicting CISG Remedies

Contractual remedies that conflict with the CISG may be ineffective. Align remedy clauses.

### Relying Only On Incoterms And CISG

Title, IP indemnity, product liability, force majeure, and dispute resolution are not covered. Fill the gaps.

### Battle Of Forms Producing Unintended Terms

The CISG mirror image rule can create a contract on the last form's terms. Manage form exchanges.

### Inconsistent Letter Of Credit Documents

Documents that do not match the Incoterm block payment. Align credit terms with delivery.

## Self-Check

- Is CISG applicability determined based on places of business in different contracting states and excluded categories?
- If excluding the CISG, is the exclusion express, clear, and consistent with the governing law clause?
- Is the Incoterm matched to the transport mode, with delivery point, risk transfer, carriage, insurance, and clearance allocated?
- Are conformity, examination, and notice duties mapped, with contractual timelines that preserve buyer remedies?
- Are remedies aligned with the CISG structure (performance, cure, price reduction, avoidance, damages, interest, mitigation)?
- Are title, retention of title, warranties, product liability, IP indemnity, force majeure, and dispute resolution filled in?
- Is the contract consistent with conduct, trade documents, letters of credit, and the battle of forms?
- Are the Incoterms version and any reservations or partial exclusions specified?
- Are excluded matters (validity, title, tort) addressed by the chosen governing law?
- Does the output recommend confirmation against the applicable CISG text, Incoterms version, and trade finance rules by qualified counsel?
