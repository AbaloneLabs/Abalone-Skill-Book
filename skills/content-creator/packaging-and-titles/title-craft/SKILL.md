# Title Craft

A title is the single line of text that decides whether someone clicks, opens, or scrolls past, and it carries a disproportionate share of a piece's success. Titles are not labels; they are promises, and the discipline of title craft is earning the click through a promise the content keeps, without drifting into the exaggeration and manipulation that earn clicks but destroy trust. The harm this skill prevents is the dual failure of titles too vague or boring to earn attention, and titles so exaggerated they earn clicks the content cannot satisfy, training audiences to distrust the creator and eroding long-term performance.

Use this skill when writing any title, when choosing among title options, when diagnosing underperformance, and when reviewing whether titles set promises the content keeps.

## Core Rules

### Treat The Title As A Promise With Stakes

A title is a contract: it tells the audience what they will get, and the content must deliver it. Write every title by first stating the contract explicitly, then crafting the title to set that contract compellingly. The title's implied stakes must be proportionate to what the content delivers. A title that implies major stakes for minor content creates the clickbait gap and destroys trust; a title that undersells major value may not earn the click.

Calibrate: promise what the content delivers, no more and no less. Audiences forgive modest promises kept; they punish inflated promises broken.

### Balance Clarity And Curiosity

Effective titles balance two forces. Clarity tells the audience exactly what they will get, reducing risk and earning clicks from those who want it. Curiosity withholds just enough to compel the click, earning clicks from those who want to resolve the tension. The balance depends on the content and audience:

- clarity-dominant titles work for utility content where the audience wants to know they will get the answer;
- curiosity-dominant titles work for narrative or surprising content where the reveal is the value;
- most titles benefit from a mix: clear enough to signal value, curious enough to compel the click.

Pure clarity can be boring; pure curiosity can feel clickbaity. Find the mix that fits the content and respects the audience.

### Make Titles Specific, Not Generic

Specificity earns clicks; generality loses them. "Tips for better sleep" is generic and ignorable. "The 4-7-8 breathing method that helped me fall asleep in 10 minutes" is specific and compelling. Specificity signals that the content contains real, concrete value rather than recycled generalities.

Inject specificity through exact numbers, named situations, concrete examples, the creator's actual experience, and specific contradictions or stakes. Vague titles signal vague content.

### Front-Load The Most Important Words

Audiences scan titles quickly, often seeing only the first few words before deciding. Front-load the most important words, the audience, the problem, the stakes, or the payoff, so the title earns attention before it is fully read. Burying the value at the end of a long title loses audiences who never read that far.

This is especially important on platforms that truncate titles in feeds. Ensure the truncated portion still conveys the core promise.

### Match Title To Audience Language And Sophistication

Titles must speak in the audience's language for their problem and match their sophistication level. A title using internal vocabulary the audience does not use will not register. A title pitched at the wrong sophistication level (too basic for advanced audiences, too advanced for beginners) misses the target. Use the audience's words and signal the sophistication level so the right audience clicks and the wrong audience self-selects out.

### Develop Multiple Title Options And Choose Deliberately

Strong titles rarely emerge on the first attempt. For important pieces, develop 3 to 5 title options varying clarity, curiosity, specificity, and angle, then choose the one that best sets a deliverable promise in the most compelling way. Track which title patterns resonate with the specific audience over time.

Where platforms allow A/B testing, test title variants and learn. Where they do not, rotate patterns and observe performance to inform future titles.

### Adapt Titles To Platform Norms Without Abandoning Honesty

Each platform has title norms: YouTube rewards curiosity and specificity in longer titles; newsletters reward clarity and benefit in subject lines; podcasts reward intrigue and naming; short-form rewards punchy on-screen text. Adapt title length, structure, and register to platform norms, but do not adopt norms that require dishonesty (fake urgency, manufactured controversy, deceptive implications). Adapt form, hold honesty.

### Ensure Titles Do Not Imply More Authority Than The Content Has

Titles that imply expert authority or guaranteed outcomes the content does not provide earn clicks but mislead. In health, financial, legal, mental health, and safety content, titles implying guaranteed results, expert diagnosis, or professional advice the creator cannot provide create harm and liability. Calibrate title claims to the content's actual authority, disclose limits where relevant, and where titles approach regulated advice, ensure the content includes professional referral.

### Avoid Manipulative Title Patterns

Certain title patterns reliably damage trust and should be avoided or used with extreme care:

- fabricated urgency ("you need to see this before it's deleted");
- manufactured controversy or rage;
- exaggerated stakes disproportionate to the content;
- deceptive implications that the content does not fulfill;
- fear-based titles, especially in health, safety, or financial content.

These patterns earn short-term clicks at the cost of long-term trust and audience quality.

## Common Traps

### The Clickbait Gap

Titles that promise more than the content delivers generate clicks but destroy trust and reduce return viewership. Calibrate title stakes to content payoff.

### Pure Curiosity Without Clarity

Titles built only on curiosity ("you won't believe...") without signaling value set contracts the content cannot keep. Balance curiosity with clarity.

### Generic Titles

Vague, category-level titles signal vague content and lose to specific competitors. Inject specifics.

### Burying The Value

Titles that front-load filler and bury the value lose audiences who scan only the first words. Front-load the most important words.

### Wrong Sophistication Level

Titles pitched at the wrong audience sophistication miss the target. Signal the level so the right audience clicks.

### First-Draft Titles

Accepting the first title that comes to mind forgoes the improvement that comes from developing and comparing options. Develop multiple options.

### Dishonest Platform Norms

Adopting platform title norms that require deception trades long-term trust for short-term clicks. Adapt form, hold honesty.

### Authority Overreach In Titles

Titles implying expert authority or guaranteed outcomes the content lacks mislead and, in regulated domains, harm. Calibrate title claims to real authority.

### Manipulative Patterns

Fabricated urgency, manufactured controversy, exaggerated stakes, and fear-based titles earn clicks at the cost of trust and audience quality. Avoid them.

### Titles In High-Stakes Domains

For health, financial, legal, mental health, or safety content, titles implying guaranteed outcomes, expert diagnosis, or professional advice the creator cannot provide create harm and liability. Titles should promise education and perspective, disclose limits where relevant, and where content approaches regulated advice, include professional referral. Escalate high-stakes titles to review before publishing.

## Self-Check

Before approving a title, verify:

- The title is treated as a contract, with stakes proportionate to what the content delivers.
- Clarity and curiosity are balanced appropriately for the content type and audience.
- The title is specific (numbers, situations, examples, experience), not generic.
- The most important words are front-loaded for scanning and truncation.
- The title uses audience language and signals the right sophistication level.
- Multiple title options were developed for important pieces, with the strongest chosen.
- The title adapts to platform norms in form without adopting dishonest tactics.
- The title does not imply more authority or guaranteed outcomes than the content provides.
- Manipulative patterns (fabricated urgency, manufactured controversy, exaggerated stakes, fear-based) are avoided.
- For high-stakes domains, the title promises education and perspective, discloses limits, includes professional referral where content approaches regulated advice, and has been reviewed before publishing.
