# Topic Architecture

Topic architecture is the internal structure of topics and subtopics within and across content pillars. Where pillar strategy decides what domains of value a channel covers, topic architecture decides how the specific subjects within those domains are organized, sequenced, and connected so that the audience can navigate the creator's body of work and the creator can see gaps and overlaps. The harm this skill prevents is the common pattern of a creator producing dozens of pieces that overlap redundantly, leave critical subtopics uncovered, or fail to build on each other, so the body of work never accumulates into a coherent map the audience can use or trust.

Use this skill when planning a quarter or year of topics, when auditing existing content for gaps and overlaps, when designing a topic cluster or learning sequence, and when a channel's content feels repetitive without progressing.

## Core Rules

### Build A Visible Topic Map For Each Pillar

For each pillar, construct an explicit map of the topics and subtopics it covers. The map is a working document, not a publication, and it should show:

- the core topics the pillar addresses;
- the subtopics under each core topic;
- the relationships between topics (prerequisite, complementary, contrast, extension);
- the audience sophistication level each topic targets;
- which topics are evergreen and which are timely;
- which topics have been covered and which remain open.

Without a visible map, the creator relies on memory and recent inspiration, which produces clustering around familiar topics and neglect of harder or less obvious ones. The map makes gaps and overlaps visible so coverage becomes deliberate.

### Sequence Topics By Audience Learning Curve

Within a pillar, topics have a natural learning sequence. Foundational topics must precede advanced ones; orientation must precede decision-making; common cases must precede edge cases. Sequence the topics so an audience member can enter at their level and progress.

Define for each pillar:

- entry topics that orient beginners and require no prerequisites;
- core topics that constitute the pillar's main value;
- advanced topics that reward practitioners ready for depth;
- update topics that revisit core topics with new developments.

A topic set with only entry topics caps the audience's growth; a set with only advanced topics excludes the audience the pillar should be building. Sequence deliberately to serve the full learning curve.

### Organize Topics Into Clusters That Compound

Rather than treating each topic as an isolated piece, organize related topics into clusters that compound over time. A cluster is a set of pieces that together constitute deeper coverage than any single piece could. Clusters:

- let the audience explore a subtopic thoroughly;
- build internal links and references that increase time on channel;
- create evergreen hubs that new pieces can feed into;
- demonstrate the creator's depth and authority in the subtopic.

For each pillar, identify 2 to 4 clusters the creator will build over months, and plan pieces that feed each cluster rather than scattering across disconnected topics.

### Identify And Fill Coverage Gaps

Use the topic map to identify gaps the audience needs but the creator has not covered. Common gap types:

- foundational topics assumed but never explicitly covered;
- decision topics where the audience must choose but the creator has not provided frameworks;
- failure and recovery topics that address what goes wrong, not just what works;
- edge cases and exceptions that the core content glosses over;
- audience questions that recur in comments but are not addressed in content.

Gaps are opportunities. Each gap the creator fills with a deliberate piece strengthens the architecture and serves an underserved audience need.

### Eliminate Redundant Overlap

Overlap is the inverse problem: multiple pieces covering the same ground without advancing. Some overlap is healthy (revisiting a core topic with new depth or context), but unintentional overlap wastes the creator's effort and the audience's attention.

Audit existing content for redundancy:

- do multiple pieces answer the same question at the same depth?
- could two pieces be consolidated or differentiated?
- is the creator returning to a comfortable topic to avoid harder ones?

Differentiate overlapping pieces by depth, angle, audience sophistication, or recency, or consolidate them. Do not publish a new piece that merely repeats an existing one without clear added value.

### Connect Topics Across Pillars

Topics in different pillars often relate, and cross-pillar connections strengthen the architecture. A topic in the "process" pillar may connect to a topic in the "mistakes" pillar; a "fundamentals" topic may support a "timely" topic. Plan cross-references and internal links so the audience can navigate between related pieces across pillars.

Cross-pillar connections also reveal whether the pillar set is coherent: if pillars never connect, they may be serving different audiences or promises, which is a strategic signal worth examining.

### Balance Evergreen And Timely Topics

Architecture must accommodate both evergreen topics (which accumulate long-term value and search traffic) and timely topics (which capture current attention). Plan the mix deliberately:

- evergreen topics form the durable backbone and should be the majority;
- timely topics connect the channel to current conversations and attract new audiences;
- timely topics should, where possible, link back to evergreen foundations.

A channel of only timely content has no accumulating asset; a channel of only evergreen content misses current attention. Architect both, with evergreen as the structural base.

### Keep The Architecture Visible And Maintainable

A topic map that lives in the creator's head degrades immediately. Maintain the map as a living document, update it after each publish, and review it on a schedule. The map is a strategic asset that improves planning, reveals gaps, and prevents drift.

## Common Traps

### Topic Clustering Around Comfort

Creators naturally return to topics they find easy or enjoyable, producing dense coverage of familiar ground while neglecting harder or less comfortable topics. The topic map reveals clustering; use it to force coverage of neglected areas.

### No Foundational Layer

Jumping to advanced or timely topics without establishing foundations excludes beginners and weakens the architecture's base. Ensure each pillar has explicit entry and foundational topics.

### Treating Every Piece As Standalone

Publishing isolated pieces without clustering or cross-referencing prevents the body of work from compounding. Plan clusters and internal links so pieces accumulate into deeper coverage.

### Confusing Overlap With Reinforcement

Some creators defend redundant overlap as "reinforcement," but reinforcement without added depth or angle wastes effort. Differentiate or consolidate; do not merely repeat.

### Ignoring Recurring Audience Questions

Questions that recur in comments and DMs are signals of architectural gaps. Ignoring them means the creator is publishing around rather than addressing the audience's real needs. Map recurring questions to topics and fill the gaps.

### Architecture That Outgrows Maintenance

An elaborate topic map that the creator cannot keep updated becomes stale and misleading. Keep the architecture simple enough to maintain on a regular schedule; a simple current map beats a complex outdated one.

### Covering Topics Beyond Competence

Architecture that includes topics requiring authority the creator lacks (especially in health, legal, financial, mental health, or safety domains) produces harmful or misleading content. Escalate: scope such topics to education and lived experience, disclose limits, and refer to qualified professionals rather than improvising expertise.

## Self-Check

Before approving a topic architecture or content plan, verify:

- A visible, maintained topic map exists for each pillar, showing core topics, subtopics, relationships, sophistication levels, and coverage status.
- Topics are sequenced by audience learning curve, with entry, core, advanced, and update topics.
- Topics are organized into clusters that compound, not treated as isolated pieces.
- Coverage gaps (foundational, decision, failure, edge cases, recurring questions) have been identified and planned.
- Redundant overlap has been audited and resolved by differentiation or consolidation.
- Cross-pillar connections are planned where topics relate.
- Evergreen and timely topics are balanced, with evergreen as the structural base.
- The architecture is maintained as a living document on a regular schedule.
- Topics requiring authority beyond the creator's competence are scoped to education with disclosure and professional referral.
