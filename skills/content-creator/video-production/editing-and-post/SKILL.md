# Editing And Post

Editing is where the video is actually made. The script and the shoot provide raw material, but the cut decides what the audience experiences. The judgment problem is that editing is treated as a mechanical stage of assembling footage, when in fact it is where pacing, clarity, retention, and tone are finalized. A well-shot video can be edited into something confusing or slow, and modest footage can be elevated by a tight, intentional cut.

The harm shows up in retention and perception. A cut that leaves in dead air, false starts, and tangents causes drop-off. Inconsistent audio levels, missing captions, and sloppy graphics signal low effort. A grade that fights the mood undermines the message. Export errors deliver a degraded file to the platform. This skill helps the agent make editing decisions that protect retention, clarity, and perceived quality, and to treat post-production as the final creative stage rather than a checkbox.

## Core Rules

### Cut For Retention First

The first job of the edit is to keep the audience watching. Every cut should be evaluated against whether it earns the next moment of attention. Remove anything that does not.

Cut for retention by:

- removing dead air, pauses, and filler words that add nothing;
- tightening tangents that delay the promise;
- front-loading the most engaging version of the hook;
- keeping beats moving so no section drags;
- preserving natural rhythm rather than cutting mechanically on every breath.

Tightness is not the same as speed. A deliberate pause can serve retention if it lands a point. The test is whether each moment earns its place.

### Build The Rough Cut Around Structure Before Polish

Before adding graphics, music, or grade, build a rough cut that proves the structure works. If the structure is weak, polish only disguises the problem temporarily.

Rough cut priorities:

- assemble beats in order and check the arc holds;
- verify the promise is delivered and the payoff lands;
- identify sections that drag or confuse;
- decide what to cut, compress, or reorder;
- confirm the total length matches the format and audience tolerance.

Do not invest in polish on a structure that may still change. Structure first, polish second.

### Use B-Roll And Graphics To Clarify, Not Decorate

B-roll, graphics, and text overlays should help the audience understand or stay engaged, not simply fill the screen. Each visual choice should have a job.

Use visuals to:

- illustrate an abstract claim with a concrete image;
- show data or steps that words alone cannot convey;
- cover cuts and restarts invisibly;
- reinforce a key point with on-screen text;
- vary the visual rhythm to prevent fatigue.

Decorative b-roll that does not serve the content distracts and dilutes. Every visual element should earn its place by aiding comprehension or retention.

### Mix Audio For Consistency And Clarity

Audio mixing is where perceived quality is often won or lost. Inconsistent levels, distracting music, and unintelligible speech undermine even strong visuals.

Mix audio by:

- normalizing speech so levels are consistent across the video;
- ducking music under voice so it supports rather than competes;
- removing or reducing background noise, hums, and clicks;
- using room tone to smooth cuts and avoid silence that sounds like drop-out;
- checking the mix on small speakers and earbuds, not just studio monitors.

The audience consumes on phones and cheap earbuds. Mix for those, and verify the mix translates.

### Add Captions And Accessibility Features

A large share of the audience watches with sound off or needs captions to access the content. Captions are not optional polish, they are core to reach and accessibility.

Provide:

- accurate captions synced to speech;
- speaker labels where useful;
- descriptions of meaningful non-speech audio where relevant;
- readable text size and contrast for mobile viewing;
- captions burned in or platform-supplied depending on the surface.

Captions also improve retention for viewers in sound-off environments and for non-native speakers.

### Grade To Support Mood, Not To Show Off

Color grading should serve the mood and credibility of the piece. Over-graded footage draws attention to the edit and away from the content.

Grade with intent:

- correct exposure and white balance first;
- match shots so cuts feel continuous;
- apply a look that supports the tone, warm, cool, contrasty, or soft;
- avoid heavy effects that signal trying too hard;
- keep skin tones natural unless a stylized look is the point.

A grade the audience does not notice is usually the right grade.

### Export And Deliver For The Platform

The final file must match the platform's specifications or quality is lost on upload. Exporting well preserves the work done in the edit.

Check export settings:

- resolution and aspect ratio match the platform's recommendation;
- bitrate balances quality and file size for the platform;
- codec and container are supported;
- audio levels meet platform loudness norms;
- metadata such as title and description are set where relevant.

A perfect edit uploaded at the wrong settings can look worse than a weaker edit uploaded correctly.

### Review The Cut Fresh Before Publishing

After living in an edit for hours, judgment about pacing and clarity degrades. Review the cut fresh, ideally after a break and on the device the audience will use.

Review for:

- pacing in the first thirty seconds where drop-off is steepest;
- clarity of the promise and payoff;
- audio consistency and caption accuracy;
- any remaining errors, typos in graphics, or mismatched shots;
- overall length versus the value delivered.

## Common Traps

### Polishing Before Structure Works

Investing in graphics and grade before the structure is solid wastes effort when the structure must change. Structure first.

### Decorative B-Roll

B-roll that does not illustrate or support the content distracts. Every visual should have a job.

### Inconsistent Audio Levels

Speech that jumps in volume or music that overwhelms the voice signals amateur work and causes drop-off.

### Skipping Captions

Without captions the video loses sound-off viewers and accessibility audiences. Captions are core, not optional.

### Over-Grading

Heavy color effects draw attention to the edit and away from the content. Grade to support mood, not to impress.

### Wrong Export Settings

A great edit uploaded at the wrong resolution, bitrate, or codec can look worse than a weaker edit delivered correctly.

### No Fresh Review

Judgment degrades during long edits. Without a fresh review, pacing and errors slip through.

## Self-Check

Before approving an edit for publish, verify:

- The cut is tightened for retention, with dead air and tangents removed.
- A rough cut confirmed the structure before polish was applied.
- Every b-roll, graphic, and text element serves comprehension or retention.
- Audio levels are consistent, music ducks under voice, and noise is reduced.
- Accurate captions are present and readable on mobile.
- Color grading supports the mood without drawing attention to itself.
- Export settings match the platform's resolution, aspect ratio, codec, and loudness norms.
- The cut was reviewed fresh, ideally after a break and on the audience's device.
- The first thirty seconds hold attention and deliver the promise.
- Total length matches the value delivered and the audience's tolerance.
