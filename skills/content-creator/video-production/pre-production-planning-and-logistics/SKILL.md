# Pre Production Planning And Logistics

Pre-production is the phase where the cost of a mistake is lowest and the leverage of getting it right is highest. A decision changed on paper costs minutes; the same decision changed on set costs hours, and changed in the edit it costs the whole shoot. The judgment problem is that pre-production is invisible work that feels like delay, so creators routinely under-invest in it, arriving at the shoot with a vague plan and discovering too late that a location does not work, a piece of gear is missing, a release was never signed, or a shot was never scheduled. Most reshoots are not failures of filming; they are failures of planning that filming merely exposes.

The harm of weak pre-production is measured in reshoots, missed shots, blown budgets, and damaged relationships. A shoot without a shot list wastes the crew's time deciding coverage on the day. A shoot without confirmed access arrives at a locked door. A shoot without releases cannot use the footage commercially. A shoot without a contingency plan collapses when weather, talent, or gear fails. This skill helps the agent separate what must be locked before filming from what can stay flexible, build checklists that catch the failures that actually happen, and treat logistics as a production decision rather than clerical overhead.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish What Must Be Locked From What Can Stay Flexible

Not everything needs to be locked before filming, but the things that do must be locked, because improvising them on the day is expensive or impossible. Sort every decision into locked, constrained, and flexible.

Lock before filming:

- locations and confirmed access, including keys, codes, and permissions;
- talent availability and call times;
- releases and permits, anything that affects whether footage can be used;
- core equipment needed to capture the essential shots;
- the essential shot list, the minimum coverage the video cannot exist without;
- budget ceiling and approval authority for overruns.

Constrain but keep adjustable:

- exact shot framing and blocking, which can adapt to the real location;
- b-roll ideas, which can be opportunistic;
- interview question order, which can follow the conversation;
- schedule padding and shot priority, which can flex to how the day goes.

Keep flexible:

- line readings and improvisation within a beat;
- optional and nice-to-have shots;
- aesthetic flourishes that depend on conditions on the day.

The discipline is to lock whatever cannot be recovered on the day, and to preserve flexibility wherever the real conditions will inform a better choice than planning in the abstract.

### Build The Shot List From The Edit Backward

A shot list is not a wish list of nice images. It is the minimum set of shots the edit requires, plus opportunistic extras if time allows. Build it by working backward from the finished video.

For each beat of the script or structure, define:

- the essential shot that must exist for the edit to work;
- the coverage that gives the editor options, such as alternate angles or cutaways;
- the b-roll that illustrates the point and hides cuts;
- the pick-ups and inserts that smooth transitions;
- the safety takes that protect against a flubbed line or bad audio.

Rank shots by priority so that if time runs short, the essential coverage is already captured and only the optional shots are dropped. A shoot that captures the nice-to-haves but misses an essential shot has failed, even if it felt productive.

### Coordinate Talent And Locations With Confirmed Specifics

Vague agreements cause most location and talent failures. Confirm specifics in writing, not in casual messages, and verify them close to the shoot date.

Confirm for locations:

- exact address, entry method, and who grants access;
- dates, times, and any access restrictions;
- power availability, noise conditions, and lighting limitations;
- what can and cannot be moved or dressed;
- whether a permit, insurance, or notification is required;
- a contact who will be present or reachable on the day.

Confirm for talent:

- call time, location, and expected preparation;
- wardrobe, grooming, and any props they must bring;
- release signing, usage rights, and compensation;
- a fallback if the talent is late, unavailable, or unable to perform.

Treat any element that is only verbally agreed as a risk, and have a backup plan for each.

### Run An Equipment And Consumables Check Against The Shot List

Gear failures are common and almost always preventable. Match every piece of equipment to a specific need in the shot list, and verify it works before the shoot, not at the start of it.

Check:

- cameras, lenses, batteries, and memory cards, with spares for each;
- audio, microphones, recorders, cables, wind protection, and headphones to monitor;
- lighting, modifiers, stands, power, and backup sources;
- support, tripods, gimbals, mounts, and rigging;
- monitoring, screens and calibration, so what is captured is actually usable;
- consumables that run out, gaffer tape, adapters, charged batteries, blank cards.

A pre-shoot gear test that records a minute of picture and sound, then reviews it, catches the failures that would otherwise be discovered in the edit.

### Schedule Realistically With Priorities And Contingency

A schedule that assumes everything goes right will break. Build the schedule around priorities and realistic setup times, with contingency for the failures that routinely occur.

Schedule realistically by:

- ordering shots by priority and by location and lighting setup, not by script order;
- budgeting generous setup and reset time, especially for lighting and audio changes;
- front-loading the essential coverage so a late-day problem does not sink the video;
- building in buffer for talent needs, breaks, and re-takes;
- identifying the shots that can be dropped if time runs short.

Have a contingency plan for the most likely failures: weather, talent no-show, gear failure, location access denial, and overrunning time. For each, decide in advance whether the fallback is to reschedule, relocate, simplify, or capture what is possible and adapt the edit.

### Secure Rights, Releases, And Permissions Before The Shoot

Footage that cannot be used commercially is footage wasted. Secure the legal and rights elements before filming, because they are far harder to fix afterward.

Secure:

- talent appearance releases for everyone recognizable on camera;
- location releases and permits, including for public and private spaces;
- music, clip, and asset licenses that cover the intended use;
- insurance or liability coverage where required by the location;
- client or stakeholder approvals for anything that cannot be changed later.

Treat rights as a gate, not paperwork. A shoot should not begin if a release or permit required for the footage to be usable is missing.

## Common Traps

### Treating Pre-Production As Delay Rather Than Leverage

Because pre-production produces no footage, it feels like procrastination. In reality it is the cheapest place to fix problems. Under-investing in it guarantees expensive failures on the day.

### Planning In Script Order Instead Of Setup Order

Scheduling shots in the order they appear in the video wastes hours resetting lighting and audio. Schedule by location and setup, prioritizing essential coverage first.

### Capturing Nice-To-Haves While Missing Essential Shots

A shoot that feels productive because it captured beautiful b-roll but missed a core beat has failed. Rank shots by priority and protect the essential coverage.

### Trusting Verbal Agreements For Locations And Talent

Casual confirmations fail in predictable ways: locked doors, double-booked spaces, no-shows. Confirm specifics in writing and verify close to the date.

### Skipping The Pre-Shoot Gear Test

Discovering that audio is unusable or a lens is soft in the edit is catastrophic. A one-minute record-and-review test before the shoot catches almost all gear failures.

### Assuming The Schedule Will Hold

Schedules built on best-case timing break the moment anything slips. Build in buffer and decide in advance which shots are droppable so a slip does not sink the video.

### Filming Before Rights And Releases Are Secured

Footage captured without releases or permits may be unusable commercially, or may require costly re-shoots or legal risk. Treat rights as a gate that must clear before filming begins.

## Self-Check

Before approving a pre-production plan for a shoot, verify:

- Every decision was sorted into locked, constrained, and flexible, and the things that cannot be recovered on the day are locked.
- The shot list was built backward from the edit, with essential coverage prioritized above nice-to-haves.
- Locations and talent are confirmed with specifics in writing, not verbal agreements, and verified close to the shoot.
- Equipment is matched to shot-list needs and a pre-shoot record-and-review gear test was run.
- The schedule is ordered by setup and priority, not script order, with realistic setup times and buffer.
- Essential coverage is front-loaded so a late-day problem does not sink the video.
- A contingency plan exists for the most likely failures: weather, talent, gear, access, and time.
- Releases, permits, licenses, and approvals required for the footage to be usable are secured before filming begins.
- The plan distinguishes the minimum the video needs from the optional shots that can be dropped.
- No element critical to usable footage depends on a verbal agreement or an untested assumption.
