# Captioning And Transcripts

Captions and transcripts convert spoken and audio content into text, and they serve a far larger audience than many creators assume. They are essential for deaf and hard-of-hearing viewers, but they are also used by the enormous share of audiences who watch with sound off, who are in noisy or quiet environments, who are non-native speakers, and who use text to skim or search. The judgment problem is that captioning is often treated as an optional finishing step or auto-generated afterthought, when in practice bad captions are worse than none: they mislead, distract, and exclude the very audiences they claim to serve. Creators fail by trusting raw auto-captions, by placing captions where they are covered by platform UI, by omitting meaningful non-speech audio, and by producing no transcript at all for audio-first content.

The harm is both an accessibility failure and a reach failure. Good captions expand the audience; bad or missing captions silently shrink it. This skill helps the agent produce accurate, readable, complete captions and transcripts.

## Core Rules

### Treat Captions As Required, Not Optional

Captions are not a bonus for a small accessibility audience; they are core to how most people actually consume video and audio. Planning content without captions is planning to lose a large share of viewers.

Build captions into the workflow:

- budget time for captioning in every video and audio production;
- choose tools and platforms that support caption upload, not only auto-generation;
- caption from the start rather than retrofitting at the end;
- treat caption quality as part of the deliverable, not an afterthought.

If a piece depends on spoken words, those words must be available in text.

### Aim For Verbatim Accuracy, Not Approximation

Auto-captions are a starting point, never a finished product. They routinely mishear names, numbers, technical terms, and context, and those errors can change meaning or cause embarrassment. The standard is verbatim accuracy after human review.

Verify accuracy by:

- reviewing auto-generated captions line by line against the audio;
- correcting names, proper nouns, numbers, and technical terms explicitly;
- preserving meaning where exact wording is impossible, never inventing words;
- re-checking after any edit to the video or audio.

A caption that is 95 percent accurate still fails the 5 percent of moments that matter most.

### Place And Style Captions For Readability

Even accurate captions fail if they are unreadable. Placement, size, timing, and contrast all determine whether captions actually communicate.

Ensure readable caption presentation:

- place captions in the safe zone, away from platform UI overlays;
- size text to remain legible on small screens;
- ensure strong contrast between text and background;
- break lines at natural phrasing boundaries, not mid-word;
- time captions to appear and disappear with the speech.

Captions that fight the platform UI or appear too fast to read are functionally useless.

### Represent Non-Speech Audio That Carries Meaning

Captions that transcribe only spoken words miss sound that conveys information, mood, or context. For deaf and hard-of-hearing audiences, meaningful non-speech audio must also be represented.

Caption meaningful sound:

- identify speakers when ambiguous, using labels or color;
- note relevant sound effects, such as a doorbell or alarm;
- indicate music and its mood where it changes the meaning of a scene;
- mark silence or pause only when it is communicative.

The goal is equivalent access to the experience, not just the words.

### Produce A Full Transcript For Audio And Long-Form Content

A transcript is the complete text version of audio or video content, and it serves search, skim-reading, accessibility, and repurposing. For audio-first content like podcasts, a transcript is often as important as the recording itself.

Provide transcripts:

- for all audio-first content, where captions are not applicable;
- for long-form video where viewers may prefer to skim or search;
- in a clean, readable format with speaker labels and paragraph breaks;
- alongside the media, not buried or omitted.

Transcripts multiply the value of content by making it searchable, quotable, and accessible.

### Time Captions Correctly

Caption timing affects comprehension. Captions that appear too late, disappear too fast, or linger after the speech has moved on break the viewing experience.

Apply timing discipline:

- sync caption appearance to the start of speech;
- keep captions on screen long enough to read at a normal pace;
- avoid overlapping captions from different speakers without clear separation;
- remove captions promptly when speech ends.

Well-timed captions feel invisible; poorly timed ones constantly pull attention.

### Handle Speaker Identification Clearly

In multi-speaker content, viewers need to know who is speaking. Without clear identification, captions become a confusing stream of unattributed text.

Identify speakers effectively:

- use speaker labels when the speaker changes or is off-screen;
- use consistent labels or colors per speaker throughout;
- clarify when speech is overlapping, off-screen, or in a different language;
- avoid over-labeling single-speaker content, which adds clutter.

Clarity about who is speaking is part of accuracy, not decoration.

### Respect Language, Dialect, And Identity In Captions

How speech is transcribed carries social meaning. Cleaning up accents, dialects, or identity-related language can erase the speaker's voice, while careless transcription can misrepresent them.

Transcribe respectfully:

- preserve a speaker's actual words and patterns unless clarity requires otherwise;
- represent names and identity terms correctly and consistently;
- avoid imposing standard grammar that changes the speaker's voice;
- mark language switches and provide translation where helpful.

Captions should represent the speaker, not sanitize them.

## Common Traps

### Trusting Raw Auto-Captions

Auto-captions mishear names, numbers, and terms and change meaning. Always review and correct.

### Captions Hidden By Platform UI

Captions placed under overlays are unreadable. Keep them in the safe zone.

### Omitting Meaningful Non-Speech Sound

Sound effects and music carry meaning that word-only captions miss. Represent them.

### No Transcript For Audio Content

Audio without a transcript excludes search, skim, and accessibility users. Always provide one.

### Captions That Move Too Fast To Read

Captions synced poorly or removed too quickly cannot be read. Time them to natural reading pace.

### Unclear Speaker Identification

Multi-speaker captions without labels become confusing. Identify speakers consistently.

### Sanitizing Accent Or Identity In Transcription

Cleaning up a speaker's voice erases them. Transcribe accurately and respectfully.

## Self-Check

Before approving captions or a transcript, verify:

- Captions were planned as part of the deliverable, not added as an afterthought.
- Auto-generated captions were reviewed and corrected line by line for verbatim accuracy.
- Names, numbers, proper nouns, and technical terms are transcribed correctly.
- Captions sit in the safe zone, away from platform UI, and are legible at small size.
- Lines break at natural phrasing boundaries, not mid-word.
- Meaningful non-speech audio, such as sound effects and music mood, is represented.
- A full transcript exists for audio-first and long-form content.
- Caption timing matches speech and stays on screen long enough to read.
- Speakers are clearly identified in multi-speaker content.
- The transcription preserves the speaker's actual words, accent, and identity rather than sanitizing them.
