# Platform Adaptation

Platform adaptation is the practice of taking one piece of content and making it work, genuinely work, on another platform. The judgment problem is that creators treat adaptation as resizing, taking a horizontal video and cropping it vertical, or pasting a blog post into a caption, when in fact each platform has its own norms, attention patterns, and audience expectations. Content that is merely resized rather than genuinely adapted performs like a foreign object in the feed and underperforms, while native-feeling content rewards the creator with reach and retention.

The harm is wasted effort and damaged reach. A repurposed piece that ignores platform norms gets suppressed by the algorithm and ignored by users. Cross-posting everything everywhere dilutes quality and signals laziness. Adapting nothing leaves value on the table, since one strong idea can serve multiple audiences. This skill helps the agent decide what to adapt versus create natively, restructure content for each platform's logic, and avoid the lazy resizing that passes for adaptation but produces poor results.

## Core Rules

### Adapt The Logic, Not Just The Dimensions

True adaptation changes how the content is structured for the platform, not just its aspect ratio. Each platform has a logic of attention that the content must match.

Adapt the logic by:

- rewriting hooks for each platform's first-second demand;
- changing length to match the platform's tolerance;
- adjusting pacing to the platform's rhythm;
- moving the payoff to where the platform's audience expects it;
- changing the call to action to fit the platform's native actions.

A vertical crop of a horizontal video is resized, not adapted. Adaptation reworks the piece so it feels native where it lands.

### Decide What To Adapt Versus Create Natively

Not every piece belongs on every platform. Some ideas adapt well across channels, others only work natively on one. Decide deliberately rather than cross-posting everything.

Adapt when:

- the core idea is strong and platform-agnostic;
- the audience overlaps across platforms;
- the format can be restructured without losing the value;
- the effort to adapt is justified by the reach gained.

Create natively when:

- the idea depends on a platform-specific feature or culture;
- the audience on a platform expects a different kind of value;
- adaptation would dilute or distort the original;
- the platform's norms conflict with the content's tone.

Forcing every piece onto every platform produces weak everywhere content. Choose where each idea belongs.

### Match The Hook To The Platform's First Seconds

Each platform decides survival in a different window. The hook must be rebuilt for each platform's attention pattern, not copied across.

Match hooks by:

- leading with visual and on-screen text for sound-off short-form;
- using the title-thumbnail combination for long-form video;
- writing a strong subject line and preview for newsletters;
- opening with a scroll-stopping line for feed posts;
- using a cold open for podcast-style audio.

A hook calibrated for one platform usually underperforms elsewhere. Rebuild the opening for each surface.

### Respect Each Platform's Norms And Culture

Each platform has unwritten norms, in-jokes, expectations, and taboos. Content that violates them reads as foreign and gets rejected, even if it is high quality elsewhere.

Respect norms by:

- learning what each platform's audience expects and rewards;
- matching tone to the platform's culture;
- avoiding formats or tactics that read as out of place;
- not importing one platform's conventions wholesale into another;
- observing what native creators do well on each platform.

A LinkedIn-style post on TikTok, or a TikTok-style post on LinkedIn, often flops not because the content is bad but because it violates platform culture.

### Sequence Adaptation Around A Source Piece

Efficient adaptation builds outward from a strong source piece rather than creating each platform version independently. Plan the source and its derivatives together.

Sequence by:

- starting with the deepest, most complete version of the idea;
- identifying which elements adapt to each platform;
- producing derivatives in priority order by reach and effort;
- ensuring each derivative stands alone, not as a fragment;
- tracking which adaptations perform to inform future sequencing.

A long-form source can yield a short clip, a carousel, a thread, a newsletter section, and a graphic, each adapted natively rather than resized.

### Preserve The Core Value Across Adaptations

While the form changes across platforms, the core value the piece delivers should remain consistent. Adaptation should not distort the message or promise.

Preserve value by:

- keeping the central insight or benefit intact;
- ensuring each adaptation delivers the promise in its own form;
- not exaggerating the source to fit a platform's reward for drama;
- maintaining the creator's voice and point of view across versions.

Adaptation changes the package, not the substance. If the substance shifts, it is a different piece, not an adaptation.

### Avoid Lazy Cross-Posting That Signals Low Effort

Audiences detect and resent content that was obviously cross-posted without care. Watermarks from other platforms, wrong aspect ratios, and mismatched tone all signal low effort and reduce reach.

Avoid laziness by:

- removing other platforms' watermarks and branding;
- matching aspect ratio and composition to the target platform;
- rewriting captions and hooks rather than pasting;
- checking that references make sense on the target platform;
- investing enough to make each version feel native.

The small effort to de-lazify an adaptation often determines whether it reaches anyone.

### Measure Each Platform Separately

Performance varies by platform, and adaptation quality shows up in platform-specific metrics. Measure each surface on its own terms.

Measure by:

- tracking retention and engagement per platform;
- comparing adaptation quality against native creators there;
- identifying which platforms reward which adaptations;
- cutting platforms where adapted content consistently underperforms;
- doubling down on platforms where adaptation works well.

Not every platform will reward every adaptation. Let the data decide where to invest adaptation effort.

## Common Traps

### Resizing Instead Of Adapting

Cropping or reformatting without restructuring produces content that feels foreign and underperforms.

### Cross-Posting Everything Everywhere

Forcing every piece onto every platform dilutes quality and signals laziness where it does not fit.

### Copying Hooks Across Platforms

Hooks calibrated for one platform's attention window usually fail elsewhere. Rebuild openings per surface.

### Violating Platform Culture

Content that ignores a platform's norms gets rejected regardless of its quality on its native platform.

### Distorting The Core Value

Exaggerating or shifting the message to fit a platform's rewards corrupts the piece and erodes trust.

### Leaving Cross-Post Evidence

Watermarks, wrong ratios, and mismatched captions signal low effort and reduce reach.

### Measuring All Platforms Together

Aggregating metrics hides which platforms reward adaptation and which do not. Measure each surface separately.

## Self-Check

Before approving a platform adaptation plan, verify:

- The adaptation reworks the logic for each platform, not just the dimensions.
- A deliberate decision was made for each piece, adapt versus create natively.
- Hooks are rebuilt for each platform's first-second attention pattern.
- The adaptation respects each platform's norms, culture, and expectations.
- Adaptations are sequenced around a strong source piece with native derivatives.
- The core value and promise remain consistent across all versions.
- No lazy cross-posting evidence, such as watermarks or mismatched captions, remains.
- Each platform is measured separately for retention and engagement.
- Platforms where adaptation consistently underperforms are deprioritized.
- The effort to adapt is justified by the reach and value gained on each platform.
