# Community Moderation And Norms

Moderation is the immune system of a community, and like an immune system it must be strong enough to protect and calibrated enough not to attack the body it defends. The judgment problem is that creators moderate reactively, swinging between neglect, where toxicity takes root, and overreaction, where the space feels policed and members leave. Good moderation is mostly invisible, it sets clear norms, applies them consistently, and intervenes early and proportionately, while bad moderation is erratic, personal, and visible, eroding the trust that makes members feel safe enough to participate.

The harm is a community that becomes unsafe or unwelcoming. Inconsistent moderation teaches members the rules do not mean what they say. Personal or public moderation creates drama that consumes the community. Under-moderation lets bad actors drive out good members, the classic death spiral where the tolerant space becomes intolerant because it tolerated the intolerant. This skill helps the agent write clear norms, apply them consistently and proportionately, handle conflict without feeding it, and balance safety against the openness that lets a community breathe.

## Core Rules

### Write Clear, Public, And Actionable Guidelines

Norms that live in the moderator's head cannot be followed or trusted. Write guidelines that members can read, understand, and predict.

Write guidelines by:

- stating explicitly what is welcome and what is not allowed;
- defining terms rather than relying on vague standards like be nice;
- explaining the consequences for violations, not just the rules;
- making the guidelines easy to find and reference;
- keeping them short enough to read and specific enough to apply.

Guidelines that are clear and public let members self-moderate and trust the system.

### Apply Rules Consistently And Impersonally

Inconsistent or personal moderation destroys trust faster than no moderation. Apply rules the same way regardless of who breaks them.

Apply consistently by:

- enforcing rules against friends, popular members, and strangers alike;
- separating the rule violation from the person committing it;
- avoiding public vendettas or favoritism;
- documenting decisions so they form a consistent precedent;
- recognizing that perceived inconsistency is as damaging as real inconsistency.

Members who see rules applied unevenly conclude the rules are arbitrary and stop trusting them.

### Intervene Early And Proportionately

Small problems left alone become large problems. Intervene early, but match the response to the severity and the pattern.

Intervene by:

- addressing low-level issues before they escalate;
- escalating responses with repeated or severe violations, from warning to mute to ban;
- not nuking a minor first offense with a ban;
- not ignoring a major violation with a warning;
- keeping a record so escalation reflects a pattern, not a mood.

Proportionate early action prevents the crises that force drastic late action.

### Handle Conflict Without Feeding It

Public conflict consumes communities, and how moderators handle it either defuses or amplifies it. Handle conflict to reduce heat, not to win it.

Handle conflict by:

- moving disputes to private channels where possible;
- not taking sides publicly in interpersonal disputes;
- focusing on behavior and rules, not on who is right;
- de-escalating rather than punishing in the heat of the moment;
- recognizing when moderator engagement itself becomes the drama.

A moderator who fights in public becomes part of the conflict they are meant to resolve.

### Protect Members, Especially The Vulnerable

The purpose of moderation is to keep the space safe enough for members to participate, especially those most likely to be driven out. Protect them deliberately.

Protect by:

- acting quickly on harassment, bigotry, and targeted attacks;
- recognizing that the absence of complaints does not mean the absence of harm;
- weighing the loss of a harmed member against the loss of a removed one;
- not tolerating bad actors because they are entertaining or popular;
- making safety a precondition for the participation that builds community.

A community that protects its loudest abusers loses its quiet contributors.

### Balance Safety With Openness And Dissent

Over-moderation is its own failure mode, producing a sterile space where members self-censor and leave. Balance safety against the openness and disagreement that make a community alive.

Balance by:

- distinguishing harmful behavior from unpopular opinion;
- allowing respectful dissent and criticism, including of the creator;
- not moderating to suppress discomfort or challenge;
- keeping rules focused on harm, not on tone policing;
- recognizing when safety arguments become cover for control.

A safe community that allows no disagreement is a waiting room, not a community.

## Common Traps

### Vague Or Unspoken Guidelines

Rules that are not written clearly cannot be followed consistently and erode trust when enforced subjectively.

### Inconsistent Or Personal Enforcement

Applying rules differently to different members teaches the community that the rules are arbitrary.

### Reactive Crisis Moderation

Ignoring problems until they explode forces drastic action that feels disproportionate and creates drama.

### Public Conflict And Vendettas

Moderators who fight disputes in public become part of the conflict and amplify the drama.

### Tolerating Popular Bad Actors

Keeping toxic but entertaining or popular members drives out the quiet contributors who sustain the community.

### Over-Moderating Into Sterility

Suppressing all dissent and discomfort in the name of safety produces a lifeless space where members self-censor and leave.

### Under-Moderating Until Decay

Neglecting moderation lets toxicity take root until good members leave and the community collapses.

## Self-Check

- Community guidelines are written, public, specific, and include consequences.
- Rules are applied consistently and impersonally, regardless of who violates them.
- Moderation intervenes early and escalates proportionately with patterns.
- Conflict is handled to reduce heat, often privately, without moderators taking public sides.
- Vulnerable members are protected, and harm is acted on even without complaints.
- Respectful dissent and criticism, including of the creator, are allowed.
- Moderation focuses on harmful behavior rather than tone policing or discomfort.
- Decisions are documented so they form a consistent precedent.
- The balance between safety and openness is consciously maintained.
- Popular or entertaining bad actors are not exempt from the rules.
