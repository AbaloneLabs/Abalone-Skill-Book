# Community Space Design

A community space is not a feature you switch on; it is an environment you design, and most are designed badly. The judgment problem is that creators pick a platform, open it to everyone, create a dozen channels, and wait for community to happen, when in fact community emerges from constraints, structure, and culture that must be deliberately built. A poorly designed space becomes either a ghost town where no one talks because there is no reason to, or a chaotic room where no one stays because there is too much noise. The space itself, its size, access, structure, and norms, determines which one it becomes.

The harm is a community that never takes root. An open, unstructured space fills with drive-by members who never return and drowns the few genuine contributors. An over-segmented space fragments conversation so nothing reaches critical mass. A space with no cultural design defaults to whoever shows up first and loudest. This skill helps the agent choose the right platform and access model, structure channels and roles to concentrate rather than scatter attention, design for belonging at a human scale, and seed the culture so the space becomes a community rather than a chat log.

## Core Rules

### Match The Platform To The Community's Purpose

Different platforms produce different community behavior, and the choice should follow the community's purpose, not the creator's familiarity. A mismatched platform strangles the community from the start.

Match platform by:

- choosing real-time chat for high-energy, event-driven communities;
- choosing threaded forums for depth, searchability, and asynchronous discussion;
- considering whether the community needs permanence, discoverability, or intimacy;
- weighing moderation tooling and role systems, not just features;
- avoiding a platform chosen because it is popular rather than because it fits.

The platform shapes what kind of conversation is even possible.

### Choose Access And Size To Match The Stage

Community health depends heavily on size and access. A space that is too open drowns; a space that is too closed never starts. Calibrate access to the community's stage.

Calibrate access by:

- starting smaller and more curated to build density and culture;
- gating access to filter for genuine interest and reduce drive-by noise;
- avoiding opening to the full audience before the culture can absorb them;
- scaling deliberately rather than all at once;
- recognizing that intimacy dies above a certain size without structure.

A community of two hundred engaged members beats a Discord of twenty thousand ghosts.

### Structure Channels To Concentrate Attention

Channel structure determines whether conversation reaches critical mass or scatters into silence. Over-segmentation kills communities by fragmenting the energy that makes them feel alive.

Structure well by:

- starting with fewer channels and adding only when conversation demands it;
- concentrating conversation where it can reach critical mass;
- avoiding a channel for every topic before the members exist to fill them;
- using purpose-driven channels tied to real activities, not abstract categories;
- pruning dead channels that signal the community is empty.

Empty channels are evidence of over-design; full ones are evidence of life.

### Design Roles And Permissions To Distribute Leadership

A community that depends entirely on the creator cannot scale and dies when the creator steps away. Design roles so leadership and care are distributed.

Distribute leadership by:

- creating roles for moderators, welcomers, and topic leads;
- giving trusted members real responsibility, not just titles;
- designing permissions that let members contribute without enabling abuse;
- recognizing and rewarding members who carry the community;
- building a structure that survives the creator's absence.

A community owned by its members outlasts one owned only by the creator.

### Design For Belonging At Human Scale

People join communities for belonging, and belonging requires being seen. Design the space so members can be recognized and known, not lost in a crowd.

Design belonging by:

- creating introductions and rituals that make new members visible;
- keeping subgroups small enough that members recognize each other;
- surfacing member contributions and milestones;
- designing spaces for both broad participation and intimate connection;
- treating every member as a person, not a metric.

Members who feel known stay; members who feel anonymous leave.

### Seed The Culture Deliberately

A new community has no culture until one is established, and if the creator does not seed it, the first loud voices will. Culture is built early and deliberately.

Seed culture by:

- modeling the behavior you want through your own participation;
- establishing norms, rituals, and tone from the first day;
- welcoming and amplifying the contributions that represent the culture;
- intervening early when behavior drifts from the intended culture;
- documenting what the community is for and how it behaves.

Culture is easiest to set at the start and hardest to change once it calcifies.

## Common Traps

### Opening To Everyone At Once

Inviting the full audience before the culture can absorb them drowns the early community in noise and strangers.

### Over-Segmenting Channels

Creating a channel for every conceivable topic fragments conversation so nothing reaches critical mass.

### Choosing A Platform For Familiarity

Picking a tool the creator knows rather than one that fits the community's purpose strangles the community from the start.

### Depending Only On The Creator

A community that requires the creator's constant presence cannot scale and collapses when they step away.

### Letting The First Loud Voices Set Culture

Failing to seed norms early lets whoever arrives first and loudest define what the community becomes.

### Ignoring Size And Density

Treating size as pure growth, without considering intimacy and density, produces a space that feels empty even when full.

### Dead Channels And Ghost Towns

Leaving abandoned channels and inactive spaces visible signals the community is lifeless and discourages participation.

## Self-Check

- The platform was chosen to match the community's purpose, not the creator's familiarity.
- Access and size are calibrated to the community's stage, starting smaller and more curated.
- Channels are structured to concentrate attention and reach critical mass, not scatter it.
- Roles and permissions distribute leadership so the community is not solely creator-dependent.
- The space is designed for belonging at a human scale where members can be known.
- Culture was seeded deliberately from the first day through modeling, norms, and rituals.
- New members are made visible through introductions and welcoming rituals.
- Dead channels are pruned so the space does not signal emptiness.
- Growth is scaled deliberately rather than opening to the full audience at once.
- The community could continue to function if the creator stepped away temporarily.
