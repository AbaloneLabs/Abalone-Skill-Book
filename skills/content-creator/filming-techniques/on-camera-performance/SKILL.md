# On-Camera Performance

Most on-camera failure is performance failure, not technical failure. A sharp image and clean audio cannot rescue a stiff, rushed, or inauthentic delivery. The judgment problem is that creators treat performance as either trivial, just be yourself, or impossible, and end up either over-rehearsed and wooden or under-prepared and rambling. Audiences sense authenticity faster than they process words, so a performance that feels performed undermines trust no matter how good the script is.

This skill helps the agent create the conditions for natural, clear, energetic delivery, prepare scripts and creators for the camera, and review takes for the qualities that actually retain viewers.

## Core Rules

### Write Scripts To Be Spoken, Not Read

Scripts written for the page fail in the mouth. Long sentences, formal phrasing, and written transitions sound stiff when spoken and force the performer to choose between accuracy and naturalism.

Write for speech by:

- using short sentences and conversational connectors;
- reading every line aloud and cutting what trips the tongue;
- allowing paraphrase so the performer can own the words;
- replacing written phrases with spoken ones, such as contractions and simple verbs.

If a line cannot be said naturally, it will not be performed naturally.

### Aim For Naturalism Over Word-Perfect Accuracy

Audiences forgive a paraphrased line and punish a wooden one. The goal is to land the meaning and the energy, not to recite the script verbatim. Perfection in wording usually costs presence.

Favor naturalism by:

- accepting restarts and using them to loosen up;
- letting the performer rephrase within the meaning;
- favoring energy and clarity over exact fidelity;
- keeping the best-feeling take even if a word differs from the script.

A natural imperfect take almost always beats a stiff perfect one.

### Deliver To A Person, Not A Lens

Talking to a glass lens feels unnatural and produces a flat, distant delivery. Performance improves when the performer has a human target for their energy and eyeline.

Create a human target by:

- having someone stand just off-camera for eyeline and reaction;
- imagining a specific viewer or friend when alone;
- treating the lens as a person's eyes rather than a machine;
- matching eyeline to the framing, direct for intimacy, off-axis for conversation.

Energy and warmth rise when the delivery has a target.

### Manage Energy And Pace Deliberately

On camera, most performers lose about thirty percent of their energy and speed up under nerves. A performance that feels lively in the room often reads flat or rushed on screen. Compensate on purpose.

Manage energy and pace by:

- performing slightly larger and warmer than feels natural;
- slowing down more than feels comfortable, especially on key lines;
- using breath and pauses to let points land;
- warming up voice and body before recording to raise baseline energy.

Flat delivery is usually under-energy, not under-talent.

### Break Long Passages Into Re-Recordable Segments

Long uninterrupted takes pile up small errors and force full restarts, which drain energy and increase stiffness. Segmenting the script makes recovery cheap and keeps takes fresh.

Segment by:

- breaking the script into beats or sentences that can be recorded alone;
- recording top-and-tail options for each segment;
- using cutaways to hide the seams between segments;
- keeping a consistent eyeline and energy across segments so they cut.

Short segments protect energy and give the editor clean options.

### Prepare The Performer Before Recording

Performance quality drops sharply under stress, fatigue, and cold starts. Preparation is not optional polish, it is what makes a take usable.

Prepare by:

- doing practice takes and read-throughs before the real take;
- warming up voice, face, and body;
- hydrating and resting before a shoot;
- reviewing the first few takes and adjusting delivery before continuing.

A warmed-up performer sounds like a different person from a cold one.

### Direct Toward A Specific Feeling, Not Vague Notes

Vague direction, such as be more energetic or sound natural, rarely helps because the performer cannot translate it into action. Direction works when it names a specific, playable adjustment.

Direct with specifics by:

- naming the emotion or intention of a beat, such as excited, reassuring, curious;
- giving an action rather than an adjective, such as explain it to a friend;
- demonstrating the energy or pace you want;
- praising what worked rather than only correcting what did not.

Playable notes produce usable takes. Abstract notes produce frustration.

### Match Performance To The Format And Audience

Performance norms differ by format. A long-form explainer wants steady authority, a short-form hook wants high energy in the first second, and a personal story wants warmth and vulnerability. Matching the register to the format is what makes delivery feel right.

Calibrate by:

- front-loading energy and clarity for short-form hooks;
- sustaining steady presence and structure for long-form;
- lowering the register and slowing pace for credibility and trust;
- raising energy and pace for entertainment and excitement.

The same creator can perform several registers. Choose the one the format needs.

## Common Traps

### Reading The Script Verbatim And Stiffly

Word-perfect but lifeless delivery kills trust. When accuracy is prized over presence, the performance feels like a recitation rather than communication.

### Under-Energizing Because It Feels Enough In The Room

Nerves and the camera both drain energy, so a take that feels lively in person often reads flat on screen. Without deliberate compensation, every take undershoots.

### Rushing Through Key Lines

Under nerves, performers speed up and swallow the lines that matter most. Rushed delivery prevents points from landing and signals discomfort.

### Talking To The Lens As A Machine

Delivering to glass rather than a person produces distant, flat energy. The audience feels the absence of a human target without knowing why.

### Long Unbroken Takes That Force Full Restarts

Recording whole scripts in one go means every small error triggers a full restart, which drains energy and increases stiffness over the session.

### Cold Starts Without Warmup

Recording without warming up voice and body produces thin, tight delivery in the early takes. The first takes are often the worst and sometimes the only ones used.

### Vague Direction That Cannot Be Played

Notes like be more natural give the performer nothing to act on. Unplayable direction wastes takes and erodes confidence.

### Mismatched Register For The Format

A credibility-driven channel performed with entertainment energy, or a short-form hook delivered with long-form calm, feels wrong to the audience even when the words are good.

## Self-Check

- The script was written to be spoken aloud and tripping phrases were cut.
- Naturalism and energy were prioritized over word-perfect accuracy.
- The delivery has a human target for eyeline and energy rather than talking to glass.
- Energy was deliberately raised and pace deliberately slowed to compensate for the camera.
- Long passages were broken into re-recordable segments with consistent eyeline.
- The performer warmed up voice and body and did practice takes before recording.
- Direction named specific playable adjustments rather than vague adjectives.
- The performance register matches the format, long-form calm versus short-form energy.
- Key lines land with pauses and emphasis rather than being rushed.
- The chosen takes favor presence and clarity over exact script fidelity.
