# Audience Positioning

Audience positioning is the deliberate choice of which specific people a creator's content is built to serve, and it is distinct from audience research. Audience research describes who currently watches; audience positioning decides who the content should be optimized for, and accepts that optimizing for one audience means de-optimizing for others. The harm this skill prevents is content that tries to serve everyone, lands with no one, attracts an audience the creator does not want, or drifts toward whoever happens to engage rather than who the creator set out to help.

Use this skill when launching a channel, when an audience has formed but does not match the creator's intent, when planning a format change, and when evaluating whether comments and engagement represent the target audience or a misleading vocal minority.

## Core Rules

### Write An Explicit Positioning Statement

Force the audience into a single positioning statement that names the situation, not just the person. A strong statement has the shape: "This content is for [specific people] who are [in a specific situation] and need [a specific outcome], and it is not for [adjacent people] even though they may also watch."

The exclusion clause matters. Stating who the content is not for forces honesty about tradeoffs. "For beginner investors" is weak. "For first-time index fund investors in their first year, who are anxious about market drops and need calm decision frameworks, and not for experienced traders looking for edge" is actionable and testable.

### Position By Situation And Sophistication, Not Demographics

Demographics (age, gender, location, income) rarely predict why someone gives attention or what they need. Position by the situation the audience is in and their level of sophistication in the topic.

Situation dimensions that matter:

- what stage they are at (just starting, mid-journey, advanced, recovering from a setback);
- what decision or problem is in front of them right now;
- what they already know and what they misunderstand;
- what emotional state they bring (anxious, bored, ambitious, overwhelmed, skeptical);
- what constraints they operate under (time, money, access, ability, language);
- what content they have already tried and why it failed them.

Sophistication determines depth. Content pitched at the wrong sophistication level either bores the advanced audience or loses the beginner. Choose the primary sophistication level deliberately and signal it in the content so the wrong audience self-selects out.

### Distinguish Target Audience From Actual Audience

Once a channel publishes, two audiences exist: the audience the creator intends to serve (target) and the audience that actually engages (actual). These diverge over time, and the divergence is a strategic signal, not noise.

Periodically compare:

- who comments, replies, and converts (actual);
- who the creator believes the content is for (target);
- whether the gap is healthy (the content attracts a related audience the creator is happy to serve) or unhealthy (the content attracts an audience that pressures the creator toward content they do not want to make).

Do not automatically optimize for the actual audience if it has drifted from the target. Decide consciously whether to follow the audience that showed up or to reposition back toward the intended audience.

### Segment The Audience By Job, Not By Persona

When a channel grows, it often serves several audience slices. Segment by the job each slice hires the content to do, not by invented personas with names and hobbies.

Common job-based segments:

- the beginner who needs orientation and confidence;
- the practitioner who needs depth and shortcuts;
- the curious outsider who needs translation and context;
- the returning user who needs accountability or updates;
- the skeptic who needs evidence and honesty.

Different pieces can serve different segments, but each piece should know which segment it is primarily for. Content that tries to serve all segments at once tends to satisfy none.

### Make The Audience Visible In The Content

Positioning is reinforced when the audience recognizes itself. Use the audience's own language for their problem, name their situation explicitly in hooks and intros, and reference the constraints they actually face. When the target audience reads or watches and thinks "this is exactly me," positioning is working.

Avoid generic audience nods ("hey everyone," "for all you creatives out there") that signal the content is for anyone and therefore for no one in particular.

### Accept Positioning Tradeoffs Deliberately

Every positioning choice closes doors. Choosing a sophisticated audience limits reach but builds depth and trust. Choosing a beginner audience maximizes reach but caps how deep any single piece can go. Choosing a narrow situation makes the content recognizable but limits the total addressable audience.

Make these tradeoffs consciously and document the reasoning, so that later pressure to "broaden the audience" can be evaluated against the original intent rather than reacted to anxiously.

### Handle Misaligned Audience Pressure

When the actual audience demands content outside the creator's positioning, the creator faces a recurring strategic test. Common pressures include demands for hotter takes, more personal disclosure, advice outside the creator's competence, or content that conflicts with the creator's values.

Decide in advance how to handle each:

- redirect to a creator better suited to that need;
- acknowledge the interest without producing the content;
- produce a version that stays within positioning and competence;
- decline explicitly and explain why.

For demands that touch health, legal, financial, mental health, or safety advice, treat the pressure as a risk signal. Individualized advice in these domains can cause harm and create liability. Escalate by narrowing to general education, disclosing limits, and referring the audience to qualified professionals rather than improvising authority.

## Common Traps

### Optimizing For The Vocal Minority

A small number of loud commenters can distort the creator's sense of who the audience is. The vocal minority may not represent the silent majority who save, share, and convert. Check behavioral signals across the whole audience before repositioning.

### Letting The Algorithm Define The Audience

Algorithms amplify whatever retains attention, which may be outrage, novelty, or the wrong audience. A channel that chases algorithmic reach can end up with an audience the creator did not choose and does not want to serve. Positioning, not the algorithm, should decide the audience.

### Confusing Reach With Right Audience

Large reach from the wrong audience is worse than modest reach from the right audience. The wrong audience does not convert, does not stay, and pressures the content in unhelpful directions. Measure audience fit, not just size.

### Sliding Toward The Easiest Audience

It is tempting to optimize for the audience that engages most easily, which is often the broadest, least committed slice. Over time this erodes positioning and leaves the dedicated core audience underserved. Protect the core even when the broad audience is louder.

### Inheriting An Audience From A Collaboration Or Trend

A viral moment or collaboration can import an audience that does not match the creator's positioning. Imported audiences often leave when the content returns to normal, and their presence can mislead the creator into repositioning for people who were never the target. Treat imported audience spikes as temporary unless the new audience proves durable and aligned.

### Over-Disclosing To Build Intimacy

Some audiences reward personal disclosure, which can pressure the creator toward sharing more than is safe or healthy. Positioning that depends on escalating personal disclosure is fragile and can harm the creator's privacy, relationships, and mental health. Set disclosure boundaries as part of positioning.

## Self-Check

Before approving an audience positioning decision, verify:

- A positioning statement exists that names the specific people, situation, and outcome, plus an explicit exclusion.
- Positioning is by situation and sophistication, not demographics alone.
- The target audience and the actual audience have been compared, and any gap is a conscious decision.
- Audience segments are defined by job, not invented personas.
- The content uses the audience's own language and references their real constraints.
- Positioning tradeoffs (reach vs depth, breadth vs recognition) have been made deliberately and documented.
- A plan exists for handling audience pressure for content outside positioning.
- For high-risk domains, individualized advice is avoided in favor of education, disclosure, and professional referral.
- The vocal minority has not been mistaken for the whole audience.
- Reach is being evaluated against audience fit, not size alone.
