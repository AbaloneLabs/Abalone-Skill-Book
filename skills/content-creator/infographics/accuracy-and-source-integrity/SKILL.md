# Accuracy And Source Integrity

An infographic turns a claim into a visual object that looks authoritative, which makes accuracy a much heavier obligation than in ordinary prose. A wrong sentence can be corrected; a wrong chart screenshotted and shared lives forever. The judgment problem is that creators often treat sourcing as a final decoration, slapping a generic institution name on the corner, when in fact source integrity is the entire foundation of whether the graphic should exist. The most common failures are outdated figures presented as current, secondary sources that misrepresent the primary data, statistics stripped of the caveats that made them true, and visuals that imply a precision the underlying data does not have.

The harm is serious and lasting. Inaccurate infographics mislead audiences, damage the creator's credibility when debunked, and can cause real-world harm when they concern health, money, or safety. This skill helps the agent verify figures, cite sources honestly, and refuse to publish claims that are not solid enough.

## Core Rules

### Trace Every Figure To A Primary Source

A number found in an article that cites a report that cites a study is not a source; it is a game of telephone. Each layer of summary can distort, and the only way to know what a figure actually means is to reach the primary source.

For every figure, trace to origin:

- find the original study, dataset, or official release;
- read the methodology and the exact wording of the finding;
- confirm the number matches what the primary source actually says;
- note the sample, timeframe, and limitations stated in the primary source.

If the primary source cannot be found or accessed, the figure is not verifiable and should not be published as fact.

### Check Recency And Relevance

Data ages. A figure that was accurate five years ago may no longer hold, and presenting it without a date implies it is current. Relevance also matters: a global average may not support a claim about a specific country.

Verify recency and relevance:

- confirm the data's collection date, not just the publication date of the article citing it;
- check whether newer data exists and changes the picture;
- ensure the geographic and demographic scope matches the claim;
- avoid substituting an available-but-imperfect figure for the right one.

A convenient old number is not a substitute for the correct current one.

### Preserve The Caveats That Make A Statistic True

Many statistics are true only under specific conditions: a defined population, a particular method, a stated confidence interval. Stripping those caveats to make a cleaner graphic turns a careful finding into a misleading claim.

Carry forward essential caveats:

- the population or sample the figure describes;
- the margin of error or confidence interval where relevant;
- any conditions or limitations the original authors stated;
- whether the finding is correlational or causal.

If a caveat is too complex for the graphic, the graphic may be too simple for the claim. Simplify the visual, not the truth.

### Cite Sources Specifically And Verifiably

A source line that says a vague institution name is nearly useless. Real source integrity means a reader can find the exact figure themselves.

Cite with enough specificity to verify:

- name the specific report, dataset, or study, not just the organization;
- include the publication or collection date;
- provide a direct link or a precise reference where possible;
- distinguish primary from secondary sources in the citation.

The test is whether a skeptical reader could locate the exact number in the source within a few minutes.

### Distinguish Solid Findings From Preliminary Ones

Not all data is equally ready to be presented as fact. Single small studies, unpublished preprints, and self-reported survey data carry different weight than large peer-reviewed datasets or official statistics.

Calibrate confidence:

- prefer replicated or large-sample findings for strong claims;
- treat single studies and preprints as provisional, and say so;
- disclose self-report and convenience-sample limitations;
- avoid presenting an estimate or projection as a measured fact.

Where the evidence is weak, the graphic should hedge or not be made. Confidence in the visual must not exceed confidence in the data.

### Avoid Misleading Precision And Visual Falsehood

The way data is visualized can lie even when the numbers are correct. Truncated axes, inconsistent scales, area-based charts with wrong proportions, and cherry-picked time windows all create false impressions.

Audit the visualization for honesty:

- bar chart axes start at zero unless a non-zero start is clearly justified and labeled;
- proportional shapes scale correctly to the data;
- time series cover a representative window, not a flattering one;
- no visual trick exaggerates the effect the data supports.

A technically accurate number in a misleading chart is still a misleading infographic.

### Fact-Check The Final Graphic As A Whole

Errors creep in during editing: a number transposed, a label swapped, a percentage turned into a fraction. The final assembled graphic must be checked against the sources one more time, not assumed correct because the draft was.

Run a final check:

- re-read every figure against its primary source;
- confirm every label matches its data;
- verify totals and percentages add up correctly;
- ensure no claim was strengthened during editing.

A graphic that was accurate at draft can become wrong at export. The last check is mandatory, not optional.

### Have The Honesty To Withhold When The Data Is Not Strong Enough

The most important judgment is knowing when not to publish. If the only available source is weak, outdated, or unverifiable, the right move is to wait, find better data, or drop the claim, not to publish a confident-looking graphic on a shaky foundation.

Apply a publication threshold:

- would you be comfortable defending every figure if challenged;
- does the strongest source actually support the headline claim;
- is the data current and relevant enough to stand alone;
- if debunked, would the correction undo the value of publishing.

When in doubt, do not publish. A missing infographic costs nothing; a false one costs credibility.

## Common Traps

### Citing Secondary Sources As If Primary

Articles summarizing studies often distort the finding. Trace to the primary source before publishing.

### Outdated Data Presented As Current

Old figures imply current reality. Confirm collection dates and check for newer data.

### Stripping Caveats For Cleanliness

Removing margin of error, sample, or scope turns a careful finding into a misleading claim. Keep essential caveats.

### Vague, Unverifiable Source Lines

A generic organization name is not a real citation. Cite specifically enough to verify.

### Presenting Preliminary Data As Settled

Single studies and preprints are provisional. Calibrate confidence and say so.

### Visually Misleading Charts With True Numbers

Truncated axes and wrong proportions lie without falsifying. Audit the visualization itself.

### Skipping The Final Whole-Graphic Check

Editing introduces errors after the draft was verified. Re-check the assembled graphic against sources.

## Self-Check

Before publishing an infographic, verify:

- Every figure traces to a primary source that has actually been read, not a secondary summary.
- Data collection dates are current and relevant to the claim being made.
- Essential caveats, such as sample, scope, and margin of error, are retained or acknowledged.
- Source citations are specific and dated enough for a reader to verify the exact figure.
- The confidence of the visual matches the strength of the underlying evidence.
- Preliminary, single-study, or self-reported data is labeled as such, not presented as settled.
- No chart uses truncated axes, wrong proportions, or cherry-picked windows that mislead.
- The final assembled graphic was re-checked figure by figure against its sources after editing.
- Totals, percentages, and labels are internally consistent.
- You would be comfortable publicly defending every claim in the graphic if challenged, and if not, the graphic was withheld or revised.
