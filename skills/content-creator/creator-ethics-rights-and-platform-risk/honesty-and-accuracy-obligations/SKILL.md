# Honesty And Accuracy Obligations

Content creators operate as informal publishers, and every factual claim they make carries an obligation to the audience that is easy to underestimate. The judgment problem is that the incentives of platforms reward confidence, simplicity, and emotional punch, while honesty often requires hedging, complexity, and restraint. This mismatch is exactly where creators fail: by stating speculation as fact, by repeating claims they did not verify, by oversimplifying until a true statement becomes misleading, and by burying or ignoring corrections when they turn out to be wrong. The harm is real because audiences make decisions about their money, health, relationships, and beliefs based on what creators say, and a confident falsehood travels further and faster than a quiet correction.

This skill helps the agent decide what is solid enough to publish, how to represent uncertainty honestly, how to source and attribute, and how to handle being wrong. The default orientation is conservative: when the evidence is not strong enough, the right move is usually to wait, hedge, or not publish.

## Core Rules

### Separate Fact From Opinion And Label Each

Audiences cannot always tell when a creator is asserting a verifiable fact versus expressing a personal view. Conflating the two lets opinion borrow the authority of fact and lets weak claims hide inside strong rhetoric. Be explicit about which mode you are in.

Distinguish clearly:

- state verifiable claims as claims and back them with sources;
- mark personal opinion, prediction, and interpretation as such;
- avoid presenting a contested view as settled consensus;
- avoid presenting an opinion as if it were the only reasonable reading.

When the audience knows what kind of statement they are hearing, they can weigh it appropriately.

### Verify Before Publishing, Especially For High-Stakes Topics

The strength of evidence required scales with the stakes. A throwaway product opinion needs less verification than a claim about health, finance, safety, law, or public events. For high-stakes topics, verification is an obligation, not a nicety.

Calibrate verification to stakes:

- trace claims to primary sources for health, money, safety, and legal topics;
- prefer replicated evidence and expert consensus over single studies or anecdotes;
- check whether the claim has been debunked or updated since you encountered it;
- distinguish what is known from what is suspected or projected.

If you cannot verify a high-stakes claim, do not state it as fact. Hedge, attribute, or omit.

### Do Not Let Simplification Become Distortion

Simplification is necessary to communicate, but each cut can bend the truth. The line between helpful simplification and misleading oversimplification is whether the simplified version still leads the audience to a correct understanding.

Simplify without distorting:

- preserve the caveats that change the meaning;
- avoid stripping context like sample size, timeframe, or scope;
- do not round a nuanced finding into an absolute;
- check that a simplified claim would still hold up if a knowledgeable person scrutinized it.

If the clean version of your claim is not actually true, the claim is the problem, not the audience's attention span.

### Attribute Sources Honestly And Specifically

Vague attribution, such as saying studies show or experts say, lends false authority to claims and prevents the audience from checking. Honest attribution is specific and verifiable.

Attribute properly:

- name the specific study, report, or expert, not a vague class;
- provide enough detail that the audience can find the source;
- distinguish primary from secondary sources;
- disclose when you are relying on a single source or a contested one.

If you cannot name the source specifically, you may not actually have one.

### Represent Uncertainty And Disagreement Honestly

Many important topics have genuine uncertainty or expert disagreement. Hiding that to present a clean, confident take is a form of dishonesty even when no individual statement is false.

Show the real state of knowledge:

- acknowledge where evidence is preliminary, mixed, or evolving;
- note genuine expert disagreement rather than picking a side silently;
- distinguish strong consensus from contested frontier;
- update the audience when the picture changes.

Confident presentation of an uncertain topic misleads the audience about how much they can rely on.

### Handle Corrections Openly And Promptly

Everyone gets things wrong; the integrity test is what happens after. Hiding, deleting, or quietly editing errors protects the creator's ego at the cost of the audience's trust and of people who acted on the original claim.

Correct properly:

- correct visibly, in the same place and at similar scale as the original;
- state what was wrong and what is right;
- do not silently edit history without acknowledging the change;
- act promptly once you know an error is real.

A track record of honest corrections builds more trust than a pretense of never being wrong.

### Resist Engagement Pressure That Bends The Truth

Platform incentives push toward exaggeration, certainty, and emotional intensity, all of which corrode accuracy. Recognize when a framing choice is being driven by engagement rather than truthfulness.

Watch for engagement-driven distortion:

- headlines or thumbnails that overstate the content;
- emotional framing that exceeds what the evidence supports;
- certainty that the underlying evidence does not warrant;
- cherry-picked examples presented as typical.

The version that performs best is often not the version that is most honest. Choose accordingly.

### Know The Limits Of Your Expertise

Creators are often tempted to speak authoritatively beyond their actual knowledge, especially on trending topics. Audiences trust perceived expertise, so overstating it causes real harm.

Stay within your lane:

- distinguish lived experience and opinion from professional expertise;
- defer to or consult qualified experts for specialized topics;
- disclose when you are not qualified to give definitive guidance;
- avoid presenting a quick research pass as deep expertise.

Honesty about the limits of your knowledge is itself a form of accuracy.

## Common Traps

### Stating Speculation As Fact

Confident assertion of unverified claims misleads the audience. Verify or hedge.

### Oversimplifying Until True Becomes Misleading

Simplification that drops essential caveats distorts. Preserve what changes the meaning.

### Vague Authority Attribution

Studies show and experts say lend false authority. Cite specifically and verifiably.

### Hiding Uncertainty Or Disagreement

Clean confidence on a contested topic is dishonest. Show the real state of knowledge.

### Silent Or Buried Corrections

Quietly editing errors betrays the audience who acted on the original. Correct visibly.

### Engagement-Driven Exaggeration

Framing chosen for performance over truth corrodes accuracy over time. Resist it.

### Speaking Beyond Real Expertise

Overstating qualifications on specialized topics causes real harm. Stay within your lane.

## Self-Check

Before publishing content containing factual claims, verify:

- Each factual claim is distinguishable from opinion and labeled appropriately.
- High-stakes claims about health, money, safety, or law trace to verified primary sources.
- Simplifications preserve the caveats and context that change the meaning.
- Sources are attributed specifically enough for the audience to verify them.
- Genuine uncertainty or expert disagreement is acknowledged rather than hidden.
- A process exists for visible, prompt corrections when errors are found.
- Headlines, thumbnails, and framing do not overstate what the content supports.
- The creator is not presenting quick research as deep expertise on specialized topics.
- No claim is stated with more certainty than the underlying evidence warrants.
- You would be comfortable defending every claim publicly if challenged, and if not, the claim was hedged, attributed, or removed.
