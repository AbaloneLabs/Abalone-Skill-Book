# Format Translation

Format translation is the specific craft of converting content from one platform's format to another's: a horizontal YouTube video into vertical short-form, a podcast episode into a newsletter or thread, a long essay into a carousel. Translation is distinct from general adaptation because it confronts concrete format requirements, aspect ratios, lengths, structures, and platform mechanics that demand structural transformation rather than light editing. The harm this skill prevents is the common pattern of format translation done as mechanical conversion, where a horizontal video is cropped to vertical losing its composition, a podcast is transcribed into an unreadable wall of text, or an essay is chunked into slides with no visual logic, producing content that fails in its new format because the translation ignored what the format demands.

Use this skill when translating any piece across platform formats, when translated content underperforms, when building translation templates, and when deciding whether a piece can be translated well or needs to be remade.

## Core Rules

### Assess Whether The Source Can Translate Well Before Translating

Not every source translates well to every format. A talking-head video may translate poorly to a visual carousel; a data-heavy essay may translate poorly to short-form video; a conversational podcast may translate poorly to a scannable thread. Before translating, assess:

- does the source's core value fit the target format's strengths?
- can the source's content be restructured to serve the target format without losing its value?
- does the source have the visual, audio, or structural elements the target format needs?
- is the source's value better served by translation or by creating new content for the target format?

If a source cannot translate well, translating it produces weak content. Choose translatable sources, or create native content for the target format instead.

### Translate The Value, Then Rebuild The Structure

Effective translation does not carry the source's structure into the new format; it extracts the core value and rebuilds a structure native to the target format. The source's structure served the source's format; the translated piece needs a structure that serves the target format. Rebuild:

- the hook, suited to the target format's attention mechanics;
- the body, structured for the target format's consumption pattern;
- the payoff, delivered at the point the target format's audience expects;
- the visual or structural elements the target format requires.

Carrying the source structure produces format-mismatched content. Rebuilding produces format-native content.

### Handle Aspect Ratio And Composition Deliberately

For video translations across aspect ratios (horizontal to vertical, vertical to horizontal), cropping is rarely sufficient. Cropping a horizontal video to vertical often loses the subject, the composition, and the visual logic. Handle aspect ratio deliberately:

- reframe for the target ratio, keeping the subject and key visual elements;
- add or change backgrounds, graphics, or text overlays suited to the target ratio;
- reshoot or supplement footage where cropping destroys the composition;
- use the target ratio's conventions (vertical's emphasis on faces and text, horizontal's emphasis on environment).

Mechanical cropping is the most common video translation failure and produces amateur-looking content.

### Convert Audio And Spoken Content To Written Deliberately

Translating podcasts or video audio into written formats (newsletters, threads, articles) by transcription alone produces unreadable content. Spoken language is redundant, rambling, and context-dependent in ways written language should not be. Convert deliberately:

- extract the core insights and restructure them for reading;
- rewrite spoken sentences into written sentences with appropriate density and rhythm;
- add structure (headings, bullets, sequences) that spoken content lacks;
- cut the redundancy and filler that spoken content requires but written content does not.

Transcription is raw material, not a finished translation. The conversion requires rewriting.

### Convert Written Content To Spoken Or Visual Deliberately

The reverse translation, written to spoken or visual, has its own demands. Written content is dense and assumes re-reading; spoken content must be followable in real time; visual content must carry meaning through composition. Convert deliberately:

- loosen written density into spoken pacing with breathing room;
- add the transitions, signposts, and repetition that audio audiences need;
- translate written abstractions into visual concrete elements for video or carousel;
- add the performance, tone, and presence that written content lacks but spoken or visual content requires.

Pasting an essay into a teleprompter produces stiff, unreadable video. Conversion requires adaptation to the medium.

### Respect Length And Pacing Requirements

Each format has length and pacing ranges the audience expects. Translating without respecting these produces content that is too long, too short, too dense, or too sparse for the format. Calibrate:

- short-form video: single payoff, fast pacing, under the format's length norms;
- long video: sustained structure, varied pacing, room for depth;
- carousel: one idea per slide, swipeable progression, complete arc;
- thread: punchy posts, cumulative build, scannable;
- newsletter: complete reading experience, voice, depth.

Forcing source length into a format with different length expectations produces mismatched content. Cut or expand to the format's range.

### Add Format-Native Elements

Each format has signature elements that audiences expect and that signal native rather than translated content: short-form's text overlays and captions; carousel's slide titles and swipe prompts; thread's numbering and summary; video's b-roll and graphics. Add the elements the target format expects, sourced from the core value.

Translated content that omits format-native elements feels foreign to the format's audience and underperforms.

### Preserve Accuracy, Context, And Disclosure Across Translation

Translation can distort meaning and strip context. A nuanced source claim can become absolute when translated to a shorter format; a source disclosure can be lost when the format changes. Preserve:

- conditions, caveats, and limits relevant to the translated claim;
- sources and citations for factual claims;
- disclosures, especially sponsored, health, financial, or safety content;
- the accuracy of the claim in the new format's brevity.

If a claim cannot be made accurately in the target format's length, choose a different value to translate or add the necessary context.

### Test Translations Against Format Performance

Translated content should be evaluated against the target format's performance norms, not the source's. A well-translated short is judged by short-form metrics, not by whether it matched the source video's views. Track translation performance by format-appropriate metrics and learn which sources and translation approaches work for each format.

## Common Traps

### Translating Untranslatable Sources

Forcing sources that do not fit the target format produces weak content. Assess translatability first; create native content where translation will not serve.

### Mechanical Cropping

Cropping video across aspect ratios without reframing loses composition and subject. Reframe deliberately for the target ratio.

### Raw Transcription

Pasting transcribed audio into written formats produces unreadable content. Rewrite spoken content for reading.

### Teleprompter Essays

Reading written content verbatim on video produces stiff, unreadable content. Adapt written content for spoken delivery.

### Ignoring Length And Pacing

Forcing source length into a format with different expectations produces mismatched content. Calibrate to the format's range.

### Omitting Format-Native Elements

Translated content without the target format's signature elements feels foreign and underperforms. Add format-native elements.

### Carrying Source Structure

Mapping the source's structure onto the target format ignores the target format's logic. Rebuild structure for the target format.

### Context And Disclosure Loss

Translation that strips conditions, caveats, sources, or disclosures distorts the source's meaning and responsibility. Preserve accuracy and disclosure across formats.

### Irresponsible Translation Of High-Stakes Content

Translating health, financial, legal, mental health, or safety content into shorter or different formats can strip the disclosures and hedging that made the source responsible, producing confident standalone claims that mislead or harm. Preserve all relevant disclosures and limits across translation, add context where the format requires it, and where translated content approaches regulated advice, escalate to expert review and professional referral. If a claim cannot be translated responsibly into the target format, do not translate it.

## Self-Check

Before approving a format translation, verify:

- The source's translatability to the target format was assessed before translating.
- The core value is extracted and the structure is rebuilt for the target format, not carried from the source.
- Aspect ratio and composition are handled deliberately for video translations, not mechanically cropped.
- Audio-to-written translation rewrites for reading, not raw transcription.
- Written-to-spoken or visual translation adapts density, pacing, and presence for the medium.
- Length and pacing are calibrated to the target format's range.
- Format-native elements (overlays, slide titles, numbering, b-roll) are added.
- Accuracy, context, sources, and disclosures are preserved across translation.
- Translations are evaluated against format-appropriate performance metrics.
- For high-stakes content, disclosures and limits are preserved, context is added where needed, and expert review or professional referral is used where translated content approaches regulated advice.
