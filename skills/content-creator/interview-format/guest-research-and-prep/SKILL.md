# Guest Research And Prep

Guest research and prep is the work of learning enough about a person and their subject before the interview that the conversation can go somewhere new instead of retracing ground the guest has already covered a hundred times. The judgment problem is that research is easy to do badly. A surface skim produces a brief full of biography and accomplishments that the guest has recited before, which leads to predictable questions and rehearsed answers. Deep, adversarial, or mismatched research can ambush a guest who was promised a friendly conversation and destroy a relationship. The discipline is to research with a purpose, to find the seams where the guest's record is inconsistent or incomplete, and to calibrate the depth and tone of research to the kind of interview being conducted.

The harm from poor prep is concrete. An under-prepared interviewer asks questions the guest has answered publicly and visibly loses credibility, the guest disengages and gives shorter answers, and the audience feels the interview is generic. An over-prepared or mis-calibrated interviewer weaponizes research in a way that betrays the agreed format, burns the source, and damages the creator's reputation with future guests. This skill helps the agent gather the right evidence, organize it into a usable brief, and decide what to surface, what to hold in reserve, and what to disclose to the guest in advance.

## Core Rules

### Research With A Purpose, Not For Completeness

Research without a goal produces a dump of facts. Before gathering anything, define what the interview is trying to achieve and what specific gaps or tensions the research should surface. Research should serve the interview's intent, not demonstrate diligence.

Define the research purpose explicitly:

- the one or two things the audience most needs from this guest;
- the contradictions, evolutions, or unresolved questions in the guest's record;
- the guest's expertise boundaries, where they are authoritative and where they are guessing;
- the guest's emotional and reputational stakes in the topic.

Every piece of research collected should connect to one of these. Material that does not connect is noise.

### Map The Guest's Public Record Before The Conversation

Before drafting questions, build a map of what the guest has already said publicly on the topic. This prevents asking answered questions and reveals where the guest has changed, contradicted, or refused to address something.

Sources to map, in order of value:

- the guest's own writing, talks, posts, and product work, primary sources first;
- the guest's previous interviews, especially recent ones on the same topic;
- credible criticism, investigations, or counterarguments from others;
- the guest's own corrections, walkbacks, or updates over time.

The most valuable output of this map is the delta, where the guest has evolved, where they disagree with their past self, and where their public claims and their record diverge.

### Find The Seams, Not Just The Highlights

Strong interviews live in the seams of a guest's record, the places where the story is unfinished, where a claim is unproven, or where a position has shifted. A brief that only lists achievements produces an interview that feels like a press release. Look deliberately for tension.

Look for seams in:

- the gap between a stated principle and a documented action;
- a claim that has been challenged and never addressed;
- a topic the guest avoids or deflects in prior interviews;
- a recent change in position, role, or affiliation and the reason for it;
- the cost or casualty behind a celebrated success.

Seams are not gotchas. They are the places where an honest question can produce an honest answer the audience has not heard.

### Build A Brief That Is Usable Under Pressure

A research brief is an operational document used live, often while listening and managing a conversation. It must be structured for retrieval, not for reading. A 40-page dossier is useless in the moment; a one or two page brief with indexed evidence is usable.

Structure the brief around:

- a short context summary of who the guest is and why they matter now;
- the two or three audience questions the interview must answer;
- the seams and tensions, each with the specific evidence and a citation;
- the prepared questions, each tagged with the follow-up it enables;
- a reserve section of harder material, flagged for whether and when to deploy.

Cite every claim in the brief to a source so the interviewer can verify a quote or fact instantly if the guest challenges it.

### Calibrate Research Depth To The Interview Type

Not every interview warrants the same depth or tone of research. A friendly long-form conversation with a collaborator needs different prep than a accountability interview with a public official. Mismatching research tone to the agreed format is an ethical and relational error.

Match depth and tone to type:

- celebratory or career-retrospective interviews need biographical and contextual depth, not opposition research;
- expert explainer interviews need the guest's best work and the strongest counterarguments so the interviewer can press for clarity;
- accountability interviews need documented contradictions, timelines, and on-the-record challenges, with evidence the interviewer can defend;
- returning-guest interviews need what has changed since the last conversation, not a full re-brief.

Disclose the intended tone to the guest in advance when the format is anything other than friendly, so the guest is not ambushed.

### Decide What To Share With The Guest In Advance

Guests often ask for questions in advance. Sharing the full list usually produces rehearsed answers and forfeits the follow-up. Sharing nothing can make a careful guest decline or arrive defensive. Decide deliberately what to disclose.

A workable default:

- share topics and the general direction, not the specific phrasing or sequencing;
- never share the seam questions or the reserve material, these are the interview's value;
- share anything that could be perceived as an ambush so the guest is not blindsided on facts;
- be honest about the format and tone even if you withhold specific questions.

The goal is informed consent without rehearsal. A guest who agrees to a topic area has consented; a guest who has rehearsed the exact question has not really been interviewed.

### Verify Quotes And Facts Before Using Them Live

Quoting a guest inaccurately, or attributing a claim to them that they did not make, destroys credibility instantly and can end the relationship. Every quote, number, and attribution in the brief must be verified against a primary source before it is used. Secondhand summaries and aggregated quotes are especially dangerous because they drift. If a quote cannot be verified to a primary source, do not use it as a direct quote; paraphrase and attribute carefully, or drop it.

## Common Traps

### Skimming Headlines And Calling It Research

Reading a few articles and the guest's Wikipedia page is a trap because it produces the same questions every other interviewer asks and signals to the guest that no real prep happened. This is a trap because the guest disengages and the audience gets a generic interview. Go to primary sources and prior interviews.

### Ambushing A Guest Promised A Friendly Format

Deploying opposition research in an interview the guest understood as friendly is a trap because it breaches trust, burns the source, and warns future guests away. This is a trap because short-term drama is traded for long-term reputational damage. Match research tone to the agreed format and disclose the tone in advance.

### Over-Briefing And Drowning The Interviewer

A 50-page brief is a trap because nothing in it is retrievable live, so the interviewer defaults to the first page anyway. This is a trap because the illusion of preparation replaces usable preparation. Build a short, indexed, cited brief.

### Treating Criticism As Established Fact

Aggregated criticism or a viral thread is not evidence. Using it as if it were is a trap because the guest can demolish it live and the interviewer loses credibility. This is a trap because the audience then trusts the guest more and the interviewer less. Verify every challenge to a primary source.

### Asking Questions The Guest Has Already Answered Publicly

Asking a well-known question the guest has answered in prior interviews is a trap because it yields the rehearsed answer and tells the audience the interviewer did not do the homework. This is a trap because it squanders scarce access. Map the public record and route around answered questions.

### Sharing The Full Question List In Advance

Handing over the exact questions is a trap because it converts the interview into a recital and removes the possibility of a genuine follow-up. This is a trap because the resulting material is indistinguishable from a press release. Share topics and direction, not phrasing.

### Ignoring The Guest's Stakes And Emotional State

Researching facts without considering what the topic costs the guest emotionally is a trap because it leads to questions that are technically fair but humanly clumsy. This is a trap because it produces defensiveness or distress that shuts down the interview. Factor the guest's stakes into what you ask and how.

## Self-Check

- Did I define the research purpose before gathering material, and does every item in the brief connect to that purpose?
- Have I mapped the guest's public record and identified the deltas, contradictions, and evolutions rather than just highlights?
- Did I look specifically for seams and tensions, and is each backed by cited primary evidence?
- Is the brief short, indexed, and cited so it is usable live rather than a dense dossier?
- Is the research depth and tone calibrated to the agreed interview type, and did I disclose the tone to the guest?
- Did I decide deliberately what to share in advance, sharing topics and direction but not specific phrasing or reserve material?
- Is every quote, number, and attribution verified to a primary source before use?
- Did I avoid asking questions the guest has already answered publicly?
- Did I consider the guest's emotional and reputational stakes in designing what to surface?
- Could the guest reasonably claim they were ambushed or misquoted? If yes, fix the prep before the interview.
