# Interview Question Design

Interview question design is the practice of deciding what to ask, in what order, and in what form, so that a conversation surfaces a story, an insight, or a confession rather than a recital of facts the audience already knows. The judgment problem is that most question lists are written as a survey of the topic instead of as an instrument for extracting something the audience cannot get elsewhere. A list of "tell me about your background, your biggest challenge, your advice" produces a polite, generic interview that could have been made by anyone, with anyone, and the audience senses this within the first minute.

The harm is measurable. Weak questions waste a scarce resource, the guest's time and the audience's attention, and they produce footage or transcript that cannot be edited into anything compelling. Worse, badly designed questions can embarrass the guest, miss the only newsworthy angle, or steer the conversation into territory the guest has rehearsed and that the audience has already heard. This skill helps the agent design questions that are specific to this guest, sequenced for trust and momentum, and phrased to invite real answers instead of PR.

## Core Rules

### Design For The Audience's Unanswered Question, Not The Topic

The starting point is not the guest's biography but the question the audience actually has that nobody has answered well. Survey the existing coverage of this guest and topic, and identify the gap. The best interviews close a gap the audience is aware of, or expose a gap the audience did not know existed.

Before drafting questions, name explicitly:

- what the audience already knows or assumes about this guest;
- what the audience most wants to know that has not been answered;
- what the audience would benefit from knowing but has not thought to ask;
- what the guest has refused or dodged in previous interviews.

Every question on the list should map to one of these. Questions that merely re-cover known ground should be cut unless they serve as a necessary on-ramp.

### Build A Funnel From Comfort To Consequence

Question order is not arbitrary; it shapes what the guest will give you. People open up sequentially. A cold confrontation in the first five minutes shuts down the rest of the interview. Sequence questions as a funnel.

A workable funnel structure:

- warm-up questions that are easy, specific, and let the guest settle, often about recent concrete events;
- building questions that establish context and let the guest tell a story they enjoy telling;
- the core questions that reach the real subject and the audience's unanswered question;
- the consequence questions that ask about impact, regret, cost, or disagreement;
- a closing question that invites reflection or a forward look.

The hard questions belong in the middle and later thirds, after trust and momentum exist, not at the start.

### Write Questions That Invite Story, Not Summary

A question phrased to elicit a summary ("What's your advice for founders?") produces a generic answer the guest has given a hundred times. A question phrased to elicit a story ("Tell me about the specific moment you realized the company was going to fail") produces something only this guest can give.

Prefer phrasings that demand specificity:

- ask about a moment, a decision, a conversation, or a number;
- ask "what happened next" rather than "how did you feel about it";
- ask "what did you actually do" rather than "what should people do";
- ask for the tradeoff or the cost, not just the lesson.

Vague questions feel respectful but they give the guest permission to retreat into talking points. Specific, slightly uncomfortable questions, asked warmly, are what produce memorable material.

### Separate Prepared Questions From Improvised Follow-Ups

A rigid question list produces a stiff interview because the interviewer stops listening and starts waiting for their turn. Treat the prepared list as a map, not a script. The prepared questions guarantee the territory gets covered; the improvised follow-ups are where the actual interview happens.

For each prepared question, plan the likely follow-up directions:

- if the guest gives a short answer, what probe opens it up;
- if the guest reveals something new, what follow-up chases it;
- if the guest dodges, whether and how to circle back;
- if the guest becomes emotional, whether to sit in silence or move on.

Budget the interview so that chasing a real follow-up is allowed to consume time that was allocated to a lower-priority prepared question.

### Identify The Guest's Rehearsed Answers And Route Around Them

Experienced guests, executives, politicians, and repeat podcast guests have rehearsed answers for common questions. Asking the obvious version of a common question yields the rehearsed answer. To get past it, change the angle of approach.

Techniques for routing around rehearsed answers:

- ask about a specific instance rather than the general principle;
- ask about the cost or the opponent rather than the victory;
- ask the question from the perspective of a critic or a competitor;
- ask what changed their mind, not what they believe;
- ask a narrower or more technical version that the rehearsed answer does not fit.

If a rehearsed answer still lands, a gentle, specific follow-up ("You've said that before; I want to push on the specific part where...") often produces the real answer.

### Calibrate Question Difficulty To The Guest And The Relationship

The same question is appropriate or destructive depending on the guest's trust level, the stakes, and the format. A confrontational question that builds the interview in a long-form recorded conversation can end a source relationship in a live broadcast. Calibrate deliberately.

Consider:

- is this guest a willing participant, a reluctant one, or an adversarial one;
- what is the guest's stake in the topic, financial, reputational, emotional;
- is the format live, recorded, edited, or text-based, and what does each allow;
- what does the guest need to feel safe enough to be honest.

Push hard where honesty is possible and the audience needs it; pull back where pushing only produces defensiveness and no usable material.

### Plan For Silence And The Non-Question

Some of the best interview moments come from what is not asked. Plan to leave space: a deliberate pause after an emotional answer, a silence that invites the guest to continue, or a decision not to ask a question that would exploit rather than illuminate. Also plan the non-question moves, a reflection ("That's heavier than I expected"), a summary that lets the guest correct you, or an explicit acknowledgment. These are part of question design because they shape what the guest offers next.

## Common Traps

### Writing A Survey Instead Of An Interview

A common trap is to write questions that cover the topic comprehensively, which feels thorough but produces a flat, encyclopedic interview. This is a trap because comprehensive coverage is the enemy of depth; the audience remembers one or two moments, not the syllabus. Choose a few questions worth going deep on and cut the rest.

### Front-Loading The Hard Questions

Asking the hardest question first feels efficient and bold, but it usually triggers defensiveness before rapport exists and poisons the rest of the conversation. This is a trap because the guest never relaxes afterward and even the easy questions yield guarded answers. Earn the right to ask hard things by building trust first.

### Asking Leading Questions That Tell The Audience What To Think

Questions that bake in the interviewer's conclusion ("Isn't it true that your policy failed?") produce either a denial or a capitulation, neither of which is illuminating. This is a trap because the audience learns the interviewer's opinion but nothing new about the guest. Ask neutral, specific questions and let the guest's answer carry the weight.

### Treating The Question List As A Script

Reading questions in order without listening is a trap because it forfeits the follow-up, which is where real material lives, and signals to the guest that they are being processed rather than heard. Use the list as a map and chase what the guest actually says.

### Copying Standard Questions From Other Interviews

Borrowing recognizable signature questions ("What's your morning routine?") is a trap because it produces recognizable, skippable answers and makes the interview interchangeable with hundreds of others. Standard questions are acceptable only as warm-up; the core questions must be specific to this guest.

### Ignoring The Guest's Expertise Level And Vocabulary

Asking a technical guest only surface questions, or asking a non-expert guest only jargon questions, is a trap because it mismatches the question to the guest and produces either condescension or confusion. Match depth and vocabulary to the guest and to the audience simultaneously, which is harder than defaulting to one.

### Overpacking The Question List

Trying to ask thirty questions in a forty-minute interview is a trap because it forces shallow answers and prevents follow-ups. This is a trap because the interview ends with breadth but no moment worth clipping. Prioritize ruthlessly and leave room to go deep on what matters.

## Self-Check

- Does every core question map to a specific audience question or gap, and have I cut questions that only re-cover known ground?
- Is the question order sequenced as a funnel from comfort to consequence, with hard questions placed after trust is built?
- Are the core questions phrased to elicit stories and specifics rather than summaries and advice?
- Have I planned follow-up directions for each prepared question instead of treating the list as a script?
- Have I identified the guest's likely rehearsed answers and designed angles to route around them?
- Is the difficulty and confrontational level calibrated to the guest's stake, trust, and the format?
- Have I planned for silence and non-question moves, not just verbal questions?
- Did I avoid leading questions that tell the audience what to think?
- Is the list ruthlessly prioritized so there is room to go deep instead of asking everything shallowly?
- Could a different guest swap in and give the same answers? If yes, the questions are not specific enough to this guest.
