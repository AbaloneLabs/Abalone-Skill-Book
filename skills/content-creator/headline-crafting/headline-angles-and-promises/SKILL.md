# Headline Angles And Promises

A headline is the single line that decides whether the rest of the work gets read, watched, or clicked. The judgment problem is that the headline is treated as a labeling task, name the piece, when it is actually a strategic decision about angle and promise. The angle is the specific way into the material, the lens that makes this piece compelling to this audience. The promise is what the reader gets in exchange for their attention. Most weak headlines fail on one or both: they have no angle, so they name the topic without making it compelling, or they make a promise the piece cannot keep, which earns the click and loses the reader. The discipline is to choose an angle that makes the material genuinely compelling to the target audience, to make a promise the piece fulfills, and to generate and compare variants rather than accepting the first workable line.

The harm from poor headlines is the burial of good work. A strong piece behind a weak headline reaches a fraction of its potential audience, because the headline is the only line most people ever see. A misleading headline earns clicks and destroys retention, signaling to platforms and audiences that the piece does not deliver, which suppresses its reach. This skill helps the agent choose angles that fit the material and audience, craft promises the piece keeps, generate multiple variants, and select the headline that earns attention honestly.

## Core Rules

### Choose The Angle Before The Words

The angle is the strategic entry point into the material, and it must be chosen before the words are crafted. The same piece can be angled in many ways, toward a benefit, a contradiction, a how-to, a warning, a story, and the angle determines which audience finds it compelling. Choose the angle deliberately based on the material and the audience.

Common angles:

- the benefit angle, what the reader gains;
- the how-to angle, the method or process revealed;
- the contradiction angle, a surprising tension or common mistake;
- the list angle, a concrete set of items;
- the story angle, a narrative or case that carries the lesson;
- the news angle, what changed or what is new;
- the warning angle, what to avoid or what is at risk.

Match the angle to what is genuinely strongest in the material and to what the target audience cares about. An angle that fits the material and the audience makes the headline almost write itself.

### Make A Promise The Piece Keeps

The headline is a promise. It sets an expectation that the piece must fulfill, or the reader feels cheated and leaves. A strong headline makes a specific promise that the content delivers fully. Calibrate the promise to what the piece actually contains.

Promise calibration practices:

- promise what the piece genuinely delivers, no more;
- be specific enough that the reader knows what they will get;
- ensure the payoff appears early enough to confirm the promise;
- avoid promising more than the piece can support, which is bait;
- avoid under-promising, which loses readers the piece deserves.

The promise is a contract. The reader gives attention; the piece gives the promised value. Break the contract and the reader leaves and does not return.

### Generate Multiple Variants And Compare

The first headline is rarely the best. Strong headlines come from generating many variants across different angles and promises, then comparing them against criteria. Treat headline writing as generation and selection, not as finding the one right line.

Variant generation practices:

- write at least five to ten variants spanning different angles;
- vary the promise, the specificity, and the emotional register;
- include some variants that push toward curiosity and some toward clarity;
- compare variants on honesty, specificity, audience fit, and platform fit;
- select the variant that best balances earning attention with keeping the promise.

Comparing variants reveals which angle and promise are strongest. Accepting the first workable line forfeits the improvement that comparison produces.

### Match The Angle And Promise To The Audience

The same material angled differently reaches different audiences. A headline that resonates with experts may bore generalists; one that draws beginners may patronize experts. Choose the angle and promise for the specific audience the piece is meant to reach.

Audience matching practices:

- identify the target audience's desires, fears, and vocabulary;
- angle toward what that audience specifically cares about;
- use language and framing that audience recognizes and trusts;
- avoid angles that appeal to a broader audience than the piece serves;
- test whether the angle would make the target reader feel this is for them.

A headline angled precisely for its audience outperforms one angled for everyone. Precision attracts the right reader and signals the piece is for them.

### Match The Headline To The Platform And Format

Headlines behave differently across platforms. A headline that works in search, where intent is explicit, differs from one that works in a social feed, where the headline must earn the scroll. Calibrate the headline to where it will be encountered.

Platform calibration practices:

- search headlines should include the query term and be clear about the content;
- social headlines should earn the scroll with stakes or curiosity in the first words;
- email subject headlines should feel personal and specific to the subscriber;
- video titles should work with the thumbnail as a system;
- on-platform headlines should match the platform's tone and length conventions.

A single piece may need different headlines for different distribution surfaces. Plan them deliberately rather than reusing one everywhere.

### Balance Specificity With Breadth

A headline must be specific enough to be compelling but not so narrow it excludes the audience. Too specific, and only a tiny audience recognizes it as for them; too broad, and it becomes generic and compelling to no one. Find the balance.

Balance practices:

- include enough specificity to signal real value, a number, a name, a concrete outcome;
- avoid specificity so narrow it excludes readers who would benefit;
- avoid breadth so wide the headline could apply to any piece on the topic;
- test whether the headline would make the target reader feel addressed;
- prefer concrete over abstract, but keep the concrete accessible.

The right specificity makes the headline feel both valuable and relevant. Generic headlines feel skippable; hyper-narrow headlines feel exclusionary.

### Ensure The Headline Reflects The Piece Honestly

The headline must represent the piece accurately. A headline that exaggerates, distorts, or misrepresents the content earns clicks and destroys trust, retention, and reach. Honesty is not a constraint on headline strength; it is the foundation of it.

Honesty practices:

- ensure every claim in the headline is supported by the content;
- avoid exaggerating the stakes, the novelty, or the scope;
- avoid framing that implies a different type of content than the piece delivers;
- if the piece is opinion, do not headline it as fact;
- if the piece is a list of five, do not headline it as the definitive guide.

Honest headlines earn attention from the right audience and keep it. Dishonest headlines earn attention from anyone and keep no one.

## Common Traps

### No Angle, Just A Topic

Headlining the topic without an angle is a trap because it names the piece without making it compelling. This is a trap because a topic headline is interchangeable with every other piece on the topic. Choose an angle first.

### Overpromising In The Headline

Promising more than the piece delivers is a trap because it earns the click and destroys retention and trust. This is a trap because bait headlines suppress reach through poor retention. Match promise to content.

### Accepting The First Headline

Using the first workable headline without generating variants is a trap because it forfeits the improvement comparison produces. This is a trap because the first headline is rarely the strongest. Generate and compare.

### Angle Mismatched To Audience

Angling for a different audience than the piece serves is a trap because it attracts the wrong readers who leave. This is a trap because mismatched angles waste the piece on the wrong audience. Match angle to the target audience.

### One Headline Across Platforms

Reusing one headline across search, social, and email is a trap because each platform rewards different headline behavior. This is a trap because a headline calibrated for one platform underperforms elsewhere. Calibrate per platform.

### Too Generic Or Too Narrow

Headlines that are either generic enough for any piece or narrow enough for almost no one are traps because they fail to attract the right audience. This is a trap because balance is what makes a headline compelling. Balance specificity and breadth.

### Dishonest Framing

Framing the headline to misrepresent the content type or stakes is a trap because it earns clicks and creates detractors. This is a trap because dishonest headlines destroy the trust that sustains reach. Reflect the piece honestly.

## Self-Check

- Did I choose the angle deliberately based on what is strongest in the material and what the audience cares about?
- Does the headline make a specific promise that the piece fulfills fully and early?
- Did I generate at least five to ten variants across angles and promises before selecting?
- Is the angle and promise matched to the specific target audience the piece is meant to reach?
- Is the headline calibrated to the platform and format where it will be encountered?
- Does the headline balance specificity with breadth, compelling but not exclusionary?
- Does the headline represent the piece honestly, with no exaggeration or misrepresentation?
- Would the target reader feel this headline is specifically for them?
- If the piece delivered on the headline, would the reader feel satisfied rather than cheated?
- Could a stronger angle or promise make the same material more compelling? If yes, generate more variants.
