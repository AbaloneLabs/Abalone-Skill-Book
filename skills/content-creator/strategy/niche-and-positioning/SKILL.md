# Niche And Positioning

Niche and positioning are often confused. A niche is the territory a creator works in. Positioning is the place the creator occupies inside that territory relative to everyone else. A creator can pick a viable niche and still fail because their positioning is interchangeable, or pick a sharp position inside a niche that is too small or too saturated to sustain a channel. The judgment problem is that niche and positioning must be chosen together, with evidence, because each constrains the other.

The harm of getting this wrong is structural. A niche that is too broad produces generic content that competes with everyone. A niche that is too narrow starves the channel of reach. Positioning that is merely a topic, with no point of view or method, makes the creator replaceable. Positioning that is distinctive but misaligned with the audience's actual problem attracts attention without loyalty. This skill helps the agent evaluate niches for viability and craft positioning that is specific, defensible, and matched to a real audience need.

## Core Rules

### Separate Niche Viability From Positioning Strength

Judge the niche and the positioning on different criteria. A viable niche has demand, reachability, and room. A strong position has differentiation, credibility, and relevance.

For niche viability check:

- Is there demonstrated demand through search, communities, and competitor traction?
- Is the audience reachable through available distribution without unsustainable spend?
- Is there room, meaning the niche is not so saturated that a new entrant cannot be noticed?
- Is the niche durable enough to support repeatable content, or is it a fleeting trend?

For positioning strength check:

- Is the creator's angle specific enough to be recognized in one sentence?
- Does the creator have credibility, access, taste, or method that competitors lack?
- Is the position aligned with what the target audience actually values?
- Is the position defensible, meaning it cannot be copied in a week by a larger account?

Do not declare a strategy sound until both checks pass. A great position in a dead niche, or a great niche with a weak position, both fail.

### Define The Niche By Problem And Audience, Not Topic Label

Topic labels like "fitness" or "personal finance" are categories, not niches. A workable niche is defined by a specific audience and a specific problem they cannot currently solve well.

Define the niche as:

- a specific audience in a specific situation;
- facing a specific problem or job to be done;
- that is currently underserved or served in a way the audience dislikes;
- that the creator can address repeatedly without running out of material.

"Productivity" is a label. "Helping knowledge workers reduce context switching without buying more apps" is a niche. The second tells you what to make, who it is for, and why it is different.

### Make Positioning A Sentence, Not A Vibe

Positioning that cannot be stated in one concrete sentence is not yet positioning. Force the creator to complete a sentence that names the audience, the promise, and the difference.

A strong positioning sentence names:

- who the content is for;
- what promise or value it delivers;
- how it is different from the obvious alternatives;
- why the creator specifically can make this promise.

If the sentence collapses to "I make content about X for people who like X," the positioning is too weak. If it cannot be said without buzzwords, it is not yet clear.

### Map The Competitive Set Before Claiming Differentiation

Differentiation only means something relative to alternatives the audience already knows. Map the competitive set before asserting what makes this creator different.

Identify:

- direct competitors serving the same audience and problem;
- adjacent competitors the audience also consumes;
- substitutes the audience uses when no creator fits, such as books, courses, or friends;
- the dominant default the audience currently settles for.

For each, note what they do well, what they ignore, and what the audience complains about. Real positioning usually lives in the gap between what competitors do and what the audience still wants.

### Choose Narrowness Deliberately, Not By Default

Narrowness is a tool, not a virtue. A narrow niche is easier to dominate but caps reach. A broad niche offers reach but dilutes distinctiveness. Choose the width based on goals and capacity.

Use a narrower niche when:

- the channel is new and needs a defensible beachhead;
- the audience is underserved and loyal;
- the creator has deep expertise that broad competitors lack;
- revenue depends on a high-intent, specific audience.

Use a broader niche when:

- the creator has distribution or brand advantage;
- the goal is reach or mass relevance;
- the topic naturally spans many situations;
- the creator can sustain variety without losing recognizability.

Document the choice and its tradeoff, so future expansion or narrowing is a deliberate decision rather than drift.

### Anchor Positioning In Something Hard To Copy

Durable positioning rests on an asset or trait that competitors cannot quickly replicate. Identify what that anchor is and protect it.

Common anchors include:

- deep domain experience or credentials;
- unique access to sources, data, or scenes;
- a distinctive method or framework;
- strong taste or curation with a consistent point of view;
- a specific personality or voice audiences bond with;
- a community the creator has built and stewards.

If the anchor is only "I started posting," the position is fragile. If a larger account could replicate it in a month, plan to deepen the anchor before relying on it.

### Align Positioning With The Creator's Real Capacity

A sharp position is only useful if the creator can sustain it. Positioning that requires weekly original research will fail if the creator has two hours a week. Positioning built on hot takes will fail if the creator dislikes conflict. Match the position to real time, skill, energy, and review capacity, and to what the creator is willing to do for years, not weeks.

## Common Traps

### Mistaking A Topic For A Niche

Choosing "travel" or "tech" and calling it a niche leaves the creator competing with everyone in the category. A topic is a label; a niche is an audience plus an underserved problem.

### Claiming Differentiation Without Mapping Competitors

Without an explicit competitive set, "I'm different" is untestable. Most self-claimed differences are already being done by someone the audience knows.

### Narrowing To The Point Of Starvation

Hyper-specific niches feel safe because competition is low, but they can be too small to sustain reach or revenue. Check size before optimizing for narrowness.

### Copying A Successful Creator's Positioning

A position that works for one creator often depends on their specific anchor, access, or audience. Copying the surface without the anchor produces an interchangeable imitation.

### Broadening Prematurely To Chase Reach

Creators often widen scope the moment growth slows, eroding the distinctiveness that built the audience. Broaden only when the core position is stable and defensible.

### Positioning On Personality Alone

Personality can differentiate, but without a clear promise or method it produces content that depends on charisma and ages poorly when trends shift.

### Ignoring The Default The Audience Settles For

The real competitor is often "nothing" or a clunky substitute. If the creator's positioning is not clearly better than the audience's current default, the niche is not as open as it looks.

## Self-Check

Before approving a niche or positioning statement, verify:

- Niche viability and positioning strength were evaluated separately, and both pass.
- The niche is defined by audience and problem, not only a topic label.
- The positioning can be stated in one concrete sentence naming audience, promise, and difference.
- A competitive set was mapped, and differentiation is claimed relative to real alternatives.
- The width of the niche was chosen deliberately against reach and capacity goals.
- The positioning is anchored in something a competitor cannot copy quickly.
- The position fits the creator's real time, skill, energy, and willingness to sustain it.
- The niche is large and reachable enough to support the channel's goals.
- The position is not copied from another creator without an independent anchor.
- Broadening or narrowing the niche would be a deliberate future decision, not drift.
