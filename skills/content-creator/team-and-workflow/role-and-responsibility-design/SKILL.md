# Role And Responsibility Design

A creator team fails first at role definition, long before it fails at execution. The judgment problem is that creators add people to relieve overload without defining what each person owns, so roles overlap, gap, or collapse back onto the creator, who becomes the bottleneck the team was supposed to remove. "Help with the videos" is not a role; it is an undefined relationship that produces friction, duplicated work, and dropped tasks. Role design is the deliberate specification of what each person owns, what decisions they can make, and what requires someone else's approval, and without it a team is just more people for the creator to coordinate.

The harm is that scaling adds chaos rather than capacity. Ambiguous roles produce overlap where two people do the same work and gaps where nobody does it. Unclear decision rights force every choice back to the creator, recreating the bottleneck. Roles defined around people rather than responsibilities break when people change. This skill helps the agent design roles by responsibility and decision rights, separate creative, editorial, operational, and administrative duties, set approval thresholds so the creator is involved only where irreplaceable, and document roles so they survive team changes and actually reduce the creator's load.

## Core Rules

### Define Roles By Responsibility, Not By Person

A role must be defined by the outcomes it owns, not by whoever currently fills it. Person-shaped roles break when the person leaves; responsibility-shaped roles survive turnover.

Define by responsibility by:

- stating the specific outcomes each role owns;
- describing the work in terms of results, not tasks;
- making the role independent of the current holder;
- distinguishing the role from the person filling it;
- documenting roles so they can be handed off or hired against.

A role that only makes sense for one person is a dependency, not a role.

### Assign Clear Decision Rights And Approval Thresholds

Most role friction comes from unclear decision rights, where no one knows what they can decide alone and what requires approval. Make decision rights explicit.

Assign rights by:

- specifying what each role can decide independently;
- defining what requires the creator's or another's approval;
- setting thresholds, for example budget or scope, that trigger approval;
- avoiding the default where everything escalates to the creator;
- documenting rights so decisions do not stall on ambiguity.

Clear decision rights let work move without routing every choice through the creator.

### Separate Creative, Editorial, Operational, And Administrative Duties

These four functions have different demands and different decision owners, and conflating them produces roles that are too broad to fill well. Separate them deliberately.

Separate by:

- distinguishing creative work like ideation and on-camera presence;
- distinguishing editorial work like writing, editing, and quality control;
- distinguishing operational work like scheduling, publishing, and analytics;
- distinguishing administrative work like contracts, finance, and compliance;
- assigning each to roles with the right skills and decision owners.

A role that mixes all four is a job description no one person can do well.

### Structure The Creator's Involvement Deliberately

The creator cannot do everything, and a team that routes every decision through them caps growth. Structure what the creator does and does not touch.

Structure involvement by:

- identifying what only the creator can do, like on-camera presence and final voice;
- delegating everything that does not require the creator uniquely;
- batching review and approval to protect deep work time;
- setting decision thresholds so minor choices do not require the creator;
- resisting the instinct to retain control over delegatable work.

The goal is to focus the creator where they are irreplaceable, not to remove them from the work.

### Design Roles To Reduce The Creator's Bottleneck

A team exists to add capacity, and the test of good role design is whether it reduces the creator's bottleneck. Design roles against that test.

Reduce bottleneck by:

- giving roles real ownership so work does not return to the creator;
- ensuring each role can produce output to standard without constant oversight;
- measuring whether the creator's involvement shrinks as the team grows;
- identifying and redesigning roles that still route through the creator;
- treating bottleneck reduction as the success metric for team design.

A team that reshapes the bottleneck without shrinking it has not added capacity.

### Document Roles So They Survive Team Changes

Roles that live in conversation or one person's head break when people change. Document roles so the structure survives turnover and scaling.

Document by:

- writing role descriptions, responsibilities, and decision rights;
- maintaining them as living documents that evolve;
- onboarding new members against documented roles;
- reviewing roles as the team and channel grow;
- ensuring no role depends on undocumented tribal knowledge.

Documented roles let the team scale and recover from changes without rebuilding from scratch.

## Common Traps

### Person-Shaped Roles

Defining roles around current people rather than responsibilities makes the structure collapse when anyone leaves.

### Unclear Decision Rights

Ambiguity about what each role can decide forces every choice back to the creator and stalls the workflow.

### Conflating Creative, Editorial, Operational, And Administrative

Mixing distinct functions into one role creates jobs too broad to fill and too vague to own.

### Routing Everything Through The Creator

A team that depends on the creator for every decision caps growth and burns out the very person the team exists to support.

### Reshaping The Bottleneck Without Shrinking It

Adding people who still route work through the creator changes the bottleneck's shape without adding real capacity.

### Undocumented Tribal Knowledge

Roles that live only in heads or conversations break under turnover and prevent clean onboarding.

### Overly Broad Helper Roles

Generic help roles like assist with content produce overlap and gaps because no one owns specific outcomes.

## Self-Check

- Each role is defined by the outcomes it owns, independent of the person currently filling it.
- Decision rights and approval thresholds are explicit for every role.
- Creative, editorial, operational, and administrative duties are separated appropriately.
- The creator's involvement is structured to focus where they are irreplaceable.
- Role design demonstrably reduces the creator's bottleneck rather than reshaping it.
- Roles are documented so the structure survives team changes and turnover.
- No role is so broad that it conflates functions requiring different skills.
- New members can be onboarded against documented role descriptions.
- Decision thresholds prevent minor choices from escalating to the creator.
- The team adds real capacity, not just more people for the creator to coordinate.
