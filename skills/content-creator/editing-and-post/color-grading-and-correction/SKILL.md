# Color Grading And Correction

Color work is split into two jobs that creators constantly confuse. Correction fixes the image so it is accurate, balanced, and consistent. Grading shapes the image so it carries a mood and a brand. The judgment problem is that editors jump to grading before correcting, so they style footage that is still broken underneath, producing looks that collapse on other screens or read as amateur because skin tones and exposure were never right. A great grade on an uncorrected image always fails in delivery.

This skill helps the agent separate the two jobs, fix the fundamentals first, build a consistent and intentional look, and validate that the color survives the platforms and devices viewers actually use.

## Core Rules

### Correct Before You Grade

Correction is the foundation. Grading a broken image amplifies the breakage. Establish an accurate, neutral, consistent baseline before any creative look is applied.

Correct first by:

- balancing exposure so nothing is crushed or blown unless intended;
- setting white balance so neutrals are neutral and skin reads naturally;
- fixing contrast and black and white points per clip;
- removing color casts from mixed light before styling.

If the corrected image already looks acceptable, the grade will hold. If it does not, no grade will save it.

### Match Shots Before Styling The Sequence

Inconsistency reads as amateur faster than any single bad shot. Viewers feel a jump in exposure or color between clips even when they cannot name it. Match the sequence before applying a global look.

Match by:

- balancing skin tones as the anchor across all shots of a subject;
- matching exposure and contrast between adjacent clips;
- using a reference frame to align shots in the same scene;
- applying scene-level corrections before any creative grade.

Consistency across the cut is more important than perfection in any one frame.

### Anchor On Skin Tones

Skin is the color viewers are most sensitive to. If skin reads wrong, green, orange, magenta, or dead, the whole image feels off no matter how stylized the background is. Skin is the reference that grounds a grade.

Protect skin by:

- using vectorscope or skin-tone line guides where available;
- correcting toward natural, believable skin before creative shifts;
- avoiding grades that push skin into unnatural hues;
- checking diverse skin tones separately, as one setting can flatter one and harm another.

A grade that ruins skin ruins the video. Different skin tones must each read naturally.

### Build One Intentional Look, Not Random Stylization

A look should be a deliberate choice that serves the content and the brand, not a collection of preset effects applied clip by clip. Random stylization produces a channel that feels incoherent.

Build a deliberate look by:

- defining the mood the content needs, warm, cool, contrasty, soft;
- creating a reusable grade or LUT for consistency across episodes;
- applying the look after correction and matching, not instead of them;
- keeping the look subtle enough that content stays primary.

The look should support the message, not compete with it.

### Protect Detail In Shadows And Highlights

Stylish crushed blacks and blown highlights look striking on a calibrated monitor and fall apart on phones, in bright daylight, or after platform compression. Detail lost in the grade cannot be recovered by the viewer's screen.

Protect detail by:

- keeping subtle detail in shadows unless a crush is intentional;
- avoiding pure-white highlights that blow out on bright screens;
- checking the grade on a phone in normal lighting;
- accounting for platform compression that deepens blacks and flattens contrast.

Grade for the worst reasonable viewing condition, not the best editing monitor.

### Match The Grade To Platform Delivery

Platforms do not display color identically. Some shift contrast, some flatten, some apply their own processing. A grade tuned only for the edit suite can look washed or crushed after upload.

Account for delivery by:

- exporting and re-watching an uploaded reference before finalizing;
- comparing your upload to reference creators on the same platform;
- avoiding extreme contrast or saturation that compression will exaggerate;
- keeping captions, UI, and safe areas legible against the graded image.

The grade is not finished until it has been seen post-upload.

### Keep The Look Consistent Across A Channel

A channel whose look shifts episode to episode feels unprofessional and weakens brand recognition. Consistency of grade is part of the channel's identity.

Maintain consistency by:

- reusing a base grade or LUT across episodes;
- documenting the grade so it can be reproduced;
- keeping correction standards stable as gear or locations change;
- evolving the look deliberately rather than by accident.

Viewers should recognize the channel's look within the first few seconds.

### Know When Good Enough Is Good Enough

Not every project needs a cinematic grade. Over-grading a casual channel can feel stiff and waste time that would be better spent on script or pacing. Match color effort to where it changes audience perception.

Calibrate effort by:

- prioritizing correction and consistency over elaborate grading for most content;
- reserving heavy grading for projects where mood is the product;
- not spending hours on looks the audience will not notice;
- shipping a clean, consistent image over a delayed perfect one.

A clean, consistent cut beats an overworked, delayed one.

## Common Traps

### Grading Before Correcting

Styling footage that is still mis-exposed or mis-balanced amplifies the flaws and produces a look that collapses on other screens.

### Inconsistent Shots Across The Cut

When exposure or color jumps between clips, the sequence feels amateur even if each clip looks fine alone. Matching is more visible than perfection.

### Ruining Skin Tones With The Grade

Pushing skin into unnatural hues, or using one setting that flatters only one skin tone, makes the whole image feel wrong and can be exclusionary.

### Crushing Blacks And Blowing Highlights For Style

Detail lost to stylized extremes cannot be recovered on phones or after compression, and the image degrades in real viewing conditions.

### Tuning Only For The Edit Monitor

A grade that looks perfect on a calibrated monitor can wash out or crush after platform upload. Not validating post-upload ships a different video than the one approved.

### Random Preset Stylization

Applying different looks clip by clip produces an incoherent channel identity and signals amateurism.

### Over-Grading Casual Content

Spending heavy effort on cinematic grading for a casual, personality-driven channel can make it feel stiff and delay shipping without changing audience perception.

### Ignoring Diverse Skin Tones

A single grade setting can flatter lighter skin and harm darker skin, or vice versa. Failing to check each tone separately produces exclusionary, unnatural results.

## Self-Check

- Exposure, white balance, and contrast were corrected on every clip before any grade was applied.
- Shots across each scene are matched for exposure, contrast, and skin tone before styling.
- Skin tones read natural and believable, and diverse skin tones were each checked separately.
- The look is one deliberate, reusable choice rather than random preset stylization.
- Shadow and highlight detail survives on a phone and in bright viewing conditions.
- The grade was validated after upload, not only on the editing monitor.
- The look is consistent across episodes and documented so it can be reproduced.
- Color effort matches the project, correction and consistency prioritized over elaborate grading for casual content.
- Captions, UI, and safe areas remain legible against the graded image.
- The finished image is clean and consistent rather than overworked and delayed.
