# Doxxing And Harassment Defense

Public creators attract hostility, and the hostility can escalate from comments to coordinated campaigns that threaten safety, finances, and mental health. The judgment problem is that harassment is treated as an emotional or reputational issue to be endured, when it is an operational and safety risk that requires preparation, triage, and a response plan built before the attack arrives. Doxxing, swatting, and coordinated pile-ons are not random bad luck; they are predictable risks for visible creators, and the difference between surviving and being destroyed by them is whether defenses and response plans existed beforehand.

The harm is severe and cascading. Doxxing exposes the creator and their family to in-person danger. Swatting can cause armed police response. Coordinated harassment drives the creator offline, damages mental health, and can trigger platform action against the creator rather than the attackers. Financial harassment targets income through mass reporting and advertiser pressure. Responding from crisis, without preparation, leads to decisions that escalate the situation or expose more information. This skill helps the agent treat harassment as a foreseeable operational risk, build defenses before attacks, triage and respond during campaigns, protect safety first, and recover afterward without compounding the harm.

## Core Rules

### Treat Harassment As A Foreseeable Operational Risk

Harassment is not a freak event for visible creators; it is a predictable risk that should be prepared for like any other. Treating it as unforeseeable leaves the creator defenseless when it arrives.

- recognize visibility increases harassment risk, especially for marginalized creators;
- prepare defenses and response plans before they are needed;
- treat harassment preparation as part of operating publicly, not paranoia;
- revisit defenses as the creator's visibility and risk change.

### Build Defenses Before An Attack

Effective defense is built in calm, not in crisis. The protections that matter most must be in place before an attack, because during one there is no time to set them up.

- lock down personal information and remove doxxable details proactively;
- secure accounts with strong authentication to resist takeover during attacks;
- set up moderation, filtering, and blocking tools in advance;
- identify support people, resources, and escalation contacts beforehand.

### Prioritize Physical Safety First

In doxxing and swatting, physical safety is the first priority, above reputation, response, or content. No online response matters more than preventing physical harm.

- treat doxxing and swatting as immediate safety threats requiring urgent action;
- have a plan for relocating or securing the home if exposed;
- inform local authorities of swatting risk where relevant;
- prioritize safety over continuing to engage with the attackers.

### Triage And Avoid Feeding The Attack

During a coordinated attack, the instinct to respond and defend often feeds the harassment. Triage means protecting safety and not giving attackers the engagement they seek.

- avoid responding in ways that escalate or provide new material;
- delegate monitoring and response to trusted others where possible;
- distinguish real threats from noise and prioritize accordingly;
- recognize that silence and disengagement are often the strongest response.

### Use Platform Tools And Mass-Reporting Defenses

Harassment campaigns often use mass reporting to trigger platform action against the creator. Use platform tools to defend and to reduce exposure.

- enable available filtering, blocking, and comment moderation;
- report coordinated abuse through platform channels designed for it;
- be aware that mass reporting can trigger automated action against the target;
- keep records of the harassment to support appeals and reports.

### Protect Income From Financial Harassment

Harassment campaigns sometimes target a creator's income through advertiser pressure, mass reporting, and payment platform complaints. Protect income streams proactively.

- diversify income so a single channel cannot be severed by a campaign;
- be cautious about advertiser and sponsor exposure to controversy;
- have direct audience support channels that bypass advertiser pressure;
- monitor monetization and payment accounts for unusual activity during attacks.

### Document Everything For Legal And Platform Action

Documentation supports reports, appeals, and potential legal action. Keep records systematically, because reconstructing after the fact is difficult.

- screenshot and archive threats, doxxing posts, and coordinated activity;
- record dates, sources, and patterns of the harassment;
- preserve evidence before platforms or attackers delete it;
- keep documentation organized for use in reports and legal contexts.

### Plan For Recovery And Mental Health Support

Harassment causes lasting harm to mental health and wellbeing, and recovery must be planned, not improvised. Treat the aftermath as part of the response.

- arrange mental health support before, during, and after attacks;
- allow time away from platforms to recover;
- debrief and update defenses based on what happened;
- recognize that recovery is part of the operational response, not optional.

## Common Traps

### Treating Harassment As Unforeseeable

Visible creators face predictable harassment risk, and failing to prepare leaves them defenseless when it arrives.

### No Defenses Built Before Crisis

Protections set up during an attack are too late, because the most important defenses require calm preparation.

### Responding In Ways That Feed The Attack

Defensive responses often provide attackers the engagement and material they seek, escalating rather than reducing harm.

### Prioritizing Reputation Over Physical Safety

In doxxing and swatting, online reputation matters less than preventing physical harm, and reversing these priorities is dangerous.

### Ignoring Financial Harassment

Campaigns that target income through advertiser and payment pressure can sever revenue unless income is diversified.

### Mass Reporting Triggering Action Against The Target

Coordinated mass reporting can cause platforms to act against the creator, so awareness and documentation defenses matter.

### No Documentation Ready For Action

Without archived evidence, reports and appeals fail because the harassment cannot be reconstructed.

### Skipping Recovery And Mental Health Support

Treating only the immediate attack and ignoring the lasting mental health harm leaves the creator vulnerable to the next one.

## Self-Check

- Is harassment treated as a foreseeable operational risk with defenses built before an attack?
- Are personal information, accounts, and moderation tools locked down proactively?
- In doxxing or swatting, is physical safety prioritized above online response?
- Is there a triage plan to avoid feeding the attack with escalatory responses?
- Are platform tools and mass-reporting defenses in place and understood?
- Is income diversified so a single channel cannot be severed by a financial campaign?
- Is harassment documented systematically with screenshots, dates, and sources?
- Are support people, resources, and escalation contacts identified in advance?
- Is mental health support planned for during and after an attack?
- Have defenses been updated based on lessons from any past incidents?
