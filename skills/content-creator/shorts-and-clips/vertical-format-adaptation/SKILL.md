# Vertical Format Adaptation

Vertical is not horizontal rotated. It is a different viewing surface with different attention, different safe areas, and different composition rules. The judgment problem is that creators reformat horizontal footage for vertical by cropping the center, then discover that faces are cut, text collides with platform UI, and the result feels cramped and amateur. Worse, they assume one vertical export works across all vertical platforms, when each platform overlays different UI that can bury the subject or the message. Vertical adaptation is a design problem, not a resize operation.

This skill helps the agent adapt footage for 9:16 viewing, place subjects and text where they survive platform UI, and avoid the crop-and-hope approach that wastes good content.

## Core Rules

### Treat Vertical As A Different Composition, Not A Crop

A horizontal frame and a vertical frame distribute attention differently. The center crop of a horizontal shot often removes the most important elements and leaves a cramped, headless, or contextless image. Adaptation requires recomposition, not just resizing.

Recompose for vertical by:

- reframing the subject to sit in the vertical safe zone, not the horizontal center;
- shooting or selecting footage with vertical delivery in mind;
- using multiple vertical reframes rather than one blind center crop;
- accepting that some horizontal footage cannot be salvaged vertically.

If the subject or emotion does not survive the reframe, the footage is not vertical-ready.

### Design For The Platform's Safe Areas

Vertical platforms overlay UI, captions, buttons, and usernames on top of the video. Content placed in those zones is buried or obscured. Each platform's safe area differs, and assuming one layout works everywhere guarantees breakage.

Respect safe areas by:

- keeping faces, text, and key action in the central vertical safe zone;
- learning each target platform's specific UI overlay layout;
- avoiding the bottom and side edges where captions and buttons sit;
- testing the export on the actual platform, not just in the editor.

The safe area is the only region the viewer reliably sees. Design within it.

### Place Text Where It Survives UI Overlays

Text is the most common casualty of vertical adaptation. Captions, descriptions, and callouts placed near edges get covered by platform UI or by the platform's own auto-captions. Text must be placed defensively.

Place text defensively by:

- keeping essential text in the central safe zone, away from all edges;
- avoiding the bottom third where platform captions typically appear;
- sizing text large enough to read on a phone held at arm's length;
- not duplicating platform auto-captions with your own overlapping text.

If the platform can cover it, assume it will, and move it.

### Keep Faces And Emotion In The Safe Zone

Faces and emotion drive clicks and retention, and in vertical they are easily cropped out of frame or buried under UI. The subject's eyes and expression must land in the region the viewer actually sees.

Protect the subject by:

- reframing so faces sit in the central vertical zone, not the top or bottom;
- ensuring eyes are never near the top edge where they get cropped;
- avoiding headroom that pushes the face into UI territory;
- checking that emotion remains visible after the reframe.

A face cut off at the chin or buried under a button reads as amateur instantly.

### Use Captions As A Core Element, Not An Afterthought

Most short-form vertical video is watched with sound off or in environments where speech is hard to hear. Captions are not accessibility decoration, they are a primary delivery channel. Uncaptioned vertical video loses a large share of viewers.

Caption effectively by:

- adding accurate, readable captions to all spoken content;
- styling captions for legibility, large, high-contrast, well-placed;
- timing captions to appear with the words, not in blocks;
- using captions to reinforce emphasis and key terms.

If the video depends on spoken words and has no captions, it is unfinished for vertical.

### Avoid Cramped Or Cluttered Vertical Frames

Vertical frames have less horizontal room, so the same number of elements that work horizontally become cluttered vertically. Crowded vertical frames confuse the eye and dilute the focal point.

Reduce clutter by:

- limiting the frame to one clear focal subject;
- removing or simplifying background elements that compete;
- using negative space to let the subject breathe;
- not stacking multiple text elements, graphics, and stickers at once.

Vertical rewards simplicity. Every added element shrinks the space for the subject.

### Match Aspect Ratio To Each Platform's Preference

Vertical is not uniform. Some platforms favor 9:16, some crop to other ratios, and some display differently in different surfaces. Exporting one ratio and hoping it fits everywhere causes unexpected cropping.

Match ratios by:

- exporting 9:16 for full vertical feeds where that is the norm;
- checking how each platform displays the ratio in its main feed;
- providing square or alternative ratios where a platform prefers them;
- not assuming one export serves every vertical destination.

The platform decides the visible frame. Design for the frame it actually uses.

### Test On A Real Phone In Real Conditions

Vertical video is almost always watched on a phone, often in bright light, often with one hand, often with sound off. An adaptation that looks right on a desktop editor can fail in all of these conditions. The phone is the only honest test surface.

Test by:

- watching the export on a phone at normal brightness, including outdoors;
- checking captions and text legibility in bright light;
- confirming sound-off comprehension through captions alone;
- viewing it in the actual platform app with its real UI overlay.

If it has not been seen on a phone in the platform app, it has not been tested.

## Common Traps

### Blind Center Cropping

Cropping the center of a horizontal frame removes the subject or context and produces a cramped, headless, or confusing vertical image.

### Ignoring Platform UI Overlays

Placing faces, text, or action in zones the platform covers with buttons and captions buries the very elements that earn attention.

### Text Placed Where Captions Cover It

Essential text placed in the bottom third collides with platform auto-captions and becomes unreadable.

### Faces Cropped Or Buried

Reframes that cut the chin, push the face under a button, or lose the eyes destroy the emotion that drives retention.

### Skipping Captions Altogether

Vertical video without captions loses the large share of viewers who watch with sound off or cannot hear speech clearly.

### Cluttered Vertical Frames

Fitting horizontal-density elements into a narrower frame creates visual noise with no clear focal point.

### One Export For Every Platform

Assuming a single aspect ratio serves all vertical platforms causes unexpected cropping and buried content on platforms that display differently.

### Testing Only On The Desktop Editor

An adaptation that looks right on a monitor can fail in bright phone light, with sound off, under the real platform UI.

## Self-Check

- The subject was recomposed for the vertical safe zone rather than blind center cropped.
- Faces, text, and key action sit in the central zone each target platform leaves visible.
- Essential text avoids the bottom third and edges where platform captions and UI appear.
- The subject's eyes and emotion remain visible and uncropped after the reframe.
- Accurate, readable, well-timed captions are present on all spoken content.
- The vertical frame is simple, with one clear focal subject and breathing negative space.
- The aspect ratio matches each target platform's preferred display, not a single export.
- The export was watched on a real phone at normal brightness, including outdoors.
- Sound-off comprehension was verified through captions alone.
- The video was viewed inside the actual platform app with its real UI overlay before publishing.
