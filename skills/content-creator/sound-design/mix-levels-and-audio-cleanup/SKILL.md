# Mix Levels And Audio Cleanup

The mix is the final judge of whether content sounds professional, and it is where most audio problems become permanent. The judgment problem is that creators treat mixing as turning levels up and cleanup as running a noise-reduction plugin, then ship audio that is noisy, inconsistent, harsh, or inaudible on the devices listeners actually use. Good mixing is a balance of voice, music, and effects against a clean noise floor, targeted to a loudness standard and validated on real playback systems. Cleanup that is too aggressive damages the voice, and mixing that ignores delivery loudness produces content that is either too quiet or distorted on platforms. This is the stage where technical care directly determines perceived quality.

This skill helps the agent clean audio without damaging it, balance the elements of a mix, target the right loudness, and validate the result across devices.

## Core Rules

### Clean Audio Gently To Avoid Damaging The Voice

Cleanup tools, noise reduction, de-essing, mouth-click removal, are powerful and dangerous. Applied too aggressively they create artifacts, watery distortion, thinning, and lisp, that sound worse than the original noise. Cleanup should be surgical, not heavy-handed.

Clean gently by:

- reducing noise just enough that it stops distracting, not to silence;
- using light settings and multiple passes rather than one heavy pass;
- preserving the natural character of the voice over total noise removal;
- comparing before and after to catch introduced artifacts.

A little residual noise always beats a voice destroyed by over-processing.

### Address Noise And Echo At The Source Where Possible

Some problems cannot be fixed in post. Echo from a hard room, wind noise, and clipping from too-hot recording are baked in and only partially recoverable. The best cleanup happens before recording.

Prevent at the source by:

- recording in a treated, soft-surfaced room to reduce echo;
- using proper mic placement and a windscreen for outdoor recording;
- setting gain correctly to avoid clipping and excessive noise floor;
- capturing room tone so the editor can match backgrounds in cuts.

Source control is cheaper and better than any plugin. Fix what you can before recording.

### Balance Voice, Music, And Effects Deliberately

A good mix is a hierarchy, voice first, music and effects supporting. Creators who mix all elements to similar levels produce muddy, competing audio where nothing is clear. Establish the hierarchy intentionally.

Balance by:

- keeping voice clearly on top as the primary element;
- ducking music under speech so words stay intelligible;
- setting effects to sit beneath voice and within the music's space;
- automating level rides so balance holds across dynamic sections.

The listener should never have to work to hear the voice. Everything else supports it.

### Target A Consistent Loudness Standard

Listeners expect content to sit at a predictable volume, and platforms increasingly normalize loudness. Mixing to wildly different levels forces listeners to adjust and can trigger platform normalization that changes your mix. Target a consistent standard.

Target loudness by:

- mixing to an integrated loudness appropriate for the content type and platform;
- matching loudness across episodes so listeners never reach for the volume;
- controlling peaks to avoid distortion and to meet platform limits;
- understanding that platform normalization may turn down overly loud mixes.

Consistency of loudness is part of professional delivery. Pick a target and hit it.

### Use Compression And EQ To Control Dynamics And Tone

Raw voice varies widely in level and often needs tonal shaping. Compression tames dynamics so quiet and loud moments sit together, and EQ removes problematic frequencies and adds clarity. Both should serve intelligibility.

Shape the voice by:

- using gentle compression to even out level variations without pumping;
- cutting muddy low frequencies and harsh highs rather than boosting broadly;
- de-essing to control sibilance without lisping the voice;
- matching processing across participants in multi-voice content.

Processing should make the voice clearer and more consistent, not obviously processed.

### Match And Smooth Levels Across Takes And Participants

In edited content, levels jump between takes, segments, and speakers. These jumps are audible and amateur. Smoothing levels across the whole piece is a core mixing job.

Smooth levels by:

- normalizing or gain-staging each clip to a consistent baseline;
- automating rides for sections that drift louder or quieter;
- matching perceived loudness, not just peak levels, across participants;
- checking transitions between takes for audible level jumps.

Consistent levels across the whole piece read as professional. Jumps read as careless.

### Validate The Mix On Real Playback Systems

A mix that sounds great on studio monitors can fail on phones, earbuds, car speakers, and laptop speakers, which is where most listening happens. The studio is not the final judge, real devices are.

Validate by:

- checking the mix on phone and laptop speakers where small drivers mask bass;
- listening on earbuds to catch harshness and sibilance;
- checking in a car for overall balance and loudness;
- confirming speech intelligibility on the worst reasonable playback system.

If it does not work on a phone, it does not work for most of the audience.

### Check For And Fix Common Audio Flaws

A short pre-publish check catches the flaws that ruin perceived quality, plosives, mouth clicks, sudden level jumps, music that drowns a line, and noise that enters mid-clip. A disciplined final pass prevents shipping avoidable problems.

Check for:

- plosives and mouth noise on the voice;
- abrupt level changes at cuts and between speakers;
- music or effects that obscure key lines;
- background noise that changes between clips;
- clipping, distortion, and digital errors.

A final flaw-check is cheap insurance against shipping amateur-sounding audio.

## Common Traps

### Over-Aggressive Noise Reduction

Heavy cleanup destroys the voice with watery artifacts and thinning that sound worse than the original noise.

### Trying To Fix Unfixable Source Problems

Echo, wind, and clipping are baked in and only partially recoverable, and over-processing to hide them causes more harm.

### Competing Element Levels

Mixing voice, music, and effects to similar levels produces muddy audio where nothing is clearly intelligible.

### Ignoring Loudness Standards

Wildly varying or excessive loudness forces listener adjustment and triggers platform normalization that alters the mix.

### Over-Compressing Or Heavy EQ

Too much compression pumps and lifelessens the voice, and broad EQ boosts introduce harshness or muddiness.

### Level Jumps Across Takes And Speakers

Unsmoothed levels between clips and participants read as careless and amateur.

### Mixing Only On Studio Monitors

A mix that works on monitors can fail on phones and earbuds where most listening actually happens.

### Skipping The Final Flaw Check

Plosives, mouth clicks, and obscuring music slip through when no disciplined pre-publish pass is done.

## Self-Check

- Noise reduction was applied gently to avoid watery artifacts and voice damage.
- Echo, noise, and clipping were addressed at the recording source where possible.
- Voice sits clearly on top, with music ducked and effects beneath in a deliberate hierarchy.
- The mix targets a consistent integrated loudness appropriate for the platform and content type.
- Compression and EQ serve intelligibility without sounding obviously processed.
- Levels are smoothed across takes and participants with no audible jumps.
- The mix was validated on phone, earbud, laptop, and car playback systems.
- Speech remains intelligible on the worst reasonable playback system.
- A final flaw check caught plosives, mouth clicks, level jumps, and obscuring music before publish.
- Loudness is consistent with prior episodes so listeners never reach for the volume.
