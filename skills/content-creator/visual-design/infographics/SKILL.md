# Infographics

Infographics and data visualizations turn complex information into something the audience can grasp quickly. The judgment problem is that creators treat infographics as decoration for data, when in fact visualization is a form of argument. Every chart type, color choice, axis, and label makes a claim about what the data means. Misleading charts, distorted axes, and decorative clutter do not just look bad, they communicate falsehoods, erode credibility, and spread misinformation that the audience acts on.

The harm is reputational and ethical. A chart with a truncated y-axis exaggerates a trend and misleads. A decorative infographic that obscures the data wastes the audience's attention. A visualization that the audience cannot read on mobile fails entirely. A shareable but dishonest chart spreads false conclusions that are hard to retract. This skill helps the agent choose visual forms that honestly represent the data, simplify without distorting, and design for comprehension and accurate sharing.

## Core Rules

### Match The Chart Type To The Data And The Question

Different chart types answer different questions. Choosing the wrong type distorts meaning. Match the visualization to what the audience needs to understand.

Match chart to purpose:

- comparison across categories, use bar charts;
- change over time, use line or area charts;
- part of a whole, use stacked bars or, rarely, pie charts;
- distribution, use histograms or box plots;
- relationship between variables, use scatter plots;
- flow or process, use flowcharts or diagrams.

A pie chart for change over time, or a line chart for unrelated categories, misleads. Start from the question, then choose the form.

### Preserve Data Honesty Above Aesthetics

The first duty of a data visualization is honesty. No aesthetic choice justifies distorting what the data actually shows. Visual decisions must preserve accurate perception.

Preserve honesty by:

- starting bar chart axes at zero unless there is a defensible reason not to;
- avoiding truncated y-axes that exaggerate differences;
- using consistent scales across compared charts;
- not using 3D effects that distort perception of values;
- sizing elements proportionally to the values they represent;
- labeling clearly so the audience is not forced to infer.

A chart that looks dramatic by distorting scale is not a better chart, it is a misleading one. Honesty constrains design.

### Simplify Without Distorting

Simplification helps the audience grasp complex information, but it must not cross into distortion. The line between helpful simplification and misleading reduction must be held deliberately.

Simplify safely by:

- removing non-essential detail while keeping the core signal;
- grouping related categories rather than dropping them silently;
- rounding only where precision is not material;
- annotating assumptions and omissions explicitly;
- avoiding the removal of data points that change the conclusion.

Simplification that hides inconvenient data is distortion. Simplification that clarifies without changing meaning is good design.

### Design For Comprehension Before Decoration

An infographic must be understood before it is admired. Decoration that impedes comprehension defeats the purpose. Establish clarity first, then add visual interest that supports it.

Prioritize comprehension by:

- leading with a clear title that states the takeaway;
- using labels directly on elements where possible;
- choosing high contrast for readability;
- limiting the color palette to what encodes meaning;
- removing chart junk, heavy borders, shadows, and ornaments.

If the audience cannot quickly grasp the point, the visualization has failed regardless of how it looks. Clarity is the design goal.

### Make Takeaways Explicit

Audiences should not have to reverse-engineer the conclusion from the chart. State the takeaway clearly so the visualization reinforces a message rather than leaving interpretation open.

Make takeaways explicit by:

- writing a title that states the finding, not just the topic;
- adding annotation that highlights the key point;
- using color to draw attention to the important element;
- summarizing the implication in a caption or callout;
- ensuring the visual emphasis matches the verbal claim.

A chart titled "Revenue by Quarter" leaves the work to the reader. A chart titled "Revenue Doubled in Q3 After Launch" delivers the message.

### Design For The Platform And Mobile

Infographics are often consumed on phones and shared across platforms. A visualization designed only for desktop viewing fails where most of the audience encounters it.

Design for context by:

- ensuring legibility at mobile screen width;
- using vertical-friendly layouts for feed consumption;
- testing whether text and labels remain readable when small;
- avoiding layouts that break when cropped or resized;
- creating platform-appropriate aspect ratios.

A detailed landscape infographic that becomes illegible on a phone reaches no one. Design for the smallest common display.

### Cite Sources And Show Methodology

Credible data visualization cites its sources and is transparent about methodology. Without this, the audience cannot assess reliability, and the visualization becomes an assertion.

Build credibility by:

- naming the data source clearly;
- dating the data and noting the time period covered;
- noting sample size and methodology where relevant;
- linking to the underlying data where possible;
- distinguishing the creator's analysis from the raw data.

Visualization without sources reads as opinion. Visualization with sources builds authority and invites verification.

### Avoid Misleading Shareability

Infographics are built to be shared, which amplifies any inaccuracy. A misleading but shareable chart spreads false conclusions faster than a correction can travel.

Guard against misleading sharing by:

- checking that the chart remains honest when detached from its context;
- ensuring the title and visual agree, with no bait;
- avoiding cherry-picked time frames or comparisons;
- testing whether the takeaway holds under scrutiny;
- being willing to simplify or not publish if honesty cannot be preserved.

Shareability is a multiplier. It multiplies accuracy and inaccuracy alike.

## Common Traps

### Wrong Chart Type For The Question

Using a chart type that does not match the data or question distorts meaning. Start from the question.

### Truncated Or Distorted Axes

Axes manipulated to exaggerate differences mislead the audience and damage credibility.

### Decoration Over Comprehension

Visual ornament that impedes understanding defeats the purpose of the visualization.

### Hidden Simplifications

Grouping or omitting data that changes the conclusion is distortion, not simplification.

### Implicit Takeaways

Forcing the audience to infer the conclusion reduces impact and invites misreading.

### Desktop-Only Design

Infographics that fail on mobile reach a fraction of their potential audience.

### Unsourced Visualizations

Data presented without sources or methodology reads as assertion and cannot be verified.

### Shareable But Dishonest

Charts designed to spread that distort the truth amplify misinformation faster than corrections can travel.

## Self-Check

Before approving an infographic or data visualization, verify:

- The chart type matches the data and the question the audience needs answered.
- Axes, scales, and proportions preserve data honesty and do not exaggerate.
- Simplification clarifies without distorting or hiding inconvenient data.
- Comprehension is established before decoration, with chart junk removed.
- The takeaway is stated explicitly in the title or annotation, not left implicit.
- The design is legible and effective on mobile, not only desktop.
- Sources, dates, and methodology are cited so the audience can verify.
- The chart remains honest when shared detached from its original context.
- Color encodes meaning rather than decorating arbitrarily.
- No cherry-picked frames or comparisons mislead the audience.
