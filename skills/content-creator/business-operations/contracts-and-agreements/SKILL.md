# Contracts And Agreements

Contracts are where creators give away rights, money, and control without realizing it. The judgment problem is that contracts are treated as formalities to sign quickly, when they are the document that defines who owns what, who pays what, what happens when something goes wrong, and what the creator is allowed to do afterward. A sponsorship agreement that looks like a simple deal can lock exclusivity, transfer usage rights, impose content approval, and bind the creator to obligations that outlast the payment. Creators sign from eagerness and asymmetry, and the harm is realized only when the terms are enforced.

The harm is concrete and often irreversible. Broad usage rights let a brand reuse the creator's work and likeness indefinitely. Exclusivity clauses block better opportunities for months. Vague scope leads to unpaid revisions and scope creep. Payment terms let brands delay or withhold payment. Termination and morality clauses let a brand walk away while keeping the work. This skill helps the agent read contracts as risk documents, identify the clauses that actually transfer value, negotiate the terms that matter, and ensure a creator never signs an agreement they do not understand or cannot live with.

## Core Rules

### Read Every Contract As A Risk Document

A contract is not a formality; it allocates risk, rights, and obligations. Read it to find what the creator is giving up, not just what they are being paid.

- identify every clause that transfers rights, money, or control;
- flag obligations that survive after the contract ends;
- check what happens if either side fails to deliver;
- confirm the deal described matches the deal discussed.

### Scrutinize Usage Rights And Ownership

Usage rights define who can use the content, where, for how long, and in what form. Broad grants let brands reuse the creator's work and likeness far beyond the original deal.

- confirm whether the creator retains ownership of the content;
- limit usage to specific channels, regions, and time periods;
- restrict edits and re-edits of the creator's work and likeness;
- avoid perpetual, unlimited, or transferable rights unless heavily compensated.

### Bound Exclusivity Narrowly

Exclusivity clauses prevent the creator from working with competitors, and broad exclusivity blocks better opportunities for the entire term. Bound it tightly.

- limit exclusivity to direct competitors in a defined category;
- set a clear time window that ends soon after the campaign;
- resist category-wide or indefinite exclusivity;
- negotiate carve-outs for existing and pre-existing relationships.

### Define Scope And Revisions Precisely

Vague scope is the source of unpaid work and scope creep. Define exactly what the creator delivers and how many revisions are included.

- specify deliverables, format, length, and number of assets;
- define the number of revision rounds included in the fee;
- set the fee and terms for additional revisions or deliverables;
- clarify whether concept, scripting, and posting are included or separate.

### Lock Payment Terms Before Starting Work

Payment terms determine whether and when the creator actually gets paid. Vague or back-loaded terms let brands delay payment until long after delivery.

- require a deposit or milestone payment before starting work;
- define net payment terms and late payment penalties;
- specify currency, payment method, and who pays fees and taxes;
- tie final payment to delivery, not to the brand's internal approval.

### Check Termination And Morality Clauses

Termination clauses define how the deal ends, and morality clauses let brands exit while keeping the work. These clauses often favor the brand heavily.

- confirm whether the creator can terminate and on what terms;
- check what happens to payment and rights if the brand terminates;
- scrutinize morality clauses for vague or one-sided standards;
- ensure termination does not let the brand keep rights without paying.

### Require Content Approval Boundaries

Brands often demand approval rights over content, and unbounded approval lets them control the creator's voice and force endless changes. Bound the approval process.

- limit approval to factual brand claims, not creative voice;
- set a response window so silence counts as approval;
- cap the number of approval rounds;
- resist approval rights that extend to the creator's other content.

### Get Legal Review For High-Stakes Or Unfamiliar Terms

For long-term, high-value, or unusual agreements, professional legal review earns its cost in avoided mistakes. Do not rely on the brand's lawyer or the creator's intuition.

- engage a lawyer for multi-year, exclusive, or high-value deals;
- have unfamiliar clauses explained in plain language before signing;
- never assume a brand's template is standard or non-negotiable;
- treat legal review as protection of rights the creator cannot recover once signed.

## Common Traps

### Signing From Eagerness And Asymmetry

Creators sign quickly out of excitement and because brands hold more leverage, giving away terms they would never accept in calmer moments.

### Broad Or Perpetual Usage Rights

Grants that let brands reuse content and likeness indefinitely transfer long-term value for a one-time fee.

### Category-Wide Or Indefinite Exclusivity

Broad exclusivity blocks better opportunities for the whole term, costing the creator far more than the deal pays.

### Vague Scope Inviting Scope Creep

Undefined deliverables and unlimited revisions turn a fixed-fee deal into unpaid labor.

### Back-Loaded Payment Terms

Terms that pay only at the end, or after brand approval, let brands delay or withhold payment long after the work is done.

### One-Sided Morality Clauses

Vague morality clauses let brands terminate while keeping the work, exposing the creator to arbitrary exit.

### Unbounded Brand Approval Rights

Approval rights that extend to creative voice let brands control the creator's content and force endless revisions.

### Trusting The Brand's Template As Standard

Brand templates are drafted to favor the brand, and assuming they are standard or non-negotiable concedes terms the creator could have changed.

## Self-Check

- Has the contract been read as a risk document identifying every transfer of rights, money, or control?
- Are usage rights limited to specific channels, regions, and time periods, with ownership retained where possible?
- Is exclusivity bounded to direct competitors for a clear, short window with carve-outs?
- Are deliverables, revision rounds, and additional-work fees defined precisely?
- Are payment terms locked before work starts, with a deposit and defined net terms?
- Do termination and morality clauses treat the creator fairly, with payment protected on brand exit?
- Are brand approval rights limited to factual claims with a response window and capped rounds?
- Has legal review been arranged for high-value, long-term, or unfamiliar agreements?
- Does the written deal match the deal that was discussed?
- Could the creator live with every clause if the relationship went badly?
