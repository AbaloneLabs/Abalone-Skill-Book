# Seasonal Planning

Seasonal planning is the practice of aligning content production and release with recurring annual cycles: holidays, industry events, product launch seasons, back-to-school, tax season, weather cycles, and cultural moments. The judgment problem is that seasonal windows are short, fixed, and high-stakes. Miss the window and the content is irrelevant for a full year; over-invest in one window and you starve the rest of the calendar; and the production lead time for seasonal content is almost always underestimated. The harm of poor seasonal planning is opportunity cost measured in years: a strong evergreen piece released in the wrong week underperforms, and a weak piece forced into a peak window wastes the multiplier. Good seasonal planning treats the year as a map of fixed high-value peaks separated by valleys, and allocates production capacity so that the best content lands in the best windows with lead time to spare.

## Core Rules

### Map The Year Of Windows Before Planning Any Single Piece

Before planning individual videos, build a year-long map of every relevant seasonal window for your niche: holidays, industry events, product release cycles, school calendars, weather seasons, and recurring cultural moments. Score each window by relevance to your audience and by competition intensity. This map is the skeleton of your seasonal plan; everything else hangs off it. A plan made window-by-window without the full-year view over-invests in early windows and starves later ones.

### Score Relevance And Competition Separately

A window's value is relevance multiplied by competition. A high-relevance window that everyone targets (for example, generic holiday gift guides) may be lower real value than a medium-relevance window with low competition (a niche industry event). Score both axes for each window and prioritize windows where your relevance is high and competition is manageable. Do not chase the loudest window just because it is loud.

### Lock Lead Times From The Release Date Backward

Seasonal content has a hard release deadline, so plan backward from it. Estimate realistic production lead time (research, shoot, edit, sponsor review, thumbnail), add a buffer, and set the production start date accordingly. The most common seasonal failure is starting too late and shipping a rushed piece, or shipping after the window closed. Work backward, not forward from when you happen to start.

### Balance Seasonal Against Evergreen Across The Year

A calendar that is all seasonal is fragile: it spikes during peaks and starves during valleys, and the content decays in relevance after the window passes. Balance seasonal content with evergreen content that performs year-round and fills the valleys. Decide a target ratio (for many channels, roughly two-thirds evergreen to one-third seasonal) and audit the year against it. Evergreen content is the durable asset; seasonal content is the multiplier.

### Prepare For Counter-Cyclical Opportunities

Not every opportunity sits in the obvious peak. Counter-cyclical content (publishing tax software reviews in the post-tax-season lull, or fitness content in the "get ready for summer" pre-peak) can capture attention when competition is low. Identify windows where being early or late, rather than on-time, gives you an edge, and plan deliberately for them rather than defaulting to peak timing.

### Reuse And Refresh Seasonal Assets Year Over Year

Seasonal content has the valuable property of recurring annually. A strong holiday piece can be refreshed and re-released the next year with far less effort than producing from scratch. Tag seasonal content in your library, track its performance, and build a refresh plan so that each year's seasonal effort builds on prior years rather than starting over. This compounds advantage over time.

### Protect Against Window Drift And Moving Dates

Some seasonal windows move (Easter, lunar holidays, sporting events, product launch dates that shift year to year). Confirm the exact dates each year before locking the plan, and do not carry forward last year's dates uncritically. A plan built on a stale date ships into an empty window.

## Common Traps

### Treating December As The Only Seasonal Window

Many creators over-index on the year-end holiday window and ignore the rest of the year's seasonal opportunities. This is a trap because it concentrates risk in one window, competes against peak competition, and leaves the other eleven months under-planned. Map the full year and distribute seasonal investment.

### Starting Production Too Late For The Lead Time

The dominant seasonal failure is underestimating lead time and starting late. This is a trap because rushed seasonal content is weak, and content shipped after the window closes earns nothing for a year. Lock production start dates by working backward from release with a realistic lead time plus buffer.

### Forcing Weak Content Into A Peak Window

A peak window tempts creators to ship whatever they have, even if it is weak. This is a trap because weak content in a peak window underperforms and wastes the multiplier, and it can damage the channel's average performance signal. Only ship into a peak window content that is strong enough to compete; otherwise hold the slot for evergreen content.

### Ignoring Competition Intensity

A high-relevance window that every competitor targets can be net-negative because your content drowns. The trap is to look only at relevance and assume the audience is captive. Score competition and prefer windows where your edge is real, even if smaller.

### Letting Seasonal Content Cannibalize Evergreen Investment

Seasonal content is exciting and deadline-driven, so it tends to crowd out evergreen investment. This is a trap because evergreen content is the long-term asset that pays off across years, while seasonal content decays. Protect a minimum evergreen production ratio even during peak seasonal pressure.

### Reusing Last Year's Dates And Assumptions Without Checking

Seasonal windows drift, audiences change, and last year's winning angle may be stale. The trap is to copy last year's plan wholesale. Re-confirm dates, re-score relevance and competition, and refresh the creative angle each year.

## Self-Check

- Did I build a full-year map of seasonal windows with relevance and competition scores before planning individual pieces?
- Did I score relevance and competition separately and prioritize windows where my edge is real?
- Did I lock production start dates by working backward from release with realistic lead time plus buffer?
- Did I set and audit a target ratio of seasonal to evergreen content across the year?
- Did I identify counter-cyclical opportunities where being early or late gives an edge?
- Did I tag seasonal content for refresh and reuse in future years, with a refresh plan?
- Did I re-confirm moving window dates for the current year rather than carrying forward last year's dates?
- Did I avoid shipping weak content into a peak window just because the window is open?
