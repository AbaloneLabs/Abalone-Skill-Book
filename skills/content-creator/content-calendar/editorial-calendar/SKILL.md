# Editorial Calendar

An editorial calendar is the system that decides what gets published, when, and in what order across a period of weeks or months. The judgment problem is that a calendar looks like a simple grid of dates and titles, but it is actually a stack of competing constraints: cadence promises to the audience, algorithm regularity, production capacity, sponsorship deliverables, seasonal relevance, and content-mix balance. The harm of a bad calendar is invisible until it breaks you: you miss weeks because you over-promised cadence, you publish a heavy investigative piece next to a sponsored lightweight piece and cannibalize both, or you burn through ideas in a sprint and have nothing left. A good editorial calendar is not a list of ideas sorted by date; it is a deliberate allocation of finite production capacity against strategic priorities, with slack built in for the inevitable surprise.

## Core Rules

### Decide Cadence From Capacity, Not Ambition

Cadence is the single most important calendar decision and it is almost always set too high. Calculate sustainable cadence from realistic production capacity, not from what you think the algorithm rewards or what a competitor does. Build the calendar around a cadence you can hit on your worst week, not your best. It is far better to promise less and over-deliver than to promise more and miss; missed cadence damages the audience relationship and the algorithm more than a lower steady cadence does.

### Balance The Content Mix Deliberately

A healthy calendar mixes content types by intent: pillar content that defines the channel, growth content designed to reach new viewers, retention content that rewards core fans, and monetization content (sponsorships, product). Decide the target ratio of each (for example, half pillar, quarter growth, quarter other) and audit the calendar against that ratio before locking it. A calendar filled with one type, especially sponsored or trending content, starves the others and weakens the channel.

### Sequence By Energy And Context, Not Just By Date

The order of publications matters as much as the dates. A long demanding investigative piece drains the team and should not be followed immediately by another demanding piece. A sponsored video should not sit next to a sensitive or controversial piece that could reflect on the sponsor. A new-series launch should not compete with a planned viral one-off. Read the calendar as a sequence of energy and context, and reorder so that heavy pieces have recovery buffer and adjacent pieces do not clash.

### Protect Seasonal And Event Windows Early

Seasonal moments (holidays, back-to-school, industry events, product launch cycles) have fixed dates and high relevance multipliers. These windows must be locked into the calendar first, often months ahead, because the production lead time is long and the relevance penalty for missing them is severe. Build the rest of the calendar around these fixed points rather than trying to squeeze them in later.

### Build In Slack And A Buffer Library

Every calendar will be disrupted: a key person gets sick, a sponsor delays approval, a trend emerges. Build slack into the calendar as buffer slots and maintain a small library of evergreen, pre-produced pieces that can fill a gap on short notice. A calendar with zero slack is guaranteed to break; a calendar with one or two flex slots per month absorbs shocks without missing cadence.

### Separate Planning Horizon From Production Horizon

Use two horizons. Plan topics and themes on a rolling monthly or quarterly horizon so direction is stable. Lock production specifics (script, shoot dates, edit schedule) on a shorter two-to-four-week horizon so detail is fresh and accurate. Do not try to script a video three months out, and do not decide topics only a week out. The two-horizon split keeps strategy stable and execution realistic.

### Make Sponsorship Fit The Calendar, Not The Reverse

Sponsorships are deliverables with deadlines, but they must fit the calendar's logic, not override it. Negotiate sponsorship slots into the calendar where they belong by content type and cadence, and refuse or reschedule placements that would force back-to-back sponsored content or displace pillar pieces. A calendar that bends to every sponsor request loses its editorial integrity and its audience.

## Common Traps

### Setting Cadence Based On Launch-Week Energy

The classic trap is to set weekly cadence based on the energy and excitement of starting, then collapse within a month. This is a trap because the launch sprint is not sustainable and the inevitable miss damages trust. Set cadence from a realistic average week, including all the non-production work (admin, sponsorships, community).

### Filling The Calendar With Ideas You Have Not Validated

A calendar full of titles is not a calendar full of viable videos. The trap is to fill dates with ideas that sound good but have not been validated against audience interest, production feasibility, or sponsorship fit. Each slot should pass a viability check before it is locked, otherwise the calendar is fiction.

### Ignoring Production Lead Times

Different content types have wildly different lead times: a talking-head video might take a day, a documentary-style piece might take six weeks. The trap is to treat all slots as interchangeable and discover too late that a heavy piece cannot be ready for its date. Tag each calendar slot with its production class and lead time, and sequence accordingly.

### Letting The Algorithm Dictate Every Slot

Algorithm signals (best day to post, best time) are real but minor. The trap is to let them override strategic logic, scheduling every piece for the "optimal" slot and producing a calendar with no rhythm or intention. Use algorithm timing as a fine-tuning factor, not as the primary structuring principle.

### Cramming Sponsorships Into Already-Full Weeks

Sponsors want specific dates and the temptation is to slot them into busy weeks. This is a trap because sponsored content competes for attention with your own content and with the team's production capacity. Treat each sponsorship as a real production load and a real attention load, and space them out.

### Never Reviewing Or Revising The Calendar

A calendar set in stone is a trap because conditions change: a trend emerges, a video over- or under-performs, capacity shifts. Build a regular review cadence (weekly) where you adjust the next few weeks based on new information. A calendar that is never revised is either ignored or wrong.

## Self-Check

- Did I set cadence from realistic worst-week capacity rather than launch-week ambition, and can I sustain it on a bad week?
- Did I define a target content-mix ratio (pillar, growth, retention, monetization) and audit the calendar against it?
- Did I read the calendar as a sequence and ensure heavy pieces have recovery buffer and adjacent pieces do not clash?
- Did I lock seasonal and event windows first, with adequate lead time, before filling the rest?
- Did I build in slack: buffer slots and a small library of evergreen fillers for disruptions?
- Did I separate the monthly/quarterly planning horizon from the two-to-four-week production horizon?
- Did I make each sponsorship fit the calendar's logic rather than letting sponsor deadlines override cadence and mix?
- Did I tag each slot with its production class and lead time, and validate each idea for audience interest and feasibility before locking it?
