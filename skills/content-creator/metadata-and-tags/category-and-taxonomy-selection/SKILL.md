# Category And Taxonomy Selection

Categories and taxonomies are the structural layer that tells platforms and audiences where content belongs. The judgment problem is that creators treat categorization as a trivial dropdown choice, when it actually shapes which audiences the content is shown to, which recommendation buckets it enters, and what viewers expect before they even click. A miscategorized piece does not just underperform. It actively trains the platform to show it to the wrong people, generating bounces that suppress everything that follows.

The harm is structural mismatch. Content placed in the wrong category reaches an audience that did not want it, signals low relevance, and gets buried. Content with no coherent internal taxonomy fragments a channel so the platform cannot learn what it is about. Internal taxonomies that are too granular become unusable, while ones that are too loose become meaningless. This skill helps the agent choose categories deliberately, build usable internal taxonomies, and align structural classification with both platform discovery and audience expectation.

## Core Rules

### Choose Platform Categories For Accuracy, Not Reach

Platform categories determine which recommendation pools the content enters. Choosing a popular but inaccurate category to chase reach backfires because the audience there does not want the content.

Choose accurately by:

- selecting the category that best describes the actual content;
- prioritizing the audience that category serves over its size;
- avoiding adjacent popular categories that mislabel the work;
- checking how comparable successful content is categorized;
- re-evaluating the category when content meaningfully shifts.

Accurate categorization connects content to the audience that will stay. Inaccurate categorization connects it to an audience that will bounce.

### Align Category With Audience Expectation

A category sets an expectation before the viewer even sees the content. When the content violates that expectation, retention collapses regardless of quality.

Align expectations by:

- understanding what viewers expect from the chosen category;
- ensuring the content delivers on that category's promise;
- not using a category whose audience wants a different format;
- matching tone and depth to category norms where honest;
- treating the category as part of the packaging contract.

A brilliant piece in the wrong category still fails because its audience was set up to want something else.

### Build A Coherent Internal Taxonomy

A channel or brand needs its own taxonomy so content clusters into navigable, recommendable groups. Without it, content fragments and nothing compounds.

Build coherence by:

- defining a small set of top-level themes the channel owns;
- creating consistent series or playlist structures under each;
- reusing the same labels and structures across related content;
- avoiding a new category for every slight topic variation;
- letting the taxonomy reflect how the audience thinks, not internal org charts.

A coherent taxonomy lets audiences browse, lets the platform learn the channel, and lets evergreen content compound within clear structures.

### Calibrate Taxonomy Granularity

Too few categories make the taxonomy useless for navigation. Too many make it impossible to maintain and fragment each category's volume.

Calibrate by:

- aiming for enough categories to be meaningful, few enough to populate;
- merging categories that consistently overlap in content;
- splitting only when a category genuinely serves distinct intents;
- ensuring each category has enough content to feel substantial;
- reviewing periodically to prune dead or redundant categories.

A taxonomy of three well-populated categories outperforms one of twenty half-empty ones. Granularity should serve the audience, not the creator's urge to classify.

### Use Taxonomy To Reinforce Topic Authority

Platforms learn channels by topic clustering. A taxonomy that groups related content helps the algorithm understand and recommend the channel as an authority.

Reinforce authority by:

- clustering content into clear topic groups;
- interlinking related pieces within the taxonomy;
- building playlists and series around each cluster;
- expanding adjacently into related categories rather than jumping;
- avoiding dramatic category shifts that reset authority.

Consistent taxonomy use teaches the platform who the audience is. Scattered categorization prevents that learning and stalls growth.

### Respect Platform-Specific Taxonomy Systems

Each platform has its own category lists, topic tags, and classification mechanics. A choice that helps on one platform may not exist or may backfire on another.

Respect platform systems by:

- learning each platform's available categories and their meanings;
- using platform-specific topic tags where provided;
- not forcing one platform's taxonomy onto another;
- updating categorization when platforms revise their systems;
- understanding which categories each platform weights for discovery.

Tailored categorization per platform outperforms a single taxonomy applied blindly everywhere.

### Keep Taxonomy Stable Enough To Compound, Flexible Enough To Evolve

A taxonomy that changes constantly prevents compounding. One that never changes goes stale as the channel and audience grow.

Balance stability and evolution by:

- keeping core categories stable over time;
- adding new categories only when a real, repeated need emerges;
- retiring categories gracefully by redirecting or merging;
- documenting the taxonomy so it stays consistent across contributors;
- reviewing the taxonomy against performance and audience feedback periodically.

Stability lets authority compound. Periodic review keeps the taxonomy aligned with where the channel is actually going.

### Avoid Categorization That Triggers Restrictions

Some categories carry advertiser or algorithmic restrictions, age-gating, or demonetization. Misunderstanding these can quietly cap a channel's reach and revenue.

Avoid restriction traps by:

- knowing which categories are brand-safe or restricted on each platform;
- not miscategorizing restricted content into safe categories deceptively;
- understanding the monetization implications of category choice;
- planning for restricted categories deliberately when the content requires them;
- not letting a careless category choice silently limit reach.

Deceptive miscategorization to dodge restrictions risks penalties. Honest categorization with awareness of consequences lets the creator plan deliberately.

## Common Traps

### Chasing Popular Categories Over Accurate Ones

Selecting a high-traffic category that mislabels the content reaches the wrong audience, generates bounces, and suppresses future reach.

### Ignoring Audience Expectation Set By Category

A category creates a pre-click expectation; content that violates it loses viewers regardless of its standalone quality.

### Over-Granular Internal Taxonomies

Too many narrow categories fragment content, leave each category underpopulated, and make navigation and recommendation harder rather than easier.

### Scattered Categorization Across A Channel

Inconsistent categories prevent the platform from learning the channel's topic, stalling the compounding authority effect.

### Applying One Platform's Taxonomy Everywhere

Ignoring platform-specific category systems and mechanics leads to miscategorization or missed discovery opportunities.

### Constantly Restructuring The Taxonomy

Frequent category changes break navigation, reset playlist and series authority, and prevent content from compounding within stable structures.

### Deceptive Category Choices To Dodge Restrictions

Miscategorizing restricted content into safe categories to protect reach risks penalties and undermines long-term channel trust.

## Self-Check

Before approving category and taxonomy choices, verify:

- Platform categories are chosen for accuracy and audience fit, not raw category size.
- The content delivers on the expectation the chosen category sets for viewers.
- The internal taxonomy has a small, coherent set of well-populated categories rather than many fragmented ones.
- Categories are reused consistently across related content to reinforce topic authority.
- Taxonomy granularity is calibrated to be meaningful for navigation without fragmenting volume.
- Categorization is tailored to each platform's specific system rather than copy-pasted.
- Core categories are stable enough to compound, with a plan for periodic review and graceful evolution.
- Restricted or brand-sensitive categories are understood and chosen deliberately, not deceptively.
- Comparable successful content was checked to inform category choices.
- The taxonomy reflects how the audience thinks and searches, not internal organizational logic.
