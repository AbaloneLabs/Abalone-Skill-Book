# Audio Accessibility And Compliance

Accessibility and compliance are production requirements, not add-ons bolted on at the end. The judgment problem is that creators treat captions, transcripts, loudness targets, and licensing as chores to be handled after the creative work, when in fact each one shapes who can consume the content, whether platforms accept it, and whether the creator stays legally safe. A brilliant podcast episode with no transcript is invisible to search and unusable to deaf and hard-of-hearing audiences. A perfectly mixed track at the wrong loudness gets attenuated by the platform and sounds quiet to the listener. A great song used without the right license can take down an entire piece.

The harm is silent and compounding. Accessibility failures exclude audiences and reduce discoverability, because transcripts and captions are also how search and feed systems understand audio. Loudness failures make content sound weak next to competitors or trigger platform normalization that crushes dynamics. Licensing failures cause takedowns, demonetization, and legal exposure that can erase months of work overnight. This skill helps the agent treat accessibility, loudness, and licensing as first-class production decisions baked into the workflow rather than last-minute cleanup, and to recognize that accessibility serves far more than the audience that explicitly needs it.

## Core Rules

### Treat Accessibility As A Production Requirement

Accessibility is not a favor to a small subset of listeners. Captions and transcripts serve deaf and hard-of-hearing audiences, people watching in sound-off environments, non-native speakers, search engines, and anyone who wants to skim or quote. Build it into the plan from the start.

Treat accessibility as core by:

- budgeting time and cost for transcripts and captions in every episode, not as an optional extra;
- deciding the accessibility standard before production so it shapes scripting and editing;
- recognizing that sound-off viewing is the default on most mobile feeds, making captions a reach decision, not only an accommodation;
- assigning clear ownership so accessibility is someone's job, not an assumption.

When accessibility is a requirement, it gets done. When it is optional, it gets skipped under deadline pressure.

### Produce Accurate Transcripts And Captions

A transcript exists for reading, captions exist for synchronized display, and both must be accurate to be useful. Auto-generated text is a starting point, not a finished product, because errors in names, numbers, and technical terms undermine trust and searchability.

Produce quality text by:

- using automated transcription as a draft, then correcting it against the audio;
- verifying proper nouns, numbers, brands, and technical terms that automation routinely gets wrong;
- adding speaker labels and paragraph breaks so the transcript is readable, not a wall of text;
- timing captions to the speech so they appear and disappear in sync, not lagging or racing ahead;
- breaking caption lines for readability, two lines maximum, with natural line breaks.

A transcript full of errors is worse than no transcript for credibility. Accuracy is the whole point.

### Plan Loudness To Platform Targets

Platforms normalize loudness to protect listeners from jumps between content. If you deliver louder than the target, the platform turns you down, often crushing dynamics. If you deliver quieter, the platform may leave you quiet relative to competitors. Mastering to the right target is a compliance and quality decision.

Master loudness by:

- targeting the platform's specified loudness, for example around negative fourteen LUFS integrated for many podcast and streaming platforms, negative fourteen to negative sixteen for music services, and near negative fourteen to negative one for short-form video depending on the platform;
- checking true peak to avoid inter-sample clipping that triggers attenuation;
- not crushing dynamics to "sound loud," because the platform will undo it and the result will sound flat;
- matching loudness across episodes so listeners do not have to reach for the volume knob;
- verifying the final exported file's measured loudness, not trusting the meter while editing.

Loudness compliance is invisible when done right and obvious when done wrong. Measure, do not guess.

### Provide Audio Description Where Video Depends On Visuals

For video content, audiences who cannot see the visuals need audio description, narration of essential visual information, to follow what is happening. Where visuals carry meaning the spoken script does not, description is an accessibility obligation.

Plan audio description by:

- writing description for charts, on-screen text, demonstrations, and visual reactions the audio does not explain;
- choosing between extended description, paused video with narration, and integrated description woven into the main script;
- integrating description into the script during writing rather than retrofitting it later;
- keeping description concise so it does not crowd the existing audio;
- deciding whether the platform supports a separate description track or whether it must be in the main mix.

Content whose meaning depends on unseen visuals is inaccessible by design. Either describe the visuals or make sure the audio stands alone.

### License Music And Sound Effects Correctly

Audio rights are real and enforceable. Using music or effects without the correct license causes content ID claims, demonetization, takedowns, and legal exposure. Licensing is part of production discipline, not a separate legal afterthought.

Handle licensing by:

- using only audio the creator has clear rights to use in the intended context;
- distinguishing personal, commercial, and monetized use, because licenses differ;
- recording the source, license type, and terms for every track so disputes can be resolved;
- avoiding recognizable commercial music unless explicitly licensed, including brief clips;
- watching for royalty-free pitfalls, some libraries restrict monetization, platforms, or derivatives in ways that bite later;
- checking whether a license covers all distribution platforms or only some.

A track that is "free" can still be restricted. Read the actual terms, and keep proof.

### Watch The Royalty-Free Pitfalls

Royalty-free does not mean unrestricted. Many creators assume a royalty-free library grants blanket permission, then discover limits when the content is monetized, syndicated, or used on a platform the license excludes.

Watch for:

- library music that forbids use in monetized or ad-supported content;
- licenses that allow personal use but not commercial or client work;
- attribution requirements that, if missed, convert a licensed use into an infringement;
- platform-specific libraries whose tracks are only cleared on that one platform;
- "free for YouTube" tracks that are not cleared elsewhere, causing takedowns on podcast or other distribution.

Royalty-free is a category, not a guarantee. The specific license terms govern, and they vary widely.

### Normalize Volume Across The Catalog

Listeners experience content in sequence and across episodes. Volume that jumps between episodes, or between segments within an episode, fatigues the audience and signals amateur work.

Normalize across the catalog by:

- applying the same loudness target to every episode, not mastering each in isolation;
- checking legacy episodes when raising the standard, so the catalog is consistent;
- leveling music, ads, and voice segments so transitions do not jar;
- avoiding the loudness war where each episode is pushed louder than the last;
- treating the listener's experience across the feed as the reference, not the single file.

Consistency reads as professionalism. Inconsistency reads as carelessness, even when each piece is technically fine alone.

### Design For Hearing-Impaired And Diverse Listening Conditions

Accessibility also means designing the audio itself for people who do not hear perfectly and for environments where hearing is hard. Clear speech, controlled dynamics, and clean mixes serve everyone.

Design inclusive audio by:

- keeping speech intelligible, avoiding mumbling, excessive reverb, and buried dialogue;
- controlling dynamic range so quiet passages remain audible in noisy environments;
- avoiding reliance on subtle audio cues alone, pair them with visual or text equivalents where possible;
- ensuring music and effects never mask the voice for listeners with partial hearing;
- testing on phone speakers and earbuds where most audiences actually listen.

Audio designed for clarity in hard conditions sounds better for everyone, not only for those who need it.

## Common Traps

### Treating Captions As An Optional Extra

Captions are skipped under deadline because they feel optional, but they gate sound-off viewers, search, and accessibility. Budget them as a requirement.

### Shipping Raw Auto-Transcripts

Auto-generated transcripts full of name and number errors undermine credibility and searchability. Always correct against the audio.

### Mastering Louder Than The Platform Target

Pushing loudness past the platform limit gets attenuated and often crushes dynamics. Master to the target and verify the measured result.

### Assuming Royalty-Free Means Unrestricted

Royalty-free libraries carry specific terms on monetization, platforms, and attribution. Read the license, not the category.

### Forgetting Audio Description For Visual-Dependent Video

When meaning lives in the visuals and the audio does not convey it, audiences who cannot see are excluded. Describe or make the audio stand alone.

### Inconsistent Loudness Across Episodes

Episodes mastered in isolation produce a feed that jumps in volume. Normalize against one target across the catalog.

### Relying On Subtle Audio Cues Alone

Quiet sound effects or faint cues that carry meaning are lost on hearing-impaired audiences and in noisy environments. Pair with visual or text equivalents.

### Losing License Documentation

Using cleared audio but failing to record the source and terms leaves the creator unable to defend a dispute. Document every track.

## Self-Check

Before approving audio content for accessibility and compliance, verify:

- Accessibility, transcripts, captions, and description, was planned as a production requirement, not bolted on.
- Transcripts and captions are corrected for accuracy, especially proper nouns, numbers, and technical terms.
- Captions are synchronized and formatted for readability, not raw auto-text.
- The final master hits the target platform loudness, verified by measurement, not by ear.
- True peak is controlled to avoid clipping and platform attenuation.
- Loudness is consistent across episodes and segments in the feed.
- Every music and sound effect track is licensed for the actual use, including monetization and all distribution platforms.
- License source and terms are documented for every track so disputes can be resolved.
- Royalty-free tracks were checked against their specific restrictions, not assumed unrestricted.
- Video content that depends on visuals includes audio description or an audio-only path.
- Speech is intelligible and dynamics are controlled for hearing-impaired and noisy-environment listeners.
