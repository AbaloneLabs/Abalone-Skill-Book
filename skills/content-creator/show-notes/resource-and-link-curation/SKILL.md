# Resource And Link Curation

Resource and link curation is the practice of selecting, annotating, and maintaining the links that accompany content. The judgment problem is that curation is easy to do lazily and hard to do well. A link dump of everything mentioned feels thorough but is useless because nobody can scan it; a sparse list misses the resources listeners actually want; an unverified list rots quietly and sends readers to dead pages; an undisclosed affiliate or sponsorship link erodes trust the moment a reader notices. Good curation is an act of editorial judgment, deciding what is worth the reader's time, explaining why, and standing behind the recommendation honestly.

The harm from poor curation is cumulative and quiet. Dead and wrong links generate support requests and signal low effort. Missing attribution alienates the creators and sources who were referenced, cutting off future collaboration. Undisclosed affiliate links, when discovered, damage credibility far beyond the value of the commission. Over-inclusion trains readers to ignore the resource section entirely, so even the good links go unused. This skill helps the agent decide what to include, annotate each link usefully, verify integrity, and handle attribution and disclosure so the resource section becomes a trusted asset rather than a liability.

## Core Rules

### Curate For The Reader's Need, Not For Completeness

The purpose of a resource section is to serve the reader who wants to go deeper, not to document every URL that appeared. Inclusion should be driven by what a reader would actually want, not by completeness. Curation means cutting.

Inclusion criteria:

- include only links a reader would plausibly want to visit after the content;
- cut passing mentions, offhand references, and links with no standalone value;
- prefer one excellent resource on a subtopic over three mediocre ones;
- group by purpose, learn more, try the tool, see the source, so the reader can scan;
- when in doubt, cut, a shorter curated list is used more than a long dumped one.

The test is whether a real reader would thank you for the link. If not, it does not belong.

### Annotate Every Link With Purpose And Value

A bare link tells the reader nothing and forces them to click blind. An annotation tells them what it is, why it matters, and whether it is for them. Annotation is what turns a list into curation.

Annotation practices:

- one line per link stating what it is and why it is included;
- note the format, article, tool, video, paper, dataset, so the reader knows what they are clicking into;
- note the audience or prerequisite where relevant, for beginners, technical, requires signup;
- note any cost or friction, free, paid, freemium, account required;
- keep annotations consistent in voice and length across the list.

Consistent, honest annotations let the reader triage without clicking, which builds trust in the whole list.

### Verify Every Link Before Publishing

Link rot and wrong links are the most common and most preventable curation failure. A link that 404s or points to the wrong page undermines the entire resource section. Verify every link as part of the publish process, not as an afterthought.

Verification practices:

- click or curl every link and confirm it resolves to the intended page, not a redirect to a homepage;
- prefer durable, canonical URLs over tracking or session-specific links;
- check that the linked page still contains what you claim it contains;
- capture or archive consequential links if the source is likely to change or disappear;
- re-verify on a regular cadence, because even good links rot over time.

For evergreen resource lists, schedule periodic re-verification, because a link that worked at publish can fail months later and quietly degrade the list.

### Handle Attribution As A Relationship And An Obligation

When you reference a person, a source, or another creator's work, linking and naming them is both an obligation and an opportunity. Attribution costs nothing and builds goodwill, while missing attribution breeds resentment and correction work.

Attribution practices:

- name and link every person, paper, tool, and source you reference;
- link to the creator's preferred surface, their site or chosen profile, not a scraper or aggregator;
- credit the origin, not the place you happened to find it, trace to the source when possible;
- when you build on someone's framework or idea, say so explicitly rather than implying originality;
- notify referenced creators when practical, it often leads to shares and relationships.

Missing attribution is the single most common cause of public disputes in content, and it is the easiest to prevent.

### Disclose Affiliate, Sponsored, And Compensated Links Clearly

When you stand to gain from a click, the reader is entitled to know. Disclosure is both an ethical norm and, in many jurisdictions, a legal requirement. Unclear or buried disclosure is a trust-destroying choice.

Disclosure practices:

- label affiliate and sponsored links clearly and proximately, near the link, not buried in a footer;
- use plain language, "affiliate link, I earn a commission," not euphemism;
- disclose any material relationship, free product, equity, ongoing sponsorship, not just per-click commission;
- keep disclosure consistent in placement so readers learn where to expect it;
- never let disclosure read as endorsement pressure, the reader should feel free to ignore it.

Honest disclosure does not reduce clicks; it preserves the trust that makes the clicks valuable over time.

### Distinguish Recommendation From Reference

Not every link is a recommendation. Some are references to things you discussed, including things you criticized. Conflating the two misleads readers and can imply endorsement you did not intend. Mark the distinction.

Distinguish explicitly:

- mark recommendations, things you suggest the reader use or buy;
- mark references, things you mentioned or analyzed, neutrally or critically;
- mark primary sources, the original document or data behind a claim;
- mark further reading, optional depth for interested readers.

A reader should never mistake a critical reference for a recommendation, or assume a link means you vouch for it.

### Maintain The Resource Section Over Time

A resource section is a living document for evergreen content. Links rot, tools shut down, and better resources appear. A list that is accurate at publish but never maintained becomes a liability. Plan for maintenance.

Maintenance practices:

- set a review cadence for evergreen resource lists, quarterly or semiannually;
- re-verify links and replace dead ones with current equivalents;
- update annotations when the linked resource changes meaningfully;
- note a last-verified date so readers know how current the list is;
- retire the list or mark it archived if you can no longer maintain it, rather than leaving a rotting list live.

An unmaintained evergreen list eventually misleads readers and should be marked or removed.

## Common Traps

### The Link Dump

Pasting every URL mentioned with no annotation is a trap because nobody uses it and it signals low effort. This is a trap because curation value comes entirely from selection and annotation. Curate and annotate.

### Publishing Without Verification

Publishing links without checking them is a trap because some will be dead or wrong, and the reader who hits a 404 blames the whole list. This is a trap because verification is cheap and the failure is visible. Verify every link.

### Missing Or Lazy Attribution

Failing to name and link the origin of an idea or resource is a trap because it withholds credit and invites disputes. This is a trap because attribution is free and its absence is conspicuous. Credit the origin.

### Buried Or Euphemistic Affiliate Disclosure

Hiding affiliate relationships in fine print or vague language is a trap because the first reader who notices assumes everything is compromised. This is a trap because the trust loss exceeds any commission gain. Disclose clearly and proximally.

### Implying Endorsement Of Everything Linked

Treating every link as a recommendation is a trap because it implies you vouch for things you only referenced or criticized. This is a trap because it misleads readers and can embroil you in others' problems. Mark recommendation versus reference.

### Letting Evergreen Lists Rot

Publishing a resource list and never revisiting it is a trap because links die and tools change, turning a useful list into a misleading one. This is a trap because the decay is invisible to you but visible to every reader. Schedule maintenance.

### Linking To Aggregators Instead Of Origins

Linking to a scraper or aggregator instead of the original creator is a trap because it redirects credit and traffic away from the source. This is a trap because it harms the creator you relied on and signals carelessness. Link to the origin.

## Self-Check

- Did I include only links a real reader would want, cutting passing mentions and duplicates?
- Is every link annotated with what it is, why it is included, and any cost or prerequisite?
- Was every link verified to resolve to the intended page immediately before publishing?
- Did I name and link every referenced person, source, and creator, preferring their origin surface?
- Are affiliate, sponsored, and compensated links clearly and proximally disclosed in plain language?
- Did I distinguish recommendations from references and primary sources so endorsement is not implied where none exists?
- For evergreen lists, is there a review cadence and a last-verified date?
- Did I prefer durable canonical URLs and archive consequential links likely to disappear?
- Would the origin creators, on seeing this list, feel credited fairly rather than scraped?
- If a reader clicked every link, would they feel curated or dumped on? If dumped on, cut and annotate further.
