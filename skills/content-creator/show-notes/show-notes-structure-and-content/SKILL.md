# Show Notes Structure And Content

Show notes are the written layer that surrounds an audio or video episode and does work the recording cannot do on its own. The judgment problem is that show notes are usually treated as an afterthought, a short summary pasted under the episode, when in fact they serve several competing audiences simultaneously. They must help a new listener decide whether to press play, help a returning listener find the part they remember, help search engines surface the episode, carry sponsorship and calls to action, and give credit and links to everyone mentioned. A show notes block that serves one of these audiences well usually fails the others, and the failure is invisible because the creator never sees the listeners who did not press play or could not find the link.

The harm is measurable. Weak show notes depress click-through and completion, hurt discoverability in search and platform recommendation, lose sponsor value because the offer is buried, and generate support load when listeners cannot find the resource that was mentioned. Worse, show notes that overpromise relative to the episode, or that misattribute a guest, erode trust and create correction work. This skill helps the agent structure show notes as a purposeful document with distinct zones, write summaries that are honest and useful, and balance the competing obligations of listener utility, discovery, sponsorship, and attribution.

## Core Rules

### Treat Show Notes As A Document With Distinct Zones

Show notes are not a single block of prose; they are a layered document where each zone does a specific job. Design the zones deliberately and keep them consistent across episodes so listeners learn where to find things.

A workable zone structure:

- a one to three sentence hook and summary that earns the press;
- the guest or topic context, who and why it matters;
- key timestamps or chapter markers for navigation;
- the main takeaways or moments, written so they are useful even without listening;
- resources, links, and references mentioned in the episode;
- the sponsor read and any offer codes;
- the call to action, subscribe, review, follow, join.

Consistency across episodes matters because returning listeners scan rather than read, and a stable structure lets them find what they need fast.

### Write The Summary To Earn The Press, Not To Recount The Episode

The summary is the highest-leverage text in the show notes because it determines whether someone presses play at all. Its job is to establish stakes and promise value, not to retell the episode. A summary that simply lists what was discussed ("we talked about X, Y, and Z") tells the listener everything and gives them no reason to listen.

Strong summary practices:

- lead with the question, tension, or stakes the episode resolves;
- name the specific value or insight the listener will get;
- be honest, do not promise something the episode does not deliver;
- keep it short, three sentences is usually enough;
- avoid spoilers for the payoff moment if the discovery is the point.

The summary is a promise. If the episode does not keep it, the summary is bait and it costs trust.

### Decide What To Summarize Versus What To Quote

Not everything in the episode belongs in the notes. Summarize the structure and the takeaways; quote only the lines that are genuinely quotable and that represent the episode honestly. Over-quoting dilutes the pull quotes; under-summarizing leaves the listener without a map.

Decision guide:

- summarize the argument, the story arc, and the main conclusions in your own words;
- quote only the one or two lines that are striking, specific, and self-contained;
- never quote a line out of context in a way that changes its meaning;
- attribute quotes clearly to the speaker, especially in multi-guest episodes;
- mark paraphrase versus direct quote so the reader is not misled.

A listener should be able to understand the episode's shape from the notes alone, and feel rewarded, not cheated, when they hear the quoted lines in context.

### Make Timestamps And Chapters Genuinely Navigable

Timestamps are a utility feature that listeners use heavily and that platforms increasingly index. Bad timestamps, too few, too vague, or inaccurate, are worse than none because they betray the listener who trusts them. Make them precise and meaningful.

Timestamp practices:

- mark chapter boundaries at real topic shifts, not at arbitrary intervals;
- label each chapter with a specific, searchable phrase, not "part 1" or "more discussion";
- verify each timestamp against the actual audio before publishing;
- keep granularity matched to episode length, more chapters for long episodes, fewer for short;
- place the most important chapters where a returning listener would look.

If the platform supports chapter metadata, populate it; if not, the text timestamps in the notes remain the fallback.

### Curate Resources And Links For Real Utility

The resources section is where listeners go after the episode, and it is where most show notes fail. A dump of every link mentioned produces a wall of text nobody uses. A curated, annotated list is a genuine service that listeners return to.

Curation practices:

- include only links a listener would actually want, not every passing reference;
- annotate each link with what it is and why it matters, one line each;
- verify every link works before publishing and use durable, canonical URLs;
- group links by type, guest links, tools, further reading, so they are scannable;
- credit and link every person and source mentioned, both for utility and attribution.

Dead links and missing credits are the most common show notes complaint; a link-check step before publish prevents both.

### Integrate Sponsorship And Calls To Action Without Burying The Listener

Sponsors and calls to action are legitimate parts of show notes, but their placement and framing determine whether they convert without degrading the listener experience. Burying the sponsor at the bottom hides the value from the sponsor; leading with it annoys the listener. Place deliberately.

Integration practices:

- give the sponsor its own clearly labeled zone, do not disguise it as content;
- include the offer code or URL prominently and verify it works;
- explain the offer in listener benefit terms, what they get, not just what the sponsor sells;
- place the primary call to action once, clearly, rather than repeating it everywhere;
- keep sponsor and CTA zones visually separate from editorial content so trust is preserved.

Listeners tolerate sponsorship that is honest and well-placed; they resent sponsorship disguised as recommendation.

### Optimize For Discovery Without Keyword Stuffing

Show notes are indexed by search engines and platform search, so they matter for discovery. But discovery optimization that degrades readability or honesty backfires. Optimize for the terms a real listener would search, in natural language.

Discovery practices:

- use the episode's core topics and the guest's name where natural;
- write chapter and takeaway labels in searchable phrases;
- include relevant proper nouns, tools, and concepts that listeners search for;
- avoid keyword stuffing, which reads as spam and can be penalized;
- write for the human first and let search benefit follow.

A show notes block that reads naturally and includes the real terms listeners use will out-rank one stuffed with repetitive keywords.

## Common Traps

### Treating Show Notes As An Afterthought

Writing show notes in two minutes after publish is a trap because it forfeits click-through, discovery, and sponsor value for the entire life of the episode. This is a trap because the cost is small to fix and the loss compounds across every episode. Budget time for show notes as part of production.

### The Recount Summary That Removes The Reason To Listen

Summarizing the episode so completely that the listener feels they got the point is a trap because it removes the press. This is a trap because the summary's job is to earn the listen, not replace it. Promise value without giving away the payoff.

### Inaccurate Or Vague Timestamps

Timestamps that are off by minutes or labeled "discussion" are a trap because they betray the listener who trusted them and never return. This is a trap because broken navigation erodes the utility that distinguishes a good podcast from a bad one. Verify every timestamp.

### The Link Dump

Pasting every URL mentioned in the episode with no annotation is a trap because nobody uses it and it signals low effort. This is a trap because curation is the whole value of the resources section. Curate and annotate.

### Disguising Sponsorship As Content

Blending the sponsor read into the editorial summary so it reads like a recommendation is a trap because it deceives the listener and violates disclosure norms. This is a trap because the first listener who notices destroys trust and may trigger a disclosure complaint. Label sponsorship clearly.

### Overpromising In The Summary

A summary that promises insights or guests the episode does not deliver is a trap because it is bait and it tanks completion and trust. This is a trap because the listener who presses play and feels cheated never returns. Keep the summary honest.

### Ignoring Attribution

Failing to credit and link guests, sources, and referenced creators is a trap because it withholds credit, invites correction, and misses discovery upside. This is a trap because attribution is cheap and its absence is conspicuous. Credit everyone mentioned.

## Self-Check

- Does the show notes block have distinct, consistent zones that a returning listener can scan?
- Does the summary establish stakes and promise value without recounting the whole episode or spoiling the payoff?
- Did I decide deliberately what to summarize versus what to quote, and are quotes attributed and in context?
- Are timestamps accurate, verified against the audio, and labeled with specific searchable phrases?
- Is the resources section curated and annotated rather than a link dump, and are all links verified?
- Is sponsorship clearly labeled, well-placed, and verified, with the offer explained in listener-benefit terms?
- Is the call to action clear and placed once rather than repeated everywhere?
- Did I optimize for natural search terms without keyword stuffing?
- Did I credit and link every guest, source, and creator mentioned?
- Would a listener who pressed play based on the summary feel the episode delivered on it? If not, rewrite the summary.
