# Interview Conduct And Statement Taking

How an interview is conducted determines whether the account it produces is reliable, fair, and defensible, or whether it is a contaminated, challengeable artifact that weakens the investigation. The failure modes are well-documented: interviewers who lead the witness toward a desired answer, who accept the first plausible response without testing, who create a dynamic where the interviewee says what they think will end the interview, or who document poorly so that the account cannot be relied upon. Each of these produces a statement that looks like evidence but collapses under scrutiny, because it reflects the interviewer's influence more than the interviewee's knowledge. The judgment problem is conducting interviews that elicit accurate, complete accounts through fair, non-leading methods, recognizing and handling evasion, and documenting statements so they withstand challenge from the interviewee, from counsel, and from decision-makers.

Use this skill when conducting investigation interviews, taking written or recorded statements, handling difficult or evasive interviewees, or reviewing whether interview conduct produced reliable accounts. The goal is to make the agent treat interview conduct as an evidence-elicitation discipline where the method determines the value, and where fairness and accuracy are not in tension but mutually reinforcing.

## Core Rules

### Establish The Interview Frame Clearly And Neutrally

The opening of the interview sets the tone and the interviewee's understanding of the process. A frame that is accusatory, threatening, or misleading distorts the account from the start. A frame that is clear, neutral, and professional establishes the conditions for an accurate statement.

Establish the frame by:

- explaining the purpose of the interview, the investigation's general nature, and the interviewee's role, without revealing more than appropriate for the stage of investigation;
- advising the interviewee of any rights or warnings applicable to their status, such as Upjohn warnings or representation rights, clearly and before substantive questioning;
- explaining how the interview will be documented, whether recorded or noted, and how the account will be used;
- setting expectations for honesty and completeness without implying consequences that coerce a particular answer;
- establishing a professional, non-threatening dynamic where the interviewee can provide their account without fear or incentive to tailor.

The frame must be honest. Misleading an interviewee about the investigation's purpose or their status may produce a statement, but it compromises the statement's fairness and defensibility.

### Use Open Questions First, Then Test With Specifics

The sequence of questioning determines account quality. Open questions, which let the interviewee narrate freely, capture their unprimed recollection. Specific questions, which probe details and test against evidence, follow to verify and challenge. Reversing this sequence, starting with specifics or leading questions, contaminates the account.

Use the sequence by:

- beginning with broad, open questions such as tell me about or walk me through, that invite narration without suggesting content;
- letting the interviewee complete their account before interrupting, noting areas for follow-up rather than derailing the narrative;
- following with specific questions that probe details, timelines, and explanations, building on what the interviewee volunteered rather than introducing external information prematurely;
- introducing evidence, such as documents or contradictions, only after the interviewee has committed to their account, so that their unprimed version is captured before confrontation;
- using closed questions, which seek yes or no, only to confirm specific facts after the account is established, not to elicit the narrative.

An interviewee who narrates freely first commits to an account they cannot later claim was suggested by the interviewer. An interviewee who is led from the start can attribute their account to the questions.

### Listen Actively And Recognize Evasion

The interviewer's primary tool is not talking but listening. Active listening detects when an account is complete, when it is evasive, and when it contradicts the evidence. An interviewer who is focused on their next question misses the signals that the account is unreliable.

Listen actively by:

- paying attention to what is said and what is conspicuously not said, since omissions can be as revealing as statements;
- recognizing evasion patterns, such as answering a different question, vague generalities instead of specifics, deflection to others, or memory claims that are selective;
- noting hedging, qualification, and changes in demeanor that may indicate discomfort with the topic;
- following up on evasion with focused re-asking, returning to the unanswered question calmly and persistently until it is addressed;
- distinguishing genuine memory gaps from strategic evasion, since people forget details, but patterns of selective forgetting warrant scrutiny.

Evasion does not prove dishonesty, but it identifies where the account is weakest and where focused questioning or corroboration is needed. An interviewer who accepts evasive answers without follow-up produces an account with holes that decision-makers cannot rely on.

### Avoid Coercion, Leading, And Suggestion

The line between vigorous questioning and coercion is critical. Coercion, through implied threats, promises, or pressure, produces unreliable accounts and creates grounds for challenge. Leading and suggestion, through questions that telegraph the desired answer, contaminate the account. Both destroy the statement's value.

Avoid by:

- never implying consequences for a particular answer, such as suggesting cooperation will be rewarded or resistance punished;
- never suggesting the content of the answer, such as wouldn't you say that was wrong?, which leads the interviewee;
- avoiding repetitive, relentless questioning on the same point that wears down the interviewee into agreement, which is a form of coercion;
- being aware of power dynamics, since an interviewer in a position of authority over the interviewee can exert coercion without intending to;
- recognizing that an interviewee who changes their account under pressure has not provided a reliable statement, regardless of whether the new account is accurate.

A statement obtained through coercion or leading is not just challengeable; it may be inadmissible, and it reflects on the investigation's fairness. The goal is an account the interviewee owns, not one the interviewer extracted.

### Confront With Evidence Fairly And Capture The Response

Confronting an interviewee with contradictory evidence is where interviews often produce their most valuable information, because the interviewee must explain the discrepancy. But confrontation must be fair, evidence-based, and documented, not ambushing or argumentative.

Confront fairly by:

- introducing the contradictory evidence specifically, such as presenting the document or stating the prior testimony, rather than vaguely asserting inconsistency;
- asking the interviewee to explain the discrepancy, giving them a genuine opportunity to reconcile or clarify;
- listening to the explanation without pre-judging, since the interviewee may have a legitimate reconciliation that resolves the contradiction;
- documenting both the evidence presented and the interviewee's response, so the confrontation and its outcome are on the record;
- recognizing when the explanation is itself contradicted or implausible, and noting this for credibility assessment without arguing.

Confrontation is not accusation. It is presenting evidence and asking for explanation. The interviewee's response, whether reconciliation, concession, or further evasion, is evidence that must be captured fairly.

### Document The Statement Accurately And Completely

The statement's value depends on its documentation. A statement remembered but not recorded, recorded partially, or recorded with the interviewer's interpretation rather than the interviewee's words, is weak evidence. Documentation must capture what was said, accurately and completely.

Document by:

- recording the interview through the method planned, whether recording, detailed notes, or written statement, consistently and completely;
- capturing the interviewee's words rather than the interviewer's paraphrase, especially for key admissions, denials, and explanations;
- including the questions asked, not just the answers, so the context and any potential leading are visible;
- having the interviewee review and confirm the written statement, making corrections and signing or acknowledging, which strengthens reliability and reduces later disputes;
- documenting the interview's frame, including any warnings or rights advised, so the statement's fairness is demonstrable;
- preserving the original recording or notes, not just the final statement, since the original may be needed to resolve disputes about what was said.

A statement that the interviewee later disputes, with no recording, no notes, and no confirmation, becomes a credibility contest between the interviewer and the interviewee. Documentation is what prevents that contest and preserves the statement's value.

### Assess Credibility Based On Documented Indicators

The interviewer's assessment of the interviewee's credibility may be relevant to the investigation's conclusions, but it must be based on documented indicators, not gut feeling. A credibility assessment stated as I didn't believe them is not defensible; one based on specific contradictions, evasion patterns, and corroboration failures is.

Assess credibility by documenting:

- specific contradictions within the account or with the evidence, with the conflicting statements referenced;
- corroboration or its absence, noting whether the account is supported or undermined by documents, other testimony, or system records;
- plausibility, assessing whether the account makes sense given the established facts, without substituting the interviewer's assumptions for evidence;
- demeanor indicators, such as hedging, selective memory, or evasive patterns, described specifically rather than generally;
- the interviewee's opportunity and incentive, since these affect whether their account is likely to be self-serving.

Credibility assessment is judgment, but defensible judgment is based on articulated reasons tied to the evidence. Document the basis so that decision-makers can evaluate the assessment rather than merely accepting the interviewer's impression.

## Common Traps

### Accusatory Or Misleading Interview Frame

A threatening or deceptive opening distorts the account and compromises fairness. Establish a clear, neutral, honest frame.

### Leading From The Start

Questions that suggest answers contaminate the account and make it challengeable. Open first, then test.

### Accepting Evasion Without Follow-Up

Evasive answers leave holes that undermine the account's reliability. Follow up persistently and note evasion patterns.

### Coercion Through Pressure Or Implication

Implied threats or relentless repetition produce unreliable, inadmissible statements. Avoid coercion and leading.

### Ambush Confrontation Without Fair Explanation

Argumentative or vague confrontation is unfair and unproductive. Present evidence specifically and capture the response.

### Incomplete Or Interpretive Documentation

A statement recorded partially or paraphrased is weak and disputable. Capture the interviewee's words and have them confirm.

### Credibility Based On Gut Feeling

An undefended credibility assessment is not reliable evidence. Base assessment on documented contradictions, corroboration, and demeanor.

## Self-Check

- Is the interview frame established clearly and neutrally, with purpose, role, rights or warnings, documentation method, and honesty expectations explained without coercion?
- Does questioning proceed from open narration to specific testing, letting the interviewee commit to an account before evidence is introduced, with closed questions only for confirmation?
- Is active listening used to detect omissions, evasion patterns, hedging, and selective memory, with focused follow-up on unanswered questions?
- Are coercion, leading, suggestion, power-dynamic pressure, and relentless repetition avoided, so the account is the interviewee's own?
- Is confrontation with contradictory evidence conducted fairly, with specific evidence introduction, genuine opportunity to explain, documented response, and noted plausibility?
- Is the statement documented accurately and completely, with the interviewee's words captured, questions included, frame and warnings recorded, interviewee confirmation obtained, and originals preserved?
- Is credibility assessment based on documented contradictions, corroboration, plausibility, demeanor indicators, and opportunity or incentive, rather than impression?
- Could the interview conduct and statement withstand challenge from the interviewee, from counsel, and from decision-makers regarding fairness, accuracy, and reliability?
