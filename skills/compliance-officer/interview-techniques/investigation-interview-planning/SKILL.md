# Investigation Interview Planning

The quality of an investigation interview is determined before anyone sits down, by the planning that defines its purpose, scope, sequence, and evidence basis. Interviews conducted without preparation, with vague objectives and no document review, produce inconsistent, leading, and challengeable accounts that weaken rather than strengthen the investigation. Worse, interviews conducted in the wrong order, where the subject is interviewed before witnesses, give the subject the opportunity to tailor their account to the evidence and coordinate stories with others, destroying testimony that could have been reliable. The judgment problem is planning interviews as an integrated sequence where each interview builds on prior evidence, where the order protects testimony from contamination, and where the interviewer enters with enough preparation to test the account rather than merely record it.

Use this skill when planning an investigation's interview sequence, preparing for specific interviews, deciding interview order and witness versus subject designation, or diagnosing why investigation interviews produce unreliable or contradictory accounts. The goal is to make the agent treat interview planning as an evidence-protection and testimony-maximization discipline, not a scheduling exercise.

## Core Rules

### Review All Available Evidence Before Interviewing

An interviewer who enters without knowing the documents, data, and prior testimony enters blind. They cannot test the interviewee's account against the evidence, cannot recognize inconsistencies, and may accept statements that contradict the record. Evidence review is what transforms an interview from a recording session into a testing session.

Review before interviewing:

- all documents, emails, system records, and data relevant to the matter, so the interviewer can probe specifics rather than generalities;
- the timeline of events, so the interviewer can test the interviewee's account against the established sequence;
- prior interview accounts, so the interviewer can identify and explore consistencies and inconsistencies;
- the control framework and policy requirements, so the interviewer can assess whether described practices meet the standard;
- known gaps and contradictions in the evidence, so the interviewer can target questions where the record is unclear.

An interview conducted without evidence review produces an account that cannot be evaluated. The interviewer may record a plausible-sounding statement that contradicts the documents, and without knowing the documents, they will not know to challenge it.

### Sequence Interviews To Protect Testimony From Contamination

Interview order is a strategic decision that affects testimony quality. Interviewing the subject first, before witnesses, gives the subject the opportunity to set a narrative before the investigator knows the full picture. Interviewing witnesses after the subject has been alerted allows coordination and story-tailoring. The general principle is to interview from the outside in, from peripheral witnesses to central figures to the subject, to capture uncontaminated testimony before those most likely to coordinate become aware.

Sequence by:

- interviewing peripheral witnesses and document custodians first, to establish the factual baseline before central figures are alerted;
- interviewing fact witnesses, those with direct knowledge but not involvement, next, to build the evidentiary picture;
- interviewing the subject or subjects last, when the investigator has the fullest picture and can test the subject's account against all prior evidence;
- considering whether to interview in order of perceived culpability, least to most, so that the most culpable cannot tailor to those already interviewed;
- recognizing exceptions, such as urgent matters where immediate subject interview is necessary to prevent ongoing harm or evidence destruction, and documenting the rationale.

Sequencing is not always possible to control perfectly, but the principle of capturing uncontaminated testimony before alerting subjects should guide the sequence wherever feasible. A subject who knows what witnesses said can tailor; a subject who does not must answer from their own recollection.

### Designate Witnesses Versus Subjects And Adjust Approach Accordingly

Not everyone interviewed is a subject of the investigation. Witnesses provide information; subjects are those whose conduct is under scrutiny. The distinction affects the interview's purpose, the interviewee's rights, the approach, and the documentation, and confusing the two creates both investigative and legal problems.

Distinguish by:

- identifying, before the interview, whether the interviewee is a witness, providing information about others' conduct, or a subject, whose own conduct is under investigation;
- adjusting the interview approach: witness interviews focus on fact-gathering, while subject interviews must account for the subject's potential self-interest, defensiveness, and rights;
- considering whether the subject is entitled to representation, notification, or other protections depending on the context, such as union representation in certain employment investigations;
- being alert to status changes, since a witness's account may reveal their own involvement, converting them to a subject mid-investigation, which requires adjusting the approach;
- documenting the designation and its basis, since treating a subject as a mere witness, or vice versa, affects the interview's defensibility.

An interview where the subject was treated as a casual witness, without the protections or scrutiny appropriate to their status, may be challenged as unfair or as failing to afford rights. Know who you are interviewing and why.

### Prepare A Question Framework That Tests Rather Than Leads

The questions prepared before the interview shape its quality. A framework of leading questions, which telegraph the desired answer, produces accounts that mirror the interviewer's expectations and are challengeable as coerced or suggestive. A framework of open, testing questions produces accounts that reflect the interviewee's actual knowledge and can be evaluated against the evidence.

Prepare by:

- starting with broad, open questions that let the interviewee narrate in their own words, establishing their unprimed account before specific probing;
- following with specific questions that test the account against the evidence, referencing documents, timelines, and prior testimony without revealing more than necessary;
- avoiding leading questions that suggest the answer, such as you knew this was wrong, didn't you?, which compromise the account's reliability;
- preparing follow-up questions for likely responses, including denials, explanations, and deflections, so the interviewer can adapt rather than accept the first answer;
- planning to ask about contradictions directly, presenting the interviewee with the inconsistent evidence and asking for explanation, since confrontation with evidence is where interviews produce the most value.

The strongest interviews move from open narration to specific testing, letting the interviewee commit to an account before the evidence is introduced. An interview that begins with leading questions contaminates the account from the start.

### Plan For Documentation And Corroboration

An interview's value depends on how it is documented. An account recorded from memory after the interview, without notes or recording, is weak evidence that the interviewee can dispute. Documentation must be planned before the interview, not improvised after.

Plan documentation by:

- deciding whether to record, transcribe, take detailed notes, or use a court reporter, considering the legal context, the interviewee's rights, and the need for accuracy;
- where note-taking is the method, planning for a note-taker so the interviewer can focus on the conversation, since an interviewer who takes notes may miss nuances;
- planning to have the interviewee review and confirm the written account, which strengthens its reliability and reduces later disputes about what was said;
- identifying corroboration to seek during or after the interview, such as documents the interviewee references, other witnesses they name, or system records they describe;
- documenting not just the substantive answers but the interviewee's demeanor, hedging, and areas of reluctance, which may be relevant to credibility assessment.

An undocumented interview is a conversation, not evidence. Plan the documentation method to match the evidentiary standard the investigation requires.

### Consider Legal And Procedural Obligations Before Interviewing

Investigation interviews operate within legal and procedural constraints that vary by jurisdiction, context, and the interviewee's status. Failing to account for these can render the interview inadmissible, create legal exposure, or violate the interviewee's rights.

Consider obligations including:

- whether the interviewee is entitled to representation, such as union representation in Weingarten situations or counsel in certain contexts;
- whether the interviewee must be notified of the investigation's nature and their status before questioning;
- whether Upjohn warnings are required in attorney-led investigations, advising the interviewee that the lawyer represents the organization, not the individual, and that the interview is not privileged for the individual;
- whether the matter involves potential criminal conduct requiring coordination with law enforcement or consideration of self-incrimination rights;
- whether cross-border considerations apply, since interview protections and data privacy rules vary by jurisdiction;
- whether the interview could affect pending or potential litigation, requiring coordination with counsel.

An interview conducted without regard to these obligations may produce evidence that cannot be used, violate the interviewee's rights, or create separate liability. Identify the applicable obligations before interviewing.

## Common Traps

### Interviewing Without Evidence Review

An unprepared interviewer cannot test accounts against the record and may accept contradictory statements. Review all evidence first.

### Interviewing The Subject Before Witnesses

A subject interviewed first can tailor their account and coordinate with others. Sequence from outside in.

### Confusing Witness And Subject Status

Treating a subject as a casual witness misses rights and scrutiny; treating a witness as a subject is overreach. Designate and adjust.

### Leading Questions That Contaminate The Account

Questions that telegraph answers produce unreliable, challengeable accounts. Use open then testing questions.

### Improvised Documentation

An account recorded from memory after the fact is weak and disputable. Plan documentation before the interview.

### Ignoring Legal And Procedural Obligations

Failing to provide representation, notification, or warnings can render evidence inadmissible and create liability. Identify obligations first.

### Uncoordinated Interview Sequencing

Random order that alerts subjects before witnesses are captured contaminates testimony. Plan the sequence strategically.

## Self-Check

- Has all available evidence, including documents, data, timeline, prior testimony, control framework, and known gaps, been reviewed before any interview?
- Is the interview sequence planned from outside in, peripheral to central to subject, to capture uncontaminated testimony, with exceptions for urgency documented?
- Is each interviewee designated as witness or subject before the interview, with the approach, rights, notifications, and documentation adjusted accordingly, and status changes anticipated?
- Is the question framework structured from open narration to specific testing, avoiding leading questions, with follow-ups prepared for denials and explanations and plans to confront contradictions?
- Is documentation planned before the interview, including recording or note-taking method, note-taker assignment, interviewee confirmation, corroboration identification, and demeanor recording?
- Are legal and procedural obligations identified and addressed, including representation rights, notifications, Upjohn warnings, criminal-conduct coordination, cross-border considerations, and litigation coordination?
- Is the interview plan integrated so that each interview builds on prior evidence and the sequence protects testimony from contamination?
- Could the interviewer enter each interview prepared to test the account against the full evidentiary picture rather than merely recording statements?
