# Employee Classification And Exempt Status

Worker classification is one of the highest-stakes, highest-frequency judgments in labor compliance. The defining difficulty is that the same working relationship can be characterized differently by different statutes and agencies, and the consequences of error are not symmetric. Misclassifying an employee as an independent contractor can trigger back wages, payroll tax liability, benefits exposure, and penalties that compound across an entire workforce. Misclassifying a non-exempt employee as exempt produces systematic unpaid overtime that is invisible until a single complaint or audit surfaces it.

Use this skill before concluding that a worker is a contractor, that an employee is exempt from overtime, or that a classification is defensible. The goal is to make the agent identify which legal test governs, apply the substance-over-form analysis, document the basis, and recognize where the conclusion is jurisdiction-dependent and must be escalated to qualified employment counsel.

## Core Rules

### Apply The Correct Classification Test For The Purpose At Hand

There is no single universal employee-versus-contractor test. The controlling test depends on the statute and agency involved.

Identify which regimes apply:

- federal wage and hour law under the Fair Labor Standards Act economic reality test;
- Internal Revenue Service common-law test focusing on behavioral, financial, and relationship control;
- state wage laws, several of which use stricter ABC tests presuming employee status;
- unemployment insurance and workers compensation statutes with their own definitions;
- tax, benefits, and immigration regimes that may classify the same person differently.

The practical rule is that a worker can be an employee for wage purposes and a contractor for tax purposes, or vice versa. Never assume one classification satisfies all regimes. Where a state ABC test applies, the burden is on the employer to prove the worker is an independent contractor, and that burden is heavy.

### Analyze Substance Over Form For Independent Contractors

A written contract labeling a worker a contractor is not controlling. Agencies and courts examine the economic reality of the relationship.

Examine the actual working relationship:

- degree of control over how, when, and where work is performed;
- whether the work is integral to the employer's business;
- permanence and continuity of the relationship;
- investment in equipment and facilities by the worker;
- opportunity for profit or loss depending on managerial skill;
- skill and initiative required;
- whether the worker offers services to others in the market.

The more the relationship resembles permanent integration into the employer's operation under employer control, the more likely the worker is an employee. Control over the result alone, rather than the means and methods, favors contractor status.

### Apply Both Salary Basis And Duties Tests For Exemptions

Exempt status under the white-collar exemptions requires both a salary basis payment and satisfaction of a specific duties test. Both must be met.

Verify the salary basis:

- payment of a predetermined amount not subject to reduction based on quality or quantity of work;
- minimum salary threshold that may be higher under state law or updated federal rules;
- permissible and impermissible deductions;
- safe harbor provisions for improper deductions.

Then verify the duties test, because job title is irrelevant:

- executive exemption managing the enterprise or a recognized department, directing the work of two or more employees, and authority to hire and fire or weighty recommendations;
- administrative exemption performing office or non-manual work directly related to management or business operations, with exercise of discretion and independent judgment on matters of significance;
- professional exemption requiring advanced knowledge acquired through prolonged specialized intellectual instruction, or originality in a recognized creative field;
- computer professional and outside sales exemptions with their own specific requirements.

The most common error is treating a well-paid employee as exempt based on salary alone when the duties test is not met. Highly compensated employee shortcuts exist but still require performing at least one exempt duty.

### Document The Classification Basis And Review Periodically

Classification is not a one-time judgment. It must be documented and revisited.

Maintain:

- written analysis of the factors supporting contractor status or exemption;
- contractor agreements reflecting actual terms including control, independence, and no benefits;
- evidence the worker operates as an independent business where applicable;
- periodic reclassification reviews as relationships evolve;
- correction pathways when a classification no longer holds.

Documentation that contradicts actual practice is worse than no documentation because it evidences a knowing gap.

### Recognize Jurisdictional Variation And State Strictures

State law frequently imposes stricter thresholds than federal law.

Account for:

- state ABC tests that presume employee status for contractors;
- higher salary thresholds for exempt status;
- state-specific duties tests or categories;
- daily overtime and alternative workweek rules that affect exposure;
- joint employer rules that reach staffing agencies and franchisors.

The highest applicable standard governs in each jurisdiction. A classification valid in one state may fail in another.

### Respect Scope and Escalation Boundaries

Know where the agent's authority and competence end. When the question requires a license, a specialist's judgment, a final approval, or expertise the agent does not hold, the correct action is to escalate rather than to produce a confident answer that overreaches. Scope discipline protects the recipient from harm caused by an unqualified conclusion and protects the agent from liability. State explicitly when the output is advisory and must be confirmed by the qualified person.

## Common Traps

### Treating A Contract Label As Dispositive

A contract calling a worker an independent contractor does not control when the economic reality shows integration and control.

### Exempting Based On Salary Or Title Alone

A high salary and a managerial title do not satisfy the duties test. The actual day-to-day duties must meet the specific exemption criteria.

### Ignoring The State ABC Test

In ABC-test states, the employer bears the burden to prove independence, and many relationships that pass the federal test fail the state test.

### Allowing Impermissible Salary Deductions

Deductions that undermine the salary basis can destroy exempt status for the individual and across a class.

### Failing To Reclassify As Relationships Change

A contractor engagement that drifts into permanent integration, or an exempt role whose duties shift to routine production, silently becomes noncompliant.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- Has the correct classification test been identified for each applicable regime including FLSA, IRS, state wage law, and any ABC test jurisdiction?
- Is the independent contractor relationship analyzed on economic reality including control, integration, permanence, investment, opportunity for profit or loss, skill, and independence, rather than the contract label?
- For exempt status, are both the salary basis requirement and the specific duties test satisfied, with the duties test evaluated on actual responsibilities rather than title?
- Is the salary threshold met under the highest applicable federal or state standard, and are permissible and impermissible deductions controlled with a safe harbor?
- Is the classification basis documented and periodically reviewed as the relationship evolves?
- Are state-specific strictures including ABC tests, higher salary thresholds, and joint employer rules applied in each jurisdiction?
- Where exposure exists, has the matter been escalated to qualified employment counsel before a conclusion is finalized, recognizing that this guidance is not jurisdiction-specific legal advice?
- Does the output stay within the agent's scope, deferring final authority, licensed judgment, or specialist sign-off to the qualified person where the question exceeds the agent's competence?
