# Wage And Hour Classification Compliance

Wage and hour classification governs whether a worker is an employee or independent contractor and whether an employee is exempt or non-exempt from overtime. The defining feature is that classification is a facts-and-circumstances determination, not a label, and the tests differ across federal and state law, tax law, benefits law, and workers' compensation. The central difficulty is that an employer can be right under one test and wrong under another, and misclassification creates not only back-wage exposure but tax, benefits, and liability consequences.

Use this skill before advising on exempt status, independent contractor classification, joint employment, or misclassification remediation. The goal is to make the agent identify the applicable tests, analyze the actual work relationship, assess multi-jurisdictional exposure, and check documentation before concluding that a classification is correct.

## Core Rules

### Apply The Correct Exemption Tests

Exempt status depends on specific duties and salary tests, not job titles.

Assess:

- the salary basis test: payment on a salary basis at or above the threshold;
- the salary level test: meeting the minimum salary amount;
- the primary duty test for each exemption;
- executive exemption: managing the enterprise or department, directing work, hiring and firing authority;
- administrative exemption: exercise of discretion and independent judgment on matters of significance;
- professional exemption: advanced knowledge in a field of science or learning, or creative work;
- computer professional exemption: specific computer-related duties and compensation;
- outside sales exemption: customarily and regularly engaged away from the employer's place of business making sales;
- highly compensated employee exemption where applicable.

A job title does not create an exemption. The actual primary duty must be analyzed. "Matters of significance" and "discretion and independent judgment" are narrowly construed.

### Evaluate Independent Contractor Classification

Worker classification depends on the economic realities of the relationship, not the contract label.

Apply:

- the common law and economic realities factors;
- the degree of control over the work;
- the worker's opportunity for profit or loss;
- the investment in facilities and equipment;
- the permanence and exclusivity of the relationship;
- the skill and initiative required;
- whether the work is integral to the employer's business;
- the ABC test in states that use it (A: free from control; B: work outside the usual course of business; C: customarily engaged in an independent trade);
- variations across wage, tax, benefits, unemployment, and workers' compensation law.

The ABC test is stricter than the economic realities test and presumes employment unless all three conditions are met. A classification correct under federal wage law may fail under a state ABC test.

### Analyze The Actual Work, Not The Job Description

Classification turns on what workers actually do, not what they are called.

Control:

- review of actual daily duties and time allocation;
- interviews with workers and managers;
- review of decision-making authority and autonomy;
- assessment of whether discretion is real or merely nominal;
- documentation of the primary duty as the most important duty;
- identification of situations where duties have drifted from the original classification.

A "manager" who spends most time on production work and lacks hire/fire authority is likely non-exempt. Job descriptions must match reality and be updated as roles change.

### Assess Multi-Jurisdictional Exposure

Classification standards vary by jurisdiction, and the strictest applicable test governs.

Map:

- federal Fair Labor Standards Act standards;
- state wage and hour laws with stricter tests;
- state ABC tests for independent contractors;
- local wage orders and industry-specific rules;
- tax classification (IRS common law factors);
- benefits eligibility (common law plus plan terms);
- unemployment and workers' compensation classification;
- immigration and I-9 implications.

A worker may be a contractor under federal tax law but an employee under state wage law. The analysis must cover all applicable frameworks.

### Manage Joint Employment Risk

Joint employment expands liability to entities that share control over workers.

Assess:

- horizontal joint employment (shared control between employers);
- vertical joint employment (one employer providing labor to another);
- staffing agency and client relationships;
- franchise and franchisor relationships;
- parent-subsidiary and affiliate structures;
- shared services arrangements;
- liability for the co-employer's wage violations.

Joint employment means both entities can be liable for wage and hour violations. Staffing arrangements require clear allocation of responsibilities.

### Calculate Exposure For Misclassification

Misclassification exposure includes back wages, penalties, and collateral consequences.

Estimate:

- back overtime for reclassified exempt employees (usually two years, three for willful);
- back wages and benefits for reclassified contractors;
- liquidated damages (double damages under federal law);
- state law penalties including wage theft and waiting time penalties;
- tax liability including payroll taxes and classification penalties;
- attorney's fees and costs;
- collateral effects on benefit plan eligibility and coverage.

Exposure compounds across wage, tax, benefits, and penalty frameworks. The two- or three-year lookback can be substantial.

### Document Classification Decisions

Classification decisions should be documented with supporting analysis.

Cover:

- written analysis of duties and salary basis for exemptions;
- documentation of the contractor relationship basis;
- contractor agreements with appropriate terms (but recognizing labels do not control);
- periodic reclassification reviews;
- escalation of classification questions to qualified counsel;
- consistent treatment of similarly situated workers.

Documentation supports a good-faith defense where available. Inconsistency—treating similar workers differently—is itself evidence of improper classification.

## Common Traps

### Job Title Treated As Exemption

Calling someone a "manager" or "administrator" does not satisfy the duties test.

### Salary Basis Without Dutits Test

Paying a salary does not create an exemption; the duties test is independent.

### Contractor Label Ignoring Economic Realities

A contract calling a worker a contractor does not control if the relationship is one of employment.

### Classification Correct Under One Law But Wrong Under Another

Federal tax classification does not determine state wage law status.

### Duties Drift Without Reclassification

A role that was properly exempt at hire may become non-exempt as duties change.

### Inconsistent Treatment Of Similarly Situated Workers

Treating identical roles differently is evidence of improper classification.

## Self-Check

- Is exempt status analyzed under salary basis, salary level, and primary duty tests for the specific exemption, not just the job title?
- Is independent contractor classification analyzed under economic realities, ABC test where applicable, and variations across wage, tax, benefits, and workers' compensation law?
- Is the analysis based on actual duties and time allocation, not job descriptions, with the primary duty identified?
- Is multi-jurisdictional exposure assessed, with the strictest applicable test applied?
- Is joint employment assessed for staffing, franchise, affiliate, and shared services arrangements?
- Is misclassification exposure calculated across back wages, liquidated damages, penalties, tax liability, benefits, and attorney's fees?
- Are classification decisions documented with analysis, contractor agreements, periodic review, and consistent treatment?
- Is the distinction between federal and state standards, and between wage law and tax law, maintained?
- Is the good-faith defense assessed where available, and are classification questions escalated to qualified counsel?
- Is the impact of reclassification on benefits, tax, and other obligations assessed before remediation?
