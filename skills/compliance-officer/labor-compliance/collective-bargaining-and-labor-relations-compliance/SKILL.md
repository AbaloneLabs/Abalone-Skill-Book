# Collective Bargaining And Labor Relations Compliance

Collective bargaining compliance governs the relationship between employers and unions and the duty to bargain over terms and conditions of employment. The defining feature is that labor law imposes affirmative duties—to bargain in good faith, to provide information, to honor contract terms—that exist independent of whether the employer wants a union relationship. The central difficulty is that "good faith" is a process standard, not an outcome standard, and employers frequently commit unfair labor practices through surface bargaining, unilateral changes, or retaliation that feel like ordinary management but violate the duty to bargain.

Use this skill before advising on union campaigns, bargaining obligations, contract administration, unilateral changes, or strike response. The goal is to make the agent identify the representation status, the scope of the bargaining duty, the information obligations, and the restrictions on employer conduct before concluding that a labor relations action is compliant.

## Core Rules

### Determine Representation Status And Certification

The employer's obligations depend on whether employees are represented and by whom.

Assess:

- whether a union has been certified as the exclusive bargaining representative;
- the bargaining unit definition and scope;
- the contract status (in negotiation, in effect, or expired);
- card check versus election certification;
- jurisdiction (NLRA versus Railway Labor Act versus state public sector law);
- supervisory and managerial exclusion from bargaining units.

The duty to bargain arises upon certification or, in some cases, upon a showing of majority support. The bargaining unit definition determines who is covered.

### Bargain In Good Faith Over Mandatory Subjects

The duty to bargain covers mandatory subjects of bargaining.

Cover:

- mandatory subjects: wages, hours, and other terms and conditions of employment;
- permissive subjects: matters that affect the bargaining process but not terms directly;
- illegal subjects: provisions that would violate law;
- the obligation to meet at reasonable times and with a genuine desire to reach agreement;
- surface bargaining (going through the motions) as a violation;
- unilateral changes to mandatory subjects during bargaining or contract gaps;
- effects bargaining on decisions that affect unit employees;
- management rights clauses and their limits.

Unilateral changes to wages, hours, or conditions without bargaining are a hallmark unfair labor practice. Even decisions that are management prerogatives may require effects bargaining on their impact on employees.

### Fulfill Information Requests From The Union

The union is entitled to information relevant to its bargaining role.

Control:

- duty to provide information relevant to collective bargaining;
- types of information covered (wage data, benefit costs, staffing, subcontracting);
- timeliness of response;
- format and completeness;
- confidentiality and privacy protections;
- cost allocation for production;
- refusals based on relevance or burden.

Unreasonable refusal to provide relevant information is an unfair labor practice. The relevance standard is broad, covering information needed to understand and negotiate terms.

### Avoid Unfair Labor Practices

Specific employer conduct constitutes unfair labor practices.

Prohibit:

- interference, restraint, or coercion of Section 7 rights;
- domination of or contribution to a labor organization;
- discrimination in hire or tenure to encourage or discourage union membership;
- discrimination for filing charges or giving testimony;
- refusal to bargain in good faith;
- unilateral changes to mandatory subjects;
- surface bargaining or Boulwarism;
- recognition of a non-majority union (subject to exceptions);
- retaliation for protected concerted activity.

Protected concerted activity includes group complaints about wages and conditions, even without a union. Retaliation for such activity is unlawful regardless of union status.

### Administer The Contract And Handle Grievances

A collective bargaining agreement creates enforceable obligations.

Implement:

- administration according to contract terms;
- grievance procedure steps and timelines;
- arbitration of unresolved grievances;
- management rights and past practice;
- consistent application of contract provisions;
- documentation of disciplinary and management actions;
- just cause standards for discipline;
- coordination with labor relations and management.

The contract is the law of the workplace. Discipline must generally meet a just cause standard, which is more demanding than at-will employment. Past practice can supplement contract language.

### Manage Strikes, Picketing, And Lockouts

Economic weapons are regulated by labor law.

Assess:

- the right to strike for economic reasons versus unfair labor practice strikes;
- strikebreaker and replacement worker rules;
- lockouts and their legality;
- picketing rules and restrictions;
- secondary boycott and hot cargo prohibitions;
- recognitional picketing limits;
- no-strike and no-lockout clauses in contracts;
- status quo and contract bar rules.

Economic strikers can be permanently replaced; unfair labor practice strikers are entitled to reinstatement. Secondary activity targeting neutral employers is prohibited.

### Avoid Retaliation For Protected Concerted Activity

Protected concerted activity extends beyond union contexts.

Cover:

- two or more employees acting together about wages, hours, or conditions;
- a single employee acting on behalf of others or seeking to initiate group action;
- social media and electronic communications as concerted activity;
- restrictions on policies that chill Section 7 rights (confidentiality, non-disparagement, social media);
- retaliation prohibition regardless of union status;
- the scope of protected activity versus personal complaints.

Handbook policies that broadly prohibit criticism of the company or discussion of wages can violate Section 7. The protection applies in non-union workplaces.

### Navigate Union Campaigns Lawfully

Employer conduct during union campaigns is restricted.

Control:

- no interrogation of employees about union support;
- no surveillance or appearance of surveillance;
- no threats of job loss or benefit loss;
- no promises of benefits to discourage union support;
- no discrimination based on union activity;
- lawful non-coercive communication of views;
- captive audience meeting limits and timing rules;
- election conduct and TIPS (threats, interrogation, promises, surveillance).

The TIPS framework—no Threats, Interrogation, Promises, or Surveillance—summarizes the core campaign restrictions. Even lawful communication can become coercive through context or repetition.

## Common Traps

### Unilateral Change To A Mandatory Subject

Changing wages, schedules, or conditions without bargaining is a clear unfair labor practice.

### Surface Bargaining Without Genuine Intent

Going through the motions without intent to reach agreement violates the duty.

### Refusal To Provide Relevant Information

Withholding bargaining-relevant information is an unfair labor practice.

### Handbook Policies Chilling Section 7 Rights

Broad confidentiality, non-disparagement, or social media policies can violate labor law.

### Discipline Without Just Cause Under A CBA

At-will standards do not apply to bargaining unit discipline; just cause is required.

### Campaign Conduct Crossing Into Coercion

Lawful communication becomes coercive through threats, interrogation, promises, or surveillance.

## Self-Check

- Is representation status determined including certification, bargaining unit, contract status, jurisdiction, and exclusions?
- Is the duty to bargain in good faith understood covering mandatory subjects, permissive subjects, surface bargaining, unilateral changes, effects bargaining, and management rights?
- Are information requests fulfilled relevant to bargaining, timely, complete, with confidentiality protections and reasonable refusals?
- Are unfair labor practices avoided including interference, domination, discrimination, refusal to bargain, unilateral changes, and retaliation for concerted activity?
- Is the contract administered through grievance procedures, arbitration, consistent application, just cause discipline, and documentation?
- Are strikes, picketing, and lockouts managed under economic versus ULP strike rules, replacement restrictions, secondary boycott prohibitions, and contract clauses?
- Is protected concerted activity protected including group complaints, social media, handbook policy review, and retaliation prevention?
- Are union campaigns conducted lawfully under the TIPS framework with no threats, interrogation, promises, or surveillance?
- Is the distinction between mandatory, permissive, and illegal subjects of bargaining maintained?
- Are state public sector labor law variations applied where relevant?
