# Union Avoidance And Organizing Response Compliance

Union avoidance and organizing response compliance governs what an employer may lawfully do when employees are considering union representation. The defining feature is that the employer has a right to express its views, but the line between lawful persuasion and unlawful interference is narrow and context-dependent. The central difficulty is that actions that feel like normal management—restructuring, discipline, policy changes—become unlawful when timed to discourage organizing, and the employer's intent is inferred from timing, context, and pattern.

Use this skill before advising on organizing response, captive audience meetings, policy enforcement during campaigns, or communication strategies. The goal is to make the agent identify the lawful bounds of employer expression, the timing and context risks, the restrictions on policy enforcement, and the retaliation prohibitions before concluding that an organizing response is compliant.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Bounds Of Lawful Employer Expression

Employers may express views but may not threaten, interrogate, promise, or surveil.

Assess:

- lawful expression of opinion about unionization (predictions of impact, factual statements);
- prohibition on threats of job loss, closure, or benefit loss;
- prohibition on promises of benefits to discourage support;
- prohibition on interrogation about union activities or sympathies;
- prohibition on surveillance or creating the appearance of surveillance;
- prohibition on retaliation for protected activity;
- the totality of conduct standard assessing all communications together.

A prediction based on economic facts ("a union cannot guarantee wages") differs from a threat ("we will close if you unionize"). The distinction turns on whether the statement is a reasonable forecast or an implicit threat of reprisal.

### Respond To Organizing Without Retaliation

Organizing activity is protected, and retaliation is unlawful.

Control:

- identification of protected concerted and organizing activity;
- no adverse action based on union sympathy or activity;
- no change in treatment, assignments, or conditions targeting organizers;
- documentation of legitimate business reasons predating the organizing;
- consistency of discipline and treatment;
- heightened scrutiny of actions taken during organizing periods;
- separation of decision-makers from knowledge of organizing where possible.

Actions taken shortly after organizing activity are presumed retaliatory absent strong contemporaneous documentation. The timing itself is evidence.

### Manage Captive Audience Meetings Lawfully

Captive audience meetings are permitted with restrictions.

Implement:

- no threats during captive audience meetings;
- lawful expression of views and predictions;
- timing restrictions (typically no captive audience within 24 hours of an election under the historic rule, though standards evolve);
- no interrogation or surveillance during meetings;
- consistency in attendance and messaging;
- documentation of meeting content;
- supervisor training on lawful communication.

Captive audience meetings are a tool for lawful expression but become unlawful if they contain threats or interrogation. The content and delivery must be carefully managed.

### Enforce Solicitation And Distribution Policies Even-Handedly

No-solicitation and no-distribution policies must be applied consistently.

Control:

- policies that restrict solicitation during working time and distribution in working areas;
- even-handed application to union and non-union solicitation;
- no targeting of union materials while permitting other solicitations;
- working time versus working area distinctions;
- restrictions on email and electronic systems for organizing (subject to law);
- no discipline for solicitation that occurs during non-working time.

A policy enforced only against union materials is discriminatory. The rule is working time (solicitation restricted) and working area (distribution restricted), not a blanket ban.

### Avoid Unilateral Changes During Organizing

Changes to terms and conditions during organizing carry heightened risk.

Assess:

- no wage or benefit increases timed to discourage organizing (could be a promise or unlawful grant);
- no changes to schedules, assignments, or conditions targeting organizers;
- no restructuring timed to affect the unit;
- maintenance of the status quo during organizing;
- effects bargaining obligations for necessary changes;
- documentation of business necessity independent of organizing.

Even beneficial changes (raises) during organizing can be challenged as promises or unlawful grants. The safest course is to maintain the status quo absent a clear, documented business necessity.

### Supervise Managers And Supervisors During Campaigns

Supervisors are agents of the employer, and their conduct binds the company.

Control:

- training on lawful communication and the TIPS framework;
- clear instructions on prohibited conduct;
- monitoring of supervisor conduct and communications;
- separation of supervisors with organizing knowledge from discipline decisions;
- documentation of supervisor training;
- accountability for supervisor violations.

A supervisor's threat or interrogation is imputed to the employer. Supervisor training and oversight are essential because supervisors are closest to employees.

### Address Electronic And Social Media Organizing

Organizing extends to digital channels.

Assess:

- Section 7 rights to discuss wages and conditions on social media;
- restrictions on employer monitoring of organizing communications;
- email system rules (employees generally have Section 7 rights to use company email for organizing during non-working time);
- social media policy compliance with Section 7;
- no retaliation for digital organizing activity.

Broad social media policies that prohibit criticism of the company violate Section 7. Email systems are generally available for organizing during non-working time.

### Prepare For And Navigate Elections

Election conduct is closely regulated.

Cover:

- election agreements on scope and conduct;
- no electioneering near the polling place;
- no threats or promises in the final days;
- compliance with Board rules on timing and conduct;
- handling of objections and challenges;
- post-election certification and bargaining obligations.

The period immediately before an election is the highest-risk for violations. Even subtle pressure can set aside an election.

## Common Traps

### Prediction Treated As A Threat

A statement that reads as a threat of job loss or closure, rather than a factual economic prediction, is unlawful.

### Discipline Of An Organizer Without Predating Documentation

Discipline of an active organizer without strong contemporaneous documentation is presumed retaliatory.

### No-Solicitation Policy Enforced Only Against Union Materials

Selective enforcement reveals anti-union motivation and is unlawful.

### Beneficial Change Timed To Discourage Organizing

Raises or benefits granted during organizing can be challenged as unlawful promises or grants.

### Supervisor Conduct Imputed To The Employer

Untrained supervisors who threaten or interrogate create employer liability.

### Social Media Policy Chilling Section 7 Rights

Broad prohibitions on criticism or wage discussion violate labor law.

## Self-Check

- Is employer expression lawful under the TIPS framework, distinguishing predictions from threats, and free of promises, interrogation, and surveillance?
- Is organizing activity protected from retaliation, with consistent treatment, documented legitimate reasons, and heightened scrutiny during organizing periods?
- Are captive audience meetings conducted lawfully with no threats, no interrogation, proper timing, consistent messaging, and documentation?
- Are solicitation and distribution policies enforced even-handedly, distinguishing working time and working area, with no targeting of union materials?
- Are unilateral changes avoided during organizing, with status quo maintenance, documented business necessity, and effects bargaining where needed?
- Are supervisors trained, monitored, and separated from discipline decisions involving organizers?
- Is electronic and social media organizing respected under Section 7, with compliant policies and no retaliation?
- Is election conduct lawful with no electioneering, no last-minute threats or promises, and proper handling of objections?
- Is the distinction between lawful persuasion and unlawful interference maintained across all communications?
- Is the totality of conduct standard considered in assessing the campaign?
