# Working Time Overtime And Regular Rate

Working time compliance governs what time must be counted and paid, and how overtime is calculated. The defining feature is that hours worked is broader than scheduled time. It includes waiting, travel, training, donning and doffing, and on-call time under specific conditions, and the regular rate of pay that drives overtime must include most forms of compensation beyond base pay. The central difficulty is that employers often undercount hours and miscalculate the regular rate, creating systematic exposure that is invisible until an audit or collective claim surfaces it.

Use this skill before advising on timekeeping practices, meal and rest break policies, overtime calculation, bonus inclusion in the regular rate, or off-the-clock work risks. The goal is to make the agent identify what counts as hours worked, how the regular rate is built, what breaks are required, and where off-the-clock risk exists before concluding that pay practices are compliant. Conclusions are jurisdiction-dependent and should be confirmed with qualified employment counsel.

## Core Rules

### Define And Capture All Hours Worked

Hours worked includes more than active productive time.

Cover:

- all time the employee is suffered or permitted to work, including unauthorized overtime;
- preliminary and postliminary activities such as donning and doffing or booting systems when integral and indispensable;
- waiting time that is predominantly for the employer's benefit;
- travel time during the workday and special one-day assignments;
- training time that is job-related or during regular hours;
- on-call time where the employee is restricted and engaged to wait;
- work performed remotely or outside scheduled hours;
- de minimis doctrine considerations, which are narrowing under recent guidance.

The principle is control. If the employer suffers or permits the work, it is hours worked, even if unauthorized. Remote and mobile work has expanded off-the-clock risk.

### Comply With Meal And Rest Break Requirements

State laws impose specific break requirements that are absent under federal law.

Implement:

- meal period timing, duration, and duty-free requirements by state;
- duty-free meal periods with complete relief from duty;
- premium pay in lieu of a missed meal or rest break where required;
- rest period frequency, duration, and timing;
- nursing break obligations and lactation space;
- recordkeeping of breaks taken or waived;
- coordination with timekeeping systems.

Federal law does not require meal or rest breaks, but many states do, with premium pay owed for missed breaks. A duty-free meal requires complete relief, not just a desk lunch.

### Calculate Overtime Using The Correct Regular Rate

Overtime is calculated on the regular rate, which includes more than base pay.

Build the regular rate:

- all remuneration for employment included unless specifically excluded;
- non-discretionary bonuses and incentives included;
- shift differentials and premium pay included;
- commissions included;
- on-call pay included;
- cost of certain board, lodging, and facilities included;
- excludable items including discretionary bonuses, gifts, payments for occasional periods not worked, premium pay for excess hours, profit-sharing under qualified plans, and certain stock and equity;
- recalculation of overtime when bonuses are paid retroactively.

The regular rate is not the base hourly rate. Excluding non-discretionary bonuses from the regular rate is a common and costly error. Retroactive bonuses require retroactive overtime recalculation.

### Avoid Off-The-Clock Work

Off-the-clock work is a primary source of wage and hour exposure.

Control:

- prohibition on working before clock-in or after clock-out;
- system controls to prevent off-the-clock entry;
- manager training to recognize and prevent off-the-clock work;
- review of time records for suspicious patterns such as always ending at the hour or no lunch;
- addressing workload that requires off-the-clock work to complete;
- remote work controls and expectations;
- complaint channels for off-the-clock pressure.

Off-the-clock work often arises from workloads that cannot be completed in scheduled hours. The system must capture all time, and managers must not implicitly require unpaid work.

### Manage Time Rounding Carefully

Time rounding is permitted but must be neutral.

Control:

- rounding to the nearest quarter hour or other increment;
- rounding that averages out neutral over time;
- prohibition on rounding that consistently favors the employer;
- analysis of rounding impact on overtime;
- state restrictions or prohibitions on rounding;
- the trend toward exact time capture eliminating rounding.

Rounding that consistently results in underpayment is not neutral. The trend is toward eliminating rounding in favor of exact capture.

### Handle On-Call, Waiting, And Travel Time Correctly

These categories require fact-intensive analysis.

Assess:

- on-call time and whether the employee can use the time effectively for personal purposes;
- engaged-to-wait versus waiting-to-be-engaged;
- travel time where home-to-work generally is not counted, travel during the day is counted, and special one-day out-of-town travel is counted;
- training time where job-related or during regular hours is counted, and outside working hours voluntary with no direct work is not counted;
- donning and doffing that is integral and indispensable to principal activities is counted.

These are fact patterns, not bright lines. The analysis must reflect the actual restrictions on the employee.

### Maintain Timekeeping Records

Accurate timekeeping is both a legal obligation and a defense.

Cover:

- records of hours worked each day and total each week;
- records of meal and rest breaks;
- records supporting the regular rate calculation;
- retention for the required period, typically two to three years;
- employee review and certification of time records;
- system controls against alteration;
- handling of disputed time records.

In a wage claim, the employer bears the burden if records are inadequate. Employee certification of time records supports accuracy.

## Common Traps

### Regular Rate Excluding Non-Discretionary Bonuses

Omitting non-discretionary bonuses from the regular rate undercalculates overtime and creates systematic exposure.

### Off-The-Clock Work From Workload Pressure

Workloads that require unpaid work to complete generate off-the-clock violations regardless of policy.

### Duty-Free Meal That Is Not Duty-Free

A meal period where the employee remains on duty or on call is not duty-free and must be paid.

### Rounding That Consistently Favors The Employer

Rounding must be neutral. Consistent underpayment is not defensible.

### On-Call Treated As Off-Duty When Restrictions Are Severe

Severe on-call restrictions make the time compensable regardless of call frequency.

## Self-Check

- Are all hours worked captured including unauthorized overtime, preliminary and postliminary activities, waiting, travel, training, on-call, and remote work?
- Are meal and rest break requirements met by state, including duty-free meals, premium pay for missed breaks, rest period frequency, nursing breaks, and recordkeeping?
- Is overtime calculated using the regular rate including non-discretionary bonuses, shift differentials, commissions, and on-call pay, with retroactive recalculation for retroactive bonuses?
- Is off-the-clock work prevented through system controls, manager training, workload assessment, remote controls, and complaint channels?
- Is time rounding neutral, analyzed for impact, and compliant with state restrictions?
- Are on-call, waiting, travel, training, and donning and doffing time analyzed based on actual restrictions and the integral and indispensable test?
- Are timekeeping records maintained, retained, employee-certified, system-controlled, and dispute-handled?
- Are state-specific rules for breaks, rounding, and daily overtime applied in each jurisdiction, with the de minimis doctrine applied narrowly?
- Where systematic exposure is identified, has the matter been escalated to qualified employment counsel, recognizing that this guidance is general and not jurisdiction-specific legal advice?
