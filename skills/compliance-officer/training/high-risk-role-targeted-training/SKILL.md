# High-Risk Role Targeted Training

General workforce compliance training cannot carry the load for high-risk functions. Sales teams facing anti-bribery, gifts, discounts, and channel-partner risk; finance teams handling payments, treasury, and books-and-records integrity; procurement teams with sourcing authority and supplier collusion exposure; executives setting tone and approving exceptions; traders facing market-abuse and sanctions risk; government-facing teams exposed to bribery and revolving-door rules; and engineers handling export-controlled technology all make decisions where a single wrong call creates regulatory, financial, or reputational damage. Training these populations with the same generic ethics module everyone else takes is not risk-based. It is checkbox compliance that leaves the highest exposure unaddressed.

Use this skill before designing or commissioning training for a specific high-risk function, responding to an incident in a function, refreshing function-specific curriculum, or defending function-specific training depth to leadership. The goal is to make the agent tailor content, scenarios, depth, frequency, and reinforcement to the actual decisions and exposures of each function.

## Core Rules

### Define Each Function's Risk Profile Before Designing Training

Targeted training begins from the function's specific risk profile, not from a generic topic list. Before designing, map for each function:

- the decisions its members make that carry compliance risk;
- the regulatory regimes that govern those decisions;
- the systems, data, and money flows they control;
- the third parties they interact with, such as customers, suppliers, officials, agents;
- the incentives and pressures that push toward misconduct;
- the historical incidents, near misses, and audit findings in the function;
- the controls in place and where they are weakest;
- the seniority and authority distribution within the function.

This profile is the design brief. A module built without it will be generic and ineffective.

### Tailor Scenarios To The Function's Real Work

The single most important feature of high-risk role training is that scenarios must look like the work the audience actually does. A salesperson will not engage with a scenario about export classification of components, and an engineer will not engage with a scenario about channel-partner discounts.

For each function, build scenarios around its genuine decisions:

- Sales: gifts and hospitality near a deal, facilitation payments, commissions and channel-partner payments, bid information, relationships with officials, expense reporting under quota pressure.
- Finance: payment approvals and vendor bank-detail changes, treasury and intercompany transfers, revenue recognition and period-end pressure, expense and books-and-records integrity, anti-money-laundering and sanctions screening.
- Procurement: bid-rigging and supplier collusion, conflicts of interest and sourcing decisions, kickbacks, sole-source justification, and post-employment restrictions.
- Executives and senior leaders: tone from the top, approving exceptions, insider trading and material non-public information, political contributions, conflicts of interest, and accountability for subordinate misconduct.
- Trading and markets: market abuse, insider dealing, communications surveillance, sanctions and restricted-party screening, personal account dealing.
- Government-facing roles: bribery and facilitation payments, gifts and hospitality with officials, revolving-door and post-employment rules, sponsorships and charitable contributions.
- Engineering and technology: export controls and deemed exports, sanctions on technology, data privacy in product design, and secure development obligations.

Scenarios should be sanitized from real internal cases where possible, because recognizable situations drive engagement and retention.

### Calibrate Depth And Frequency To The Function's Exposure

High-risk functions need more depth and often more frequency than the general workforce. A single annual module is rarely sufficient for a function with continuous, high-stakes decisions.

Set depth and frequency by considering:

- the severity and likelihood of the function's risks;
- the pace of regulatory change affecting the function;
- the turnover and tenure mix in the function;
- the pressure cycles, such as quarter-end for sales or period-close for finance;
- the function's recent incident history;
- the maturity of its controls.

For the highest-exposure functions, combine annual training with event-triggered refreshers after regulatory changes, incidents, or policy updates, and with spaced reinforcement through microlearning and workflow prompts.

### Address The Pressures And Incentives That Drive Misconduct

Training that teaches the rules but ignores why people break them is incomplete. High-risk functions often face real pressures: quota, margin, deadline, cost, and career pressure.

Effective targeted training should:

- name the pressures honestly rather than pretending they do not exist;
- show how the pressure creates the exact misconduct the rule prevents;
- teach the legitimate paths to meet the goal without violating the rule;
- explain the escalation and support available when pressure conflicts with compliance;
- connect to incentive and accountability structures so the training is not contradicted by how people are paid and promoted.

If the incentive system rewards the behavior the training prohibits, no amount of training will hold. Flag the misalignment to leadership rather than papering over it.

### Include The Function's Leaders As Co-Designers And Deliverers

High-risk role training lands best when the function's own leaders visibly own it. Compliance-designed content delivered without functional sponsorship is often dismissed as overhead.

Engage function leaders by:

- co-designing scenarios with sales, finance, or procurement leadership;
- having senior leaders introduce or co-deliver key sessions;
- equipping people managers with talking points that reinforce the training;
- tying training completion and behavior to functional performance metrics;
- asking leaders to share sanitized real examples from their own experience.

Leader involvement signals that the training reflects the function's reality, not just compliance's priorities.

### Verify Understanding With Function-Specific Assessments

A generic assessment cannot verify that a salesperson or trader can apply the rule to their decisions. Build assessments from the same function-specific scenarios as the content.

Strong function-specific assessments:

- present decisions the audience actually faces;
- include distractors that reflect common real mistakes in that function;
- require choosing an action and an escalation, not just identifying a term;
- provide feedback explaining the reasoning in the function's context;
- set a meaningful passing standard;
- are refreshed so they do not become known;
- are supplemented for the highest-risk roles with simulation, case discussion, or manager sign-off that cannot be faked.

For some functions, layer in attestation of specific obligations, such as conflicts of interest or outside activities, where the attestation is genuinely meaningful.

### Plan Onboarding, Event-Triggered, And Refresher Touchpoints

High-risk functions have churn. New joiners, promotions into authority, transfers between roles, and new product or geography launches all change an individual's risk exposure.

Design a lifecycle of touchpoints:

- onboarding training before the new joiner is exposed to the risk;
- promotion-triggered training when an individual gains new authority, such as a first-time people manager or a new approval authority;
- event-triggered training after regulatory change, incident, or policy update;
- annual refreshers calibrated to the function's risk;
- workflow prompts at the moment of risk.

A one-time module at hire does not protect a function whose members gain authority and face new risks over time.

## Common Traps

### Generic Ethics Module For A High-Risk Function

Treating sales, finance, or procurement like the general workforce under-trains the highest exposure. Tailor content to the function's decisions.

### Scenarios That Do Not Resemble The Work

Learners dismiss content that does not look like their job. Use sanitized real cases from the function.

### Teaching Rules While Ignoring Pressures

If training pretends quota or period-close pressure does not exist, it will not change behavior. Address pressures and legitimate alternatives.

### Incentives That Contradict The Training

When the function is paid for the behavior the training prohibits, training fails. Flag and fix the misalignment.

### Compliance-Only Delivery Without Functional Sponsorship

Training imposed without function leadership ownership is dismissed as overhead. Co-design and co-deliver with leaders.

### Generic Assessments For Specialized Roles

A function-specific module deserves a function-specific assessment. Test the real decision, not a definition.

### One-Time Training At Hire

Risk exposure grows with authority and new responsibilities. Add promotion-triggered and event-triggered touchpoints.

### Treating Executives As Exempt

Senior leaders are high-risk and often the least trained. Design executive-specific content, not waivers.

## Self-Check

- Has each high-risk function's risk profile been mapped to its decisions, regulations, systems, third parties, pressures, incidents, and controls before content design?
- Are scenarios built around the function's genuine work, sanitized from real internal cases where possible?
- Are depth and frequency calibrated to the function's exposure, with event-triggered refreshers for the highest-risk functions?
- Does training honestly address the pressures and incentives that drive misconduct, and flag incentive misalignments to leadership?
- Are function leaders engaged as co-designers and co-deliverers, with managers equipped to reinforce the training?
- Are assessments function-specific, scenario-based, with plausible distractors, meaningful standards, and refresh cycles?
- Are onboarding, promotion-triggered, event-triggered, and refresher touchpoints planned across the role lifecycle?
- Are executives and senior leaders included as a high-risk population with their own tailored content rather than exempted?
- Is targeted training connected to the function's performance, incentive, and accountability structures so it is not contradicted in practice?
- Could the organization defend, to a regulator, that each high-risk function received depth and scenarios matched to its specific exposure?
