# Transaction Monitoring STR SAR Filing Decisions

The decision to file a suspicious transaction report (STR) or suspicious activity report (SAR) is the culmination of the monitoring and investigation process, and it is the point at which the institution discharges its reporting obligation to the authorities. Under FATF Recommendation 20, the BSA, the EU AMLD, FinCEN SAR rules, and equivalent national regimes, obliged entities must report transactions or activity that they know, suspect, or have reason to suspect involve illicit funds. The central judgment problem is that the filing decision requires judgment about what constitutes suspicion, and that judgment is made under time pressure, confidentiality constraints, and the risk of both over-filing and under-filing. Filing too liberally floods the financial intelligence unit with noise. Failing to file when suspicion exists is a serious violation that can expose the institution and individuals to enforcement and, in some regimes, criminal liability.

Use this skill before making STR or SAR filing decisions, evaluating suspicion thresholds, defining filing procedures, or managing continuing activity and confidentiality obligations. The goal is to make the agent think about the suspicion standard, timing, supporting documentation, and the decisions that are easy to make too casually. A filing decision that is deferred, diluted, or avoided is a reporting failure.

This skill addresses jurisdiction-specific obligations. STR and SAR thresholds, deadlines, forms, and confidentiality rules differ across FATF member states and national regimes. Always confirm the applicable national law, regulator guidance, and financial intelligence unit requirements, and consult qualified AML or legal counsel for specific filing decisions.

## Core Rules

### Apply The Correct Suspicion Standard

The filing threshold is suspicion, not certainty. The institution does not need to prove that the activity is illicit. It needs to have reason to suspect, based on specific facts, that the transaction or activity involves illicit funds or activity.

Suspicion standard elements:

- the applicable regime defines the threshold, commonly know, suspect, or have reason to suspect;
- suspicion must be based on specific, articulable facts, not a general unease;
- the facts should connect the activity to a laundering, terrorist financing, sanctions evasion, fraud, or other reportable typology;
- the institution does not need to identify the underlying crime, only that the funds or activity are suspicious;
- the standard is lower than proof beyond reasonable doubt or even probable cause in many regimes.

The institution should train investigators to recognize when the suspicion standard is met and to document the specific facts that support it. A filing decision based on a generic statement that the activity looked unusual, without connecting it to a typology or specific red flags, is weak.

### File Within The Regulatory Deadline

STR and SAR filing is time-sensitive. The deadline runs from the point at which the institution forms the suspicion, not from the transaction date.

Deadline considerations:

- many regimes require filing within a defined period, such as 30 days, after the initial detection of suspicious activity;
- some regimes require immediate or prompt notification for certain categories, such as terrorist financing;
- the clock starts when the institution, through its personnel, detects or should have detected the suspicion;
- delays in investigation do not extend the deadline;
- continuing suspicious activity may require additional or continuing reports.

The institution must have a process that moves from detection to investigation to filing decision within the deadline. A backlog of unfiled cases is a serious deficiency.

### Document The Factual Basis For The Filing

The STR or SAR must contain a clear, factual narrative that explains what is suspicious and why. The narrative is what the financial intelligence unit uses to assess and act on the report.

Narrative quality standards:

- describe the transactions or activity in specific terms, including dates, amounts, accounts, and counterparties;
- explain the customer's profile and expected activity from CDD;
- identify the specific red flags or typology indicators that generated suspicion;
- describe the investigation performed and what it revealed;
- avoid conclusory statements without supporting facts;
- reference supporting documentation that is retained in the file.

A narrative that says the activity appeared unusual, without describing what was unusual and why it connects to a reportable typology, provides little value to investigators and is hard to defend.

### Handle Continuing Activity Appropriately

Suspicious activity often continues over time. The institution must decide how to handle ongoing suspicious activity in a relationship that is not exited.

Continuing activity options:

- file continuing or supplemental reports at defined intervals if the activity persists;
- conduct periodic reviews of the relationship to assess whether suspicion continues;
- consider whether to restrict, exit, or place the relationship under enhanced monitoring;
- document the decision to continue the relationship and the rationale;
- assess whether continuing the relationship creates unacceptable risk or requires law enforcement guidance.

Some regimes have specific rules for continuing activity, including requirements to file periodic supplemental reports. The institution must understand and follow the applicable rule. Continuing a relationship with known suspicious activity without a documented risk decision is a deficiency.

### Maintain Strict Confidentiality And No-Tipping-Off

STR and SAR filings are confidential. The institution and its personnel are prohibited from tipping off the customer or any other party that a report has been or will be filed.

Confidentiality obligations:

- restrict access to filing information to personnel with a need to know;
- prohibit disclosure of the filing to the customer, including through account closure notices that imply a report;
- train staff on the tipping-off prohibition and the consequences of violation;
- handle law enforcement requests for information through controlled channels;
- protect the filing from discovery in civil litigation where the regime provides protection.

Tipping off is a criminal offense in many regimes. Account closure following a SAR must be handled so that the customer cannot infer that a report was filed. The institution should have procedures for post-SAR relationship management that respect confidentiality.

### Manage The Decision Not To File

Not every investigated alert results in a filing. The decision not to file must be as defensible as the decision to file.

No-file decision requirements:

- document the specific rationale for concluding that the suspicion standard was not met;
- record the evidence reviewed and why it did not support suspicion;
- identify the reviewer or approver of the no-file decision;
- assess whether monitoring or CDD changes are needed even without filing;
- retain the no-file documentation for the required period.

A no-file decision with no documented rationale is indistinguishable from a missed filing. The institution should apply the same documentation rigor to no-file as to file decisions.

### Retain Records For The Required Period

STR and SAR records, including the filing, supporting documentation, and investigation, must be retained for the period required by the applicable regime.

Retention considerations:

- retain the filed report, the supporting transaction records, and the investigation file;
- retention periods are jurisdiction-specific, commonly five years or more;
- retain no-file decisions and their rationale for the same period;
- ensure records are retrievable for law enforcement requests and examinations;
- apply legal hold if the matter becomes subject to investigation or litigation.

Missing or incomplete filing records are a serious deficiency, particularly if law enforcement requests the supporting documentation.

## Common Traps

### Requiring Proof Instead Of Suspicion

The filing threshold is suspicion, not certainty. Demanding proof of the underlying crime before filing sets the bar too high and misses the reporting obligation.

### Missing The Filing Deadline

The deadline runs from detection of suspicion, not from the transaction date. Investigation backlogs do not extend it.

### Weak Narrative Without Specific Facts

A narrative that says the activity looked unusual, without describing the red flags and typology connection, provides little value and weak defensibility.

### Continuing Activity Without Periodic Review

Continuing a relationship with known suspicious activity without supplemental reports or a documented risk decision is a deficiency.

### Tipping Off Through Account Closure

Closing an account in a way that implies a SAR was filed violates the no-tipping-off rule. Post-SAR relationship management must respect confidentiality.

### No-File Decisions Without Rationale

A no-file decision with no documented rationale is indistinguishable from a missed filing. Document no-file with the same rigor as file decisions.

### Incomplete Record Retention

Missing filing records or supporting documentation creates exposure if law enforcement or an examiner requests them.

## Self-Check

- Is the suspicion standard applied correctly, based on specific articulable facts rather than requiring proof of the underlying crime?
- Are STRs or SARs filed within the regulatory deadline running from detection of suspicion, with a process that prevents backlog?
- Does the filing narrative describe the activity, customer profile, red flags, typology connection, and investigation in specific factual terms?
- Is continuing suspicious activity handled through supplemental reports, periodic review, and documented risk decisions about the relationship?
- Are confidentiality and no-tipping-off obligations enforced through access controls, training, and post-SAR relationship management?
- Are no-file decisions documented with specific rationale, evidence reviewed, and approver, with the same rigor as file decisions?
- Are filing records, supporting documentation, and no-file decisions retained for the required period and retrievable for requests?
- Is the filing decision process confirmed against the applicable national law, regulator guidance, and financial intelligence unit requirements?
- Are staff trained to recognize the suspicion standard and to document the factual basis for filing decisions?
- Is legal hold applied to filing records when the matter becomes subject to investigation or litigation?
