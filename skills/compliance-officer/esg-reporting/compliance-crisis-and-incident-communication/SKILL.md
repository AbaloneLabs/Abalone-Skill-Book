# Compliance Crisis And Incident Communication

In a crisis, communication is compliance. Every public statement, internal memo, regulator letter, and media response becomes part of the evidentiary and legal record. A poorly chosen phrase can constitute an admission, a waiver of privilege, a misleading statement that supports a fraud or obstruction charge, or a breach of disclosure duty. At the same time, silence or stonewalling can destroy trust, violate transparency obligations, and let speculation fill the vacuum. Crisis communication is not public relations layered on top of a legal matter; it is an integrated function in which legal, compliance, and communication professionals must align so that what is said externally is accurate, consistent, privileged where appropriate, and compliant with every disclosure duty.

Use this skill when managing communication during a compliance incident or crisis, when drafting public statements or internal announcements, when coordinating what can be said with legal and regulatory duties, when handling media and stakeholder inquiries, or when preventing communication choices that create legal exposure. The goal is to make the agent treat crisis communication as a disciplined, legally-aware, consistency-driven function rather than a reactive messaging exercise.

## Core Rules

### Stand Up An Integrated Response Structure Immediately

A crisis generates communication demands from every direction before facts are known. Without an integrated structure, different functions speak independently and create a contradictory, legally dangerous record.

Establish immediately:

- a single coordination function owning all external and internal communication;
- membership spanning legal, compliance, communications, and the relevant business;
- clear authority for what may be said and by whom;
- a single spokesperson for external communication;
- a protocol for rapid legal and compliance review of statements before release;
- a holding statement ready for the immediate period before facts are known;
- escalation paths for media, regulator, and stakeholder inquiries;
- a log of every statement made, to whom, and when.

No one speaks outside the protocol. The cost of an uncoordinated statement, an admission, an inconsistency, or a privilege waiver, far exceeds the cost of a brief delay for review.

### Separate What Is Known From What Is Under Investigation

The central discipline of crisis communication is distinguishing established fact from matters still under inquiry. Statements that present speculation as fact become admissions; statements that refuse to acknowledge known facts become misleading.

For every communication:

- state what is factually established and the basis;
- identify what is under investigation without characterizing the likely outcome;
- avoid speculative minimization, such as we believe this was isolated, before the investigation supports it;
- avoid speculative maximization that overstates harm or liability;
- commit to updating as facts develop, and then follow through;
- avoid language that implies conclusions about intent, culpability, or individual responsibility before supported.

The safest early communication acknowledges the situation, states what is being done, and reserves judgment on cause and responsibility. This is harder than it sounds under pressure to reassure, but the discipline prevents later contradiction.

### Coordinate Communication With Disclosure Duties

What is said publicly must align with regulatory and contractual disclosure obligations. A public statement that contradicts a regulatory filing, or that omits what a duty requires, creates separate violations.

Coordinate:

- materiality judgments for financial and regulatory disclosure, with securities and accounting counsel;
- consistency between public statements, regulator notifications, and board reporting;
- the sequence, so that parties owed direct notice receive it before or alongside public statement;
- contractual notice obligations to customers, partners, lenders, and insurers;
- the risk that a public statement triggers or waives duties to other regulators;
- cross-border disclosure obligations where the matter spans jurisdictions.

A public statement made before a required regulator notification can itself be a problem, as can a statement that is inconsistent with a contemporaneous filing. Align the communication plan with the disclosure plan from the outset.

### Protect Privilege In Communication Choices

Crisis communication can waive privilege and create admissions in several ways. Communications prepared by or for counsel may be privileged, but only if the privilege purpose is genuine and distribution is controlled.

Protect privilege by:

- directing sensitive factual development and analysis through counsel where privilege is intended;
- marking privileged materials and limiting distribution to need-to-know;
- avoiding putting privileged investigation content into public statements;
- understanding that sharing privileged findings with the public or broad internal audiences waives privilege as to that content;
- separating the privileged investigation workstream from the public communication workstream;
- recognizing that work product and attorney-client protections apply differently and may be lost through disclosure.

A public statement that reveals counsel's analysis or findings can waive privilege over the underlying materials. Decide deliberately what privilege, if any, the organization will assert, and keep public communication separate from privileged analysis.

### Avoid Misleading Statements And Omissions

A statement that is literally true but misleading, or that omits a material fact necessary to make it not misleading, can support fraud, misrepresentation, or disclosure violation claims. The standard is not whether the statement is technically accurate, but whether it misleads.

Avoid:

- technically true statements that create a false impression;
- half-truths that omit material context;
- reassurances not supported by the investigation;
- denials of matters that are under active investigation and may be true;
- statements that shift blame to individuals before the investigation supports it;
- language that minimizes known problems.

When in doubt, say less, say it accurately, and commit to updates. A restrained, accurate statement ages far better than a reassuring but unsupported one that the next disclosure contradicts.

### Manage Internal Communication To Prevent Leaks And Inconsistency

Employees are a communication channel, whether the organization intends it or not. In a crisis, internal messages leak, and employees speak to media, regulators, and each other. Unmanaged internal communication creates inconsistency and exposure.

Manage internal communication by:

- providing employees with accurate, approved talking points or referral language;
- instructing staff to route all external inquiries to the designated function;
- communicating enough to prevent rumor without waiving privilege or disclosing improperly;
- coordinating with HR on communications to affected teams;
- reminding relevant personnel of confidentiality and preservation obligations;
- anticipating that internal messages may become public and drafting accordingly.

Treat internal communication as potentially external. Draft it so that disclosure would not harm the organization, because it may be disclosed.

### Handle Media And Stakeholder Inquiries Disciplininely

Media pressure in a crisis is intense and can drive reactive, damaging statements. A disciplined inquiry-handling process prevents impromptu admissions.

Handle inquiries by:

- routing all media contact through the designated spokesperson;
- using a holding statement and bridging to confirmed facts;
- refusing to speculate on matters under investigation;
- avoiding off-the-record commentary that can be attributed or reconstructed;
- tracking every inquiry and response in the communication log;
- coordinating responses to different outlets for consistency;
- recognizing that no comment, used carelessly, can imply guilt or concealment.

A consistent, restrained, fact-based posture across all inquiries builds credibility. Inconsistency across outlets is quickly exposed and exploited.

### Plan The Communication Arc, Not Just The First Statement

A crisis is not a single statement; it is an arc of communication as facts develop, the investigation progresses, and resolution approaches. Planning only the first statement leaves the organization reactive later.

Plan the arc by:

- mapping anticipated milestones, such as investigation completion, regulator engagement, and resolution;
- preparing update cadences to maintain trust during quiet periods;
- aligning communication with disclosure duties at each milestone;
- anticipating the hardest questions and preparing responses;
- preparing for escalation scenarios, such as enforcement action or litigation;
- planning internal communication at each stage to maintain alignment;
- preparing the resolution communication to be consistent with all prior statements.

Consistency across the arc is what preserves credibility. A first statement that is contradicted by a later one is more damaging than a cautious first statement that is confirmed over time.

## Common Traps

### Speaking Before Facts Are Known

Reassuring statements made under pressure become admissions or contradictions when the investigation reveals more. Restrain early communication to established facts and actions taken.

### Presenting Speculation As Fact

Characterizing cause, intent, or scope before the investigation supports it creates admissions. Reserve judgment on what is under inquiry.

### Inconsistent Statements Across Audiences

Different messages to media, regulators, employees, and the board create a contradictory record. Centralize and align all communication.

### Waiving Privilege Through Disclosure

Putting counsel's analysis or findings into public statements waives privilege over the underlying materials. Separate privileged analysis from public communication.

### Technically True But Misleading Statements

Half-truths and misleading omissions can support fraud and disclosure claims even when literally accurate. Test statements for the impression they create, not just technical accuracy.

### Unmanaged Internal Communication

Leaked internal messages and employee commentary create inconsistency and exposure. Treat internal communication as potentially public.

### Reactive, Impromptu Media Responses

Off-the-record commentary and impromptu answers create attributions and admissions. Route all media contact through the designated spokesperson.

### Planning Only The First Statement

A crisis is an arc. Failing to plan updates and milestone communication leaves the organization reactive and inconsistent over time.

## Self-Check

- Is an integrated response structure standing, with a single coordination function, designated spokesperson, legal and compliance review protocol, holding statement, and a log of all statements?
- Does every communication separate established fact from matters under investigation, avoiding speculative minimization or maximization?
- Is communication coordinated with regulatory, securities, contractual, and cross-border disclosure duties, with consistent sequence and content across audiences?
- Is privilege protected by directing sensitive analysis through counsel, controlling distribution, and keeping public statements separate from privileged findings?
- Are statements tested for the impression they create, avoiding technically true but misleading content, half-truths, and unsupported reassurances or denials?
- Is internal communication managed with approved talking points, inquiry routing, confidentiality reminders, and drafting that anticipates public disclosure?
- Are media and stakeholder inquiries handled through the designated spokesperson, with consistent, fact-based responses and a tracked log?
- Is the communication arc planned across milestones, with update cadences, anticipated hard questions, escalation scenarios, and resolution messaging consistent with prior statements?
- Is no comment used carefully to avoid implying concealment, with bridging to confirmed facts and actions?
- Does the communication record withstand review by regulators, plaintiffs, and the board for accuracy, consistency, and compliance?
