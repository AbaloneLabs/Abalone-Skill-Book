# Social And Governance Metrics Reporting

Social and governance metrics reporting converts workforce, community, and governance realities into disclosed data that investors, regulators, and stakeholders use to judge a company. The defining difficulty is that social metrics lack the standardization of environmental metrics, governance disclosure touches sensitive compensation and board dynamics, and the definitions and boundaries vary across regimes in ways that produce non-comparable numbers. Inconsistent or undefined metrics undermine credibility and create both compliance and reputational risk.

Use this skill before defining social and governance metrics, preparing diversity and workforce disclosure, reporting board and executive pay data, or managing human capital metrics. The goal is to make the agent apply consistent definitions, set appropriate boundaries, ensure data quality, and align disclosure with governance reality. Social and governance disclosure regimes vary and reporting should involve qualified counsel and human resources expertise.

## Core Rules

### Define Workforce And Diversity Metrics Consistently

Social metrics require clear definitions applied consistently across periods and geographies.

Define:

- the categories and classifications used for diversity reporting and their basis;
- self-identification versus assigned categorization and the role of voluntary disclosure;
- geographic and entity boundary for workforce counts;
- full-time, part-time, and contingent worker inclusion;
- the distinction between representation at a point in time and flow metrics such as hiring and promotion;
- consistent application across reporting periods with documented methodology.

Diversity categories are jurisdiction-specific and culturally sensitive. Self-identification is generally required, and assigned categorization is both inaccurate and problematic. Representation snapshots and flow metrics answer different questions and both may be required.

### Set Appropriate Boundaries For Social Data

Boundaries determine what is included and affect comparability.

Set:

- organizational boundary aligned with financial reporting where possible;
- treatment of joint ventures, subsidiaries, and non-consolidated entities;
- geographic segmentation reflecting where the workforce and operations are;
- temporal boundary aligned with reporting period and headcount date;
- treatment of acquisitions, divestitures, and restructuring;
- disclosure of boundary and any changes from prior periods.

Boundary changes between periods undermine comparability and must be disclosed. Acquisitions and divestitures affect headcount and diversity metrics and must be normalized or disclosed. Consistency with financial reporting boundaries supports credibility.

### Manage Human Capital And Workforce Data Quality

Human capital metrics require data infrastructure and quality controls comparable to financial data.

Manage:

- data collection systems with defined fields and validation;
- data quality controls and reconciliation against source systems;
- handling of missing, ambiguous, or declining-to-state data;
- privacy and data protection for sensitive workforce data;
- aggregation that protects individual identification;
- audit readiness with documented methodology and source data.

Sensitive workforce data including health, accommodation, and certain diversity data requires privacy protection. Small-cell aggregation prevents identification of individuals in disclosed data. Missing data must be handled transparently rather than imputed without disclosure.

### Report Board Composition And Governance Structure

Governance disclosure covers board composition, structure, and processes.

Report:

- board size, composition, and the balance of executive, non-executive, and independent directors;
- diversity of the board by gender, background, skills, and experience;
- committee structure, membership, and responsibilities;
- director independence assessment and related-party relationships;
- board evaluation and refresh practices;
- separation or combination of chair and chief executive roles.

Board diversity disclosure is increasingly mandated. Independence assessment must reflect actual relationships and transactions, not merely formal titles. Committee structure disclosure must reflect actual authority and activity.

### Disclose Executive Compensation Transparently

Executive pay disclosure is sensitive, heavily regulated, and closely scrutinized.

Disclose:

- total compensation including salary, bonus, equity, and other elements;
- the performance metrics and targets linking pay to performance;
- the role of compensation consultants and committee independence;
- peer group benchmarking and its rationale;
- severance and change-of-control arrangements;
- the ratio of chief executive to median employee pay where required.

Pay-for-performance claims must be supported by the actual metrics and outcomes. Peer group selection that flatters the comparison is scrutinized. The pay ratio requires a defensible methodology for identifying the median employee.

### Address Supply Chain And Community Social Metrics

Social disclosure extends beyond the direct workforce into the value chain and community.

Address:

- supplier diversity and responsible sourcing metrics;
- human rights and labor practice metrics in the supply chain;
- community investment and local impact metrics;
- grievance and incident data where relevant;
- the boundary and verification challenges of value chain data;
- disclosure of methodology and limitations for estimated metrics.

Value chain social metrics are harder to verify than direct workforce data, and the methodology and limitations must be disclosed. Claims of supplier diversity or responsible sourcing require verification, not assertion. Community metrics must be defined to avoid vague or self-serving claims.

### Align Governance Disclosure With Actual Practice

Governance disclosure must reflect how the company is actually governed, not an idealized version.

Align:

- disclosure of board oversight of risk, strategy, and sustainability;
- the actual operation of committees and their authority;
- shareholder rights and engagement practices;
- related-party transactions and their approval;
- codes of conduct and their enforcement;
- the connection between stated governance and observed outcomes.

Governance disclosure that describes robust oversight while the company experiences governance failures is internally inconsistent. Codes of conduct that are disclosed but not enforced provide false assurance. Disclosure must align with the governance reality that stakeholders can observe.

### Manage Stakeholder Engagement And Materiality

Social and governance disclosure should reflect stakeholder engagement and materiality assessment.

Manage:

- identification of material social and governance topics through stakeholder engagement;
- disclosure of the materiality process and its results;
- responsiveness to stakeholder concerns in disclosure content;
- consistency between stakeholder engagement findings and disclosed priorities;
- the distinction between materiality for disclosure and materiality for management.

Materiality assessment that does not reflect genuine stakeholder input produces disclosure that misses what matters. The process and its results should be disclosed so readers can assess credibility. Disclosure priorities should align with materiality findings.

## Common Traps

### Inconsistent Diversity Category Definitions Across Periods

Definitions must be stable and documented; changes undermine comparability.

### Boundary Changes Without Disclosure

Acquisitions, divestitures, and boundary changes must be disclosed and normalized.

### Pay-For-Performance Claims Without Supporting Metrics

Claims must be supported by actual metrics, targets, and outcomes.

### Value Chain Metrics Presented Without Methodology Or Limitations

Estimated supply chain and community metrics require methodology and limitation disclosure.

### Governance Disclosure Out Of Step With Observed Practice

Disclosure that contradicts observed governance failures is internally inconsistent.

## Self-Check

- Are workforce and diversity metrics defined consistently with self-identification, geographic and entity boundaries, worker inclusion, point-in-time versus flow distinction, and documented methodology?
- Are boundaries set appropriately and aligned with financial reporting, with joint ventures, segmentation, temporal alignment, transaction treatment, and change disclosure?
- Is human capital data quality managed through systems, validation, missing-data handling, privacy protection, aggregation, and audit readiness?
- Is board composition and governance structure reported including balance, diversity, committees, independence, evaluation, and chair and chief executive roles?
- Is executive compensation disclosed transparently including total pay, performance links, consultant independence, peer benchmarking, severance, and pay ratio methodology?
- Are supply chain and community social metrics addressed with verification, methodology, and limitation disclosure?
- Is governance disclosure aligned with actual practice including oversight, committee operation, shareholder rights, related parties, code enforcement, and outcome consistency?
- Is stakeholder engagement and materiality managed with genuine input, process disclosure, responsiveness, priority alignment, and disclosure-versus-management distinction, and where reporting obligations arise, has qualified counsel and human resources expertise been engaged, recognizing that social and governance disclosure regimes vary and this guidance is not legal advice?
