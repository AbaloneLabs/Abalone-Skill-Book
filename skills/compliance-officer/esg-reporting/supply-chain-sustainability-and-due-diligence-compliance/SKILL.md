# Supply Chain Sustainability And Due Diligence Compliance

Supply chain sustainability due diligence governs the identification, prevention, and mitigation of environmental and social harms in the value chain. The defining feature is that mandatory due diligence laws now require companies to identify, assess, and address adverse impacts in their supply chains—not merely report on them—and the obligation extends beyond first-tier suppliers to where the most severe impacts typically occur. The central difficulty is that supply chains are long, opaque, and cross jurisdictions with weak governance, and due diligence that stops at tier one misses the highest-risk segments.

Use this skill before advising on supply chain due diligence programs, supplier codes, audit programs, traceability, or mandatory due diligence compliance. The goal is to make the agent identify the applicable mandates, the risk areas, the due diligence methodology, and the remediation obligations before concluding that a supply chain sustainability program is compliant.

## Core Rules

### Identify Applicable Mandatory Due Diligence Laws

Supply chain due diligence is increasingly mandatory.

Map:

- corporate sustainability due diligence directives (e.g., EU CSDDD);
- forced labor and modern slavery acts;
- conflict minerals and responsible minerals regulations;
- deforestation-free supply chain regulations;
- supply chain transparency acts;
- applicability based on size, sector, and market presence;
- the scope of covered impacts (human rights, environment, climate);
- the interaction with reporting obligations.

Mandatory due diligence laws require substantive action—risk identification, prevention, mitigation, remediation—not just reporting. The scope extends to established business relationships and, in some cases, the full value chain. Applicability varies by company size and market.

### Conduct Risk-Based Supply Chain Mapping

Due diligence begins with understanding the supply chain.

Implement:

- mapping beyond tier one to high-risk tiers and origins;
- risk assessment by geography, commodity, and sector;
- identification of high-risk inputs and materials;
- prioritization based on severity and likelihood of harm;
- use of supplier data, third-party data, and on-the-ground assessment;
- continuous updating as supply chains change;
- transparency and traceability to the source for high-risk materials.

Tier-one mapping misses the highest-risk parts of the chain. Traceability to origin is required for high-risk commodities (minerals, deforestation commodities, textiles). Risk assessment prioritizes where due diligence effort should focus.

### Implement A Supplier Code Of Conduct

A code of conduct sets expectations for suppliers.

Cover:

- comprehensive standards covering labor, health and safety, environment, ethics, and management systems;
- alignment with international frameworks (UNGP, OECD, ILO);
- communication and acknowledgment by suppliers;
- integration into contracts and purchase orders;
- training and capacity building;
- escalation and consequences for non-compliance;
- periodic review and update.

A code of conduct must be comprehensive, aligned with international standards, and contractually binding. Communication and training drive understanding. Consequences for non-compliance provide leverage.

### Conduct Supplier Assessment And Audit

Assessment and audit verify supplier compliance.

Implement:

- self-assessment questionnaires for risk screening;
- risk-based audit programs for high-risk suppliers;
- on-site audits with worker interviews;
- corrective action plans and follow-up verification;
- capacity building and support for improvement;
- third-party audit and verification programs;
- unannounced audits for high-risk contexts;
- documentation and tracking of findings and closure.

Self-assessments screen risk; on-site audits verify. Worker interviews reveal conditions that documents and management presentations do not. Corrective action plans must be tracked to closure. Capacity building supports sustained improvement.

### Manage Traceability For High-Risk Commodities

Specific commodities require chain-of-custody traceability.

Cover:

- deforestation commodities (palm oil, soy, cattle, cocoa, coffee, rubber, timber);
- minerals (tin, tungsten, tantalum, gold, cobalt, lithium);
- textiles and apparel from forced labor regions;
- seafood from illegal and forced labor sources;
- agricultural commodities with pesticide and labor risks;
- chain-of-custody models (identity preservation, segregation, mass balance, book and claim);
- verification and certification systems.

Deforestation regulations require traceability to the geolocation of production. Minerals require traceability to the smelter or refiner. Chain-of-custody models determine the level of physical traceability versus certificate-based assurance.

### Provide Access To Remedy And Grievance

Due diligence includes providing remedy for harm.

Implement:

- operational-level grievance mechanisms for workers and communities;
- accessibility considerations (language, literacy, fear of retaliation);
- effectiveness criteria (legitimate, accessible, predictable, equitable, transparent, rights-compatible);
- investigation of complaints;
- remediation of confirmed harm;
- non-retaliation protections;
- escalation for unresolved grievances.

A grievance mechanism that affected stakeholders cannot or will not use is not effective. The UNGP effectiveness criteria define legitimacy. Remediation is required when the company causes or contributes to harm.

### Comply With Deforestation-Free Supply Chain Rules

Deforestation regulations require supply chain assurance.

Cover:

- the scope of covered commodities and derivatives;
- the cutoff date for deforestation-free production;
- geolocation data and traceability to the plot of land;
- due diligence statements and reporting;
- risk benchmarking by country of origin;
- benchmarking and risk-based controls;
- interaction with existing certification systems;
- the risk of import denial for non-compliance.

Deforestation regulations require geolocation data proving the commodity was not produced on land deforested after the cutoff date. Due diligence statements accompany imports. Non-compliance can result in import denial and penalties.

### Integrate Due Diligence Into Procurement

Due diligence must be embedded in procurement decisions.

Cover:

- supplier qualification criteria incorporating due diligence;
- contracts with flow-down obligations;
- sourcing decisions informed by risk assessment;
- incentives aligned with sustainability performance;
- training for procurement teams;
- escalation of high-risk suppliers;
- new supplier and new material due diligence;
- coordination between procurement, sustainability, and compliance.

Procurement decisions drive supply chain risk. Without embedding due diligence in procurement, the program remains a parallel process. Contracts must flow down obligations to sub-tier suppliers.

## Common Traps

### Due Diligence Stopping At Tier One

The most severe impacts typically occur beyond tier one in raw materials and extraction.

### Self-Assessment Treated As Verification

Supplier self-assessments screen risk but do not verify conditions without audit.

### Code Of Conduct Without Contractual Integration

A code that is not contractually binding lacks enforcement leverage.

### Deforestation Regulation Treated As Reporting Only

Deforestation rules require traceability and due diligence, not just disclosure.

### Grievance Mechanism That Stakeholders Fear To Use

A mechanism with accessibility barriers or retaliation risk is ineffective.

### Procurement Decisions Uninformed By Due Diligence

Sourcing decisions made without risk assessment undermine the due diligence program.

## Self-Check

- Are applicable mandatory due diligence laws identified including CSDDD, modern slavery, conflict minerals, deforestation, and transparency acts, with applicability and scope assessed?
- Is risk-based supply chain mapping conducted beyond tier one with risk assessment, high-risk input identification, prioritization, data sources, continuous updating, and traceability?
- Is a supplier code of conduct implemented with comprehensive standards, international alignment, communication, contractual integration, training, consequences, and review?
- Is supplier assessment and audit conducted through self-assessment, risk-based audits, worker interviews, corrective action, capacity building, third-party verification, and tracking?
- Is traceability managed for high-risk commodities including deforestation commodities, minerals, textiles, seafood, and agriculture, with chain-of-custody models and certification?
- Is access to remedy provided through effective grievance mechanisms meeting UNGP criteria, investigation, remediation, and non-retaliation?
- Is deforestation-free compliance maintained through commodity scope, cutoff dates, geolocation, due diligence statements, risk benchmarking, and import controls?
- Is due diligence integrated into procurement through qualification criteria, contracts, sourcing decisions, incentives, training, escalation, and coordination?
- Is the distinction between reporting obligations and substantive due diligence obligations understood?
- Are severity and likelihood used to prioritize due diligence effort?
