# Climate Risk Disclosure And Tcfd

Climate risk disclosure translates long-horizon environmental change into near-term financial disclosure that investors and regulators rely on. The defining difficulty is that climate risk splits into physical and transition risks with different time horizons and evidence bases, scenario analysis requires assumptions about deeply uncertain futures, and disclosure that is too vague fails the regime while disclosure that is too specific can create liability if assumptions prove wrong. The discipline is to disclose enough to satisfy the regime without overstating the precision of inherently uncertain projections.

Use this skill before preparing climate disclosure, conducting scenario analysis, assessing governance and strategy disclosure, or managing climate liability. The goal is to make the agent structure disclosure across the pillars, conduct defensible scenario analysis, calibrate claims to evidence, and manage the liability of forward-looking statements. Climate disclosure regimes are evolving and reporting should involve qualified counsel and climate expertise.

## Core Rules

### Structure Disclosure Across Governance Strategy Risk And Metrics

Climate disclosure is organized around four interrelated pillars that must be internally consistent.

Structure:

- governance describing board and management oversight of climate risk;
- strategy describing climate risks and opportunities and their business and financial effects;
- risk management describing how climate risk is identified, assessed, and integrated into enterprise risk;
- metrics and targets describing the measurements and goals used to assess and manage risk.

The pillars must be consistent; governance claims must be reflected in strategy, and metrics must align with the risks described. Disclosure that describes robust governance in one section while showing no integration in risk management is internally inconsistent and invites scrutiny.

### Distinguish Physical And Transition Risk

Climate risk has two distinct categories with different drivers, time horizons, and evidence.

Distinguish:

- physical risk from acute events such as storms and floods and chronic shifts such as temperature and sea level;
- transition risk from policy, legal, technology, and market changes in the move to a lower-carbon economy;
- the different time horizons, with some physical risks materializing over decades and some transition risks materializing within planning cycles;
- the interaction where transition risk can crystallize physical risk and vice versa;
- the geographic and asset-specific variation in exposure.

Physical risk is location and asset specific, while transition risk is sector and market specific. A coastal manufacturing facility faces acute and chronic physical risk, while a fossil-fuel-dependent business model faces transition risk. Both must be assessed and disclosed where material.

### Conduct Defensible Scenario Analysis

Scenario analysis is the core analytical method for climate risk, and its defensibility turns on the scenarios chosen and the transparency of assumptions.

Conduct:

- selection of scenarios including those aligned with global temperature goals and business-as-usual pathways;
- analysis over multiple time horizons relevant to the business;
- assessment of resilience under each scenario;
- transparency about assumptions, inputs, and limitations;
- integration of results into strategy and risk management;
- periodic updating as science and policy evolve.

Scenario analysis is not forecasting; it is structured exploration of plausible futures. Using only favorable scenarios, or concealing assumptions, undermines defensibility. The results must inform strategy, not merely populate a disclosure template.

### Calibrate Forward-Looking Statements To Evidence

Climate disclosure necessarily contains forward-looking statements that must be calibrated to avoid liability.

Calibrate:

- clear identification of forward-looking statements and the assumptions underlying them;
- appropriate hedging and qualification for uncertain projections;
- consistency between scenario assumptions and disclosed strategy;
- avoidance of definitive claims about uncertain outcomes;
- disclosure of material assumptions and sensitivities;
- cautionary language that is specific rather than boilerplate.

Forward-looking statements that are presented as certain, or that omit material assumptions, create liability when reality diverges. Boilerplate cautionary language that does not address the specific risks provides little protection. Assumptions must be disclosed with enough specificity to be meaningful.

### Align Metrics And Targets With Strategy

Metrics and targets must measure what the strategy addresses, and must be auditable.

Align:

- greenhouse gas metrics across scopes and boundaries consistent with strategy;
- physical and transition risk metrics where material;
- targets with defined base years, scopes, and timeframes;
- progress tracking against targets with transparent methodology;
- consistency between targets and the scenarios assessed;
- avoidance of targets presented without the methodology to verify progress.

Metrics that do not connect to the disclosed risks, or targets without base years and methodologies, are not credible. Targets must be accompanied by the strategy and capital plan to achieve them, or they are aspirational marketing rather than disclosure.

### Manage Scope Emissions Boundaries And Quality

Greenhouse gas metrics require careful boundary setting and data quality management.

Manage:

- scope 1 direct emissions and scope 2 purchased energy emissions;
- scope 3 value chain emissions where material, with appropriate estimation and disclosure of uncertainty;
- consistent organizational and operational boundaries;
- emission factor selection and documentation;
- recalculation policy for base year when structural changes occur;
- third-party verification of emissions data.

Scope 3 often dominates a company's footprint but is the hardest to measure accurately. Estimation methods and uncertainty must be disclosed, and targets that include scope 3 must address the data quality challenge. Base year recalculation policy ensures comparability over time.

### Integrate Climate Into Enterprise Risk Management

Climate risk must be integrated into, not separated from, enterprise risk management.

Integrate:

- inclusion of climate in enterprise risk registers and assessments;
- connection of climate risk to financial planning and capital allocation;
- assessment of climate risk in major investment and acquisition decisions;
- stress testing of the business against climate scenarios;
- board-level engagement with climate as a strategic risk;
- disclosure of how climate risk affects financial statements where material.

Climate risk segregated in a sustainability function, disconnected from financial planning and capital allocation, is not effectively managed. Integration means climate considerations appear in investment decisions, financial planning, and board strategy discussions.

## Common Traps

### Internally Inconsistent Disclosure Across Pillars

Governance, strategy, risk management, and metrics must align; contradictions invite scrutiny.

### Scenario Analysis Using Only Favorable Pathways

Defensibility requires multiple scenarios including adverse ones with transparent assumptions.

### Forward-Looking Statements Presented As Certain

Uncertain projections presented as fact create liability when reality diverges.

### Targets Without Methodology Or Capital Plan

Targets without base years, methodologies, and a plan to achieve them are marketing.

### Scope 3 Omitted Despite Materiality

Scope 3 often dominates the footprint; omission where material produces misleading disclosure.

## Self-Check

- Is disclosure structured across governance, strategy, risk management, and metrics and targets with internal consistency?
- Are physical and transition risks distinguished by driver, time horizon, evidence, interaction, and geographic and asset variation?
- Is scenario analysis defensible through multiple scenarios, relevant time horizons, resilience assessment, transparent assumptions, strategy integration, and periodic updating?
- Are forward-looking statements calibrated to evidence with clear identification, hedging, consistency, material assumption disclosure, and specific cautionary language?
- Are metrics and targets aligned with strategy, including scopes, base years, timeframes, progress tracking, and the capital plan to achieve targets?
- Are emissions boundaries and quality managed across scopes with consistent boundaries, documented factors, recalculation policy, and third-party verification?
- Is climate integrated into enterprise risk management through risk registers, financial planning, capital allocation, investment decisions, stress testing, and board engagement?
- Where mandatory climate disclosure, scenario analysis, or liability exposure arises, has qualified counsel and climate expertise been engaged, recognizing that climate disclosure regimes are evolving and this guidance is not legal advice?
