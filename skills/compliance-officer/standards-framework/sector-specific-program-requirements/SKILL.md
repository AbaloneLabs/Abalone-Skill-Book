# Sector Specific Program Requirements

A general compliance program is necessary but rarely sufficient. Most organizations operate under sector-specific regimes that impose detailed, mandatory requirements well beyond a baseline program. Anti-bribery laws such as the FCPA, health privacy under HIPAA, financial controls and certifications under SOX, consumer financial data under GLBA, and anti-money-laundering standards from the FATF each create distinct obligations, regulators, recordkeeping duties, and enforcement risks. The trap is building a generic program and assuming it covers these regimes, when in fact each regime has specific elements, such as a designated privacy officer, an AML compliance officer, SOX management certifications, or FCPA internal accounting controls, that a generic program will miss. The task is to overlay sector requirements onto the general program, resolve overlaps and conflicts, and ensure each regime's specific elements are present and evidenced.

Use this skill before applying a sector regime, mapping sector obligations to controls, designing a program that spans multiple regimes, or assessing whether a general program satisfies sector-specific requirements. The goal is to make the agent identify the specific elements each regime demands and integrate them without duplication or gaps.

## Core Rules

### Identify All Applicable Sector Regimes Before Designing

A program cannot satisfy regimes it has not identified. Start by mapping every regime that applies based on industry, activities, data, customers, and geography.

Identify regimes by asking:

- what industry or sector does the organization operate in, and what is the primary regulator;
- does it touch foreign officials, government business, or international payments, triggering anti-bribery regimes like the FCPA or UK Bribery Act;
- does it handle protected health information, triggering HIPAA or health-sector rules;
- is it a public company or does it prepare financial statements for public filing, triggering SOX;
- is it a financial institution or does it handle consumer financial data, triggering GLBA, banking, or securities rules;
- does it move money or provide financial services, triggering AML and FATF obligations;
- does it operate in regulated sectors such as telecommunications, energy, insurance, food, pharmaceuticals, defense, or transportation;
- does it cross borders, triggering extraterritorial regimes and conflict-of-laws questions.

List every applicable regime with its source, regulator, and core obligations. A program built without this list will have structural gaps.

### Map Each Regime's Specific Program Elements

Each regime demands specific program elements that a generic program will not naturally include. Map them explicitly.

Examples of regime-specific elements:

- FCPA requires internal accounting controls, anti-bribery provisions, and oversight of third parties and business partners;
- HIPAA requires a privacy officer, security officer, risk analysis, safeguards, breach notification, and business associate agreements;
- SOX requires management assessment and external auditor attestation on internal control over financial reporting, and CEO and CFO certifications;
- GLBA requires a written information security program, safeguards rule compliance, and privacy notices;
- FATF and national AML rules require a designated compliance officer, customer due diligence, suspicious activity reporting, recordkeeping, and program independence;
- sector regulators such as the FTC, SEC, CFPB, HHS, FERC, or FDA impose specific program, reporting, and recordkeeping duties.

For each regime, list the specific elements, the owner, the evidence, and how it integrates with the general program. Do not assume the general program covers them.

### Designate Required Officers And Roles

Many regimes require designated individuals with specific responsibilities. These are not optional and cannot be left to general role descriptions.

Confirm designation of:

- a HIPAA privacy official and security official;
- a BSA or AML compliance officer with sufficient authority and independence;
- a data protection officer where required by privacy law;
- officers responsible for SOX certification and control assessment;
- export compliance officers or empowered officials where trade controls apply;
- a chief compliance officer with overall program authority.

Designated officers must have the authority, resources, and access the regime requires. A designation in name only, without empowerment, fails the requirement.

### Resolve Overlaps And Conflicts Across Regimes

Where multiple regimes apply, they overlap and sometimes conflict. The program must reconcile them deliberately rather than letting them collide in operation.

Reconcile:

- overlapping risk assessments, such as a combined assessment or a clear map of regime-specific assessments;
- overlapping training, so employees receive coherent, role-relevant content rather than contradictory modules;
- overlapping incident response, so a single breach triggers the right notices under each applicable regime within each deadline;
- overlapping recordkeeping, so retention satisfies the longest applicable period;
- conflicting requirements, such as data localization versus access obligations, resolved through legal analysis;
- reporting lines that satisfy each regime's independence and authority expectations.

A patchwork of regime-specific programs that contradict each other confuses the business and creates gaps. Integrate where possible and document where regimes require separate treatment.

### Build Incident Response That Triggers All Applicable Regimes

A single incident can trigger duties under multiple regimes with different clocks. Incident response must be designed to catch every applicable trigger.

Design response to:

- identify which regimes a given incident type implicates;
- map the notification deadlines and recipients for each regime;
- assign responsibility for each required notice;
- preserve evidence and records across regimes;
- coordinate with legal, privacy, security, and sector leads;
- document decisions where a regime's threshold is not met.

A breach handled under one regime while missing a mandatory notice under another creates separate liability. Map the triggers before an incident occurs.

### Tailor Monitoring And Testing To Sector Risk

Sector regimes often specify monitoring, audit, or testing expectations. Generic testing will not satisfy them.

Tailor to:

- AML transaction monitoring and independent testing requirements;
- HIPAA risk analysis and periodic security risk updates;
- SOX management testing of internal control over financial reporting;
- FCPA-focused reviews of high-risk third parties, payments, and intermediaries;
- regulator-mandated audits or assessments in regulated sectors;
- the specific recordkeeping each regime requires.

Use the regime's own testing expectations as the floor, and build risk-based testing on top.

### Track Regulatory Change Across Regimes

Sector regimes change frequently through rulemaking, guidance, and enforcement. A program that does not track change drifts out of compliance.

Maintain:

- a regulatory change process that monitors each applicable regime;
- a source for each regime, such as the regulator's portal, alerts, or a subscription service;
- a process to assess the impact of each change and assign remediation;
- evidence that changes were evaluated and acted upon within required timeframes.

### Maintain Evidence For Each Regime Separately And Together

Regulators examine programs through their own lens. Evidence must be organized so that each regulator can see what it needs, while the organization maintains a coherent overall view.

Organize evidence by:

- regime-specific files for designated officer appointments, assessments, notices, and testing;
- a cross-regime view for the board and CCO;
- clear linkage between each control and the regime it satisfies;
- readiness to produce regime-specific evidence on demand during an exam.

## Common Traps

### Assuming A Generic Program Covers Sector Regimes

A baseline program misses regime-specific elements like designated officers, specific notices, and mandated controls. Overlay each regime explicitly.

### Missing A Regime Entirely

Operating under a regime the program never identified creates structural, unmanaged liability. Map all applicable regimes first.

### Designating Officers Without Empowerment

A named privacy or AML officer without authority or resources fails the regime's requirement. Empower designated roles.

### Letting Regime Programs Contradict Each Other

Patchwork programs with overlapping or conflicting requirements confuse the business. Integrate and reconcile.

### Single-Regime Incident Response

Handling an incident under one regime while missing another's notice deadline creates separate liability. Map all triggers in advance.

### Generic Testing That Misses Regime Requirements

Each regime has specific testing expectations. Use them as the floor and build risk-based testing above.

### Ignoring Regulatory Change

Sector regimes change constantly. Without a change-tracking process, the program drifts out of compliance.

## Self-Check

- Are all applicable sector regimes identified by industry, activity, data, customers, and geography, with source, regulator, and core obligations listed?
- Is each regime's specific program element, such as FCPA internal controls, HIPAA officers, SOX certifications, GLBA safeguards, or FATF AML officer, mapped to owner, evidence, and integration?
- Are required officers and roles designated with the authority, resources, and access each regime requires?
- Are overlaps and conflicts across regimes reconciled in risk assessment, training, incident response, recordkeeping, and reporting lines?
- Does incident response identify every applicable regime, map notice deadlines and recipients, assign responsibility, and preserve cross-regime evidence?
- Are monitoring and testing tailored to each regime's specific expectations and used as the floor for risk-based testing?
- Is regulatory change tracked across all applicable regimes with impact assessment and timely remediation?
- Is evidence organized both by regime for examiner demands and cross-regime for board and CCO oversight?
- Could the program demonstrate compliance with each applicable sector regime, not just a generic baseline?
