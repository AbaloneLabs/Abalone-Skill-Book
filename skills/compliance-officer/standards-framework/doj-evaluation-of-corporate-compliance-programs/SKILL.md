# DOJ Evaluation Of Corporate Compliance Programs

The DOJ Evaluation of Corporate Compliance Programs, known as the ECCP, is the framework federal prosecutors use to assess corporate compliance programs when deciding whether to charge, how to resolve, and what penalty and monitor terms to seek. It is not a statute and not a checklist; it is a set of probing questions organized around three fundamental questions: is the program well-designed, is it being applied earnestly and in good faith, and does it work in practice. The ECCP is updated periodically to reflect enforcement priorities, most recently emphasizing compensation structures, personal device and communications, lessons-learned integration, and testing of controls. The central trap is reading the ECCP as a document to satisfy rather than as a mirror that exposes whether the program actually prevents and detects misconduct. Prosecutors are trained to look past polished policies to behavior, resources, data, and outcomes.

Use this skill before assessing a program against the ECCP, preparing for a government investigation, responding to prosecutor questions, advising on cooperation credit, or strengthening a program to withstand government scrutiny. The goal is to make the agent answer the ECCP's questions with evidence of effectiveness, not with assertions of existence.

## Core Rules

### Organize The Assessment Around The Three Fundamental Questions

The ECCP is built on three questions that prosecutors actually ask. Structure any assessment around them.

The three questions are:

- is the program well-designed, examining risk assessment, policies, training, reporting, and third-party management;
- is the program adequately resourced and empowered, examining whether compliance has authority, independence, access, and senior leadership and board support;
- does the program work in practice, examining whether it is tested, whether it learns from lessons, and whether it adapts.

A program can be well-designed on paper and still fail the second and third questions. Always carry the analysis through to resourcing and effectiveness, because prosecutors do.

### Make Risk Assessment The Foundation Of Design

The ECCP begins with risk assessment because a program that is not risk-based is not credible. Prosecutors look for a program tailored to the company's specific risks.

Confirm the assessment addresses:

- the company's specific risk profile, including products, geographies, customers, and business model;
- the methods used, including data, interviews, and scenario analysis;
- the frequency of refresh and the triggers for interim update;
- how the assessment drives resource allocation and control design;
- how the company learned from prior issues, near-misses, and investigations;
- whether high-risk areas receive proportionate attention.

A program that applies equal effort to low- and high-risk areas signals that the assessment is not driving the program. Risk tailoring is a core ECCP expectation.

### Show That Policies And Training Are Tailored And Understood

The ECCP asks not only whether policies and training exist, but whether they are designed for the audience and whether they are understood.

For policies and training, demonstrate:

- that policies are written in plain language and accessible;
- that training is risk-based, role-based, and not generic;
- that the company tests comprehension and effectiveness, not just completion;
- that policies and training are updated after lessons learned;
- that the company reaches high-risk populations, including third parties, acquired entities, and remote workers;
- that the content addresses the company's actual risk scenarios.

Completion metrics alone do not satisfy the ECCP. Prosecutors ask whether employees actually understand and apply the guidance.

### Prove The Program Is Empowered And Resourced

The second fundamental question targets whether compliance has real authority. This is where many programs are exposed.

Demonstrate:

- the compliance function's independence and direct reporting to the board;
- the seniority, expertise, and authority of compliance leaders;
- adequate staffing and budget relative to risk and complexity;
- protection of the compliance function from business pressure and budget cuts;
- the ability of compliance to obtain data and access across the company;
- the willingness to stop or escalate questionable conduct;
- whether compliance advice is followed or overridden, and how overrides are handled.

A compliance officer who is overruled, underfunded, or cut off from data fails this question regardless of the org chart. Empowerment is judged by behavior.

### Demonstrate That The Program Works In Practice

The third question is the hardest and the most important. Prosecutors want evidence that the program detects and prevents misconduct.

Show:

- that monitoring and testing are risk-based and cover the right populations;
- that control failures are identified, escalated, and remediated;
- that investigations are competent, independent, and resourced;
- that the company tests the program through simulations, data analytics, and root-cause analysis;
- that lessons learned from investigations and audits change the program;
- that metrics measure effectiveness, not only activity;
- that the program has evolved in response to incidents, new risks, and regulatory change.

A program that never finds anything is suspicious, not reassuring. Prosecutors view a clean record with skepticism if the testing is shallow.

### Examine Compensation, Consequences, And Accountability

Recent ECCP updates emphasize whether the company uses compensation and discipline to reinforce compliance. This is now a distinct area of focus.

Assess:

- whether compliance performance is part of compensation, including bonuses and clawbacks;
- whether discipline is consistent across seniority and performance level;
- whether high performers are held accountable;
- whether incentives reward the right behavior, not just results;
- whether the company tracks and analyzes disciplinary consistency.

A company that pays for results and excuses high performers signals that compliance is not a real priority. Compensation structures are now squarely in scope.

### Address Communications, Personal Devices, And Data Access

The ECCP now asks whether the company can access its own data to detect and investigate misconduct, including on personal devices and messaging platforms.

Confirm:

- policies on personal devices, ephemeral messaging, and unofficial communication channels;
- the company's ability to preserve and access business communications;
- data governance that supports detection and investigation;
- coordination with legal on preservation and privilege;
- whether off-channel communications have been a prior issue and how it was remediated.

A company that cannot access its own communications cannot investigate itself, which undermines cooperation credit and program credibility.

### Prepare To Demonstrate, Not Assert

Prosecutors see many polished program descriptions. What distinguishes credible programs is evidence. Build the ability to demonstrate, not assert.

Maintain:

- artifacts for each claim, such as risk assessments, testing reports, investigation files, and board minutes;
- data that supports effectiveness claims;
- examples of the program stopping, detecting, or remediating issues;
- a coherent narrative connecting risk, design, resources, and outcomes;
- honesty about gaps and remediation plans.

Honesty about weaknesses with a credible remediation plan is more persuasive than a flawless paper program that does not match reality.

## Common Traps

### Reading The ECCP As A Checklist To Satisfy

The ECCP is a set of probing questions, not a box to check. Satisfying it on paper while the program is weak invites prosecutor skepticism.

### Stopping At Design

A well-designed program that is under-resourced and untested fails the second and third questions. Carry analysis through to empowerment and effectiveness.

### Completion Metrics As Effectiveness

Training completion and policy attestation do not prove effectiveness. Prosecutors ask whether employees understand and apply the guidance.

### A Clean Record With Shallow Testing

Never finding a problem can signal weak testing, not strong compliance. Risk-based testing should find issues and remediate them.

### Ignoring Compensation And Discipline

Recent ECCP focus on compensation means incentive misalignment is now an explicit area of scrutiny. Review pay, clawback, and discipline consistency.

### Inability To Access Own Data

If the company cannot preserve or access communications and data, it cannot investigate itself. Data access is now a core expectation.

### Asserting Instead Of Demonstrating

Polished assertions without artifacts and data do not persuade. Build the evidence base for every claim.

## Self-Check

- Is the program assessment organized around the three fundamental questions of design, empowerment, and effectiveness?
- Is risk assessment risk-tailored, refreshed, and demonstrably driving resource allocation and control design?
- Are policies and training shown to be tailored, understood, and effective, not merely completed?
- Is the compliance function demonstrably empowered, resourced, independent, and able to access data and escalate?
- Is there evidence that the program works in practice, including monitoring, testing, investigations, lessons learned, and adaptation?
- Are compensation, consequences, clawback, and disciplinary consistency examined as an ECCP focus area?
- Are personal devices, ephemeral messaging, and data access addressed so the company can investigate itself?
- Does the company demonstrate effectiveness with artifacts and data rather than asserting it?
- Could the program withstand a prosecutor asking "show me" rather than "tell me"?
