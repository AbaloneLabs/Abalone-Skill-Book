# Healthcare Licensing Credentialing And Scope Of Practice

Healthcare licensing and credentialing ensure that only qualified individuals and facilities provide healthcare services, and that services are provided within authorized scopes of practice. Under US federal and state healthcare licensing frameworks, EU professional qualification directives, and equivalent national healthcare regulation, providing services without proper licensure, beyond authorized scope, or through uncredentialed providers can result in penalties, payment recoupment, malpractice exposure, and patient safety risks. The central judgment problem is that healthcare licensing is jurisdiction-specific, scope of practice boundaries are nuanced and evolving, and new care models such as telehealth blur traditional geographic and professional boundaries. A healthcare organization that deploys professionals across state lines, expands service lines, or adopts telehealth without assessing licensing and scope can inadvertently authorize unlicensed practice, creating liability that extends to billing, malpractice, and regulatory enforcement.

Use this skill before assessing licensing requirements, evaluating scope of practice, reviewing telehealth arrangements, or advising on credentialing verification. The goal is to make the agent think about who must be licensed, what each license authorizes, and the decisions that are easy to make too casually. A professional who is fully licensed in one jurisdiction may be practicing without authorization in another, and a service that is within scope for one professional type may be outside scope for another.

This skill addresses jurisdiction-specific obligations. Healthcare licensing, scope of practice, telehealth, and credentialing rules differ fundamentally across US states, EU member states, and national regimes. Always confirm the applicable national healthcare regulation and licensing board guidance, and consult qualified healthcare legal counsel for specific licensing and scope assessments. This skill does not constitute legal advice.

## Core Rules

### Map Licensing Requirements For All Professional Roles

The first step in licensing compliance is mapping the licensing requirements for every professional role in the organization.

Professional licensing mapping elements:

- identify all professional roles that provide clinical services, including physicians, nurses, advanced practice providers, therapists, pharmacists, and technicians;
- determine the license or registration required for each role in each jurisdiction where services are provided;
- determine the specific scope of practice authorized by each license, including delegated authority and supervision requirements;
- identify any additional certifications, registrations, or privileges required for specific procedures or services;
- assess the licensing implications of professional roles in supervisory, consulting, or advisory capacities;
- document the licensing map and the basis for each determination.

The mapping should be updated whenever roles change, services expand, or the organization enters new jurisdictions. Roles that are incidental to healthcare, such as care coordinators or health coaches, may still require licensure depending on the services provided.

### Verify Credentialing And Privileging

Credentialing and privileging verify that licensed professionals are qualified for the specific services they provide within the organization.

Credentialing and privileging elements:

- primary source verification of licenses, certifications, education, training, and work history;
- verification of board certification, malpractice history, and disciplinary actions through national practitioner databases;
- peer reference and competency assessment for specific procedures or services;
- privileging, which grants authority to perform specific procedures or services within the facility;
- periodic re-credentialing and re-privileging, typically every two years;
- documentation of credentialing decisions, including any denials or restrictions.

Credentialing must be based on primary source verification, not on applicant self-reporting or secondary sources. Failure to verify credentials thoroughly can create organizational liability for negligent credentialing.

### Assess Scope Of Practice Boundaries

Scope of practice defines what services a licensed professional may provide, and the boundaries vary by license type, jurisdiction, and service setting.

Scope of practice considerations:

- the services that each license type may independently provide;
- services that require delegation, supervision, or collaboration with another licensed professional;
- prescriptive authority, including the types of medications that may be prescribed and any supervisory requirements;
- the authority to order tests, make diagnoses, or develop treatment plans;
- the authority to perform procedures, including invasive or high-risk procedures;
- restrictions based on setting, such as independent practice authority that may differ between facility and community settings.

Scope of practice is defined by state or national law and by professional board regulations. The organization must ensure that each professional practices within their authorized scope and that any delegation or supervision meets legal requirements.

### Evaluate Telehealth And Cross-Jurisdiction Practice

Telehealth and cross-jurisdiction practice create licensing challenges because the professional and the patient may be in different jurisdictions.

Telehealth licensing considerations:

- whether the professional must be licensed in the jurisdiction where the patient is located, which is the general rule in most frameworks;
- whether any interstate compacts or recognition mechanisms allow practice across state lines without separate licensure;
- whether the services provided via telehealth are within the professional's scope of practice in the patient's jurisdiction;
- whether telehealth modalities, such as audio-only, video, or asynchronous, are authorized for the services provided;
- whether prescribing controlled substances via telehealth is permitted and under what conditions;
- whether informed consent and documentation requirements specific to telehealth are met.

The general rule that the professional must be licensed where the patient is located means that telehealth across jurisdictional lines typically requires licensure in each patient jurisdiction. Interstate compacts facilitate but do not eliminate multi-jurisdiction licensing.

### Review Facility Licensing And Accreditation

Healthcare facilities must be licensed, and many seek accreditation as a mark of quality and as a condition of payment.

Facility licensing elements:

- state or national facility licenses for hospitals, clinics, ambulatory surgery centers, and other facility types;
- the services authorized under each facility license and any conditions or restrictions;
- certification for participation in government healthcare programs, such as Medicare certification in the US;
- accreditation by recognized bodies, such as Joint Commission accreditation, which may be required for payment or credentialing;
- compliance with facility standards, including staffing, equipment, safety, and quality requirements;
- reporting obligations for incidents, infections, and adverse events.

Facility licensing is separate from professional licensing. A professional may be licensed but may not practice in a facility that lacks the appropriate license for the services provided.

### Assess Delegation And Supervision Arrangements

Delegation and supervision arrangements allow licensed professionals to extend services through delegated authority to other providers.

Delegation and supervision elements:

- the legal requirements for delegation, including which services may be delegated and to whom;
- the level of supervision required, ranging from general supervision to direct on-site supervision;
- the documentation of delegation agreements and collaborative practice arrangements;
- the liability implications of delegation, including supervisory liability for delegated acts;
- the authority of supervising professionals to intervene or modify delegated services;
- periodic review of delegation arrangements for continued appropriateness.

Delegation that does not meet legal requirements, or that delegates services beyond the delegate's qualifications, can create liability for both the delegating professional and the organization.

### Monitor License Status And Renewal

License status and renewal must be monitored continuously to ensure that all professionals maintain active, unrestricted licenses.

Monitoring elements:

- tracking license expiration dates and renewal requirements for all professionals;
- verification of license status through primary source checks at hire and periodically thereafter;
- monitoring of disciplinary actions, restrictions, or sanctions against licensed professionals;
- protocols for suspending or terminating practice privileges when licenses lapse or are restricted;
- documentation of license status and any actions taken in response to lapses or restrictions.

Practicing with a lapsed or restricted license constitutes unlicensed practice, regardless of the professional's qualifications or the reason for the lapse.

## Common Traps

### Assuming One Jurisdiction's License Applies Elsewhere

A license in one jurisdiction generally does not authorize practice in another. Telehealth and cross-jurisdiction practice require assessment of each patient jurisdiction.

### Scope Of Practice Based On Professional Preference

Scope of practice is defined by law and regulation, not by professional preference or organizational convenience. Services must be within the authorized scope.

### Credentialing Without Primary Source Verification

Credentialing based on self-reporting or secondary sources creates negligent credentialing liability. Primary source verification is required.

### Telehealth Without Patient-Jurisdiction Licensing

Providing telehealth to patients in jurisdictions where the professional is not licensed constitutes unlicensed practice under the general rule.

### Facility Services Beyond Licensed Scope

Providing services in a facility that lacks the appropriate license, or beyond the facility's licensed scope, violates facility licensing requirements.

### Delegation Without Meeting Legal Requirements

Delegation that does not meet delegation, supervision, and documentation requirements creates liability for both the delegating professional and the organization.

### Lapsed Licenses Continuing To Practice

Practicing with a lapsed or restricted license constitutes unlicensed practice, regardless of qualifications or reason for lapse.

## Self-Check

- Are licensing requirements mapped for all professional roles in all jurisdictions where services are provided?
- Is credentialing based on primary source verification of licenses, education, training, malpractice history, and disciplinary actions?
- Is scope of practice assessed for each license type, including independent services, delegation, supervision, and prescriptive authority?
- Are telehealth and cross-jurisdiction practice arrangements assessed for patient-jurisdiction licensing, scope, modality, and prescribing authority?
- Are facility licenses maintained for all facility types, with services within licensed scope and accreditation current?
- Are delegation and supervision arrangements assessed for legal compliance, documentation, and liability implications?
- Is license status monitored continuously, with tracking of renewals, restrictions, and disciplinary actions?
- Are practice privileges suspended or terminated promptly when licenses lapse or are restricted?
- Is the licensing and credentialing map updated whenever roles, services, or jurisdictions change?
- Is the licensing and scope assessment confirmed against the applicable national healthcare regulation and licensing board guidance with qualified counsel, rather than a generic standard?
