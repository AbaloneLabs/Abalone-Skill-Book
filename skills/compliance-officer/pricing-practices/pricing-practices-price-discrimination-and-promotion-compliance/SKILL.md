# Pricing Practices Price Discrimination And Promotion Compliance

Pricing is one of the most heavily scrutinized areas of commercial conduct, because it sits at the intersection of antitrust law, consumer protection law, and industry-specific pricing regulation. Under the US Robinson-Patman Act, the EU Treaty and national competition laws, consumer protection statutes on pricing and advertising, and sector-specific pricing rules, companies must assess price discrimination among customers, promotional and discount structures, differential pricing across channels, and the transparency and fairness of pricing representations. The central judgment problem is that pricing decisions that look like ordinary commercial strategy can constitute illegal price discrimination, deceptive promotional pricing, or unfair treatment of competing buyers. A compliance officer who reviews pricing only for profitability and market positioning, without assessing the antitrust and consumer protection dimensions, embeds risk in the most fundamental commercial activity of the firm.

Use this skill before reviewing pricing practices, assessing price discrimination exposure, evaluating promotional pricing structures, or advising on differential pricing across customers and channels. The goal is to make the agent think about the competitive effects of price differences, the deception risk in promotional pricing, the treatment of competing buyers, and the decisions that are easy to make too casually. A pricing structure that treats customers differently without competitive justification, or a promotion that creates a false reference price, creates exposure that margin analysis will not reveal.

This skill addresses jurisdiction-specific obligations. Price discrimination rules, promotional pricing standards, and consumer protection requirements differ across the US, the EU, and national regimes. Always confirm the applicable national law and regulator guidance, and consult qualified antitrust, consumer protection, or legal counsel for specific pricing decisions.

## Core Rules

### Assess Price Discrimination Among Competing Buyers

Price discrimination, charging different prices to different buyers for goods of like grade and quality, can violate antitrust law when it injures competition between the favored and disfavored buyers.

Price discrimination analysis:

- identify whether the buyers are competitors at the same level of distribution, since the concern is injury to competition between them;
- assess whether the price differences are cost-justified by differences in manufacture, sale, or delivery;
- assess whether the price differences reflect meeting competition in good faith;
- evaluate whether the discrimination has a reasonable possibility of injuring competition, not merely affecting individual transactions;
- consider both direct price differences and indirect differences through discounts, rebates, services, or promotional allowances.

Price discrimination analysis is not limited to the invoice price. Differences in discounts, rebates, advertising allowances, services, or payment terms can constitute discrimination. The compliance program must look at the totality of the price and terms offered to competing buyers.

### Justify Price Differences With Cost Or Competitive Justification

Price differences that are not cost-justified or driven by competition carry elevated risk. The compliance program should ensure that material price differences are supported by documented justification.

Justification types:

- cost justification, where the price difference reflects actual cost savings from quantity, delivery method, or service level;
- meeting competition, where the lower price is a good-faith response to a competitor's equally low price;
- changing conditions, such as distress sales of perishable goods or obsolescent inventory;
- functional discounts to buyers who perform distribution functions that the seller would otherwise perform.

The justification must be documented at the time of the pricing decision. Retroactive rationalization of a price difference is not a defense. The documentation should show the cost analysis or competitive intelligence that supported the decision.

### Evaluate Promotional Pricing For Deception Risk

Promotional pricing, discounts, and sales representations are subject to consumer protection rules that prohibit deceptive reference prices and false savings claims.

Promotional pricing compliance:

- reference prices, such as was or regular prices, must be genuine and not inflated to exaggerate the discount;
- the reference price must reflect a bona fide price at which the product was offered for a reasonable period;
- limited-time offers must genuinely be limited in time and not perpetually renewed;
- clearance and liquidation claims must reflect genuine inventory clearance;
- bundled pricing must clearly disclose the components and any conditions;
- dynamic pricing must not mislead consumers about the basis for the price or the availability of a better price.

A reference price that was never genuinely offered, or a perpetual sale, constitutes deceptive pricing. The compliance program should review promotional pricing against the actual price history and the genuineness of the reference price.

### Assess Differential Pricing Across Channels

Differential pricing across channels, such as online versus in-store, or direct versus distributor, raises both antitrust and consumer protection considerations.

Channel pricing assessment:

- assess whether channel price differences reflect cost differences, service levels, or genuine channel strategy;
- evaluate whether channel differences disadvantage competing distributors or retailers in a way that injures competition;
- consider whether differential pricing deceives consumers about the availability or basis of the price;
- assess whether the channel strategy constitutes a vertical restraint, such as inducing distributors to adhere to prices;
- review online and offline pricing consistency claims for accuracy.

Channel pricing that reflects legitimate cost and service differences is generally defensible. Channel pricing designed to disadvantage competitors or to disguise RPM carries risk.

### Review Discount And Rebate Structures For Competitive Effects

Discount and rebate structures can have competitive effects beyond their commercial intent, particularly when deployed by firms with market power.

Discount and rebate review:

- volume discounts that reflect cost savings are generally defensible;
- loyalty and fidelity rebates that induce exclusivity can constitute abuse of dominance or vertical restraint;
- retroactive rebates that apply to all volume when a threshold is met can penalize partial switching;
- target rebates set just above typical purchase levels can induce increased purchases;
- multi-product rebates that leverage dominance in one product to foreclose competition in another;
- conditional discounts tied to exclusivity, market share, or non-dealing with competitors.

Each discount and rebate structure should be assessed for foreclosure effects, particularly when the firm has market power. Structures that induce exclusivity or penalize dealing with competitors carry elevated risk.

### Ensure Pricing Transparency And Disclosure

Pricing transparency and disclosure obligations apply across consumer protection and sector-specific regulation.

Transparency requirements:

- disclose the full price, including all mandatory fees and charges, before the consumer commits;
- avoid drip pricing, where mandatory fees are added progressively through the purchase process;
- clearly disclose any conditions, limitations, or qualifications on the price;
- provide clear pricing information in advertising and at the point of sale;
- comply with sector-specific pricing disclosure rules, such as unit pricing in retail or APR disclosure in credit;
- honor advertised prices and correct genuine errors through established processes.

Drip pricing and incomplete price disclosure are enforcement priorities. The full price must be visible before the consumer makes a commitment, not revealed at the final stage of purchase.

### Document The Pricing Decision And Justification

Pricing decisions should be documented with their commercial rationale and competitive justification, particularly for material price differences and promotional pricing.

Documentation should include:

- the basis for any price differences among customers, including cost or competitive justification;
- the genuineness of reference prices for promotions, with price history support;
- the rationale for channel and discount structures, including efficiency justification;
- the competitive analysis for pricing by firms with market power;
- the individuals involved in the decision and the date.

A pricing decision without documented justification is harder to defend. The documentation should exist at the time of the decision and reflect genuine analysis, not retroactive rationalization.

## Common Traps

### Reviewing Pricing Only For Profitability

Pricing decisions have antitrust and consumer protection dimensions. Reviewing only margin and market positioning embeds risk.

### Price Differences Without Cost Or Competitive Justification

Material price differences among competing buyers require documented cost or competitive justification. Unsupported differences carry Robinson-Patman or equivalent risk.

### Deceptive Reference Prices In Promotions

Inflated or fictitious reference prices exaggerate discounts and constitute deceptive pricing. Reference prices must be genuine.

### Loyalty And Retroactive Rebates Without Foreclosure Analysis

Discount structures that induce exclusivity or penalize switching carry foreclosure risk, particularly for firms with market power.

### Drip Pricing And Incomplete Disclosure

Adding mandatory fees progressively through purchase deceives consumers. The full price must be visible before commitment.

### Channel Pricing That Disguises RPM

Differential pricing designed to induce distributor price adherence constitutes RPM. Channel strategy must be assessed for restraint effects.

### No Documentation Of Pricing Justification

Pricing decisions without documented rationale are harder to defend. Justification must exist at the time of the decision.

## Self-Check

- Are price differences among competing buyers assessed for antitrust injury to competition, including indirect differences through discounts, rebates, and services?
- Are material price differences supported by documented cost justification, meeting competition, or other recognized defense?
- Are promotional reference prices genuine, supported by price history, and not inflated to exaggerate discounts?
- Are limited-time offers, clearance claims, and bundled pricing accurate and not deceptive?
- Is differential pricing across channels assessed for both competitive effects and consumer deception risk?
- Are discount and rebate structures reviewed for foreclosure effects, particularly for firms with market power?
- Is pricing transparent, with full price including mandatory fees disclosed before consumer commitment?
- Are sector-specific pricing disclosure rules, such as unit pricing or APR, complied with?
- Are pricing decisions documented with commercial rationale and competitive justification at the time of the decision?
- Is the pricing compliance review confirmed against the applicable national antitrust, consumer protection, and sector-specific law rather than a generic standard?
