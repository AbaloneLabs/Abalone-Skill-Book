# Air Quality And Emissions Compliance

Air quality compliance governs the emission of pollutants from stationary sources into the atmosphere. The defining feature is that the air regulatory framework operates through multiple layered programs—criteria pollutants, hazardous air pollutants, new source review, Title V operating permits, and greenhouse gas requirements—that can apply simultaneously to a single source, each with its own limits, monitoring, and reporting. The central difficulty is that the programs interact: a physical or operational change can trigger pre-construction review under one program while the source remains subject to operating permit conditions under another.

Use this skill before advising on air permits, New Source Review applicability, NESHAP compliance, emissions monitoring, or greenhouse gas reporting. The goal is to make the agent identify the applicable programs, the emission limits, the monitoring and reporting duties, and the modification triggers before concluding that an air emissions operation is compliant.

## Core Rules

### Identify All Applicable Air Programs For Each Source

A single source may be subject to multiple overlapping programs.

Map:

- New Source Review (PSD for attainment areas, nonattainment NSR for nonattainment areas);
- Title V operating permits;
- National Emission Standards for Hazardous Air Pollutants (NESHAP) and Maximum Achievable Control Technology (MACT);
- New Source Performance Standards (NSPS) for new and modified sources;
- Criteria pollutant national ambient air quality standards (NAAQS) and state implementation plans (SIPs);
- greenhouse gas permitting and reporting;
- state and local air toxics programs;
- regional haze and visibility rules.

Each program has its own applicability triggers, limits, and requirements. Operating without recognizing a program's applicability is a common violation. The programs are layered, not alternative.

### Comply With New Source Review For Modifications

Physical or operational changes can trigger pre-construction permitting.

Assess:

- the definition of a modification and whether a change triggers NSR;
- major source thresholds for PSD and nonattainment NSR;
- significant emission rate thresholds for individual pollutants;
- actual-to-projected-actual emissions analysis for existing sources;
- plantwide applicability limits (PALs) as a compliance option;
- best available control technology (BACT) for PSD;
- lowest achievable emission rate (LAER) for nonattainment NSR;
- emissions offsets in nonattainment areas;
- the applicability of the major NSR rules to greenhouse gases.

A production increase or equipment change can trigger NSR even if existing permit limits are not exceeded. The actual-to-projected-actual test looks at whether emissions will increase, not just capacity. PALs can provide operational flexibility.

### Maintain Title V Operating Permit Compliance

Title V permits consolidate applicable requirements into a single document.

Control:

- timely permit applications and renewals;
- compliance with all incorporated requirements (emission limits, monitoring, reporting);
- compliance certifications by a responsible official;
- monitoring and recordkeeping to demonstrate compliance;
- prompt deviation reporting;
- permit modifications for changes;
- the permit shield concept and its limits;
- annual compliance certification.

The Title V permit is the comprehensive operating permit. The permit shield provides that compliance with the permit terms is deemed compliance with the underlying applicable requirements, but only for terms that are in the permit.

### Implement NESHAP And MACT Standards

Hazardous air pollutants are regulated by source category standards.

Cover:

- identification of the applicable NESHAP for the source category;
- MACT emission limits based on best-performing sources;
- area source versus major source NESHAPs;
- residual risk and technology review;
- compliance demonstrations (emissions averaging, testing, monitoring);
- risk management for remaining risks after MACT;
- the effect of area source reclassification to major source.

NESHAPs apply by source category and require MACT or generally available control technology. A source that increases hazardous air pollutant emissions above the major source threshold becomes subject to major source NESHAP rather than area source standards.

### Monitor Emissions Continuously And Periodically

Monitoring demonstrates compliance with limits.

Implement:

- continuous emissions monitoring systems (CEMS) for major sources;
- performance specifications and relative accuracy testing;
- data availability requirements (minimum operating hours);
- periodic emissions testing (stack testing);
- parametric monitoring and predictive emission monitoring systems (PEMS);
- recordkeeping of monitoring data;
- quality assurance and quality control;
- handling of monitor downtime and missing data.

CEMS data is the primary compliance evidence. Monitor downtime creates missing data periods that must be addressed through substitute data methods. Low data availability is itself a violation.

### Report And Certify Compliance

Reporting keeps regulators informed of compliance status.

Cover:

- semiannual monitoring reports;
- annual compliance certifications;
- prompt reporting of deviations;
- excess emissions reports;
- greenhouse gas reporting rule submissions;
- emissions inventory reporting;
- toxics release inventory (TRI) reporting;
- accuracy and certification by a responsible official.

False certification by a responsible official creates personal liability. Deviations must be reported promptly, not just in semiannual reports. Reporting obligations span multiple programs.

### Manage Greenhouse Gas Emissions

Greenhouse gases are regulated under multiple programs.

Assess:

- greenhouse gas reporting rule applicability and annual reporting;
- Clean Air Act permitting for major GHG sources;
- emission performance standards for power plants and other sectors;
- the interaction of GHG regulation with criteria pollutant programs;
- corporate carbon footprint and Scope 1, 2, and 3 emissions;
- the relationship to climate disclosure obligations;
- state and regional GHG programs (cap-and-trade, low carbon fuel standards).

GHG reporting is mandatory for sources above thresholds. Permitting requirements have evolved through rulemaking and judicial review. Corporate GHG accounting and climate disclosure are increasingly linked to regulatory reporting.

### Address Area Source And Fugitive Emissions

Not all emissions come from stacks.

Cover:

- fugitive emissions from equipment leaks, storage tanks, and material handling;
- area source standards for categories like boilers, reciprocating engines, and gas stations;
- fugitive dust from construction, roads, and material storage;
- leak detection and repair (LDAR) programs;
- storage vessel standards;
- the effect of fugitive emissions on source classification.

Fugitive emissions can be significant and are regulated through work practice standards and LDAR. Equipment leaks from valves, flanges, and pumps are a common source of volatile organic compound and hazardous air pollutant emissions.

## Common Traps

### Modification Triggering NSR Not Recognized

A production increase or equipment change can trigger pre-construction review even within permit limits.

### CEMS Downtime Creating Missing Data Violations

Low data availability or improper substitute data is itself a violation.

### Area Source Reclassified To Major Source

Increasing hazardous air pollutant emissions above thresholds changes the applicable standards.

### Title V Permit Shield Assumed To Cover Unlisted Requirements

The permit shield applies only to terms actually in the permit.

### Fugitive Emissions Overlooked

Equipment leaks and fugitive dust can be significant and are separately regulated.

### Deviation Reported Late Or Only Semiannually

Prompt deviation reporting is required, not just inclusion in periodic reports.

## Self-Check

- Are all applicable air programs identified for each source including NSR, Title V, NESHAP/MACT, NSPS, NAAQS/SIP, GHG, air toxics, and regional haze?
- Is New Source Review applicability assessed for modifications including major source thresholds, significant emission rates, actual-to-projected-actual analysis, PALs, BACT/LAER, and offsets?
- Is Title V operating permit compliance maintained with timely applications, incorporated requirements, certifications, monitoring, deviation reporting, modifications, and permit shield?
- Are NESHAP and MACT standards implemented including source category identification, MACT limits, area versus major source, compliance demonstrations, and reclassification effects?
- Is emissions monitoring conducted through CEMS, performance specifications, data availability, stack testing, parametric monitoring, recordkeeping, QA/QC, and missing data handling?
- Is compliance reported and certified through semiannual reports, annual certifications, prompt deviation reporting, excess emissions, GHG reporting, emissions inventory, TRI, and responsible official certification?
- Are greenhouse gas emissions managed through reporting rule, permitting, performance standards, criteria pollutant interaction, corporate footprint, climate disclosure, and state programs?
- Are area source and fugitive emissions addressed through fugitive standards, area source NESHAPs, fugitive dust, LDAR, storage vessels, and source classification?
- Is the interaction between programs understood when modifications or changes occur?
- Are state and local air quality requirements that may be stricter than federal applied?
