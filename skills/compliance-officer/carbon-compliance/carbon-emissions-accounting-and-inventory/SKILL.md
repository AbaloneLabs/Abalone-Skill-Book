# Carbon Emissions Accounting And Inventory

A greenhouse gas inventory is the foundation of all carbon compliance, target-setting, and disclosure, and its credibility depends on decisions made before any number is calculated. The defining difficulty is that boundary setting, scope inclusion, and methodology choices determine the result as much as actual emissions do, and inconsistent choices across periods or entities produce numbers that cannot be compared. An inventory that looks precise but rests on weak scope 3 estimates or shifting boundaries provides false confidence and creates liability when verified.

Use this skill before preparing an emissions inventory, setting boundaries, calculating scopes, selecting methodologies, or preparing data for verification. The goal is to make the agent set defensible boundaries, apply consistent methodologies across scopes, manage data quality and uncertainty, and produce an inventory that withstands verification. Inventory standards are technical and verification should involve qualified carbon accounting expertise.

## Core Rules

### Set Organizational And Operational Boundaries Defensibly

Boundaries determine which emissions are counted, and the choice must be consistent and documented.

Set:

- organizational boundary using an equity share or control approach applied consistently;
- operational boundary defining direct, energy indirect, and other indirect emissions;
- consistency with financial reporting boundaries where stakeholders expect alignment;
- treatment of leased assets, joint ventures, and non-consolidated entities;
- documentation of the boundary approach and any changes from prior periods.

The equity share approach counts emissions proportional to ownership, while control approaches count all emissions of controlled operations. The choice affects comparability and must be applied consistently. Changes in boundary approach or scope require restatement of prior periods for comparability.

### Calculate Scope 1 And Scope 2 Accurately

Direct and energy emissions are the most measurable and the most scrutinized.

Calculate:

- scope 1 direct emissions from owned or controlled sources including stationary combustion, mobile combustion, process emissions, and fugitive emissions;
- scope 2 indirect emissions from purchased electricity, steam, heat, and cooling;
- the location-based and market-based methods for scope 2 where relevant;
- activity data collection from meters, fuel records, and operational data;
- emission factor selection appropriate to the fuel, region, and period;
- documentation of activity data and factors for each source.

Scope 1 and 2 are calculated from activity data and emission factors, and the quality of activity data determines accuracy. Dual reporting of scope 2 under location and market-based methods is required where instruments are used. Fugitive emissions from refrigerants are frequently underestimated.

### Address Scope 3 Where Material

Value chain emissions often dominate the footprint and are the hardest to measure.

Address:

- identification of material scope 3 categories relevant to the business model;
- data collection from value chain partners where feasible;
- estimation using spend-based, average-data, or supplier-specific methods;
- disclosure of the methodology and uncertainty for each category;
- prioritization of categories by magnitude and influence;
- progressive improvement toward supplier-specific data over time.

Scope 3 includes 15 categories from purchased goods and services to use of sold products, and materiality varies by sector. A manufacturer's purchased goods and a financial institution's financed emissions dominate, while a service firm's may be travel and procurement. Estimation methods and uncertainty must be transparent, and improvement over time is expected.

### Select Emission Factors And Methodologies Consistently

Methodology consistency is essential for trend analysis and target tracking.

Select:

- emission factors from recognized sources appropriate to the geography and period;
- global warming potentials from the applicable assessment report;
- consistent application across periods with documented changes;
- recalculation of prior periods when factors or methodologies change materially;
- documentation of factor sources and selection rationale;
- treatment of biogenic emissions separately from fossil emissions.

Changing emission factors or global warming potentials without restating prior periods creates artificial trends. Factor selection must be documented, and material methodology changes require recalculation. Biogenic emissions are reported separately from the inventory totals.

### Manage Data Quality And Uncertainty

Data quality varies across sources, and uncertainty must be understood and disclosed.

Manage:

- primary data from meters and direct measurement where available;
- secondary data and estimates with documented methodology;
- data quality indicators assessing representativeness, completeness, and reliability;
- uncertainty assessment for estimated categories, particularly scope 3;
- progressive data quality improvement;
- documentation sufficient for verification.

Primary data is more reliable than estimated data, and the mix affects overall inventory quality. Uncertainty is highest in scope 3 and in fugitive emissions, and must be assessed and disclosed. Verification providers will test data quality, and weak data produces qualified verification opinions.

### Maintain A Base Year And Recalculation Policy

Targets and trends are measured against a base year that must remain comparable.

Maintain:

- a defined base year with documented inventory;
- a recalculation policy triggering restatement for structural changes such as acquisitions and divestitures;
- recalculation for methodology or factor changes that are material;
- recalculation for discovery of errors that are material;
- documentation of all recalculations and their rationale;
- a significance threshold defining when recalculation is triggered.

Without a recalculation policy, acquisitions and divestitures distort trends, and the base year becomes meaningless. The policy must define what triggers recalculation and the threshold of significance. All recalculations must be documented and disclosed.

### Prepare For Verification And Assurance

Verification is increasingly required and demands audit-ready documentation.

Prepare:

- a greenhouse gas assertion clearly stating the inventory scope and boundaries;
- a verification plan with evidence organized by source and scope;
- internal review before the verification engagement;
- management of verification findings and corrections;
- selection of an accredited and independent verifier;
- understanding of the levels of assurance and their evidentiary demands.

Verification tests the inventory against the stated assertion, the methodology, and the evidence. Disorganized evidence produces delays and qualified opinions. The level of assurance, limited or reasonable, determines the depth of testing and the confidence the opinion provides.

### Integrate The Inventory With Targets And Strategy

The inventory must connect to targets, strategy, and capital allocation to be meaningful.

Integrate:

- targets set against the verified base year with defined scopes and timeframes;
- progress tracking with the same methodology as the inventory;
- capital allocation aligned with the reduction pathway;
- strategy that addresses the largest emission sources;
- governance that connects inventory results to decision-making;
- disclosure that links inventory, targets, and progress.

An inventory that does not drive targets, strategy, and capital allocation is a reporting exercise rather than a management tool. Targets must use the same methodology as the inventory, or progress cannot be measured credibly.

## Common Traps

### Boundary Changes Without Recalculation

Acquisitions, divestitures, and methodology changes require base year restatement for comparability.

### Scope 3 Omitted Despite Materiality

Value chain emissions often dominate; omission where material understates the footprint.

### Weak Activity Data Treated As Precise

Estimated data carries uncertainty that must be disclosed, not presented as exact.

### Methodology Or Factor Changes Without Restatement

Changes without restatement create artificial trends and undermine target tracking.

### Inventory Disconnected From Targets And Strategy

An inventory that does not drive targets and capital allocation is not a management tool.

## Self-Check

- Are organizational and operational boundaries set defensibly with a consistent equity or control approach, financial alignment, treatment of leased and joint venture assets, and documented changes?
- Are scope 1 and 2 calculated accurately from activity data and appropriate emission factors, including stationary, mobile, process, and fugitive emissions, with dual location and market-based scope 2 where relevant?
- Is scope 3 addressed where material with category identification, partner data collection, estimation methodology, uncertainty disclosure, prioritization, and progressive improvement?
- Are emission factors and methodologies selected consistently from recognized sources with applicable global warming potentials, documented selection, material-change recalculation, and separate biogenic treatment?
- Is data quality and uncertainty managed through primary data preference, secondary data documentation, quality indicators, uncertainty assessment, progressive improvement, and verification-ready documentation?
- Is a base year maintained with a recalculation policy covering structural changes, methodology changes, error discovery, significance thresholds, and documentation?
- Is the inventory prepared for verification with a clear assertion, organized evidence, internal review, finding management, accredited verifier selection, and assurance-level understanding?
- Is the inventory integrated with targets, strategy, and capital allocation, and where verification or material decisions arise, has qualified carbon accounting expertise been engaged, recognizing that inventory standards are technical and this guidance is not legal advice?
