# Harassment Investigation And Due Process

Harassment investigation is where an employer either satisfies or breaches its legal duty to prevent and correct harassment. The defining difficulty is that the investigation must be prompt, thorough, impartial, and documented, while simultaneously protecting complainants from retaliation, preserving the rights of the accused, and operating under confidentiality constraints that limit what can be shared. A flawed investigation can convert a defensible position into liability, and the investigation record itself becomes the central evidence in any subsequent claim.

Use this skill before launching a harassment investigation, planning its scope, conducting interviews, making findings, or determining corrective action. The goal is to make the agent design an investigation that is prompt, impartial, thorough, and documented, protect the parties from retaliation, and preserve due process. Investigation obligations are jurisdiction-dependent and high-risk matters should involve qualified counsel.

## Core Rules

### Launch Promptly Upon Notice

The duty to investigate is triggered by notice, and promptness is measured from the moment the employer knew or should have known.

Act promptly by:

- acknowledging the complaint and outlining next steps to the complainant;
- launching the investigation within a defined short timeframe;
- taking immediate interim measures to separate parties or adjust reporting where needed;
- avoiding delay that appears to minimize the concern;
- documenting the timeline from notice to action.

Delay signals indifference and allows harm to continue. Interim measures must be designed to protect the complainant without penalizing them, such as changing the accused's schedule rather than the complainant's where possible.

### Ensure Impartiality And Independence

The investigator's credibility determines the investigation's credibility.

Ensure impartiality by:

- selecting an investigator with no conflict of interest and no reporting line to either party;
- using trained internal investigators, human resources, or qualified external investigators for complex matters;
- ensuring the investigator has appropriate authority and access;
- avoiding investigation by someone who will later decide discipline, where feasible;
- disclosing and addressing any appearance of conflict.

An investigation conducted by the accused's direct manager, or by someone whose outcome is predetermined, lacks credibility regardless of the actual findings. For senior leaders or high-stakes matters, external investigators protect both fairness and the appearance of fairness.

### Plan And Scope The Investigation Thoroughly

A thorough investigation covers the allegations, the context, and the credibility of all accounts.

Plan:

- the specific allegations and policy provisions at issue;
- the witnesses to interview, starting with the complainant and accused and extending to corroborating witnesses;
- the documents and evidence to review including communications, schedules, and prior complaints;
- the questions for each interview, open-ended and non-leading;
- the order of interviews to preserve evidence and protect parties;
- the need for follow-up as new information emerges.

The scope must be broad enough to capture the full picture, including patterns and prior complaints, but focused enough to conclude. Expanding scope without follow-through signals a lack of seriousness.

### Conduct Interviews To Elicit Credible Information

Interview technique determines the quality of information gathered.

Conduct interviews by:

- explaining the process, the confidentiality expectations, and the no-retaliation assurance;
- asking open-ended questions and allowing narrative responses;
- avoiding leading questions or conclusions;
- probing inconsistencies with follow-up rather than accusation;
- documenting interviews contemporaneously and accurately;
- allowing the accused a full opportunity to respond to the specific allegations;
- recognizing trauma responses in complainants without treating them as inconsistency.

The accused must be informed of the specific allegations with enough detail to respond, consistent with confidentiality. Contemporaneous documentation, reviewed for accuracy, is far more defensible than reconstructed notes.

### Manage Confidentiality Appropriately

Confidentiality protects the investigation and the parties, but it is not absolute.

Manage confidentiality by:

- sharing information strictly on a need-to-know basis;
- explaining to parties that confidentiality protects the integrity of the process, not the employer;
- recognizing that some jurisdictions restrict blanket confidentiality or non-disclosure for harassment, particularly for complainants;
- avoiding promises of absolute confidentiality that cannot be kept;
- protecting witness identities where feasible while acknowledging limits.

Blanket confidentiality directives that prevent complainants from discussing harassment may be restricted or unenforceable in some jurisdictions. Confidentiality should protect the process, not silence the complainant.

### Protect Against Retaliation Throughout

Retaliation is a separate violation and the most common post-investigation liability.

Protect against retaliation by:

- communicating the no-retaliation policy to all parties and witnesses;
- monitoring for adverse actions against the complainant and witnesses after the investigation;
- scrutinizing performance, discipline, or schedule changes following a complaint;
- documenting the legitimate business basis for any adverse action;
- providing channels for reporting suspected retaliation;
- acting promptly on retaliation concerns.

Retaliation can be subtle, including isolation, undesirable assignments, or exclusion from opportunities. Any adverse action following protected activity invites scrutiny and must be documented with a legitimate, independent basis.

### Make Credible Findings And Document The Basis

Findings must be based on the evidence and documented, even when inconclusive.

Document:

- the specific allegations addressed;
- the evidence considered including interviews and documents;
- the credibility assessment and reasoning;
- the conclusion reached using the appropriate standard, typically preponderance of the evidence;
- the rationale where evidence is conflicting or inconclusive;
- the corrective action determined and its basis.

An inconclusive finding is acceptable if the investigation was thorough and the reasoning is documented. A conclusory finding without documented reasoning is indefensible. The standard of proof is typically preponderance of the evidence, meaning more likely than not.

### Take Corrective Action Proportionate To Findings

Corrective action must address the conduct and prevent recurrence.

Take action that:

- is proportionate to the severity and frequency of the conduct;
- addresses the root behavior, not just the symptom;
- protects the complainant and the workplace from recurrence;
- is applied consistently with how similar conduct has been treated;
- is documented with the rationale.

Inconsistent discipline across similar conduct evidences pretext or bias. Corrective action should escalate with severity and repetition, and termination is appropriate for severe or repeated harassment.

## Common Traps

### Delay In Launching After Notice

Delay signals indifference and allows harm to continue, weakening any defense.

### Investigator With A Conflict Or Predetermined Outcome

An investigation by a conflicted or biased investigator lacks credibility regardless of findings.

### Leading Questions And Conclusory Interviews

Leading questions and reconstructed notes produce indefensible findings.

### Blanket Confidentiality That Silences Complainants

Restrictions preventing complainants from discussing harassment may be unenforceable in some jurisdictions.

### Inconsistent Or Undocumented Corrective Action

Discipline inconsistent with similar cases or lacking documented rationale evidences pretext.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- Is the investigation launched promptly upon notice with interim measures that protect the complainant without penalizing them?
- Is the investigator impartial and independent, with no conflict and no predetermined outcome, and are external investigators used for senior or high-stakes matters?
- Is the scope planned to cover allegations, policy provisions, witnesses, documents, prior complaints, and patterns, with follow-up as new information emerges?
- Are interviews conducted with open-ended questions, contemporaneous documentation, full opportunity for the accused to respond, and trauma-aware technique?
- Is confidentiality managed on a need-to-know basis without blanket directives that may be restricted, protecting the process rather than silencing the complainant?
- Is retaliation protected against through communication, monitoring, documentation of legitimate bases, channels, and prompt response?
- Are findings documented with allegations, evidence, credibility assessment, reasoning, the preponderance standard, and rationale for inconclusive results?
- Is corrective action proportionate, root-cause addressing, consistent, and documented, and has qualified counsel been involved in high-risk matters, recognizing that investigation obligations are jurisdiction-dependent and this guidance is not legal advice?
