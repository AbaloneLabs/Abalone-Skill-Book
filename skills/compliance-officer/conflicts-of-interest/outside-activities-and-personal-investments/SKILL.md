# Outside Activities And Personal Investments

Outside employment, board seats, consulting roles, and personal investments are legitimate in most cases, but they are also a primary vehicle for conflicts of interest, insider-information misuse, undisclosed self-dealing, and indirect corruption. An employee's second job can compete with their duties, their board seat can align them with a competitor or supplier, and their personal trades can exploit information they obtained at work. The compliance problem is that these activities occur outside the workplace, are often invisible to the employer, and are frequently rationalized as purely personal. A program that does not surface and govern outside activities cannot detect the relationships and trades that enable misconduct.

Use this skill before approving an outside role, designing personal trading and outside activity restrictions, evaluating whether an investment creates conflict risk, or investigating potential insider trading or undisclosed self-dealing. The goal is to make the agent examine each outside activity and holding for overlap with the employee's duties, access to information, and potential to benefit a counterparty, supplier, competitor, or government official.

## Core Rules

### Require Pre-Clearance For Material Outside Activities

Outside activities should not be a right exercised in secret. Employees in roles with decision authority, access to sensitive information, or external relationships should pre-clear outside employment, directorships, consulting, and significant volunteer governance roles. Pre-clearance surfaces the activity while it can still be conditioned or declined, rather than discovered after harm.

Require pre-clearance for:

- outside employment and consulting, paid or unpaid;
- board, advisory, and trustee roles, including for private companies and nonprofits;
- roles with a supplier, customer, competitor, or counterparty of the company;
- roles with a trade association, standards body, or regulator where the employee represents the company;
- political or government roles that could create revolving-door exposure;
- founding or operating a separate business.

Pre-clearance should assess overlap with duties, conflicts of commitment, confidentiality, and the risk that the role creates a channel for improper benefit.

### Distinguish Conflict Of Interest From Conflict Of Commitment

Two distinct risks arise from outside activity. A conflict of interest is a financial or relational overlap that could bias decisions. A conflict of commitment is a diversion of time, attention, or company resources to an outside pursuit. Both matter, and a role can create either or both. Evaluate the time commitment, the use of company resources, the overlap with working hours, and whether the outside role competes with the company's business.

### Restrict Investments That Overlap With Duties Or Information

Personal investments become a compliance problem when they overlap with the employee's responsibilities or with information the employee obtains at work. An employee who selects suppliers should not hold a financial interest in those suppliers. An employee with access to deal or earnings information should not trade on it. The restriction should follow the overlap, not merely the size of the holding.

Restrict:

- holdings in suppliers, customers, competitors, or counterparties the employee evaluates, selects, or supervises;
- holdings in bidders, acquisition targets, or joint venture partners the employee can influence;
- holdings whose value could be materially affected by the employee's decisions;
- trades in company securities outside approved windows by insiders;
- trades based on material nonpublic information obtained at work, which is unlawful regardless of policy;
- investments in funds or vehicles whose underlying holdings the employee cannot see but that may overlap, requiring review for blind-pool risk.

### Define And Enforce Insider Trading Controls

Insider trading is both a conflict failure and a standalone securities offense. Employees with access to material nonpublic information, about the company, about customers, or about counterparties, can misuse it in personal trades or tip others. The controls must address both direct trading and tipping.

Insider trading controls include:

- identifying employees with regular access to material nonpublic information;
- maintaining insider lists and trading windows;
- pre-clearance of trades in company securities for covered personnel;
- blackout periods around earnings and material events;
- prohibitions on tipping, including to family and friends;
- prohibitions on trading in securities of customers, suppliers, and counterparties based on information obtained at work;
- training that covers the mosaic theory and the breadth of material nonpublic information;
- certification and acknowledgment of the policy.

### Screen Outside Roles For Anti-Corruption And Government Exposure

An outside role can create anti-corruption exposure if it involves government officials, state-owned enterprises, or entities that do business with the government in ways that overlap with the employee's duties. A board seat at a distributor, a consulting role with a government-facing charity, or an advisory role with a trade body that lobbies can all create indirect channels for improper influence. Screen outside roles for government touchpoints and for overlap with the company's government-facing activity.

### Control Use Of Company Resources And Information

Outside activities often draw on company resources, relationships, information, or brand. An employee consulting for a customer, using company data in a side business, or leveraging the company's relationships for a personal venture creates conflict and potentially theft or misuse of assets. State clearly that company resources, information, systems, and relationships are for company business and may not be used for outside gain.

### Manage Senior Personnel And Specialized Roles Differently

Executives, board members, and employees in finance, mergers, procurement, and government-facing roles carry elevated outside-activity and investment risk. Apply stricter pre-clearance, broader investment restrictions, and more frequent review to these roles. Senior personnel should generally be prohibited from board roles at competitors and from material holdings in suppliers or customers without disclosure and approval.

### Review And Renew Periodically

Circumstances change. A pre-cleared outside role can become a conflict when the employee's duties change, when the outside entity begins doing business with the company, or when the employee gains access to new information. Require periodic renewal of outside-activity approvals and re-screen investments when duties change.

## Common Traps

### Allowing Outside Activities To Be Self-Judged

Employees routinely underestimate the conflict in their own outside roles. Pre-clearance and review by a neutral party, not self-assessment, is the control.

### Ignoring Conflict Of Commitment

A role that is financially benign can still consume the employee's time and attention at the company's expense. Evaluate time and resource diversion, not only financial overlap.

### Overlooking Trades In Customer And Supplier Securities

Policies often focus on company stock and miss trading in the securities of customers, suppliers, and counterparties based on information obtained at work, which is equally unlawful.

### Treating Blind-Pool Funds As Automatically Safe

Employees may hold investments in funds whose underlying assets they cannot see but that may overlap with their duties. Review the fund's investment focus and the employee's ability to influence or know the holdings.

### Failing To Address Tipping

The greatest insider-trading risk is often tipping family or friends, not direct trading. Policies and training must address tipping explicitly.

### Letting Senior Personnel Bypass Pre-Clearance

Executives are sometimes allowed to manage their own outside roles and investments. The highest-risk personnel need the strongest controls, not the weakest.

### Missing The Government Touchpoint In Outside Roles

A board or advisory role at a government-facing entity can create indirect corruption exposure. Screen outside roles for government interaction, not only commercial overlap.

## Self-Check

- Is pre-clearance required for outside employment, board seats, consulting, and significant governance roles, with assessment of overlap, commitment, confidentiality, and improper-benefit risk?
- Are conflicts of interest and conflicts of commitment both evaluated for time, resource use, and business competition?
- Are investments restricted where they overlap with the employee's duties, decision authority, or access to material information, including supplier, customer, competitor, and counterparty holdings?
- Are insider trading controls in place covering insider lists, trading windows, blackout periods, tipping, and trades in customer and supplier securities?
- Are outside roles screened for government touchpoints and anti-corruption exposure, not only commercial overlap?
- Is the use of company resources, information, systems, and relationships for outside gain clearly prohibited?
- Are senior personnel and specialized roles subject to stricter pre-clearance, investment restrictions, and renewal?
- Are outside-activity approvals renewed periodically and investments re-screened when duties change?
- Are blind-pool fund holdings reviewed for overlap rather than assumed safe?
- Is training on insider trading broad enough to cover the mosaic theory, tipping, and non-company securities?
