# Revolving Door And Post Government Rules

Hiring a current or former government official, or having an employee move between the company and government, is legitimate and often valuable, but it is one of the most sensitive areas in anti-corruption compliance. The same relationships and knowledge that make a former official attractive to a company are the relationships and knowledge that create bribery, conflict, and undue-influence exposure. Every major anti-corruption framework, the FCPA, the UK Bribery Act, OECD guidance, and the DOJ Evaluation of Corporate Compliance Programs, treats the revolving door as a risk surface requiring specific controls. The compliance problem is that post-employment restrictions are jurisdiction-specific, time-limited, and easy to violate inadvertently, and that the appearance of trading a job for an official act is often as damaging as the reality.

Use this skill before hiring a former government official, engaging one as a consultant, assigning a former official to matters they handled in government, or managing an employee who leaves for government and may return. The goal is to make the agent identify the specific post-employment restrictions that apply, control the contact and gift exposure, and document the basis for each decision rather than treating the hire as routine.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Person's Government Status And Restrictions First

Before any hire, engagement, or assignment involving a current or former government official, identify their exact government status and the restrictions that follow them. Restrictions vary by jurisdiction, by the level and type of role, and by the matters the person handled. They cannot be assumed; they must be determined and documented.

Determine:

- the jurisdiction and government body where the person served;
- the seniority and decision authority of the role;
- whether the person handled matters involving the company, its competitors, or its sector;
- the specific post-employment restrictions that apply, including cooling-off periods, lobbying bars, and matter-specific recusal;
- whether the person is subject to a contractual or ethical restriction from their government employer;
- whether the person holds a security clearance or access to non-public government information;
- the date the person left government, which starts most cooling-off clocks.

When restrictions are unclear, obtain the person's own ethics opinion or engage local counsel. Do not rely on the candidate's self-assessment alone.

### Apply Cooling-Off And Lobbying Restrictions

Many jurisdictions impose a cooling-off period during which a former official cannot lobby, communicate with, or represent others before their former agency. Some impose a permanent bar on representing others in specific matters the official handled. Violating these is an offense for both the individual and, in many cases, the company.

Controls for cooling-off and lobbying restrictions:

- do not assign the former official to lobby or communicate with their former agency during the restricted period;
- do not use the former official as a backchannel through colleagues to reach their former agency;
- record the cooling-off period and set a reminder for its expiration;
- restrict the former official from matters they personally and substantially handled in government;
- review the proposed role against the applicable restrictions before the hire is finalized;
- obtain a written acknowledgment from the individual of their restrictions.

### Manage The Hire To Avoid Improper Influence And Appearance

Hiring a former official who oversees, regulated, or awarded business to the company creates the appearance that the job was a reward for favorable treatment. Even where no quid pro quo existed, the appearance can trigger investigation and reputational harm. Manage the hire to address both substance and appearance.

Apply:

- pre-hire review of the person's prior dealings with the company, including procurement, regulatory, and enforcement matters;
- a documented business rationale for the hire that does not depend on the person's government relationships;
- a waiting period where appropriate between the person's government role and the company role;
- assignment to matters unrelated to those the person handled in government;
- restrictions on the person contacting their former agency on the company's behalf during the cooling-off period;
- review of any compensation arrangement that could be characterized as a delayed reward, such as large signing bonuses tied to prior awards.

### Control Gifts And Hospitality Involving Former Officials

Gifts, meals, and hospitality to former officials remain sensitive during the period when they could return to government or could influence their former colleagues. Some jurisdictions extend gift and contact restrictions to former officials for a defined period. Apply government-official gift rules to former officials where there is any ongoing government interaction, and prohibit gifts to current officials in connection with a potential or recent hire.

Specific controls:

- no gifts, meals, or hospitality to a current official in connection with prospective employment;
- no offers of employment, consulting, or benefits conditioned on official action;
- application of government-official gift rules to former officials during the restricted period;
- prohibition on providing benefits to family members of current or former officials that could be routed as influence;
- documentation of any hospitality involving former officials.

### Manage The Outbound Revolving Door

The revolving door runs both ways. An employee who leaves the company for a government role that regulates, procures from, or oversees the company creates exposure, especially if they return later. The company must manage the transition to avoid improper influence and to preserve the integrity of future interactions.

For outbound transitions:

- review the employee's access to sensitive information before departure;
- restrict their handling of matters involving the prospective government employer during the notice period;
- document any severance or deferred compensation that could be characterized as a future reward;
- apply post-employment restrictions on the company's contact with the former employee once in government;
- manage the employee's return to the company after government service under the inbound rules.

### Screen Consulting And Intermediary Engagements

Engaging a former official as a consultant, advisor, lobbyist, or intermediary carries the same risks as hiring them as an employee, sometimes more, because the engagement can be structured to obscure the relationship. Apply the full revolving-door analysis to any engagement of a former official, including the origin of the relationship, the scope, the compensation, and the government touchpoints.

### Document The Analysis And Decision

The defense against a revolving-door allegation is documentation. Record the person's government status, the applicable restrictions, the pre-hire review of prior dealings, the business rationale, the assigned scope, the cooling-off management, and the gift controls. A hire that is defensible on paper but undocumented is hard to distinguish from one that was improper.

## Common Traps

### Treating The Hire As Routine

A former official is never a routine hire. The restrictions, appearance, and controls must be assessed before the offer, not after.

### Relying On The Candidate's Self-Assessment Of Restrictions

Candidates often do not know, or do not fully disclose, their post-employment restrictions. Verify through the applicable rules, an ethics opinion, or counsel.

### Using The Former Official As A Backchannel

Routing communications through colleagues to reach the former official's agency during a cooling-off period is a circumvention and is treated as a violation.

### Ignoring The Appearance Of A Reward

A large signing bonus or deferred arrangement tied to a person who previously awarded business to the company looks like a delayed bribe regardless of intent.

### Overlooking Outbound Transitions

An employee leaving for government can take information, relationships, and influence. Manage the departure, not only the arrival.

### Failing To Track Cooling-Off Expiration

Cooling-off periods are time-limited and easy to lose track of. Without a tracking mechanism, the company may inadvertently assign restricted work or may miss when restrictions lift.

### Engaging Former Officials As Consultants To Bypass Hire Controls

Consulting engagements are not a loophole. Apply the same revolving-door analysis to any engagement of a former official.

## Self-Check

- Is the person's exact government status, jurisdiction, role, prior matters, and post-employment restrictions identified and documented before any hire or engagement?
- Are cooling-off and lobbying restrictions applied, tracked to expiration, and enforced against backchannel contact with the former agency?
- Is the hire reviewed for prior dealings with the company, documented business rationale, waiting period, matter assignment, and compensation that could appear to be a reward?
- Are gift and hospitality rules for current and former officials applied, with no benefits conditioned on official action and no hospitality during prospective employment?
- Are outbound transitions managed for information access, notice-period conduct, severance, and post-employment contact restrictions?
- Are consulting and intermediary engagements of former officials subjected to the same revolving-door analysis as hires?
- Is the candidate's self-assessment of restrictions verified through rules, ethics opinions, or counsel rather than accepted at face value?
- Are records of status, restrictions, review, rationale, scope, cooling-off management, and gift controls retained to defend the decision?
- Is the appearance of a quid pro quo addressed in addition to the substance?
- Are returning employees who left for government managed under the inbound revolving-door rules?
