# Regulatory Obligation Update And Control Remediation

Once a regulatory change is assessed and a gap is confirmed, the work shifts from detection to remediation: updating the obligation register to reflect the new or amended requirement, designing or modifying the controls that satisfy it, and driving the implementation across policies, procedures, systems, training, and contracts to closure before the effective date. This is the execution phase of regulatory change management, and its failure mode is the gap between a documented obligation update and an operational control that actually works. An obligation added to a register but not reflected in a procedure, a procedure updated but a system not reconfigured, or a system changed but staff not trained, leaves the organization nominally compliant on paper and non-compliant in practice.

The harm this skill prevents is an obligation register that is updated but disconnected from the controls that must enforce it, a remediation that addresses the documentation but not the operational behavior, a control change that is designed but never tested, or a program that misses the effective date because the dependencies (system development, training rollout, contract amendments) were not sequenced. Compliance officers tend to treat the obligation update as the deliverable and the control change as the business's problem, but the obligation-to-control traceability and the remediation closure are compliance accountabilities, and a gap at any link in the chain is a compliance failure.

## Core Rules

### Update The Obligation Register With Traceable Change Records

The obligation register (or regulatory inventory) is the authoritative list of the requirements that apply to the organization, and a regulatory change must be reflected in it with a traceable change record. Each new or amended obligation should record the source (the statute, the rule, the citation), the effective date, the obligation text or summary, the in-scope entities and activities, the control owner, and the link to the implementing controls.

Maintain the change history, because regulators and auditors ask not only what the current obligation is but when and how it changed. An obligation register that is overwritten with each update loses the audit trail; a register with versioned change records demonstrates that the organization tracked the change through to implementation. The register is the backbone of the obligation-to-control traceability.

### Map Each Obligation To The Specific Controls That Implement It

Every obligation in the register must map to the specific control or controls that implement it, and the mapping must be bi-directional: from the obligation to the control (how is this requirement met?), and from the control to the obligation (why does this control exist?). An obligation with no mapped control is an unimplemented requirement; a control with no mapped obligation is an orphan that may be unnecessary or may implement a requirement that was not captured.

Build and maintain the obligation-to-control map as the core artifact of the remediation. When a regulatory change amends an obligation, the map identifies the controls that must change. When a control is modified, the map identifies the obligations affected. A remediation that updates the obligation but not the map leaves the organization unable to demonstrate how the requirement is met.

### Design Control Changes At The Right Level: Preventive, Detective, Corrective

A regulatory gap may require a preventive control (to stop the non-compliant action), a detective control (to identify it after it occurs), or a corrective control (to remediate it). The control design should match the risk and the requirement. A requirement to prevent a transaction requires a preventive control (a block in the system); a requirement to report a transaction requires a detective and reporting control; a requirement to remediate a breach requires a corrective control.

Design the control change at the level the requirement demands, and do not substitute a weaker control for a stronger one because it is easier. A requirement that implies prevention, met with a detective control, leaves the non-compliant action occurring. Document the control type and the rationale, so the design decision is auditable.

### Sequence The Remediation Across All Implementation Surfaces

A regulatory change is implemented across multiple surfaces: the policy, the procedure, the system, the training, the contract, and the reporting. Each surface has its own lead time and its own dependencies, and the remediation must sequence them so that all are complete before the effective date. A policy updated in a week, a system change in a quarter, and a training rollout in a month have different timelines, and the effective date is the constraint.

Build a remediation plan that lists every implementation surface, the owner, the dependency, and the target date, sequenced to the effective date with contingency. A plan that treats the documentation as the deliverable and leaves the system or the training to the end misses the date, because the system development is usually the long pole. Sequence the long-lead items first.

### Test The Changed Control Before Declaring Compliance

A control that is designed and documented but not tested is a hypothesis, not a control. Before the remediation is declared complete and the obligation is marked as implemented, the changed control must be tested: does the system block actually block, does the report actually generate the correct data, does the procedure actually produce the documented outcome? Testing validates that the control works as designed in the operational environment.

Design a test that exercises the control under realistic conditions, with the expected outcome defined in advance. A test that is run without a defined expected outcome, or that is run by the control owner without independence, is weak evidence. Document the test, the result, and the evidence, because the test record is the proof of implementation.

### Update Training And Communications To Match The Changed Behavior

A control change that alters what staff must do requires corresponding training and communication, or the staff will follow the old behavior. The training must reach the affected population before the effective date, and it must convey the specific behavioral change, not just the regulatory background. A staff member who is unaware of the new procedure follows the old one, and the control fails at the point of execution.

Identify the affected population for each control change, design the training or communication to convey the specific behavioral change, and deliver it before the effective date. Verify completion, because training that is assigned but not completed leaves the population untrained. Link the training to the control change, so the training record demonstrates that the population was prepared.

### Close The Remediation With Documented Evidence Of Implementation

A remediation is closed when the obligation is implemented, the controls are changed and tested, the training is delivered, and the evidence is assembled. The closure should be documented with a closure record that references the obligation, the control changes, the test results, the training completion, and the sign-off. A remediation declared closed without assembled evidence is a claim, not a closure.

Assemble the closure evidence as the remediation proceeds, not at the end, because evidence assembled retrospectively is incomplete. The closure record is the artifact a regulator or auditor reviews to confirm that the organization implemented the change, and it should tell the complete story from the impact assessment through the tested control.

## Common Traps

### Obligation Updated But Control Not Changed

An obligation added to the register with no corresponding control change leaves the requirement unimplemented. The trap is treating the register update as the deliverable.

### No Obligation-To-Control Traceability

An obligation with no mapped control, or a control with no mapped obligation, breaks the traceability that demonstrates compliance. The trap is maintaining the register and the controls as separate artifacts.

### Weak Control Substituted For Strong Requirement

A detective control substituted for a requirement that implies prevention leaves the non-compliant action occurring. The trap is designing the control for ease rather than for the requirement.

### Documentation Done, System And Training Deferred

A remediation that updates the policy and procedure but leaves the system change and training to the end misses the effective date. The trap is treating documentation as the bulk of the work.

### Control Designed But Not Tested

A control declared implemented without a test is a hypothesis. The trap is treating the design as the completion.

### Training Not Linked To The Behavioral Change

Training that conveys the regulatory background but not the specific behavioral change leaves staff following the old behavior. The trap is treating training as awareness rather than preparation.

### Closure Without Assembled Evidence

A remediation declared closed without the test results, training completion, and sign-off assembled is a claim. The trap is treating closure as a status change rather than an evidence package.

## Self-Check

- Is the obligation register updated with a versioned change record showing the source, effective date, obligation, scope, and control owner?
- Does every changed obligation map bi-directionally to the specific controls that implement it, with no orphan obligations or controls?
- Is the control change designed at the level (preventive, detective, corrective) the requirement demands, with the rationale documented?
- Does the remediation plan sequence every implementation surface (policy, procedure, system, training, contract, reporting) to the effective date, with long-lead items first?
- Is the changed control tested under realistic conditions with a defined expected outcome, and is the test result documented?
- Is training delivered to the affected population before the effective date, conveying the specific behavioral change, with completion verified?
- Is the remediation closed with an assembled evidence package (obligation, control changes, test results, training completion, sign-off), not just a status change?
- Can the closure record tell the complete story from impact assessment through tested control for a regulator or auditor?
