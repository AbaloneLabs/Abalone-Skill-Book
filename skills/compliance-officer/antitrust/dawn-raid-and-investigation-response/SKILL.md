# Dawn Raid And Investigation Response

A dawn raid, an unannounced inspection by competition authorities or prosecutors, is one of the most disruptive events a company can face, and the company's response in the first hours shapes the entire investigation. The European Commission and national competition authorities in the EU have broad dawn raid powers, the DOJ and state enforcers execute search warrants in the US, and the conduct of the company during the raid, what is disclosed, what is withheld on privilege, how employees behave, whether documents are preserved, is itself scrutinized. A compliance officer does not need to lead the legal response, but must know the immediate steps, the scope limits to assert, the privilege protections to preserve, and the employee conduct to manage, because mistakes during a raid can create obstruction exposure, waive privilege, and hand investigators the evidence that secures a case.

Use this skill before a raid occurs, when designing and testing a dawn raid response plan, and during the immediate response to an unannounced inspection. The goal is to make the agent execute a calm, lawful, privilege-preserving response that cooperates within legal limits without over-disclosing, obstructing, or allowing employees to improvise.

## Core Rules

### Have A Tested Dawn Raid Response Plan Before The Raid

A dawn raid cannot be managed with an improvised response. The plan must exist, be known to key personnel, and be rehearsed, because the raid will arrive without warning and the first hours are decisive.

The plan must cover:

- a defined response team with named primary and backup members, including legal, compliance, IT, security, communications, and a senior business sponsor;
- clear escalation and notification chains, including external counsel, for EU Commission or national authority raids and for US search warrants;
- a front-desk and reception protocol to verify the inspectors' authority, identities, and the legal basis and scope of the inspection before allowing access;
- a protocol for shadowing inspectors, copying documents, and logging what is taken;
- a protocol for managing employees, including instructing them on conduct and on their rights;
- a privilege protocol for identifying and protecting legally privileged materials;
- an IT protocol for preserving systems, managing device imaging, and preventing destruction;
- a communications protocol for internal messaging and any external or media response.

The plan should be tested through tabletop exercises, because under real pressure, untrained teams default to either over-cooperation or obstruction, both of which create risk.

### Verify Authority And Scope Before Granting Access

Inspectors must act within their legal authority and the scope of their warrant or decision. The company's first task is to verify that authority and to understand and record the scope, not to refuse entry, but also not to consent to anything beyond what is authorized.

Verify and record:

- the inspectors' identities and official credentials;
- the legal instrument authorizing the raid (a Commission decision, a national authority order, a judicial search warrant) and its terms;
- the scope of the inspection, including the premises, the subject matter, the period, and the types of documents and data covered;
- any limitations on time, on access to specific areas, or on seizure of particular materials;
- whether the scope permits forensic imaging of devices, access to personal devices, or access to off-site or cloud data.

If the scope is exceeded, the company should object, record the objection, and seek immediate legal recourse, but should not physically obstruct. Obstruction can be a separate offense and destroys credibility.

### Assert Legal Privilege Correctly

Legal professional privilege is the company's right to withhold legally privileged communications from inspection, but it must be asserted correctly and consistently, and the rules differ sharply between the EU and US.

Privilege handling:

- in the EU, the Commission's view of privilege is narrow, generally covering only written communications between EU-qualified external counsel and the client for the purpose of legal advice; internal counsel communications and some work-product may not be protected in the same way;
- in the US, attorney-client privilege and work-product doctrine are broader, covering internal counsel advice and litigation preparation, but privilege can be waived by over-disclosure;
- privileged materials should be identified, segregated, and withheld with a clear privilege log;
- the company should not allow inspectors to review privileged materials on the promise of later return, because review itself can waive or compromise privilege;
- where inspectors dispute a privilege claim, follow the prescribed procedure (sealing materials, seeking judicial or tribunal review) rather than surrendering them.

Privilege is easily lost through carelessness. A trained team member must oversee any access to areas or systems where privileged materials may be held.

### Shadow The Inspectors And Record Everything

The company should assign trained personnel to shadow each inspector or team, to copy or photograph every document taken, and to maintain a comprehensive log of the inspection.

Record:

- who entered, when, and where they went;
- every document, device, or data set examined, copied, or seized, with copies retained by the company;
- every question asked of employees and the substance of answers given;
- every instance where the company objected to scope or asserted privilege;
- the timing and duration of the inspection and any breaks.

This record is the company's evidence if the inspection exceeded scope, if materials were taken improperly, or if the company later needs to challenge the admissibility of evidence. Without a contemporaneous log, the company cannot effectively contest over-broad conduct.

### Manage Employee Conduct And Rights

Employees are often the weakest link during a raid. Unprepared employees may volunteer information, destroy documents, or make statements that harm the company. They also have rights that must be respected.

Instruct employees:

- to cooperate politely and not to obstruct;
- not to destroy, alter, or conceal any documents or data;
- to answer factual questions truthfully but concisely, without speculating or volunteering;
- to consult the response team or counsel before answering substantive questions where possible;
- not to sign statements or admissions without legal review;
- to report any request to delete or alter records immediately.

Respect that employees may have individual rights and exposure, particularly in criminal investigations, and that they may be entitled to separate counsel. The company should not interfere with employees seeking independent advice, and should not instruct employees in ways that could be seen as obstruction or witness tampering.

### Preserve Documents And Suspend Routine Destruction

A dawn raid triggers an immediate obligation to preserve all potentially relevant documents and data, and to suspend any routine deletion or destruction. Failure to preserve can create obstruction or spoliation exposure and can destroy leniency eligibility.

Immediate preservation steps:

- issue a global legal hold covering all relevant custodians, systems, and locations;
- suspend auto-deletion, retention-purge, and routine destruction cycles;
- preserve email, messaging, shared drives, devices, and backup systems;
- confirm the hold is acknowledged by key personnel and is technically enforced;
- document the hold and its implementation.

The preservation obligation applies from the moment the company is on notice of an investigation, which is the moment the raid begins, if not before.

### Coordinate External Counsel And Communications

The raid must be managed with external counsel engaged immediately, and internal and external communications must be controlled to avoid inadvertent disclosures or market disruption.

Coordinate:

- notify external antitrust counsel immediately, and involve local counsel in each relevant jurisdiction;
- control internal communications so that employees do not discuss the investigation over monitored channels that may be discoverable;
- prepare a controlled communications approach for any board, regulator, customer, or media contact;
- avoid any statement that could be construed as admitting liability or as directing employee testimony.

## Common Traps

### No Tested Plan, Leading To Improvisation

Without a rehearsed plan, the response team over-cooperates or obstructs, both of which create risk. The plan must be exercised before it is needed.

### Over-Cooperating Beyond The Scope

Inspectors may seek materials or access beyond their authority. Passive cooperation waives the company's right to contest scope and may disclose privileged or irrelevant sensitive material.

### Obstructing Or Destroying Documents

Physical obstruction, delay, or document destruction is a separate offense, destroys credibility, and can create individual criminal exposure for obstruction of justice.

### Waiving Privilege Through Carelessness

Allowing inspectors to review privileged materials, even temporarily, or failing to segregate them, can waive privilege. Privilege must be actively asserted and protected.

### Letting Employees Improvise Answers

Unprepared employees volunteer damaging information or speculation. Employees must be instructed on concise, truthful, non-speculative responses and on consulting counsel.

### Failing To Preserve And Suspending Destruction

Routine deletion that continues after notice of investigation is spoliation or obstruction. The legal hold must be immediate and technically enforced.

### Ignoring Employee Individual Rights And Exposure

Employees may face personal criminal exposure and are entitled to independent counsel. Interfering with that, or appearing to direct testimony, is obstruction or witness tampering.

### No Contemporaneous Record Of The Inspection

Without a log of what was examined and seized, the company cannot contest over-broad conduct or challenge evidence admissibility later.

## Self-Check

- Does a tested dawn raid response plan exist, with a named response team, escalation chains, and protocols for access verification, shadowing, privilege, IT preservation, and communications?
- Are front-desk and reception staff trained to verify inspector authority, identities, and the legal instrument and scope before granting access?
- Is the scope of the inspection verified and recorded, with objections raised and logged where inspectors exceed their authority, without physical obstruction?
- Is legal privilege asserted correctly, with privileged materials segregated, withheld with a privilege log, and not surrendered for review, accounting for the narrow EU privilege scope and broader US protections?
- Are trained personnel shadowing inspectors, copying all documents and data taken, and maintaining a comprehensive contemporaneous log?
- Are employees instructed to cooperate without obstructing, to avoid destruction, to answer concisely without speculating, and to consult counsel before substantive answers?
- Are employee individual rights and potential personal exposure respected, including access to independent counsel, without interference that could constitute obstruction?
- Is a global legal hold issued immediately, with routine deletion and destruction suspended and technically enforced across all systems and devices?
- Is external antitrust counsel engaged immediately, with local counsel in each relevant jurisdiction?
- Are internal and external communications controlled to avoid inadvertent disclosure, admissions, or direction of testimony?
