# Cartel And Price-Fixing Risk Prevention

Hard-core cartel conduct is the most serious antitrust risk a company can carry. Price-fixing, bid-rigging, customer or territory allocation, and output limitation are treated as per se unlawful in the United States and as object restrictions by object under EU TFEU Article 101, with no need to prove competitive harm. The exposure is not only civil: Sherman Act Section 1 violations are federal crimes, executives can be imprisoned, and EU and many national regimes impose director disqualification and personal fines. A compliance officer does not need to be an antitrust litigator, but must be able to recognize where cartel risk is hiding, because it rarely appears labeled as "price-fixing" in a contract.

Use this skill before approving sales practices, bidding procedures, competitor engagement rules, trade association participation, or responses to a cartel investigation or leniency opportunity. The goal is to make the agent identify the hard-core conduct categories, the indirect channels through which they occur, the high-risk roles, and the preservation and escalation steps before recommending or approving any practice.

## Core Rules

### Treat Hard-Core Conduct As Per Se Or By-Object Unlawful

Certain agreements between competitors are treated as so inherently harmful that no competitive justification is considered. A compliance officer should never approve or tolerate these, even temporarily, even with a "good reason," and even if framed as non-binding.

The hard-core categories are:

- price-fixing, including agreements to raise, stabilize, or maintain prices, or to coordinate price increases or surcharges;
- bid-rigging, including cover bidding, bid rotation, complementary bidding, or agreements on who will win;
- market allocation, including agreements to divide customers, territories, product lines, or sales channels;
- output limitation, including agreements to restrict production, capacity, sales volumes, or investment.

These apply whether the agreement is formal or informal, written or oral, implemented or not, and whether the parties are current competitors, potential competitors, or competitors in some markets only. Even an agreement that was never carried out can be enough to establish liability.

### Recognize Indirect And Informal Channels

Cartel conduct rarely appears in a signed document. It surfaces through indirect contacts that compliance must monitor.

Watch for:

- "gentlemen's agreements" or verbal understandings reached at dinners, golf, or social events after trade association meetings;
- signaling through public announcements, press releases, investor calls, or price lists intended to communicate future pricing intent to competitors;
- exchanges of competitively sensitive information through consultants, common distributors, customers, or trade associations;
- parallel conduct that follows a private contact, even without an explicit agreement;
- "industry consensus" or "standard practice" language used to justify coordinated behavior;
- responses to a competitor's price increase that look coordinated rather than independent.

The absence of a written contract does not create safety. US courts can infer an agreement from circumstantial evidence, and EU and many national authorities treat concerted practices as actionable even without proof of an explicit agreement.

### Map High-Risk Roles And Activities

Cartel risk concentrates in specific functions. A compliance program that trains everyone equally will under-protect the people who actually create exposure.

High-risk roles include:

- sales and pricing leaders who set list prices, discounts, or bids;
- commercial leaders responsible for tenders, RFPs, and public procurement;
- executives who attend trade associations, industry dinners, or standard-setting bodies;
- business unit heads in concentrated markets with few competitors;
- corporate development and strategy staff who interact with competitors about markets;
- anyone who hires from a competitor and may receive their pricing data.

Training, monitoring, and audit should be weighted toward these roles, not spread evenly. The compliance officer should know by name who attends competitor-facing events and what they are authorized to discuss.

### Enforce Rules On Competitor Contact

Clear, enforceable rules reduce the chance that an ambiguous contact becomes a cartel allegation. The rules should be operational, not aspirational.

Require that:

- any meeting with a competitor have a legitimate business purpose and, where sensitive, legal review in advance;
- trade association meetings have a compliance attendee or written agenda, and that social conversations after formal sessions are treated as part of the meeting;
- topics such as price, cost, margin, output, capacity, customer allocation, bidding intent, and future strategy are never discussed with competitors;
- if a competitor raises a prohibited topic, the employee disengages immediately, documents the incident, and reports it to compliance;
- joint conduct with competitors (purchasing, research, standards, lobbying) be structured through a written agreement with antitrust counsel review.

Document the rule, the training, and the exceptions. If an exception was granted, the record should show who approved it and why.

### Understand Leniency And Criminal Exposure

Because cartel exposure can be catastrophic, the compliance officer must understand the leniency landscape well enough to act quickly, even if counsel runs the application.

Know that:

- in the US, the DOJ Antitrust Division's Corporate Leniency Policy grants immunity to the first company to report and cooperate, and a parallel Individual Leniency Policy protects the first individual;
- the EU and most national regimes operate marker-based leniency programs where the first applicant can receive full immunity and later applicants reduced fines;
- leniency is typically available only before the authority has enough evidence to act, so speed is decisive;
- applying for leniency triggers document preservation, witness availability, and cooperation obligations that can last years;
- failing to preserve evidence after a report can destroy leniency eligibility and create obstruction exposure.

The compliance officer should know the internal escalation path from a credible cartel allegation to a leniency decision, and should not delay reporting to counsel while "gathering more facts."

### Coordinate Across Jurisdictions

Cartel conduct almost always spans borders, and leniency in one jurisdiction does not protect the company in another.

Account for:

- multi-jurisdictional leniency filing, because markers are jurisdiction-specific and time-sensitive;
- differing standards for what constitutes an "agreement" or "concerted practice";
- extraterritorial reach of US and EU law over conduct affecting their markets;
- national criminal regimes that may impose individual liability even where the EU regime is administrative;
- data protection limits on sharing employee communications across borders during investigation.

## Common Traps

### Treating "We Never Agreed" As A Defense

The law does not require a signed contract. Concerted action, parallel pricing after contact, or acquiescence in a competitor's signal can be enough. Asserting there was "no agreement" without examining the surrounding contacts is a trap.

### Assuming Public Announcements Are Safe

Announcing your own prices publicly can be lawful, but announcements timed or structured to coordinate with competitors, or that disclose future pricing strategy, can be treated as unlawful signaling. The line depends on intent and market context.

### Ignoring The Procurement Side

Bid-rigging is cartel conduct whether the company is the bidder or the buyer who facilitates it. A buyer who shares bid information, rotates awards, or steers tenders is exposed, and procurement staff are often undertrained.

### Letting Consultants And Distributors Carry The Risk

Using a consultant, trade body, or distributor to exchange information with competitors does not insulate the company. The company is generally responsible for the conduct of intermediaries acting on its behalf.

### Delaying A Leniency Decision

The most common and damaging trap is waiting too long. Leniency is a race, and second place gets nothing. A credible cartel allegation must reach counsel immediately, with a preservation hold, not after an internal fact-finding exercise.

### Underestimating Individual Exposure

Executives may face personal criminal liability, travel restrictions, and disqualification. A program that protects the company but leaves individuals exposed will not produce self-reporting. Individual leniency paths must be understood.

### Assuming "Industry Practice" Is Lawful

The fact that an industry commonly coordinates prices, allocations, or bidding does not make it lawful. Long-standing practice is sometimes the strongest evidence of a cartel.

## Self-Check

- Are price-fixing, bid-rigging, market allocation, and output limitation identified as per se or by-object unlawful conduct that cannot be approved under any justification?
- Are indirect channels (social events, signaling, consultants, distributors, trade associations) included in the risk map, not only formal agreements?
- Are high-risk roles (sales, pricing, bidding, trade association attendees, corporate development) identified by name and given targeted training and monitoring?
- Do competitor-contact rules prohibit discussion of price, cost, margin, output, capacity, customer allocation, and bidding intent, with a documented disengagement and reporting step?
- Is there a defined escalation path from a credible cartel allegation to counsel and a leniency decision, with a preservation hold triggered immediately?
- Are the DOJ Corporate and Individual Leniency Policies, EU marker-based leniency, and national programs understood well enough to act within hours, not days?
- Is multi-jurisdictional exposure accounted for, including extraterritorial reach and data protection limits on cross-border investigation?
- Are procurement and buyer-side bid-rigging risks covered, not only sales-side conduct?
- Is the company's reliance on consultants, trade bodies, or distributors assessed for intermediary-created exposure?
- Is individual criminal and disqualification exposure understood, and is the individual leniency path known to affected executives?
