# Platform And Digital Market Rules

Digital platforms create antitrust risks that traditional frameworks were not built for. Network effects, data accumulation, gatekeeping power, and vertical integration combine to produce durable positions that share analysis alone often misses. The EU Digital Markets Act (DMA) has responded with a set of ex ante obligations for designated gatekeepers, while US and national authorities apply Sherman Act Section 2 and new digital regulations to the same conduct. For a compliance officer, the challenge is that platform conduct, ranking your own product first, combining data across services, limiting app sideloading, restricting multi-homing, can simultaneously be core product strategy and a regulated or abusive practice. The risk surface is broad, the rules are new and changing, and the penalties for non-compliance with regimes like the DMA are severe.

Use this skill before advising on any platform ranking, data combination, app store policy, interoperability decision, or self-preferencing practice by a large online platform. The goal is to make the agent identify whether DMA or equivalent gatekeeper obligations apply, map the specific conduct to the relevant rule, and avoid the assumption that conventional antitrust analysis is sufficient for digital markets.

## Core Rules

### Determine Whether Gatekeeper Or Platform Regulation Applies

The first question is whether the firm or service is subject to a special digital regime, because that changes the analysis from effects-based antitrust to rule-based compliance.

Assess applicability of:

- the EU DMA, which applies to designated gatekeepers meeting thresholds related to turnover, market capitalization, and user numbers in core platform services (search engines, social networks, app stores, online marketplaces, advertising services, messaging, cloud, and more);
- national digital regulations that may apply even below DMA thresholds;
- US enforcement under Section 2, the FTC Act, and any sector-specific platform rules;
- other jurisdictions' digital competition laws that mirror or extend the DMA approach.

If the firm is a designated gatekeeper, specific obligations apply directly and the analysis is compliance with the regulation, not a rule-of-reason judgment. If the firm is large but not designated, conventional dominance and Section 2 analysis still applies, often with heightened scrutiny.

### Map The Conduct To The Specific DMA Obligation

For designated gatekeepers, the DMA sets out specific do's and don'ts. The compliance officer must map each business practice to the relevant article, because the obligations are precise and breaches carry large fines.

Key DMA gatekeeper obligations include:

- no self-preferencing in ranking or indexing the gatekeeper's own products or services over rivals';
- no use of business users' non-public data to compete against them;
- restrictions on combining personal data across services without consent;
- allowing app developers to inform users of alternative purchase channels and offer them outside the app store;
- allowing uninstalling pre-installed apps and changing defaults;
- no requiring use of the gatekeeper's identification, payment, or browser engine services;
- interoperability obligations for messaging and certain other services;
- no anti-steering provisions that prevent business users from promoting offers or concluding contracts outside the platform;
- no most-favored-nation or parity clauses restricting business users' pricing or terms elsewhere;
- transparency and data access for advertisers and publishers.

Each obligation maps to specific platform features. The compliance review must trace the feature to the obligation and confirm compliance, not assume general good faith is enough.

### Control Self-Preferencing And Ranking Conduct

Self-preferencing is a central DMA prohibition and a live Section 2 theory. A vertically integrated platform that ranks, displays, or promotes its own offerings over rivals' must review the mechanics of that preference.

Review:

- ranking algorithms and whether they systematically advantage the platform's own products;
- display, prominence, and default placement of own versus third-party offerings;
- whether rivals have equivalent access to features, formats, or data that affect visibility;
- whether the preference is justified by objective, non-discriminatory technical reasons or reflects commercial favoritism;
- the treatment of rivals' data, including whether business users' non-public data informs the platform's own competing products.

For a gatekeeper, self-preferencing is prohibited outright. For a non-gatekeeper dominant platform, it is assessed under Section 2 or Article 102 for exclusionary effect. Either way, ranking and display policies need documented, objective criteria.

### Enforce Data Combination And Use Restrictions

Combining data across services is heavily restricted for gatekeepers and scrutinized for all large platforms. The risk is both antitrust and privacy.

Controls:

- do not combine personal data across core platform services unless users have given specific, informed consent in line with GDPR requirements;
- do not use business users' non-public data to compete against them;
- maintain separation between data sourced from business users and data used for the platform's own competing services;
- document the basis for any cross-service data use and the consent mechanism;
- coordinate antitrust data rules with privacy and data protection compliance, because the two regimes interact.

Data combination is an area where antitrust and privacy compliance must work together. A practice that is lawful under privacy consent rules may still violate DMA or competition rules, and vice versa.

### Manage App Store, Interoperability, And Access Rules

App stores and platform access points are a major focus of digital regulation. The rules now require openness that platforms historically resisted.

Requirements and risk areas:

- allow developers to inform users of alternative purchase options and to offer products outside the app store;
- allow sideloading and use of third-party app stores where required;
- do not force use of the gatekeeper's payment, identification, or browser engine services;
- provide interoperability for messaging and other services on fair, reasonable, and non-discriminatory terms;
- allow uninstalling pre-installed apps and changing default settings;
- ensure APIs and developer access terms do not disadvantage rivals.

App store policies that bundle payment processing, restrict alternative channels, or impose parity are high-risk under the DMA and increasingly under other regimes. Review all such policies against the current rules.

### Protect Multi-Homing And Prevent Anti-Steering

Multi-homing, the ability of users and business users to use multiple platforms and to transact outside any single platform, is a core competitive safeguard in digital markets. Restrictions on it are a priority.

Avoid:

- anti-steering clauses that prevent business users from promoting or concluding contracts outside the platform;
- parity or MFN clauses restricting business users' prices or terms elsewhere;
- exclusivity incentives or penalties that effectively prevent multi-homing;
- technical or contractual barriers that lock users or business users into a single platform.

For gatekeepers, anti-steering and wide parity are prohibited. For other platforms, they are assessed for foreclosure and exclusionary effect, and remain a focus of enforcement.

## Common Traps

### Assuming Conventional Antitrust Analysis Is Enough For Platforms

Network effects, data advantages, and gatekeeping power make digital markets different. Share analysis alone can understate power, and conduct that looks like ordinary strategy can be a regulated or abusive practice.

### Ignoring DMA Designation Status

A designated gatekeeper has direct obligations, not a rule-of-reason defense. Failing to confirm designation status leads to applying the wrong framework entirely.

### Self-Preferencing Under The Banner Of Product Quality

Ranking your own products first is core to the DMA prohibition and a Section 2 theory. Labeling it as quality optimization does not remove the risk; the mechanism and effect must be examined.

### Combining Data Across Services Without Consent

Cross-service data combination without specific consent violates DMA rules for gatekeepers and creates privacy and competition exposure for all platforms. Consent under one regime does not automatically satisfy the other.

### Using Business Users' Data To Compete Against Them

Using a seller's or advertiser's non-public performance data to launch or favor the platform's own competing product is a specific DMA prohibition and a serious abuse theory.

### Restricting Multi-Homing Through Anti-Steering Or Parity

Anti-steering and parity clauses that prevent users from transacting elsewhere are prohibited for gatekeepers and heavily scrutinized for all platforms. They are a priority enforcement area.

### Treating App Store Rules As Purely Commercial

App store payment, sideloading, and default policies are now directly regulated. Policies designed for monetization may breach openness duties.

### Failing To Coordinate Antitrust And Privacy Compliance

Data rules sit at the intersection of competition and privacy law. Siloed compliance teams can approve a practice under one regime that violates the other.

## Self-Check

- Is the firm's status under the EU DMA (designated gatekeeper), national digital regulation, and US Section 2 confirmed before platform conduct is assessed?
- If designated, is each platform practice mapped to the specific DMA obligation it touches, rather than assessed under general good faith?
- Are ranking, display, default, and prominence policies reviewed for self-preferencing, with documented objective criteria and equal access for rivals?
- Are data combination practices controlled, with no cross-service combination of personal data without specific consent and no use of business users' non-public data to compete?
- Are app store policies reviewed for sideloading, third-party stores, alternative payment, anti-steering, and default/uninstall rights?
- Are interoperability duties identified and met on fair, reasonable, non-discriminatory terms where applicable?
- Are anti-steering, parity, and exclusivity clauses removed or reviewed for multi-homing restrictions?
- Is platform conduct assessed for network effects, data advantages, and gatekeeping power, not only market share?
- Are antitrust and privacy compliance coordinated on all data combination and use practices?
- Are platform policies that bundle payment, identification, or browser engine services reviewed against current openness requirements?
