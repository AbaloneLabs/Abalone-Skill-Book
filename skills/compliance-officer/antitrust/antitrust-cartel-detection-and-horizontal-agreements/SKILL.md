# Antitrust Cartel Detection And Horizontal Agreements

Cartel conduct is the most serious antitrust violation and the one most likely to carry criminal consequences. Under the US Sherman Act, the EU Treaty and competition regulation, national competition laws, and equivalent regimes, agreements among competitors to fix prices, rig bids, allocate markets, or limit output are treated as the most harmful restraints of competition, often subject to per se or object infringement treatment, criminal prosecution, and substantial fines. The central judgment problem is that cartel conduct almost never appears in formal contracts or official communications. It lives in informal contacts among competitors, at trade association meetings, in consultant-facilitated discussions, in side conversations at industry events, and in indirect signaling through public announcements. A compliance officer who monitors only formal records will never detect it, and the failure to detect, prevent, or report cartel conduct can expose the company and individual executives to criminal prosecution, imprisonment, and massive fines.

Use this skill before assessing cartel risk, designing controls for competitor contacts, evaluating horizontal agreement exposure, or advising on criminal antitrust and leniency. The goal is to make the agent think about where cartel conduct originates, how to detect informal coordination, how to control competitor contacts, and the decisions that are easy to make too casually. A compliance program that addresses cartel risk only through generic policy text, without reaching the settings and roles where coordination occurs, will not prevent or detect it.

This skill addresses jurisdiction-specific obligations. Cartel prohibitions, criminal liability, leniency programs, and penalty levels differ across national regimes. Always confirm the applicable national law and competition authority guidance, and consult qualified antitrust or legal counsel for specific cartel risk and leniency decisions.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Core Cartel Offenses

Cartel conduct encompasses a defined set of horizontal agreements among competitors that are treated as the most serious antitrust violations. The institution must understand what these offenses are and how they manifest.

Core cartel offenses:

- price-fixing, including agreements on prices, discounts, price increases, or pricing formulas;
- bid-rigging, including agreements on who will bid, at what price, or who will win a tender;
- market allocation, including agreements on territories, customers, or product lines;
- output limitation, including agreements to restrict production or capacity;
- group boycotts or collective refusals to deal with certain customers or suppliers.

These offenses are typically treated as per se illegal or by-object infringements, meaning no market power or competitive justification is needed to establish a violation. The agreement itself is the offense.

### Identify Where Cartel Conduct Originates

Cartel conduct does not appear in formal contracts. It originates in informal and indirect settings that the compliance program must reach.

Settings where cartel conduct originates:

- trade association meetings, where competitors gather and may discuss pricing, customers, or strategy;
- industry conferences and events, including social events and dinners around the formal program;
- benchmarking and survey engagements, where competitors share sensitive information through a consultant or aggregator;
- joint ventures and standard-setting activities, where legitimate cooperation can shade into coordination;
- customer or supplier-led discussions, where a buyer may facilitate information exchange among suppliers;
- online forums and messaging groups among industry participants;
- executive job transitions, where a former competitor employee brings knowledge of coordination.

The compliance program must map these settings for the company's industry and design controls that reach them. A program that monitors only internal emails and signed contracts will miss every one of these channels.

### Control Competitor Contacts

Because cartel conduct originates in competitor contacts, controlling those contacts is the primary preventive control. The institution must have clear rules and processes.

Competitor contact controls:

- prohibit discussions of prices, discounts, customers, territories, output, bidding strategy, or costs with competitors;
- require pre-clearance for attendance at trade association meetings and review of agendas;
- require a compliance chaperone or documented compliance check for high-risk meetings;
- require immediate disengagement and reporting if a competitor raises a prohibited topic;
- document the disengagement, including who was present, what was said, and how the employee responded;
- train high-risk employees on the disengagement and reporting sequence;
- track and periodically audit competitor contacts reported by employees.

The disengagement and reporting sequence is critical. If a competitor raises pricing at a dinner, the employee must leave the conversation, document what occurred, and report it to compliance immediately. Failing to disengage, or staying silent while the topic is discussed, can implicate the employee in the conduct.

### Detect Indirect Signaling And Information Exchange

Cartel conduct can be facilitated by indirect signaling, where competitors coordinate without explicit agreement through public announcements, price leadership, or information exchange.

Indirect coordination to detect:

- public announcements of future price changes that appear to invite parallel conduct;
- price leadership patterns where one competitor consistently moves first and others follow;
- information exchange through consultants, surveys, or trade associations that reveals individualized pricing or strategy data;
- parallel bidding patterns in tenders that suggest rotation or complementary bidding;
- sudden uniform changes in discounting, terms, or market coverage across competitors.

The compliance program should monitor for these patterns in the company's own communications and conduct. Investor relations, public announcements, and pricing communications should be reviewed for signaling risk.

### Assess Individual Criminal Exposure

Cartel conduct carries individual criminal exposure in many regimes, including imprisonment for executives who participate. The institution must understand this exposure.

Individual exposure considerations:

- the US Sherman Act and many national laws provide for criminal prosecution of individuals who participate in cartels;
- executives can face imprisonment, personal fines, and disqualification;
- the company cannot indemnify against criminal liability in some regimes;
- individual exposure means that executives have personal incentive to seek leniency, which can expose the company;
- the compliance program must make clear that cartel conduct has personal criminal consequences, not just corporate fines.

The personal exposure changes the dynamic. An executive who understands that a cartel conversation can lead to their own prosecution is more likely to disengage and report.

### Prepare For Leniency And Investigation Response

When cartel conduct is discovered or suspected, the speed of response is critical. Leniency programs reward the first company to report, and the window can be hours, not days.

Leniency and response readiness:

- understand the leniency programs in all jurisdictions where the company operates;
- have a tested internal investigation protocol that can be triggered immediately;
- have a dawn raid response plan, including document preservation and external counsel coordination;
- have escalation paths from compliance to counsel to leadership that operate in hours;
- understand that the first reporter typically receives the best leniency, and second or third reporters receive less or none;
- preserve documents immediately upon suspicion, as destruction can constitute obstruction.

A company that discovers cartel conduct and takes days to respond may lose the leniency window. The readiness must be built before the discovery, not assembled in the crisis.

### Align Incentives And Discipline With Prevention

A compliance program whose incentives contradict its cartel prohibitions will not work. Sales incentives that reward coordinated market stability, or high performers who attend trade associations without discipline for boundary violations, undermine the program.

Incentive and discipline alignment:

- review sales and pricing incentives for pressure that encourages coordination;
- ensure bonus metrics do not reward share or margin gains achieved through coordination;
- apply discipline consistently, including to high performers who violate competitor contact rules;
- make clear through tone from the top that cartel conduct has criminal consequences and will not be tolerated;
- protect employees who report concerns from retaliation.

## Common Traps

### Monitoring Only Formal Records

Cartel conduct lives in informal settings. A program that monitors only contracts and emails will miss it.

### No Disengagement And Reporting Sequence

If employees do not know how to respond when a competitor raises a prohibited topic, they may stay silent and become implicated.

### Trade Association Attendance Without Controls

Trade associations are a primary setting for cartel conduct. Attendance without pre-clearance, agenda review, and reporting is a gap.

### Ignoring Indirect Signaling

Public announcements and price leadership can facilitate coordination without explicit agreement. These channels must be monitored.

### Underestimating Individual Criminal Exposure

Cartel conduct carries personal criminal liability. Executives who do not understand this exposure may take risks they would otherwise avoid.

### No Leniency Readiness

The leniency window can be hours. A company without a tested response plan may lose the first-reporter advantage.

### Incentives That Reward Coordination

Sales incentives that reward market stability or margin gains without compliance gating push toward the very conduct the program prohibits.

## Self-Check

- Are the core cartel offenses, including price-fixing, bid-rigging, market allocation, and output limitation, understood and addressed in the compliance program?
- Are the settings where cartel conduct originates, including trade associations, conferences, benchmarking, and consultant engagements, mapped and controlled?
- Are competitor contacts controlled through prohibitions, pre-clearance, agenda review, disengagement, and reporting?
- Is indirect signaling and information exchange monitored in public announcements, pricing communications, and investor relations?
- Is individual criminal exposure understood and communicated, so that executives recognize the personal consequences of cartel conduct?
- Is the company ready for leniency and investigation response, with tested protocols, dawn raid plans, and fast escalation paths?
- Are sales incentives, bonus metrics, and discipline aligned with cartel prevention, including consistent discipline for high performers?
- Are trade association attendance, agendas, and incident reports tracked and reviewed for prohibited-topic exposure?
- Is the cartel risk assessment and control design confirmed against the applicable national law and competition authority guidance rather than a generic standard?
- Are employees trained on the disengagement and reporting sequence using real scenarios rather than abstract legal definitions?
