# Antitrust Vertical Restraints And Distribution Agreements

Vertical restraints are conditions imposed in agreements between firms at different levels of the supply chain, such as a manufacturer and its distributors, that can either promote efficiency or harm competition. Under the EU Treaty and Vertical Block Exemption Regulation, the US Sherman Act and Clayton Act, and equivalent national competition laws, resale price maintenance, territorial and customer restrictions, exclusivity requirements, most-favored-nation clauses, and tying in distribution arrangements must be assessed for their competitive effects. The central judgment problem is that vertical restraints are not per se illegal in most regimes, unlike horizontal cartels, but they can still harm competition when used by firms with market power or when they facilitate collusion. The analysis depends on market position, the type and intensity of the restraint, and whether the restraint produces efficiencies that benefit consumers. A compliance officer who treats all distribution terms as ordinary commercial provisions, without assessing their competitive effects, can embed antitrust risk in standard contracts.

Use this skill before reviewing distribution agreements, assessing vertical restraints, evaluating MFN clauses, or advising on supplier-distributor arrangements. The goal is to make the agent think about the type of restraint, the market position of the parties, the competitive effects, and the decisions that are easy to make too casually. A distribution agreement that contains standard but problematic restraints, deployed by a firm with market power, creates exposure that template review will not catch.

This skill addresses jurisdiction-specific obligations. Vertical restraints rules, block exemptions, safe harbors, and per se treatment differ across the EU, the US, and national regimes. Always confirm the applicable national law and competition authority guidance, and consult qualified antitrust or legal counsel for specific vertical restraints assessments.

## Core Rules

### Classify The Type Of Vertical Restraint

Vertical restraints come in different forms, each with different risk profiles. The first step is classifying the restraint correctly.

Restraint types:

- resale price maintenance (RPM), where the supplier sets the resale price or minimum price, or coerces the distributor through pressure or incentives;
- maximum resale price, generally lower risk but potentially problematic if it effectively caps prices;
- territorial and customer restrictions, where the distributor is limited to certain territories or customers;
- exclusive distribution and purchasing, where one party agrees to deal only with the other;
- selective distribution, where the supplier restricts resale to authorized dealers meeting quality criteria;
- most-favored-nation or MFN clauses, where one party guarantees the best terms it offers anyone;
- tying and bundling in distribution, where purchase of one product is conditioned on another.

Each type has a different analytical framework. RPM and certain hard-core restrictions receive harsher treatment, while others are assessed under a rule of reason or exempted under defined conditions.

### Assess Resale Price Maintenance Risk

RPM is one of the most scrutinized vertical restraints. While US law treats minimum RPM under a rule of reason following Leegin, many regimes, including the EU, treat certain RPM as a restriction by object with limited justification.

RPM risk factors:

- direct minimum price agreements between supplier and distributor;
- maximum or suggested prices that function as minimums through monitoring and pressure;
- agreements on price components, such as discounts, margins, or surcharges;
- coercion through threats of supply termination, reduced allocation, or withheld benefits for non-compliance with suggested prices;
- RPM achieved through indirect means, such as loyalty rebates conditioned on price adherence.

RPM is not limited to explicit price agreements. A supplier that monitors distributor pricing and penalizes discounting is engaging in RPM even without a written price term. The compliance program must look at conduct, not just contract language.

### Evaluate Territorial And Customer Restrictions

Territorial and customer restrictions allocate where and to whom a distributor can sell. Their treatment depends on whether they are exclusive, selective, or absolute.

Restriction assessment:

- absolute territorial protection, where the distributor cannot sell into other territories, is typically the most problematic;
- restrictions on active selling into other territories are generally more acceptable than restrictions on passive sales;
- restrictions on customer categories may be acceptable for legitimate allocation but problematic if they facilitate market allocation;
- restrictions that prevent cross-supply between authorized distributors within a selective distribution network are scrutinized;
- online sales restrictions are a particular focus, as preventing internet sales raises concerns.

The distinction between active and passive sales is important. Restrictions on active selling, such as targeted advertising in another territory, are more defensible than restrictions on passive sales, such as fulfilling unsolicited orders from another territory.

### Assess Exclusivity And Selective Distribution

Exclusive and selective distribution systems can be pro-competitive when they improve service and quality, but they can harm competition when they foreclose competitors or facilitate collusion.

Exclusivity and selective distribution assessment:

- single branding and exclusive purchasing obligations, where the distributor agrees to buy only from the supplier;
- exclusive distribution, where the supplier grants territorial exclusivity to one distributor;
- selective distribution, where resale is restricted to authorized dealers meeting qualitative criteria;
- the market share of the parties, as exclusivity by a firm with market power carries greater foreclosure risk;
- the cumulative effect of parallel selective distribution networks that collectively foreclose the market.

The competitive effects depend on market position and the intensity of the exclusivity. A 20 percent supplier using selective distribution to ensure service quality is different from an 80 percent supplier using exclusivity to foreclose competitors.

### Evaluate Most-Favored-Nation And MFN Clauses

MFN clauses, where one party guarantees the best terms it offers any counterparty, have drawn increased antitrust scrutiny, particularly in platform and insurance markets.

MFN risk factors:

- wide MFNs that apply across all products and channels, not just comparable transactions;
- MFNs that facilitate information exchange and price coordination among suppliers or distributors;
- MFNs that create barriers to entry by preventing new entrants from offering better terms;
- MFNs used by dominant platforms or suppliers to prevent competitors from gaining price advantages;
- the market coverage of MFNs, as widespread use can approximate the effect of price-fixing.

MFNs are not per se illegal, but their competitive effects must be assessed, particularly when used by firms with market power or when widespread across a market.

### Apply Market Share Thresholds And Safe Harbors

Many regimes provide safe harbors or block exemptions for vertical restraints when the parties' market shares are below defined thresholds.

Safe harbor considerations:

- the EU Vertical Block Exemption provides a safe harbor when both supplier and buyer have market share below 30 percent;
- US antitrust does not have formal market share safe harbors for vertical restraints but considers market power in the rule of reason;
- safe harbors typically exclude hardcore restrictions such as certain RPM and absolute territorial protection;
- market share must be assessed in the relevant market, and exceeding the threshold removes the safe harbor;
- even within safe harbors, certain restraints may be excluded or require individual assessment.

The compliance program must assess market share for each restraint and confirm whether a safe harbor applies. Assuming a safe harbor without market share analysis is not defensible.

### Document The Efficiency Justification

Vertical restraints are more defensible when supported by efficiency justifications. The compliance program should ensure that restraints are documented with their pro-competitive rationale.

Justification documentation:

- document the efficiency rationale, such as preventing free-riding, ensuring service quality, or incentivizing investment;
- assess whether the restraint is necessary to achieve the efficiency or whether less restrictive alternatives exist;
- ensure the justification is genuine and not a pretext for foreclosure;
- avoid restraints that go beyond what is necessary to achieve the stated efficiency;
- retain the analysis for potential competition authority review.

A restraint without documented efficiency justification is harder to defend. The documentation should show that the restraint was chosen for legitimate reasons and is proportionate to the objective.

## Common Traps

### Treating Distribution Terms As Ordinary Commercial Provisions

Vertical restraints have competitive effects that must be assessed. Treating them as standard contract terms embeds antitrust risk.

### RPM Through Indirect Means

RPM achieved through monitoring, pressure, or incentives is still RPM. The compliance program must look at conduct, not just contract language.

### Absolute Territorial Protection

Absolute territorial restrictions, including on passive sales, are typically the most problematic and often outside safe harbors.

### MFNs Without Competitive Assessment

MFNs, particularly wide MFNs by dominant firms, have drawn enforcement focus. They must be assessed for foreclosure and coordination effects.

### Assuming Safe Harbor Without Market Share Analysis

Safe harbors depend on market share thresholds. Assuming a safe harbor without assessing share in the relevant market is not defensible.

### No Efficiency Justification For Restraints

A restraint without documented efficiency justification is harder to defend. The rationale should exist at the time of the decision.

### Exclusivity By Dominant Firms Without Foreclosure Analysis

Exclusivity by a firm with market power carries greater foreclosure risk. Market position must factor into the assessment.

## Self-Check

- Is each vertical restraint classified correctly, distinguishing RPM, territorial and customer restrictions, exclusivity, selective distribution, MFNs, and tying?
- Is RPM assessed for both explicit agreements and indirect means, including monitoring, pressure, and incentive-based coercion?
- Are territorial and customer restrictions assessed for the distinction between active and passive sales and the acceptability of each?
- Are exclusivity and selective distribution assessed for foreclosure effects, particularly based on the market share of the parties?
- Are MFN clauses assessed for width, foreclosure, and coordination effects, particularly for dominant platforms or suppliers?
- Are market share thresholds and safe harbors applied based on actual market share analysis in the relevant market?
- Is each restraint documented with a genuine efficiency justification and an assessment of less restrictive alternatives?
- Are distribution agreements and templates periodically audited for RPM, MFN, territorial, and exclusivity terms?
- Are restraints involving firms with market power subjected to heightened foreclosure analysis?
- Is the vertical restraints assessment confirmed against the applicable national law, block exemptions, and competition authority guidance rather than a generic standard?
