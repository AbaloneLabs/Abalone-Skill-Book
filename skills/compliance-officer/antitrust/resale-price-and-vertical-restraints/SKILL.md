# Resale Price And Vertical Restraints

Vertical restraints, agreements between a supplier and its reseller rather than between competitors, are generally less dangerous than cartels but are still a major antitrust exposure. The trap is that many restraints look like ordinary commercial contract terms: a minimum resale price, a most-favored-nation clause, an exclusive territory, a non-compete on a distributor. Some of these are lawful in some jurisdictions and per se or by-object unlawful in others, and the US and EU diverge sharply on resale price maintenance. A compliance officer approving distribution contracts must know which restraints are high-risk, how the jurisdictions differ, and when counsel review is mandatory rather than optional.

Use this skill before approving resale price terms, MFN clauses, exclusive dealing, territorial or customer restrictions, or any distribution contract that limits a reseller's freedom. The goal is to make the agent classify the restraint, apply the correct jurisdictional treatment, and identify when a term that looks commercial is actually an antitrust problem.

## Core Rules

### Classify The Restraint Before Assessing Risk

Vertical restraints fall into categories with different risk profiles. The first step is always to identify what the restraint actually does.

The main categories are:

- resale price maintenance (RPM), including minimum resale price, maximum resale price, and recommended price;
- territorial and customer restrictions, including exclusive territories, allocated customer groups, and restrictions on active or passive sales;
- exclusive dealing and requirements contracts, where the reseller agrees to buy mainly or only from the supplier;
- most-favored-nation (MFN) or parity clauses, guaranteeing the supplier the best terms the reseller offers anyone;
- selective distribution, where the supplier limits resale to authorized dealers meeting qualitative or quantitative criteria;
- tying and bundling, where buying one product is conditioned on buying another.

The risk depends on the category, the market position of the supplier, the coverage of the restraint, and the jurisdiction. A restraint that is defensible for a small supplier can be unlawful for a dominant one.

### Apply The Correct RPM Treatment By Jurisdiction

Resale price maintenance is the area where US and EU law diverge most sharply, and where a single contract can be lawful in one place and unlawful in another.

Know the divergence:

- in the US, after Leegin (2007), minimum RPM is judged under the rule of reason, not per se unlawful, but agreements that coerce a reseller's pricing can still be challenged, and maximum resale prices are also rule-of-reason under Khan (1997);
- in the EU, minimum RPM and fixed resale prices are generally a by-object restriction under TFEU Article 101, with very narrow exceptions, and maximum resale prices are also presumptively restrictive;
- in the EU, recommended or maximum resale prices can be acceptable under the VBER if they do not amount to a fixed or minimum price under pressure, coercion, or incentives;
- many US states still treat RPM more harshly than federal law, and several have per se prohibitions or strong presumptions;
- national regimes outside the EU and US vary widely, with some treating RPM as a hard-core restriction.

The safest global posture is to avoid minimum or fixed resale prices entirely and to use non-binding recommended prices with no coercion, monitoring, or penalties for deviation. If RPM is contemplated anywhere, counsel review is mandatory.

### Scrutinize MFN And Parity Clauses

Most-favored-nation and parity clauses, promising the supplier the best price or terms the reseller offers any supplier, are increasingly scrutinized, especially in digital and platform markets.

Risk factors for MFNs:

- broad MFNs that apply across products or platforms, not just to the specific contract;
- MFNs held by a supplier or platform with market power, which can soften competition and raise rivals' costs;
- retail MFNs (platform parity clauses) that prevent a hotel, seller, or provider from offering better terms elsewhere;
- MFNs that function as price coordination across a market.

EU enforcement and several national authorities have challenged wide parity clauses in online travel, e-commerce, and platform markets. In the US, MFNs have been challenged under both Sherman Act Section 1 and, where a dominant firm is involved, Section 2. Narrow MFNs tied to a specific procurement may be defensible; broad MFNs held by powerful firms are high-risk.

### Evaluate Territorial And Customer Restrictions

Restrictions on where or to whom a reseller can sell are common in distribution but are treated differently across jurisdictions and depend on the type of restriction.

Key distinctions:

- exclusive territories (one distributor per area) are generally higher-risk than exclusive purchasing;
- restrictions on active sales into another distributor's exclusive territory are more defensible than restrictions on passive sales (unsolicited requests from customers in another territory);
- absolute territorial protection, blocking even passive sales, is typically a by-object restriction in the EU;
- customer restrictions that allocate customer groups can be lawful if tied to legitimate wholesale/retail separation but are high-risk if they segment the market to reduce competition;
- under the EU VBER, the treatment depends on market share thresholds and the specific combination of restraints.

In the US, vertical territorial and customer allocations are generally judged under the rule of reason but remain scrutinized, especially where the supplier has market power. Passive sales restrictions are a persistent red flag in the EU.

### Assess Exclusive Dealing And Market Coverage

Exclusive dealing, where a reseller agrees to stock mainly or only the supplier's products, is lawful in many cases but becomes problematic when it forecloses competitors.

Assess:

- the supplier's market share and market power;
- the proportion of the market covered by exclusive deals;
- the duration of the exclusivity;
- whether rivals can realistically reach customers through other channels;
- whether the exclusivity is de facto (through discounts and rebates) rather than contractual.

High market share, broad coverage, long duration, and lack of alternative outlets create foreclosure risk. In the EU, exclusive supply obligations above VBER thresholds can fall outside the safe harbor. In the US, exclusive dealing is challenged under Section 1 or Section 2 when it forecloses a substantial share of the market.

### Require Counsel Review For High-Risk Combinations

Some restraints and combinations should never be approved without antitrust counsel. The compliance officer's role is to route these, not to clear them.

Mandatory counsel review for:

- any minimum or fixed resale price, in any jurisdiction;
- maximum resale prices with monitoring or penalties;
- broad MFN or parity clauses, especially for firms with market power;
- absolute territorial protection or passive sales restrictions;
- exclusive dealing with high market share or broad coverage;
- tying by a firm with market power;
- any restraint imposed by a firm that may be dominant in the relevant market.

The compliance officer should flag these in contract templates and in the approval workflow so they cannot be signed without legal review.

## Common Traps

### Treating RPM As A Standard Commercial Term

Minimum resale prices look like ordinary pricing clauses but are among the most scrutinized vertical restraints globally. Treating them as routine is a serious error, especially in the EU where they are typically by-object restrictions.

### Assuming Leegin Made RPM Safe In The US

Leegin moved minimum RPM from per se to rule-of-reason under federal law, but it did not make it safe. Several states retain harsher treatment, and even under rule-of-reason, RPM by a firm with market power can be challenged.

### Using Recommended Prices With Coercion

A non-binding recommended price is generally lower-risk, but if the supplier monitors compliance, penalizes deviation, or withholds supply from discounters, the recommendation becomes de facto RPM and is treated as a fixed or minimum price.

### Ignoring Passive Sales Restrictions

Blocking a distributor from responding to unsolicited customer requests from another territory is a persistent EU red flag. Active sales restrictions may be defensible; passive sales restrictions generally are not.

### Overlooking De Facto Exclusive Dealing

Loyalty rebates and targeted discounts that achieve exclusivity in practice carry the same foreclosure risk as contractual exclusive dealing, and may be harder to defend because they are less transparent.

### Applying Broad MFNs Without Market Power Analysis

MFNs are not automatically safe for small firms, but they become serious risks when held by firms with market power. The market position must be assessed before approving an MFN.

### Using One Global Contract Template Without Local Review

A template that is defensible in one jurisdiction may be unlawful in another. RPM, territorial, and MFN terms need local antitrust review, especially across US and EU markets.

## Self-Check

- Is each vertical restraint classified (RPM, territorial, customer, exclusive dealing, MFN, selective distribution, tying) before risk is assessed?
- Is the US-EU divergence on RPM understood, including Leegin rule-of-reason, EU by-object treatment, state-level harshness, and the VBER treatment of recommended prices?
- Are minimum or fixed resale prices routed to mandatory counsel review, with a default global posture of avoiding them?
- Are recommended prices checked for coercion, monitoring, or penalties that would convert them to de facto RPM?
- Are MFN and parity clauses assessed for breadth, market power, and platform-parity risk, rather than treated as routine boilerplate?
- Are territorial and customer restrictions distinguished by active versus passive sales, with passive sales restrictions flagged as high-risk in the EU?
- Is exclusive dealing assessed for market share, coverage, duration, and foreclosure of rival access to customers?
- Is de facto exclusivity through rebates and discounts treated with the same scrutiny as contractual exclusivity?
- Are high-risk restraints (RPM, broad MFNs, absolute territorial protection, tying by dominant firms) routed to mandatory counsel review in contract templates and approval workflows?
- Are global distribution templates subjected to local antitrust review rather than assumed to be universally safe?
