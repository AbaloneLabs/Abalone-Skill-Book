# Competitor Information Exchange And Signaling

Not every conversation between competitors is a cartel, but the line between lawful benchmarking and unlawful information exchange is narrow and easy to cross. Exchanging competitively sensitive information, even without an explicit agreement on price, can itself violate Sherman Act Section 1 and EU TFEU Article 101, because it enables coordination and removes independent competitive pressure. The danger for compliance officers is that information exchange often looks legitimate: a benchmarking study, an industry survey, a consultant's report, a customer request, or a public price announcement. The risk is hidden in what data is shared, how recent it is, how granular it is, and whether it concerns future rather than past conduct.

Use this skill before approving any benchmarking exercise, industry survey, consultant engagement, trade association data submission, or public pricing communication that involves competitor information. The goal is to make the agent distinguish legitimate exchange from unlawful coordination, identify the red flags in data freshness and granularity, and apply the structural safeguards that make an exchange defensible.

## Core Rules

### Identify What Counts As Competitively Sensitive Information

The first step is knowing which information, if exchanged, creates exposure. Not all data is dangerous, but the categories below are treated as high-risk because they reduce strategic uncertainty between competitors.

Competitively sensitive information includes:

- current or future prices, discounts, rebates, surcharges, or pricing strategy;
- individual company costs, margins, or profitability;
- output, production, capacity, or inventory levels, especially future plans;
- individual customer identities, customer allocations, or contract terms;
- bid or tender intentions, including whether the company will bid and at what level;
- marketing, launch, or expansion plans;
- R&D direction or technology roadmaps that affect competitive positioning.

The risk is highest for future-oriented and individualized data. Aggregated, historical, and sufficiently anonymized data is generally lower risk, but only if the aggregation genuinely prevents identification of any single contributor.

### Apply The Structural Safeguards For Lawful Exchange

Information exchange is not automatically unlawful, but lawful exchange usually requires structural protections. A compliance officer should require these before approving any benchmarking or survey.

The standard safeguards are:

- use of a genuinely independent third party (consultant, trade association secretariat) to collect and aggregate the data;
- aggregation across enough companies so no individual contributor's data can be reverse-engineered;
- historical data only, typically a defined lag such as several months or a prior accounting period, never current or future data;
- no individualized data points shared back to participants, only aggregate statistics;
- clear scope, definitions, and methodology agreed in advance;
- no discussion of the data's implications for pricing or strategy among participants;
- written terms of reference reviewed by antitrust counsel.

If any safeguard is missing, the exchange should not be approved. A survey that shares current individual price data, even through a consultant, is high-risk.

### Distinguish Legitimate Benchmarking From Unlawful Exchange

Legitimate benchmarking exists and is common, but the distinction depends on purpose, content, and structure.

Lawful benchmarking typically:

- compares non-pricing metrics such as safety incidents, employee turnover, energy efficiency, or process cycle times;
- uses aggregated, historical data;
- serves a genuine efficiency purpose such as operational improvement;
- does not identify individual contributors;
- is not used to coordinate commercial conduct.

Unlawful exchange typically:

- focuses on price, cost, margin, output, or customer allocation;
- uses current or future data;
- identifies individual contributors;
- is used, directly or indirectly, to align commercial behavior;
- lacks structural safeguards.

The purpose matters. A benchmarking exercise that is nominally operational but whose results are used to justify a coordinated price increase is unlawful regardless of its label.

### Scrutinize Third-Party Surveys And Consultants

Using a consultant or trade association does not create a safe harbor. The intermediary must be genuinely independent and the safeguards must be real.

Check that:

- the consultant is not effectively a conduit for individualized data between participants;
- the consultant does not share raw, individualized responses with participants;
- the number of participants is large enough that aggregation is meaningful;
- the data definitions prevent easy identification;
- the consultant's methodology and output have been reviewed by counsel;
- participants cannot use the survey to infer competitors' future conduct.

A consultant who simply collects price data and distributes an average is not providing protection. The aggregation must be robust enough that no participant can reconstruct another's position.

### Manage Public Announcements And Signaling

Public announcements are not automatically safe. Announcing your own prices can be lawful, but announcements can become unlawful signaling depending on content, timing, and context.

Risk factors include:

- announcing future price increases well in advance of effectiveness;
- announcements timed to coincide with or follow a competitor's announcement;
- disclosures of future pricing strategy in investor calls, press releases, or customer letters that go beyond what customers need;
- communications that invite or confirm competitor response;
- use of industry forums to telegraph intent.

The safer practice is to announce prices that are effective immediately or near-term, addressed to customers, without commentary on competitor behavior or industry coordination. Future-oriented strategic pricing disclosures should be reviewed by counsel.

### Recognize Indirect Exchange Channels

Information can be exchanged indirectly, and indirect exchange carries the same risk as direct exchange.

Indirect channels include:

- customers who share competitor pricing to solicit a better offer;
- common distributors or agents who carry information between suppliers;
- trade associations where informal conversations occur outside formal sessions;
- online forums, messaging groups, or shared spreadsheets;
- employee movement, where a new hire brings a competitor's pricing data.

The company is generally responsible for receiving and using competitively sensitive information obtained through these channels. A policy should require employees to refuse, document, and report unsolicited competitor information.

## Common Traps

### Assuming Aggregation Alone Is Sufficient

An average of three companies' current prices is not meaningfully aggregated. If participants can identify each other's contribution, the aggregation provides no protection.

### Sharing Current Or Future Data

The single most dangerous trap is exchanging current or forward-looking price, output, or customer data. Historical data with a meaningful lag is dramatically lower risk.

### Treating A Consultant As A Shield

A consultant who passes individualized data between participants is not a safeguard. The protection comes from the methodology and output, not the intermediary's involvement.

### Ignoring Informal Conversations

The formal agenda of a trade association meeting may be clean, but the dinner afterward is where information is exchanged. Compliance must address social and informal settings, not only the meeting room.

### Using Benchmarking Results To Justify Coordination

Even a lawful benchmarking exercise becomes unlawful if its results are used to align prices or strategy. The use of the information matters as much as the exchange itself.

### Receiving Unsolicited Information Without Acting

If a customer or distributor volunteers a competitor's pricing and the employee uses it, the company can be exposed. Receiving competitively sensitive information requires a refusal, documentation, and report.

### Disclosing Too Much In Investor Communications

Forward-looking pricing or strategy disclosures in earnings calls can be treated as signaling. Investor relations content touching on pricing strategy needs antitrust review.

## Self-Check

- Are price, cost, margin, output, capacity, customer allocation, bid intent, and future strategy identified as competitively sensitive information that should not be exchanged?
- Is future-oriented and individualized data treated as the highest-risk category, with historical and aggregated data as lower risk?
- Do approved benchmarking and survey exercises require an independent third party, robust aggregation, historical data with a meaningful lag, and counsel-reviewed terms of reference?
- Is the distinction between legitimate operational benchmarking and unlawful commercial exchange clear, including the use to which results are put?
- Are third-party surveys and consultants assessed for whether aggregation genuinely prevents identification, rather than treated as an automatic safe harbor?
- Are public price announcements reviewed for signaling risk, including advance notice of future increases and strategic disclosures in investor communications?
- Are indirect channels (customers, distributors, trade association social settings, online forums, new hires) covered by rules requiring refusal, documentation, and reporting of unsolicited competitor information?
- Can participants in any approved data exchange genuinely not reconstruct another contributor's individual data?
- Is the use of benchmarking results monitored to ensure they are not used to justify coordinated commercial conduct?
- Are employee training and contact rules explicit that receiving, not only sending, competitively sensitive information creates exposure?
