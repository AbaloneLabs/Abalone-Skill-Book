# Market Power And Dominance Assessment

Almost every unilateral-conduct question depends on one threshold judgment: does the firm have market power? The answer changes everything. A firm without market power can generally refuse to deal, set prices, bundle products, and choose its trading partners as it sees fit. A firm with monopoly power in the US or a dominant position in the EU is subject to a special responsibility not to distort competition and can be liable for conduct that would be lawful for a smaller competitor. The compliance officer's challenge is that market definition and dominance are not mechanical: they depend on substitutability, supply-side constraints, buyer power, innovation, and the specific product and geographic market. Getting this wrong, either by over-claiming safety or by missing dominance, is the root error behind most failed unilateral-conduct advice.

Use this skill before advising on any single-firm conduct (pricing, refusal to deal, tying, exclusivity, self-preferencing), before a merger that may create or strengthen dominance, or when reviewing whether a business unit is subject to special obligations. The goal is to make the agent define the relevant market rigorously, estimate share with appropriate caution, and recognize when dominance-level scrutiny is triggered.

## Core Rules

### Define The Relevant Market Before Claiming A Share

A market share number is meaningless without a defined market, and the definition is where the analysis is won or lost. The relevant market has product and geographic dimensions, and sometimes a temporal dimension.

Define the product market by asking:

- what products or services are substitutable for the firm's offering on the demand side, such that customers would switch if prices rose?
- are there supply-side substitutes, firms that could begin producing the product quickly if prices rose?
- how narrowly or broadly do customers actually choose, and what are the next-best alternatives?
- do the products compete on functionality, price, or both?

Define the geographic market by asking:

- over what area do customers realistically source the product?
- do language, regulation, transport cost, brand, or network effects limit switching across regions or countries?
- is the market national, regional, global, or local?

A firm can look small in a broad market and dominant in a narrow one. The definition must reflect actual customer switching behavior, not the firm's preferred framing.

### Apply The SSNIP And Substitution Logic

The standard tool for market definition is the small but significant non-transitory increase in price (SSNIP) test, and the compliance officer should understand its logic even if economists run the formal analysis.

The logic:

- start with the firm's product and ask whether a hypothetical monopolist could profitably impose a small (commonly 5 to 10 percent), non-transitory price increase;
- if customers would switch to substitutes in such numbers that the increase would be unprofitable, the substitute is in the market and the market expands;
- repeat until the hypothetical monopolist's price increase would be profitable; that set of products is the relevant market;
- supply-side substitution and entry are considered, often at a later stage of the analysis.

The SSNIP test is conceptual, not a formula a compliance officer computes, but understanding it prevents the common error of defining the market by the firm's product category rather than by customer substitution. If customers would readily switch to a different category on a price increase, that category is in the market.

### Estimate Market Share With Appropriate Caution

Market share is a starting point, not a conclusion. It depends entirely on the market definition, the data source, and the metric used.

Be cautious about:

- the metric: value share, volume share, unit share, capacity share, or share of bids won can give very different numbers;
- the data source: internal estimates, competitor estimates, industry surveys, and regulator data can diverge widely;
- the reference period: a single year can mislead in volatile or seasonal markets;
- dynamic markets: share in fast-moving technology markets can change rapidly and may understate or overstate durable power;
- the denominator: defining the total market size is as error-prone as estimating the firm's sales.

Never present a market share as a precise fact. Present it as a range with stated assumptions, and always ask how the share changes under alternative market definitions.

### Recognize Dominance Thresholds And Their Limits

There is no fixed market share that automatically confers dominance, but there are useful reference points that the compliance officer should know.

Reference points:

- in the EU, shares below 40 percent rarely support a finding of dominance on their own, shares at or above 40 percent often do, and shares above 50 percent create a strong presumption that may require rebuttal;
- in the US, monopoly power typically requires a very high share (often 70 percent or above) plus barriers to entry, and the courts look for durable power, not a transient spike;
- these are starting points, not rules: a firm with 35 percent can be dominant if rivals are tiny and entry is hard, and a firm with 60 percent may not be if buyers are powerful and entry is easy;
- dominance can exist in markets smaller than the firm's overall business, such as a single product line or a specific customer segment.

The compliance officer should treat any share at or above the EU reference zone (roughly 40 percent and up) as triggering dominance-level review for EU-facing conduct, and any share suggesting monopoly power (very high, with barriers) as triggering Section 2 review for US conduct.

### Look Beyond Share To The Factors That Make Power Durable

Market share is a snapshot. Dominance and monopoly power require durability, and that depends on factors share alone does not capture.

Assess:

- barriers to entry and expansion, including scale economies, network effects, switching costs, regulation, IP, and access to inputs or distribution;
- countervailing buyer power, whether large customers can constrain the firm;
- the strength and number of competitors;
- innovation and the pace of technological change;
- the firm's control of essential inputs, infrastructure, or data;
- the firm's ability to act independently of competitors, customers, and suppliers.

A high share in a market with easy entry and powerful buyers may not be dominance. A moderate share in a market with high barriers and weak rivals may be. The durability analysis is what separates a competitive position from a dominant one.

### Treat Dominance As Triggering Special Obligations

Once dominance is established or plausible, the firm is not free to do what smaller firms do. The compliance officer must shift posture.

Dominance triggers:

- a special responsibility not to impair genuine undistorted competition (EU framing);
- scrutiny of pricing, rebates, tying, refusals to deal, and exclusivity that would be lawful for a non-dominant firm;
- potential essential-facilities or duty-to-deal arguments in narrow circumstances;
- heightened merger and acquisition review;
- in digital markets, possible gatekeeper obligations under regimes such as the EU DMA.

The practical rule: if dominance is plausible, counsel review of unilateral conduct becomes mandatory, not optional.

## Common Traps

### Defining The Market To Suit The Desired Conclusion

The most common error is drawing the market boundary to make the firm look small. A broad, convenient market definition that ignores actual customer substitution produces a falsely low share and false safety.

### Treating Market Share As The Whole Analysis

Share is a starting point. Dominance depends on durability, barriers, buyer power, and the firm's ability to act independently. A share without these factors is incomplete.

### Ignoring Narrow Or Aftermarkets

Dominance can exist in a narrow product, a spare-parts market, an aftermarket for services, or a specific customer segment. Assessing only the firm's overall business misses dominance in the market that matters for the conduct at issue.

### Overstating Buyer Power

Large buyers do constrain sellers, but buyer power that is fragmented, transactional, or unable to sponsor entry does not defeat dominance. Do not assume buyer power without testing whether it actually constrains the firm.

### Underestimating Network Effects And Data Advantages

In digital and platform markets, network effects, data accumulation, and switching costs can make dominance durable even with a modest-looking share and apparent alternatives. Traditional share analysis can understate power in these markets.

### Confusing US And EU Thresholds

US monopoly power requires a very high share plus barriers; EU dominance can be found at lower shares. Applying US thresholds to EU conduct, or vice versa, leads to wrong conclusions about whether special obligations apply.

### Relying On Stale Share Data

Markets move. A share from three years ago may not reflect current conditions, especially in technology, regulated, or recently disrupted markets. Update the assessment before relying on it.

## Self-Check

- Is the relevant product market defined by actual demand-side and supply-side substitutability, not by the firm's product category or preferred framing?
- Is the relevant geographic market defined by where customers actually source, accounting for regulation, language, transport, brand, and network effects?
- Is the SSNIP logic understood and applied conceptually, such that substitutes customers would switch to on a price increase are included in the market?
- Are market shares presented as ranges with stated metric, data source, reference period, and assumptions, rather than as precise facts?
- Are EU dominance reference points (roughly 40 percent and up) and US monopoly power requirements (very high share plus barriers) understood and applied to the right jurisdiction?
- Are barriers to entry, network effects, switching costs, IP, data advantages, and control of inputs assessed alongside share?
- Is countervailing buyer power tested for whether it actually constrains the firm, rather than assumed?
- Are narrow markets, aftermarkets, and specific product or customer segments assessed, not only the firm's overall business?
- Is digital and platform market power assessed for durability from network effects and data, even where share looks moderate?
- Does plausible dominance trigger mandatory counsel review of pricing, rebates, tying, refusals, exclusivity, mergers, and any gatekeeper obligations?
