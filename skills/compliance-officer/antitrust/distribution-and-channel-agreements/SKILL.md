# Distribution And Channel Agreements

Distribution design has become one of the most active antitrust fronts because of e-commerce and platform markets. The questions that used to be routine, whether a brand can stop a distributor selling on Amazon, whether selective distribution can exclude discounters, whether a platform can demand parity, are now the subject of enforcement, legislation, and litigation in parallel. A compliance officer reviewing channel agreements must understand that the same restraint can be a legitimate anti-free-riding tool or an anticompetitive foreclosure device, and that the answer depends on market position, the type of restriction, and whether it is qualitative or quantitative. The risk is that commercial teams treat channel rules as brand-protection decisions when they are actually antitrust decisions.

Use this skill before approving selective distribution criteria, marketplace bans, platform parity terms, exclusive distribution territories, or e-commerce restrictions. The goal is to make the agent distinguish legitimate channel management from anticompetitive foreclosure, apply the qualitative-versus-quantitative distinction, and recognize where digital market rules and platform regulation have changed the analysis.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Selective Distribution From Exclusive Distribution

Selective distribution and exclusive distribution are different systems with different risk profiles, and confusing them leads to wrong conclusions.

The distinction:

- selective distribution limits the number of authorized resellers based on criteria, but authorized resellers can sell to any end customer, including online;
- exclusive distribution grants a distributor exclusivity in a territory or for a customer group, typically with protections against active sales by other distributors;
- selective distribution is generally more defensible when the criteria are qualitative (objective, uniform, necessary to preserve brand quality) rather than quantitative (capped numbers of dealers);
- exclusive distribution carries higher territorial-foreclosure risk and triggers active versus passive sales analysis.

A common error is to impose selective distribution criteria that are actually quantitative caps dressed as quality standards. The compliance officer must test whether the criteria are genuinely about product integrity or about limiting the number and type of outlets.

### Apply The Qualitative Versus Quantitative Test

In the EU, selective distribution is most defensible when criteria are qualitative, and the VBER sets out when quantitative criteria or combinations of restraints fall outside the safe harbor.

Test the criteria:

- are the criteria objective, applied uniformly to all applicants, and necessary to preserve the product's character or ensure proper distribution?
- are the criteria not more restrictive than necessary for the legitimate purpose?
- do the criteria exclude discounters or online sellers purely on the basis of price or format, which signals a quantitative or foreclosure purpose?
- do the criteria require investments or certifications that are proportionate, or are they structured to keep out certain channel types?

Qualitative criteria tied to showroom standards, staff training, installation capability, or after-sales service are more defensible. Criteria that effectively ban discounters, online-only sellers, or certain platforms look like market segmentation and are higher-risk, especially for brands with market power.

### Manage Online Marketplace And Platform Restrictions

Banning distributors from selling on specific marketplaces (such as a general online marketplace) is one of the most contested channel issues. The treatment has shifted significantly.

Know the current landscape:

- in the EU, the revised VBER and Court of Justice guidance allow suppliers to prohibit the use of a marketplace in a selective distribution system only under specific conditions, and absolute marketplace bans are heavily restricted;
- quality requirements for online sales (interface standards, display rules) are generally permissible, but blanket bans on online sales are not;
- in the US, marketplace and channel restrictions are judged under the rule of reason but face scrutiny where the supplier has market power or the restriction forecloses a channel;
- national authorities, particularly in Europe, have actively challenged marketplace bans in consumer electronics, luxury, and other branded goods.

The safer posture is to impose legitimate quality and presentation standards for online sales rather than to ban specific marketplaces outright. Any marketplace ban for a brand with market share requires counsel review.

### Scrutinize Parity And Platform MFN Clauses

Parity clauses, requiring a distributor or seller to offer the platform or supplier terms no worse than anywhere else, are a major enforcement focus.

Risk factors:

- wide parity clauses (across all channels and platforms) are higher-risk than narrow parity (within the same platform);
- parity held by a platform or supplier with market power can soften competition, raise rivals' costs, and prevent entry;
- retail parity clauses in online travel, accommodation, and app markets have been challenged or banned in multiple EU jurisdictions;
- parity can function as a coordination device across a market.

Narrow parity tied to a specific transaction may be defensible; broad parity held by powerful firms is high-risk and increasingly restricted by both competition enforcement and sector-specific regulation.

### Assess Foreclosure And Market Coverage

Channel restrictions are most exposed when they foreclose rivals or segment markets. The compliance officer must look at coverage, not just the individual contract.

Assess:

- the supplier's market share and whether the brand is must-stock for retailers;
- the proportion of the relevant market operating under selective or exclusive distribution;
- whether rival suppliers can realistically reach end customers through other channels;
- whether the restrictions, in combination across the market, make it hard for new entrants or online-only sellers to compete;
- whether the system discourages cross-border or cross-territory sales in ways that segment the single market (a particular EU concern).

A selective distribution system covering a large share of a market, imposed by a powerful brand, with criteria that exclude discounters and online sellers, is a foreclosure risk even if each individual criterion looks reasonable.

### Preserve Legitimate Anti-Free-Riding Justifications

Many channel restraints have a legitimate purpose: preventing free-riding, where a discounter benefits from a full-service dealer's pre-sale investment, or protecting brand integrity for products that require demonstration or installation. These justifications are real but must be genuine and proportionate.

To rely on them:

- document the specific free-riding or quality problem the restraint addresses;
- ensure the restraint is no broader than necessary;
- avoid using the justification to cloak a price-maintenance or foreclosure purpose;
- apply criteria uniformly, not selectively to disadvantage certain channel types;
- review periodically, because a justification that was valid may become a pretext as markets change.

## Common Traps

### Disguising Quantitative Caps As Quality Standards

Criteria that look qualitative but function as caps on dealer numbers or as exclusions of discounters are treated as quantitative and lose the more defensible treatment.

### Imposing Absolute Marketplace Bans

Blanket bans on selling through named marketplaces are heavily restricted in the EU and scrutinized elsewhere. Quality and presentation standards are the safer tool.

### Using Wide Parity Clauses For Powerful Platforms

Broad parity clauses held by platforms or suppliers with market power are a priority enforcement target and increasingly restricted by law.

### Confusing Selective And Exclusive Distribution

Applying the more permissive selective distribution analysis to a system that is actually exclusive distribution leads to under-assessment of territorial foreclosure risk.

### Ignoring Cumulative Foreclosure Across A Market

Each distributor agreement may look reasonable, but if the whole market operates under similar restrictions, rivals and new entrants may be foreclosed. The compliance view must be market-level, not contract-level.

### Blocking Cross-Border Or Passive Sales

Restrictions on distributors responding to unsolicited cross-border or cross-territory customer demand are a persistent EU red flag and undermine the single market.

### Treating Channel Rules As Brand Decisions Alone

Channel design has antitrust consequences regardless of brand intent. Excluding a channel type to preserve premium positioning can be lawful, but doing so through restrictions that foreclose competition is an antitrust problem.

## Self-Check

- Are selective and exclusive distribution correctly distinguished, with the more permissive selective analysis applied only to genuinely selective systems?
- Are selective distribution criteria tested for whether they are genuinely qualitative and proportionate, or whether they function as quantitative caps or discounter exclusions?
- Are online marketplace restrictions assessed under the current EU VBER and Court of Justice guidance, US rule-of-reason, and national rules, rather than treated as automatic brand rights?
- Are quality and presentation standards for online sales preferred over absolute marketplace bans?
- Are parity and platform MFN clauses assessed for breadth, market power, and coordination effect, with wide parity for powerful firms flagged as high-risk?
- Is foreclosure assessed at the market level, including the supplier's share, coverage of the system, and rival access to channels?
- Are cross-border and passive sales restrictions identified as EU red flags and avoided?
- Are anti-free-riding and brand-quality justifications documented, specific, proportionate, and periodically reviewed for pretext?
- Are channel rules routed to counsel review when the brand has market share, the system covers a large part of the market, or discounters and online sellers are excluded?
- Is the distinction between active and passive sales applied correctly in any territorial or customer restriction?
