# Pricing And Discounting Antitrust Risk

Pricing and discounting are the core of competition, but they are also where antitrust risk concentrates in two opposite directions. The same commercial practice can be challenged as unlawful price discrimination against smaller buyers, or as exclusionary predation against smaller rivals, depending on the facts. A compliance officer reviewing pricing programs must understand that uniform pricing, discriminatory pricing, loyalty rebates, and bundled discounts each carry distinct risks, and that the Robinson-Patman Act (US price discrimination), Sherman Act Section 2 (US exclusion), and EU TFEU Article 102 (abuse of dominance) apply different tests. The danger is that sales teams design discount and rebate structures to win business without recognizing that a loyalty rebate held by a dominant firm can be an abuse of dominance in the EU while being a legitimate competitive tool for a smaller firm.

Use this skill before approving pricing programs, customer-specific discounts, promotional allowances, loyalty or volume rebates, bundled discounts, or exclusivity incentives. The goal is to make the agent identify whether the firm may hold market power, classify the pricing practice, and apply the correct legal framework rather than treating pricing as purely commercial.

## Core Rules

### Determine Whether The Firm May Hold Market Power First

The legal treatment of almost every pricing practice depends on whether the firm has market power. The compliance officer must establish this before assessing any specific discount or rebate.

Assess:

- the firm's share of the relevant market, by product and geography;
- whether the firm is or may be considered dominant in the EU (commonly associated with shares at or above 40 percent, though not a fixed threshold) or possessing monopoly power in the US;
- whether the firm is a price-setter or a price-taker;
- the number and strength of competitors;
- barriers to entry and expansion;
- customer switching and countervailing buyer power.

A practice that is low-risk for a 10 percent competitor can be an abuse of dominance for a 70 percent incumbent. If market power is plausible, the entire pricing analysis shifts, and counsel review becomes mandatory.

### Apply The Robinson-Patman Framework For Price Discrimination

In the US, the Robinson-Patman Act prohibits price discrimination between competing buyers where the effect may be to injure competition. It is narrowly enforced today but remains live law, and compliance officers should understand its structure.

Key points:

- it applies to sales of commodities of like grade and quality to competing buyers at different prices;
- it targets harm to competition, not just to a disfavored buyer, with both primary-line (harm to the discriminating seller's competitors) and secondary-line (harm to the disfavored buyer's competitors) theories;
- recognized defenses include cost justification (price differences reflect actual cost savings) and meeting competition (matching a rival's lawful price);
- promotional allowances and services must be made available to all competing customers on proportionally equal terms;
- the Act has been rarely enforced in recent years, but private litigation and Federal Trade Commission attention can revive exposure.

Even where enforcement is light, the compliance officer should ensure that customer-specific pricing differences are documented with a legitimate basis (volume, cost, competition) rather than left unexplained.

### Evaluate Loyalty And Conditional Rebates For Exclusionary Effect

Loyalty rebates, retroactive discounts that kick in when a customer reaches a volume or share target, are a central focus of EU abuse-of-dominance law and a Section 2 concern in the US for dominant firms.

Risk factors:

- retroactive or incremental rebates that apply to all units once a threshold is met, creating strong incentive to concentrate purchases;
- single-branding or exclusivity rebates that condition the discount on buying mainly or only from the dominant firm;
- rebates structured to make it impossible or uneconomic for a rival to win any meaningful share;
- targeted fidelity rebates offered selectively to customers a rival is trying to win;
- dominance of the firm offering the rebate.

For a dominant firm in the EU, loyalty-inducing rebates that foreclose rivals are typically treated as abusive by object or with a strong presumption. In the US, the post-Lexmark and AMEX era applies a rule-of-reason that requires proof of competitive harm, but exclusive-dealing-style rebates by monopolists remain challengeable. The compliance posture: loyalty and exclusivity rebates should be reviewed by counsel whenever the firm may be dominant.

### Assess Bundled And Multi-Product Discounts

Bundled discounts, where a lower price applies only when the customer buys a package of products, can be exclusionary when offered by a dominant firm with a product rivals cannot match.

Assess:

- whether the bundle includes a product in which the firm is dominant (the tying or leverage product) and products where it faces competition;
- whether the discount makes it impossible for a rival that sells only the competitive product to match the effective price;
- whether the bundle is offered across the market or targeted at customers a rival is pursuing;
- the discount attribution test, which asks whether the dominant firm's price on the tied product, after attributing the full discount to that product, is below cost.

Bundled discounts by dominant firms are a recognized abuse category in the EU and have been litigated in the US (for example, in the LePage's and Microsoft contexts). Bundling by non-dominant firms is generally lower-risk.

### Distinguish Cost-Justified From Predatory Pricing

Selling below cost can be lawful competitive pricing or unlawful predation, depending on structure, duration, and market power. The compliance officer must know the cost benchmarks.

Know the tests:

- in the US, predatory pricing under Section 2 requires pricing below an appropriate measure of cost (often average variable cost or marginal cost) with a dangerous probability of recouping the losses through higher prices later;
- in the EU, pricing below average variable cost is presumed abusive for a dominant firm, and pricing below average total cost but above average variable cost can be abusive if part of an exclusionary plan;
- below-cost pricing by a non-dominant firm is generally not an antitrust violation, though it may raise unfair-trading or sector-specific issues;
- the recoupment requirement in the US makes pure predatory pricing cases hard to win, but EU law does not require proof of recoupment in the same way.

Any sustained below-cost pricing by a firm that may be dominant should be reviewed by counsel, with clear cost data and a documented competitive rationale.

### Document The Legitimate Basis For Every Pricing Difference

Because pricing practices are fact-intensive, documentation is the primary compliance defense. A pricing difference with a clear, contemporaneous, legitimate basis is far easier to defend than one that is unexplained.

Require documentation of:

- the cost basis for any differential, where cost justification is claimed;
- the competitive response basis, where meeting competition is claimed;
- the volume or efficiency basis for rebates and discounts;
- the business rationale for bundled or targeted discounts;
- the absence of exclusionary intent or effect where dominance is plausible.

## Common Traps

### Ignoring Market Power In Pricing Decisions

The most common trap is treating loyalty rebates or bundled discounts as ordinary sales tools without asking whether the firm is dominant. The same rebate is a competitive tool for a small firm and a potential abuse for a large one.

### Assuming Robinson-Patman Is Dead

Robinson-Patman enforcement is rare but the statute is live, private suits are possible, and FTC attention can resurface. Unexplained price differences between competing buyers remain a documented risk.

### Retroactive Loyalty Rebates Without Review

Retroactive rebates that apply to all units once a threshold is met create the strongest foreclosure incentive and are a priority EU abuse theory. They should never be deployed by a possibly-dominant firm without counsel review.

### Bundling A Dominant Product With Competitive Ones

Bundled discounts that leverage a dominant product to exclude rivals in adjacent markets are a recognized abuse category and have been litigated in both EU and US courts.

### Below-Cost Pricing Without Cost Data

Selling below cost without clear cost benchmarks and a documented rationale is indefensible if challenged. The firm must know which cost measure it is pricing against.

### Unexplained Customer-Specific Pricing

Pricing differences that are not tied to cost, volume, or competition look like discrimination. The absence of documentation is itself a risk, because it prevents the firm from raising a defense later.

### Targeting A Rival's Customers Selectively

Fidelity rebates or targeted discounts aimed specifically at customers a rival is trying to win are treated as especially exclusionary, particularly by dominant firms.

## Self-Check

- Is the firm's market power assessed (share, dominance, price-setting, barriers, countervailing power) before any specific pricing or discounting practice is approved?
- Is Robinson-Patman price-discrimination risk considered for differential pricing between competing buyers, with cost-justification or meeting-competition defenses documented?
- Are loyalty and conditional rebates reviewed for foreclosure effect whenever the firm may be dominant, with retroactive and exclusivity-inducing structures flagged?
- Are bundled and multi-product discounts assessed for whether they leverage a dominant product to exclude rivals in competitive markets?
- Is below-cost pricing tested against the correct cost benchmark (average variable cost, marginal cost, average total cost) with a documented competitive rationale and recoupment analysis where relevant?
- Is every material pricing difference documented with a contemporaneous legitimate basis (cost, volume, competition, efficiency)?
- Are targeted fidelity rebates aimed at a rival's customers identified as high-risk exclusionary conduct for dominant firms?
- Is counsel review mandatory for loyalty rebates, bundled discounts, or below-cost pricing whenever market power is plausible?
- Are promotional allowances and services made available to competing customers on proportionally equal terms?
- Is the EU-US divergence understood, including EU presumptions for below-average-variable-cost pricing and the US recoupment requirement?
