# Antitrust Dominance And Abuse Of Market Power

Dominance changes a firm's obligations. A company without market power can compete aggressively, set prices, and choose its trading partners with broad freedom. A company with significant market power faces special obligations under the EU Treaty, the US Sherman and Clayton Acts, and equivalent national competition laws, because conduct that is lawful for a smaller firm can constitute abuse of dominance when undertaken by a dominant one. The central judgment problem is that firms often do not recognize their own dominance, or they apply the rules of a non-dominant competitor to a position that carries special obligations. Exclusionary pricing, loyalty-inducing rebates, tying and bundling, refusal to supply, and self-preferencing can all constitute abuse when undertaken by a dominant firm, and the analysis depends on market definition, market share, and the competitive effects of the conduct. A compliance officer who does not assess dominance, or who treats the firm as an ordinary competitor, will miss the elevated risk.

Use this skill before assessing market dominance, evaluating exclusionary practices, reviewing pricing and rebate structures for dominant firms, or advising on special obligations. The goal is to make the agent think about market definition, dominance thresholds, the conduct that becomes problematic at scale, and the decisions that are easy to make too casually. A dominant firm that prices, discounts, and bundles as if it were a small competitor is creating antitrust exposure it does not recognize.

This skill addresses jurisdiction-specific obligations. Dominance thresholds, abuse categories, and safe harbors differ across the EU, the US, and national regimes. Always confirm the applicable national law and competition authority guidance, and consult qualified antitrust or legal counsel for specific dominance and abuse assessments.

## Core Rules

### Assess Whether The Firm Holds A Dominant Position

The first question is whether the firm is dominant, because dominance triggers special obligations. The assessment requires market definition and share analysis.

Dominance assessment:

- define the relevant product and geographic market, including demand-side and supply-side substitutability;
- assess market share, with dominance commonly presumed above 40 to 50 percent in many regimes, though no fixed threshold applies universally;
- consider other indicators of market power, including barriers to entry, countervailing buyer power, and the firm's ability to act independently of competitors and customers;
- assess dominance in each relevant market separately, since a firm can be dominant in one market and not another;
- revisit the assessment periodically, as market conditions and shares change.

A firm that assumes it is not dominant without analysis, or that uses a market definition broad enough to dilute its share, is not conducting a genuine assessment. The market definition must reflect economic reality, not a definition chosen to avoid dominance.

### Understand The Special Obligations Of Dominance

Dominance creates a special responsibility not to impair genuine undistorted competition. The dominant firm cannot engage in conduct that a non-dominant firm could undertake.

Special obligations include:

- the duty not to eliminate competitors through means other than competition on the merits;
- the duty not to weaken competition through tying, bundling, or exclusionary practices;
- the duty to deal with certain trading partners in some circumstances, including refusal to supply obligations for essential inputs;
- the duty not to engage in predatory pricing below cost;
- the duty not to impose unfair prices or trading conditions;
- the duty not to discriminate between trading partners where competition is harmed.

These obligations are not intuitive. Conduct that is normal commercial practice for a small firm can be abuse for a dominant one. The compliance program must train commercial teams on the special obligations that attach to dominance.

### Evaluate Pricing For Predation And Unfairness

Pricing is a primary area of dominance abuse. A dominant firm's pricing must be assessed for predation and unfairness.

Pricing risk for dominant firms:

- predatory pricing, selling below an appropriate measure of cost with the intent or effect of eliminating competitors;
- margin squeeze, where the firm prices wholesale inputs so high and retail outputs so low that efficient competitors cannot compete;
- unfair pricing, where prices are excessively high or unfairly low in a way that exploits customers or suppliers;
- loyalty-inducing rebates and discounts that foreclose competitors by making it unattractive for customers to switch;
- retroactive or conditional rebates that punish customers for dealing with competitors.

Pricing decisions for a dominant firm should be reviewed against cost benchmarks and competitive effects. A pricing structure that cannot be justified by efficiency or competition on the merits carries abuse risk.

### Assess Rebates Discounts And Conditional Practices

Rebate and discount structures are a frequent source of dominance abuse findings, particularly loyalty and fidelity rebates.

Rebate and discount risk:

- fidelity rebates that reward customers for buying exclusively or predominantly from the dominant firm;
- retroactive rebates that apply to all volume when a threshold is met, punishing partial switching;
- target rebates set just above the customer's typical purchase, inducing the customer to increase purchases to reach the target;
- conditional pricing that ties discounts to exclusivity, market share, or non-dealing with competitors;
- multi-product rebates that leverage dominance in one market to foreclose competition in another.

Each rebate and discount structure for a dominant firm should be assessed for foreclosure effects. Structures that induce exclusivity or penalize dealing with competitors carry elevated risk and may require redesign.

### Review Tying Bundling And Self-Preferencing

Tying, bundling, and self-preferencing can constitute abuse when a dominant firm leverages market power in one market to foreclose competition in another.

Tying and bundling risk:

- tying a dominant product to a separate product, forcing customers to buy both;
- bundling products in a way that makes it difficult for competitors in the bundled products to compete;
- pure versus mixed bundling, with different foreclosure effects;
- self-preferencing on platforms, where a dominant platform favors its own products or services over those of competitors;
- leveraging dominance in an input or infrastructure market to advantage downstream products.

These practices are not per se illegal in all regimes, but for a dominant firm they carry significant abuse risk. The compliance program should review product bundling, platform conduct, and cross-market leveraging for foreclosure effects.

### Handle Refusal To Supply And Essential Facilities

A dominant firm may have an obligation to deal with certain trading partners, particularly where it controls an essential input or facility.

Refusal to supply considerations:

- a dominant firm's refusal to supply can constitute abuse where it eliminates competition or harms consumers;
- obligations to license intellectual property may apply where the IP is indispensable to competition;
- essential facilities doctrine may require access to infrastructure that cannot be practically duplicated;
- margin squeeze obligations apply where the dominant firm supplies both wholesale and retail;
- termination of a longstanding supply relationship can be more problematic than an initial refusal.

The compliance program should assess refusal to supply and access decisions for dominance implications. A decision to refuse, terminate, or restrict supply should be reviewed for competitive effects and documented with rationale.

### Document The Competitive Justification

Dominant firm conduct is more defensible when supported by objective justification. The compliance program should ensure that commercial decisions are documented with their competitive rationale.

Justification documentation:

- document the efficiency or pro-competitive rationale for pricing, rebates, bundling, and access decisions;
- assess whether the conduct produces efficiencies that benefit consumers and could not be achieved with less restrictive alternatives;
- avoid retroactive rationalization; the justification should exist at the time of the decision;
- ensure the justification is genuine and not a pretext for exclusion;
- retain the analysis for potential competition authority review.

Conduct without documented objective justification is harder to defend. The documentation should show that the decision was made for legitimate competitive reasons, not to exclude competitors.

## Common Traps

### Assuming The Firm Is Not Dominant

Firms often underestimate their market position or use a broad market definition to dilute their share. A genuine dominance assessment is essential.

### Applying Non-Dominant Rules To A Dominant Position

Conduct that is lawful for a small firm can be abuse for a dominant one. The special obligations must be understood and applied.

### Fidelity And Retroactive Rebates Without Review

Loyalty-inducing and retroactive rebates are a frequent source of abuse findings. They must be assessed for foreclosure effects.

### Pricing Without Cost Benchmarks

Predatory pricing and margin squeeze require cost analysis. Pricing decisions for dominant firms must be reviewed against cost benchmarks.

### Tying And Bundling Across Markets

Leveraging dominance in one market through tying and bundling forecloses competition in another. Cross-market practices must be reviewed.

### No Documentation Of Competitive Justification

Conduct without documented objective justification is harder to defend. The rationale should exist at the time of the decision.

### Refusal To Supply Without Competitive Analysis

A dominant firm's refusal to supply can constitute abuse. Supply and access decisions must be assessed for competitive effects.

## Self-Check

- Has the firm assessed whether it holds a dominant position in each relevant market, with market definition and share analysis based on economic reality?
- Are the special obligations of dominance, including the duty not to impair competition, understood and applied to commercial decisions?
- Is pricing reviewed for predation, margin squeeze, and unfairness against cost benchmarks and competitive effects?
- Are rebate and discount structures assessed for foreclosure effects, including fidelity, retroactive, target, and conditional rebates?
- Are tying, bundling, and self-preferencing reviewed for leveraging dominance across markets?
- Are refusal to supply and access decisions assessed for dominance implications, including essential facilities and margin squeeze?
- Is competitive justification documented at the time of commercial decisions, with genuine pro-competitive rationale?
- Is the dominance assessment revisited periodically as market conditions and shares change?
- Are commercial teams trained on the special obligations that attach to dominance, using real scenarios?
- Is the dominance and abuse assessment confirmed against the applicable national law and competition authority guidance rather than a generic standard?
