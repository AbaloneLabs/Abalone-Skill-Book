# Antitrust Investigations And Leniency Decisions

When a credible antitrust allegation surfaces inside a company, the decisions made in the first hours and days determine whether the company faces catastrophic fines and criminal charges or secures immunity and cooperation credit. The defining feature of cartel enforcement is the leniency race: the first company to report and cooperate can receive full immunity, and everyone else faces substantial liability. This creates a brutal timing pressure that conflicts with the natural instinct to investigate quietly and gather facts before acting. A compliance officer involved in an internal investigation or leniency decision must understand that delay is the single most damaging choice, that evidence preservation is non-negotiable, and that the cooperation and witness-management decisions made early shape the outcome for years. The compliance officer is not the lead lawyer, but must know the triggers, the leniency landscape, and the steps that protect or destroy the company's position.

Use this skill when a credible cartel or antitrust allegation arises, when deciding whether to seek leniency, when running an internal investigation, and when managing cooperation and witnesses. The goal is to make the agent act fast on preservation and leniency, run a controlled investigation, and avoid the decisions that forfeit immunity or create obstruction exposure.

## Core Rules

### Recognize The Triggers For Immediate Action

Not every rumor requires a leniency application, but certain triggers demand immediate escalation to counsel and a preservation hold, because they indicate credible exposure that cannot wait for full fact-finding.

Triggers for immediate escalation:

- an employee reports, or a document reveals, an agreement with a competitor on price, bids, customers, territories, or output;
- a competitor, customer, or distributor alleges coordinated conduct;
- a trade association attendee reports a prohibited-topic discussion that was not properly handled;
- a regulator or prosecutor contacts the company, serves a subpoena, or conducts a dawn raid;
- an employee seeks personal counsel or asks about immunity, which can signal individual exposure;
- a departing or terminated employee with pricing or bidding knowledge threatens a report;
- internal audit or monitoring reveals parallel conduct following competitor contact.

The compliance officer's role is to ensure these triggers reach counsel immediately with a preservation hold, not to investigate and verify before escalating. Leniency is a race, and verification delay can forfeit immunity.

### Understand The Leniency Landscape Before Deciding

Leniency programs are the engine of cartel enforcement, and the compliance officer must understand them well enough to support a fast decision, even if counsel runs the application.

Know the programs:

- in the US, the DOJ Antitrust Division's Corporate Leniency Policy grants full immunity to the first company to self-report, cooperate, and meet the policy's conditions, and a parallel Individual Leniency Policy protects the first individual to report;
- the European Commission and most national competition authorities operate marker-based leniency where the first applicant can receive full immunity and subsequent applicants reduced fines based on the order of application;
- leniency is typically available only before the authority has sufficient evidence to act, so the first applicant's advantage is decisive and time-sensitive;
- a leniency marker can often be secured quickly with a preliminary description, with full evidence provided within a set period;
- leniency triggers cooperation obligations: continued cooperation, document production, witness availability, and non-interference, often lasting years;
- leniency in one jurisdiction does not protect the company in others, so multi-jurisdictional filing must be planned simultaneously.

The decision to apply is counsel's, but the compliance officer must ensure the decision is made fast, that markers are pursued across all relevant jurisdictions, and that the company understands the cooperation obligations it is taking on.

### Preserve Evidence Immediately And Comprehensively

The moment a credible allegation arises, the company must preserve all potentially relevant evidence. Failure to preserve can destroy leniency eligibility, create obstruction or spoliation exposure, and supply the inference that evidence was damaging.

Preservation requires:

- a global legal hold covering all relevant custodians, systems, locations, and time periods;
- suspension of all routine deletion, retention-purge, and destruction cycles;
- preservation of email, messaging, shared drives, personal devices used for business, backups, and physical documents;
- technical enforcement of the hold, not merely a written instruction;
- confirmation of acknowledgment by custodians and periodic re-issuance;
- documentation of when the hold was issued and how it was implemented.

The preservation obligation is immediate and non-negotiable. Any gap between notice of the allegation and the hold is a window in which destruction, even routine, creates exposure.

### Run A Controlled Internal Investigation

An internal investigation is necessary to understand exposure, support a leniency application, and prepare a defense, but it must be controlled to avoid waiving privilege, tipping off subjects, or creating inconsistent accounts.

Controls for the investigation:

- engage external counsel to lead, to preserve privilege and independence;
- define the scope, the key questions, and the custodians at the outset;
- conduct interviews in a sequence that protects the investigation, typically from peripheral to central witnesses;
- instruct witnesses on confidentiality and preservation, without coaching that could be seen as directing testimony;
- collect and review documents systematically, with privilege review before any production;
- avoid creating investigation documents that are not privileged or that could be discoverable;
- do not tip off subjects who may destroy evidence or coordinate accounts.

The investigation must support, not undermine, any leniency or cooperation strategy. Inconsistent or coached witness accounts, or premature document creation, can destroy credibility.

### Manage Witnesses And Individual Exposure

Cartel investigations create individual criminal exposure, and witness management is one of the most sensitive areas. Employees may face personal charges and are entitled to independent counsel, and the company must not interfere with their rights.

Witness management:

- inform witnesses of the preservation obligation and of the confidentiality of the investigation;
- do not direct or coach witness testimony, which can be obstruction or witness tampering;
- respect that employees with potential personal exposure are entitled to separate counsel, and do not interfere with their seeking it;
- consider whether to advance legal fees under corporate policy, and the implications for cooperation;
- manage the employment status of implicated employees carefully, balancing cooperation obligations with employment law;
- avoid any action that could be construed as retaliation against a reporting employee.

The company's cooperation credit can depend on making witnesses available, but the company cannot compel self-incriminating testimony and must respect individual rights. This tension requires careful legal management.

### Coordinate Cooperation Credit Across Jurisdictions

Cooperation can reduce penalties even where full leniency is not available, but cooperation requirements and credit differ by jurisdiction and must be coordinated.

Account for:

- what each authority requires for cooperation credit (document production, witness availability, admissions);
- the order-of-application rules for partial leniency in the EU and national programs;
- the US approach to cooperation and plea arrangements, including individual accountability;
- the interaction between cooperation in one jurisdiction and exposure in others;
- data protection and privilege limits on what can be shared cross-border;
- the risk that cooperation in one forum affects the company's position in private litigation.

Cooperation is not automatic and must be structured. Uncoordinated or over-broad cooperation in one jurisdiction can create exposure or waive protections elsewhere.

### Plan For Follow-On Private Litigation

Cartel findings almost always trigger follow-on civil damages claims, and the investigation and leniency decisions affect that exposure.

Plan for:

- the evidentiary consequences of any admissions or settlement with authorities;
- the interaction between leniency and civil liability, including any leniency recipient protection in some jurisdictions;
- document preservation and privilege in light of anticipated civil discovery;
- coordination between the regulatory defense and the civil litigation strategy.

## Common Traps

### Delaying Escalation To Verify Facts

The most damaging trap. Leniency is a race, and the first applicant wins. Waiting to fully investigate before reporting to counsel can forfeit immunity permanently.

### Late Or Incomplete Preservation

Any gap between notice and the legal hold is a destruction window. Routine deletion that continues after notice is spoliation or obstruction and can destroy leniency eligibility.

### Filing Leniency In Only One Jurisdiction

Leniency is jurisdiction-specific. Filing in the US or EU but missing national programs leaves the company exposed where immunity was available.

### Coaching Or Directing Witness Testimony

Any attempt to shape witness accounts is obstruction or witness tampering, destroys cooperation credibility, and creates separate criminal exposure.

### Interfering With Employee Independent Counsel

Employees with personal exposure are entitled to their own lawyers. Interference is obstruction and undermines the company's cooperation position.

### Tipping Off Subjects During Investigation

Premature disclosure of the investigation's scope or subjects can lead to evidence destruction or coordinated accounts, defeating the investigation and creating obstruction risk.

### Creating Discoverable Investigation Documents

Investigation notes, summaries, and communications that are not privileged can become evidence against the company. Privilege must be structured and preserved from the start.

### Ignoring Follow-On Civil Exposure

Regulatory cooperation and admissions shape civil damages litigation. Failing to plan for follow-on claims can multiply the financial impact of a cartel finding.

## Self-Check

- Are credible cartel or antitrust allegations escalated to counsel immediately with a preservation hold, without waiting for full verification?
- Are the DOJ Corporate and Individual Leniency Policies, EU marker-based leniency, and national programs understood well enough to support a decision within hours?
- Is a global, technically enforced legal hold issued immediately upon notice, suspending all routine deletion and destruction across all systems and custodians?
- Is the internal investigation run by external counsel with a defined scope, sequenced interviews, confidentiality, and no tipping-off of subjects?
- Are witnesses instructed on preservation and confidentiality without coaching or direction that could constitute obstruction or witness tampering?
- Are employees with potential personal exposure informed of their right to independent counsel, without interference?
- Is leniency pursued simultaneously across all relevant jurisdictions, recognizing that protection in one does not cover others?
- Are cooperation requirements and credit assessed jurisdiction by jurisdiction, with coordination to avoid cross-border privilege or data protection conflicts?
- Is the investigation structured to preserve privilege, avoiding discoverable documents that could be used against the company?
- Is follow-on civil litigation exposure planned for, including the evidentiary consequences of admissions, settlements, and leniency status?
