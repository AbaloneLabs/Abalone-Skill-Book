# Antitrust Compliance Program Design

Antitrust compliance fails predictably, and the failures are rarely about a lack of policy text. They happen because the program trains everyone equally instead of targeting the few roles that create the exposure, because it cannot detect the informal contacts where cartel conduct originates, because incentives reward the very behavior the policy prohibits, and because leadership treats antitrust as a legal checkbox rather than a business risk with criminal consequences. A compliance officer designing an antitrust program must understand that the highest-risk conduct, cartel agreements, information exchange, exclusionary practices by dominant firms, is almost never visible in the formal records the program monitors. The program's value lies in reaching the high-risk roles, the high-risk settings, and the high-risk decisions before they become investigations.

Use this skill before designing or refreshing an antitrust compliance program, evaluating its effectiveness, or advising what controls, training, and monitoring to prioritize. The goal is to make the agent build a risk-based program that targets where exposure actually concentrates, rather than a generic policy-and-training library.

## Core Rules

### Build The Program Around Specific Antitrust Risks

An antitrust program is not a single thing; it is a set of controls mapped to distinct risk categories, each with different owners, settings, and detection challenges. The program must be organized by risk, not by a generic "follow the law" message.

Map the program to the distinct risks:

- cartel conduct (price-fixing, bid-rigging, market allocation, output limitation), concentrated in sales, pricing, bidding, and trade association roles;
- information exchange and signaling, concentrated in benchmarking, trade association, consultant, and investor-relations activities;
- vertical restraints, concentrated in distribution, channel, and contract management;
- dominance abuse, concentrated in pricing, rebates, bundling, and platform conduct for firms with market power;
- merger and JV conduct, concentrated in corporate development, integration, and clean team functions.

Each risk needs its own controls, training, monitoring, and escalation path. A program that lumps these into one antitrust module will under-serve every one of them.

### Target High-Risk Roles With Specific Training

Generic annual antitrust training for all employees produces minimal behavior change in the roles that matter. Training must be risk-weighted, role-specific, and scenario-based.

High-risk roles requiring targeted training:

- sales and pricing leaders who set list prices, discounts, and bids;
- commercial leaders responsible for tenders, RFPs, and public procurement;
- executives and business unit heads who attend trade associations and industry events;
- distribution and channel managers who negotiate vertical restraints;
- pricing and rebate designers in firms with market power;
- corporate development and integration teams handling M&A and JVs;
- investor relations and communications staff whose disclosures can signal.

For these roles, training should use real scenarios (a competitor raises pricing at a dinner, a customer shares a rival's bid, a trade association agenda includes market outlook), not abstract legal definitions. The training should teach the specific disengagement, documentation, and reporting sequence, and it should be refreshed more often than the generic program.

### Design Monitoring For The Settings Where Risk Concentrates

Antitrust risk is hard to detect because it lives in informal settings and indirect channels. The program must monitor the settings and channels, not only the formal records.

Monitoring approaches:

- track trade association attendance, agendas, and incident reports, and review them for prohibited-topic exposure;
- review benchmarking, survey, and consultant engagements for information exchange safeguards before they proceed;
- audit distribution contracts and templates for RPM, MFN, territorial, and exclusivity terms;
- monitor pricing and rebate structures for dominance firms for exclusionary patterns;
- review investor communications and public announcements for signaling risk;
- track competitor contacts, joint venture interactions, and pre-merger clean team operations;
- survey high-risk employees periodically about competitor contacts and pressure points.

Monitoring that looks only at signed contracts and formal emails will miss the dinners, the hallway conversations, and the consultant reports where the real risk lives.

### Align Incentives And Discipline With The Program

A program whose incentives contradict its rules will not work. If sales bonuses reward market-share gains achieved through coordinated conduct, or if high performers who attend trade associations are never disciplined for boundary violations, the policy is irrelevant.

Review:

- sales and pricing incentive structures for pressure that encourages coordination or exclusion;
- bonus metrics that reward share or margin gains without compliance gating;
- promotion criteria and whether compliance behavior is weighted;
- disciplinary processes and whether they apply consistently, including to high performers;
- tone from the top, including whether leadership models and enforces the rules or treats them as legal overhead;
- retaliation controls for employees who report concerns.

The DOJ Evaluation of Corporate Compliance Programs and equivalent guidance emphasize that incentives and discipline must reinforce, not undermine, the program. A compliance officer should flag incentive-conflict as a program risk.

### Ensure The Program Is Empowered And Resourced

An antitrust program cannot function if the compliance function lacks authority, access, budget, or independence. The program design must address empowerment, not only content.

Assess:

- whether compliance can stop or escalate a high-risk practice, not merely advise;
- whether compliance has access to the data, systems, and people needed to monitor;
- whether the reporting line protects compliance independence from business pressure;
- whether budget and staffing match the risk profile, especially in high-risk industries;
- whether the board and senior leadership receive meaningful antitrust risk reporting, not generic attestations.

### Incorporate Audit, Testing, And Continuous Improvement

A program that is never tested cannot be claimed to be effective. Audit and testing generate the evidence that the program works and reveal where it does not.

Build in:

- periodic audits of high-risk activities (trade association participation, distribution contracts, pricing structures, clean team operations);
- testing of training effectiveness, not only completion rates;
- monitoring of incident reports, hotline patterns, and escalation outcomes;
- review of any near-miss, complaint, or investigation finding for program lessons;
- documented updates to controls, training, and monitoring based on findings.

### Plan For Investigation And Enforcement Readiness

An antitrust program must be ready for the day an allegation, dawn raid, or leniency decision arrives. Readiness is part of program design, not an afterthought.

Ensure:

- a dawn raid response plan exists, is tested, and key personnel know their roles;
- document preservation and legal hold processes are defined and triggerable immediately;
- an internal investigation protocol exists for credible allegations;
- the leniency and cooperation landscape is understood well enough to act within hours;
- escalation paths from compliance to counsel to leadership are clear and fast.

## Common Traps

### Generic Training For All Employees

Training everyone equally under-protects the few roles that create the exposure. Antitrust training must be risk-weighted and role-specific.

### Monitoring Only Formal Records

Cartel conduct and information exchange live in informal settings. A program that monitors only contracts and emails misses the real risk.

### Incentives That Contradict The Rules

Bonuses and promotion criteria that reward share or margin gains without compliance gating push employees toward the very conduct the program prohibits.

### Treating Antitrust As Legal Overhead

When leadership treats antitrust as a legal checkbox, the business ignores it. Tone from the top must treat it as a business risk with criminal consequences.

### No Testing Or Evidence Of Effectiveness

A program that is never audited or tested cannot be defended as effective. Completion rates and policy existence are not evidence of impact.

### Underestimating Dominance Obligations

Firms with market power have special obligations. A program designed for a non-dominant firm will miss the pricing, rebate, and bundling risks that dominance creates.

### No Readiness For Dawn Raids Or Leniency

When an allegation or raid arrives, the program must act in hours, not days. Lack of a tested plan and leniency understanding is a critical gap.

## Self-Check

- Is the program organized around distinct antitrust risks (cartel, information exchange, vertical restraints, dominance, mergers) rather than a single generic module?
- Are high-risk roles (sales, pricing, bidding, trade association attendees, channel managers, pricing designers in dominant firms, corporate development) identified and given targeted, scenario-based training?
- Does monitoring reach the informal settings and indirect channels (trade associations, consultants, benchmarking, investor communications, clean teams) where risk concentrates, not only formal records?
- Are distribution contracts, pricing structures, and rebate programs audited for RPM, MFN, territorial, exclusivity, and exclusionary patterns?
- Are sales incentives, bonus metrics, promotion criteria, and discipline reviewed for alignment with, or contradiction of, the antitrust rules?
- Does tone from the top treat antitrust as a business risk with criminal consequences, enforced consistently including for high performers?
- Is the compliance function empowered, resourced, and independent enough to stop or escalate high-risk practices?
- Are periodic audits, training-effectiveness testing, hotline and incident monitoring, and post-investigation lessons built into continuous improvement?
- Are special obligations for firms with market power reflected in the program's pricing, rebate, bundling, and self-preferencing controls?
- Is the program ready for dawn raids, document preservation, internal investigations, and fast leniency decisions, with tested plans and clear escalation paths?
