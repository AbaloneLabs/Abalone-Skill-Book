# Abuse Of Dominance And Exclusionary Conduct

Once a firm is dominant, conduct that would be lawful for a smaller competitor can become unlawful. This is the core of abuse of dominance (EU TFEU Article 102) and monopolization or attempted monopolization (US Sherman Act Section 2). The compliance challenge is that the conduct in question, pricing aggressively, refusing to supply a rival, bundling products, offering loyalty discounts, preferring one's own services on a platform, looks like ordinary business strategy. The line between hard competition and unlawful exclusion is subtle, jurisdiction-dependent, and turns on whether the conduct harms the competitive process rather than just hurting a rival. A compliance officer advising a dominant firm must recognize the exclusionary categories, understand the cost and foreclosure tests, and know when a business decision has crossed from competition on the merits into abuse.

Use this skill before approving any pricing, supply, bundling, exclusivity, or self-preferencing decision by a firm that may be dominant. The goal is to make the agent identify the specific abuse category, apply the dominance and effect analysis, and route the decision to counsel before it is implemented.

## Core Rules

### Establish Dominance Before Assessing The Conduct

Abuse of dominance requires dominance. If the firm is not dominant in the relevant market, Article 102 does not apply and Section 2 monopolization claims face a much higher bar. The first step is always the market power assessment.

Confirm:

- the relevant product and geographic market;
- the firm's market share and whether it crosses the dominance reference zones;
- the durability of the position (barriers, network effects, buyer power);
- whether the firm can act independently of competitors, customers, and suppliers.

If dominance is not established, the conduct analysis changes substantially, though Section 2 attempted monopolization and Section 1 concerted action may still apply. Never analyze exclusionary conduct without first fixing the dominance question.

### Identify The Specific Exclusionary Category

Abuse of dominance is not a single test; it is a set of recognized categories, each with its own analysis. The compliance officer must classify the conduct correctly.

The main categories are:

- predatory pricing, selling below cost to eliminate a rival with a plan to recoup;
- exclusive dealing and loyalty rebates that induce customers to buy mainly from the dominant firm and foreclose rivals;
- tying and bundling, conditioning the purchase of one product on another, or offering discounts only for the bundle;
- refusal to deal, including refusal to supply existing customers or to grant access to an input or facility;
- margin squeeze, where the dominant firm sets a high wholesale price and a low retail price such that an efficient rival cannot compete;
- self-preferencing, where a vertically integrated platform favors its own services over rivals';
- discrimination, charging different prices or offering different terms to equivalent trading partners in ways that distort competition;
- essential facilities doctrine, in narrow circumstances requiring access to a facility a rival cannot duplicate.

The analysis differs by category. Predatory pricing turns on cost benchmarks and recoupment; exclusive dealing on foreclosure share and duration; tying on separate-products and market power; margin squeeze on the spread between upstream and downstream prices. Classify first, then apply the right test.

### Apply The Correct Cost Test For Predatory Pricing

Predatory pricing is the most common but also one of the hardest abuses to prove, and the cost benchmark is central.

Know the benchmarks:

- in the EU, pricing below average variable cost is presumed abusive for a dominant firm; pricing below average total cost but above average variable cost can be abusive if part of an exclusionary plan;
- in the US, Section 2 predatory pricing requires pricing below an appropriate measure of cost (commonly average variable cost or marginal cost) plus a dangerous probability of recouping losses through later price increases;
- the US recoupment requirement makes pure predatory pricing claims difficult, but EU law does not require recoupment proof in the same way;
- above-cost pricing, even if aggressive, is generally not predatory in the US, though EU law can reach some above-cost pricing in exclusionary schemes.

Any sustained below-cost pricing by a dominant firm requires counsel review with clear cost data. The firm must know which cost measure it is pricing against and have a documented competitive rationale.

### Assess Foreclosure For Exclusive Dealing And Loyalty Rebates

Exclusive dealing and loyalty rebates are lawful for non-dominant firms but can be abusive when used by a dominant firm to foreclose rivals. The analysis focuses on the degree and effect of foreclosure.

Assess:

- the share of the market covered by the exclusive deals or fidelity rebates;
- the duration of the commitments;
- whether rivals can realistically reach customers through remaining channels;
- whether the rebate is retroactive or incremental, creating strong single-branding incentives;
- whether the rebate is targeted at customers a rival is pursuing;
- whether the rebate makes it impossible for an equally efficient rival to compete.

Retroactive loyalty rebates and single-branding incentives offered by dominant firms are treated with particular suspicion in the EU. In the US, exclusive dealing is evaluated under a rule of reason that requires substantial foreclosure. The compliance posture: any loyalty or exclusivity structure by a possibly-dominant firm needs counsel review.

### Apply The Separate-Products And Leverage Test For Tying And Bundling

Tying and bundling can be efficient, but when practiced by a dominant firm they can leverage power from one market into another.

Assess:

- whether the tied and tying products are genuinely separate (customers want to buy them separately);
- whether the firm has market power in the tying product;
- whether the tie forecloses rivals in the tied product market;
- whether the bundle is offered across the market or targeted at rivals' customers;
- the effective price of the tied product after attributing the discount, and whether it is below cost.

Pure tying (you must buy B to get A) by a dominant firm is higher-risk than mixed bundling (a discount for buying both, but each available separately). The EU and US both recognize leveraging as a theory of harm, though the tests differ.

### Evaluate Margin Squeeze In Regulated Or Two-Sided Markets

Margin squeeze occurs when a dominant upstream supplier sets a wholesale price so high relative to its downstream retail price that an equally efficient downstream rival cannot survive. It is a recognized abuse in both EU and US law, particularly in telecoms and regulated industries.

Check:

- whether the firm is dominant upstream and competes downstream;
- whether the spread between the upstream access price and the downstream retail price is positive or negative after accounting for the rival's incremental costs;
- whether regulation caps the firm's ability to adjust either price;
- whether the squeeze is sustained or transient.

Margin squeeze analysis is technical and typically requires economist input, but the compliance officer should know to flag any structure where the firm supplies a rival's input and competes with that rival downstream.

### Recognize Self-Preferencing As A Live Abuse Category

Self-preferencing, a vertically integrated firm favoring its own products or services over rivals' on its platform or distribution channel, has become a priority enforcement area, especially in digital markets.

Risk factors:

- the firm operates a platform or marketplace and also sells its own competing products or services;
- the firm ranks, displays, or promotes its own offerings more favorably;
- the firm uses rivals' data to benefit its own offerings;
- the firm limits rivals' access to features, APIs, or interoperability available to its own services.

Self-preferencing by dominant platforms is a focus of EU enforcement and DMA obligations, and has been challenged in the US under Section 2. Any platform that also competes with its users needs specific counsel review of ranking, data use, and access policies.

## Common Traps

### Analyzing Conduct Without Establishing Dominance

Abuse of dominance requires dominance. Skipping the market power assessment and jumping to the conduct leads to wrong conclusions, because the same conduct is lawful for a non-dominant firm.

### Assuming Aggressive Pricing Is Always Lawful

Above-cost pricing is generally lawful in the US, but EU law can reach above-cost pricing in exclusionary schemes, and below-cost pricing by a dominant firm is presumptively abusive in the EU. Do not assume aggressive pricing is safe.

### Ignoring Retroactive Rebate Structures

Retroactive loyalty rebates create the strongest foreclosure incentive and are a priority EU abuse theory. Treating them as ordinary volume discounts is a serious error for a dominant firm.

### Treating Bundling As Always Efficient

Bundling can be efficient, but bundling that leverages a dominant product to exclude rivals in an adjacent market is a recognized abuse. The leverage effect must be assessed.

### Missing Margin Squeeze In Vertically Integrated Firms

Where a firm supplies a rival's input and competes downstream, a margin squeeze is possible even if no individual price looks abusive. The relationship between the two prices is what matters.

### Overlooking Self-Preferencing In Platform Businesses

A platform that ranks its own products first, uses rivals' data, or limits rival access is engaged in conduct that regulators now treat as a core abuse category. Branding it as product strategy does not remove the risk.

### Confusing EU And US Standards

EU Article 102 reaches conduct that US Section 2 would not, and applies at lower dominance thresholds. Applying US standards to EU-facing conduct understates the risk.

## Self-Check

- Is dominance in the relevant market established or assessed before any exclusionary conduct is approved?
- Is the conduct classified into a specific abuse category (predatory pricing, exclusive dealing, tying, refusal to deal, margin squeeze, self-preferencing, discrimination, essential facilities)?
- Is predatory pricing tested against the correct cost benchmark (average variable cost, marginal cost, average total cost) with recoupment analysis where US law applies?
- Are exclusive dealing and loyalty rebates assessed for foreclosure share, duration, retroactivity, targeting of rivals' customers, and effect on an equally efficient rival?
- Is tying and bundling assessed for separate products, market power in the tying product, leverage into the tied market, and the effective price after discount attribution?
- Is margin squeeze considered wherever the firm supplies a rival's input and competes downstream, with the spread between upstream and downstream prices examined?
- Is self-preferencing identified and reviewed for any platform that also competes with its users, including ranking, data use, and access policies?
- Are EU-US divergences understood, including EU presumptions for below-cost pricing, lower dominance thresholds, and treatment of above-cost exclusionary pricing?
- Is counsel review mandatory for any of these categories whenever the firm may be dominant?
- Is there a documented competitive rationale and cost basis for any pricing, rebate, or bundling decision by a dominant firm?
