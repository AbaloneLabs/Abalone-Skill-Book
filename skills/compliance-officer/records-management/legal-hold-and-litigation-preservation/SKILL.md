# Legal Hold and Litigation Preservation

The duty to preserve relevant evidence arises the moment litigation is reasonably anticipated, not when it is filed, and it falls on the organization before any lawyer formally instructs preservation. Agents often underestimate how early the duty attaches, issue holds that name individuals but not the systems and data sources that hold the evidence, or suspend disposition for named custodians while automated destruction continues to delete the same records elsewhere. The judgment problem is recognizing the trigger promptly, scoping the hold to cover all relevant custodians and sources without over-preserving everything, and sustaining the hold accurately until the matter concludes and it is released.

This skill applies to legal operations, compliance, IT, and records management whenever litigation, investigation, audit, or regulatory inquiry is anticipated or underway. Preservation obligations and spoliation consequences differ by jurisdiction and forum. This is high-stakes work where preservation failures can result in sanctions, adverse inferences, and lost claims. Confirm the applicable preservation duty with litigation counsel and act immediately on any trigger.

## Core Rules

### Recognize the Trigger Early and Act Before Formal Instructions

The duty to preserve attaches when litigation is reasonably anticipated, which can be well before a complaint is filed. Triggers include a formal demand letter, a credible threat of suit, a regulatory inquiry notice, a government subpoena, an employee complaint that signals likely litigation, or an incident such as a serious injury or data breach that foreseeably leads to claims. The compliance and records functions must be able to recognize these triggers and initiate the hold process immediately, because the window between the trigger and routine destruction can be short. Waiting for litigation counsel to issue a formal hold after the trigger may be too late for records on short retention cycles.

### Scope the Hold to Custodians and Sources, Not Just People

A hold that names custodians but does not identify the systems and data sources where their relevant records live is incomplete. Modern evidence lives in email, collaboration platforms, shared drives, databases, HR and finance systems, mobile devices, and backups. The hold must identify the custodians, the systems and data sources likely to contain relevant records, the time period covered, and the record types in scope. Work with IT to map where each custodian's relevant data resides, because custodians themselves often do not know the full footprint of their own records. A hold that relies on custodians to self-collect from systems they do not fully understand will miss evidence.

### Suspend Routine Disposition Comprehensively

Issuing a hold notice to custodians does not, by itself, stop automated destruction. Retention schedules, auto-deletion policies on email and collaboration tools, backup rotation cycles, and device-wipe processes continue to operate unless explicitly suspended for the held records. The hold process must interface with the records-management system, the IT systems, and the disposition schedules to suspend destruction across all affected sources. Verify the suspension technically, not just by policy, because a policy suspension that the system does not enforce is a preservation failure. The most common spoliation events are records deleted by automated policies that the hold notice did not reach.

### Avoid Both Under-Preservation and Over-Preservation

Under-preservation causes spoliation and sanctions. Over-preservation, holding everything indefinitely, causes excessive cost, ongoing risk, and the practical problem that holds never get released. Scope the hold proportionately to the matter: identify the issues, the time period, the key custodians, and the relevant record types, and preserve that scope. Resist the instinct to hold the entire organization's data because it feels safe, because permanent over-preservation defeats the records program and makes future matters harder. Document the scoping reasoning so it is defensible if the scope is later challenged as too narrow.

### Sustain and Track the Hold Accurately Over Time

A hold is not a one-time notice; it is an ongoing obligation that must be sustained for the life of the matter. Custodians join and leave the organization, systems are migrated, data is archived, and the scope of the matter may expand. The hold process must track all active holds, re-issue or confirm notices periodically, add new custodians and sources as the scope evolves, and ensure that departing custodians's records are preserved before their accounts are deactivated and their devices wiped. A hold that was correctly issued at the outset but not maintained is a common source of later spoliation.

### Release the Hold Promptly and Completely When the Matter Concludes

When litigation concludes, the appeal period expires, or the investigation closes, the hold must be released promptly so that routine disposition can resume and the preserved data does not accumulate indefinitely. The release must reach all systems and custodians, confirm that the suspension is lifted, and document the release. Failing to release holds is the primary cause of the over-preservation problem that eventually paralyzes records programs. Build a release process that is as disciplined as the issuance process.

## Common Traps

### Waiting for Formal Litigation to Preserve

The duty attaches at reasonable anticipation, which can precede filing. Delaying preservation until counsel issues a formal hold may allow short-retention records to be destroyed in the gap.

### Naming Custodians but Not Sources

A hold notice to people does not stop automated system destruction. The hold must identify and suspend disposition in the systems where the evidence actually lives.

### Policy Suspension Without Technical Enforcement

A policy statement that disposition is suspended is meaningless if the retention engine continues to delete. Verify the suspension technically in each affected system.

### Over-Preserving Everything Indefinitely

Holding all data to feel safe defeats the records program, increases cost and breach exposure, and creates the problem that holds are never released. Scope proportionately and document the reasoning.

### Failing to Sustain or Release the Hold

A hold issued once and never maintained misses new custodians and migrated data. A hold never released after the matter concludes causes permanent over-preservation. Both are process failures.

### Wiping Departing Custodians's Devices and Accounts

Deactivating a departing custodian's accounts and wiping their devices without first preserving held records destroys evidence. Integrate hold status into the offboarding process.

## Self-Check

- Did I recognize the preservation trigger at the point of reasonable anticipation and initiate the hold before formal litigation instructions?
- Did I scope the hold to identify both the custodians and the systems and data sources where relevant records live, with IT mapping the data footprint?
- Did I suspend routine disposition comprehensively across all affected systems, and verify the suspension technically rather than only by policy?
- Did I scope proportionately to the matter, avoiding both under-preservation that risks spoliation and over-preservation that defeats the records program?
- Am I tracking the hold over time, re-issuing notices, adding custodians and sources as scope evolves, and preserving departing custodians's records before offboarding?
- Did I release the hold promptly and completely when the matter concluded, reaching all systems and custodians and documenting the release?
- Did I confirm the applicable preservation duty with litigation counsel and act immediately on any trigger, given the sanctions exposure for spoliation?
- Does the output stay within the agent's scope, deferring final authority, licensed judgment, or specialist sign-off to the qualified person where the question exceeds the agent's competence?
