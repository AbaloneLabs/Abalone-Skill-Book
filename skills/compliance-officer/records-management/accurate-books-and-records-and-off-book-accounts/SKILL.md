# Accurate Books And Records And Off Book Accounts

The accounting provisions of the FCPA are the backbone of anti-corruption enforcement. They require issuers to make and keep books, records, and accounts that accurately and fairly reflect transactions and to maintain a system of internal accounting controls. These provisions matter because almost every bribery scheme depends on concealment: the bribe must be hidden in the books as a consulting fee, a commission, a promotional expense, or a miscellaneous charge. A company that cannot detect off-book accounts, slush funds, false invoices, and mischaracterized payments cannot detect bribery, and regulators know that the books-and-records violation is often easier to prove than the underlying bribe. The compliance problem is that concealment is designed to look ordinary, and that the controls that would catch it are frequently the same controls that business pressure pushes employees to bypass.

Use this skill before designing internal accounting controls for corruption risk, auditing for off-book accounts and slush funds, reviewing unusual invoices or payment patterns, or investigating a suspected concealed payment. The goal is to make the agent look at the books as evidence of integrity, not merely as a financial record, because the character of a transaction in the books is often the proof of whether it was corrupt.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Two FCPA Accounting Provisions

The FCPA accounting provisions have two distinct requirements, and both must be operationalized. The books-and-records provision requires that the issuer make and keep records that accurately and fairly reflect, in reasonable detail, the transactions and dispositions of assets. The internal-controls provision requires a system of internal accounting controls sufficient to provide reasonable assurance that transactions are executed with management authorization, recorded properly, access to assets is controlled, and recorded assets are compared with existing assets. Bribery schemes violate both: the payment is misrecorded, and the controls that should have prevented or detected it failed.

The implications for compliance design:

- accuracy is about the true character of the transaction, not only the dollar amount;
- reasonable detail means enough for a reviewer to understand what occurred;
- internal controls must prevent and detect, not only record;
- the provisions apply to the consolidated books of the issuer, including subsidiaries and JVs;
- individuals and the company can be liable for knowing falsification.

### Detect Off-Book Accounts And Slush Funds

Off-book accounts and slush funds are pools of money outside the formal accounting system, funded by unrecorded transactions, retained rebates, cash advances, or diverted receipts, and used to make payments the company does not want to record. They are the classic vehicle for bribery because they are invisible to normal controls. Detecting them requires looking beyond the general ledger.

Indicators of off-book accounts and slush funds:

- cash-intensive operations or locations with large petty cash balances;
- unexplained bank accounts, including in the name of employees or affiliates;
- payments to or from accounts not in the vendor master;
- retained commissions, rebates, or credits that are not returned to the company;
- round-dollar transfers between affiliates without business purpose;
- unrecorded receipts, such as scrap sales or customer refunds;
- pressure to keep transactions outside the formal system;
- discrepancies between recorded and actual cash or inventory;
- requests to process payments without recording the counterparty.

Investigate each indicator to the underlying transactions, not merely to the explanation offered.

### Identify False And Misleading Invoices

False invoices are the most common concealment device. A bribe is paid and then recorded against an invoice that describes services that were not performed, were inflated, or were performed by a different party. The invoice looks ordinary, which is the point, but its substance does not match the payment.

Review invoices for:

- vague descriptions such as consulting, market development, or professional services without detail;
- services disproportionate to the amount or to the vendor's apparent capacity;
- round-dollar, recurring amounts with no supporting documentation;
- invoices from vendors in jurisdictions unconnected to the services;
- invoices backdated or created shortly after the payment;
- invoices from entities related to employees or officials;
- invoices that do not match the contracted scope;
- payments to the invoice issuer routed to a different account.

Require supporting documentation that evidences actual services performed, and reject invoices that cannot substantiate the charge.

### Control The Vendor Master And Payment Authorization

The vendor master and the payment authorization process are where concealment is either enabled or stopped. False vendors, duplicate vendors, and employee-related vendors are created in the vendor master and then paid through ordinary channels. Controlling who is added to the vendor master and how payments are authorized is a core anti-corruption control.

Controls include:

- vendor onboarding with identity, ownership, and conflict verification;
- periodic review and cleansing of the vendor master for duplicates and inactive vendors;
- segregation of duties between vendor creation, invoice approval, and payment;
- dual approval for payments above thresholds or to high-risk jurisdictions;
- matching of invoices to purchase orders and receipts;
- review of payments to offshore accounts or third-party payees;
- blocking payments to vendors flagged by compliance or sanctions screening;
- periodic audit of new vendors and high-value payments.

### Match The Recording To The True Character Of The Transaction

The books-and-records provision is about the character of the transaction, not the label. A payment to a government official's relative recorded as a consulting fee is a mischaracterization even if the invoice exists. A charitable donation made at the official's direction to a charity they control is a thing of value, not a donation. Train finance and compliance staff to record the true nature and purpose of each transaction and to escalate when the stated purpose does not match the substance.

### Apply Controls Across Subsidiaries, Joint Ventures, And Acquired Entities

The accounting provisions reach the consolidated entity. Misconduct in a subsidiary, a joint venture, or a newly acquired company creates liability for the issuer. This is especially important in acquisitions, where inherited books, vendor masters, and payment practices may carry concealed corruption.

For acquisitions and JVs:

- conduct pre-acquisition anti-corruption due diligence on books, controls, and payment practices;
- integrate the acquired entity's controls promptly after closing;
- apply the company's vendor, payment, and recording standards to JVs where the company has influence;
- remediate identified control gaps with documented timelines;
- report material weaknesses and control deficiencies through the financial reporting process.

### Make Controls Preventive And Detective

A control that only records a transaction after the fact cannot prevent a mischaracterized payment. Effective anti-corruption accounting controls combine preventive controls, that stop the improper transaction, with detective controls, that find it after the fact.

Preventive controls:

- pre-approval of high-risk payments and vendors;
- system blocks on payments to unapproved or flagged parties;
- mandatory supporting documentation before payment release;
- segregation of duties and dual authorization.

Detective controls:

- periodic audit of high-risk payment categories;
- data analytics on vendor master, payment patterns, and journal entries;
- reconciliation of cash, bank, and intercompany accounts;
- review of manual journal entries and period-end adjustments;
- trend analysis of promotional, consulting, and commission spend.

## Common Traps

### Treating The Invoice As Proof Of The Transaction

An invoice proves a request for payment, not that the underlying service occurred. Require evidence of actual performance.

### Allowing The Vendor Master To Accumulate

Stale, duplicate, and false vendors are the foundation of concealed payment schemes. Cleanse and review the vendor master regularly.

### Ignoring Cash And Unrecorded Receipts

Off-book funds are often built from cash and diverted receipts that never enter the formal books. Cash-intensive operations need heightened monitoring.

### Recording The Label Rather Than The Character

A transaction recorded by its invoice label rather than its true nature satisfies the form of accounting while violating its substance. Train for character, not label.

### Assuming Subsidiaries And JVs Are Separate

The accounting provisions consolidate. Misconduct in a subsidiary or JV is the issuer's misconduct. Extend controls across the consolidated entity.

### Relying Only On Detective Controls

Finding a concealed payment after it is made limits the loss but does not prevent it. Combine preventive and detective controls.

### Bypassing Controls Under Time Pressure

End-of-period and urgent payment pressure is when controls are most often overridden and when concealment is most likely. Enforce controls most strictly under pressure.

## Self-Check

- Do books, records, and accounts reflect the true character and reasonable detail of each transaction, with internal controls that both prevent and detect mischaracterization?
- Are off-book accounts, slush funds, unexplained bank accounts, retained rebates, and cash-intensive operations actively detected and investigated?
- Are invoices reviewed for vague descriptions, disproportionate amounts, round-dollar recurrence, jurisdiction mismatch, and missing documentation?
- Is the vendor master controlled through onboarding verification, cleansing, segregation of duties, and dual approval of high-risk payments?
- Is each transaction recorded by its true character and purpose, with escalation when the stated purpose does not match the substance?
- Do accounting controls extend across subsidiaries, joint ventures, and acquired entities, with pre-acquisition diligence and post-acquisition integration?
- Are preventive controls, pre-approval, system blocks, mandatory documentation, combined with detective controls, audit, analytics, reconciliation?
- Are manual journal entries, period-end adjustments, and promotional, consulting, and commission spend analyzed for concealment patterns?
- Are material weaknesses and control deficiencies reported and remediated through the financial reporting process?
- Are controls enforced most strictly under time and payment pressure rather than relaxed for urgency?
