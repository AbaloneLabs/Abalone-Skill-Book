# Records Retention Schedule and Disposition

A retention schedule is the central instrument that determines how long each type of record is kept and when it may be destroyed. Agents often treat retention as a storage-cost question or copy retention periods from another company without mapping them to the specific legal, tax, and operational requirements that apply. The judgment problem is building a defensible retention schedule that satisfies every applicable retention obligation, destroys records promptly once those obligations lapse to limit liability and storage risk, and never destroys anything that is under legal hold or that a regulator, court, or counterparty could require.

This skill applies to records management, compliance, legal operations, and IT functions across all record types. Retention obligations are jurisdiction-specific, sector-specific, and record-type-specific. The periods and rules differ across countries, states, and regulated industries. Verify the applicable retention requirements and consult counsel before finalizing or changing any retention period.

## Core Rules

### Build the Schedule From a Record-Type Inventory, Not From Storage Buckets

A defensible retention schedule begins with an inventory of the record types the organization actually creates and receives, each mapped to the legal, tax, regulatory, and operational drivers that set its retention period. Record types should be defined by their business and legal function, such as employment contracts, payroll registers, customer transaction records, board minutes, environmental monitoring logs, or product-safety test reports, not by where they are stored. A schedule organized by storage location or application produces inconsistent retention because the same record type may exist in multiple systems with different rules. Map each record type to the longest applicable retention driver, because a record subject to a three-year tax rule and a seven-year employment rule must be kept for seven years.

### Identify All Retention Drivers, Including the Non-Obvious Ones

Retention is driven by more than the obvious tax and corporate requirements. Employment records carry specific periods for payroll, benefits, safety, and discrimination claims. Environmental and safety records may have long retention for exposure monitoring and incident documentation. Regulated industries carry sector-specific periods for financial transactions, customer communications, and compliance evidence. Consumer-protection and privacy laws may set both minimum retention for certain evidence and maximum retention limits for personal data, creating tension that must be resolved deliberately. Product-liability and warranty records may need retention tied to the product lifecycle rather than a fixed calendar period. Failing to identify a driver leads to premature destruction; failing to identify a maximum-retention limit leads to over-retention that itself creates risk.

### Reconcile Minimum Retention With Privacy Maximum-Retention Limits

Privacy regimes increasingly impose a principle of data minimization and storage limitation, requiring that personal data not be kept longer than necessary for the purpose. This can conflict with legal retention obligations that require keeping the same data for a defined minimum period. The resolution is purpose-specific: data retained to satisfy a legal obligation is kept for that period and no longer, and the retention schedule should reflect the legal minimum as the operational target. Over-retention beyond the legal or business need, even of data the company is allowed to keep, increases breach exposure and privacy-law risk. Build the schedule to destroy at the earliest lawful moment, not to retain indefinitely by default.

### Make Disposition Defensible and Documented

Destruction of records must be routine, documented, and executed under the retention schedule, not ad hoc. A defensible disposition process records what was destroyed, when, under what retention authority, and by whom. This documentation is what distinguishes routine disposition under a schedule from the destruction of evidence, which becomes a serious problem if the records are later relevant to litigation or investigation. Regular disposition also reduces the volume of data exposed in a breach and the volume subject to discovery. Implement disposition on a defined cadence, and require confirmation that no legal hold applies before any destruction.

### Treat the Legal Hold as an Absolute Override

A legal hold suspends the routine retention schedule for any records it covers, regardless of whether their retention period has expired. The hold process must be able to identify and preserve all affected records across all systems, including email, collaboration tools, shared drives, databases, and backups, before any disposition occurs. The two most dangerous failures are destroying records that are under hold, which is evidence spoliation, and applying holds so broadly that they never expire and the organization effectively never destroys anything. Scope holds precisely to the matter, track them, and release them promptly when the matter concludes.

### Govern the Full Record Lifecycle Across Systems

Retention rules must be enforceable across the systems where records live, including structured databases, email, collaboration platforms, cloud storage, and physical paper. A retention schedule that exists as a policy document but is not implemented in the systems is aspirational, not operational. Work with IT to implement retention and disposition in each system, and recognize that legacy systems, archived backups, and shadow repositories often retain data that the primary systems have disposed of. A record that survives in a backup after disposition is not truly destroyed.

## Common Traps

### Copying Another Company's Retention Periods

Retention obligations depend on the organization's jurisdictions, industry, and record types. Periods copied from another company may omit a driver that applies here or retain a period that conflicts with a local rule.

### Over-Retaining by Default to Avoid Thinking About It

Indefinite retention feels safe but increases breach exposure, discovery costs, and privacy-law risk. The schedule should destroy at the earliest lawful moment, with retention as the exception justified by a specific driver.

### Organizing Retention by Storage Location

The same record type in multiple systems should have one retention rule. Organizing by storage bucket produces inconsistent retention and makes defensible disposition impossible.

### Destroying Records Under Legal Hold

The most serious records-management failure is destroying records subject to a legal hold. The hold process must identify and preserve affected records across all systems before any disposition.

### Treating Backups as Out of Scope

Records that survive in backups after primary disposition are not truly destroyed. Backup retention and disposition must be part of the lifecycle governance, or the disposition is illusory.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- Did I build the retention schedule from an inventory of record types defined by business and legal function, each mapped to its retention drivers?
- Did I identify all applicable drivers, including non-obvious sector, environmental, product-liability, and privacy maximum-retention limits?
- Did I reconcile minimum retention obligations with privacy storage-limitation principles, targeting destruction at the earliest lawful moment?
- Is disposition routine, documented, and executed under the schedule, with confirmation that no legal hold applies before destruction?
- Does the legal-hold process precisely scope, preserve across all systems, track, and release holds, avoiding both spoliation and permanent over-preservation?
- Is retention implemented and enforceable across all systems, including legacy systems and backups, not only documented as policy?
- Did I verify the applicable retention periods for each jurisdiction, industry, and record type, and consult counsel before changing any retention period?
- Are assumptions, uncertainties, and confidence levels stated explicitly rather than buried in a confident-sounding conclusion?
