# Policy For Regulatory Inquiries And Subpoenas

When a regulator, prosecutor, or government agency issues an inquiry, subpoena, civil investigative demand, or informal request, the organization's response in the first hours and days often determines the outcome of the entire matter. Missteps are easy and consequential: destroying or altering records, making inaccurate statements, volunteering harmful information, waiving privilege, missing deadlines, or allowing uncoordinated employees to respond directly. The challenge is that these requests are time-pressured, legally technical, and often arrive without warning, yet they require disciplined coordination, preservation, and judgment. The purpose of a policy for regulatory inquiries and subpoenas is to ensure that when a request arrives, the organization responds in a controlled, timely, privilege-protected, and accurate manner, rather than reacting chaotically. The judgment is building a response process, managing preservation and privilege, coordinating the many parties involved, and meeting deadlines without sacrificing accuracy.

Use this skill before designing a response process for government requests, managing privilege and preservation, or reviewing response readiness. The goal is to make the agent think about intake and routing, preservation, privilege, coordination, deadlines, accuracy, and the evidence of a controlled response.

## Core Rules

### Establish A Single Intake And Routing Point

The first failure mode is a request that lands with an employee who does not recognize its significance or routes it poorly. Every regulatory or legal request must flow to a defined intake point quickly.

Establish:

- a single intake owner, typically legal or compliance, with backup;
- instructions to all employees to forward any government or legal request immediately and not to respond directly;
- awareness so that employees in front-line roles, such as reception, security, customer service, and facilities, know what to do if a request arrives;
- a protocol for informal inquiries, not just formal subpoenas, since informal requests carry the same risks;
- logging of every request upon receipt, regardless of perceived significance.

The intake point is the trigger for preservation, assessment, and coordination. A request that sits unreported defeats every downstream control.

### Trigger Preservation Immediately Upon Receipt

The duty to preserve attaches as soon as litigation or investigation is reasonably foreseeable, which is certainly upon receipt of a subpoena or inquiry. Preservation must be triggered immediately, before scope is fully assessed.

Trigger preservation by:

- issuing a legal hold covering the custodians, systems, and time period plausibly relevant;
- suspending routine retention and destruction for in-scope records;
- coordinating with records management, IT, and backup administration;
- verifying that the hold reaches all relevant systems, including email, messaging, and backups;
- documenting the hold's issuance, scope, and effective date.

Do not wait to refine scope before preserving. Preserve broadly first, then narrow as the matter is assessed. Destruction after receipt of a request, even routine destruction, can constitute spoliation.

### Assess Scope, Obligations, And Strategy Before Responding

Before producing anything or making statements, assess the request's scope, the organization's obligations, and the response strategy. Reacting without assessment leads to over-production, under-production, or harmful disclosures.

Assess:

- the legal authority behind the request and whether it is binding;
- the scope of information requested and whether it is proportionate;
- the deadline and whether extension or negotiation is appropriate;
- privileges that may protect some requested information;
- the organization's legal position, exposure, and objectives;
- whether to assert privileges, negotiate scope, or seek clarification;
- whether voluntary cooperation, qualified privilege, or other strategic options apply.

Some requests are overbroad or premature, and scope negotiation is legitimate. Assessment is what allows a calibrated rather than a reflexive response.

### Protect Privilege And Confidentiality

Regulatory inquiries often touch privileged and confidential information. Uncontrolled production can waive privilege and disclose sensitive content. Privilege must be protected deliberately.

Protect privilege by:

- conducting privilege review before production;
- using privilege logs to withhold justified documents with explanation;
- marking and handling confidential and trade-secret material appropriately;
- seeking protective orders or confidentiality treatment where warranted;
- coordinating communications to avoid waiving privilege, especially with shared counsel or joint defenses;
- ensuring employees understand that communications with lawyers may be privileged and should not be disseminated.

Privilege, once waived, is generally lost. The response process must treat privilege protection as a core function, not an afterthought.

### Coordinate A Multi-Function Response

A regulatory response involves legal, compliance, business units, IT, records, communications, and often executive leadership. Uncoordinated response produces inconsistent information and gaps.

Coordinate by:

- forming a response team with defined roles and a single lead, typically legal;
- identifying the business and technical experts who can locate and explain the requested information;
- establishing clear internal communication channels and avoiding informal side conversations;
- coordinating public or customer communications so they do not conflict with the legal response;
- keeping executive leadership informed without creating uncontrolled privileged or sensitive records;
- managing the involvement of third parties, such as vendors and former employees.

Coordination is what allows the organization to speak accurately and consistently. Uncoordinated responses create contradictions that authorities exploit.

### Manage Deadlines Without Sacrificing Accuracy

Regulatory deadlines are often short, but accuracy cannot be sacrificed to speed. An inaccurate or incomplete production on time can be worse than a negotiated extension.

Manage deadlines by:

- calendaring the deadline immediately and assessing feasibility;
- requesting extensions early where the scope or volume warrants, rather than at the last moment;
- prioritizing production by the request's apparent focus;
- avoiding over-collection that delays review and increases cost and risk;
- conducting quality review before production, even under time pressure;
- documenting any inability to meet a deadline and the steps taken.

Meet deadlines through planning and negotiation, not by cutting corners on accuracy or privilege review. A missed deadline is a problem; a careless production is a bigger one.

### Control Employee Communications With Authorities

Employees may be contacted directly by investigators, inspectors, or regulators, especially in inspections and interviews. Uncontrolled employee communications create inconsistency and exposure.

Control by:

- instructing employees to notify legal before speaking with authorities where legally permitted;
- providing counsel or representation for employee interviews where appropriate;
- preparing employees who will be interviewed, within legal bounds, so they understand the process and their obligations;
- reminding employees of their obligation to be truthful and not to speculate;
- addressing the limits on employer control, since employees have independent rights and some jurisdictions restrict employer interference with government communications.

Balance the organization's interest in coordination with employees' independent rights and the obligation not to obstruct. Coordination is legitimate; interference is not.

### Maintain Evidence Of A Controlled Response

The organization should be able to show it responded in a controlled, timely, and good-faith manner. Evidence of the response process is itself part of the defense.

Maintain:

- the request and its intake log;
- the preservation hold and its scope;
- the assessment, strategy, and privilege decisions;
- the production, its review, and any privilege logs;
- communications with the authority, including extensions and clarifications;
- the coordination record showing who did what and when.

A documented, controlled response demonstrates good faith and competence. An undocumented, chaotic response suggests the opposite and invites adverse inferences.

### Plan For Inspection And On-Site Presence

Some inquiries involve on-site inspections or visits, which require immediate, coordinated handling distinct from document requests.

Plan for inspections by:

- designating a point of contact to receive and accompany inspectors;
- instructing staff to notify the point of contact immediately if inspectors arrive;
- understanding the scope of the inspectors' authority and the organization's rights;
- documenting what is requested, examined, and taken during an inspection;
- ensuring preservation is triggered by an inspection as by any other request.

Inspections are time-pressured and disorienting. A pre-established protocol prevents confusion and ensures the organization's rights are preserved while cooperating lawfully.

## Common Traps

### Request Not Recognized Or Routed

A request that lands with an unaware employee and goes unreported defeats every downstream control and creates preservation gaps.

### Destruction After Receipt

Routine or deliberate destruction after a request arrives constitutes spoliation, regardless of intent, and carries severe consequences.

### Responding Before Assessment

Reacting without assessing scope, obligations, and strategy leads to over-production, under-production, or harmful voluntary disclosures.

### Waiving Privilege Through Uncontrolled Production

Producing privileged material without review or logging waives privilege, generally irretrievably.

### Uncoordinated Multi-Function Response

When functions respond independently, contradictions and gaps emerge that authorities can exploit.

### Sacrificing Accuracy To Meet Deadlines

Cutting privilege review or quality checks to meet a deadline produces careless productions that are worse than a negotiated extension.

### Uncontrolled Employee Communications

Employees speaking directly with authorities without coordination create inconsistency and exposure.

### No Evidence Of A Controlled Response

Without documentation of intake, preservation, assessment, and production, the organization cannot demonstrate good faith or competence.

## Self-Check

- Is there a single intake and routing point with instructions for all employees, including front-line roles, to forward requests immediately and not respond directly?
- Is preservation triggered immediately upon receipt, covering plausibly relevant custodians and systems, with verification and documentation?
- Is scope, legal authority, obligations, and strategy assessed before any production or statement, with scope negotiation where appropriate?
- Is privilege protected through pre-production review, privilege logs, confidentiality treatment, and careful communication handling?
- Is a multi-function response team formed with defined roles, a single lead, and controlled internal communication?
- Are deadlines managed through early calendaring, extension requests, prioritization, and quality review rather than by sacrificing accuracy?
- Are employee communications with authorities coordinated within legal bounds, with preparation and representation where appropriate?
- Is evidence of the controlled response maintained, including intake, preservation, assessment, production, privilege, and authority communications?
- Is there a protocol for on-site inspections, with a designated contact, scope awareness, and documentation of what is examined?
- Does the process distinguish informal inquiries from formal subpoenas and apply the same discipline to both?
