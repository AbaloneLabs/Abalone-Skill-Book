# Facilitation Payments And Small Bribes

Facilitation payments, sometimes called grease payments, are small sums paid to low-level government officials to expedite or secure the routine performance of a duty they are already legally required to perform. They sit at the most contested intersection of anti-corruption law. The FCPA historically carved out a narrow affirmative defense for facilitation payments, while the UK Bribery Act prohibits them entirely, and most modern corporate policies prohibit them regardless of jurisdiction because a global company cannot safely operate two standards. The practical compliance problem is that the line between a permissible facilitation payment, an impermissible bribe, and genuine extortion is thin, ambiguous, and often decided under pressure at a border, a customs shed, or a licensing counter.

Use this skill before advising whether a payment to an official is permissible, drafting a zero-tolerance anti-bribery provision, responding to an employee facing an extortionate demand, or reviewing whether historical facilitation payments were properly recorded. The goal is to make the agent refuse the false comfort of the FCPA carve-out, recognize extortion as a distinct category, and design controls that work in the field rather than only on paper.

## Core Rules

### Treat Facilitation Payments As Prohibited By Default

A modern global anti-corruption program should prohibit facilitation payments outright. The FCPA affirmative defense is narrow, jurisdiction-specific, and shrinking in practical value, and relying on it creates three serious problems. First, the UK Bribery Act, the OECD Anti-Bribery Convention as implemented by most member states, and many local laws criminalize facilitation payments with no exception. Second, a payment that looks like facilitation can easily be recharacterized as a bribe if the underlying duty was discretionary rather than routine. Third, the accounting provisions of the FCPA still require accurate recording, and disguised facilitation payments are a frequent books-and-records violation.

A zero-tolerance policy is simpler to train, enforce, and defend. The policy should state unambiguously that no employee or third party acting on the company's behalf may make facilitation payments, and that the company will not rely on the FCPA carve-out even where it might technically apply.

### Distinguish Facilitation Payments From Bribes And From Extortion

The three categories are legally distinct, and conflating them produces bad decisions. A facilitation payment expedites a routine, non-discretionary duty the official must perform anyway, such as processing a visa the applicant is entitled to. A bribe induces a discretionary or improper act, such as awarding a contract, clearing goods that should be seized, or overlooking a violation. Extortion occurs when the official demands payment under threat of harm to person, liberty, property, or vital business interests, and the payer has no meaningful alternative.

The distinction matters because:

- extortion may be defensible under a duress or necessity rationale, while a voluntary bribe never is;
- a facilitation payment under the FCPA defense still requires accurate recording and a showing that the duty was routine and non-discretionary;
- a payment to influence a discretionary decision is a bribe even if it is small and paid to a low-level official;
- the label the payer applies is irrelevant; regulators examine the underlying duty and the official's authority.

When evaluating a payment, ask what duty the official was performing, whether that duty was routine or discretionary, whether the payer had a legal entitlement to the underlying action, and whether there was a threat.

### Design For The Field, Not The Policy Manual

A prohibition that fails at a customs checkpoint is not a real control. Employees in high-corruption environments face real demands, often from uniformed officials with the power to detain, delay, or seize. The program must give them a way to refuse, escalate, and survive.

Build field controls including:

- a clear written instruction to refuse facilitation payments and to escalate to a designated compliance contact;
- a 24-hour escalation channel for officials who threaten delay or detention;
- pre-cleared language employees can use to decline politely while citing company policy and anti-corruption law;
- authority for a designated senior manager to approve an exception only where there is a credible threat to personal safety;
- coordination with security, legal, and where appropriate the embassy or local counsel;
- documentation of the demand, the official, the location, the threat, and the response;
- a debrief process so that recurring extortion points are mapped and addressed operationally.

### Handle Genuine Duress And Safety Threats Explicitly

When an official threatens physical harm, unlawful detention, or destruction of property, the decision is no longer about corruption policy; it is about safety. The program should acknowledge that an employee may pay under genuine duress to protect life, liberty, or vital property, and should define how that is documented and reported after the fact. Failing to address duress honestly forces employees to choose between their safety and policy compliance, and drives payments underground where they are never recorded or learned from.

A defensible duress framework requires:

- an imminent and credible threat to person, liberty, or critical property;
- no reasonable safe alternative, such as escalation, delay, or intervention by counsel or authorities;
- the minimum payment necessary to end the threat;
- prompt reporting after the event;
- accurate recording of the payment and the circumstances.

### Require Accurate Recording Of Any Payment That Occurs

If any payment to an official occurs, whether under duress or in error, it must be recorded accurately and transparently. Disguising a payment as a consulting fee, a customs charge, or a miscellaneous expense is itself a violation of the books-and-records provisions and converts a disclosure problem into a concealment problem. The general ledger should reflect the true character of the payment, and the compliance function should review and investigate any payment to a government official regardless of how it is coded.

### Map And Reduce Government Touchpoints

The most effective control for facilitation payment risk is structural: reducing the number of situations in which employees must interact with low-level officials who can extract payments. Operational measures include using licensed customs brokers, pre-clearing shipments, digitizing permit applications, consolidating logistics through reputable providers, and avoiding jurisdictions or routes with chronic extortion risk where alternatives exist. Risk assessment should identify the specific touchpoints, borders, ports, licensing offices, inspections, and registrations where facilitation demands are most likely, and should target those with process redesign.

### Train With Realistic Scenarios

Generic "do not pay bribes" training does not prepare employees for a uniformed official demanding cash at a remote border. Use scenario-based training drawn from the company's actual geography and operations, with role-play on refusal language, escalation, and duress documentation. Training should reach not only headquarters staff but logistics, procurement, facilities, and field personnel who actually encounter officials.

## Common Traps

### Over-Relying On The FCPA Affirmative Defense

The defense is narrow, jurisdiction-specific, and does not exist under the UK Bribery Act or most other laws. Designing policy around it creates a false safe harbor and exposes the company wherever the defense does not apply.

### Calling A Bribe A Facilitation Payment

A small payment to influence a discretionary decision is still a bribe. The dollar amount and the official's rank do not determine the category; the nature of the duty does.

### Ignoring The Books-And-Records Dimension

Even a payment that might qualify for an affirmative defense becomes a violation if it is recorded as something else. Concealment is often the more serious and more provable offense.

### Punishing Employees Who Report Extortion

If employees who report extortionate demands face discipline or suspicion, future payments will be hidden. The program must separate duress reporting from misconduct and treat honest reporting as compliance behavior.

### Failing To Distinguish Discretionary From Routine Duties

Officials often have mixed duties. A payment to speed a routine visa is different from a payment to obtain a permit the official has discretion to deny. Analyze the specific duty, not the official's job title.

### Assuming Local Custom Legalizes The Payment

"Everyone does it here" is not a defense under the FCPA, the UK Bribery Act, or the OECD Convention. Local tolerance does not cure a criminal violation, and customary practice can itself be a red flag rather than a justification.

### Letting Urgency Override Controls

Cargo stuck at a port, a flight about to be missed, or a shipment spoilage deadline all create pressure to pay. Urgency is when controls matter most; pre-cleared escalation and decision authority must exist before the crisis, not be invented during it.

## Self-Check

- Does the policy prohibit facilitation payments outright rather than relying on the FCPA affirmative defense?
- Can the agent distinguish a facilitation payment, a bribe, and extortion based on the nature of the duty and the presence of a threat?
- Is there a field-ready escalation channel with refusal language and a designated decision-maker for duress situations?
- Does the duress framework require an imminent credible threat, no safe alternative, minimum payment, prompt reporting, and accurate recording?
- Are any payments to officials recorded accurately in their true character rather than disguised?
- Has the company mapped and reduced the specific government touchpoints where facilitation demands arise?
- Does scenario-based training reach logistics, procurement, and field personnel with realistic refusal and escalation practice?
- Are employees who report extortion protected from retaliation and not conflated with wrongdoers?
- Is the distinction between routine and discretionary duties applied to each payment analysis rather than assumed from the official's title?
- Are recurring extortion points reviewed operationally for process redesign rather than accepted as unavoidable?
