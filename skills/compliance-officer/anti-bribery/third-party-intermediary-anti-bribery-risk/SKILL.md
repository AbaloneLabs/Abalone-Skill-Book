# Third Party Intermediary Anti-Bribery Risk

Third party intermediaries are the dominant vehicle for corporate bribery. Agents, distributors, resellers, commission sales representatives, consultants, lobbyists, fixers, and promoters allow a company to obtain improper advantages while maintaining distance from the transaction. Anti-bribery regulators know this and have built an entire enforcement theory around it: a company is liable for bribes paid by intermediaries when it knew, should have known, or willfully ignored red flags. The FCPA, the UK Bribery Act section 7 failure-to-prevent offense, and the OECD Convention all impose responsibility for third-party misconduct that is not adequately controlled. The compliance problem is that intermediaries are also legitimate and often essential to market entry, so the task is not to avoid them but to distinguish the genuine business partner from the bribery conduit.

Use this skill before engaging a sales agent or distributor, structuring a commission arrangement, reviewing payment requests from an intermediary, evaluating red flags in a third-party relationship, or deciding whether to suspend or terminate an intermediary. The goal is to make the agent look past the contract and the invoice at who the intermediary really is, how they are paid, why they were chosen, and what they actually do.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Intermediary's True Function And Beneficiaries

Bribery risk is determined by what the intermediary actually does and who benefits, not by the label on the contract. A "consultant" who introduces the company to government decision-makers is a high-risk government-facing intermediary regardless of the title. A distributor who simply resells product at arm's length may be lower risk.

Map for every intermediary:

- the actual services performed, not the contracted scope;
- whether the intermediary interacts with government officials or decision-makers;
- whether the intermediary was recommended by a government official or someone with influence;
- the jurisdictions of operation;
- the customers or tenders the intermediary touches;
- who owns and controls the intermediary, including beneficial owners;
- whether any owner or principal is a government official, a relative of one, or a politically exposed person;
- whether the intermediary represents or is affiliated with the customer or the government;
- the replacement difficulty and whether the intermediary was sole-sourced.

If the intermediary cannot explain in concrete terms what they will do and why they are uniquely qualified, the relationship is not ready for approval.

### Apply Heightened Diligence To Government-Connected Intermediaries

An intermediary with government connections is simultaneously the most commercially valuable and the most legally dangerous. The connection that opens doors is the same connection that makes a bribe plausible. Regulators treat government-connected intermediaries as a primary corruption vector.

For these intermediaries, require enhanced due diligence including:

- identification of every beneficial owner and principal;
- screening against sanctions, debarment, and adverse media databases;
- confirmation of whether any principal is a current or former government official, and if former, when they left office;
- analysis of how the relationship originated and who introduced the intermediary;
- review of the intermediary's own compliance program;
- reference checks from prior principals;
- litigation, enforcement, and debarment history;
- review of the intermediary's ownership of assets disproportionate to known income.

Document the diligence, the rating, the rationale, and the decision. A government connection is not disqualifying by itself, but it must be understood, justified, and controlled.

### Test The Business Rationale And Compensation

Excessive or unexplained compensation is the single most common red flag in intermediary bribery cases. If the intermediary is paid more than the market would bear for the services actually performed, the excess is presumptively a fund for bribery. The DOJ and SEC repeatedly cite disproportionate commissions, success fees, and large upfront payments as evidence of corrupt intent.

Evaluate compensation against:

- the scope and complexity of actual services;
- market rates for comparable services in the jurisdiction;
- whether compensation is tied to legitimate, measurable deliverables;
- whether a large success fee creates pressure to win by improper means;
- whether payment is commensurate with the intermediary's actual capacity and infrastructure;
- whether the intermediary has the staff, systems, and qualifications to perform the work.

If the compensation cannot be justified by reference to genuine services at market rates, restructure it or decline the relationship.

### Control Payment Mechanics

How an intermediary is paid matters as much as how much. Payment structure can either expose or conceal bribery. Require payment controls that make concealed payments difficult.

Controls include:

- payment only to the intermediary's own account in its country of incorporation, not to third-party or offshore accounts;
- payment only against documented evidence of services performed;
- prohibition of cash payments;
- review of invoices for specificity, rejecting vague descriptions such as consulting services;
- prohibition of round-dollar, recurring invoices with no detail;
- matching of payments to contract terms and delivered milestones;
- flagging of requests for advance payment, split payments, or payments through unrelated entities;
- periodic audit of payment patterns for anomalies.

### Recognize And Act On Red Flags

Red flags are not automatic proof of misconduct, but each one requires investigation and documented resolution before approval continues. Ignoring a red flag is, in the eyes of regulators, close to conscious disregard.

Common intermediary red flags:

- the intermediary was recommended by a government official;
- the intermediary refuses to disclose beneficial ownership;
- the intermediary requests payment to a third party, offshore account, or in cash;
- compensation is disproportionate to services;
- the intermediary insists on unusually high commission or success fees;
- the intermediary has no physical office, staff, or demonstrable capability;
- the intermediary was formed shortly before the engagement or the relevant tender;
- the intermediary requests that the relationship be kept confidential without legitimate reason;
- the intermediary has a history of short-lived entities or name changes;
- the intermediary is related to the customer or to a government decision-maker;
- the intermediary is in a jurisdiction with no connection to the transaction;
- invoices are vague, round-dollar, or recurring without supporting detail.

For each red flag, document the issue, the investigation, the evidence requested, the intermediary's response, the compliance decision, and any conditions imposed.

### Contract For The Right Obligations

The contract converts diligence into enforceable commitments. Boilerplate anti-bribery language is not enough for a government-connected intermediary. Include specific provisions where appropriate:

- a precise scope of services tied to compensation;
- an anti-bribery and anti-corruption representation and covenant referencing FCPA, UK Bribery Act, and local law;
- a prohibition on subcontracting or sub-agents without consent;
- an obligation to maintain accurate books and records;
- audit and inspection rights;
- certification of beneficial ownership and agreement to notify of changes;
- training or certification requirements;
- cooperation with investigations;
- termination rights for breach and for red flags that remain unresolved;
- flow-down of these obligations to any permitted sub-agent.

### Monitor Throughout The Relationship

Onboarding diligence decays. Ownership changes, sub-agents appear, commissions drift, and new tenders arise. Monitoring must continue for the life of the relationship and intensify for high-risk intermediaries.

Monitor:

- invoices and payments against scope and market rates;
- changes in beneficial ownership;
- new sub-agents or related parties;
- adverse media, sanctions, and debarment updates;
- performance versus the contracted services;
- the intermediary's ongoing interactions with government officials;
- renewal and expansion requests;
- complaints or allegations from employees, competitors, or the public.

Do not allow renewals to auto-approve. Each renewal is a fresh risk decision.

## Common Traps

### Treating The Intermediary As A Customer Or Vendor Problem

The company is liable for bribes paid by intermediaries acting on its behalf. Outsourcing the relationship does not outsource the liability.

### Accepting Vague Invoices

"Consulting services" and "market development" without detail are classic concealment vehicles. Require specificity and reject what cannot be substantiated.

### Ignoring How The Relationship Originated

An intermediary handed to the company by a government official is a serious red flag regardless of how well they otherwise appear. Trace the origin of every relationship.

### Paying To Third-Party Or Offshore Accounts

Payment to an account other than the intermediary's own, or to an offshore jurisdiction unconnected to the work, is a hallmark of bribery schemes. Prohibit it.

### Letting Commission Structure Create Bribery Pressure

A huge success fee contingent on winning a government tender incentivizes the intermediary to win at any cost. Structure compensation to reward legitimate work, not only outcomes.

### Failing To Re-Diligence At Renewal

Ownership, sub-agents, and risk change over time. A clean onboarding file from three years ago does not justify an unreviewed renewal.

### Keeping A Terminated Intermediary's Records Accessible Only To Procurement

Compliance, legal, and investigations functions need access to intermediary records. Siloed files prevent pattern detection and allow rejected parties to reappear under new names.

## Self-Check

- Is the intermediary's true function, government interaction, beneficial ownership, and origin of the relationship documented?
- Are government-connected intermediaries subject to enhanced due diligence covering beneficial owners, political exposure, debarment, and adverse media?
- Is compensation tested against market rates and actual services, with disproportionate commissions restructured or rejected?
- Do payment controls prohibit cash, third-party accounts, offshore routing, and vague invoices?
- Is each red flag documented with investigation, evidence, intermediary response, decision, and conditions?
- Does the contract include specific anti-bribery, sub-agent, audit, cooperation, and termination provisions rather than boilerplate?
- Is ongoing monitoring tied to risk level and applied at every renewal rather than only at onboarding?
- Are rejected or terminated intermediaries tracked to prevent informal re-engagement under another name or entity?
- Can compliance, legal, and investigations access intermediary records for pattern detection?
- Has the agent distinguished a genuine business partner from a bribery conduit based on function, compensation, and control rather than contract labels?
