# Anti Corruption Policies And Training Design

A written anti-corruption policy and a training module are the visible artifacts of a compliance program, and they are also where programs most often fail. A policy that is generic, legalistic, and disconnected from how work is actually done will not change behavior. Training that certifies completion without testing judgment will not prepare employees for the real decisions they face. The DOJ Evaluation of Corporate Compliance Programs asks not only whether a policy exists but whether it is communicated, understood, applied, and reinforced, and whether training is tailored to risk and role. The compliance problem is that policy and training are usually built for coverage and documentation rather than for decision quality, and that the employees who need the most preparation, sales, procurement, and government-facing staff, often receive the least tailored content.

Use this skill before drafting or revising an anti-corruption policy, designing training, deciding who must be trained and how, building certification, or evaluating whether the program's guidance actually reaches the decisions that matter. The goal is to make the agent build policy and training around the real scenarios and roles where corruption risk is concentrated, not around a generic legal summary.

## Core Rules

### Write Policy For Decisions, Not For Coverage

A policy should help an employee make the right decision in a specific situation. Policies written as legal summaries of statutes are accurate but unusable, because they do not tell an employee what to do when a customs official demands a fee, when a distributor offers to handle a permit, or when a client requests travel for a family member. Design policy around the decisions employees actually face.

For each high-risk scenario, the policy should state:

- what the rule is in plain language;
- what the employee should do, step by step;
- who to contact and how to escalate;
- what documentation is required;
- what the consequences of violation are;
- where the boundaries and exceptions are.

Organize the policy by situation, gifts, hospitality, intermediaries, government interaction, procurement, charitable giving, not only by legal provision.

### Tailor Training To Role And Risk

Generic annual training that treats every employee the same wastes the time of low-risk staff and under-prepares high-risk staff. The DOJ guidance explicitly asks whether training is tailored to the audience. Design training intensity and content to match role risk.

Tailor by role:

- sales and business development personnel need scenarios on intermediaries, commissions, hospitality, and government interaction;
- procurement staff need scenarios on vendor conflicts, kickbacks, and bid integrity;
- government-facing and regulatory affairs staff need scenarios on gifts, travel, revolving door, and political engagement;
- finance and accounts payable staff need scenarios on false invoices, off-book accounts, and payment red flags;
- senior leaders need scenarios on tone, escalation, investigations, and self-disclosure decisions;
- logistics and operations staff need scenarios on facilitation payments and extortion;
- third parties and agents need training on the company's expectations and their obligations.

Higher-risk roles should receive more frequent, deeper, and scenario-driven training, not the same annual module as everyone else.

### Use Scenario-Based Training That Tests Judgment

Knowledge of the rule is not the same as the ability to apply it. Scenario-based training tests whether the employee can recognize a red flag, decide what to do, and escalate correctly. Build training around realistic scenarios drawn from the company's actual geography, business model, and prior incidents.

Effective scenarios include:

- a customs official demanding an expedited fee;
- a distributor offering to handle licensing for a success fee;
- a procurement evaluator requesting a plant visit with tourism;
- a consultant invoice with vague descriptions;
- a request to make a charitable contribution at an official's direction;
- a competitor suggesting coordination on a tender;
- a high-performing salesperson whose expenses cluster near thresholds;
- a former official being considered for hire.

Each scenario should require the employee to choose a course of action, explain it, and receive feedback. Scenario training that only presents information without testing decisions does not build judgment.

### Make The Policy Reachable At The Point Of Decision

A policy stored on an intranet is not reachable when an employee is at a border, in a negotiation, or reviewing an invoice at quarter-end. Deliver guidance where decisions happen: quick-reference cards, decision tools embedded in approval workflows, escalation contacts in mobile-accessible formats, and just-in-time prompts in relevant systems. The best policy is the one the employee can find and use in the moment.

### Build Certification That Means Something

Certification and attestation are evidence of communication, but only if they are meaningful. A click-through certification that no one reads provides documentation without comprehension. Design certification to confirm understanding, not only receipt.

Strengthen certification by:

- requiring acknowledgment of the specific provisions relevant to the role;
- including a few comprehension questions tied to scenarios;
- requiring disclosure of unresolved issues or exceptions during the period;
- refreshing at a cadence tied to risk, not only annually;
- capturing the version acknowledged so updates are traceable;
- linking certification failure or non-completion to consequences.

### Reinforce Continuously, Not Only Annually

Annual training decays. Behavior is shaped by continuous reinforcement: leadership messaging, real examples communicated after incidents, reminders tied to high-risk periods such as quarter-end or tender seasons, and integration of anti-corruption expectations into performance and incentive systems. Build a reinforcement cadence so that the message is present when decisions are made.

### Train Leaders On Tone And Escalation

Senior leaders set the tone and make the hardest calls on investigations, self-disclosure, and discipline. Leaders need training not only on the rules but on how their behavior signals what is tolerated, how to respond to reports without retaliation, when to escalate, and how to weigh commercial pressure against compliance. Leader training is often the weakest part of the program and the most consequential.

### Measure Effectiveness, Not Only Completion

Completion rates measure delivery, not effectiveness. Measure whether the program is working by tracking comprehension, the quality and frequency of questions and reports, the handling of escalations, the pattern of exceptions, and the recurrence of incidents. Use the data to refine content, target weak spots, and demonstrate effectiveness to regulators and the board.

### Include Third Parties In The Training Reach

Agents, distributors, and consultants acting on the company's behalf create the company's liability, and their understanding of the rules matters as much as employees'. Require training or certification for high-risk third parties, and document it. A third party that cannot or will not complete the training is itself a red flag.

## Common Traps

### Writing Policy As A Legal Summary

Accurate but unusable policy does not change behavior. Write for decisions, not for coverage.

### One-Size Training For Everyone

Treating all employees the same under-prepares the highest-risk roles and wastes the time of low-risk staff. Tailor by role and risk.

### Presenting Without Testing

Training that informs without testing decisions builds awareness, not judgment. Use scenarios that require action.

### Storing Policy Where Decisions Do Not Happen

An intranet policy is unreachable in the field. Deliver guidance at the point of decision.

### Click-Through Certification

Certification that no one reads provides false evidence of communication. Test comprehension and capture disclosures.

### Annual-Only Reinforcement

Annual training decays before the next high-risk period. Reinforce continuously and tie reminders to risk events.

### Excluding Third Parties

Third parties create the company's liability and often receive no training. Extend training reach to high-risk intermediaries.

### Measuring Only Completion

Completion is delivery, not effectiveness. Measure comprehension, reporting, escalation quality, and incident recurrence.

## Self-Check

- Is the policy organized around the decisions employees face, with plain-language rules, steps, escalation contacts, documentation, and consequences, rather than legal summaries?
- Is training tailored by role and risk, with deeper and more frequent content for sales, procurement, government-facing, finance, and senior leader audiences?
- Does training use realistic, scenario-based exercises that test judgment and require the employee to choose and explain a course of action?
- Is guidance reachable at the point of decision through quick-reference tools, embedded workflow prompts, and mobile-accessible escalation contacts?
- Does certification test comprehension and capture disclosures rather than functioning as a click-through acknowledgment?
- Is the message reinforced continuously through leadership messaging, real examples, risk-period reminders, and integration with performance and incentives?
- Are leaders trained on tone, escalation, non-retaliation, and the balance of commercial pressure and compliance?
- Is effectiveness measured through comprehension, reporting quality, escalation handling, exception patterns, and incident recurrence, not only completion rates?
- Are high-risk third parties included in the training reach with documented completion?
- Is training content refreshed from real incidents, geography, and business-model scenarios rather than reused unchanged year after year?
