# Anti Corruption Investigations And Self Disclosure

How a company responds to an allegation of corruption often determines the outcome more than the underlying conduct. A prompt, thorough, and well-scoped investigation can identify the scope of the problem, preserve evidence, protect employees who raise concerns, and position the company for remediation and, where appropriate, voluntary self-disclosure. A mishandled investigation, delayed, narrow, conflicted, or overbroad, can destroy evidence, taint witnesses, waive privilege, and convert a manageable issue into an enforcement disaster. The self-disclosure decision is among the highest-stakes judgments a compliance program makes, because disclosure can secure cooperation credit and declination while a failure to disclose can be treated as concealment. The compliance problem is that both investigation and disclosure decisions must be made quickly, under uncertainty, and often before the facts are clear.

Use this skill before scoping an internal anti-corruption investigation, preserving evidence, deciding on privilege and counsel, evaluating a voluntary self-disclosure to the DOJ or other authorities, or planning remediation. The goal is to make the agent treat the response as a sequence of high-stakes, time-sensitive decisions where process discipline, independence, and documentation matter as much as the factual findings.

## Core Rules

### Triage Every Allegation Promptly And Document The Decision

Every corruption allegation, whether from a hotline, an audit, an exit interview, or a third party, should be triaged promptly by a defined function. Triage decides whether to open an investigation, the initial scope, the urgency, and the team. Document the triage decision and its rationale even when the conclusion is no action, because a later regulator will ask what the company knew and when, and why it did or did not investigate.

Triage should assess:

- the specificity and credibility of the allegation;
- the seniority and role of the people implicated;
- the jurisdictions and business units involved;
- the potential legal and financial exposure;
- the risk of evidence loss or witness coordination;
- whether the conduct is ongoing;
- whether government touchpoints or officials are involved;
- whether there is a legal or regulatory duty to disclose.

### Preserve Evidence Immediately

Evidence preservation must begin as soon as a credible allegation is identified, before the investigation scope is finalized. Employees can delete messages, documents can disappear, and systems overwrite logs. Delay in preservation is the most common and most damaging investigation failure.

Preserve:

- email, chat, and messaging data for implicated personnel;
- documents, spreadsheets, and shared drives;
- expense, payment, and accounting records;
- vendor master and contract files;
- travel, gift, and hospitality records;
- system and access logs;
- mobile devices and laptops as appropriate;
- third-party and intermediary records.

Issue a litigation-style preservation hold promptly, and confirm compliance with the hold. Document what was preserved, when, and by whom.

### Scope The Investigation To The Allegation And The Risk

Scope drives cost, speed, and credibility. A scope that is too narrow misses related misconduct; a scope that is too broad becomes unmanageable and delays findings. Scope should be tied to the specific allegation and the risk factors identified at triage, and it should be revisable as facts develop.

Define scope around:

- the specific transactions, payments, or relationships at issue;
- the time period, entities, and jurisdictions;
- the categories of evidence and witnesses;
- the legal questions to be answered;
- the relationships to other investigations or audits;
- the privilege and reporting strategy.

Document the scope and the reasons for any expansion or contraction.

### Ensure Independence And Appropriate Privilege Handling

An investigation into senior personnel, high-exposure conduct, or government-facing activity must be independent of the business unit involved. Independence protects credibility and reduces the risk of interference. Privilege, where available, protects the investigation's work product and the company's legal strategy.

Address independence and privilege by:

- using independent counsel or investigators for high-risk matters, separate from day-to-day business advisors;
- structuring the investigation at the direction of legal counsel to preserve privilege where applicable;
- ensuring the investigators have access, authority, and resources;
- avoiding conflicts of interest in the investigation team;
- reporting to an appropriate authority within governance, such as the audit committee, for the most serious matters;
- documenting the independence arrangements.

Recognize that privilege rules vary by jurisdiction and that some work product may be discoverable; design the investigation accordingly.

### Conduct Interviews With Discipline

Witness interviews are where investigations succeed or fail. Poorly conducted interviews elicit incomplete or coached testimony, create inconsistency, and can tip off subjects who then coordinate or destroy evidence.

Interview discipline includes:

- interviewing documentary witnesses before key subjects where possible;
- preparing from the documents rather than from assumptions;
- avoiding leading questions that suggest desired answers;
- documenting interviews contemporaneously;
- controlling the sequence to prevent coordination;
- using appropriate cautions and, where required, representation;
- escalating to compelled cooperation or external authority where internal cooperation fails.

### Evaluate Voluntary Self-Disclosure Deliberately

The decision to voluntarily self-disclose to the DOJ, SEC, or other authorities is one of the most consequential judgments in an anti-corruption matter. Disclosure can secure cooperation credit, declination, or reduced penalties under frameworks such as the DOJ Corporate Enforcement Policy; failure to disclose can forfeit those benefits and be treated as aggravating. The decision must be made deliberately, with counsel, and on the facts.

Consider in the disclosure decision:

- whether the conduct is likely to be discovered anyway by regulators or others;
- whether there is a legal or regulatory duty to disclose;
- the strength of the evidence and the likelihood of substantiation;
- the timing, since delayed disclosure may lose credit;
- the adequacy of the investigation to date;
- the remediation underway or planned;
- the potential for parallel proceedings in multiple jurisdictions;
- the impact on individuals, including potential criminal exposure;
- the company's overall posture and risk tolerance, informed by counsel.

Disclosure is generally a decision for senior management and the board, with legal advice, not for the compliance function alone. Document the decision and its rationale.

### Cooperate Meaningfully If Disclosure Is Made

Cooperation credit depends on meaningful cooperation, not merely on disclosure. That includes preserving evidence, making witnesses available, providing facts developed in the investigation, and timely updating the government on developments. Cooperation should be conducted under legal guidance and should be documented, because the company may need to demonstrate the nature and extent of its cooperation.

### Remediate Substantiated Misconduct

When misconduct is substantiated, remediation is both an ethical obligation and a central factor in how authorities view the company. Remediation should address the root causes, the control failures, the individuals involved, and the cultural and incentive drivers.

Remediation includes:

- disciplinary action against involved personnel, proportionate and consistent;
- termination or suspension of involved third parties;
- recovery of improper payments where possible;
- control fixes targeted at the specific failure;
- enhanced diligence or monitoring in the affected area;
- training and communication reinforcing expectations;
- changes to incentives that may have driven the conduct;
- confirmation that the conduct has ceased.

Document the remediation and its timing, since prompt and effective remediation is itself a factor in enforcement outcomes.

### Protect Those Who Raise Concerns

Retaliation against whistleblowers and witnesses destroys the reporting channel and is itself a violation. Make non-retaliation explicit, monitor the treatment of those who raise concerns, and act quickly on any indication of retaliation. The integrity of the investigation and of the program depends on people being willing to come forward.

### Capture Lessons And Feed Them Back

Every investigation should produce lessons that strengthen the program. Feed findings back into risk assessment, training, controls, and monitoring. An investigation that resolves a single matter without changing the program has not delivered its full value, and a pattern of similar findings indicates that prior lessons were not absorbed.

## Common Traps

### Delaying Triage And Preservation

The most damaging failure is slow response. Evidence is lost and the company's position deteriorates with every day of delay.

### Investigating With Conflicted Or Insufficient Resources

An investigation run by the business unit involved, or by investigators without authority or access, lacks credibility and invites interference.

### Scoping Too Narrowly To Limit Findings

A scope designed to avoid uncomfortable findings will miss related misconduct and will be apparent to regulators as a controlled exercise.

### Overlooking The Self-Disclosure Window

Disclosure credit is often time-sensitive. Delaying the decision past the window can forfeit benefits that prompt disclosure would have secured.

### Treating Cooperation As Automatic

Cooperation requires affirmative, meaningful acts. Passive or selective cooperation may not earn credit.

### Remediation That Stops At Discipline

Disciplining individuals without fixing the controls, incentives, and culture that enabled the conduct leaves the root cause in place.

### Retaliation Against Witnesses

Retaliation silences the reporting channel and creates a separate, serious violation. Protect those who come forward.

### Failing To Learn From Findings

Repeated similar findings indicate that lessons were not fed back into the program. Close the loop.

## Self-Check

- Is every allegation triaged promptly by a defined function, with the decision and rationale documented whether or not an investigation is opened?
- Is evidence, email, documents, payment records, devices, and logs, preserved immediately under a documented hold?
- Is the investigation scope tied to the allegation and risk, documented, and revisable as facts develop?
- Is the investigation independent of the involved business unit, with appropriate privilege handling and reporting to the right governance authority?
- Are witness interviews conducted with discipline, sequenced to prevent coordination, and documented contemporaneously?
- Is the voluntary self-disclosure decision made deliberately with counsel, considering discovery likelihood, legal duties, evidence strength, timing, remediation, and multi-jurisdictional exposure?
- If disclosure is made, is cooperation meaningful, documented, and conducted under legal guidance?
- Does remediation address discipline, third-party termination, recovery, control fixes, monitoring, training, incentives, and confirmation that conduct ceased?
- Are whistleblowers and witnesses protected from retaliation, with monitoring and prompt action on any retaliation signal?
- Are investigation findings fed back into risk assessment, training, controls, and monitoring so that the program learns and does not repeat the same findings?
