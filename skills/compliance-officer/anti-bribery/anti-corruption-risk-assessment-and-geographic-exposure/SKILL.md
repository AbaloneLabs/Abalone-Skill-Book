# Anti Corruption Risk Assessment And Geographic Exposure

An anti-corruption program that is not risk-based is not defensible. The DOJ Evaluation of Corporate Compliance Programs, the UK Bribery Act adequate-procedures guidance, and the OECD Good Practice Guidance all require that anti-corruption effort be proportionate to risk. A company that applies the same controls to a low-risk domestic supplier and to a high-risk government-facing intermediary in a corrupt jurisdiction is wasting resources on one and under-protecting the other. The compliance problem is that corruption risk is multidimensional, it depends on geography, industry, government interaction, third-party use, transaction structure, and prior conduct, and that a risk assessment that treats any single factor as decisive will misallocate the entire program.

Use this skill before conducting or refreshing an anti-corruption risk assessment, prioritizing controls by geography or business unit, deciding where to deploy enhanced diligence, or evaluating whether the program's intensity matches its exposure. The goal is to make the agent build a multidimensional risk picture tied to specific operations, not a generic country ranking, and to use that picture to drive where controls are deepest.

## Core Rules

### Assess Inherent Risk Before Controls

Inherent risk is the exposure before considering what the company does to mitigate it. Assessing inherent risk first prevents the common error of rating a high-risk operation as low simply because controls exist, when those controls may be untested or ineffective. Inherent risk drives where controls should be most intense.

Assess inherent risk across:

- geography and country risk;
- industry and sector;
- government touchpoints and official interaction;
- third-party and intermediary use;
- transaction and payment structure;
- business model and go-to-market channel;
- regulatory and licensing exposure;
- prior incidents and enforcement history;
- growth pressure and incentive intensity;
- ownership structure, including state ownership of counterparties.

### Map Geographic And Country Risk Specifically

Geography is one of the strongest predictors of corruption exposure, but it must be mapped to actual operations, not applied as a generic label. Use recognized indices as inputs, not as conclusions, and combine them with operational knowledge of where the company actually transacts, hires, ships, and seeks licenses.

Geographic inputs:

- Transparency International Corruption Perceptions Index scores and trends;
- country risk ratings from export credit and multilateral agencies;
- enforcement history and active investigations in the jurisdiction;
- the presence of state-owned enterprises as material counterparties;
- customs, licensing, and permitting friction reported by operations;
- local-law anti-corruption strength and enforcement record;
- political instability and governance quality;
- sanctions and export-control exposure overlaid on corruption risk.

Map each country to the specific business activity there, because the same country can be high-risk for government procurement and lower-risk for arm's-length commercial sales.

### Map Industry And Sector Risk

Some industries are structurally more exposed to corruption because of their government interaction, their contract size, their use of intermediaries, or their regulatory intensity. An industry lens refines the geographic picture.

Higher-risk sectors and characteristics:

- defense, aerospace, and government procurement-heavy industries;
- extractive industries and natural resource concessions;
- pharmaceuticals and medical devices with public health systems;
- construction and infrastructure with public contracts;
- financial services with licensing and regulatory dependence;
- telecommunications with spectrum and licensing;
- logistics and customs-intensive operations;
- any sector where a small number of officials control large awards.

### Map Government Touchpoints Precisely

Corruption risk concentrates where company employees and money meet government officials. The most useful risk-assessment output is a map of the specific touchpoints, because that map drives where controls are deployed.

Map touchpoints including:

- procurement and tender participation;
- customs, import, and export interactions;
- licensing, permitting, and registration;
- inspections, audits, and regulatory examinations;
- tax, customs, and duty interactions;
- interactions with state-owned enterprise personnel;
- government-facing sales and marketing;
- political engagement and lobbying;
- charitable and community engagement directed by officials.

Each touchpoint should be rated by frequency, value at stake, and the discretion of the officials involved.

### Evaluate Third-Party And Intermediary Exposure

Intermediaries are the dominant corruption vector, and their risk depends on their function, location, compensation, and government interaction. Incorporate third-party risk into the assessment by aggregating the risk ratings of the intermediary population, not by treating third parties generically.

Assess:

- the number and risk tier of agents, distributors, and consultants;
- the jurisdictions where intermediaries operate;
- the government interaction of intermediaries;
- commission and compensation structures;
- the concentration of payments to a few high-risk intermediaries;
- the use of intermediaries recommended by officials.

### Incorporate Transaction And Payment Structure

How money moves is itself a risk factor. Cash-intensive operations, large success fees, payments to offshore accounts, and complex payment chains all elevate risk independent of geography. Include transaction structure in the assessment so that controls address the mechanics of concealment, not only the location of the activity.

### Factor In Growth Pressure And Incentives

Corruption often follows pressure: aggressive growth targets, entry into new markets, deadline-driven deals, and incentive structures that reward revenue at any cost. A risk assessment that ignores the commercial pressure environment misses a primary driver of misconduct. Assess incentive intensity, quota structure, and the historical relationship between pressure periods and compliance exceptions.

### Use Prior Incidents And Enforcement Intelligence

The company's own history is a leading indicator. Prior investigations, hotline reports, audit findings, terminated intermediaries, and disciplinary actions point to where risk has materialized. External enforcement against peers in the same sector and geography indicates where regulators are focused. Weight both heavily, and treat a pattern of incidents in a business unit or geography as a mandate for enhanced controls.

### Drive The Program From The Assessment and reassess On Triggers, Not Only On A Calendar

A risk assessment that does not change the program is paperwork. Each high-risk finding should drive a specific control response: enhanced diligence, deeper monitoring, targeted training, approval authority changes, or audit focus. Document the link between the risk finding and the control response so that the program can be defended as risk-based.

Risk changes. New markets, new products, acquisitions, new intermediaries, regulatory shifts, and incidents all change the profile. Reassess on defined triggers in addition to a periodic cycle, and keep a record of the trigger, the reassessment, and the resulting changes.

## Common Traps

### Using A Country Index As The Whole Assessment

Country risk is one input. A CPI score does not capture industry, touchpoint, intermediary, or transaction risk, and applying it alone misallocates the program.

### Rating Residual Risk To Look Good

Rating an operation low because controls exist, before testing those controls, hides the real exposure. Assess inherent risk first.

### Ignoring The Touchpoint Map

Generic country ratings do not tell employees where to focus. The touchpoint map is the operational output that drives daily decisions.

### Treating All Third Parties As The Same Risk

A low-value domestic distributor and a government-connected commission agent in a corrupt jurisdiction are not the same risk. Tier and aggregate intermediary risk.

### Overlooking Incentive Pressure

Aggressive targets and deadline pressure are proximate causes of many violations. Include the commercial pressure environment in the assessment.

### Failing To Link Findings To Controls

A risk assessment that produces no change in the program provides no protection and signals that the assessment is performative. Connect each finding to a control response.

### Reassessing Only Annually

Annual cycles miss the new market, the acquisition, and the incident. Reassess on triggers and document the changes.

## Self-Check

- Is inherent anti-corruption risk assessed before controls across geography, industry, government touchpoints, intermediaries, transaction structure, regulatory exposure, prior incidents, and incentive pressure?
- Is geographic risk mapped to actual operations using recognized indices combined with operational knowledge, rather than applied as a generic country label?
- Are higher-risk industries and sector characteristics identified and incorporated into the assessment?
- Is there a precise map of government touchpoints, procurement, customs, licensing, inspections, SOE interactions, rated by frequency, value, and official discretion?
- Is third-party and intermediary risk aggregated by tier, jurisdiction, government interaction, compensation, and concentration?
- Are transaction and payment structures, cash intensity, success fees, offshore routing, assessed as independent risk factors?
- Are growth targets, incentive intensity, and historical pressure-period exceptions factored into the assessment?
- Are prior incidents, hotline reports, audit findings, and peer enforcement weighted as leading indicators?
- Does each high-risk finding drive a specific, documented control response so the program is demonstrably risk-based?
- Is the assessment refreshed on triggers such as new markets, products, acquisitions, intermediaries, and incidents, not only on an annual calendar?
