# Regulatory Obligation Mapping

Regulatory obligation mapping is the discipline of connecting each legal and regulatory requirement the organization must meet to the specific controls, processes, owners, and evidence that satisfy it. It is the backbone of demonstrable compliance, because it lets the organization answer, for any obligation, "what do we do about this, who does it, and can we prove it." Without obligation mapping, controls float free of the requirements they are meant to satisfy, and the organization cannot tell a regulator or auditor whether a given rule is actually covered. The work is unglamorous and detailed, and it is exactly where programs succeed or fail under scrutiny. The trap is treating the obligation register as a legal inventory that stops at the citation, never reaching the operational control and the evidence.

Use this skill before building an obligation register, mapping obligations to controls, responding to a regulatory change, preparing traceability for an exam, or diagnosing why a control exists. The goal is to make the agent build end-to-end traceability from citation to evidence, with ownership and change management.

## Core Rules

### Capture Obligations At The Right Level Of Granularity

Obligations mapped at too high a level, such as "comply with GDPR," are unactionable. Mapped at too low a level, they become unmanageable. Find the actionable unit.

Decompose obligations to the level that:

- corresponds to a distinct duty or prohibition;
- can be assigned to a single control or process;
- has a clear pass or fail condition;
- can be tested independently;
- carries its own penalty or consequence.

For example, decompose a data protection law into duties such as lawful basis, notice, access response, breach notification, and transfer safeguards. Each becomes a mappable obligation with its own control and evidence. Capture the citation, the jurisdiction, the effective date, and the authority for each.

### Build End-To-End Traceability From Citation To Evidence

The core value of obligation mapping is traceability. Every obligation should connect through to the control and the evidence.

For each obligation, map:

- the citation and source authority;
- the obligation text or plain-language summary;
- the business process or activity it governs;
- the control or controls that satisfy it, preventive and detective;
- the control owner and operator;
- the evidence the control produces;
- the testing or monitoring that validates it;
- the residual risk and any open issues.

This chain is what a regulator follows during an exam. If the chain breaks anywhere, the obligation is not demonstrably covered. Build the full chain and keep it intact.

### Assign Ownership For Each Obligation

Obligations without owners are unmet obligations. Ownership must be explicit and at the right line.

Assign:

- a first-line owner accountable for executing the control that satisfies the obligation;
- a second-line owner, typically compliance, accountable for interpretation, framework, and challenge;
- a senior sponsor for significant obligations;
- clarity on who updates the mapping when the obligation or control changes.

Ownership should reflect the three-lines model. The business executes; compliance oversees; audit assures. Document the ownership in the register.

### Manage Regulatory Change As A Structured Process

Obligations change constantly through new rules, amendments, guidance, and repeals. A static register becomes wrong quickly. Build a change process.

The change process should:

- monitor regulatory sources for each jurisdiction and regime;
- screen incoming changes for applicability;
- assess the impact on existing obligations, controls, and processes;
- assign remediation with owner and deadline;
- update the register and control mapping;
- evidence that changes were assessed and acted upon within required timeframes.

A register that cannot show how it handled a recent regulatory change is a liability. Make change management a visible, evidenced process.

### Reconcile Overlapping And Conflicting Obligations

Organizations frequently face overlapping or conflicting obligations across jurisdictions and regimes. The mapping must reconcile them rather than leaving the conflict for the first line to discover.

Reconcile:

- overlapping duties by mapping to a single control where possible, with cross-references;
- stricter obligations by applying the highest common standard where feasible;
- genuine conflicts through legal analysis and documented decisions;
- extraterritorial and local obligations that reach the same activity.

Document the reconciliation so that a regulator can see the reasoning. Unresolved conflicts that surface during an exam signal weak program management.

### Connect Mapping To Risk Assessment And Monitoring

Obligation mapping should not be a standalone legal artifact. It should feed the risk assessment and the monitoring plan.

Connect by:

- using the obligation register as an input to inherent risk rating;
- prioritizing monitoring and testing toward obligations with high risk or weak controls;
- feeding obligation changes into risk reassessment triggers;
- linking testing results back to the obligation to show whether it is met.

When mapping, risk assessment, and monitoring are disconnected, each becomes weaker. Integrate them into one operating loop.

### Make The Register Exam-Ready And Auditable

The register must be producible and navigable during an exam or audit. A sprawling spreadsheet that no one can query is not exam-ready.

Make it auditable by:

- structuring it so an examiner can trace any obligation to its control and evidence;
- keeping version history and change logs;
- supporting filtering by jurisdiction, regime, process, owner, and risk;
- producing summary views for the board and detailed views for examiners;
- validating periodically that links are intact and evidence is current.

An examiner who can quickly trace obligations to evidence forms a positive view of the program. One who cannot forms the opposite.

## Common Traps

### Stopping At The Citation

A register that lists legal citations but never reaches controls and evidence is a legal inventory, not obligation mapping. Build the full chain.

### Wrong Granularity

Obligations mapped too high are unactionable; too low are unmanageable. Decompose to the actionable, testable unit.

### Unowned Obligations

Obligations without first-line and second-line owners go unmet. Assign ownership explicitly.

### Static Register

A register that does not track regulatory change becomes wrong and misleading. Build a structured change process.

### Unresolved Overlaps And Conflicts

Overlapping or conflicting obligations left for the first line to navigate create inconsistency and gaps. Reconcile and document.

### Disconnected From Risk And Monitoring

A register that does not feed risk assessment and monitoring is a dead document. Integrate the three.

### Un-Navigable For Exams

A sprawling, unstructured register cannot be used under exam pressure. Make it queryable and version-controlled.

## Self-Check

- Are obligations decomposed to an actionable, testable level of granularity with citation, jurisdiction, effective date, and authority?
- Does each obligation have end-to-end traceability from citation to control, owner, evidence, testing, residual risk, and issue status?
- Is ownership assigned at first-line, second-line, and sponsor levels for every obligation?
- Is regulatory change managed through a structured process of monitoring, applicability screening, impact assessment, remediation, and evidenced update?
- Are overlapping and conflicting obligations reconciled through single controls, highest-common-standard application, or documented legal decisions?
- Does the register feed the risk assessment and monitoring plan, and do testing results feed back to obligation status?
- Is the register exam-ready, structured, queryable, version-controlled, and periodically validated for intact links and current evidence?
- Could an examiner trace any obligation to its control and evidence quickly and confidently?
- Is the mapping maintained as a living operational asset rather than a one-time legal project?
