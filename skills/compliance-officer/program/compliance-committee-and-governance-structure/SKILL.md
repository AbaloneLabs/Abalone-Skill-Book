# Compliance Committee And Governance Structure

A compliance program is governed through bodies, not through a single hero. The board, a board committee, a management compliance committee, working groups, and first-line risk owners all play distinct roles. When these bodies are poorly defined, compliance decisions either stall in committee or get made informally by whoever shouts loudest. Regulators look at whether oversight is real: whether the right people attend, whether decisions are documented, whether escalation reaches the board, and whether the committee can actually change business behavior. A governance structure that exists only to receive reports is not oversight.

Use this skill before establishing a compliance committee, revising governance roles, defining decision rights between the board and management, setting meeting cadence, or diagnosing why compliance decisions are not being made or enforced. The goal is to make the agent define membership, authority, decision rights, escalation, and evidence rather than leaving governance to custom.

## Core Rules

### Separate The Layers Of Oversight

Governance fails when layers blur. The board sets strategy and holds management accountable; a board committee provides focused oversight; a management committee runs the program and resolves operational issues; working groups execute on specific risk domains. Each layer has a different question to answer.

Clarify the distinct purpose of each body:

- the board or board committee asks whether the program is effective, adequately resourced, and aligned with strategy, and whether leaders are accountable;
- the management compliance committee sets program priorities, allocates resources, resolves cross-functional issues, and reviews significant risks and incidents;
- working groups or risk councils handle specific domains such as anti-bribery, sanctions, privacy, trade, or conduct, and escalate to the management committee;
- first-line risk owners execute controls and report upward.

If the board committee is doing operational work, or the management committee is doing board work, the layers are confused. Align authority to the right altitude.

### Define Membership With The Right Authority And Independence

A compliance committee is only as strong as its members. If attendees lack authority to commit their functions, decisions are deferred and never executed.

For each body, define:

- the chair and whether the chair is independent of the business being overseen;
- voting versus non-voting or advisory members;
- required functions represented, such as compliance, legal, risk, finance, HR, technology, security, operations, and business leadership;
- whether business leaders are members or attendees, and how conflicts are managed when their units are under review;
- the CCO's role, typically as chair or secretary with a reporting line to the board;
- quorum and decision rules;
- attendance expectations and consequences for chronic absence.

The business must be represented so decisions are executable, but compliance must not be outnumbered or outvoted on matters of legal obligation. Structure the balance deliberately.

### Assign Decision Rights Explicitly

The most common governance failure is ambiguity about who decides what. Committees stall because no one is sure whether they can approve, or decisions get made outside the committee by whoever acts first.

Create a decision-rights matrix that maps:

- which body approves the compliance charter, risk assessment, policies, and program changes;
- which body approves risk acceptances and exceptions, and at what threshold;
- which body decides on investigations, disciplinary referrals, and self-disclosures;
- which body can halt a product, payment, or relationship;
- which decisions require board approval versus management approval;
- the escalation path when a committee cannot agree or when an issue exceeds its authority.

Without a matrix, authority defaults to whoever is loudest or most senior in the room. Write it down and approve it at the board level.

### Set A Cadence That Matches Risk Velocity

Meeting too rarely means risks age without attention; meeting too often with thin agendas breeds fatigue and rubber-stamping. Cadence should follow the pace of risk.

Set:

- board or committee oversight frequency, typically quarterly with an annual deep dive;
- management committee frequency, typically monthly or at least quarterly;
- working group frequency driven by the risk domain, such as weekly for sanctions operations or quarterly for policy review;
- triggers for ad hoc meetings, such as a major incident, enforcement action, exam, acquisition, or regulatory change;
- a requirement that the agenda be risk-driven, not routine report reading.

A committee that meets only to receive status slides is not governing. Build agendas around decisions, risks, and exceptions, not around attendance.

### Define Escalation And De-Escalation Paths

Governance must move information up and decisions down. A strong structure defines how an issue travels from first line to board, and how a board directive travels back.

Define:

- what must be escalated, such as red-flag findings, significant control failures, high-risk exceptions, regulator contacts, and material incidents;
- the timeframes for escalation at each layer;
- who is notified and who decides at each level;
- how the board is informed of significant issues, including the right to private sessions with the CCO and internal audit;
- how board directives and risk decisions are communicated back down and tracked to completion;
- when and how an issue is de-escalated or closed, and what evidence is required.

Escalation that depends on someone choosing to speak up is unreliable. Make the triggers and paths automatic.

### Require Evidence And Documentation Of Decisions

Governance that is not recorded did not happen, from a regulator's perspective. Every oversight body must leave a trail.

Maintain:

- agendas, minutes, attendance, and conflict declarations;
- decisions made, with rationale, owner, and deadline;
- action items tracked to completion with status;
- materials reviewed, including risk reports, testing results, investigation summaries, and exception logs;
- evidence that the committee challenged management rather than only receiving reports;
- a record of escalations to and directives from the board.

Minutes that say "the committee noted the report" are weak evidence of oversight. Record the questions asked, the decisions taken, and the follow-up required.

### Connect Governance To Accountability And Incentives

A committee structure changes behavior only when its decisions affect how people are measured, paid, and promoted. Governance disconnected from incentives is advisory.

Ensure:

- compliance performance is part of leader scorecards and compensation;
- the committee can refer misconduct or interference for discipline;
- exception approvals and risk acceptances are visible to the responsible leader's management;
- the board evaluates executives partly on compliance and culture outcomes;
- chronic exception approvers or incident-prone units receive scrutiny.

## Common Traps

### Creating A Committee Only To Receive Reports

If the committee's main activity is listening to updates, it is not governing. Governance requires decisions, challenges, and follow-up.

### Letting The Business Outvote Compliance On Legal Obligations

Business leaders should inform decisions, but compliance obligations are not negotiable by majority vote. Protect compliance authority on matters of law and regulation.

### Ambiguous Decision Rights

When no one knows who can approve an exception or halt a launch, decisions drift to the informal path. A decision-rights matrix prevents this.

### Infrequent Or Ritual Cadence

Annual oversight of a fast-moving risk domain, or monthly meetings with no real agenda, produces governance theater. Match cadence to risk velocity.

### No Private Board Access For The CCO

If the CCO can only reach the board through the CEO, bad news is filtered. Regulators expect a direct, unfiltered line.

### Poorly Documented Decisions

Undocumented decisions cannot be evidenced later and look like no oversight at all. Minutes must capture decisions, rationale, owners, and follow-up.

### Overlapping Or Competing Bodies

Multiple committees with unclear boundaries create duplication, gaps, and turf battles. Map the bodies and their scopes once and resolve overlaps.

## Self-Check

- Are the layers of oversight, board, committee, management, and working groups, each given a distinct purpose and altitude?
- Is membership defined with chair independence, required functions, voting rules, quorum, and a deliberate balance between business and compliance authority?
- Is there a decision-rights matrix covering charter, policies, exceptions, risk acceptances, investigations, halts, and board-level approvals?
- Does meeting cadence match the velocity of the risks each body oversees, with ad hoc triggers for incidents and regulatory change?
- Are escalation and de-escalation paths defined with triggers, timeframes, notifications, board access, and closure evidence?
- Are agendas, minutes, decisions, rationale, action items, and board directives documented as evidence of real oversight?
- Is governance connected to accountability through scorecards, compensation, discipline, and board evaluation of executives?
- Does the CCO have a private, unfiltered line to the board or audit committee?
- Can the structure be evidenced to a regulator as active oversight rather than ceremonial reporting?
