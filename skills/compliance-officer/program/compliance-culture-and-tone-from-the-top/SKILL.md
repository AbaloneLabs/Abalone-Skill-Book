# Compliance Culture And Tone From The Top

Culture is what people do when no one is watching, and it is shaped far more by what leaders reward, tolerate, and ignore than by what policies say. A company can have a flawless code of conduct and still have a culture in which sales teams believe hitting target excuses cutting corners, employees fear retaliation for speaking up, and high performers are untouchable. Regulators and prosecutors now treat culture as a core component of program effectiveness, not as a soft topic. The difficulty is that culture cannot be measured by reading policies; it must be measured by behavior, perception, and outcomes. A culture assessment that relies on attestation and slogans will miss the real signals.

Use this skill before assessing culture, designing a culture survey, evaluating tone from the top, reviewing incentive alignment, investigating why misconduct occurred, or advising leaders on culture risk. The goal is to make the agent look at behavior, incentives, perception, and accountability rather than at stated values.

## Core Rules

### Measure Behavior And Outcomes, Not Just Stated Values

Stated values are aspirational and often identical across companies. Culture shows up in what actually happens. Measure the gap between what is said and what is done.

Behavioral and outcome indicators include:

- misconduct prevalence and the gap between observed and reported misconduct;
- hotline and speak-up volume, trends, and the proportion acted upon;
- retaliation reports and outcomes;
- disciplinary actions and whether they are consistent regardless of seniority or performance;
- exception and override frequency and who approves them;
- policy and training completion versus comprehension and application;
- exit interview themes;
- customer, patient, or employee complaint patterns;
- audit and testing findings that indicate normalization of deviance;
- whether high performers are disciplined the same as others.

If observed misconduct is high and reporting is low, or if discipline is inconsistent, the culture is weak regardless of the values poster.

### Assess Tone From The Top In Practice

Tone from the top is not the CEO's ethics video. It is the cumulative signal leaders send through decisions, priorities, and reactions under pressure.

Assess:

- how often and how specifically leaders talk about compliance in operational settings, not just in formal messages;
- whether leaders model the behavior expected, including escalating their own mistakes;
- how leaders react to bad news, whether with curiosity and support or with blame and punishment;
- whether leaders protect or sideline those who raise concerns;
- whether leaders celebrate results achieved through compliance shortcuts;
- whether leaders resource compliance or treat it as overhead;
- whether leaders intervene when middle managers create pressure;
- what leaders measure and reward, because people follow the metrics.

A leader who praises "whatever it takes" results while ignoring how they were achieved sets a tone that overrides any policy. Evaluate tone through decisions, not speeches.

### Examine Middle Management As Tone In The Middle

Middle managers are where culture is made or broken. They translate, distort, or reinforce the message from the top. Frontline employees experience the culture through their direct manager.

Assess:

- whether middle managers feel empowered and required to enforce compliance;
- the pressure middle managers face from above and how they pass it down;
- whether middle managers shield their teams from compliance demands to hit targets;
- whether managers are evaluated on compliance and culture, not only on delivery;
- whether managers know how to handle concerns raised by staff;
- the rate of compliance issues and turnover in specific manager spans.

A strong tone from the top with a weak tone in the middle produces a weak culture on the front line. Do not stop the assessment at the executive floor.

### Align Incentives, Consequences, And Accountability

People optimize for what is measured and rewarded. If compliance is not in the incentive structure, the culture will not support it regardless of training.

Review:

- whether variable compensation, bonuses, and commissions include compliance and culture components;
- whether compliance failures trigger clawback, forfeiture, or reduction;
- whether promotions consider compliance record and culture behavior;
- whether discipline is consistent across seniority, tenure, and performance level;
- whether high performers are exempt from consequences;
- whether leaders are held accountable for culture in their units;
- whether risk-taking is rewarded without regard to how risk was taken.

A system that pays for results and punishes for raising concerns will produce misconduct. Align the incentives with the behavior you want.

### Design Culture Measurement That Reaches The Truth

Culture surveys fail when employees do not trust that responses are anonymous, when questions are leading, and when nothing changes afterward. Design measurement to surface reality.

Use:

- anonymous, third-party-administered surveys with demographic cuts by unit, geography, tenure, and level;
- behavioral questions, such as whether the respondent has felt pressure to cut corners or has observed misconduct;
- psychological safety questions, such as whether it is safe to challenge a manager or raise bad news;
- triangulation through focus groups, exit interviews, hotline analysis, and pulse surveys;
- trend tracking over time and benchmarking against credible external data;
- follow-up to show that leadership acted on results.

Asking about culture and then ignoring the results damages trust more than not asking. Commit to action and communication.

### Investigate Culture As A Root Cause Of Misconduct

When misconduct occurs, culture is often a contributing factor. Treating each incident as a lone bad actor misses the pattern.

In investigations and root-cause analysis, examine:

- the incentives and pressure present at the time;
- whether the behavior was known, tolerated, or rewarded;
- whether prior concerns were raised and ignored;
- whether the control failure was technical or cultural;
- whether the unit has a history of similar issues;
- whether leaders created or ignored the conditions.

If the same culture conditions produce repeated misconduct, the fix is cultural, not just a new control.

### Connect Culture Remediation To Leadership Accountability

Culture change requires sustained leadership action, not a campaign. Make remediation specific and hold leaders accountable.

Define:

- the specific culture problems identified and the target state;
- the leadership actions required, such as changing metrics, discipline, communication, and resourcing;
- the owners, typically business leaders, not only compliance;
- the timeline and the measures of progress;
- the board visibility into culture risk and remediation;
- the consequences for leaders whose units do not improve.

Culture owned only by compliance will not change. Business leaders must own the culture in their units.

## Common Traps

### Equating Stated Values With Culture

Values statements are nearly identical across firms and tell you little. Measure behavior, perception, and outcomes.

### Trusting Attestation Over Behavior

High training completion and policy acknowledgement do not prove a strong culture. Look at reporting rates, retaliation, and discipline consistency.

### Stopping The Assessment At The Top

A strong executive message with weak middle management produces a weak front-line culture. Assess tone in the middle.

### Ignoring Incentive Misalignment

If pay and promotion reward only results, culture will follow the money. Compliance and culture must be in the incentive structure.

### Surveying And Then Doing Nothing

Asking about culture and ignoring the results destroys trust. Commit to action and communicate what changed.

### Exempting High Performers From Consequences

Inconsistent discipline signals that results excuse misconduct. Apply consequences regardless of performance.

### Treating Misconduct As Lone Bad Actors

Repeated incidents with shared culture conditions indicate a systemic problem. Investigate culture as a root cause.

## Self-Check

- Does the assessment measure behavior, perception, and outcomes rather than relying on stated values and attestation?
- Is tone from the top evaluated through leadership decisions, reactions to bad news, resourcing, and modeled behavior, not just speeches?
- Is tone in the middle assessed, including manager pressure, empowerment, evaluation, and front-line experience?
- Are incentives, consequences, clawback, promotion, and discipline reviewed for alignment with compliance and consistency across seniority and performance?
- Is culture measurement anonymous, third-party, behaviorally framed, triangulated, trended, and followed by visible action?
- Is culture examined as a root cause in misconduct investigations, including pressure, tolerance, and prior ignored concerns?
- Are culture remediation actions specific, owned by business leaders, time-bound, measured, and visible to the board?
- Are high performers held to the same consequences as others?
- Could the culture assessment be defended to a regulator as a genuine diagnosis rather than a public-relations exercise?
