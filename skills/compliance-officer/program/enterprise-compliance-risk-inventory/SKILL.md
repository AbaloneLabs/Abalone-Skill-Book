# Enterprise Compliance Risk Inventory

A compliance risk inventory is the master list of the compliance risks and obligations an organization faces. It is the foundation under the risk assessment, the control mapping, the monitoring plan, and the board reporting. When the inventory is incomplete or poorly structured, the program systematically misses risks because it never named them. The inventory is also where taxonomy discipline matters: if risks are defined at different levels of granularity, double-counted, or scattered across business-unit lists that never roll up, the organization cannot prioritize coherently and cannot prove to a regulator that it has identified its risk universe. The task is to build a structured, comprehensive, owned, and living inventory that connects obligations to risks to controls to evidence.

Use this skill before building a risk inventory, defining a risk taxonomy, compiling the universe of obligations, structuring a risk register, or consolidating fragmented risk lists into an enterprise view. The goal is to make the agent build completeness, consistency, ownership, and linkage rather than a flat list of worries.

## Core Rules

### Build The Universe Of Obligations First

A compliance risk inventory should be anchored to obligations, because obligations are concrete and traceable. Start by compiling what the organization must do.

Compile:

- laws and regulations by jurisdiction and activity;
- regulator rules, orders, and guidance;
- license and registration conditions;
- contract and customer obligations;
- consent orders, settlements, and undertakings;
- voluntary standards and certifications the organization has adopted;
- internal policies and commitments;
- industry and platform rules.

For each obligation, capture the source, the authority level, the jurisdiction, the activity it governs, and the penalty for breach. An inventory built from a generic risk list rather than from actual obligations will miss regime-specific duties and will be hard to evidence to a regulator.

### Define A Consistent Risk Taxonomy

A taxonomy is the structure that makes the inventory comparable and roll-up-able. Without consistent definitions, risks are counted differently and cannot be aggregated.

Define:

- a hierarchy from risk category to sub-category to specific risk;
- consistent naming so that "data privacy" means the same thing everywhere;
- the level of granularity, so risks are comparable in scope;
- relationships between risks, such as parent and child, or cause and consequence;
- boundaries between compliance risk and other risk types such as operational, financial, or strategic.

A taxonomy where one unit lists "FCPA risk" and another lists "improper payments to foreign officials" creates duplication and gaps. Standardize the language and structure.

### Ensure Completeness Through Multiple Sources

No single source produces a complete inventory. Triangulate to avoid blind spots.

Build completeness from:

- the business model, products, customers, geographies, and channels;
- the obligation universe compiled from laws and regulations;
- prior incidents, investigations, audit findings, and enforcement history;
- regulator priorities, risk alerts, and peer enforcement actions;
- complaints, hotline data, and litigation;
- third-party and supply-chain exposure;
- technology, data, and AI use;
- acquisitions, joint ventures, and new markets.

Cross-check the draft inventory against each source. A risk that exists in the business but not in the inventory is the most dangerous kind, because the program will not manage what it has not named.

### Assign Clear Ownership For Each Risk

Every risk in the inventory needs an accountable owner. Unowned risks are unmanaged risks.

For each risk, assign:

- a first-line owner accountable for executing controls;
- a second-line oversight owner, typically in compliance or risk;
- a senior executive sponsor for significant risks;
- clarity on who assesses, who remediates, and who reports.

Ownership should follow the three-lines model. The business owns the risk and the first-line controls; compliance provides oversight, frameworks, and challenge; internal audit provides independent assurance. Document the ownership explicitly.

### Link Risks To Obligations, Controls, And Evidence

An inventory is most useful when it connects each risk to the obligation that creates it, the controls that mitigate it, and the evidence that proves it. A flat list of risks cannot drive the program.

For each risk, link:

- the underlying obligation or source;
- the inherent risk rating;
- the controls, preventive and detective, that address it;
- the control owners and evidence;
- the residual risk rating;
- the monitoring and testing that covers it;
- the status of any open issues or remediation.

This linkage is what turns an inventory into an operating tool. It lets the organization see, for any obligation, what risk it creates, what controls address it, and whether they are working.

### Structure The Register For Roll-Up And Reporting

The inventory must roll up from specific risks to categories to an enterprise view, and must be reportable to different audiences.

Structure the register to support:

- enterprise, business-unit, and risk-domain views;
- reporting to the board on top risks and trends;
- reporting to business leaders on their owned risks;
- filtering by jurisdiction, product, process, or control owner;
- trend analysis over time;
- linkage to the risk assessment and the monitoring plan.

A register that only works at one level of detail cannot serve the board, the CCO, and the first line. Design it for multiple views from the start.

### Govern The Inventory As A Living Asset

An inventory written once becomes stale. Govern it as a living asset with maintenance, change control, and review.

Govern by:

- assigning a custodian, typically the compliance risk function;
- setting a refresh cycle, typically annual with interim updates;
- defining triggers for update, such as new products, jurisdictions, regulations, incidents, or acquisitions;
- version-controlling changes with rationale;
- validating completeness periodically through challenge and independent review.

A regulator will ask when the inventory was last updated and how the organization knows it is complete. Have a defensible answer.

## Common Traps

### Building From A Generic Risk List

An inventory copied from a template misses the organization's specific obligations. Anchor to the actual obligation universe.

### Inconsistent Taxonomy

Risks named and scoped differently across units create duplication and gaps. Standardize the taxonomy.

### Single-Source Completeness

Relying on one source, such as a legal scan, misses business-driven and emerging risks. Triangulate across sources.

### Unowned Risks

Risks without a clear owner are not managed. Assign first-line, second-line, and sponsor ownership.

### Flat List Without Linkage

A list of risks not linked to obligations, controls, and evidence cannot drive the program. Build the connections.

### No Roll-Up Structure

A register that works only at one level cannot serve multiple audiences. Design for enterprise, unit, and domain views.

### Static Inventory

An inventory never refreshed drifts out of relevance. Govern it as a living asset with triggers and review.

## Self-Check

- Is the inventory anchored to a compiled universe of obligations with source, authority, jurisdiction, activity, and penalty?
- Is a consistent risk taxonomy defined with hierarchy, naming, granularity, relationships, and boundaries to other risk types?
- Is completeness triangulated across business model, obligations, incidents, regulator priorities, complaints, third parties, technology, and acquisitions?
- Does every risk have clear first-line, second-line, and sponsor ownership?
- Is each risk linked to its obligation, inherent rating, controls, owners, evidence, residual rating, monitoring, and issue status?
- Does the register roll up to enterprise, business-unit, and domain views and support board, CCO, and first-line reporting?
- Is the inventory governed as a living asset with a custodian, refresh cycle, triggers, version control, and independent completeness review?
- Could the organization defend the inventory's completeness and structure to a regulator?
- Are gaps and overlaps between business-unit lists identified and reconciled into one enterprise view?
