# Compliance Program Risk Assessment

A compliance program should be built around the risks the organization actually faces. It is not enough to have a code of conduct, a policy library, and annual training if the program does not address the business model, geography, customers, products, data, payments, third parties, incentives, and prior misconduct. Compliance work fails when it becomes a generic checklist instead of a risk-based operating system.

Use this skill before designing a compliance program, refreshing a risk assessment, prioritizing compliance work, evaluating whether a program is effective, or advising an agent what to ask before recommending policies or controls. The goal is to make the agent identify risks, owners, evidence, resources, and monitoring before proposing a compliance plan.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Business Before Naming Risks

Compliance risk depends on what the organization does, where it does it, and who participates. Do not start from a template list of laws. Start from operations.

Map:

- products and services;
- customer types;
- jurisdictions;
- sales channels;
- regulated activities;
- licenses or registrations;
- payment flows;
- data collected and shared;
- government customers or funds;
- health, financial, employment, consumer, safety, environmental, export, antitrust, anti-corruption, privacy, or securities exposure;
- third-party relationships;
- acquisitions or integrations;
- use of AI, automated decision-making, or high-risk technology;
- prior issues, complaints, audits, enforcement, and investigations.

If the business model is unclear, the risk assessment is not ready. A compliance program cannot be evaluated in the abstract.

### Identify Applicable Obligations And Sources

Compliance risk should be tied to obligations. Vague phrases such as "follow applicable law" are not enough.

For each risk area, identify:

- governing law or regulation;
- regulator or enforcement authority;
- contract requirement;
- license condition;
- certification standard;
- consent order or settlement obligation;
- internal policy;
- industry rule;
- platform rule;
- customer requirement;
- recordkeeping or reporting duty.

Label the authority level. Binding law, regulator guidance, contractual duty, voluntary framework, and internal preference have different weight. Use current official sources when the output depends on legal or regulatory requirements, and route unclear obligations to legal or compliance counsel.

### Evaluate Inherent Risk Before Controls

Inherent risk is the risk before considering controls. It helps decide where the program should focus.

Assess:

- likelihood;
- impact;
- transaction volume;
- customer harm;
- regulatory penalty exposure;
- criminal, civil, or administrative consequences;
- privacy or security harm;
- financial exposure;
- reputational exposure;
- management incentives;
- geographic risk;
- complexity;
- change velocity;
- history of issues;
- external enforcement focus.

Do not let existing controls hide the underlying exposure. A low residual risk conclusion is not credible until the control design and operation have been evaluated.

### Match Controls To Specific Risks

Controls should prevent, detect, or correct a defined risk. They should not exist merely because a framework mentions them.

For each risk, define:

- control objective;
- policy or procedure;
- accountable owner;
- preventive control;
- detective control;
- escalation path;
- evidence generated;
- frequency;
- population covered;
- exception handling;
- system or manual dependency;
- testing method.

If a control does not leave evidence, it will be hard to prove that it worked. If a control has no owner, it will degrade.

### Evaluate Program Design, Authority, And Resources

A compliance program may be well designed on paper but ineffective because the compliance function lacks independence, access, authority, budget, data, or management support.

Review:

- board or senior leadership oversight;
- compliance officer authority;
- reporting line;
- budget;
- staffing;
- access to data;
- ability to stop or escalate risky activity;
- independence from business pressure;
- relationship with legal, HR, audit, security, finance, and operations;
- technology support;
- investigation capability;
- training capability;
- monitoring capacity.

The DOJ and OIG guidance both emphasize practical effectiveness, not only design. Ask whether the program is empowered to function.

### Prioritize With A Risk-Based Plan

Not all risks can be fixed at once. A good compliance plan prioritizes by risk and feasibility, while documenting why lower-priority items are deferred.

Prioritize using:

- legal urgency;
- potential harm;
- enforcement exposure;
- likelihood;
- control gap severity;
- number of affected people;
- customer or patient impact;
- management attention;
- remediation complexity;
- available resources;
- dependencies;
- deadline.

For each priority, define action, owner, target date, interim control, success evidence, and reporting cadence. Avoid saying "implement compliance program" as a single action.

### Incorporate Culture, Incentives, And Accountability

Compliance risk is affected by incentives. People behave according to what is rewarded, tolerated, and punished.

Review:

- sales targets;
- bonus metrics;
- promotion criteria;
- disciplinary process;
- leadership messaging;
- pressure to meet revenue or deadlines;
- willingness to report concerns;
- retaliation controls;
- management overrides;
- exception approvals;
- treatment of high performers who violate rules.

A program that rewards risky behavior and punishes reporting will not work, even with good policies.

### Reassess After Change

Risk assessments should not be static. New products, markets, laws, technologies, vendors, acquisitions, enforcement actions, incidents, leadership changes, and complaint patterns can change the risk profile.

Set reassessment triggers:

- new jurisdiction;
- new product or service;
- new data use;
- new third party;
- acquisition or integration;
- material system change;
- AI or automation deployment;
- regulator update;
- complaint spike;
- investigation result;
- audit finding;
- major policy exception;
- business model change.

Keep a record of the trigger, assessment, decision, and follow-up. A risk assessment should show that the program learns.

## Common Traps

### Starting With A Policy Template

Templates are useful only after risks are understood. Copying a policy library can create false confidence while leaving the highest-risk activity uncontrolled.

### Treating Compliance As Legal Only

Legal interpretation matters, but compliance also requires controls, training, evidence, monitoring, escalation, remediation, and culture. A legal memo alone does not make behavior compliant.

### Ignoring Program Resources

An under-resourced compliance officer may know the right answer but lack authority or capacity to execute it. Resource constraints should be reported as a risk.

### Confusing Existence With Effectiveness

A hotline, policy, training module, or approval workflow exists only as evidence of design. It does not prove employees use it, managers enforce it, or exceptions are resolved.

### Ranking Risk Without Rationale

High, medium, and low labels are weak without criteria. State the likelihood, impact, exposure, control gap, and judgment behind the rating.

### Missing New Technology Risk

AI, automated approvals, data analytics, chat tools, and workflow automation can create false records, biased decisions, privacy exposure, unauthorized access, or control bypasses. Include technology in risk assessment.

### Letting Business Owners Self-Certify Without Testing

Business owner attestations are useful, but they should be corroborated through data, testing, monitoring, or independent review for significant risks.

## Self-Check

- Is the business model understood by product, customer, geography, data, payments, third parties, technology, and regulated activity?
- Are specific laws, regulators, contracts, licenses, policies, standards, and reporting obligations identified?
- Is inherent risk assessed before considering controls?
- Are controls mapped to specific risks with owner, evidence, frequency, population, and exception handling?
- Are compliance authority, independence, budget, staffing, data access, and escalation power evaluated?
- Is prioritization based on likelihood, impact, legal urgency, harm, enforcement exposure, and control gap?
- Are culture, incentives, discipline, retaliation, and management override considered?
- Are new products, jurisdictions, vendors, AI, acquisitions, incidents, and regulatory changes used as reassessment triggers?
- Are risk ratings supported by rationale rather than labels?
- Does the plan define concrete actions, owners, dates, interim controls, and success evidence?
