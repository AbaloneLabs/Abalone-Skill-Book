# Compliance In Mergers Acquisitions And Divestitures

Transactions are among the highest-risk moments in the life of a compliance program. An acquisition can import years of undetected misconduct, weak controls, unresolved enforcement matters, and cultural problems that then become the acquirer's liability and reputational problem. A divestiture can leave obligations, data, and liabilities orphaned or transferred in ways that breach law or contract. The DOJ and other regulators have made clear that they examine how companies handle compliance in transactions, and that acquiring a company with misconduct, without adequate diligence and integration, can itself become an enforcement matter. The discipline is to treat compliance as a first-class workstream across the deal lifecycle, from pre-diligence through integration or separation, with the authority to influence price, conditions, walk-away, and integration sequencing.

Use this skill before conducting transaction compliance diligence, planning integration or carve-out, advising on deal conditions, or managing compliance during separation. The goal is to make the agent run compliance as a deal-critical workstream with real influence, not a post-close afterthought.

## Core Rules

### Engage Compliance Before The Deal Is Signed

Compliance influence is highest before signing, when price, conditions, and structure can still change. Late engagement limits compliance to reacting to a done deal.

Engage compliance in:

- pre-diligence, to scope the target's risk profile and identify red flags;
- diligence planning, to define what must be reviewed and by whom;
- deal structuring, to advise on risk allocation, indemnities, and conditions;
- negotiation, to support walk-away or repricing where risk warrants;
- signing and closing conditions, to require remediation before or after close.

A compliance team that first sees the deal at signing can only inherit its problems. Bring compliance in at the strategy and target-screening stage.

### Conduct Risk-Based Compliance Diligence

Diligence should match the target's risk profile, not apply a generic checklist. A generic review misses the risks that matter.

Scope diligence to:

- the target's business model, products, customers, geographies, and channels;
- anti-bribery, sanctions, export, and trade exposure;
- data privacy, security, and breach history;
- financial controls, books and records, and any accounting irregularities;
- prior and pending litigation, investigations, and enforcement matters;
- regulatory licenses, consent orders, and undertakings;
- third-party and intermediary relationships;
- employment, labor, and human rights exposure;
- ESG, environmental, and supply-chain obligations;
- the target's compliance program maturity and culture;
- AI, technology, and intellectual property risk.

For high-risk targets, require enhanced diligence including forensic review of payments, interviews, and independent investigation. Document findings, red flags, and the basis for any risk acceptance.

### Evaluate The Target's Program And Culture

The target's compliance maturity and culture determine how much integration work will be required and how much latent risk exists.

Assess:

- whether the target has a credible compliance program or only paper;
- the tone from the top and history of misconduct;
- the quality of policies, training, monitoring, and investigations;
- prior audit and regulator findings and their remediation;
- incentive structures and disciplinary consistency;
- whistleblower activity and retaliation history;
- the gap between the target's program and the acquirer's standards.

A target with a weak program and a history of issues is not necessarily a deal-breaker, but it changes the price, conditions, and integration plan. Price the risk honestly.

### Use Diligence Findings To Influence Deal Terms

Diligence that does not influence price, conditions, or the decision to proceed is wasted effort. Connect findings to deal outcomes.

Use findings to:

- reprice the deal to reflect remediation cost and residual risk;
- require specific representations, warranties, and indemnities;
- set closing conditions requiring remediation before or after close;
- require a pre-closing compliance remediation plan with milestones;
- trigger walk-away where risk is unacceptable or undisclosed;
- allocate responsibility for known and unknown liabilities.

Document how each material finding was addressed in the deal terms. A regulator may later ask what the acquirer knew and what it did about it.

### Plan Integration Before Close

Integration planning should begin during diligence and be substantially complete before close, so the acquired entity moves quickly into the acquirer's program.

Plan:

- the day-one controls, such as sanctions screening, code of conduct, and reporting channels;
- the integration sequence, from immediate must-dos to longer-term alignment;
- the mapping of the target's obligations into the acquirer's register;
- the training and communication plan for acquired employees;
- the technology and data integration, including privacy and security;
- the retention or replacement of key compliance and risk personnel;
- the cultural integration and tone-setting by acquirer leadership;
- the monitoring plan to detect issues surfacing post-close.

An acquired entity left on its own program for months after close is a period of elevated risk. Plan and resource integration to start immediately.

### Manage Compliance In Divestitures And Carve-Outs

Divestitures carry the reverse risk: obligations, data, and liabilities can be transferred or orphaned in ways that breach law or contract. Compliance must manage the separation.

Manage divestiture by:

- identifying which obligations, licenses, and consents transfer and which require regulator approval;
- ensuring data is transferred or deleted in compliance with privacy and retention law;
- addressing contractual consents and change-of-control provisions;
- allocating liabilities in the sale agreement, including known and unknown;
- preserving records and investigation materials that the seller must retain;
- ensuring the buyer has the capability to meet transferred obligations;
- managing ongoing reporting or undertakings that survive the sale.

A divestiture that transfers data without a lawful basis, or that strands a regulatory consent, creates separate liability. Treat separation with the same rigor as integration.

### Integrate Acquired Obligations And Issues Into The Program

Post-close, the acquired entity's obligations, risks, and open issues must enter the acquirer's program, not remain separate.

Integrate:

- the target's obligation register into the enterprise register;
- the target's risks into the enterprise risk inventory and assessment;
- open findings, investigations, and remediation into the acquirer's tracking;
- the target's regulators and examinations into the engagement plan;
- the target's third parties into the vendor oversight process.

An acquired entity whose obligations stay siloed creates a permanent gap. Fold them into the enterprise view promptly.

### Monitor Post-Close And Feed Lessons Back

Integration is not complete at the legal close. Monitor the acquired entity for issues that surface as integration proceeds.

Monitor:

- for misconduct or control failures surfacing from the acquired entity;
- for cultural clashes and retention of problematic practices;
- for regulator interest in the combined entity;
- integration milestones and their completion;
- and feed lessons from the transaction back into future deal diligence.

## Common Traps

### Late Compliance Engagement

Engaging compliance only at signing limits it to inheriting problems. Engage at strategy and screening.

### Generic Diligence Checklist

A checklist applied regardless of risk misses the target's specific exposures. Scope diligence to the risk profile.

### Ignoring Program And Culture

A weak target program and poor culture signal latent risk and heavy integration work. Assess and price them.

### Diligence That Does Not Influence Terms

Findings that do not affect price, conditions, or the decision to proceed are wasted. Connect findings to deal outcomes.

### Integration Planned After Close

Planning integration only after close extends the period of elevated risk. Plan before close and execute day-one.

### Treating Divestiture As Seller's Relief

Divestiture can strand obligations and transfer data unlawfully. Manage separation with the same rigor as integration.

### Leaving Acquired Obligations Siloed

Obligations that stay separate from the enterprise program create permanent gaps. Integrate promptly.

### No Post-Close Monitoring

Issues surface as integration proceeds. Monitor and feed lessons back into future deals.

## Self-Check

- Is compliance engaged at strategy, screening, and pre-diligence, before the deal is signed?
- Is diligence risk-based, scoped to the target's business model, geographies, data, payments, third parties, litigation, licenses, program, and culture?
- Is the target's compliance program maturity and culture assessed, with the gap to acquirer standards identified and priced?
- Do diligence findings influence price, representations, indemnities, closing conditions, remediation plans, walk-away, or liability allocation?
- Is integration planned before close, with day-one controls, obligation mapping, training, technology, personnel, culture, and monitoring sequenced?
- In divestitures, are obligation transfer, regulator consents, data transfer or deletion, liability allocation, record preservation, and buyer capability managed?
- Are acquired obligations, risks, open issues, regulators, and third parties integrated into the enterprise program rather than left siloed?
- Is post-close monitoring in place to detect surfacing issues, cultural clashes, and regulator interest, with lessons fed back into future diligence?
- Could the transaction compliance process be defended to a regulator as deal-critical rather than perfunctory?
