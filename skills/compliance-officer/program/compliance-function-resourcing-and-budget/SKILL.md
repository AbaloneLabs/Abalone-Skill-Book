# Compliance Function Resourcing And Budget

A compliance program lives or dies on resourcing. A charismatic CCO with an underfunded team, outdated tools, and no data access will be outrun by the business no matter how good the policies read. Resourcing is also a question regulators and prosecutors now ask directly: is the compliance function adequately staffed and funded for the size, complexity, and risk of the organization, and has it ever been cut to hit an earnings target? Justifying a budget is therefore not an internal finance exercise. It is part of demonstrating that the program is empowered to work. The challenge is moving the conversation from "how many people do you want" to "what risk does this function cover, and what happens if it is not resourced."

Use this skill before building a compliance budget, justifying headcount, sizing the function, evaluating build versus buy, or responding to a resource challenge from finance or the board. The goal is to make the agent tie resources to risk and obligation, quantify the cost of gaps, and document adequacy in defensible terms.

## Core Rules

### Size The Function To Risk, Complexity, And Volume

Headcount should not be picked by feel or by copying a peer. It should be derived from what the function must do and how much of it there is.

Size against:

- the number and risk level of obligations the function owns;
- transaction volumes, such as payments screened, alerts reviewed, third parties onboarded, or marketing items reviewed;
- the number of business units, geographies, entities, products, and regulated activities in scope;
- the complexity of the operating model, including subsidiaries, joint ventures, and offshore operations;
- the maturity of automation, since manual processes need more people;
- language and time-zone coverage requirements;
- the incident and investigation workload;
- regulatory exam and reporting workload.

Build a workload model that estimates hours or full-time equivalents per activity, then compare to current capacity. A function that is sized to a smaller or simpler version of the company is structurally under-resourced.

### Justify Budget In Risk And Obligation Terms

A budget request framed as "we need more headcount" loses to revenue-generating requests. Frame it in terms of risk and obligation.

For each resource ask, articulate:

- the specific risk or regulatory obligation it addresses;
- the control or activity that would be impaired without it;
- the likelihood and impact of the exposure if the gap persists;
- the regulatory, financial, reputational, and criminal consequences;
- whether the gap creates a program effectiveness question under DOJ or sector guidance;
- the interim controls that would be required and their cost.

Translate headcount into risk coverage. "Two additional analysts allow full alert review within SLA, reducing the risk of missed sanctions hits" is stronger than "we are short-staffed."

### Benchmark Internally And Externally, With Caveats

Benchmarks are useful but dangerous. Use them to inform, not to dictate.

Use:

- peer benchmarks for compliance spend as a percentage of revenue or total operating cost;
- headcount ratios such as compliance staff per thousand employees or per billion in revenue;
- internal benchmarks over time, showing whether resourcing has kept pace with growth and risk;
- sector and regulator expectations where published;
- maturity and scope differences that make raw comparisons misleading.

Always caveat benchmarks. A peer with lower headcount may have lower risk, more automation, or simply a weaker program. Benchmark to risk-adjusted peers, and never use a low peer number to justify underfunding a higher-risk profile.

### Distinguish One-Time, Recurring, And Variable Costs

Budget confusion often comes from mixing cost types. Separate them so the board and CFO see the true run-rate.

Separate:

- one-time program build costs, such as policy rewrite, platform implementation, or certification;
- recurring run costs, such as salaries, licenses, subscriptions, and ongoing monitoring;
- variable or surge costs, such as investigations, litigation support, exam response, or remediation;
- business-funded versus centrally funded costs;
- mandatory versus discretionary spend.

A budget that hides recurring cost inside one-time projects understates the steady-state need. Be honest about what the program costs to run, not only to build.

### Make Build Versus Buy Decisions Deliberately

Resourcing is not only headcount. Technology and services can multiply a small team or create dependency and risk. Decide sourcing deliberately.

For each capability, weigh:

- build, for high-volume, core, or sensitive activities where control and institutional knowledge matter;
- buy technology, to automate screening, monitoring, case management, training, or reporting;
- buy services, for specialized or surge needs such as forensic accounting, legal review, translation, or certification;
- co-source, for flexibility and expertise without permanent headcount;
- the compliance, vendor, data, and continuity risk each option creates;
- the total cost of ownership including implementation, integration, and exit.

Automating a well-designed process multiplies capacity; automating a broken one multiplies failure. Fix the process before scaling it with technology.

### Document Resource Adequacy And Escalate Gaps

Resource adequacy is a board-level question and a regulator-relevant fact. Document it and escalate gaps rather than absorbing them silently.

Maintain:

- a resource plan tied to the risk assessment and roadmap;
- a statement of adequacy, including any gaps and their risk;
- a record of budget requests, approvals, and cuts, with rationale;
- escalation of gaps that create program risk to the board or audit committee;
- protection against cuts made to hit earnings without board awareness.

If the function is asked to do more with less, the gap and its consequences must be visible. Silently absorbing underfunding hides the risk and lets the program be blamed later.

### Protect Independence Through Funding Control

How compliance is funded affects independence. If the business funds compliance directly and can withhold funding, independence erodes.

Address:

- centralized funding of the compliance function rather than business-unit funding that creates dependency;
- board awareness and approval of the compliance budget;
- protection against unilateral cuts by the business or by finance;
- the CCO's ability to propose budget directly to the board or audit committee.

### Plan For Talent, Retention, And Succession

Headcount is not capacity if the seats are empty or filled with the wrong skills. Resourcing includes building and keeping the team.

Plan for:

- the skill mix required, including legal, investigative, data, technology, and domain expertise;
- training and certification to keep skills current;
- retention through compensation, career path, and workload management;
- succession for the CCO and critical roles;
- coverage for leave, turnover, and surge demand.

A function that depends on one irreplaceable person is fragile. Build depth.

## Common Traps

### Sizing By Peer Count Without Risk Adjustment

Copying a peer's headcount ignores differences in risk, volume, automation, and scope. Size to your own workload and risk.

### Justifying Budget In Headcount Terms

"We need more people" loses to revenue asks. Justify in risk, obligation, and consequence terms.

### Hiding Recurring Cost In One-Time Projects

Mixing run and build costs understates the steady-state budget and sets up future shortfalls. Separate them.

### Absorbing Underfunding Silently

Quietly doing more with less hides the risk and lets the program be blamed when something fails. Escalate and document gaps.

### Automating A Broken Process

Technology applied to a poorly designed control speeds up failure and creates false confidence. Fix design first.

### Business-Controlled Funding

If the business funds and can cut compliance, independence is compromised. Centralize funding with board visibility.

### Ignoring Talent And Succession

Counting filled seats without skill mix, retention, and succession planning creates a fragile function. Build depth.

## Self-Check

- Is headcount derived from a workload model tied to obligations, volumes, geographies, complexity, and automation maturity?
- Is each budget ask justified in risk, obligation, control impairment, likelihood, impact, and regulatory consequence terms?
- Are benchmarks used with caveats, risk-adjusted, and never used to justify underfunding a higher-risk profile?
- Are one-time, recurring, variable, mandatory, and discretionary costs separated in the budget?
- Are build, buy, and co-source decisions made deliberately with total cost of ownership and vendor risk considered?
- Is resource adequacy documented, with gaps and their risk escalated to the board or audit committee?
- Is funding centralized and protected from unilateral business or finance cuts, with board awareness?
- Are talent, skill mix, retention, succession, and surge coverage planned, not just seat count?
- Could the resourcing be defended to a regulator as adequate for the organization's size, complexity, and risk?
