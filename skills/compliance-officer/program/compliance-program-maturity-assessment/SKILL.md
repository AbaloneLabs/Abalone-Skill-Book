# Compliance Program Maturity Assessment

A maturity assessment asks not only whether a compliance program has the right components, but how capable, consistent, automated, and evidence-producing those components are. Two organizations can both "have training" while one delivers targeted, tracked, attested training with effectiveness testing and the other sends a generic module once a year. Maturity models turn that difference into a structured judgment so that improvement is planned rather than accidental. The trap is treating maturity as a score to boast about rather than as a diagnostic that exposes where the program is paper-thin. A self-awarded high maturity rating that cannot be evidenced to a regulator is worse than an honest low one with a remediation plan.

Use this skill before conducting a maturity assessment, benchmarking the program against a model or peers, scoring capability areas, presenting maturity to the board, or building a capability improvement roadmap. The goal is to make the agent define levels with evidence, separate design from effectiveness, and avoid inflated self-scoring.

## Core Rules

### Choose A Maturity Model And Define Levels Explicitly

Maturity only means something against a defined scale. State the model and the meaning of each level before scoring anything.

A typical scale runs from ad hoc to optimized:

- level one, initial or ad hoc, where activities are reactive, uncoordinated, and person-dependent;
- level two, repeatable, where basic processes exist but are inconsistent or undocumented;
- level three, defined, where processes are documented, standardized, and communicated across the organization;
- level four, managed, where processes are measured, monitored, and improved using data;
- level five, optimizing, where processes are continuously improved, automated where appropriate, and integrated across functions.

Define what evidence distinguishes each level, such as documented procedures, consistent execution, metrics, testing results, automation, and continuous improvement. Without evidence criteria, scoring collapses into opinion.

### Assess Across Capability Areas, Not As A Single Score

A single maturity number hides strengths and weaknesses. Break the program into capability areas and score each.

Common capability areas include:

- governance, charter, authority, and board oversight;
- risk assessment and risk inventory;
- policies, standards, and procedures;
- training and communication;
- controls design and preventive and detective controls;
- monitoring, testing, and assurance;
- investigations, reporting, and remediation;
- third-party and vendor management;
- technology, data, and analytics;
- culture, incentives, and accountability;
- continuous improvement and program adaptation.

A program can be mature in training and immature in monitoring, or strong in policy and weak in investigations. Score the areas separately and show the spread.

### Separate Design Maturity From Operating Effectiveness

A program can look mature in design and fail in operation. Regulators care about both. Score them separately.

For each capability area, assess:

- design maturity, whether the policy, process, control, or system is defined and appropriate to the risk;
- operating effectiveness, whether it actually works in practice, consistently, across the population, with evidence;
- coverage, whether it reaches all relevant business units, geographies, products, and people;
- sustainability, whether it survives staff turnover, reorganizations, and leadership change.

A well-designed control that operates only when a particular employee remembers to run it is low maturity in practice. Do not let strong design mask weak operation.

### Ground Every Score In Evidence

Maturity is a claim about reality, and claims need proof. Each score should cite the evidence that supports it.

Evidence types include:

- documented policies, procedures, and standards with version control;
- process maps and control matrices;
- training records, completion rates, and effectiveness testing;
- monitoring and testing reports with findings and remediation;
- audit and assurance reports;
- investigation records and outcomes;
- system configurations, logs, and automation evidence;
- metrics and dashboards reported to the board;
- board and committee minutes showing oversight.

If the only evidence is an assertion that something happens, the score should be low. Insist on artifacts.

### Benchmark Against Risk, Peers, And Expectations

Maturity is relative. A score means more when compared to the risk it must address, to peer organizations, and to regulator and prosecutor expectations.

Benchmark against:

- the organization's own risk profile, so that high-risk domains are held to a higher maturity bar;
- peer and industry benchmarks, recognizing that peers may also be immature;
- regulator and prosecutor guidance, such as DOJ ECCP, ISO 37301, Sentencing Guidelines, and sector-specific expectations;
- prior assessments, to show trajectory and whether improvement is real;
- external assurance or certification results.

A program that is "as mature as peers" may still be inadequate if peers are weak or if the risk is high. Benchmark to expectation and risk, not only to peers.

### Avoid Inflated Self-Scoring

Self-assessment naturally drifts upward because teams want to look good and because the people who built the process score it. Build in checks against inflation.

Use:

- independent reviewers for significant scores, such as internal audit or external advisors;
- evidence requirements that must be produced before a high score is awarded;
- testing or sampling that validates claims;
- calibration sessions so different assessors apply levels consistently;
- a willingness to score low and pair it with a remediation plan, which is more credible than a high score with no evidence.

A regulator will probe a high score far harder than a low one with a plan. Honesty is a strategic asset.

### Turn Maturity Gaps Into A Prioritized Roadmap

The point of a maturity assessment is improvement, not a trophy. Convert gaps into a roadmap that is risk-based and resourced.

For each gap, define:

- the capability area and current versus target maturity;
- the risk exposed by the gap;
- the actions required to close it;
- the owner, dependencies, resources, and target date;
- interim controls while the gap remains;
- the evidence that will show the gap is closed;
- the re-assessment trigger.

Prioritize by risk and feasibility, not by what is easiest to fix. Document why lower-priority gaps are deferred.

### Reassess On A Cycle And After Change

Maturity is not static. A program can regress through attrition, reorganization, or new risk. Build reassessment in.

Set:

- a full reassessment cycle, typically every one to two years;
- interim pulse checks on high-risk capability areas;
- triggers for reassessment after major incidents, enforcement actions, acquisitions, leadership change, or significant regulatory change;
- tracking of maturity trajectory over time.

## Common Traps

### Scoring Design Without Testing Operation

A beautiful policy library that no one follows scores high on paper and low in reality. Always pair design scoring with operating evidence.

### Single Overall Score That Hides Weaknesses

One number obscures that the program is strong in training and weak in monitoring. Score capability areas separately.

### Inflated Self-Scores With No Evidence

High scores awarded by the team that built the process, with no artifacts, are not credible and invite regulator challenge. Require proof and independent review.

### Benchmarking Only To Weak Peers

"As good as peers" is meaningless if peers are immature or if the risk demands more. Benchmark to risk and regulator expectation.

### Maturity As A Trophy Instead Of A Diagnostic

A high maturity rating used for marketing, with no remediation of gaps, misrepresents the program. Use maturity to drive improvement.

### Ignoring Coverage And Sustainability

A mature process in headquarters that does not reach a subsidiary or a recent acquisition is not enterprise mature. Assess coverage and resilience to turnover.

### Treating Automation As Automatically Mature

Automating a bad or poorly designed control creates fast failure. Process must be sound before it is scaled through technology.

## Self-Check

- Is a maturity model chosen with explicit level definitions and the evidence that distinguishes each level?
- Is the program scored across separate capability areas rather than as a single number?
- Are design maturity and operating effectiveness scored separately, with coverage and sustainability considered?
- Is every score grounded in concrete evidence such as policies, records, testing, metrics, and minutes?
- Is the program benchmarked against its own risk profile, peers, regulator and prosecutor expectations, and prior assessments?
- Are inflation controls in place, including independent review, evidence requirements, sampling, and calibration?
- Do maturity gaps translate into a risk-based, resourced roadmap with owners, dates, interim controls, and closure evidence?
- Is reassessment scheduled on a cycle and triggered by incidents, enforcement, acquisitions, leadership change, and regulatory change?
- Could the assessment be defended to a regulator with artifacts rather than assertions?
