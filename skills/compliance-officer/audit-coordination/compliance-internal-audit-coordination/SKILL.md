# Compliance Internal Audit Coordination

Compliance and internal audit both provide assurance over compliance risks, and without deliberate coordination they either duplicate each other's work or leave gaps that each assumes the other covers. Duplication wastes scarce resources, fatigues the business with repeated requests, and produces conflicting conclusions that confuse leadership. Gaps are worse: a risk that both functions assume the other is covering goes unassured until it fails. Effective coordination requires clarity about each function's role and independence, explicit decisions about scope allocation and reliance, shared understanding of risk, and disciplined communication. It does not mean merging the functions or compromising audit independence.

Use this skill before planning compliance coverage alongside the audit plan, deciding whether audit can rely on compliance testing, responding to overlapping requests, or diagnosing why assurance feels both redundant and incomplete. The goal is to make the agent treat coordination as an assurance-architecture decision that protects independence while maximizing coverage and efficiency.

## Core Rules

### Establish Clear Role Boundaries That Protect Audit Independence

Coordination begins with role clarity. Internal audit's independence is its core value and must not be compromised by closeness to compliance.

Boundaries to maintain:

- compliance (second line) owns oversight, frameworks, monitoring, and advice; it is part of management;
- internal audit (third line) provides independent assurance and reports to the audit committee; it does not own or operate compliance controls;
- audit does not approve compliance exceptions, own monitoring systems, or perform operational compliance tasks;
- compliance does not direct audit scope, methodology, or conclusions;
- each function maintains its own professional standards and reporting lines.

When boundaries blur, such as audit performing compliance operational work or compliance directing audit, independence is impaired and must be corrected. Document the boundaries in a charter or coordination agreement so they are explicit and reviewable.

### Build A Shared View Of Risk And Coverage

Coordination is impossible if the two functions see the risk landscape differently. Align on the underlying picture even while maintaining independent judgment.

Share and align:

- the enterprise compliance risk assessment and its inherent and residual ratings;
- regulatory priorities and emerging risks;
- significant issues, investigations, and control failures;
- monitoring results and control test results;
- prior audit findings and open remediation;
- known process, system, and organizational changes.

Each function retains independent interpretation, but both should work from a common factual base. Divergent risk views produce duplicated effort on some risks and ignored gaps on others. Reconcile material differences rather than letting them persist silently.

### Map Combined Coverage To Identify Overlap And Gaps

Create a combined assurance map showing, for each significant risk, what compliance monitors or tests and what audit independently reviews. The map makes overlap and gaps visible.

For each top risk capture:

- the compliance oversight and monitoring activities and their coverage;
- the audit engagements and their scope and frequency;
- where the activities overlap and whether the overlap is intentional;
- where no assurance exists and the gap is accepted or compensated;
- the reliance decisions and their basis.

Review the map jointly at least annually and when risks or plans change. An unmapped assurance landscape is where both duplication and gaps hide.

### Make Reliance Decisions Explicit And Evidence-Based

Audit may rely on compliance testing to avoid duplication, and compliance may leverage audit findings. Reliance is legitimate only when documented and supported.

For any reliance decision document:

- the specific work relied upon and its scope, period, and methodology;
- the competence and independence-within-role of those who performed it;
- the evidence quality and documentation;
- the timeliness relative to the assurance period;
- the outcomes and any issues identified;
- the residual risk accepted by relying rather than performing independent work.

Reliance is a judgment, not a default. If compliance testing is shallow, undocumented, or stale, audit cannot responsibly rely on it. Conversely, audit findings can inform compliance monitoring, but compliance should not treat audit as a substitute for its own oversight.

### Coordinate Plans To Complement Rather Than Collide

Audit and compliance plans should be developed with awareness of each other to target gaps and reduce duplication.

Coordinate planning by:

- sharing draft plans and calendars before finalization;
- aligning on which function covers which risk in the coming cycle;
- sequencing engagements so that audit can leverage recent compliance work or compliance can act on recent audit findings;
- identifying risks where joint or back-to-back coverage adds value;
- avoiding simultaneous requests to the same business unit that create unnecessary burden.

Coordination does not mean the plans must be identical. Audit may independently select areas based on its risk assessment, including areas compliance has covered, to provide independent confirmation. The point is that the choices are deliberate, not accidental.

### Coordinate Information Requests To Reduce Business Burden

Repeated, overlapping requests from compliance and audit frustrate the business and can produce inconsistent data. Coordinate the demand side.

Reduce burden by:

- sharing evidence and documentation already collected where appropriate;
- batching or sequencing requests to the same unit;
- using common data definitions and request templates where practical;
- designating a single point of contact for major engagements;
- avoiding duplicate interviews on the same topic where one set suffices.

The business experiences assurance as a cost. Coordinated, respectful requests preserve goodwill and the quality of cooperation that both functions depend on.

### Maintain Independent Conclusions Despite Coordination

Coordination must not produce captured conclusions. Audit must reach its own view even when leveraging compliance work.

Preserve independence by:

- audit performing sufficient independent procedures to support its opinion, not merely accepting compliance conclusions;
- audit reserving the right to expand scope or disagree with compliance findings;
- documenting where audit's conclusion differs from compliance's and why;
- compliance not pressuring audit to soften or align findings;
- escalation to the audit committee when independence is impaired or pressure occurs.

Two functions that always agree may indicate capture rather than convergence. Productive disagreement, documented and escalated appropriately, is a sign of healthy independence.

### Handle Conflicting Findings Through Reconciliation

When compliance and audit reach different conclusions on the same area, the conflict must be reconciled, not ignored. Divergence can reveal that one view is wrong or that the risk is more complex than either appreciated.

Reconcile by:

- comparing scope, methodology, population, and period to understand the basis of each conclusion;
- identifying whether the difference reflects evidence, judgment, or timing;
- discussing the difference openly between the functions;
- where appropriate, performing additional procedures to resolve the gap;
- reporting material unresolved differences to leadership or the audit committee.

Silently accepting the more favorable conclusion, or letting the two reports stand in contradiction without explanation, undermines both. Reconciliation strengthens the credibility of both functions.

## Common Traps

### Blurring Roles Until Audit Performs Compliance Operational Work

When audit owns controls or approvals, its independence is gone. Keep operational ownership in the first or second line.

### Assuming The Other Function Covers A Risk Without Confirming

Unverified assumptions create gaps. Map combined coverage explicitly.

### Reliance Without Evaluating The Relied-Upon Work

Undocumented reliance transfers assurance risk silently. Evaluate competence, scope, independence, evidence, and timeliness.

### Duplicated Requests Fatiguing The Business

Overlapping requests waste effort and erode cooperation. Coordinate the demand side.

### Captured Conclusions Where Audit Always Agrees With Compliance

Constant agreement may signal capture. Preserve independent judgment and document disagreement.

### Contradictory Reports Left Unexplained

Unreconciled conflicts confuse leadership. Reconcile differences through evidence and escalation.

### Plans Developed In Silos

Independent plans that ignore each other produce collisions and gaps. Share and align plans before finalization.

## Self-Check

- Are role boundaries documented in a charter or coordination agreement, with compliance owning oversight and audit providing independent assurance free of operational compliance tasks?
- Is audit's independence protected through audit committee reporting, freedom of scope and conclusion, and prohibition on compliance directing audit work?
- Do the two functions share and align on risk assessment, regulatory priorities, significant issues, monitoring and test results, prior findings, and known changes while retaining independent interpretation?
- Does a combined assurance map show, for each top risk, compliance coverage, audit coverage, intentional overlap, identified gaps, and reliance decisions with basis?
- Are reliance decisions documented with evaluation of scope, period, methodology, competence, independence-within-role, evidence quality, timeliness, outcomes, and residual risk?
- Are audit and compliance plans shared and aligned before finalization to complement coverage, sequence engagements, and reduce duplication?
- Are information requests coordinated through shared evidence, batching, common definitions, and single points of contact to reduce business burden?
- Does audit perform sufficient independent procedures to support its opinion, with the right to expand scope or disagree, and with disagreements documented and escalated?
- Are conflicting findings reconciled through comparison of scope, methodology, population, and timing, additional procedures where needed, and escalation of material unresolved differences?
- Is coordination reviewed when risks, plans, organizational structure, or regulations change, and is the independence boundary reaffirmed even as collaboration deepens?
