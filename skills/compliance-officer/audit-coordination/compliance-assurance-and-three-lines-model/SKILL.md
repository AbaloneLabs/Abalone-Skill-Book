# Compliance Assurance And Three Lines Model

The three lines of defense model assigns compliance assurance responsibilities across the business (first line), compliance and risk functions (second line), and internal audit (third line). When the model works, assurance is layered, independent, and efficient. When it fails, the same work is duplicated by multiple functions while real risks go unassured, independence is compromised because the second line both owns and reviews controls, or the third line becomes a rubber stamp on the second line's work. The model is not self-implementing; it requires deliberate role definition, independence boundaries, coordination, and honest acknowledgment of where assurance is thin.

Use this skill before defining or revising assurance roles, clarifying the boundary between compliance and internal audit, allocating assurance across the lines, or diagnosing why assurance feels redundant yet incomplete. The goal is to make the agent treat the three lines as an assurance architecture with explicit roles, independence requirements, and coordination points, not as an org chart label.

## Core Rules

### Define Each Line's Role In Terms Of Assurance, Not Org Chart Position

The three lines are defined by the assurance they provide, not by department names. Confusion arises when functions are labeled by name rather than by role.

- The first line owns and manages risk and controls. It performs the controls, monitors their operation, and self-tests. Its assurance is management assurance, which is valuable but not independent.
- The second line oversees risk and compliance, sets frameworks and standards, advises, monitors aggregate risk, and challenges the first line. Its assurance is oversight assurance, more independent than the first line but still part of management.
- The third line provides independent assurance through internal audit, reporting to the board or audit committee rather than management. Its assurance is the most independent within the organization.

Map each assurance activity to the line that should perform it based on this logic. An activity performed by the wrong line either lacks independence or lacks operational grounding.

### Preserve The Independence Of The Third Line

The value of internal audit depends on independence. If the third line reports to management it audits, is pressured to soften findings, or performs operational compliance work, its assurance is compromised.

Protect independence by:

- a reporting line to the audit committee or board, with functional reporting to management only for administration;
- audit committee approval of the audit charter, plan, budget, and the chief audit executive's appointment and compensation;
- prohibition on auditors performing operational management or compliance ownership roles;
- freedom to set scope, access people and information, and report findings without management veto;
- protection of auditors from retaliation for adverse findings.

If internal audit also performs second-line compliance tasks, such as owning a monitoring system or approving exceptions, independence is impaired and must be remediated. The third line advises and assures; it does not own or operate controls.

### Clarify The Second Line's Oversight Versus Operational Boundary

The second line frequently drifts into operating controls, which then removes those controls from independent oversight and creates a gap. The boundary between oversight and operation must be explicit.

The second line should:

- set standards, frameworks, policies, and thresholds;
- advise and support the first line in designing controls;
- monitor aggregate risk and challenge first-line control performance;
- report on risk and compliance to management and governance;
- escalate issues and ensure remediation.

The second line should not:

- own or perform the day-to-day operation of business controls;
- approve routine business transactions as a control owner;
- take on tasks that it would later need to independently oversee.

When the second line does perform a control, such as running a centralized monitoring function, that control itself requires independent assurance from the third line. Document this explicitly so the control is not assumed to be overseen by the very function that operates it.

### Allocate Assurance To Avoid Both Duplication And Gaps

Duplication wastes resources and frustrates the business; gaps leave risks unassured. Both stem from unclear allocation.

For each significant risk and control, define:

- what the first line self-tests and how reliably;
- what the second line monitors or challenges;
- what the third line independently audits;
- where reliance is placed on another line's work and the basis for that reliance;
- where no line provides assurance and the gap is accepted or compensated.

Maintain an assurance map showing, for each top risk, which line provides which assurance, at what frequency, and with what coverage. Review it jointly across the lines so that overlaps and gaps are visible to all.

### Make Reliance Decisions Evidence-Based

The third line, and sometimes the second, may place reliance on work done by another line to avoid duplication. Reliance is legitimate only when the relied-upon work is competent, sufficiently independent within its role, and adequately documented.

Before placing reliance, evaluate:

- the competence and qualifications of those who did the work;
- the scope, methodology, and coverage of their work;
- the independence of the work from the area being tested;
- the documentation and evidence quality;
- the timeliness relative to the assurance period;
- the outcomes and any identified issues.

Document the reliance decision and its basis. Reliance placed without evaluation transfers assurance risk silently. If reliance cannot be supported, perform independent work.

### Coordinate Across The Lines Without Compromising Independence

Coordination improves efficiency and coverage; it must not erode independence. The lines should share information, not merge roles.

Coordinate by:

- sharing risk assessments, monitoring results, and issues;
- aligning audit plans with second-line monitoring to target gaps and reduce duplication;
- establishing regular forums among the lines;
- agreeing on common methodologies and definitions where practical;
- routing information requests and findings through defined channels.

Coordination boundaries: the third line may inform its plan based on second-line input, but it must retain independent judgment over scope and conclusions. The second line may not direct the third line's work. Keep the independence boundary explicit even while collaborating.

### Recognize External Assurance As A Fourth Perspective

External audit, regulators, and external assessors provide assurance beyond the three internal lines. They are not a substitute for internal lines but add an independent external view.

Account for external assurance by:

- mapping where external audit or regulatory review covers internal risks;
- avoiding over-reliance on external parties to fill internal gaps;
- coordinating information provision to externals to reduce business disruption;
- feeding external findings back into the internal assurance map.

External assurance is periodic and scoped to external needs; it cannot replace continuous internal assurance. Treat it as a complement and a challenge to internal conclusions.

### Address Common Structural Weaknesses

Certain structural weaknesses recur and undermine the model regardless of role definitions.

Watch for:

- a weak first line that under-tests, leaving the second line to compensate and becoming operational;
- a second line that both owns and reviews the same controls, eliminating independence;
- a third line that reports to management and softens findings to preserve relationships;
- assurance activities that exist on paper but are unfunded or understaffed;
- coordination that has decayed into silos, with each line unaware of the others' work;
- reliance claims that are undocumented and unevaluated.

Each weakness is a risk that should be identified, escalated, and remediated, not tolerated as normal.

## Common Traps

### Labeling By Department Name Rather Than Assurance Role

Functions with similar names can play different lines. Define roles by the assurance they provide.

### Second Line Owning Controls It Then Oversees

This eliminates independent oversight of those controls. Keep operation and oversight separate.

### Third Line Reporting To Management It Audits

Management reporting lines compromise audit independence. Report functionally to the audit committee.

### Reliance Without Evaluating The Relied-Upon Work

Undocumented reliance transfers assurance risk silently. Evaluate competence, scope, independence, and evidence first.

### Duplication That Frustrates The Business

Multiple lines testing the same control wastes effort. Coordinate through an assurance map.

### Gaps Where No Line Provides Assurance

Some risks fall between lines. Identify and assign or accept these gaps explicitly.

### Coordination That Erodes Independence

Sharing information is healthy; merging judgment is not. Keep the independence boundary explicit.

## Self-Check

- Is each assurance activity mapped to the correct line based on the assurance it provides, with first line owning and self-testing, second line overseeing and challenging, and third line independently assuring?
- Is the third line's independence protected through audit committee reporting, charter and budget approval, appointment protection, scope and access freedom, and prohibition on operational roles?
- Is the second line's boundary between oversight and operation explicit, with any second-line-operated controls themselves subject to independent third-line assurance?
- Does an assurance map show, for each top risk, which line provides which assurance, at what frequency and coverage, with overlaps and gaps visible to all?
- Are reliance decisions documented with evaluation of competence, scope, independence, documentation, timeliness, and outcomes of the relied-upon work?
- Do the lines coordinate through shared risk assessments, aligned plans, common definitions, and forums, without the second line directing the third line's scope or conclusions?
- Is external assurance from external audit, regulators, and assessors mapped as a complement, with findings fed back into the internal assurance map?
- Are structural weaknesses such as weak first line, second-line self-review, compromised audit reporting lines, unfunded assurance, siloed coordination, and undocumented reliance identified, escalated, and remediated?
- Are gaps where no line provides assurance explicitly assigned, compensated, or formally accepted with documented residual risk?
- Is the assurance architecture reviewed when organizational structure, risks, or regulations change?
