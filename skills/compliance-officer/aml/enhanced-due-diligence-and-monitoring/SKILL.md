# Enhanced Due Diligence and Monitoring

Enhanced due diligence (EDD) is the deep-dive scrutiny applied to customers who present elevated money laundering or terrorist financing risk. Where standard CDD establishes who the customer is and what to expect, EDD interrogates the harder questions: where does the wealth come from, is the activity consistent with a legitimate economic purpose, does the customer present corruption or sanctions exposure, and should the relationship continue at all? EDD is resource-intensive and judgment-heavy, and it concludes with consequential decisions: approve with enhanced monitoring, restrict, or exit. This skill addresses the judgment involved in conducting EDD substantively and acting on its findings.

## Core Rules

### Treat EDD as qualitatively deeper, not just more of the same

EDD is not standard CDD plus a few extra documents. It is a deeper investigation that seeks to understand the customer's wealth, activity, and risk at a level sufficient to make a confident decision about the relationship. EDD typically includes:

- Detailed source of funds and source of wealth analysis with supporting evidence;
- Adverse media and negative news review;
- Expanded screening of the customer, UBOs, and related parties;
- Understanding of the customer's business model and the economic logic of the activity;
- Assessment of the jurisdictional and political risk profile;
- Senior management approval of the relationship.

Apply these measures substantively. An EDD file that collects extra forms without deeper analysis is not EDD.

### Investigate source of funds and source of wealth with evidence

Source of funds (where the money for this specific activity comes from) and source of wealth (how the customer accumulated their overall wealth) are central to EDD. Investigate with evidence, not assertion:

- Obtain documentation of the source (salary records, business financials, sale proceeds, inheritance records, investment returns);
- Assess whether the documented source is plausible given the customer's profile and the scale of activity;
- Identify inconsistencies between the stated source and the available evidence;
- Where the source cannot be substantiated, treat that as a significant risk factor.

A customer whose wealth cannot be explained by verifiable evidence presents a risk that EDD must surface, not paper over.

### Apply specific EDD measures for PEPs

Politically exposed persons (PEPs) — individuals entrusted with prominent public functions, and their family members and known close associates — require specific EDD:

- Senior management approval to establish or continue the relationship;
- Adequate measures to establish the source of wealth and source of funds;
- Enhanced ongoing monitoring of the relationship.

The PEP designation is risk-relevant because of the elevated corruption and bribery exposure. Determine whether the customer is a PEP (domestic, foreign, or international organisation), apply the enhanced measures, and document senior approval. Do not treat PEP status as a mere flag; it triggers defined obligations.

### Apply specific EDD for correspondent banking

Cross-border correspondent banking relationships carry heightened risk because the respondent bank's customers are indirect. EDD for correspondent banking includes:

- Gathering sufficient information about the respondent to understand its business, reputation, and supervision;
- Assessing the respondent's AML controls;
- Obtaining senior management approval;
- Documenting the respective responsibilities;
- Approving the respondent's target market and monitoring for payable-through account risks.

Do not onboard a correspondent respondent without this deeper assessment; the exposure extends to the respondent's entire customer base.

### Design enhanced ongoing monitoring calibrated to the specific risk

Enhanced monitoring for high-risk customers is not just more frequent review; it is monitoring calibrated to the specific risk profile. For example:

- For a PEP, monitor for transactions consistent with corruption (large cash deposits, transfers to secrecy jurisdictions, payments to associated entities);
- For a high-risk-jurisdiction customer, monitor for cross-border patterns and sanctions-adjacent activity;
- For a cash-intensive business, monitor for structuring and unusual cash volumes;
- For a complex structure, monitor for layering patterns across the structure.

Define the enhanced monitoring rules based on the specific risk, document them, and review their effectiveness periodically.

### Require senior management approval and document it

EDD relationships typically require senior management approval to establish and to continue. This is a control: a senior decision-maker must assess the risk and decide to accept it. Document:

- Who approved (name, role, date);
- What information was considered;
- Any conditions or restrictions imposed;
- The review and re-approval cadence.

Approval that is rubber-stamped without genuine assessment defeats the control. Ensure the approver has the information and authority to make a real decision.

### Decide to retain, restrict, or exit based on the EDD findings

EDD culminates in a decision:

- **Retain** with enhanced monitoring, where the risk is understood and manageable;
- **Restrict** the relationship (limit products, transactions, or geographies) to reduce risk to a manageable level;
- **Exit** the relationship (terminate) where the risk cannot be managed or where the customer cannot or will not provide the information needed to assess it.

Document the decision and the basis. Exiting may itself trigger a suspicious activity report if the activity is suspicious. Do not retain a high-risk customer by default because exit is operationally difficult.

### Apply EDD not only at onboarding but on risk change

EDD is not only for new high-risk customers. Apply enhanced measures when an existing customer's risk elevates: a customer becomes a PEP, adverse media emerges, activity patterns become suspicious, or ownership changes to a higher-risk structure. Define trigger events that escalate an existing customer to EDD, and conduct the enhanced measures when triggered.

## Common Traps

### EDD as extra forms without deeper analysis

The EDD file collects additional documents but does not investigate source of wealth, economic logic, or inconsistencies. The risk is not actually assessed. Conduct substantive analysis.

### Accepting asserted source of funds without evidence

The customer states a source; the organisation records it without documentation or plausibility assessment. An unsubstantiated source is not a verified source.

### PEP flag without the required measures

The customer is identified as a PEP but senior approval, source-of-wealth analysis, and enhanced monitoring are not applied. The flag without the measures is a half-implemented obligation.

### Generic enhanced monitoring not calibrated to the specific risk

Enhanced monitoring means "more frequent review" with the same generic rules. This misses the specific risk patterns (corruption indicators for PEPs, layering for complex structures). Calibrate to the risk.

### Rubber-stamp senior approval

The approver signs without genuine assessment of the information. The control is nominal. Ensure the approver reviews and decides substantively.

### Retaining high-risk customers by default

Exit is operationally difficult, so high-risk customers are retained indefinitely with inadequate management. The risk accumulates. Decide deliberately to retain, restrict, or exit.

### Applying EDD only at onboarding

Existing customers whose risk elevates are not reassessed with enhanced measures. Define and enforce trigger-based escalation to EDD.

## Self-Check

- Is EDD conducted as a substantive deeper investigation (source of wealth, economic logic, expanded screening, senior approval), not just additional forms?
- Are source of funds and source of wealth investigated with verifiable evidence and assessed for plausibility against the customer's profile?
- For PEPs, are the specific measures applied: senior approval, source-of-wealth analysis, and enhanced ongoing monitoring?
- For correspondent banking, is the respondent's business, reputation, supervision, and AML controls assessed, with senior approval and documented responsibilities?
- Is enhanced ongoing monitoring calibrated to the specific risk profile (corruption indicators, cross-border patterns, structuring, layering), not just more frequent generic review?
- Is senior management approval documented with the approver, information considered, conditions, and re-approval cadence, reflecting a genuine decision?
- Is the retain, restrict, or exit decision based on the EDD findings, documented, with exit considered where risk is unmanageable or information is withheld?
- Are trigger events defined to escalate existing customers to EDD when risk elevates, and are they enforced?
