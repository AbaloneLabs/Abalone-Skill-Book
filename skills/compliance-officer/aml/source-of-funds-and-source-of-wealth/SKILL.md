# Source Of Funds And Source Of Wealth

Source of funds (SoF) and source of wealth (SoW) are distinct but related obligations. Source of funds is the origin of the specific money used in a particular transaction or relationship. Source of wealth is the origin of the customer's total body of assets. FATF Recommendation 10 and 12, the EU AMLD, FinCEN guidance, and the BSA require institutions to establish SoF and SoW for higher-risk customers, especially politically exposed persons, high-risk jurisdiction customers, and relationships involving complex ownership or large unexpected transactions.

Use this skill before designing SoF/SoW evidence standards, deciding how deep verification should go for a given risk, reviewing high-risk products, or escalating inconsistencies between declared income and observed activity. The goal is to make the agent think about what counts as evidence, how much is proportionate, and when an inconsistency becomes a red flag rather than a curiosity. Treating SoF/SoW as a box to tick produces files that explain nothing.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Source Of Funds From Source Of Wealth

The two concepts are often conflated, but they answer different questions.

- source of funds: where did the money for this specific transaction come from? (e.g., the sale of a property, a salary payment, a business distribution);
- source of wealth: how did the customer accumulate their overall net worth? (e.g., a career of executive compensation, inherited wealth, sale of a business, investment gains).

A transaction can have a clear source of funds while the customer's overall wealth remains unexplained. For higher-risk relationships, both must be established and corroborated.

### Apply Risk-Based Depth

The amount of evidence required should scale with risk. A low-risk retail customer making a small expected deposit does not require the same SoF/SoW scrutiny as a PEP, a customer in a high-risk jurisdiction, or a private banking relationship with large flows.

Depth factors:

- customer risk rating;
- PEP or RCA status;
- jurisdiction risk;
- product risk (private banking, correspondent banking, virtual assets, high-value services);
- transaction size and complexity;
- ownership structure complexity;
- presence of cash, third-party payments, or unusual geographies.

Document why a given level of evidence is sufficient for the risk. Proportionality is defensible; uniform minimal effort is not.

### Match Evidence To The Claimed Source

Evidence should correspond to the specific source claimed. Generic documents that do not tie to the stated origin are insufficient.

Common evidence types:

- employment income: payslips, employer confirmation, tax returns, bank statements showing salary credits;
- business income: audited financial statements, tax filings, dividend records, sale agreements;
- property or asset sale: sale contracts, title transfer records, conveyancer confirmation, receipt of proceeds;
- inheritance: grant of probate, will, executor confirmation, estate distribution records;
- investment gains: brokerage statements, trade confirmations, realized gain records;
- gifts or family transfers: donor SoF, gift documentation, donor bank statements;
- loan or financing: loan agreements, lender confirmation, drawdown records.

Avoid accepting a single vague document. The evidence should be specific, dated, and traceable to the customer.

### Corroborate, Do Not Merely Collect

Collecting a document is not the same as verifying the claim. Corroboration means checking that the evidence plausibly supports the wealth or funds observed.

Corroboration checks:

- does the declared income history plausibly accumulate to the observed net worth?
- do bank statements show the claimed inflows?
- is the timing of a sale or distribution consistent with the funds arriving?
- are tax filings consistent with declared income?
- does a third party (employer, conveyancer, broker) confirm the source?
- are there gaps or contradictions between documents?

If the evidence does not add up, escalate. Do not paper over the gap with a customer explanation alone.

### Treat High-Risk Products With Heightened SoF/SoW

Certain products and channels elevate the importance of SoF/SoW because they can move or hide large value quickly.

Heightened scrutiny applies to:

- private banking and wealth management;
- correspondent banking and nested relationships;
- trust and company service providers;
- high-value real estate financing;
- virtual asset and crypto-asset services;
- trade finance and cross-border payments;
- prepaid and stored value products;
- anonymous or bearer-style instruments.

For these, SoF/SoW should be established at onboarding and refreshed when activity deviates materially from expectations.

### Investigate Inconsistencies As Red Flags

Inconsistencies between declared wealth and observed activity are among the most important red flags. Do not rationalize them away.

Watch for:

- transaction volume or value far exceeding declared income;
- rapid accumulation of wealth inconsistent with career or business history;
- funds arriving from unrelated third parties or geographies;
- large cash deposits inconsistent with the stated profession;
- wealth concentrated in jurisdictions unrelated to the customer;
- circular flows, pass-through activity, or funds returning to origin;
- reluctance or evasiveness when asked to explain a source.

When inconsistency is material, escalate to EDD, file a SAR/STR if suspicion is reached, and consider restrictions or exit if the customer cannot explain the discrepancy.

### Document The SoF/SoW Conclusion

The file should record the claimed source, the evidence obtained, the corroboration performed, the conclusion reached, and any residual concerns. A statement that "source of wealth verified" with no supporting analysis is not defensible.

### Refresh SoF/SoW When Activity Changes

SoF/SoW is not static. A material change in activity, ownership, jurisdiction, or product exposure should trigger a refresh. Periodic review of high-risk customers should re-examine whether the original source conclusion still holds.

## Common Traps

### Conflating Source Of Funds With Source Of Wealth

Verifying where a specific transaction came from does not explain how the customer became wealthy. For higher-risk relationships, both layers matter.

### Accepting Self-Certification As Verification

A customer's own statement of source is a claim, not evidence. Independent corroboration is required for higher risk.

### Uniform Minimal Evidence Regardless Of Risk

Applying the same thin SoF/SoW package to a PEP and a retail customer is not risk-based and will not withstand examination.

### Ignoring Inconsistencies To Preserve The Relationship

Pressure to keep a profitable relationship can push staff to accept weak explanations. An unresolved inconsistency is a risk that must be escalated, not managed around.

### Treating A Single Document As Sufficient

One bank statement or one payslip rarely proves wealth. Look for a coherent picture across multiple, consistent records.

### Failing To Refresh

Wealth sources change, and so does the risk. A SoF/SoW conclusion from five years ago may no longer be valid for a customer whose activity has shifted.

### Overlooking Third-Party And Pass-Through Patterns

Funds routed through family, associates, or unrelated entities can obscure the true source. Trace the chain rather than stopping at the immediate sender.

## Self-Check

- Are source of funds and source of wealth distinguished and both established for higher-risk customers?
- Is the depth of evidence proportionate to customer, product, jurisdiction, and transaction risk?
- Does the evidence match the specific claimed source, with dated and traceable documents?
- Is corroboration performed, not merely document collection, including consistency checks across records?
- Are high-risk products such as private banking, correspondent banking, and virtual assets subject to heightened SoF/SoW?
- Are inconsistencies between declared wealth and observed activity treated as red flags and escalated?
- Is the SoF/SoW conclusion documented with the claim, evidence, corroboration, and residual concerns?
- Is SoF/SoW refreshed on material activity, ownership, or jurisdiction change and during periodic review?
- Are third-party and pass-through patterns traced to the true origin rather than accepted at face value?
- Are unresolved inconsistencies escalated to EDD, SAR/STR, restrictions, or exit as appropriate?
