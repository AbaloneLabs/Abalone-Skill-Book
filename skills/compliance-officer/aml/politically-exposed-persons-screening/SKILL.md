# Politically Exposed Persons Screening

A politically exposed person (PEP) is an individual entrusted with a prominent public function, along with their family members and close associates. Because of the position and influence they hold, PEPs present elevated risk of corruption, bribery, and the laundering of proceeds of those crimes. FATF Recommendation 12, the EU AMLD, FinCEN guidance, and most national AML regimes require financial institutions to identify PEPs, apply enhanced due diligence (EDD), obtain senior management approval to maintain the relationship, and establish source of wealth and source of funds.

Use this skill before designing PEP screening workflows, defining PEP categories, calibrating EDD depth, deciding whether to onboard or retain a PEP, or advising on de-risking. The goal is to make the agent think about who counts as a PEP, how wide the screening net should be, what evidence proves source of wealth, and when a PEP relationship should be declined or exited. PEP decisions are high-stakes because both under-screening and indiscriminate de-risking create harm.

## Core Rules

### Use The Correct PEP Definition And Categories

PEP status is about the function entrusted, not the nationality or title alone. The standard definition covers foreign, domestic, and international organization PEPs.

Categories include:

- foreign PEPs: heads of state or government, senior politicians, senior government, judicial, or military officials, senior executives of state-owned enterprises, important political party officials;
- domestic PEPs: individuals holding equivalent prominent public functions domestically;
- international organization PEPs: senior management of international organizations, such as directors, deputy directors, and board members;
- family members (RCAs): spouses, children, parents, siblings, and extended family or partners socially equivalent to spouses;
- close associates (RCAs): individuals publicly known to have joint beneficial ownership or close business or personal ties to a PEP.

Treat each category with appropriate rigor. Foreign PEPs historically carry the highest corruption risk, but domestic and international organization PEPs are equally in scope. Do not screen only foreign officials.

### Decide Whether PEP Status Is Permanent Or Time-Limited

FATF allows risk-based treatment of former PEPs. Some regimes require PEP measures to continue for a defined minimum period after the person leaves office (commonly 12 months under EU AMLD), and then to apply continued measures only where risk remains. Decide and document the institution's policy: whether PEP status is treated as permanent or time-limited, and what triggers a re-evaluation after the person leaves office. Former officials may still wield influence, access, and illicit proceeds long after leaving the role.

### Screen At Onboarding And Ongoing

PEP screening is not a one-time onboarding check. Individuals become PEPs, or become related to PEPs, during the relationship.

Screen:

- at onboarding for all customers and beneficial owners;
- against beneficial owners, not only the named account holder;
- on an ongoing basis through periodic rescreening;
- on trigger events such as adverse media, ownership change, or new public office.

Use a reputable PEP database, but do not rely solely on list matching. Adverse media, news, and official gazettes can reveal PEP links that structured lists miss.

### Apply Enhanced Due Diligence Proportionate To Risk

PEP relationships require EDD. The depth should scale with the risk: a current foreign head of state warrants far more than a former low-level local official who left office years ago.

EDD for PEPs includes:

- obtaining senior management approval to establish or continue the relationship;
- establishing source of wealth and source of funds with corroborating evidence;
- enhanced ongoing monitoring of transaction activity;
- more frequent periodic review;
- additional scrutiny of related parties, family members, and connected entities.

Document each EDD element. Senior management approval must be a genuine decision, evidenced and dated, not a rubber stamp.

### Verify Source Of Wealth For PEPs

For PEPs, source of wealth (the origin of the customer's overall assets) and source of funds (the origin of the specific funds in the transaction) must be established and corroborated. Acceptable evidence depends on the claimed source and may include salary records, official asset declarations, business financials, inheritance documents, or sale proceeds. Public salary alone often cannot explain a high net worth, which should prompt deeper inquiry.

Be alert to:

- wealth inconsistent with official remuneration;
- funds routed through family members or associates to obscure the PEP link;
- luxury assets, large cash, or real estate unexplained by declared income;
- rapid wealth accumulation after taking office.

### Decide De-Risking Versus Managed Risk

De-risking, the wholesale termination of PEP relationships, is sometimes the right answer but is often a blunt instrument that harms legitimate customers and pushes risk into less transparent channels. The decision to onboard, retain, restrict, or exit a PEP should be risk-based and documented.

Factors:

- the seniority and recency of the public role;
- the corruption risk of the jurisdiction;
- the quality of source of wealth evidence;
- the product and channel exposure;
- the institution's risk appetite and expertise;
- whether enhanced monitoring can reasonably mitigate the risk.

Restrictive measures short of exit include product limits, enhanced monitoring, senior approval requirements, and transaction caps. Reserve exit for relationships where risk cannot be mitigated or where the customer refuses to provide information.

### Treat Family Members And Close Associates With The Same Rigor

Corrupt PEPs frequently launder proceeds through family members and close associates. RCAs should be screened and, when identified, subjected to PEP-level EDD. Do not treat an account in a spouse's or child's name as automatically lower risk simply because the named holder is not the official.

## Common Traps

### Screening Only Foreign PEPs

Limiting screening to foreign officials misses domestic and international organization PEPs, which are equally in scope under FATF and EU AMLD.

### Matching Only The Named Account Holder

PEP risk often sits with a beneficial owner, signer, or related party, not the account holder. Screening only the front name misses the relationship.

### Treating Senior Approval As A Tick-Box

Senior management approval must be a substantive, documented decision. A post-hoc signature with no analysis is not approval and will not survive examination.

### Accepting Self-Declared Source Of Wealth

A PEP's statement that wealth comes from legitimate income is not verification. Corroborate with independent evidence proportionate to the risk.

### Indiscriminate De-Risking

Closing all PEP accounts without risk assessment harms legitimate customers, attracts reputational and access criticism, and may breach financial inclusion expectations. De-risking should be a last resort, not a default.

### Failing To Rescreen

A customer who was not a PEP at onboarding may become one later. Without ongoing rescreening, the institution misses new PEP status and the EDD it requires.

### Ignoring Close Associates

Joint owners, business partners, and politically connected intermediaries can be RCAs. Excluding them from screening creates an obvious gap.

## Self-Check

- Does the PEP definition cover foreign, domestic, and international organization PEPs, plus family members and close associates?
- Is the policy on time-limited versus permanent PEP status documented and applied consistently?
- Is screening performed at onboarding and on an ongoing basis, including against beneficial owners and related parties?
- Is EDD applied proportionate to risk, with senior management approval, source of wealth, and enhanced monitoring?
- Is source of wealth corroborated by independent evidence, not merely self-declared?
- Are restrictive measures considered before outright exit, with de-risking reserved for relationships that cannot be mitigated?
- Are family members and close associates screened and subjected to PEP-level controls?
- Is each PEP decision documented with rationale, approver, and date?
- Are rescreening triggers defined for new public office, adverse media, and ownership change?
- Does monitoring reflect the elevated risk of PEP relationships rather than standard thresholds?
