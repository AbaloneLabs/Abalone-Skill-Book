# Suspicious Activity Reporting and Tipping-Off

The suspicious activity report (SAR) — or suspicious transaction report (STR) — is the mechanism by which the financial system reports suspected money laundering, terrorist financing, or other criminal activity to the authorities. The decision to file is one of the highest-stakes judgments in AML compliance: a failure to file lets criminal proceeds flow undetected and exposes the institution to enforcement; an unjustified or defensive filing floods the financial intelligence unit (FIU) with noise that degrades the system. Compounding the difficulty, once a report is filed, the institution is legally prohibited from tipping off the customer, which constrains how it manages the relationship. This skill addresses the judgment involved in recognising suspicion, filing defensibly, and navigating the post-filing constraints.

## Core Rules

### Recognise suspicion through indicators, not certainty

A SAR is filed where the institution knows, suspects, or has reasonable grounds to suspect that funds are the proceeds of crime or relate to terrorist financing. Suspicion is a lower threshold than knowledge or evidence; it is a reasonable, articulable belief based on indicators. Common indicators include:

- Transactions inconsistent with the customer's known profile;
- Structuring to avoid reporting thresholds;
- Rapid movement of funds with no apparent purpose;
- Use of multiple accounts or entities to obscure the flow;
- Transactions with high-risk jurisdictions without business rationale;
- Reluctance to provide CDD information or providing inconsistent information;
- Unusual urgency or complexity without economic logic.

No single indicator is necessarily conclusive; suspicion arises from the pattern and context. Document the indicators and the reasoning that leads to the suspicion.

### Base the filing decision on the totality of the context

A transaction that is unusual in isolation may be explainable in context. Before filing, gather and assess:

- The customer's profile, business, and expected activity;
- The transaction history and whether the activity is a deviation;
- Any explanation the customer has provided (where appropriate to inquire without tipping off);
- The results of internal investigation and CDD refresh;
- Adverse media or other intelligence.

Do not file defensively on every unusual transaction; investigate first and file where the suspicion remains after context is considered. Conversely, do not rationalise away genuine suspicion with weak explanations.

### File within the regulatory timeframe

Most jurisdictions require SARs to be filed promptly upon forming the suspicion, often within a defined period (for example, 30 days under the US SAR rule, or "without delay" under FATF-aligned regimes). Track the timeline from suspicion formation to filing. Delays beyond the permitted period, without justification, are violations. Where the institution needs time to investigate, understand the jurisdiction's rules on when the clock starts and whether a preliminary review period is permitted.

### Draft the SAR with sufficient specificity and clarity

The SAR is useful to the FIU only if it is specific, clear, and complete. Include:

- The who: the subject's identifying information (individuals, entities, accounts);
- The what: the suspicious activity, with dates, amounts, and transaction details;
- The why: the indicators and reasoning supporting suspicion;
- The how: the mechanism (accounts, channels, jurisdictions) used;
- Any supporting documentation referenced or attached per the FIU's requirements.

A vague SAR ("customer activity appears unusual") provides little actionable intelligence. Describe the activity concretely and explain the basis for suspicion articulately.

### Apply the tipping-off prohibition strictly

Once a SAR is filed (or in some jurisdictions, once suspicion is formed), the institution and its personnel are prohibited from disclosing the report or the suspicion to the subject or to any unauthorised third party ("tipping off"). Tipping off is itself a criminal offence in many jurisdictions because it enables the subject to frustrate the investigation. Ensure:

- Access to SAR filings is restricted to authorised personnel;
- Communications with the customer do not reveal the suspicion or the filing;
- Internal discussions are confined to need-to-know personnel;
- Staff are trained on what constitutes tipping off, including indirect signals.

The constraint is absolute: even confirming that a report exists, or behaving in a way that implies it, may constitute tipping off.

### Manage the relationship after a filing without tipping off

Filing a SAR does not automatically require exiting the relationship, and in some cases maintaining it (under law enforcement request or to continue monitoring) is the desired course. Decide:

- **Maintain** with enhanced monitoring, where continued observation provides intelligence value or where exit would tip off;
- **Restrict** the relationship (limit transactions or products) to contain risk without signalling;
- **Exit** the relationship where the risk is unacceptable, recognising that the exit itself must be handled to avoid tipping off (using routine-sounding business reasons).

Coordinate with law enforcement where they request that the relationship be maintained. Document the decision and the basis.

### Maintain confidentiality and access controls around SARs

SARs are confidential. Restrict access to a defined group (the MLRO or compliance officer and authorised personnel). Protect SAR records from unauthorised access, and ensure that the institution's responses to subpoenas, litigation discovery, or data subject requests do not disclose SARs except where legally required and permitted. Many jurisdictions provide statutory protection for SAR confidentiality and limits on disclosure.

### Protect the institution and personnel through the reporting safe harbour

Most AML regimes provide a safe harbour: institutions and personnel who file SARs in good faith are protected from liability for breach of confidentiality or other claims arising from the filing. Understand and rely on this protection, but ensure filings are made in good faith and through the proper channels. The safe harbour does not protect malicious or knowingly false reports.

### Coordinate with the FIU and law enforcement as appropriate

Maintain the channel to the FIU and respond to requests for additional information. Where law enforcement requests that the institution maintain a relationship, take specific actions, or provide ongoing reporting, coordinate and document the instructions. Do not unilaterally exit a relationship that law enforcement has asked be maintained.

## Common Traps

### Defensive filing of every unusual transaction

Filing on every alert without investigation floods the FIU with low-value reports, degrades the system, and consumes resources. Investigate first; file where suspicion remains.

### Rationalising away genuine suspicion

The mirror error. Weak explanations or the customer's reassurances are accepted without verification, and genuine suspicion is dismissed. Apply the indicators and context rigorously.

### Vague SAR narratives that provide no actionable intelligence

The SAR describes "unusual activity" without specifics. The FIU cannot act on it. Describe the who, what, why, and how concretely.

### Tipping off through direct or indirect signals

Telling the customer a report was filed, or behaving in a way (sudden account closure, unusual questioning) that implies it. Train staff on direct and indirect tipping off.

### Filing late without justification

The suspicion is formed but the SAR is delayed by investigation, indecision, or workload. Track the timeline and file within the permitted period.

### Disclosing SARs in litigation or data subject responses

SARs are produced in discovery or in response to access requests without recognising the confidentiality protection. Apply access controls and legal review to disclosures.

### Unilaterally exiting a relationship law enforcement wants maintained

The institution exits a customer that law enforcement asked be kept open for investigation. Coordinate before exiting where law enforcement is involved.

## Self-Check

- Is suspicion recognised through documented indicators and patterns, at the appropriate threshold (suspicion, not certainty)?
- Is the filing decision based on the totality of context (profile, history, explanations, investigation), not defensive filing or rationalised dismissal?
- Is the SAR filed within the regulatory timeframe from suspicion formation, with the timeline tracked?
- Does the SAR narrative include the who, what, why, and how with sufficient specificity for the FIU to act?
- Is the tipping-off prohibition understood and enforced, with access restricted, customer communications managed, and staff trained on direct and indirect tipping off?
- Is the post-filing relationship decision (maintain, restrict, exit) made deliberately, with law enforcement coordination where they request maintenance?
- Are SARs held confidentially with restricted access, protected from unauthorised disclosure in litigation, discovery, and data subject responses?
- Is the reporting safe harbour relied upon for good-faith filings, with the FIU and law enforcement channel maintained for coordination and information requests?
