# New Product And Channel AML Risk Assessment

Every new product, delivery channel, customer segment, or geography changes an institution's money laundering and financial crime exposure. FATF Recommendation 1, the BSA, the US AML Act, FinCEN expectations, and EU AMLD require institutions to assess risk before introducing new products, business practices, or delivery channels, and before applying new technologies. A new product program (NPP) or money laundering risk (MLR) review is the gating mechanism that ensures controls are designed before launch, not retrofitted after harm. Products launched without an AML risk assessment often create exposures that monitoring, CDD, and sanctions were never built to cover.

Use this skill before approving a new product, channel, customer segment, or geography, or when advising on the tradeoff between de-risking and financial access. The goal is to make the agent think about the laundering potential of the new offering, the controls it requires, the gating decision, and the risk of excluding legitimate customers through over-broad de-risking. Launching first and assessing later is a regulatory and reputational failure.

## Core Rules

### Gate Launches Through A Formal Risk Assessment

No new product, channel, segment, or geography should launch without a documented AML and financial crime risk assessment completed before go-live. The assessment should be a precondition for approval, not a parallel workstream.

In scope:

- new products and services;
- new delivery channels (digital onboarding, agent networks, marketplaces);
- new customer segments;
- new geographies or jurisdictions;
- new technologies (AI decisioning, biometric verification, virtual assets);
- significant changes to existing products;
- new third-party or correspondent relationships.

The assessment should be signed off by compliance, with risk owners accountable for the controls.

### Assess The Inherent Laundering Potential

The core of the assessment is identifying how the new offering could be abused for money laundering, terrorist financing, fraud, or sanctions evasion.

Assess:

- speed and irreversibility of transactions;
- anonymity or pseudonymity of users;
- ability to move value cross-border;
- cash intensity;
- thresholds and exemption points;
- integration with virtual assets or high-risk rails;
- complexity of ownership or counterparty structures;
- exposure to high-risk geographies or customer types;
- reliance on third parties or agents with their own risk profiles.

A product that allows fast, high-value, anonymous, cross-border transfers has high inherent laundering potential and demands strong controls.

### Define Required Controls Before Launch

The assessment must identify the controls needed to bring residual risk within appetite, and those controls must be in place, tested, and operational before launch.

Required controls:

- CDD and KYC standards appropriate to the product and channel;
- transaction monitoring scenarios covering the new typologies;
- sanctions and PEP screening calibrated to the new exposure;
- SAR and reporting procedures;
- recordkeeping and audit trails;
- threshold and limit structures;
- escalation and exception handling;
- training for staff handling the new product.

If controls cannot be ready by launch, the launch should be delayed, scoped down, or declined. Launching with controls "to follow" is a common and serious failure.

### Apply Risk-Based Gating Decisions

The output of the assessment is a gating decision: approve, approve with conditions, or decline.

Decision options:

- approve: residual risk is within appetite and controls are ready;
- approve with conditions: launch with restrictions, enhanced controls, pilots, or sunset reviews;
- decline: residual risk exceeds appetite or controls cannot mitigate it;
- defer: more information or control build is required before a decision.

Document the decision, the conditions, the owner, and the review date. Conditions should be specific and tracked to closure.

### Run Pilots And Sunset Reviews For Higher-Risk Launches

For higher-risk products, a pilot or phased launch allows the institution to observe actual behavior and tune controls before full rollout. A sunset review confirms whether to continue, expand, modify, or withdraw the product based on observed risk.

Pilot elements:

- limited customer base or volume;
- enhanced monitoring and review cadence;
- defined success and risk thresholds;
- a checkpoint to decide on full launch.

Do not treat a pilot as a quiet full launch. Enforce the limits and hold the checkpoint.

### Balance De-Risking Against Financial Access

De-risking, the wholesale exit from products, segments, or geographies to avoid risk, can harm legitimate customers, reduce financial inclusion, and push activity into less transparent channels. The assessment should weigh access against risk rather than defaulting to exclusion.

Balancing factors:

- whether risk can be mitigated with controls rather than avoided;
- the financial inclusion impact of declining;
- the availability of alternative, safer product designs;
- the institution's risk appetite and expertise;
- regulatory expectations on access and fairness.

De-risking is sometimes the right answer, but it should be a deliberate decision, not a reflex. Document the rationale for exit or restriction.

### Reassess On Material Change

A product's risk profile changes over time. New typologies, customer mix shifts, control failures, or regulatory developments should trigger reassessment. Build reassessment triggers into the product governance lifecycle.

## Common Traps

### Launching Before The Assessment Is Complete

Pressure to meet launch dates can push products out before the AML assessment is finished or controls are ready. This is a core failure.

### Generic Assessments That Do Not Change The Product

An assessment that concludes "standard controls apply" without analyzing the specific laundering potential adds no value and will not survive examination.

### Controls Promised But Not Built

Approving launch on the promise that controls will follow is accepting unmitigated risk. Controls must be operational and tested.

### No Pilot Or Sunset Review

Higher-risk products launched without observation or checkpoint miss the chance to catch emerging abuse early.

### Reflexive De-Risking

Exiting entire segments to avoid risk can harm legitimate customers and attract criticism. Balance access against risk.

### Ignoring Channel Risk

A low-risk product delivered through a high-risk channel (e.g., anonymous digital onboarding) can still carry high risk. Assess the channel, not just the product.

### No Reassessment After Launch

Products evolve. An assessment done once at launch and never revisited becomes stale.

## Self-Check

- Is every new product, channel, segment, geography, and technology gated through a documented pre-launch AML risk assessment?
- Does the assessment identify the specific laundering, terrorist financing, fraud, and sanctions potential of the offering?
- Are required controls defined, built, tested, and operational before launch?
- Is the gating decision (approve, condition, decline, defer) documented with owner and review date?
- Are higher-risk launches subject to pilots, phased rollout, and sunset reviews?
- Is de-risking weighed against financial access, with exit decisions documented and deliberate?
- Is channel risk assessed alongside product risk?
- Are conditions tracked to closure before full rollout?
- Are reassessment triggers defined for material change after launch?
- Is compliance sign-off a precondition for approval, not a parallel workstream?
