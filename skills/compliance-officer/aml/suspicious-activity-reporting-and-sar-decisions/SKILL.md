# Suspicious Activity Reporting And SAR Decisions

The suspicious activity report (SAR), or suspicious transaction report (STR) in many jurisdictions, is the mechanism by which financial institutions report suspected money laundering, terrorist financing, fraud, sanctions evasion, and other financial crime to financial intelligence units (FIUs). Under the BSA, FinCEN rules, FATF Recommendation 20, and EU AMLD, institutions must file when they know, suspect, or have reason to suspect that a transaction involves illicit funds, is designed to evade reporting, or has no apparent lawful purpose. The decision to file is consequential: filing too late or not at all is a violation, while filing carelessly or without basis wastes FIU resources and can itself be criticized.

Use this skill before deciding whether to file a SAR/STR, applying filing deadlines, handling continuing activity, assembling supporting documentation, or managing confidentiality. The goal is to make the agent think about the suspicion standard, the timing rules, the narrative quality, the continuing activity obligation, and the severe consequences of tipping-off. SAR work is where detective monitoring meets legal obligation, and it is heavily scrutinized in examinations.

## Core Rules

### Apply The Correct Suspicion Standard

The trigger to file is suspicion or reason to suspect, not certainty or proof. The institution does not need to investigate the underlying crime or identify the victim. It needs to recognize that the activity is unusual, lacks apparent lawful purpose, or matches a laundering or financing typology.

Factors supporting suspicion:

- activity inconsistent with the customer's known profile;
- structuring or evasion of reporting thresholds;
- transactions with no apparent economic or lawful purpose;
- use of third parties, shells, or nominees to obscure ownership;
- high-risk geography or sanctions nexus;
- inconsistent or evasive explanations from the customer;
- law enforcement inquiries, subpoenas, or grand jury requests;
- patterns matching known typologies.

Document the basis for suspicion in the investigation file. The SAR narrative should explain what happened, why it is suspicious, and what was reviewed.

### Respect The Filing Deadline

Timing is a hard obligation. Under FinCEN rules, a SAR must generally be filed within 30 calendar days of the initial detection of facts that constitute a basis for filing. If no suspect is identified, the institution may delay up to 60 days, but no longer. Jurisdictions implementing FATF standards have their own deadlines, often shorter.

Key timing points:

- the clock starts at initial detection, not at the end of investigation;
- the 30-day period includes the time to investigate;
- extensions are limited and must be justified;
- continuing activity triggers a new review and potential re-filing;
- late filing is a violation regardless of intent.

Track detection dates and filing dates. Do not let an alert age in a queue past the deadline.

### Handle Continuing Activity

Suspicion is often ongoing. When suspicious activity continues after a SAR is filed, the institution must review the continuing activity and file additional reports at defined intervals.

Continuing activity practice:

- review activity at least every 90 days (common FinCEN expectation) while suspicion persists;
- file a new SAR for continuing activity that meets the threshold;
- reference prior SARs where appropriate without disclosing them externally;
- document the continuing review decision, whether to file or to close;
- escalate to exit when risk cannot be mitigated.

Continuing activity is a frequent examination focus. A single SAR followed by silence while activity continues is a gap.

### Write A Clear, Specific Narrative

The SAR narrative is the most important field. FIUs and law enforcement rely on it to understand and prioritize the report. A vague or generic narrative undermines the filing.

A strong narrative includes:

- who is involved, including subjects, accounts, and related parties;
- what transactions occurred, with dates, amounts, and instruments;
- when the activity happened and the pattern over time;
- where funds moved, including counterparties and geographies;
- why the activity is suspicious, tied to a typology or red flag;
- what the institution did, including CDD, monitoring, and internal review;
- any law enforcement contact or related investigations.

Avoid boilerplate. Write each narrative to the specific facts. Do not include legal conclusions or accusations of criminality; describe the suspicious facts.

### Maintain Supporting Documentation

SARs must be supported by documentation that substantiates the filing. Under FinCEN rules, the SAR and supporting documentation must be retained for five years from the filing date.

Supporting documentation includes:

- account agreements and KYC records;
- transaction records and statements;
- monitoring alerts and investigation workpapers;
- internal communications and escalation records;
- adverse media, sanctions, or PEP screening results;
- customer explanations and any corroborating documents.

The SAR itself is confidential and is not disclosed to the customer. Supporting documentation must be available on request from FinCEN or law enforcement.

### Protect Confidentiality And Avoid Tipping-Off

SAR confidentiality is a legal requirement. Disclosing the existence of a SAR to the subject, or taking action that reveals a SAR has been filed, is "tipping-off" and is a criminal offense in many jurisdictions under AMLD and similar laws.

Confidentiality controls:

- restrict SAR access to authorized personnel;
- do not inform the customer that a SAR was filed;
- avoid account actions that signal suspicion, such as sudden unexplained closure without a business reason;
- train staff on tipping-off rules;
- handle law enforcement requests through controlled channels.

If the institution decides to exit a relationship after filing, the exit should be handled through normal business processes with a documented business or risk rationale, not in a way that implies a SAR.

### Escalate And Decide On The Relationship

Filing a SAR is not always the end of the relationship, but it should prompt a risk decision. Consider enhanced monitoring, restrictions, EDD, or exit depending on the severity, recurrence, and the customer's response. Document the decision and its rationale.

## Common Traps

### Waiting For Certainty Before Filing

The standard is suspicion, not proof. Waiting until the institution can prove a crime often means filing late or not at all.

### Starting The Clock Late

The 30-day clock runs from initial detection, not from when the analyst gets around to the alert. Queued alerts can age into violations.

### Filing Once And Going Silent

Continuing activity requires continuing review and re-filing. A single SAR is rarely the end of a suspicious relationship.

### Generic, Boilerplate Narratives

A narrative that says "activity appears suspicious" without facts is useless to the FIU and weak in examination. Write to the specific facts.

### Tipping-Off Through Clumsy Action

Suddenly freezing or closing an account without a business reason can reveal that a SAR was filed. Coordinate account actions with a documented rationale.

### Over-Filing Without Basis

Filing defensively on everything wastes resources and can itself be criticized as a failure of investigation. Each SAR should meet the suspicion standard.

### Failing To Retain Supporting Documentation

A SAR without retrievable supporting documentation is incomplete. Retention and retrievability are mandatory.

## Self-Check

- Is the suspicion standard applied correctly, recognizing that proof of crime is not required?
- Is the 30-day filing deadline tracked from initial detection, with extensions used only where justified?
- Is continuing activity reviewed at defined intervals and re-filed when it meets the threshold?
- Does the SAR narrative explain who, what, when, where, why, and what the institution did, without boilerplate?
- Is supporting documentation retained for the required period and retrievable on request?
- Are confidentiality controls in place to prevent tipping-off, including restricted access and careful account actions?
- Is the post-filing relationship decision documented, including enhanced monitoring, restrictions, or exit?
- Are law enforcement contacts and related SARs cross-referenced appropriately?
- Is over-filing avoided by ensuring each SAR meets the suspicion standard?
- Are staff trained on tipping-off rules and the consequences of disclosure?
