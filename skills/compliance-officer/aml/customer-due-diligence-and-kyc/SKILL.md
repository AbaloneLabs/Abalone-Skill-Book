# Customer Due Diligence And KYC

Customer due diligence (CDD) is the foundation of every anti-money-laundering (AML) program. Under the FATF Recommendations, the Bank Secrecy Act (BSA), the US AML Act of 2020, and the EU AML Directives (AMLD, 6AMLD), financial institutions must know who their customers are, who ultimately owns or controls them, what activity to expect, and when to escalate. CDD is not a one-time intake form. It is a risk-based obligation that runs across the entire customer relationship.

Use this skill before designing onboarding workflows, KYC collection standards, risk-rating models, beneficial ownership identification, simplified or enhanced due diligence (EDD) triggers, and ongoing CDD refresh cycles. The goal is to make the agent think about evidence quality, risk segmentation, refresh cadence, and the decisions that are easy to make too casually. Weak CDD produces accounts that are easy to open and impossible to defend in an examination.

## Core Rules

### Apply CDD On The Right Triggers

CDD must be performed at defined moments, not only at onboarding. FATF Recommendation 10 and the BSA require CDD when establishing a business relationship, conducting occasional transactions above thresholds, suspecting money laundering or terrorist financing, or doubting the reliability of previously obtained identification.

Triggers include:

- new account, product, or service opening;
- occasional wire transfers or transactions above the applicable threshold;
- cross-border correspondent banking relationships;
- new beneficial owners added to a legal entity customer;
- suspicion or unusual activity that invalidates prior CDD;
- doubt over the veracity or adequacy of existing identification data;
- material change in customer profile, ownership, or expected activity;
- periodic review due date.

Document the trigger for each CDD event. A relationship that started with simplified measures may need full or enhanced CDD when the profile changes.

### Collect Identity And Verify It Independently

KYC collection has two layers: collecting identification information and verifying it against reliable, independent sources. The BSA Customer Identification Program (CIP) rule requires name, date of birth, address, and identification number for individuals, plus equivalent identifying details for legal entities.

For individuals, collect and verify:

- full legal name and any aliases;
- date of birth;
- residential address;
- government identification number (e.g., Social Security Number, passport, national ID);
- nationality and dual nationality where relevant;
- contact details.

For legal entities, collect and verify:

- legal name and any trade names;
- formation jurisdiction and registration number;
- registered and principal business addresses;
- formation documents, articles, and certificates of good standing;
- management and authorized signers;
- nature of business and expected activity;
- ownership structure down to beneficial owners.

Verification should use independent sources: government databases, registry lookups, documentary checks, biometric matching, or reliable third-party providers. Self-attested data without verification is not CDD.

### Identify Beneficial Ownership To The Threshold

Beneficial ownership identification is mandatory under FATF Recommendation 24, the FinCEN Customer Due Diligence Rule, the EU AMLD, and the US Corporate Transparency Act framework. A beneficial owner is the natural person who ultimately owns or controls the customer or exercises control through other means.

Apply the ownership threshold correctly:

- identify natural persons holding a direct or indirect ownership interest at or above the defined threshold (commonly 25 percent, with some regimes lower);
- identify at least one senior managing official where no owner is found or ownership is unclear;
- trace through chains of legal entities, trusts, nominees, and holding companies until the natural person is reached;
- capture control through means other than ownership, such as voting rights, board appointment power, or contractual control;
- re-collect beneficial ownership when the structure changes.

Do not accept "no beneficial owner" without evidence. If ownership cannot be traced, escalate and consider EDD or exit.

### Segment Customers Into Risk Levels

Risk rating drives the depth of due diligence, monitoring intensity, and refresh cadence. A flat risk rating defeats the purpose of a risk-based program.

Use risk factors such as:

- customer type (individual, private company, public company, trust, NGO, financial institution);
- product or service used;
- geography of residence, incorporation, and activity;
- delivery channel (face-to-face, non-face-to-face, agent, digital);
- expected transaction volume and value;
- occupation or business sector;
- exposure to cash, virtual assets, or high-risk products;
- PEP status;
- adverse media or sanctions history;
- complexity of ownership structure.

Calibrate the model so that risk levels are meaningful. If nearly every customer is medium risk, the model is not discriminating. Validate the rating methodology periodically and document overrides with rationale.

### Apply Simplified, Standard, Or Enhanced Measures Proportionately

CDD depth should match risk. Simplified due diligence (SDD) is appropriate only for demonstrably low risk, such as listed companies subject to disclosure obligations or low-value products with limited laundering potential. Standard CDD applies to typical customers. Enhanced due diligence (EDD) is required for higher risk, including PEPs, high-risk jurisdictions, complex ownership, correspondent banking, and high-value or anonymous products.

EDD measures include:

- senior management approval of the relationship;
- establishing source of funds and source of wealth;
- enhanced ongoing monitoring of the relationship;
- additional identification or independent corroboration;
- more frequent periodic review.

Document the decision to apply SDD. SDD cannot be used simply to reduce workload; it must be justified by low inherent risk.

### Set Risk-Based Ongoing CDD And Periodic Review

CDD does not end at onboarding. Ongoing CDD requires keeping information current, monitoring against expected activity, and conducting periodic reviews whose frequency matches risk.

Typical cadence:

- high-risk customers: annual or more frequent review;
- medium-risk customers: every two to three years;
- low-risk customers: every three to five years or trigger-based.

Reviews should re-confirm identity, beneficial ownership, expected activity, occupation, and risk rating. Trigger event-driven reviews also apply when monitoring detects material deviation, ownership change, adverse media, or sanctions hit.

### Document The CDD File Defensibly

An examiner reconstructs the relationship from the file. Each CDD event should show what was collected, how it was verified, what risk was assigned, what EDD was performed, who approved, and when the next review falls due. Undocumented diligence is indistinguishable from no diligence.

## Common Traps

### Treating KYC As A Form Instead Of A Process

A completed intake form is not CDD if the data was never verified, the beneficial owner was never traced, and the expected activity was never defined. CDD is a process of knowing the customer and keeping that knowledge current.

### Defaulting Everyone To Medium Risk

When most customers land in the same band, the institution cannot focus resources. Calibrate the rating so that genuinely high-risk relationships are elevated and genuinely low-risk relationships are simplified.

### Accepting Nominee Or Bear Ownership At Face Value

Nominees, professional directors, and bearer-style structures can hide the true controller. Trace through them rather than recording the nominee as the beneficial owner.

### Skipping EDD For Convenience

EDD is slower and harder, which creates pressure to downgrade a relationship to standard CDD. Downgrading without documented rationale is an examination finding waiting to happen.

### Letting Expected Activity Drift Without Recalibration

Expected activity set at onboarding becomes stale. If monitoring is tuned to outdated expectations, normal later behavior looks suspicious and genuinely suspicious behavior looks normal.

### Ignoring Trigger-Based Review

Calendar-driven reviews alone miss material changes that happen mid-cycle, such as a new beneficial owner, a jurisdiction change, or a sudden activity spike. Trigger events must force a CDD refresh.

### Relying On Self-Certified Source Of Funds

A customer statement that funds come from salary or business income is not verification. Corroborate with payslips, bank statements, audited accounts, or sale contracts for higher-risk relationships.

## Self-Check

- Is CDD triggered at the correct events, not only at onboarding, and is each trigger documented?
- Does the KYC collection include the CIP-required fields for individuals and equivalent entity data, with independent verification?
- Are beneficial owners traced to natural persons at the correct ownership threshold, including control through non-ownership means?
- Is the risk-rating model calibrated to discriminate between risk levels rather than defaulting everyone to medium?
- Is simplified due diligence applied only to demonstrably low-risk customers with documented justification?
- Is enhanced due diligence applied to PEPs, high-risk jurisdictions, complex ownership, correspondent banking, and high-risk products?
- Are periodic review cycles risk-based, and are trigger-based reviews in place for material changes?
- Is the CDD file complete enough for an examiner to reconstruct the decision, including verification sources and approvals?
- Is expected activity current and used to tune monitoring, rather than a static onboarding artifact?
- Are overrides, downgrades, and SDD decisions documented with rationale and approver?
