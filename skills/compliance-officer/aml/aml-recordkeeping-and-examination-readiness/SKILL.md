# AML Recordkeeping And Examination Readiness

AML recordkeeping is a legal obligation and the evidence base on which the entire program is judged. The Bank Secrecy Act, FinCEN rules, FATF Recommendation 11, and EU AMLD require institutions to retain records of transactions, CDD, SARs, and supporting documentation for defined periods, commonly five years, and to make them available to regulators and law enforcement on request. Examinations by FinCEN, prudential regulators, and national authorities test whether the program is designed and operating effectively, and findings are formalized as Matters Requiring Attention (MRAs) and Matters Requiring Immediate Attention (MRIAs). An institution that cannot retrieve its records, cannot demonstrate its decisions, and cannot show timely remediation will not survive an examination, regardless of how good its controls are in practice.

Use this skill before designing recordkeeping standards, preparing for an examination, responding to MRAs and MRIAs, or managing regulator relations. The goal is to make the agent think about what must be retained, for how long, in what form, and how to demonstrate effectiveness to an examiner. Recordkeeping is not archiving; it is the ability to reconstruct any decision and any transaction on demand.

## Core Rules

### Retain Records For The Required Period

Retention periods are mandatory and must be applied consistently across record types.

Retention standards:

- CTRs and supporting documentation: retain for five years from the filing date;
- SARs and supporting documentation: retain for five years from the filing date;
- CDD and KYC records, including beneficial ownership: retain for five years after the account is closed or the relationship ends;
- transaction records: retain for five years from the transaction date;
- EDD and ongoing monitoring records: retain for five years;
- compliance program policies, training, and testing records: retain for five years or longer where required.

Confirm the specific period under the applicable regime, since some jurisdictions or record types differ. Apply retention automatically through systems, not through manual effort.

### Maintain Retrievability, Not Just Storage

Retention is meaningless if records cannot be retrieved. Examiners and law enforcement will request specific records within defined timeframes.

Retrievability requirements:

- index records by customer, account, transaction, and date;
- ensure SARs and supporting documentation are retrievable within the timeframe regulators expect;
- maintain access controls that protect SAR confidentiality;
- preserve records across system migrations and format changes;
- test retrieval periodically to confirm it works.

A record that exists but cannot be found in time is treated as not retained.

### Document Decisions Defensibly

Examiners reconstruct the institution's decisions from the file. Each significant decision should be documented with the facts, the analysis, the conclusion, the approver, and the date.

Decision types to document:

- customer risk ratings and overrides;
- CDD and EDD outcomes, including source of wealth conclusions;
- beneficial ownership determinations;
- alert dispositions and SAR decisions;
- exemption approvals and renewals;
- tuning changes and model validation;
- de-risking and exit decisions.

A decision without a documented rationale cannot be defended. Build documentation into workflows so it is created at the time, not reconstructed later.

### Prepare For Examinations Continuously

Examination readiness is not a last-minute scramble. It is the state of being able to demonstrate program design and effectiveness at any time.

Continuous readiness practices:

- maintain a current risk assessment and program documentation;
- keep policies, procedures, and training current;
- track internal and independent testing findings to closure;
- maintain metrics on alerts, SARs, exceptions, and remediation;
- prepare examination binders and data requests in advance;
- conduct mock examinations or self-assessments;
- ensure staff can explain their roles and decisions.

An institution that is always examination-ready experiences examinations as validation rather than disruption.

### Respond To MRAs And MRIAs Effectively

MRAs and MRIAs are formal findings that require remediation. MRIAs carry greater urgency and shorter timelines. The response determines whether the finding escalates to enforcement.

Response practices:

- understand the root cause, not just the symptom;
- develop a remediation plan with specific actions, owners, and dates;
- commit to realistic timelines and report progress;
- validate that remediation is effective, not merely implemented;
- escalate slippage early rather than hiding it;
- close findings with evidence that the issue is resolved.

Treating an MRA as a paperwork exercise invites escalation to a consent order or enforcement action.

### Manage Regulator Relations Professionally

Regulator relations shape the tone and outcome of examinations and enforcement. Manage them deliberately.

Relations practices:

- designate a primary point of contact for regulators;
- respond to requests promptly and completely;
- be transparent about issues and remediation;
- do not surprise regulators with bad news they will discover anyway;
- maintain a constructive, non-defensive tone;
- document communications and commitments.

Concealment or evasion destroys credibility and worsens outcomes. Transparency, even about problems, builds the trust that mitigates enforcement severity.

### Protect SAR Confidentiality In Recordkeeping

SAR records must be stored with access controls that prevent unauthorized disclosure and tipping-off. Maintain a clear inventory of who can access SAR records and audit that access.

## Common Traps

### Retaining Records But Not Retrieving Them

Storage without retrievability fails the obligation and surfaces as a finding in examination.

### Reconstructing Decisions After The Fact

Decisions documented retrospectively to satisfy an examiner are unreliable and often inconsistent. Document at the time.

### Treating Examinations As Last-Minute Events

Scrambling to prepare for an examination produces incomplete records and stressed staff. Continuous readiness is the standard.

### Symptom-Level MRA Remediation

Fixing the immediate symptom without addressing the root cause leads to repeat findings and escalation.

### Hiding Problems From Regulators

Concealment, when discovered, destroys credibility and converts manageable findings into enforcement actions.

### Inconsistent Retention Across Systems

Migrations, format changes, and siloed systems create retention gaps. Test retrievability across the full estate.

### Weak SAR Access Controls

SAR records stored without access controls risk tipping-off and confidentiality breaches.

## Self-Check

- Are CTRs, SARs, CDD, EDD, transaction records, and program documentation retained for the required periods?
- Are records retrievable by customer, account, transaction, and date within examiner expectations?
- Are significant decisions documented with facts, analysis, conclusion, approver, and date at the time they are made?
- Is the institution continuously examination-ready, with current documentation, metrics, and self-assessment?
- Are MRAs and MRIAs remediated at root cause with specific actions, owners, dates, and effectiveness validation?
- Are regulator relations managed through a designated contact, prompt responses, and transparency?
- Are SAR records protected by access controls and an audited access inventory?
- Is retrieval tested periodically across systems and formats?
- Are remediation slippages escalated early rather than hidden?
- Does the institution demonstrate effectiveness through evidence rather than assertions?
