# High-Risk Jurisdiction And Geographic Risk

Geography is one of the most powerful drivers of money laundering, terrorist financing, corruption, and sanctions risk. Funds move through, to, or from jurisdictions that are weakly regulated, corrupt, narcotics-producing, conflict-affected, or sanctioned. FATF maintains lists of high-risk and monitored jurisdictions; the UN, OFAC, the EU, HMT, and national regulators maintain sanctions and restricted-party lists; and bodies such as Transparency International and the Basel Institute publish governance and corruption indices. A geographic risk framework that reduces the world to a single "high-risk countries" list is too crude to be defensible.

Use this skill before assessing jurisdictional risk, applying FATF lists, calibrating geographic screening, or deciding enhanced measures for high-risk geographies. The goal is to make the agent think about the different categories of geographic risk, the sources of truth, the nexus to sanctions, and the controls that should follow. Treating geography as a checkbox misses the reality that the same jurisdiction can pose corruption, narcotics, proliferation, and sanctions risk simultaneously.

## Core Rules

### Apply FATF High-Risk And Monitored Jurisdiction Lists

FATF publishes two lists that must drive enhanced measures. The "black list" of high-risk jurisdictions subject to a call for action requires comprehensive enhanced due diligence and often effective prohibition of new relationships. The "grey list" of jurisdictions under increased monitoring requires enhanced measures proportionate to the risks identified by FATF.

FATF list application:

- screen customers, counterparties, beneficial owners, and transaction paths against current FATF lists;
- apply enhanced due diligence to relationships with nexus to listed jurisdictions;
- consider whether to limit or prohibit new relationships in black-listed jurisdictions;
- refresh screening on each FATF update, since lists change;
- document the basis for the applied measures.

FATF lists are a floor, not a ceiling. Many high-risk jurisdictions are not on a FATF list but still require enhanced measures.

### Assess Corruption And Governance Risk

Corrupt jurisdictions generate illicit wealth that is then laundered abroad. Corruption risk is relevant for PEP exposure, source of wealth, and the integrity of corporate registries.

Corruption indicators:

- low rankings on Transparency International Corruption Perceptions Index;
- weak rule of law and judicial independence;
- history of grand corruption cases involving senior officials;
- opaque corporate ownership regimes;
- reliance on nominee directors and professional enablers;
- citizenship or residency-by-investment programs that grant access with limited due diligence.

Corruption havens and secrecy jurisdictions warrant enhanced source of wealth scrutiny and beneficial ownership tracing.

### Assess Narcotics And Source Country Risk

Narcotics-producing and transit countries generate large volumes of cash proceeds that are structured, smuggled, and layered through the financial system. This is especially relevant for cash-intensive products, trade-based money laundering, and funnel account activity.

Narcotics risk indicators:

- major drug production or transit countries identified by national and international authorities;
- high cash intensity in the local economy;
- weak AML supervision;
- presence of bulk cash smuggling and trade-based laundering typologies;
- links to correspondent banking or money service businesses with weak controls.

Narcotics nexus should elevate customer risk, monitoring intensity, and structuring detection focus.

### Assess Conflict, Fragility, And Terrorist Financing Risk

Fragile and conflict-affected states have weak governance, porous borders, and active terrorist financing or proliferation networks. Funds flowing to or from these jurisdictions carry elevated risk of diversion to sanctioned parties or designated groups.

Consider:

- conflict zones and adjacent transit countries;
- jurisdictions with active terrorist financing designations;
- weak capacity to cooperate with financial intelligence requests;
- proliferation financing networks and procurement front companies;
- charitable or NGO flows into high-risk regions requiring enhanced scrutiny.

### Manage The Nexus To Sanctions

Geographic risk and sanctions risk overlap but are not identical. A jurisdiction may be comprehensively embargoed, partially restricted, or simply a known hub for sanctions evasion. OFAC country sanctions, EU restrictive measures, UN Security Council resolutions, and national programs each impose different obligations.

Sanctions nexus handling:

- screen all parties and transaction paths against current sanctions lists;
- apply blocking, rejection, or license requirements for comprehensively embargoed jurisdictions;
- apply enhanced screening for jurisdictions known as sanctions evasion hubs;
- watch for routing through transshipment or neighboring jurisdictions to obscure origin;
- coordinate geographic risk with sanctions screening rather than treating them as separate silos.

A transaction that does not directly hit a sanctions list can still be prohibited or reportable based on the jurisdiction.

### Calibrate Geographic Screening And Enhanced Measures

Geographic risk should drive calibrated controls, not a binary high/low flag.

Controls by risk level:

- low risk: standard CDD and monitoring;
- elevated risk: enhanced screening, additional review of counterparty geography, and documentation of the business rationale;
- high risk: EDD, senior approval, source of funds and wealth, enhanced monitoring, and consideration of restrictions;
- prohibited: no new relationships and exit of existing ones where lawful.

Document the methodology for classifying jurisdictions and the measures applied. Use multiple sources (FATF, sanctions lists, corruption indices, typology guidance) rather than a single list.

### Watch For Routing And Transshipment Evasion

Criminals and sanctions evaders route transactions through third countries to obscure origin or destination. A payment that originates in a high-risk jurisdiction may appear to come from a low-risk intermediary. Assess the full transaction path, not only the immediate counterparty.

## Common Traps

### Treating Geography As A Single Binary Flag

A single high-risk list cannot capture the distinct corruption, narcotics, proliferation, and sanctions dimensions. Use a layered framework.

### Relying Only On FATF Lists

FATF lists are important but incomplete. Many high-risk jurisdictions are not listed but still require enhanced measures.

### Ignoring The Transaction Path

Screening only the immediate counterparty misses routing through intermediaries and transshipment hubs designed to obscure origin.

### Stale Jurisdiction Classifications

Lists and risk profiles change. A classification that is never refreshed becomes inaccurate and indefensible.

### Disconnecting Geography From Other Controls

Geographic risk should trigger EDD, enhanced monitoring, and sanctions review. Classifying a jurisdiction without changing the controls adds no protection.

### Overlooking Citizenship-By-Investment Programs

Golden passport and residency programs can grant access to lower-risk identity documents with limited due diligence, masking higher-risk origin.

### Missing Sanctions Nexus

A transaction can be prohibited based on jurisdiction even when no party hits a sanctions list. Geographic and sanctions screening must be coordinated.

## Self-Check

- Are FATF black and grey lists applied with enhanced or prohibitive measures and refreshed on each update?
- Is corruption and governance risk assessed using credible indices and reflected in source of wealth and beneficial ownership scrutiny?
- Is narcotics and source country risk reflected in structuring detection and cash-intensive monitoring?
- Are conflict, fragility, and terrorist financing risks considered for relevant geographies?
- Is the sanctions nexus assessed across the full transaction path, including routing through intermediaries?
- Are geographic controls calibrated by risk level rather than a binary flag?
- Is the jurisdiction classification methodology documented and multi-source?
- Are classifications refreshed on a cycle and on trigger events?
- Is geographic risk coordinated with sanctions screening rather than siloed?
- Are citizenship-by-investment and secrecy jurisdictions treated as elevated risk for identity and ownership?
