# Customer Due Diligence and Risk Rating

Customer due diligence (CDD) is the front door of AML compliance. It is the process by which the organisation identifies who its customer is, understands the nature of the relationship, assesses the money laundering and terrorist financing risk the customer presents, and assigns a risk rating that drives the intensity of ongoing scrutiny. A wrong risk rating — under-rating a high-risk customer or over-rating a low-risk one — cascades through the entire relationship: insufficient monitoring for the former, wasted resources for the latter. The judgment problem is that risk rating is probabilistic and evidence-based, and the evidence is often incomplete, provided by the customer (who may have reason to mislead), and interpreted under time pressure at onboarding. This skill addresses the judgment involved in conducting CDD and assigning defensible risk ratings.

## Core Rules

### Identify and verify the customer using reliable, independent sources

CDD begins with identifying the customer and verifying that identity. For individuals, this means obtaining identifying information (name, date of birth, address, identification number) and verifying it through reliable, independent sources (government-issued ID, identity verification services, database checks). For legal entities, this means identifying the entity (name, registration number, registered address) and verifying through corporate registries and similar authoritative sources. Do not rely solely on self-reported information; corroborate independently. Document what was obtained, what was verified, the source, and the date.

### Identify and verify the ultimate beneficial owner

For legal entity customers, identify the natural persons who ultimately own or control the entity — the ultimate beneficial owners (UBOs). Apply the applicable ownership threshold (typically 25 percent under FATF-aligned regimes, with some jurisdictions using lower thresholds). Where no individual meets the ownership threshold, identify the senior managing official. Verify the identity of UBOs using the same reliable, independent sources as for the customer. Document the ownership chain from the customer to each UBO, including the percentage and the basis for the determination.

### Understand the nature and purpose of the relationship

CDD requires understanding why the customer wants the relationship and what it will look like:

- The expected products and services to be used;
- The expected transaction volumes, values, and frequencies;
- The source of funds and source of wealth (especially for higher-risk customers);
- The geographic footprint of the customer's activity;
- The nature of the customer's business or occupation.

This understanding forms the baseline against which ongoing monitoring detects anomalies. A relationship onboarded without an understood expected activity profile cannot be monitored effectively.

### Assign a risk rating based on a documented, weighted methodology

Risk rating should be the output of a documented methodology that weights risk factors, not an analyst's intuition. Typical factors include:

- **Customer type**: individual, entity, PEP, high-risk profession;
- **Geography**: high-risk jurisdictions, sanctions-adjacent territories, tax havens;
- **Products and services**: cash-intensive, cross-border, private banking, crypto;
- **Channel**: non-face-to-face onboarding;
- **Transaction profile**: high value, complex structures, rapid movement.

Weight the factors, define the thresholds for low, medium, and high ratings, and document the rating rationale. A risk rating that cannot be explained by the methodology is not defensible.

### Apply simplified due diligence only where risk is demonstrably low

Simplified due diligence (SDD) — reduced CDD measures — is permitted only where the customer and relationship present a demonstrably low risk of money laundering or terrorist financing, based on the risk assessment. Examples may include listed companies subject to disclosure requirements, public authorities, or certain low-value products. Do not default to SDD for convenience; the low-risk determination must be documented and supported by the risk assessment. If any factor elevates risk, apply standard or enhanced CDD.

### Escalate to enhanced due diligence for high-risk customers

Enhanced due diligence (EDD) is required for higher-risk relationships, including:

- PEPs (politically exposed persons) and their associates and family;
- Customers in high-risk jurisdictions;
- Complex or unusual transactions without apparent economic purpose;
- Cross-border correspondent banking;
- High-value or cash-intensive relationships.

EDD means more: more information obtained, more sources consulted, senior approval for the relationship, and more intensive ongoing monitoring. Do not treat EDD as standard CDD with an extra form; it is a qualitatively deeper level of scrutiny. Document the EDD measures applied.

### Refresh CDD periodically and on trigger events

CDD is not a one-time onboarding exercise. Refresh on:

- A periodic schedule tied to risk rating (more frequently for high-risk);
- Trigger events (significant transaction change, adverse media, ownership change, suspicious activity);
- Material changes to the customer's profile.

Define the refresh cadence by risk tier and enforce it. A customer onboarded five years ago and never refreshed may have changed beyond recognition.

### Apply ongoing monitoring against the established profile

The CDD profile established at onboarding (and refreshed) is the baseline for ongoing monitoring. Transaction monitoring compares actual activity to expected activity and generates alerts for deviations. Ensure the monitoring rules are calibrated to the customer's profile, not generic thresholds that generate noise for normal activity and miss anomalies for unusual customers. Review and tune monitoring rules periodically.

## Common Traps

### Accepting self-reported identity and ownership without independent verification

The customer states its UBOs; the organisation records them without verifying through registries or identity services. The stated ownership may conceal the true controller. Verify independently.

### Defaulting to medium risk to avoid the consequences of low or high

Medium becomes the catch-all because low triggers reduced scrutiny (risk of missing something) and high triggers EDD (resource cost). This defeats risk-based allocation. Apply the methodology honestly.

### Onboarding without understanding expected activity

The relationship is opened without a profile of expected transactions. Monitoring then has no baseline and either generates noise or is effectively disabled. Establish the profile at onboarding.

### Applying SDD for convenience rather than documented low risk

SDD reduces workload, so it is applied broadly. But SDD requires a demonstrable low-risk basis. Convenience is not a basis.

### Treating EDD as a checkbox rather than deeper scrutiny

EDD is reduced to collecting a few extra documents. Real EDD means deeper source-of-funds and source-of-wealth analysis, senior approval, and more intensive monitoring. Apply it substantively.

### Never refreshing CDD after onboarding

The customer's profile is established once and never revisited. Ownership changes, adverse media, and transaction pattern shifts go unaddressed. Enforce periodic and trigger-based refresh.

### Monitoring rules uncalibrated to customer profiles

Generic monitoring thresholds generate false positives for normal high-value customers and miss anomalies for unusual ones. Calibrate to the profile and tune periodically.

## Self-Check

- Is the customer's identity identified and verified through reliable, independent sources, with the source and date documented?
- Are ultimate beneficial owners identified to the applicable threshold and verified, with the ownership chain documented?
- Is the nature and purpose of the relationship understood, including expected products, transaction profile, and source of funds?
- Is the risk rating the output of a documented, weighted methodology, with the rationale explainable?
- Is simplified due diligence applied only where low risk is demonstrable and documented, not for convenience?
- Is enhanced due diligence applied to high-risk customers (PEPs, high-risk jurisdictions, complex structures) with substantive deeper measures, senior approval, and intensive monitoring?
- Is CDD refreshed periodically by risk tier and on trigger events, with the cadence enforced?
- Are ongoing monitoring rules calibrated to the customer's established profile and tuned periodically?
