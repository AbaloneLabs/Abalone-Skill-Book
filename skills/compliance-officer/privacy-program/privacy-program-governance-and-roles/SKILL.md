# Privacy Program Governance and Roles

Data protection law is built on accountability: the controller must not only comply but be able to demonstrate compliance. This requires a governance structure — clear ownership, defined responsibilities, reporting lines, resources, and oversight — that makes privacy someone's job and the organisation's collective discipline. A privacy program without clear governance becomes a set of unowned tasks scattered across departments, each assuming someone else is handling it, with gaps that no one sees until a breach or audit exposes them. This skill addresses the judgment involved in structuring privacy governance so that accountability is real, not nominal.

## Core Rules

### Determine whether a Data Protection Officer is required or voluntary

Under the GDPR, a DPO must be designated when:

- The organisation is a public authority or body;
- The core activities involve large-scale, regular, and systematic monitoring of data subjects;
- The core activities involve large-scale processing of special category data or criminal data.

Even where not required, an organisation may designate a DPO voluntarily, and many do for governance credibility. Where a DPO is designated (required or voluntary), the role has protected characteristics: the DPO reports to the highest management level, must be involved in all data protection issues, cannot be instructed on how to perform the role, and cannot be dismissed or penalised for performing it. Document the DPO designation, contact details (provided to the supervisory authority), and the independence safeguards.

### Ensure the DPO role is structurally independent and adequately resourced

A DPO who also holds conflicting duties (for example, determining purposes of processing, or managing the very operations being overseen) lacks independence. Ensure:

- The DPO does not determine purposes or means of processing (no conflict of interest);
- The DPO has a direct reporting line to the highest management level;
- The DPO is given adequate resources, staff, and budget to perform the role;
- The DPO is involved early in all matters relating to data protection.

A DPO in name only, without independence or resources, does not satisfy the accountability requirement and may itself be a violation.

### Allocate privacy responsibilities across the three lines of defence

Privacy governance typically follows a three-lines model:

- **First line (business operations)**: the units that process data and own the day-to-day compliance of their processing (collecting consent, minimising data, honouring rights, maintaining records);
- **Second line (privacy/compliance)**: the DPO and privacy function that set policy, advise, monitor, and challenge the first line;
- **Third line (internal audit)**: independent assurance that the first and second lines are operating effectively.

Define which responsibilities sit in each line and document them in a RACI or responsibility matrix. Accountability that is not assigned to a specific role is accountability that is not discharged.

### Maintain the record of processing activities (ROPA)

The controller and processor must maintain a record of processing activities (ROPA). For each processing activity, the ROPA records:

- The purposes of processing;
- The categories of data subjects and personal data;
- The categories of recipients;
- Transfers to third countries and safeguards;
- Retention periods;
- General security measures.

The ROPA is the evidence that the organisation knows what it does with personal data. An incomplete or outdated ROPA is both a violation and a sign that the organisation does not have control of its processing. Assign ownership for maintaining it and review it periodically.

### Establish privacy policy, procedures, and training

Governance requires documented policy and procedures that translate legal requirements into operational practice:

- A privacy policy setting principles and expectations;
- Procedures for data subject rights, breach response, DPIAs, vendor management, and retention;
- Training tailored to roles (general awareness for all staff, deep training for those handling sensitive data or rights requests);
- Periodic refresh and attestation.

Policy that exists only as a document no one reads or follows is not governance. Pair policy with training, communication, and enforcement.

### Define escalation and decision pathways for privacy issues

Privacy issues arise across the organisation: a new product feature, a vendor request, a data subject complaint, a potential breach. Define:

- Who is contacted and in what timeframe for each issue type;
- The escalation path to the DPO and to senior management;
- The decision authority for privacy trade-offs (for example, whether a new use is compatible);
- The threshold for board or executive reporting.

Ad hoc escalation, where each issue is handled differently depending on who encounters it, produces inconsistent decisions and missed escalations.

### Implement oversight and metrics for the privacy program

Governance requires measuring whether the program works. Define metrics:

- ROPA completeness and currency;
- DPIA completion for high-risk processing;
- Data subject request response times and volumes;
- Breach incidents and response times;
- Training completion rates;
- Audit findings and remediation status.

Report metrics to senior management and the board periodically. Metrics that are collected but not reviewed or acted upon provide false assurance.

### Align privacy governance with security, risk, and legal functions

Privacy does not operate in isolation. Align it with:

- **Information security** (the technical and organisational measures that protect data);
- **Enterprise risk management** (privacy risk as part of organisational risk);
- **Legal** (regulatory interpretation, contracts, enforcement response);
- **Procurement and vendor management** (third-party data governance).

Define the interfaces and avoid gaps where responsibilities hand off between functions. A privacy program that is siloed from security and legal misses critical dependencies.

## Common Traps

### A DPO in name only without independence or resources

The role is designated to satisfy the requirement, but the DPO holds conflicting duties, lacks authority, or is not consulted. The designation is nominal and the accountability is hollow.

### No ROPA or an outdated ROPA

The organisation cannot enumerate its processing activities. This is both a direct violation and evidence of a lack of governance that compounds every other issue.

### Accountability that is diffuse and unassigned

"Everyone is responsible for privacy" often means no one is. Assign specific responsibilities to specific roles and document them.

### Policy without training, enforcement, or measurement

A privacy policy that is published but not trained, not enforced, and not measured is aspirational, not operational. Governance requires the full cycle.

### Privacy siloed from security and legal

Privacy decisions made without security input produce unprotectable processing; decisions made without legal input produce non-compliant processing. Align the functions.

### Escalation paths that are undefined or ignored

When issues arise, no one knows who to contact, or the path is defined but not followed. Define and test the escalation pathways.

## Self-Check

- Has it been determined, with documented reasoning, whether a DPO is required, and is the DPO designated with independence, no conflicts, direct reporting to top management, and adequate resources?
- Are privacy responsibilities allocated across the three lines of defence (operations, privacy/compliance, audit) in a documented responsibility matrix?
- Is a record of processing activities (ROPA) maintained, complete, current, and assigned to an owner with periodic review?
- Are privacy policy, procedures, role-specific training, and attestation in place and refreshed periodically?
- Are escalation and decision pathways defined for each issue type, with thresholds for senior management and board reporting?
- Are program metrics defined (ROPA, DPIA, requests, breaches, training, audit) and reported to senior management with action on findings?
- Is privacy governance aligned with security, risk, legal, and procurement, with defined interfaces and no gaps?
- Can the organisation demonstrate accountability — not just assert compliance — through documented governance, records, and reporting?
