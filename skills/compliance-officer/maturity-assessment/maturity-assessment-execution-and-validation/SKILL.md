# Maturity Assessment Execution And Validation

A well-designed maturity model produces nothing useful if the assessment that applies it is poorly executed. The execution is where maturity assessments most often fail: the people who built the processes score them, evidence is asserted rather than produced, scores drift upward under pressure to show progress, and the result is a flattering picture that cannot withstand scrutiny. The harm is that an inflated assessment is worse than none, because it leaves the organization believing its program is stronger than it is, directs no improvement to the real gaps, and creates a record that a regulator will probe and find wanting. Executing a maturity assessment credibly means scoring strictly against evidence, validating scores through testing and independent challenge, calibrating across assessors, and being willing to produce uncomfortable findings. The discipline of execution is what separates a diagnostic that drives improvement from one that decorates a slide.

Use this skill before executing a maturity assessment, scoring capability areas, validating or reviewing scores, or preparing assessment results for leadership, the board, or a regulator. The goal is to make the agent score against evidence, validate through independent means, resist inflation, and produce credible, defensible results.

## Core Rules

### Score Strictly Against Evidence, Not Assertion

The foundational rule of execution is that a score is only as good as the evidence behind it. Every score must cite the specific artifact that supports it, and where evidence is absent or weak, the score must be low regardless of what the assessor believes to be true.

Score against evidence by:

- requiring the assessor to produce or cite the supporting artifact for each score before it is recorded;
- distinguishing evidence of design, such as a documented policy, from evidence of operation, such as execution records and testing results;
- refusing to award upper-level scores without operating evidence, since a documented process that does not run is not mature;
- recording what evidence was sought but not found, since absence of evidence is itself a finding;
- treating assertion, "we do this," as insufficient for any score above the lowest levels.

If the evidence does not exist, the score is low. The discomfort of a low score is the assessment working as intended.

### Separate Design Maturity From Operating Effectiveness In Scoring

A capability can look mature in design and fail in operation, and conflating the two produces misleading scores. Score design and operating effectiveness separately so that strong documentation does not mask weak execution.

Score separately by assessing:

- design maturity, whether the policy, process, control, or system is defined and appropriate to the risk;
- operating effectiveness, whether it actually works in practice, consistently, across the population, with evidence;
- coverage, whether the capability reaches all relevant units, geographies, products, and people;
- sustainability, whether it survives turnover, reorganization, and leadership change.

A well-designed control that operates only when a particular employee remembers to run it is low maturity in operation. Do not let design maturity carry the score.

### Validate Scores Through Testing And Independent Review

Self-assessment naturally inflates, and the people closest to a process are the least objective about it. Validation through testing and independent review is what makes scores credible and is increasingly expected by regulators.

Validate by:

- using independent reviewers for significant scores, such as internal audit or external advisors who did not build the process;
- testing a sample of claims against actual performance, such as reviewing execution records or re-performing a control;
- comparing self-scores to testing and audit findings, and investigating contradictions;
- applying calibration sessions so different assessors apply level definitions consistently;
- documenting the validation method and its results for each significant score.

A score that survives independent testing is credible; one that has only been self-asserted is not. Build validation into execution.

### Calibrate Across Assessors To Ensure Consistency

When multiple assessors score different capability areas, inconsistency undermines the comparability that makes the assessment useful. One assessor's level three is another's level four, and the resulting map is noise unless calibrate.

Calibrate by:

- running calibration sessions where assessors score the same example and reconcile differences;
- providing worked examples of what constitutes each level for each capability area;
- having a lead assessor review scores across areas for consistency;
- documenting scoring decisions and rationale so they can be reviewed and challenged;
- re-calibrating when the model or team changes.

An assessment where scores reflect the assessor more than the capability cannot be used to compare areas or track progress. Calibrate deliberately.

### Disaggregate Scores To Reveal Local Variation

Enterprise-level maturity scores hide the units, geographies, and risk domains where maturity is lowest, and those are where risk concentrates. A strong enterprise score can conceal a subsidiary or region where the capability barely exists.

Disaggregate by scoring:

- by business unit and geography, not only enterprise-wide;
- by risk domain, so maturity in high-risk areas is visible separately;
- by coverage, distinguishing headquarters maturity from reach into acquired entities or offshore operations;
- over time, so regression in a specific area is detectable.

Set thresholds that trigger deeper review where a segment scores significantly below the enterprise. Maturity that exists only at headquarters is not enterprise maturity.

### Document The Assessment So It Is Repeatable And Defensible

An assessment that cannot be repeated or examined cannot be defended. The execution must produce a record that allows another reviewer, or a regulator, to understand how each score was reached.

Document by recording:

- the scores, the evidence cited for each, and the validation performed;
- the assessors, their independence, and the calibration applied;
- the areas where evidence was absent and the resulting lower scores;
- the disagreements encountered and how they were resolved;
- the limitations of the assessment, what it did and did not examine.

A regulator will probe how scores were reached. The documentation is the defense.

### Resist Pressure To Inflate And Report Honestly

There is predictable pressure to show maturity improvement, especially when the assessment is reported to leadership or the board. Yielding to this pressure converts the assessment into a risk. Honest reporting, including uncomfortable findings, is what makes the assessment useful and credible.

Resist by:

- holding the line on evidence requirements even when scores are lower than desired;
- presenting low scores paired with remediation plans, which are more credible than unsupported high scores;
- ensuring the CCO and board see the real picture, including weak areas, not a sanitized version;
- tracking score changes only when backed by evidence of real improvement, not by re-interpretation;
- treating a downward score honestly, since regression is a finding that demands attention.

## Common Traps

### Scoring On Assertion Without Evidence

A score based on "we do this" without an artifact is opinion, not assessment. Require and cite evidence for every score.

### Conflating Design And Operating Effectiveness

Strong documentation masking weak execution produces inflated scores. Score design and operation separately.

### Self-Assessment Without Independent Validation

The people who built the process are the least objective scorers. Validate significant scores through testing and independent review.

### Inconsistent Scoring Across Assessors

When scores reflect the assessor rather than the capability, comparison is impossible. Calibrate through sessions, examples, and lead review.

### Enterprise Scores Hiding Low-Maturity Segments

A strong enterprise score can conceal a weak subsidiary or high-risk domain. Disaggregate by unit, geography, and risk domain.

### Undocumented Assessment That Cannot Be Defended

Without records of evidence, validation, and limitations, scores cannot withstand scrutiny. Document the assessment thoroughly.

### Inflation Under Pressure To Show Progress

Yielding to pressure for higher scores creates false confidence and a false record. Hold the line and report honestly with plans.

## Self-Check

- Is every score supported by cited evidence, with design and operating evidence distinguished, upper levels requiring operating evidence, and absence of evidence treated as a finding?
- Are design maturity and operating effectiveness scored separately, with coverage and sustainability considered, so documentation does not mask weak execution?
- Are significant scores validated through independent reviewers, sampling and testing of claims, comparison to audit findings, calibration sessions, and documented validation methods?
- Is consistency ensured across assessors through calibration sessions, worked examples, lead review, documented rationale, and re-calibration on change?
- Are scores disaggregated by business unit, geography, risk domain, coverage, and time, with thresholds triggering deeper review of low segments?
- Is the assessment documented with scores, evidence, validation, assessor independence, absent evidence, disagreements, and limitations, sufficient for repeat and defense?
- Is inflation resisted through evidence requirements, honest low scores paired with plans, board visibility of weak areas, and evidence-backed score changes?
- Could the assessment be defended to a regulator as credible, consistent, and evidence-based rather than inflated self-scoring?
