# Maturity Model Design And Customization

A maturity model is only as useful as the thinking that went into defining what each level means and what evidence distinguishes one from another. Off-the-shelf models are attractive because they appear objective and ready to use, but a generic model applied without adaptation produces generic findings: the organization scores itself favorably against vague criteria, the assessment confirms what leadership wanted to hear, and the model becomes a trophy rather than a diagnostic. The harm is that a poorly designed model obscures the real capability gaps. An organization can score itself at "optimized" against a model whose top level requires little more than having documented processes, while its controls fail in operation and its high-risk domains are under-covered. Designing or customizing a maturity model is a judgment task: the levels must be meaningful, the capability areas must reflect the organization's actual risk, and the evidence criteria must be strict enough that a high score means something real and defensible.

Use this skill before designing, selecting, or customizing a maturity model, defining levels and capability areas, or setting the evidence criteria that will govern scoring. The goal is to make the agent build a model that is calibrated to the organization's risk, strict in its evidence requirements, and resistant to inflated self-scoring rather than a generic scale that flatters.

## Core Rules

### Choose Between Adopting, Adapting, And Building, Deliberately

The first decision is whether to adopt an external model wholesale, adapt one to the organization, or build a custom model. Each has tradeoffs, and the choice should be conscious. Adopting a recognized model, such as one based on ISO 37301 or a sector framework, lends credibility and comparability; adapting preserves that credibility while fitting the organization; building maximizes fit but sacrifices comparability and requires more rigor to be defensible.

Decide by weighing:

- adopting, for credibility, comparability, and speed, suited when the organization's profile is close to the model's assumptions;
- adapting, the most common sound choice, preserving external credibility while tailoring capability areas and evidence to the organization's risk;
- building, for organizations with unusual profiles where no external model fits, accepting the burden of defending a proprietary scale;
- the need for external defensibility, since regulators may give more weight to recognized frameworks;
- the cost and rigor each option demands.

State the choice and its rationale. A model whose provenance is unclear cannot be defended.

### Define Levels So Each Represents A Meaningful Capability Step

Maturity levels must represent genuine differences in capability, not gradations of documentation. The frequent failure is a model whose levels distinguish mainly how much is written down, so that an organization with extensive policy libraries scores highly regardless of whether anything works.

Define meaningful levels, commonly:

- level one, ad hoc, where activities are reactive, uncoordinated, and dependent on individuals;
- level two, repeatable, where basic processes exist but are inconsistent or undocumented;
- level three, defined, where processes are documented, standardized, and communicated;
- level four, managed, where processes are measured, monitored, and improved using data;
- level five, optimizing, where processes are continuously improved, automated where appropriate, and integrated across functions.

Crucially, define what distinguishes each level in terms of behavior, consistency, measurement, and evidence, not just documentation. A model where level five is reachable by writing more documents is not measuring maturity.

### Set Capability Areas That Reflect The Organization's Actual Risk

The set of capability areas determines what the assessment examines, and a generic set will miss what matters most to a specific organization. The areas must be derived from the organization's risk profile, regulatory environment, and operating model.

Define capability areas that include:

- the core program components, such as governance, risk assessment, policies, training, controls, monitoring, investigations, and technology;
- the risk domains material to the organization, such as anti-bribery, sanctions, privacy, trade, safety, or financial crime, weighted by exposure;
- the enabling capabilities, such as data, analytics, culture, and third-party management;
- coverage dimensions, such as geography, entity, and business unit reach;
- adaptation capabilities, such as how the program responds to change and learns from failure.

An organization whose dominant risk is sanctions but whose maturity model has no sanctions-specific capability area is assessing the wrong things. Tailor the areas to the risk.

### Specify Strict Evidence Criteria For Each Level

The single most important design choice is the evidence required to award each level. Without strict, specific evidence criteria, scoring collapses into opinion and drifts upward. The criteria must demand artifacts that prove the capability operates, not merely that it is described.

Specify evidence such that:

- lower levels require only basic evidence, such as the existence of a process;
- higher levels require progressively stronger evidence, such as consistent execution records, measurement, testing results, and demonstrated improvement;
- the top levels require evidence of measurement, monitoring, automation, and continuous improvement, not just documentation;
- assertion alone never suffices for a high level;
- the criteria are specific enough that two assessors would reach the same score.

If the evidence criteria allow a high score based on a policy's existence, the model is too weak. Demand operating evidence for the upper levels.

### Calibrate The Model To Risk So High-Risk Domains Face A Higher Bar

Not all capability areas should be held to the same maturity expectation. A high-risk domain, such as anti-bribery in a corruption-exposed market, should be held to a higher target maturity than a low-risk administrative area. The model should encode this calibration so that improvement is prioritized correctly.

Calibrate by:

- setting target maturity levels by capability area based on risk, so high-risk areas target level four or five;
- making clear that a lower maturity in a high-risk domain is a more serious gap than the same level in a low-risk domain;
- weighting gap severity by risk when the model informs roadmaps;
- revisiting calibration when the risk assessment changes.

A flat target maturity across all areas misallocates improvement effort. Calibrate the bar to the risk.

### Build In Resistance To Inflation From The Start

Maturity models drift upward because the people who built a process score it, because high scores are rewarded, and because vague criteria allow generous interpretation. The model must be designed to resist this drift structurally, not merely by exhorting honesty.

Build in resistance by:

- requiring evidence production before any high score is awarded, with no evidence meaning no high score;
- defining criteria specifically enough to limit subjective generosity;
- building in independent review for significant scores, such as internal audit or external advisors;
- including calibration sessions so different assessors apply levels consistently;
- designing the model so that an honest low score with a plan is explicitly more credible than a high score without evidence.

### Document The Model So It Is Repeatable And Defensible

A maturity model that exists only in the assessor's head cannot be repeated, compared over time, or defended. The model must be documented as a living artifact.

Document:

- the levels and their definitions, the capability areas, and the evidence criteria for each;
- the rationale for the chosen model and its calibration to risk;
- the scoring rules and how disagreements are resolved;
- the relationship to external frameworks where relevant;
- the version and change history, so evolution is tracked.

## Common Traps

### Adopting A Generic Model Without Adaptation

An off-the-shelf model applied unchanged misses the organization's specific risk and produces generic findings. Adapt capability areas and evidence to the profile.

### Levels That Distinguish Documentation, Not Capability

A model where higher levels require only more written material flatters without measuring maturity. Define levels by behavior, consistency, measurement, and improvement.

### Capability Areas That Ignore The Organization's Risk

A model without the risk domains material to the organization assesses the wrong things. Tailor areas to the risk profile.

### Vague Evidence Criteria Allowing Opinion-Based Scoring

Without strict, specific evidence requirements, scores drift upward. Demand operating evidence for upper levels.

### Flat Target Maturity Ignoring Risk

Holding all areas to the same maturity bar misallocates improvement. Calibrate target maturity to risk.

### No Structural Resistance To Inflation

Models drift upward because builders score their own work and high scores are rewarded. Require evidence, independent review, and calibration.

### Undocumented Model That Cannot Be Repeated Or Defended

A model in the assessor's head cannot be compared over time or defended to a regulator. Document levels, areas, criteria, rationale, and version history.

## Self-Check

- Is the choice to adopt, adapt, or build the maturity model made deliberately, with tradeoffs in credibility, comparability, fit, and defensibility weighed and the rationale documented?
- Do the maturity levels represent meaningful capability steps in behavior, consistency, measurement, and improvement, rather than gradations of documentation?
- Are capability areas derived from the organization's risk profile, regulatory environment, and operating model, including material risk domains and enabling capabilities?
- Are evidence criteria strict and specific, requiring operating evidence, measurement, testing, and improvement for upper levels, with assertion never sufficient for a high score?
- Is target maturity calibrated by risk so high-risk domains face a higher bar and gap severity is weighted by risk?
- Is inflation resistance built in through evidence production requirements, independent review, calibration sessions, and credibility of honest low scores with plans?
- Is the model documented with levels, areas, criteria, scoring rules, external relationships, rationale, and version history so it is repeatable and defensible?
- Could the model be defended to a regulator as a rigorous, risk-calibrated diagnostic rather than a generic scale that flatters?
