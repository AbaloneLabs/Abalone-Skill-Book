# OFAC Comprehensive And List-Based Programs

OFAC administers dozens of overlapping sanctions programs, and they do not work the same way. Some comprehensively embargo a country or region: nearly all transactions with that territory are prohibited and property is blocked. Others are list-based: only designated persons are blocked, while the broader jurisdiction remains open. Still others are sectoral, targeting specific industries through directives rather than full blocking, and some reach conduct by non-US persons through secondary sanctions. Treating "OFAC sanctions" as a single regime leads to wrong answers about what is permitted, what must be blocked, and who is exposed.

Use this skill before advising on whether a transaction is prohibited, which program governs, whether blocking or rejection applies, whether a license is available, and how comprehensive, list-based, sectoral, and secondary sanctions interact. The compliance officer must identify the specific program and directive, not gesture at sanctions generally.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Program Type Before Concluding Prohibition

OFAC programs fall into broad categories with different legal effects. Identify which category governs before deciding what to do.

- Comprehensive embargoes prohibit broadly the import, export, trade, and transactions involving the covered jurisdiction. Current and recent examples include Cuba, Iran, North Korea (DPRK), Syria, and the Crimea, Donetsk, and Luhansk regions of Ukraine. Property of the governments and persons of these territories is blocked.
- List-based programs designate specific individuals and entities as SDNs. Transactions with non-designated parties in the same country may remain permitted, subject to risk and due diligence.
- Sectoral sanctions target specific sectors of an economy through directives, typically restricting debt, equity, or energy-related transactions with named entities on the SSI List.
- Secondary sanctions can reach non-US persons for significant transactions with certain sanctioned parties, even with no US nexus.

The correct control depends on the program type. A list-based program does not embargo the whole country; a comprehensive embargo is not satisfied by screening only the SDN list.

### Handle Comprehensive Embargoes As Near-Total Prohibitions

Comprehensive embargoes are broad. Do not assume a transaction is permitted merely because the counterparty is not listed.

- Imports from and exports to the comprehensively embargoed territory are generally prohibited.
- Services, financing, and brokering involving the territory are generally prohibited.
- Property of the government and persons of the territory is blocked.
- Travel-related transactions may require specific authorization.
- Humanitarian, informational, and certain personal communications exceptions may apply but are narrow and must be confirmed against current regulations.

For Crimea, and the so-called DNR/LNR regions of Ukraine, OFAC prohibits the export, reexport, sale, or supply of goods, services, and technology, and prohibits new investment. These are effectively comprehensive for the covered regions.

### Treat List-Based Programs As Targeted, Not Territorial

List-based programs block designated persons but do not embargo the entire country.

- Only parties designated as SDNs under the program are blocked.
- The 50 Percent Rule extends blocking to entities owned 50 percent or more in the aggregate by designated persons, even if unlisted.
- Transactions with non-designated parties are not per se prohibited but require screening and risk-based diligence.
- Sectoral or end-use risks may still apply even where the party is not designated.

The error to avoid is treating a list-based program as a country-wide prohibition, or treating a comprehensive embargo as a screening exercise.

### Apply Sectoral Sanctions Through The Applicable Directive

Sectoral sanctions operate through directives that impose specific transactional restrictions on named entities, primarily under the Russia sectoral program.

- Directive 1 limits dealings in new debt of named financial institutions.
- Directive 2 limits dealings in new debt of named energy firms.
- Directive 3 limits dealings in new debt and new equity of named defense and other firms.
- Directive 4 prohibits the provision of goods, services, and technology in support of exploration or production for deepwater, Arctic offshore, or shale projects.
- Each directive defines the maturity, asset class, and effective date boundaries.

Sectoral restrictions are not full blocking. The named entities are not SDNs, but the specified transactions are prohibited. Read the directive text; the boundaries are precise and change over time.

### Recognize Secondary Sanctions Reach Non-US Conduct

Some OFAC programs expose non-US persons to designation for significant transactions with certain sanctioned targets, even without a US nexus.

- Secondary sanctions can apply to significant financial, petroleum, petrochemical, defense, or related transactions with designated parties.
- CAATSA and related authorities expanded secondary exposure, including for transactions with Russian defense and intelligence sectors and Nord Stream 2 related conduct.
- The consequence is typically designation of the non-US person as an SDN, cutting them off from the US financial system.
- Non-US financial institutions face mandatory or discretionary sanctions for knowing significant transactions with certain designated parties.

Secondary sanctions are jurisdictionally distinct from primary sanctions. A non-US person with no US nexus may still face designation. See the dedicated secondary sanctions skill.

### Confirm Whether A License Is Available And Required

Many programs allow specific activity under a general or specific license. Do not conclude a transaction is impossible without checking licensing.

- General licenses authorize categories of activity without application; verify the activity fits the license terms.
- Specific licenses require application to OFAC with details of the proposed transaction.
- License conditions must be followed exactly; deviation can void the authorization.
- Some activities are statutorily excluded from licensing.

### Map The Program To The Required Action

The program type determines the operational response: block, reject, decline, license, or proceed with diligence. Map each relationship and transaction to the governing program and directive, and document the basis for the action taken.

## Common Traps

### Treating All OFAC Programs As Equivalent

Comprehensive, list-based, sectoral, and secondary programs have different scopes and consequences. A screening-only response is wrong for a comprehensive embargo.

### Assuming A Country Is Open Because The Party Is Unlisted

In a list-based program the country is open, but in a comprehensive embargo the territory is broadly prohibited regardless of listing. Confirm the program type.

### Ignoring The 50 Percent Rule In List-Based Programs

An unlisted entity owned in the aggregate by SDNs is blocked. Screening only the named party misses this perimeter.

### Reading Sectoral Directives As Full Blocking

SSI-listed entities are not SDNs. Only the transactions described in the applicable directive are prohibited; other dealings may be permitted.

### Overlooking Secondary Sanctions For Non-US Persons

A non-US counterparty with no US nexus can still be designated for significant transactions with certain targets. Primary sanctions analysis alone is incomplete.

### Assuming Humanitarian Exceptions Are Broad

Humanitarian, informational, and personal communications exceptions exist but are narrow and program-specific. Confirm the current regulation rather than assuming coverage.

### Relying On Outdated Program Descriptions

OFAC programs change frequently through executive orders, determinations, and general licenses. Confirm the current state of the program before advising.

## Self-Check

- Has the specific OFAC program governing the party, transaction, or jurisdiction been identified, rather than gesturing at sanctions generally?
- Is the program correctly classified as comprehensive embargo, list-based, sectoral, or secondary, and does the operational response match that type?
- For comprehensive embargoes, is the near-total prohibition respected for imports, exports, services, financing, and new investment in the covered territory?
- For list-based programs, is the 50 Percent Rule applied so that unlisted SDN-owned entities are treated as blocked?
- For sectoral sanctions, is the applicable directive read to identify the precise debt, equity, energy, or service restrictions and their boundaries?
- Are secondary sanctions considered for non-US persons engaging in significant transactions with designated targets, independent of any US nexus?
- Has licensing been checked, distinguishing general license coverage from specific license requirements, with license conditions followed exactly?
- Is the program-to-action mapping documented with the governing authority and the basis for block, reject, decline, license, or proceed?
- Are humanitarian and other exceptions confirmed against current regulations rather than assumed to apply?
- Is the current state of the program verified against the latest executive orders, determinations, and general licenses rather than outdated descriptions?
