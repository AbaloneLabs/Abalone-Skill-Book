# Secondary Sanctions And Non-US Exposure

Primary sanctions apply to US persons, US-origin items, and US-dollar transactions, and their scope is relatively well understood. Secondary sanctions are different and more unsettling, because they reach conduct by non-US persons that has no US nexus at all, imposing US sanctions risk on foreign companies for dealings with specified targets. This is the mechanism by which the United States extends its sanctions influence globally, and it creates a compliance problem that primary sanctions analysis cannot solve. A non-US company, with no US persons, no US goods, and no US-dollar clearing, can nonetheless face sanctions exposure, up to being cut off from the US financial system or the US market, for transactions that are legal under its own jurisdiction's law. The compliance challenge is that secondary sanctions exposure does not appear on any list and does not trigger through any US nexus, so it is invisible to primary-focused screening, and it requires a distinct analytical framework centered on the conduct and the target rather than the party.

Use this skill before assessing secondary sanctions exposure for non-US parties, evaluating extraterritorial reach, or deciding whether a counterparty's conduct affects the company's risk. Secondary sanctions law is complex, politically driven, and changes with US foreign policy; exposure assessments must be confirmed with qualified counsel and are not matters of internal determination. This skill provides a framework for identifying the issues, not a legal opinion on any party's exposure.

## Core Rules

### Recognize That No US Nexus Does Not Mean No Exposure

The most dangerous assumption in sanctions compliance is that a transaction with no US nexus carries no US sanctions risk. For primary sanctions, this is largely correct; if no US person, US item, US-dollar clearing, or US territory is involved, primary sanctions generally do not apply. For secondary sanctions, the assumption fails entirely. Secondary sanctions are designed precisely to reach non-US conduct with no US nexus, imposing consequences on foreign parties for specified dealings with targeted jurisdictions, sectors, or parties. A non-US company that concludes it has no exposure because it has no US nexus has applied the wrong framework and may be accumulating risk it cannot see.

Assess secondary exposure through a distinct analysis that does not depend on US nexus. The relevant questions are what the conduct is, which target it involves, whether the conduct falls within a secondary sanctions scope, and whether the conduct is significant enough to trigger the consequence. Do not allow a primary-sanctions-only analysis to substitute for this assessment. Any transaction involving Iran, North Korea, Russia in specified sectors, or other secondary sanctions targets should trigger a secondary exposure analysis regardless of US nexus, because the exposure attaches to the conduct with the target, not to the connection to the United States.

### Identify The Conduct And Target Categories That Trigger Secondary Risk

Secondary sanctions do not apply to all conduct with all sanctioned parties; they apply to specified categories of conduct with specified targets, defined by statute, executive order, and agency guidance. Common trigger categories include significant transactions with specified Iranian, Russian, North Korean, or Venezuelan sectors and parties; transactions supporting specified weapons proliferation or terrorism activities; and dealings with parties designated for secondary sanctions specifically rather than for primary blocking. The categories change with US foreign policy, and a category that did not trigger exposure last year may trigger it this year.

Identify whether the conduct falls within a current secondary sanctions category. Assess the target, whether it is a jurisdiction, a sector, or a specifically designated party. Assess the nature of the transaction, whether it is a significant transaction, a petroleum purchase, a defense or proliferation-related dealing, or a financial transaction. Assess the applicable threshold, because some secondary sanctions require significant transactions, a standard that considers the size, number, frequency, and type of dealing. Map the conduct against the current secondary sanctions categories, recognizing that this mapping must be updated as US policy evolves.

### Assess Consequences Beyond Legal Prohibition

Secondary sanctions exposure is not typically about a legal prohibition that the company itself violates; it is about consequences that the US government can impose on the exposed party. The primary consequence is designation, being added to the SDN List or a related list, which cuts the party off from the US financial system and from US-dollar transactions. For a non-US company, this can be existential, because loss of US-dollar access affects the ability to transact globally. Other consequences include restrictions on US procurement, restrictions on US financial assistance, and reputational damage that affects commercial relationships beyond the US.

Assess consequences in terms of the party's vulnerability to them. A non-US company heavily dependent on US-dollar clearing or US market access is highly vulnerable to secondary sanctions consequences, and even conduct that is legally permissible under its own law may be imprudent because of the consequence risk. A counterparty that incurs secondary sanctions designation may become unable to transact, creating commercial and operational risk for companies that depend on it. Secondary sanctions analysis is therefore not only about whether the company itself is exposed but about whether its counterparties are exposed, because their exposure becomes the company's risk through dependency.

### Evaluate Counterparty Secondary Exposure As Dependency Risk

A company may have no direct secondary sanctions exposure but face risk through counterparties that do. If a key supplier, customer, or financial partner engages in conduct that creates secondary sanctions exposure, that party may be designated, and the designation disrupts the company's operations regardless of the company's own conduct. This dependency risk is particularly acute for non-US companies whose supply chains, financing, or markets run through parties active in secondary sanctions target jurisdictions.

Assess counterparty exposure as part of counterparty due diligence, especially for critical relationships. Identify whether key counterparties have significant dealings with secondary sanctions targets. Consider the concentration risk, whether the company depends on a few counterparties whose designation would be severely disruptive. For higher-risk counterparties, consider contractual protections, diversification, and contingency planning. Dependency risk is not theoretical; it has materialized when major non-US companies lost counterparties to secondary sanctions designation and faced operational disruption that their own compliance programs had not anticipated because they assessed only their own exposure.

### Navigate Conflict Between US Secondary Sanctions And Other Law

Secondary sanctions create legal conflict for non-US companies, because conduct that the US seeks to deter through secondary sanctions may be lawful, or even protected, under the company's home jurisdiction law. The EU has adopted blocking statutes that prohibit compliance with certain US secondary sanctions and that purport to protect EU companies from the consequences. This creates a genuine dilemma, because the non-US company may face US secondary sanctions risk for the conduct and home-jurisdiction risk for complying with US secondary sanctions rather than engaging in it.

This conflict cannot be resolved by compliance judgment alone; it requires legal analysis of the specific statutes, the applicable blocking regime, and the company's options. Recognize the conflict when it arises and escalate to counsel who can assess the interaction of US secondary sanctions, home-jurisdiction law, and any blocking statute. Do not assume that the US secondary sanctions position or the home-jurisdiction position automatically prevails; the analysis is fact-specific and jurisdiction-specific. Document the legal analysis and the basis for the company's decision, because the conflict means the company may face scrutiny from more than one regulator regardless of what it decides.

### Monitor Policy Changes That Alter Secondary Exposure

Secondary sanctions are an instrument of US foreign policy, and they change with that policy. Sanctions are imposed, tightened, relaxed, or lifted in response to geopolitical developments, and a category of conduct that carried no secondary exposure can become exposed, or vice versa, through a policy change. The Iran nuclear deal and its subsequent withdrawal, the evolution of Russia sanctions, and changes in Venezuela policy all illustrate how secondary exposure shifts with political decisions.

Maintain awareness of US foreign policy developments that affect secondary sanctions, not only of list and program updates. Establish a process to assess the impact of policy changes on current transactions, existing positions, and counterparty relationships. A secondary exposure assessment that was current six months ago may be outdated by a policy change last month, and only ongoing monitoring keeps the assessment reliable. For companies with material secondary exposure, this monitoring is not optional; it is the control that prevents the company from being caught by a change it did not see.

## Common Traps

### Applying Primary Sanctions Logic To Secondary Exposure

Concluding there is no exposure because there is no US nexus, when secondary sanctions reach non-US conduct precisely through the absence of nexus.

### Ignoring Counterparty Dependency Risk

Assessing only the company's own secondary exposure, missing that a critical counterparty's designation would disrupt operations regardless of the company's conduct.

### Treating Secondary Sanctions As Static

Failing to monitor policy changes that alter secondary exposure, leaving the assessment outdated as US foreign policy evolves.

### Assuming Legal Permissibility Means No Consequence Risk

Concluding that conduct lawful under home-jurisdiction law is safe, when secondary sanctions impose consequences regardless of legality under other law.

### Overlooking Blocking Statute Conflict

Complying with US secondary sanctions without assessing whether a home-jurisdiction blocking statute prohibits that compliance, creating exposure under multiple regimes.

### Missing The Significance Threshold

Treating any transaction with a secondary target as exposed, or treating none as exposed, without assessing the significance threshold that some secondary sanctions require.

### Concentrating Risk In Vulnerable Counterparties

Depending heavily on counterparties active in secondary sanctions target jurisdictions without diversification or contingency planning, creating concentrated dependency risk.

## Self-Check

- Is secondary sanctions exposure assessed through a distinct analysis that does not depend on US nexus, recognizing that no nexus does not mean no exposure?
- Is the conduct mapped against current secondary sanctions categories, including the target, transaction type, and significance threshold?
- Are consequences assessed in terms of the party's vulnerability, including US-dollar access, US market access, and designation risk?
- Is counterparty secondary exposure assessed as dependency risk, especially for critical relationships and concentrated dependencies?
- Is the potential conflict between US secondary sanctions and home-jurisdiction law, including blocking statutes, recognized and escalated to counsel?
- Is ongoing monitoring of US foreign policy developments maintained to keep the secondary exposure assessment current?
- For non-US counterparties active in target jurisdictions, is their secondary exposure evaluated as part of due diligence rather than dismissed?
- Are significance thresholds applied where secondary sanctions require them, rather than treating all or no transactions as exposed?
- Is the company's vulnerability to counterparty designation assessed, with diversification or contingency planning for critical dependencies?
- Would the company identify a policy change that newly exposed its conduct or its counterparties before the consequence materialized, or only after?
