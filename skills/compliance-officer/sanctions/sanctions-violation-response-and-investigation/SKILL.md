# Sanctions Violation Response And Investigation

When a sanctions violation is suspected, the response in the first hours and days determines the institution's legal and regulatory position for years afterward. The wrong initial action, returning funds that should be blocked, destroying records, or making admissions without privilege, can convert a manageable issue into an enforcement disaster. The right response is disciplined: freeze or hold the transaction, preserve evidence, establish privilege, scope the investigation, identify root cause, implement interim controls, and prepare for reporting and disclosure decisions. Sanctions liability is generally strict, so the focus is not on proving intent but on understanding the scope, the cause, and the remediation that will mitigate.

Use this skill before responding to a suspected sanctions violation, scoping an investigation, managing blocking and rejecting actions, preserving privilege, or implementing interim controls. The compliance officer must drive the response as a structured process, not as improvisation under pressure.

## Core Rules

### Act Immediately To Freeze Or Hold The Transaction

The first action on suspecting a sanctions issue is to prevent the transaction from completing in a way that compounds the violation.

- if an SDN match or blockable interest is suspected, freeze or hold the funds or property;
- do not return, transfer, or unwind the transaction pending analysis;
- recognize that returning blocked funds is itself a separate violation;
- if the transaction is rejectable rather than blockable, decline to process and prepare a rejection report.

The instinct to reverse a problematic transaction to make it go away is the most dangerous reflex. Freeze first, analyze second.

### Preserve Evidence Immediately

Evidence preservation must begin at the first sign of an issue, before scoping.

- preserve screening records, transaction records, communications, and system logs;
- issue a litigation or investigation hold to prevent routine deletion;
- identify custodians and data sources;
- preserve the list versions and timestamps relevant to the screening event;
- document the preservation steps taken and when.

Records lost after notice of a potential issue cannot be reconstructed and create adverse inferences.

### Establish Privilege Early

Investigations should be conducted under privilege where possible to protect the analysis and strategic decisions.

- engage legal counsel to direct or oversee the investigation;
- use counsel or outside counsel to conduct interviews and analyses where privilege is intended;
- structure communications and work product to maintain privilege;
- recognize that purely business communications are not privileged;
- coordinate with compliance on the non-privileged operational record.

Privilege is not automatic; it must be designed into the investigation from the start.

### Scope The Investigation Systematically

A sanctions investigation must determine what happened, how broadly it occurred, and why.

- identify the specific transaction or transactions at issue;
- determine the screening event, the list version, and the disposition;
- reconstruct the data available at the time of the decision;
- determine whether the violation is isolated or systemic;
- assess whether other transactions are affected by the same root cause;
- quantify the scope: number of transactions, value, parties, time period.

Scoping that stops at the single flagged transaction misses systemic issues and undermines the completeness of any later disclosure.

### Identify Root Cause, Not Just The Symptom

Regulators expect root cause analysis. A conclusion of "human error" without understanding why the error occurred is not a root cause.

- was the screening population incomplete?
- was a list stale or misconfigured?
- was a threshold set too high?
- was a data field missing?
- was a control bypassed or overridden?
- was training inadequate?
- was a third party or system failure involved?

Map the root cause to specific control failures and remediation.

### Implement Interim Controls Promptly

While the investigation proceeds, implement interim controls to prevent recurrence.

- tighten screening parameters or add manual reviews;
- expand the screened population;
- add four-eyes checks for affected transaction types;
- suspend or restrict affected relationships or corridors;
- enhance monitoring on related flows.

Interim controls demonstrate good faith and limit ongoing exposure. Document them and their effective dates.

### Prepare For Reporting And Disclosure Decisions

The investigation feeds reporting and voluntary self-disclosure decisions.

- determine whether blocking or rejection reports are required and whether they have been filed;
- assess whether the matter constitutes an apparent violation requiring VSD analysis;
- coordinate with legal on the timing and scope of any disclosure;
- recognize the time sensitivity of voluntariness for mitigation.

See the dedicated VSD and recordkeeping skills. The investigation must produce the complete and accurate record on which disclosure decisions depend.

### Coordinate With Business And Operations

Sanctions responses affect business operations. Coordinate so that holds, freezes, and interim controls are implemented consistently, while maintaining the integrity of the investigation and privilege.

## Common Traps

### Reversing Or Returning Funds To Resolve The Issue

Returning blockable funds is a separate violation. Freeze first and analyze; do not unwind to make the problem disappear.

### Failing To Preserve Evidence At First Notice

Records deleted after notice create adverse inferences and gaps. Preservation must be immediate and documented.

### Conducting The Investigation Without Privilege

Purely business investigations are not privileged. Privilege must be designed in through counsel from the start.

### Stopping At The Single Flagged Transaction

A single violation often reflects a systemic control failure. Scoping must look for the population of affected transactions.

### Concluding Human Error Without Root Cause

Human error is a symptom. The root cause is the control gap that allowed the error and must be identified and remediated.

### Delaying Interim Controls Until The Investigation Concludes

Ongoing exposure must be addressed immediately. Interim controls limit recurrence and demonstrate good faith.

### Making Admissions Without Privilege Or Strategy

Premature or uncoordinated admissions can prejudice the institution. Coordinate through counsel.

### Overlooking Reporting Timelines

Blocking and rejection reports have short windows. Delay creates independent violations.

## Self-Check

- On suspecting a sanctions issue, is the transaction frozen or held rather than returned, transferred, or unwound pending analysis?
- Is evidence preservation initiated immediately with a documented hold covering screening records, transaction records, communications, and system logs?
- Is privilege established early through legal counsel directing or overseeing the investigation, with communications and work product structured to maintain it?
- Is the investigation scoped systematically to determine the specific transactions, the screening event, list versions, and whether the issue is isolated or systemic?
- Is the scope quantified across number of transactions, value, parties, and time period?
- Is root cause identified beyond human error, mapping to specific control failures such as population gaps, stale lists, threshold errors, missing fields, or overrides?
- Are interim controls implemented promptly to prevent recurrence, with effective dates documented?
- Are blocking and rejection reports assessed for filing within required windows?
- Is the voluntary self-disclosure decision coordinated with legal, recognizing the time sensitivity of voluntariness?
- Is coordination with business and operations managed so holds and interim controls are implemented consistently without compromising the investigation or privilege?
