# EU, UK, UN, And Multilateral Sanctions

Sanctions are not a single global regime. The UN Security Council imposes binding measures on all member states, the EU imposes restrictive measures binding on EU persons and conduct in the EU, and the UK (through OFSI) imposes sanctions binding on UK persons and UK conduct. Each authority publishes its own consolidated lists, defines its own scope of "person" and "territorial" jurisdiction, and updates on its own schedule. For a multinational, several regimes can apply to the same transaction at once, and they can conflict, especially when US extraterritorial sanctions meet an EU or UK blocking statute that prohibits compliance with those sanctions.

Use this skill before advising on which regimes apply to a given party or transaction, how to reconcile overlapping measures, how to handle conflicts between US sanctions and EU or UK blocking statutes, and how to scope screening and controls to all applicable authorities. The compliance officer must identify every regime with jurisdiction, not only the most familiar one.

## Core Rules

### Identify Every Regime With Jurisdiction Over The Conduct

A transaction can trigger multiple regimes simultaneously. Enumerate jurisdictional hooks before concluding which measures apply.

- UN sanctions bind all member states and apply globally to listed parties; they are implemented nationally.
- EU restrictive measures bind EU nationals, entities incorporated in the EU, and conduct in whole or in part within the EU, including transactions with EU nexus.
- UK sanctions bind UK nationals, UK-incorporated entities, and conduct in the UK, and some measures have extra-territorial reach to UK persons abroad.
- US OFAC sanctions bind US persons and US-origin transactions, and reach non-US persons through secondary sanctions in defined cases.
- Other national regimes (Canada, Australia, Switzerland, and others) impose their own measures with their own scope.

A bank in London clearing a dollar payment through New York on behalf of an EU customer can be subject to EU, UK, and US measures at once. Map all hooks.

### Apply The UN Layer As The Global Floor

UN Security Council sanctions are the baseline. All member states must implement them.

- The UN Consolidated List captures designated individuals and entities.
- Member states must freeze assets, impose travel bans, and enforce arms embarges as directed.
- Implementation differs by country; the national list may be broader than the UN minimum.
- UN measures rarely conflict with each other, but national implementations can diverge in detail.

Treat UN sanctions as a floor, then layer national and regional measures on top.

### Apply EU Restrictive Measures By Person And Territorial Scope

EU sanctions bind more than EU-domiciled entities. The scope is broad.

- EU nationals wherever located;
- legal persons incorporated in the EU;
- conduct in whole or in part within EU territory, including transactions using EU financial infrastructure;
- EU-flagged vessels and EU-registered aircraft;
- sometimes non-EU subsidiaries of EU parent companies under national implementing laws.

A dollar or euro payment cleared through the EU can bring EU measures into scope even if the parties are non-EU. Confirm the financial nexus.

### Apply UK OFSI Measures By Person And Territorial Scope

UK sanctions apply post-Brexit under independent authority, often mirroring but not identical to EU measures.

- UK nationals and UK-resident persons;
- legal persons incorporated or constituted under UK law;
- conduct in the UK, including through UK financial infrastructure;
- certain extra-territorial provisions reaching UK persons abroad;
- overseas territories and crown dependencies maintain aligned but separate regimes.

UK and EU lists have diverged since Brexit. Do not assume parity. Screen against both.

### Reconcile Overlapping And Conflicting Measures

Where multiple regimes apply, the strictest combined standard generally governs, but conflicts require legal analysis.

- When regimes align, apply the most restrictive cumulative effect.
- When regimes conflict, particularly US extraterritorial sanctions versus EU/UK blocking statutes, the institution faces competing legal duties.
- The EU Blocking Statute (Council Regulation 2271/96) and analogous UK rules prohibit compliance with certain US extraterritorial sanctions, notably Iran and Cuba-related, and require notification of affected activity.
- A non-US entity cannot both comply with US secondary sanctions and obey the blocking statute; this requires legal and sometimes governmental engagement.

Do not resolve conflicts by defaulting to the US position or by ignoring the conflict. Escalate conflicts to legal counsel and document the analysis.

### Manage List Currency Across Multiple Authorities

Each authority updates on its own schedule. A consolidated screening list must track all relevant sources.

- UN list updates flow to national implementations with a lag.
- EU publishes consolidated list updates in the Official Journal.
- UK OFSI publishes consolidated list updates separately.
- US OFAC updates on its own cadence, sometimes intraday.

Record the source and version for each list against each screening event. A single stale source undermines the whole screen.

### Scope Controls To The Strictest Applicable Combination

Design controls to satisfy the strictest combination of applicable regimes. If EU measures prohibit a transaction that US measures would permit, an EU-bound institution must decline. If US secondary sanctions would designate for conduct the EU permits, the institution must analyze both and decide with legal input.

## Common Traps

### Defaulting To The US Regime Only

US OFAC is prominent, but EU, UK, and UN obligations are independent and often broader in person-scope. Screening only OFAC misses EU and UK designations.

### Assuming EU And UK Lists Are Identical Post-Brexit

The lists and measures have diverged. A party cleared under EU measures may be designated under UK measures or vice versa.

### Ignoring Financial Infrastructure Nexus

EU and UK measures reach conduct using EU or UK financial infrastructure, even for non-EU or non-UK parties. A payment cleared through London or Frankfurt triggers UK or EU scope.

### Treating UN Sanctions As Self-Executing

UN measures require national implementation. The binding list is the national one, which may be broader. Screen the national implementation, not only the UN consolidated list.

### Resolving US Versus Blocking Statute Conflicts By Guessing

The conflict between US extraterritorial sanctions and EU/UK blocking statutes is a serious legal problem. It must not be resolved by defaulting to one side without analysis and notification duties.

### Overlooking Overseas Territories And Crown Dependencies

UK sanctions extend through aligned regimes in overseas territories and crown dependencies that are distinct financial centers. Confirm scope for activity routed through them.

### Failing To Track Multiple List Update Cadences

Each authority updates separately. A consolidated list that lags on one source creates a gap. Monitor all sources and record versions.

## Self-Check

- Has every regime with jurisdiction been enumerated, including UN, EU, UK, US, and other national measures, based on person, territorial, and financial nexus?
- Is the UN consolidated list treated as a global floor with national implementations layered on top, recognizing that national lists may be broader?
- Are EU restrictive measures applied by their broad person and territorial scope, including EU financial infrastructure nexus and EU-flagged vessels?
- Are UK OFSI measures applied independently of EU measures, recognizing post-Brexit divergence in lists and scope?
- Where multiple regimes apply, is the strictest cumulative standard applied, with conflicts between US extraterritorial sanctions and EU/UK blocking statutes escalated to legal counsel?
- Is the EU Blocking Statute and UK equivalent considered where US secondary sanctions create compliance conflicts, with notification duties assessed?
- Are overseas territories and crown dependencies considered for UK-routed activity?
- Are list sources and versions recorded for UN, EU, UK, and US against each screening event, given divergent update cadences?
- Are controls designed to satisfy the strictest applicable combination rather than the most familiar single regime?
- Is the financial infrastructure nexus assessed so that payments cleared through EU or UK systems trigger EU or UK scope even for non-EU or non-UK parties?
