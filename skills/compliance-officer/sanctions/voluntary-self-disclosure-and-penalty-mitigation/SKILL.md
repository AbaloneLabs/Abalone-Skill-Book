# Voluntary Self-Disclosure And Penalty Mitigation

Voluntary self-disclosure (VSD) is one of the most consequential strategic decisions in sanctions and export enforcement. Done correctly and promptly, it can substantially mitigate penalties under the OFAC enforcement framework, the EAR, and the ITAR. Done late, incompletely, or after the government has begun to discover the violation, it forfeits mitigation and can create additional exposure for misrepresentation. The decision is not mechanical: it requires assessing whether the conduct constitutes an apparent violation, whether disclosure is truly voluntary, whether the investigation is complete enough to support an accurate submission, and what compliance commitments will accompany the disclosure. The penalty matrix and the aggravating and mitigating factors are the framework within which the strategy must be built.

Use this skill before advising on whether to submit a VSD, structuring the disclosure, applying the OFAC enforcement framework and penalty matrix, evaluating mitigating and aggravating factors, or designing compliance commitments to support mitigation. The compliance officer must approach VSD as a legal and strategic judgment, not as a default reflex.

## Core Rules

### Determine Whether The Conduct Constitutes An Apparent Violation

Before disclosing, confirm the conduct is an apparent violation under the applicable regime.

- under OFAC, assess whether a prohibited transaction, blocking failure, or other violation occurred;
- under the EAR, assess whether an unlicensed export, misclassification, or end-use violation occurred;
- under the ITAR, assess whether an unlicensed export, technical data release, or brokering violation occurred;
- distinguish actual violations from potential issues that require further investigation;
- do not disclose prematurely on incomplete facts, but do not delay past the voluntariness window.

Disclosure should follow a complete enough investigation to be accurate, but must precede government discovery.

### Confirm Voluntariness Before Relying On It

Mitigation for VSD depends on the disclosure being voluntary, which has a specific meaning.

- the disclosure must be made before the government discovers the violation through other means;
- a disclosure prompted by a subpoena, inquiry, or investigation of the specific conduct is not voluntary;
- the timing must be assessed against what the government knew or was investigating;
- partial or strategic disclosures may not qualify for full mitigation.

Assess voluntariness realistically. A disclosure made after a regulator has begun looking is not voluntary.

### Apply The OFAC Enforcement Framework

OFAC's Framework for OFAC Compliance Commitments structures enforcement and mitigation around the compliance program and the conduct.

- the framework considers whether the institution had a compliance program at the time of the violation;
- it considers whether the violation was the result of a deficient program or an isolated failure;
- it considers the response, including VSD and remediation;
- it considers aggravating factors such as willfulness, harm to sanctions objectives, and pattern of conduct;
- it considers mitigating factors such as VSD, cooperation, remediation, and compliance commitment.

The framework is the lens through which OFAC calibrates penalties. Build the disclosure and remediation to address it.

### Work Through The Penalty Matrix

The OFAC penalty matrix and the EAR and ITAR penalty provisions provide ranges based on culpability and circumstances.

- civil penalties vary by statute and can be substantial per violation;
- the matrix considers the egregiousness of the conduct;
- it considers the harm to sanctions or national security objectives;
- it considers the violator's conduct, including VSD, cooperation, and remediation;
- criminal exposure applies in cases of willfulness.

Understand the applicable penalty range and the factors that move within it. Mitigation is earned by addressing the aggravating factors.

### Evaluate Mitigating Factors

Mitigating factors reduce penalty exposure and should be developed and documented.

- voluntary self-disclosure made promptly;
- full cooperation with the investigation;
- remediation of the root cause;
- a compliance commitment, including the five essential components of an effective program;
- the isolated versus systemic nature of the violation;
- the absence of willfulness or harm;
- prior clean record and good faith.

Document each mitigating factor with evidence. VSD alone is not sufficient without remediation and commitment.

### Evaluate Aggravating Factors Honestly

Aggravating factors increase exposure and must be assessed honestly to calibrate strategy.

- willfulness or recklessness;
- harm to sanctions or national security objectives;
- pattern of violations over time;
- management involvement or tolerance;
- deficient or absent compliance program;
- failure to remediate prior issues;
- obstruction or lack of cooperation.

Ignoring aggravating factors does not make them disappear. A credible strategy addresses them.

### Structure The Compliance Commitment

A meaningful compliance commitment accompanies the disclosure and supports mitigation.

- commit to the five essential components: management commitment, risk assessment, internal controls, testing and auditing, and training;
- describe specific remediation taken and planned;
- tie remediation to the root cause identified in the investigation;
- provide timelines and ownership for remediation;
- avoid generic commitments unconnected to the specific failure.

See the dedicated sanctions compliance program framework skill. The commitment must be specific and credible.

### Coordinate Timing And Scope With Counsel

VSD timing and scope are strategic and must be coordinated with counsel.

- complete the investigation sufficiently to support an accurate disclosure;
- file before the voluntariness window closes;
- cooperate fully and provide supplemental information as developed;
- maintain privilege over deliberative strategy while providing complete factual disclosure.

## Common Traps

### Disclosing Prematurely On Incomplete Facts

An inaccurate or incomplete disclosure can create misrepresentation exposure and forfeit mitigation. Complete the investigation sufficiently first.

### Disclosing After The Government Has Begun To Discover The Violation

Late disclosure is not voluntary and loses mitigation. Assess the voluntariness window realistically.

### Treating VSD As A Default Reflex

VSD is a strategic decision. Not every potential issue warrants disclosure, and some disclosures may create exposure without benefit.

### Ignoring Aggravating Factors

Honest assessment of willfulness, harm, pattern, and program deficiency is necessary to calibrate strategy. Ignoring them is not viable.

### Offering Generic Compliance Commitments

A commitment unconnected to the root cause and the five essential components is not credible and does not support mitigation.

### Overlooking The Penalty Matrix And Framework

Strategy must be built on the applicable penalty range and the enforcement framework. Guessing at exposure leads to poor decisions.

### Disclosing Strategically Rather Than Completely

Partial or strategic disclosures may not qualify for full mitigation and can create misrepresentation exposure.

### Failing To Document Mitigating Factors

Mitigation must be evidenced. Undocumented VSD, cooperation, and remediation cannot be credited.

## Self-Check

- Has the conduct been confirmed as an apparent violation under the applicable regime before disclosure, with the investigation complete enough to support an accurate submission?
- Is voluntariness confirmed, with disclosure made before the government discovers the violation through other means?
- Is the OFAC enforcement framework applied, considering the compliance program at the time, the cause, the response, and the aggravating and mitigating factors?
- Is the applicable penalty matrix understood, including civil and criminal ranges and the factors that move within them?
- Are mitigating factors identified and documented, including VSD, cooperation, remediation, compliance commitment, isolated nature, absence of willfulness, and prior record?
- Are aggravating factors assessed honestly, including willfulness, harm, pattern, management involvement, program deficiency, and obstruction?
- Is the compliance commitment structured around the five essential components and tied to the specific root cause with timelines and ownership?
- Are timing and scope coordinated with counsel, balancing investigation completeness against the voluntariness window?
- Is the disclosure complete and accurate rather than strategic or partial?
- Are deliberative strategy protected by privilege while complete factual disclosure is provided?
