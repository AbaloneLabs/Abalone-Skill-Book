# Sanctions Compliance Program Framework

OFAC's Framework for OFAC Compliance Commitments defines what an effective sanctions compliance program looks like, and it is the standard against which programs are measured in enforcement. The framework rests on five essential components: management commitment, risk assessment, internal controls, testing and auditing, and training. A program missing or weak in any component is treated as deficient, and deficiency is an aggravating factor in enforcement. But the framework is not a checklist to satisfy; it is an operating system that must be calibrated to the institution's actual sanctions risk, resourced to function, and evidenced to prove effectiveness. A program that exists on paper but cannot stop, detect, or remediate violations provides no mitigation.

Use this skill before designing or evaluating a sanctions compliance program, applying the OFAC framework, conducting a sanctions risk assessment, building internal controls, designing testing and training, or establishing governance. The compliance officer must build the program around the institution's real exposure and prove it works, not assemble a generic framework.

## Core Rules

### Secure Senior Management Commitment

The first essential component is management commitment. Without it, the program lacks authority, resources, and the ability to enforce decisions against business pressure.

- senior leadership must openly support sanctions compliance;
- the compliance function must have authority, independence, and a direct reporting line;
- adequate resources, staffing, and technology must be provided;
- leadership must empower compliance to escalate and, where necessary, stop transactions;
- compliance must have access to relevant data and systems;
- the tone from the top must make clear that sanctions compliance is non-negotiable.

A compliance officer without authority to act cannot run an effective program. Resource and authority gaps are themselves risks to report.

### Conduct A Sanctions-Specific Risk Assessment

The second component is risk assessment, tailored to sanctions exposure rather than generic compliance.

- assess the institution's products, services, customers, geographies, and channels;
- identify sanctions exposure by jurisdiction, counterparty type, and transaction flow;
- assess exposure to comprehensive, list-based, sectoral, and secondary sanctions;
- evaluate ownership and control exposure under the 50 Percent Rule;
- consider dollar clearing and US financial system nexus;
- assess third-party, correspondent, and transshipment exposure;
- rate inherent risk and then evaluate controls to determine residual risk;
- reassess on defined triggers: new products, jurisdictions, customers, or regulatory changes.

A risk assessment that does not drive the rest of the program is decorative. Use it to allocate resources and design controls.

### Build Internal Controls Matched To Risk

The third component is internal controls: the policies, procedures, and systems that prevent and detect violations.

- screening controls covering the full population at the right points;
- ownership and control analysis for beneficial owners;
- license determination and management for exports;
- end-use, end-user, and restricted-party screening;
- blocking and rejection procedures with reporting;
- escalation paths for true matches and red flags;
- system controls over screening technology, list currency, and thresholds;
- exception handling with documented approval;
- recordkeeping that reconstructs each decision.

Controls must be matched to the specific risks identified in the assessment. A control that does not address a defined risk is not a control.

### Implement Testing And Auditing

The fourth component is testing and auditing: independent verification that controls are designed and operating effectively.

- test screening by injecting known-listed names;
- audit match dispositions for quality and rationale;
- test list currency and update monitoring;
- audit license management and condition compliance;
- test blocking and rejection reporting timeliness;
- review exception handling for appropriateness;
- assess the screening population for completeness;
- use independent testers where possible;
- report findings, root causes, and remediation to management.

Testing must be risk-based and frequent enough to catch degradation. Findings must drive remediation.

### Deliver Targeted Training

The fifth component is training, tailored to role and risk.

- train operations staff on screening, blocking, and rejection;
- train relationship managers on sanctions red flags and KYC;
- train trade and export staff on classification, licensing, and end-use;
- train leadership on governance and escalation;
- train on new programs, list changes, and enforcement trends;
- test comprehension, not just attendance;
- refresh on a defined cadence and on material changes.

Generic annual training does not build capability. Training must be role-specific and current.

### Establish Governance And Oversight

Governance structures hold the program together.

- define the sanctions compliance officer role with clear authority;
- establish a committee or forum for sanctions decisions and escalations;
- define reporting to the board or senior management on a regular cadence;
- maintain a sanctions policy approved at the appropriate level;
- define roles and responsibilities across compliance, operations, legal, and technology;
- coordinate with AML, export, trade, and anti-bribery functions.

### Calibrate The Program To The Institution

The framework is not one-size-fits-all. A small institution with limited exposure needs a proportionate program; a global bank with complex flows needs a far more elaborate one. The program must be calibrated to the institution's size, sophistication, and risk, while still addressing all five components.

### Evidence Effectiveness Continuously

A program is only as good as the evidence it produces. Maintain records of risk assessments, controls, testing, training, governance, and remediation. In enforcement, the institution must prove the program existed and operated.

## Common Traps

### Building A Program On Paper Without Authority Or Resources

A program without management commitment cannot function. Authority and resource gaps must be reported as risks.

### Generic Risk Assessment Not Tailored To Sanctions

A risk assessment that does not address jurisdiction, counterparty, ownership, secondary sanctions, and clearing nexus does not drive the program.

### Controls Unmatched To Specific Risks

A control that does not address a defined risk is decorative. Controls must be risk-driven.

### Testing That Confirms Design Without Verifying Operation

A control can be well designed but fail in operation. Testing must verify operation, including by injecting test cases.

### Generic Annual Training

Role-irrelevant training does not build capability. Training must be targeted and current.

### No Governance Or Escalation Path

Without governance, decisions are ad hoc and escalations stall. Define the structures and reporting.

### One-Size-Fits-All Program

A program must be calibrated to the institution's size and risk. Over- or under-building both create problems.

### No Evidence Of Effectiveness

A program that cannot prove it existed and operated provides no mitigation. Evidence is continuous, not occasional.

## Self-Check

- Does senior management openly support sanctions compliance, with the compliance function granted authority, independence, resources, data access, and escalation power?
- Is a sanctions-specific risk assessment conducted covering products, customers, geographies, channels, jurisdictional programs, ownership, secondary sanctions, clearing nexus, and third-party exposure?
- Does the risk assessment rate inherent and residual risk and drive resource allocation and control design, with reassessment on defined triggers?
- Are internal controls matched to specific risks, including screening population, ownership analysis, licensing, end-use and end-user controls, blocking and rejection, escalation, system controls, exception handling, and recordkeeping?
- Is testing and auditing independent, risk-based, and frequent enough to catch degradation, with findings driving remediation and management reporting?
- Is training targeted to role and risk, testing comprehension, and refreshed on a defined cadence and on material changes?
- Is governance established with a defined sanctions compliance officer role, committee, board reporting, policy approval, role clarity, and coordination with AML, export, trade, and anti-bribery functions?
- Is the program calibrated to the institution's size, sophistication, and risk while addressing all five components?
- Is effectiveness evidenced continuously through records of risk assessment, controls, testing, training, governance, and remediation?
- Can the institution prove in enforcement that the program existed and operated, not merely that it was documented?
