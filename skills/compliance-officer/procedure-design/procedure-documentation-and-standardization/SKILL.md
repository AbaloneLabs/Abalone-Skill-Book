# Procedure Documentation And Standardization

Procedure documentation is treated as a formality, and that is why it so often fails the moment it matters. Procedures are written to satisfy an audit checklist, filed in a repository, and never reconciled with how the work is actually done. Over time they decay: the process changes but the document does not, units interpret the same requirement differently, and the gap between the documented procedure and the real one grows until the documentation is fiction. The harm is serious. When a control fails or a regulator asks how a process works, the organization points to documentation that no longer matches reality, which is worse than having no documentation because it demonstrates that the organization believed a false picture. Standardization fails similarly: in the name of local flexibility, the same risk is managed to different standards across units, and the organization cannot claim a single coherent program. Documentation and standardization are not paperwork; they are how an organization makes a process repeatable, trainable, auditable, and defensible.

Use this skill before documenting or standardizing compliance procedures, writing standard operating procedures, reconciling documentation with practice, or diagnosing inconsistency across units. The goal is to make the agent produce documentation that matches reality, standardize where consistency protects the program, and keep both current rather than treating documentation as a one-time artifact.

## Core Rules

### Document The Real Process, Not The Aspirational One

The most common documentation failure is writing the process as it should be rather than as it is, or as compliance wishes it were. Aspirational documentation creates a false record and sets up the organization to fail an audit or a regulator's test, because the documented process will be compared to actual practice and found wanting.

Document reality by:

- basing the procedure on observation of the actual work, including the steps people really perform;
- capturing the informal workarounds and deciding explicitly whether to formalize or eliminate them;
- distinguishing mandatory steps from guidance, so the document reflects what must happen;
- acknowledging where the process varies and documenting the permitted variation;
- reviewing the draft with the people who execute it to confirm accuracy.

Documentation that does not match reality is a liability. If the real process is unacceptable, the task is to fix the process, not to document a fiction.

### Standardize Where Consistency Protects, Adapt Where Context Requires

Standardization is valuable but not absolute. Total uniformity can impose a process that fits no unit well, while total flexibility fragments the program. The judgment is where standardization protects the program and where local adaptation is necessary.

Standardize by:

- setting enterprise-wide mandatory standards and minimum controls that all units must meet, with no local variation below the floor;
- allowing local adaptation only within defined boundaries, for steps where context genuinely changes the right approach;
- documenting the boundary clearly, so units know what they must do uniformly and where they may adapt;
- centralizing reporting and metrics so performance is comparable across units despite local variation;
- reviewing local variations periodically to confirm they remain justified.

The enterprise floor is non-negotiable; above it, allow adaptation that improves fit without compromising the standard. Never let adaptation become divergence below the floor.

### Write Procedures That The Executor Can Actually Follow

A procedure that is technically accurate but unusable is ignored. Procedures are written for the people who must execute them under real conditions, not for the auditor who will read them afterward. Usability is a documentation requirement, not a nicety.

Write for usability by:

- writing in plain language, defining terms, and avoiding jargon the executor does not use;
- structuring the procedure as clear sequential steps with decision points and expected actions;
- including the inputs, tools, and references the executor needs at each step;
- specifying what to do when something goes wrong, not only the happy path;
- keeping the document concise enough to be used in practice, since a hundred-page procedure will not be read.

A procedure the executor cannot understand or apply is not a control. Test it with the people who will use it.

### Assign Clear Ownership For Each Procedure And Its Accuracy

Documentation decays when no one owns it. A procedure without an owner is edited by no one, reconciled with nothing, and trusted by everyone until it fails. Every procedure needs a named owner accountable for its accuracy and currency.

Assign ownership by:

- naming a role, not a person, as owner, so ownership survives turnover;
- making the owner responsible for reviewing the procedure on a schedule and after process change;
- requiring the owner to reconcile the document with actual practice, not merely re-read it;
- tracking review dates so stale procedures are visible;
- ensuring the owner has the authority and knowledge to maintain accuracy.

A procedure owned by everyone is maintained by no one. Name the owner and hold them to the review.

### Reconcile Documentation With Practice Continuously

The gap between documentation and practice is the central risk, and it grows silently. Reconciliation, checking that the document still matches reality, must be a continuous discipline, not a one-time effort.

Reconcile by:

- scheduling regular reviews tied to the risk and volatility of the process;
- triggering review after any process change, system change, or regulatory change;
- using walkthroughs and testing to compare documented steps to actual execution;
- capturing feedback from executors when the document does not match their work;
- treating a discovered gap as a finding that requires either updating the document or fixing the practice.

A document reviewed only on a fixed cycle will be stale within it. Reconcile continuously, especially after change.

### Version Control And Make Documentation Auditable

Documentation must be traceable: a regulator or auditor must be able to see what the procedure required at a given time, not only what it requires now. Without version control, the organization cannot demonstrate what standard applied when an event occurred.

Make auditable by:

- applying version control with dates, authors, and change summaries;
- retaining prior versions so historical requirements can be reconstructed;
- recording approvals and the authority under which each version was issued;
- maintaining a repository that is organized, searchable, and access-controlled;
- ensuring the current version is unambiguously the one in use.

A procedure that cannot be shown as it existed at a relevant time cannot be defended. Version control is not optional.

### Connect Documentation To Training And Testing

Documentation that is not connected to how people are trained and how controls are tested becomes a standalone artifact with no operational force. The procedure must be the basis for both.

Connect by:

- aligning training to the documented procedure, so people are taught what they must do;
- basing control testing on the documented procedure, so testing verifies the real standard;
- using testing findings to identify where documentation, training, or practice diverge;
- ensuring changes to the procedure flow into retraining and updated testing.

## Common Traps

### Documenting The Aspirational Process

Writing the process as it should be rather than as it is creates a false record. Document reality or fix the process.

### Total Flexibility Fragmenting The Program

Allowing each unit to manage the same risk differently prevents a coherent program. Standardize the floor and allow bounded adaptation.

### Procedures Written For Auditors, Not Executors

A technically accurate but unusable procedure is ignored. Write for the people who execute under real conditions.

### No Owner, So Documentation Decays

A procedure owned by everyone is maintained by no one. Name a role owner and hold them to review and reconciliation.

### Fixed-Cycle Review Letting Gaps Grow

Documentation reviewed only on a schedule goes stale within it. Reconcile continuously and after change.

### No Version Control

Without traceable versions, the organization cannot show what standard applied when. Version control and retain history.

### Documentation Disconnected From Training And Testing

A procedure not aligned with training and testing has no operational force. Connect all three and use findings to reconcile.

## Self-Check

- Is documentation based on observation of the actual process, capturing real steps and workarounds, distinguishing mandatory from guidance, and reviewed with executors for accuracy?
- Is standardization applied where consistency protects the program through enterprise mandatory floors, with bounded local adaptation, clear boundaries, comparable reporting, and periodic review of variations?
- Are procedures written in plain language, structured as clear steps with decision points, including inputs and exception handling, concise enough to use, and tested with executors?
- Is a role owner assigned for each procedure, responsible for scheduled review, reconciliation with practice, and with the authority and knowledge to maintain accuracy?
- Is documentation reconciled with practice continuously through scheduled reviews, change triggers, walkthroughs, executor feedback, and gap treatment as findings?
- Are procedures version-controlled with dates, authors, change summaries, retained prior versions, approval records, and an organized, searchable, access-controlled repository?
- Is documentation connected to training and testing, with training aligned to the procedure, testing based on it, and findings used to reconcile the three?
- Could the documentation be defended to a regulator as an accurate, current, and standardized reflection of how the process really works?
