# Trade Associations Statistical Programs And Benchmarking Controls

Trade association statistical programs and industry benchmarking initiatives collect, aggregate, and distribute business data among competitors. While these programs serve legitimate purposes such as performance comparison, trend analysis, and industry research, they are also channels for exchanging competitively sensitive information. Under the US Sherman Act Section 1, the EU Treaty Article 101, and equivalent national competition laws, statistical programs that share current or future-oriented data, that are insufficiently aggregated, or that allow identification of individual competitors can constitute unlawful information exchange. The central judgment problem is that a statistical program can appear well-structured with an independent administrator and aggregated reports, while still allowing participants to identify individual competitor data through small participant counts, detailed breakouts, or insufficient historical lag. A compliance officer who approves a program based on its general structure without stress-testing its specific aggregation standards can leave the firm exposed to coordination risk.

Use this skill before reviewing statistical programs, establishing benchmarking controls, evaluating reporting templates, or advising on collective data sharing. The goal is to make the agent think about how data flows through the program, what safeguards genuinely prevent identification, and the decisions that are easy to make too casually. A benchmarking program that produces useful industry insights can still violate antitrust law if it enables participants to identify and respond to individual competitor strategies.

This skill addresses jurisdiction-specific obligations. Statistical program standards, safe harbors, and enforcement approaches differ across the US, the EU, and national regimes. Always confirm the applicable national competition law and authority guidance, and consult qualified antitrust or legal counsel for specific statistical program assessments.

## Core Rules

### Assess The Program Purpose And Legitimacy

Before participating in a statistical program, the firm should assess whether its purpose is legitimate and whether the data collected is appropriate for that purpose.

Legitimate program purposes:

- industry-wide performance benchmarking against aggregated standards;
- trend analysis and forecasting based on historical data;
- safety, environmental, or technical research;
- regulatory reporting or compliance support;
- workforce or compensation benchmarking through independent surveys.

Problematic program purposes:

- facilitating pricing coordination through exchange of current price data;
- enabling customer allocation through exchange of customer information;
- supporting output or capacity coordination through exchange of production data;
- providing individual competitor intelligence that enables strategic response.

The program purpose should be documented and should justify the type and granularity of data collected. Programs that collect competitively sensitive data without a clear legitimate purpose carry significant risk.

### Require Independent Administration And Information Barriers

Statistical programs must be administered by an independent party that collects, processes, and reports data without disclosing individual company information.

Independent administration requirements:

- the administrator must be independent of all participants, such as a certified consultant or dedicated association staff;
- the administrator must collect data directly from participants, not through a participant or through general association staff who serve participants;
- the administrator must enforce strict information barriers, preventing any participant from accessing individual company data;
- the administrator must apply aggregation and suppression standards before reporting;
- the administrator must certify compliance with safeguards and document its methodology;
- the administrator must not retain or share individual data outside the defined program scope.

Using general association staff, a participant employee, or a consultant with commercial interests in the data does not create genuine independence and does not protect the exchange.

### Establish Aggregation And Suppression Standards

Aggregation and suppression standards determine whether individual company data can be identified from reports. Standards must be set to genuinely prevent identification.

Aggregation standard elements:

- require a minimum number of contributors to each reported data point, typically at least five;
- ensure no single contributor represents more than a defined percentage, typically 25 percent or less, of the aggregated total;
- suppress data cells where minimum contributor or dominance thresholds are not met, and do not disclose suppressed cells;
- apply thresholds at every level of reporting granularity, not just at the total level;
- review all breakouts, sub-categories, and time series for identification risk.

A report that is aggregated at the top level but includes detailed breakouts by product, region, or customer segment can reveal individual data in the breakouts even when the total is protected.

### Design Historical Lag And Data Currency Controls

The lag between data collection and reporting affects whether exchanged data reveals current competitive strategies.

Historical lag requirements:

- pricing data should have a lag of at least one year, and preferably longer;
- cost and margin data should have a lag appropriate to the industry's pricing and planning cycle;
- output and capacity data may have shorter lags if less strategically sensitive, but must be historical, not current or future;
- future-oriented data, including planned price changes, investment intentions, or entry plans, must never be collected or exchanged;
- the lag should account for the industry's strategic planning cycle, because data that is sufficiently old in a slow-moving industry may still reveal current strategies in a fast-moving one.

The program documentation should specify the historical lag for each data type and the rationale based on industry characteristics.

### Control Reporting Granularity And Format

The granularity and format of reports affect identification risk. Reports must be designed to prevent triangulation and re-identification.

Reporting granularity controls:

- limit the number of breakouts and sub-categories in reports;
- avoid reporting data at the product, region, or customer level if it enables identification;
- aggregate across dimensions, such as combining regions or products, to increase contributor counts;
- suppress breakouts that do not meet aggregation thresholds;
- avoid reporting ranges, percentiles, or distributions that enable identification of outliers;
- avoid time series that reveal trends attributable to individual companies.

The reporting template should be reviewed for granularity risk, and the administrator should be required to suppress fine-grained breakouts.

### Assess Re-Identification And Triangulation Risk

Even with aggregation and independent administration, data can be re-identified through triangulation, where participants combine program data with their own knowledge or other sources.

Re-identification risk factors:

- small participant counts that allow process-of-elimination identification;
- detailed breakouts that reveal individual data;
- data combined with public information, such as market share or revenue, that enables identification;
- complementary programs that collectively enable identification when individual programs do not;
- industry structure where participants know each other's operations well enough to attribute data.

The firm should assess re-identification risk by attempting to identify competitors from sample reports and should require the administrator to conduct similar assessments.

### Establish Participant Obligations And Program Governance

Participants must understand and comply with their obligations, and the program must have governance to enforce safeguards.

Participant obligation elements:

- provide accurate and complete data to the administrator;
- do not attempt to identify individual competitors from reports;
- do not use reports to facilitate coordination;
- maintain confidentiality of reports within authorized personnel;
- report any identification or coordination attempts to the administrator and to compliance.

Program governance elements:

- written program rules approved by antitrust counsel;
- periodic review of safeguards, typically annually;
- participant agreements that codify obligations and provide for termination;
- incident handling and investigation protocols;
- documentation of program design, safeguards, and any modifications.

## Common Traps

### Administrator That Is Not Genuinely Independent

Using general association staff or a consultant with commercial interests does not create independence and does not protect the exchange.

### Aggregation Only At The Top Level

Aggregation that meets thresholds at the total level but reveals individual data in breakouts does not prevent identification.

### Insufficient Historical Lag For The Industry

Data that is historical in a slow-moving industry may reveal current strategies in a fast-moving industry. The lag must account for the strategic cycle.

### Detailed Breakouts Enabling Triangulation

Breakouts by product, region, or customer that allow process-of-elimination identification defeat aggregation at the total level.

### Future-Oriented Data In Surveys

Collecting planned prices, investments, or entry plans facilitates coordination and is treated as a restriction by object.

### No Re-Identification Testing

Aggregation standards that are not stress-tested against actual reports can fail to prevent identification in practice.

### Complementary Programs Enabling Collective Identification

Participants who combine data from multiple programs can identify competitors even when each program is individually safe.

## Self-Check

- Is the program purpose assessed for legitimacy, with data collection justified by a clear legitimate purpose?
- Is the administrator genuinely independent, collecting data directly, enforcing information barriers, and certifying compliance?
- Do aggregation standards require minimum contributor counts, typically five or more, and maximum dominance thresholds, typically 25 percent or less, at every reporting level?
- Is historical lag set to at least one year for pricing data and appropriate to the industry cycle for other data types?
- Is future-oriented data, including planned prices, investments, and entry plans, excluded from all collection?
- Is reporting granularity controlled through limited breakouts, suppression of fine-grained categories, and avoidance of ranges that reveal outliers?
- Is re-identification and triangulation risk assessed by attempting to identify competitors from sample reports?
- Are participant obligations codified in written agreements, including accuracy, non-identification, non-coordination, and confidentiality?
- Is the program governance framework documented with rules, periodic review, participant agreements, and incident protocols?
- Is the statistical program assessment confirmed against the applicable national competition law and authority guidance rather than a generic standard?
