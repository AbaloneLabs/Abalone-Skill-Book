# Advertising And Marketing Practices Compliance

Advertising is governed by rules against unfair and deceptive acts and practices that require claims to be truthful, substantiated, and not misleading. The defining feature is that liability turns on the net impression of an advertisement on a reasonable consumer, not on technical accuracy in isolation, and that substantiation must exist before the claim is made. The central difficulty is that modern marketing spans reviews, influencers, native content, dark patterns, subscription traps, and algorithmic targeting, and the old distinction between advertising and content has blurred. Compliance failure can mean corrective advertising, consumer redress, injunctions, and state and federal enforcement.

Use this skill before advising on an advertising campaign, reviewing marketing claims, evaluating an influencer program, assessing subscription or pricing practices, or planning a sweepstakes. The goal is to make the agent identify the claim, the substantiation, the net impression, the disclosure needs, and the audience sensitivity before concluding compliance.

## Core Rules

### Evaluate Claims By Net Impression And Substantiation

Advertising liability turns on whether the net impression of an advertisement is misleading to a reasonable consumer and whether claims are substantiated.

For each advertisement:

- identify express and implied claims;
- assess the net impression on a reasonable consumer, not on an expert;
- require competent and reliable substantiation before making the claim;
- match the level of substantiation to the claim, especially health, safety, performance, and comparative claims;
- avoid material omissions that change the impression;
- consider the audience, including whether children or vulnerable populations are targeted;
- test claims that require evidence rather than relying on assumption.

A claim need not be literally false to be deceptive if the net impression misleads. Substantiation must exist before dissemination, not after a challenge.

### Disclose Material Connections And Endorsements

Endorsements, testimonials, and influencer content must disclose material connections.

Implement:

- clear and conspicuous disclosure of material connections between endorsers and the brand;
- disclosure in a manner consumers notice and understand, near the claim;
- controls on influencer and affiliate programs ensuring disclosure;
- truthfulness of endorsements reflecting honest opinion and experience;
- prohibition on endorsements implying a result that is not typical without disclosure;
- monitoring of third-party reviews and incentivized reviews;
- controls on deceptive review practices including fake reviews and review suppression.

Material connections include payment, free product, family or employment relationships, and any other connection consumers would not expect. Disclosure must be unavoidable, not buried.

### Comply With Pricing, Savings, And Reference Price Rules

Pricing and savings claims are heavily scrutinized.

Control:

- substantiation for original or reference prices in comparison claims;
- prohibition on fictitious former prices;
- clearance and sale claims reflecting genuine reductions;
- dynamic pricing disclosure where material;
- bait and switch prohibition on advertising unavailable products;
- shipping and handling disclosure;
- total price disclosure for offers and add-ons;
- state laws on automatic renewals and recurring billing.

A former price that was not the actual recent selling price is fictitious. Savings claims must reflect a genuine, recent, and regular price.

### Follow Subscription And Recurring Billing Rules

Automatic renewal and negative option programs have detailed disclosure and cancellation rules.

Cover:

- clear and conspicuous disclosure of automatic renewal terms before consent;
- affirmative express consent to the recurring charge;
- easy cancellation mechanisms matching the enrollment method;
- acknowledgment with terms and cancellation instructions;
- trial period disclosure and charge timing;
- material changes to terms requiring renewed consent;
- state automatic renewal laws that may be stricter.

Negative option and subscription traps are a priority enforcement area. Cancellation must be as easy as enrollment.

### Meet Sweepstakes, Contests, And Promotional Rules

Promotions must distinguish lawful sweepstakes from illegal lotteries.

Implement:

- no purchase necessary path to entry for sweepstakes;
- clear official rules with eligibility, odds, prizes, and conditions;
- disclosure of no-purchase-necessary and odds;
- avoidance of consideration that converts a sweepstakes into a lottery;
- skill contest structure with judging and disclosure;
- compliance with state registration and bonding for large promotions;
- winner selection and prize fulfillment integrity.

The three elements of a lottery are prize, chance, and consideration. Removing consideration through a free alternate method of entry is essential for sweepstakes.

### Control Telemarketing And Direct Marketing

Telemarketing and direct marketing have specific conduct and disclosure rules.

Assess:

- do-not-call list compliance and internal do-not-call honoring;
- caller identification and call abandonment limits;
- prohibition on deceptive telemarketing;
- disclosure of material terms before payment;
- restrictions on certain products and high-risk offers;
- robocall consent requirements;
- text message marketing consent and opt-out;
- state telemarketing registration and bonding.

Telemarketing rules distinguish calls from texts and require consent for certain automated contacts. Do-not-call compliance is a frequent enforcement area.

### Substantiate Environmental And Green Marketing Claims

Green and environmental claims require specific substantiation and clear qualification.

Control:

- general environmental benefit claims that are difficult to substantiate;
- specific claims like recyclable, biodegradable, compostable, and carbon neutral with required substantiation;
- qualification of claims to avoid overstating benefit;
- third-party certification reliability and disclosure;
- life-cycle basis for comparative and footprint claims;
- avoidance of claims that overstate minor benefits to distract from major harms.

Broad, unqualified green claims are inherently difficult to substantiate and often deceptive. Qualify specific attributes with reliable evidence.

### Avoid Dark Patterns And Manipulative Design

Digital interfaces must not manipulate consumers through deceptive design.

Assess:

- drip pricing and hidden fees disclosed late in the process;
- countdown timers and scarcity claims that are false;
- pre-checked boxes and default choices that mislead;
- burying or obscuring material terms;
- making cancellation difficult;
- misleading comparison and navigation;
- manipulative consent and dark patterns in subscriptions and data choices.

Dark patterns that trick consumers into purchases or unwanted recurring charges are deceptive practices.

## Common Traps

### Literal Truth Defended While Net Impression Misleads

An advertisement can be literally true yet deceptive in net impression. Defending on literal truth misses the standard.

### Substantiation Created After The Claim

Substantiation must exist before dissemination. Creating evidence after a challenge does not cure the violation.

### Influencer Disclosure Buried Or Absent

Disclosure must be clear and conspicuous. Hashtags buried at the end or absent connections are inadequate.

### Former Price That Was Not The Actual Selling Price

A reference price must reflect a genuine, recent, regular price. Inflated former prices are fictitious.

### Subscription Cancellation Harder Than Enrollment

Cancellation must match enrollment ease. Forcing phone calls when enrollment was online is a violation.

### Green Claims Too General To Substantiate

General environmental benefit claims are difficult to substantiate. Qualify specific attributes instead.

### Dark Patterns Treated As Good UX

Design that manipulates consumers into unwanted actions is a deceptive practice, not merely user experience.

## Self-Check

- Are express and implied claims assessed by net impression on a reasonable consumer, with competent and reliable substantiation existing before dissemination?
- Are material connections, endorsements, and influencer content disclosed clearly and conspicuously, with honest opinion and typicality disclosure?
- Are pricing, savings, reference prices, shipping, and total price claims substantiated by genuine recent regular prices?
- Are automatic renewal, negative option, and recurring billing disclosures, consent, cancellation, and state law requirements met?
- Are sweepstakes structured with no-purchase-necessary, odds disclosure, official rules, and state registration and bonding?
- Are telemarketing do-not-call, caller ID, disclosure, robocall and text consent, and state registration rules followed?
- Are environmental and green claims specific, substantiated, qualified, and supported by reliable life-cycle evidence?
- Are dark patterns, drip pricing, hidden fees, false scarcity, and manipulative consent avoided?
- Is substantiation matched to the type of claim, especially health, safety, performance, and comparative claims?
- Are vulnerable audiences and children considered in net impression analysis?
