# Food Safety And Labeling Compliance

Food is governed by a prevention-based safety regime and a detailed labeling regime that together control how food is made, held, transported, labeled, and recalled. The defining feature is that food safety failures can cause widespread illness and death, and the rules are designed to prevent contamination before it reaches consumers rather than detect it after. Labeling compliance is equally rigorous because consumers and health professionals rely on labels for allergens, nutrition, and accurate identity. The central difficulty is that food production involves biological variability, complex supply chains, and rapid distribution, so controls must operate continuously, not at a single inspection.

Use this skill before advising on a food production operation, reviewing labeling, assessing a food safety plan, planning a recall, or evaluating imported food compliance. The goal is to make the agent identify the facility type, the safety plan, the labeling requirements, and the recall readiness before concluding compliance.

## Core Rules

### Register Facilities And Meet Preventive Controls

Food facilities must register and implement preventive controls based on hazard analysis.

For each facility:

- register the facility and renew registration as required;
- implement a food safety plan with hazard analysis;
- identify preventive controls for process, allergen, sanitation, supply chain, and recall;
- establish process controls including critical control points where applicable;
- validate and verify controls with monitoring and records;
- implement a recall plan;
- designate a preventive controls qualified individual;
- manage reanalysis when changes occur;
- maintain records available for inspection.

A food safety plan that is generic rather than tied to the facility's actual hazards is inadequate. Hazards must be identified for the specific ingredients, processes, and products.

### Apply Hazard Analysis And Critical Control Points

For certain products and processors, HACCP is required and provides a systematic approach to significant hazards.

Implement:

- conduct hazard analysis for each product and process;
- identify critical control points;
- establish critical limits;
- monitor critical control points;
- establish corrective actions for deviations;
- verify the system through records review and testing;
- maintain records and documentation.

HACCP is mandatory for juice, seafood, and meat and poultry under respective jurisdictions. Deviations from critical limits require documented corrective action, not just adjustment.

### Comply With Good Manufacturing Practices

Food facilities must follow current good manufacturing practices covering personnel, plant, and equipment.

Cover:

- personnel qualifications, training, and health;
- plant and grounds cleanliness and maintenance;
- sanitation of equipment and utensils;
- water and plumbing adequacy;
- pest control;
- waste and sewage disposal;
- equipment design and maintenance;
- process controls and warehousing.

GMP violations are foundational findings that undermine the credibility of the entire food safety system.

### Meet Labeling Requirements For Identity, Nutrition, And Allergens

Food labels must declare required information accurately and in compliant format.

Control:

- statement of identity and common or usual name;
- net quantity declaration;
- ingredient list in descending order of predominance;
- allergen declaration for major food allergens;
- nutrition labeling with required nutrients and format;
- nutrient content claims and their conditions;
- health claims and their authorization or qualification;
- country of origin labeling where required;
- food date marking and use of open dating;
- bioengineered food disclosure.

Allergen labeling errors are a leading cause of recalls. Major allergens must be declared clearly, and cross-contact must be controlled.

### Manage Recalls And Reportable Food Registry

Food companies must be able to execute recalls and report certain safety events.

Implement:

- recall plan with roles, contacts, and procedures;
- reportable food registry reporting when a food poses a safety risk;
- classification of recalls by health risk;
- notification to the regulator, distributors, and consumers;
- effectiveness checks confirming recall reach;
- root-cause analysis and corrective action;
- records of product retrieved and disposition.

A recall plan that has never been tested or that lacks current contacts will fail under pressure. Reportable food registry reporting has deadlines.

### Control Imported Food And Supply Chain

Imported food must meet the same safety standards as domestic food, and importers have verification duties.

Assess:

- foreign supplier verification program requirements;
- importer of record responsibilities;
- prior notice of imported food shipments;
- certification and accreditation of foreign facilities and auditors;
- detention, refusal, and import alerts;
- supplier qualification and approval;
- traceability of ingredients and suppliers.

Importers are responsible for verifying that foreign suppliers produce food meeting safety standards. Reliance on a certificate without verification is insufficient.

### Address Special Product Categories

Certain food categories have additional requirements.

Cover:

- infant formula requirements including nutrient content and quality control;
- dietary supplement current GMP, adverse event reporting, and new ingredient notification;
- juice and seafood HACCP;
- meat, poultry, and egg products under respective jurisdiction;
- low-acid canned food and acidified food processing;
- food additives and generally recognized as safe determinations;
- color additive certification;
- organic certification and labeling.

Misclassifying a product or missing a category-specific rule can invalidate the safety basis for the product.

### Maintain Traceability And Records

Traceability is essential for recalls and investigations.

Implement:

- lot coding and identification;
- records of receipt, production, and distribution;
- key data elements and critical tracking events for high-risk foods;
- records retention for inspection;
- ability to trace forward and backward through the supply chain.

Enhanced traceability requirements apply to certain high-risk foods. Inability to trace quickly extends the scope and harm of recalls.

## Common Traps

### Generic Food Safety Plan Not Tied To Facility Hazards

A plan copied from a template that does not address the facility's actual ingredients, processes, and products is inadequate.

### Allergen Cross-Contact And Labeling Errors

Allergens are a leading recall cause. Cross-contact controls and accurate declaration are essential.

### Recall Plan Untested Or With Stale Contacts

An untested plan with outdated contacts will fail during a real recall. Test and update regularly.

### Imported Food Verified Only By Certificate

Importer verification requires assessing foreign supplier practices, not just accepting a certificate.

### Health And Nutrient Claims Without Authorization

Health claims require authorization or qualification. Using an unauthorized claim is misbranding.

### Category-Specific Rules Missed

Infant formula, dietary supplements, low-acid canned foods, and other categories have specific rules that general food GMP does not cover.

### Traceability Insufficient For Rapid Recall

Without lot coding and distribution records, recalls expand in scope and duration, increasing harm and cost.

## Self-Check

- Is the facility registered and does it implement a facility-specific food safety plan with hazard analysis, preventive controls, validation, monitoring, recall plan, and qualified individual?
- Is HACCP implemented for applicable products with hazard analysis, critical control points, limits, monitoring, corrective action, verification, and records?
- Are good manufacturing practices for personnel, plant, sanitation, water, pest control, equipment, and warehousing followed?
- Does labeling meet identity, net quantity, ingredient, allergen, nutrition, claims, country of origin, date marking, and bioengineered disclosure requirements?
- Is a recall plan in place with reportable food registry reporting, classification, notification, effectiveness checks, root-cause analysis, and disposition records?
- Are imported food foreign supplier verification, importer responsibilities, prior notice, certification, detention, and traceability addressed?
- Are special categories including infant formula, dietary supplements, juice, seafood, meat, low-acid canned, additives, color additives, and organic handled?
- Is traceability maintained through lot coding, receipt, production, distribution records, and critical tracking events for high-risk foods?
- Are allergen cross-contact controls and accurate declaration implemented?
- Is the food safety plan reanalyzed when changes occur to ingredients, processes, or products?
