# Consumer Product Safety Compliance

Consumer products are governed by a safety regime that requires testing, certification, labeling, and recall capabilities before products reach consumers, with continuing duties after sale. The defining feature is that the obligations attach at import or manufacture and continue throughout distribution, and a company cannot wait for incidents to act on foreseeable hazards. The central difficulty is that safety is judged against foreseeable use and misuse, not only intended use, and small design or sourcing changes can change a product's compliance status. Failure can mean product recalls, civil penalties, and in cases of concealed hazards, criminal liability.

Use this skill before advising on a consumer product launch, reviewing testing and certification, assessing children's product requirements, planning a recall, or evaluating hazard reporting. The goal is to make the agent identify the product category, the applicable safety standards, the certification and labeling duties, and the post-sale obligations before concluding compliance.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Product Category And Applicable Standards

Consumer product obligations depend on the product type and the applicable mandatory standards.

Classify:

- general consumer product versus a product with specific mandatory standards;
- children's product status, which triggers the strictest requirements;
- durable infant or toddler product status with registration and tracking;
- toys, with specific limits on lead, phthalates, and small parts;
- electrical products with safety standards;
- chemical and household products with labeling requirements;
- sporting, recreational, and outdoor products;
- products subject to specific bans or rules.

Misclassifying a product as a general consumer product when it is a children's product misses the strictest requirements. Children's product status depends on marketing and intended use for children.

### Test And Certify Products Before Distribution

Most consumer products require testing and certification before distribution.

Implement:

- testing by accredited third-party laboratories for children's products and certain regulated products;
- certification of compliance based on testing;
- general conformity certification for non-children's products;
- testing at the component and finished product level;
- periodic testing to support continuing certification;
- recordkeeping of tests and certificates;
- tracking of production lots and changes that require retesting.

Children's products must be tested by accredited third-party labs for applicable rules. A certificate without compliant testing is invalid.

### Apply Children's Product Requirements

Children's products carry the strictest obligations due to vulnerability of the user group.

Cover:

- lead content limits in substrates and paint;
- phthalate limits;
- small parts requirements for products intended for children under three;
- tracking labels enabling source identification;
- testing and certification by accredited labs;
- registration cards for durable infant or toddler products;
- advertising and marketing directed to children;
- sleep product and crib standards;
- toy safety standards including flammability and toxicity.

Tracking labels must identify the manufacturer, location, date, and cohort. Without them, recalls cannot target specific production.

### Maintain Tracking Labels And Records

Tracking enables recalls and source identification.

Implement:

- tracking labels on children's products with required identifiers;
- production records linking lots to materials and tests;
- supplier records for components and materials;
- distribution records enabling recall targeting;
- retention of records for the product's useful life.

Without tracking, recalls must be broader, more expensive, and less effective.

### Report Hazards And Comply With Recall Duties

Companies must report certain hazards and execute recalls when required.

Assess:

- reporting of products that contain a defect creating a substantial hazard;
- reporting of products that create an unreasonable risk of serious injury or death;
- reporting of civil actions alleging a product safety defect;
- immediate reporting within deadlines of obtaining information;
- recall or corrective action plans;
- notification to retailers, distributors, and consumers;
- remedy through repair, replacement, or refund;
- effectiveness checks and corrective action;
- cooperation with the regulator.

Reporting is mandatory and time-limited. Concealing a known hazard can lead to criminal prosecution and severe penalties.

### Meet Advertising, Warranty, And Claims Rules

Consumer product marketing and warranties carry their own obligations.

Control:

- substantiation for safety, performance, and comparative claims;
- warranty disclosure and Magnuson-Moss requirements for full warranties;
- prohibition on deceptive advertising;
- green and environmental claims substantiation;
- Made in USA and country of origin claims;
- online and social media advertising disclosure;
- influencer and endorsement disclosure;
- pricing and savings claims.

Deceptive claims create separate liability under consumer protection law, independent of safety compliance.

### Manage Importer And Supply Chain Obligations

Importers are responsible for the compliance of imported products.

Assess:

- importer of record responsibilities for certification and testing;
- foreign manufacturer qualification;
- component and material sourcing compliance;
- supply chain transparency for materials;
- detention and import surveillance;
- certificate of compliance at import.

The importer, not only the foreign manufacturer, is responsible for compliance. Reliance on supplier assurances without verification is inadequate.

### Address Chemical And Substance Restrictions

Chemicals in products are subject to restrictions and reporting.

Cover:

- restrictions on specific chemicals and heavy metals;
- reporting of chemical use and exposure;
- state chemical restrictions that may be stricter than federal;
- restrictions on flame retardants, formaldehyde, and other substances;
- cosmetics and personal care ingredient restrictions.

State chemical laws can be stricter and reach products that federal rules do not. Product formulation changes require reassessment.

## Common Traps

### Children's Product Misclassified As General Product

Marketing or intended use for children triggers children's product status. Misclassification misses the strictest rules.

### Certification Without Accredited Third-Party Testing

Children's products require accredited third-party testing. A self-certified or unaccredited test does not satisfy the rule.

### Tracking Labels Missing Or Inadequate

Without tracking labels, recalls cannot target production, and the product may be noncompliant on its face.

### Hazard Reporting Delayed Or Concealed

Reporting deadlines run from obtaining information. Delay or concealment compounds exposure and can be criminal.

### Safety Judged Only Against Intended Use

Foreseeable use and misuse must be considered. A hazard that appears only in foreseeable misuse is still a hazard.

### Deceptive Claims Treated As Separate From Safety

Deceptive marketing creates independent liability. Substantiation is required for all material claims.

### Importer Assuming Foreign Manufacturer Handles Compliance

The importer is responsible. Reliance on the manufacturer without verification is inadequate.

## Self-Check

- Is the product classified correctly as general, children's, durable infant, toy, electrical, chemical, or category-specific with applicable mandatory standards?
- Is testing by accredited third-party labs and certification completed before distribution, with periodic testing and recordkeeping?
- Are children's product lead, phthalate, small parts, tracking label, registration, sleep product, and toy standards met?
- Are tracking labels and production, supplier, and distribution records maintained to enable targeted recalls?
- Are hazard reporting, substantial product hazard, unreasonable risk, civil action reporting, recall, corrective action, remedy, and effectiveness checks implemented?
- Are advertising substantiation, warranty disclosure, deceptive ad prohibition, green claims, country of origin, and endorsement disclosure controlled?
- Are importer responsibilities, foreign manufacturer qualification, component sourcing, and import certificate duties addressed?
- Are chemical restrictions, reporting, state chemical laws, and formulation change reassessment handled?
- Is safety assessed against foreseeable use and misuse, not only intended use?
- Is hazard reporting triggered by obtaining information rather than full confirmation?
