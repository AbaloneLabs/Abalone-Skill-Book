# Evidence Spoliation Prevention

Spoliation is the destruction, alteration, or failure to preserve evidence once the duty to preserve has attached, and it is one of the most damaging failures an organization can commit. The consequences extend far beyond the underlying matter: courts and regulators impose sanctions ranging from adverse inference instructions, which tell the fact-finder to assume the destroyed evidence was harmful, to default judgment and financial penalties, and regulatory criticism of the organization's preservation practices. The organization may lose a matter it would have won because it cannot prove its case, or because the spoliation itself becomes the case. The judgment problem is recognizing when the duty attaches, which is earlier than most assume, ensuring preservation reaches every relevant system before destruction occurs, and treating the gap between foreseeability and effective preservation as the highest-risk window in the entire process.

Use this skill when an investigation or dispute is foreseeable, when issuing or managing preservation holds, when assessing whether preservation is effective across systems, or when responding to actual or suspected destruction of evidence. The goal is to make the agent treat spoliation prevention as a high-stakes, time-critical discipline where the duty attaches early, preservation must be comprehensive and verified, and any gap is a liability.

## Core Rules

### Recognize That The Duty Attaches When Preservation Is Reasonably Foreseeable

The most common spoliation error is waiting too long. The duty to preserve does not begin when a lawsuit is filed or a subpoena arrives; it begins when litigation, investigation, or regulatory action is reasonably foreseeable, which may be far earlier, when a dispute is escalating, when an incident occurs, or when misconduct is alleged. Waiting for formality creates a gap.

Recognize the trigger from:

- escalating disputes, demand letters, or threats of legal action;
- incidents, accidents, or events likely to generate claims;
- whistleblower reports or internal allegations of misconduct;
- regulatory inquiries, examinations, or information requests;
- internal investigations that may uncover violations requiring disclosure;
- contract disputes approaching breakdown or termination.

Establish a process by which legal, compliance, HR, security, and business leaders surface potential triggers to a preservation decision-maker promptly. When preservation is reasonably foreseeable, preserve first and scope later. The cost of over-preservation is far less than the cost of spoliation.

### Issue Preservation Holds Promptly And Specifically

A preservation hold is the instruction that suspends routine destruction for relevant records. A hold that is issued late, that is vague, or that does not reach the right people and systems is ineffective. The hold must be prompt, specific, and comprehensive.

Issue holds by:

- issuing the hold as soon as preservation is reasonably foreseeable, not after investigation or assessment;
- specifying what is preserved, including the custodians, the systems, the data types, and the time period, so recipients understand their obligations;
- directing the hold to all relevant custodians, including those who may have left the organization, whose records must be preserved before offboarding;
- requiring acknowledgment so there is evidence of receipt and understanding;
- providing clear instructions on what recipients must do and must not do, in plain language that non-technical staff can follow;
- naming a contact for questions, since recipients who are unsure may guess wrong.

A hold that says preserve all relevant documents, sent to a generic distribution list, with no specifics and no acknowledgment, is a hold in name only. Specificity and confirmation are what make a hold effective.

### Suspend Routine Destruction Across All Relevant Systems

The operational effect of a hold is suspending the routine retention and deletion processes that would otherwise destroy relevant records. This suspension must reach every system where relevant data lives, and it must be verified, not assumed. The most dangerous spoliation occurs in systems the hold did not reach.

Suspend destruction by:

- identifying all systems with potentially relevant data, including email, messaging, collaboration tools, file shares, cloud storage, applications, databases, and backups;
- coordinating with records management, IT, and system administrators to suspend deletion, purging, and overwriting for the identified systems;
- addressing auto-deletion and ephemeral messaging settings, which may destroy records on a schedule regardless of human action;
- addressing backup systems, which may overwrite preserved records during routine backup cycling if not specifically held;
- verifying that the suspension is in effect for each system, through confirmation from the system administrator or system logs, not merely that a request was made.

Systems that continue routine destruction because the hold did not reach them, or because the suspension was requested but not implemented, defeat the hold silently. The organization believes it is preserving while destruction continues. Verify system by system.

### Address The Highest-Risk Destruction Sources

Certain sources carry disproportionate spoliation risk because they destroy evidence automatically, rapidly, or in ways that are difficult to detect. These require specific attention beyond general hold notices.

Address high-risk sources:

- ephemeral and auto-deleting messaging applications, which may destroy messages within hours or days, and which require immediate preservation action and potentially collection before messages expire;
- backup systems with finite retention, which overwrite older backups on a cycle, so a hold issued after the cycle may find the relevant backup already overwritten;
- personal devices and bring-your-own-device situations, where the organization has limited control and where users may delete data independently;
- departing employees, whose devices and accounts may be wiped or reassigned during offboarding if preservation is not flagged;
- cloud and SaaS platforms, where deletion behavior, retention settings, and administrator access may differ from on-premises systems and require platform-specific preservation.

A hold that reaches email and file shares but misses the messaging platform where the actual communications occurred preserves the wrong records. Identify and address high-risk sources specifically.

### Prevent Subject-Initiated Destruction

When subjects learn or suspect they are under investigation, they may attempt to destroy evidence deliberately. Preservation must account for this risk, which means acting before subjects are alerted and controlling access after they are.

Prevent subject destruction by:

- preserving and collecting evidence before notifying subjects, whenever feasible, so that subjects do not have the opportunity to destroy before preservation occurs;
- suspending subjects' ability to delete or alter records upon notification, through system access controls, where appropriate and lawful;
- monitoring subjects' system activity for deletion attempts after notification, since attempted destruction is itself evidence of consciousness of guilt;
- securing physical devices, access badges, and materials from subjects who are suspended or terminated, before they can remove or destroy them;
- recognizing that subjects may attempt destruction through others, such as asking colleagues to delete messages, and addressing this through hold notices and monitoring.

A subject who is notified of an investigation and then deletes relevant emails has committed spoliation, but the organization's failure to preserve before notification may be scrutinized as contributory. Preserve first, notify second.

### Monitor Preservation Effectiveness Continuously

A hold issued once and never checked may decay: custodians depart, systems change, new data sources emerge, and retention settings drift. Preservation effectiveness must be monitored throughout the matter, not assumed from the initial hold.

Monitor by:

- tracking custodian status and preserving departing custodians' records before offboarding;
- verifying periodically that system suspensions remain in effect, since system updates, migrations, or configuration changes may reinstate deletion;
- updating the hold as the matter evolves, adding custodians, systems, or time periods that become relevant;
- re-issuing hold reminders for long-running matters, since custodians forget or become complacent over time;
- documenting ongoing preservation efforts, since the organization must be able to show not just that a hold was issued but that preservation was maintained.

A hold issued three years ago for a matter still in progress, with no reminders, no updates, and no verification, is likely failing in ways no one has noticed. Preservation is an active, ongoing obligation.

### Respond To Actual Or Suspected Spoliation Immediately

If spoliation is suspected or discovered, the response affects the consequences. Ignoring it, concealing it, or failing to investigate compounds the harm. Immediate, transparent response, while not undoing the destruction, positions the organization far better than concealment.

Respond by:

- immediately investigating the scope of the destruction, what was lost, when, how, and by whom;
- preserving all evidence of the spoliation itself, including logs showing deletion, which may be necessary to demonstrate the circumstances;
- assessing whether the destruction was intentional, negligent, or routine, since intent affects the severity of consequences;
- notifying counsel immediately, since spoliation has legal consequences requiring legal guidance;
- assessing remediation options, such as attempting recovery from backups, alternative sources, or forensic reconstruction;
- evaluating disclosure obligations, since spoliation may need to be disclosed to courts, regulators, or counterparties;
- taking disciplinary action against those who caused spoliation, since failure to address it signals tolerance.

Spoliation that is discovered, investigated, disclosed, and remediated is far more defensible than spoliation that is concealed. The cover-up, as in many contexts, is worse than the underlying failure.

## Common Traps

### Waiting For Formal Litigation To Preserve

The duty attaches when preservation is reasonably foreseeable, often well before a suit is filed. Waiting creates a destruction gap.

### Vague Or Unacknowledged Holds

A hold without specifics, confirmation, or clear instructions is ineffective. Issue promptly, specifically, and require acknowledgment.

### Destruction Continuing In Unreached Systems

Backups, ephemeral messaging, and cloud systems that the hold did not reach continue destroying evidence. Verify suspension system by system.

### Missing High-Risk Sources

Ephemeral messaging, overwriting backups, personal devices, and departing employees carry disproportionate spoliation risk. Address them specifically.

### Notifying Subjects Before Preserving

Subjects alerted before preservation may destroy evidence deliberately. Preserve and collect before notification.

### Set-And-Forget Holds

Holds that are never checked, updated, or reminded decay over long matters. Monitor preservation continuously.

### Concealing Discovered Spoliation

Ignoring or hiding destruction compounds the harm. Investigate, preserve spoliation evidence, notify counsel, and remediate immediately.

## Self-Check

- Is there a process to recognize when preservation is reasonably foreseeable, surface triggers promptly, and preserve before scoping, with the cost of over-preservation accepted as less than spoliation?
- Are preservation holds issued promptly, specifically, to all relevant custodians including departed employees, with acknowledgment, clear instructions, and a named contact?
- Is routine destruction suspended across all relevant systems, including email, messaging, collaboration, file shares, cloud, applications, databases, and backups, with system-by-system verification?
- Are high-risk sources, ephemeral messaging, overwriting backups, personal devices, departing employees, and cloud platforms, addressed with specific preservation action?
- Is subject-initiated destruction prevented by preserving before notification, controlling access after notification, monitoring deletion attempts, securing materials, and addressing destruction through others?
- Is preservation effectiveness monitored continuously through custodian tracking, system suspension verification, hold updates, reminders for long matters, and ongoing documentation?
- Is actual or suspected spoliation responded to immediately through scope investigation, spoliation evidence preservation, intent assessment, counsel notification, remediation, disclosure evaluation, and disciplinary action?
- Could the organization demonstrate that it recognized the duty early, preserved comprehensively, verified effectiveness, and responded properly to any gaps, rather than allowing silent destruction?
