# Authority Delegation And Decision Rights

Every compliance program makes decisions constantly: approve an exception, accept a risk, waive a control, halt a transaction, clear a third party, permit a marketing claim, escalate an investigation. The question that determines whether these decisions are sound is not what the policy says but who actually has the authority to make them, under what limits, with what escalation, and with what evidence. In most organizations this is undefined, and the result is predictable: decisions stall because no one is sure who can approve them, drift to whoever acts first, or get made informally by the most senior or loudest person in the room. The harm is that informal, undocumented authority lets high-risk decisions bypass compliance entirely, while over-centralized authority creates bottlenecks that the business routes around. Defining decision rights deliberately is what converts authority from a source of confusion into a controlled, auditable, and risk-appropriate process.

Use this skill before defining or revising a delegation of authority, mapping compliance decision rights, designing approval and exception workflows, or diagnosing why decisions are not being made or enforced. The goal is to make the agent assign authority explicitly, calibrate it to risk, build escalation, and make it auditable rather than leaving it to custom and seniority.

## Core Rules

### Map Every Material Compliance Decision To A Named Authority

The starting point is identifying the decisions that matter and assigning each a clear owner. Compliance-relevant decisions are scattered across the business, and unmapped decisions are the ones most likely to be made wrongly or by the wrong person.

Map decisions including:

- risk acceptances and control exceptions, with thresholds by risk level;
- new product, market, customer, or third-party approvals;
- transaction approvals, halts, and releases, such as payments or shipments;
- marketing, disclosure, and communications approvals;
- investigation initiation, scope changes, and closure;
- self-disclosure, regulator notification, and reporting decisions;
- policy waivers, training exemptions, and access grants;
- hiring, promotion, and termination decisions involving compliance findings.

For each, name the role that decides, the conditions, the limits, and the escalation path. A decision that is not mapped will be made by whoever gets there first.

### Calibrate Authority To Risk Level And Consequence

Not all decisions should sit at the same level. A low-risk exception can be approved by a first-line manager within limits; a decision with regulatory, criminal, or reputational consequence must escalate. Calibrating authority to risk prevents both dangerous decentralization and crippling centralization.

Calibrate by:

- defining risk tiers for decisions based on likelihood, impact, and reversibility;
- setting approval authority that increases with risk tier, so high-risk decisions escalate;
- requiring dual approval or committee approval for the highest-risk decisions;
- ensuring decisions with legal or regulatory consequence involve legal and compliance, not only the business;
- defining which decisions are non-delegable, such as self-disclosure or halting a business line.

A flat authority structure where any manager can approve any exception is a control failure. Tier authority to the consequence of being wrong.

### Define Escalation Triggers, Paths, And Timeframes

Authority without escalation creates two failures: decisions that should escalate stay buried, and decisions that cannot be resolved locally stall indefinitely. Define when and how a decision moves up.

Define escalation by specifying:

- the triggers that force escalation, such as risk tier, value threshold, repeated exceptions, or regulator involvement;
- the path, which role escalates to which role or body at each level;
- the timeframes within which escalation must occur;
- who must be notified, including the board for the most significant matters;
- the criteria for de-escalation and closure, with required evidence;
- what happens when authorities disagree, including a tie-breaker or committee resolution.

Escalation that depends on someone choosing to speak up is unreliable. Make the triggers automatic and the path explicit.

### Separate Business Authority From Compliance Authority On Legal Obligations

A frequent and dangerous pattern is allowing the business to outvote or override compliance on matters of legal obligation. Business leaders should inform decisions about commercial tradeoffs, but whether an action complies with law is not a majority vote. The decision rights structure must protect compliance authority where it matters.

Protect compliance authority by:

- making compliance or legal approval mandatory and non-overridable for defined decisions, such as regulatory filings, sanctions, anti-bribery, and disclosures;
- preventing the business from approving its own exceptions to controls designed to constrain it;
- ensuring the CCO or general counsel can halt a transaction or relationship that presents legal risk;
- documenting any case where the business proceeds over compliance objection, with board awareness.

Where the law is the question, the business does not get to decide the answer. Structure the rights accordingly.

### Make Approval Workflows Enforce The Intended Authority

A decision-rights matrix on paper is useless if the actual workflows let decisions bypass it. The systems through which approvals happen must enforce the mapped authority.

Enforce through workflows by:

- building approval routing into the systems where decisions are made, so bypass requires effort;
- requiring the approver's role and limits to be validated at the point of decision;
- preventing self-approval and ensuring segregation between requestor and approver;
- capturing the decision, the approver, the rationale, and the limits applied as a record;
- blocking progression when a required approval is missing or when limits are exceeded;
- monitoring for patterns of workaround, such as splitting transactions to stay under thresholds.

If the workflow allows the business to proceed without the required compliance approval, the decision rights are fictional. Make the system enforce them.

### Document Decisions And Rationale For Auditability

Every significant compliance decision should leave a record that allows a regulator or auditor to understand who decided, under what authority, with what rationale, and within what limits. Undocumented decisions cannot be defended and look like no control at all.

Document by capturing:

- the decision, the requestor, and the approver, with roles and dates;
- the authority and limits under which the decision was made;
- the rationale, including the risk considered and the conditions imposed;
- any conditions, expiry, or review triggers attached to the approval;
- the escalation path if the decision was escalated;
- exceptions to standard authority, with the higher approval that authorized them.

A decision with no record did not happen, from an assurance perspective. Build documentation into the workflow, not as an afterthought.

### Review And Refresh Authority As The Business And Risk Change

Authority structures decay. New products, geographies, and risks appear; roles change; people leave. An authority matrix that is not maintained becomes increasingly fictional.

Maintain by:

- reviewing the decision-rights matrix on a schedule and after significant business change;
- updating authorities when risks, products, or regulations change;
- re-issuing authority when roles turn over, with clear handover;
- auditing a sample of decisions to confirm authority was exercised correctly;
- retiring authorities that are no longer needed to prevent stale permissions.

## Common Traps

### Unmapped Decisions Made By Whoever Acts First

Decisions not assigned to a named authority drift to the informal path. Map every material decision to an owner.

### Flat Authority Ignoring Risk Tier

Allowing any manager to approve any exception lets high-risk decisions stay local. Tier authority to consequence.

### No Escalation Triggers Or Paths

Without defined escalation, serious decisions stay buried or unresolved. Define triggers, paths, timeframes, and resolution rules.

### Business Overriding Compliance On Legal Obligations

Letting the business outvote compliance on whether an action is lawful is a structural control failure. Protect compliance authority where the law is the question.

### Workflows That Allow Bypass

If the approval system lets decisions proceed without the required approval, the matrix is fictional. Enforce authority in the workflow.

### Undocumented Decisions

A decision with no record cannot be defended and looks like no control. Capture decision, authority, rationale, and conditions.

### Stale Authority Matrix

An unmaintained matrix becomes fictional as the business and roles change. Review, update, and audit authority regularly.

## Self-Check

- Is every material compliance decision, including exceptions, approvals, halts, disclosures, and waivers, mapped to a named authority with conditions, limits, and escalation?
- Is approval authority calibrated to risk tier, consequence, and reversibility, with escalation, dual approval, or committee approval for the highest-risk decisions?
- Are escalation triggers, paths, timeframes, notifications, de-escalation criteria, and disagreement resolution defined and automatic rather than discretionary?
- Is compliance and legal authority protected as mandatory and non-overridable on matters of legal obligation, with documented cases of business override requiring board awareness?
- Do approval workflows enforce the mapped authority through routing, role validation, segregation, blocking, and bypass monitoring?
- Are decisions documented with requestor, approver, authority, limits, rationale, conditions, and escalation, sufficient for audit and regulator review?
- Is the decision-rights matrix reviewed on schedule and after business change, with authorities updated, re-issued on turnover, audited by sampling, and retired when obsolete?
- Could the organization evidence to a regulator that compliance decisions were made by the right authority, within limits, with rationale, and not bypassed?
