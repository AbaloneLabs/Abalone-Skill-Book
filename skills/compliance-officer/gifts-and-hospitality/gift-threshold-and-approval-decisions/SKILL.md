# Gift Threshold And Approval Decisions

Gifts and hospitality are the most common entry point into bribery liability because they occupy the ambiguous zone between legitimate relationship-building and improper influence. A meal with a customer, a branded pen, or event tickets can be entirely proper, or they can be the vehicle through which an improper advantage is purchased. The compliance challenge is that the same item, at the same value, in the same currency, can be acceptable or criminal depending on who the recipient is, what business is pending, what intent accompanies it, and what jurisdiction's law applies. A threshold-based policy gives the appearance of clarity, but thresholds are only one factor, and treating them as decisive is the most dangerous oversimplification in this area.

Use this skill before deciding whether a specific gift or hospitality item is permissible, designing threshold and approval rules, or reviewing whether past gifts were properly handled. The goal is to make the agent treat the recipient's status, the business context, and the intent as primary, with monetary thresholds as a secondary screen rather than the governing test. Gifts and hospitality to government officials carry heightened criminal exposure under laws such as the FCPA, the UK Bribery Act, and local anti-corruption statutes; decisions in this area should be reviewed with qualified counsel and are not a matter of internal discretion alone.

## Core Rules

### Treat The Recipient's Status As The Primary Variable

The single most important fact about any gift or hospitality decision is who the recipient is, because that determines which law applies and how strictly. A gift to a private commercial contact is governed by commercial bribery law, company policy, and the recipient's employer rules. A gift to a government official, a state-owned enterprise employee, a political candidate, or a family member of any of these triggers anti-corruption law with criminal penalties. Many companies apply a near-total prohibition on gifts to government officials regardless of value, because the legal and reputational risk dwarfs any relationship benefit.

Classify the recipient before evaluating the gift. Government officials include not only elected officials and civil servants but employees of state-owned enterprises, political party officials, candidates, and public international organization staff. When in doubt about whether an entity is state-owned or whether a person qualifies as a government official, treat them as one and escalate. Misclassifying a government official as a commercial contact is a frequent and serious error.

### Apply The Business Context Test, Not Just The Value

A gift that is within the monetary threshold can still be improper if it is connected to pending business, a regulatory decision, a procurement process, or a licensing matter. The threshold is a necessary but insufficient screen. A two-hundred-dollar dinner is within many policies, but if it occurs the week before a contract award decision involving that customer, the context converts it into influence-seeking regardless of the value.

Assess the timing and pending decisions. Ask whether any matter is pending between the company and the recipient's organization that the gift could influence, including contract awards, regulatory approvals, license renewals, audits, or disputes. If such a matter is pending, the gift should be declined, deferred, or escalated regardless of value, even if the threshold would otherwise permit it. No policy threshold overrides the corruption risk of a gift tied to a pending official decision.

### Define Thresholds As Ceilings With Contextual Overrides, Not Safe Harbors

Monetary thresholds are useful because they create a default and reduce the volume of decisions requiring individual review. But they must be framed as ceilings that apply only when no contextual red flag is present, not as safe harbors that immunize any gift below the line. A policy that says gifts under one hundred dollars require no approval invites abuse, because employees learn that anything under the line is exempt and structure their giving accordingly, a practice known as splitting or salami-slicing.

Set thresholds conservatively and pair them with absolute prohibitions on certain categories regardless of value. Typical absolute prohibitions include cash and cash equivalents, gifts during pending business decisions, gifts to government officials above a nominal amount or at all, gifts that would embarrass the company if disclosed, and gifts conditioned on any action or inaction. Require approval for anything near the threshold and for any gift involving a higher-risk recipient, even if the value is low.

### Require Pre-Approval For Higher-Risk Categories

Pre-approval shifts the decision from the gift-giver, who has an interest in proceeding, to an independent reviewer who can assess context. Pre-approval should be required for any gift or hospitality to a government official, anything above a defined percentage of the threshold, anything in a higher-risk country, anything involving entertainment or travel rather than a modest meal, and anything from a third party acting on the company's behalf.

The approver must be independent of the business unit seeking to give the gift and must have visibility into pending business with the recipient. An approver who rubber-stamps requests or who reports to the gift-giver provides no real control. Record the approval decision, the basis, and the approver's identity.

### Account For Aggregate And Annual Exposure

A single gift within threshold is one analysis; a pattern of gifts to the same recipient is another. An employee who gives a ninety-nine-dollar gift every month to the same official has created over a thousand dollars of annual influence while never breaching the per-gift threshold. Aggregate limits prevent this salami-slicing.

Define annual aggregate limits per recipient and per giver, and track gifts cumulatively so that the hundredth small gift triggers review even if each individual gift was within threshold. Aggregate tracking requires a central register; without it, no one can see the pattern until an investigation reconstructs it after the fact.

### Capture Third-Party And Indirect Giving

Gifts given through intermediaries, agents, distributors, or joint venture partners are attributable to the company under many anti-corruption laws. A policy that governs only direct employee giving ignores the channel through which much improper giving occurs. Extend the gift policy to third parties acting on the company's behalf, contractually require their compliance, and require them to report gifts given on the company's behalf.

Indirect giving also includes gifts to family members of officials or decision-makers. A gift to a spouse or child of a procurement officer is functionally a gift to the officer and is treated as such under anti-corruption law.

## Common Traps

### Treating The Threshold As A Safe Harbor

Assuming any gift below the monetary limit is automatically permissible regardless of recipient, timing, or intent. The threshold is a screen, not a defense against a corruption charge.

### Misclassifying A Government Official As Commercial

Failing to recognize state-owned enterprise employees, political candidates, or public organization staff as government officials, and applying the more lenient commercial rules. This is a frequent and serious error.

### Ignoring Pending Business Context

Approving a within-threshold gift without checking whether a contract, license, or regulatory matter is pending with the recipient's organization. The timing converts an innocent gift into influence.

### Splitting Gifts To Stay Under Threshold

Structuring multiple small gifts to the same recipient to avoid triggering approval, exploiting the absence of aggregate tracking.

### Rubber-Stamp Approvals

Routing approvals to a person who lacks independence, context, or authority to decline, so that the approval step provides documentation without control.

### Forgetting Cash Equivalents And Indirect Gifts

Overlooking that gift cards, vouchers, and prepaid cards are cash equivalents generally prohibited, and that gifts to family members or through third parties are attributable to the company.

### Assuming Local Custom Normalizes The Gift

Believing that lavish hospitality is expected in a particular culture and therefore permissible. Cultural expectation does not override anti-corruption law, and lavish hospitality is a recognized bribery vehicle.

## Self-Check

- Is the recipient's status classified first, with government officials, state-owned enterprise employees, and political figures treated under the stricter anti-corruption standard rather than commercial rules?
- Is the business context assessed for pending decisions before relying on the monetary threshold, so that gifts tied to pending matters are declined regardless of value?
- Are thresholds framed as ceilings with contextual overrides and absolute prohibitions, rather than as safe harbors that immunize any sub-threshold gift?
- Does pre-approval apply to higher-risk categories including government officials, near-threshold values, high-risk countries, entertainment and travel, and third-party giving?
- Are annual aggregate limits defined per recipient and per giver, with cumulative tracking that catches salami-slicing patterns invisible at the per-gift level?
- Does the policy extend to third parties acting on the company's behalf, with contractual requirements and reporting of indirect giving?
- Are cash and cash equivalents, gifts to family members, and gifts conditioned on action treated as prohibited or high-risk regardless of value?
- Is the approver independent of the gift-giver with visibility into pending business, rather than a rubber stamp?
- Is the approval decision, basis, and approver identity recorded for each gift requiring approval?
- Would the gift withstand disclosure, meaning it could be publicly reported without embarrassment or suspicion of improper intent?
