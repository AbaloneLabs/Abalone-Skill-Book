# Gift Register Recordkeeping And Monitoring

A gift register is the control that makes every other gift and hospitality rule enforceable. Without a complete and accurate register, thresholds cannot be applied, aggregate limits cannot be tracked, approvals cannot be verified, and a regulator investigating a suspicion has nothing to review. Yet gift registers are among the most poorly maintained compliance records, because they depend on voluntary disclosure by employees who have an interest in not recording gifts that might be questioned. A register that captures only the gifts employees choose to report is not a control; it is a self-selected confession log that systematically excludes the very items it most needs to capture. The compliance officer's job is to design a register that captures gifts comprehensively, that is reconciled against independent data sources, and that is actively monitored for patterns rather than passively stored.

Use this skill before designing or revising a gift register, defining recording obligations, setting up monitoring, or assessing whether the register would withstand external scrutiny. Accurate books and records are required under laws including the FCPA accounting provisions and securities regulations; deliberate misrecording or omission can itself constitute a violation. This skill addresses control design, not legal advice on specific obligations.

## Core Rules

### Define The Recording Obligation Clearly And Comprehensively

Ambiguity about what must be recorded produces gaps. Employees must know precisely which transactions require a register entry, within what timeframe, and with what data fields. The obligation should cover gifts given and received, hospitality provided and accepted, sponsorships, entertainment, travel, and anything of value transferred to or from a third party in a business context, regardless of value, with sub-threshold items captured for aggregate tracking even if they need no approval.

Define the data fields required for each entry. A defensible entry captures the date, the giver and recipient including their organization and role, the recipient's status as government or commercial, a description of the item or event, the value, the business purpose, the pending business if any, the approval reference if required, and the cost coding. Entries missing these fields cannot support threshold, aggregate, or context analysis and are effectively useless for monitoring.

### Make Recording The Default, Not The Exception

The recording obligation should be framed so that the question is why something would not be recorded, not why it would be. A policy that requires recording only gifts above a threshold guarantees that the register misses everything below it, which is exactly where salami-slicing and pattern detection matter. Require recording of all gifts and hospitality regardless of value, with the threshold governing only whether approval is also needed.

Reduce friction in recording so that compliance is easier than non-compliance. A cumbersome paper form or a system requiring many fields for a twenty-dollar item discourages reporting. A simple digital submission with required fields and optional context, accessible from mobile devices, increases capture rates. The easier it is to record, the less excuse there is for omission.

### Reconcile The Register Against Independent Data Sources

A self-reported register will always understate reality. The control that gives the register credibility is reconciliation against data sources that do not depend on employee self-reporting. Expense reports, corporate card transactions, marketing event budgets, sponsorship payments, and travel booking systems all contain evidence of gifts and hospitality that should appear in the register.

Periodically, and on a risk-based sampling basis for higher-risk units, reconcile the register against these source systems. Identify transactions in the source systems that have no corresponding register entry and investigate the gap. This reconciliation is the single most effective control for detecting under-reporting, because it catches the gifts that were expensed but never registered, which are often the ones that matter.

### Monitor For Patterns, Not Just Individual Items

A register that is checked only for individual threshold compliance misses the patterns that reveal corruption risk. Active monitoring looks for concentrations and trends that no single entry would reveal. Monitor for repeated giving to the same recipient approaching or exceeding aggregate limits, clusters of gifts around pending business decisions, gifts concentrated in higher-risk jurisdictions, gifts from the same business unit spiking over time, and sub-threshold gifts given at a frequency suggesting splitting.

Define monitoring rules and review them on a defined cadence. Escalate patterns that suggest influence-building, splitting, or targeting of specific decision-makers. The register's value is realized through monitoring, not through passive storage; a register that is never analyzed is merely an archive.

### Apply Special Attention To Government Official Entries

Entries involving government officials carry the highest legal exposure and should receive heightened review. Monitor for any gift or hospitality to officials, verify that approvals were obtained and recorded, confirm that the official's employer authorized attendance where required, and flag any entry where the value, timing, or context suggests influence. Even modest gifts to officials should be reviewed because the legal standard is broad and the reputational risk is disproportionate to the value.

Maintain the ability to produce a complete report of all benefits provided to any government official or organization on demand, because regulators and auditors frequently request this. A register that cannot quickly produce an official-specific report signals that the data is not structured for oversight.

### Preserve Records For The Required Retention Period

Gift and hospitality records are subject to retention requirements under anti-corruption law, securities law, and tax law, which often require retention for several years. The register and its supporting evidence, approvals, receipts, and event documentation must be retained for the longest applicable period across all relevant jurisdictions. Premature deletion is itself a violation and destroys the ability to defend past decisions.

Apply legal hold to gift records when they become relevant to an investigation, audit, or regulatory inquiry. A records management process that allows routine deletion to override a legal hold can convert a reviewable matter into a spoliation issue.

## Common Traps

### Threshold-Only Recording

Recording only gifts above the approval threshold, leaving the register blind to sub-threshold giving and unable to detect splitting or aggregate patterns.

### Self-Reported Register Without Reconciliation

Trusting the register as complete when it depends entirely on voluntary disclosure, with no reconciliation against expense, card, or event data to detect omissions.

### Missing Or Inconsistent Data Fields

Allowing entries without recipient status, value, business purpose, or pending business, so that the data cannot support threshold, aggregate, or context analysis.

### Passive Storage Without Monitoring

Maintaining the register but never analyzing it for patterns, so that concentrations, splitting, and targeting of decision-makers go undetected.

### Treating Government Official Entries Like Any Other

Applying the same recording and review standard to official gifts as to commercial gifts, missing the heightened legal and reputational exposure.

### Premature Or Unmanaged Deletion

Allowing routine retention schedules to delete gift records before the end of the required period, or failing to apply legal hold during investigations.

### High-Friction Recording That Suppresses Reporting

Requiring cumbersome forms for minor items, so that employees avoid recording low-value gifts and the register develops systematic gaps.

## Self-Check

- Does the recording obligation cover all gifts and hospitality given and received regardless of value, with sub-threshold items captured for aggregate tracking?
- Are required data fields defined for each entry, including recipient identity and status, value, business purpose, pending business, and approval reference?
- Is recording framed as the default with low friction, so that compliance is easier than omission?
- Is the register periodically reconciled against independent data sources such as expense reports, corporate cards, event budgets, and travel systems to detect unreported gifts?
- Are monitoring rules defined to detect patterns including aggregate limit approach, clustering around pending business, high-risk jurisdiction concentration, and splitting?
- Do entries involving government officials receive heightened review and can an official-specific report be produced on demand?
- Are records retained for the longest applicable period across relevant jurisdictions, with legal hold applied during investigations?
- Would the register withstand a regulatory or audit review, meaning a reviewer could reconstruct who received what, at what value, for what purpose, with what approval?
- Are reconciliation gaps between source systems and the register investigated rather than ignored?
- Is the register actively monitored on a defined cadence rather than passively stored?
