# Financial Services Licensing Authorization And Permissible Activities

Financial services licensing determines what activities a firm may conduct, in which jurisdictions, and under what conditions. Under the US federal and state banking and securities frameworks, the EU CRD, MiFID, and payment services directives, and equivalent national financial regulation, conducting regulated activities without proper authorization can result in regulatory penalties, transaction unwinding, reputational damage, and in some cases criminal liability. The central judgment problem is that the boundary of regulated activities is not always intuitive, business models evolve faster than licensing frameworks, and cross-border services create complex jurisdictional questions. A firm that launches a new product, enters a new market, or modifies its service model can inadvertently conduct unauthorized regulated activities, particularly in fintech and embedded finance where traditional boundaries are blurred. A compliance officer who relies on the firm's existing license without assessing whether new activities fall within its scope can leave the firm exposed to significant regulatory risk.

Use this skill before assessing licensing requirements, evaluating permissible activities, reviewing cross-border service rules, or advising on regulated activity boundaries. The goal is to make the agent think about what activities are regulated, what the existing license covers, and the decisions that are easy to make too casually. A product that is marketed as technology or information services can constitute regulated financial activity depending on its substance, not its label.

This skill addresses jurisdiction-specific obligations. Financial services licensing frameworks, permissible activities, and cross-border rules differ fundamentally across the US, the EU, the UK, and national regimes. Always confirm the applicable national financial regulation and authority guidance, and consult qualified financial services legal counsel for specific licensing assessments. This skill does not constitute legal advice.

## Core Rules

### Map Regulated Activities Against The Business Model

The first step in licensing compliance is mapping the firm's actual and planned activities against the regulated activities defined in each applicable jurisdiction.

Regulated activity mapping elements:

- identify all services the firm provides, including those embedded in partnerships or platforms;
- classify each service against the regulated activities defined in applicable frameworks, such as deposit-taking, lending, investment advice, portfolio management, payment services, or insurance mediation;
- assess whether technology or information services, such as comparison tools, robo-advice, or account aggregation, constitute regulated activities based on their substance;
- assess whether the firm's role in transactions, such as agent, principal, or platform, affects the regulated activity classification;
- document the mapping and the basis for each classification.

The mapping should be updated whenever the business model changes, including new products, partnerships, or markets. Activities that are incidental to non-financial services can still be regulated if they meet the statutory definition.

### Understand The Scope Of Existing Licenses

Each license authorizes specific activities, often with conditions and limitations. The firm must understand exactly what its existing licenses cover.

License scope assessment:

- the regulated activities authorized by each license, including any sub-categories or permissions;
- the products and services that fall within each authorized activity;
- the conditions, limitations, or restrictions attached to each license;
- the geographic scope of each license, including any passporting or cross-border rights;
- the reporting, capital, and governance obligations attached to each license;
- any pending variations or conditions imposed by the regulator.

A license to provide one type of service does not automatically authorize related services. For example, a payment services license does not authorize deposit-taking or lending, and an investment advice license does not authorize portfolio management unless specifically included.

### Assess Permissible Activities And Boundary Issues

The boundary between permissible and impermissible activities is a frequent source of licensing risk, particularly for firms expanding their service offerings.

Common boundary issues:

- whether providing information or comparison tools constitutes investment advice or financial promotion;
- whether arranging or introducing transactions constitutes brokerage or mediation requiring a license;
- whether holding client funds, even temporarily, constitutes deposit-taking or payment services;
- whether providing credit references or scoring constitutes credit information services requiring authorization;
- whether operating a marketplace or platform for financial products constitutes arranging or dealing;
- whether providing custody or safekeeping of assets constitutes regulated custody services.

The analysis depends on the substance of the activity, not the label. A service described as technology or information can constitute regulated activity if it meets the statutory definition.

### Evaluate Cross-Border And Passporting Rules

Cross-border financial services create complex licensing questions, particularly in regimes with passporting mechanisms such as the EU and the EEA.

Cross-border considerations:

- whether the firm's home state license passports into the host state for the intended activities;
- whether the activities fall within the scope of the passporting directive, such as CRD for banking or MiFID for investment services;
- whether the firm must establish a branch or subsidiary in the host state or can provide services on a cross-border basis;
- whether host state rules on marketing, conduct, or consumer protection apply in addition to home state rules;
- whether reverse solicitation exemptions apply for services provided to clients in other jurisdictions;
- post-Brexit changes for UK-EU cross-border services, which may require separate licensing in each jurisdiction.

Passporting is not automatic and does not cover all activities or all host state requirements. The firm must assess passporting rights for each activity in each target jurisdiction.

### Review Fintech And Embedded Finance Arrangements

Fintech and embedded finance arrangements, where financial services are provided through partnerships, platforms, or technology interfaces, create particular licensing challenges.

Fintech and embedded finance considerations:

- assess whether the technology provider is conducting regulated activity or merely providing technology services to a licensed partner;
- assess the regulatory status of each party in BaaS (banking-as-a-service), embedded payments, or embedded lending arrangements;
- assess whether the arrangement creates regulatory dependency or outsourcing that requires notification or approval;
- assess conduct of business obligations, including who is responsible for customer disclosures, complaints, and protections;
- assess whether the arrangement creates regulatory arbitrage concerns that could attract scrutiny;
- ensure the licensed partner retains responsibility for regulated activities and compliance.

Regulators have increased scrutiny of fintech and embedded finance arrangements, particularly where the licensed entity appears to be lending its license to an unlicensed technology provider.

### Assess Licensing Gaps And Remediation

Where the firm identifies activities that fall outside its existing licenses, it must assess the gap and develop a remediation plan.

Licensing gap remediation:

- cease or suspend the unlicensed activity pending authorization, unless a transitional or exemption regime applies;
- apply for the necessary license variation or new authorization;
- assess whether partnerships with licensed entities can bridge the gap on an interim or permanent basis;
- assess the impact on existing customers and contracts, including any unwinding or migration requirements;
- assess notification obligations to the regulator and to customers;
- document the gap assessment and remediation plan with counsel review.

Conducting unlicensed activity, even inadvertently, can result in penalties and transaction unwinding. Prompt remediation and transparency with the regulator can mitigate consequences.

### Document The Licensing Compliance Framework

The licensing compliance framework should be documented to demonstrate that the firm understands and manages its licensing obligations.

Documentation elements:

- the regulated activity mapping and classification basis;
- the scope and conditions of each existing license;
- the permissible activity assessments for new products and services;
- the cross-border and passporting analysis for each jurisdiction;
- the fintech and embedded finance arrangement assessments;
- any licensing gap assessments and remediation plans;
- regulatory communications and approvals;
- periodic review and update of the framework.

Documentation that the firm systematically assessed its licensing obligations and obtained qualified advice is critical to demonstrating compliance and managing regulatory examinations.

## Common Traps

### Assuming Existing License Covers New Activities

A license for one service does not automatically authorize related or new services. Each new activity must be assessed against the license scope.

### Labeling Regulated Activity As Technology

The substance of an activity, not its label, determines whether it is regulated. Technology or information services can constitute regulated financial activity.

### Overlooking Host State Rules In Passporting

Passporting does not eliminate host state requirements. Marketing, conduct, and consumer protection rules may still apply in the host state.

### Embedded Finance Without Clear Responsibility

BaaS and embedded finance arrangements without clear allocation of regulatory responsibility create compliance gaps and regulatory scrutiny.

### Cross-Border Services Based On Reverse Solicitation

Reverse solicitation exemptions are narrow and easily undermined by active marketing. Relying on reverse solicitation for systematic cross-border services is risky.

### Not Updating Mapping When Business Model Changes

Business model changes, including new products, partnerships, or markets, can create new regulated activities that require licensing assessment.

### Continuing Unlicensed Activity While Seeking Authorization

Conducting unlicensed activity while an application is pending, unless a transitional regime applies, can result in penalties regardless of the application status.

## Self-Check

- Are all actual and planned activities mapped against regulated activities defined in each applicable jurisdiction?
- Is the scope of each existing license understood, including authorized activities, conditions, limitations, and geographic reach?
- Are permissible activity boundaries assessed for information services, arrangement, fund-holding, and platform models based on substance not label?
- Are cross-border and passporting rights assessed for each activity in each target jurisdiction, including host state rules?
- Are fintech and embedded finance arrangements assessed for regulatory status, responsibility allocation, and outsourcing notification?
- Are licensing gaps identified promptly, with cessation, application, partnership, and remediation planning?
- Is the regulated activity mapping updated whenever the business model changes, including new products, partnerships, or markets?
- Are reverse solicitation exemptions assessed narrowly and not relied upon for systematic cross-border services?
- Is the licensing compliance framework documented with mapping, license scope, assessments, cross-border analysis, and counsel review?
- Is the licensing assessment confirmed against the applicable national financial regulation and authority guidance with qualified counsel, rather than a generic standard?
