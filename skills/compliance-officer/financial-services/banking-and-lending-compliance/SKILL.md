# Banking And Lending Compliance

Banking is one of the most heavily regulated activities an organization can undertake, and the rules differ sharply depending on whether the entity is a chartered depository, a non-bank lender, a buy-now-pay-later provider, a mortgage originator, a credit card issuer, or a technology company partnering with a bank. Compliance failure here is not abstract: it can trigger license revocation, capital penalties, loan rescission, restitution to borrowers, personal liability for officers, and loss of access to payment systems. The hardest mistakes come from assuming a generic consumer-protection posture is sufficient when the actual obligation is a detailed, prescriptive lending or safety-and-soundness rule.

Use this skill before advising on a lending product, reviewing a bank compliance program, preparing for a supervisory exam, evaluating a bank-fintech partnership, or assessing whether a credit product meets consumer protection and safety-and-soundness standards. The goal is to make the agent identify the charter, the product, the applicable rule set, the disclosures, the fair-lending controls, and the examination posture before concluding that the activity is compliant.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Charter And The Regulator First

Banking compliance is charter-dependent. The same activity can be governed by different regulators and different rules depending on who conducts it.

Map:

- type of charter or license (national bank, state bank, credit union, industrial loan company, non-bank lender, mortgage company);
- primary federal regulator (OCC, Federal Reserve, FDIC, NCUA, CFPB, state banking authority);
- whether the entity holds deposits, makes loans, services loans, or only provides technology;
- whether the entity relies on a partner bank's charter;
- state licensing for lending, money transmission, or debt collection;
- membership in the Federal Reserve System or deposit insurance.

The regulator determines the examination cycle, reporting requirements, enforcement style, and applicable safe harbors. Do not assume federal rules preempt state rules without checking the specific preemption analysis.

### Apply The Right Consumer Credit Rule Set

Consumer lending is governed by a dense layer of federal and state rules. Each product triggers its own disclosure, underwriting, and servicing obligations.

Identify which apply:

- Truth in Lending (Regulation Z) for consumer credit disclosures and APR accuracy;
- Real Estate Settlement Procedures Act (RESPA) for mortgage servicing and settlement;
- Equal Credit Opportunity Act (Regulation B) for fair lending and adverse action notices;
- Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA) for credit report use and furnishing;
- Home Mortgage Disclosure Act (HMDA) for mortgage data reporting;
- Military Lending Act for service-member rate caps and protections;
- Servicemembers Civil Relief Act for rate caps on pre-service obligations;
- state usury, rate exportation, and licensing rules;
- state debt collection and servicing rules.

Each rule has specific timing, format, content, and recordkeeping requirements. A disclosure that is timely but formatted incorrectly can be a violation.

### Evaluate Fair Lending And UDAAP Risk

Fair lending and unfair, deceptive, or abusive acts and practices (UDAAP) are top enforcement priorities. They apply to marketing, underwriting, pricing, and servicing.

Assess:

- whether underwriting models produce disparate impact on protected classes;
- whether pricing varies by prohibited factor or proxy;
- whether marketing targets or excludes protected groups;
- whether steering occurs toward higher-cost products;
- whether adverse action notices are accurate and timely;
- whether servicing practices impose unfair fees or barriers;
- whether repricing or default terms are deceptive.

Fair lending analysis should include comparative file review, statistical testing where volume supports it, and review of proxies and less discriminatory alternatives. Document the legitimate business interests and the less discriminatory alternatives considered.

### Assess Safety And Soundness For Depositories

For chartered institutions, compliance is not only consumer protection. Safety-and-soundness rules govern capital, liquidity, risk management, and governance.

Review:

- capital adequacy and leverage ratios;
- liquidity coverage and contingency funding;
- loan concentration limits;
- allowance for loan and lease losses;
- interest rate risk;
- third-party and correspondent banking risk;
- insider lending restrictions;
- anti-tying rules;
- board oversight and reporting;
- internal audit and loan review.

A compliance program that ignores safety-and-soundness will fail a bank examination even if consumer disclosures are perfect.

### Control The Bank-Fintech Partnership Surface

Many technology companies offer banking or lending through a partner bank. The bank remains responsible for compliance, and regulators increasingly hold both parties accountable.

For partnerships, define:

- which entity performs each compliance function;
- who owns the customer relationship and disclosures;
- how underwriting decisions are made and documented;
- how fair lending testing is conducted across the program;
- data sharing, security, and privacy allocation;
- complaint handling and resolution;
- regulatory examination access to the fintech;
- termination and customer transition;
- reputational risk to the bank;
- vendor oversight by the bank over the fintech.

Regulators have stated that a bank cannot outsource its compliance responsibility. The program design should make the bank's oversight real, not nominal.

### Prepare For The Examination Cycle

Supervisory examinations are the primary enforcement mechanism. Readiness is a continuous discipline, not a pre-exam scramble.

Maintain:

- a compliance management system covering board and management oversight, policies, training, monitoring, and consumer complaint response;
- a risk assessment tied to products, volumes, and prior findings;
- self-identified issues with remediation tracking;
- board and committee reporting;
- policy and procedure version control;
- training records by role;
- monitoring and audit results;
- consumer complaint log with root-cause analysis;
- prior examination findings and remediation evidence.

Expect examiners to request policies, board minutes, monitoring reports, loan files, adverse action notices, complaint records, and fair-lending analysis. If these do not exist or are inconsistent, the examination will be difficult.

### Track State Licensing And Reporting

Lending, money transmission, debt collection, and servicing often require state licenses with separate reporting, bonding, and examination obligations.

Track:

- licensed activities by state;
- license renewals and conditions;
- NMLS reporting;
- call reports or condition reports;
- surety bonds and minimum net worth;
- state-specific disclosure or fee caps;
- state examination history;
- license surrender when exiting a state.

Operating without a required license can void loans, trigger restitution, and create criminal exposure in some jurisdictions.

## Common Traps

### Treating A Fintech As Outside Banking Regulation

A technology company that brands and operates a credit product is often treated by regulators as a lender or servicer regardless of the partner-bank charter. State true-lender and valid-when-made analysis must be checked.

### Assuming Federal Preemption Covers Everything

National bank preemption is powerful but not unlimited, and it varies by activity and court circuit. Do not assume a federal charter eliminates state licensing or consumer protection duties.

### Disclosures That Are Timely But Noncompliant

Many lending rules require specific format, font, grouping, and timing. A correct number delivered in the wrong form is still a violation.

### Fair Lending Treated As A Documentation Exercise

A fair lending policy without statistical testing, comparative file review, or monitoring is design only. Examiners look for evidence of analysis and action on findings.

### Ignoring Servicing And Default Practices

Most consumer complaints and enforcement actions arise from servicing: fees, loss-mitigation handling, foreclosure timing, and communication. Underwriting compliance does not cover servicing risk.

### UDAAP Applied Only To Disclosures

UDAAP reaches practices, not just words. A clearly disclosed fee can still be unfair if it is unavoidable and exploitative.

### Partner Bank Oversight That Is Nominal

A bank that cannot show how it monitors its fintech partner's underwriting, complaints, and fair lending will face criticism. Oversight must be documented and operational.

## Self-Check

- Is the charter, license, primary regulator, and applicable state licensing identified for each activity?
- Are the specific federal and state consumer credit rules mapped to each product, including disclosure format, timing, and content?
- Is fair lending assessed through underwriting, pricing, marketing, steering, adverse action, and statistical testing where volume supports it?
- Are UDAAP risks evaluated for both disclosures and underlying practices across the product lifecycle?
- For depositories, are capital, liquidity, concentration, interest-rate, insider, and governance safety-and-soundness obligations addressed?
- For bank-fintech partnerships, is responsibility allocation, underwriting control, fair-lending testing, complaint handling, examination access, and bank oversight documented and operational?
- Is examination readiness maintained continuously through a compliance management system, risk assessment, monitoring, complaints, and prior-finding remediation?
- Are state licensing, NMLS reporting, bonds, net worth, and state-specific rules tracked for each jurisdiction?
- Are servicing and default practices, including fees, loss mitigation, and foreclosure, covered and not only origination disclosures?
- Is preemption analyzed specifically rather than assumed, with state true-lender and licensing exposure checked for fintech structures?
