# Forced Labor And Modern Slavery Compliance

Forced labor compliance governs the prevention, detection, and remediation of forced labor and modern slavery in operations and supply chains. The defining feature is that forced labor is a severe human rights violation that triggers both criminal liability and import restrictions, and the legal standard in some regimes creates a rebuttable presumption that goods from specific regions are made with forced labor. The central difficulty is that forced labor is often hidden through legitimate-appearing recruitment, subcontracting, and labor schemes, and detection requires looking beyond documents to the actual conditions of work.

Use this skill before advising on forced labor compliance programs, import compliance, modern slavery reporting, recruitment practices, or responses to forced labor findings. The goal is to make the agent identify the legal prohibitions, the risk indicators, the due diligence controls, and the remediation obligations before concluding that a forced labor program is compliant.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Legal Prohibitions And Import Regimes

Forced labor is prohibited by international, criminal, and trade law.

Map:

- international conventions prohibiting forced labor and slavery;
- criminal prohibitions on human trafficking and forced labor;
- import bans on goods made with forced labor (e.g., US Section 307);
- rebuttable presumption regimes for specific regions (e.g., Xinjiang, North Korea);
- customs enforcement and detention authority;
- modern slavery and supply chain transparency reporting;
- mandatory due diligence laws covering forced labor;
- civil liability under emerging supply chain laws.

The import ban is a trade enforcement tool that can seize goods at the border. The rebuttable presumption shifts the burden to the importer to prove the goods are not made with forced labor—a high standard.

### Identify Forced Labor Risk Indicators

Forced labor manifests through recognizable indicators.

Assess:

- retention of identity documents (passports, work permits);
- restriction of movement and communication;
- debt bondage through recruitment fees and advances;
- deception about terms and conditions of work;
- withholding of wages or excessive deductions;
- intimidation, threats, and violence;
- excessive overtime under threat;
- abusive living conditions tied to employment;
- isolation and dependency on the employer;
- vulnerability factors (migration status, poverty, language).

No single indicator is conclusive, but the presence of multiple indicators signals high risk. Documents may appear compliant while conditions are coercive.

### Control Recruitment And Labor Agency Practices

Forced labor often enters through the recruitment pipeline.

Implement:

- the employer-pays principle: workers pay no fees for employment;
- prohibition on recruitment fees and related costs;
- reimbursement of fees found to have been paid;
- due diligence on labor recruiters and agents;
- written contracts in the worker's language provided before departure;
- contract transparency on wages, hours, and conditions;
- monitoring of recruiter practices;
- prohibition on document retention by recruiters or employers.

Recruitment fees create debt bondage, the most common pathway to forced labor in supply chains. The employer-pays principle shifts the cost to the employer where it belongs.

### Conduct Supply Chain Due Diligence For Forced Labor

Due diligence must penetrate beyond tier one.

Implement:

- mapping of high-risk supply chains to raw material and extraction level;
- risk assessment by region, sector, and commodity;
- worker voice and engagement tools (interviews, hotlines, surveys);
- audits with worker interviews and off-site access;
- review of recruitment and employment documents;
- assessment of wage records, overtime, and deductions;
- unannounced visits and verification;
- use of credible third-party assessors in high-risk contexts.

Standard social audits frequently miss forced labor because workers fear retaliation and auditors rely on documents. Effective detection requires worker engagement, off-site interviews, and experienced assessors.

### Comply With Import Restrictions And Customs Enforcement

Import compliance requires proactive supply chain assurance.

Cover:

- identification of goods and regions subject to import bans;
- supply chain tracing to the source of goods;
- documentation of labor conditions at the source;
- preparation of rebuttal evidence for detained shipments;
- customs response procedures for detention and seizure;
- supply chain diversification away from high-risk regions;
- continuous monitoring of enforcement actions and lists;
- coordination with customs brokers and counsel.

Once goods are detained, the importer faces a high burden and limited timeline. The most effective compliance is upstream assurance and, where necessary, exit from high-risk sourcing.

### Remediate Forced Labor Findings

When forced labor is found, remediation is required.

Implement:

- immediate steps to protect affected workers;
- support for workers including safe exit, back wages, and services;
- reimbursement of recruitment fees;
- corrective action with the supplier;
- escalation to contract termination where the supplier will not remediate;
- reporting to authorities where required;
- collaboration with NGOs and remediation experts;
- verification that remediation is effective.

Remediation is not just ending the relationship—it includes supporting the affected workers. Cutting and running can leave workers in worse conditions.

### Report Under Modern Slavery And Transparency Laws

Reporting requirements mandate disclosure of risks and actions.

Cover:

- applicability based on revenue, operations, and jurisdiction;
- content requirements (structure, operations, supply chains, risks, actions, effectiveness);
- board approval and signatory requirements;
- publication and accessibility;
- annual refresh;
- substantive content reflecting actual due diligence, not boilerplate;
- coordination across the organization for accurate reporting.

A modern slavery statement that is boilerplate or does not reflect actual due diligence is both a compliance and reputational risk. Reporting should be substantive and honest about gaps.

### Address State-Imposed Forced Labor

State-imposed forced labor in specific regions requires enhanced due diligence.

Assess:

- regions with documented state-imposed forced labor systems;
- rebuttable presumption regimes and their scope;
- labor transfer and poverty alleviation schemes as risk indicators;
- due diligence sufficient to overcome or respond to presumptions;
- supply chain tracing through high-risk regions;
- exit strategies where due diligence cannot provide assurance.

Labor transfer programs that move workers from minority regions to industrial settings can be indicators of state-imposed forced labor. The rebuttable presumption makes sourcing from affected regions extremely high-risk.

## Common Traps

### Standard Social Audit Missing Forced Labor

Audits that rely on documents and on-site interviews without worker trust miss coercion.

### Recruitment Fees Treated As A Worker Cost

Fees charged to workers create debt bondage; the employer-pays principle is the standard.

### Import Ban Assessed Only When Goods Are Detained

By the time of detention, the burden is on the importer and remedies are limited.

### Modern Slavery Statement As Boilerplate

Generic reporting that does not reflect actual due diligence is a risk.

### Cutting And Running Without Worker Remediation

Terminating a supplier relationship without supporting affected workers can worsen harm.

### Labor Transfer Programs Treated As Ordinary Employment

State-sponsored labor transfers can be vehicles for forced labor requiring enhanced scrutiny.

## Self-Check

- Are the legal prohibitions and import regimes understood including criminal law, import bans, rebuttable presumptions, customs enforcement, reporting, due diligence, and civil liability?
- Are forced labor risk indicators assessed including document retention, movement restriction, debt bondage, deception, wage withholding, intimidation, excessive overtime, living conditions, isolation, and vulnerability?
- Is recruitment controlled through the employer-pays principle, fee prohibition, reimbursement, recruiter due diligence, contract transparency, and document retention prohibition?
- Is supply chain due diligence conducted beyond tier one with worker engagement, off-site interviews, document review, wage assessment, unannounced visits, and credible assessors?
- Is import compliance managed through tracing, documentation, rebuttal evidence preparation, customs response, diversification, and enforcement monitoring?
- Is remediation implemented through worker protection, support, fee reimbursement, supplier corrective action, termination where necessary, authority reporting, and expert collaboration?
- is modern slavery and transparency reporting substantive, board-approved, published, refreshed annually, and reflective of actual due diligence?
- Is state-imposed forced labor assessed for specific regions, labor transfer programs, rebuttable presumptions, and exit strategies?
- Is the distinction between standard labor violations and forced labor indicators understood?
- Are vulnerable worker populations (migrants, women, minorities) given specific attention?
