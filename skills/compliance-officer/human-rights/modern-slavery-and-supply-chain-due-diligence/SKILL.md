# Modern Slavery And Supply Chain Due Diligence

Modern slavery compliance requires looking beyond the first tier of suppliers into the opaque depths of global supply chains where forced labor, debt bondage, and worker exploitation are most likely to hide. The defining difficulty is that the harm occurs far from the company's direct control, the indicators are subtle and easily explained away, and the legal obligations have shifted from voluntary disclosure toward mandatory due diligence and import restrictions. A company cannot rely on a supplier's assurance of compliance when the supplier itself may lack visibility into its own subcontractors and labor agents.

Use this skill before assessing supply chain human rights risk, conducting supplier due diligence, preparing a modern slavery statement, or responding to forced labor import enforcement. The goal is to make the agent identify the applicable due diligence regime, map the risk in the supply chain, design verification beyond questionnaires, and recognize where remediation obligations attach. Human rights due diligence obligations are jurisdiction-specific and contested situations should involve qualified counsel and human rights specialists.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Applicable Due Diligence And Disclosure Regime

Modern slavery obligations vary significantly by jurisdiction and are evolving rapidly.

Identify which regimes apply:

- modern slavery statement and reporting obligations based on revenue and operations thresholds;
- mandatory human rights due diligence laws requiring risk analysis, prevention, and remediation;
- forced labor import prohibitions creating customs enforcement risk;
- transparency in supply chains disclosure requirements;
- procurement and public contracting human rights conditions;
- sector-specific regimes for high-risk industries such as extractives and garments.

The regimes differ in whether they require disclosure, due diligence, or both, and in the standard of care expected. A company may be subject to several regimes simultaneously based on where it operates, sells, and sources.

### Map Supply Chain Risk Beyond The First Tier

Forced labor risk concentrates in lower tiers and in specific geographies, sectors, and labor models.

Map risk across:

- geography, including regions with weak rule of law, state-imposed forced labor, or conflict;
- sector, including high-risk industries such as extractives, garments, agriculture, construction, and electronics;
- labor model, including recruitment through labor agents, migrant labor, and temporary or agency labor;
- tier depth, recognizing that risk increases with distance from direct control;
- raw material origins where traceability is weakest.

First-tier supplier questionnaires provide false assurance because the highest risk sits in subcontracting, raw materials, and labor recruitment that the first tier may not itself understand. Risk mapping must reach into the supply chain, not stop at the contracting counterparty.

### Recognize The Indicators Of Forced Labor

Forced labor is identified through indicators, not admissions, and the indicators are the international standard.

Assess indicators including:

- abuse of vulnerability through isolation, language barriers, or immigration status;
- deception in recruitment about wages, conditions, or job content;
- restriction of movement and confinement to the workplace or living quarters;
- retention of identity documents such as passports;
- debt bondage through recruitment fees advanced to workers;
- withholding or delay of wages;
- abusive working and living conditions;
- excessive overtime that is involuntary;
- physical or sexual violence and intimidation.

The presence of multiple indicators strengthens the inference of forced labor. Recruitment fees paid by workers are a leading indicator of debt bondage and are the focus of employer-pays-principle commitments. Document retention, even when characterized as safekeeping, is a red flag.

### Verify Beyond Supplier Self-Reporting

Supplier self-reporting is the starting point, not the end, of due diligence.

Verify through:

- risk-based supplier assessment rather than uniform questionnaires;
- worker voice mechanisms including confidential interviews and surveys in workers' languages;
- on-site audits by competent assessors, with awareness of audit limitations and deception;
- review of recruitment and employment documentation including contracts and wage records;
- grievance mechanism accessibility for workers deep in the supply chain;
- triangulation with credible external sources including NGO and government reports.

Audits can be deceived, and workers may not speak freely to auditors connected to the buyer. Worker voice and grievance mechanisms that bypass the supplier hierarchy are essential to detect hidden exploitation.

### Design Remediation Rather Than Exit

When forced labor is found, the obligation is remediation of harm, not termination of the relationship, except in defined circumstances.

Remediate by:

- addressing the harm to affected workers including repayment of fees and back wages;
- correcting the root cause in the supplier's practices;
- escalating consequences for suppliers who refuse to remediate;
- engaging with the supplier to build capacity where willing;
- protecting affected workers from retaliation and further harm;
- documenting remediation outcomes.

Terminating a supplier relationship without remediation can worsen harm to workers who lose income and may be moved to worse conditions. Remediation is the expected response; exit is appropriate where the supplier refuses to engage or where the risk cannot be addressed.

### Prepare Credible Statements And Reports

Modern slavery statements and due diligence reports must be specific, accurate, and supported by evidence.

Ensure:

- description of operations, supply chain, and risk;
- actions taken including due diligence, assessment, and remediation;
- effectiveness measured through indicators, not just activity counts;
- consultation with stakeholders where required;
- board or responsible official approval where mandated;
- consistency between public statements and internal practice.

Generic statements that describe policies without evidence of action are increasingly scrutinized and may be treated as misleading. Statements that overstate performance create misrepresentation risk.

### Manage Import And Enforcement Risk

Forced labor import prohibitions create customs enforcement risk for goods tainted by forced labor.

Manage:

- supply chain traceability for goods from high-risk regions;
- documentation of due diligence to rebut forced labor presumptions where available;
- readiness for customs detention and rebuttal processes;
- coordination with logistics and customs brokers;
- awareness of rebuttable and irrebuttable presumptions that vary by regime.

Import enforcement can halt shipments and impose costs regardless of the company's knowledge. Traceability and due diligence documentation are the primary defenses.

## Common Traps

### Relying On First-Tier Questionnaires For Deep Supply Risk

The highest risk sits in lower tiers, subcontracting, and labor recruitment that first tiers may not understand.

### Treating Document Retention As Safekeeping

Retention of identity documents is a forced labor indicator regardless of characterization.

### Exit Without Remediation When Exploitation Is Found

Termination without remediation can worsen harm to affected workers.

### Generic Statements Without Evidence Of Action

Policy descriptions without evidence of action are scrutinized and may be misleading.

### Ignoring Recruitment Fees As A Debt Bondage Indicator

Worker-paid recruitment fees are a leading indicator of debt bondage.

## Self-Check

- Has the applicable due diligence and disclosure regime been identified based on revenue, operations, and sourcing geography?
- Is supply chain risk mapped beyond the first tier across geography, sector, labor model, tier depth, and raw material origin?
- Are forced labor indicators assessed including vulnerability abuse, deception, movement restriction, document retention, debt bondage, wage withholding, abusive conditions, involuntary overtime, and violence?
- Is verification conducted beyond self-reporting through worker voice, competent audits, document review, grievance mechanisms, and external triangulation?
- Is remediation prioritized over exit, with harm addressed, root causes corrected, consequences escalated for non-cooperation, and affected workers protected?
- Are statements and reports specific, evidence-supported, effectiveness-measured, and consistent with internal practice?
- Is import and customs enforcement risk managed through traceability, due diligence documentation, and rebuttal readiness?
- Where exploitation is suspected or import enforcement is triggered, has the matter involved qualified counsel and human rights specialists, recognizing that due diligence obligations are jurisdiction-specific and this guidance is not legal advice?
