# Conflict Minerals And Responsible Sourcing Compliance

Conflict minerals and responsible sourcing compliance governs the due diligence required to ensure that minerals and high-risk materials in products do not finance conflict or involve human rights abuses. The defining feature is that mineral supply chains are long, fragmented, and consolidated at the smelter or refiner level, making traceability difficult and requiring industry-wide assurance systems. The central difficulty is that compliance requires tracing materials from the finished product back through manufacturing, component suppliers, smelters, and ultimately to the mine of origin, and the further upstream, the less visibility and control.

Use this skill before advising on conflict minerals reporting, responsible sourcing due diligence, mineral chain of custody, or responses to sourcing certifications. The goal is to make the agent identify the applicable regulations, the OECD due diligence framework, the supply chain traceability requirements, and the assurance mechanisms before concluding that a responsible sourcing program is compliant.

## Core Rules

### Comply With Conflict Minerals Reporting Requirements

Specific regulations mandate conflict minerals disclosure.

Implement:

- identification of whether products contain conflict minerals (tin, tungsten, tantalum, gold—3TG);
- reasonable country-of-origin inquiry (RCOI) for minerals that may originate from the covered region;
- due diligence on the source and chain of custody;
- Conflict Minerals Report for products with minerals from or potentially from the covered region;
- description of due diligence framework;
- smelter or refiner identification and country-of-origin information;
- independent private sector audit of the Conflict Minerals Report;
- product declaration and filing.

The reporting obligation is triggered by manufacturing products in which conflict minerals are necessary to functionality or production. The RCOI is the initial threshold inquiry; due diligence and reporting follow where minerals may be from the covered region.

### Apply The OECD Due Diligence Framework

The OECD Due Diligence Guidance is the international standard for mineral supply chains.

Implement the five steps:

- establish strong company management systems (policy, team, supplier engagement, grievance mechanism);
- identify and assess risk in the supply chain (mapping to smelter or refiner, risk assessment);
- design and implement a strategy to respond to identified risks (risk management plan, mitigation, escalation);
- carry out independent third-party audit of smelter and refiner due diligence;
- report on supply chain due diligence.

The OECD framework applies to all minerals from conflict-affected and high-risk areas, not just 3TG. It is the reference for responsible sourcing beyond legal minimums.

### Map The Supply Chain To The Smelter Or Refiner Level

Traceability requires identifying the consolidation point in the chain.

Implement:

- engagement with direct suppliers to identify smelters and refiners;
- use of industry reporting templates (e.g., Conflict Minerals Reporting Template);
- identification of all smelters and refiners in the chain;
- cross-reference against validated smelter lists;
- mapping of countries of origin;
- identification of high-risk smelters or origins;
- continuous updating as supply chains change.

The smelter or refiner is the pinch point where origin can be verified. Once minerals are refined, they are commingled and origin cannot be traced. Smelter identification is therefore the critical control.

### Leverage Industry Assurance Programs

Industry programs provide smelter-level assurance.

Assess:

- the Responsible Minerals Assurance Process (RMAP) and equivalent programs;
- smelter and refiner participation and audit status;
- conformant versus active versus non-conformant smelters;
- coverage gaps for specific minerals and regions;
- cross-recognition among programs;
- limitations of assurance (process audit, not origin guarantee);
- engagement to expand smelter participation.

Industry assurance programs audit the smelter's due diligence process, not each shipment. Smelter conformity provides reasonable assurance but does not guarantee origin of every unit.

### Address Artisanal And Small-Scale Mining

Artisanal and small-scale mining (ASM) presents distinct risks and opportunities.

Assess:

- the prevalence of ASM in specific minerals (cobalt, gold, diamonds, 3TG);
- human rights risks including child labor and hazardous conditions;
- the role of ASM in livelihoods and development;
- formalization and responsible sourcing programs for ASM;
- traceability challenges in ASM supply chains;
- the decision to source, formalize, or exit ASM;
- due diligence specific to ASM contexts.

ASM is not inherently non-compliant, but it carries elevated risks. Responsible sourcing may involve engagement and formalization rather than exit, recognizing ASM's role in livelihoods.

### Manage Non-3TG High-Risk Minerals

Responsible sourcing extends beyond conflict minerals.

Cover:

- cobalt from high-risk regions with child labor concerns;
- lithium, nickel, and rare earth elements for batteries and electronics;
- copper, iron, and steel with environmental and labor risks;
- gemstones and precious metals with smuggling and conflict risk;
- oil, gas, and minerals funding conflict;
- commodity-specific due diligence frameworks;
- emerging regulations on battery materials and critical minerals.

Regulatory and stakeholder focus is expanding to battery minerals and critical materials. The OECD framework applies, and sector initiatives are developing for these materials.

### Respond To Risks Through Mitigation Or Exit

When risks are identified, a response strategy is required.

Implement:

- risk assessment based on severity and the company's leverage;
- mitigation plans with time-bound improvement targets;
- engagement with suppliers and smelters to drive improvement;
- escalation where mitigation fails;
- suspension or exit where risks cannot be mitigated;
- consideration of harm to workers and communities from exit;
- documentation of the response and its effectiveness.

Exit is not always the responsible choice, particularly where it harms workers or communities that depend on the operation. The response should be proportionate to severity and informed by leverage.

### Integrate Responsible Sourcing Into Procurement

Responsible sourcing must be embedded in procurement decisions.

Cover:

- supplier contracts with responsible sourcing requirements;
- supplier scorecards and qualification criteria;
- training for procurement teams;
- escalation of non-compliant suppliers;
- alignment of incentives with sourcing decisions;
- coordination between compliance, procurement, and product teams;
- new product design considerations for material selection.

Procurement decisions drive sourcing risk. Without embedding responsible sourcing into procurement, due diligence remains a parallel process rather than an integrated control.

## Common Traps

### Supplier Survey Treated As Due Diligence

Collecting supplier responses without verification or smelter identification is insufficient.

### Smelter Conformity Treated As Origin Guarantee

Smelter audits verify process, not the origin of every unit of mineral.

### 3TG Focus Ignoring Non-3TG High-Risk Minerals

Cobalt, lithium, and other materials carry significant risks requiring similar due diligence.

### Exit Without Considering Harm To Workers And Communities

Responsible exit considers the impact on those who depend on the operation.

### Reporting As Compliance Without Supplier Improvement

Reporting that does not drive supplier engagement and improvement misses the purpose.

### ASM Treated As Inherently Non-Compliant

ASM carries risks but also supports livelihoods; engagement and formalization may be more responsible than exit.

## Self-Check

- Are conflict minerals reporting requirements met including 3TG identification, RCOI, due diligence, Conflict Minerals Report, smelter identification, independent audit, and filing?
- Is the OECD five-step due diligence framework applied including management systems, risk identification, response strategy, independent audit, and reporting?
- Is the supply chain mapped to the smelter or refiner level with template use, validated list cross-reference, country-of-origin mapping, and continuous updating?
- Are industry assurance programs leveraged including RMAP participation, conformant status, coverage gaps, cross-recognition, and limitations?
- Is artisanal and small-scale mining assessed for human rights risks, livelihoods, formalization, traceability, and the source-engage-or-exit decision?
- Are non-3TG high-risk minerals including cobalt, lithium, rare earths, and precious metals subject to due diligence?
- Are identified risks responded to through mitigation, engagement, escalation, suspension, or exit with consideration of harm and documentation?
- Is responsible sourcing integrated into procurement through contracts, scorecards, training, escalation, incentives, and design considerations?
- Is the distinction between process assurance and origin guarantee understood?
- Is the OECD framework applied to all minerals from conflict-affected and high-risk areas, not just 3TG?
