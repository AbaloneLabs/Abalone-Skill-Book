# Human Rights Due Diligence And Supply Chain Compliance

Human rights due diligence governs the identification, prevention, and remediation of human rights impacts across operations and supply chains. The defining feature is that the responsibility to respect human rights extends beyond legal compliance to the international standard in the UN Guiding Principles, and an increasing number of laws impose mandatory due diligence, reporting, and import restrictions. The central difficulty is that supply chains are long, opaque, and cross jurisdictions with weak governance, and the presence of forced labor or child labor anywhere in the chain can create legal liability, import seizure, and reputational harm.

Use this skill before advising on supply chain human rights due diligence, modern slavery reporting, forced labor import compliance, conflict minerals, or human rights impact assessments. The goal is to make the agent identify the applicable frameworks, the supply chain risk areas, the due diligence methodology, and the remediation obligations before concluding that a human rights program is compliant.

## Core Rules

### Apply The UN Guiding Principles Framework

The UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights (UNGPs) establish the global standard.

Implement:

- the state duty to protect human rights;
- the corporate responsibility to respect human rights;
- access to remedy for victims;
- human rights policy commitment;
- ongoing human rights due diligence;
- integration of findings into business processes;
- tracking of effectiveness;
- communication and reporting;
- remediation of harm the business causes or contributes to.

The UNGPs are the reference standard even where not codified. They require ongoing due diligence, not a one-time assessment, and they extend to the supply chain where the business contributes to or is directly linked to harm.

### Conduct Risk-Based Supply Chain Mapping

Due diligence begins with understanding the supply chain.

Implement:

- mapping of suppliers beyond tier one to high-risk tiers;
- risk assessment by geography, commodity, and sector;
- identification of high-risk inputs (minerals, agriculture, textiles, electronics);
- assessment of supplier labor practices and conditions;
- use of questionnaires, audits, and third-party data;
- prioritization based on severity and likelihood of harm;
- continuous updating as supply chains change.

A tier-one-only view misses the highest-risk parts of the chain. Forced labor and child labor most often appear deeper in the chain, particularly in raw materials and extraction.

### Comply With Forced Labor And Modern Slavery Laws

Forced labor prohibitions are strict and expanding.

Cover:

- import bans on goods made with forced labor (e.g., US Section 307, targeting specific regions);
- modern slavery and supply chain transparency reporting requirements;
- criminal and civil liability for forced labor in operations;
- supplier code of conduct prohibitions;
- recruitment fee prohibition and employer-pays principle;
- retention of identity documents prohibition;
- due diligence on labor recruiters and agents;
- remediation of forced labor findings including worker support.

Forced labor import bans can result in seizure of goods at the border with limited recourse. The standard is rebuttable presumption in some regimes (e.g., goods from specific regions presumed made with forced labor).

### Implement Human Rights Due Diligence

Due diligence is a continuous process of identification and response.

Implement:

- identification of actual and potential human rights impacts;
- assessment of the business's involvement (cause, contribute, directly linked);
- integration of findings into procurement and operational decisions;
- action to prevent and mitigate impacts;
- tracking of effectiveness;
- communication of performance;
- remediation and grievance mechanisms;
- attention to vulnerable groups (women, migrants, indigenous peoples, children).

Due diligence must be ongoing and risk-based. The severity of harm, not just likelihood, drives prioritization. Vulnerable groups require specific attention.

### Manage Conflict Minerals And High-Risk Commodities

Specific commodities carry elevated human rights risks.

Assess:

- conflict minerals (tin, tungsten, tantalum, gold) from conflict-affected areas;
- cobalt and lithium from artisanal mining with child labor risk;
- cotton and textiles from forced labor regions;
- agricultural commodities (palm oil, cocoa, sugar) with labor and land rights risks;
- timber and seafood with forced labor and illegal harvesting risk;
- due diligence frameworks specific to each commodity;
- traceability and chain of custody verification;
- smelter and refiner verification.

Conflict minerals reporting requires a reasonable country-of-origin inquiry and due diligence. Other commodities have sector-specific frameworks. Traceability to the source is the control.

### Provide Access To Remedy And Grievance Mechanisms

The responsibility to respect includes providing remedy.

Implement:

- operational-level grievance mechanisms for workers and communities;
- accessibility (language, literacy, fear of retaliation);
- effectiveness criteria (legitimate, accessible, predictable, equitable, transparent, rights-compatible, source of continuous learning);
- investigation of complaints;
- remediation of confirmed harm;
- non-retaliation against complainants;
- escalation paths for unresolved grievances.

A grievance mechanism that workers fear to use is not effective. The UNGP effectiveness criteria define what makes a mechanism legitimate.

### Comply With Mandatory Due Diligence And Reporting

Mandatory human rights and environmental due diligence laws are expanding.

Cover:

- modern slavery statements and reporting;
- supply chain due diligence acts requiring risk analysis, prevention, and remediation;
- import restrictions and customs enforcement;
- corporate sustainability due diligence directives;
- deforestation-free supply chain regulations;
- transparency in supply chain acts;
- penalties for non-compliance and failure to report.

Mandatory regimes differ in scope—some require reporting, others require due diligence and remediation. The trend is toward substantive obligations, not just transparency.

### Address Human Rights In Operations, Not Just Supply Chains

The responsibility extends to the company's own operations.

Assess:

- labor rights in direct operations (freedom of association, nondiscrimination, safety);
- community impacts (land, water, indigenous rights);
- security arrangements and human rights;
- environmental impacts with human rights dimensions;
- government interactions and human rights defenders;
- high-risk operating environments and conflict-affected areas.

Supply chain focus should not overshadow the company's own operations. Security forces, community displacement, and environmental harm can create direct human rights impacts.

## Common Traps

### Tier-One-Only Supply Chain View

Forced labor and child labor most often appear beyond tier one, in raw materials and extraction.

### Due Diligence As A One-Time Assessment

Human rights due diligence must be ongoing, risk-based, and integrated into business decisions.

### Reporting Without Substantive Remediation

Transparency reporting that does not drive prevention and remediation is insufficient.

### Grievance Mechanism That Workers Fear To Use

A mechanism with accessibility barriers or retaliation risk is not effective.

### Import Ban Assessed Only At The Border

By the time goods are seized, the remedy is limited; due diligence must be upstream.

### Forced Labor Region Treated As Ordinary Sourcing Risk

Specific regions carry rebuttable presumptions and require enhanced due diligence or exit.

## Self-Check

- Is the UNGP framework applied including policy commitment, ongoing due diligence, integration, tracking, communication, and remediation?
- Is the supply chain mapped beyond tier one with risk assessment by geography, commodity, and sector, prioritizing high-risk inputs?
- Are forced labor and modern slavery laws complied with including import bans, reporting, criminal liability, recruitment fee prohibition, document retention prohibition, recruiter due diligence, and remediation?
- Is human rights due diligence conducted continuously including impact identification, involvement assessment, integration, prevention, tracking, communication, and grievance mechanisms?
- Are conflict minerals and high-risk commodities managed through commodity-specific frameworks, traceability, chain of custody, and smelter verification?
- Is access to remedy provided through effective operational grievance mechanisms meeting UNGP effectiveness criteria?
- Are mandatory due diligence and reporting laws complied with including modern slavery statements, due diligence acts, import restrictions, and deforestation regulations?
- Are human rights impacts in the company's own operations assessed including labor rights, community impacts, security, environment, and high-risk operating environments?
- Is the distinction between causing, contributing to, and being directly linked to harm assessed for remediation responsibility?
- Are vulnerable groups given specific attention in due diligence and remediation?
