# Human Rights Impact Assessment And Grievance

Human rights due diligence requires understanding a company's actual and potential impacts on people, not merely its compliance with law, and providing accessible remedy when harm occurs. The defining difficulty is that the framework is risk-to-people rather than risk-to-company, the assessment must reach stakeholders who have reason not to trust the company, and grievance mechanisms that are inaccessible, feared, or ineffective provide false assurance while harm continues. The gap between a published human rights policy and the lived experience of affected people is where human rights failure lives.

Use this skill before conducting a human rights impact assessment, designing a grievance mechanism, engaging with affected communities, or planning remedy for adverse impacts. The goal is to make the agent identify salient human rights, design assessment that reaches affected stakeholders, build grievance mechanisms that workers and communities will actually use, and meet remedy obligations. Human rights due diligence should align with international frameworks and contested situations should involve qualified counsel and human rights specialists.

## Core Rules

### Focus On Salient Human Rights, Not All Rights Equally

Human rights due diligence prioritizes the rights most at risk of severe impact, which may differ from the rights a company finds convenient to discuss.

Identify salient rights through:

- assessment of where the company's operations, products, and relationships may cause, contribute, or be linked to severe harm;
- consideration of the severity of potential impact in terms of scale, scope, and irremediability;
- engagement with affected stakeholders about which impacts matter most to them;
- mapping across the value chain including suppliers, customers, and end-use;
- distinction between rights the company directly impacts and those it is linked to through business relationships.

Salience is about risk to people, not risk to the company, though the two often overlap. A company that focuses only on the rights where it performs well, while ignoring the rights where its impact is severe, has not conducted meaningful due diligence.

### Distinguish Cause, Contribution, And Linkage

The nature of a company's involvement in an adverse impact determines its responsibility and the required response.

Determine:

- where the company causes the impact through its own actions, requiring cessation and remedy;
- where the contributes to the impact through its own actions or omissions alongside others, requiring contribution to remedy and use of leverage;
- where the impact is directly linked to the company's operations, products, or services through a business relationship, requiring use of leverage to influence the relationship and encourage remedy.

The distinction determines whether the company must provide or contribute to remedy, and whether its primary lever is change within its own operations or leverage over a business partner. Mischaracterizing linkage as non-involvement is a common error that avoids responsibility.

### Design Assessment That Reaches Affected Stakeholders

Impact assessment that relies on internal perspectives and desktop review will miss the impacts that matter to affected people.

Design assessment that:

- engages directly with affected workers, communities, and their legitimate representatives;
- uses methods that are safe, confidential, and culturally appropriate;
- accounts for power imbalances that suppress candor;
- includes the perspectives of vulnerable and marginalized groups;
- triangulates stakeholder input with documentation, observation, and external evidence;
- is repeated periodically rather than treated as a one-time exercise.

Workers and communities will not share concerns through channels they do not trust. The method must reach people where they are, in their languages, through intermediaries they trust when direct engagement is unsafe.

### Build Grievance Mechanisms That People Will Use

A grievance mechanism that affected people cannot or will not use provides false assurance.

Build mechanisms that are:

- legitimate, with appropriate governance and independence from operational management;
- accessible, in languages and through channels affected people can reach;
- predictable, with clear timelines and transparent process;
- equitable, ensuring affected people can participate without disadvantage;
- transparent, providing information about progress and outcomes;
- rights-compatible, ensuring outcomes accord with internationally recognized human rights;
- a source of continuous learning, feeding back into prevention;
- based on engagement and dialogue rather than imposition.

Mechanisms housed within operational management that affected people fear are not legitimate grievance channels. Anonymous reporting options, worker-elected representatives, and independent ombuds functions increase legitimacy and use.

### Provide Or Enable Remedy For Adverse Impacts

Where a company has caused or contributed to harm, it must provide or cooperate in remedy.

Provide remedy through:

- cessation of the harmful practice;
- apology and acknowledgement;
- restitution and rehabilitation;
- financial compensation;
- guarantees of non-repetition through systemic change;
- engagement with affected people on what remedy means to them.

Remedy is defined partly by the affected people's own needs, not solely by what the company prefers to offer. Where the company is linked to harm through a business relationship, it must use leverage to encourage the partner to provide remedy.

### Use And Increase Leverage Over Business Relationships

Where a company lacks control over a business partner causing harm, it must use and increase its leverage.

Use leverage by:

- engaging the partner on the finding and required change;
- conditioning contracts on human rights performance;
- collaborating with industry peers to raise standards;
- escalating consequences including potential exit as a last resort;
- increasing leverage through longer-term commitments, capacity building, or collective action.

Exit without attempting to use leverage can abandon affected people to worse conditions. Leverage should be used before exit, and exit reserved for situations where leverage fails or the risk is irremediable.

### Integrate Findings And Track Effectiveness

Due diligence is a continuous process, not a report.

Integrate by:

- embedding findings in procurement, operations, and risk management decisions;
- assigning responsibility for prevention and remediation actions;
- tracking effectiveness through indicators of outcomes, not just activity;
- communicating with affected stakeholders about what was done;
- repeating the cycle as operations and contexts change.

A report that does not change decisions or practice has not conducted due diligence. Effectiveness is measured by reduced harm to people, not by completed assessments.

## Common Traps

### Focusing On Rights Where The Company Performs Well

Salience is about risk to people; convenient topics are not necessarily salient.

### Desktop Assessment Without Affected Stakeholder Voice

Internal and documentary review misses the impacts that matter to affected people.

### Grievance Mechanisms Housed Where They Are Feared

Mechanisms within feared management structures are not legitimate and will not surface harm.

### Characterizing Linkage As Non-Involvement

Business relationships that link the company to harm carry responsibility to use leverage.

### Measuring Activity Instead Of Outcome

Completed assessments and audits are not evidence of reduced harm.

## Self-Check

- Are salient human rights identified based on severity of potential impact to people across the value chain, distinguishing rights the company directly impacts from those linked through relationships?
- Is the company's involvement characterized correctly as cause, contribution, or linkage, determining whether it must provide remedy, contribute to remedy, or use leverage?
- Does the impact assessment reach affected workers, communities, and marginalized groups through safe, confidential, culturally appropriate, and triangulated methods?
- Is the grievance mechanism legitimate, accessible, predictable, equitable, transparent, rights-compatible, learning-oriented, and based on engagement, with independence from feared management structures?
- Where harm is caused or contributed to, is remedy provided through cessation, acknowledgement, restitution, compensation, and guarantees of non-repetition, informed by affected people's needs?
- Is leverage used and increased over business relationships linked to harm before considering exit?
- Are findings integrated into decisions, tracked for outcome effectiveness, and communicated to affected stakeholders, with the cycle repeated as contexts change?
- Where severe impacts or contested involvement are identified, has the assessment involved qualified counsel and human rights specialists, recognizing that due diligence should align with international frameworks and this guidance is not legal advice?
