# Outside Employment And Activities Policy

Employees increasingly hold second jobs, side ventures, board seats, investments, and public-facing roles such as content creation. An outside activities policy must balance legitimate employee freedom against the risks these activities create: conflicts of interest, misuse of confidential information, divided loyalty, depletion of time and energy, use of company resources, and reputational association. The failure mode is a policy that either bans everything, which employees ignore or resent and which overreaches into lawful off-duty conduct, or that allows everything, which leaves the organization exposed to conflicts, IP leakage, and fatigue-related errors. The judgment is defining which activities require disclosure and approval, which are prohibited, and which are the employee's private business.

Use this skill before drafting or revising an outside employment and activities policy, designing disclosure and approval workflows, or reviewing whether the policy addresses the real risk surfaces. The goal is to make the agent think about conflicts, confidentiality, time, resources, reputation, legal limits on employer control, and the evidence the policy generates.

## Core Rules

### Define The Scope Of Covered Activities Clearly

Ambiguity about what counts as a covered activity produces inconsistent disclosure and enforcement. Define the activities the policy reaches.

Cover at least:

- second jobs and moonlighting, paid or unpaid;
- self-employment, consulting, and freelance work;
- board, advisory, and fiduciary roles in for-profit entities;
- leadership or board roles in nonprofits and associations;
- significant investments, especially in customers, suppliers, or competitors;
- personal ventures, startups, and business ownership;
- public-facing roles such as speaking, writing, media, and content creation that could be attributed to the employer;
- political candidacy or government service.

Distinguish activities that require disclosure and approval from those that are the employee's private business. A policy that purports to govern all off-duty conduct is both unenforceable and, in many jurisdictions, unlawful.

### Separate Conflict, Confidentiality, Time, And Resource Risks

Outside activities create several distinct risks, and the policy should address each rather than collapsing them into a single "conflict" concern.

Address each risk surface:

- conflict of interest, where the outside role involves a customer, supplier, competitor, or decision intersecting with the employer;
- confidentiality and IP, where the outside activity could use or disclose employer information, or where IP ownership is unclear;
- time and commitment, where the outside role detracts from the employee's duties, focus, or availability;
- resources, where employer equipment, systems, time, or brand are used for outside activity;
- reputation and association, where the outside activity could be attributed to the employer or create reputational harm;
- legal and regulatory, where the outside role creates licensing, securities, or other compliance issues for the employer.

For each risk, define the standard. A board seat at a competitor is a conflict and likely prohibited; a weekend retail job with no intersection is a time question; a personal blog on industry topics is a reputation and confidentiality question. Different risks need different controls.

### Respect Legal Limits On Employer Control

In many jurisdictions, employers cannot lawfully regulate all off-duty conduct. Off-duty conduct laws, labor protections, and emerging rules on employer reach over personal expression limit what a policy can prohibit.

Consider:

- off-duty conduct protections that limit discipline for lawful outside activities;
- labor and works-council consultation requirements before imposing restrictions;
- protections for political, religious, or union activity;
- limits on monitoring personal devices and accounts;
- differences for contractors and gig workers, who may have more autonomy;
- jurisdictional variation, since a global policy must defer to stricter local protections.

A policy that overreaches is unenforceable and can itself create liability. Calibrate restrictions to what the employer can lawfully control, and document the legal basis for sensitive restrictions.

### Set Disclosure And Approval Triggers Proportionate To Risk

Not every outside activity needs approval. Requiring approval for everything produces noise and delay; requiring it for nothing misses real risk. Segment by risk.

A workable model:

- low-risk activities with no conflict, confidentiality, or time impact require no approval, only a general duty to disclose if circumstances change;
- moderate-risk activities, such as a second job or a nonprofit board, require disclosure and manager acknowledgment;
- high-risk activities, such as a for-profit board, a role with a competitor or customer, or a venture in the same industry, require compliance or legal review and formal approval;
- prohibited activities, such as a role with a direct competitor or use of confidential information, are denied regardless of approval.

State the approval authority for each tier, the decision standard, the turnaround expectation, and what happens if approval is not obtained before the activity begins. Default to requiring pre-approval for high-risk activities and accepting post-notification for low-risk ones.

### Address Confidentiality, IP, And Non-Compete Intersections

Outside activities frequently involve information and intellectual property, and the policy must clarify ownership and boundaries.

Address:

- the duty not to use or disclose employer confidential information in outside work;
- IP ownership, including that employer IP rules typically cover work related to the job, and clarifying ownership of outside-created IP;
- non-compete and non-solicitation obligations that may restrict certain outside roles;
- the boundary between general industry knowledge, which the employee may use, and trade secrets, which they may not;
- the risk that outside work creates derivative or competing products.

Coordinate the outside activities policy with the confidentiality, IP, and non-compete frameworks so they are consistent. A gap between them is where disputes and leakage occur.

### Manage Time, Fatigue, And Safety Risk

Outside employment can degrade performance and, in safety-sensitive roles, create real harm. The policy should address time and fitness for duty.

Address:

- expectations that outside work will not occur during working hours or on employer equipment;
- limits on hours for safety-sensitive, regulated, or on-call roles;
- fatigue management in transport, healthcare, manufacturing, and similar roles;
- performance expectations and the consequence if outside work affects duties;
- on-call and travel roles where time boundaries are harder to enforce.

In safety-sensitive roles, fatigue from a second job is a compliance and safety issue, not merely a performance one. The policy should name these roles and apply stricter limits.

### Govern Board Service And Investments Specifically

Board service and material investments warrant dedicated treatment because they create fiduciary and conflict exposure that ordinary second jobs do not.

For board service:

- require pre-approval for any for-profit board;
- consider nonprofit boards, which may still create conflicts or time demands;
- address board service at competitors, customers, suppliers, or portfolio companies;
- set expectations for time commitment and recusal from intersecting decisions.

For investments:

- define materiality thresholds that trigger disclosure;
- prohibit or restrict investments in competitors, customers, suppliers, and entities the employee influences;
- address trading on inside information and coordinate with securities compliance;
- consider investment funds and family accounts that may be indirect holdings.

Board seats and investments are the outside activities most likely to create enforcement-level conflict, and they deserve the most explicit controls.

### Build Records, Monitoring, And Refresh

An outside activities program must be able to show what was disclosed, what was approved, and what conditions applied. Records are the evidence the program operates.

Maintain:

- disclosure records by employee and period;
- approval decisions, conditions, and expiry;
- a register of board seats and material investments;
- periodic re-attestation, since outside activities change;
- monitoring for undisclosed activities, such as cross-checks with public records and social media where lawful;
- a process to revoke approval when circumstances change.

Refresh disclosures on a cycle and on triggers such as role change, new venture, or new investment. A one-time disclosure that is never updated misses the activities that arise later.

## Common Traps

### Banning All Outside Activity

A blanket ban overreaches, is often unlawful, and is widely ignored, which undermines the policy's credibility for the activities that genuinely need control.

### Requiring Approval For Everything

Requiring approval for trivial activities floods the process, delays legitimate requests, and trains employees to avoid disclosure rather than seek it.

### Ignoring Confidentiality And IP Boundaries

Outside activities that use employer information or create IP disputes are a major source of litigation and leakage, yet are often under-addressed.

### Overreaching Into Lawful Off-Duty Conduct

Restricting political, personal, or lawful off-duty activity can violate off-duty conduct laws and create employer liability.

### No Treatment Of Board Service And Investments

Board seats and material investments create the highest conflict exposure and need dedicated, explicit controls, not a generic clause.

### Missing Safety-Sensitive Roles

In transport, healthcare, and manufacturing, fatigue from second jobs is a safety issue. A policy that ignores these roles creates real harm.

### One-Time Disclosure With No Refresh

Outside activities change constantly. A disclosure collected once and never updated misses most of the risk.

### No Monitoring For Undisclosed Activities

If the only mechanism is self-disclosure, the program cannot detect undisclosed conflicts and cannot know its own coverage gap.

## Self-Check

- Does the policy define covered activities clearly, distinguishing those requiring disclosure and approval from private off-duty conduct?
- Are conflict, confidentiality, time, resource, reputation, and legal risks each addressed with distinct standards rather than collapsed into one?
- Are restrictions calibrated to lawful employer authority, respecting off-duty conduct protections and local labor law?
- Are disclosure and approval triggers segmented by risk, with defined authority, decision standard, and turnaround?
- Are confidentiality, IP ownership, and non-compete intersections coordinated with the relevant frameworks?
- Are time, fatigue, and safety risks addressed, especially in safety-sensitive, regulated, and on-call roles?
- Are board service and material investments given dedicated, explicit controls proportionate to their conflict exposure?
- Are records of disclosure, approval, conditions, and expiry maintained, with periodic re-attestation?
- Is there lawful monitoring for undisclosed activities, such as public records or social media review where permitted?
- Does the policy refresh on a cycle and on triggers, rather than relying on one-time disclosure?
