# Anti-Bribery And Anti-Corruption Policy

Anti-bribery and anti-corruption (ABAC) law is unusual in that it reaches conduct far beyond the borders of the enacting country, imposes liability for the acts of third parties, and treats modest payments to low-level officials as serious offenses. An ABAC policy that merely states "do not pay bribes" fails because the real risk lives in the gray zones: facilitation payments, third-party intermediaries, political contributions, sponsorships, and the difference between a permissible business expense and an improper advantage. The judgment in ABAC policy is anticipating the channels through which corrupt payments are disguised and building controls that detect them, while keeping the business able to operate.

Use this skill before drafting or revising an ABAC policy, designing third-party diligence, addressing facilitation payments, or reviewing whether controls actually prevent and detect corruption. The goal is to make the agent think about the legal reach, the disguised channels, third-party liability, government officials, and the evidence a defensible program generates.

## Core Rules

### Define The Prohibitions Against The Actual Legal Reach

ABAC law reaches more than direct cash bribes. Define prohibitions to match the scope of FCPA, UKBA, and applicable local law, which collectively cover offering, promising, authorizing, or accepting anything of value to obtain or retain business or an improper advantage.

Cover:

- direct payments to government officials or private recipients;
- offers and promises, even if not paid;
- authorizing a payment through another person;
- receiving or accepting a benefit, not only giving;
- obtaining or retaining business or any improper advantage, not only direct contract awards;
- anything of value, including cash, gifts, travel, jobs, charitable contributions, and political support;
- conduct through third parties, agents, and intermediaries;
- conduct that occurs anywhere in the world if it touches the organization.

State that the policy applies to all employees, officers, directors, agents, consultants, distributors, joint venture partners, and any third party acting on the organization's behalf. A prohibition limited to direct cash bribes to officials misses the channels where liability actually arises.

### Treat Government Officials As The Highest-Risk Category

Most ABAC enforcement involves government officials, and the definition of official is broader than employees assume. It includes officials of state-owned enterprises, political parties, candidates, public international organizations, and intermediaries acting for any of them.

For government officials:

- presume against any payment, gift, travel, or benefit unless explicitly permitted by law and approved;
- recognize that state-owned enterprises and state-controlled companies often count as government;
- apply heightened diligence to dealings in sectors with heavy state involvement;
- require legal review before offering anything of value to an official;
- account for the official's own rules, which may prohibit what local ABAC law permits.

The single most common enforcement fact pattern is an improper benefit to an official of a state-owned enterprise that the company did not realize counted as government. Train employees to recognize the breadth of the definition.

### Address Facilitation Payments Explicitly

Facilitation or grease payments, small payments to expedite routine non-discretionary government action, are a defined exception under some laws such as the FCPA but are prohibited under others such as the UKBA, and are almost always a poor risk choice even where technically permitted.

Address facilitation payments by:

- prohibiting them as a matter of policy regardless of local legality, which is the defensible default;
- defining what they are and why they are prohibited;
- providing a procedure for extortion or duress situations, where payment is made under threat to safety, with a requirement to report immediately;
- recording any payment made under duress, with the circumstances and approval;
- training employees in high-risk jurisdictions to recognize and refuse facilitation requests.

Silence on facilitation payments leaves employees to guess, and guessing in a high-risk jurisdiction produces violations. An explicit prohibition with a duress exception is clearer and more defensible.

### Build Third-Party And Intermediary Controls

ABAC liability extends to corrupt payments made through third parties, including agents, consultants, distributors, joint venture partners, and suppliers, when the organization knew or should have known. Third-party risk is where most avoidable liability lives.

Third-party controls should include:

- risk-based due diligence before engagement, proportionate to the risk and the role;
- review of the third party's ownership, reputation, government connections, and prior issues;
- contractual anti-bribery commitments and audit rights;
- controls on how the third party is paid, including reasonableness of compensation and avoidance of unusual payment terms;
- ongoing monitoring of the third party's conduct and red flags;
- training or certification for higher-risk third parties;
- a process to escalate and investigate red flags, up to termination.

A third party with an unusual commission, an offshore account, a request for cash, or a government relative is a red flag, not a routine onboarding. The diligence must be proportionate but real, and it must continue after onboarding.

### Close The Disguised-Channel Risks

Corrupt payments are rarely labeled as bribes. They are disguised as commissions, consulting fees, sponsorships, charitable contributions, political donations, travel, discounts, rebates, or marketing funds. The policy must address these channels directly.

Address:

- political contributions and the prohibition on conduit giving;
- charitable contributions that could benefit a decision-maker or their family;
- sponsorships and association payments directed by officials;
- marketing and promotion funds provided to distributors or partners;
- consulting and commission arrangements that exceed market or lack deliverables;
- travel and per diem payments for officials that exceed genuine business need;
- discounts and rebates that could be converted to cash.

For each channel, define the control: who approves, what evidence of legitimate purpose is required, and what red flags trigger review. A policy that closes only the direct-payment channel leaves the disguised channels wide open.

### Design Detection, Not Just Prevention

Prevention controls, such as approvals and prohibitions, are necessary but insufficient. Detection controls identify corrupt payments that prevention missed.

Detection mechanisms include:

- expense review for unusual, cash, or high-risk jurisdictions;
- audit of commission and consulting payments for reasonableness;
- review of third-party payment terms and destinations;
- monitoring of discounts, rebates, and marketing fund use;
- hotline and exit interview themes;
- review of customs, licensing, and permitting payments;
- data analytics on payment patterns, including round numbers, urgent payments, and payments to high-risk jurisdictions;
- audit of joint venture and distributor transactions.

Detection should be risk-based and focused on the disguised channels. A program with strong prevention but no detection will miss the payments that matter most.

### Define Consequences, Reporting, And Remediation

An ABAC policy must state consequences and provide a workable reporting and remediation path, or it will not be followed and violations will be concealed.

Include:

- a clear statement that violations result in discipline up to termination and contract termination, and may be reported to authorities;
- a non-retaliation commitment for good-faith reporting;
- multiple reporting channels, including a confidential hotline;
- a requirement to seek guidance before acting when uncertain;
- an investigation process with defined authority and preservation of evidence;
- remediation expectations, including control fixes and training, after a violation;
- voluntary self-disclosure considerations, routed to legal.

The policy should make clear that asking for help before acting is expected and protected, and that concealment is treated more severely than the underlying conduct.

### Keep The Program Aligned To Law And Refreshed

ABAC law and enforcement priorities evolve. The policy must be reviewed against current law, guidance, and enforcement actions, and refreshed when the business enters new markets, products, or third-party structures.

Maintain:

- a named policy owner, usually compliance or legal;
- a review cycle and event-driven triggers;
- tracking of legal and enforcement developments;
- updates to high-risk jurisdiction lists;
- re-training of high-risk roles after material changes;
- version control and re-acknowledgment.

A static ABAC policy in a changing enforcement environment is a liability. The program must visibly learn and update.

## Common Traps

### Limiting Prohibitions To Direct Cash Bribes

The disguised channels, commissions, sponsorships, travel, and political contributions are where liability actually arises. A cash-only prohibition misses them.

### Failing To Recognize State-Owned Enterprises As Government

State-owned companies and state-controlled entities often count as government under ABAC law. Treating them as private recipients creates direct exposure.

### Ambiguity On Facilitation Payments

Leaving facilitation payments unaddressed forces employees to guess in high-risk jurisdictions. An explicit prohibition with a duress exception is clearer.

### Onboarding Third Parties Without Ongoing Monitoring

Initial diligence that is never refreshed misses the red flags that emerge after engagement, when the corrupt conduct usually surfaces.

### Closing Only The Direct-Payment Channel

If commissions, charitable contributions, and marketing funds are uncontrolled, corrupt payments migrate to those channels.

### Prevention Without Detection

Approvals and prohibitions do not identify payments that bypass them. Without detection, the program cannot show it works.

### Inconsistent Enforcement For Revenue Producers

ABAC violations by top performers that go unaddressed signal that the policy is selective and destroy the program's credibility with regulators.

### No Procedure For Duress Or Extortion

Without a duress procedure, employees facing threats either pay and conceal it or refuse and risk harm. Both outcomes are bad; a defined procedure handles both.

## Self-Check

- Do the prohibitions match the legal reach of FCPA, UKBA, and applicable local law, covering offers, promises, authorization, acceptance, anything of value, and improper advantage?
- Is the definition of government official broad enough to include state-owned enterprises, political parties, candidates, and intermediaries?
- Are facilitation payments explicitly prohibited with a defined duress or extortion exception and reporting requirement?
- Are third-party controls risk-based, documented, contractual, and continuing after onboarding, with red-flag escalation?
- Are the disguised channels, commissions, sponsorships, charitable and political contributions, travel, and marketing funds, addressed with controls?
- Are there detection mechanisms focused on expense, payment terms, high-risk jurisdictions, and analytics, not only prevention approvals?
- Are consequences, non-retaliation, reporting channels, and a pre-action guidance path clearly stated?
- Is there an investigation and remediation process with evidence preservation and voluntary disclosure routed to legal?
- Is the policy owned, reviewed on a cycle and on events, and refreshed against legal and enforcement developments?
- Is enforcement consistent regardless of the violator's revenue contribution or seniority?
