# Values, Principles, And Rules Hierarchy

Organizations express expectations through several layers of documents: values, principles, codes, policies, procedures, standards, work instructions, and laws. Each layer has a different purpose, authority, and stability. Confusion about which layer governs, what happens when layers conflict, and where a given expectation belongs produces inconsistent decisions, unenforceable standards, and disputes that escalate to legal because no one knows which document controls. Designing the hierarchy deliberately is what separates a coherent governance system from a pile of overlapping documents.

Use this skill before building a governance document architecture, deciding where a new standard belongs, reconciling conflicting documents, or advising how a value should cascade into enforceable rules. The goal is to make the agent think about authority, precedence, stability, conflict resolution, and the cost of layering.

## Core Rules

### Define Each Layer By Purpose, Authority, And Stability

A hierarchy only works if each layer has a distinct job. Do not let documents at different layers do the same work, or they will drift and conflict.

A typical layered model:

- values state what the organization stands for; they are aspirational, slow to change, owned at the top, and not individually enforceable;
- principles translate values into behavioral guidance that applies broadly; they are more specific than values but still general;
- the code of conduct states the core mandatory standards binding all covered persons; it is enforceable and board-approved;
- policies state mandatory requirements for specific risk areas, such as anti-bribery or conflicts; they are enforceable and approved by a defined authority;
- procedures and work instructions give step-by-step process for executing policies; they change more often;
- standards specify technical or measurement requirements, such as thresholds or formats;
- laws and regulations are external and always supersede internal documents where they conflict by being stricter.

For each document, record its layer, owner, approval authority, review cycle, and change velocity. A value that changes every year is not a value; a procedure that never changes is probably stale.

### Assign Each Expectation To The Right Layer

A common failure is putting the wrong content in the wrong layer. Detailed thresholds belong in standards or procedures, not in values. Aspirational language belongs in values, not buried in a procedure where it cannot be enforced.

Use these tests:

- if it must be enforceable and applies to everyone, it belongs in the code or a policy;
- if it is aspirational and framing, it belongs in values or principles;
- if it is a specific number, threshold, or format, it belongs in a standard;
- if it is a step-by-step process, it belongs in a procedure;
- if it changes frequently because of operations, keep it out of the code, which should be stable.

Putting volatile operational detail into the code forces constant code revisions and erodes the code's authority. Putting enforceable rules only into values makes them unenforceable. Match content to layer deliberately.

### Establish Precedence And Conflict Resolution Rules

Layers will conflict eventually. The organization must state in advance which document wins, and the default rule is not always "the stricter one."

Establish precedence:

- law and regulation supersede internal documents where they conflict;
- within internal documents, the higher layer generally governs unless a lower layer is intentionally more specific and stricter;
- where a policy and a procedure conflict, the policy governs and the procedure must be corrected;
- where two policies conflict, the more recent or the higher-authority one governs, and the conflict must be logged for harmonization;
- local law that is stricter than a global policy controls in that jurisdiction.

Document the precedence rule in a governance charter or the code itself, so that managers and auditors can resolve disputes without escalating every time. Without a stated rule, conflicts fester and are resolved by whoever shouts loudest.

### Cascade Values Downward Without Losing Meaning

Values are meant to inform lower layers, but cascading is not copying. A value such as "integrity" must be translated through principles into concrete rules, or it remains decorative.

Cascade deliberately:

- state the value;
- derive principles that interpret the value in behavioral terms;
- derive rules or policies that make the principle enforceable;
- derive procedures and standards that make the rule operational;
- derive training and controls that test the rule is followed.

At each step, check that the lower layer is faithful to the higher one and that the higher one is not being silently reinterpreted. If a procedure contradicts the value it descends from, either the procedure is wrong or the value was never genuinely held.

### Control The Number Of Layers And Documents

Every layer added increases coordination cost, drift risk, and confusion. A six-layer hierarchy that no one understands is worse than a three-layer one that everyone uses.

Before adding a layer or document type, ask:

- does an existing layer already serve this purpose;
- will the new layer be maintained, or will it drift;
- will employees understand which document governs;
- is the cost of coordination worth the precision gained.

Prefer fewer, well-owned documents over many overlapping ones. Consolidate where a single policy can replace several fragments.

### Make Authority And Ownership Explicit At Every Layer

Each document must have a named owner, an approval authority, and a maintenance owner. Documents without owners drift, contradict each other, and become unenforceable.

Record for each document:

- the owner accountable for content;
- the approval authority that must sign changes;
- the review cycle;
- the change trigger;
- the relationship to documents above and below it.

A governance map or register that shows these relationships is what allows conflict resolution and audit. Without it, the hierarchy is invisible and therefore unmanageable.

### Plan For Change Propagation Across Layers

When law or risk changes, multiple layers may need updating. A change to a regulation may require changes to a policy, a procedure, a standard, training, and a control. If change propagation is not planned, some layers update and others do not, creating internal inconsistency.

For each significant change:

- identify all layers and documents affected;
- assign owners and dates for each update;
- check that updated documents still align with the layers above and below;
- re-issue, re-distribute, and re-acknowledge as needed;
- retire or supersede documents that no longer apply.

Change propagation is where most hierarchies break. Treat it as a process, not an afterthought.

## Common Traps

### Treating Values As Enforceable Rules

Values inspire but cannot be enforced individually. Disciplining someone for "violating a value" without a corresponding rule is legally and culturally fragile.

### Burying Enforceable Rules In Procedures

If the only place a mandatory rule appears is a procedure that changes often, the rule is effectively unstable and hard to defend. Move enforceable content up to the policy or code layer.

### No Stated Precedence Rule

Without a documented precedence rule, conflicts are resolved ad hoc, inconsistently, and often by seniority rather than logic.

### Too Many Layers

Adding layers for granularity creates a system no one can navigate. Employees default to ignoring the hierarchy and acting on the last email they received.

### Letting Lower Layers Silently Override Higher Ones

A procedure that quietly contradicts a policy, often because the procedure was updated and the policy was not, inverts the intended authority and creates audit findings.

### Copying A Framework's Layers Without Mapping To Your Documents

Adopting a standard's taxonomy without mapping it to your existing documents produces two parallel systems that no one reconciles.

### Ignoring Local Law In The Precedence Stack

A global hierarchy that does not explicitly defer to stricter local law will conflict with mandatory obligations and create violations.

### No Governance Register

If there is no map of which documents exist, who owns them, and how they relate, the hierarchy cannot be maintained or audited.

## Self-Check

- Does each layer have a distinct purpose, owner, approval authority, review cycle, and change velocity?
- Is each expectation assigned to the layer that matches its enforceability, specificity, and stability?
- Is there a documented precedence rule covering law, code, policy, procedure, and standard conflicts?
- Do values cascade downward through principles to rules to procedures without losing or silently changing meaning?
- Has the number of layers and documents been kept to what employees can actually navigate?
- Does every document have a named owner, approval authority, and relationship to documents above and below?
- Is change propagation across layers planned as a process, with affected documents identified and updated together?
- Are local-law deferrals explicitly built into the precedence stack?
- Is there a governance register or map showing the relationships among documents?
- Are enforceable rules kept out of volatile procedure layers, and aspirational content kept out of enforceable layers?
