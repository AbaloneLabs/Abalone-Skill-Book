# Conflict Of Interest Policy And Disclosure

A conflict of interest exists when a person's private interest could improperly influence, or appear to influence, their duties to the organization. Conflicts are not inherently misconduct; they are conditions that must be disclosed, assessed, and managed. The failure mode of a COI program is treating disclosure as the endpoint: collecting forms once a year, filing them, and assuming the absence of reported conflicts means there are none. A real COI control detects conflicts, evaluates their severity, applies management measures, records waivers, and re-checks when circumstances change. The hardest judgment is deciding when a disclosed conflict is manageable with controls versus when it must be eliminated.

Use this skill before drafting or revising a COI policy, designing disclosure forms and cycles, building waiver and management processes, or evaluating whether COI controls are effective. The goal is to make the agent think about who must disclose, what triggers disclosure, how conflicts are assessed and managed, and what evidence the program generates.

## Core Rules

### Define Conflict Broadly And Give Recognizable Examples

Employees often fail to disclose conflicts not from dishonesty but because they do not recognize them. Define conflict to cover actual, potential, and perceived conflicts, since perceived conflicts can damage trust and create appearance problems even when no improper influence occurs.

Cover at least:

- financial interests in customers, suppliers, competitors, or partners;
- outside employment, directorships, and advisory roles;
- family and personal relationships with employees, candidates, suppliers, or customers;
- gifts and hospitality that create obligation;
- investments that could be affected by the employee's decisions;
- board or leadership roles in organizations that intersect with the employer;
- prior employment that creates ongoing loyalty or information advantage;
- personal relationships that affect hiring, supervision, or procurement decisions.

Use concrete examples that employees will recognize. Abstract definitions such as "any competing interest" are technically correct but operationally useless. Pair each category with a recognizable scenario.

### Decide Who Must Disclose And On What Trigger

Not everyone faces the same conflict risk. A blanket annual disclosure for all employees may produce noise and dilute attention, while a narrow disclosure only for executives may miss procurement, hiring, and sales conflicts.

Segment disclosure by risk:

- senior leaders, finance, procurement, sales, hiring managers, and compliance-sensitive roles should disclose on a defined cycle and on event triggers;
- all employees should disclose specific high-risk conflicts, such as a family relationship with a candidate or supplier, when they arise;
- board members should disclose at appointment, annually, and at the start of relevant agenda items;
- agents, distributors, and joint venture partners should disclose ownership and relationships that could create conflicts.

Triggers for disclosure include new outside employment, new investments, new family relationships affecting work, changes in role or responsibilities, and new procurement or hiring involvement. Do not rely on annual disclosure alone; most conflicts arise mid-cycle.

### Design The Disclosure Form To Elicit, Not To Lead

A disclosure form shapes what people report. Forms that ask only narrow yes-or-no questions invite negative answers. Forms that ask open, specific questions elicit more useful information.

Design forms to:

- ask specific scenarios rather than abstract questions;
- request the nature of the relationship or interest, the parties involved, the dollar scope, the duration, and the work intersection;
- separate actual, potential, and perceived conflicts;
- ask what management measure the discloser suggests;
- require acknowledgment of the duty to update when circumstances change;
- avoid language that implies disclosure is an admission of wrongdoing.

A form that reads as an accusation will suppress disclosure. A form that reads as a routine business hygiene check will surface more real conflicts.

### Assess Severity And Apply The Right Management Measure

Not every conflict requires elimination. The correct response depends on the severity, the role, the ability to mitigate, and the risk of improper influence.

Assess:

- the financial or decision-making stakes involved;
- the role and authority of the person;
- whether the conflict can be managed by recusal, reassignment, supervision, or disclosure to affected parties;
- whether the conflict creates a regulatory or legal issue beyond policy;
- whether the conflict is one-time or ongoing.

Management measures range from recording and monitoring, to recusal from specific decisions, to reassignment of duties, to divestiture, to termination of the conflicting relationship, to termination of employment in severe cases. Match the measure to the severity and document the rationale. Over-managing minor conflicts erodes trust; under-managing serious ones creates liability.

### Govern Waivers With Authority, Conditions, And Expiry

Some conflicts cannot be fully eliminated but can be waived with conditions. A waiver is not an exemption from the policy; it is a documented decision to permit a conflict subject to controls, made by someone with authority.

Waiver governance should specify:

- who has authority to grant waivers, with higher authority required for senior leaders and board members;
- the conditions and compensating controls attached to the waiver;
- the duration or expiry of the waiver;
- the review trigger if circumstances change;
- the recording and reporting of waivers, including to the board or audit committee for executive waivers;
- the standard for denial, not just approval.

Permanent, unconditional waivers are usually control gaps dressed as approvals. A waiver that never expires and imposes no conditions is functionally an exemption and should be treated with suspicion.

### Build Detection Beyond Self-Disclosure

Self-disclosure will always under-detect. A credible COI program corroborates disclosure with independent detection.

Detection mechanisms include:

- periodic re-attestation with consequences for nondisclosure;
- screening of employees against supplier and customer ownership lists;
- review of hiring decisions for family relationships;
- review of procurement awards for relationships with bidders;
- monitoring of expense and gift records for patterns;
- review of outside employment approvals against time and resource use;
- hotline and exit interview themes;
- data analytics matching employee, vendor, and customer records.

Detection does not replace disclosure; it tests whether disclosure is working. If detection routinely finds undisclosed conflicts, the disclosure process needs redesign, not just enforcement.

### Maintain Records And Evidence Of Management

A COI program must be able to show what was disclosed, who assessed it, what management was applied, and what waivers exist. This evidence is what demonstrates the program operates, not merely exists.

Maintain:

- disclosure records by person and period;
- assessment decisions and rationale;
- management measures applied and their review dates;
- waiver register with authority, conditions, and expiry;
- detection results and follow-up actions;
- board or committee reporting on executive conflicts and waivers.

In an enforcement or audit context, the absence of records is treated as the absence of management. Records are the proof.

## Common Traps

### Treating Annual Disclosure As The Whole Program

Annual disclosure catches only conflicts that exist on the form date and that the employee chooses to report. Most conflicts arise and resolve between cycles.

### Defining Conflict Too Narrowly

A definition limited to direct financial interest misses family relationships, outside roles, and perceived conflicts that cause the most reputational damage.

### Collecting Forms Without Assessing Them

A drawer of disclosure forms that no one reviews is not a control. Unassessed disclosures can be worse than none, because they imply the conflict was considered.

### Waivers With No Conditions Or Expiry

A blanket, permanent waiver is an exemption. It removes the conflict from management and creates a hidden control gap.

### Ignoring Procurement And Hiring Conflicts

The highest-frequency conflicts are often in procurement awards and hiring decisions, where relationships and incentives are strongest and oversight is weakest.

### Over-Managing Trivial Conflicts

Requiring recusal or divestiture for minor, immaterial conflicts erodes trust and encourages concealment of more serious ones.

### Failing To Detect Undisclosed Conflicts

If the only mechanism is self-report, the program will miss conflicts and will not know how many. Detection is what makes disclosure credible.

### No Records Of Management Decisions

Without records of assessment, management measures, and waivers, the program cannot demonstrate it operated and cannot defend decisions later.

## Self-Check

- Does the definition cover actual, potential, and perceived conflicts, with recognizable examples across financial, relationship, outside-role, and gift categories?
- Is disclosure segmented by role risk and triggered by events, not only by an annual cycle?
- Does the disclosure form elicit specific information rather than leading employees to answer no?
- Is each disclosed conflict assessed for severity and matched to a proportionate management measure?
- Are waivers granted only by defined authority, with conditions, expiry, review triggers, and recording, including board reporting for executives?
- Are there independent detection mechanisms that test whether disclosure is working, such as vendor and hiring cross-checks?
- Are conflicts in procurement, hiring, and sales specifically addressed, given their frequency and severity?
- Are management measures neither over-applied to trivial conflicts nor under-applied to serious ones?
- Are records of disclosure, assessment, management, waivers, and detection maintained as evidence the program operates?
- Are employees reminded of the duty to update disclosure when circumstances change, not only at annual cycle?
