# Gifts And Entertainment Policy

Gifts, meals, travel, entertainment, and gratuities are areas where normal business hospitality shades into improper influence and, at the extreme, into bribery. The difficulty is that the same act, buying a client dinner, can be legitimate relationship-building or an attempt to obtain favorable treatment, depending on intent, value, frequency, the recipient's role, and whether a decision is pending. A gifts policy that simply bans everything is easy to enforce but ignored in practice; one that allows everything is an open channel for corruption. The judgment is setting thresholds, approval triggers, and recording rules that separate acceptable hospitality from influence that creates obligation, while staying usable enough that employees actually follow it.

Use this skill before drafting or revising a gifts and entertainment policy, setting thresholds, designing the approval and registry workflow, or reviewing whether the policy closes the bribery nexus. The goal is to make the agent think about value, intent, frequency, the recipient's decision-making role, government officials, and the evidence the policy must generate.

## Core Rules

### Start From The Improper-Influence Risk, Not From Hospitality Norms

The purpose of a gifts policy is not to regulate courtesy; it is to prevent gifts from becoming a vehicle for improper influence or bribery. Begin from the risk: a gift creates obligation, and obligation can affect a business decision. Everything in the policy should be traceable to that risk.

Identify the risk surfaces:

- gifts to or from customers, suppliers, prospects, and regulators;
- meals, entertainment, travel, lodging, and event tickets;
- gratuities and tips to service providers or officials;
- charitable contributions and sponsorships that benefit a decision-maker;
- discounts and personal benefits not available to the public;
- gifts during active bidding, negotiation, or regulatory review;
- gifts to government officials, which carry far higher legal risk.

The policy must be stricter where the influence risk is higher, not uniform across all situations.

### Set Thresholds That Are Usable And Defensible

Thresholds are the operational core of the policy. They must be low enough to catch influence and high enough that routine hospitality does not require approval, or employees will ignore the policy.

Set thresholds for:

- a de-minimis value below which recording or approval is not required;
- a value above which pre-approval is required;
- a value above which the gift is prohibited regardless of approval;
- an aggregate annual limit per recipient relationship, to catch death-by-a-thousand-cuts patterns;
- separate, lower thresholds for government officials.

State whether value includes tax, shipping, and per-person or per-event totals, and define the currency and conversion method for global operations. Ambiguous thresholds produce inconsistent application and audit findings. Review thresholds against inflation and against enforcement expectations periodically.

### Treat Government Officials As A Separate, Stricter Category

Gifts, meals, travel, and entertainment involving government officials carry legal risk that private-sector hospitality does not. Anti-bribery laws such as the FCPA and UKBA reach modest benefits provided to officials, and many jurisdictions have specific gift and hospitality rules for officials.

For government officials:

- apply a presumption against any gift, meal, or benefit unless explicitly permitted;
- require senior compliance or legal approval, often in advance;
- consider whether the benefit is lawful under local law and the official's own rules;
- record the business purpose, the official's role, the value, and the approval;
- recognize that even permissible ceremonial gifts can create appearance issues;
- coordinate with government contracting rules, which often impose their own restrictions.

Do not apply private-sector thresholds to officials. The legal exposure is different in kind, not only in degree.

### Define Approval Authority And The Decision Standard

When a gift or entertainment requires approval, the approver must know what standard to apply. "Use judgment" is not a standard.

Specify:

- who can approve at each value tier;
- what the approver must consider: business purpose, relationship, pending decisions, frequency, recipient role, and local law;
- whether approval is pre-event or post-event, and the default when approval is not obtained in time;
- what evidence of approval is recorded;
- the escalation path when the approver is unsure or conflicted.

The decision standard should ask whether a reasonable observer would see the benefit as legitimate business hospitality or as an attempt to influence. A pending bid, contract renewal, or regulatory matter should weigh heavily against approval.

### Require A Registry And Aggregate Monitoring

Individual approvals do not reveal patterns. A registry that captures all reportable gifts and entertainment, by giver and recipient, enables aggregate review that single transactions cannot.

The registry should record:

- date, giver, recipient, and recipient organization;
- description and business purpose;
- value and how it was determined;
- approval and approver;
- whether a decision was pending;
- the cost center or project charged.

Monitor the registry for patterns: repeated gifts to the same recipient, clusters around bidding periods, entertainment concentrated on a few decision-makers, and approvers approving their own team's requests. Aggregate monitoring is what distinguishes a policy that works from one that merely exists.

### Address Frequency And Cumulative Effect

A single modest gift may be permissible; the same gift weekly creates obligation. Thresholds that look only at single events miss cumulative influence.

Build in:

- annual aggregate limits per recipient relationship;
- review of frequent hospitality to the same parties;
- attention to patterns that correlate with contract renewals or awards;
- flags for entertainment that recurs around regulatory or sales milestones.

Cumulative limits are harder to administer but are essential to closing the influence channel that single-event thresholds leave open.

### Cover The Full Spectrum Of Benefits

A policy limited to physical gifts misses the channels through which influence actually flows. Cover the full range:

- meals and beverages;
- entertainment, sports, and event tickets;
- travel, lodging, and transportation;
- discounts and rebates not available to the public;
- loans and personal favors;
- charitable contributions directed by a decision-maker;
- internships, jobs, or offers to family members;
- sponsorships and association dues paid on behalf of a recipient.

The bribery nexus moves to whatever channel is unregulated. A policy that closes only the gift channel leaves the others open.

### Connect To Anti-Bribery Law And Training

A gifts policy is a frontline anti-bribery control. It must be consistent with the organization's anti-bribery policy and with the laws that apply, and employees must understand the connection.

Ensure:

- the policy references and does not contradict the anti-bribery policy;
- training explains why thresholds exist and how to recognize influence risk;
- high-risk roles, such as sales, procurement, and government-facing teams, receive targeted guidance;
- the reporting path for suspect offers is clear;
- violations are treated consistently, including for high performers.

A gifts policy that employees experience as bureaucracy rather than corruption prevention will be circumvented exactly where the risk is highest.

## Common Traps

### Setting Thresholds So Low Everything Needs Approval

If a modest lunch requires approval, employees will stop reporting rather than stop lunching. The policy becomes noise and is ignored.

### Setting Thresholds So High Nothing Is Caught

Thresholds set to avoid inconvenience will let significant influence flow unrecorded, especially in bidding and regulatory contexts.

### Applying Private-Sector Rules To Government Officials

Private thresholds applied to officials create direct anti-bribery exposure. Officials require a separate, stricter regime.

### Ignoring Cumulative Frequency

Single-event thresholds miss the death-by-a-thousand-cuts pattern that is the most common influence technique.

### Approving Without A Decision Standard

Approvers who rubber-stamp requests provide no control. The approval must apply a stated standard, not just sign.

### Missing The Non-Gift Channels

Loans, jobs for family, sponsorships, and discounts are common influence channels that a gift-only policy leaves open.

### No Aggregate Monitoring

Without registry review, patterns that individual approvals miss go undetected, and the policy provides no detection value.

### Inconsistent Enforcement For Producers

If top salespeople are allowed to exceed thresholds, the policy loses credibility and the highest-risk conduct goes uncontrolled.

## Self-Check

- Is the policy designed around the improper-influence risk, with stricter rules where decision-making, bidding, or government involvement raises that risk?
- Are thresholds set at usable levels, with de-minimis, approval, and prohibition tiers, plus aggregate annual limits?
- Is there a separate, stricter category for government officials that presumes against benefits unless lawfully permitted and approved?
- Does each approval tier have a named authority and a stated decision standard, not just sign-off?
- Is there a registry that captures all reportable hospitality and is monitored for patterns, frequency, and correlation with decisions?
- Are cumulative frequency and aggregate effect addressed, not only single-event value?
- Does the policy cover the full spectrum of benefits, including meals, travel, entertainment, discounts, loans, jobs, and sponsorships?
- Is the policy consistent with and connected to the anti-bribery policy and training?
- Are high-risk roles given targeted guidance, and is the reporting path for suspect offers clear?
- Is enforcement consistent, including for high performers and senior people?
