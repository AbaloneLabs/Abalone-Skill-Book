# Compliance Key Risk And Performance Indicators

Key risk indicators (KRIs) and key performance indicators (KPIs) are meant to give leadership an early, reliable view of whether compliance risk is rising and whether the compliance program is working. In practice, most compliance metrics are activity counts: number of trainings delivered, number of policies published, number of hotline calls. These measure effort, not effectiveness, and they can paint a reassuring picture while real risk goes unmeasured. A metric that does not change when risk changes is not an indicator; it is decoration. Designing KRIs and KPIs that actually reflect risk and performance requires tying each metric to a defined risk or objective, setting thresholds that trigger action, and resisting the temptation to report what is easy to count.

Use this skill before selecting metrics, setting thresholds, building a metrics framework, evaluating whether existing indicators are meaningful, or reporting trends to leadership. The goal is to make the agent treat each indicator as a hypothesis about risk or performance that must be validated, thresholded, and interpreted in context.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Risk Indicators From Performance Indicators And From Activity Counts

These three categories are often conflated, and the confusion undermines reporting. Each serves a different purpose.

- A key risk indicator measures exposure: it should rise when risk rises and fall when risk falls. Examples include percentage of transactions exceeding thresholds, number of policy exceptions, overdue remediation, control test failure rates, and concentration of high-risk third parties.
- A key performance indicator measures whether the program is doing its job effectively: it should reflect outcomes and quality, not only volume. Examples include time to close investigations, substantiation rate of allegations, training effectiveness scores, and percentage of issues remediated on time.
- An activity count measures effort: number of trainings, meetings, policies, reviews. Activity counts have a place as context, but they are neither risk nor performance indicators and should not be presented as evidence of effectiveness.

Label each metric by type. Reporting an activity count as a KPI creates false assurance. A program can deliver more trainings and have worse outcomes.

### Tie Every Indicator To A Specific Risk Or Objective

An indicator without a defined risk or objective is a number in search of a purpose. For each metric, state the risk it is meant to surface or the objective it is meant to measure.

For each indicator document:

- the risk or objective it addresses;
- the direction of concern, such as rising value meaning increasing risk;
- the data source and calculation;
- the population and time window;
- the owner accountable for the metric;
- the audience and reporting cadence;
- the action that an out-of-threshold result should trigger.

If the action triggered by a breach is unclear, the indicator is incomplete. A metric that nobody acts on is not worth reporting.

### Choose Leading Indicators Over Lagging Ones Where Possible

Lagging indicators measure harm that has already occurred, such as enforcement actions, confirmed breaches, or losses. They confirm risk but do not provide early warning. Leading indicators measure conditions that precede harm, such as rising exceptions, declining training completion in a high-risk unit, increasing access conflicts, or growing overdue remediation.

Favor a mix weighted toward leading indicators for risk management, because the value of a KRI is early action. Use lagging indicators to validate that the leading ones are calibrated. If a leading indicator rose and a lagging event followed, the leading indicator was meaningful; if not, recalibrate.

### Set Thresholds That Trigger Action, Not Just Colors

A threshold that only turns a cell red does nothing. Thresholds should define when action is required, who acts, and what they do.

For each threshold define:

- the green, amber, and red bands, or equivalent, with explicit values;
- the basis for the thresholds, tied to risk tolerance, historical baseline, peer benchmark, or regulatory expectation;
- the action required at each level, such as investigate, escalate, remediate, or report;
- the owner of the action and the timeframe;
- the escalation path if action does not occur.

Avoid thresholds set by rounding or gut feel. A threshold of "10 percent" with no basis will be challenged. Document the rationale and revisit thresholds as risk and context change.

### Validate That The Indicator Actually Moves With Risk

A metric that does not change when the underlying risk changes provides false comfort. Validate indicators against known events.

Validate by:

- back-testing against historical incidents to see whether the indicator moved before or during the event;
- comparing across business units with different risk profiles to see whether the indicator differentiates;
- checking whether the indicator responds to control changes, such as improved training or tighter approvals;
- identifying whether the indicator is artificially flat due to data issues, narrow definition, or manipulation.

An indicator that is suspiciously stable may reflect a broken metric rather than stable risk. Investigate stability before trusting it.

### Avoid Vanity Metrics And Gaming-Prone Measures

Some metrics are chosen because they look good rather than because they inform. Others invite gaming because they are tied to incentives or scrutiny.

Watch for:

- completion rates that measure clicking through training, not comprehension;
- policy attestation rates that measure signing, not following;
- hotline call volumes interpreted as good or bad without context;
- metrics defined so loosely that they cannot fail;
- metrics that shift behavior toward hitting the number rather than reducing risk, such as closing investigations quickly by classifying them as unsubstantiated.

Where a metric is tied to performance evaluation or scrutiny, design it to resist gaming: combine rate with quality, pair speed with outcome, and audit samples of the underlying activity.

### Interpret Trends In Context, Not In Isolation

A rising or falling metric can mean many things. Interpretation requires context.

Consider:

- changes in the underlying population, such as a new product or market expanding volume;
- changes in detection sensitivity, such as a new rule generating more alerts;
- changes in data quality or source systems;
- seasonality or one-time events;
- organizational changes affecting reporting boundaries;
- whether the trend reflects real risk change or measurement change.

Present trends with the context needed to interpret them. A chart without context invites misreading. When a metric improves, ask whether risk actually fell or whether the measurement changed.

### Aggregate And Report At The Right Level For Each Audience

Different audiences need different aggregation. The board needs a small number of strategic indicators tied to top risks; the compliance team needs operational detail; business owners need actionable indicators for their area.

Design reporting so that:

- each level rolls up from consistent underlying data;
- indicators are comparable across periods and units;
- the board view focuses on trends and thresholds, not raw counts;
- operational views enable drill-down to drivers;
- significant threshold breaches are highlighted with action taken or planned.

Inconsistent definitions across reports create confusion. Maintain a single source of truth for each metric and its calculation.

## Common Traps

### Reporting Activity Counts As Performance Indicators

Volume of activity is not effectiveness. Distinguish effort from outcome.

### Thresholds That Only Change Color

A threshold without a defined action and owner does not manage risk. Tie each band to a response.

### Indicators That Never Move

A suspiciously stable metric may be broken or manipulated. Validate responsiveness to risk.

### Interpreting Hotline Volume As Good Or Bad Without Context

Rising calls may mean growing trust or growing problems. Interpret with substantiation rates and trends.

### Gaming-Prone Metrics Tied To Incentives

Measures tied to scrutiny invite behavior aimed at the number, not the risk. Pair rate with quality and audit samples.

### Presenting Trends Without Context

A rising metric may reflect population, detection, or data changes rather than risk. Provide context for interpretation.

### Defining Metrics So Loosely They Cannot Fail

A metric that always looks good provides no signal. Design indicators that can reveal problems.

## Self-Check

- Is each metric classified as a key risk indicator, key performance indicator, or activity count, and are activity counts presented as context rather than effectiveness evidence?
- Is every indicator tied to a specific risk or objective, with direction of concern, data source, calculation, population, owner, audience, cadence, and triggered action documented?
- Does the indicator set favor leading indicators that provide early warning, validated by lagging indicators?
- Are thresholds defined with explicit bands, documented basis, required actions, owners, timeframes, and escalation paths rather than only colors?
- Has each indicator been validated to confirm it moves with risk, differentiates across risk profiles, responds to control changes, and is not artificially flat?
- Are vanity and gaming-prone metrics identified and redesigned to combine rate with quality, speed with outcome, and sampled audit?
- Are trends interpreted with context including population, detection sensitivity, data quality, seasonality, organization, and measurement changes?
- Is reporting aggregated appropriately for each audience, rolling up from consistent underlying data with a single source of truth?
- Are threshold breaches highlighted with action taken or planned, and are indicators that cannot fail redesigned?
- Is the metric set periodically reviewed to retire indicators that no longer inform and add indicators for emerging risks?
