# Policy Conflict Resolution And Harmonization

As policy libraries grow, contradictions accumulate. Two policies define the same term differently, set different thresholds for the same risk, assign the same responsibility to different owners, or state incompatible rules. These conflicts are rarely intentional; they emerge as different authors write policies at different times for different audiences without a coordinating view. The cost is real: employees and managers cannot determine which rule governs, approvals become inconsistent, auditors issue findings, and enforcement defenses weaken because the organization cannot show a coherent standard. Conflict resolution and harmonization are the disciplines that turn a pile of policies into a coherent governance system with a single source of truth. The judgment is detecting conflicts, deciding which source governs, reconciling definitions and thresholds, and preventing the conflicts from recurring.

Use this skill before reconciling conflicting policies, harmonizing a library, establishing precedence, or building a single source of truth. The goal is to make the agent think about conflict detection, precedence, harmonization of terms and thresholds, ownership, and the structural changes that prevent recurrence.

## Core Rules

### Detect Conflicts Systematically, Not By Accident

Conflicts are usually discovered during an audit, an incident, or a manager's question, by which point they have already caused inconsistency. Detect them proactively through systematic review.

Detect conflicts by:

- maintaining a policy map or register that shows relationships and overlaps;
- reviewing new and changed policies against existing ones before approval;
- running periodic harmonization reviews of the whole library;
- cross-checking definitions, thresholds, roles, and approval authorities across policies;
- tracking audit findings, hotline themes, and manager questions for conflict signals;
- checking that local policies and addenda align with global policies.

A conflict found in review is cheap to fix. A conflict found in an enforcement action is expensive. Build detection into the policy lifecycle rather than relying on chance.

### Establish A Precedence Rule Before You Need It

When two policies conflict, someone must decide which governs. Deciding ad hoc, case by case, produces inconsistent resolutions and disputes. Establish a precedence rule in advance and document it.

A workable precedence stack:

- law and regulation supersede internal policy where they conflict;
- within internal documents, the higher governance layer generally governs, so a code over a policy over a procedure;
- a policy intentionally more specific and stricter than a higher layer can govern, if the relationship is documented;
- the more recent policy governs where two peer policies conflict, and the conflict is logged for harmonization;
- local law that is stricter than a global policy controls in that jurisdiction.

Document the precedence rule in a governance charter or the code itself, so managers and auditors can resolve disputes without escalation. Without a stated rule, conflicts are resolved by whoever has the loudest voice.

### Reconcile Definitions Across The Library

Definitions are the most common source of silent conflict. If "gift," "government official," "conflict," or "related party" means different things in different policies, employees cannot apply them consistently, and the organization cannot defend a uniform standard.

Reconcile definitions by:

- maintaining a controlled glossary of terms used across policies;
- using the same definition for the same concept in every policy that uses it;
- where a policy needs a specialized definition, documenting the variation and the reason;
- reviewing definitions whenever a policy is created or revised;
- ensuring translations preserve the reconciled meaning.

A controlled glossary is the single source of truth for meaning. Without it, each author reinvents definitions and contradictions accumulate.

### Harmonize Thresholds, Roles, And Approval Authorities

Beyond definitions, conflicts often hide in thresholds, role assignments, and approval authorities. One policy sets a gift threshold, another sets a different one; one assigns approval to compliance, another to the business.

Harmonize:

- monetary and quantitative thresholds for the same risk across policies;
- role names and the responsibilities attached to them;
- approval authorities, so the same decision is not assigned to different approvers;
- escalation paths and reporting channels;
- effective dates and review cycles for related policies.

Where harmonization is not possible because different contexts genuinely require different thresholds, document the variation explicitly and the reason for it. Undocumented variation reads as contradiction.

### Designate A Single Source Of Truth For Each Topic

For each topic or risk, there should be one authoritative policy that other documents reference, not duplicate. Multiple policies stating the same rule independently guarantee drift.

Establish a single source of truth by:

- designating the lead policy for each topic;
- having related policies cross-reference rather than restate the rule;
- centralizing volatile detail, such as thresholds, in standards or procedures that the lead policy points to;
- recording the lead policy in the register so authors know where to point.

When the rule lives in one place and is referenced elsewhere, a change updates everywhere. When the rule is copied, a change updates some copies and not others, and conflict returns.

### Assign Ownership For Harmonization

Harmonization is ongoing work, not a one-time project, and it needs an owner. Without ownership, conflicts recur as new policies are written in isolation.

Assign:

- a policy program owner responsible for the coherence of the library;
- topic owners responsible for the lead policy in each area;
- a review step in the approval workflow that checks new policies for conflict;
- a periodic harmonization review with documented decisions.

The program owner is the guardian of the single source of truth. Topic owners keep their area internally consistent and aligned with the rest of the library.

### Resolve Conflicts With A Documented Decision

When a conflict is found, the resolution must be documented so the same conflict does not re-emerge and so the resolution is defensible.

Document:

- the policies in conflict and the nature of the conflict;
- the precedence rule or rationale applied;
- the decision on which governs and what changes to make;
- the actions taken, such as revising, retiring, or cross-referencing;
- the owner and date of the resolution.

An undocumented resolution is a temporary fix that the next policy revision can undo. Documented resolutions build a record that stabilizes the library over time.

### Prevent Recurrence Through Governance

Conflict resolution treats symptoms; governance changes prevent recurrence. Build conflict prevention into how policies are created and maintained.

Prevent recurrence by:

- requiring a conflict check before a new policy is approved;
- using the controlled glossary and lead-policy designations as inputs to drafting;
- coordinating policy changes across affected areas;
- training policy authors on the precedence rule and the single-source principle;
- reviewing the library periodically for drift.

A library that is governed this way stays coherent. One that is not will re-accumulate conflicts as fast as they are resolved.

## Common Traps

### Discovering Conflicts Only In Audits

Relying on audits or incidents to surface conflicts means the conflict has already caused inconsistency and exposure. Detect proactively.

### No Stated Precedence Rule

Without a documented precedence rule, conflicts are resolved ad hoc and inconsistently, often by seniority rather than logic.

### Divergent Definitions

The same term defined differently across policies makes consistent application impossible and undermines the uniform standard defense.

### Copying Rules Instead Of Cross-Referencing

When the same rule is restated in multiple policies, changes update some copies and not others, and conflict returns.

### No Single Source Of Truth

Without a designated lead policy for each topic, authority is diffuse and contradictions accumulate.

### Harmonization As A One-Time Project

A one-time cleanup that is not sustained re-accumulates conflicts as new policies are written in isolation.

### Undocumented Resolutions

A resolution that is not recorded can be undone by the next revision, and the conflict re-emerges.

### Ignoring Local Versus Global Tension

Failing to reconcile global policies with stricter local requirements creates conflicts that local law will win anyway, but only after confusion and exposure.

## Self-Check

- Is there a systematic process to detect conflicts through a policy map, pre-approval review, and periodic harmonization, rather than relying on audits?
- Is there a documented precedence rule covering law, code, policy, procedure, peer conflicts, and local law?
- Is there a controlled glossary ensuring the same term is defined consistently across the library?
- Are thresholds, roles, approval authorities, and escalation paths harmonized across policies, with variations documented?
- Is there a designated single source of truth, a lead policy, for each topic, with related policies cross-referencing rather than duplicating?
- Is there a policy program owner and topic owners responsible for ongoing coherence?
- Is each conflict resolution documented with the policies, rationale, decision, actions, owner, and date?
- Are conflict prevention steps built into the approval workflow, glossary use, and author training?
- Is harmonization sustained as ongoing governance rather than a one-time project?
- Are global policies reconciled with stricter local requirements so conflicts are resolved deliberately, not by default?
