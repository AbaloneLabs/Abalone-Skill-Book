# Policy Drafting And Plain Language

A compliance policy is an instruction to people who must act on it, not a legal brief written for a regulator. The central drafting problem is that a policy must be precise enough to be enforceable and defensible, yet readable enough that a front-line employee can understand what to do, what to avoid, and when to ask. These pressures pull in opposite directions: precision invites legalism, and readability invites vagueness. The failure mode is a policy written by lawyers for lawyers, full of defined terms, cross-references, and conditional clauses, that no employee can apply and that therefore changes no behavior. Plain language is not dumbing down; it is making the policy operational.

Use this skill before drafting or revising a policy, choosing its structure, writing definitions, or reviewing readability. The goal is to make the agent think about audience, structure, definitions, tone, ambiguity, and the test of whether a reasonable reader could apply the policy to a real decision.

## Core Rules

### Draft For The Reader Who Must Act, Not The Author Who Must Defend

The primary reader is the employee, contractor, or manager who must decide what to do. The secondary reader is the auditor or regulator who must assess the policy. Write for the primary reader first. A policy that the primary reader cannot apply will fail in practice, and a policy that fails in practice cannot be defended regardless of its legal precision.

Write so that a person in the affected role, reading the relevant section for the first time, can answer:

- does this apply to me;
- what must I do;
- what must I not do;
- when must I get approval and from whom;
- when must I escalate or report;
- what records must I keep;
- where do I go with questions.

If a reader cannot answer these after a careful read, the policy is not plain enough.

### Structure The Policy So The Reader Can Navigate

People do not read policies cover to cover. They search for the section that applies to their situation. Structure must support that behavior.

A workable structure:

- purpose: what risk the policy addresses and why it exists;
- scope: who and what the policy covers, and what it does not;
- definitions: only the terms that need defining, defined plainly;
- policy statement: the core requirements and prohibitions;
- responsibilities: who does what, by role;
- procedures and approval: how to comply, including approvals and thresholds;
- exceptions: how to request an exception;
- enforcement: consequences of violation;
- related documents and contacts: where to get help.

Put the most important rules early. Do not bury the prohibition or the approval requirement on page four. Use headings, short sections, and a logical flow from "does this apply to me" to "what do I do" to "what happens if I do not."

### Use Plain Language And Active Voice

Plain language is a drafting discipline, not a tone preference. It reduces ambiguity, improves application, and strengthens enforceability.

Apply:

- active voice with a clear actor, not passive constructions that hide who must act;
- short sentences, generally under 25 words, with one idea each;
- common words instead of legal jargon, unless a term of art is necessary and defined;
- bullets and tables for lists, thresholds, and comparisons;
- concrete examples for ambiguous rules;
- "must" for obligations, "should" for strong guidance, "may" for permissions, used consistently;
- avoidance of double negatives and stacked conditions.

The goal is not to remove precision. It is to make the precision visible. A plainly written rule is easier to enforce precisely than a legalistic one that no one interprets the same way.

### Define Only What Needs Defining, And Define It Plainly

Definitions are necessary where a term has a specialized meaning or where ambiguity would cause inconsistent application. Over-defining common words makes a policy harder to read; under-defining critical terms makes it unenforceable.

Define:

- terms with a specialized compliance or legal meaning;
- terms where the policy's meaning differs from common usage;
- threshold concepts such as "gift," "government official," "conflict," or "related party";
- roles and authorities specific to the policy.

Define in plain language and, where useful, with an example. Avoid circular definitions, definitions that refer to other defined terms in a chain, and definitions that are longer than the rule they support. Place definitions where the reader encounters them, or in a clearly marked section.

### Make The Rule Testable And The Exception Visible

A rule that cannot be tested against conduct is not operational. For each requirement, a reader should be able to determine whether they are complying.

Make rules testable by:

- stating the required action or prohibited action concretely;
- specifying thresholds, deadlines, and approval authorities where they exist;
- identifying the evidence the rule generates, such as an approval record or a log entry;
- distinguishing mandatory from recommended conduct;
- providing examples of compliant and non-compliant conduct for gray areas.

Equally, make the exception path visible. Employees who hit a situation the rule does not fit need to know how to request an exception, who approves, and what happens meanwhile. A rule with no visible exception path invites silent non-compliance.

### Calibrate Detail To Risk And Audience

Not every policy needs the same depth. A high-risk area such as anti-bribery or data privacy warrants more detail than a low-risk administrative policy. Match the detail to the risk and to the audience's need.

Calibrate by:

- giving more detail where the conduct is high-risk, ambiguous, or frequently misunderstood;
- giving more detail where the audience is broad and includes non-specialists;
- keeping low-risk or specialist-audience policies shorter;
- separating a short, readable policy from detailed procedures and standards that specialists can use;
- avoiding the temptation to put every edge case in the main policy, which overwhelms the primary reader.

A policy that tries to cover every scenario becomes unreadable. Put the core rule in the policy and the edge cases in supporting procedures.

### Coordinate With Related Policies And Law

A policy does not exist in isolation. It must be consistent with related policies, with the code of conduct, and with applicable law, and it must say how it relates to them.

Coordinate by:

- cross-referencing related policies rather than duplicating their content;
- stating which document controls when there is overlap;
- ensuring thresholds and definitions are consistent across related policies;
- aligning with the code of conduct so the policy operationalizes rather than contradicts it;
- confirming the policy is consistent with applicable law and defers to stricter local law.

Inconsistency between policies is a common source of audit findings and employee confusion. A policy that contradicts a sibling policy undermines both.

### Plan For Readability Testing

A policy's readability is a claim that should be tested, not assumed. Before finalizing, test whether the intended audience can understand and apply it.

Test by:

- having a sample of the affected audience read the relevant section and explain what to do;
- checking reading level against the audience;
- reviewing whether common questions are answered;
- checking that the structure supports finding information quickly;
- verifying that defined terms are used consistently;
- confirming translations preserve meaning.

If test readers cannot apply the policy, revise it. A policy that passes legal review but fails a reader test is not ready.

## Common Traps

### Writing For Lawyers Instead Of Users

Legalistic drafting satisfies the author's defensibility instinct but fails the reader who must act. The result is a policy that exists but does not operate.

### Passive Voice That Hides The Actor

"Approval must be obtained" does not say who must obtain it or who grants it. Active voice makes responsibility visible.

### Burying The Key Rule

If the prohibition or approval requirement is buried in dense text, readers miss it. Put the operational rule where readers will find it.

### Over-Defining Common Words

Defining every ordinary word inflates the policy and signals that the author does not trust the reader. Define only what genuinely needs it.

### Stacking Conditions And Exceptions

Rules with multiple nested conditions and exceptions are impossible to apply consistently. Flatten them into a table or a decision path.

### Inconsistent Use Of Must, Should, And May

If "must," "should," and "may" are used interchangeably, readers cannot tell what is mandatory. Use them consistently and define their force.

### Duplicating Content Across Policies

Copying the same rule into multiple policies creates drift and inconsistency. Cross-reference a single source instead.

### No Readability Test

Assuming a policy is readable without testing it on the audience is a common cause of policies that look fine but fail in practice.

## Self-Check

- Is the policy written so the primary reader, in the affected role, can determine whether it applies and what to do?
- Does the structure move from scope to requirements to procedures to exceptions to enforcement in a navigable order?
- Is plain language used throughout, with active voice, short sentences, common words, and consistent use of must, should, and may?
- Are only the necessary terms defined, and defined plainly with examples where useful?
- Is each rule testable against conduct, with thresholds, deadlines, approvals, and evidence specified?
- Is the exception path visible, with who approves and what happens meanwhile?
- Is the detail calibrated to the risk and the audience, with edge cases moved to supporting procedures?
- Is the policy consistent with related policies, the code of conduct, and applicable law, with cross-references instead of duplication?
- Has the policy been tested for readability with a sample of the affected audience?
- Can a reader find the answer to a real decision within the policy without reading it cover to cover?
