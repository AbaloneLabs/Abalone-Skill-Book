# Policy Approval And Version Control

A policy's authority comes from who approved it, when it took effect, and which version is current. Without controlled approval and versioning, an organization ends up with multiple versions of the same policy in circulation, no one knows which one governs, approvals cannot be traced, and a regulator or auditor cannot establish what the policy required at a given time. Version control is not bookkeeping; it is what makes a policy a defensible control rather than a suggestion. The judgment is designing an approval workflow that matches the policy's risk and authority, maintaining a change history that tells the story of the policy, and ensuring superseded versions are retired without disappearing.

Use this skill before designing approval workflows, setting up version control, planning supersession, or reviewing whether policy authority and history are auditable. The goal is to make the agent think about approval authority, effective dates, change records, supersession, and the evidence chain a policy must maintain.

## Core Rules

### Match Approval Authority To The Policy's Risk And Reach

Not every policy should be approved at the same level. A policy that binds the whole organization or addresses high-risk conduct needs higher authority than a departmental procedure. Approval authority signals the policy's weight.

Assign approval authority by considering:

- the breadth of the population the policy binds;
- the risk the policy addresses;
- whether the policy operationalizes a legal or regulatory obligation;
- whether board, audit committee, or regulator expectations require specific approval;
- whether the policy affects executive conduct or financial reporting.

A code of conduct and high-risk policies such as anti-bribery typically require board or senior executive approval. Operational policies may be approved by a functional leader. Record the approving authority for each policy so the approval can be traced and so the authority matches the policy's weight.

### Design An Approval Workflow That Captures Real Review

Approval should mean the policy was reviewed and accepted, not merely signed. A workflow that routes a policy for a signature without review produces approvals without substance.

A credible workflow includes:

- drafting by the policy owner with subject-matter input;
- legal review for consistency, defensibility, and legal alignment;
- review by affected business units for operational feasibility;
- review by compliance, HR, security, or other relevant functions;
- approval by the designated authority;
- recording of the approver, date, and version approved;
- a check that related policies, training, and controls are updated in step.

Sequence the reviews so legal and business input happen before approval, not after. An approval that precedes substantive review is a formality, not a control.

### Maintain A Controlled Version History

Every policy must have a version history that records what changed, when, why, and by whose authority. This history is what allows the organization to establish what the policy required at any past date, which is essential in investigations, audits, and enforcement.

Each version record should capture:

- version number or identifier;
- effective date;
- approval authority and date;
- summary of changes from the prior version;
- reason for the change, such as legal update, risk event, or review cycle;
- the owner who drafted the change;
- the prior version it supersedes.

Keep the history with the policy, not in a separate file that drifts. A policy with no visible history looks unmanaged and cannot be defended as a living control.

### Govern Effective Dates And Transition

A policy takes effect on a defined date, and the transition from the old version to the new one must be managed so there is no gap or ambiguity about which version governs.

Govern:

- the effective date of each version, stated in the policy;
- the relationship to the prior version's effective and retirement dates;
- a transition plan for communication, training, and attestation when the change is material;
- grace periods only where operationally necessary and documented;
- treatment of conduct that occurred under the prior version.

State clearly which version applies to conduct on a given date. Ambiguity about effective dates creates enforcement problems and undermines the policy's authority.

### Control Supersession So Old Versions Do Not Govern

The most common version-control failure is superseded versions continuing to circulate and be applied. Employees find an old PDF, a stale intranet page, or a cached copy and act on it, believing it is current.

Control supersession by:

- marking each version with its status, current or superseded;
- dating supersession explicitly;
- archiving superseded versions in a controlled repository, not deleting them;
- removing or clearly marking old copies in distribution channels;
- redirecting common access points to the current version;
- checking that contracts, customer commitments, and third-party packs reference the current version or are updated.

Superseded versions must be preserved for history but made inaccessible for application. The distinction between archive and active is what prevents old rules from governing.

### Use A Single Authoritative Source

Each policy should have one authoritative source of truth, and all other copies should derive from it or point to it. Multiple editable copies guarantee divergence.

Establish:

- a designated repository or system as the authoritative source;
- a named owner responsible for the authoritative copy;
- a rule that printed copies, local downloads, and email attachments are uncontrolled and may be out of date;
- periodic checks that distribution channels reflect the authoritative version;
- a process to update the authoritative source as the single point of change.

A policy that exists in several editable places will drift, and the drift will not be detected until an audit or an incident exposes it.

### Coordinate Changes With Training, Controls, And Acknowledgment

A policy change is not complete when it is approved. It must propagate to the training, controls, attestations, and systems that depend on it, or the organization will train and attest to a version that no longer governs.

For each material change:

- update training and job aids that reference the policy;
- update controls, approval forms, and system configurations;
- re-issue for acknowledgment where the change is material;
- notify affected roles of what changed and what it means for them;
- check that related policies and procedures are updated for consistency.

Change propagation is where most version-control programs break. A policy updated in the repository but not in training or controls creates a gap between what is required and what is practiced.

### Retain Evidence Of The Approval And Version Chain

The approval and version chain must be reconstructable from evidence, not from memory. In an enforcement or audit context, the absence of records is treated as the absence of control.

Retain:

- the approved version with the approver and date;
- the review trail showing legal, business, and functional input;
- the change history with rationale;
- the effective and supersession dates;
- the acknowledgment records for each version;
- the distribution records showing who received which version.

Treat these records as compliance records subject to retention and legal hold. They are the proof the policy was controlled.

## Common Traps

### Approval As Signature Without Review

Routing a policy for a signature without substantive review produces an approval that means nothing and provides no control.

### No Version History

A policy with no recorded change history cannot establish what it required in the past, which undermines investigations and defenses.

### Superseded Versions Still In Circulation

Old copies in intranets, shared drives, and contracts cause employees to apply outdated rules, creating both compliance gaps and inconsistency.

### Multiple Authoritative Copies

When several editable copies exist, they diverge, and no one knows which is current.

### Effective Date Ambiguity

If it is unclear when a version took effect or which version governed a past act, enforcement and defense become impossible.

### Changes Not Propagated To Training And Controls

A policy updated in the repository but not in training, forms, or systems creates a gap between the rule and the practice.

### Approval Authority Mismatched To Risk

A high-risk policy approved at too low a level signals weak governance and may not satisfy regulator expectations.

### Deleting Old Versions Instead Of Archiving

Deleting superseded versions destroys the history needed to establish past requirements; archive them as controlled but inactive.

## Self-Check

- Is each policy's approval authority matched to its risk, reach, and population, and is the approver recorded?
- Does the approval workflow include substantive legal, business, and functional review before sign-off, not just a signature?
- Is there a controlled version history capturing version, effective date, approver, change summary, and rationale?
- Are effective dates and the transition between versions managed so it is clear which version governs at any time?
- Are superseded versions archived, marked inactive, and removed from active distribution so they cannot be applied?
- Is there a single authoritative source for each policy, with other copies marked as uncontrolled?
- Are material changes propagated to training, controls, forms, systems, and acknowledgment?
- Are related policies and procedures checked for consistency when a policy changes?
- Is the approval and version chain reconstructable from retained evidence, including review trail and distribution records?
- Are old versions archived rather than deleted, preserving the history needed for past-conduct questions?
