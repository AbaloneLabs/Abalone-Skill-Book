# Policy Exception And Waiver Management

Exceptions are inevitable. Real operations sometimes cannot meet a policy as written, and a blanket refusal to grant exceptions drives non-compliance underground rather than eliminating it. The purpose of an exception process is not to prevent all deviations but to ensure that every deviation is requested, assessed, approved by the right authority, covered by compensating controls, time-limited, and visible. The failure mode is an exception process that functions as a rubber stamp: permanent, unconditional approvals with no compensating controls and no review, which are functionally control gaps dressed as approvals. The judgment is deciding when an exception is acceptable, what controls must replace the waived requirement, who can approve, and when the exception must end.

Use this skill before designing an exception or waiver process, defining approval authority, setting compensating controls, or reviewing whether existing exceptions are controlled. The goal is to make the agent think about justification, authority, compensating controls, expiry, visibility, and the evidence an exception must generate.

## Core Rules

### Treat Exceptions As Controlled Deviations, Not Exemptions

An exception is a temporary, conditional, approved deviation from a policy, supported by a rationale and compensating controls. An exemption is a permanent removal of the requirement. Conflating the two produces exceptions that never expire and impose no controls, which are hidden gaps.

Frame exceptions as:

- temporary, with a defined expiry or review date;
- conditional, with compensating controls that address the waived risk;
- justified, with a documented business or operational rationale;
- approved by an authority appropriate to the risk;
- visible, recorded in a register and reported where material.

If an exception cannot meet these conditions, it is likely a control gap that should be remediated, not waived. The default disposition of a policy requirement is compliance; exception is the controlled alternative, not the easy out.

### Require A Real Justification, Not Convenience

The most common failure is granting exceptions for convenience: the policy is inconvenient, so it is waived. A credible process requires a substantive justification and assesses whether the deviation is genuinely necessary.

Require the requester to state:

- the specific requirement that cannot be met;
- the operational or business reason it cannot be met;
- the alternatives considered, including complying as written;
- the duration needed and why;
- the compensating controls proposed;
- the risk of granting the exception.

Assess whether the justification is substantive. A reason such as "it would slow down the process" is convenience, not necessity, unless the slowdown creates a documented operational or safety problem. Push back on weak justifications; the ease of requesting an exception should not determine whether it is granted.

### Match Approval Authority To Risk And Materiality

Not all exceptions should be approved at the same level. The authority must match the risk the waived requirement addresses and the materiality of the deviation.

Assign approval authority by considering:

- the risk the original requirement mitigates;
- the population or process affected;
- the duration of the exception;
- whether the exception affects financial reporting, regulated activity, or executive conduct;
- whether compensating controls fully offset the risk or leave residual exposure.

High-risk or material exceptions should require senior compliance, legal, or executive approval, and some may require board or audit committee reporting. Low-risk, short-duration exceptions may be approved at a functional level. Record the approver and the authority basis so the approval can be assessed.

### Require Compensating Controls That Actually Offset The Risk

When a requirement is waived, the risk it addressed does not disappear. Compensating controls must replace the waived control and reduce the risk to an acceptable level.

For each exception, define:

- the specific risk the waived requirement addressed;
- the compensating control or controls that will mitigate that risk during the exception;
- whether the compensating control fully offsets the risk or leaves residual exposure;
- who operates the compensating control and how its operation is evidenced;
- how the compensating control will be tested.

A waiver with no compensating control is an uncontrolled gap. A waiver with a compensating control that no one operates or evidences is the same gap with paperwork. The control must be real, owned, and verifiable.

### Set Expiry And Mandatory Review

Permanent exceptions are control gaps. Every exception should have an expiry or a mandatory review date, after which it lapses or must be re-justified.

Set:

- a defined duration tied to the justification, not an arbitrary long date;
- a review date at which the exception is reassessed;
- a renewal process that requires fresh justification, not automatic rollover;
- a lapse rule so that an unreviewed exception expires automatically;
- a path to remediation, so the exception ends when the underlying issue is fixed.

Review should ask whether the original justification still holds, whether the compensating controls are operating, and whether the exception can now be closed. Exceptions that roll over year after year without genuine review are permanent exemptions in disguise.

### Maintain An Exception Register And Report Material Exceptions

Exceptions must be visible. A register records what has been waived, by whom, with what controls, and until when, and it enables aggregate review that individual approvals cannot.

The register should capture:

- the policy and requirement waived;
- the requester, approver, and approval date;
- the justification;
- the compensating controls and their owners;
- the duration and review date;
- the status, active, renewed, or closed;
- the residual risk.

Report material exceptions to compliance leadership, and aggregate or individual exceptions to the board or audit committee where appropriate. Visibility is what prevents exceptions from accumulating into systemic control erosion.

### Plan Remediation, Not Just Accommodation

An exception should be a bridge to compliance, not a permanent accommodation. The process should drive remediation of the underlying issue so the exception can be closed.

Plan remediation by:

- identifying the root cause that prevents compliance;
- assigning an owner and target date for remediation;
- tracking remediation alongside the exception;
- closing the exception when compliance is achieved;
- escalating exceptions that persist beyond their intended duration.

If exceptions persist indefinitely, the policy itself may be unworkable and should be revised, or the operation may be non-compliant by design. Either way, persistent exceptions signal a problem that remediation, not renewal, must address.

### Coordinate Exceptions With Related Controls And Audit

An exception to one requirement can affect related controls, training, and audit testing. Coordinate so the exception does not silently invalidate other elements of the control environment.

Coordinate by:

- updating control tests and audit plans to reflect the exception and its compensating controls;
- ensuring training and procedures acknowledge the exception where relevant;
- checking that system configurations and approvals reflect the exception;
- recording the exception so auditors and regulators can assess it rather than discovering it.

An exception that auditors discover as an unexplained deviation is a finding. An exception that is documented, controlled, and reported is a managed risk.

## Common Traps

### Permanent, Unconditional Exceptions

Exceptions that never expire and impose no controls are hidden control gaps. Every exception should be temporary, conditional, and reviewed.

### Granting Exceptions For Convenience

Waiving a requirement because it is inconvenient, without a substantive justification, erodes the policy and the control environment.

### No Compensating Controls

A waiver with no control to replace the waived requirement leaves the risk unmanaged and is functionally non-compliance.

### Approval Authority Too Low

High-risk exceptions approved at a low level signal weak governance and may not satisfy regulator expectations.

### Automatic Renewal Without Review

Exceptions that roll over without genuine review become permanent and stop being assessed against current risk.

### No Visibility Or Register

Without a register and reporting, exceptions accumulate invisibly, and aggregate control erosion goes undetected.

### No Remediation Path

Exceptions that accommodate rather than remediate persist indefinitely, signaling either an unworkable policy or non-compliant operations.

### Uncoordinated With Audit And Controls

An exception that is not reflected in control tests and audit plans appears as an unexplained deviation and becomes a finding.

## Self-Check

- Is each exception framed as a temporary, conditional, justified, approved, and visible deviation rather than a permanent exemption?
- Does the request process require a substantive justification, alternatives considered, and an assessment of necessity rather than convenience?
- Is approval authority matched to the risk, materiality, duration, and population affected, with the approver and authority basis recorded?
- Are compensating controls defined for each exception, with owners, evidence of operation, and a test method, fully offsetting the risk where possible?
- Does every exception have an expiry or mandatory review date, with renewal requiring fresh justification and lapse on non-review?
- Is there an exception register capturing policy, requester, approver, justification, controls, duration, status, and residual risk?
- Are material exceptions reported to compliance leadership and, where appropriate, the board or audit committee?
- Is there a remediation plan for each exception, with owner, target date, and closure when compliance is achieved?
- Are persistent exceptions escalated to assess whether the policy is unworkable or the operation is non-compliant by design?
- Is the exception coordinated with control tests, audit plans, training, and system configurations so it is managed rather than discovered?
