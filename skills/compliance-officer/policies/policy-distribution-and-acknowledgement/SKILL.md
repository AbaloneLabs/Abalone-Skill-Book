# Policy Distribution And Acknowledgement

A policy that the people it binds cannot find, read, or acknowledge is not an effective control. Distribution and acknowledgment are what close the gap between a policy that exists and a policy that operates. The failure mode is assuming that publishing a policy on an intranet, or collecting a once-a-year click-through, proves that people received and understood it. In an enforcement or audit context, the organization must show who was required to acknowledge which version, who actually did, what happened to those who did not, and how the policy reached populations that are not employees, such as contractors and agents. The judgment is choosing channels that reach people at the point of risk, designing attestations that mean something, ensuring access for all covered populations, and producing evidence of reach.

Use this skill before designing distribution, planning attestations, ensuring access, or reviewing whether reach can be proven. The goal is to make the agent think about channels, audience access, attestation design, non-employee populations, and the evidence of distribution.

## Core Rules

### Match Distribution Channels To Where The Audience Actually Works

Distribution fails when it uses channels the audience does not use. A policy posted only on an intranet that field workers never visit does not reach them. Choose channels based on how the audience works.

Consider channels:

- intranet or policy portal for desk-based employees;
- mobile-accessible formats for field, retail, and frontline workers;
- printed postings for locations without reliable digital access;
- workflow prompts and in-application messages at the point of risk;
- email for time-sensitive updates, with read tracking where appropriate;
- manager-led communication for teams that gather in huddles or shifts;
- onboarding flows for new joiners;
- contract appendices and portals for contractors, agents, and distributors.

No single channel reaches everyone. Use a combination, and confirm that the channels you choose actually reach the population you must reach.

### Ensure Access For Every Covered Population

Acknowledgment is only meaningful if the population could actually access the policy. Access barriers, such as language, technology, literacy, or role, must be identified and addressed.

Address access barriers:

- provide translations for workforces that operate in other languages, with attention to legal meaning;
- provide accessible formats for workers with disabilities;
- ensure offline or low-bandwidth access for remote and field locations;
- account for workers without company email or computers, such as manufacturing and retail staff;
- account for non-employees, including contractors, temps, agents, and joint venture partners;
- account for workers in jurisdictions with restricted technology.

If a population cannot access the policy, requiring their acknowledgment is a hollow exercise. Map the populations and confirm each one has a viable access path before designing the attestation.

### Design Attestations That Mean Something

An attestation is a statement that the person received, understood, and agrees to comply. A click-through that no one reads produces a record without substance. Design attestations to carry meaning.

Make attestations meaningful by:

- requiring acknowledgment of the specific policy and version, not a blanket agreement;
- using clear, specific language about what the person is attesting to;
- timing the attestation at onboarding, at material change, and on a cycle, not only annually;
- including a confirmation that the person knows how to ask questions and report concerns;
- recording the version acknowledged, the date, and the person's identity;
- following up with people who do not attest, with consequences for persistent noncompletion.

Avoid attestations so broad that they cover everything and signify nothing. A meaningful attestation is tied to a specific policy version and a specific, understandable commitment.

### Time Distribution And Attestation To Risk

Distribution should reach people before they face the risk, and attestation should reinforce the policy when it is most relevant, not only on a fixed annual cycle.

Time to risk:

- distribute and attest at onboarding before the person begins covered work;
- re-attest when a material policy change takes effect;
- target attestations to roles when they enter higher-risk assignments, such as a new sales region or a government-facing role;
- use just-in-time prompts at the point of a risky decision, such as before submitting an expense or approving a gift;
- re-attest after a related incident to reinforce the rule.

Annual attestation alone is too distant from daily decisions to change behavior. Combine cyclical attestation with moment-of-risk reinforcement.

### Prove Reach With Evidence

The organization must be able to prove who was required to acknowledge the policy, who did, who did not, and what follow-up occurred. Evidence of reach is what makes distribution a control rather than an assumption.

Maintain evidence:

- the population required to acknowledge each policy and version;
- completion records by person, policy, version, and date;
- noncompletion lists and follow-up actions;
- distribution records showing which channels were used and when;
- attestation language and version;
- exceptions, such as workers on leave, with a plan to complete on return.

In an audit, the question is whether the organization can show reach. Vague claims that "the policy was communicated" are not evidence. Records are.

### Handle Noncompletion Deliberately

Some people will not complete attestations on time. How the organization handles noncompletion determines whether the attestation is a control or a formality.

Handle noncompletion by:

- tracking noncompletion in real time, not after the deadline;
- escalating to managers with a defined cadence;
- providing reminders and support for access barriers;
- applying consequences for persistent noncompletion, consistent with how other policy obligations are enforced;
- recording the reasons for any exceptions, such as leave or system issues;
- ensuring high performers and senior people are not exempted.

A program that ignores noncompletion signals that the attestation does not matter. Consistent follow-up is what gives the attestation force.

### Reach Non-Employee Populations Through Contract And Process

Contractors, agents, distributors, and joint venture partners are often the source of the highest-risk conduct, yet they are frequently outside the standard distribution and attestation process. They must be reached through contract and dedicated process.

Reach non-employees by:

- incorporating policy obligations into contracts and partner agreements;
- requiring acknowledgment as a condition of engagement or renewal;
- providing training or certification for higher-risk third parties;
- verifying that distributors and agents cascade requirements to their own people;
- tracking which third parties have acknowledged which policies;
- reserving the right to audit or terminate for non-compliance.

A policy that binds the organization through third-party conduct but does not reach those third parties leaves the exposure uncontrolled.

### Coordinate Distribution With Change Management

When a policy changes, distribution and attestation must be coordinated with the change so that people attest to the current version, not the old one. Uncoordinated change produces attestations to superseded policies.

Coordinate by:

- triggering re-attestation when a material change takes effect;
- retiring the old attestation and opening the new one at the effective date;
- communicating what changed and why, not just that something changed;
- aligning training and job aids with the new version;
- confirming that systems and forms reflect the new version before requiring attestation.

Change without coordinated distribution creates a window where people act under the old rule while the new one governs, exposing both them and the organization.

## Common Traps

### Single-Channel Distribution

Relying on one channel, such as an intranet, misses populations who do not use it. Distribution must be multi-channel and audience-aware.

### Annual Click-Through As The Whole Program

A once-a-year click-through proves a click, not understanding or behavior. It is one element, not the program.

### Ignoring Access Barriers

Language, technology, literacy, and role barriers prevent acknowledgment. If they are unaddressed, the attestation is hollow for those populations.

### Attesting To A Version That No Longer Governs

If attestation is not coordinated with change, people attest to superseded policies, creating a gap between the rule and the record.

### No Evidence Of Reach

Without records of who was required, who completed, and what follow-up occurred, the organization cannot prove distribution in an audit.

### Ignoring Noncompletion

Allowing noncompletion without follow-up signals the attestation does not matter and undermines the whole program.

### Leaving Out Non-Employees

Contractors, agents, and partners often cause the highest-risk conduct and are frequently outside the attestation process, leaving the exposure uncontrolled.

### Attestation Language Too Broad To Signify

A blanket attestation to "all policies" covers everything and signifies nothing. Tie attestation to specific policies and versions.

## Self-Check

- Are distribution channels chosen based on where the audience actually works, using multiple channels to reach all populations?
- Is access ensured for every covered population, accounting for language, technology, literacy, role, and non-employee status?
- Do attestations require acknowledgment of specific policies and versions, with clear, specific language and recorded identity?
- Is distribution and attestation timed to onboarding, material change, role transitions, and moments of risk, not only an annual cycle?
- Can the organization prove reach with records of required population, completion, noncompletion follow-up, and distribution channels?
- Is noncompletion tracked in real time, escalated to managers, and addressed with consequences for persistent cases, without exempting high performers?
- Are contractors, agents, distributors, and partners reached through contract, dedicated process, and tracked acknowledgment?
- Is distribution coordinated with policy change so people attest to the current version, with training and systems aligned?
- Are attestations specific enough to signify a real commitment rather than a blanket agreement?
- Is the evidence retained sufficient to demonstrate reach in an audit or enforcement context?
