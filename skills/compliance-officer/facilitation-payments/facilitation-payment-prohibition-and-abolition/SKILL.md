# Facilitation Payment Prohibition And Abolition

Facilitation payments sit at the most contested intersection of anti-corruption law because different legal regimes treat them oppositely, and a global company cannot safely operate two standards. The FCPA historically carved out a narrow affirmative defense for facilitation payments to expedite routine non-discretionary official duties. The UK Bribery Act prohibits them entirely. The OECD Anti-Bribery Convention, as implemented by most member states, increasingly prohibits them. Modern corporate practice has converged on outright prohibition, because relying on the FCPA carve-out creates a false safe harbor that collapses wherever the defense does not apply, and because the line between a permissible facilitation payment and an impermissible bribe is too thin to police reliably in the field. The compliance challenge for an organization that still permits facilitation payments, or that relies on the FCPA defense, is to recognize that this position is no longer defensible and to manage the transition to prohibition without exposing the company to a gap period of unmanaged risk.

Use this skill before designing or revising a facilitation payment policy, deciding whether to abolish reliance on the FCPA defense, or managing the transition from a permissive to a prohibitive regime. The legal treatment of facilitation payments varies by jurisdiction and is a matter for qualified counsel; this skill provides the compliance judgment for policy design and abolition, not a determination of what any law permits.

## Core Rules

### Recognize That The FCPA Defense Is A False Safe Harbor

The FCPA affirmative defense for facilitation payments is narrow, jurisdiction-specific, and shrinking in practical value, and relying on it creates three serious problems that make prohibition the only defensible position for a global company. First, the defense does not exist under the UK Bribery Act, which has broad extraterritorial reach over companies carrying on business in the UK, nor under most OECD Convention implementations, so a payment that is defensible under FCPA can be criminal under another applicable law. Second, a payment that looks like facilitation can be recharacterized as a bribe if the underlying duty was discretionary rather than routine, and the distinction is often unclear at the point of payment. Third, the FCPA accounting provisions still require accurate recording of facilitation payments, and disguised facilitation payments are a frequent books-and-records violation regardless of the affirmative defense.

A policy that permits facilitation payments under the FCPA defense creates a false sense of safety. Employees and country managers believe there is a legal category of permissible small payments, and that belief erodes the anti-bribery culture and produces payments that do not qualify for the defense but are made in its shadow. The defense's existence does more harm than good as a policy anchor, because it signals that some payments to officials are acceptable when the safer and simpler message is that none are.

### Design Prohibition As Absolute And Unambiguous

A zero-tolerance policy must be stated so clearly that no employee or third party can interpret it as permitting exceptions. The policy should state that no employee or third party acting on the company's behalf may make facilitation payments, that the company will not rely on the FCPA carve-out even where it might technically apply, and that any payment to a government official requires escalation and approval through the defined process. Ambiguity in the prohibition, such as permitting facilitation payments in exceptional cases or in specific jurisdictions, recreates the false safe harbor and undermines the absolute standard.

Pair the prohibition with affirmative guidance on what employees should do when faced with a demand, because a prohibition without alternatives leaves employees to improvise. The policy must connect to the escalation channel, the duress framework, and the refusal tools, so that the prohibition is supported by a realistic field process. A prohibition that exists only on paper, without the field infrastructure to make it survivable, will be violated in practice and will drive payments underground.

### Manage The Transition From Permissive To Prohibitive

An organization moving from a policy that permitted facilitation payments to one that prohibits them must manage the transition deliberately, because the change affects established practices, existing relationships with officials and intermediaries, and employee expectations. A sudden prohibition without transition planning can create a gap period where employees continue paying out of habit while the new policy is not yet embedded, and where the controls and escalation channels are not yet operational.

Plan the transition in phases. Update the policy and communicate the absolute prohibition clearly. Train affected employees, especially those in field roles who encounter officials, on the prohibition, the escalation channel, and the duress framework, before the policy takes effect. Stand up the field infrastructure, the hotline, the refusal language, the designated duress authority, before employees need it. Review existing third-party relationships, agents, customs brokers, and distributors, to align them with the prohibition contractually. Identify the operational touchpoints where facilitation payments previously occurred and put alternatives in place, such as licensed brokers or pre-clearance, before the prohibition removes the prior practice. A transition that changes the policy without changing the infrastructure leaves employees without tools and produces violations.

### Align Third Parties And Intermediaries With The Prohibition

Facilitation payments are often made by third parties acting on the company's behalf, customs brokers, freight forwarders, agents, and distributors who handle official interactions. A company policy that prohibits facilitation payments is undermined if the company's intermediaries continue making them, because payments by intermediaries on the company's behalf are attributable to the company under anti-corruption law. The prohibition must extend to all third parties, contractually and operationally.

Require third parties to contractually commit to the prohibition, to report any demands they receive, and to follow the company's escalation process. Due diligence on third parties should assess whether they have historically made facilitation payments and whether their own controls support prohibition. Monitor third-party conduct through audits, transaction review, and feedback from the company's own employees who interact with them. A third party that continues facilitation payments while claiming compliance creates attributed liability that the company's policy cannot prevent without enforcement.

### Require Accurate Recording During And After Transition

During the transition from permissive to prohibitive, and afterward, any facilitation payment that occurs must be recorded accurately. Under the FCPA accounting provisions and equivalent regimes, facilitation payments that are made must be recorded in their true character, and disguising them is a separate violation. A company that is abolishing facilitation payments may be tempted to stop recording historical payments to avoid creating evidence, but concealment is more damaging than the payment itself and is independently criminal.

Maintain accurate recording throughout the transition. Payments that occurred under the prior permissive policy should be recorded as they were made. Payments that occur after prohibition, whether under duress or in violation, must be recorded accurately and investigated. The recording discipline supports the duress classification, enables monitoring for violations of the new policy, and creates the honest record that distinguishes a company managing its risk from one concealing it.

### Embed The Prohibition In Training And Culture

A policy change achieves nothing if the workforce does not internalize it. Employees who previously understood that facilitation payments were sometimes permissible must unlearn that understanding and replace it with the absolute prohibition. This requires training that reaches the field roles who actually encounter officials, not only headquarters staff, and that uses realistic scenarios from the company's geography rather than abstract principles.

Training should connect the prohibition to the field process, so that employees understand not only that they must not pay but what to do instead. Test understanding through scenarios that require employees to apply the prohibition, the escalation channel, and the duress framework. Repeat training at intervals and after incidents, because a single session does not change embedded practice. Culture change, the shift from facilitation payments as normal to facilitation payments as unacceptable, is the ultimate control, and it requires sustained reinforcement beyond the policy update.

## Common Traps

### Relying On The FCPA Defense As A Policy Anchor

Designing policy around the affirmative defense, creating a false safe harbor that collapses under the UK Bribery Act, OECD implementations, or when the underlying duty proves discretionary.

### Prohibition Without Field Infrastructure

Updating the policy to zero-tolerance without standing up the escalation channel, duress framework, and refusal tools, leaving employees to improvise and pay anyway.

### Abrupt Transition Without Planning

Changing the policy overnight without training, third-party alignment, or operational alternatives, creating a gap period of unmanaged violations.

### Ignoring Third-Party Facilitation Payments

Prohibiting facilitation payments by employees while intermediaries continue making them, creating attributed liability that the company policy does not prevent.

### Concealing Historical Payments During Transition

Stopping accurate recording of facilitation payments to avoid evidence, creating books-and-records violations and concealment that is more damaging than the payments.

### Headquarters-Only Training

Training headquarters staff on the prohibition while field roles who encounter officials receive no scenario-based preparation, so the policy never reaches the point of risk.

### Permitting Jurisdiction-Specific Exceptions

Allowing facilitation payments in specific countries or cases, recreating the false safe harbor and signaling that the prohibition is not absolute.

## Self-Check

- Does the policy prohibit facilitation payments absolutely and unambiguously, stating that the company will not rely on the FCPA carve-out even where it might technically apply?
- Is the prohibition paired with field infrastructure, escalation channel, duress framework, and refusal tools, so that employees have alternatives to payment?
- Is the transition from any prior permissive policy planned in phases, with training, third-party alignment, and operational alternatives in place before the prohibition takes effect?
- Do third parties acting on the company's behalf contractually commit to the prohibition, with due diligence and monitoring of their conduct?
- Is accurate recording of any facilitation payment maintained throughout and after the transition, with no concealment to avoid evidence?
- Does training reach field roles who encounter officials, using realistic scenarios from the company's geography, and is it repeated at intervals?
- Has the company identified and put alternatives in place for the operational touchpoints where facilitation payments previously occurred?
- Would an employee facing a demand understand from the policy and training that they must not pay, who to call, and what to do under duress?
- Are jurisdiction-specific exceptions prohibited so that the standard is genuinely absolute rather than variable by geography?
- Does the policy and training reflect that the UK Bribery Act and OECD implementations prohibit facilitation payments regardless of the FCPA defense?
