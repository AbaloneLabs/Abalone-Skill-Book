# Extortion And Duress Payment Response

The most dangerous moment in anti-corruption compliance is when an employee faces a uniformed official demanding payment under threat at a border, a port, a licensing office, or a checkpoint. In that moment, the employee must decide whether to pay, refuse, or escalate, and the decision is made under fear, time pressure, and incomplete information. A policy that says do not pay bribes provides no guidance for the person who is being told their shipment will be seized or their passport confiscated. The compliance challenge is to distinguish genuine extortion, where a payment under duress may be defensible, from a disguised bribe, where the threat is a pretext, and to give employees a field-ready process that protects both their safety and the company's legal position. Failing to address extortion honestly forces employees to choose between their security and policy compliance, and drives payments underground where they are never recorded or learned from.

Use this skill before designing duress response procedures, evaluating whether a reported payment was extortion, or advising an employee facing a demand. Payments to government officials carry criminal exposure under the FCPA, UK Bribery Act, and local anti-corruption law; whether a duress payment is defensible is a legal determination that depends on jurisdiction and facts, and must be reviewed with qualified counsel. This skill provides operational judgment for field response, not a legal opinion that any duress payment is permissible.

## Core Rules

### Establish That Genuine Duress Is Distinct From A Bribe

The legal and ethical analysis of a payment depends on whether it was extortion or a bribe, and conflating them produces bad decisions in both directions. Extortion occurs when an official demands payment under a credible threat to person, liberty, property, or vital business interests, and the payer has no meaningful safe alternative. A bribe occurs when the payer voluntarily offers payment to induce an improper or discretionary act. The distinction matters because extortion may be defensible under a duress or necessity rationale, while a voluntary bribe never is, and because the appropriate response, field escalation versus discipline, differs entirely.

To classify a payment, examine four elements. First, was there a credible and imminent threat, to physical safety, to liberty such as unlawful detention, to property such as seizure or destruction, or to vital business interests? Second, was there a meaningful safe alternative, such as escalation, delay, intervention by counsel, or contacting authorities? Third, was the payment the minimum necessary to end the threat, or was it larger or repeated beyond what the threat required? Fourth, was the underlying duty routine and non-discretionary, or was the official using the threat to extract payment for a discretionary act? A payment that lacks a genuine threat, that had available alternatives, or that exceeded the minimum is not extortion regardless of how it is characterized.

### Build A Field-Ready Escalation Channel Before The Crisis

An employee facing a threat cannot consult a policy manual; they need an immediate, reachable, empowered contact. The escalation channel must exist, be known, and be tested before it is needed. A 24-hour compliance or security hotline staffed by someone with authority to advise, to contact local counsel or the embassy, and to authorize an exception under genuine duress is the control that makes a zero-tolerance policy survivable in the field.

The channel must be accessible from the locations where demands occur, including remote borders and ports with poor connectivity. Employees must be trained to use it, must have the contact information physically available, and must trust that calling will help rather than punish them. If employees believe escalation will be slow, ineffective, or will get them in trouble, they will pay and hide it. The escalation channel's credibility determines whether payments surface for recording and learning or disappear into unrecorded transactions.

### Define A Duress Framework With Clear Conditions

The company should acknowledge honestly that an employee may pay under genuine duress to protect life, liberty, or critical property, and should define the conditions under which that is documented and reported. Refusing to acknowledge duress does not prevent payments; it prevents reporting. A defensible duress framework specifies the conditions that must be met, the authority that can approve an exception, and the documentation required after the event.

The conditions should require a credible and imminent threat, no reasonable safe alternative, the minimum payment necessary to end the threat, and prompt reporting after the event with accurate recording. The authority to approve a duress exception should rest with a designated senior manager, not the employee under threat, and should be exercisable in real time through the escalation channel. The framework should make clear that paying under duress and reporting it is compliance behavior, while paying voluntarily and concealing it is a violation, so that employees are not punished for honest reporting.

### Provide Refusal Language And De-Escalation Tools

Many demands can be refused or de-escalated without payment if the employee has the tools and confidence to do so. An official who is testing whether the employee will pay may back down when confronted with a firm, polite refusal that references company policy and anti-corruption law. Employees should be equipped with pre-cleared refusal language, an understanding that citing policy is an acceptable and expected response, and the authority to delay, to ask for a supervisor, or to request written justification for any fee.

Train refusal and de-escalation as a skill, not a slogan. Role-play realistic scenarios drawn from the company's actual geography. Provide language in the local language where relevant. Equip employees with the knowledge that many legitimate government fees can be paid officially and that a demand for cash outside official channels is a red flag. Refusal is not always possible or safe, but it should be the first response, with payment reserved for genuine duress where refusal has failed or is clearly unsafe.

### Document And Record Any Payment That Occurs

If any payment to an official occurs, whether under duress or in error, it must be recorded accurately and transparently. Disguising the payment as a consulting fee, a customs charge, or a miscellaneous expense is itself a books-and-records violation and converts a disclosure problem into a concealment problem that is often more serious and more provable than the underlying payment. The general ledger must reflect the true character of the payment, and compliance must review any payment to an official regardless of coding.

Require prompt post-event documentation of the demand, including the official's identity and authority, the location, the nature of the threat, the response including escalation, the amount paid, and the alternatives considered. This documentation supports the duress classification, enables operational response to recurring extortion points, and creates the record needed if the payment is later questioned. A payment that is made but not documented cannot be defended as duress; it looks like a concealed bribe.

### Map And Reduce Recurring Extortion Points

The most effective control for extortion risk is structural, reducing the situations in which employees encounter officials who can extract payments. Once extortion points are identified through reported incidents, the company can redesign processes to avoid them. Operational measures include using licensed customs brokers who handle official interactions, pre-clearing shipments, digitizing permit applications, consolidating logistics through reputable providers, routing around chronically corrupt borders or ports where alternatives exist, and coordinating with security, local counsel, and where appropriate embassies.

Treat reported extortion incidents as operational intelligence, not just compliance records. A pattern of demands at a specific port or from a specific agency indicates a systemic risk that process redesign can reduce. Failing to act on recurring patterns leaves employees exposed to the same threats repeatedly, which erodes the credibility of the zero-tolerance policy and eventually drives payments underground.

## Common Traps

### Treating All Payments As Equivalent

Failing to distinguish genuine extortion from a bribe, so field employees get no guidance for duress situations and either pay everything or refuse unsafely.

### No Usable Escalation Channel

Having a hotline number that is unstaffed, slow, unreachable from remote locations, or staffed by someone without authority, so employees pay and hide rather than escalate.

### Punishing Employees Who Report Extortion

Disciplining or suspecting employees who report extortionate payments, ensuring that future payments are concealed and the company loses all visibility.

### Calling A Bribe Extortion

Characterizing a voluntary payment to influence a discretionary act as extortion because the employee felt pressure, when the elements of credible threat and no alternative are absent.

### Concealing The Payment In The Ledger

Recording a duress payment as a customs fee or miscellaneous expense, creating a books-and-records violation layered on the payment itself.

### No Refusal Tools

Expecting employees to refuse demands without providing language, training, or the authority to delay, leaving them to improvise under threat.

### Accepting Recurring Extortion As Unavoidable

Treating chronic extortion at a port or border as a cost of doing business rather than as an operational risk to be reduced through process redesign.

## Self-Check

- Does the framework distinguish genuine extortion, requiring a credible imminent threat and no safe alternative, from a voluntary bribe, with the distinction applied to each reported payment?
- Is there a 24-hour escalation channel that is reachable from field locations, staffed by someone with authority, and trusted by employees to help rather than punish?
- Does the duress framework specify credible threat, no reasonable alternative, minimum payment, prompt reporting, and accurate recording as the conditions for a defensible exception?
- Are employees equipped with pre-cleared refusal language, de-escalation techniques, and the authority to delay or request supervisors, trained through realistic role-play?
- Is any payment to an official, whether under duress or in error, recorded accurately in its true character rather than disguised?
- Is post-event documentation required capturing the official, location, threat, response, amount, and alternatives, so the duress classification is supportable?
- Are reported extortion incidents treated as operational intelligence used to map and reduce recurring touchpoints through process redesign?
- Are employees who report extortion protected from retaliation and not conflated with wrongdoers who pay voluntarily and conceal?
- Would the documentation for a reported duress payment support the classification if questioned by a regulator or auditor?
- Has the company tested the escalation channel to confirm it works before a real crisis, rather than assuming it will function when needed?
