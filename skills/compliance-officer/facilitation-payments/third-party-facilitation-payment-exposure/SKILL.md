# Third Party Facilitation Payment Exposure

The most insidious source of facilitation payment liability is the third party who makes the payment without the company's knowledge. Customs brokers clear shipments, freight forwarders handle ports, agents navigate licensing offices, and distributors manage local logistics, and in each of these roles the intermediary interacts with officials who may demand payments. Under anti-corruption law, a payment made by a third party acting on the company's behalf is attributable to the company, and the company's lack of actual knowledge is not a defense if it failed to exercise diligence and oversight. A company with a pristine internal policy and a clean gift register can nonetheless incur facilitation payment liability through an intermediary who pays quietly to keep shipments moving, and the company will not learn of it until a regulator, a whistleblower, or an audit surfaces the pattern. The compliance challenge is to extend facilitation payment controls beyond employees to the intermediaries who handle the official interactions where demands arise.

Use this skill before selecting or retaining intermediaries with official-interaction exposure, designing due diligence for facilitation payment risk, or assessing whether existing intermediaries may be making attributed payments. Third-party payments on the company's behalf carry the same anti-corruption exposure as direct payments under the FCPA, UK Bribery Act, and equivalent laws; legal obligations should be confirmed with counsel. This skill provides compliance judgment for managing third-party exposure, not a legal determination.

## Core Rules

### Identify Which Intermediaries Carry Facilitation Payment Exposure

Not all third parties carry the same exposure. The intermediaries at risk are those who interact with government officials on the company's behalf, where facilitation demands typically arise. Customs brokers who clear shipments through customs, freight forwarders who handle ports and borders, logistics providers who navigate checkpoints, agents who obtain licenses and permits, and distributors who manage local regulatory interactions all occupy the roles where officials extract payments. A vendor who supplies office equipment carries no such exposure.

Map the third-party population by official-interaction exposure. Identify every intermediary that handles customs, logistics, licensing, permitting, inspections, registrations, or any other process involving government officials. This mapping is the foundation for risk-based due diligence and oversight. A company that treats all vendors uniformly will either over-invest in low-risk suppliers or, more dangerously, under-protect the high-exposure intermediaries whose payments are attributed to the company.

### Conduct Due Diligence Proportionate To Exposure

Due diligence on high-exposure intermediaries must go beyond standard vendor screening to assess facilitation payment risk specifically. The question is not only whether the intermediary is legitimate but whether its own controls, culture, and practices support the company's prohibition on facilitation payments. An intermediary that has historically paid facilitation payments as standard practice, that lacks its own anti-corruption controls, or that operates in a way that depends on official payments will continue that practice on the company's behalf regardless of contractual prohibitions.

Assess the intermediary's anti-corruption program, its history of facilitation payments, its own training and controls, its willingness to report demands and follow escalation, and its transparency about how it handles official interactions. Request references and, for higher-risk intermediaries, conduct site visits or independent reviews. An intermediary that cannot or will not demonstrate facilitation payment controls is a liability regardless of its commercial value, because its payments will be attributed to the company. Decline or replace intermediaries whose facilitation payment practices cannot be verified, especially in higher-risk jurisdictions.

### Contractually Extend The Prohibition And Reporting Obligation

Contractual terms are necessary but not sufficient. The contract must explicitly prohibit facilitation payments on the company's behalf, require the intermediary to follow the company's escalation process for any demand, require reporting of any demand received, require accurate recordkeeping of all official interactions and payments, and provide audit and termination rights for compliance failures. Standard anti-corruption representations that do not specifically address facilitation payments leave the most relevant risk uncontracted.

The contract should also address how the intermediary is compensated, because compensation structures can incentivize facilitation payments. An intermediary paid by results, such as per shipment cleared, may pay facilitation payments to expedite throughput and protect its fee, while an intermediary paid a transparent fee for documented services has less incentive to pay. Align compensation with compliance so that the intermediary is not rewarded for speed achieved through improper payments.

### Monitor Intermediary Conduct, Not Just Contract Terms

A contract that prohibits facilitation payments achieves nothing if the intermediary's actual conduct is not monitored. Intermediaries operate at a distance from the company, and the absence of visible facilitation payments may reflect either genuine compliance or concealment. Monitoring must create visibility into how the intermediary handles official interactions, rather than relying on the intermediary's assurances.

Monitor through multiple channels. Require periodic reporting of official interactions and any demands received. Review the intermediary's invoices and charges for unusual or unexplained fees that could mask facilitation payments. Conduct periodic audits of higher-risk intermediaries, with rights to inspect records of official interactions. Seek feedback from the company's own employees who interact with the intermediary, who may observe practices the intermediary does not report. Track performance metrics for anomalies, such as clearance times that are suspiciously fast compared to market norms, which can indicate facilitation payments. Monitoring that relies solely on the intermediary's self-reporting provides no real assurance.

### Manage The Information Asymmetry Between Company And Intermediary

The fundamental challenge of third-party facilitation payment exposure is information asymmetry. The intermediary knows whether it is paying officials; the company does not, unless the intermediary reports honestly or the payment surfaces through another channel. This asymmetry means that the company's controls must be designed to reduce reliance on the intermediary's candor and to create independent visibility.

Reduce asymmetry through structural choices. Prefer intermediaries that operate transparently and that the company can audit over opaque local agents. Where feasible, consolidate official interactions through a smaller number of vetted intermediaries rather than scattering them across many unaudited local providers, because oversight is more feasible over fewer relationships. Use technology to create visibility, such as shipment tracking and customs documentation that flows to the company directly rather than only through the intermediary. Where the asymmetry cannot be reduced, treat the relationship as higher risk and apply enhanced diligence, monitoring, and contractual protection. Accepting information asymmetry without mitigation is accepting attributed liability without visibility.

### Respond To Indicators Of Intermediary Facilitation Payments

When indicators suggest that an intermediary may be making facilitation payments, whether through an audit finding, an invoice anomaly, an employee observation, or a whistleblower report, the response must be immediate and proportionate. Ignoring indicators because they are unconfirmed allows attributed liability to accumulate. Investigating promptly, with rights to inspect records and interview personnel, either confirms the concern and triggers remediation or provides assurance that the practice is compliant.

Define the response framework in advance. Establish triggers for investigation, the process for conducting it, the authority to suspend or terminate the relationship pending findings, and the escalation path for confirmed facilitation payments, which may include self-disclosure analysis. An intermediary confirmed to be making facilitation payments on the company's behalf creates current, ongoing liability that cannot be managed by contract amendment alone; it may require termination, remediation of past transactions, and legal assessment of disclosure obligations.

## Common Traps

### Uniform Vendor Treatment

Applying the same due diligence to an office supplier and a customs broker, under-protecting the intermediary whose facilitation payments are attributed to the company.

### Contract Without Monitoring

Relying on contractual anti-corruption clauses without monitoring the intermediary's actual conduct, so payments continue despite prohibitions on paper.

### Results-Based Compensation Incentivizing Payments

Paying intermediaries per shipment or per clearance, incentivizing facilitation payments to protect throughput and fees.

### Trusting Intermediary Self-Reporting

Relying solely on the intermediary's assurances that it does not pay, with no independent audit, invoice review, or employee feedback to create visibility.

### Ignoring Speed Anomalies

Accepting clearance or processing times that are suspiciously fast compared to market norms, which can indicate facilitation payments rather than efficiency.

### Scattered Unaudited Local Agents

Using many small local agents across regions without consolidated oversight, making monitoring infeasible and creating widespread attributed exposure.

### Slow Response To Indicators

Treating audit findings or invoice anomalies as minor rather than as triggers for immediate investigation, allowing attributed liability to accumulate.

## Self-Check

- Has the third-party population been mapped by official-interaction exposure, distinguishing customs brokers, freight forwarders, agents, and distributors from low-exposure vendors?
- Is due diligence on high-exposure intermediaries proportionate to risk, assessing their anti-corruption controls, facilitation payment history, and willingness to report demands?
- Do contracts explicitly prohibit facilitation payments on the company's behalf, require escalation and reporting of demands, provide audit rights, and align compensation with compliance?
- Is intermediary conduct monitored through reporting, invoice review, audits, employee feedback, and performance anomaly tracking rather than through self-reporting alone?
- Is the information asymmetry between company and intermediary reduced through transparency, consolidation, technology, and enhanced diligence where it cannot be eliminated?
- Are indicators of intermediary facilitation payments, from audits, invoices, or observations, treated as triggers for immediate investigation rather than deferred?
- Is the response framework for confirmed intermediary payments defined in advance, including suspension, termination, remediation, and disclosure assessment?
- Would the company learn of facilitation payments by an intermediary through its own controls, or only through a regulator or whistleblower?
- Are compensation structures reviewed to ensure they do not incentivize intermediaries to pay for speed?
- Is the number of high-exposure intermediaries manageable enough that oversight is feasible, or has proliferation made monitoring impossible?
