# Customs Audit and Internal Controls

Customs compliance is not a single filing; it is a system of internal controls, documentation, and self-review that must withstand audit. Customs authorities conduct audits (focused assessments, compliance audits, verification visits) to test whether the organisation has exercised "reasonable care" and "informed compliance." An organisation that files entries but has no internal controls, no post-entry review, and no audit readiness is not compliant regardless of whether individual entries happen to be correct. This skill addresses the judgment involved in building, testing, and defending a customs compliance control environment.

## Core Rules

### Build internal controls around the full customs lifecycle

A customs internal control environment must cover:

- **Product master and classification**: documented classifications with rationale, maintained and reviewed;
- **Valuation**: assist and royalty identification, additions checklists, related-party analysis;
- **Origin and preference**: determination documentation, supplier declarations, certification validity;
- **Entry filing**: broker instructions, accuracy verification, reconciliation;
- **Post-entry review**: periodic sampling and testing of filed entries;
- **Recordkeeping**: retention, retrievability, and completeness;
- **Correction**: prior disclosure and protest processes for discovered errors.

Map the lifecycle, identify the control points, and document the control at each point. A control that exists only in someone's head is not a control.

### Implement post-entry review as a core control

Post-entry review — sampling and testing filed entries against underlying documents — is the single most important customs control. It catches errors before customs does, demonstrates reasonable care, and provides the basis for voluntary correction. Define:

- The sampling methodology (statistical or risk-based, covering a defined percentage or all high-value entries);
- The frequency (monthly, quarterly);
- The scope (classification, value, origin, preference, documentation completeness);
- The reviewer (independent of the filer where possible);
- The escalation and correction path for findings.

Document the reviews and their findings. An unreviewed entry population is an unmanaged risk.

### Exercise informed compliance and reasonable care

Many customs regimes operate on a shared-responsibility model: the importer must use reasonable care to properly declare value, classification, and origin, and customs must communicate its requirements. Reasonable care is demonstrated through:

- Documented classification, valuation, and origin determinations;
- Consultation of rulings, explanatory notes, and expert advice for complex issues;
- Binding rulings for material or uncertain matters;
- Post-entry review and correction of errors;
- Trained personnel and defined procedures.

The absence of these is treated as a lack of reasonable care, which elevates penalty exposure from negligence to gross negligence or fraud standards.

### Manage customs brokers with oversight, not delegation

Brokers file entries, but the importer retains legal responsibility. Broker management requires:

- Written instructions (classification, valuation, origin, power of attorney scope);
- Periodic review of broker-filed entries through post-entry review;
- Service-level agreements on accuracy, timeliness, and communication;
- Periodic broker performance reviews;
- Confirmation that the broker retains records and can produce them.

Delegation of filing is not delegation of liability. An unmanaged broker is a liability.

### Prepare for audits with retrievable documentation and defined processes

When customs initiates an audit, the organisation must produce documentation promptly and consistently. Prepare by:

- Maintaining a customs compliance manual documenting policies and procedures;
- Ensuring all entries and supporting documents are retrievable within the audit timeline;
- Designating audit response personnel and a single point of contact;
- Establishing a process for reviewing document requests for scope and privilege;
- Conducting internal mock audits periodically.

During an audit, cooperate, produce documents on schedule, track requests and responses, and engage trade counsel for material disputes or potential violations.

### Track and correct errors through prior disclosure

When post-entry review or an audit reveals errors, the organisation should correct proactively. Many jurisdictions offer prior disclosure or voluntary self-disclosure programs that substantially reduce penalties when the importer reports the error before customs discovers it. Establish:

- A process to identify and quantify errors;
- A threshold for mandatory disclosure review with trade counsel;
- A disclosure preparation and filing process;
- Stop-and-correct procedures so erroneous filing does not continue.

Continuing to file known-incorrect entries while "evaluating" forfeits the benefits of disclosure and increases exposure.

### Monitor regulatory changes and update controls

Customs law changes through tariff updates (HS revisions every five years), new FTAs, valuation guidance, and enforcement priorities. Assign responsibility for monitoring changes and updating classifications, valuations, and procedures accordingly. A control environment built five years ago and never updated is stale.

### Benchmark and test control effectiveness

Controls must be tested, not just documented. Conduct periodic self-testing:

- Sample entries and confirm controls operated as designed;
- Test that classifications in the database match filed entries;
- Test that assist and royalty additions were made;
- Test record retrievability with timed sample requests.

Document the testing and remediate weaknesses. Untested controls provide false assurance.

## Common Traps

### Filing entries with no internal controls or post-entry review

The organisation relies on the broker and files entries without any internal verification. Errors accumulate undetected until an audit reveals systemic problems. Post-entry review is the essential backstop.

### Treating reasonable care as a slogan rather than documented practice

Reasonable care is demonstrated through documented determinations, rulings, reviews, and corrections. Claiming reasonable care without the supporting evidence is ineffective in an audit or enforcement context.

### Delegating fully to the broker without oversight

The broker files, but the importer is liable. Without broker oversight and entry reconciliation, broker errors become importer liability. Manage the broker as a controlled vendor.

### Failing to correct discovered errors promptly

Discovering an error and deferring correction while continuing to file incorrectly multiplies the exposure. Correct promptly and consider prior disclosure.

### Letting the classification database and procedures go stale

Products change, tariff schedules update, and FTAs are renegotiated. A control environment that is never refreshed drifts into error. Assign ownership for periodic refresh.

### Producing inconsistent or incomplete documentation during an audit

An audit tests not just correctness but the ability to substantiate. Disorganized, incomplete, or late document production undermines credibility even where entries are substantially correct.

## Self-Check

- Does the internal control environment cover the full customs lifecycle (classification, valuation, origin, filing, post-entry review, recordkeeping, correction)?
- Is post-entry review implemented with a defined sampling methodology, frequency, scope, independent reviewer, and documented findings?
- Is reasonable care demonstrated through documented determinations, rulings, expert consultation, and correction processes?
- Are customs brokers managed with written instructions, periodic review, performance assessment, and recordkeeping confirmation?
- Is the organisation audit-ready with a compliance manual, retrievable documentation, designated responders, and mock audit practice?
- Is there a defined prior disclosure process with thresholds, counsel engagement, and stop-and-correct procedures?
- Is responsibility assigned for monitoring regulatory changes and refreshing classifications, valuations, and procedures?
- Are controls periodically self-tested, with weaknesses documented and remediated?
