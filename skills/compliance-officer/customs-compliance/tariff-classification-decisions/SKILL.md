# Tariff Classification Decisions

Tariff classification — assigning the correct Harmonized System (HS) code to a good — is the foundation of customs compliance. The classification determines the duty rate, the applicable origin rules, whether antidumping or countervailing duties apply, whether quotas or licensing requirements attach, and whether the good qualifies for preference. A wrong classification cascades into wrong duty payments, wrong origin determinations, and wrong trade statistics. Classification is also inherently judgment-intensive: the Harmonized System is vast, goods are increasingly complex and multi-functional, and the rules for resolving competing headings require careful legal analysis. This skill addresses the judgment involved in classifying goods defensibly.

## Core Rules

### Apply the General Rules of Interpretation in order

The Harmonized System is not applied by intuition or by finding the heading that "seems right." It is applied through the General Rules of Interpretation (GRIs), which are applied sequentially:

- **GRI 1**: Classification is determined by the terms of the headings and any section or chapter notes. This is the primary rule.
- **GRI 2**: Addresses incomplete or unfinished articles and mixtures or composite goods put up in sets.
- **GRI 3**: Resolves goods prima facie classifiable under two or more headings — most specific description (3a), essential character (3b), and last-in-numerical-order (3c).
- **GRI 4**: Most akin (for goods not specifically provided for).
- **GRI 5**: Packaging rules.
- **GRI 6**: Application of subheadings.

Work through the GRIs in order and document which rule resolves the classification. Jumping to a heading without applying GRI 1 and the chapter notes is the most common classification error.

### Read the section and chapter notes before settling on a heading

Section and chapter notes are legally binding interpretive rules. They define scope, exclude goods, and resolve edge cases. A heading that appears to describe the good may be narrowed or overridden by a chapter note. Always read the relevant section and chapter notes for any candidate heading and for competing headings. Document the note analysis.

### Resolve multi-function and composite goods through essential character

Modern goods are frequently multi-functional (a device with many capabilities) or composite (a set of articles). GRI 3 governs these. For composite goods put up in sets, classify by the component that gives the set its essential character. For goods prima facie classifiable under multiple headings, apply GRI 3(a) most-specific, then 3(b) essential-character, then 3(c) last-heading. Document the essential-character analysis: which component dominates function, value, or use, and why.

### Base classification on the good as imported, not as sold or used

Classification is generally based on the condition of the good at the time of importation. A good imported as a component and later incorporated into a finished product is classified as the component, not the finished product. Conversely, a good imported in finished form is classified as finished, even if it is later disassembled or repurposed. Analyze the condition at import.

### Use explanatory notes, rulings, and court decisions as interpretive aids

The Harmonized System Explanatory Notes (WCO) and national explanatory notes (for example, US Harmonized Tariff Schedule notes, EU Combined Nomenclature explanatory notes) provide authoritative guidance on heading scope. Published customs rulings (US CBP CROSS, EU BTI) and court decisions show how authorities have classified similar goods. Research these for any non-trivial classification and cite them in the classification file. They are not binding in all jurisdictions but carry significant weight.

### Maintain a documented classification database with rationale

Every product the organisation imports or exports should have a documented classification in a classification database, with:

- The HS code (to the required digit level for the jurisdiction);
- The product description;
- The GRI analysis and the heading/notes relied upon;
- Explanatory-note and ruling citations;
- The classifier, date, and review status;
- Any binding ruling number.

Classifications should not live only in the broker's system or in an individual's memory. They are corporate records that must be maintained and reviewed.

### Obtain binding rulings for high-volume or uncertain classifications

For goods with significant duty exposure, genuine classification ambiguity, or high import volume, seek a binding classification ruling (US CBP binding ruling, EU Binding Tariff Information). A binding ruling provides certainty, consistency across ports, and protection against retrospective reassessment if followed. Document the ruling and ensure the actual goods match the ruling's scope.

### Re-classify when the product or the law changes

Classification is not permanent. A product reformulation, a functional change, a new model, or a change to the tariff schedule (HS updates every five years) can change the correct classification. Establish a process to review classifications on product changes and on tariff updates.

## Common Traps

### Choosing a heading by description-match without applying the GRIs

The heading text seems to fit, so it is selected. But the GRIs and chapter notes may direct classification elsewhere. Always apply GRI 1 and the notes before settling.

### Ignoring competing headings and the GRI 3 analysis

When a good could fit multiple headings, the analyst must resolve it through GRI 3. Picking the "obvious" one without analyzing essential character or specificity is a common error for multi-function goods.

### Classifying based on the finished product rather than the imported condition

A component imported for assembly is classified as the component, not the finished good it becomes. Classifying based on end-use rather than import condition overstates or understates the correct code.

### Failing to consult explanatory notes and rulings

Published guidance often resolves the exact question. Skipping the research step leads to idiosyncratic classifications that diverge from how authorities view the good.

### Treating classification as a one-time data entry

Classifications made years ago may be stale after product changes or HS updates. Without a review process, the database drifts into error.

### Inconsistent classification of the same product across entries or brokers

If different entries classify the same product differently, customs will notice and may suspect manipulation. Maintain one authoritative classification per product and enforce it.

## Self-Check

- Did I apply the General Rules of Interpretation in sequence and document which rule resolved the classification?
- Did I read and apply the relevant section and chapter notes for the candidate and competing headings?
- For multi-function or composite goods, did I perform and document the GRI 3 (essential character or most specific) analysis?
- Did I classify based on the good as imported, not as sold or used downstream?
- Did I consult Harmonized System Explanatory Notes, national notes, and published rulings for guidance?
- Is each product's classification documented in a maintained database with rationale, citations, and review status?
- For high-volume or uncertain goods, did I obtain or rely on a binding classification ruling?
- Is there a process to re-classify on product changes and tariff schedule updates?
