# Customs Valuation and Additions

Customs valuation determines the base on which duty is calculated. Under the WTO Valuation Agreement (incorporated into most national customs laws), the primary method is the transaction value — the price actually paid or payable for the goods, with specified additions and deductions. The judgment problem is that the "price paid" is not always the "invoice price," and the additions (assists, royalties, selling commissions, packing) are frequently overlooked or misunderstood. Wrong valuation understates or overstates duty, triggers audits, and can carry penalty exposure. This skill addresses the judgment involved in determining and substantiating the correct customs value.

## Core Rules

### Start with transaction value and determine if it can be used

The transaction value method is the primary and preferred method. It applies when:

- There is a sale for export to the country of importation;
- The price is not subject to disqualifying conditions (the value is not contingent on a condition that cannot be quantified);
- The buyer and seller are not related, or if related, the relationship did not influence the price;
- The buyer does not directly or indirectly receive any part of the proceeds of resale;
- Sufficient information is available to make required additions.

If any condition fails, transaction value cannot be used and you must proceed to the alternative methods in sequence. Document the determination that transaction value applies.

### Identify and add all statutory additions to the price actually paid

The transaction value is the price actually paid or payable plus specified additions. The most commonly missed additions are:

- **Assists**: tools, dies, moulds, engineering, design, or plans supplied by the buyer to the seller free of charge or at reduced cost, used in the production of the imported goods. Value the assist and add it.
- **Royalties and license fees**: amounts the buyer must pay, directly or indirectly, as a condition of sale of the imported goods, that are not included in the price. Add if they are a condition of sale.
- **Selling commissions**: commissions paid by the buyer to the seller's agent. Add. (Buying commissions, paid to the buyer's own agent, are not added.)
- **Packing**: costs of packing and labour for packing undertaken by the buyer. Add.
- **Proceeds of resale**: any part of the proceeds of resale that accrues to the seller. Add.

Build a checklist for these additions and apply it to every transaction. The omission of assists and royalties is the most common valuation error.

### Apply the correct deductions where permitted

Certain charges may be deducted from the transaction value if they are separately identified on the invoice:

- Freight, insurance, and other transport charges after importation (post-importation costs are not dutiable);
- Charges for construction, erection, assembly, or technical assistance after importation;
- Customs duty and taxes of the country of importation;
- Buying commissions.

Do not deduct charges that are not permitted (for example, pre-importation freight in jurisdictions where it is dutiable). Understand the jurisdiction-specific rules on freight and insurance treatment.

### Handle related-party transactions with care

When the buyer and seller are related (as defined in the Valuation Agreement), transaction value may still be used if the relationship did not influence the price. This requires demonstrating that the price was settled in a manner consistent with normal commercial practice, often through:

- Circumstances of sale analysis (the price was negotiated like an arm's-length deal);
- Test values (the transaction value closely approximates a previously accepted method — identical or similar goods, deductive or computed value).

Related-party valuation is complex and heavily scrutinized. Document the analysis and, for material relationships, consider a ruling or advance valuation arrangement.

### Apply alternative valuation methods in strict sequence only when required

If transaction value cannot be used, apply the alternative methods in this order, unless a permitted reversal of deductive and computed value applies:

1. Transaction value of identical goods;
2. Transaction value of similar goods;
3. Deductive value (based on resale price in the country of importation);
4. Computed value (based on cost of production plus profit and general expenses);
5. Fallback method (reasonable means consistent with the principles of the Agreement).

You may not skip a method unless you can demonstrate it cannot be used. Document why each prior method was rejected.

### Substantiate the valuation with documentation

Maintain for each entry:

- The commercial invoice and price;
- Assist valuation worksheets and the basis for the assist value;
- Royalty or license agreements and the determination that they are a condition of sale;
- Commission agreements;
- Any related-party circumstances-of-sale or test-value analysis;
- Alternative-method worksheets where used.

Valuation is auditable documentation. An unsupported value is a vulnerable value.

### Reconcile declared values with payment and accounting records

The declared customs value should reconcile with what was actually paid. Discrepancies between the declared value, the payment, and the accounting records are audit triggers. Implement reconciliation as a control.

## Common Traps

### Omitting assists from the customs value

The buyer supplies a mould or design to the foreign producer at no charge. The assist has value and must be added to the transaction value. This is the single most common valuation omission.

### Treating all royalties as non-dutiable

Royalties are dutiable if they are a condition of the sale of the imported goods. Royalties paid to a third party for the right to distribute may or may not be dutiable depending on whether they are a condition of sale. Analyze each royalty, do not assume.

### Confusing selling commissions with buying commissions

Selling commissions (paid to the seller's agent) are dutiable additions. Buying commissions (paid to the buyer's own agent) are not. Mischaracterizing a selling commission as a buying commission understates value.

### Failing to add resale proceeds accruing to the seller

If the seller receives a share of the buyer's resale revenue, that is part of the price actually paid and must be added. Omitting it understates value.

### Using transaction value for related parties without examining influence

Related-party transactions can use transaction value, but only if the relationship did not influence the price. Assuming it is fine because "they are separate legal entities" is insufficient. Document the analysis.

### Skipping alternative methods without justification

When transaction value fails, you cannot jump to a convenient alternative. Each method must be considered and rejected with reasons before moving on.

## Self-Check

- Did I confirm that transaction value applies (sale for export, no disqualifying conditions, relationship test if applicable)?
- Did I identify and add all statutory additions (assists, royalties as condition of sale, selling commissions, packing, resale proceeds)?
- Did I apply only permitted deductions, with jurisdiction-correct treatment of freight and insurance?
- For related-party transactions, did I document that the relationship did not influence the price (circumstances of sale or test values)?
- Where transaction value cannot be used, did I apply the alternative methods in strict sequence with documented rejection of each prior method?
- Is each valuation substantiated with retained documentation (invoice, assist worksheets, royalty analysis, commission agreements)?
- Does the declared value reconcile with payment and accounting records?
- Did I build and apply an additions checklist so that assists and royalties are not overlooked?
