# Speak-Up Culture And Reporting Promotion

A compliance program's ability to detect problems depends almost entirely on whether people are willing to raise them. Most misconduct is witnessed by someone before it is detected by any control, yet a large share of witnesses never report. They stay silent because they do not know the channels, do not trust the channels, fear retaliation, believe nothing will happen, or convince themselves it is not their concern. Promoting speak-up culture is therefore not a soft messaging exercise; it is the difference between a program that learns about risk early and one that discovers it through a regulator, a lawsuit, or the press. The work is to make reporting channels known, trusted, easy, and safe, and to make speaking up feel like a normal, valued part of how the organization operates.

Use this skill before designing a speak-up campaign, reviewing why reporting is low or skewed, improving channel awareness and trust, addressing populations that do not report, or responding to evidence that employees fear retaliation. The goal is to make the agent treat speak-up as a measurable cultural condition, not a poster slogan.

## Core Rules

### Diagnose Why People Are Not Speaking Up Before Campaigning

Low or skewed reporting is a symptom with multiple possible causes, and the right intervention depends on the cause. Campaigning about channels when the real problem is fear of retaliation will not move the number.

Diagnose by gathering evidence on:

- whether employees know the channels and how to use them;
- whether they trust that reports are taken seriously and acted on;
- whether they fear retaliation from managers or peers;
- whether they believe reporting is futile because nothing changes;
- whether certain populations, such as a region, function, or contractor group, report at much lower rates;
- whether past reports were handled slowly, dismissively, or leaked;
- whether the channels themselves are hard to use, inaccessible, or not anonymous where promised.

Use surveys, focus groups, exit interviews, and reporting data patterns to diagnose. Then target the actual barrier, not the assumed one.

### Make Channels Known, Multiple, And Accessible

Employees cannot use channels they do not know exist, and no single channel fits every situation or every employee. Promote a genuine choice of routes.

Ensure and promote:

- a hotline with phone and web options, available in relevant languages and time zones;
- a web or app-based reporting form;
- an email or compliance-contact route;
- the ability to report to a manager, with an alternative when the manager is involved;
- HR and other functional routes where appropriate;
- anonymous options where legally permitted, with clear explanation of their limits;
- access for contractors, suppliers, and third parties, not just employees;
- channels that work for non-desk and field staff, not only those with corporate email.

Communicate the channels repeatedly, in plain language, with examples of what to report. A single mention in onboarding is not awareness.

### Build Trust Through Visible Response

The strongest driver of future reporting is whether past reports were handled well. If employees see that reports disappear into a black hole, are dismissed, or result in retaliation, no campaign will overcome the distrust.

Build trust by:

- acknowledging reports promptly and explaining next steps;
- communicating outcomes back to reporters where possible, within confidentiality limits;
- acting visibly on substantiated concerns, including discipline and control fixes;
- closing the loop with the broader workforce through anonymized case summaries that show reports lead to action;
- tracking and reducing investigation cycle times;
- protecting reporters and witnesses from retaliation and acting on retaliation when it occurs.

Trust is earned through demonstrated behavior over time, not declared through messaging. If the organization cannot show that reporting works, fix the response before promoting the channel.

### Reduce Friction And Fear In The Reporting Experience

Even aware and trusting employees may not report if the experience is intimidating, cumbersome, or risky. Design the reporting experience to lower barriers.

Reduce friction by:

- making intake simple, with clear questions and no unnecessary fields;
- allowing reports in the reporter's preferred language;
- avoiding requiring the reporter to repeat their story to multiple people;
- providing realistic expectations about timeline and process;
- ensuring intake staff are trained, empathetic, and culturally competent;
- protecting the reporter's identity rigorously where anonymity is offered;
- making clear what confidentiality means and its limits.

Reduce fear by visibly enforcing non-retaliation, monitoring the experience of reporters after they report, and addressing retaliation decisively. A reporting channel that is technically available but socially or practically unusable is not a real channel.

### Normalize Speaking Up As Part Of The Culture

Reporting should not feel like an extraordinary act of courage. In a healthy culture, raising a concern is as normal as reporting a safety hazard or a system outage.

Normalize speaking up by:

- framing it as a professional responsibility and a sign of care for the organization;
- encouraging people to ask questions and raise concerns early, before issues escalate;
- recognizing and thanking those who raise concerns in good faith;
- having leaders and managers model speaking up and inviting challenge;
- distinguishing raising concerns from whistleblowing heroics, so everyday issues feel welcome;
- integrating speak-up into team norms, onboarding, and performance expectations.

The goal is a culture where the default is to speak, not to stay silent.

### Address Populations That Report Less

Reporting rates often vary sharply by geography, function, seniority, gender, and employment type. Aggregate numbers can hide populations that are effectively voiceless.

Identify and address low-reporting populations by:

- disaggregating reporting data and survey trust data by segment;
- understanding the specific barriers each population faces, which may include language, culture, local law, or manager behavior;
- tailoring channel design and promotion to those barriers;
- ensuring contractors, temps, and non-employees have real access;
- addressing cultural or legal contexts where reporting is especially risky, such as certain jurisdictions.

A program that reports strong aggregate numbers while a major population stays silent has a hidden vulnerability.

### Measure Speak-Up Health Beyond Hotline Volume

Hotline volume alone is ambiguous: low volume may mean few issues or low trust, and high volume may mean awareness or serious problems. Measure a balanced set of indicators.

Track:

- awareness of channels across populations;
- trust that reports are acted on, from surveys;
- fear of retaliation, from surveys;
- reporting volume and mix across channels;
- substantiation rates and cycle times;
- retaliation allegations and outcomes;
- repeat reporting, which can indicate trust or unresolved issues;
- the gap between witnessed misconduct and reported misconduct, the clearest single indicator of speak-up health.

Interpret trends in context and act on the signals, rather than celebrating or panicking over any single number.

### Coordinate With Leadership And Investigation Functions

Speak-up promotion fails if it is not backed by leadership commitment and a credible investigation function. Promoting reporting without the capacity to handle the resulting volume ethically and promptly creates a worse situation than low reporting.

Coordinate by ensuring:

- leaders visibly endorse and model speak-up;
- the investigation function has the capacity, independence, and competence for expected volume;
- intake, triage, and investigation handoffs are smooth;
- retaliation monitoring is built into the post-report process;
- lessons from reports feed back into training, controls, and communication.

Promotion must be matched by response capacity. Do not drive reporting into a system that cannot handle it.

## Common Traps

### Campaigning Without Diagnosing

Promoting channels when the barrier is fear or distrust wastes effort. Diagnose the real cause first.

### Single Channel Reliance

Relying on one route, especially the direct manager, misses those who cannot use it. Offer multiple accessible routes.

### Black-Hole Reporting

Reports that disappear destroy trust faster than no channel. Show visible response and close the loop.

### Intimidating Or Cumbersome Intake

A reporting experience that is hard, slow, or risky suppresses reports even when channels exist. Reduce friction and fear.

### Aggregate Numbers Hiding Silent Populations

Strong overall reporting can conceal a region or function that never reports. Disaggregate and address gaps.

### Hotline Volume Misinterpreted

Low or high volume is ambiguous without context. Interpret with awareness, trust, retaliation, and witnessed-versus-reported data.

### Promoting Without Response Capacity

Driving reporting into an under-resourced investigation function causes harm. Match promotion to capacity.

### Speak-Up As Heroics, Not Norm

If reporting feels like an extraordinary act, most people will not do it. Normalize everyday speaking up.

## Self-Check

- Has the cause of low or skewed reporting been diagnosed through surveys, focus groups, and data before campaigning?
- Are there multiple, accessible reporting channels, including anonymous options where legal, available to employees, contractors, and third parties in relevant languages?
- Is trust built through prompt acknowledgment, visible action on substantiated concerns, outcome communication, cycle-time reduction, and retaliation enforcement?
- Is the reporting experience low-friction and low-fear, with empathetic intake, identity protection, and clear confidentiality limits?
- Is speaking up normalized as a professional responsibility, modeled by leaders, and recognized rather than treated as heroics?
- Have low-reporting populations been identified through disaggregated data and addressed with tailored channel design and promotion?
- Is speak-up health measured across awareness, trust, retaliation fear, volume, substantiation, cycle time, retaliation allegations, and witnessed-versus-reported gaps?
- Are trends interpreted in context rather than celebrated or panicked over single numbers?
- Is speak-up promotion coordinated with visible leadership endorsement and a credible, resourced investigation function?
- Could the organization show that speaking up is known, trusted, easy, and safe across all populations, not merely that channels exist?
