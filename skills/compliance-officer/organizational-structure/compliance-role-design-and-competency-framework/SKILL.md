# Compliance Role Design And Competency Framework

Compliance roles are often designed backwards from the person who happens to hold them rather than forwards from the work the function must do. A job description is copied from a template, responsibilities accumulate through inertia, and the result is a set of positions whose actual duties bear little resemblance to their titles, whose required competencies are undefined, and whose career paths lead nowhere. The harm is that the function cannot recruit, develop, or retain the people it needs; high-potential professionals leave because there is no path; critical capabilities sit in one irreplaceable person; and the organization discovers, during a crisis or an audit, that the people in the roles were never actually equipped to do them. Role design and competency frameworks are not HR bureaucracy; they are how a compliance function builds the human capability that the entire program depends on, and they are increasingly examined by regulators who ask whether the function has the right people with the right competence for its risk.

Use this skill before designing or revising compliance roles, building a competency framework, defining career paths, recruiting for compliance positions, or diagnosing capability gaps. The goal is to make the agent derive roles from risk and work, define competencies concretely, and build paths that develop and retain capability rather than filling seats.

## Core Rules

### Derive Roles From Risk And Work, Not From Titles Or Precedent

The starting point for role design is what the function must accomplish against the organization's risk profile, not what roles already exist or what a template says. A role designed from precedent inherits the gaps of the predecessor; a role designed from work fits the actual need.

Derive roles by:

- starting from the risk assessment and the activities required to manage each material risk;
- mapping the work, such as screening, monitoring, advisory, investigation, training, reporting, and technology, that the function must perform;
- grouping work into roles based on coherent skill sets and accountability, not on historical accident;
- sizing each role against volume, complexity, and coverage requirements;
- identifying work that currently has no owner, which is a gap, and work duplicated across roles, which is waste.

A role whose description does not trace back to a risk or a required activity is filling a seat, not covering a need.

### Define Competencies Concretely, Including Behavioral And Technical

A competency framework that lists "communication" and "integrity" is too vague to develop or assess against. Competencies must be defined at a level that distinguishes strong from weak performance and that can be taught and measured.

Define competencies across:

- technical or domain competencies, such as knowledge of specific regulations, investigation technique, data analysis, and control design;
- functional competencies, such as risk assessment, policy drafting, testing, and reporting;
- behavioral competencies, such as the ability to challenge respectfully, hold a difficult line under pressure, and build trust with the business;
- leadership competencies for roles that manage teams or influence senior leaders;
- ethical judgment, the ability to navigate ambiguity and make defensible decisions under pressure.

For each competency, describe what proficient performance looks like, so that development and assessment are grounded rather than impressionistic.

### Distinguish Levels And Build Genuine Career Paths

Compliance functions lose talent when there is no path. If the only way to advance is to leave the function or become a manager, specialists stall, and the function loses depth. A competency framework must support progression in expertise as well as in management.

Build paths by:

- defining levels, from analyst through specialist, manager, director, and executive, with distinct competency expectations at each;
- creating a technical or expert track parallel to the management track, so deep specialists can advance without managing;
- mapping the competencies and experiences required to move between levels;
- providing lateral moves across risk domains and geographies to build breadth;
- making progression transparent so people can see and pursue the path.

A function with one path, into management, will lose its best specialists and concentrate expertise in too few people. Build depth through multiple paths.

### Identify And Close Capability Gaps Deliberately

Once roles and competencies are defined, the gap between what the function has and what it needs becomes visible. Ignoring the gap leaves the function unable to perform critical activities; closing it requires a deliberate plan.

Close gaps by:

- assessing current staff against the competency framework to identify strengths and gaps honestly;
- building development plans, training, certification, and stretch assignments to grow internal capability;
- recruiting externally for competencies that cannot be built in time, such as specialized technical or domain expertise;
- using co-sourcing or contractors for surge or specialized needs without creating permanent dependency;
- prioritizing gap closure by the risk the gap creates, not by what is easiest to fix.

A capability gap in a high-risk activity is a program risk. Name it, plan for it, and track closure.

### Design For Resilience Against Single Points Of Failure

A function where one person holds irreplaceable knowledge or access is fragile. Resilience must be designed into roles so that turnover, leave, or departure does not cripple a critical activity.

Design for resilience by:

- avoiding single points of failure where one person is the only one who can perform a critical task;
- cross-training and documentation so critical activities have backup coverage;
- succession planning for the CCO and for critical specialist roles;
- planning coverage for leave, turnover, and surge demand;
- capturing institutional knowledge in systems and procedures rather than only in people.

A function that depends on one irreplaceable person will fail when that person is unavailable, and regulators notice the dependency.

### Align Role Authority, Accountability, And Incentives

A role designed without the authority to do its work, or held accountable without the incentives to perform it, will not function. Authority, accountability, and incentives must be aligned within and across roles.

Align by:

- giving each role the authority, access, and decision rights its responsibilities require;
- holding each role accountable for outcomes within its control, not for activity it cannot influence;
- reflecting compliance performance in goals, ratings, and compensation for both compliance and business roles;
- ensuring business roles that depend on compliance cooperation are measured on that cooperation;
- avoiding incentives that reward the business for bypassing the compliance roles designed to constrain it.

A compliance role told to oversee a process it has no authority over, or a business role rewarded for speed regardless of compliance, is misaligned by design.

### Make The Framework Living And Maintained

A competency framework built once and never updated becomes fiction as risks, regulations, and technology change. The framework must be maintained as the function and its environment evolve.

Maintain by:

- reviewing roles and competencies on a schedule and after significant risk, regulatory, or technology change;
- updating competency expectations as new skills become critical, such as data and analytics capability;
- refreshing job descriptions so they match actual work;
- using exit interviews and performance data to identify where the framework is failing to develop or retain;
- tying the framework to recruitment, development, and succession so it is actually used.

## Common Traps

### Roles Designed From Precedent Or Template

A job description copied from elsewhere inherits gaps and bears little relation to the work. Derive roles from risk and required activities.

### Vague Competencies That Cannot Be Developed Or Assessed

"Communication" and "integrity" are too abstract to build or measure. Define competencies at a level that distinguishes performance.

### Single Management Career Path

If advancement requires becoming a manager, specialists stall and the function loses depth. Build a parallel technical or expert track.

### Unaddressed Capability Gaps

Gaps left unnamed and unplanned leave the function unable to perform critical activities. Assess, prioritize by risk, and close gaps deliberately.

### Single Points Of Failure

A function dependent on one irreplaceable person fails when that person is unavailable. Cross-train, document, and plan succession.

### Misaligned Authority, Accountability, And Incentives

A role without authority, or accountability without incentives, will not function. Align what each role must do with what it can and is rewarded for.

### Framework Built Once And Forgotten

A static framework becomes fiction as risks and technology change. Maintain it and tie it to recruitment, development, and succession.

## Self-Check

- Are roles derived from the risk assessment and required activities, grouped by coherent skill and accountability, sized to volume and complexity, with gaps and duplication identified?
- Are competencies defined concretely across technical, functional, behavioral, leadership, and ethical judgment dimensions, with descriptions of proficient performance?
- Are there multiple career paths, including a technical or expert track parallel to management, with transparent level expectations and lateral development opportunities?
- Are capability gaps assessed honestly against the framework, with development plans, external recruitment, and co-sourcing prioritized by the risk each gap creates?
- Is the function designed for resilience against single points of failure, through cross-training, documentation, succession planning, and surge coverage?
- Are role authority, accountability, and incentives aligned, with compliance performance reflected in goals and compensation for both compliance and business roles?
- Is the framework maintained on schedule and after change, with competencies updated for new skills, job descriptions refreshed, and the framework tied to recruitment, development, and succession?
- Could the role and competency design be defended to a regulator as equipping the function with the right people and competence for its risk?
