# Charitable Organization Due Diligence

Due diligence on a charitable organization is the control that separates genuine philanthropy from a payment that will be diverted, wasted, or used to launder an improper benefit. The difficulty is that charities, like any organization, vary enormously in integrity and effectiveness, and the surface indicators, a respected-sounding name, a registration number, a website, are easy to fabricate or to sustain while funds flow elsewhere. A charity may be entirely legitimate but financially unsound, with most donations consumed by administration and related parties. It may be a shell created specifically to receive a single improper payment. Or it may be genuine but operating in a way that exposes the donor to sanctions, terrorism-financing, or reputational risk. The compliance officer's job is to verify enough about the recipient to confirm that funds will reach a legitimate charitable purpose and that the recipient does not carry hidden risk.

Use this skill before conducting due diligence on a charitable recipient, verifying tax-exempt status, assessing governance and fund use, or evaluating a charity for sanctions or financial-crime risk. Due diligence on charitable recipients, particularly in cross-border or higher-risk contexts, should be proportionate to risk and may require specialist verification services or counsel. This skill provides a framework for the diligence process, not a legal opinion on any organization.

## Core Rules

### Verify Legal Status And Registration Independently

A charity's claim about its own status is not verification. Charities operate under legal frameworks that require registration, tax-exempt status, or recognition by a competent authority, and that status can be confirmed through independent sources rather than through the charity itself. A shell charity may cite a registration number that is fabricated, belongs to a different entity, or has been revoked.

Verify registration and tax-exempt status through the relevant government or regulatory registry, not through the charity's documents. Confirm that the entity name, registration number, and tax-exempt status are current and match the organization seeking the donation. For cross-border donations, verify status in the recipient's home jurisdiction and confirm whether the donation qualifies for any tax treatment the company expects. A charity whose status cannot be independently confirmed, or whose registration has lapsed or been revoked, should not receive funds until the discrepancy is resolved.

### Examine Governance For Independence And Conflicts

A charity's governance structure reveals whether it operates independently for a charitable purpose or whether it is controlled by parties with other interests. A charity whose directors, officers, or founders are all related to a single individual, a government official, or a commercial entity is not independent, and donations to it may benefit the controlling party rather than a charitable class.

Review the governance structure. Identify the directors, officers, founders, and any board or oversight body. Assess whether governance is independent or whether control is concentrated in parties connected to officials, commercial interests, or the donor's own counterparties. Screen leadership against sanctions lists, politically exposed person lists, and adverse media. A charity whose leadership is opaque, whose directors cannot be identified, or whose governance is dominated by a single interested party carries elevated risk and requires either deeper diligence or declination.

### Assess Financial Integrity And Fund Use

The most important diligence question is whether donated funds reach charitable programs or are consumed by administration, fundraising, related-party transactions, or diversion. A charity may be registered and governed acceptably but still direct most of its spending to overhead, to related parties, or to purposes that do not match its stated mission. Financial review reveals this.

Request and review financial statements or annual reports, proportionate to the donation size. Assess the ratio of program spending to administrative and fundraising costs. Identify any related-party transactions, where funds flow to entities or individuals connected to the charity's leadership. Look for indicators of financial distress or instability that could jeopardize fund use. For larger donations, consider independent verification through charity evaluators or specialist due diligence providers. A charity that cannot or will not provide financial information, or whose financials show heavy related-party or administrative spending, is a poor philanthropic vehicle and a potential diversion risk.

### Screen For Sanctions, Terrorism Financing, And Financial Crime Risk

Charities have been used, wittingly and unwittingly, to channel funds to sanctioned parties, terrorist organizations, and money laundering schemes. This is a recognized typology in financial crime compliance, and donors bear responsibility for not funding prohibited parties through charitable channels. A charity operating in or near sanctioned jurisdictions, in conflict zones, or with opaque cross-border fund flows carries elevated risk.

Screen the charity, its leadership, and its operating geography against relevant sanctions and terrorism lists. Assess whether the charity operates in or transfers funds to sanctioned or high-risk jurisdictions. Review whether the charity's programs and beneficiaries create any exposure to prohibited parties. For charities operating in higher-risk geographies, apply enhanced due diligence proportionate to the exposure. A charity that cannot explain its cross-border fund flows or that operates in sanctioned jurisdictions without clear, verifiable humanitarian purpose should not receive funds without specialist review.

### Confirm The Charitable Purpose Matches The Donation

A donation should serve a specific, identifiable charitable purpose, not merely support a general organization whose activities may be broad. Confirming the purpose ensures that funds are directed to programs consistent with the company's philanthropic intent and reduces the risk that funds are diverted to unrelated or improper activities within the charity.

Document the intended purpose of the donation and, for larger or higher-risk donations, obtain agreement that funds will be used for that purpose. Request reporting on how funds were used, proportionate to the donation size. A charity that resists purpose specification or fund-use reporting may have reasons that warrant deeper scrutiny. Purpose restriction is both a philanthropic discipline and a control against diversion.

### Apply Risk-Based Diligence Depth

Not every donation warrants the same diligence depth. A modest recurring donation to a well-known, independently evaluated charity in a low-risk jurisdiction requires less diligence than a large one-time donation to an obscure charity in a higher-risk region connected to pending business. Calibrate diligence to the donation size, the recipient's risk profile, the geography, and any government or business connection.

Define diligence tiers. Low-risk donations may require registration verification and basic screening. Medium-risk donations add governance and financial review. High-risk donations, those involving government connections, higher-risk geographies, large amounts, or opaque structures, require enhanced diligence including leadership screening, financial statement review, and possibly independent verification or counsel review. Applying uniform minimal diligence to all donations wastes effort on low-risk giving while under-protecting the donations that matter.

## Common Traps

### Trusting Self-Provided Status Documentation

Accepting a charity's own registration certificate or tax letter without independent verification through the relevant registry, missing fabricated or revoked status.

### Ignoring Governance Concentration

Overlooking that a charity's leadership is dominated by an official, a commercial party, or a single family, so donations benefit the controller rather than a charitable class.

### Skipping Financial Review

Donating based on mission and reputation without examining how funds are actually spent, missing high administrative ratios, related-party transactions, or diversion.

### Missing Sanctions And Terrorism-Financing Exposure

Failing to screen charities operating in or near sanctioned jurisdictions or with opaque cross-border flows, exposing the donor to financial-crime liability.

### Uniform Minimal Diligence

Applying the same shallow diligence to a small recurring gift and a large one-time donation to an obscure higher-risk charity, under-protecting the donations that warrant depth.

### No Purpose Restriction Or Fund-Use Reporting

Donating unrestricted funds without agreeing on purpose or requiring reporting, accepting opacity that could conceal diversion within an otherwise legitimate charity.

### Charity Refusing Transparency Accepted

Proceeding with a donation despite the charity's refusal to provide financial information or fund-use reporting, treating the refusal as a minor issue rather than a red flag.

## Self-Check

- Is registration and tax-exempt status verified through the relevant independent registry rather than through the charity's own documents?
- Is governance reviewed for independence, with leadership screened against sanctions, PEP, and adverse media, and control concentration flagged?
- Are financial statements or annual reports reviewed for program-to-administrative ratio, related-party transactions, and indicators of distress or diversion?
- Has the charity, its leadership, and its operating geography been screened for sanctions and terrorism-financing exposure, with enhanced diligence for higher-risk regions?
- Is the donation tied to a specific charitable purpose with agreement on fund use and reporting proportionate to size?
- Is diligence depth calibrated to donation size, recipient risk, geography, and any government or business connection, with defined tiers rather than uniform application?
- For cross-border donations, is status verified in the recipient's home jurisdiction and are cross-border fund flows understood?
- Would the diligence file support a defense that the donor verified the recipient's legitimacy and fund use before donating?
- Are charities that refuse financial transparency or fund-use reporting declined or subjected to enhanced scrutiny rather than approved by default?
- Is the distinction between a charity that is legitimate on paper and one that uses funds effectively reflected in the diligence depth?
