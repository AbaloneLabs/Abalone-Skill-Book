# Charitable Donation Approval And Anti-Bribery Risk

Corporate charitable donations occupy a dangerous intersection between legitimate philanthropy and corruption risk. A donation to a recognized charity that serves a genuine social purpose is proper and often admirable. A donation to a charity controlled by, affiliated with, or beneficial to a government official whose decisions affect the company is a disguised bribe, and anti-corruption regulators have pursued exactly this pattern repeatedly. The compliance challenge is that the same payment, to the same type of entity, can be either, and the distinguishing facts, the recipient's connections, the timing, the solicitation source, and the business context, are easy to overlook when the transaction carries the socially favorable label of charity. A donation that no one would approve as a direct payment to an official can slip through as a charitable contribution because the label disarms scrutiny.

Use this skill before approving a charitable donation, conducting due diligence on a charitable recipient, or designing charitable contribution controls. Donations involving foreign officials carry exposure under the FCPA, UK Bribery Act, and other anti-corruption laws; such donations require legal review. This skill provides compliance judgment for identifying and managing the risk, not a determination of legality.

## Core Rules

### Screen Every Donation For Connections To Government Officials

The single highest-risk feature of a charitable donation is a connection between the recipient charity and a government official. Officials, their family members, their political parties, and their associates frequently establish or control charitable foundations, and a donation to such a foundation is functionally a benefit to the official. Under anti-corruption law, anything of value given to benefit an official can constitute a bribe, and a donation to an official's charity is a well-established vehicle for indirect bribery.

Screen every proposed recipient for connections to government officials before approval. Identify the charity's founders, directors, officers, and significant beneficiaries. Determine whether any government official, candidate, political party, or their family members are affiliated with the charity in any capacity. Determine whether the charity operates in a jurisdiction where the company has pending or potential business with the government. If a connection exists, the donation requires heightened scrutiny and in most cases should be declined unless an independent legitimate philanthropic purpose is clearly established and documented with counsel review.

### Assess The Solicitation Source And Business Context

Who solicited the donation and why are facts that transform the analysis. A donation solicited by a government official, by a person seeking to influence an official, or in the context of pending official action carries corruption risk that a donation emerging from an independent philanthropic review does not. A donation requested by a minister whose agency is reviewing the company's license is presumptively an improper payment regardless of the charity's legitimacy.

Assess the solicitation source. Was the donation requested by an official, by a consultant or intermediary, or identified through an independent philanthropic process? Assess the timing relative to pending business. Is any license, contract, regulatory matter, or procurement decision pending with a government that the charity or its affiliates could influence? If the donation is connected to pending or potential official action, decline it regardless of the charity's status, because the context creates the inference of improper influence that defines bribery.

### Require Due Diligence On The Charitable Recipient

A charity's name and tax status are not sufficient evidence of legitimacy. Shell charities, charities that funnel most funds to administrative or related-party expenses, and charities that serve as pass-throughs for improper payments exist in every jurisdiction. Due diligence must establish that the charity is a genuine operating organization, that it pursues a legitimate charitable purpose, that it has independent governance, and that funds will be used for the stated purpose rather than diverted.

Conduct due diligence proportionate to the donation size and risk. Verify the charity's registration or tax-exempt status in its jurisdiction. Review its governance, leadership, and public reporting. Screen its leadership and beneficial controllers against sanctions and politically exposed person lists. For larger or higher-risk donations, review financial statements to confirm that funds reach charitable programs rather than administrative or related-party channels. A donation made without any verification of the recipient relies entirely on the label and is not defensible.

### Centralize Approval And Prohibit Decentralized Giving

Charitable donations must not be made at the discretion of country managers, business development teams, or individual employees, because decentralized giving is the channel through which influence-seeking donations most often flow. A country manager who donates to an official's charity to smooth a pending license is making a charitable contribution in form and a bribe in substance, and centralized oversight is the control that catches it.

Centralize charitable donation approval in a designated function, typically corporate foundation, legal, or compliance, with a defined approval process that includes connection screening, due diligence, and business context assessment. Require that all corporate charitable contributions, regardless of size or source, flow through the central process. Monitor expense reports and local budgets for donations made outside the process, because decentralized giving often appears as miscellaneous local expenditures rather than formal donations.

### Restrict And Structure Donations In Higher-Risk Contexts

Certain donation contexts carry elevated risk and require either prohibition or stringent structuring. Donations to charities in countries where the company is pursuing government business, donations solicited in connection with a pending decision, donations to charities with political affiliations, and donations that benefit a specific individual rather than a general charitable class all warrant heightened controls.

For donations that proceed in higher-risk contexts, impose structural safeguards. Require that the donation be made through a written agreement specifying the charitable purpose and prohibiting use of funds for any political or personal benefit. Require reporting on the use of funds. Consider making the donation through an independent corporate foundation or a recognized intermediary rather than directly. Decline any donation where the recipient will not agree to transparency on fund use, because opacity about how funds are used is itself a red flag.

### Record And Disclose Charitable Contributions Transparently

Charitable contributions must be recorded accurately in their true character and, where required, disclosed. Disguising a donation as a sponsorship, a consulting fee, or a marketing expense is a books-and-records violation that often proves more damaging than the underlying donation. The general ledger should reflect the donation as a charitable contribution, with the recipient and purpose identified.

Maintain a register of all charitable contributions that can support transparency reporting and that reconciles to the general ledger. For donations involving any government connection, maintain documentation of the due diligence, the approval analysis, and the basis for proceeding. A register that cannot produce a complete account of charitable giving, especially giving with any official connection, cannot withstand regulatory or audit scrutiny.

## Common Traps

### Assuming The Charitable Label Disarms Scrutiny

Treating a donation as low-risk because it is to a charity, missing that the charity is connected to an official whose decisions affect the company.

### Missing The Official Connection

Failing to identify that a charity's founder, director, or beneficiary is a government official, family member, or political affiliate, so a disguised bribe is approved as philanthropy.

### Ignoring The Solicitation Source And Timing

Approving a donation solicited by an official or made while government business is pending, where the context creates the inference of improper influence.

### No Due Diligence On The Recipient

Relying on the charity's name and tax status without verifying governance, financial integrity, or fund use, leaving the donation vulnerable to diversion.

### Decentralized Giving

Allowing country managers or business units to make donations locally, the channel through which influence-seeking donations flow undetected.

### Mischaracterizing The Donation In Records

Recording an official-connected donation as a sponsorship or marketing expense, creating a concealment problem layered on the corruption risk.

### No Transparency On Fund Use

Making a donation without requiring reporting on how funds are used, accepting opacity that would conceal diversion to improper purposes.

## Self-Check

- Is every proposed charitable recipient screened for connections to government officials, candidates, political parties, or their family members before approval?
- Is the solicitation source identified and the timing assessed relative to pending government business, with donations connected to pending action declined regardless of charity legitimacy?
- Is due diligence conducted on the recipient proportionate to size and risk, verifying registration, governance, leadership screening, and fund use?
- Is approval centralized in a designated function with a defined process, with monitoring for donations made outside the process through local budgets or expense reports?
- Are higher-risk donations, those in countries with pending government business, politically affiliated, or individually beneficial, either prohibited or structured with written agreements and fund-use reporting?
- Are donations recorded accurately as charitable contributions in the general ledger rather than disguised under other expense categories?
- Is a charitable contributions register maintained that reconciles to the general ledger and can support transparency reporting?
- For donations with any government connection, is the due diligence, approval analysis, and basis documented so the decision could be defended if questioned?
- Would the donation withstand disclosure, meaning it could be publicly reported without creating an inference of improper influence?
- Are recipients that refuse transparency on fund use declined, recognizing that opacity is itself a red flag?
