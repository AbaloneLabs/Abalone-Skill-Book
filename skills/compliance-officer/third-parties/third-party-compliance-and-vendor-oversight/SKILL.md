# Third Party Compliance And Vendor Oversight

Third parties can create compliance risk even when the organization itself never touches the problematic transaction. Vendors, agents, distributors, resellers, brokers, consultants, processors, affiliates, outsourced service providers, lobbyists, recruiters, and implementation partners may interact with customers, regulators, data, money, government officials, patients, employees, or sensitive systems. A contract clause is not enough if the business cannot show why the third party was chosen, what risk it creates, how it was screened, what it is allowed to do, and how it is monitored.

Use this skill before onboarding a third party, reviewing vendor compliance, drafting due diligence requirements, assessing reseller or agent risk, approving high-risk payments, changing a vendor scope, or deciding whether to suspend or terminate a third-party relationship. The goal is to force a risk-based lifecycle rather than one-time onboarding.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Third Party's Actual Role

Third-party risk depends on what the third party actually does, not only its category label.

Map:

- services provided;
- jurisdictions;
- customer interactions;
- government interactions;
- access to funds;
- access to personal or confidential data;
- access to systems;
- subcontractors;
- authority to bind the company;
- sales or referral activity;
- licensing or professional status;
- compensation model;
- contract value;
- duration;
- prior relationship;
- replacement difficulty.

A low-dollar consultant can be high risk if they interact with government officials or sensitive data. A high-dollar infrastructure vendor may be lower compliance risk in some areas but high operational risk.

### Risk-Rate Before Onboarding

Due diligence should match risk. Do not require the same review for every vendor, and do not skip review because the business wants speed.

Risk factors include:

- country or jurisdiction risk;
- government touchpoints;
- anti-bribery exposure;
- sanctions or export exposure;
- data access;
- financial services or health information;
- child or consumer data;
- labor and human rights exposure;
- environmental or safety exposure;
- subcontracting;
- cash or commission payments;
- unusual pricing;
- political connections;
- adverse media;
- prior misconduct;
- conflict of interest;
- urgency or sole-source request.

Document the rating and rationale. If risk is high, require enhanced due diligence and compliance approval.

### Verify Business Rationale And Beneficial Ownership

The organization should know why the third party is needed and who stands behind it.

Confirm:

- business need;
- selection rationale;
- alternatives considered;
- ownership and control;
- management identity;
- related-party connections;
- employee or government official relationships;
- qualifications;
- licenses;
- references;
- financial stability;
- litigation or enforcement history;
- reputation;
- location and operational capability.

If the third party cannot explain what it will do, who owns it, or why compensation is reasonable, stop and escalate.

### Contract For Compliance Obligations

Contracts should convert due diligence conclusions into enforceable obligations.

Include where appropriate:

- precise scope of services;
- compensation and payment terms;
- compliance with applicable laws;
- anti-bribery and sanctions clauses;
- data protection and security obligations;
- confidentiality;
- audit rights;
- records retention;
- subcontractor restrictions;
- training or certification requirements;
- reporting obligations;
- incident notice;
- cooperation with investigations;
- right to suspend or terminate;
- indemnity;
- flow-down obligations.

Do not rely on broad boilerplate if the third party handles regulated data, government interactions, funds, high-risk sales, or critical operations.

### Monitor Throughout The Relationship

Third-party oversight is not complete at onboarding. Risk changes when scope, geography, personnel, ownership, systems, complaints, payments, or law changes.

Monitor:

- invoices against contract scope;
- unusual payment requests;
- performance against service obligations;
- new subcontractors;
- ownership changes;
- adverse media;
- sanctions changes;
- complaints;
- audit results;
- data incidents;
- training completion;
- certifications;
- policy exceptions;
- renewed due diligence;
- contract renewal.

Higher-risk third parties need more frequent and deeper monitoring. Do not let renewals auto-approve without review.

### Review Payment And Compensation Red Flags

Payments can reveal misconduct. Compliance should review unusual payment structures before approval.

Watch for:

- excessive commissions;
- success fees without clear basis;
- cash payments;
- payments to offshore accounts;
- payments to unrelated parties;
- split invoices;
- vague descriptions;
- round-dollar invoices;
- urgent payment pressure;
- charitable or political contributions;
- travel or gift reimbursements;
- payments outside contract terms;
- prepayment without deliverables.

Tie payment approval to evidence of actual services. DOJ guidance emphasizes whether the third party is actually performing work and whether compensation is commensurate with services.

### Control Data And Security Risk

Third parties that handle data or systems need specific controls. General confidentiality terms are not enough.

Review:

- data types;
- systems accessed;
- authentication;
- access least privilege;
- encryption;
- retention;
- deletion;
- breach notice;
- subcontractor access;
- security assessments;
- audit reports;
- incident response coordination;
- cross-border transfer;
- return or destruction at termination.

FTC guidance repeatedly emphasizes data minimization, access control, vendor expectations, and verification. If sensitive data is involved, coordinate with privacy, security, and legal.

### Escalate And Remediate Red Flags

Red flags should be tracked to resolution. Ignoring a red flag can be worse than not finding it.

For each red flag, document:

- issue;
- source;
- risk area;
- owner;
- immediate action;
- evidence requested;
- business response;
- compliance decision;
- approval conditions;
- monitoring requirement;
- suspension or termination decision.

Do not approve by silence. If the business accepts residual risk, record who approved and what controls were required.

### Manage Offboarding And Termination

Ending a third-party relationship can create compliance obligations.

Check:

- termination rights;
- notice requirements;
- final invoices;
- data return or deletion;
- system access removal;
- equipment return;
- record retention;
- customer transition;
- subcontractor closure;
- regulator or customer notice;
- investigation preservation;
- non-rehire flag if terminated for compliance reasons.

If a third party failed due diligence or was terminated for compliance reasons, prevent informal re-engagement through another business unit.

## Common Traps

### Treating Vendor Onboarding As Procurement Only

Procurement may manage price and process, but compliance must identify bribery, sanctions, data, labor, privacy, safety, and regulatory risks where relevant.

### Applying The Same Due Diligence To Everyone

One-size diligence wastes resources on low-risk vendors and misses high-risk relationships. Use risk tiers.

### Ignoring Subcontractors

Critical work may be performed by subcontractors the organization never reviewed. Contract for disclosure and flow-down obligations.

### Accepting Vague Invoices

"Consulting services" is not enough for high-risk payments. Require evidence of actual services and business purpose.

### Letting Business Urgency Override Red Flags

Urgency is not a control. If a red flag is unresolved, use interim controls or senior approval rather than quiet approval.

### Forgetting Renewals

Renewals often extend risk without fresh review. Use renewal as a reassessment point.

### Losing Track Of Rejected Third Parties

A rejected or terminated third party may return through another employee, affiliate, reseller, or renamed entity. Keep records and screening controls.

## Self-Check

- Is the third party's actual role, jurisdiction, access, customer contact, government touchpoint, data use, subcontracting, and compensation model clear?
- Is risk rated before onboarding with documented rationale?
- Are business rationale, ownership, control, related parties, qualifications, licenses, financial stability, and adverse history verified?
- Does the contract include specific compliance, data, audit, record, incident, subcontractor, training, suspension, and termination obligations where needed?
- Is ongoing monitoring tied to risk level, renewals, complaints, payments, ownership changes, incidents, and law changes?
- Are payment red flags reviewed against contract scope and evidence of actual services?
- Are privacy, security, data retention, access control, encryption, breach notice, and deletion obligations handled for data vendors?
- Are red flags tracked to evidence, decision, conditions, and residual risk approval?
- Are offboarding obligations, access removal, data return, retention, final payments, and non-rehire controls completed?
- Does the process prevent a rejected or terminated third party from being re-engaged informally?
