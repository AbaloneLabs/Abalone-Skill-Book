# High-Risk Function Training Design For Finance And Treasury

Finance and treasury functions control the organization's money flows, and money flows are where financial crime, sanctions evasion, fraud, and books-and-records violations are realized. These are the people who approve payments, change vendor bank details, execute treasury transfers, recognize revenue under period-end pressure, maintain the books, and screen counterparties against sanctions and watchlists. A single wrong approval, a bypassed control, or a pressured revenue recognition decision can create regulatory liability, financial loss, and integrity failures that surface only in an investigation. Training this population with generic compliance content is not risk-based, because the generic module never addresses the specific decisions, the specific pressures, and the specific manipulation techniques that target finance and treasury staff.

The harm from generic finance training is that the controls exist on paper but the people operating them do not recognize the schemes designed to defeat them. A payment approver who has never trained on vendor bank-detail fraud will approve the change. A treasury analyst who has never trained on sanctions evasion typologies will execute the transfer. An accountant under period-end pressure who has never trained on revenue recognition manipulation will make the adjusting entry. Use this skill before designing or commissioning finance-specific compliance training, responding to a finance-function incident, or defending finance training depth.

## Core Rules

### Map The Finance Function's Specific Risk Profile Before Designing

Before designing, map the decisions finance and treasury staff make that carry compliance risk: approving payments and expense reimbursements, changing vendor bank details, executing treasury and intercompany transfers, recognizing revenue at period-end, maintaining books and records, screening counterparties against sanctions and watchlists, handling cash and virtual assets, and responding to internal or external pressure to adjust figures. Map the regulatory regimes governing those decisions, the systems and data they control, the manipulation techniques that target them, the pressures that push toward misconduct, and the historical incidents and audit findings in the function.

This profile is the design brief. Finance risk is specific and technical, and a module built without this mapping will be too generic to prepare staff for the schemes they will actually encounter.

### Build Scenarios Around Real Finance Decisions And Fraud Typologies

Finance-targeted training must use scenarios that resemble the work and the threats. Build scenarios around: a vendor bank-detail change request that may be business email compromise, a payment approval that bypasses segregation of duties, a treasury transfer to a counterparty with ambiguous sanctions exposure, a revenue recognition adjustment under period-close pressure, an expense reimbursement that may mask a bribe, an intercompany transfer designed to obscure beneficial ownership, or a request from a senior leader to accelerate or defer recognition.

Include the manipulation techniques that target finance staff specifically: social engineering, pressure to bypass controls for urgency, instructions that circumvent the normal approval chain, and rationalizations that small adjustments do not matter. Sanitize scenarios from real internal cases where possible, because recognizable situations drive engagement and retention.

### Address Period-End And Performance Pressure Honestly

Finance misconduct is frequently driven by pressure: period-close deadlines, earnings targets, budget constraints, and senior leadership expectations. Training that teaches the rules without acknowledging these pressures will not hold when the pressure is real. Name the pressure, show how it creates the exact manipulation the rule prevents, teach the legitimate paths to meet the deadline or target without violating the rule, and explain the escalation and support available when pressure conflicts with integrity.

If the performance management or compensation system rewards finance staff for outcomes that require integrity compromises to achieve, flag the misalignment to leadership. Training cannot overcome an incentive structure that rewards the wrong behavior.

### Include Sanctions, AML, And Financial Crime Typologies Specifically

Finance and treasury staff are the front line against sanctions evasion, money laundering, and financial crime, yet they are often trained on these topics at a superficial awareness level. Effective finance-targeted training must address the specific typologies they will encounter: structuring and smurfing patterns, unusual payment routing through high-risk jurisdictions, rapid in-and-out account activity, mismatched payment purposes and counterparty profiles, sanctions ownership and control exposure through hidden beneficial owners, and virtual asset and emerging-channel risk.

Teach staff not just the rules but the recognition skills: what makes a transaction or counterparty suspicious, what to do when a red flag appears, and how to escalate without tipping off the subject. For screening roles, include scenario-based training on match handling, false positive reduction, and true positive escalation.

### Cover Books-And-Records Integrity And Revenue Recognition

Books-and-records integrity is a foundational compliance obligation, and it is where finance pressure most often produces misconduct. Training must address the specific integrity risks: premature revenue recognition, reserve and accrual manipulation, related-party transaction concealment, expense capitalization misclassification, and the documentation and approval standards that make the books defensible. These are technical topics, and generic ethics training does not cover them.

Build scenarios around the judgment calls that integrity violations hide in: a deal that could be recognized this quarter or next, a reserve that could be released to smooth earnings, an expense that could be capitalized or expensed. Teach the correct accounting treatment, the documentation required, and the escalation path when the correct treatment conflicts with the desired outcome.

### Engage Finance Leadership As Co-Designer And Co-Deliverer

Finance-targeted training lands best when finance leadership visibly owns it. Compliance-designed content delivered without finance sponsorship is dismissed as not understanding the technical reality. Engage finance leaders by co-designing scenarios with controllership, treasury, and accounts payable leadership, having the CFO or controller introduce or co-deliver key sessions, equipping finance managers with talking points, tying training to finance performance and control metrics, and sharing sanitized real examples.

Finance leader involvement signals that the training reflects the function's technical reality and its real pressures, not just compliance's priorities.

### Design Lifecycle Touchpoints And Control-Integrated Prompts

Finance functions have specific risk moments: period-close, audit preparation, new system implementations, and new product or entity launches. A single annual module does not protect a function whose risk concentrates at specific times. Design touchpoints that include onboarding before exposure to payment or accounting systems, promotion-triggered training when an individual gains new approval authority, event-triggered training after control changes or incidents, period-close reinforcement through workflow prompts in the ERP or payment system, and annual refreshers calibrated to the function's risk.

Embed point-of-risk prompts in the finance systems themselves: a reminder of the bank-detail-change verification protocol at the point of change, a sanctions screening prompt at the point of payment, a revenue recognition checklist at the point of close. These prompts reach the decision at the moment it is made.

### Verify With Finance-Specific Technical Assessments

A generic assessment cannot verify that a payment approver or treasury analyst can apply the rule to their decisions. Build assessments from finance-specific scenarios with distractors that reflect common real mistakes: approving a payment that bypassed segregation of duties, accepting a bank-detail change without independent verification, executing a transfer with ambiguous sanctions exposure, or recognizing revenue that does not meet the criteria. Require choosing an action and an escalation, and provide feedback explaining the technical reasoning.

For the highest-risk finance roles, supplement with simulation, case discussion, or manager sign-off. Consider certification or qualification requirements for roles with specific regulatory obligations, such as sanctions screening or AML monitoring.

## Common Traps

### Generic Ethics Module For A Finance Function

Treating finance like the general workforce under-trains the technical and financial-crime exposure. Tailor to the function's decisions.

### Scenarios That Do Not Resemble Finance Work

Learners dismiss content that does not look like their systems and processes. Use sanitized real cases.

### Ignoring Period-End And Performance Pressure

If training pretends close pressure does not exist, it will not change behavior. Address pressure and legitimate alternatives.

### Superficial Sanctions And AML Awareness

Finance staff need recognition skills for specific typologies, not general awareness. Train on the schemes they will encounter.

### Missing Books-And-Records And Revenue Recognition Integrity

These technical integrity risks are where pressure produces misconduct. Cover them specifically.

### Compliance-Only Delivery Without Finance Sponsorship

Training without finance leadership ownership is dismissed. Co-design and co-deliver with finance leaders.

### No Control-Integrated Prompts

Annual training is too far from the decision. Embed prompts in the ERP and payment systems.

## Self-Check

- Has the finance function's risk profile been mapped to its decisions, regulations, systems, manipulation techniques, pressures, and incidents before design?
- Are scenarios built around genuine finance decisions and fraud typologies, including payment approvals, bank-detail changes, treasury transfers, and revenue recognition?
- Does training honestly address period-end and performance pressure, teach legitimate alternatives, and flag incentive misalignment?
- Are sanctions, AML, and financial crime typologies trained at a recognition level, not just awareness, including specific schemes and escalation without tipping off?
- Are books-and-records integrity and revenue recognition covered as technical topics with correct treatment, documentation, and escalation?
- Are finance leaders engaged as co-designers and co-deliverers, with managers equipped to reinforce the training?
- Are lifecycle touchpoints and control-integrated prompts planned for period-close, system changes, and point-of-risk moments in the ERP and payment systems?
- Are assessments finance-specific and technical, with plausible distractors and supplemented by simulation or certification for the highest-risk roles?
- Could the organization defend, to a regulator, that the finance function received depth and scenarios matched to its specific exposure?
