# Customs And Trade Compliance Controls

Customs compliance governs how goods enter and leave a country: how they are classified, valued, and described, what origin is claimed, what duties are owed, and what records are kept. It is distinct from export controls and sanctions, but it overlaps with them at the border, and failures carry their own penalty regimes. Misclassification, undervaluation, false origin claims, and inadequate records can trigger duty assessments, penalties, and, in serious cases, criminal exposure. The prior disclosure mechanism offers mitigation for certain violations, but only when used correctly and promptly. Customs work is where trade compliance meets revenue, and the controls must be precise.

Use this skill before classifying goods under the Harmonized Tariff Schedule, determining valuation, establishing origin, claiming free trade agreement preferences, designing customs recordkeeping, or addressing penalties and prior disclosure. The compliance officer must drive each determination from the facts and the applicable rules, not from the broker's default.

## Core Rules

### Classify Goods Accurately Under The HTS

Tariff classification determines the duty rate, the applicable trade remedies, and the statistical reporting. It must be grounded in the General Rules of Interpretation (GRIs).

- apply the GRIs in order to determine the correct heading and subheading;
- classify based on the item's objective characteristics, not on the desired duty rate;
- consider the item's composition, function, and essential character;
- for sets and composite goods, apply the specific GRI for those cases;
- document the classification rationale, because customs can challenge it years later.

Classification is not a matter of choosing the lowest-rate heading that plausibly fits. It must reflect the correct analysis.

### Determine Valuation By The Applicable Method

Customs valuation determines the value on which duty is assessed. The primary method is transaction value, with fallback methods in sequence.

- transaction value is the price actually paid or payable for the goods, plus specified additions;
- additions include assists, royalties, packing, and selling commissions to the extent required;
- certain deductions and exclusions apply, such as international freight and duties;
- if transaction value cannot be used, apply the sequential fallback methods: identical or similar merchandise, deductive, computed, and derived value;
- related-party transactions require scrutiny to confirm the relationship did not influence the price.

Undervaluation, whether through omitted additions or understated price, creates duty exposure and penalty risk.

### Establish Country Of Origin Correctly

Origin determines eligibility for trade preference programs, marking requirements, and the application of trade remedies and sanctions.

- for non-preference origin, the item is origin of the country where it was wholly obtained or last substantially transformed;
- substantial transformation is a facts-and-circumstances test focused on whether a new and different article of commerce emerged;
- for preference origin under free trade agreements, apply the specific rule of origin in the agreement, often tariff-shift or regional-value-content based;
- origin claims must be supported by documentation and, where required, certifications;
- false origin claims, particularly to evade trade remedies, carry serious penalties.

Do not assume origin from where the goods were shipped or assembled. Apply the substantial transformation or agreement-specific test.

### Apply Free Trade Agreement Preferences With Support

FTA preferences reduce or eliminate duties, but they require compliance with the agreement's rules and documentation.

- confirm the goods meet the agreement's rule of origin;
- obtain and retain the required certification or claim of origin;
- ensure the claim is supported by records demonstrating origin;
- comply with any transit, direct shipment, or documentation requirements;
- be alert to origin claims that cannot be substantiated, which create preference recovery and penalty risk.

A preference claimed without supporting documentation is a liability waiting to be assessed.

### Manage Customs Recordkeeping

Customs requires records of entries, classifications, valuations, origin claims, and supporting documentation, retained for a defined period.

- retain entry packages, invoices, classifications, valuation analyses, origin certifications, and broker communications;
- the US retention period is generally five years from the date of entry;
- records must be producible in customs audits and investigations;
- maintain a recordkeeping system that can reconstruct each entry.

### Address Penalties Through The Correct Mechanism

Customs violations can result in various penalties depending on intent and severity.

- negligence penalties for failure to exercise reasonable care;
- gross negligence penalties for higher culpability;
- fraud penalties for intentional false statements or omissions;
- prior disclosure offers mitigation for certain violations when made voluntarily and before customs discovers the violation.

Prior disclosure is time-sensitive and must be made before customs has begun an investigation of the specific violation. It requires tendering the lost duties and cooperating with the investigation. Engage counsel before submitting.

### Exercise Reasonable Care

US customs law imposes a standard of reasonable care on importers. The importer is responsible for the accuracy of entries even when a broker files them.

- review broker filings for accuracy;
- implement classification, valuation, and origin procedures;
- conduct internal reviews and reconciliations;
- maintain a compliance program addressing customs obligations;
- document the reasonable care exercised.

Delegating to a broker does not transfer the reasonable care obligation.

## Common Traps

### Classifying For The Lowest Rate

Classification must reflect the correct GRI analysis. Choosing the lowest plausible rate invites challenges and penalties.

### Omitting Valuation Additions

Assists, royalties, and selling commissions are commonly omitted, understating value and duty. Include all required additions.

### Assuming Origin From Shipping Or Assembly Location

Origin turns on substantial transformation or agreement-specific rules, not on where goods were shipped from or last assembled.

### Claiming FTA Preferences Without Supporting Documentation

A preference without origin records creates recovery and penalty risk. Documentation must support every claim.

### Delegating Customs To The Broker And Stopping

The importer retains the reasonable care obligation. Broker filings must be reviewed for accuracy.

### Failing To Retain Records For The Required Period

Records must be retained and producible. Missing records undermine any defense in audit.

### Missing The Prior Disclosure Window

Prior disclosure must precede the start of a customs investigation of the specific violation. Delay forfeits mitigation.

### Treating Customs As Separate From Export Controls And Sanctions

Customs entries can surface export control and sanctions issues. The border is where these regimes intersect.

## Self-Check

- Is tariff classification determined by applying the GRIs in order based on objective characteristics, not on the desired duty rate?
- Is valuation determined using transaction value with all required additions, or the correct fallback method, with related-party transactions scrutinized?
- Is country of origin established by substantial transformation or agreement-specific rules, not assumed from shipping or assembly location?
- Are FTA preferences supported by certifications and origin documentation sufficient to substantiate the claim?
- Are customs records retained for the required period (five years from entry in the US) and producible in audit?
- Is the reasonable care obligation met through broker review, procedures, internal review, and a documented compliance program?
- Are penalties assessed by culpability level (negligence, gross negligence, fraud) with prior disclosure considered before customs begins investigation?
- Is prior disclosure made promptly with tender of lost duties and cooperation, recognizing the time-sensitive window?
- Are broker filings reviewed for accuracy rather than accepted by default?
- Are customs entries cross-checked for export control, sanctions, and restricted-party exposure at the border?
