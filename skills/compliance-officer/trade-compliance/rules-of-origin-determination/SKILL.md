# Rules of Origin Determination

Determining the country of origin of a good is one of the most consequential and most error-prone judgments in trade compliance. Origin drives tariff rates, antidumping and countervailing duty exposure, marking requirements, quota eligibility, government procurement eligibility, and free trade agreement preference. In modern global supply chains, where raw materials, components, processing, and assembly span many countries, origin is rarely obvious. A wrong origin determination can mean lost preference (overpaying duty), wrongful preference claims (underpaying duty and facing penalties), or incorrect marking that blocks goods at the border. This skill addresses the judgment involved in determining and substantiating origin.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish non-preferential origin from preferential origin

These are two different legal questions with different rules:

- **Non-preferential origin** determines the country of origin for marking, most-favoured-nation duty rates, quotas, antidumping, and trade statistics. It is typically determined by where the good underwent its "last substantial transformation."
- **Preferential origin** determines eligibility for reduced or zero duty under a specific free trade agreement or preference scheme. Each agreement has its own product-specific rules (tariff shift, regional value content, or specific processing requirements).

A good can have different origins for preferential and non-preferential purposes. Never conflate them. Determine which question you are answering before applying any rule.

### Apply the substantial transformation test rigorously for non-preferential origin

Where a good is wholly obtained in one country, origin is straightforward. Where inputs from multiple countries are combined, the decisive test (under US law and many others) is where the last substantial transformation occurred. A substantial transformation means a manufacturing or processing operation that results in a new and different article of commerce having a distinctive name, character, or use. Mere assembly, packaging, repackaging, or minor processing generally does not constitute substantial transformation. Document:

- The country of origin of each material input;
- The processing operations performed in each country;
- Whether the processing created a new article with a distinctive name, character, or use;
- The cost, skill, and time involved (relevant but not determinative).

Borderline cases (assembly that approaches manufacture) require careful, documented legal analysis and often a binding ruling.

### Apply the agreement-specific preferential origin rules exactly

Each FTA defines origin differently. Do not assume rules transfer across agreements. For the relevant agreement, determine:

- Whether the good is wholly obtained or produced in the territory (simplest case);
- The product-specific rule of origin for the tariff classification of the good (tariff shift, regional value content threshold, or specific processing);
- Whether a de minimis exception applies to minor non-originating inputs;
- Whether cumulation with other parties to the agreement is permitted;
- Whether the good satisfies any direct consignment or transit rules.

Apply the rule to the specific tariff classification of the finished good. A wrong classification cascades into a wrong origin determination.

### Trace the full bill of materials and processing chain

Origin cannot be determined from the finished good alone. Trace:

- Every material input and its origin (including origin of inputs to components);
- Every processing step and its location;
- The value added at each stage;
- Any intermediate origins that must be established before the final origin can be determined.

For complex manufactured goods, this requires cooperation from suppliers and may require supplier declarations of origin. Maintain this documentation as the basis for the origin claim.

### Obtain binding origin rulings for high-volume or borderline cases

For goods where origin is genuinely ambiguous or where the duty differential is material, seek a binding origin ruling from the relevant customs authority (for example, a US CBP origin ruling, an EU Binding Origin Information decision). A binding ruling provides certainty and protects against retrospective penalty if followed. Document the ruling, its scope, and its applicability to the specific goods.

### Substantiate every origin claim with documentation

An origin claim is only as defensible as its documentation. Maintain:

- Bills of materials with input origins;
- Process descriptions and manufacturing flow;
- Supplier declarations or certificates of origin;
- Binding rulings, if obtained;
- Calculations of regional value content where applicable;
- Tariff classifications supporting the origin rule applied.

Retention periods are typically long (3-5 years or more). Ensure documentation is retrievable for audit.

### Re-evaluate origin when the supply chain changes

Origin is not static. A change of supplier, a relocation of processing, a reformulation of inputs, or a change in tariff classification can change origin. Establish a process to flag supply-chain changes that require re-determination, and re-document origin when material changes occur.

## Common Traps

### Claiming FTA preference without satisfying the product-specific rule

Each good has a specific origin rule in each agreement. Claiming preference because "it feels like it qualifies" or because a different good qualified is a common and costly error that leads to denial of preference and penalties for wrongful claims.

### Confusing substantial transformation with value added

Adding significant value through simple assembly does not necessarily constitute substantial transformation. The test is about the nature of the transformation, not the cost. High-value assembly of foreign components may still yield foreign origin.

### Assuming the country of assembly is the country of origin

Assembly location is a factor but not determinative. If the components are foreign and the assembly is minor, origin may remain with the component country. Analyze the transformation, not just the location.

### Failing to trace inputs beyond the first tier

A component's origin depends on the origin of its inputs. Accepting a supplier's "origin" claim without understanding whether the supplier itself applied the rules correctly creates cascading error. Obtain supplier declarations and, for critical goods, verify.

### Claiming origin once and never revisiting it

Supply chains change. An origin determination made three years ago may be invalid after a supplier change. Without a change-triggered re-determination process, the organisation continues claiming a stale, incorrect origin.

### Using the wrong tariff classification as the basis for the origin rule

Preferential origin rules are tied to the tariff classification of the good. A misclassification leads to applying the wrong product-specific rule. Confirm classification before applying the origin rule.

## Self-Check

- Did I determine whether the question is non-preferential or preferential origin, and apply the correct test?
- For non-preferential origin, did I analyze substantial transformation (new article, distinctive name, character, or use) rather than just location or value?
- For preferential origin, did I apply the exact product-specific rule of the relevant agreement to the correct tariff classification?
- Did I trace the full bill of materials and processing chain, including origins of component inputs?
- For high-volume or borderline cases, did I seek or rely on a binding origin ruling?
- Is every origin claim substantiated with retained documentation (BOMs, supplier declarations, calculations, rulings)?
- Is there a process to re-determine origin when the supply chain materially changes?
- Did I confirm the tariff classification before applying the preferential origin rule?
