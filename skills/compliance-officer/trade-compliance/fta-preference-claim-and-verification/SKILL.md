# FTA Preference Claim and Verification

Free trade agreements offer significant duty savings, but claiming preference is a legal representation backed by documentation and subject to verification. A preference claim that cannot be substantiated on audit results in denial of preference, recovery of differential duty, interest, and potential penalties for negligent or fraudulent claims. The judgment problem is not whether the good qualifies (that is origin determination) but how to claim, document, support, and defend the claim across a complex supplier network and over time. This skill addresses the operational and evidentiary discipline required to claim FTA preference defensibly.

## Core Rules

### Treat a preference claim as a legal representation requiring a basis

Claiming preference (by entering a claim on a customs entry, signing a certificate of origin, or issuing a supplier declaration) is a representation that the good qualifies under the agreement. This representation must be based on a reasonable determination that the origin rules are satisfied. Do not claim preference speculatively or on the assumption that "it probably qualifies." Establish the basis before claiming.

### Understand the certification mechanism for each agreement

FTAs differ in how preference is claimed and certified:

- Some require a specific certificate of origin form (for example, a government-issued certificate);
- Some permit self-certification by the exporter, producer, or importer (for example, USMCA self-certification);
- Some require a data element set rather than a prescribed form;
- Some have different rules for claiming as importer, exporter, or producer.

Use the correct mechanism for the agreement. A preference claim supported by the wrong certification format may be denied.

### Secure and retain supplier declarations for inputs

For goods using non-originating inputs that undergo processing, the final producer often relies on supplier declarations confirming the origin and processing of inputs. Obtain and retain:

- Supplier declarations of origin for material inputs;
- Information on the origin and value of inputs;
- Any intermediate processing certifications.

Without supplier declarations, the regional value content or tariff-shift analysis cannot be substantiated, and the claim is indefensible. Build supplier declaration collection into procurement and renewal processes.

### Calculate regional value content correctly where required

Where the origin rule requires a regional value content (RVC) threshold, apply the correct method specified by the agreement:

- **Transaction value method**: RVC based on the transaction value of the good minus the value of non-originating materials;
- **Net cost method**: RVC based on net cost minus non-originating material costs;
- **Build-up or build-down** variations where permitted.

Use the correct method, the correct values, and the correct non-originating material identification. Document the full calculation. Recalculate when costs or inputs change.

### Apply the correct importer knowledge and supporting documentation standard

Under many modern FTAs, the importer claims preference based on its own knowledge or based on a certification from the exporter or producer. The importer must possess or obtain the supporting documentation (certification, supplier declarations, RVC calculations) at the time of the claim or within a defined period after. "Possess" does not mean "file with the entry" but the importer must be able to produce it on request. Establish systems to hold this documentation retrievably.

### Manage the preference claim lifecycle, not just the single entry

Preference is claimed entry by entry, but qualification is determined by the good and its supply chain, which persist across entries. Manage:

- **Initial determination**: establish qualification and document the basis;
- **Ongoing validity**: re-verify periodically and when supply chains change;
- **Blanket or periodic certifications**: where an agreement permits a blanket certification for identical goods over a period, manage its scope and expiry;
- **Revocation**: if qualification ceases, stop claiming immediately and reassess.

A blanket claim that outlives its valid basis is a systemic overclaim.

### Prepare for and cooperate with preference verification

Customs authorities conduct preference verifications (audits, questionnaires, on-site visits to producers). Prepare by:

- Maintaining retrievable, complete documentation for each claim;
- Understanding the verification timelines in the agreement (often 30-60 days to respond);
- Designating responsible personnel and escalation paths;
- Conducting internal mock verifications for high-value claims.

During verification, cooperate, produce documentation promptly, and engage trade counsel for material disputes. Failure to substantiate leads to denial and recovery.

### Distinguish denial of preference from a penalty

A good may qualify but a claim may be denied for procedural reasons (missing certification, late documentation). Denial means recovery of differential duty but not necessarily a penalty. Penalties arise where the claim was made without reasonable basis (negligence) or with knowledge of ineligibility (fraud). Understand the standard and ensure claims have a documented reasonable basis to avoid penalty exposure.

## Common Traps

### Claiming preference without possessing the supporting documentation

The most common failure. The importer claims preference, cannot produce the certification or supplier declarations on audit, and the claim is denied with recovery and interest. Establish documentation possession as a precondition.

### Using a blanket certification after the basis has changed

A blanket certification for identical goods is valid only while the goods and supply chain remain unchanged. A supplier change or reformulation invalidates it, and continued claims become wrongful.

### Miscalculating regional value content

Errors in identifying non-originating materials, using the wrong valuation method, or arithmetic mistakes produce incorrect RVC figures. Recalculate and peer-review high-value RVC claims.

### Assuming self-certification means no documentation is needed

Self-certification shifts the certification from a government to a party, but the obligation to substantiate remains. Self-certification without supporting documentation is an unsupported claim.

### Failing to obtain supplier declarations and claiming anyway

Without supplier declarations, the producer cannot prove the origin of inputs. Claiming preference in this state is speculative and indefensible. Make declaration collection a procurement requirement.

### Continuing to claim after learning of disqualification

If analysis reveals a good no longer qualifies, continued claims become knowing overclaims with penalty exposure. Stop, reassess, and document the change.

## Self-Check

- Is every preference claim based on a documented determination that the origin rules are satisfied?
- Is the correct certification mechanism for the specific agreement used, and is the certification valid and current?
- Are supplier declarations for material inputs obtained and retained?
- Where RVC applies, is the correct method used, with a documented calculation and correct non-originating material identification?
- Does the importer possess or can it obtain supporting documentation within the required period for every claim?
- Is there a lifecycle process for initial determination, periodic re-verification, and change-triggered reassessment?
- Is the organisation prepared for preference verification with retrievable documentation and defined response processes?
- Is the distinction between denial (recovery) and penalty (negligence or fraud) understood, and do all claims have a documented reasonable basis?
