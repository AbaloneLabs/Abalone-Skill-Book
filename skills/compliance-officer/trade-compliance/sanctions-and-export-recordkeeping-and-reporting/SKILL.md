# Sanctions And Export Recordkeeping And Reporting

Recordkeeping and reporting are the evidentiary backbone of sanctions and export compliance. Regulators reconstruct what happened from records, not from memory or intent, and the obligations are specific: OFAC requires blocking and rejected transaction reports on defined timelines, the EAR and ITAR require five-year retention of export records, and voluntary self-disclosure depends on a complete, documented investigation. An institution that screens, blocks, or licenses but keeps no records cannot prove it did so, and an institution that discovers a violation but reports late or incompletely forfeits mitigation. The reporting and recordkeeping discipline is where compliance either holds up or collapses under scrutiny.

Use this skill before establishing recordkeeping retention, preparing OFAC blocked property or rejected transaction reports, managing voluntary self-disclosure submissions, or designing internal reporting for sanctions and export violations. The compliance officer must treat each record and report as evidence that will be examined, and must know the timelines that govern each.

## Core Rules

### Maintain Five-Year Recordkeeping For Export And Sanctions Activity

Both the EAR and ITAR impose retention requirements, and OFAC recordkeeping overlays for sanctions activity.

- EAR records must be retained for a minimum of five years from the date of export, reexport, or transfer, or from the date of the activity requiring a record.
- ITAR records must be retained for a minimum of five years from the expiration of the authorization or the date of the activity.
- OFAC requires retention of records relating to blocked property and rejected transactions, generally for five years.
- Records include licenses, classifications, applications, party screening, end-use documentation, shipping records, and condition compliance evidence.
- Records must be organized so they can be retrieved and produced on request.

Retention is not passive storage. Records must be retrievable and reconstruct the decision.

### File OFAC Blocked Property Reports On Time

When property is blocked under OFAC authority, specific reports are required.

- an initial report of blocked property must be filed with OFAC within ten business days of the blocking;
- an annual report of blocked property must be filed by September 30 each year for property blocked as of June 30;
- reports must identify the blocked party, the property, the date of blocking, and the authority;
- blocked property must be placed in a blocked account and held pending authorization from OFAC.

Late or missing reports are independent violations. The blocking itself is not the end of the obligation.

### File Rejected Transaction Reports

Where a transaction is rejected rather than blocked, reporting is still required.

- rejected transactions under OFAC programs must be reported, generally within ten business days;
- the report identifies the parties, the transaction, the basis for rejection, and the authority;
- rejection occurs where the transaction is prohibited but there is no blockable interest.

Do not assume that because funds were not blocked, no report is owed. Rejection triggers its own reporting.

### Manage Voluntary Self-Disclosure Submissions

Voluntary self-disclosure (VSD) is a distinct reporting path for apparent violations, offering potential mitigation.

- under OFAC, the framework provides for VSD as a mitigating factor;
- under the EAR, BIS provides for VSD with potential mitigation;
- under the ITAR, DDTC provides for VSD with potential mitigation;
- VSD requires a complete and accurate description of the apparent violations, the facts, the root cause, and remediation;
- VSD must be voluntary, made before the government discovers the violation through other means;
- the submission triggers a government review and potential enforcement resolution.

VSD is a strategic decision requiring legal judgment. See the dedicated VSD skill. Incomplete or strategic-but-incomplete disclosures can forfeit mitigation and create additional exposure.

### Design Internal Reporting For Apparent Violations

Internal reporting structures feed the external reporting obligations.

- establish a process for staff to report suspected violations to compliance;
- triage reports promptly to determine blocking, rejection, or investigation needs;
- document the triage decision and its basis;
- escalate apparent violations for VSD analysis within defined timeframes;
- maintain privilege appropriately through legal involvement.

### Maintain Audit-Quality Evidence

Records must withstand regulatory examination. Design them for that purpose.

- time-stamped screening events with list versions;
- match dispositions with rationale and identifiers;
- license files with conditions and utilization tracking;
- blocking and rejection records with reports filed;
- investigation files with scope, evidence, root cause, and remediation.

### Reconcile Records Across Systems

Sanctions and export records often span screening tools, trade systems, and case management. Reconcile them so the complete picture is reconstructable. Disconnected records create gaps that undermine defense.

## Common Traps

### Treating Recordkeeping As Passive Storage

Records must be retrievable and reconstruct the decision. Storage without organization is insufficient.

### Missing The Ten-Business-Day Reporting Window

OFAC blocked property and rejected transaction reports have short windows. Late filing is an independent violation.

### Forgetting The Annual Blocked Property Report

Annual reporting is required for property still blocked as of June 30. Omission is a violation.

### Filing An Incomplete Voluntary Self-Disclosure

VSD must be complete and accurate. Strategic omissions forfeit mitigation and can create misrepresentation exposure.

### Disclosing After The Government Discovers The Violation

Mitigation requires voluntariness. Disclosure after discovery is not voluntary and loses the benefit.

### Failing To Document Triage And Escalation Decisions

Internal reports must be triaged and the decision documented. Undocumented triage cannot be defended.

### Overlooking Privilege In Investigation Records

Investigation files may warrant privilege protection through legal involvement. Failing to structure privilege can waive protections.

### Leaving Records Disconnected Across Systems

Screening, trade, and case records must reconcile. Gaps undermine the ability to reconstruct decisions.

## Self-Check

- Are EAR and ITAR records retained for the minimum five-year period from the relevant date, and OFAC records for the required period?
- Are records organized and retrievable so each export, screening, and sanctions decision can be reconstructed?
- Are OFAC initial blocked property reports filed within ten business days of blocking, with the annual report filed by September 30 for property blocked as of June 30?
- Are rejected transaction reports filed within the required window, recognizing that rejection triggers reporting even without blocking?
- Is voluntary self-disclosure managed as a strategic decision requiring legal judgment, with complete and accurate submissions?
- Is VSD made before the government discovers the violation through other means to preserve voluntariness and mitigation?
- Is an internal reporting process established for staff to report suspected violations, with prompt triage and documented decisions?
- Are apparent violations escalated for VSD analysis within defined timeframes?
- Are investigation files structured to preserve privilege through appropriate legal involvement?
- Are records reconciled across screening, trade, and case management systems so the complete picture is reconstructable?
