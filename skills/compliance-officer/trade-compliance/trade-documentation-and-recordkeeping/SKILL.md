# Trade Documentation and Recordkeeping

International trade runs on documents, and customs authorities enforce through documents. Every duty determination, origin claim, valuation, classification, and preference claim is evidenced (or not) by the documents that accompany and follow the transaction. Inconsistent, incomplete, or missing documents create a cascade of risk: delays, denied preference, valuation challenges, penalty exposure, and audit failure. This skill addresses the judgment involved in creating, reconciling, retaining, and producing trade documents that will withstand customs and regulatory scrutiny.

## Core Rules

### Ensure accuracy and consistency across all documents in a transaction

A single trade transaction generates multiple documents — commercial invoice, packing list, bill of lading, certificate of origin, customs entry, payment records — and customs authorities cross-check them. Inconsistencies are red flags. Ensure consistency across:

- Product descriptions and tariff classifications;
- Quantities, weights, and units of measure;
- Values, currencies, and incoterms;
- Country of origin and consignee/shipper details;
- Dates and reference numbers.

A discrepancy between the invoice value and the payment value, or between the declared origin and the certificate of origin, triggers scrutiny. Reconcile documents before filing.

### Capture the true transaction value on the commercial invoice

The customs value is generally based on the transaction value (the price actually paid or payable). The commercial invoice is the primary evidence of this value. Ensure the invoice reflects:

- The full price paid, including any additions required by the valuation rules (assists, royalties, packing, selling commissions);
- The correct incoterm, so duties and freight are treated correctly;
- Any non-dutiable charges (freight, insurance after importation) separately and clearly;
- No omissions of payments (side agreements, indirect payments) that are part of the price actually paid.

Understating the invoice to reduce duty, or omitting assists and royalties, is a valuation violation. The invoice must tell the truth of the transaction.

### Maintain the full document set for each transaction

A defensible trade record includes, at minimum:

- Commercial invoice and proforma invoice;
- Packing list;
- Bill of lading or airway bill (transport document);
- Customs entry and any supporting worksheets;
- Certificate of origin or preference certification;
- Valuation worksheet and any assists or royalty documentation;
- Payment records and any related agreements;
- Correspondence relevant to the transaction (price, origin, classification).

Retain the complete set, not just the entry summary. An audit reconstructs the transaction from the documents.

### Apply the correct recordkeeping retention period

Trade records must be retained for periods defined by jurisdiction (typically 5 years from the date of entry for US imports, with variations elsewhere). The retention obligation applies to importers, exporters, and their brokers. Define and enforce retention that meets the longest applicable period. Premature destruction is a recordkeeping violation that compounds any underlying substantive error.

### Ensure document retrievability, not just retention

Retaining records that cannot be located within the time customs allows is functionally equivalent to not retaining them. Maintain:

- Organised, indexed storage (by entry number, date, product, supplier);
- Defined retrieval timelines (often 30 days or less to produce on request);
- A system that survives personnel turnover and system migrations.

Test retrievability periodically with sample requests.

### Reconcile broker-filed entries against underlying documents

Customs brokers file entries on behalf of importers, but the importer retains legal responsibility for accuracy. Do not assume the broker's entry is correct. Implement a post-entry review process that reconciles:

- Declared classification against the actual product;
- Declared value against the invoice and any assists/royalties;
- Declared origin against the certificate and supplier declarations;
- Any preference claims against supporting certifications.

Broker errors become importer liability if not caught. Post-entry review is a critical control.

### Handle corrections proactively through prior disclosure

When a documentation or substantive error is discovered (wrong classification, undervaluation, wrongful preference), the organisation may have a duty or a strong incentive to correct it. Many jurisdictions offer prior disclosure or voluntary self-disclosure programs that significantly reduce penalties where the error is reported before customs discovers it. Engage trade counsel promptly on material errors, and do not continue filing known-incorrect entries while "studying" the issue.

### Manage special documentation for restricted or controlled goods

Certain goods require additional documentation: export licenses, end-user certificates, phytosanitary certificates, certificates of analysis, or country-of-origin marking. Identify the documentation requirements for each product category and ensure they are obtained and filed. Missing a required certificate can block the shipment or create penalty exposure.

## Common Traps

### Inconsistent values or descriptions across documents

An invoice showing one value and a related agreement showing another, or a packing list quantity that does not match the invoice, is an immediate audit trigger. Reconcile before filing.

### Omitting assists, royalties, or selling commissions from the customs value

These are commonly overlooked additions to the transaction value. Their omission understates duty and is a valuation violation. Build a process to identify and declare them.

### Relying entirely on the broker without post-entry review

The broker files, but the importer is liable. Without reconciliation, broker errors propagate uncorrected. Implement post-entry review as a standard control.

### Destroying records prematurely or losing them in system migrations

Retention periods are mandatory. Records lost in an ERP migration or destroyed under a generic retention policy create recordkeeping violations. Align trade record retention with the specific legal requirement.

### Failing to correct known errors promptly

Continuing to file incorrect entries after discovering an error, while deferring a decision, increases the volume of error and the penalty exposure. Correct promptly and consider prior disclosure.

### Treating the commercial invoice as a formality rather than evidence

The invoice is the primary valuation document. A casually prepared invoice with rounded values or omitted charges undermines the entire customs position. Prepare it with accuracy.

## Self-Check

- Are product descriptions, quantities, values, origins, and dates consistent across all documents in the transaction?
- Does the commercial invoice reflect the true transaction value, including assists, royalties, packing, and commissions?
- Is the complete document set (invoice, packing list, transport doc, entry, certificates, worksheets, payment records) retained for each transaction?
- Does retention meet the longest applicable legal period (typically 5 years), and is this enforced across systems and migrations?
- Can records be retrieved within the time customs allows, and is retrievability periodically tested?
- Is there a post-entry review process that reconciles broker-filed entries against underlying documents?
- When errors are discovered, are they corrected promptly with consideration of prior disclosure, rather than deferred?
- Are special documentation requirements for restricted or controlled goods identified and met?
