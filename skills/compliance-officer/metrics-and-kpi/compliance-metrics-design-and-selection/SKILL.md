# Compliance Metrics Design And Selection

Most compliance metrics programs measure what is easy to count rather than what matters. Training completion rates, policies issued, hotline calls received, and attestations collected dominate dashboards because they are available and trend favorably, while the questions that actually reveal program health, whether misconduct is being prevented, whether controls are working, whether culture is strong, go unmeasured. The harm is that these activity metrics create false confidence: leadership sees green dashboards and concludes the program is effective, while the metrics would have stayed green right up to a major failure. Worse, the wrong metrics can drive the wrong behavior, as teams optimize for the countable, closing alerts quickly to hit a target, rather than for the outcome the metric was meant to proxy. Designing compliance metrics is therefore a high-stakes judgment task: the metrics chosen determine what the organization sees, what it rewards, and what it remains blind to.

Use this skill before designing or revising compliance metrics, selecting KPIs, building a metrics framework, or diagnosing why current metrics fail to reveal risk. The goal is to make the agent select metrics that measure outcomes and risk movement, balance leading and lagging indicators, avoid gaming, and connect measurement to action rather than producing comfortable activity counts.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Activity, Output, And Outcome Metrics, And Favor Outcomes

The first discipline is recognizing the hierarchy of metrics and where each sits. Activity metrics count what was done, output metrics count what was produced, and outcome metrics count whether the desired result was achieved. Most compliance dashboards are dominated by activity and output, which is precisely why they fail to reveal effectiveness.

Understand the hierarchy:

- activity metrics, such as training sessions delivered, policies published, or meetings held, which prove effort but not effect;
- output metrics, such as alerts reviewed, third parties screened, or investigations closed, which prove throughput but not quality or prevention;
- outcome metrics, such as misconduct detected, control failures prevented, risk reduction achieved, or culture improvement, which speak to whether the program works;
- each has a place, but a dashboard of only activity and output cannot demonstrate effectiveness.

Favor outcome and risk-movement metrics, and use activity and output metrics only as context, never as the headline measure of success.

### Measure Risk Movement And Effectiveness, Not Just Volume

The metrics that matter most tell whether risk is being reduced and whether controls are effective, not how busy the function is. A function can process enormous volume and still fail to prevent misconduct if its controls are poorly designed.

Measure risk movement and effectiveness through:

- whether residual risk is trending toward appetite over time;
- control effectiveness rates from testing, including design and operating effectiveness;
- detection rates and whether the program finds issues before they become incidents;
- recurrence rates, whether the same findings repeat, which indicates ineffective remediation;
- coverage rates, what proportion of the risk population is reached by controls;
- timeliness, whether issues are detected and remediated within acceptable windows.

A metric that shows volume rising while effectiveness is unknown is not a measure of a working program. Insist on effectiveness.

### Balance Leading And Lagging Indicators

Lagging indicators, such as incidents, enforcement actions, and confirmed misconduct, tell you what already went wrong. Leading indicators, such as control performance, culture survey results, and exception trends, suggest what may go wrong. A program measured only by lagging indicators learns of failure too late to prevent it.

Balance by including:

- lagging indicators, such as confirmed violations, enforcement actions, financial losses, and disciplinary outcomes, which confirm past effectiveness;
- leading indicators, such as control testing pass rates, training comprehension, culture and psychological safety scores, exception and override frequency, and backlog trends, which predict future performance;
- the relationship between them, since leading indicators moving while lagging stay stable suggests prevention is working.

A dashboard of only lagging indicators is a rearview mirror. Add leading indicators that give time to act.

### Design Metrics To Resist Gaming And Goodhart's Law

When a metric becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure. Compliance metrics are especially vulnerable: a target to close alerts quickly incentivizes cursory review; a target to reduce hotline calls incentivizes discouraging reporting; a target for high training completion incentivizes clicking through without learning. Every metric must be designed with gaming in mind.

Resist gaming by:

- pairing count-based metrics with quality measures, such as alert closure rate with accuracy sampling;
- avoiding targets that reward suppressing the behavior being measured, such as rewarding low hotline volume;
- using comprehension and application testing rather than completion for training;
- rotating and revising metrics so they cannot be optimized in isolation;
- measuring outcomes that are hard to game, such as recurrence and effectiveness, alongside activity.

If a metric can be improved without improving the underlying reality, it will be, and it will then mislead. Design metrics that align improvement with real effectiveness.

### Disaggregate Metrics To Reveal Local Problems

Aggregate metrics hide the segments where risk concentrates. A strong company-wide training completion rate can conceal a region where completion is low, and a stable hotline volume can conceal a unit where reporting has collapsed due to retaliation. Disaggregation is what turns a comfortable average into a useful diagnostic.

Disaggregate by:

- business unit, function, geography, and entity;
- management level and, where sample size allows, specific manager;
- tenure, employment type, and demographic segments where appropriate;
- risk domain, so that performance in anti-brybery, sanctions, privacy, and conduct can be compared;
- time, to detect trends and sudden changes.

Set thresholds that trigger investigation when a segment deviates significantly from the organization. A single weak segment can carry disproportionate risk, and aggregates hide it.

### Define Each Metric's Data Source, Definition, And Limitations

A metric that means different things to different people, or that rests on undefined data, produces noise and dispute. Every metric must be precisely defined so that it is comparable over time and defensible.

Define for each metric:

- the exact data source and how it is collected;
- the precise definition, including numerator, denominator, and exclusions;
- the frequency of measurement and reporting;
- the known limitations and what the metric does and does not show;
- the owner accountable for its accuracy and interpretation.

A metric without a defined source and definition will be argued about rather than acted on. Specify it so the conversation is about the risk, not the number.

### Connect Metrics To Decisions And Action

Metrics that are reported but do not drive decisions consume attention and breed cynicism. Every metric should have a defined response when it moves, and the organization should be able to point to actions taken because of metric movement.

Connect by:

- defining thresholds and triggers that require investigation or action for each metric;
- assigning owners for responding to metric movement;
- closing the loop by reporting what action was taken and whether it moved the metric;
- reviewing the metric set periodically to retire metrics that drive no action and add metrics for emerging risks;
- ensuring leadership asks what the metric means and what will be done, not just what the number is.

A metric that never changes a decision is not worth measuring. Make measurement a precursor to action.

## Common Traps

### Activity And Output Mistaken For Effectiveness

Counting training delivered and alerts reviewed proves effort, not effect. Favor outcome and risk-movement metrics.

### Volume Rising While Effectiveness Is Unknown

High throughput with no measure of whether controls work is not a working program. Measure effectiveness and recurrence.

### Only Lagging Indicators

A dashboard of incidents and enforcement actions reveals failure too late. Add leading indicators that allow prevention.

### Metrics That Can Be Gamed

Targets that reward cursory review, suppressed reporting, or click-through completion mislead. Pair counts with quality and measure hard-to-game outcomes.

### Aggregate Averages Hiding Weak Segments

A comfortable company-wide metric can conceal a region or unit where risk concentrates. Disaggregate and set investigation thresholds.

### Undefined Metrics Producing Dispute

A metric without a defined source, numerator, denominator, and limitation is argued about, not acted on. Define each precisely.

### Metrics Reported But Driving No Action

A metric that never changes a decision breeds cynicism. Define triggers, owners, and follow-up, and retire metrics that drive nothing.

## Self-Check

- Does the metrics framework distinguish activity, output, and outcome metrics, and favor outcome and risk-movement metrics as the headline measures of success?
- Are effectiveness measures included, such as residual risk trend, control effectiveness from testing, detection rate, recurrence rate, coverage, and timeliness, rather than only volume?
- Is there a balance of lagging indicators, such as incidents and enforcement, and leading indicators, such as control performance, culture, exceptions, and backlog?
- Are metrics designed to resist gaming through quality pairing, avoidance of suppression-rewarding targets, comprehension testing, rotation, and hard-to-game outcomes?
- Are metrics disaggregated by unit, geography, manager level, tenure, risk domain, and time, with thresholds that trigger investigation of deviating segments?
- Is each metric defined with exact data source, definition, frequency, limitations, and an accountable owner?
- Does each metric connect to defined thresholds, triggers, owners, and follow-up action, with periodic retirement of metrics that drive no action?
- Could the metrics framework be defended to a regulator as measuring effectiveness and risk rather than as a comfortable activity count?
