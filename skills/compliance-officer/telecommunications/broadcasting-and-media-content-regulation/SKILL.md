# Broadcasting And Media Content Regulation

Broadcasting and video distribution are governed by a public interest licensing regime that controls who may hold a license, what content may air, how advertising is presented, and how political and public interest obligations are met. The defining feature is that broadcast spectrum is a public trust, and license holders owe obligations to their communities that pure publishers and streamers do not. Compliance failure can mean license revocation, forfeitures, denial of renewal, and restrictions on transactions. The difficulty is that the same content can be protected expression in one medium and regulated speech in another, and the rules shift across broadcast, cable, satellite, and streaming.

Use this skill before advising on a broadcast license, reviewing content standards, evaluating political advertising obligations, planning a media transaction, or assessing children's programming duties. The goal is to make the agent identify the medium, the license, the content rules, the advertising limits, and the public interest obligations before concluding compliance.

## Core Rules

### Secure And Maintain The Appropriate License

Broadcasting requires a license, and license status determines the full scope of obligations.

For broadcast operations:

- identify the service (full-power television, radio, low-power, translator, Class A) and its license class;
- maintain construction permit and license terms;
- meet renewal standards and file renewal applications on time;
- comply with ownership eligibility including character qualifications;
- maintain a main studio and public inspection file;
- broadcast station identification;
- comply with antenna structure registration, marking, and lighting;
- address license transfer and assignment consent requirements.

A license is not permanent; it must be renewed, and renewal can be challenged. Character qualifications include the applicant's compliance history.

### Observe Media Ownership And Attribution Rules

Ownership limits and attribution rules control concentration and transparency.

Assess:

- national and local ownership caps for television and radio;
- newspaper-broadcast and television-radio cross-ownership where applicable;
- local television multiple ownership and radio market limits;
- attribution of ownership including joint sales agreements and shared services;
- cable-operator and broadcast-network cross-ownership;
- foreign ownership limits and review;
- equity and debt attribution thresholds;
- public file disclosure of ownership and agreements.

Attribution rules determine who is treated as an owner. Certain agreements can attribute ownership even without equity.

### Apply Content Standards And Protected Speech Limits

Content rules differ by medium, with broadcast subject to the strictest standards.

Control:

- indecency standards for broadcast content, limited to safe harbor hours;
- profanity and obscenity prohibitions;
- sponsorship identification for paid content and product placement;
- broadcast hoax rules prohibiting disruptive false alerts;
- lottery and contest rules;
- news distortion and rigging prohibitions;
- retaliation against employees for refusing to violate rules.

Broadcast indecency rules do not apply to cable or streaming in the same way, but sponsorship identification and contest rules apply broadly. Obscenity is unprotected in any medium.

### Meet Political Broadcasting And Advertising Obligations

Political advertising triggers detailed access, rate, disclosure, and recordkeeping duties.

Implement:

- reasonable access requirements for legally qualified federal candidates;
- equal opportunities for opposing candidates with limited exceptions;
- lowest unit charge during defined pre-election periods;
- sponsorship identification and stand-by-your-ad disclaimers;
- public file documentation of political ad requests and rates;
- online public inspection file maintenance;
- handling of issue advertising and third-party ads;
- rate disclosure and non-discrimination among candidates.

Political broadcasting rules are a frequent audit and complaint area. Documentation in the public file is essential.

### Satisfy Children's Programming And Educational Duties

Broadcasters owe specific duties to children, including programming and advertising limits.

Cover:

- core educational and informational programming requirements;
- processing of programming for children;
- commercial limits in children's programming;
- host-selling restrictions in program-length commercials;
- public file documentation of children's programming;
- limits on interactive ads embedded in children's content.

Children's advertising limits and host-selling rules protect child audiences from over-commercialization.

### Support Accessibility Through Captioning And Description

Video distributors and broadcasters must provide accessibility features.

Implement:

- closed captioning for television programming with quality standards;
- audio description for visually impaired audiences where required;
- accessibility for emergency information presented visually and aurally;
- captioning for online video clips and programming from television;
- accessibility complaint handling and recordkeeping.

Accessibility obligations extend to streaming of content that previously aired on television and to emergency information.

### Manage Sponsorship, Payola, And Contest Integrity

Paid content and contests must be transparent and fair.

Control:

- sponsorship identification for paid placements, endorsements, and sponsored programming;
- payola and plugola rules requiring disclosure of consideration;
- contest conduct with publicly available rules and fair administration;
- news and public affairs sponsorship disclosure;
- separation of advertising from programming for children.

Failure to disclose sponsorship or consideration is a recurring enforcement area, especially in music, influencer, and native advertising contexts.

### Address Online, Streaming, And Platform Rules

Streaming and online video carry their own obligations, some inherited from television and some new.

Assess:

- captioning for online programming derived from television;
- accessibility for streaming originals where required;
- accessibility and consumer protection for direct-to-consumer services;
- state and local franchise and taxation for video service;
- broadband and data privacy obligations for bundled services;
- platform liability and content moderation under applicable law;
- advertising disclosure for sponsored online content.

Streaming services that distribute television-derived content inherit captioning duties, and accessibility expectations are expanding.

## Common Traps

### Indecency Rules Applied To The Wrong Medium

Broadcast indecency rules do not apply to cable or streaming. Apply the correct medium's standards.

### Political File Treated As Optional

The online public inspection file must document political ad requests, rates, and sponsors. Missing documentation is a common audit finding.

### Sponsorship And Payola Disclosure Skipped

Paid placements, endorsements, and consideration must be disclosed. Omitting disclosure is an enforcement risk.

### Ownership Attribution Misjudged

Certain agreements attribute ownership without equity. Joint sales and shared services agreements can create attribution.

### Children's Commercial Limits Exceeded

Children's programming has strict commercial time limits and host-selling restrictions. Exceeding them is a violation.

### Captioning Treated As Optional For Streaming Of TV Content

Online programming derived from television must be captioned. Streaming does not erase the duty.

### Renewal Treated As Automatic

Renewal can be challenged, and character and compliance history matter. Treat renewal as a substantive process.

## Self-Check

- Is the service and license class identified, with renewal, main studio, public file, identification, and antenna structure obligations met?
- Are national, local, cross-ownership, attribution, foreign ownership, and public file disclosure rules analyzed for ownership and transactions?
- Are indecency, profanity, obscenity, sponsorship identification, hoax, lottery, and news distortion rules applied to the correct medium?
- Are political broadcasting reasonable access, equal opportunities, lowest unit charge, disclaimers, and public file documentation implemented?
- Are children's core programming, commercial limits, host-selling restrictions, and public file documentation satisfied?
- Are closed captioning, audio description, emergency information accessibility, and online captioning implemented with complaint handling?
- Are sponsorship identification, payola and plugola disclosure, and contest integrity controls in place?
- Are streaming, online video, franchise, accessibility, privacy, and platform obligations addressed for digital distribution?
- Is ownership attribution correctly judged including agreements that attribute without equity?
- Is renewal treated as a substantive process with character and compliance history considered?
