# Telecommunications And Spectrum Regulation

Telecommunications is governed by a licensing and conduct regime that controls who may provide service, how spectrum is used, how carriers interconnect, how consumers are billed, and how emergency and universal service obligations are met. The defining feature is that providing communications service is a privilege tied to public interest obligations, and the rules attach to the act of carrying communications, not merely to owning infrastructure. A company that transmits voice, data, or video can become a common carrier or telecommunications provider subject to duties it did not anticipate, including contribution to universal service funds, lawful intercept support, accessibility, privacy, and interconnection.

Use this skill before advising on a communications service, reviewing spectrum licensing, evaluating common carrier duties, planning a broadband deployment, or assessing a telecom transaction. The goal is to make the agent identify the service classification, the licensing and contribution duties, the consumer protection obligations, and the public interest requirements before concluding compliance.

## Core Rules

### Classify The Service And Identify The Regulatory Regime

Telecom obligations depend on service classification, which determines common carrier duties, contribution obligations, and consumer protection rules.

Classify:

- telecommunications service versus information service or enhanced service;
- common carrier versus private carriage;
- broadband internet access service classification and its regulatory consequences;
- voice over internet protocol and its regulatory treatment by type;
- wireless and mobile service classification;
- cable and video service;
- over-the-top and application-layer services.

Classification has shifted over time, and the current classification determines which rules apply. Do not assume a service is unregulated because it uses internet protocol.

### Secure Spectrum Licensing And Authorizations

Use of radio spectrum generally requires authorization, and unauthorized transmission can trigger enforcement and forfeiture.

For wireless operations:

- identify the bands and frequencies used;
- determine whether the band requires a license, registration, or operates under unlicensed rules;
- obtain the appropriate license or authorization for fixed, mobile, or satellite use;
- comply with buildout and coverage requirements;
- operate within power, emission, and interference limits;
- coordinate with adjacent users and protect incumbents;
- renew licenses before expiration and meet renewal standards;
- comply with spectrum aggregation and leasing rules;
- address auction and bidding obligations.

Spectrum is a scarce public resource. Operating without authorization or outside license terms can lead to license revocation and forfeitures.

### Meet Common Carrier And Interconnection Obligations

Providers classified as common carriers or telecommunications carriers owe duties to serve, charge just and reasonable rates, avoid unjust discrimination, and interconnect.

Assess:

- duty to offer service on a nondiscriminatory basis;
- tariff or rate obligations where applicable;
- interconnection duties with other carriers;
- resale and wholesale access obligations;
- number portability support;
- dialing parity and equal access;
- prohibitions on unjust or unreasonable practices;
- traffic exchange and compensation for intercarrier compensation;
- obligations to contribute to universal service.

Common carrier duties are public interest obligations. A carrier cannot refuse interconnection or discriminate among similarly situated customers without justification.

### Contribute To Universal Service And Support Programs

Telecommunications carriers generally must contribute to universal service funds and may be eligible to draw support for serving high-cost areas, low-income consumers, schools, libraries, and rural healthcare.

Track:

- contribution base and quarterly or annual filing obligations;
- universal service fund contribution calculations;
- eligibility to receive support and the conditions attached;
- lifeline and affordable connectivity program participation and verification;
- E-rate and rural healthcare program rules;
- state universal service fund obligations;
- prohibitions on passing through contributions in a manner that masks the charge.

Support recipients face ongoing compliance, audit, and recapture obligations. Misuse of support funds can lead to recapture, penalties, and exclusion.

### Support Emergency Communications And Public Safety

Carriers have specific duties to support emergency communications and public safety.

Implement:

- enhanced emergency call routing and location accuracy for voice services;
- text-to-emergency support where required;
- outage reporting to public safety and the regulator;
- priority service and priority communications for emergency responders;
- disaster response and network resiliency obligations;
- alert distribution obligations for mobile broadcast alerts;
- continuity of service during emergencies;
- coordination with public safety answering points.

Emergency communications failures, especially during disasters, are a significant enforcement and reputational risk.

### Honor Consumer Protection And Billing Rules

Telecom consumer protection rules govern billing, disclosures, switching, and privacy.

Cover:

- truth-in-billing requirements and clear charge descriptions;
- prohibitions on cramming unauthorized charges;
- customer proprietary network information privacy rules;
- consent for use and disclosure of customer information;
- number porting and service transfer rules;
- slamming prohibitions on unauthorized carrier changes;
- broadband consumer labels with pricing, speed, and latency disclosure;
- accessibility for people with disabilities;
- marketing and early termination fee rules;
- state utility commission consumer rules.

CPNI rules restrict how customer information can be used and require safeguards and approval for certain marketing uses.

### Address Privacy, Surveillance, And Lawful Intercept

Telecom providers face privacy duties and obligations to assist lawful surveillance.

Assess:

- customer proprietary network information privacy and consent;
- obligations to support lawful intercept and pen register capabilities;
- assistance to law enforcement under court orders;
- transparency reporting on government requests;
- data retention and deletion obligations;
- restrictions on disclosure of communications content and metadata;
- state communications privacy laws.

Lawful intercept capabilities must be engineered into the network, and failure to comply with court-ordered assistance can create liability.

### Manage Foreign Ownership And National Security Review

Telecom licenses and transactions involving foreign ownership can trigger national security review.

For transactions and license transfers:

- assess foreign ownership thresholds triggering review;
- team telecom or equivalent national security and law enforcement review;
- mitigation agreements and compliance obligations;
- Committee on Foreign Investment review where applicable;
- license transfer and consent requirements;
- foreign carrier traffic and landing station obligations.

National security review can impose ongoing compliance, reporting, and equipment restrictions.

## Common Traps

### Assuming Internet Protocol Means Unregulated

VoIP and broadband are often regulated despite using IP. Classification, not technology, determines obligations.

### Spectrum Use Without Authorization

Even short-range or low-power use can require authorization in certain bands. Verify band rules before deployment.

### Universal Service Contributions Ignored By New Entrants

Carriers entering the market may overlook contribution filings. Contribution obligations apply to revenue from regulated services.

### CPNI Treated As Ordinary Marketing Data

Customer proprietary network information has specific use and consent rules. Marketing use requires compliant approval.

### Emergency Location Accuracy Treated As Aspirational

Location accuracy for emergency calls has specific benchmarks. Failing to meet them is an enforcement priority, especially indoors and for multistory buildings.

### Broadband Consumer Labels Skipped

Broadband providers must display standardized labels with pricing, speed, latency, and data allowances. Omitting them is a violation.

### Foreign Ownership Review Overlooked In Transactions

Telecom license transfers with foreign ownership can require national security review. Closing without review can unwind the transaction.

## Self-Check

- Is the service classified as telecommunications, information service, common carrier, broadband, VoIP, or video, with the matching regulatory duties?
- Are spectrum bands, licensing, authorization, buildout, power, interference, coordination, renewal, and aggregation rules addressed for wireless operations?
- Are common carrier duties, nondiscrimination, interconnection, resale, number portability, intercarrier compensation, and universal service contribution obligations met?
- Are universal service contribution filings, support eligibility conditions, lifeline, E-rate, and rural healthcare program rules tracked?
- Are emergency call routing, location accuracy, outage reporting, alert distribution, resiliency, and public safety coordination obligations implemented?
- Are truth-in-billing, cramming, slamming, CPNI privacy, number porting, broadband labels, and accessibility rules followed?
- Are lawful intercept support, law enforcement assistance, transparency reporting, and communications privacy duties addressed?
- Are foreign ownership, national security review, license transfer, and mitigation obligations analyzed for transactions?
- Is the distinction between technology and classification applied so that IP-based services are correctly regulated?
- Are state utility commission and state telecom consumer rules addressed alongside federal obligations?
