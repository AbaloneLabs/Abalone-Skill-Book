# Telecommunications Big Tech Platform Regulation And Interconnection

Digital platform regulation and interconnection obligations address the growing market power of large technology platforms and the responsibilities of telecommunications operators in interconnected digital ecosystems. Under the EU Digital Markets Act (DMA), Digital Services Act (DSA), the US net neutrality rules, and equivalent national platform regulation, designated gatekeepers, very large platforms, and telecommunications operators face specific obligations regarding interoperability, access, traffic management, and fair dealing. The central judgment problem is that platform regulation is evolving rapidly, the obligations attach to designation thresholds that can change with business growth, and the technical implementation of interoperability and access obligations is complex. A large technology company that structures its operations without assessing gatekeeper or very large platform designation can face obligations it has not built systems to meet, and a telecom operator that manages traffic without net neutrality compliance can face enforcement.

Use this skill before assessing platform regulation obligations, evaluating interconnection commitments, reviewing net neutrality compliance, or advising on gatekeeper designation. The goal is to make the agent think about what designations apply, what obligations attach, and the decisions that are easy to make too casually. A platform that meets designation thresholds but has not assessed its obligations can face enforcement for systemic non-compliance.

This skill addresses jurisdiction-specific obligations. Platform regulation, gatekeeper rules, net neutrality, and interconnection requirements differ across the EU, the US, and national regimes and are evolving rapidly. Always confirm the applicable national regulation and authority guidance, and consult qualified digital regulatory and legal counsel for specific platform and interconnection assessments. This skill does not constitute legal advice.

## Core Rules

### Assess Gatekeeper And Very Large Platform Designation

Platform regulation attaches obligations to designation based on quantitative and qualitative thresholds that must be assessed continuously.

Designation assessment elements:

- gatekeeper designation under the DMA, based on financial thresholds, user numbers, and entrenchment of position in core platform services;
- very large online platform (VLOP) and very large online search engine (VLOSE) designation under the DSA, based on user numbers in the jurisdiction;
- designation thresholds that can be met through growth, acquisitions, or expansion into new services;
- self-assessment obligations, where companies must notify regulators when thresholds are met;
- the timeline from designation to compliance, which may be short and require pre-existing systems;
- the implications of designation across multiple jurisdictions, which may impose overlapping obligations.

Designation is not always formally imposed. In some frameworks, companies must self-assess and notify, and failure to do so constitutes a violation. Companies approaching thresholds must prepare systems before designation.

### Understand Gatekeeper Obligations Under DMA

Gatekeeper designation under the DMA imposes specific do's and don'ts on core platform services.

DMA obligation elements:

- no self-preferencing, where the gatekeeper favors its own products or services over those of competitors on its platform;
- no anti-steering, preventing businesses from informing customers about offers outside the platform;
- allow installation and use of third-party apps and app stores;
- allow interoperability with other services for messaging and communication;
- provide business users with access to data generated by their activity;
- allow business users to promote offers and conclude contracts outside the platform;
- transparency in ranking, advertising, and data use;
- no combination of personal data across services without consent.

DMA obligations are specific and prescriptive, and compliance requires system and process changes, not merely policy commitments. Non-compliance can result in significant fines and structural remedies.

### Evaluate DSA Obligations For Very Large Platforms

Very large platforms under the DSA face enhanced obligations for content moderation, transparency, and risk management.

DSA obligation elements:

- risk assessment and mitigation for systemic risks, including illegal content, fundamental rights, and civic discourse;
- content moderation systems that are transparent, proportionate, and subject to due process;
- transparency reporting on content removal, advertising, and algorithmic systems;
- data access for researchers and regulators;
- internal complaint handling and out-of-court dispute settlement;
- crisis response protocols for major events;
- independent audits of compliance systems.

DSA obligations are risk-based and require ongoing assessment and mitigation. A platform that meets VLOP thresholds must implement comprehensive risk management and transparency systems.

### Manage Net Neutrality And Traffic Management

Telecommunications operators providing internet access must comply with net neutrality rules governing traffic management.

Net neutrality elements:

- treat all internet traffic equally, without blocking, throttling, or paid prioritization;
- traffic management must be reasonable, transparent, non-discriminatory, and proportionate;
- specialized services, such as IPTV or remote surgery, may be permitted but must not degrade general internet access;
- zero-rating and sponsored data arrangements must be assessed for net neutrality compliance;
- transparency requirements for traffic management practices, including disclosure to consumers and regulators;
- monitoring and enforcement by national regulators, including measurement of traffic management practices.

Net neutrality violations, including paid prioritization or anti-competitive zero-rating, can result in enforcement and remedial orders. Operators must design traffic management to be reasonable and transparent.

### Assess Interconnection And Access Obligations

Interconnection and access obligations require operators and platforms to provide access to their networks or services on fair terms.

Interconnection and access elements:

- telecommunications interconnection, requiring operators to interconnect with competitors for call and data exchange;
- access obligations for SMP operators, including wholesale access to networks;
- peering and transit arrangements for internet connectivity, which may be commercial or regulated;
- platform interoperability under DMA, requiring gatekeepers to enable third-party service interoperability;
- access to application programming interfaces (APIs) and functionality for third-party developers;
- fair, reasonable, and non-discriminatory (FRAND) terms for access arrangements.

Interconnection and access obligations vary by service type, market position, and jurisdiction. Operators and platforms must assess which obligations apply and ensure compliance with terms.

### Review Data Portability And Switching Rights

Data portability and switching rights enable users to move between services without lock-in.

Portability elements:

- data portability under GDPR and platform regulation, enabling users to receive their data in a structured, machine-readable format;
- portability of social graphs, contacts, and content to competing platforms;
- continuous and real-time portability under DMA for gatekeepers;
- switching assistance, including number portability for telecom and account portability for platforms;
- no barriers to switching, including no data deletion on termination and no contractual lock-in.

Data portability that is technically available but practically difficult does not satisfy the obligation. Portability must be functional and accessible to enable genuine user choice.

### Monitor Evolving Regulation And Enforcement

Platform and digital regulation is evolving rapidly, with new rules, guidance, and enforcement actions emerging continuously.

Monitoring elements:

- monitor regulatory developments, including new legislation, guidance, and enforcement actions;
- participate in consultations and regulatory processes that affect the company's services;
- assess the impact of enforcement actions against other companies for implications;
- update compliance frameworks to reflect regulatory evolution;
- engage with industry associations and regulatory bodies on policy development;
- prepare for designation thresholds through advance system and process development.

Companies operating in digital ecosystems must maintain active regulatory monitoring and must build systems proactively rather than reactively. The pace of regulatory change rewards preparation.

## Common Traps

### Missing Designation Through Failure To Self-Assess

Companies that meet designation thresholds but fail to self-assess and notify regulators violate self-assessment obligations.

### DMA Compliance As Policy Not Systems

DMA obligations require system and process changes, not merely policy commitments. Non-compliance can result in significant fines.

### Net Neutrality Violations Through Paid Prioritization

Paid prioritization, anti-competitive zero-rating, or unreasonable traffic management violates net neutrality rules.

### Interoperability That Is Technical But Not Functional

Interoperability and access that are technically available but practically difficult do not satisfy obligations.

### Data Portability That Is Inaccessible

Portability that is technically available but practically difficult does not enable genuine user choice and does not satisfy obligations.

### Reactive Rather Than Proactive Compliance

The pace of digital regulatory evolution rewards preparation. Companies that react to designation rather than preparing systems face enforcement.

### Assuming One Jurisdiction's Rules Apply Globally

Platform regulation, gatekeeper rules, and net neutrality differ across jurisdictions. Companies operating across borders must assess each applicable regime.

## Self-Check

- Are gatekeeper and very large platform designation thresholds assessed continuously, with self-assessment and notification when met?
- Are DMA obligations understood, including no self-preferencing, interoperability, data access, and anti-steering, with systems and processes to comply?
- Are DSA obligations implemented for VLOP designation, including risk assessment, content moderation, transparency, and independent audits?
- Is net neutrality maintained through equal treatment, reasonable traffic management, transparent practices, and compliant specialized services and zero-rating?
- Are interconnection and access obligations assessed and met, including telecom interconnection, SMP access, peering, and platform interoperability?
- Is data portability and switching provided functionally and accessibly, including continuous portability and no switching barriers?
- Are regulatory developments monitored continuously, with compliance frameworks updated and systems prepared proactively?
- Is the impact of enforcement actions against other companies assessed for implications?
- Are multi-jurisdiction obligations assessed for each applicable regime, recognizing that rules differ across jurisdictions?
- Is the platform regulation and interconnection assessment confirmed against the applicable national regulation and authority guidance with qualified counsel, rather than a generic standard?
