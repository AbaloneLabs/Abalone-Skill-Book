# Energy Operational Safety And Process Safety Management

Energy sector operational safety and process safety management address the obligation to prevent major accidents, protect workers and communities, and manage the inherent hazards of energy operations. Under the EU Seveso directives, the US OSHA Process Safety Management standard and Bureau of Safety and Environmental Enforcement rules, and equivalent national safety frameworks, failures in process safety, hazard management, or operational controls can result in catastrophic accidents, fatalities, environmental damage, and criminal liability. The central judgment problem is that major accident hazards in energy operations have low frequency but catastrophic consequences, and the systems that prevent them require sustained vigilance that can erode during long periods without incidents. An energy company that focuses on personal safety metrics without addressing process safety can experience a major accident despite excellent personal safety records. A compliance officer who treats safety as compliance documentation rather than operational discipline can miss the systemic failures that precede catastrophic events.

Use this skill before assessing operational safety, evaluating process safety management, reviewing major accident controls, or advising on inspection readiness. The goal is to make the agent think about what prevents major accidents, what distinguishes process from personal safety, and the decisions that are easy to make too casually. A safety system that produces documentation but does not influence operational decisions provides false assurance.

This skill addresses jurisdiction-specific obligations. Safety, process safety, and major accident prevention rules differ across the US, the EU, and national regimes, with sector-specific requirements for nuclear, offshore, and onshore operations. Always confirm the applicable national safety regulation and authority guidance, and consult qualified safety engineering and legal counsel for specific assessments. This skill does not constitute legal or engineering advice.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Process Safety From Personal Safety

Process safety and personal safety address different risk categories and require different management approaches.

Process versus personal safety:

- personal safety addresses slips, trips, falls, and occupational injuries, with metrics like lost time injury rates;
- process safety addresses major accident hazards, including explosions, fires, toxic releases, and structural failures, with metrics like leading indicators and barrier health;
- excellent personal safety metrics do not indicate good process safety, because major accidents are low-frequency events;
- process safety requires focus on barrier integrity, management of change, and hazard recognition, not on personal behavior;
- both are essential, but conflating them can mask process safety deterioration behind good personal safety metrics.

Major accidents often occur at facilities with excellent personal safety records, because the two categories require different management attention and metrics.

### Implement Major Accident Hazard Assessment

Major accident hazards must be identified, assessed, and managed through systematic processes.

Hazard assessment elements:

- identification of major accident hazards, including flammable, toxic, reactive, and pressurized hazards;
- consequence assessment for credible scenarios, including release, fire, explosion, and toxic dispersion;
- likelihood assessment based on equipment failure rates, human factors, and external events;
- risk assessment combining consequence and likelihood, with comparison to tolerability criteria;
- identification of safety critical elements (SCEs) and performance standards that prevent or mitigate major accidents;
- demonstration that risks are as low as reasonably practicable (ALARP).

Hazard assessment must be living documentation that is updated when operations, equipment, or understanding change. Static assessments become outdated and fail to reflect actual risk.

### Maintain Safety Case And Safety Report Requirements

High-hazard facilities may require safety cases or safety reports demonstrating that major accident risks are managed.

Safety case elements:

- description of the facility, operations, and hazards;
- demonstration of the safety management system and its effectiveness;
- identification of safety critical elements and their performance standards;
- demonstration that major accident risks are ALARP;
- emergency response arrangements for credible scenarios;
- periodic review and update of the safety case, including after modifications or incidents.

Safety cases must demonstrate, not assert, that risks are managed. The demonstration must be evidence-based and must address the specific hazards of the facility. Safety cases that are generic or boilerplate do not satisfy the obligation.

### Control Management Of Change

Management of change (MOC) ensures that modifications to equipment, procedures, or organization are assessed for safety implications before implementation.

MOC elements:

- identification of changes requiring MOC, including equipment, procedures, personnel, and organizational changes;
- hazard assessment of the proposed change, including impact on safety critical elements and barriers;
- review and approval by competent personnel before implementation;
- communication and training for affected personnel;
- post-implementation review to verify that the change achieved its purpose without unintended consequences;
- documentation of the MOC process, including the assessment, approval, and verification.

Unmanaged change is a leading cause of major accidents. Changes that appear minor can have significant safety implications when they affect safety critical elements or introduce new failure modes.

### Implement Permit-To-Work And Operational Controls

Permit-to-work and operational controls ensure that high-risk activities are planned, authorized, and executed safely.

Permit-to-work elements:

- identification of activities requiring permits, including hot work, confined space entry, and energy isolation;
- hazard assessment for the specific activity and location;
- specification of precautions, including isolation, monitoring, and personal protective equipment;
- authorization by competent personnel before work begins;
- verification that precautions are in place before work starts;
- control of simultaneous operations that could interact dangerously;
- closure and documentation when work is complete.

Permit systems that become administrative exercises, with permits signed without genuine assessment, provide false assurance and can enable accidents. The system must influence how work is actually performed.

### Maintain Asset Integrity And Mechanical Integrity

Asset integrity ensures that equipment containing or processing hazardous materials remains fit for purpose throughout its life.

Asset integrity elements:

- inspection and testing programs for pressure vessels, piping, and safety critical equipment;
- corrosion management, including monitoring, mitigation, and material selection;
- maintenance management, including preventive, predictive, and corrective maintenance;
- management of equipment that is beyond its design life or operating outside design conditions;
- reliability engineering for safety critical elements;
- documentation of inspection, testing, and maintenance results.

Asset integrity failures, including corrosion, fatigue, and erosion, are leading causes of major accidents. Equipment that is not inspected or maintained can fail catastrophically, particularly when it contains hazardous materials.

### Ensure Emergency Preparedness And Response

Emergency preparedness ensures that the facility and community can respond effectively to major accidents.

Emergency preparedness elements:

- emergency response plans for credible scenarios, including escalation, evacuation, and medical response;
- emergency equipment, including detection, alarm, suppression, and communication systems;
- trained emergency response teams, including mutual aid arrangements with neighboring facilities and public services;
- coordination with local authorities and community emergency planning;
- regular exercises and drills, including full-scale exercises with external responders;
- post-incident review and improvement of emergency response.

Emergency response that is planned but not practiced will fail under stress. Exercises must be realistic and must test the actual response capability, not just the documentation.

### Foster Safety Culture And Human Factors

Safety culture and human factors address the organizational and behavioral dimensions of safety performance.

Safety culture elements:

- leadership commitment to safety, demonstrated through decisions, resources, and personal engagement;
- a reporting culture where personnel can report concerns, near misses, and errors without fear of punishment;
- a learning culture that investigates incidents and implements improvements;
- competence assurance, ensuring that personnel have the skills and knowledge for their roles;
- management of fatigue, stress, and workload that can impair performance;
- alarm management, ensuring that operators are not overwhelmed during upset conditions.

Safety culture is assessed through indicators like reporting rates, near miss reporting, and the gap between procedures and practice. A culture where procedures exist but are not followed is a leading indicator of major accident risk.

## Common Traps

### Conflating Process And Personal Safety

Excellent personal safety metrics do not indicate good process safety. Major accidents occur at facilities with excellent personal safety records.

### Safety Case As Documentation Not Demonstration

Safety cases that are generic or boilerplate do not demonstrate that risks are managed. The demonstration must be evidence-based.

### Unmanaged Change Introducing New Hazards

Changes that appear minor can have significant safety implications. Unmanaged change is a leading cause of major accidents.

### Permit-To-Work As Administrative Exercise

Permit systems that become paperwork exercises, with permits signed without genuine assessment, enable accidents.

### Asset Integrity Failures In Aging Equipment

Equipment beyond design life or operating outside design conditions can fail catastrophically without proper inspection and maintenance.

### Emergency Response Planned But Not Practiced

Emergency response that is documented but not exercised through realistic drills will fail under stress.

### Culture Where Procedures Are Not Followed

A culture where procedures exist but are not followed, or where near misses are not reported, is a leading indicator of major accident risk.

## Self-Check

- Are process safety and personal safety distinguished, with separate metrics and management attention for major accident hazards?
- Are major accident hazards identified, assessed, and managed through living documentation with ALARP demonstration?
- Are safety cases or safety reports evidence-based demonstrations, not generic boilerplate, with periodic review and update?
- Is management of change applied to equipment, procedures, personnel, and organization, with hazard assessment and competent approval?
- Are permit-to-work systems genuine controls that influence how work is performed, not administrative exercises?
- Is asset integrity maintained through inspection, corrosion management, maintenance, and reliability engineering for safety critical elements?
- Is emergency preparedness maintained through plans, equipment, trained teams, coordination, and realistic exercises?
- Is safety culture assessed through reporting, near miss reporting, competence assurance, and the gap between procedures and practice?
- Are human factors, including fatigue, stress, workload, and alarm management, addressed in facility design and operations?
- Is the operational safety assessment confirmed against the applicable national regulation and authority guidance with qualified counsel and safety engineering expertise, rather than a generic standard?
