# Compliance Investigation Interview Technique

Interviews are where investigations are won or lost, and where they are most often mishandled. A poorly planned interview telegraphs what the investigator already knows, lets a subject shape a story around the gaps, contaminates witnesses by revealing others' accounts, or produces statements that cannot be relied upon because no one warned the witness about privilege or confidentiality. The technique matters because interview content drives findings, discipline, regulatory disclosure, and litigation. A statement taken casually is a statement that can be impeached later.

Use this skill before conducting interviews in a compliance investigation, when sequencing witnesses, when preparing an interview plan and question architecture, when giving Upjohn or corporate counsel warnings, when assessing credibility, or when deciding how to handle a subject interview versus a witness interview. The goal is to make the agent treat each interview as a structured, recorded, privilege-aware event rather than a conversation.

## Core Rules

### Sequence Interviews To Build Before You Confront

Interview order is a strategic decision. Generally, collect documents first, then interview peripheral witnesses, then central witnesses, then the subject last. Confronting the subject before you have the documentary and testimonial foundation lets them tailor an explanation to what they guess you know.

Plan the sequence around:

- who holds unique knowledge versus corroboration;
- who can be recalled for a follow-up;
- who is most likely to alert others;
- who is a subject versus a witness;
- which interviews are time-sensitive due to departure, access loss, or regulator interest;
- which witnesses depend on what others say, to avoid contamination.

Witness contamination is a real risk. Do not reveal one witness's account to another before that person has given their own version. The value of independent recollection is destroyed once a witness hears what others said.

### Prepare An Interview Plan, Not A Script

Each interview should have a written plan. The plan is not a rigid script but a structured set of topics, the documents to be shown, the order of inquiry, and the specific facts to test.

The plan should capture:

- the interview's objective and what facts it must establish;
- the witness's role and relationship to the events;
- documents to introduce and when, generally after the witness gives their own account;
- open questions before leading questions;
- the specific contradictions to probe and the evidence behind them;
- the Upjohn warning to be given;
- the confidentiality instruction;
- the note-taker and recording method;
- the expected duration and breaks;
- contingencies if the witness invokes counsel, refuses, or raises new allegations.

Lead with open questions that let the witness narrate, then narrow to specifics. If you show documents too early, the witness anchors to them and stops reconstructing independently.

### Give A Clear Upjohn Warning At The Outset

When counsel conducts or directs an interview of an employee in a corporate investigation, the attorney-client privilege belongs to the company, not the employee. The employee must understand this. A muddled or omitted warning can later be used to argue that the employee reasonably believed counsel represented them personally, undermining both privilege and the reliability of the statement.

A clear Upjohn warning covers:

- counsel represents the company, not the employee personally;
- the interview is for the purpose of providing legal advice to the company;
- the privilege belongs to the company and the company may waive it;
- the employee may consult personal counsel;
- the employee should keep the interview confidential;
- the employee is not obligated to answer, though consequences of refusal should be explained consistent with law.

Repeat or adapt the warning when the witness becomes a potential subject, when the interview turns to sensitive topics, or when the witness asks whom counsel represents. Document that the warning was given and acknowledged.

### Separate Witness Interviews From Subject Interviews

The way you treat a witness differs from how you treat a subject. A witness is someone with relevant knowledge who is not, at present, accused of misconduct. A subject is someone whose conduct is under scrutiny and who may face discipline or referral.

For witnesses, emphasize cooperation, accuracy, and confidentiality. For subjects, the stakes are different. Consider:

- whether the subject should be told they are a subject;
- whether the subject should have personal counsel present, and what rights they have under local law and policy;
- whether works council, labor law, or procedural protections apply;
- whether to provide documents in advance or during the interview;
- whether to confront the subject with findings or reserve conclusions;
- the risk that the subject will resign, delete evidence, or coordinate with others after learning the scope.

In many jurisdictions, an employee's right to counsel, to refuse self-incrimination, and to prior disclosure of allegations varies. Coordinate with employment counsel before a subject interview, especially outside the United States.

### Use Funnel Technique: Open To Closed

Effective interviews move from the broad to the specific. Begin with an open invitation to describe the events in the witness's own words. Listen fully before interrupting. Then ask expanding questions about what they described. Only then move to closed, specific questions and document confrontation.

The funnel serves several purposes:

- it captures the witness's unaided recollection before anchoring;
- it reveals what the witness volunteers versus omits;
- it surfaces facts the investigator did not know to ask;
- it lets the investigator compare the volunteered account against documents and other witnesses;
- it preserves credibility assessment material, such as consistency, detail, and demeanor.

Resist the urge to jump to the key question. The key question asked too early teaches the witness what answer is expected.

### Document Consistently And Contemporaneously

Interview notes are evidence. Their reliability depends on how and when they are taken. Avoid reconstructing notes from memory days later, and avoid verbatim transcripts that capture tone without analysis.

Best practice includes:

- a designated note-taker so the interviewer can focus;
- notes that distinguish what the witness said from the interviewer's questions and from documents shown;
- contemporaneous notation of the Upjohn warning and acknowledgment;
- specific language for key admissions or denials, marked as direct quotes where possible;
- notation of non-verbal cues only where relevant and not as conclusive evidence;
- a witness statement or affirmation where appropriate and lawful;
- storage under privilege and access controls.

In some jurisdictions, employees have a right to receive or correct a copy of their statement. Know the local rule before promising or denying access.

### Assess Credibility With Discipline, Not Intuition

Investigators must weigh conflicting accounts. Credibility assessment is necessary but dangerous when it relies on demeanor, accent, or gut feeling, which are unreliable and biased.

Ground credibility in:

- internal consistency within the statement;
- consistency with documents and system records;
- consistency with independent witnesses;
- plausibility given opportunity, motive, and knowledge;
- detail and specificity versus vagueness;
- corroboration of contested points;
- motive to misrepresent;
- prior inconsistent statements.

Document the basis for credibility findings. Avoid stating that someone seemed evasive without tying it to specific contradictions. Demeanor alone should never sustain a finding against a person.

### Handle Reluctant, Hostile, Or Self-Interested Witnesses Deliberately

Not every witness cooperates. Some are frightened, some are complicit, some are pursuing personal grievances, and some are protected by law from adverse consequences for raising concerns.

For reluctant witnesses:

- clarify obligations under policy and law, and consequences of non-cooperation;
- distinguish legitimate refusal, such as self-incrimination, from obstruction;
- avoid coercive tactics that taint the statement or create retaliation exposure;
- consider whether the witness is also a whistleblower with protection.

For witnesses with grievances:

- separate verifiable facts from characterization;
- test for embellishment and motive;
- corroborate before relying on allegations.

Never threaten discipline to extract a statement from a whistleblower. The retaliation risk, legal and reputational, is severe and often independent of whether the underlying concern was substantiated.

## Common Traps

### Confronting The Subject Too Early

Interviewing the subject before building the documentary and testimonial record hands them the investigation's boundaries and lets them craft a defense around the gaps. Save the subject for last.

### Contaminating Witnesses By Sharing Accounts

Telling a witness what others said before they give their own account destroys independent recollection and makes later corroboration meaningless. Let each witness speak first.

### Omitting Or Muddling The Upjohn Warning

A vague warning that the company's lawyer is here to help invites the later argument that the employee thought counsel represented them personally. Give the warning clearly, contemporaneously, and in writing where possible.

### Leading With The Key Question

Asking did you approve the off-books payment before the witness has narrated teaches them the expected answer and forfeits the chance to capture their unaided account.

### Treating Demeanor As Proof

Concluding someone lied because they seemed nervous conflates stress with dishonesty and invites bias. Anchor credibility in consistency and corroboration, not gut.

### Promising Confidentiality You Cannot Keep

Telling a witness their statement will be kept completely confidential is a promise the investigation may not be able to honor, given disclosure, litigation, and regulator demands. Promise only need-to-know sharing.

### Letting Notes Drift Into Reconstruction

Notes written from memory days later are weak evidence and easy to challenge. Take contemporaneous notes or a same-day memorandum, and distinguish quotes from summary.

## Self-Check

- Is the interview sequence planned so documents and peripheral witnesses precede central witnesses and the subject, with a rationale for any deviation?
- Does each interview have a written plan covering objectives, documents to be shown after open narration, and the specific contradictions to probe?
- Is a clear Upjohn warning given and acknowledged at the outset, documented contemporaneously, and repeated if the witness becomes a subject?
- Are witness interviews distinguished from subject interviews, with employment counsel consulted on local rights to personal counsel, prior notice, and statement access?
- Does the interview use funnel technique, moving from open narration to expanding questions to closed confrontation, before showing documents?
- Are notes taken contemporaneously by a designated note-taker, distinguishing quotes from summary and questions from answers, and stored under privilege?
- Are credibility findings grounded in internal consistency, documentary consistency, corroboration, and motive rather than demeanor or intuition?
- Are reluctant, hostile, or grievance-driven witnesses handled without coercion, with whistleblower protections respected and threats of discipline avoided?
- Has witness contamination been avoided by not revealing other accounts before each witness gives their own?
- Are confidentiality promises limited to need-to-know sharing rather than absolute secrecy the investigation cannot guarantee?
