# Attorney Client Privilege And Work Product In Investigations

Privilege is the difference between an investigation the organization controls and one it must hand over. When an internal investigation is conducted at the direction of counsel for legal advice or defense, the analysis, interview notes, and findings may be protected from disclosure to regulators, plaintiffs, and other parties. But privilege in internal investigations is fragile, jurisdiction-dependent, and frequently lost through choices made early and carelessly. A company that conducts an investigation as a compliance or HR exercise, then claims privilege when a regulator demands the file, often discovers too late that no privilege ever attached. Outside the United States, the analysis differs further, and in some jurisdictions internal investigation materials enjoy little or no protection at all.

Use this skill when structuring an investigation to preserve privilege, when giving Upjohn warnings to employees, when deciding who directs the inquiry, when managing the risk of waiver through disclosure or regulator cooperation, or when handling privileged materials across jurisdictions and audiences. The goal is to make the agent treat privilege as something designed and maintained, not assumed, and to recognize that the decision to seek or assert privilege carries strategic costs as well as benefits.

## Core Rules

### Establish The Privilege Purpose Before Work Begins

Privilege attaches only when the investigation is conducted for the purpose of obtaining or providing legal advice, or in anticipation of litigation, by or at the direction of a lawyer. A compliance-driven or HR-driven inquiry, however thorough, does not become privileged just because a lawyer is involved.

To establish privilege purpose:

- have counsel, preferably outside counsel for sensitive matters, formally direct and oversee the investigation;
- document the instruction and the legal purpose, such as defending potential claims or advising on disclosure obligations;
- ensure the work is done for counsel, not as a routine business or compliance function;
- separate privileged investigative work from ordinary compliance monitoring that would exist regardless;
- confirm the legal purpose is genuine and primary, not a post-hoc label.

If the real purpose is operational remediation or a routine compliance review, do not pretend it is privileged. Mislabeling invites waiver findings and credibility loss. Choose the structure honestly, and understand what each choice costs in terms of protection.

### Identify The Client Correctly

In a corporate investigation, the client is the entity, not its employees, directors, or officers. This is the foundation of the Upjohn doctrine in the United States. Confusion about who the client is the most common source of privilege disputes.

Clarify:

- counsel represents the company, and the privilege belongs to the company;
- the company, through appropriate governance, decides whether to assert or waive privilege;
- individual employees are not the client merely because they cooperate;
- directors and officers may raise questions of personal counsel and separate representation, especially where their interests may diverge from the entity;
- the entity's control group, not the investigator, decides waiver.

When an employee asks whom counsel represents, the answer must be the company. Ambiguity here can lead a court or regulator to find that the employee reasonably believed counsel represented them personally, complicating both privilege and the use of their statements.

### Give A Clear And Documented Upjohn Warning

The Upjohn warning is the notice to an employee that counsel represents the company, that the interview is for legal advice to the company, that the privilege belongs to the company and may be waived, and that the employee may consult personal counsel. A clear warning protects privilege and protects the employee from misunderstanding.

A sound warning covers:

- counsel represents the company, not the employee personally;
- the interview is confidential and conducted to provide legal advice to the company;
- the privilege belongs to the company, which may waive it and disclose what is said;
- the employee is not required to answer, though the consequences of refusal should be explained consistent with policy and law;
- the employee may have personal counsel present where permitted;
- the employee should keep the interview confidential.

Give the warning at the outset, document that it was given and acknowledged, and repeat or refine it if the employee's status shifts toward subject, or if sensitive topics arise. A warning buried in a long preamble, or given orally without notation, is weak protection.

### Distinguish Attorney-Client Privilege From Work Product

These are two different protections with different requirements and different weaknesses. Conflating them leads to mismanagement.

Attorney-client privilege protects confidential communications between lawyer and client for legal advice. In internal investigations it covers communications with employees when the Upjohn conditions are met. It is lost if the communication is shared with non-need-to-know parties or used for a non-legal purpose.

Work product protection covers materials prepared in anticipation of litigation by or for a party or its representative, including the investigator's analysis, interview notes, and mental impressions. It is generally more robust against disclosure of counsel's mental impressions but can be overcome on a showing of substantial need and undue hardship for fact materials.

Note that work product protection for interview notes can be fragile. Some courts have held that a witness's own statement is not work product as to that witness, and that disclosure to the government may waive work product as to third parties. Analyze each protection separately rather than assuming one covers everything.

### Manage Waiver Risk Deliberately

Privilege is easily waived and rarely restored. The most common waiver routes are selective disclosure, sharing with non-need-to-know parties, using privileged materials for a non-legal purpose, and providing materials to the government without a common-interest or confidentiality understanding.

To manage waiver:

- limit distribution to those who need the material for the legal purpose;
- avoid selective disclosure that waives privilege as to the shared content and potentially the underlying subject;
- do not use privileged findings for ordinary business decisions in a way that converts them to non-privileged use;
- if sharing with the government, consider a common interest, joint defense, or confidentiality arrangement, and understand that some waiver may still occur;
- track every disclosure of privileged material;
- separate privileged deliverables from non-privileged HR, audit, or operational outputs.

The decision to share privileged materials with a regulator is strategic. Cooperation credit may be valuable, but it can come at the cost of waiver that exposes the materials to private plaintiffs. Weigh the tradeoff explicitly, and document who authorized the disclosure.

### Coordinate Across Jurisdictions

Privilege in internal investigations varies dramatically by jurisdiction. What is protected in the United States may enjoy no protection in the European Union, where legal professional privilege may be narrower and may not extend to in-house counsel or to materials gathered for compliance rather than litigation.

Address cross-border issues:

- determine which jurisdictions' privilege law applies to each set of materials;
- recognize that in many civil law jurisdictions, internal investigation files have limited or no privilege protection;
- consider whether materials created in one jurisdiction can be compelled in another;
- structure collection and storage to minimize exposure in less protective jurisdictions;
- involve local counsel where employees, data, or conduct span borders;
- account for data protection law, which may restrict transferring investigation data and may create employee access rights that conflict with privilege claims.

A U.S.-style privileged investigation conducted across European operations without local counsel input can produce materials that are unprotected abroad and that employees may have rights to access. Plan the cross-border architecture before collecting data or interviewing staff.

### Decide Whether To Assert Or Waiver Strategically

Sometimes the organization chooses to waive privilege, for example to demonstrate cooperation, to support a self-disclosure, or to share findings with a board or auditor. Waiver should be a deliberate strategic decision, not an accident.

Consider:

- the cooperation credit or relationship benefit sought;
- the risk of waiver exposing materials to private litigation;
- whether partial waiver creates subject-matter waiver risk;
- whether a confidentiality or common-interest agreement limits collateral waiver;
- the governance authority required to waive, often board or general counsel;
- the inability to un-waive once disclosure occurs.

Do not waive incrementally through carelessness. If waiver is the strategy, do it deliberately and understand the scope. If preservation is the strategy, enforce it consistently.

### Document The Privilege Architecture Throughout

Privilege is proven by records. If challenged, the organization must show the legal purpose, counsel's direction, the client identity, the Upjohn warnings, the distribution controls, and the disclosure log.

Maintain:

- the engagement or instruction documenting counsel's direction and legal purpose;
- Upjohn warning records per interview;
- distribution and access logs for privileged materials;
- a privilege log for any withheld documents in production;
- records of any authorized disclosure and the basis;
- separation between privileged and non-privileged workstreams.

A privilege claim without supporting documentation is an assertion, not a protection. Build the record contemporaneously; it cannot be reconstructed credibly after a challenge.

## Common Traps

### Assuming Privilege Attaches Because A Lawyer Is Involved

Mere involvement of counsel does not privilege an investigation. The work must be directed by counsel for legal advice or defense, with a genuine and primary legal purpose.

### Muddling Who The Client Is

Letting employees believe counsel represents them personally undermines both privilege and statement reliability. Identify the entity as the client and give clear Upjohn warnings.

### Treating Work Product As Absolute

Work product, especially fact work product and witness statements, can be overcome and waived. Analyze it separately from attorney-client privilege and do not assume it shields everything.

### Waiving Through Selective Disclosure

Sharing privileged findings with business leaders, auditors, or a regulator without analysis can waive privilege as to the shared subject and expose materials to plaintiffs. Control every disclosure.

### Ignoring Cross-Border Differences

A U.S.-style privileged investigation conducted abroad may produce unprotected, even accessible, materials. Involve local counsel and structure for the least protective jurisdiction.

### Using Privileged Materials For Non-Legal Purposes

Relying on privileged findings for ordinary business or compliance decisions can convert them to non-privileged use and waive protection. Separate privileged analysis from operational use.

### Failing To Document The Privilege Record

A privilege claim challenged later needs contemporaneous records of purpose, direction, warnings, and distribution. Build the record as you go, not after a demand arrives.

## Self-Check

- Is the investigation directed by counsel for a genuine and primary legal purpose, documented at the outset, rather than labeled privileged after the fact?
- Is the client correctly identified as the entity, with the privilege belonging to the company and waiver decisions reserved to appropriate governance?
- Is a clear Upjohn warning given and acknowledged at each employee interview, documented contemporaneously, and repeated if status shifts toward subject?
- Are attorney-client privilege and work product analyzed separately, recognizing the different requirements and the fragility of witness-statement work product?
- Is waiver risk managed through need-to-know distribution, avoidance of selective disclosure, separation of privileged from non-privileged deliverables, and a tracked disclosure log?
- Has any disclosure to the government been weighed for cooperation benefit against waiver exposure to private parties, with the authority and basis documented?
- Are cross-border privilege differences accounted for, with local counsel involved and collection and storage structured for the least protective jurisdiction?
- Is the privilege architecture documented throughout, including instruction, warnings, distribution logs, privilege logs, and disclosure records?
- Has the strategic choice to assert or waive privilege been made deliberately at the right governance level, rather than through incremental accident?
- Are privileged investigation materials kept separate from ordinary compliance and operational workstreams to avoid converting them to non-privileged use?
