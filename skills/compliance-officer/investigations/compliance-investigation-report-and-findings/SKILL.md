# Compliance Investigation Report And Findings

The investigation report is where months of work become a conclusion, and where most investigations are undermined. A report that overstates, that blurs fact and inference, that omits the standard of proof, or that waives privilege through careless distribution can convert a sound inquiry into a liability. Regulators, courts, boards, and disciplinary bodies read the report as the organization's own account of what happened, and they will hold the organization to every word. A report is not a summary of impressions; it is a defensible analytical product whose every finding must trace back to evidence and whose every recommendation must be tied to root cause.

Use this skill when drafting an investigation report or findings memorandum, when framing conclusions and applying the standard of proof, when deciding what to include or omit, when protecting privilege in the deliverable, when writing recommendations, or when determining the audience and retention of the report. The goal is to make the agent treat the report as the most scrutinized output of the investigation, written for hostile readers as well as friendly ones.

## Core Rules

### Decide The Report's Purpose And Audience Before Drafting

A report written for the board, for HR discipline, for a regulator, and for litigation are different documents. Drafting one report to serve all audiences usually serves none well and often waives privilege or exposes the organization.

Define before writing:

- the primary decision-maker and what decision the report must support;
- whether the report is privileged or intended for disclosure;
- the level of detail, from full findings to executive summary;
- whether individuals are named or role-based;
- whether legal conclusions are included or only facts;
- the retention and access controls on the report;
- whether a written report is needed at all, or an oral briefing suffices.

A privileged oral briefing to counsel and the board may be safer than a written report that a regulator later demands. The decision to create a durable written record is itself consequential; make it deliberately.

### State The Scope, Methodology, And Standard Of Proof

A report's findings are only as credible as the process behind them. Readers need to know what was investigated, how, and against what standard.

Open the report with:

- the allegation or trigger and its source;
- the factual questions the investigation set out to answer;
- the scope, including what was in and out and why;
- the documents and data reviewed and the systems covered;
- the witnesses interviewed and the basis for selection;
- the methodology, including forensic or analytical steps;
- the standard of proof applied and the meaning of each finding label;
- the limitations, such as unavailable witnesses, deleted data, or time constraints.

Without this framing, a reader cannot judge whether a finding of unsubstantiated means the conduct did not occur or merely was not proven. State the standard explicitly and apply it consistently.

### Separate Fact, Inference, And Conclusion

The most common report failure is blurring these layers. Facts are supported by evidence; inferences are reasonable deductions from facts; conclusions are judgments applying the standard of proof. Mixing them lets a reader treat an inference as a proven fact or a conclusion as self-evident.

Structure findings so that:

- each factual statement cites the supporting document or interview;
- inferences are labeled as such, with their basis stated;
- conclusions explicitly apply the standard of proof;
- the reasoning chain from evidence to inference to conclusion is visible;
- load-bearing assumptions are identified;
- alternative explanations are addressed and why they are rejected is stated.

A reader should be able to follow how the investigator got from the evidence to the conclusion without accepting any unsupported leap. Where the chain depends on credibility, say so and explain the credibility basis.

### Apply Consistent Finding Labels

Inconsistent labeling destroys a report's usefulness. Define what each label means and use it uniformly.

Typical labels:

- substantiated: the evidence establishes the conduct more likely than not, or to the applicable standard;
- partially substantiated: some elements are established, others are not;
- unsubstantiated: the evidence does not establish the conduct, which is not the same as establishing it did not occur;
- unfounded: the evidence establishes the conduct did not occur;
- unable to determine: the evidence is insufficient to reach a conclusion.

The distinction between unsubstantiated and unfounded is critical and frequently botched. Unsubstantiated means not proven; unfounded means disproven. Mislabeling an unsubstantiated matter as unfounded can be cited later as a cover-up. Define the labels in the methodology and apply them mechanically to the evidence.

### Tie Findings To Root Cause, Not Just Misconduct

A report that stops at who did wrong misses why it was possible. Root cause analysis converts an investigation from blame into learning and is what regulators and boards expect.

For each finding, analyze:

- the policy or control that failed or was absent;
- the supervision or oversight gap;
- the incentive or pressure that contributed;
- the training or awareness deficiency;
- the system or access weakness;
- the cultural or environmental factor;
- whether the issue is isolated or systemic;
- whether it recurred from a prior unremediated finding.

Root cause drives the recommendations. A report that recommends discipline without addressing the control failure invites recurrence and signals a superficial inquiry to any regulator who reads it.

### Draft Recommendations That Are Specific, Owned, And Verifiable

Vague recommendations such as improve training or strengthen controls are unenforceable and unverifiable. Each recommendation should be actionable and tracked.

For each recommendation:

- state the specific action, not a theme;
- identify the root cause it addresses;
- assign an owner and a target date;
- define what completion looks like, the evidence required;
- identify any interim control needed pending full remediation;
- specify how effectiveness will be tested, not just implementation;
- tie to the remediation tracking process for closure.

Distinguish corrective action, fixing the immediate issue, from preventive action, addressing the systemic cause. A report that recommends only corrective action leaves the root cause intact.

### Protect Privilege And Minimize Collateral Exposure

A report can waive privilege and create collateral exposure through what it says and to whom it goes. Privilege is fragile and easily lost.

Protect privilege by:

- directing the investigation and report through counsel where privilege is intended;
- marking drafts and the final report appropriately, though marking alone does not create privilege;
- limiting distribution to those who need it for the legal purpose;
- avoiding selective sharing that waives privilege as to shared content;
- separating the privileged findings from any non-privileged HR or operational deliverable;
- minimizing personal data of non-subjects;
- avoiding legal conclusions in documents likely to be disclosed unless intended.

Consider whether the report should exist in written form at all. For sensitive matters, a privileged oral report to counsel and the governing body, with a separate controlled written summary, may better preserve options. Once a written report is created, it is discoverable in most circumstances.

### Address Limitations And Inconclusive Findings Honestly

A report that papers over gaps reads as a cover-up. Honest limitations build credibility and reduce the risk of later contradiction.

Disclose:

- witnesses who were unavailable or declined;
- data that was deleted, incomplete, or not collected;
- time constraints that limited depth;
- conflicts that could not be resolved;
- findings that rest on credibility assessments;
- areas where the standard of proof was not met.

An inconclusive finding, properly framed, is defensible. A falsely conclusive finding is not. Regulators distinguish organizations that acknowledge the limits of their own inquiries from those that overclaim.

## Common Traps

### Overstating Findings Beyond The Evidence

Writing that the conduct clearly occurred when the evidence only supports more likely than not invites challenge and undermines the whole report. Calibrate language to the evidence and standard.

### Blurring Fact And Inference

Letting inferences read as facts hides the reasoning and lets readers treat judgment as proof. Label inferences and show the chain.

### Confusing Unsubstantiated With Unfounded

Labeling an unproven matter as disproven can be cited as exculpatory misrepresentation. Use the labels precisely as defined.

### One Report For Every Audience

A single sprawling report for board, HR, regulator, and litigation usually waives privilege and over-discloses. Match the deliverable to the audience.

### Recommendations Without Root Cause

Recommending discipline or training without addressing the control failure leaves the cause intact and signals a shallow inquiry. Tie every recommendation to root cause.

### Vague, Unowned Recommendations

Improve controls is not a recommendation. Specify the action, owner, evidence of completion, and effectiveness test.

### Careless Distribution Waiving Privilege

Forwarding the report broadly, or to a regulator without considering waiver, can forfeit privilege over the analysis. Control distribution deliberately.

## Self-Check

- Is the report's purpose, primary audience, privilege posture, detail level, naming approach, and retention decided before drafting?
- Does the report open with the allegation, scope, methodology, documents and witnesses, standard of proof, and limitations clearly stated?
- Are facts, inferences, and conclusions separated, with each factual statement cited to evidence and each inference's basis shown?
- Are finding labels defined and applied consistently, with the distinction between unsubstantiated and unfounded respected?
- Does each finding include root-cause analysis addressing policy, supervision, incentives, training, systems, and culture?
- Are recommendations specific, owned, dated, tied to root cause, with defined completion evidence and effectiveness testing?
- Is privilege protected through counsel direction, controlled distribution, marking, and separation of privileged from non-privileged deliverables?
- Are limitations, unavailable evidence, unresolved conflicts, and inconclusive findings disclosed honestly rather than papered over?
- Is the language calibrated to the evidence and standard of proof, avoiding overstatement that invites contradiction?
- Has the option of an oral privileged briefing with a controlled written summary been considered instead of a single broad written report?
