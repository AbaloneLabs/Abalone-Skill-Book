# Compliance Investigation Planning And Scoping

An investigation that begins without a defined scope tends to expand without discipline. Investigators start with a narrow allegation, then drift into adjacent conduct, historical grievances, and personal disputes, until the workplan, budget, privilege, and reporting obligations lose coherence. The opposite failure is just as damaging: a scope so tight that it answers a comfortable question while ignoring the obvious adjacent risk that triggered concern. Planning and scoping is the stage where an investigation is most likely to be saved or ruined, because every later choice, who is interviewed, what documents are collected, whether privilege attaches, what is reported to the regulator, depends on decisions made before evidence is touched.

Use this skill before launching an internal compliance investigation, when converting a triaged hotline report into a formal inquiry, when defining a workplan for outside counsel or forensic accountants, or when an existing investigation has lost focus and needs rescoping. The goal is to force deliberate decisions about objectives, boundaries, team composition, independence, privilege, and reporting, rather than letting the investigation define itself through ad hoc discovery.

## Core Rules

### Define Objectives As Questions, Not Outcomes

An investigation needs an explicit set of questions to answer. Vague objectives such as look into the payments issue produce vague, unfocused work. Write the objectives as testable questions.

For each investigation, record:

- the specific allegation or trigger and its source;
- the precise factual questions the investigation must answer;
- the policy, law, or obligation against which conduct will be assessed;
- whether the inquiry covers a single transaction, a time period, a business unit, or a person;
- whether the objective is fact-finding, legal conclusion, disciplinary support, regulatory disclosure, or a combination;
- what is explicitly out of scope and why;
- who the decision-maker is for scope changes.

Distinguish what the organization needs to know from what would merely be interesting. Scope creep is usually driven by curiosity and by the absence of a written boundary. When a new thread appears, decide deliberately whether to expand scope, open a separate investigation, or document and defer.

### Assess The Trigger And Determine The Reporting Posture First

Before defining depth, understand what legal or regulatory obligations the trigger has already created. A whistleblower complaint, a subpoena, a regulator inquiry, a mandatory breach notification clock, and an anonymous hotline tip carry very different obligations and urgency.

Identify early:

- any mandatory notification deadline, such as data breach, suspicious activity, or material event reporting;
- whether a regulator, law enforcement, or litigation hold already exists;
- whether the matter is likely to require self-disclosure;
- whether the allegation implicates senior management, board members, or a controlling shareholder;
- whether there is parallel litigation or government interest;
- whether there are insurance or contractual notice obligations;
- whether employee protection, works council, or labor law constraints apply.

The reporting posture shapes everything: a matter heading toward voluntary disclosure may justify a deeper, privilege-protected investigation led by outside counsel, while a routine internal concern may be handled by compliance directly. Do not let the depth of inquiry be set by accident.

### Confirm Independence And Manage Conflicts

Investigation credibility depends on independence. An investigator who reports to the subject, who personally benefits from a particular outcome, or who is enmeshed in the conduct under review cannot produce a credible result, regardless of competence.

Assess for each team member:

- reporting line relative to any subject;
- personal or financial relationship with the subject or affected business;
- prior involvement in the transactions or approvals under review;
- incentive to minimize or maximize findings;
- whether the function, such as HR or internal audit, has a stake in the outcome;
- whether outside counsel or an independent reviewer is required for appearance as well as substance.

For matters involving senior executives, board members, or potential disclosure, default to outside counsel or an independent committee. Document the independence analysis. If a conflict is discovered mid-investigation, pause and restructure rather than continue and rationalize.

### Build A Workplan With Sequenced Steps

A workplan converts objectives into a defensible process. It also creates the record that demonstrates thoroughness and fairness later.

A credible workplan includes:

- the legal and policy framework being applied;
- the universe of documents and data to be collected and from which systems;
- the custodian list and the basis for inclusion;
- a litigation or preservation hold with issuance and confirmation tracked;
- the forensic or analytical steps required, such as email review, transaction testing, or system log analysis;
- the witness list, interview sequence, and rationale for order;
- interim controls to be applied while the investigation proceeds;
- privilege approach and who directs the investigation for privilege purposes;
- evidence handling and storage protocol;
- timeline, milestones, and budget;
- reporting audience and format;
- criteria for substantiation and the standard of proof applied.

Sequence matters. Generally, preserve and collect documents before interviews, interview peripheral witnesses before central ones, and interview the subject last. Investigating with partial information produces inconsistent statements and missed corroboration.

### Define The Standard Of Proof And Substantiation Criteria

Investigators often reach different conclusions from the same facts because they applied different standards. Decide in advance how findings will be characterized.

Define:

- the standard of proof, such as more likely than not, clear and convincing, or sufficient evidence to support a conclusion;
- what constitutes corroboration and how many sources are needed;
- how credibility conflicts will be resolved and documented;
- how to characterize evidence that is suggestive but not conclusive;
- whether findings will be framed as substantiated, unsubstantiated, or unable to determine, and what each label means.

Applying the standard consistently prevents the tendency to find misconduct where it is suspected and to excuse it where it is inconvenient. Write the standard into the workplan, not into the conclusion.

### Establish Privilege And Confidentiality Architecture Early

Privilege is not automatic. It depends on why the investigation is conducted, who directs it, who performs the work, and how findings are communicated. Decisions made at the planning stage determine whether privilege can later be claimed.

Address:

- whether the investigation is conducted at the direction of counsel for the purpose of legal advice or defense;
- who is the client for privilege purposes, usually the entity, not individual employees;
- whether Upjohn warnings will be given and in what form;
- how interview notes, workpapers, and drafts will be marked and stored;
- who receives findings and on what basis;
- the risk of waiver through selective sharing or reporting to regulators;
- how the work product doctrine may apply separately from attorney-client privilege.

Privilege decisions are easy to lose and nearly impossible to reconstruct. Plan the privilege architecture before the first interview, not after a disclosure demand.

### Set Interim Controls And Risk Containment

An investigation takes time. Meanwhile, the conduct under review may continue, evidence may be altered, and witnesses may coordinate. Interim controls contain risk while the inquiry proceeds.

Consider:

- preservation or litigation hold on relevant data;
- suspension or restriction of the subject's access to systems, funds, or counterparties;
- enhanced monitoring of the relevant process or business unit;
- temporary reassignment or paid leave where appropriate and lawful;
- confidentiality reminders to those who must know;
- travel, payment, or approval freezes on specific transactions;
- communication restrictions to prevent witness coordination.

Interim controls must be proportionate and lawful. Suspending an employee before any finding can itself create liability, especially under labor and whistleblower protection laws. Document the basis for each interim measure.

### Plan The Reporting Audience And Format

Who learns the outcome, and in what form, is a scoping decision. A report written for the board differs from one written for HR discipline, a regulator, or litigation.

Define:

- the primary audience and decision-maker;
- whether a written report is required or an oral briefing suffices;
- the level of detail and whether findings will be documented in a privileged form;
- whether personal data will be minimized;
- how the report handles individuals who are not substantiated subjects;
- whether and when regulators, auditors, insurers, or the board will be informed;
- retention and access controls on the report itself.

Avoid the trap of a single sprawling report that tries to serve every audience and ends up waiving privilege or exposing the organization. Plan the deliverable as deliberately as the fact-finding.

## Common Traps

### Scoping To The Comfortable Question

Defining scope around what is easy to investigate, rather than what triggered concern, produces a clean report that misses the real risk. Test the scope against the original trigger before approving it.

### Letting Scope Drift Without Documentation

Investigators discover adjacent issues and quietly absorb them. Each expansion should be a deliberate, documented decision with an updated workplan, not an organic accretion.

### Investigating With A Conflicted Team

Using the subject's direct reports, the business owner of the affected process, or an HR partner with a prior grievance against the subject undermines credibility even when the work is sound. Independence is judged by appearance as well as substance.

### Skipping The Preservation Step

Beginning interviews before data is preserved invites spoliation, witness coordination, and lost evidence. The hold should be among the first acts, documented and confirmed.

### Treating Privilege As An Afterthought

Conducting the investigation as a compliance or HR exercise, then claiming privilege when a regulator arrives, frequently fails. Privilege must be designed in from the first instruction.

### Setting Depth By Resource Comfort

Limiting interviews or document collection because the budget is tight, then declaring the matter unsubstantiated, confuses inconclusive with exonerating. If resources are insufficient for the scope, either expand resources or narrow and document the limitation honestly.

### Failing To Define The Standard Of Proof

Reaching a conclusion without a stated standard lets the investigator apply intuition. Define substantiation criteria before findings, then apply them evenhandedly.

## Self-Check

- Are the investigation objectives written as specific factual questions, with an explicit statement of what is in scope and what is out of scope and why?
- Has the trigger been assessed for mandatory notification deadlines, parallel proceedings, senior management involvement, and disclosure likelihood before depth was set?
- Is the independence of every team member documented, with conflicts assessed and outside counsel or independent review used where the subject is senior or disclosure is likely?
- Does the workplan sequence preservation, document collection, peripheral witnesses, central witnesses, and subject interview, with a rationale for the order?
- Is the standard of proof and the meaning of substantiated, unsubstantiated, and unable to determine defined in the workplan before findings are reached?
- Has the privilege architecture been designed before the first interview, including who directs the investigation, who the client is, and how notes and reports will be handled?
- Are interim controls in place to contain ongoing risk, with each measure documented as proportionate and lawful?
- Is the reporting audience and format planned, distinguishing privileged findings from HR, board, and regulator deliverables?
- Are scope changes documented as deliberate decisions with updated workplans rather than silent drift?
- Does the scope honestly distinguish what the organization needs to know from what would merely be interesting, and is the inconclusive outcome distinguished from exoneration?
