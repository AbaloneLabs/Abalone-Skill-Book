# Environmental Impact Assessment And NEPA Compliance

Environmental impact assessment governs the process of evaluating the environmental consequences of proposed projects before approval. The defining feature is that the assessment is a process obligation—the decision-maker must consider environmental impacts and alternatives before acting—and courts will set aside decisions that fail to follow the process, regardless of the substantive outcome. The central difficulty is that the assessment must be rigorous enough to inform decision-making but cannot become an endless search for perfect information, and the line between sufficient analysis and arbitrary corner-cutting is contested.

Use this skill before advising on environmental review triggers, impact statement preparation, alternatives analysis, public participation, or judicial review risks. The goal is to make the agent identify the review triggers, the required level of analysis, the alternatives and cumulative impact duties, and the documentation standards before concluding that an environmental review is compliant.

## Core Rules

### Determine Whether Environmental Review Is Triggered

Environmental review is triggered by federal action and, under state laws, by other actions.

Assess:

- federal action triggers (agency decisions, permits, funding, approvals);
- major federal action significance threshold;
- state environmental review laws (mini-NEPAs) for non-federal actions;
- private projects requiring government approval;
- categorical exclusions for actions with no significant impact;
- the distinction between actions requiring review and those that do not.

Not every project requires federal environmental review, but many projects involving federal permits, funding, or land require analysis. State mini-NEPAs extend review to projects with state or local approval.

### Determine The Required Level Of Review

The level of review depends on the significance of impacts.

Assess:

- categorical exclusion (CE) for actions with no significant environmental impact;
- environmental assessment (EA) to determine significance;
- finding of no significant impact (FONSI) if the EA shows no significant impact;
- environmental impact statement (EIS) for actions significantly affecting the environment;
- the threshold question of whether an EIS is required;
- supplementation when circumstances change.

The EIS is the most rigorous level and is required for significant impacts. An EA that properly concludes with a FONSI avoids an EIS, but a flawed EA can be challenged for failing to prepare an EIS.

### Prepare A Legally Sufficient Impact Statement

An EIS must meet specific content and process requirements.

Implement:

- notice of intent to prepare an EIS;
- scoping to identify significant issues;
- draft EIS with required sections (purpose and need, alternatives, affected environment, environmental consequences);
- public comment period and hearings;
- response to comments in the final EIS;
- record of decision (ROD) explaining the choice;
- supplementation for significant new information or circumstances.

The EIS must take a hard look at impacts, but it need not be exhaustive. The purpose and need statement frames the analysis; the alternatives section is the heart of the EIS.

### Analyze Alternatives Rigorously

The alternatives analysis is the core of environmental review.

Cover:

- reasonable alternatives that accomplish the purpose and need;
- the no-action alternative;
- the agency's preferred alternative;
- alternatives eliminated from detailed study with reasons;
- environmental impacts of each alternative;
- mitigation measures;
- the range of alternatives must be reasonable, not infinite.

The no-action alternative provides a baseline. The range of alternatives must be reasonable—broad enough to inform choice but not so broad as to be unmanageable. Unreasonable alternatives can be eliminated with explanation.

### Assess Cumulative And Indirect Impacts

Impacts include more than direct project effects.

Assess:

- direct effects of the proposed action;
- indirect effects that are reasonably foreseeable;
- cumulative impacts combined with past, present, and reasonably foreseeable actions;
- connected and cumulative actions that should be analyzed together;
- the geographic and temporal scope of analysis;
- baseline conditions and trends.

Cumulative impacts assess the project in context with other actions. Ignoring cumulative impacts is a common ground for challenge. The analysis must cover the full scope of effects, not just the project footprint.

### Conduct Meaningful Public Participation

Public participation is a required element of the process.

Implement:

- scoping notice and public involvement;
- draft EIS availability and comment period (minimum 45 days);
- public hearings during the comment period;
- meaningful response to substantive comments;
- consideration of comments in the final decision;
- accessibility of documents;
- engagement with affected communities, including environmental justice considerations.

Public participation must be meaningful, not pro forma. Comments must receive substantive responses, and the decision must reflect consideration of input. Environmental justice requires engaging affected communities.

### Address Mitigation And Monitoring

Mitigation can reduce impacts and inform the decision.

Cover:

- mitigation measures to avoid, minimize, rectify, reduce, or compensate for impacts;
- incorporation of mitigation into the preferred alternative;
- mitigation as a basis for a FONSI (avoiding an EIS);
- monitoring and enforcement of mitigation commitments;
- adaptive management;
- the distinction between enforceable mitigation and aspirational measures.

Mitigation can reduce impacts below the significance threshold, but mitigation commitments must be enforceable and monitored. Mitigation used to justify a FONSI must be firm.

### Coordinate Across Agencies And Jurisdictions

Environmental review often involves multiple agencies.

Assess:

- lead agency designation and responsibility;
- cooperating agencies with jurisdiction or special expertise;
- joint EIS processes for multi-agency decisions;
- state and local coordination;
- tribal consultation and government-to-government engagement;
- consistency with other environmental laws (Endangered Species Act, Clean Water Act, National Historic Preservation Act).

Multi-agency projects require coordination to avoid duplication and conflict. Tribal consultation is a government-to-government obligation, not just public participation.

### Manage Judicial Review Risks

Environmental review decisions are subject to judicial challenge.

Cover:

- the arbitrary and capricious standard of review;
- the requirement to take a hard look at impacts;
- adequacy of the alternatives analysis;
- completeness of the scientific record;
- response to comments;
- timing of challenges and standing;
- the risk of injunction halting the project;
- supplementation triggers.

Courts will set aside decisions that fail to take a hard look or that have a flawed alternatives analysis. Challenges can halt projects for years, making thorough preparation essential.

## Common Traps

### EA Used To Avoid An EIS Without Proper Analysis

A rushed EA that concludes FONSI without rigorous analysis invites challenge.

### Alternatives Analysis Too Narrow

Defining purpose and need so narrowly that only the preferred alternative fits is a common flaw.

### Cumulative Impacts Ignored

Analyzing only direct impacts and ignoring cumulative effects is a frequent ground for reversal.

### Public Participation Treated As Pro Forma

Inadequate comment response or minimal engagement undermines the process.

### Mitigation Promised But Not Enforceable

Aspirational mitigation used to justify a FONSI or ROD is not sufficient.

### Supplementation Skipped After Significant New Information

Failing to supplement when circumstances change invalidates the review.

## Self-Check

- Is environmental review triggered by federal action, state mini-NEPA, or government approval, and is the level of review (CE, EA/FONSI, EIS) correctly determined?
- Is the EIS legally sufficient with notice of intent, scoping, required sections, public comment, response to comments, record of decision, and supplementation?
- Is the alternatives analysis rigorous with reasonable alternatives, no-action, preferred alternative, eliminated alternatives, impacts, and mitigation?
- Are cumulative and indirect impacts assessed with connected actions, geographic and temporal scope, and baseline conditions?
- Is public participation meaningful with scoping, comment periods, hearings, substantive responses, accessibility, and environmental justice engagement?
- Are mitigation measures incorporated, enforceable, monitored, and adaptive, and properly used for FONSI or ROD?
- Are agencies coordinated including lead, cooperating, state, local, and tribal consultation?
- Is judicial review risk managed through hard look, adequate alternatives, complete record, comment response, timing, and supplementation?
- Is the distinction between process sufficiency and substantive outcome understood?
- Are connected and cumulative actions analyzed together rather than segmented?
