# Chemical Safety And Hazard Communication Compliance

Chemical safety compliance governs the communication of chemical hazards and the management of chemical risks in the workplace and commerce. The defining feature is that chemical information—about identity, hazards, and safe handling—must flow accurately and consistently from manufacturer through distributor to end user, and the failure at any point in the chain leaves downstream users unprotected. The central difficulty is that hazard communication requires both accurate hazard classification (a technical determination) and effective communication (labels, SDS, training), and gaps in either create exposure.

Use this skill before advising on hazard communication programs, SDS and labeling, chemical inventory reporting, process safety management, or chemical security. The goal is to make the agent identify the chemical inventory, the classification and communication duties, the process safety risks, and the security obligations before concluding that a chemical safety program is compliant.

## Core Rules

### Implement A Comprehensive Hazard Communication Program

HazCom requires a written program and effective communication.

Implement:

- written hazard communication program;
- chemical inventory of all hazardous chemicals in the workplace;
- safety data sheets (SDS) maintained and accessible;
- labeling of containers including primary and secondary;
- employee training on hazards, SDS access, labeling, and protective measures;
- training of contractors and temporary workers;
- periodic program review and update;
- multi-employer workplaces and access to information.

The program must be written and implemented, not just on paper. Training must be effective and in a language workers understand. Secondary containers must be labeled.

### Classify Chemical Hazards Accurately

Hazard classification drives labeling and SDS content.

Cover:

- health hazards (acute toxicity, skin and eye, sensitization, carcinogenicity, reproductive, target organ);
- physical hazards (flammable, combustible, gas, explosive, oxidizer, self-reactive);
- the Globally Harmonized System (GHS) criteria;
- classification based on data, not assumption;
- mixtures and bridging principles;
- self-classification by manufacturers and importers;
- updates when new information becomes available.

Classification is a technical determination using GHS criteria. Incorrect classification leads to inadequate labels and SDS, leaving users uninformed of the actual hazards.

### Maintain Accurate SDS And Labels

SDS and labels are the primary communication tools.

Control:

- 16-section SDS format with required content;
- accuracy and currency of SDS;
- accessibility to employees and emergency responders;
- manufacturer, importer, or distributor responsibility for classification and SDS;
- labels with product identifier, signal word, hazard statements, precautionary statements, pictograms, and supplier identification;
- workplace labeling of secondary containers;
- shipped container labels.

The SDS must follow the 16-section format with accurate information. Labels must include all required elements. Secondary containers used in the workplace must be labeled.

### Comply With TSCA Chemical Inventory And Reporting

TSCA governs the manufacture, processing, and import of chemicals.

Cover:

- TSCA inventory and the distinction between existing and new chemicals;
- premanufacture notice (PMN) for new chemicals;
- significant new use rules (SNURs);
- chemical data reporting (CDR) rule;
- TSCA Section 5(e) consent orders and exposure-based review;
- existing chemical evaluation and risk management under amended TSCA;
- recordkeeping and allegations of significant adverse reactions;
- import certification and certification rules.

New chemicals require PMN before manufacture or import. Existing chemicals may be subject to SNURs, risk evaluations, and risk management rules. CDR requires periodic reporting of production and use data.

### Implement Process Safety Management For Highly Hazardous Chemicals

PSM prevents catastrophic releases of highly hazardous chemicals.

Implement:

- applicability based on quantity of highly hazardous chemicals;
- process hazard analysis (PHA);
- operating procedures;
- training for employees involved in the process;
- contractors and contractor responsibilities;
- mechanical integrity of equipment;
- management of change (MOC);
- pre-startup safety review;
- compliance audits;
- incident investigation;
- employee participation;
- trade secrets and emergency planning.

PSM is a comprehensive management system for processes involving highly hazardous chemicals. MOC is frequently overlooked—changes to equipment, procedures, or chemicals require review. PHA must be updated periodically.

### Manage Chemical Security Obligations

Chemical security addresses the risk of deliberate misuse.

Assess:

- Chemical Facility Anti-Terrorism Standards (CFATS) applicability;
- chemicals of interest and screening threshold quantities;
- security vulnerability assessment and site security plan;
- risk-based performance standards;
- personnel surety and insider threat;
- CFATS reporting and compliance;
- coordination with other security programs;
- maritime and transportation security requirements.

CFATS applies to facilities with quantities of chemicals of interest above threshold quantities. The security plan must address access control, monitoring, cyber, and insider threats. Reporting and compliance are ongoing.

### Address Chemical Incident Prevention And Response

Chemical facilities must prevent and prepare for incidents.

Cover:

- risk management plans (RMP) under the Clean Air Act;
- general duty clause obligations;
- emergency response planning and community right-to-know;
- release notification (EPCRA);
- coordination with local emergency planning committees;
- accidental release prevention programs;
- root-cause analysis and incident investigation.

RMP applies to stationary sources with regulated substances above threshold quantities. EPCRA requires release notification and community right-to-know reporting. The general duty clause requires safe management of extremely hazardous substances.

### Manage Chemical Testing And Animal Welfare

Chemical testing may involve animal welfare considerations.

Assess:

- testing requirements under TSCA and other laws;
- alternative test methods and reducing animal testing;
- data sharing to avoid duplicative testing;
- the intersection of chemical regulation and animal welfare;
- emerging test methods (in vitro, computational).

Amended TSCA encourages reducing and replacing animal testing. Alternative methods and data sharing should be considered where they provide equivalent information.

## Common Traps

### Secondary Containers Unlabeled

Decanting chemicals into unlabeled secondary containers is a common and easily cited violation.

### Classification Based On Assumption Rather Than Data

Guessing at hazards without testing or data leads to inadequate communication.

### SDS Outdated Or Inaccessible

SDS that do not reflect current formulations or that workers cannot access fail the communication purpose.

### Management Of Change Skipped For "Minor" Modifications

Small changes to equipment or procedures can introduce hazards that PSM requires review of.

### CFATS Threshold Overlooked

Facilities that acquire chemicals of interest without assessing CFATS thresholds can be in violation.

### New Chemical Manufactured Or Imported Without PMN

Introducing a new chemical without PMN is a serious violation.

## Self-Check

- Is a comprehensive HazCom program implemented with written plan, chemical inventory, SDS, labeling, training, contractor coverage, and periodic review?
- Are chemical hazards classified accurately using GHS criteria for health and physical hazards, mixtures, and self-classification?
- Are SDS maintained in 16-section format with accuracy, currency, accessibility, and are labels complete with all required elements on primary and secondary containers?
- Is TSCA compliance maintained including inventory status, PMN for new chemicals, SNURs, CDR, consent orders, existing chemical rules, recordkeeping, and import certification?
- Is PSM implemented for highly hazardous chemicals including PHA, operating procedures, training, contractors, mechanical integrity, MOC, pre-startup review, audits, incident investigation, and employee participation?
- Are chemical security obligations assessed including CFATS applicability, chemicals of interest, vulnerability assessment, security plan, personnel surety, and reporting?
- Is chemical incident prevention and response managed through RMP, general duty, emergency planning, EPCRA notification, LEPC coordination, and incident investigation?
- Are chemical testing and animal welfare considerations addressed through alternative methods and data sharing?
- Is the flow of information from manufacturer through distributor to end user maintained?
- Are state chemical regulations that may be stricter than federal applied?
