# Water Quality And Discharge Compliance

Water quality compliance governs the discharge of pollutants into waters of the United States and the management of water as a resource. The defining feature is that the regulatory framework distinguishes between point source discharges (regulated by permit), stormwater (regulated by sector-specific general permits), and nonpoint source pollution (largely unregulated at the federal level), and a single facility can be subject to all three regimes. The central difficulty is that water quality standards are becoming more stringent as watersheds are assessed, and discharges that complied with permit limits can still violate water quality-based requirements.

Use this skill before advising on discharge permits, stormwater management, pretreatment, water withdrawal, or TMDL compliance. The goal is to make the agent identify the discharge points, the applicable permits, the monitoring and reporting duties, and the watershed-level constraints before concluding that a water quality program is compliant.

## Core Rules

### Identify All Point Source Discharges And Applicable Permits

Point source discharges require NPDES permits.

Map:

- process wastewater outfalls;
- cooling water intake and discharge;
- stormwater discharges associated with industrial activity;
- construction stormwater;
- municipal separate storm sewer systems (MS4s);
- concentrated animal feeding operation (CAFO) discharges;
- discharges to publicly owned treatment works (Pretreatment);
- the definition of waters of the United States and jurisdictional status.

Every point source discharge to a water of the United States requires a permit. The definition of waters of the United States has been subject to regulatory and judicial change, affecting jurisdiction. Discharges to a POTW are regulated through pretreatment rather than direct discharge.

### Comply With NPDES Permit Conditions

NPDES permits impose specific limits and conditions.

Control:

- technology-based effluent limits (best practicable, best available, best conventional);
- water quality-based effluent limits where technology limits are insufficient;
- monitoring and reporting requirements;
- discharge monitoring reports (DMRs);
- whole effluent toxicity testing;
- best management practices;
- compliance schedules;
- spill prevention and notification.

Effluent limits include both technology-based and water quality-based components. Water quality-based limits apply where technology limits would not achieve water quality standards. DMRs are the primary compliance reporting mechanism.

### Manage Industrial Stormwater Compliance

Industrial stormwater is regulated through general permits.

Implement:

- coverage under the multi-sector general permit or state equivalent;
- stormwater pollution prevention plan (SWPPP);
- best management practices for pollution control;
- monitoring and benchmark monitoring;
- corrective action for exceedances;
- employee training;
- annual site evaluations;
- the distinction between industrial and construction stormwater.

Industrial stormwater permits require a SWPPP, BMPs, and monitoring. Benchmark exceedances trigger corrective action, not immediate violation, but persistent exceedances require enhanced controls. Construction stormwater is a separate permit triggered by land disturbance.

### Address Pretreatment And Indirect Discharge

Discharges to POTWs are regulated through pretreatment standards.

Cover:

- categorical pretreatment standards for specific industries;
- local limits imposed by the POTW;
- significant indirect discharger requirements;
- pollutant prohibitions (pass-through, interference, hazardous waste);
- sampling and monitoring;
- baseline monitoring reports and compliance schedules;
- the POTW's pretreatment program obligations.

Indirect dischargers must comply with categorical standards and local limits. Discharging pollutants that pass through the POTW untreated or interfere with its operation is prohibited. The POTW administers the pretreatment program.

### Manage Water Withdrawal And Rights

Water withdrawal is regulated by state law and interstate compacts.

Assess:

- riparian, prior appropriation, and regulated riparian systems;
- withdrawal permits and registrations;
- interbasin transfer restrictions;
- consumptive use permits;
- interstate compact allocations;
- water conservation and efficiency requirements;
- the effect of withdrawal on water levels and ecosystems.

Water rights vary dramatically by state. Eastern states generally follow riparian doctrine; western states follow prior appropriation. Interstate compacts allocate water among states and can constrain withdrawals.

### Comply With Total Maximum Daily Loads

TMDLs allocate pollutant loads in impaired watersheds.

Cover:

- identification of impaired waters and TMDL development;
- wasteload allocations for point sources;
- load allocations for nonpoint sources;
- incorporation of TMDL allocations into NPDES permits;
- the effect on existing and new discharges;
- watershed-based trading as a compliance option;
- monitoring to assess progress.

A TMDL constrains the total pollutant load in a watershed. Point source wasteload allocations are incorporated into permits, potentially requiring more stringent limits. New or expanded discharges may be constrained by TMDL capacity.

### Implement Spill Prevention And Response

Spill prevention protects waters from accidental discharges.

Implement:

- Spill Prevention, Control, and Countermeasure (SPCC) plans for oil;
- Facility Response Plans for substantial harm facilities;
- spill containment and diversionary structures;
- inspection and testing;
- training and drills;
- release notification (CWA Section 311, EPCRA);
- coordination with response contractors and agencies.

SPCC plans apply to facilities storing oil above threshold quantities. Facility Response Plans are required for facilities posing substantial harm. Release notification is required under multiple statutes.

### Address Water Reuse And Water Quality Trading

Emerging approaches offer compliance flexibility.

Assess:

- water reuse and reclaimed water standards;
- nutrient and pollutant trading programs;
- wetlands and stream mitigation banking;
- green infrastructure for stormwater management;
- the interaction of water quantity and quality regulation;
- watershed-based permitting.

Water quality trading allows dischargers to meet limits through reductions elsewhere in the watershed. Reuse reduces discharge volumes but must meet water quality standards. Green infrastructure manages stormwater through natural systems.

## Common Traps

### Discharge Point Overlooked Or Misclassified

Failing to identify a discharge point or misclassifying it as non-point source is a fundamental violation.

### Water Quality-Based Limit More Stringent Than Technology Limit

Technology compliance does not ensure water quality compliance; water quality-based limits may be stricter.

### Stormwater Benchmark Exceedance Not Corrected

Persistent benchmark exceedances require enhanced controls, not just continued monitoring.

### Pretreatment Pollutant Causing Pass-Through Or Interference

Discharging pollutants that pass through the POTW or interfere with treatment violates pretreatment standards.

### TMDL Capacity Not Assessed For New Or Expanded Discharge

New discharges may be constrained or denied based on TMDL allocations.

### SPCC Plan Not Implemented Or Updated

An SPCC plan that exists on paper but is not implemented or current is a violation.

## Self-Check

- Are all point source discharges identified including process wastewater, cooling water, stormwater, construction, MS4, CAFO, and pretreatment, with jurisdictional status assessed?
- Are NPDES permit conditions complied with including technology-based and water quality-based limits, monitoring, DMRs, toxicity testing, BMPs, compliance schedules, and spill prevention?
- Is industrial stormwater managed through general permit coverage, SWPPP, BMPs, benchmark monitoring, corrective action, training, and annual evaluation?
- Is pretreatment compliance maintained including categorical standards, local limits, significant indirect discharger requirements, pollutant prohibitions, monitoring, and reporting?
- Is water withdrawal managed under applicable state law including permits, interbasin transfer, consumptive use, interstate compacts, conservation, and ecosystem impact?
- Are TMDLs assessed for impaired watersheds including wasteload allocations, permit incorporation, effect on discharges, trading options, and monitoring?
- Is spill prevention implemented through SPCC plans, Facility Response Plans, containment, inspection, training, notification, and coordination?
- Are water reuse, trading, mitigation banking, green infrastructure, and watershed-based permitting assessed for compliance flexibility?
- Is the distinction between point source and nonpoint source regulation understood?
- Are state water quality requirements that may be stricter than federal applied?
