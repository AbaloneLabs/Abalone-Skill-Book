# Site Remediation And Contaminated Property Compliance

Site remediation governs the investigation and cleanup of contaminated property. The defining feature is that liability for contamination is strict, retroactive, and joint and several under CERCLA, meaning that current and past owners and operators can be held responsible for cleanup costs regardless of fault, and liability can exceed the property's value. The central difficulty is that contamination is often invisible, discovered during transactions or redevelopment, and the legal defenses available depend on due diligence conducted before acquisition.

Use this skill before advising on environmental due diligence, cleanup obligations, liability defenses, brownfield redevelopment, or long-term stewardship. The goal is to make the agent identify the contamination, the liable parties, the applicable cleanup framework, and the available defenses before concluding that a property transaction or cleanup is compliant.

## Core Rules

### Conduct Appropriate Environmental Due Diligence

Due diligence before acquisition can preserve liability defenses.

Implement:

- Phase I Environmental Site Assessment (ESA) following ASTM standards;
- all appropriate inquiry (AAI) requirements for bona fide prospective purchaser (BFPP) protection;
- review of historical records, regulatory databases, and site reconnaissance;
- interviews with knowledgeable parties;
- identification of recognized environmental conditions (RECs);
- Phase II ESA with sampling where RECs are identified;
- the distinction between AAI and more comprehensive due diligence;
- timing of the assessment (within one year of acquisition, with updates within 180 days).

Phase I ESA conducted to ASTM/AI standards is the threshold for BFPP protection. Without it, the purchaser may inherit liability. Phase II sampling follows where Phase I identifies RECs.

### Understand CERCLA Liability Framework

CERCLA liability is strict, retroactive, and joint and several.

Assess:

- potentially responsible party (PRP) categories: current owner, past owner at time of disposal, generator, transporter, arranger;
- strict liability regardless of fault or legality of disposal at the time;
- retroactive liability for disposal before CERCLA;
- joint and several liability where harm is indivisible;
- contribution actions among PRPs;
- the de micromis and municipal solid waste exemptions;
- the settlement and cost recovery framework.

Liability extends to current owners who did not cause the contamination. The BFPP defense, available to purchasers who conduct AAI, is a critical protection. Joint and several liability means any PRP can be held for the full cost.

### Navigate The Liability Defenses

Several defenses can limit or eliminate CERCLA liability.

Cover:

- bona fide prospective purchaser (BFPP): AAI before purchase, no affiliation with a PRP, appropriate care, cooperation, compliance with information requests, no impediment to institutional controls;
- innocent landowner: did not know and had no reason to know (after AAI);
- contiguous property owner: no affiliation, did not contribute, cooperation, appropriate care;
- third-party defense: contamination caused solely by a third party with no contractual relationship;
- the act of God defense.

BFPP is the primary defense for purchasers, but it requires ongoing appropriate care and cooperation. Affiliation with a PRP (e.g., through corporate structure or business relationship) can defeat the defense.

### Manage Cleanup Under CERCLA Or State Programs

Cleanup follows specific frameworks and processes.

Implement:

- CERCLA cleanup: National Priorities List (NPL) listing, remedial investigation and feasibility study (RI/FS), record of decision (ROD), remedial design and action;
- RCRA corrective action;
- state voluntary cleanup programs;
- administrative orders and consent decrees;
- PRP-led cleanups with agency oversight;
- the remedy selection process and criteria;
- long-term operation, maintenance, and monitoring;
- five-year reviews for remedies leaving waste in place.

CERCLA cleanups are comprehensive and agency-driven. State voluntary cleanup programs offer a faster path for less complex sites. The remedy must be protective and comply with applicable or relevant and appropriate requirements (ARARs).

### Conduct Remedial Investigation And Select Remedies

Investigation and remedy selection follow a structured process.

Cover:

- characterization of contamination nature, extent, and source;
- risk assessment (human health and ecological);
- remedial action objectives;
- development and screening of alternatives;
- detailed analysis of alternatives against criteria;
- selection of the remedy;
- community involvement and public comment;
- the nine criteria for remedy selection including overall protection, compliance with ARARs, long-term effectiveness, reduction of toxicity, short-term effectiveness, implementability, cost, state acceptance, and community acceptance.

The remedy must be protective and cost-effective. The nine criteria guide selection. ARARs are non-negotiable requirements that the remedy must meet.

### Address Vapor Intrusion And Emerging Pathways

Vapor intrusion is an evolving area of concern.

Assess:

- vapor intrusion of volatile contaminants from soil and groundwater into buildings;
- sampling and assessment methodologies;
- mitigation systems (sub-slab depressurization, ventilation);
- indoor air quality impacts;
- emerging contaminants (PFAS, 1,4-dioxane) and evolving standards;
- the implications of evolving standards for closed sites.

Vapor intrusion can create indoor air risks that were not assessed in original cleanups. Emerging contaminants like PFAS are creating new liability at sites previously considered clean. Evolving standards may require reopening closed sites.

### Manage Brownfield Redevelopment

Brownfield redevelopment transforms contaminated property for productive use.

Cover:

- brownfield site assessment grants and resources;
- state voluntary cleanup program enrollment;
- prospective purchaser agreements and comfort letters;
- cleanup integrated with redevelopment;
- institutional controls and activity use limitations;
- financing and liability protections;
- community engagement and environmental justice.

Brownfield redevelopment requires coordinating cleanup with construction. Institutional controls restrict use to ensure the remedy remains protective. State programs provide liability protection upon completion.

### Implement Long-Term Stewardship And Institutional Controls

Remedies that leave contamination require long-term management.

Implement:

- institutional controls (deed restrictions, zoning, easements);
- engineering controls (caps, barriers, treatment systems);
- monitoring and maintenance of controls;
- five-year reviews for CERCLA remedies;
- stewardship plans and responsibility allocation;
- notice to future owners and occupants;
- enforcement of use restrictions.

Institutional controls are only effective if monitored and enforced. Future owners must be notified of restrictions. Five-year reviews assess whether the remedy remains protective.

## Common Traps

### Acquisition Without Phase I ESA

Purchasing contaminated property without AAI forfeits the BFPP defense and inherits liability.

### BFPP Defense Lost Through Affiliation Or Failure Of Appropriate Care

Corporate affiliation with a PRP or failure to maintain appropriate care defeats the defense.

### Remedy Selected Without Considering ARARs

A remedy that does not comply with applicable or relevant requirements is not protective.

### Institutional Controls Not Monitored Or Enforced

Unenforced use restrictions allow activities that expose contamination.

### Vapor Intrusion Or Emerging Contaminants Not Assessed

Overlooking vapor intrusion or PFAS leaves risks unaddressed at closed sites.

### Joint And Several Liability Assumed To Be Proportional

Any PRP can be held for the full cleanup cost until contribution is resolved.

## Self-Check

- Is environmental due diligence conducted through Phase I ESA per ASTM/AAI, with Phase II sampling for RECs, and within timing requirements for BFPP protection?
- Is the CERCLA liability framework understood including strict, retroactive, joint and several liability, PRP categories, contribution, and exemptions?
- Are liability defenses navigated including BFPP, innocent landowner, contiguous property owner, third-party, and act of God, with ongoing appropriate care and cooperation?
- Is cleanup managed under CERCLA (NPL, RI/FS, ROD), RCRA corrective action, or state voluntary programs, with remedy selection, ARARs, and long-term monitoring?
- Is remedial investigation and remedy selection conducted with contamination characterization, risk assessment, objectives, alternatives, nine criteria, and community involvement?
- Are vapor intrusion and emerging contaminants assessed including sampling, mitigation, PFAS, evolving standards, and reopening of closed sites?
- Is brownfield redevelopment managed through assessment, voluntary cleanup enrollment, prospective purchaser agreements, integrated cleanup, institutional controls, and community engagement?
- Is long-term stewardship implemented through institutional and engineering controls, monitoring, five-year reviews, stewardship plans, owner notice, and enforcement?
- Is the distinction between AAI for BFPP and more comprehensive due diligence understood?
- Are state voluntary cleanup program protections and limitations assessed?
