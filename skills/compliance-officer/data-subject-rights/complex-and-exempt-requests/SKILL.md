# Complex and Exempt Requests

Most data subject requests follow a predictable pattern, but a significant minority are complex, voluminous, legally sensitive, or potentially abusive. These cases test whether the organisation can apply exemptions correctly, manage privilege and confidentiality, handle requests that are weaponised (for example, in employment disputes or litigation), and refuse genuinely excessive requests without deterring legitimate exercise. The judgment in these cases is high-stakes: wrongly granting a request may disclose privileged or third-party data; wrongly refusing may violate the subject's rights and attract enforcement. This skill addresses the judgment involved in navigating the difficult edge cases of data subject rights.

## Core Rules

### Apply exemptions specifically and document the basis

Data protection law provides exemptions from certain rights in specific contexts, including:

- Legal professional privilege and litigation;
- Law enforcement and criminal investigations;
- National security and defence;
- Self-incrimination;
- Certain regulatory and supervisory functions;
- Management forecasting and negotiations (in some jurisdictions).

Exemptions must be applied specifically to the data and the right to which they relate, not invoked as a blanket shield. For each piece of withheld data, identify the specific exemption, the reason it applies, and document the analysis. Blanket exemption claims are routinely rejected by regulators and courts.

### Handle legal professional privilege with precision

Legal advice and litigation privilege protect communications between lawyer and client for legal advice, and communications created for the dominant purpose of contemplated litigation. In access requests:

- Privileged communications may be withheld from the access response;
- But privilege belongs to the client, not the data subject, and the organisation must assert it correctly;
- Internal legal advice embedded in broader documents may be redacted while non-privileged portions are disclosed;
- The fact that a document mentions legal matters does not make it privileged.

Over-claiming privilege to withhold inconvenient data is an abuse that regulators detect. Apply privilege precisely and document the assertion.

### Manage employee subject access requests, especially in disputes

Employee access requests are frequently made in the context of grievances, disciplinary actions, or employment litigation. They may be voluminous and may seek data about managers, colleagues, and the dispute itself. Handle by:

- Applying the standard access scope (all personal data relating to the employee);
- Balancing third-party (colleague, manager) data carefully, redacting where appropriate;
- Applying litigation or privilege exemptions where genuinely applicable;
- Not using the request's context as a reason to delay or refuse a legitimate request.

Do not assume an employee request is abusive because it arises in a dispute; fulfill it on the merits. Conversely, do not disclose third-party personal data without the required balancing.

### Identify and handle manifestly unfounded or excessive requests

A request may be refused (or charged) where it is manifestly unfounded or excessive. Apply these standards narrowly:

- **Manifestly unfounded**: the request has no reasonable basis, is repetitive after being addressed, or is abusive or harassing in nature or intent;
- **Excessive**: the request is disproportionate to the context (for example, requesting all data then immediately re-requesting, or requesting in a manner designed to disrupt).

Before refusing or charging:

- Confirm the request meets the standard (not merely inconvenient or voluminous);
- Document the basis;
- Consider whether a reasonable fee or a narrower scope could address the burden;
- Inform the requester of the refusal or fee and their right to complain.

Routine use of these grounds to reduce workload is a violation. Most requests, even voluminous ones, must be fulfilled.

### Use the two-month extension only for genuine complexity

The extension to three months is available where the request is complex or numerous. Complexity is genuine where, for example, the data spans many legacy systems, requires extensive third-party coordination, or involves difficult exemption analysis. Complexity is not established by poor data organisation or staffing shortages. If the extension is used, notify the requester within the first month with reasons. Do not use the extension as a routine buffer.

### Balance transparency against confidentiality and third-party rights

Access responses may require disclosing data that includes trade secrets, confidential business information, or third-party personal data. Balance:

- The requester's right to their personal data;
- The confidentiality of business information (redact where it can be separated from the personal data);
- The rights of third parties (redact identifiers, apply consent or justification).

The balance is specific, not categorical. "This document is confidential" is not a sufficient reason to withhold the requester's own personal data within it.

### Coordinate with processors and joint controllers for complex requests

Where data is held by processors or shared with joint controllers, fulfilling a complex request may require coordination. Ensure contracts and procedures define:

- How processors support access, erasure, and other requests;
- How joint controllers allocate responsibility;
- Timelines that respect the overall response deadline.

Do not let processor coordination delays cause missed deadlines; build the coordination into the response workflow.

### Maintain a defensible audit trail for complex and refused requests

For every complex, exempted, or refused request, maintain:

- The request and identity verification;
- The systems searched and data located;
- The exemptions applied, with specific reasoning per item or category;
- The third-party and privilege analyses;
- The decision (disclosed, redacted, withheld, refused) and the response;
- The dates and personnel involved.

This audit trail is the defence if the requester complains to the supervisory authority or seeks judicial remedy. Undocumented decisions in complex cases are indefensible.

## Common Traps

### Invoking exemptions as a blanket shield

Claiming "legal privilege" or "law enforcement" over entire categories without specific, documented analysis per item. Regulators and courts reject blanket claims.

### Over-claiming legal professional privilege

Treating any document that mentions legal matters as privileged. Privilege is precise; apply it to genuinely privileged communications and redact rather than withhold where possible.

### Assuming employee requests in disputes are abusive

Treating a grievance-related access request as inherently unfounded. Fulfill on the merits, applying third-party balancing and genuine exemptions.

### Using "manifestly unfounded or excessive" to avoid inconvenient work

Refusing or charging for requests that are merely voluminous or inconvenient. These grounds are narrow; most requests must be fulfilled.

### Routine use of the two-month extension

Claiming complexity for requests that are merely large or that strain staffing. Genuine complexity must exist and be documented, and the requester must be notified.

### Withholding the requester's personal data under "confidentiality"

Asserting trade secret or business confidentiality to deny access to the requester's own data. Balance specifically; redact confidential elements rather than withholding entirely.

### Missing deadlines due to processor coordination failures

The request is delayed because a processor did not respond in time. Build coordination timelines into the workflow to meet the overall deadline.

## Self-Check

- Are exemptions applied specifically to the data and right, with documented reasoning per item or category, not as a blanket shield?
- Is legal professional privilege asserted precisely (genuine legal advice or litigation-purpose communications), with redaction preferred over wholesale withholding?
- Are employee requests in dispute contexts fulfilled on the merits, with third-party data balanced and genuine exemptions applied?
- Are manifestly unfounded or excessive requests identified against the narrow standard, with the basis documented and the requester informed of their rights?
- Is the two-month extension used only for genuine complexity, with the requester notified within the first month and reasons given?
- Is the transparency-confidentiality balance applied specifically, redacting confidential or third-party elements rather than withholding the requester's personal data?
- Are processor and joint-controller coordination timelines built into the response workflow to meet deadlines?
- Is a complete audit trail maintained for every complex, exempted, or refused request (request, searches, exemptions, analyses, decisions, dates, personnel)?
