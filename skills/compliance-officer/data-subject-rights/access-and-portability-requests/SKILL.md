# Access and Portability Requests

The right of access lets a data subject ask what personal data an organisation holds about them; the right to portability lets them receive that data in a structured, reusable format to transfer to another provider. These rights are among the most exercised and most operationally demanding: a single request can require searching dozens of systems, identifying relevant data among millions of records, applying exemptions, and producing a coherent response within one month. The judgment problems are not procedural (the deadlines are fixed) but substantive: what counts as the data subject's personal data, what must be included or excluded, how to handle data about other people, and when portability applies versus access. This skill addresses the judgment involved in fulfilling access and portability requests accurately and lawfully.

## Core Rules

### Verify the identity of the requester before disclosing

Before providing personal data, confirm the requester is who they claim to be. The verification standard should be proportionate: for low-sensitivity data held about a known customer, routine account verification may suffice; for sensitive data or where impersonation risk is elevated, stronger verification is warranted. Do not require excessive identity documents that themselves collect unnecessary personal data. If the organisation has reasonable doubts about identity and cannot resolve them, it may refuse the request, documenting the basis.

### Search comprehensively across all relevant systems

A data subject's personal data may reside in customer databases, email, support tickets, logs, backups, analytics, marketing tools, HR systems, and third-party processors. Define the search scope by system inventory and data mapping, not by convenience. A response that searches only the primary CRM and misses email and ticketing systems is incomplete. Document which systems were searched and the results.

### Apply the scope of "personal data" correctly

The right of access covers personal data relating to the requester — any information relating to an identified or identifiable natural person. This includes:

- Obvious identifiers (name, email, account data);
- Transaction and interaction history;
- Inferences and profiles derived about the person;
- Opinions about the person recorded by staff (in many cases);
- Data in free-text fields (emails, notes) that relates to the person.

It does not require disclosing a "reason" for every past decision in all cases (some exemptions apply), but the underlying data is generally within scope. Do not narrowly interpret "personal data" to exclude inconvenient categories.

### Handle data about other people carefully

Access responses often contain data about other individuals (family members, colleagues, other customers). The organisation must balance the requester's right of access against the rights and freedoms of others. Approaches include:

- Redacting third-party identifiers where they can be removed without defeating the response;
- Providing the information where the third party has consented or where disclosure is justified;
- Withholding where disclosure would adversely affect the rights of third parties and no justification applies.

Do not use third-party rights as a blanket excuse to withhold; apply the balance specifically and document the reasoning.

### Distinguish the right of access from the right to portability

The rights overlap but differ:

- **Access** covers all personal data processed, regardless of basis, and includes information beyond what the subject provided (inferences, staff notes). The format is not mandated (though it must be intelligible).
- **Portability** applies only to data the subject provided, where processing is based on consent or contract and is carried out by automated means. It must be provided in a structured, commonly used, machine-readable format, and transmitted to another controller where feasible.

Determine which right the requester is exercising (or both) and apply the correct scope and format. Portability does not cover data inferred by the controller or data processed on other bases.

### Meet the deadline and manage extensions correctly

The standard response period is one month from receipt of the request (and from identity verification, if that was necessary). A two-month extension is permitted where requests are complex or numerous, but:

- The extension requires the organisation to inform the requester within the first month of the extension and the reasons;
- Complexity must be genuine (a voluminous request across many systems), not manufactured by poor data organisation;
- Extensions cannot be used routinely to mask operational delays.

Track deadlines from receipt and escalate approaching deadlines. A missed deadline is a violation regardless of the substantive response quality.

### Provide the response in an intelligible, transparent form

The response should include:

- Confirmation that data is being processed;
- A copy of the personal data;
- The purposes, categories, recipients, retention periods, and rights information;
- The source of the data (if not obtained from the subject);
- The existence of automated decision-making and meaningful information about the logic.

Provide the copy in a form that is intelligible (clear, permanent, accessible). Translating codes, explaining abbreviations, and presenting data coherently is part of the obligation, not optional polish.

### Refuse or charge only on valid grounds

A request may be refused where it is manifestly unfounded or excessive (for example, repetitive requests for the same data). A reasonable fee may be charged for manifestly unfounded or excessive requests, or for additional copies. These grounds are narrow and must be documented; they cannot be used to deter legitimate requests. Routine requests are free.

## Common Traps

### Searching only the primary system and missing email, logs, and free-text data

The response covers the CRM but not the support tickets, emails, and analytics where substantial personal data resides. The response is incomplete and the omission is discoverable.

### Narrowly defining personal data to exclude inferences, notes, and free text

Treating only structured database fields as personal data, excluding staff notes, derived profiles, and free-text references. The right of access is broader.

### Using third-party rights as a blanket excuse to withhold

Refusing to provide any data that mentions another person, without applying the specific balance. This over-withholds and defeats the right.

### Applying portability scope to access requests or vice versa

Providing only data the subject provided when access was requested (which is broader), or claiming portability for inferred data (which is not portable). Apply the correct scope.

### Missing the deadline or using extensions routinely

The one-month deadline slips because requests are not tracked, or the two-month extension is claimed without genuine complexity or without notifying the requester. Track and justify.

### Charging fees or refusing requests to deter legitimate exercise

Applying "manifestly unfounded" or fees broadly to reduce workload. These are narrow grounds and misuse is itself a violation.

## Self-Check

- Is the requester's identity verified proportionately before disclosure, with the basis documented if refused?
- Does the search cover all relevant systems identified in the data inventory, including email, logs, analytics, and free-text sources?
- Is the scope of personal data applied broadly, including inferences, profiles, and staff notes where relevant?
- Is third-party data handled by specific balancing (redaction, consent, justification), not blanket withholding?
- Is the distinction between access (all data, any basis) and portability (provided data, consent or contract, machine-readable) applied correctly?
- Is the one-month deadline tracked from receipt, with genuine, notified two-month extensions only for complex requests?
- Does the response include the required supplementary information (purposes, categories, recipients, retention, rights, sources, automated logic)?
- Are refusals and fees limited to manifestly unfounded or excessive requests, with documented justification?
