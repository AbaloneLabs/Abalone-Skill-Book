# Policy Portfolio Management And Rationalization

A policy library is not a collection of individual documents; it is a portfolio that either covers the organization's risk surface or leaves gaps that expose it. Most policy problems are not drafting problems at the document level but portfolio problems at the system level: the library grew organically, each policy written to address a specific trigger, and no one ever asked whether the whole set is complete, coherent, and right-sized. The result is a portfolio that simultaneously has too many policies (redundant, overlapping, stale) and too few (gaps where no policy addresses a real risk). Rationalization is the discipline of stepping back from individual documents and managing the library as a strategic asset. The judgment is cataloging what exists, classifying it by authority and risk, mapping coverage against the risk inventory, identifying gaps and redundancies, and deciding what to keep, consolidate, retire, or create. The harm of getting this wrong is a library that looks comprehensive on paper but leaves the organization exposed where it matters, while drowning employees in documents that overlap and contradict.

Use this skill before rationalizing a policy library, building a policy catalog or register, mapping policy coverage to risk, tiering policies, or reviewing whether the portfolio is complete and coherent. The goal is to make the agent think about portfolio architecture, coverage analysis, tiering, rationalization sequencing, and the governance that keeps the portfolio healthy over time.

## Core Rules

### Build A Policy Catalog Before Rationalizing

You cannot rationalize what you cannot see. The first step is a complete inventory of every document that functions as a policy, wherever it lives: the formal policy repository, intranets, shared drives, HR handbooks, contracts, and embedded procedures. Many organizations discover that a significant fraction of their governing documents were never in the official library at all.

A usable catalog captures for each document:

- title, identifier, and version;
- owner and approving authority;
- the risk, obligation, or topic it addresses;
- the population it binds;
- classification or tier;
- effective date, last review date, and next review due;
- status (active, under revision, retired);
- relationships to other policies (supersedes, cross-references, depends on).

The catalog is the single source of truth for the portfolio. Without it, rationalization decisions are made on incomplete information, and documents that were forgotten remain forgotten.

### Map Coverage Against The Risk Inventory

A policy portfolio exists to cover risk. The test of completeness is not how many policies exist but whether every material risk and obligation has governing policy coverage. Map the catalog against the organization's risk assessment, regulatory inventory, and strategic obligations.

Coverage mapping should answer:

- For each material risk in the risk assessment, is there a governing policy?
- For each regulatory obligation, is there a policy that operationalizes it?
- Are there policies that address risks no longer in the risk assessment (candidates for retirement)?
- Are there risks with multiple overlapping policies (candidates for consolidation)?
- Are there policies that address no identified risk (orphans)?

A coverage map reveals gaps and redundancies that are invisible when you look at policies one at a time. A gap where a material risk has no governing policy is the most dangerous finding, because it means the organization has exposure with no documented standard of conduct.

### Tier Policies By Authority And Risk

Not all policies carry the same weight, and the portfolio should reflect that. A flat library where the code of conduct and a travel-expense guideline look the same dilutes the authority of the documents that matter and overwhelms employees with undifferentiated content.

A typical tiering structure:

- **Tier 1, governance policies:** the code of conduct, ethics charter, and foundational policies that bind the whole organization and require board or executive approval. These are few and authoritative.
- **Tier 2, risk and regulatory policies:** policies addressing specific high-risk domains such as anti-bribery, data privacy, trade controls, and conflicts of interest. These operationalize legal obligations and bind broad populations.
- **Tier 3, operational policies and standards:** departmental or process-specific policies that implement Tier 2 policies for particular activities.
- **Tier 4, procedures and work instructions:** step-by-step guidance that supports policies but is not itself a policy.

Tiering drives approval authority, review cadence, distribution breadth, and acknowledgment requirements. A policy's tier should be visible in the catalog and in the document itself so employees and auditors understand its weight.

### Identify Gaps, Redundancies, And Orphans

Rationalization is driven by three findings, each requiring a different response.

**Gaps** are material risks or obligations with no governing policy. The response is to create a policy, or to confirm that an existing policy covers the risk and update the coverage map. Never leave a material gap unaddressed; it is the portfolio equivalent of an uninsured risk.

**Redundancies** are multiple policies addressing the same risk with overlapping or slightly different rules. The response is consolidation: designate one as the lead, fold the others into it or retire them, and cross-reference. Redundancy is not harmless; it creates the contradictions and drift that the harmonization skill addresses.

**Orphans** are policies that address no current risk, bind no current population, or govern an activity that has ceased. The response is retirement through the sunsetting process. Orphans accumulate silently and are a primary cause of library bloat.

Document each finding and the planned response. Rationalization without a documented decision record becomes an exercise that is never finished and never defensible.

### Sequence Rationalization To Avoid Disruption

Rationalizing a large library all at once disrupts the organization and rarely finishes. Sequence the work to deliver value early and manage change.

A practical sequence:

1. **Retire clear orphans first:** policies with no current risk or population. This is low-risk, high-clutter-reduction work.
2. **Fill critical gaps:** create policies for material risks with no coverage. This is the highest-value work because it closes real exposure.
3. **Consolidate major redundancies:** merge overlapping policies into single lead documents.
4. **Tier and reclassify:** assign tiers and align approval authority, review cadence, and distribution.
5. **Harmonize definitions and thresholds:** reconcile the controlled glossary and thresholds across the consolidated library.

Communicate changes to affected populations at each step. A rationalization that retires or consolidates policies without communication leaves employees uncertain about what governs.

### Govern The Portfolio As An Ongoing Discipline

Rationalization is not a project with an end date; it is a governance discipline. Without ongoing management, the portfolio re-accumulates gaps, redundancies, and orphans as the organization changes.

Ongoing portfolio governance includes:

- a periodic coverage review that re-maps the catalog against an updated risk assessment;
- a review cadence for each policy tied to its tier, with policies reviewed on schedule;
- a gate in the policy-creation process that checks whether a new policy is needed or whether an existing one should be updated;
- a periodic orphan and redundancy sweep;
- portfolio-level metrics such as coverage rate, overdue-review count, and policy-per-employee ratio.

Assign a policy program owner with accountability for portfolio health. Without an owner, the portfolio drifts back to its organic, unmanaged state.

### Align The Portfolio With Organizational Change

The policy portfolio must track the organization. Mergers, acquisitions, new products, new markets, divestitures, and reorganizations all change the risk surface and therefore the required policy coverage. A portfolio that is not updated when the organization changes develops gaps that no one notices until an incident.

Build triggers that prompt a coverage review:

- entry into a new market or jurisdiction with unfamiliar regulation;
- launch of a new product, service, or business line;
- a merger or acquisition that brings a new policy estate;
- a divestiture that renders policies obsolete;
- a regulatory change that creates new obligations or removes old ones.

Each trigger should produce a documented coverage assessment and portfolio adjustment, not an assumption that existing policies suffice.

## Common Traps

### Rationalizing Without A Complete Catalog

Starting consolidation or retirement decisions without a complete inventory leads to forgotten policies being missed, then surfacing later as gaps or contradictions. Build the catalog first.

### Counting Policies Instead Of Measuring Coverage

A large number of policies feels comprehensive but may leave material risks uncovered. The test is coverage against the risk inventory, not document count.

### Flat Library With No Tiering

A library where every document looks equally authoritative dilutes the code of conduct and overwhelms employees. Tiering signals weight and drives differentiated governance.

### Gaps Treated As Lower Priority Than Redundancies

Organizations often focus on reducing clutter (retiring and consolidating) because it is visible, while leaving coverage gaps unaddressed because they are invisible. Gaps are the more dangerous finding.

### Big-Bang Rationalization

Attempting to rationalize the entire library in one effort disrupts the organization, overwhelms the team, and usually stalls. Sequence the work and deliver value incrementally.

### No Ongoing Portfolio Governance

A one-time rationalization that is not sustained re-accumulates gaps, redundancies, and orphans as the organization changes. Portfolio management is a continuous discipline.

### Ignoring Policies Outside The Official Library

Many governing documents live in HR handbooks, intranets, and contracts, outside the policy repository. A catalog that misses them leaves shadow governance unmanaged.

### No Coverage Review After Organizational Change

Mergers, new markets, and new products change the risk surface. A portfolio that is not reviewed after these events develops gaps that surface only in an incident.

## Self-Check

- Is there a complete policy catalog covering every document that functions as a policy, including those outside the official repository?
- Has the catalog been mapped against the risk assessment and regulatory inventory to identify gaps, redundancies, and orphans?
- Are material coverage gaps identified and addressed with new or updated policies, not left open?
- Are policies tiered by authority and risk, with tiering driving approval authority, review cadence, and distribution?
- Is rationalization sequenced to retire orphans, fill gaps, consolidate redundancies, and harmonize, rather than attempted all at once?
- Is each rationalization decision documented with the finding, rationale, and action taken?
- Is there a policy program owner with ongoing accountability for portfolio health, including periodic coverage reviews and orphan sweeps?
- Does the policy-creation process include a gate that checks whether a new policy is needed before it is drafted?
- Are organizational changes such as mergers, new markets, and new products triggering documented coverage assessments?
- Are portfolio-level metrics such as coverage rate, overdue-review count, and tier distribution tracked and reviewed?
