# Work Authorization Verification And I9

Employment eligibility verification is a strict-liability area where good faith and intent are largely irrelevant to the core obligation. The defining difficulty is that the employer must verify identity and work authorization without engaging in document abuse or discrimination, must retain records precisely, and faces significant penalties for both knowingly hiring unauthorized workers and for technical paperwork violations. The verification process sits on a knife edge between under-verification, which creates knowing-hire liability, and over-verification, which creates document abuse and discrimination liability.

Use this skill before designing or auditing I-9 and verification processes, handling reverification, managing E-Verify, or responding to an immigration inspection. The goal is to make the agent apply the correct verification procedure, avoid document abuse and discrimination, manage retention and reverification, and prepare for inspection. Immigration verification rules are jurisdiction-specific and enforcement-intensive, and inspections should involve qualified immigration counsel.

## Core Rules

### Complete Verification Within The Strict Timeframe

The timing of verification is fixed and unforgiving.

Complete:

- section 1 by the employee on or before the first day of work for pay;
- section 2 by the employer within three business days of the first day of work;
- section 2 within the same day for employees hired for less than three business days;
- verification for every new hire including citizens and long-term employees.

The timeframe runs from the first day of work for pay, not from orientation or offer acceptance. Missing the three-business-day window is a paperwork violation regardless of the employee's actual authorization. Every new hire must be verified without exception.

### Examine Documents Without Document Abuse Or Discrimination

The employer must accept documents that reasonably appear genuine and relate to the person, without demanding specific documents.

Apply:

- acceptance of any document or combination from the lists that establishes identity and work authorization;
- no demand for more or different documents than required;
- no demand for a specific document such as a particular card;
- no rejection of genuine-appearing documents that relate to the employee;
- examination in the physical or authorized virtual presence of the employee;
- reasonable appearance of genuineness as the standard, not forensic authentication.

Requesting more or different documents than the lists require, or rejecting valid document choices, is document abuse and national origin discrimination. The employer's role is to examine what the employee chooses to present, not to direct the choice.

### Manage E-Verify Participation Correctly

E-Verify is mandatory for some employers and voluntary for others, with specific procedural requirements.

Manage:

- mandatory participation for federal contractors and in required states or situations;
- timely case creation after completion of verification;
- handling of tentative nonconfirmations including employee notice and referral;
- no adverse action during the tentative nonconfirmation process;
- resolution of cases and retention of records;
- awareness that E-Verify does not replace the underlying verification requirement.

E-Verify supplements but does not replace the I-9 process. Taking adverse action against an employee during a tentative nonconfirmation, before resolution, is a violation. Mandatory participation carries its own penalties for non-enrollment.

### Handle Reverification And Work Authorization Expiration

Reverification is required when work authorization expires, but only for the specific authorization, not indefinitely.

Handle:

- reverification before the expiration of temporary work authorization;
- no reverification of citizens, nationals, or lawful permanent residents;
- no reverification based on document expiration for List B identity documents;
- completion of a new verification form rather than reusing an expired one where required;
- tracking of expiration dates to prompt timely reverification;
- no termination solely for failure to provide updated documents without proper process.

Reverification is triggered by the expiration of the specific work authorization, not by the expiration of a List B identity document or a permanent resident card. Tracking systems must distinguish authorization expiration from document expiration.

### Retain Records For The Required Period

Retention is mandatory and the period depends on the employment duration.

Retain:

- for three years after the date of hire, or one year after the date employment ends, whichever is later;
- the verification form and supporting documentation;
- records in a manner that allows retrieval for inspection;
- the choice of maintaining original paper, electronic, or microfilm copies per the rules;
- organized records that can be produced within three business days of an inspection request.

Retention errors are among the most common violations. The later-of calculation must be applied correctly, and records must be retrievable on demand. Electronic storage must meet specific integrity and access requirements.

### Distinguish Good Faith From Knowing Hire

The penalty structure distinguishes paperwork violations from knowingly hiring unauthorized workers.

Understand:

- paperwork violations carry penalties per violation regardless of the employee's status;
- knowing hire or continued employment of unauthorized workers carries significantly higher penalties;
- constructive knowledge, meaning circumstances that would cause a reasonable person to know, can establish knowing hire;
- actual knowledge includes direct knowledge of unauthorized status;
- good faith compliance with verification procedures provides a good faith defense to knowing hire in some circumstances.

Constructive knowledge can arise from obvious document fraud, inconsistent statements, or information indicating unauthorized status. Relying on obviously fraudulent documents while accepting them as genuine-appearing can create constructive knowledge. The good faith defense depends on consistent compliance with verification procedures.

### Prepare For Inspections And Audits

Immigration inspections can occur with little notice and require preparedness.

Prepare by:

- conducting self-audits of verification forms and correcting errors with proper procedures;
- designating response personnel and counsel;
- establishing a protocol for inspection reception and document production within three business days;
- training on what to produce and what requires review;
- maintaining organized, retrievable records;
- planning for the consequences of identified violations.

Self-audits allow correction before an inspection, but corrections must follow the proper procedure for corrections, not alteration. Designating counsel and response personnel in advance prevents panic and error during an inspection.

### Avoid Immigration-Related Unfair Employment Practices

Verification must not become a vehicle for discrimination.

Avoid:

- document abuse by demanding specific or additional documents;
- national origin or citizenship status discrimination in hiring and verification;
- disparate treatment in verification timing or scrutiny based on national origin or perceived status;
- over-documentation of certain employees based on appearance or accent;
- retaliation against employees who assert verification-related rights.

Treating employees differently in the verification process based on appearance, accent, or perceived national origin is discrimination. The verification standard must be applied uniformly to all hires.

## Common Traps

### Missing The Three-Business-Day Verification Window

The timeframe is fixed and missing it is a paperwork violation regardless of authorization.

### Document Abuse By Demanding Specific Or Additional Documents

Directing document choice or demanding more than required is discrimination.

### Adverse Action During E-Verify Tentative Nonconfirmation

Acting before resolution of a tentative nonconfirmation is a violation.

### Reverification Based On List B Or Permanent Resident Expiration

Reverification is triggered by work authorization expiration, not identity document or permanent resident card expiration.

### Constructive Knowledge From Obvious Document Fraud

Accepting obviously fraudulent documents can establish knowing hire despite the genuine-appearing standard.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- Is verification completed within the strict timeframe with section 1 by the first day and section 2 within three business days for every new hire?
- Are documents examined without abuse or discrimination, accepting any valid combination that appears genuine and relates to the employee, without demanding specific or additional documents?
- Is E-Verify managed with timely case creation, proper tentative nonconfirmation handling, no adverse action during resolution, and awareness that it supplements rather than replaces verification?
- Is reverification handled only for expiring work authorization, not for List B identity documents or permanent resident cards, with expiration tracking and proper process?
- Are records retained for the later of three years from hire or one year from termination, in retrievable form meeting integrity requirements?
- Is the distinction between paperwork violations and knowing hire understood, with constructive knowledge risks from obvious fraud managed and good faith compliance maintained?
- Is the organization prepared for inspections through self-audits, designated counsel and personnel, a three-business-day production protocol, and organized records?
- Are immigration-related unfair employment practices avoided through uniform application of verification standards, and where inspections or violations arise, has qualified immigration counsel been involved, recognizing that verification rules are jurisdiction-specific and this guidance is not legal advice?
