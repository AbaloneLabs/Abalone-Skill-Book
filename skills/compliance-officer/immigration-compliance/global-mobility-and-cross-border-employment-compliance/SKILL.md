# Global Mobility And Cross-Border Employment Compliance

Global mobility compliance governs the movement of employees across borders for work. The defining feature is that sending an employee to work in another country—even temporarily—triggers immigration, tax, employment law, payroll, data protection, and corporate obligations in the destination country simultaneously. The central difficulty is that business travel and remote work blur the line between a permissible visit and taxable, regulated employment, and the obligations can arise from days or weeks of presence.

Use this skill before advising on international assignments, business traveler compliance, remote work across borders, permanent establishment risk, or global payroll. The goal is to make the agent identify the immigration, tax, employment, payroll, and corporate obligations triggered by cross-border work before concluding that a global mobility arrangement is compliant.

## Core Rules

### Assess Immigration And Work Authorization For Each Destination

Work authorization depends on the nature and duration of activity.

Assess:

- the distinction between business visitor and work activities by country;
- permitted activities under business visitor waivers and visa exemptions;
- work permit and visa requirements for productive work;
- duration thresholds that trigger permit requirements;
- specific programs (intra-company transfer, skilled worker, treaty);
- processing timelines and lead times;
- dependent and family immigration;
- compliance with conditions of stay.

Business visitor status allows limited activities (meetings, conferences) but generally not productive work. The definition of "work" varies by country, and activities that seem routine (training, client troubleshooting) can require a work permit.

### Manage Cross-Border Tax Obligations

Cross-border work creates tax obligations in multiple jurisdictions.

Cover:

- individual income tax residency and sourcing rules;
- tax treaty relief and tiebreaker rules;
- short-term business visitor exemptions (e.g., 183-day rules);
- social security totalization agreements;
- payroll withholding in the work country;
- tax equalization and tax protection policies;
- reporting of foreign assets and accounts;
- employer tax registration and filing obligations.

An employee working in a country can trigger individual tax residency, employer withholding obligations, and corporate tax presence. Tax treaties provide relief but require proper application and documentation.

### Address Permanent Establishment Risk

Employee activity can create a taxable presence for the employer.

Assess:

- the definition of permanent establishment (PE) under treaties and domestic law;
- fixed place of business PE (office, facility, ongoing presence);
- dependent agent PE (concluding contracts or habitually exercising authority);
- service PE thresholds (time-based presence providing services);
- digital economy and modified PE rules;
- exemptions for preparatory or auxiliary activities;
- the risk from remote work creating PE in the employee's country;
- documentation and treaty relief positions.

An employee working in a country can create a PE that subjects the employer to corporate tax. Remote work from another country is an emerging PE risk that requires assessment.

### Comply With Local Employment Law

Local employment law may apply to cross-border workers.

Cover:

- choice of law and mandatory local protections;
- minimum wage, working time, and leave requirements;
- termination protections and notice;
- employee representation and works council obligations;
- health and safety duties;
- discrimination and harassment protections;
- mandatory benefits and insurance;
- coordination with home country employment law.

Local employment law often applies regardless of the choice of law in the contract, particularly for mandatory protections. The worker may be entitled to the more protective of home or host country standards.

### Manage Global Payroll And Reporting

Cross-border work requires compliant payroll in multiple jurisdictions.

Implement:

- payroll registration in the work country;
- withholding and reporting of income tax and social security;
- split payroll between home and host countries;
- shadow payroll for tax equalization;
- currency and compensation adjustments;
- benefit administration across borders;
- coordination with totalization agreements;
- year-end reporting and reconciliation.

Split payroll and shadow payroll manage the complexity of taxing in multiple countries while maintaining a single compensation package. Social security totalization prevents double contributions but requires certificates of coverage.

### Navigate Data Protection For Global Mobility

Cross-border employment involves international data transfers.

Cover:

- transfer of employee personal data across borders;
- adequacy, standard contractual clauses, and binding corporate rules;
- works council and employee representation consultation on data transfers;
- data minimization for mobility processing;
- retention of immigration and mobility data;
- access rights and transparency;
- coordination with global data protection frameworks.

Employee data transferred for global mobility purposes requires a lawful transfer mechanism. Several jurisdictions require works council or employee representative consultation before implementing international data transfers.

### Conduct AML And Sanctions Screening For Global Workforce

Global mobility intersects with AML and sanctions obligations.

Assess:

- sanctions screening of assignees and host country entities;
- export control considerations for technology and knowledge transfer;
- anti-boycott regulations;
- anti-bribery considerations for assignees in high-risk regions;
- immigration security and background screening;
- enhanced due diligence for high-risk destinations;
- coordination with trade compliance and ethics functions.

Sending employees to sanctioned countries or entities can violate sanctions law. Technology and knowledge transfer to assignees can trigger export control obligations.

### Manage Business Traveler Tracking And Compliance

Short-term business travel creates cumulative obligations.

Implement:

- tracking of business travel by destination and duration;
- assessment of immigration and tax thresholds;
- pre-trip compliance review for high-risk destinations;
- traveler education on permissible activities;
- monitoring of cumulative days in each country;
- escalation when thresholds are approached;
- coordination with travel management and mobility teams.

Individual short trips may seem harmless, but cumulative presence can trigger tax residency, PE, and work permit obligations. Tracking and threshold monitoring are essential.

## Common Traps

### Business Travel Treated As Exempt From Work Permit Requirements

Activities that constitute work, even during a short visit, can require a permit.

### Remote Work Creating Undetected Permanent Establishment

An employee working remotely from another country can create corporate tax presence.

### Tax Treaty Relief Assumed Without Documentation

Treaty relief requires proper application, documentation, and sometimes advance filing.

### Local Employment Law Ignored In Favor Of Home Country Contract

Mandatory local protections apply regardless of choice of law.

### Social Security Double Contributions Without Totalization

Without totalization agreements and certificates, both home and host social security may be owed.

### Sanctions Screening Skipped For Internal Employee Transfers

Assignees to or from sanctioned regions can create sanctions exposure.

## Self-Check

- Is immigration and work authorization assessed for each destination distinguishing business visitor from work activities, permit requirements, duration thresholds, and processing timelines?
- Are cross-border tax obligations managed including residency, treaty relief, short-term visitor exemptions, totalization, withholding, equalization, and employer registration?
- Is permanent establishment risk assessed for fixed place, dependent agent, service PE, digital economy rules, exemptions, remote work, and documentation?
- Is local employment law applied including mandatory protections, minimum standards, termination, representation, safety, discrimination, and benefits?
- Is global payroll managed through registration, withholding, split and shadow payroll, currency, benefits, totalization, and reporting?
- Is data protection for global mobility addressed including transfer mechanisms, works council consultation, minimization, retention, and access rights?
- Is AML and sanctions screening conducted for assignees, host entities, technology transfer, export controls, anti-boycott, and anti-bribery?
- Is business traveler tracking implemented with destination and duration monitoring, threshold assessment, pre-trip review, traveler education, and escalation?
- Is the distinction between permissible business visitor activity and regulated work understood for each country?
- Are the cumulative obligations across immigration, tax, employment, payroll, data, and corporate law coordinated?
