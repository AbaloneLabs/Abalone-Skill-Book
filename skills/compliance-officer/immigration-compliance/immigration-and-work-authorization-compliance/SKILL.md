# Immigration And Work Authorization Compliance

Immigration and work authorization compliance governs the verification of employment eligibility and the sponsorship of foreign workers. The defining feature is that the employer must navigate two potentially conflicting duties: verifying work authorization to avoid employing unauthorized workers, and avoiding discrimination based on national origin or citizenship status in the verification process. The central difficulty is that the same verification process that enforces immigration law can become the basis for document abuse and discrimination claims if applied unevenly.

Use this skill before advising on I-9 processes, E-Verify participation, visa sponsorship, immigration audit response, or anti-discrimination compliance in hiring. The goal is to make the agent identify the verification duties, the anti-discrimination limits, the recordkeeping requirements, and the audit risks before concluding that an immigration compliance program is compliant.

## Core Rules

### Conduct Proper I-9 Employment Verification

Form I-9 verification is required for every new hire.

Implement:

- completion of Section 1 by the employee on or before the first day of work;
- completion of Section 2 by the employer within three business days of hire;
- physical or authorized virtual examination of original documents;
- acceptance of documents that reasonably appear genuine and relate to the person;
- no request for more or different documents than required (document abuse);
- no specification of which documents to present;
- retention of I-9 forms for the required period (three years after hire or one year after termination, whichever is later);
- proper correction of errors;
- reverification when work authorization expires.

The employer must accept any document from List A or a combination from List B and List C that appears genuine. Requesting specific documents or more than required is document abuse, a form of discrimination.

### Comply With E-Verify Requirements Where Applicable

E-Verify is mandatory for some employers and voluntary for others.

Assess:

- mandatory enrollment for federal contractors and in certain states;
- timely submission of cases after completion of I-9;
- handling of tentative nonconfirmations (TNCs);
- employee notice and opportunity to contest;
- no adverse action during the TNC process;
- final nonconfirmation handling and termination;
- recordkeeping and case closure;
- coordination with I-9 processes.

E-Verify supplements but does not replace I-9. Adverse action during a tentative nonconfirmation, before the employee has the opportunity to contest, is prohibited.

### Avoid Immigration-Related Discrimination

The same laws that require verification also prohibit discrimination.

Control:

- no discrimination based on national origin or citizenship status in hiring, firing, or recruitment;
- no document abuse (requesting more or different documents than required);
- no retaliation for protected activity;
- fair treatment of work-authorized individuals regardless of citizenship status (citizens, nationals, lawful permanent residents, refugees, asylees, and certain visa holders);
- consistent application of verification standards to all employees;
- no over-documentation of non-citizens;
- training for hiring managers and HR on anti-discrimination rules.

Protected classes include citizens, nationals, lawful permanent residents, refugees, asylees, and recent permanent residents. Treating work-authorized non-citizens differently from citizens in verification is discrimination.

### Manage Visa Sponsorship And Compliance

Employer sponsorship of foreign workers carries ongoing obligations.

Cover:

- labor condition applications and prevailing wage obligations for H-1B and similar visas;
- non-displacement and no-strike attestations;
- wage and benefit equivalence for sponsored workers;
- notification requirements to U.S. workers;
- public access files and documentation;
- reporting of material changes (location, duties, termination);
- reimbursement of required costs (employer obligations, not employee);
- compliance with site visit and audit procedures;
- coordination with immigration counsel.

H-1B sponsorship requires maintaining a public access file, paying the prevailing wage, and not displacing U.S. workers. Material changes must be reported, and certain costs cannot be charged to the employee.

### Prepare For And Respond To Immigration Audits

Immigration authorities conduct I-9 audits and site visits.

Implement:

- periodic internal I-9 audits;
- segregation of I-9 forms from personnel files;
- preparation for Notices of Inspection;
- timely response to inspection notices;
- management of substantive versus technical violations;
- correction of technical violations within the allowed period;
- defense against substantiated violations;
- coordination with counsel for audits and investigations;
- response to site visits for visa programs.

Internal audits identify and correct errors before an enforcement action. Substantive violations carry significant penalties; technical violations can be corrected within a grace period.

### Handle Social Security And No-Match Situations

Mismatched records require careful handling.

Assess:

- Social Security no-match letters and their implications;
- the distinction between a no-match and unauthorized employment;
- the employer's duty to inquire without overreacting;
- reasonable steps to resolve discrepancies;
- no adverse action based solely on a no-match;
- documentation of good-faith efforts;
- anti-retaliation and anti-discrimination considerations.

A no-match is not proof of unauthorized employment. The employer should give the employee a reasonable opportunity to resolve the discrepancy and should not terminate based solely on the no-match.

### Comply With State And Local Immigration Laws

State and local laws add layers of obligation.

Cover:

- state E-Verify mandates;
- state and local work authorization verification requirements;
- restrictions on asking about immigration status;
- sanctuary and non-cooperation laws;
- state licensing penalties for unauthorized employment;
- coordination of federal and state obligations.

State requirements can exceed federal mandates or conflict with anti-discrimination protections. The employer must navigate both layers.

### Address Public Charge And Benefits Considerations

Immigration status affects benefits eligibility and employer obligations.

Assess:

- sponsorship and affidavit of support obligations;
- public charge implications for sponsored workers;
- coordination of immigration and benefits administration;
- access to benefits for different immigration categories;
- avoidance of benefits discrimination based on immigration status.

Sponsorship can create financial obligations. Benefits administration must account for immigration-category eligibility while avoiding discrimination.

## Common Traps

### Requesting More Or Specific Documents Than Required

Over-documentation or specifying which documents to present is document abuse and discrimination.

### Adverse Action During E-Verify Tentative Nonconfirmation

Acting before the employee can contest a TNC is prohibited.

### Verification Standards Applied Differently To Non-Citizens

Treating work-authorized non-citizens more strictly than citizens is discrimination.

### I-9 Errors Left Uncorrected

Substantive errors carry penalties; periodic internal audits catch correctable errors.

### No-Match Treated As Proof Of Unauthorized Employment

A no-match requires inquiry and a reasonable opportunity to resolve, not termination.

### Visa Sponsorship Costs Charged To The Employee

Certain sponsorship costs are the employer's obligation and cannot be passed to the worker.

## Self-Check

- Is I-9 verification completed properly including Section 1 timing, Section 2 document examination, genuine appearance standard, no over-documentation, retention, correction, and reverification?
- Is E-Verify used correctly where applicable including timely submission, TNC handling, no adverse action during contest, final nonconfirmation termination, and recordkeeping?
- Is immigration-related discrimination avoided including national origin and citizenship status protection, no document abuse, no retaliation, consistent verification, and training?
- Is visa sponsorship managed including prevailing wage, non-displacement, notification, public access files, material change reporting, cost allocation, and site visit response?
- Is the organization prepared for immigration audits through internal I-9 audits, segregation, inspection response, violation management, correction, and counsel coordination?
- Are Social Security no-match situations handled through inquiry, reasonable opportunity to resolve, no sole-basis adverse action, documentation, and anti-discrimination awareness?
- Are state and local immigration laws navigated including E-Verify mandates, verification requirements, status inquiry restrictions, and licensing penalties?
- Are public charge and benefits considerations addressed including sponsorship obligations, immigration-category eligibility, and non-discrimination?
- Is the tension between verification duties and anti-discrimination duties balanced?
- Are I-9 forms retained for the correct period and properly segregated?
