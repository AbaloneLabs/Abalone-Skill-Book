# Proceeds Of Crime Third Party Risk And Enabler Liability

Money laundering and proceeds-of-crime offenses are not limited to the principal criminal. They extend to third parties and professional enablers who facilitate, assist, or turn a blind eye to the movement of criminal property. Under the UN Convention against Transnational Organized Crime, FATF Recommendations, national money laundering statutes, and gatekeeper regulation, lawyers, accountants, real estate agents, trust and company service providers, and other professionals can face criminal liability, civil claims, and regulatory enforcement for their role in laundering chains. The central judgment problem is that the line between legitimate professional service and criminal facilitation is not always obvious. Providing advice, forming a company, managing a trust, or processing a transaction can cross into money laundering, aiding and abetting, or conspiracy when the professional knows or suspects that the client or the funds are connected to crime. The professional who treats these engagements as routine, without recognizing the enabler exposure, can become part of the offense.

Use this skill before assessing third-party and enabler liability, evaluating professional service exposure to laundering, handling tipping off and failure to report, or advising on complicity and aiding risk. The goal is to make the agent think about where legitimate service ends and facilitation begins, what knowledge or suspicion triggers exposure, and the decisions that are easy to make too casually. A professional who provides services to a client with tainted funds without recognizing the exposure can face personal criminal liability.

This skill addresses jurisdiction-specific obligations. Enabler liability, gatekeeper duties, tipping off, and failure to report elements differ across national regimes. Always confirm the applicable national law and regulator guidance, and consult qualified legal counsel for specific third-party and enabler liability assessments.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Scope Of Enabler And Facilitator Liability

Money laundering offenses in most regimes extend beyond the principal to anyone who assists in the laundering process with the requisite knowledge or suspicion. The institution must understand how broadly the offense reaches.

Enabler liability scope:

- the offense typically covers acquiring, using, possessing, concealing, converting, transferring, or facilitating the transfer of criminal property;
- liability can attach to professionals who form companies, manage trusts, provide legal or tax advice, arrange transactions, or hold funds, where the property is criminal and the professional knows or suspects;
- the mental element is often knowledge or suspicion, not intent to launder;
- the offense can apply even where the professional did not originate the laundering and played a limited role;
- corporate liability can attach to firms where the offense is committed by employees or agents in the course of business.

The institution must map its services against the offense elements. A service that involves handling, moving, or structuring client funds or assets can create enabler exposure if those funds are criminal.

### Identify Gatekeeper Obligations For Regulated Professions

Lawyers, accountants, notaries, real estate agents, trust and company service providers, and other gatekeepers face specific AML and proceeds-of-crime obligations under FATF Recommendation 22 and national gatekeeper regulation.

Gatekeeper obligations:

- conduct customer due diligence on clients and beneficial owners;
- monitor transactions and activity for suspicion;
- file suspicious activity or transaction reports when suspicion arises;
- maintain records for the required period;
- apply enhanced diligence for high-risk clients and transactions;
- comply with the tipping-off prohibition.

Gatekeepers are often the first or only point of detection for laundering through professional structures. A gatekeeper who fails to apply CDD, monitor, or report can face both regulatory enforcement and criminal liability for the underlying laundering.

### Recognize The Boundary Between Legitimate Service And Facilitation

The most difficult judgment in enabler liability is distinguishing legitimate professional service from criminal facilitation. The distinction often turns on knowledge, suspicion, and the nature of the assistance.

Factors that move service toward facilitation:

- the professional knows or suspects that the client or the funds are connected to crime;
- the professional structures transactions or entities in a way designed to obscure ownership or origin;
- the professional continues to provide services after red flags emerge without inquiry or escalation;
- the service has no legitimate business purpose beyond obscuring the source of funds;
- the professional ignores or rationalizes information that would lead a reasonable professional to question the transaction;
- the professional relies on the client's assurances without independent verification where verification is warranted.

Legitimate service requires genuine professional purpose, appropriate diligence, and a willingness to stop or escalate when red flags appear. Facilitation occurs when the professional provides the service despite knowledge or suspicion, or deliberately avoids acquiring knowledge that would trigger obligations.

### Assess Knowledge And Suspicion As The Trigger

Enabler liability typically requires knowledge or suspicion that the property is criminal. The institution must understand how these mental elements are assessed.

Knowledge and suspicion:

- actual knowledge is not always required; many regimes apply an objective standard of what the professional knew or should have known;
- willful blindness, deliberately avoiding knowledge, can satisfy the knowledge element;
- suspicion is assessed based on whether a reasonable professional in the position would have suspected;
- the professional cannot insulate themselves by failing to inquire when red flags are present;
- continuing to provide services after suspicion arises, without reporting or escalation, strengthens the exposure.

The institution must train professionals to recognize red flags and to understand that ignoring them does not eliminate liability, it creates it. Deliberate avoidance of inquiry is willful blindness, not a defense.

### Manage Tipping Off And Failure To Report Exposure

Two obligations create particular exposure for enablers: the prohibition on tipping off and the duty to report suspicion.

Tipping off:

- the professional cannot inform the client or any third party that a report has been or will be made;
- tipping off is itself a criminal offense in many regimes;
- the professional must manage the client relationship after suspicion without revealing the basis;
- account closure or service termination must be handled to avoid implying a report.

Failure to report:

- where the professional forms suspicion and does not report, the failure can itself be an offense;
- the failure can also strengthen a money laundering charge, because the unreported suspicion supports the knowledge element;
- the defense to both tipping off and failure to report is compliance with the reporting regime.

The professional who suspects but neither reports nor stops the service faces exposure on multiple fronts. The reporting regime provides the legal safe harbor, and operating outside it removes the protection.

### Apply Due Diligence To High-Risk Engagements

Certain engagements carry elevated enabler risk and require heightened diligence.

High-risk engagements:

- forming companies or trusts in secrecy jurisdictions with no apparent business purpose;
- managing structures for PEPs or clients from high-risk jurisdictions;
- handling large cash or asset transactions with unclear source of funds;
- transactions involving real estate, art, or other high-value assets with opaque ownership;
- engagements where the client resists diligence or provides inconsistent information;
- transactions that appear designed to obscure the source, ownership, or destination of funds.

For these engagements, the professional should apply enhanced diligence, document the source of funds and wealth, and be prepared to decline or exit where the risk cannot be managed. Accepting a high-risk engagement without diligence is not neutral service, it is assumption of enabler risk.

### Document The Decision To Provide Or Decline Service

The decision to provide, continue, or decline service in the face of red flags must be documented. The documentation is the primary defense against enabler liability.

Documentation should include:

- the diligence performed and its findings;
- the red flags identified and how each was assessed;
- the source of funds and wealth verification for high-risk engagements;
- the rationale for providing, continuing, or declining service;
- any reports filed and the reporting decision rationale;
- the individuals involved in the decision.

A service provided to a client with tainted funds, with no documented diligence or decision rationale, is difficult to defend. The documentation must show that the professional acted with appropriate care, not blind facilitation.

## Common Traps

### Treating Professional Service As Always Legitimate

Service crosses into facilitation when the professional knows or suspects criminal property. Legitimacy depends on diligence and good faith, not on professional title.

### Willful Blindness As A Defense

Deliberately avoiding knowledge of red flags does not eliminate liability. Willful blindness can satisfy the knowledge element.

### Continuing Service After Suspicion Without Reporting

Continuing to provide services after suspicion arises, without reporting or escalation, strengthens both failure-to-report and money laundering exposure.

### Ignoring Gatekeeper Obligations

Lawyers, accountants, and other gatekeepers have CDD, monitoring, and reporting duties. Treating these as optional creates enabler exposure.

### Tipping Off Through Service Termination

Closing an account or declining service in a way that implies a report violates the tipping-off prohibition.

### No Diligence On High-Risk Engagements

Accepting high-risk engagements without diligence is assumption of enabler risk, not neutral service.

### Undocumented Service Decisions

A service decision in the face of red flags, with no documented rationale, is difficult to defend against enabler liability.

## Self-Check

- Is the scope of enabler and facilitator liability understood, including how the offense extends to professionals who handle, move, or structure client funds with knowledge or suspicion?
- Are gatekeeper obligations for regulated professions, including CDD, monitoring, reporting, and recordkeeping, identified and applied?
- Is the boundary between legitimate service and facilitilitation assessed based on knowledge, suspicion, purpose, and response to red flags?
- Is knowledge and suspicion assessed under both subjective and objective standards, recognizing that willful blindness can satisfy the element?
- Are tipping-off and failure-to-report obligations understood, with the reporting regime as the legal safe harbor?
- Is enhanced diligence applied to high-risk engagements such as secrecy jurisdiction structures, PEP clients, and opaque high-value transactions?
- Are decisions to provide, continue, or decline service documented with diligence findings, red flag assessment, source verification, and rationale?
- Are reports filed when suspicion arises, and is the relationship managed to avoid tipping off?
- Are professionals trained to recognize red flags and to understand that ignoring them creates rather than eliminates liability?
- Is the enabler liability assessment confirmed against the applicable national law and regulator guidance rather than a generic standard?
