# Proceeds Of Crime Detection And Reporting

Proceeds of crime are the financial benefits derived from criminal conduct, and the obligation to detect and report them sits at the intersection of anti-money-laundering law and proceeds-of-crime legislation. Under the UN Convention against Transnational Organized Crime, FATF Recommendations, national proceeds of crime acts such as the US money laundering statutes, the UK Proceeds of Crime Act, and equivalent regimes, obliged entities must identify and report activity where funds may constitute criminal property. The central judgment problem is that proceeds-of-crime detection is not the same as generic transaction monitoring. It requires recognizing that the funds themselves may be tainted by a predicate offense, and that dealing with them, whether by receiving, transferring, converting, or concealing, can itself constitute a money laundering offense. The institution that processes criminal property without detection or reporting can become complicit in the laundering chain.

Use this skill before designing detection for proceeds of crime, assessing whether activity involves criminal property, or deciding reporting and escalation obligations. The goal is to make the agent think about predicate offense recognition, criminal property identification, the distinction between suspicion and proof, and the decisions that are easy to make too casually. Treating proceeds-of-crime risk as ordinary AML monitoring without understanding the criminal property element misses the legal exposure.

This skill addresses jurisdiction-specific obligations. Predicate offense lists, criminal property definitions, reporting duties, and money laundering elements differ across national regimes. Always confirm the applicable national law and regulator guidance, and consult qualified legal counsel for specific detection and reporting decisions.

## Core Rules

### Understand What Constitutes Criminal Property

The foundation of proceeds-of-crime detection is understanding what makes property criminal. Criminal property is property that derives from criminal conduct, in whole or in part, and where the handler knows or suspects that it is criminal.

Criminal property elements:

- the property constitutes a benefit, in whole or in part, directly or indirectly from criminal conduct;
- the handler knows or suspects that the property derives from criminal conduct;
- the benefit can be any economic advantage, including money, goods, real property, or intangible rights;
- the property need not be the direct proceeds of a specific identified crime, but can be connected to criminal conduct generally;
- mixing criminal property with clean funds taints the entire commingled amount in some regimes.

The institution does not need to identify the specific predicate offense. It needs to recognize when the origin or pattern of funds creates suspicion that they derive from criminal conduct. The predicate offense list is broad in most regimes, encompassing drug trafficking, fraud, theft, corruption, tax evasion, human trafficking, sanctions evasion, and many other crimes.

### Recognize Predicate Offense Typologies In Activity

Detection depends on recognizing the fingerprints of predicate offenses in transaction patterns. Each predicate crime tends to generate characteristic financial behavior.

Predicate offense indicators:

- drug trafficking: large cash deposits inconsistent with occupation, structured below thresholds, funnel accounts;
- fraud: rapid movement of victim funds, mule account networks, elderly or vulnerable customer exploitation;
- corruption and bribery: payments to or from PEPs, shell companies in secrecy jurisdictions, consulting fees with no clear service;
- tax evasion: movement to low-tax or secrecy jurisdictions, undeclared accounts, layering to obscure origin;
- human trafficking: regular small deposits consistent with exploitation, third-party control of accounts, movement to source countries;
- sanctions evasion: counterparty or geography exposure to sanctioned persons or regions, use of front companies;
- cybercrime: rapid conversion of fraud proceeds to virtual assets, mule networks, mixing services;
- trade-based money laundering: over- or under-invoicing, phantom shipments, circular trade flows.

Each typology should map to monitoring scenarios and investigation guidance. Investigators should be trained to connect observed patterns to predicate offenses, not just to label activity as unusual.

### Distinguish Suspicion From Proof

The reporting threshold for proceeds of crime is suspicion, not proof. The institution does not need to establish that a crime occurred or that specific funds are the proceeds of that crime.

Suspicion versus proof:

- suspicion requires specific, articulable facts that connect the activity to potential criminal conduct;
- suspicion does not require identifying the predicate offense or the perpetrator;
- suspicion does not require evidence admissible in a criminal trial;
- the institution reports its suspicion and lets the financial intelligence unit and law enforcement investigate;
- demanding proof before reporting sets the threshold too high and results in missed reports.

The institution should train investigators to recognize when the suspicion standard is met and to document the specific facts. A decision not to report because the activity is not proven is a misapplication of the standard.

### Meet Reporting And Escalation Obligations

When the institution suspects that property is criminal, it must report through the applicable mechanism and escalate internally.

Reporting and escalation:

- file a suspicious transaction or activity report with the financial intelligence unit within the regulatory deadline;
- escalate internally to the money laundering reporting officer or equivalent;
- do not conduct further transactions that would constitute tipping off or assistance to the suspected laundering;
- consider whether to restrict, freeze, or exit the relationship;
- assess whether consent or defense is required before proceeding with a transaction in some regimes.

Some regimes require the institution to obtain consent or a defense before processing a transaction where suspicion exists. Processing without the required consent can itself constitute an offense. The institution must understand whether its regime has such a consent requirement.

### Avoid The Offense Of Dealing With Criminal Property

In many regimes, dealing with criminal property, by acquiring, using, possessing, transferring, or concealing it, is itself an offense when the handler knows or suspects the property is criminal.

Offense exposure:

- the offense can apply to individual employees who process the transaction with knowledge or suspicion;
- the institution can face corporate liability and enforcement;
- the offense can apply even where the institution has filed a report, if the required consent regime was not followed;
- continuing to process transactions after forming suspicion, without the proper legal basis, increases exposure.

The institution must have clear procedures for what happens after suspicion is formed, including whether to process, hold, or reject transactions, and how to manage the relationship pending the reporting outcome.

### Manage The Relationship After Suspicion

Once suspicion is formed, the institution must decide how to manage the relationship. This decision affects both compliance and the integrity of any law enforcement investigation.

Relationship management options:

- continue the relationship under enhanced monitoring to generate intelligence for law enforcement, where the regime permits and law enforcement requests;
- restrict the account or products to limit exposure;
- exit the relationship, taking care not to tip off;
- freeze assets where a legal obligation or order requires it;
- coordinate with law enforcement where there is an active investigation.

The decision should be documented with rationale and, where relevant, with law enforcement guidance. Exiting immediately after filing, without considering intelligence value, can disrupt an investigation. Continuing without restriction can increase exposure. The balance requires judgment.

### Document The Suspicion And The Decision

The record of the suspicion, the investigation, and the reporting decision must be complete and defensible.

Documentation should include:

- the specific facts that generated suspicion and the predicate offense typology;
- the investigation performed and its findings;
- the reporting decision, including the filed report or the documented rationale for not filing;
- the relationship management decision and its rationale;
- any law enforcement coordination or guidance;
- the individuals involved in the decision and the dates.

A suspicion that is investigated and closed without a documented rationale is indistinguishable from a missed report. The documentation must show that the decision was reasoned, not reflexive.

## Common Traps

### Treating Proceeds Of Crime As Ordinary AML Monitoring

Generic unusual-activity alerts do not capture the criminal property element. Detection must connect patterns to predicate offenses.

### Demanding Proof Before Reporting

The threshold is suspicion. Requiring proof of the predicate crime before reporting results in missed obligations.

### Not Recognizing The Consent Requirement

Some regimes require consent before processing where suspicion exists. Processing without consent can be an offense even after filing.

### Continuing To Process Without Managing Exposure

Processing transactions after forming suspicion, without restriction or legal basis, increases money laundering offense exposure.

### Exiting Immediately Without Considering Intelligence Value

Immediate exit after filing can disrupt a law enforcement investigation. The exit decision should consider intelligence value and law enforcement guidance.

### No Documentation Of The No-Report Decision

A suspicion closed without a report must have a documented rationale. Undocumented closures are indistinguishable from missed reports.

### Predicate Offense Typologies Not Trained

Investigators who cannot connect patterns to predicate offenses will label activity as unusual without recognizing the criminal property element.

## Self-Check

- Is criminal property defined and understood under the applicable regime, including the broad predicate offense list and the knowledge or suspicion element?
- Are monitoring scenarios and investigation guidance mapped to specific predicate offense typologies such as drug trafficking, fraud, corruption, tax evasion, and human trafficking?
- Is the suspicion standard applied correctly, with specific articulable facts, rather than demanding proof of the predicate offense?
- Are reports filed with the financial intelligence unit within the regulatory deadline, with internal escalation to the reporting officer?
- Is the consent or defense requirement understood and followed where the regime requires it before processing?
- Are procedures in place to avoid the offense of dealing with criminal property, including what happens after suspicion is formed?
- Is the relationship management decision, whether continue, restrict, exit, or freeze, documented with rationale and law enforcement coordination where relevant?
- Is the suspicion, investigation, reporting decision, and relationship decision documented completely and defensibly?
- Are investigators trained to connect transaction patterns to predicate offenses rather than merely labeling activity as unusual?
- Is the detection and reporting design confirmed against the applicable national law and regulator guidance rather than a generic standard?
