# Proceeds Of Crime Seizure Forfeiture And Asset Recovery

Asset seizure, forfeiture, and confiscation are the mechanisms by which the state recovers criminal proceeds and instrumentalities, and obliged entities are frequently the instruments through which these mechanisms operate. Under the UN Convention against Transnational Organized Crime, the UN Convention against Corruption, national proceeds of crime acts, civil and criminal forfeiture statutes, and equivalent regimes, financial institutions and other obliged entities must comply with freezing, seizure, restraint, and forfeiture orders, manage frozen assets, and cooperate with law enforcement asset recovery. The central judgment problem is that once an order is served, the institution's obligations change fundamentally. Assets must be frozen and cannot be moved, records must be preserved, and any dealing with the restrained property can constitute contempt, obstruction, or a standalone offense. The institution that mishandles a forfeiture order, whether by releasing frozen funds, tipping off the target, or failing to preserve records, faces serious enforcement and potential criminal exposure.

Use this skill before responding to seizure or forfeiture orders, managing frozen assets, handling confiscation proceedings, or coordinating with law enforcement on asset recovery. The goal is to make the agent think about the scope of the order, the freeze and preservation obligations, the tipping-off prohibitions, and the decisions that are easy to make too casually. A mishandled forfeiture order can unwind an investigation and expose the institution.

This skill addresses jurisdiction-specific obligations. Forfeiture procedures, freeze obligations, third-party rights, and cooperation duties differ across national regimes. Always confirm the applicable national law, the specific terms of the order, and consult qualified legal counsel for specific seizure and forfeiture response decisions.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Type And Scope Of The Order

Asset recovery orders come in different forms, each with different obligations. The institution must first identify what kind of order it has received and what it requires.

Order types and their effects:

- freezing or restraint orders prevent dealing with specified assets pending further proceedings, typically issued early in an investigation or prosecution;
- seizure orders take physical or constructive possession of assets, often cash or tangible property;
- confiscation or forfeiture orders permanently deprive the target of the assets, following conviction or in civil forfeiture proceedings;
- production orders require the institution to produce records or information about identified assets;
- civil forfeiture targets the property itself, without requiring a criminal conviction, based on the property's connection to crime.

The institution must read the order carefully to determine what assets are covered, what actions are prohibited or required, what the deadlines are, and what disclosures are permitted. Treating all orders the same is an error, because a freeze order, a production order, and a forfeiture order impose different duties.

### Freeze The Identified Assets Immediately And Accurately

When a freeze or restraint order is received, the institution must act immediately to prevent any dealing with the identified assets.

Freeze implementation:

- identify all accounts and assets within the institution's control that fall within the order's scope;
- place blocks or holds to prevent withdrawals, transfers, and other dealings;
- capture the asset position as of the effective date of the order, since subsequent fluctuations may be relevant;
- confirm that the freeze covers all branches, subsidiaries, and systems that could access the assets;
- verify that joint accounts, trust accounts, and nominee accounts are handled according to the order's terms;
- document the freeze implementation with timestamps and the personnel involved.

Speed is critical. A delay that allows the target to move assets before the freeze takes effect can defeat the order and expose the institution. The institution should have a tested process for rapid freeze implementation.

### Preserve Records And Avoid Tipping Off

The institution must preserve all records relevant to the order and must not tip off the target or any third party about the order or the investigation.

Record preservation:

- preserve account records, transaction history, correspondence, and identification records;
- apply legal hold to prevent routine deletion or retention cycles;
- capture records that might otherwise be purged under normal retention;
- identify custodians and systems holding relevant records;
- be prepared to produce records under a production order within the required deadline.

Tipping off is prohibited in most regimes. The institution cannot inform the target that an order exists, that assets are frozen for investigative reasons, or that a report has been made. Communications with the target about account access must be handled so as not to reveal the existence or nature of the order. Tipping off is itself a criminal offense in many jurisdictions.

### Cooperate With Law Enforcement Within Legal Limits

The institution must cooperate with law enforcement asset recovery, but cooperation must occur within legal limits and through proper channels.

Cooperation principles:

- respond to lawful orders and requests within the required deadlines;
- provide accurate and complete information within the scope of the request;
- maintain the confidentiality of the investigation;
- seek clarification or legal review when a request is ambiguous or appears to exceed legal authority;
- coordinate through designated legal or compliance channels, not through individual employees;
- document all interactions, requests, and productions.

Cooperation does not mean unconditional compliance with informal requests. The institution should distinguish between lawful orders that compel action and informal requests that may require separate legal basis or customer authorization.

### Manage Frozen Assets According To The Order

Frozen assets remain on the institution's books and may require ongoing management. The institution must manage them according to the order and applicable law.

Frozen asset management:

- continue to charge normal fees and accrue interest unless the order prohibits it;
- do not release, transfer, or set off frozen assets without court authorization;
- handle income or proceeds generated by frozen assets according to the order;
- monitor for attempted access and report violations to law enforcement;
- maintain accurate records of the frozen asset position over time;
- address customer inquiries about account access without tipping off.

Some orders specify how frozen assets are to be managed, including whether the target can access funds for living expenses or legal fees. The institution must follow the order's terms and seek court guidance where they are unclear.

### Handle Third-Party Rights And Claims

Forfeiture and confiscation can affect third parties with interests in the targeted assets, such as joint account holders, innocent owners, lienholders, and beneficiaries.

Third-party considerations:

- identify third parties with potential interests in the frozen or forfeited assets;
- follow the order's treatment of third-party interests;
- recognize that third parties may have rights to petition the court or claim an interest;
- do not unilaterally release assets to third parties without court authorization;
- document third-party claims and refer them to law enforcement or the court.

Third-party rights are a common source of complexity in forfeiture. The institution should not adjudicate claims itself but should follow the court's directions and preserve the status quo.

### Prepare For Confiscation And Distribution

When a confiscation or forfeiture order becomes final, the institution must be prepared to transfer the assets to the designated authority.

Confiscation readiness:

- confirm the order is final and not subject to further appeal within the relevant period;
- verify the recipient authority and the transfer instructions;
- execute the transfer of assets to the designated authority;
- obtain receipts or confirmations of the transfer;
- update records to reflect the transfer;
- address any residual accounts or relationships after transfer.

Premature transfer, before the order is final, can expose the institution to liability if the order is later reversed. The institution must confirm finality before releasing assets.

## Common Traps

### Treating All Orders The Same

Freeze, seizure, production, and forfeiture orders impose different duties. Reading the order carefully is essential.

### Delay In Implementing The Freeze

A delay that allows the target to move assets defeats the order. Freeze implementation must be immediate and tested.

### Tipping Off The Target

Informing the target about the order or the investigation is a criminal offense. Communications must be managed to avoid tipping off.

### Releasing Frozen Assets Without Authorization

Releasing, transferring, or setting off frozen assets without court authorization can constitute contempt or a standalone offense.

### Over-Compliance With Informal Requests

Cooperating with informal requests that exceed legal authority can create liability. Distinguish lawful orders from informal requests.

### Mishandling Third-Party Claims

Unilaterally releasing assets to third parties without court authorization is an error. Third-party claims must be directed to the court.

### Premature Transfer Before Finality

Transferring assets before a confiscation order is final exposes the institution if the order is reversed.

## Self-Check

- Is the type and scope of each order identified, distinguishing freeze, seizure, production, and forfeiture orders and their specific duties?
- Are identified assets frozen immediately and accurately across all branches, systems, and related accounts?
- Are records preserved under legal hold, and is tipping off prohibited and enforced in all communications?
- Is cooperation with law enforcement conducted through proper legal and compliance channels within legal limits?
- Are frozen assets managed according to the order, including fees, interest, income, and access for living expenses or legal fees where permitted?
- Are third-party rights and claims identified and directed to the court rather than adjudicated by the institution?
- Is confiscation and distribution handled only after the order is final, with verified recipient and transfer confirmation?
- Are all interactions, requests, productions, and freeze implementations documented with timestamps and personnel?
- Is there a tested process for rapid freeze implementation to prevent asset movement before the freeze takes effect?
- Is the seizure and forfeiture response confirmed against the applicable national law, the specific order terms, and legal counsel guidance?
