# Hazard Assessment And Hierarchy Of Controls

Hazard assessment is the foundation of safety compliance, and the hierarchy of controls is the decision framework that determines whether controls are legally and ethically adequate. The defining difficulty is that the duty is proactive and feasibility-based rather than perfection-based, the gap between a written program and actual field practice is where violations occur, and reliance on personal protective equipment where engineering controls are feasible is itself a violation. Safety is judged by what is actually done at the worksite, not by what is written in a binder.

Use this skill before designing a safety program, conducting hazard assessment, selecting controls, responding to a citation, or evaluating whether controls meet the feasibility standard. The goal is to make the agent identify applicable standards, apply the hierarchy correctly, verify field implementation, and recognize where the situation requires a qualified safety professional. Safety obligations are jurisdiction-dependent and high-hazard operations should involve competent safety professionals and counsel.

## Core Rules

### Apply The General Duty Clause For Unregulated Hazards

The general duty clause requires a workplace free of recognized hazards likely to cause death or serious harm, filling gaps where no specific standard applies.

Assess:

- whether the hazard is recognized in the industry or workplace;
- whether abatement is feasible;
- whether feasible controls have been implemented;
- industry consensus standards as evidence of recognition and feasibility;
- documentation of hazard assessments.

A hazard recognized in the industry is recognized even if the employer claims ignorance. The general duty clause is increasingly used for emerging hazards such as heat and workplace violence that lack specific standards.

### Apply The Hierarchy Of Controls In Strict Order

Hazard control follows a hierarchy from most to least effective, and the order is mandatory where feasible.

Implement in order:

- elimination of the hazard entirely;
- substitution with a less hazardous process or material;
- engineering controls such as ventilation, guarding, and machine design;
- administrative controls such as procedures, scheduling, and training;
- personal protective equipment as the last resort.

PPE is the least effective control because it relies on consistent use, fit, and maintenance. Reliance on PPE where engineering or administrative controls are feasible is a violation. The hierarchy must be applied in order, skipping levels only where higher controls are genuinely infeasible, and that infeasibility must be documented.

### Comply With Specific Applicable Standards

Specific standards apply to general industry, construction, maritime, and agriculture, each with detailed requirements.

Cover:

- hazard communication and chemical safety;
- personal protective equipment standards;
- fall protection and walking-working surfaces;
- electrical safety and lockout and tagout;
- machine guarding;
- respiratory protection and fit testing;
- hearing conservation;
- bloodborne pathogens;
- confined spaces and permit-required entry;
- process safety management of highly hazardous chemicals;
- excavation, trenching, scaffolding, and steel erection in construction.

Each standard has specific assessment, control, training, and documentation requirements. Hazard communication, lockout and tagout, and respiratory protection are among the most frequently cited.

### Conduct Baseline And Ongoing Hazard Assessment

Hazard assessment must be systematic and continuous, not reactive.

Implement:

- baseline hazard assessments for each worksite and task;
- job safety analysis for high-risk tasks breaking each task into steps, hazards, and controls;
- periodic reassessment after incidents, process changes, or new equipment;
- incident and near-miss investigation;
- root-cause analysis examining system factors beyond individual error;
- employee involvement in hazard identification;
- tracking of corrective actions to completion.

Near-miss reporting is a leading indicator of safety culture. Investigations that stop at employee error without examining system causes miss root causes and guarantee recurrence.

### Verify Field Implementation Against Written Programs

A program that exists on paper but is not followed in practice is not a defense and can be an aggravating factor.

Verify through:

- worksite audits and observations;
- employee interviews;
- inspection of equipment and controls;
- review of training delivery and comprehension;
- tracking of corrective action completion;
- assessment of the gap between policy and practice.

The gap between written program and field practice is where most violations are found. An audit that reviews only documents without observing the worksite misses the real risk.

### Address Emerging And State-Specific Hazards

Several hazards are emerging enforcement priorities or are regulated only at the state level.

Cover:

- heat illness prevention including water, shade, acclimatization, and training;
- workplace violence prevention with risk assessment and controls;
- infectious disease and aerosol transmissible disease standards;
- ergonomics where regulated;
- state-specific standards that are stricter than federal rules.

Heat and workplace violence are cited under the general duty clause where no specific federal standard exists, and several states have specific standards. Multi-state employers must track variations and apply the strictest applicable standard.

### Prepare For Inspections And Citation Response

Inspections require preparation and a defined response process.

Cover:

- inspection reception and opening procedures;
- walkaround and employee interview management;
- document and record production controls;
- citation contestment and informal conference strategy;
- abatement verification;
- repeat and willful violation enhancement awareness;
- state plan variation awareness.

Willful and repeat violations carry significantly higher penalties and potential criminal exposure. The employer has rights during inspection including representation, and the response should be planned in advance.

## Common Traps

### Reliance On PPE Where Engineering Controls Are Feasible

PPE is the last resort; feasible engineering or administrative controls must be implemented first.

### Written Program Without Field Implementation

A program followed only on paper is not a defense and can evidence a knowing gap.

### Investigation Stopping At Employee Error

Root-cause analysis must examine system factors, not just individual actions.

### Ignoring Emerging Hazards As Unregulated

Heat and workplace violence are cited under the general duty clause and state standards.

### Applying Federal Standards When State Plans Are Stricter

State plans must be at least as effective as federal rules and often are stricter.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- Are recognized hazards assessed under the general duty clause where no specific standard applies, with feasible abatement implemented and documented?
- Is the hierarchy of controls applied in strict order, with PPE used only where higher controls are genuinely infeasible and that infeasibility documented?
- Are specific applicable standards implemented including hazard communication, PPE, fall protection, electrical, lockout and tagout, machine guarding, respiratory protection, hearing conservation, bloodborne pathogens, confined spaces, and process safety?
- Are baseline and ongoing hazard assessments conducted including job safety analysis, near-miss reporting, root-cause investigation, and corrective action tracking?
- Is field implementation verified against written programs through audits, observations, and interviews, with the policy-to-practice gap assessed?
- Are emerging and state-specific hazards including heat, workplace violence, and infectious disease addressed under the strictest applicable standard?
- Is the organization prepared for inspections with reception, walkaround, interview, document, citation, and abatement procedures?
- Where high-hazard operations are involved, has the assessment involved a qualified safety professional and counsel, recognizing that safety obligations are jurisdiction-dependent and this guidance is not legal advice?
