# Injury Illness Recordkeeping And Severe Reporting

Injury and illness recordkeeping is both a legal obligation and the evidentiary record that regulators, plaintiffs, and auditors use to judge an employer's safety performance. The defining difficulty is that the rules governing what must be recorded, when work-relatedness attaches, and how severe injuries must be reported are technical and counterintuitive, and the records themselves become the evidence in enforcement and litigation. Underreporting and misclassification are common violations that can be cited independently and that undermine every downstream safety metric.

Use this skill before maintaining injury records, determining work-relatedness, responding to a severe injury, preparing for an audit, or evaluating recordkeeping practices. The goal is to make the agent apply the correct recording and reporting triggers, determine work-relatedness properly, maintain records that withstand scrutiny, and protect against retaliation for reporting. Recordkeeping rules have state variations and contested cases should involve qualified counsel.

## Core Rules

### Maintain The Required Injury And Illness Records

Covered employers must maintain specific records of work-related injuries and illnesses.

Maintain:

- the log of work-related injuries and illnesses;
- the annual summary and certification by a company executive;
- individual incident reports for each recordable event;
- physician or healthcare provider reports where applicable;
- records retained for the required period, typically five years for the log and summary;
- updates to the log as new information emerges within the retention period.

Partial exemptions exist for certain low-hazard industries and small employers, but those exemptions do not apply once a severe injury reporting threshold is triggered. The annual summary must be certified by a company executive, not merely a safety manager.

### Determine Work-Relatedness Correctly

Work-relatedness determines whether an injury or illness must be recorded, and the determination has specific rules.

An injury or illness is work-related when:

- an event or exposure in the work environment caused or contributed to the condition;
- the work environment aggravated a pre-existing condition;
- the condition occurred while the employee was working, including remote and travel situations subject to exceptions.

Exceptions apply for limited situations including self-medication for non-work conditions, common cold and flu, voluntary personal activities outside working hours, and certain motor vehicle accidents in parking lots. The exceptions are narrow and must be applied carefully. Mental illness is recordable only when voluntarily provided by a licensed professional.

### Apply The Recordability Criteria

Not every work-related event is recordable. Recordability turns on specific outcomes.

Record when the event results in:

- death;
- days away from work;
- restricted work or job transfer;
- medical treatment beyond first aid;
- loss of consciousness;
- a significant injury or illness diagnosed by a physician or licensed professional.

First aid is defined by a specific list, and treatment beyond that list is medical treatment. The distinction between first aid and medical treatment is a frequent source of error. Diagnostic procedures alone, such as x-rays or blood draws, are not medical treatment, but treatment resulting from diagnosis may be.

### Count And Record Days Correctly

Days away from work and restricted work drive recording and must be counted accurately.

Count:

- calendar days, not scheduled work days, for days away and restriction;
- the count beginning the day after the injury;
- a cap on the total day count for recording purposes, while actual days continue for benefits;
- restriction and transfer days even when the employee works in a restricted capacity;
- the distinction between restricted work and limited duty.

Stopping the day count prematurely, or counting scheduled work days instead of calendar days, understates severity and misrepresents the record.

### Report Severe Injuries Within Deadlines

Severe injuries must be reported to the agency within strict deadlines, separate from recording.

Report within:

- eight hours for any work-related fatality;
- twenty-four hours for work-related inpatient hospitalization, amputation, or loss of an eye;
- the applicable deadlines under state plans, which may differ or be stricter.

The reporting is triggered by the event, not by the recordability determination. Failure to report a severe injury within the deadline is a standalone citation regardless of the underlying cause.

### Submit Electronic Reports As Required

Electronic submission requirements apply to certain establishments.

Submit:

- summary data for covered establishments;
- detailed case data for larger establishments in designated industries;
- data through the agency's electronic portal by the deadline;
- accurate establishment and identification information.

Electronic submission data is used for enforcement targeting and public disclosure. Inaccurate or incomplete submissions create both compliance and reputational risk.

### Protect Against Underreporting And Retaliation

Underreporting is a common and independently citable violation, and retaliation for reporting is unlawful.

Control:

- training on recording criteria for those maintaining records;
- review of records for suspicious patterns such as no days away or all first aid;
- separation of safety reporting from disciplinary incentives that suppress reporting;
- protection of employees who report injuries or safety concerns from retaliation;
- anti-retaliation program elements including complaint channels;
- audit of records against medical and workers compensation data.

Incentive programs that reward low injury rates can suppress reporting and are scrutinized. Discipline for reporting, or programs that imply reporting will harm the reporter, violate anti-retaliation protections.

### Manage Records During Inspections And Audits

Records are the first thing an inspector examines and the foundation of enforcement.

Manage:

- organized and current records available for inspection;
- consistent application of recording criteria;
- documentation of work-relatedness determinations including exceptions applied;
- explanation of any anomalies or corrections;
- awareness that records are discoverable in litigation.

Inconsistent or gap-filled records invite scrutiny and shift burdens. Records that show underreporting relative to industry norms or to workers compensation claims are red flags.

## Common Traps

### Misclassifying Medical Treatment As First Aid

The first aid list is specific; treatment beyond it is medical treatment and triggers recording.

### Stopping Day Counts Prematurely

Calendar days must be counted to the cap; stopping at scheduled work days understates severity.

### Missing Severe Injury Reporting Deadlines

Reporting is triggered by the event and is independent of recording; late reporting is a standalone citation.

### Incentive Programs That Suppress Reporting

Rate-based incentives that discourage reporting violate anti-retaliation principles and create underreporting.

### Applying Narrow Work-Relatedness Exceptions Too Broadly

The exceptions are narrow; over-applying them to avoid recording is a violation.

## Self-Check

- Are the required injury and illness records maintained including the log, annual summary certified by an executive, and incident reports, retained for the required period and updated as information emerges?
- Is work-relatedness determined correctly, applying the work environment contribution and aggravation rules with narrow exceptions applied carefully?
- Are recordability criteria applied including death, days away, restricted work, medical treatment beyond first aid, loss of consciousness, and significant diagnosed conditions?
- Are days counted in calendar days from the day after injury, to the recording cap, distinguishing restricted work from limited duty?
- Are severe injuries reported within eight hours for fatalities and twenty-four hours for hospitalization, amputation, or eye loss, under the applicable state deadlines?
- Are electronic reports submitted accurately and on time for covered establishments?
- Is underreporting controlled through training, pattern review, anti-retaliation protections, and audit against medical and workers compensation data?
- Are records managed for inspection consistency and discoverability, and where contested determinations arise, has qualified counsel been involved, recognizing that recordkeeping rules have state variations and this guidance is not legal advice?
