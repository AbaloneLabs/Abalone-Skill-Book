# Workplace Wellness And Health Privacy Compliance

Workplace wellness and health privacy compliance governs the tension between employer interest in employee health and the employee's right to medical privacy. The defining feature is that health information flows through multiple legal channels—wellness programs, health plans, fitness-for-duty exams, accommodation requests—and each channel has different rules on what the employer may collect, see, and use. The central difficulty is that the employer as employer is generally prohibited from knowing employee medical information, while the employer as plan sponsor may receive aggregate data under strict conditions, and the boundary between the two roles is easily blurred.

Use this skill before advising on wellness program design, health information handling, medical inquiries, fitness-for-duty exams, or genetic information compliance. The goal is to make the agent identify the privacy frameworks, the permissible design of wellness incentives, the separation between employment and plan functions, and the limits on medical inquiry before concluding that a program is compliant.

## Core Rules

### Separate Employer And Plan Sponsor Functions

The employer and the health plan sponsor are legally distinct for privacy purposes.

Control:

- HIPAA privacy rules apply to the health plan, not the employer-as-employer;
- the employer may receive only summary health information and enrollment/disenrollment data;
- the employer may not receive individual medical information from the plan except under strict conditions;
- firewalls between plan and employment functions;
- certification of the firewall and restricted access;
- prohibition on using plan information for employment decisions.

Information that flows from the plan to the employer for employment decisions is a serious violation. The firewall must be documented and enforced.

### Design Wellness Programs Within Legal Limits

Wellness programs must satisfy HIPAA nondiscrimination rules and the ADA.

Assess:

- participatory versus health-contingent wellness programs;
- participatory programs: available to all similarly situated, no limit on reward;
- health-contingent programs: activity-only or outcome-based, with a maximum reward (typically 30% of coverage cost, up to 50% for tobacco);
- reasonable alternative standards for health-contingent programs;
- notice of availability of alternatives;
- avoidance of discrimination based on health factors;
- ADA limits on medical inquiries and exams in wellness programs;
- voluntary participation standards under the ADA.

Health-contingent programs must offer reasonable alternative standards so employees with health factors can earn the reward. The ADA restricts medical inquiries even in wellness programs and requires that participation be voluntary.

### Comply With HIPAA Privacy In Employer Plans

Employer-sponsored health plans are covered entities under HIPAA.

Implement:

- identification of the plan as a covered entity;
- privacy notices to participants;
- minimum necessary standard for uses and disclosures;
- authorization requirements for non-routine disclosures;
- safeguards on protected health information (PHI);
- breach notification obligations;
- business associate agreements with vendors;
- training of plan workforce members.

The plan's PHI is subject to HIPAA even though the employer is not directly regulated as an employer. Vendors handling PHI must have business associate agreements.

### Limit Medical Inquiries And Exams

Medical inquiries and exams are restricted in employment.

Control:

- no medical inquiries or exams before a conditional offer of employment;
- post-offer entrance exams under specific conditions;
- no medical inquiries of employees unless job-related and consistent with business necessity;
- fitness-for-duty exams when disability-related and job-related;
- voluntary wellness program medical inquiries under ADA limits;
- confidentiality of medical information obtained;
- restriction on genetic information inquiries.

Medical inquiries must be job-related and consistent with business necessity, except in limited contexts (post-offer, wellness). The ADA limits are strict and frequently misunderstood.

### Protect Genetic Information

The Genetic Information Nondiscrimination Act (GINA) restricts collection and use of genetic information.

Implement:

- prohibition on requesting, requiring, or purchasing genetic information;
- the inadvertent acquisition exception (listening to an employee mention a condition);
- restriction on requesting family medical history;
- wellness programs and genetic information limits;
- confidentiality of any genetic information obtained;
- separation of genetic information from personnel files;
- prohibition on using genetic information for employment decisions.

Wellness programs that ask about family medical history violate GINA. The prohibition on acquiring genetic information is broad, with a narrow inadvertent acquisition exception.

### Maintain Confidentiality Of Medical Information

Medical information must be kept confidential and separate from personnel files.

Cover:

- ADA confidentiality for medical records;
- separation of medical files from personnel files;
- restricted access to medical information;
- secure storage (physical and electronic);
- disclosure only to those with a need to know;
- retention and disposal practices;
- breach response for medical information.

Medical records include accommodation requests, fitness-for-duty results, and wellness data. Access must be limited to those with a legitimate need.

### Handle Fitness-For-Duty And Return-To-Work Appropriately

Fitness-for-duty inquiries require legal grounding.

Assess:

- job-related and consistent with business necessity standard;
- reasonable belief that the employee cannot perform essential functions;
- direct threat assessment;
- medical inquiry versus medical exam;
- return-to-work exams after leave or injury;
- consistency and non-discrimination in requiring exams;
- scope limited to the specific concern.

A fitness-for-duty exam requires a reasonable belief based on objective evidence, not speculation. The exam scope must be limited to the specific concern.

### Coordinate With Leave And Accommodation

Wellness and health privacy intersect with leave and accommodation.

Cover:

- medical certification for FMLA and ADA accommodation;
- the employer's right to clarification and second opinions;
- confidentiality of certification information;
- the interactive process for accommodation;
- return-to-work and essential function analysis;
- coordination between HR, leave administrators, and managers.

Medical certification for leave or accommodation is permitted, but the information must be handled confidentially and used only for the leave or accommodation purpose.

## Common Traps

### Employer Receiving Individual Medical Information From The Plan

Plan-to-employer disclosure of individual medical information for employment use is a serious violation.

### Wellness Program Without Reasonable Alternative Standards

Health-contingent programs that lack alternatives discriminate against employees with health factors.

### Medical Inquiry Not Job-Related And Consistent With Business Necessity

Broad medical questionnaires or exams without justification violate the ADA.

### Genetic Or Family Medical History Request In Wellness

Asking about family medical history in wellness programs violates GINA.

### Medical Information In Personnel Files

Storing medical information in personnel files violates confidentiality requirements.

### Fitness-For-Duty Exam Without Objective Evidence

Requiring an exam based on speculation rather than objective evidence is unlawful.

## Self-Check

- Are employer and plan sponsor functions separated with firewalls, restricted access, certification, and no use of plan information for employment decisions?
- Are wellness programs designed within participatory and health-contingent rules, with maximum reward limits, reasonable alternative standards, notice, ADA medical inquiry limits, and voluntary participation?
- Is HIPAA privacy implemented in the health plan including privacy notices, minimum necessary, authorization, safeguards, breach notification, business associate agreements, and training?
- Are medical inquiries and exams limited to post-offer, job-related, fitness-for-duty, and voluntary wellness contexts, with confidentiality?
- Is genetic information protected under GINA including no requests, inadvertent acquisition exception handling, family history restrictions, wellness limits, confidentiality, and separation?
- Is medical information kept confidential, separate from personnel files, with restricted access, secure storage, need-to-know disclosure, and breach response?
- Are fitness-for-duty and return-to-work exams based on objective evidence, job-related, consistent, limited in scope, and non-discriminatory?
- Is medical certification for leave and accommodation handled confidentially with clarification rights, interactive process, and coordination?
- Is the distinction between employer-as-employer and employer-as-plan-sponsor maintained?
- Are wellness incentives assessed for both HIPAA nondiscrimination and ADA medical inquiry compliance?
