# Occupational Safety And Health Compliance

Occupational safety and health compliance governs the employer's duty to provide a workplace free of recognized hazards. The defining feature is that the duty is proactive—the employer must anticipate and abate hazards before injury occurs—and the standard is feasibility, not perfection. The central difficulty is that safety compliance requires translating general standards into specific engineering, administrative, and personal protective equipment controls for each worksite, and the gap between a written program and actual field practice is where violations and injuries occur.

Use this skill before advising on safety program design, hazard assessment, OSHA citation response, injury reporting, or industry-specific safety standards. The goal is to make the agent identify the applicable standards, the hierarchy of controls, the training and recordkeeping duties, and the reporting obligations before concluding that a safety program is compliant.

## Core Rules

### Provide A Workplace Free Of Recognized Hazards

The general duty clause requires a workplace free of recognized hazards likely to cause death or serious harm.

Assess:

- recognition of hazards in the industry and workplace;
- feasibility of abatement;
- implementation of feasible controls;
- coverage of hazards not addressed by specific standards;
- industry consensus standards as evidence of recognition;
- documentation of hazard assessments.

The general duty clause fills gaps where no specific standard applies. A hazard recognized in the industry is "recognized" even if the employer claims ignorance.

### Apply The Hierarchy Of Controls

Hazard control follows a hierarchy from most to least effective.

Implement in order:

- elimination of the hazard;
- substitution with a less hazardous process or material;
- engineering controls (ventilation, guarding, machine design);
- administrative controls (procedures, scheduling, training);
- personal protective equipment (PPE) as the last resort.

PPE is the least effective control because it relies on consistent use and fit. Reliance on PPE where engineering controls are feasible is a violation.

### Comply With Specific OSHA Standards

Specific standards apply to general industry, construction, maritime, and agriculture.

Cover:

- hazard communication and chemical safety (HazCom 2012 / GHS);
- personal protective equipment standards;
- fall protection and walking-working surfaces;
- electrical safety and lockout/tagout;
- machine guarding;
- respiratory protection and fit testing;
- hearing conservation;
- bloodborne pathogens;
- confined spaces (permit-required);
- process safety management of highly hazardous chemicals;
- excavation and trenching (construction);
- scaffolding and steel erection (construction).

Each standard has specific requirements for assessment, control, training, and documentation. HazCom, lockout/tagout, and respiratory protection are among the most frequently cited.

### Conduct Hazard Assessment And Job Safety Analysis

Hazard assessment identifies risks before they cause injury.

Implement:

- baseline hazard assessments for each worksite and task;
- job safety analysis (JSA) for high-risk tasks;
- periodic reassessment after incidents, changes, or new equipment;
- incident and near-miss investigation;
- root-cause analysis;
- employee involvement in hazard identification;
- tracking of corrective actions to completion.

Near-miss reporting is a leading indicator. Investigations that stop at "employee error" without examining system causes miss root causes.

### Provide Safety Training And Certification

Training must be effective, documented, and in a language workers understand.

Cover:

- training on specific hazards and controls;
- language and literacy accessibility;
- verification of understanding;
- documentation of who was trained, when, and on what;
- refresher training and new-hire training;
- certification for specialized work (forklift, scaffolding, confined space entry);
- training of contractors and temporary workers.

Training that workers cannot understand is no training. Documentation must demonstrate not just delivery but comprehension.

### Maintain Injury And Illness Records And Reporting

Recordkeeping and reporting are mandatory and scrutinized.

Control:

- OSHA 300 log of work-related injuries and illnesses;
- OSHA 300A annual summary and posting;
- OSHA 301 incident reports;
- work-relatedness determination;
- severe injury reporting within deadlines (fatality within 8 hours, amputation or hospitalization within 24 hours);
- retention for required periods;
- protection against retaliation for reporting.

Underreporting is a common violation and can itself be cited. The work-relatedness determination has specific rules (e.g., pre-existing conditions aggravated by work).

### Implement Workplace Violence Prevention

Workplace violence is an emerging enforcement priority.

Assess:

- workplace violence risk assessment;
- engineering controls (access, lighting, alarms);
- administrative controls (staffing, procedures, threat assessment teams);
- training on de-escalation and response;
- incident reporting and investigation;
- coordination with security and law enforcement;
- high-risk settings (healthcare, late-night retail, social services).

OSHA has cited employers under the general duty clause for workplace violence hazards. Healthcare and late-night retail are high-risk settings requiring specific controls.

### Prepare For Inspections And Citation Response

OSHA inspections require preparation and a defined response.

Cover:

- inspection preparation including reception procedures;
- walkaround and employee interviews;
- document and record production;
- citation contestment and informal conference;
- abatement verification;
- repeat and willful violation enhancement;
- state plan variations.

The employer has rights during inspection, including representation. Willful and repeat violations carry significantly higher penalties and potential criminal exposure.

### Address State Plan Variations

State plans may be stricter than federal OSHA.

Assess:

- state plan coverage and jurisdiction;
- state-specific standards (e.g., aerosol transmissible diseases, heat illness);
- state reporting thresholds;
- state consultation programs;
- multi-state employer consistency.

State plans must be at least as effective as federal OSHA and often are stricter. Multi-state employers must track variations.

## Common Traps

### Reliance On PPE Where Engineering Controls Are Feasible

PPE is the last resort; feasible engineering or administrative controls must be implemented first.

### Written Program Without Field Implementation

A program that exists on paper but is not followed in practice is not a defense.

### Investigation Stopping At Employee Error

Root-cause analysis must examine system factors, not just individual actions.

### Training In A Language Workers Do Not Understand

Inaccessible training is ineffective and does not satisfy the standard.

### Underreporting Or Misclassifying Injuries

Underreporting or misclassifying to avoid recording is a violation and can be cited.

### Heat And Workplace Violence Treated As Non-Regulated

These are emerging enforcement priorities under the general duty clause and state standards.

## Self-Check

- Is the workplace assessed for recognized hazards with feasible abatement under the general duty clause and specific standards?
- Is the hierarchy of controls applied with elimination, substitution, engineering, administrative, and PPE in order?
- Are specific OSHA standards implemented including HazCom, PPE, fall protection, electrical, lockout/tagout, machine guarding, respiratory protection, hearing conservation, bloodborne pathogens, confined spaces, and process safety?
- Are hazard assessments and JSAs conducted, with near-miss reporting, root-cause investigation, and corrective action tracking?
- Is safety training accessible, comprehension-verified, documented, refreshed, and certified for specialized work?
- Are injury and illness records (300, 300A, 301) maintained, work-relatedness determined, severe injuries reported within deadlines, and records retained?
- Is workplace violence prevention implemented through risk assessment, controls, training, and incident response?
- Is the organization prepared for OSHA inspections with reception, walkaround, interview, document, citation, and abatement procedures?
- Are state plan variations and stricter state standards applied?
- Is the gap between written programs and field practice assessed through audits and observations?
