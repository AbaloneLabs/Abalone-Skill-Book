# Insurance Suitability Appropriateness And Disclosure

Insurance suitability, appropriateness, and disclosure obligations ensure that customers receive insurance products that meet their needs and that they understand the products they purchase. Under the EU Insurance Distribution Directive (IDD) conduct of business rules, the UK FCA Insurance Conduct of Business Sourcebook (ICOBS), the US state insurance suitability regulations for annuities and life insurance, and equivalent national frameworks, failures in suitability assessment, disclosure, or advice standards can result in customer harm, mis-selling, and regulatory enforcement. The central judgment problem is that insurance products are complex, long-term, and often sold to customers who lack the expertise to evaluate them. A product that is technically compliant with disclosure rules can still be unsuitable for the customer's needs, and disclosure that is technically complete can still fail to inform. A compliance officer who focuses on disclosure completeness without assessing customer understanding, or who treats suitability as a checkbox exercise, can leave the firm exposed to mis-selling and customer harm.

Use this skill before assessing suitability and appropriateness systems, evaluating disclosure practices, reviewing sales and advice standards, or advising on conduct of business compliance. The goal is to make the agent think about whether the customer's interests are genuinely protected, not merely whether forms have been completed. Suitability is a substantive assessment of whether the product meets the customer's needs, not a procedural step.

This skill addresses jurisdiction-specific obligations. Insurance conduct of business, suitability, and disclosure rules differ across the EU, the UK, the US state system, and national regimes. Always confirm the applicable national insurance regulation and authority guidance, and consult qualified insurance and legal counsel for specific suitability and conduct assessments. This skill does not constitute legal or insurance advice.

## Core Rules

### Assess Suitability And Appropriateness

Suitability and appropriateness assessments must determine whether the insurance product meets the customer's needs, objectives, and financial situation.

Suitability assessment elements:

- identification of the customer's demands and needs, through a structured fact-find or needs analysis;
- assessment of the customer's financial situation, including income, assets, and ability to pay premiums;
- assessment of the customer's risk profile, including risk tolerance and capacity for loss;
- evaluation of whether the product meets the identified demands and needs;
- documentation of the suitability assessment and the basis for the recommendation;
- for advised sales, a reasoned recommendation explaining why the product is suitable;
- for non-advised sales, an appropriateness assessment for complex products, evaluating whether the customer has the knowledge and experience to understand the product.

Suitability that is assessed after product selection, or that is documented to justify a pre-determined recommendation, does not protect the customer. The assessment must genuinely inform the product selection.

### Provide Clear And Fair Disclosure

Disclosure must enable customers to make informed decisions about insurance products.

Disclosure elements:

- insurance product information document (IPID), providing standardized key information about the product;
- pre-contractual disclosure of coverage, exclusions, limits, deductibles, and conditions;
- disclosure of premium, including the total cost and payment structure;
- disclosure of the insurer's identity and the intermediary's role and remuneration;
- disclosure of cancellation rights and cooling-off periods;
- disclosure in clear, plain language that the customer can understand;
- timing of disclosure, ensuring that information is provided before the customer is bound by the contract.

Disclosure that is technically complete but that is buried in fine print, uses legal jargon, or is provided after the purchase decision does not enable informed choice. The test is whether the customer understands, not whether the information was provided.

### Manage Sales Practices And Incentives

Sales practices and incentive structures must not encourage mis-selling or inappropriate recommendations.

Sales practice elements:

- incentive structures that do not reward volume or specific products at the expense of customer suitability;
- monitoring of sales outcomes for indicators of mis-selling, including cancellation rates, complaint rates, and concentration in specific products;
- controls on cross-selling and bundling, ensuring that additional products are suitable and not imposed;
- controls on replacement and churning, ensuring that policy replacements benefit the customer;
- training and competence requirements for sales staff and intermediaries;
- oversight of intermediary conduct, including monitoring of suitability assessments and disclosure;
- management of conflicts of interest, including commission disclosure and inducement restrictions.

Incentive structures that reward sales volume without regard to suitability create systemic mis-selling risk. The design of incentives is a compliance issue, not merely a commercial one.

### Ensure Fair Treatment Throughout The Product Lifecycle

Fair treatment of customers must extend throughout the product lifecycle, from design and distribution to claims and renewal.

Product lifecycle elements:

- product design and governance, ensuring that products meet customer needs and that target markets are identified;
- distribution strategy, ensuring that products reach the customers for whom they are designed;
- claims handling, ensuring that claims are handled fairly, promptly, and without unreasonable obstacles;
- renewal and cancellation practices, ensuring that renewals are fair and that customers are not locked in unfairly;
- complaints handling, ensuring that complaints are resolved fairly and promptly;
- vulnerable customer treatment, ensuring that customers in vulnerable circumstances receive appropriate support.

Fair treatment that focuses on the point of sale but ignores claims, renewals, and complaints does not protect customers throughout the relationship. Claims handling is often the point at which customers discover the limitations of their coverage.

### Manage Product Governance And Approval

Product governance ensures that products are designed for identified target markets and distributed appropriately.

Product governance elements:

- identification of the target market, including customer needs, objectives, and characteristics;
- identification of negative target markets, customers for whom the product is not suitable;
- product testing, assessing whether the product meets the target market's needs under a range of scenarios;
- distribution channel assessment, ensuring that channels can reach the target market and provide appropriate advice;
- product review, monitoring whether the product is reaching the target market and performing as expected;
- remediation when products are distributed to non-target markets or fail to perform.

Product governance that identifies a broad target market without meaningful constraints does not prevent mis-selling. The target market and negative target market must be specific enough to guide distribution.

### Address Cross-Border And Distribution Channel Risk

Cross-border distribution and multi-channel distribution create additional suitability and disclosure challenges.

Cross-border and channel elements:

- applicable jurisdiction rules, determining which conduct of business rules apply to cross-border sales;
- language and currency considerations, ensuring that disclosure is understandable in the customer's jurisdiction;
- intermediary oversight across jurisdictions, ensuring that intermediaries meet applicable standards;
- digital and online distribution, ensuring that online sales processes meet suitability and disclosure requirements;
- comparison websites and aggregators, ensuring that presentation is fair and not misleading.

Cross-border and digital distribution can reach customers in jurisdictions where the product is not designed to be sold, creating suitability and regulatory risks.

## Common Traps

### Suitability As A Checkbox Exercise

Assessments that are completed to justify a pre-determined recommendation, rather than to inform product selection, do not protect customers.

### Disclosure That Is Complete But Not Understood

Disclosure that is technically complete but buried in fine print or legal jargon does not enable informed choice.

### Incentive Structures That Encourage Mis-Selling

Incentives that reward volume or specific products without regard to suitability create systemic mis-selling risk.

### Fair Treatment Focused On Point Of Sale Only

Fair treatment that ignores claims handling, renewals, and complaints does not protect customers throughout the relationship.

### Broad Target Markets Without Constraints

Product governance that identifies a broad target market without meaningful negative target markets does not prevent mis-selling.

### Claims Handling As An Afterthought

Claims handling that is not subject to the same conduct standards as sales creates customer harm and reputational risk.

### Cross-Border Distribution Without Local Assessment

Distribution that reaches customers in jurisdictions where the product is not designed to be sold creates regulatory and suitability risks.

## Self-Check

- Does the suitability assessment genuinely inform product selection, identifying customer demands, needs, financial situation, and risk profile?
- Is disclosure clear, fair, and provided in time to enable informed decision, using plain language the customer can understand?
- Are sales practices and incentive structures designed to avoid mis-selling, with monitoring of cancellation, complaint, and concentration indicators?
- Does fair treatment extend throughout the product lifecycle, including claims handling, renewals, complaints, and vulnerable customer support?
- Is product governance specific about target markets and negative target markets, with distribution channel assessment and ongoing review?
- Are intermediary conduct and conflicts of interest managed, including commission disclosure and inducement restrictions?
- Is cross-border and digital distribution assessed for applicable jurisdiction rules, language, and suitability requirements?
- Is the suitability and conduct assessment confirmed against the applicable national insurance regulation and authority guidance with qualified counsel, rather than a generic standard?
