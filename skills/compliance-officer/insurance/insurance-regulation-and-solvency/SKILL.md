# Insurance Regulation And Solvency

Insurance is governed by a state-based regime that controls who may transact insurance, how policies are priced and designed, how insurers remain solvent, and how they treat policyholders. The defining feature is that insurance regulation is primarily state-based, with each state licensing insurers, approving rates and forms, requiring reserves and capital, and conducting market conduct examinations. The central difficulty is that an insurer operates under dozens of state regimes simultaneously, and the rules differ in licensing, rate approval, form filing, reserves, and market conduct. Compliance failure can mean license revocation, solvency intervention, restitution, and in insolvency, policyholder protection proceedings.

Use this skill before advising on an insurance product, reviewing licensing, evaluating solvency and reserves, assessing rate and form compliance, or planning a holding company transaction. The goal is to make the agent identify the line of business, the licensing status, the rate and form requirements, the solvency controls, and the market conduct posture before concluding compliance.

## Core Rules

### Secure Licensing For Each Line And State

Transacting insurance requires a license, and the license is line- and state-specific.

For each insurer:

- identify the lines of business, such as life, health, property, casualty, title, and surplus lines;
- obtain a certificate of authority in each state where business is written;
- meet capital, surplus, and deposit requirements;
- maintain admitted versus surplus lines status and the conditions for each;
- license producers and agents appropriately;
- address eligibility for placement on state guarantee funds;
- comply with alien insurer requirements for foreign-domiciled insurers.

Transacting insurance without a certificate of authority is unlicensed insurance, which can void coverage and trigger penalties. Surplus lines have different placement rules and protections.

### Meet Rate And Form Filing Requirements

Rates and policy forms may require filing and approval or filing and use, depending on the line and state.

Assess:

- whether the line requires prior approval, file-and-use, use-and-file, or no filing;
- rate justification and actuarial support;
- form compliance with required and prohibited provisions;
- consumer disclosure and readability requirements;
- rate classification and nondiscrimination rules;
- territorial and risk-based pricing limits;
- flex band and competitive market exemptions;
- filing of endorsements, amendments, and renewals.

Rate regulation balances competition with consumer protection. Rates must not be excessive, inadequate, or unfairly discriminatory.

### Maintain Solvency Through Reserves And Capital

Insurer solvency protects policyholders and is the core of insurance regulation.

Implement:

- statutory accounting and reporting;
- reserve requirements for unearned premium, loss, and loss adjustment;
- risk-based capital requirements and action levels;
- investment limitations and asset adequacy;
- reinsurance and risk transfer;
- annual statement filing and examination;
- financial analysis and early warning triggers;
- holding company and affiliate transaction restrictions;
- enterprise risk reporting.

Risk-based capital ratios trigger regulatory action at defined thresholds. Insurers must monitor and respond to declining ratios before intervention.

### Manage Reinsurance And Risk Transfer

Reinsurance is central to insurer risk management and is itself regulated.

Assess:

- reinsurer licensing and accreditation;
- credit for reinsurance on the statutory statement;
- collateral requirements for non-admitted reinsurers;
- risk transfer analysis for reinsurance agreements;
- affiliate reinsurance and avoidance of circular transactions;
- finite reinsurance and its accounting limits;
- run-off and commutation obligations;
- international reinsurance and cross-border recognition.

Credit for reinsurance depends on the reinsurer's status and collateral. Transactions that transfer little risk but improve accounting can be improper.

### Comply With Market Conduct And Claims Handling

Market conduct rules govern how insurers sell, underwrite, and settle claims.

Cover:

- underwriting standards and prohibited factors;
- cancellation, nonrenewal, and notice requirements;
- claims handling timeliness and fairness;
- unfair claims settlement practices prohibitions;
- advertising and sales practices;
- producer compensation and conflicts;
- consumer complaint handling and reporting;
- market conduct examination readiness.

Unfair claims practices, such as unreasonable delays, lowball settlements, and misrepresentation of policy terms, are enforcement priorities.

### Control Holding Company And Affiliate Transactions

Insurance holding companies face restrictions on affiliate transactions to protect insurer solvency.

Assess:

- registration of insurance holding company systems;
- prior notice and approval of certain acquisitions and divestitures;
- affiliate transactions including reinsurance, investments, and service agreements;
- dividend and extraordinary dividend limitations;
- cost sharing and transfer pricing;
- prohibition on harmful affiliate transactions;
- enterprise risk reporting from the insurer to the group.

Dividends above thresholds require notice or approval. Affiliate transactions must be fair and not harmful to the insurer's policyholders.

### Address Unclaimed Property And Escheat

Insurers must report and remit unclaimed property, including unclaimed life insurance proceeds.

Implement:

- obligation to identify unclaimed policies and benefits;
- death master file and other matching for deceased policyholders;
- reporting and remittance of unclaimed property to states;
- recordkeeping and due diligence;
- coordination with state unclaimed property audits.

Failure to use available death data to identify and pay life insurance claims has been a major enforcement area.

### Meet Producer And Distribution Compliance

Insurance producers and distributors have licensing and conduct obligations.

Cover:

- producer licensing for lines and states;
- appointment by insurers;
- continuing education;
- compensation disclosure where required;
- rebating and inducement prohibitions;
- suitability for certain products;
- antibribery and anti-corruption in international insurance.

Rebating, or giving inducements not specified in the policy, is prohibited in most states. Suitability applies to life and annuity products.

## Common Traps

### Transacting Insurance Without A Certificate Of Authority

Unlicensed insurance can void coverage and trigger penalties. Surplus lines placement has specific rules.

### Rate And Form Changes Without Required Filing

Lines differ in filing requirements. Deploying a rate or form without the required filing or approval is a violation.

### Reserves Inadequate Or Unsupported

Reserves must be actuarially supported. Inadequate reserves threaten solvency and trigger intervention.

### Reinsurance Credit Taken Without Proper Risk Transfer

Credit for reinsurance requires genuine risk transfer. Transactions that improve accounting without transferring risk can be improper.

### Unfair Claims Settlement Practices

Delays, lowball offers, and misrepresentation are unfair claims practices and enforcement priorities.

### Dividends Paid Without Required Notice Or Approval

Dividends above thresholds require notice or approval. Paying without it violates holding company rules.

### Unclaimed Life Insurance Not Matched To Death Data

Failure to match policies to death data and pay or escheat benefits is a major enforcement area.

## Self-Check

- Is the insurer licensed for each line and state with certificates of authority, capital, surplus, deposit, admitted or surplus lines status, and producer licensing?
- Are rate and form filings made according to the applicable method, with actuarial support, required provisions, readability, and nondiscrimination?
- Are statutory accounting, reserves, risk-based capital, investments, annual statements, examination, and early warning monitoring maintained?
- Is reinsurance licensed, accredited, collateralized, supported by genuine risk transfer, and free of improper affiliate circularity?
- Are market conduct, underwriting, cancellation, claims handling, unfair practices, advertising, producer compensation, and complaint handling compliant?
- Are holding company registration, affiliate transactions, dividend limitations, cost sharing, and enterprise risk reporting addressed?
- Is unclaimed property identified, matched to death data, reported, and remitted with due diligence?
- Are producer licensing, appointment, education, compensation disclosure, rebating, suitability, and antibribery obligations met?
- Are risk-based capital action levels monitored and responded to before intervention?
- Is the distinction between admitted and surplus lines reflected in placement and consumer protection?
