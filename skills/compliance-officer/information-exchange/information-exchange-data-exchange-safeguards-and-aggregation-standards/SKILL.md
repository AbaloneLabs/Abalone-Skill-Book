# Information Exchange Data Exchange Safeguards And Aggregation Standards

Data exchange safeguards are the structural and procedural controls that make competitor information exchanges compatible with antitrust law. Even when the business purpose is legitimate, the specific design of aggregation thresholds, anonymization, historical lag, and intermediary arrangements determines whether an exchange reduces antitrust risk or creates it. Under the US Sherman Act Section 1, the EU Treaty Article 101, and equivalent national competition laws, exchanges that are insufficiently aggregated, that allow identification of individual competitors, or that share current or future-oriented data can constitute unlawful coordination. The central judgment problem is that a data exchange program can be superficially compliant, with a third-party consultant and aggregated reports, while still allowing participants to identify individual competitor data through triangulation, small participant counts, or insufficient historical lag. A compliance officer who approves a benchmarking program based on its structure without stress-testing its aggregation standards can leave the firm exposed to coordination risk.

Use this skill before designing data exchange safeguards, establishing aggregation standards, reviewing clean room arrangements, or evaluating statistical disclosure control. The goal is to make the agent think about how data can be re-identified, what thresholds genuinely prevent identification, and the decisions that are easy to make too casually. An aggregation standard that looks robust on paper can fail in practice if participants can triangulate or if the intermediary does not enforce information barriers.

This skill addresses jurisdiction-specific obligations. Aggregation standards, safe harbors, and intermediary requirements differ across the US, the EU, and national regimes. Always confirm the applicable national competition law and authority guidance, and consult qualified antitrust or legal counsel for specific data exchange safeguard assessments.

## Core Rules

### Establish Aggregation Thresholds That Prevent Identification

Aggregation thresholds determine whether individual company data can be identified from an aggregated report. Thresholds must be set to genuinely prevent identification, not merely to create the appearance of aggregation.

Aggregation threshold elements:

- require a minimum number of contributors to each data point, typically at least five, so that no single company's data dominates;
- ensure that no single contributor represents more than a defined percentage, typically 25 percent or less, of the aggregated total;
- suppress data cells where the minimum contributor count or dominance threshold is not met;
- apply thresholds at the level of granularity at which data is reported, not just at the total level;
- review sub-categories and breakouts for identification risk, because a report that is aggregated at the top level may reveal individual data in sub-categories.

The firm should require the intermediary to certify that aggregation thresholds are met and that suppressed cells are not disclosed.

### Design Historical Lag Requirements

The historical lag between data collection and reporting affects whether exchanged data reveals current competitive strategies. Short lags enable identification of current behavior, while longer lags reduce this risk.

Historical lag considerations:

- pricing data should have a lag of at least one year, and preferably longer, to ensure it does not reveal current pricing strategies;
- cost and margin data should have a lag appropriate to the industry's pricing cycle;
- output and capacity data may have shorter lags if less strategically sensitive, but should still be historical, not current or future;
- future-oriented data, including planned price changes, investment intentions, or entry plans, should never be exchanged;
- the lag should account for the industry's strategic planning cycle, because data that is historical in a fast-moving industry may still reveal current strategies.

The EU case law and authority guidance have emphasized that data must be sufficiently old to prevent identification of current strategies, and that the appropriate lag depends on market characteristics.

### Evaluate Clean Room And Intermediary Arrangements

Clean room arrangements, where an independent intermediary collects, processes, and reports data without disclosing individual company information, are a primary safeguard. The intermediary must be genuinely independent and must enforce information barriers.

Clean room and intermediary requirements:

- the intermediary must be independent of all participants, with no commercial interest in the data or its exchange;
- the intermediary must collect data directly from participants and not through a participant or trade association staff;
- the intermediary must enforce strict information barriers, preventing any participant from accessing individual company data;
- the intermediary must apply aggregation and suppression standards before reporting;
- the intermediary must certify compliance with safeguards and document its methodology;
- the intermediary must destroy or retain individual data according to defined protocols, not share it.

An intermediary that is not genuinely independent, that shares individual data, or that does not enforce barriers does not create a safe exchange. The use of a trade association staff member as intermediary is particularly risky.

### Assess Re-Identification And Triangulation Risk

Even with aggregation and intermediaries, data can be re-identified through triangulation, where participants combine exchanged data with their own knowledge or other sources to identify individual competitors.

Re-identification risk factors:

- small participant counts that allow process-of-elimination identification;
- detailed breakouts or sub-categories that reveal individual data;
- data combined with public information, such as market share or revenue figures, that enables identification;
- time series data that reveals trends attributable to individual companies;
- complementary exchanges across multiple programs that collectively enable identification.

The firm should assess re-identification risk by attempting to identify competitors from sample reports, and should require the intermediary to conduct similar assessments.

### Control Data Granularity And Reporting Categories

The granularity at which data is collected and reported affects identification risk. Fine-grained data carries greater risk than aggregated data.

Granularity controls:

- limit the number of breakouts and sub-categories in reports;
- avoid reporting data at the product, region, or customer level if it enables identification;
- aggregate across dimensions, such as combining regions or products, to increase contributor counts;
- suppress breakouts that do not meet aggregation thresholds;
- avoid reporting ranges or distributions that enable identification of outliers.

The firm should review reporting templates for granularity risk and require the intermediary to suppress fine-grained breakouts.

### Establish Participant Obligations And Safeguards

Participants in data exchange programs must understand and comply with their obligations, including providing accurate data and not attempting to identify competitors.

Participant obligation elements:

- participants must provide accurate and complete data to the intermediary;
- participants must not attempt to identify individual competitors from reports;
- participants must not use reports to facilitate coordination with competitors;
- participants must maintain confidentiality of reports and not share them outside authorized personnel;
- participants must report any identification or coordination attempts to the intermediary and to compliance.

The program should include written participant agreements that codify these obligations and provide for termination of participation for violations.

### Document The Safeguard Framework And Review Cycle

The safeguard framework for each data exchange program should be documented and reviewed periodically to ensure continued effectiveness.

Documentation elements:

- the business purpose and legitimate justification for the exchange;
- the aggregation thresholds, historical lag, and intermediary arrangements;
- the re-identification and triangulation risk assessment;
- the participant obligations and agreements;
- antitrust counsel review and approval;
- periodic review, typically annual, of the safeguard framework and any modifications.

Documentation that the program was designed with safeguards and reviewed periodically is critical to defending against allegations of unlawful exchange.

## Common Traps

### Aggregation Only At The Top Level

Aggregation that meets thresholds at the total level but reveals individual data in sub-categories or breakouts does not prevent identification.

### Insufficient Historical Lag For The Industry Cycle

Data that is historical in a slow-moving industry may reveal current strategies in a fast-moving industry. The lag must account for the strategic cycle.

### Intermediary That Is Not Genuinely Independent

A trade association staff member or consultant with commercial interests in the data does not create a safe exchange.

### Triangulation Through Multiple Programs

Participants who combine data from multiple exchange programs can identify individual competitors even when each program is individually aggregated.

### Future-Oriented Data In Surveys

Surveys that collect planned price changes, investment intentions, or entry plans facilitate coordination and are treated as restrictions by object.

### No Re-Identification Testing

Aggregation standards that are not stress-tested against actual reports can fail to prevent identification in practice.

### Assuming A Consultant Guarantees Safety

A consultant who collects and passes data without genuine aggregation and barriers does not create a safe exchange.

## Self-Check

- Do aggregation thresholds require a minimum contributor count, typically five or more, and a maximum dominance percentage, typically 25 percent or less, at every reporting level?
- Is historical lag set to at least one year for pricing data and appropriate to the industry's strategic cycle for other data types?
- Is the intermediary genuinely independent, collecting data directly, enforcing information barriers, and certifying compliance?
- Is re-identification and triangulation risk assessed by attempting to identify competitors from sample reports?
- Is data granularity controlled through limited breakouts, suppression of fine-grained categories, and avoidance of ranges that reveal outliers?
- Are participant obligations codified in written agreements, including accuracy, non-identification, non-coordination, and confidentiality?
- Is future-oriented data, including planned prices, investments, and entry plans, excluded from all exchange programs?
- Is the safeguard framework documented with business purpose, thresholds, intermediary arrangements, risk assessment, and counsel review?
- Is the program reviewed periodically, typically annually, to ensure continued safeguard effectiveness?
- Is the data exchange safeguard assessment confirmed against the applicable national competition law and authority guidance rather than a generic standard?
