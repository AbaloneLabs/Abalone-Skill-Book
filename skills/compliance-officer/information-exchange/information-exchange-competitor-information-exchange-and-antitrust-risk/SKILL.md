# Information Exchange Competitor Information Exchange And Antitrust Risk

Information exchange between competitors, even of seemingly innocuous business data, can facilitate coordination and violate antitrust law. Under the US Sherman Act Section 1, the EU Treaty Article 101, and equivalent national competition laws, exchanges of competitively sensitive information, particularly on pricing, output, capacity, costs, or future plans, can constitute a restriction by object or an appreciable restriction of competition. The central judgment problem is that legitimate business needs for market intelligence, benchmarking, and industry analysis often require obtaining information from or about competitors, and the line between lawful information gathering and unlawful exchange is not always clear. A firm that participates in industry surveys, hires consultants to gather competitive intelligence, or exchanges data through trade associations can cross into antitrust violation without recognizing the risk.

Use this skill before reviewing competitor information exchanges, benchmarking and survey participation, competitive intelligence gathering, or industry data sharing. The goal is to make the agent think about what information is competitively sensitive, what exchange mechanisms are permissible, and the decisions that are easy to make too casually. An information exchange that serves a legitimate business purpose can still violate antitrust law if it facilitates coordination or reduces independent competitive decision-making.

This skill addresses jurisdiction-specific obligations. Information exchange standards, safe harbors, and enforcement approaches differ across the US, the EU, and national regimes. Always confirm the applicable national competition law and authority guidance, and consult qualified antitrust or legal counsel for specific information exchange assessments.

## Core Rules

### Identify Competitively Sensitive Information

The first step in managing information exchange risk is identifying what information is competitively sensitive and therefore carries the greatest antitrust risk if exchanged.

Competitively sensitive information categories:

- prices, including current, future, list, transaction, and discount prices;
- costs, including production, input, and operational costs;
- output and production volumes, capacity, and utilization rates;
- future business plans, including expansion, contraction, entry, or exit plans;
- customer information, including customer lists, terms, and allocation;
- profit margins and profitability data;
- bid and tender information, including pricing and strategy.

Exchanges of this information between competitors carry the greatest risk and are treated as restrictions by object in many jurisdictions, meaning they are presumed illegal without detailed competitive analysis.

### Understand Lawful Versus Unlawful Exchange Mechanisms

The mechanism of information exchange affects the antitrust analysis. Some mechanisms are permissible, while others facilitate coordination.

Lawful exchange mechanisms:

- exchanges of genuinely historical data, typically more than one year old, that does not enable identification of current strategies;
- exchanges through independent third parties, such as certified consultants or trade associations, that aggregate and anonymize data;
- public information, such as published annual reports, press releases, and regulatory filings;
- exchanges with aggregate, non-company-specific data that prevents identification of individual competitor strategies;
- exchanges subject to sufficient aggregation thresholds, typically requiring a minimum number of contributors and masking of individual data points.

Unlawful exchange mechanisms:

- direct exchanges between competitors of current or future competitively sensitive data;
- exchanges through intermediaries that do not genuinely aggregate or anonymize;
- exchanges that allow identification of individual company data despite aggregation;
- exchanges of individualized data even if described as historical;
- exchanges combined with social interaction or discussions that facilitate coordination.

### Assess Benchmarking And Survey Participation

Industry benchmarking and surveys are common business practices that can be lawful if structured properly but carry antitrust risk if not.

Benchmarking and survey compliance elements:

- use an independent third party to collect, aggregate, and report data;
- ensure the third party does not disclose individual company data to participants;
- require a minimum number of participants, typically at least five, so that individual data cannot be identified;
- limit data to historical periods, typically more than one year old for pricing data;
- ensure aggregated reports do not allow triangulation to identify individual company data;
- avoid surveys that collect future-oriented data, such as planned price changes or investment intentions.

The firm should review each benchmarking or survey program against these criteria before participating and should obtain antitrust counsel review for new programs.

### Evaluate Competitive Intelligence Gathering

Competitive intelligence gathering, where the firm collects information about competitors from public and legitimate sources, is generally lawful but must be conducted carefully.

Competitive intelligence compliance elements:

- rely on public sources, such as annual reports, press releases, regulatory filings, and published interviews;
- use legitimate market research from independent firms;
- avoid obtaining competitor information through improper means, such as misrepresentation, theft, or breach of confidentiality;
- avoid obtaining current pricing or strategy information from customers or suppliers in a way that facilitates coordination;
- document the source of all competitive intelligence;
- train employees on the distinction between lawful intelligence gathering and unlawful exchange.

Competitive intelligence that is obtained improperly or that includes current competitively sensitive information from indirect sources can create antitrust risk.

### Review Trade Association And Industry Forum Exchanges

Trade associations and industry forums are settings where information exchanges can occur and create antitrust risk.

Trade association exchange controls:

- ensure the association has written antitrust compliance guidelines;
- use independent staff to collect and aggregate any statistical programs;
- require minimum participation thresholds and historical data for statistical programs;
- prohibit discussion of pricing, customers, costs, or future plans in meetings;
- require agendas that exclude competitively sensitive topics;
- maintain minutes that document compliance and any incidents;
- require walkout and reporting protocols if improper discussions occur.

Trade associations serve legitimate purposes but are also frequent settings for unlawful information exchanges and coordination.

### Assess Hub-And-Spoke Risk In Common Suppliers And Customers

Information exchanges can occur indirectly through common suppliers or customers, creating hub-and-spoke coordination risk.

Hub-and-spoke risk factors:

- a common supplier or customer that shares competitor pricing or strategy information;
- discussions with a common intermediary about competitor behavior;
- responses to intermediary questions that reveal competitively sensitive information;
- patterns where a common intermediary facilitates parallel conduct.

The firm should train employees to recognize and reject attempts by suppliers or customers to share competitor information and to report such attempts.

### Document Legitimate Purpose And Information Barriers

Information exchanges and intelligence gathering should be documented with legitimate business purposes and information barriers.

Documentation elements:

- the legitimate business purpose for the exchange or intelligence gathering;
- the sources used and their legitimacy;
- the aggregation, anonymization, or historical nature of data received;
- the information barriers between intelligence gathering and pricing or strategy decisions;
- antitrust counsel review for new or significant programs.

Documentation that the firm relied on legitimate sources and mechanisms, and that competitively sensitive information was not used for coordination, is critical to defending against allegations.

## Common Traps

### Exchanging Current Or Future Pricing Information

Exchanges of current or future pricing data between competitors are treated as restrictions by object and are presumed illegal.

### Insufficient Aggregation In Benchmarking Programs

Benchmarking programs that allow identification of individual company data through triangulation or insufficient participants create exchange risk.

### Consultant As A Mere Conduit

Using a consultant who collects and passes individual company data without genuine aggregation does not create a safe exchange.

### Obtaining Competitor Pricing From Customers

Obtaining current competitor pricing from shared customers can facilitate coordination and create antitrust risk.

### Trade Association Meetings Without Controls

Trade association meetings without agendas, minutes, and antitrust guidelines are frequent settings for unlawful exchanges.

### Historical Data That Enables Current Strategy Identification

Historical data that is recent enough or detailed enough to enable identification of current strategies carries exchange risk.

### Assuming Public Source Means Lawful Exchange

Public information can be gathered lawfully, but exchanging it between competitors in a way that facilitates coordination can still create risk.

## Self-Check

- Is competitively sensitive information, including pricing, costs, output, plans, customers, and margins, identified and protected from exchange?
- Are exchange mechanisms assessed for lawfulness, including historical data, independent third parties, aggregation thresholds, and anonymization?
- Are benchmarking and survey programs reviewed for independent collection, minimum participants, historical data, and prevention of individual identification?
- Is competitive intelligence gathered from public and legitimate sources, with proper documentation and employee training?
- Are trade association exchanges controlled through guidelines, independent staff, minimum thresholds, agendas, minutes, and walkout protocols?
- Is hub-and-spoke risk through common suppliers and customers assessed, with training to recognize and reject improper information sharing?
- Are information exchanges and intelligence gathering documented with legitimate purposes, sources, and information barriers?
- Is antitrust counsel review obtained for new or significant benchmarking, survey, or intelligence programs?
- Is the information exchange assessment confirmed against the applicable national competition law and authority guidance rather than a generic standard?
- Are employees trained on the distinction between lawful intelligence gathering and unlawful exchange, including indirect channels?
