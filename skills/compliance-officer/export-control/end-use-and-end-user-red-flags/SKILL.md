# End-Use And End-User Red Flags

Export controls are not only about what is exported and where; they are about who receives it and what they will do with it. The EAR imposes end-use and end-user controls that operate independently of item classification and destination. An EAR99 item shipped to an open country still requires a license, or is prohibited entirely, if the end-use is military, intelligence, or weapons of mass destruction related, or if the end-user appears on a restricted-party list. These controls fail most often not because the lists were missed, but because the red flags in the transaction were ignored. A customer who refuses normal information, an order inconsistent with their business, or routing through a known transshipment hub are signals that demand inquiry before shipment.

Use this skill before clearing an export, evaluating end-use and end-user risk, screening against restricted-party lists, or advising on know-your-customer (KYC) diligence for exports. The compliance officer must treat red flags as triggers for inquiry, not as curiosities, and must know when the duty to inquire becomes a duty to refrain.

## Core Rules

### Apply End-Use Controls Independent Of Classification

End-use rules prohibit or license exports based on the intended use, regardless of the ECCN or the destination.

- Military end-use rules restrict exports for ultimate military use to certain destinations, including integration into military items.
- Intelligence end-use rules restrict exports for use by intelligence organizations.
- WMD end-use rules prohibit exports known to be intended for nuclear, chemical, or biological weapons, or missile delivery systems.
- Certain semiconductor, supercomputer, and quantum controls restrict specific end-uses regardless of destination.
- Certain aircraft and vessel controls restrict military end-uses.

A correct ECCN does not resolve end-use exposure. Assess end-use on every export.

### Screen Against All Restricted-Party Lists

Restricted-party lists impose license requirements, presumptions of denial, or prohibitions independent of classification and destination.

- Entity List: parties for which there is reasonable cause to believe involvement in activities contrary to US national security or foreign policy; license requirements and review policies are entity-specific.
- Unverified List: parties whose bona fides BIS could not verify; triggers a requirement for a statement of unreliability and due diligence.
- Military End-User (MEU) List: parties representing elevated risk of military end-use in specified countries.
- Denied Persons List: parties whose export privileges have been denied; no exports may be made to them.
- OFAC SDN and sanctions lists, and State Department debarred parties.

Screen every party to the transaction: buyer, intermediate consignee, ultimate consignee, end-user, freight forwarder, and bank. A clean buyer with a listed end-user is a prohibited export.

### Recognize And Act On Red Flag Indicators

Red flags are indicators that the transaction may be improper. The EAR expects exporters to inquire when red flags appear and to refrain if they cannot be resolved.

Customer and order red flags:

- the customer or its address is similar to a listed party;
- the customer is reluctant to offer information about the end-use;
- the product capability does not fit the customer's line of business;
- the customer has little or no business background;
- the customer is willing to pay cash for a sensitive item;
- the customer is unfamiliar with the product's performance characteristics.

Order and shipping red flags:

- the item is incompatible with the technical level of the destination country;
- the customer declines normal installation, training, or maintenance;
- delivery dates are vague or delivery is to a freight forwarder;
- routing is illogical or through a known transshipment hub;
- packaging is inconsistent with the stated routing;
- the order is for spare parts for items known to be in a restricted country.

Documentation and behavior red flags:

- the freight forwarder is located in a third country unrelated to the transaction;
- the shipping route is abnormal;
- the customer refuses routine post-shipment verification;
- a letter of credit names an unusual beneficiary;
- the transaction involves a shell company or opaque ownership.

When red flags appear, the exporter must inquire, document the response, and refrain from exporting if concerns are not resolved.

### Conduct Know-Your-Customer Diligence For Exports

Export KYC goes beyond knowing the buyer's name. It requires understanding the end-use, end-user, and routing.

- identify the ultimate end-user, not only the immediate purchaser;
- obtain and verify end-use statements for sensitive items;
- screen all parties against all relevant lists at the time of the transaction;
- assess the consistency of the order with the customer's business;
- understand the routing and any intermediate consignees;
- for distributors and resellers, understand downstream customers and controls.

### Apply The Duty To Inquire And The Duty To Refrain

The EAR imposes an affirmative obligation. When a red flag is known, the exporter must inquire and document. If the red flag cannot be resolved, the exporter must refrain from the export. Proceeding despite unresolved red flags is a basis for liability even where no list hit occurred.

### Layer End-Use, End-User, And Sanctions Controls

End-use and end-user controls interact with sanctions and embargo controls. A transaction may be cleared on classification but prohibited by an Entity List entry, a WMD end-use rule, or a sanctions prohibition. Layer all three analyses on every export.

## Common Traps

### Treating A Clean Classification As Sufficient

EAR99 or a license exception does not resolve end-use, end-user, or sanctions controls. These operate independently.

### Screening Only The Buyer

The end-user, intermediate consignee, freight forwarder, and bank must all be screened. A clean buyer with a listed end-user is prohibited.

### Ignoring Red Flags As Coincidence

Red flags are triggers for inquiry. Dismissing them as coincidence is the most common path to diversion and enforcement.

### Accepting The Order At Face Value When It Does Not Fit The Customer

An order inconsistent with the customer's business or technical level is a red flag requiring inquiry, not a windfall.

### Failing To Identify The Ultimate End-User

Selling to a distributor without understanding downstream customers hides the real end-user and the real risk.

### Overlooking Routing Red Flags

Illogical routing, freight forwarders in unrelated countries, and abnormal shipping paths are diversion indicators.

### Proceeding Despite Unresolved Red Flags

If a red flag cannot be resolved through inquiry, the export must be refrained from. Proceeding creates liability.

### Treating The Unverified List As Minor

Unverified List parties trigger due diligence and statement requirements. They signal that bona fides could not be confirmed.

## Self-Check

- Are end-use controls (military, intelligence, WMD, semiconductor, aircraft) applied independently of item classification and destination?
- Are all parties screened against the Entity List, Unverified List, Military End-User List, Denied Persons List, OFAC, and State Department debarred parties at the time of each transaction?
- Are red flag indicators recognized across customer, order, shipping, documentation, and behavior, and treated as triggers for inquiry?
- When red flags appear, is inquiry conducted, documented, and the export refrained from if concerns are unresolved?
- Is the ultimate end-user identified, not only the immediate purchaser, with end-use statements obtained and verified for sensitive items?
- Is the consistency of the order with the customer's business, technical level, and line of work assessed?
- Is routing analyzed for illogical paths, unrelated freight forwarders, and transshipment hub exposure?
- For distributors and resellers, are downstream customers and controls understood?
- Are end-use, end-user, and sanctions controls layered on every export rather than treated as alternatives?
- Is the Unverified List treated as triggering due diligence and statement requirements, not as minor?
