# Trade-Based Money Laundering

Trade-based money laundering (TBML) is the process of moving value across borders through trade transactions in order to disguise the illicit origin of funds. It is estimated to be one of the largest channels for international money laundering. FATF, FinCEN, the Wolfsberg Group, and the Egmont Group have published extensive guidance on TBML typologies and indicators. TBML exploits the legitimacy and volume of global trade: invoices, shipping documents, letters of credit, and open-account payments all look routine, which makes it easy to hide over- or under-invoicing, phantom shipments, and third-party payment structures. A financial institution that processes trade or cross-border payments without TBML controls is a conduit for laundering and, increasingly, for proliferation financing.

Use this skill before designing TBML controls, reviewing trade transactions for manipulation, assessing third-party payment structures, or managing the overlap with dual-use goods and sanctions. The goal is to make the agent think about the value transfer mechanism, the documentation that should corroborate it, and the red flags that distinguish legitimate trade from laundering. TBML is hard to detect precisely because it is dressed as ordinary commerce, so controls must look beneath the documents.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Value Transfer Mechanism

TBML works by manipulating the price, quantity, or existence of goods so that the payment does not reflect true value. The excess or shortfall moves value between the buyer and seller outside the apparent trade.

Mechanisms:

- over-invoicing: the buyer pays more than the goods are worth, moving excess value to the seller;
- under-invoicing: the buyer pays less than the goods are worth, moving value to the buyer on resale;
- over- or under-shipment: quantities or descriptions do not match what was actually shipped;
- phantom shipments: no goods move at all, only payments and fabricated documents;
- multiple invoicing: the same invoice is financed or paid several times;
- circular trade: goods and payments cycle between related parties to create movement without economic purpose.

The key question is whether the payment reflects the genuine value and movement of goods.

### Compare Invoice Value To Market Value

The most direct TBML test is whether the invoiced price is plausible for the goods and market. Large deviations indicate value transfer.

Checks:

- compare unit prices to market or reference prices for the commodity;
- flag goods invoiced far above or below comparable transactions;
- watch for luxury or high-value goods with unusually low invoices;
- watch for low-value goods with unusually high invoices;
- consider the quality, age, and origin of goods relative to price.

Reference price databases and commodity benchmarks help, but judgment is required for specialized goods. Document the rationale for accepting outliers.

### Verify The Goods And The Shipment

Phantom shipments and mis-described goods are core TBML methods. Documentation should corroborate that goods exist and match the invoice.

Corroboration:

- bills of lading, packing lists, and customs documents consistent with the invoice;
- shipping routes and ports consistent with the buyer, seller, and goods;
- vessel and container tracking where feasible;
- insurance and inspection certificates proportionate to value;
- goods consistent with the stated business of the parties.

Discrepancies between documents, or documents that appear fabricated, recycled, or templated, are red flags.

### Scrutinize Third-Party Payments

In legitimate trade, the buyer pays the seller. Third-party payments, where an unrelated party pays or receives funds on behalf of a buyer or seller, are a strong TBML indicator unless there is a clear commercial reason.

Third-party patterns:

- payments from or to parties with no apparent connection to the trade;
- payments routed through multiple intermediaries;
- use of money service businesses or cash-intensive channels to settle trade;
- payments from jurisdictions unrelated to either party;
- offset or barter arrangements that obscure the money trail.

Require a documented commercial rationale for third-party payments and assess the third party's relationship to the trade.

### Assess The Parties For Substance And Relatedness

TBML often runs between related parties disguised as independent trade. Shell companies and front companies appear as buyers, sellers, or intermediaries.

Party checks:

- do the buyer and seller have genuine business operations?
- are the parties related through ownership, family, or control?
- do the parties have a history of trade in these goods?
- are the parties in jurisdictions consistent with the goods and trade?
- are nominee directors or opaque ownership structures present?

Related-party trade is not inherently illicit, but it requires heightened scrutiny because value can be transferred without an arm's-length price.

### Coordinate Trade Finance, Monitoring, And Sanctions

TBML controls must span the trade finance, transaction monitoring, and sanctions functions. A siloed approach misses the full picture.

Coordination:

- feed trade transaction data into monitoring scenarios;
- screen all parties, vessels, ports, and goods against sanctions and restricted-party lists;
- apply enhanced review to high-risk jurisdictions and dual-use goods;
- escalate discrepancies to a single investigative view;
- document decisions across functions.

The overlap with proliferation financing is significant: dual-use and controlled goods are often moved through TBML channels to evade export controls and sanctions.

### Manage Dual-Use And Proliferation Overlap

Proliferation financing, the raising and movement of funds for weapons of mass destruction programs, frequently uses TBML methods. FinCEN advisories and FATF guidance highlight front companies, third-party payments, and dual-use goods in proliferation networks.

Proliferation indicators:

- goods with potential dual-use or controlled applications;
- end-users in jurisdictions with active proliferation networks;
- front companies with vague business descriptions;
- payments inconsistent with the goods or parties;
- routing through known transshipment hubs;
- sudden changes in end-user or destination after approval.

Dual-use and controlled goods require export control coordination in addition to AML review.

## Common Traps

### Accepting Documents At Face Value

Invoices and bills of lading can be fabricated. Documents that are internally consistent do not prove the goods exist or the price is real.

### Ignoring Price Plausibility

Without comparing invoice value to market value, over- and under-invoicing go undetected. Price reasonableness is a core TBML control.

### Allowing Unexplained Third-Party Payments

Third-party payments without a commercial rationale are a hallmark of TBML. Do not process them without documented justification.

### Missing Related-Party Trade

Related parties can set any price, enabling value transfer. Identify and scrutinize related-party trade.

### Siloing Trade Finance From Monitoring

If trade finance does not feed monitoring, TBML patterns across transactions are invisible. Integrate the data.

### Overlooking Proliferation Nexus

Dual-use goods and proliferation networks use TBML channels. Export control and AML must coordinate.

### Focusing On Single Transactions

TBML runs across sequences of transactions. Single-transaction review misses the pattern.

## Self-Check

- Is the value transfer mechanism understood, with controls for over- and under-invoicing, phantom shipments, multiple invoicing, and circular trade?
- Are invoice values compared to market or reference prices, with outliers documented?
- Are goods and shipments corroborated through consistent bills of lading, customs, insurance, and tracking?
- Are third-party payments scrutinized for commercial rationale and party relationship?
- Are parties assessed for economic substance, relatedness, and opaque ownership?
- Are trade finance, transaction monitoring, and sanctions functions integrated?
- Are dual-use and controlled goods flagged for export control and proliferation review?
- Are high-risk jurisdictions and transshipment hubs subject to enhanced review?
- Are TBML patterns analyzed across sequences of transactions, not only single deals?
- Are discrepancies escalated to a single investigative view with documented decisions?
