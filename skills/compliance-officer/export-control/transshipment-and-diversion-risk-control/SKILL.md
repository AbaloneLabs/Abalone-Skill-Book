# Transshipment And Diversion Risk Control

Diversion is the deliberate rerouting of controlled items to an unauthorized end-user or destination, and it is the dominant method by which export controls are defeated. Items shipped to an open country are transshipped through an intermediary hub, relabeled, re-invoiced, and forwarded to a restricted destination or end-user. The original exporter may have a clean order, a clean classification, and a clean destination on paper, while the goods end up in a military program or a sanctioned country. Transshipment hubs, opaque freight forwarders, and layered intermediaries are the infrastructure of diversion, and the EAR expects exporters to recognize and respond to diversion risk, not merely to ship to the named consignee and stop thinking.

Use this skill before clearing a shipment, evaluating routing and intermediary risk, conducting freight forwarder due diligence, or building controls against diversion. The compliance officer must treat the routing, the intermediaries, and the documentation as evidence to be interrogated, not as logistics details to be accepted.

## Core Rules

### Understand How Transshipment Enables Diversion

Transshipment is the movement of goods through an intermediate country en route to a final destination. It is legitimate in most trade, but it is also the mechanism by which diversion occurs.

- Goods are shipped to a hub with loose controls or high trade volume;
- they are cleared, stored, and re-exported, often with new documentation;
- the original exporter sees only the first destination;
- the real end-user and final destination are concealed.

Hubs are not inherently illicit, but certain hubs have elevated diversion risk due to volume, free trade zones, re-export activity, and proximity to restricted markets.

### Identify High-Risk Transshipment Hubs

Certain jurisdictions are recognized as elevated-risk transshipment points based on enforcement data and diversion patterns.

- hubs with large free trade zones and re-export economies;
- jurisdictions adjacent to comprehensively embargoed or restricted destinations;
- hubs with histories of front-company and shell-company activity;
- ports with high container turnover and limited inspection.

Risk is dynamic. Use current intelligence from BIS, OFAC, and trade enforcement advisories to identify elevated-risk hubs, and update the assessment as patterns shift.

### Conduct Freight Forwarder And Intermediary Due Diligence

Freight forwarders, customs brokers, and trading companies are the intermediaries through which diversion is executed. Diligence on these parties is essential.

- identify the freight forwarder and any sub-forwarders;
- screen the forwarder and all intermediaries against restricted-party lists;
- assess whether the forwarder is known and reputable or newly formed and opaque;
- evaluate whether the forwarder's location is consistent with the routing;
- for trading companies and resellers, identify the ultimate end-user and the basis for the relationship;
- be alert to forwarders that decline to provide information or that change details mid-transaction.

A freight forwarder with no traceable history, located in an unrelated country, handling a sensitive item, is a red flag requiring inquiry.

### Recognize Routing And Documentation Red Flags

Diversion leaves signals in the routing and documentation. The EAR expects exporters to recognize them.

Routing red flags:

- the shipping route is illogical or unnecessarily circuitous;
- the freight forwarder is in a country unrelated to either the buyer or the destination;
- the item's capability exceeds the technical level of the destination country;
- delivery is to a freight forwarder rather than the end-user;
- the routing passes through a known transshipment hub for a restricted destination.

Documentation red flags:

- the order and shipping documents are inconsistent;
- packaging is inconsistent with the stated routing method;
- the letter of credit names an unusual beneficiary or third party;
- the invoice undervalues the goods or misdescribes them;
- the customer refuses routine post-shipment verification;
- documentation references a different end-user than the order.

When red flags appear, the exporter must inquire, document the response, and refrain if concerns are not resolved.

### Identify The Ultimate End-User, Not Only The Purchaser

Diversion relies on obscuring the real end-user. Selling to a distributor, trading company, or reseller without identifying the downstream customer hides the risk.

- require identification of the ultimate end-user for sensitive items;
- obtain end-use statements and verify consistency;
- for distributors, understand the downstream customer base and controls;
- be alert to end-users that are shell companies, recently formed, or with opaque ownership;
- cross-check the end-user's address and business against the item's technical requirements.

### Apply Post-Shipment Verification And Monitoring

Diversion can be detected after shipment through verification.

- request post-shipment verification for sensitive items, and treat refusal as a red flag;
- monitor for returns, claims, or anomalies inconsistent with the stated end-use;
- use BIS and end-use check resources where available;
- track the lifecycle of sensitive shipments to confirm delivery to the authorized end-user.

### Layer Sanctions, Entity List, And End-Use Controls

Diversion risk interacts with sanctions and end-use controls. A shipment routed through a hub to an Entity List party, a sanctioned destination, or a military end-user is prohibited regardless of the first destination. Layer all three analyses on the routing assessment.

## Common Traps

### Shipping To The Named Consignee And Stopping The Analysis

The named consignee may be a front. The real end-user and final destination are concealed behind the first leg. Analyze the full routing.

### Treating Transshipment Hubs As Low Risk By Default

Hubs are legitimate but elevated-risk. Default clearance without hub risk assessment misses diversion.

### Accepting Freight Forwarders Without Diligence

Forwarders are the diversion mechanism. An opaque or unrelated forwarder is a red flag, not a logistics detail.

### Ignoring Illogical Routing

Circuitous routing, unrelated forwarder locations, and capability-destination mismatches are diversion signals demanding inquiry.

### Selling To Distributors Without Downstream Visibility

Distributors and resellers obscure the end-user. Without downstream identification, the real risk is hidden.

### Dismissing Documentation Inconsistencies

Inconsistent orders, undervalued invoices, and misdescribed goods are deliberate diversion techniques, not clerical errors.

### Failing To Conduct Post-Shipment Verification

For sensitive items, the absence of verification allows diversion to proceed undetected. Refusal of verification is itself a red flag.

### Treating Diversion As A Logistics Problem Rather Than A Compliance Problem

Diversion is the core export control threat. It must be owned by compliance, not delegated entirely to logistics.

## Self-Check

- Is transshipment understood as the primary mechanism of diversion, with goods routed through hubs and re-documented to conceal the final destination?
- Are high-risk transshipment hubs identified using current enforcement intelligence and updated as patterns shift?
- Is freight forwarder and intermediary diligence conducted, including screening, reputation, location consistency, and willingness to provide information?
- Are routing red flags recognized, including illogical routes, unrelated forwarder locations, capability-destination mismatches, and hub transit for restricted destinations?
- Are documentation red flags recognized, including inconsistent orders, undervalued invoices, misdescribed goods, and unusual letter-of-credit beneficiaries?
- Is the ultimate end-user identified, not only the purchaser, with end-use statements obtained and verified for distributors and resellers?
- Is post-shipment verification requested for sensitive items, with refusal treated as a red flag?
- Are shell companies, recently formed entities, and opaque ownership structures treated as elevated diversion risk?
- Are sanctions, Entity List, and end-use controls layered on the routing assessment so that hub-routed shipments to restricted parties are prohibited?
- Is diversion owned by compliance as a core export control threat rather than delegated entirely to logistics?
