# Dual Use Classification And EAR ITAR Jurisdiction

Export control compliance begins with classification, and classification is where most errors originate because it is treated as a paperwork exercise rather than a legal determination with consequences. An item's classification under the Export Administration Regulations or the International Traffic in Arms Regulations determines whether it requires a license, to whom it can be exported, and what conditions attach, and getting it wrong means either exporting controlled items without required authorization or over-controlling commercial items and disrupting trade. The challenge is that classification requires interpreting technical parameters against regulatory criteria that are detailed, frequently updated, and often ambiguous for items that sit between civil and military applications. An item is not whatever the company prefers to call it; it is what the regulations make it, and a self-serving classification that the company cannot defend is worse than no classification because it creates a false assurance that drives unlicensed exports.

Use this skill before classifying an item under the EAR or ITAR, determining jurisdiction, or resolving classification uncertainty. Export control classification is a legal determination with significant penalties for error; classifications should be confirmed with qualified trade counsel or through official classification request processes such as CCATS or commodity classification. This skill provides a framework for the classification judgment, not a legal classification of any item.

## Core Rules

### Determine Jurisdiction Before Classification

The first question is not how to classify the item but which regime has jurisdiction, because the EAR and ITAR are distinct regimes with different scope, different control lists, and different consequences. ITAR, administered by the State Department Directorate of Defense Trade Controls, governs defense articles, defense services, and related technical data on the United States Munitions List. The EAR, administered by the Commerce Department Bureau of Industry and Security, governs dual-use items, commercial items with potential military application, on the Commerce Control List, and also items not specifically listed that are subject to the EAR through other criteria.

Jurisdiction turns on whether the item is predominantly military, in which case ITAR applies, or dual-use or commercial, in which case the EAR applies. The USML categories are specific and the order rule applies, meaning an item specifically designed for a military application is controlled under ITAR even if it has a civil use. An item with both military and civil applications that is not specifically described on the USML falls under the EAR. This jurisdiction determination must be made first, because classifying under the wrong regime produces a classification that is invalid regardless of its technical accuracy. When jurisdiction is unclear, the item should be treated as ITAR-controlled, the more restrictive regime, until a definitive determination is made, because an erroneous EAR classification of an ITAR item leads to unlicensed defense exports with severe consequences.

### Determine Whether The Item Is Subject To The EAR At All

Not every item is subject to the EAR, and the scope of EAR applicability is broader than the Commerce Control List. An item is subject to the EAR if it is a US-origin item, if it incorporates more than a de minimis threshold of US-origin controlled content, if it is a direct product of US-origin technology or software, or if it is located in the United States. Items that meet none of these criteria may not be subject to the EAR, but determining this requires careful analysis because the criteria, particularly the de minimis calculation and the foreign direct product rule, are complex and have been expanded significantly in recent years.

Assess EAR subjectivity through each criterion. For items incorporating US content, calculate the de minimis percentage, which varies by destination and by the classification of the US content, recognizing that certain highly controlled content counts toward the threshold at any percentage. For items produced using US technology or software, assess the foreign direct product rules, which have been expanded to reach a broad range of foreign-produced items, particularly in the semiconductor and advanced computing sectors. An item that the company believes is outside US jurisdiction may be subject to the EAR through content, production, or location, and exporting it without analysis on the assumption it is foreign is a common and serious error.

### Classify Against The Correct Control List With Technical Precision

Once jurisdiction and subjectivity are established, classification requires mapping the item's technical parameters against the relevant control list entries. Under the EAR, this means identifying the Export Control Classification Number on the Commerce Control List, which organizes items by category and product group with specific technical descriptions. Under ITAR, it means identifying the USML category and paragraph that describes the defense article. The classification must reflect the item's actual technical characteristics, not a convenient approximation, because the control parameters are often defined by precise thresholds.

Classify using the item's technical specifications, design documentation, and intended capabilities. For software and technology, assess the functionality and the level of technical detail, because controls often turn on specific performance parameters such as encryption strength, processing capability, or technical readiness level. Document the technical basis for the classification, including the specifications relied upon and how they map to the control list parameters. A classification that states an ECCN without the technical justification is an assertion, not a determination, and cannot be defended if questioned. For items where the technical parameters are ambiguous or the control list entry is unclear, use the formal commodity classification process or seek counsel rather than guessing.

### Handle Items That Are EAR99 With Care

Many commercial items are classified as EAR99, meaning they fall under the EAR but are not specifically listed on the Commerce Control List and therefore generally do not require a license for most destinations. EAR99 is not a license to export freely; it is a classification that carries no license requirement for most destinations but that remains subject to the EAR's end-use, end-user, and prohibited-conduct restrictions. An EAR99 item exported to a sanctioned party, for a prohibited end-use, or in violation of a general prohibition is still a violation regardless of the classification.

Do not treat EAR99 as a conclusion that no compliance analysis is required. Even EAR99 items require screening of the end-user and end-use, assessment of the destination against comprehensive and license-requirement frameworks, and attention to catch-all controls that impose licensing based on end-use rather than classification. The classification determines whether the item itself triggers a license; the end-use and end-user analysis determines whether the transaction triggers a license regardless of classification. An EAR99 determination without end-use and end-user screening is incomplete and exposes the export to violation through channels the classification does not address.

### Reassess Classification When Items Or Regulations Change

Classification is not permanent. Items evolve through design changes, capability upgrades, or new applications, and a classification that was correct for a prior version may be wrong for the current one. Regulations change too, through list updates, new controls on emerging technologies, and rule changes such as the expansion of foreign direct product rules. A classification made two years ago may be invalid today through changes on either side.

Establish triggers for reassessment. Reassess classification when the item's technical specifications change, when the item is modified or incorporated into a larger system, when new end-uses or applications emerge, and when regulations affecting the item's category are updated. Maintain a classification register that records the basis, the date, and the applicable regulatory version, and review classifications periodically to confirm they remain current. A static classification that is never revisited becomes stale, and a stale classification is an unvalidated assumption that may no longer reflect either the item or the law.

### Document The Classification Determination And Its Basis

A classification must be documented with the jurisdiction determination, the EAR subjectivity analysis, the technical basis for the control list mapping, and the reasoning for any judgment calls. Regulators and customs authorities reconstruct classifications from documentation, and a classification that cannot be supported by recorded analysis is indefensible regardless of whether it was correct. The documentation should be sufficient for a reviewer to understand how the classification was reached and to assess whether it remains valid.

Document not only the conclusion but the process. Record which technical specifications were relied upon, which control list entries were considered and rejected, what uncertainty existed and how it was resolved, and whether the classification was confirmed through formal processes or counsel. For items where classification was uncertain, document the basis for the resolution and any conservative assumptions applied. A classification file that contains only an ECCN or USML category with no supporting analysis provides no defense and signals that the classification was not genuinely analyzed.

## Common Traps

### Classifying Under The Wrong Regime

Determining an EAR classification for an item that is ITAR-controlled, or vice versa, producing an invalid classification regardless of technical accuracy.

### Assuming Foreign Items Are Outside The EAR

Treating a foreign-produced item as outside US jurisdiction without analyzing de minimis content, foreign direct product rules, or US-location criteria.

### Imprecise Technical Mapping

Classifying based on a convenient approximation rather than the item's actual technical parameters, missing controls defined by precise thresholds.

### Treating EAR99 As A Free Pass

Concluding that no compliance analysis is needed for EAR99 items, missing end-use, end-user, and catch-all controls that apply regardless of classification.

### Static Classification

Failing to reassess classification when items change or regulations are updated, leaving stale classifications that no longer reflect the item or the law.

### Undocumented Classification

Recording only the ECCN or USML category without the jurisdiction, subjectivity, technical, and reasoning basis, leaving the determination indefensible.

### Self-Serving Classification To Avoid Licensing

Choosing the classification that avoids license requirements rather than the one the technical parameters support, creating false assurance that drives unlicensed exports.

## Self-Check

- Is jurisdiction between ITAR and EAR determined first, with ambiguous items treated under the more restrictive ITAR regime until definitively resolved?
- Is EAR subjectivity assessed through origin, de minimis content, foreign direct product rules, and US location rather than assumed away for foreign items?
- Is the classification mapped against the correct control list using the item's actual technical parameters, with the technical basis documented?
- Are EAR99 items still subjected to end-use, end-user, and catch-all control analysis rather than treated as requiring no compliance review?
- Are classifications reassessed on triggers including item changes, new applications, and regulatory updates, with a register tracking currency?
- Is each classification documented with jurisdiction, subjectivity, technical basis, reasoning, and any formal confirmation or counsel review?
- Would a reviewer reconstructing the classification find complete analysis supporting the conclusion rather than a bare ECCN or USML category?
- Are uncertain classifications resolved through formal processes or counsel rather than guessed, with conservative assumptions where ambiguity remains?
- Is the distinction between the item's classification and the transaction's end-use and end-user controls reflected in separate analyses?
- Are regulatory changes in the item's category monitored and assessed for impact on existing classifications?
