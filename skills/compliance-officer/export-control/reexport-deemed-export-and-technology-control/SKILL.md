# Reexport Deemed Export And Technology Control

Export control is not only about shipping items across borders. Some of the most significant and most overlooked export control risks arise from transactions that do not look like exports at all, a reexport of a US-origin item from one foreign country to another, a release of controlled technology to a foreign national within the United States, or a visual inspection or technical discussion that transfers controlled technical data without any physical shipment. These are reexports and deemed exports, and they are subject to the same licensing requirements as direct exports, yet they are routinely missed because the people involved do not recognize that what they are doing constitutes an export. A research collaboration that shares controlled technology with foreign-national researchers, a foreign subsidiary that reexports US-origin components to a third country, or a facility tour that exposes controlled technical data to visitors can each be an unlicensed export with severe consequences, and none of them involves a shipping container.

Use this skill before assessing reexport authorization, evaluating deemed export risk from technology release to foreign nationals, or controlling technical data in research, collaboration, and employment. Reexport and deemed export requirements are governed by the EAR and ITAR with detailed rules; determinations should be confirmed with qualified trade counsel. This skill provides a framework for identifying and managing these risks, not a legal opinion on any specific transaction.

## Core Rules

### Recognize That A Reexport Is An Export Requiring Authorization

A reexport is the transfer of an item from one foreign country to another, and under the EAR and ITAR, reexports of US-origin items are subject to the same authorization requirements as exports from the United States. A foreign subsidiary that ships US-origin components to a customer in a third country, a foreign distributor that transfers US-origin products to another foreign party, or a foreign manufacturer that incorporates US-origin content into a product and ships it onward is conducting a reexport that may require authorization. The fact that the item is already outside the United States does not remove US export control jurisdiction.

Assess reexport requirements for any movement of US-origin items between foreign countries. The requirement depends on the item's classification, the destination of the reexport, the end-use, and the end-user, applying the same four-dimension analysis as a direct export. A foreign party that reexports US-origin items without authorization creates a violation that may implicate both the foreign party and the US principal through the reexport controls. Contracts with foreign subsidiaries, distributors, and customers must address reexport restrictions explicitly, prohibiting unauthorized reexport and retransfer, because the US principal remains connected to the item's downstream movement through its origin jurisdiction.

### Treat Technology Release To Foreign Nationals As A Deemed Export

A deemed export is the release of controlled technology or source code to a foreign national, and it is treated as an export to the person's country of nationality even though the release occurs within the United States or within a foreign facility. Sharing controlled technical data with a foreign-national employee, granting a foreign-national researcher access to controlled technology, or discussing controlled design details with a foreign-national visitor are each deemed exports to the person's country of nationality, potentially requiring authorization. The deemed export rule catches transfers that occur through employment, research, tours, and collaboration, none of which look like traditional exports.

Assess deemed export risk whenever controlled technology or source code may be accessible to foreign nationals. Identify the technology or source code that is controlled, identify the foreign nationals who may access it, and determine whether the release requires authorization based on the classification and the person's country of nationality. The analysis is complicated by the fact that a person with multiple nationalities may trigger requirements for each, and that access can occur through shared networks, shared facilities, or informal discussion rather than through formal transfer. A deemed export that is not authorized is an unlicensed export, and the most common source is employment and research contexts where technology access is granted without export control screening.

### Control Technical Data Release Through All Transfer Mechanisms

Controlled technical data can be transferred through mechanisms that do not involve documents or downloads, and these are easy to miss. A visual inspection of controlled equipment by a visitor, a technical discussion that reveals controlled design parameters, a demonstration that exposes controlled operating procedures, or access to a facility where controlled technology is visible can each constitute a release of controlled technical data requiring authorization. The transfer mechanism is irrelevant; what matters is whether controlled technical data was made accessible.

Control technical data release across all mechanisms. For physical access, assess whether facility tours, laboratory visits, or equipment inspections would expose controlled technology and require authorization or restrictions. For discussions, assess whether technical exchanges at conferences, meetings, or collaborations would reveal controlled parameters. For digital access, assess whether shared systems, networks, or repositories make controlled technology accessible to unauthorized parties. The control approach must address the full range of transfer mechanisms, because controlling documents while allowing visual or verbal access leaves the most informal and most easily missed transfer channels unprotected.

### Manage Deemed Export Risk In Employment And Research

Employment and research contexts are where deemed export risk concentrates, because these are the settings where foreign nationals most routinely access technology through the normal course of their work. Hiring a foreign-national engineer into a role involving controlled technology, admitting a foreign-national researcher into a controlled research program, or assigning a foreign-national employee to a project with controlled technical data are each deemed export decisions that may require authorization before the access is granted.

Integrate deemed export screening into hiring, research admission, and project assignment processes. Before granting technology access, identify the controlled technology involved, screen the individual's nationality against the classification's requirements, and obtain any required authorization before access is granted. This must occur before the access, not after, because a deemed export cannot be authorized retroactively. For research contexts, assess whether the research involves controlled technology and whether foreign-national participation requires authorization, recognizing that fundamental research exclusions may apply but have specific conditions that must be met. Employment and research deemed export failures are particularly damaging because they are systemic, affecting many individuals over time, and because they often surface only during audits or investigations that reconstruct access patterns.

### Address Reexport And Redelivery Risk In Contracts And Controls

The downstream movement of US-origin items after the initial export creates ongoing reexport and redelivery risk that the initial export's authorization does not cover. A customer who reexports a US-origin product, a foreign manufacturer who ships a product incorporating US-origin content to a third country, or a foreign end-user who transfers a US-origin item to another party may be conducting an unauthorized reexport that implicates the US exporter through the item's origin. Contracts and controls must address this downstream risk explicitly.

Include reexport and retransfer restrictions in contracts with foreign subsidiaries, distributors, customers, and collaborators. Prohibit unauthorized reexport and retransfer, require compliance with US export control law, and provide audit and termination rights for violations. For higher-risk relationships, monitor compliance through transaction reporting, audit, and end-use verification. The contract is necessary but not sufficient; the foreign party must understand and be capable of complying with the restrictions, and the US exporter must have visibility into downstream movement sufficient to detect unauthorized reexport. A contract that prohibits reexport but provides no mechanism to detect it relies on the foreign party's candor, which is exactly what reexport controls are designed to test.

### Distinguish Between EAR And ITAR Treatment Of Technology

The deemed export and technology release rules differ between the EAR and ITAR in ways that matter for compliance. Under the EAR, deemed exports of technology and source code are evaluated based on the foreign national's country of nationality and the technology's classification. Under ITAR, the release of technical data to a foreign person is generally treated as an export requiring authorization, and the rules are stricter, with fewer exceptions and broader coverage of what constitutes technical data. The regime that applies depends on whether the technology is EAR-controlled or ITAR-controlled, and applying the more lenient EAR framework to ITAR-controlled technical data is a serious error.

Determine the applicable regime for the technology before assessing deemed export requirements. For ITAR-controlled technical data, apply the ITAR framework with its stricter rules. For EAR-controlled technology, apply the EAR framework with its nationality-based analysis. Do not assume that the two regimes are equivalent or that compliance with one satisfies the other. A deemed export analysis that applies the wrong regime produces a determination that is invalid regardless of its analytical quality, and the consequences of an ITAR deemed export violation are particularly severe.

## Common Traps

### Missing Reexports Entirely

Failing to recognize that movement of US-origin items between foreign countries is a reexport requiring authorization, treating it as outside US jurisdiction because it does not involve the United States.

### Employment Access Without Deemed Export Screening

Granting foreign-national employees access to controlled technology through normal hiring and assignment without screening nationality against classification requirements.

### Visual And Verbal Transfer Channels Uncontrolled

Controlling documents and downloads while allowing controlled technical data exposure through facility tours, inspections, and technical discussions.

### Retroactive Deemed Export Authorization

Discovering after access was granted that a deemed export license was required and seeking to authorize it after the fact, which cannot cure the unlicensed release.

### Research Contexts Assumed Exempt

Assuming fundamental research exclusions apply without confirming their specific conditions, exposing controlled technology through foreign-national research participation.

### Downstream Reexport Left To Contract Alone

Including reexport restrictions in contracts without mechanisms to detect unauthorized reexport, relying on foreign-party candor.

### Applying EAR Framework To ITAR Technical Data

Using the more lenient EAR deemed export analysis for ITAR-controlled technical data, under-controlling releases that require the stricter ITAR framework.

## Self-Check

- Is every movement of US-origin items between foreign countries assessed as a potential reexport requiring authorization based on classification, destination, end-use, and end-user?
- Is deemed export screening integrated into hiring, research admission, and project assignment before technology access is granted to foreign nationals?
- Are all transfer mechanisms for controlled technical data controlled, including visual inspection, verbal discussion, facility access, and digital systems, not only documents?
- Is the applicable regime, EAR or ITAR, determined for the technology before the deemed export analysis, with the stricter ITAR framework applied where it governs?
- Are fundamental research exclusions confirmed against their specific conditions rather than assumed, before relying on them for foreign-national research participation?
- Do contracts with foreign subsidiaries, distributors, customers, and collaborators address reexport and retransfer restrictions with detection mechanisms, not only prohibitions?
- Is deemed export authorization obtained before access rather than sought retroactively after an unlicensed release?
- Are multi-nationality individuals assessed against each nationality's requirements rather than only one?
- Would the company detect an unauthorized reexport by a foreign party, or would it rely solely on the party's contractual representation?
- Is the distinction between physical export, reexport, and deemed export reflected in distinct controls that each catch their respective risks?
