# Military End-Use And MEU End-User Rules

The EAR's military end-use and military end-user (MEU) rules impose license requirements that reach far beyond the Entity List. For specified countries, currently including China, Russia, and Venezuela, the export, reexport, or transfer of items that will be used for a military end-use or to a military end-user requires a license, regardless of whether the item is controlled for the destination on the country chart. These rules have expanded repeatedly: the definition of military end-use has broadened, the list of covered items has grown, the Military End-User List has been published and supplemented, and separate military-intelligence end-use and end-user rules have layered additional prohibitions. A compliance program that treats military controls as a narrow, ITAR-only concern will miss this entire regime.

Use this skill before clearing an export to China, Russia, or Venezuela, evaluating whether an end-use is military, assessing MEU List exposure, applying the expanded semiconductor and military-intelligence controls, or determining whether a license is required. The compliance officer must analyze the end-use definition, the covered item scope, and the end-user status together.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Countries Subject To MEU Rules

The military end-use and end-user rules apply to specified destinations. Confirm which countries are in scope at the time of analysis, as the list has expanded.

- China, Russia, and Venezuela are the principal destinations subject to the MEU rules under EAR section 744.21.
- The rules apply to exports, reexports, and in-country transfers to these destinations.
- Additional country-specific military and intelligence end-use rules apply to other destinations, including the comprehensive restrictions on Russia and Belarus.
- The scope of covered countries changes; verify the current rule.

Do not assume MEU rules apply only to embargoed destinations. China remains open for many exports but subject to MEU controls.

### Apply The Military End-Use Definition

The definition of military end-use is broad and has been expanded. Assess the intended use against the current definition.

- Integration, development, production, testing, or maintenance of military items;
- integration of items into military items;
- use in the design, development, production, or use of items by military organizations;
- certain operations, installations, or activities of military organizations;
- certain support to defense intelligence organizations.

The definition reaches more than building weapons. Supplying components that will be integrated into a military system, or providing items to a military-affiliated research institute, can be a military end-use.

### Identify Military End-Users

A military end-user includes more than uniformed armed forces. The definition captures a range of actors.

- national armed services, national guards, and police with military functions;
- government intelligence organizations;
- entities identified on the Military End-User List;
- any person whose actions or functions are intended to support military end-uses.

The MEU List is a non-exhaustive indicator. An entity not on the list can still be a military end-user if it meets the definition. Conversely, listing is strong evidence of MEU status.

### Determine The Covered Item Scope

MEU rules apply to a defined set of items, not to all EAR items. Identify whether the item is within scope.

- Items identified by ECCN with a reason for control of NS, AT, MT, or CC for the destination;
- certain items in categories across the CCL specified in the rule;
- EAR99 items are generally not covered by the MEU rule, though other end-use rules and the foreign direct product rule can reach them.

Confirm the item is within the MEU item scope before concluding the rule applies. An EAR99 item may not be MEU-controlled but may be caught by other rules.

### Apply The License Requirement And Review Policy

Where the MEU rule applies, a license is required.

- The review policy is generally a presumption of denial.
- License applications must address the end-use and end-user with supporting documentation.
- License exceptions are largely unavailable for MEU-controlled exports.
- The exporter bears the burden of knowing whether the end-use is military.

### Layer The Military-Intelligence End-Use And End-User Rules

Separate rules target military-intelligence end-uses and end-users, broadening the prohibition.

- Exports for use by military-intelligence organizations are restricted.
- The definition captures intelligence organizations supporting military end-uses.
- These rules apply across destinations, not only to China, Russia, and Venezuela.

### Layer The Expanded Semiconductor, Supercomputer, And Related Controls

Recent expansions impose end-use controls on advanced computing, supercomputing, and semiconductor manufacturing items, particularly affecting China.

- Advanced computing items and supercomputers for military end-use or military-intelligence end-use;
- semiconductor manufacturing equipment for certain fabs;
- foreign direct product rules that reach foreign-made items produced with US technology or software;
- end-use and end-user restrictions that operate independent of ECCN country chart intersections.

These controls are technically complex and frequently updated. Engage specialist review for advanced technology exports to affected destinations.

### Conduct Diligence To Determine Military Status

Because the license requirement turns on end-use and end-user status, the exporter must gather information.

- obtain end-use statements;
- screen against the MEU List and other restricted-party lists;
- research the end-user's affiliations, particularly military or intelligence links;
- assess whether the end-user is a military-affiliated research institute, defense conglomerate, or subsidiary of a military entity;
- for Chinese end-users, assess military-civil fusion exposure.

Where the end-user's military status is unclear, treat conservatively or seek a license.

## Common Traps

### Assuming MEU Rules Apply Only To Embargoed Countries

China is not comprehensively embargoed but is subject to MEU controls. Treating China as open is wrong.

### Treating The MEU List As Exhaustive

The MEU List is a non-exhaustive indicator. Entities not listed can be military end-users by definition.

### Equating Military End-Use With Weapons Production

Integration into military systems, support to military organizations, and supply to military-affiliated research institutes can be military end-use.

### Overlooking Military-Affiliated Research And Conglomerates

Defense conglomerates, military-civil fusion entities, and affiliated research institutes can be military end-users even with civilian branding.

### Assuming EAR99 Items Are Exempt

EAR99 items may escape the MEU rule but can be caught by other end-use rules, the foreign direct product rule, or sanctions.

### Ignoring The Expanded Semiconductor And Computing Controls

Advanced computing and semiconductor manufacturing items face end-use and FDP controls that are technically complex and frequently updated.

### Applying A Single Review Policy Across All MEU Cases

The presumption of denial and the unavailability of license exceptions are specific to MEU-controlled exports. Confirm the policy for each rule.

### Failing To Research Military And Intelligence Affiliations

End-user military status often requires research beyond the customer's name. Affiliations, ownership, and research links must be checked.

## Self-Check

- Are the countries subject to MEU rules (China, Russia, Venezuela, and others per current rule) identified before clearing an export?
- Is the military end-use definition applied broadly, including integration, production, testing, maintenance, and support to military organizations and defense intelligence?
- Are military end-users identified, including armed services, intelligence organizations, MEU List entities, and any person supporting military end-uses?
- Is the covered item scope confirmed, recognizing that EAR99 items are generally outside MEU but may be caught by other rules?
- Is the license requirement and presumption-of-denial review policy applied where MEU controls are triggered?
- Are military-intelligence end-use and end-user rules layered on top of MEU analysis, recognizing their cross-destination scope?
- Are expanded semiconductor, supercomputer, and advanced computing end-use controls and foreign direct product rules applied to affected destinations with specialist review?
- Is the end-user researched for military, intelligence, and military-civil fusion affiliations beyond the customer name?
- Is the MEU List treated as a non-exhaustive indicator rather than a complete roster of military end-users?
- Are end-use statements obtained and the export treated conservatively where military status is unclear?
