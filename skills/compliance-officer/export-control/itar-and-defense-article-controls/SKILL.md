# ITAR And Defense Article Controls

The International Traffic in Arms Regulations (ITAR), administered by the State Department's Directorate of Defense Trade Controls (DDTC), controls defense articles, defense services, and related technical data. ITAR is jurisdictionally and philosophically distinct from the EAR: it presumes control over military items and their technical data, reaches brokering and defense services, requires registration of manufacturers, exporters, and brokers, and imposes license requirements that are far stricter than EAR license exceptions. The single most consequential decision in US export control is whether an item is on the US Munitions List (USML), making it ITAR-controlled, or on the Commerce Control List, making it EAR-controlled. Getting this wrong can mean illegal exports of defense articles, with criminal exposure.

Use this skill before classifying an item under the USML, determining ITAR versus EAR jurisdiction, evaluating defense services or brokering activity, addressing registration obligations, controlling technical data, or pursuing a defense trade license. The compliance officer must drive the jurisdictional analysis from the USML category text and the "specially designed" analysis, not from a generic label of "military."

## Core Rules

### Resolve ITAR Versus EAR Jurisdiction First

The jurisdictional determination controls everything downstream. An item is ITAR-controlled if it is a defense article on the USML; otherwise it falls to the EAR.

- Review the USML categories (I through XXI) to determine whether the item is specifically described, captured by the "specially designed" catch, or covered by a note.
- Apply the "specially designed" definition and its exclusions carefully; many parts and components turn on this analysis.
- Use the Order of Review: determine USML coverage before concluding EAR classification.
- Items in or for a defense article are often ITAR-controlled even if they have civilian uses.
- A commodity jurisdiction (CJ) request can be submitted to DDTC for ambiguous items; until resolved, treat conservatively.

Do not classify under the EAR without first excluding USML coverage. The default for genuinely military items is ITAR.

### Identify Defense Articles On The USML

The USML is a positive list of defense articles. Review the relevant category text for the item type.

- Firearms, close assault weapons, combat shotguns (Category I);
- guns and armament (Category II);
- ammunition and ordnance (Category III);
- launch vehicles, missiles, torpedoes (Category IV);
- explosives and energetic materials (Category V);
- vessels of war and special naval craft (Category VI);
- tanks and military vehicles (Category VII);
- aircraft and related material (Category VIII);
- military training equipment (Category IX);
- protective personnel equipment (Category X);
- military electronics (Category XI);
- fire control and surveillance (Category XII);
- auxiliary military equipment (Category XIII);
- toxicological agents and equipment (Category XIV);
- spacecraft systems and related material (Category XV);
- nuclear weapons (Category XVI);
- classified articles (Category XVII);
- directed energy weapons (Category XVIII);
- gas turbine engines (Category XIX);
- submersible vessels (Category XX);
- miscellaneous articles (Category XXI).

Each category contains technical parameters and "see" and "related" references. Read the full category, not just the heading.

### Control Technical Data And Defense Services

ITAR controls more than physical articles. Technical data and defense services are independently controlled.

- Technical data includes design, development, production, manufacture, assembly, operation, repair, testing, maintenance, or modification of defense articles, in any form.
- Technical data does not include general scientific, mathematical, or engineering principles taught in schools, or basic marketing information.
- A defense service is assistance, training, or guidance in the design, development, engineering, maintenance, manufacture, production, assembly, testing, repair, or modification of a defense article, or in the employment of a defense article.
- Furnishing technical data or a defense service to a foreign person is an export requiring authorization, including a deemed export to a foreign person in the United States.

The release of technical data or performance of a defense service is an export even if nothing physical crosses a border.

### Apply Registration Requirements

Manufacturers, exporters, and brokers of defense articles, defense services, or technical data must register with DDTC.

- Registration is a prerequisite to applying for a license or other approval.
- Registration must be renewed annually.
- Brokers of defense articles and defense services must register separately and comply with brokering rules.
- Failure to register is itself a violation, independent of any export.

Confirm registration status before any manufacture, export, or brokering of defense articles or services.

### Manage Brokering Activity

Brokering is broadly defined and separately controlled.

- Brokering includes facilitating the manufacture, export, import, or transfer of a defense article or defense service, including by financing, shipping, or negotiating.
- Brokers must register, obtain brokering authorization where required, and submit brokering reports.
- Certain activities are exempt, but the exemptions are narrow and must be confirmed.
- A person can be a broker even if not the exporter of record.

Review intermediary, financing, and facilitation roles for brokering obligations.

### Pursue Authorization Through The Correct Mechanism

ITAR authorization takes several forms.

- DSP-5 for permanent exports of defense articles;
- DSP-73 for temporary imports;
- TAA (Technical Assistance Agreement) for defense services and exchanges of technical data;
- MLA (Manufacturing License Agreement) for offshore production;
- GW (Global/Warehouse) and other aggregated authorizations;
- Exemptions for certain low-value, government, or exhibition activities, narrowly construed.

Match the authorization to the activity. A hardware license does not authorize a defense service or technical data release.

### Apply ITAR Prohibitions And Policy Denials

Certain destinations and parties are subject to policy denial or prohibition.

- ITAR section 126.1 prohibited countries face a policy of denial;
- embargoes and sanctions overlay ITAR;
- end-use and end-user vetting applies;
- dual and third-country nationals and foreign ownership can affect eligibility under the FMS and direct commercial sales frameworks.

## Common Traps

### Assuming An Item Is EAR Because It Has Civilian Uses

Many defense articles have civilian analogs. The USML captures items "in or for" a defense article regardless of dual-use potential. Resolve USML coverage first.

### Skipping The Specially Designed Analysis

Parts and components often turn on "specially designed" and its exclusions. Concluding EAR without this analysis is a common error.

### Treating Technical Data As Uncontrolled

Technical data and defense services are independently controlled and broadly defined. Sharing engineering data or providing support is an export.

### Overlooking Brokering Obligations

Facilitating, financing, or negotiating a defense article transfer is brokering requiring registration and possibly authorization. Intermediaries are not exempt.

### Failing To Register Before Activity

Registration is a prerequisite and an independent obligation. Manufacturing or exporting before registration is a violation even if a license is later sought.

### Using The Wrong Authorization Type

A DSP-5 authorizes hardware, not defense services or technical data exchanges. Using the wrong mechanism leaves the activity unauthorized.

### Ignoring Deemed Exports Of Technical Data

Releasing ITAR technical data to a foreign person in the United States is an export requiring authorization. Domestic workforce sharing is not automatically exempt.

### Assuming ITAR Exemptions Are Broad

ITAR exemptions are narrowly construed. Confirm each element before relying on one.

## Self-Check

- Has ITAR versus EAR jurisdiction been resolved by reviewing the USML categories and the "specially designed" analysis before concluding EAR classification?
- Is the relevant USML category read in full, including technical parameters, "see" and "related" references, and notes?
- Is technical data controlled as a defense article, with the definition applied to design, development, production, and maintenance information in any form?
- Are defense services identified, including assistance, training, or guidance in the design, production, or employment of defense articles?
- Is registration with DDTC confirmed for manufacturers, exporters, and brokers before any activity?
- Are brokering obligations assessed for intermediary, financing, shipping, and negotiation roles?
- Is the correct authorization mechanism selected (DSP-5, DSP-73, TAA, MLA, or aggregated authorization) matching the activity type?
- Are ITAR section 126.1 prohibited countries, embargoes, and end-use and end-user controls applied as overlays?
- Are deemed exports of technical data to foreign persons in the United States treated as exports requiring authorization?
- Are ITAR exemptions confirmed element-by-element before reliance, recognizing they are narrowly construed?
- Is a commodity jurisdiction request pursued for genuinely ambiguous items, with conservative treatment pending resolution?
