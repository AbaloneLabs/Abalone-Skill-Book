# Merger Notification Gun Jumping And Preclosing Conduct

Gun-jumping occurs when parties to a notifiable merger implement the transaction, coordinate competitive conduct, or exchange competitively sensitive information before receiving antitrust clearance. Under the US Hart-Scott-Rodino Act, the EU Merger Regulation, and equivalent national merger control regimes, the standstill obligation requires parties to remain independent competitors until closing, and violations can result in substantial fines, transaction delay, and in some jurisdictions, invalidity of the transaction. The central judgment problem is that commercially rational pre-closing integration planning, which is necessary for a smooth day-one transition, can cross into gun-jumping if it involves premature transfer of control, impermissible information exchange, or coordination of competitive decisions. A compliance officer who allows integration planning without structured controls can expose the firm to gun-jumping penalties that are separate from and additional to any substantive merger concerns.

Use this skill before assessing gun-jumping risk, evaluating pre-closing integration planning, reviewing information exchange arrangements, or advising on interim conduct. The goal is to make the agent think about what conduct is permissible before closing, what safeguards are required, and the decisions that are easy to make too casually. A clean team arrangement that is superficially structured but allows information flow to commercial decision-makers can still constitute gun-jumping.

This skill addresses jurisdiction-specific obligations. Gun-jumping standards, standstill rules, and clean team requirements differ across the US, the EU, and national regimes, with the EU taking a particularly strict approach. Always confirm the applicable standard with qualified antitrust or legal counsel, and do not rely on this skill as a substitute for jurisdiction-specific legal advice.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Standstill Obligation

The standstill obligation requires merging parties to act as independent competitors until the transaction closes. This means no transfer of control, no coordination of competitive conduct, and no impermissible information exchange.

Standstill obligation elements:

- no transfer of beneficial ownership or control before clearance;
- no exercise of decisive influence over the target's competitive conduct before closing;
- no coordination of pricing, output, customer terms, or commercial strategy;
- no integration of operations, systems, or personnel that constitutes implementation;
- no actions that would make the transaction irreversible before clearance;
- continued independent decision-making by each party on all competitive matters.

The standstill applies from signing until closing, regardless of whether notification has been filed. Some jurisdictions also apply a suspension effect during the review period.

### Distinguish Permissible From Impermissible Pre-Closing Conduct

Pre-closing conduct falls on a spectrum from clearly permissible to clearly impermissible, with a gray zone that requires careful analysis.

Permissible pre-closing conduct:

- planning for integration, provided it does not involve implementation or control transfer;
- obtaining regulatory approvals and preparing filings;
- communicating the transaction to employees, customers, and suppliers in ways that do not coordinate conduct;
- maintaining ordinary course business relationships between the parties;
- establishing governance for the combined entity post-closing.

Impermissible pre-closing conduct:

- transferring assets, contracts, or personnel before closing;
- exercising control over the target's decisions, including pricing, hiring, or investments;
- coordinating competitive conduct, such as aligning pricing or customer approaches;
- integrating IT systems, supply chains, or operations before closing;
- making the transaction irreversible through actions that prevent withdrawal.

The gray zone includes integration planning, information exchange through clean teams, and transitional arrangements that must be assessed case by case.

### Structure Clean Team Arrangements For Information Exchange

Pre-closing information exchange is necessary for integration planning and synergy assessment but must be structured to prevent competitively sensitive information from reaching commercial decision-makers.

Clean team requirements:

- clean team members should be personnel who do not make competitive decisions for either party, supplemented by external advisors;
- competitively sensitive information shared with the clean team should not be disclosed to competitive decision-makers;
- the clean team should operate under written protocols defining what information can be shared and with whom;
- information should be aggregated, anonymized, or redacted where possible;
- clean team outputs to competitive decision-makers should be limited to non-sensitive summaries;
- clean team activities should be documented for defensibility.

A clean team that includes competitive decision-makers, or that allows information flow to such decision-makers, does not provide protection and can constitute gun-jumping.

### Control Ordinary Course Business Interactions

The parties may continue ordinary course business interactions, such as supplier-customer relationships, but these must not be used to coordinate competitive conduct.

Ordinary course controls:

- maintain existing contractual terms and commercial arrangements without modification;
- do not use supplier-customer interactions to exchange competitively sensitive information;
- do not align pricing, output, or customer terms through ordinary course interactions;
- document that interactions remain at arm's length and consistent with pre-signing practice;
- monitor interactions for any drift into coordination or information exchange.

The parties should be particularly careful where they are both competitors and trading partners, because the dual relationship creates opportunities for improper exchange.

### Assess Interim Operating Covenants

The transaction agreement may include interim operating covenants that govern the target's conduct between signing and closing. These must be scoped to avoid gun-jumping.

Interim covenant considerations:

- ordinary course covenants, requiring the target to operate in the ordinary course, are generally permissible;
- covenants restricting extraordinary actions, such as major acquisitions or divestitures, are generally permissible;
- covenants that give the buyer approval rights over ordinary competitive decisions are impermissible;
- covenants must not effectively transfer control before closing;
- covenants must be tailored to preserve the target's value, not to coordinate competitive conduct.

The scope of permissible interim covenants is jurisdiction-specific, with the EU taking a stricter approach than the US on approval rights.

### Evaluate Remedies And Divestiture Planning

Where remedies are required, the planning and implementation of divestitures or behavioral commitments must comply with standstill obligations.

Remedy planning considerations:

- identifying divestiture assets and buyers can begin before closing, provided it does not involve implementation;
- the divestiture business must continue to operate independently until the remedy is implemented;
- information exchange for remedy planning must follow clean team protocols;
- the appointment of a monitoring trustee, where required, must not involve premature integration;
- hold-separate arrangements must preserve the divestiture business as a viable competitor.

Remedy implementation that begins before closing, or that integrates the non-divested business prematurely, can constitute gun-jumping.

### Document The Compliance Framework

The gun-jumping compliance framework should be documented to demonstrate that pre-closing conduct was structured to comply with standstill obligations.

Documentation elements:

- the standstill analysis and the basis for permissible conduct determinations;
- clean team protocols, membership, and information flows;
- interim covenant scope and tailoring;
- ordinary course interaction controls and monitoring;
- antitrust counsel review and advice on gray zone issues;
- incident logs for any potential gun-jumping concerns and their resolution.

Documentation that the firm structured pre-closing conduct carefully and obtained qualified advice is critical to defending against gun-jumping allegations.

## Common Traps

### Treating Integration Planning As Implementation

Integration planning is permissible, but actions that constitute implementation, such as transferring control or assets, constitute gun-jumping.

### Clean Team With Competitive Decision-Makers

A clean team that includes or reports to competitive decision-makers does not provide protection and can itself constitute gun-jumping.

### Exercising Approval Rights Over Ordinary Decisions

Interim covenants that give the buyer approval rights over ordinary competitive decisions effectively transfer control before closing.

### Coordinating Pricing Or Customer Terms

Aligning pricing, output, or customer approaches between the parties before closing violates the standstill obligation.

### Premature IT Or Operational Integration

Integrating IT systems, supply chains, or operations before closing constitutes implementation and gun-jumping.

### Using Supplier-Customer Interactions For Information Exchange

Ordinary course interactions that are used to exchange competitively sensitive information or coordinate conduct violate standstill.

### Assuming US Standards Apply In The EU

The EU takes a stricter approach to gun-jumping than the US, particularly on approval rights and information exchange. Jurisdiction-specific analysis is required.

## Self-Check

- Is the standstill obligation understood, requiring independent competition, no control transfer, no coordination, and no impermissible information exchange until closing?
- Is pre-closing conduct assessed on the permissible-to-impermissible spectrum, with gray zone issues analyzed case by case?
- Are clean team arrangements structured with non-decision-making personnel, written protocols, information redaction, and no flow to competitive decision-makers?
- Are ordinary course business interactions maintained at arm's length, without modification, coordination, or information exchange?
- Are interim operating covenants scoped to ordinary course and extraordinary action restrictions, without approval rights over competitive decisions?
- Is remedy and divestiture planning conducted with standstill compliance, hold-separate arrangements, and trustee independence?
- Is the compliance framework documented with standstill analysis, clean team protocols, covenant scope, counsel review, and incident logs?
- Are integration planning activities confined to planning, without implementation, control transfer, or operational integration before closing?
- Is the gun-jumping assessment confirmed against the applicable jurisdiction standard, recognizing the EU's stricter approach, with qualified counsel?
- Are employees involved in integration and transition trained on standstill obligations and clean team protocols?
