# Merger Notification Substantive Review And Competition Assessment

Substantive merger review evaluates whether a proposed transaction will significantly impede effective competition, create or strengthen a dominant position, or otherwise harm consumers through higher prices, reduced output, diminished innovation, or weakened rivalry. Under the US Clayton Act Section 7, the EU Merger Regulation, and equivalent national merger control regimes, authorities can block transactions or require remedies before clearance. The central judgment problem is that a transaction that is commercially attractive and strategically sound can fail substantive review if it eliminates important competition, particularly in concentrated markets or where the parties are close competitors. A compliance officer who focuses on filing mechanics without assessing substantive risk can leave the firm unprepared for Phase II review, remedy demands, or prohibition.

Use this skill before preparing substantive review submissions, assessing competitive effects, evaluating market definition, or advising on remedies. The goal is to make the agent think about how authorities will analyze the transaction, what evidence supports or undermines clearance, and the decisions that are easy to make too casually. A transaction with modest market shares can still raise concerns if the parties are close competitors, if the market is already concentrated, or if innovation competition is at risk.

This skill addresses jurisdiction-specific obligations. Substantive merger standards, market definition approaches, and remedy requirements differ across the US, the EU, and national regimes. Always confirm the applicable standard and authority practice with qualified antitrust or legal counsel, and do not rely on this skill as a substitute for jurisdiction-specific legal advice.

## Core Rules

### Conduct A Preliminary Substantive Risk Assessment

Before filing, the firm should conduct a preliminary substantive risk assessment to identify potential concerns and prepare the clearance strategy.

Preliminary assessment elements:

- identify all horizontal overlaps, where the parties compete in the same market;
- identify all vertical relationships, where one party supplies or purchases from the other;
- identify conglomerate concerns, where the transaction combines complementary products;
- assess market concentration using metrics such as HHI and market share levels;
- assess the parties' closeness of competition, including product positioning and switching patterns;
- assess potential competition concerns, where one party is a likely future entrant;
- identify potential concerns from internal documents, such as strategic plans or competitive analysis.

The preliminary assessment should identify low-risk transactions suitable for Phase I clearance and higher-risk transactions that may require Phase II preparation or remedy planning.

### Develop The Market Definition And Competitive Analysis

Market definition and competitive analysis are the foundation of substantive review. The firm must develop a defensible analysis that supports clearance.

Market definition elements:

- define relevant product markets based on demand-side substitutability and, where relevant, supply-side substitutability;
- define relevant geographic markets based on customer location, shipping patterns, and competitive conditions;
- assess market shares and concentration, recognizing that high shares do not automatically block clearance;
- identify market participants, including actual and potential competitors;
- assess barriers to entry and expansion, including regulatory, technical, and economic barriers;
- assess countervailing buyer power that may constrain post-merger pricing.

The market definition should be supported by evidence, including customer surveys, switching data, pricing analysis, and internal documents. Authorities will scrutinize definitions that appear tailored to minimize shares.

### Assess Horizontal Overlap Concerns

Horizontal overlaps, where the parties compete in the same market, are the primary source of substantive concern in most reviews.

Horizontal concern assessment:

- calculate post-merger market shares and HHI levels and changes;
- assess whether the parties are close competitors, based on product positioning, pricing, and customer switching;
- assess unilateral effects, where the merged firm could profitably raise prices by eliminating competition between the parties;
- assess coordinated effects, where the transaction could facilitate tacit coordination in the market;
- assess the role of the parties as mavericks or disruptors that constrain the market;
- assess the impact on innovation competition, particularly in technology or pharmaceutical markets.

Horizontal concerns are greater when the parties are close competitors, when the market is concentrated, and when barriers to entry are high.

### Evaluate Vertical And Conglomerate Concerns

Vertical and conglomerate relationships can raise concerns about foreclosure, raising rivals' costs, or leveraging market power across markets.

Vertical concern assessment:

- assess input foreclosure, where the merged firm could restrict rivals' access to inputs or distribution;
- assess customer foreclosure, where the merged firm could restrict suppliers' access to customers;
- assess the materiality of foreclosure, based on the merged firm's market position and rivals' alternatives;
- assess raising rivals' costs, where the transaction could increase competitors' input or distribution costs.

Conglomerate concern assessment:

- assess tying and bundling risk, where the merged firm could leverage strong positions across complementary products;
- assess portfolio effects, where the transaction could create advantages from offering a range of products.

Vertical and conglomerate concerns are scrutinized more heavily in the EU than in the US, and the analysis must reflect jurisdiction-specific approaches.

### Assess Entry, Expansion, And Countervailing Power

Authorities consider whether entry, expansion, or countervailing buyer power can constrain the merged firm post-transaction.

Entry and expansion assessment:

- assess the likelihood, timeliness, and sufficiency of entry or expansion;
- assess barriers to entry, including regulatory, technical, scale, and network effects;
- assess the credibility of potential entrants and their track record;
- assess whether entry would be at sufficient scale to constrain pricing.

Countervailing power assessment:

- assess the strength and commercial significance of large buyers;
- assess whether buyer power has historically constrained pricing;
- assess whether buyer power would survive the transaction.

Entry and countervailing power arguments must be supported by evidence and must be credible, not hypothetical.

### Prepare For Phase II And Remedy Discussions

Higher-risk transactions may proceed to Phase II review and may require remedies to obtain clearance. The firm should prepare for this possibility.

Phase II preparation:

- prepare comprehensive economic evidence and analysis;
- identify and prepare customer and third-party witnesses;
- anticipate authority concerns and prepare rebuttals;
- assess the timeline and resource requirements of Phase II.

Remedy preparation:

- identify potential structural remedies, such as divestiture of overlapping businesses;
- identify potential behavioral remedies, such as access commitments or non-discrimination obligations;
- assess the viability and acceptability of each remedy option;
- identify potential divestiture buyers and assess their credibility;
- prepare a remedy package that addresses identified concerns without undermining the transaction rationale.

Structural remedies, particularly divestitures, are generally preferred by authorities. Behavioral remedies are accepted in some jurisdictions but face skepticism in others.

### Review Internal Documents For Consistency

Authorities will review the parties' internal documents, including board materials, strategic plans, and competitive analysis. These documents must be consistent with the clearance narrative.

Internal document review:

- identify documents that characterize the parties as close competitors, the market as concentrated, or barriers as high;
- assess whether these characterizations are consistent with the clearance narrative;
- prepare explanations for any inconsistent documents;
- ensure that ordinary course business documents support the competitive analysis;
- avoid creating documents that could undermine clearance during the review period.

Inconsistent internal documents are a primary source of authority concern and can trigger Phase II review even when the external analysis supports clearance.

## Common Traps

### Focusing On Filing Mechanics Over Substantive Risk

A transaction that is filed correctly but raises unaddressed substantive concerns can proceed to Phase II or prohibition.

### Market Definition Tailored To Minimize Shares

Market definitions that appear designed to minimize market shares attract authority scrutiny and undermine credibility.

### Ignoring Closeness Of Competition

Modest market shares can raise concerns if the parties are close competitors. Closeness analysis is essential to horizontal assessment.

### Overlooking Vertical And Conglomerate Concerns

Vertical and conglomerate concerns, particularly in the EU, can trigger review or remedies even without horizontal overlap.

### Hypothetical Entry And Countervailing Power Arguments

Entry and buyer power arguments that are not supported by evidence are rejected by authorities and do not support clearance.

### Unprepared For Phase II Or Remedies

Transactions that proceed to Phase II without preparation for evidence, witnesses, or remedies face greater risk of prohibition or onerous commitments.

### Inconsistent Internal Documents

Internal documents that characterize the parties as close competitors or the market as concentrated can trigger Phase II and undermine clearance.

## Self-Check

- Is a preliminary substantive risk assessment conducted, identifying horizontal overlaps, vertical relationships, conglomerate concerns, and concentration levels?
- Is the market definition developed with evidence on product and geographic scope, market shares, barriers, and countervailing power?
- Are horizontal overlap concerns assessed for unilateral effects, coordinated effects, maverick roles, and innovation competition?
- Are vertical and conglomerate concerns assessed for foreclosure, raising rivals' costs, tying, bundling, and portfolio effects?
- Are entry, expansion, and countervailing buyer power assessed with credible evidence, not hypothetical arguments?
- Is Phase II preparation considered, including economic evidence, third-party witnesses, authority concern anticipation, and timeline planning?
- Are remedy options identified, including structural divestitures, behavioral commitments, viable buyers, and acceptability assessment?
- Are internal documents reviewed for consistency with the clearance narrative, with explanations prepared for any inconsistencies?
- Is the substantive analysis supported by customer surveys, switching data, pricing analysis, and ordinary course business documents?
- Is the substantive merger assessment confirmed against the applicable jurisdiction standard and authority practice with qualified counsel, rather than a generic standard?
