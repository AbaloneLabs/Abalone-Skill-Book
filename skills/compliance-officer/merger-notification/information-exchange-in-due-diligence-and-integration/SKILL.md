# Information Exchange In Due Diligence And Integration

The period between signing and closing is one of the highest-risk windows in any transaction. Two companies that are still competitors, or that deal with each other at arm's length, suddenly need to share vast amounts of sensitive information to price the deal, conduct due diligence, plan integration, and prepare for day-one operations. The antitrust danger is acute: exchanging competitively sensitive information before closing can be gun-jumping, an information exchange violation, or both, even where the transaction itself is lawful and ultimately cleared. The compliance officer's job is to build the controls, the clean teams, the data room rules, and the integration guardrails that let the deal proceed without the parties coordinating competitive conduct or receiving information they should not yet have.

Use this skill during due diligence, during the pre-closing period, and throughout integration planning. The goal is to make the agent design clean team structures that genuinely wall off sensitive information, enforce pre-closing separation of commercial operations, and keep integration planning from becoming implementation.

## Core Rules

### Treat Pre-Closing Parties As Competitors

Until closing, the acquirer and target remain separate companies and, in many cases, competitors. The information exchange rules that apply between competitors apply in full during due diligence and integration planning.

Implications:

- the parties cannot exchange current or future prices, costs, margins, output, customer data, or strategy except within controlled structures and only where necessary for the deal;
- the acquirer cannot direct or influence the target's competitive conduct before closing;
- employees cannot be moved between the parties in ways that transmit competitively sensitive information;
- any information shared must be justified by a specific deal need and protected from commercial teams.

The baseline is separation. Information sharing is the exception, permitted only within controlled channels and for defined purposes.

### Design A Clean Team That Actually Walls Off Information

The clean team is the primary control for sensitive information exchange in a transaction. A clean team only works if it is structured correctly; a nominal clean team that funnels data to commercial teams provides no protection.

A genuine clean team requires:

- membership limited to personnel who do not make or influence commercial decisions for either party, typically external advisors (lawyers, economists, consultants) or internal employees walled off from commercial functions;
- access to competitively sensitive data restricted to clean team members, with commercial teams receiving only aggregated, sanitized, or high-level outputs;
- clear rules on what data enters the clean team, how it is processed, and what form of output may leave;
- physical and electronic separation, with dedicated systems or data rooms that commercial teams cannot access;
- confidentiality and non-use obligations that survive the deal, in case it does not close;
- logging of what sensitive information was accessed, by whom, and for what purpose.

The test is whether a commercial decision-maker on either side could reconstruct the other party's competitively sensitive position from what the clean team produces. If yes, the clean team has failed.

### Control The Data Room

The data room is where due diligence information is shared, and it must be tiered so that the most sensitive information is accessible only under controlled conditions.

Structure the data room:

- tier access so that general commercial teams see only non-sensitive diligence materials;
- place competitively sensitive materials (current pricing, customer lists, cost data, strategy documents, bid information) in a separate clean-team-only section;
- use view-only, watermarking, download restrictions, and audit logging for sensitive documents;
- require clean team mediation for any analysis that combines the parties' sensitive data;
- remove or sequester materials if the deal does not close, with confirmation that no copies remain in commercial hands.

A data room where commercial teams can browse the target's current pricing and customer data is a gun-jumping and information exchange risk, regardless of the deal's ultimate lawfulness.

### Enforce Pre-Closing Separation Of Operations

Gun-jumping is not only about information; it is about control. The acquirer must not exercise beneficial ownership or operational control over the target before closing.

Separation rules:

- the target continues to be managed by its own management, making its own commercial decisions;
- the acquirer does not direct pricing, marketing, hiring, customer approaches, or procurement before closing;
- integration planning is limited to preparation, not implementation;
- no combined operations, joint marketing, or coordinated customer engagement occurs before closing;
- the parties do not present themselves to customers or suppliers as combined before closing.

The line between planning and implementation is fact-specific, but the principle is clear: the acquirer can plan but cannot run the target before closing.

### Bound Integration Planning To Avoid Implementation

Integration planning is permitted and necessary, but it must not cross into implementation. This is where well-intentioned deal teams most often create gun-jumping risk.

Permitted planning includes:

- designing the post-closing organization structure, systems, and processes;
- identifying synergies and the steps to realize them after closing;
- preparing day-one readiness plans;
- conducting clean-team-mediated analysis of combined operations.

Impermissible implementation includes:

- beginning to combine systems, contracts, or operations before closing;
- coordinating pricing, customer approaches, or marketing;
- making joint procurement or supplier decisions;
- moving employees, assets, or contracts between the parties;
- taking any action that assumes the deal has closed.

Counsel should review the integration plan to confirm it stays on the planning side of the line, and the compliance officer should enforce the boundary operationally.

### Coordinate With Merger Notification And Clearance

Information exchange controls and gun-jumping rules operate alongside merger notification. The controls must remain in place until clearance and closing, and the scope of permitted coordination expands only after closing.

Account for:

- the waiting period or review timeline in each jurisdiction;
- the possibility that conditions or remedies change the deal and the integration plan;
- the need to maintain separation until all required clearances are obtained;
- the risk that aggressive pre-closing coordination weakens the parties' position in review, by suggesting the deal has competitive effects.

### Prepare For Deal Failure

Not every deal closes. If the transaction fails, the parties have exchanged sensitive information and must ensure it is not used competitively.

Controls for deal failure:

- return or destroy sensitive materials, with confirmation;
- ensure clean team members and commercial personnel are bound by continuing confidentiality;
- prevent any use of the diligence information in the parties' ongoing competition;
- document the sequestration so the company can demonstrate non-use if later challenged.

A failed deal that leaves one party with the other's pricing or customer data, and that is followed by competitive use of that data, is a serious information exchange violation.

## Common Traps

### A Nominal Clean Team That Leaks To Commercial Teams

A clean team staffed by commercial personnel, or that produces outputs commercial teams can use to reconstruct the other party's position, provides no protection. The wall must be real.

### Open Data Room Access To Commercial Teams

Allowing commercial teams to browse sensitive diligence materials, even read-only, creates information exchange exposure. Sensitive data must be clean-team-gated.

### Treating Integration Planning As Implementation

Integration teams under pressure to deliver synergies begin combining operations before closing, crossing from planning to gun-jumping. The boundary must be enforced.

### Coordinating Commercial Conduct Pre-Closing

Even informal coordination of pricing, customer approaches, or marketing before closing is gun-jumping and potentially a cartel-like information exchange.

### Ignoring Deal Failure Risk

If the deal fails and sensitive information remains in commercial hands, subsequent competitive use is a violation. Sequestration must be planned before, not after, failure.

### Assuming A Lawful Deal Excuses Pre-Closing Exchange

A transaction that will ultimately be cleared does not license gun-jumping or information exchange before clearance. The pre-closing rules apply regardless of the deal's merits.

### Moving Employees In Ways That Transmit Sensitive Data

Secondments or early hires that move personnel with knowledge of the other party's pricing or strategy create an information channel that must be controlled.

## Self-Check

- Are the parties treated as separate competitors until closing, with information exchange permitted only within controlled structures and for defined deal purposes?
- Is the clean team structured so that commercial decision-makers cannot reconstruct the other party's competitively sensitive position from its outputs?
- Is clean team membership limited to non-commercial personnel or external advisors, with physical and electronic separation from commercial teams?
- Is the data room tiered so that competitively sensitive materials are accessible only through the clean team, with view-only, watermarking, and audit logging?
- Is the target managed independently until closing, with the acquirer exercising no operational control over pricing, marketing, hiring, or procurement?
- Is integration planning limited to preparation, with no combination of systems, contracts, operations, or commercial conduct before closing?
- Are integration plans reviewed by counsel to confirm they stay on the planning side of the gun-jumping line?
- Do information exchange controls and separation remain in place until all merger clearances are obtained and closing occurs?
- Is deal failure planned for, with return or destruction of sensitive materials and continuing confidentiality to prevent competitive use?
- Are employee moves between the parties controlled to prevent transmission of competitively sensitive information?
