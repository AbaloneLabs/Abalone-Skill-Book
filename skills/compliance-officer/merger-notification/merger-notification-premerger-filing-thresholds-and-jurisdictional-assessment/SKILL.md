# Merger Notification Premerger Filing Thresholds And Jurisdictional Assessment

Merger notification obligations require parties to notify competition authorities before completing certain transactions that meet jurisdiction-specific thresholds. Under the US Hart-Scott-Rodino (HSR) Act, the EU Merger Regulation (EUMR), and equivalent national merger control regimes, failure to notify a notifiable transaction, or gun-jumping by implementing before clearance, can result in substantial fines and transaction invalidity. The central judgment problem is that a single cross-border transaction can trigger notification obligations in dozens of jurisdictions, each with different thresholds, timing, and procedural requirements, and missing a filing or sequencing it incorrectly can derail the deal and create liability. A compliance officer who assesses notification obligations based on only the primary jurisdictions, or who relies on outdated threshold tables, can leave the firm exposed to gun-jumping or failure-to-notify penalties in overlooked jurisdictions.

Use this skill before assessing merger notification obligations, evaluating thresholds, determining filing requirements, or advising on timing and sequencing. The goal is to make the agent think about the full scope of jurisdictional analysis, what triggers notification, and the decisions that are easy to make too casually. A transaction that is below thresholds in the primary jurisdictions may still be notifiable in secondary jurisdictions with lower thresholds or different nexus tests.

This skill addresses jurisdiction-specific obligations. Merger notification thresholds, timing, and procedural requirements differ across more than 100 national regimes and are updated periodically. Always confirm current thresholds and requirements with qualified antitrust or legal counsel in each relevant jurisdiction, and do not rely on this skill as a substitute for jurisdiction-specific legal advice.

## Core Rules

### Map All Potentially Relevant Jurisdictions

The first step in merger notification assessment is identifying all jurisdictions that may have notification jurisdiction over the transaction. This requires analyzing where the parties have operations, sales, assets, and nexus.

Jurisdictional mapping elements:

- jurisdictions where the parties have registered offices or headquarters;
- jurisdictions where the parties have physical operations, subsidiaries, or branches;
- jurisdictions where the parties generate turnover or sales, including exports;
- jurisdictions where the parties hold assets, including intellectual property;
- jurisdictions with effects-based jurisdiction that may claim nexus even without local operations.

The mapping should be broad initially and then narrowed through threshold analysis. Overlooking a jurisdiction with mandatory notification can result in failure-to-notify penalties.

### Understand Threshold Tests And Nexus Requirements

Each jurisdiction applies its own threshold tests to determine whether a transaction is notifiable. Thresholds typically combine turnover and market presence tests.

Common threshold test structures:

- global or combined turnover thresholds, requiring the parties' aggregate turnover to exceed a specified level;
- local or national turnover thresholds, requiring at least one party to have local turnover above a specified level;
- two-party local turnover tests, requiring both parties to have local presence;
- market share thresholds, used in some jurisdictions as an alternative or additional test;
- transaction value thresholds, used in some jurisdictions for acquisitions of businesses with limited local turnover but significant value;
- nexus tests, requiring that the transaction has a local effect or that the parties have local operations.

Threshold tests are updated periodically, and the firm should use current rather than historical thresholds. Many jurisdictions also have different rules for financial investors, intragroup transactions, and acquisitions of partial control.

### Assess The Type Of Transaction Covered

Merger notification obligations apply to different types of transactions, and the scope varies by jurisdiction.

Transaction types commonly covered:

- mergers and statutory consolidations;
- acquisitions of sole or majority control through purchase of shares or assets;
- acquisitions of joint control through purchase of a minority stake;
- acquisitions of decisive influence through contractual or other means;
- acquisitions of assets, including major business units or IP portfolios;
- joint ventures that perform on a lasting basis with autonomous economic identity.

The acquisition of minority stakes, particularly between 25 and 50 percent, can trigger notification in jurisdictions that recognize joint control. The acquisition of decisive influence, even without majority ownership, can also trigger notification.

### Evaluate Timing And Sequencing Requirements

Merger notification timing affects deal scheduling, and different jurisdictions have different timing rules that must be sequenced.

Timing considerations:

- mandatory waiting periods before closing, typically 15 to 30 days for simple filings and longer for complex reviews;
- pre-notification consultations that may be required or advisable in some jurisdictions;
- sequencing of filings to align review timelines and avoid one jurisdiction blocking others;
- coordination with other regulatory approvals, such as foreign investment screening or sector-specific clearances;
- the risk that complex reviews in one jurisdiction extend the global timeline.

The firm should develop a filing strategy that identifies all required filings, their timing, and their interdependencies. Closing should not occur until all mandatory waiting periods have expired.

### Assess Gun-Jumping Risk

Gun-jumping occurs when parties implement a transaction before receiving clearance, or when they coordinate conduct before closing in ways that violate the standstill obligation.

Gun-jumping risk areas:

- transferring beneficial ownership or control before clearance;
- integrating operations, systems, or personnel before closing;
- exchanging competitively sensitive information outside clean team arrangements;
- coordinating pricing, output, or commercial decisions before closing;
- exercising influence over the target's competitive conduct before clearance.

The standstill obligation generally requires the parties to act independently until closing. Pre-closing integration and coordination must be structured carefully through clean teams and limited information exchange.

### Review Referral And Multi-Jurisdictional Coordination

In multi-jurisdictional transactions, referral mechanisms can transfer review from national authorities to supranational bodies, such as the EU Commission under the EUMR.

Referral considerations:

- one-stop-shop mechanisms, such as the EUMR, that allow single notification to the EU Commission when thresholds are met;
- referral requests from national authorities to the EU Commission or vice versa;
- cooperation between authorities through frameworks such as the ICN;
- the risk of divergent outcomes across jurisdictions.

The firm should assess whether a transaction qualifies for one-stop-shop treatment and should coordinate strategy across jurisdictions to manage divergent outcomes.

### Document The Notification Assessment And Strategy

The notification assessment and filing strategy should be documented to support decision-making and demonstrate compliance.

Documentation elements:

- the full jurisdictional mapping and threshold analysis;
- the filing requirements identified and the basis for each determination;
- the filing strategy, timing, and sequencing;
- the gun-jumping risk assessment and mitigation measures;
- antitrust counsel review and advice;
- periodic updates as the transaction structure evolves.

Documentation that the firm conducted a thorough jurisdictional analysis and obtained qualified advice is critical to defending against failure-to-notify or gun-jumping allegations.

## Common Traps

### Overlooking Secondary Jurisdictions

Focusing on primary jurisdictions and overlooking secondary jurisdictions with lower thresholds or effects-based jurisdiction can result in missed filings.

### Using Outdated Threshold Tables

Merger notification thresholds are updated periodically. Using outdated thresholds can result in incorrect filing determinations.

### Missing Minority Stake Notification Requirements

Acquisitions of minority stakes that confer joint control can trigger notification in jurisdictions that recognize this concept.

### Gun-Jumping Through Pre-Closing Integration

Integrating operations, exchanging sensitive information, or coordinating conduct before clearance violates the standstill obligation.

### Not Sequencing Filings Across Jurisdictions

Failing to sequence filings can result in one jurisdiction's timeline extending the global deal schedule or blocking closing.

### Assuming One-Stop-Shop Applies Automatically

One-stop-shop treatment under the EUMR requires meeting specific thresholds and may be subject to referral. Do not assume it applies without analysis.

### Treating Threshold Analysis As A Simple Checklist

Threshold analysis requires assessing transaction type, control acquisition, nexus, and evolving structures, not just applying a turnover table.

## Self-Check

- Are all potentially relevant jurisdictions mapped, including those with effects-based jurisdiction and secondary markets?
- Are current threshold tests applied, including global and local turnover, transaction value, market share, and nexus requirements?
- Is the transaction type assessed for coverage, including sole control, joint control, decisive influence, asset acquisitions, and joint ventures?
- Are timing and sequencing requirements assessed, with a filing strategy that aligns review timelines across jurisdictions?
- Is gun-jumping risk assessed, with standstill compliance and clean team arrangements for pre-closing information exchange?
- Are referral and one-stop-shop mechanisms evaluated, including EUMR qualification and referral risk?
- Are foreign investment screening and sector-specific clearances coordinated with merger notification timing?
- Is the notification assessment documented with jurisdictional mapping, threshold analysis, filing strategy, and counsel review?
- Are filing determinations updated as the transaction structure evolves, including changes in control acquisition or stake size?
- Is the merger notification assessment confirmed against current thresholds and requirements in each relevant jurisdiction with qualified counsel, rather than a generic standard?
