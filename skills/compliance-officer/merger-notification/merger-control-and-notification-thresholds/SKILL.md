# Merger Control And Notification Thresholds

Closing a transaction before clearing the required merger control filings is one of the most expensive compliance mistakes a company can make. The penalties for gun-jumping, implementing a deal before clearance, and for failing to notify can be severe, and in some jurisdictions the transaction can be held void or unwound. The compliance challenge is that notification obligations are triggered by mechanical thresholds that vary by jurisdiction, that multiple filings are often required for a single deal, and that the line between permissible pre-closing planning and impermissible gun-jumping is narrow. A compliance officer involved in M&A must know when to trigger the merger control analysis, how to map the filing requirements, and how to keep the parties separate until closing.

Use this skill before a transaction signs, during the pre-closing period, and when planning integration. The goal is to make the agent identify notification triggers early, map multi-jurisdictional obligations, and enforce the separation that prevents gun-jumping.

## Core Rules

### Trigger The Merger Control Analysis Early

Merger control must be considered before the deal is signed, not after. Waiting until signing to assess filing obligations delays closing and risks gun-jumping.

Build the analysis into deal planning by:

- running a jurisdiction-by-jurisdiction threshold screen as soon as the target and deal terms are known;
- identifying all jurisdictions where the parties have turnover, assets, or local presence;
- estimating timelines for each filing, because some reviews take weeks and others many months;
- building filing and clearance conditions into the signing and closing structure;
- assigning deal counsel and local counsel in each relevant jurisdiction early.

A late-discovered filing can push closing by months and can breach signing conditions. The screen should be a standard step in every transaction.

### Apply The HSR Thresholds For US Notification

In the US, the Hart-Scott-Rodino (HSR) Act requires notification of certain acquisitions when they exceed size-of-transaction and size-of-person thresholds, which are adjusted annually.

Key mechanics:

- size-of-transaction threshold: the acquisition must exceed a set dollar value (adjusted annually for GNP);
- size-of-person test: generally met when one party has certain annual net sales or total assets and the other meets a lower threshold, though it is waived at higher transaction values;
- the notification is required only where both the acquiring and acquired person are engaged in commerce or activities affecting commerce;
- certain exemptions apply, such as for certain intraperson transactions, acquisitions of goods in the ordinary course, or certain investment vehicles;
- a waiting period (commonly 30 days, 15 days for cash tender offers and bankruptcy) applies after filing, during which the parties cannot close, extendable by a second request.

The compliance officer does not need to memorize the dollar figures, which change yearly, but must know to run the analysis, to use current thresholds, and that the waiting period bars closing until it expires or is terminated.

### Apply The EUMR Thresholds For EU Notification

The EU Merger Regulation (EUMR) has a one-stop-shop for concentrations with a Community dimension, using combined and individual turnover thresholds.

Key mechanics:

- the combined aggregate worldwide turnover of all undertakings exceeds a set threshold; and
- the aggregate EU-wide turnover of each of at least two undertakings exceeds a set threshold; and
- the aggregate nationwide turnover in any single EU member state of each of at least two undertakings exceeds a set threshold, unless each achieves more than two-thirds of its EU-wide turnover in one and the same member state (the two-thirds rule, which can send the case to national review).

If the EUMR thresholds are met, the European Commission has exclusive jurisdiction and national filings are not required. If they are not met, national merger control rules in each member state apply, and multiple national filings may be needed. The compliance officer must run both the EUMR test and the national tests, because falling below EUMR thresholds does not mean no filing is required.

### Map National And Multi-Jurisdictional Filing Obligations

Most significant transactions require filings in multiple jurisdictions, each with its own thresholds, timelines, and substantive tests.

Map obligations by:

- identifying every jurisdiction where either party has turnover, assets, operations, or local nexus;
- applying each jurisdiction's specific thresholds, which may be based on local turnover, assets, market share, or transaction value;
- noting jurisdictions with mandatory pre-closing suspension (standstill) versus voluntary or post-closing regimes;
- accounting for jurisdictions where closing before clearance can trigger fines or voidness;
- sequencing filings to manage timelines, evidence gathering, and information exchange constraints.

Common traps include missing a national filing because the EUMR one-stop-shop was assumed to cover it, or missing a filing in a jurisdiction where the nexus is asset-based rather than turnover-based. The map must be explicit and reviewed by local counsel in each jurisdiction.

### Prevent Gun-Jumping Before Closing

Gun-jumping is the implementation of a transaction before clearance, and it is a serious violation in both the US and EU. The risk arises because integration planning inevitably creates pressure to coordinate before closing.

Gun-jumping takes two forms:

- premature exercise of beneficial ownership or control, such as taking operational decisions for the target before closing;
- pre-closing information exchange or coordination that goes beyond what is necessary for planning and that harms competition, such as exchanging competitively sensitive information or coordinating pricing.

Controls to prevent gun-jumping:

- maintain separate management and decision-making until closing, with the acquirer not directing target operations;
- limit pre-closing integration to planning that does not involve implementation;
- use clean teams and data rooms to control information flow, especially for competitively sensitive data;
- do not coordinate commercial conduct, pricing, customer approaches, or marketing before closing;
- document the boundary between permissible planning and impermissible implementation;
- train deal teams and integration teams on the gun-jumping rules.

The line between planning and implementation is fact-specific and counsel must guide it, but the compliance officer enforces the structural separation.

### Manage The Timeline And Conditions

Merger review can be the longest part of a transaction, and conditions imposed by authorities can reshape the deal.

Plan for:

- initial review periods and the possibility of extended or second-phase review;
- the possibility of remedies or divestitures required to clear the deal;
- coordination of clearance conditions across jurisdictions, because a remedy in one may affect the deal elsewhere;
- long-stop dates and break fees that depend on regulatory outcomes;
- communication with the business about what can and cannot happen during the waiting period.

## Common Traps

### Treating Merger Notification As A Closing Formality

Notification is a pre-closing obligation with waiting periods. Treating it as paperwork to be filed at closing guarantees a violation.

### Missing National Filings After Assuming EUMR Coverage

Falling below EUMR thresholds sends the case to national review, which may require multiple filings. Assuming the EU one-stop-shop applies when it does not is a common and costly error.

### Using Stale HSR Thresholds

HSR thresholds are adjusted annually. Using last year's figures can produce a wrong conclusion about whether notification is required.

### Gun-Jumping Through Aggressive Integration

Integration teams under pressure to deliver synergies may begin implementing before closing, crossing from planning to control. The separation must be enforced structurally.

### Exchanging Competitively Sensitive Information Pre-Closing

Even with good intentions, exchanging pricing, customer, or strategy data before closing can be gun-jumping or an information exchange violation, unless controlled through clean teams.

### Missing Asset-Based Or Nexus-Based Filings

Some jurisdictions trigger on assets or local presence rather than turnover. A target with no local turnover but significant local assets can still trigger a filing.

### Closing Before All Clearances

Closing before every required clearance expires or is granted can void the transaction and trigger fines. The closing condition must verify all clearances.

## Self-Check

- Is a jurisdiction-by-jurisdiction threshold screen run before signing, using current HSR, EUMR, and national thresholds?
- Are all jurisdictions with turnover, asset, or local nexus identified, including those where the trigger is asset-based rather than turnover-based?
- Is the EUMR one-stop-shop correctly assessed, with national filings planned where EUMR thresholds are not met or the two-thirds rule applies?
- Are waiting periods, standstill obligations, and pre-closing suspension rules understood for each jurisdiction?
- Is gun-jumping prevented through separate management, planning-only integration, clean teams, and no pre-closing commercial coordination?
- Is pre-closing information exchange controlled through data rooms and clean teams, with no exchange of competitively sensitive data outside those controls?
- Are timelines, possible second-phase review, and potential remedies or divestitures built into the deal structure and long-stop dates?
- Are clearance conditions coordinated across jurisdictions, recognizing that a remedy in one may affect the deal elsewhere?
- Are deal and integration teams trained on the gun-jumping boundary between permissible planning and impermissible implementation?
- Does the closing condition verify that all required clearances have expired or been granted before the transaction is implemented?
