# Compliance Audit Program And Planning

A compliance audit program is the structured plan that decides which areas of the organization will be independently tested, how deeply, how often, and by whom. Without a disciplined planning process, audit effort drifts toward what is easy, familiar, or recently criticized, while high-risk areas go untested for years. A plan that is not risk-based, not resourced, or not reviewed will be challenged by regulators and auditors as inadequate regardless of how good individual audits are.

Use this skill before drafting an annual or multi-year audit plan, defining audit scope and objectives, setting coverage frequency, allocating auditors, or defending the plan to the audit committee. The goal is to make the agent treat audit planning as a risk-based resource-allocation exercise with documented rationale, not as a rotation of favorite topics.

## Core Rules

### Start Planning From The Enterprise Risk Assessment, Not From Last Year's Plan

The audit plan should be derived from the organization's compliance risk assessment and regulatory landscape, not inherited from the prior year. A plan that repeats because it is convenient is not risk-based.

Inputs to planning should include:

- the enterprise compliance risk assessment and its inherent and residual risk ratings;
- regulatory priorities, enforcement trends, and examination findings;
- prior audit results, open issues, and repeat findings;
- monitoring and testing results from the first and second lines;
- investigations, incidents, and whistleblower reports;
- new products, markets, technologies, vendors, and acquisitions;
- legal and regulatory changes;
- control changes, system migrations, and reorganizations;
- complaints, customer harm signals, and operational losses.

Document how each input influenced the plan. If a high-risk area is excluded, state why and what compensating coverage exists.

### Define Audit Objectives, Scope, And Boundaries Explicitly

An audit without a defined objective will produce unfocused findings and miss the real risk. Each audit in the plan should have a clear purpose.

For each engagement specify:

- audit objective and the question it answers;
- in-scope regulations, processes, entities, geographies, and period;
- out-of-scope items and the reason;
- the controls or obligations to be evaluated;
- the criteria against which compliance will be measured;
- the assurance level sought;
- dependencies on other audits, monitoring, or third-party reports;
- the expected deliverable and audience.

Scope creep during execution often traces back to an unclear objective. A tight objective also helps the auditee understand what is being tested and prepare relevant evidence.

### Set Coverage Frequency Based On Risk, Not Rotation

Frequency should reflect risk, volatility, and history. Auditing every area on the same cycle wastes effort on stable low-risk areas and leaves volatile high-risk areas under-covered.

Factors influencing frequency:

- inherent risk level and residual risk after controls;
- rate of change in the process, system, regulation, or personnel;
- prior audit findings and whether they recurred;
- control failures detected by monitoring;
- regulatory expectations for specific areas;
- financial exposure, customer harm potential, and reputational sensitivity;
- time since last independent review;
- reliance placed on first- and second-line testing.

For high-risk, volatile, or recently failed areas, plan more frequent or deeper coverage. For stable, well-controlled, low-risk areas, a longer cycle may be defensible but should be documented. Some regulators expect coverage of certain topics at defined intervals; respect those expectations.

### Allocate Resources Against Risk, Not Against Availability

Resource allocation is where many plans fail. The plan may list twenty audits and staff ten. A plan that cannot be executed is not a plan.

Address:

- total available audit hours and skills;
- hours required per engagement based on scope, complexity, and locations;
- skill gaps for specialized areas such as data analytics, privacy, sanctions, AI, or trading;
- need for co-sourcing, subject-matter experts, or technology-assisted audit techniques;
- contingency for unplanned work such as investigations, exams, or urgent requests;
- training and development time;
- quality assurance and review time.

If resources are insufficient to cover high-risk areas, escalate the gap to the audit committee as a risk. Do not silently drop audits to fit the budget.

### Build A Multi-Year View, Not Only An Annual Snapshot

Some risks warrant coverage every year; others every two or three years. An annual plan that ignores the multi-year cycle can leave an area untested for an unacceptably long time.

Maintain:

- a coverage map showing when each risk area was last audited and is next due;
- a rolling horizon that anticipates future engagements;
- carryover audits and their expected completion;
- thematic reviews that cut across processes;
- unallocated capacity for emerging risks.

Review the multi-year map each cycle to confirm no high-risk area is overdue and that the plan remains aligned with current risk.

### Coordinate With The First And Second Lines To Avoid Duplication

The audit plan should account for testing already performed by compliance monitoring, risk management, and business control functions. Duplication wastes resources and frustrates the business, while gaps leave risks untested.

Map:

- what the first line self-tests and how reliably;
- what the second line monitors and reports;
- where audit can place reliance on that work after evaluating its quality;
- where independent audit coverage is still required regardless;
- how findings and issues flow between the lines.

Reliance decisions must be documented and based on evidence of the other function's competence, independence within its role, and testing quality. Do not place reliance merely because work was done.

### Include Flexibility For Emerging And Unplanned Risks

A rigid plan that cannot respond to a major incident, enforcement action, or new regulation is fragile. Reserve capacity and define a change process.

Build in:

- a reserve of unallocated hours for emerging issues;
- a process to add, defer, or reprioritize engagements during the year;
- criteria for what triggers a plan change;
- approval and documentation requirements for changes;
- communication to the audit committee of material plan changes.

Document the reason for each change so the plan's evolution is transparent and defensible.

### Define Quality, Independence, And Competence Requirements

The plan is only credible if the auditors are independent, competent, and held to quality standards. Plan for these as deliberately as for scope.

Ensure:

- auditor independence from the areas audited, including no operational responsibility;
- competence matched to the risk, with training or experts where needed;
- a quality assurance review program for the audit function itself;
- documentation standards and review of workpapers;
- time for post-audit follow-up and issue verification.

### Present And Defend The Plan To The Audit Committee

The audit committee or board should approve the plan and understand its risk basis. A plan presented without rationale will be rubber-stamped and will not provide genuine oversight.

In the presentation include:

- the risk assessment basis and key drivers;
- coverage of high-risk areas and any gaps;
- resource capacity and constraints;
- reliance on other assurance providers;
- changes from the prior plan and why;
- flexibility for emerging risks;
- how plan performance will be reported.

## Common Traps

### Inheriting Last Year's Plan Without Re-Justifying It

Convenience produces stale plans. Re-derive coverage from current risk each cycle.

### Auditing What Is Easy Or Familiar

Stable, well-documented processes get audited repeatedly while complex or opaque areas are avoided. Allocate effort by risk, not by comfort.

### Overstating Coverage With A Plan That Cannot Be Executed

A plan listing more audits than resources allow creates a false impression of coverage. Match scope to capacity and escalate gaps.

### Setting Frequency By Rotation Rather Than Risk

Equal cycles for all areas under-cover volatile risks. Differentiate frequency by risk, change, and history.

### Placing Reliance On Second-Line Work Without Evaluating It

Relying on monitoring or business testing without assessing its quality transfers assurance risk silently. Evaluate independence, competence, and evidence before relying.

### Excluding Areas Because They Are Hard To Audit

Complex trading, AI systems, offshore entities, or opaque third parties are often exactly where risk concentrates. Excluding them because they are hard is a planning failure.

### Ignoring Carryover And Unplanned Work In Capacity

Plans that assume uninterrupted execution collapse when investigations or exams arise. Reserve capacity and define a change process.

## Self-Check

- Is the audit plan derived from the enterprise risk assessment, regulatory priorities, prior findings, monitoring results, incidents, and changes rather than inherited from last year?
- Does each engagement have a defined objective, scope, in-scope and out-of-scope items, criteria, assurance level, and audience?
- Is coverage frequency based on risk, volatility, history, and regulatory expectation rather than uniform rotation?
- Are total resources, required hours, skill gaps, co-sourcing, contingency, and training accounted for, with gaps escalated?
- Is there a multi-year coverage map showing last-audited and next-due dates with no high-risk area overdue?
- Is coordination with first- and second-line testing documented, with reliance decisions based on evaluated quality?
- Is there reserved capacity and a defined change process for emerging risks, investigations, and regulatory events?
- Are independence, competence, quality assurance, and workpaper review standards built into the plan?
- Is the plan presented to the audit committee with its risk basis, coverage, constraints, reliance, and changes?
- Are material plan changes during the year documented, approved, and communicated?
