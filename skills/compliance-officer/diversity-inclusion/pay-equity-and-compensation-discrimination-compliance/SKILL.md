# Pay Equity And Compensation Discrimination Compliance

Pay equity compliance governs whether compensation differences across protected classes are justified by legitimate, job-related factors. The defining feature is that pay discrimination can persist invisibly across a workforce because compensation is set incrementally—offer at hire, raise at review, promotion, bonus—without anyone examining the cumulative pattern. The central difficulty is that a pay gap may reflect current discrimination, historical discrimination, or legitimate market and performance factors, and distinguishing among them requires rigorous analysis rather than assertion.

Use this skill before advising on pay equity audits, compensation structure design, salary history bans, pay transparency laws, or responses to pay discrimination claims. The goal is to make the agent identify the legal frameworks, the legitimate justifying factors, the audit methodology, and the remediation risks before concluding that pay practices are equitable.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Legal Frameworks For Pay Equity

Multiple laws govern pay equity, with different standards and scopes.

Map:

- equal pay for equal work laws requiring equal pay for substantially equal jobs;
- broader pay equity laws requiring equal pay for comparable or comparable worth work;
- Title VII prohibition on compensation discrimination as part of broader discrimination law;
- Lilly Ledbetter Fair Pay Act and the resetting of the limitations clock with each paycheck;
- state and local expansions including broader job comparison standards;
- pay transparency and salary range posting requirements;
- salary history ban laws.

The legal standard varies—some laws require "equal work," others "comparable work." State law often provides broader protection and additional tools.

### Identify Legitimate Factors That Justify Pay Differences

Pay differences are lawful if explained by legitimate, job-related factors.

Assess:

- seniority and tenure;
- merit and performance, documented and consistent;
- quantity or quality of production, measured objectively;
- education, training, or experience relevant to the job;
- shift differentials, location, and working conditions;
- market factors and competitive offers, where recognized by law;
- job level, scope, and responsibility differences.

The factors must be job-related and consistently applied. A factor that justifies one difference may not justify another. Some jurisdictions limit which factors are recognized.

### Conduct Rigorous Pay Equity Audits

A pay equity audit identifies unexplained pay gaps and their causes.

Implement:

- define the analysis group and comparison units (similarly situated employees);
- factor analysis controlling for legitimate factors;
- identification of unexplained gaps by protected class;
- statistical significance and practical magnitude;
- root-cause analysis of unexplained gaps;
- assessment of whether gaps reflect current or historical factors;
- privilege protection for the audit (attorney-client where available);
- remediation planning.

An audit that does not control for legitimate factors is meaningless. An audit that finds gaps and does not remediate creates evidence of knowledge. Privilege strategy should be determined before the audit begins.

### Comply With Salary History Bans

Many jurisdictions prohibit asking about or relying on salary history.

Control:

- prohibition on asking applicants about salary history;
- prohibition on relying on salary history to set pay;
- application and interview materials free of salary history questions;
- training for hiring managers and recruiters;
- reliance on market data and internal equity instead of history;
- exception for voluntary disclosure where limited.

Relying on salary history perpetuates historical discrimination. Pay should be set based on the role, market, and the applicant's qualifications, not prior pay.

### Comply With Pay Transparency Requirements

Pay transparency laws require disclosure of pay ranges.

Implement:

- posting of salary or wage ranges in job advertisements where required;
- providing pay ranges to applicants upon request or interview;
- providing pay ranges to current employees for their role;
- internal posting of promotion opportunities;
- definitions of what must be included (bonus, equity, benefits);
- state and local variations in scope and timing.

Pay transparency is expanding rapidly. Job postings that omit required ranges violate the law in covered jurisdictions.

### Design Equitable Compensation Structures

Compensation structures should be designed to prevent inequity.

Cover:

- pay bands or ranges for each role and level;
- criteria for placement within bands;
- guidelines for raises, promotions, and bonuses;
- market benchmarking with equity review;
- manager training on equitable pay decisions;
- oversight and approval for out-of-band pay;
- periodic review of structure effectiveness.

Discretionary pay decisions without guidelines create inequity. Structure and oversight reduce the risk of both discrimination and perception of unfairness.

### Manage Variable Pay And Incentive Equity

Bonuses, commissions, and equity introduce additional equity risks.

Assess:

- objective criteria for variable pay eligibility and amounts;
- disparate impact of performance ratings on variable pay;
- commission structures that disadvantage protected classes;
- equity grant practices and vesting;
- access to high-visibility projects that drive performance ratings;
- transparency of variable pay criteria;
- documentation of variable pay decisions.

Variable pay often rests on subjective performance ratings that can carry bias. The criteria and their application must be examined.

### Remediate Identified Gaps Carefully

Remediation of pay gaps must be done without creating new legal risks.

Control:

- analysis of whether to adjust pay up or restructure;
- avoidance of pay reductions to achieve equity;
- communication that does not admit liability;
- consistency in remediation approach;
- documentation of the legitimate basis for adjustments;
- ongoing monitoring to prevent recurrence;
- consideration of whether adjustments create claims for back pay.

Pay reductions to achieve equity are rarely advisable and can generate claims. Adjustments up, structured as market corrections, are generally safer.

## Common Traps

### Pay Gap Attributed To Market Without Rigorous Analysis

"Market factors" asserted without factor analysis do not explain a gap and can mask discrimination.

### Salary History Used To Set Pay In A Banned Jurisdiction

Asking about or relying on salary history where prohibited is a violation regardless of intent.

### Audit Without Privilege Strategy

An unprivileged audit that finds gaps creates discoverable evidence of knowledge without protection.

### Pay Transparency Required Range Omitted From Postings

Job postings without required salary ranges violate pay transparency laws.

### Performance-Based Pay With Subjective Ratings Carrying Bias

Variable pay resting on subjective ratings can perpetuate discrimination invisibly.

### Remediation By Pay Reduction

Reducing higher-paid employees to achieve equity generates claims and morale damage.

## Self-Check

- Is the applicable legal framework identified, including equal pay, comparable worth, Title VII, state expansions, pay transparency, and salary history bans?
- Are pay differences analyzed against legitimate factors including seniority, merit, production, education, experience, location, and job level?
- Is a rigorous pay equity audit conducted with similarly situated groupings, factor analysis, unexplained gap identification, root-cause analysis, privilege strategy, and remediation planning?
- Are salary history bans followed in hiring, with no questions about or reliance on prior pay?
- are pay transparency requirements met including range posting, applicant and employee disclosure, and internal promotion posting?
- Are compensation structures designed with bands, criteria, guidelines, benchmarking, manager training, oversight, and periodic review?
- Is variable pay assessed for disparate impact, objective criteria, commission equity, and documentation?
- Is remediation done through pay increases, consistent communication, and ongoing monitoring, without reductions or admissions?
- Is the distinction between current discrimination, historical discrimination, and legitimate factors assessed rigorously?
- Are state and local pay equity, transparency, and history rules applied in each jurisdiction?
