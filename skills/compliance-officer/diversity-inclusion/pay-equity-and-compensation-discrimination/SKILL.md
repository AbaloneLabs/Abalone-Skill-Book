# Pay Equity And Compensation Discrimination

Pay equity analysis is one of the most sensitive and consequential judgments in workplace compliance. The defining difficulty is that compensation disparities can arise from legitimate structural factors or from hidden bias, and the line between them turns on rigorous statistical analysis rather than intuition. A poorly designed audit can either miss real disparities or manufacture apparent violations through flawed methodology, and the results themselves can become discoverable evidence in litigation.

Use this skill before conducting a pay equity audit, designing compensation structures, responding to an equal pay claim, or advising on pay transparency obligations. The goal is to make the agent select defensible comparison groups, apply appropriate statistical methods, control for legitimate explanatory factors, and recognize where the analysis requires expert and legal supervision. Pay equity rules vary by jurisdiction and any audit should be conducted under privilege with qualified counsel.

## Core Rules

### Define Defensible Comparison Groups

Pay equity analysis depends entirely on comparing the right employees. A comparison group that is too broad or too narrow distorts results.

Build comparison groups around:

- substantially similar or equal work, depending on the jurisdiction's standard;
- jobs requiring substantially equal skill, effort, and responsibility;
- performance under similar working conditions;
- comparable worth or equal value standards where a jurisdiction applies them;
- job architecture and leveling that groups genuinely comparable roles.

The federal equal pay standard requires equal work, while several state laws use the broader substantially similar work standard. The standard determines who can be compared, and mis-scoping the comparison group is the most common methodological error.

### Apply Appropriate Statistical Methods

Statistical method determines whether a disparity is real or noise.

Use:

- regression analysis controlling for legitimate explanatory factors;
- appropriate tests for statistical significance;
- analysis of both average pay and pay range placement;
- examination of starting pay, raises, and promotion velocity;
- cohort and longitudinal analysis to detect accumulation of bias over time;
- sufficient sample size to support reliable conclusions.

Simple averages without controlling for legitimate factors will overstate disparities. Conversely, over-controlling for factors that are themselves contaminated by bias will mask real disparities. Methodology must be defensible and ideally supervised by a labor economist or statistician.

### Control For Legitimate Explanatory Factors

Legitimate factors explain part of any pay gap, but the factors themselves must be scrutinized.

Legitimate factors may include:

- seniority and tenure;
- education, experience, and specialized skills;
- performance and merit;
- location and cost of labor differentials;
- shift differentials and hazard pay;
- prior salary, but only where a salary history ban does not prohibit reliance on it.

Each factor must be examined for whether it is itself a product of bias. Prior salary is increasingly prohibited as a factor because it imports historical discrimination. Performance ratings can encode subjective bias and must be validated.

### Examine The Full Compensation Lifecycle

Pay equity is not only base salary. Disparities accumulate across the compensation lifecycle.

Analyze:

- starting pay and offer practices;
- annual raise allocation and merit increase processes;
- bonus, commission, and incentive pay;
- equity and stock grants;
- promotion velocity and access to higher-level roles;
- access to high-visibility projects and stretch assignments.

A clean base-pay analysis can hide large disparities in bonus and equity. Promotion velocity disparities are often the root cause of pay gaps that appear at each level.

### Address Pay Transparency And Salary History Rules

Pay transparency and salary history bans are expanding rapidly and change the compliance landscape.

Implement:

- pay range posting requirements in listed jurisdictions;
- prohibition on asking about or relying on salary history;
- employee rights to discuss and disclose pay;
- pay scale disclosure on request where mandated;
- recordkeeping of pay range basis.

These rules are designed to expose and reduce pay gaps but create their own compliance obligations. Posting a range that does not reflect actual practice creates exposure.

### Remediate Disparities Safely And Consistently

When disparities are found, remediation must be careful and consistent.

Remediate by:

- addressing only unexplained disparities that survive legitimate factor controls;
- avoiding across-the-board raises that create new inequities;
- documenting the legitimate basis for pay decisions;
- protecting the analysis under privilege to the extent possible;
- avoiding retaliation against employees who raise pay equity concerns.

Remediation that is not based on sound analysis can itself become evidence. Inconsistent or retaliatory adjustment creates new liability.

### Conduct Audits Under Privilege

Pay equity audits are sensitive and should be conducted under attorney-client privilege where possible.

Structure:

- engage qualified employment counsel to direct the audit;
- use economists or statisticians as agents of counsel;
- control distribution of results;
- document the privilege structure;
- recognize that privilege protections vary and are not absolute.

Privilege does not protect the underlying pay decisions, only the analysis and communications. Mandatory pay data reporting to agencies may override privilege for some data.

## Common Traps

### Comparing Too Broadly Or Too Narrowly

A comparison group that mixes non-comparable roles, or splits comparable roles too finely, distorts the analysis.

### Over-Controlling For Contaminated Factors

Controlling for prior salary or subjective performance that encodes bias masks real disparities.

### Ignoring Bonus And Equity Disparities

A clean base-pay analysis can hide large and growing disparities in variable compensation.

### Relying On Prior Salary Where Banned

Using salary history in a ban jurisdiction is itself a violation regardless of intent.

### Remediation That Creates Retaliation Or New Inequity

Poorly designed raises or targeting of those who complained create new liability.

## Self-Check

- Are comparison groups defined around substantially similar or equal work using the correct jurisdictional standard, with job architecture supporting the groupings?
- Is the statistical methodology appropriate, controlling for legitimate factors with sufficient sample size and significance testing, ideally supervised by an expert?
- Are explanatory factors themselves scrutinized for bias contamination, with prior salary excluded where a salary history ban applies?
- Does the analysis examine the full compensation lifecycle including starting pay, raises, bonus, equity, and promotion velocity?
- Are pay transparency and salary history ban obligations met in each listed jurisdiction, with ranges reflecting actual practice?
- Is remediation based only on unexplained disparities, documented, consistent, and non-retaliatory?
- Is the audit structured under attorney-client privilege with qualified counsel directing the work, recognizing privilege limits and mandatory reporting?
- Where disparities or claims arise, has the matter been escalated to qualified employment counsel, recognizing that this guidance is general and not jurisdiction-specific legal advice?
