# Journey Map Creation

A journey map is a synthesis artifact, not a decoration. Its value is forcing the team to look at an experience longitudinally, across stages, and to hold actions, thoughts, emotions, and pain points in one view so that invisible problems become visible. The judgment problem is not how to fill in the rows of a template. It is deciding whose journey is being mapped, whether the map is grounded in real evidence or invented optimism, how honestly the emotional dimension is represented, and whether the map will actually change decisions or just look impressive on a wall. Agents tend to fail by mapping a generic average user who does not exist, by inventing positive emotions to avoid uncomfortable findings, by collapsing multiple distinct journeys into one blurred map, and by producing a map so abstract that no one can act on it.

Use this skill when creating or revising a journey map, experience map, current-state map, or future-state map for a product, service, or relationship lifecycle. The goal is a map that is grounded, honest, scoped to a real user, and specific enough to drive design and prioritization decisions.

## Core Rules

### Ground The Map In A Specific Persona And Scenario

A journey map is only meaningful when it represents a specific type of user in a specific context. A map of "the user" maps no one, because no single user has one journey. Before laying out stages, define whose journey this is: their goals, constraints, prior experience, and the scenario that triggers the journey. A first-time buyer, a returning expert, and a reluctant participant have different actions, emotions, and pain points at every stage.

Define before mapping:

- which persona or segment this map represents, and why that one;
- the triggering scenario and the user's initial goal;
- what the user already knows and expects entering the journey;
- what constraints they carry, such as time, money, device, or stress.

If the team needs to understand multiple segments, create separate maps rather than averaging them into a meaningless composite.

### Decide Current-State Versus Future-State Explicitly

Current-state maps document an experience as it is today, including its real problems. Future-state maps propose an experience as it could be. These serve different purposes and must not be blurred together. A current-state map is a diagnostic: it surfaces pain to fix. A future-state map is a hypothesis: it proposes a better journey that still needs validation.

State which one you are making, and do not let aspiration leak into a current-state map. The most common credibility failure is a "current-state" map that quietly smooths over the bad parts because the team does not want to document how poor the experience really is. If you are mapping the current state, document the friction honestly, including moments the team is embarrassed by.

### Build Stages From The User's Perspective, Not The Product's

Stages should describe phases of the user's experience, not internal product modules or organizational handoffs. "Discover, evaluate, purchase, onboard, use, get support, renew" is a user-facing lifecycle. "Marketing page, sales team, onboarding module, ticketing system" is an org chart. The first reveals the user's journey; the second reveals the company's structure, which is rarely what the user cares about.

Test each stage label by asking whether a user would recognize and name that phase of their own experience. If the label only makes sense inside the company, reframe it from the user's viewpoint. Stages that track internal departments hide the seams where the user experience actually breaks.

### Represent Emotions Honestly, Including The Negative

The emotional row is what distinguishes a journey map from a process flow, and it is the row most often distorted. Teams tend to either omit negative emotions, soften them into neutral, or draw a tidy upward curve toward delight that does not match reality. A journey map that shows no real lows is not grounded in research; it is a story the team is telling itself.

Represent emotions based on evidence:

- cite research, interviews, or observed behavior for emotional claims;
- include the frustrating, anxious, confused, and disappointed moments, not just the peaks;
- mark the emotional low points explicitly, because they are where design effort pays off most;
- distinguish mild dissatisfaction from genuine distress or anger.

The dips in the emotional line are the most valuable part of the map. They point directly at the problems worth solving.

### Separate Actions, Thoughts, Emotions, And Pain Points Into Distinct Rows

Each dimension reveals different problems, and collapsing them loses information. Actions show what the user does; thoughts show what they believe or wonder; emotions show how they feel; pain points show what blocks or frustrates them. A row that mixes "user feels confused and clicks the wrong button and cannot find help" conflates three different insights and three different possible fixes.

Keep rows distinct and aligned by stage, so that at each stage the reader can see the action, the thought, the emotion, and the barrier together. This alignment is what lets the team spot, for example, that a negative emotion at one stage is caused by a missing thought or a blocking pain point at the previous stage.

### Anchor Every Claim In Research Or Label It As Assumption

A journey map carries an authority that exceeds its evidence, because it looks definitive. Teams treat well-designed maps as fact. This is dangerous when the map is built from assumptions, anecdotes, or the designer's imagination. Be explicit about the evidentiary basis of each part of the map.

For each stage and row, note:

- whether it is grounded in research, observation, support data, or analytics;
- whether it is an assumption or hypothesis that needs validation;
- where the evidence is thin or contested.

Labeling assumptions protects the map's credibility and tells the team where more research is needed before acting. A map that pretends everything is confirmed will mislead decisions.

### Convert Pain Points Into Specific Opportunities, Not Vague Aspirations

The purpose of surfacing pain points is to act on them. Each significant pain point should connect to a specific opportunity: a concrete change that could reduce the friction. "Make onboarding better" is not an opportunity; it is a wish. "Reduce the number of fields the user must fill before reaching the first useful screen" is an opportunity.

For each major pain point, specify:

- what specifically causes the pain;
- what a better experience would look like, concretely;
- which stage and action it affects;
- whether it is a quick win or a structural change.

Opportunities that are specific and tied to stages become a backlog. Opportunities that are generic become wallpaper.

### Keep The Map Scoped To A Traversable Length

A journey map that tries to cover an entire customer relationship at high resolution becomes unmanageable and unreadable. Decide the scope deliberately: a full lifecycle at low resolution, or a critical subset at high resolution. A map of the entire relationship with five stages is useful for strategy; a map of the onboarding subset with ten detailed steps is useful for design. Trying to do both in one artifact fails at both.

## Common Traps

### Mapping A Generic Average User

A composite "typical user" represents no real person and smooths away the differences that matter. Map a specific persona, or make separate maps for distinct segments.

### Smoothing Over The Negative Emotions

A journey line that trends comfortably upward is usually a sign the team avoided documenting real friction. The lows are the most actionable part of the map.

### Confusing Current And Future State

Letting aspiration leak into a current-state map hides how bad the present experience is. Decide which you are mapping and keep them separate.

### Stages That Mirror Internal Departments

Stage names that track the org chart hide the seams where the user experience breaks. Reframe stages from the user's perspective.

### Collapsing Dimensions Into One Row

Mixing actions, thoughts, emotions, and pain points loses the ability to diagnose causes. Keep dimensions in separate, aligned rows.

### Presenting Assumptions As Confirmed Findings

A polished map carries false authority. Label what is researched versus assumed so the team knows where to trust and where to validate.

### Generic Opportunities

Pain points converted into "improve the experience" produce no actionable backlog. Tie each opportunity to a specific, stage-level change.

### Over-Scoping The Map

Covering an entire relationship at full resolution produces an unreadable wall. Choose breadth or depth deliberately, not both at once.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The map represents a specific persona and triggering scenario, not a generic average user.
- [ ] It is explicitly labeled as current-state or future-state, with no aspiration leaking into a current-state map.
- [ ] Stage labels describe phases of the user's experience, not internal departments or product modules.
- [ ] The emotional row includes genuine negative moments based on evidence, not a smoothed upward curve.
- [ ] Actions, thoughts, emotions, and pain points occupy distinct rows aligned by stage.
- [ ] Each claim is marked as research-grounded or assumption, so the team knows what to trust and what to validate.
- [ ] Significant pain points connect to specific, stage-level opportunities, not vague aspirations.
- [ ] The scope is deliberately chosen for breadth or depth, not an unmanageable attempt at both.
- [ ] The map is specific enough that a reader can point to stages and decide what to design or fix next.
