# Representation In Design

Representation in design is not decorative diversity. The people shown, named, and implied by an interface send a constant message about who the product is for and who belongs. Stock photos chosen quickly, default avatars, example names in mockups, and the skin tones of emoji and icons each make a claim about the intended audience. When those choices are made casually, they often default to a narrow norm and quietly tell everyone else they are an afterthought.

Use this skill before selecting imagery, building avatar systems, writing example data, designing characters, choosing iconography, or reviewing a design's visual representation. The goal is to prevent the agent from shipping tokenistic, stereotyped, or erasing representation, or from treating diversity as a finish applied on top of an unchanged default.

## Core Rules

### Treat Representation As Structure, Not Decoration

Representation appears in places teams forget to audit: the default profile picture, the placeholder name in a form, the example transaction in a demo, the illustration style, the skin tone of the default emoji, the silhouette in an onboarding screen. Each is a default that tells users who the product imagines.

Map every place a human is represented or implied, and decide each deliberately. A diverse hero image paired with an all-default-avatar system still signals that diversity is for marketing, not for the product.

### Avoid Tokenism And Performative Inclusion

Tokenism is the inclusion of a single representative of a group to appear inclusive without changing the underlying default. One darker-skinned figure in an otherwise homogeneous set, one wheelchair user shown only in accessibility marketing, or one woman in a male-coded role reads as a checkbox, not as belonging.

Strong representation is:

- proportional and varied, not a single token in a corner;
- present across roles, including leadership, expertise, and authority;
- not confined to "diversity" contexts such as accessibility or inclusion pages;
- consistent between marketing imagery and in-product imagery.

### Reject Stereotypes And Reductive Categorization

Imagery that relies on stereotypes, whether of gender, age, ethnicity, disability, religion, or region, flattens people into caricature. Common failures include coding technical roles as male, care roles as female, older adults as helpless, disabled people as inspirational props, or entire regions as a single look.

When selecting or creating representation, ask:

- is this person shown with agency, or as a prop for a narrative about someone else;
- does the role assignment repeat a cultural stereotype;
- is the group shown with internal variation, or as a monolith;
- would a member of the group recognize themselves, or feel reduced.

### Make Avatar And Identity Systems Flexible

Default avatars and identity systems encode assumptions about gender, names, and appearance. A binary gender avatar set, a name field that rejects non-Latin scripts, or a profile that forces a title all impose a structure on identity.

Design avatar and identity systems to:

- offer a range of skin tones, hair, features, and body types, not a single default;
- allow non-representational options such as initials, objects, or custom uploads;
- avoid forcing gendered defaults or binary choices;
- accept diverse name formats, scripts, and name lengths;
- let users opt out of representation entirely where it is not needed.

### Audit Example Data And Mockups

Example data is representation too. The names, photos, addresses, and scenarios used in prototypes, documentation, and demos shape what feels normal. A mockup full of homogeneous names trains the team and stakeholders to imagine a narrow user base.

Use varied, realistic example data across names, regions, family structures, and scenarios. Avoid placeholder names that are jokes, culturally loaded, or stereotyped. Treat demo data as part of the design, not as filler.

### Consider Cultural Meaning Of Imagery

The same image carries different meaning across cultures. Gestures, clothing, colors, symbols, and composition can signal respect in one context and offense in another. Imagery that reads as neutral in one region may be exclusionary or inappropriate elsewhere.

When imagery may travel across markets:

- avoid gestures and symbols with region-specific negative meaning;
- check clothing, religious symbols, and body exposure against target audiences;
- prefer context-appropriate photography or illustration over universal reuse;
- involve reviewers familiar with the target culture where feasible.

### Represent Disability Without Reducing People To It

Disabled people are often shown only as objects of inspiration, pity, or accessibility compliance. Represent disabled people as full users, customers, professionals, and community members, not only in accessibility-adjacent contexts. Avoid the inspiration narrative, where a disabled person's presence is framed as uplifting rather than ordinary.

When showing disability, prefer authentic representation, accurate depiction of assistive technology, and roles that are not defined by the disability.

## Common Traps

### The Single Token Figure

Adding one representative of a group to claim diversity, while the rest of the design defaults to a narrow norm, signals that inclusion is performative.

### Diversity Only In Marketing

Hero pages and ads show varied people, while the in-product avatars, defaults, and examples remain homogeneous. Users notice the gap.

### Default Avatar As A Hidden Norm

A light-skinned, male-coded, short-haired default avatar quietly defines the assumed user across the entire product.

### Stereotyped Role Assignment

Repeatedly casting the same groups into the same roles, such as women in care roles and men in technical roles, reinforces the stereotype the design claims to move past.

### Inspiration-Style Disability Representation

Showing disabled people only to evoke emotion or to signal accessibility compliance, rather than as ordinary users with agency.

### Unaudited Example Data

Mockups and demos filled with homogeneous names and photos that quietly train the team to imagine a narrow audience.

### Reused Imagery Across Cultures

Treating an image as universal when its gestures, clothing, or symbols carry different, sometimes offensive, meaning in another market.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Every place a human is represented or implied, including defaults, avatars, and example data, was identified and decided deliberately.
- [ ] Representation is proportional and varied across roles, not a single token figure confined to diversity contexts.
- [ ] No role assignment repeats a gender, age, ethnic, disability, or regional stereotype.
- [ ] Avatar and identity systems support varied skin tones, features, non-representational options, and diverse name formats without forced binary defaults.
- [ ] Example data, mockups, and demos use varied, realistic names, regions, and scenarios rather than homogeneous filler.
- [ ] Disabled people are shown with agency in ordinary roles, not only as inspiration or accessibility symbols.
- [ ] Imagery intended for multiple markets was checked for culturally specific meaning of gestures, clothing, colors, and symbols.
- [ ] In-product representation matches the diversity shown in marketing, with no gap between the two.
- [ ] The default avatar, placeholder name, and example user were chosen deliberately, not inherited uncritically.
- [ ] A member of each represented group would more likely recognize themselves than feel reduced or tokenized.
