# Brand Guidelines

Brand guidelines are the rules that keep a brand recognizable when many people apply it. They look like a style guide document, but they are really a system of decisions about voice, visual identity, and usage boundaries that determine whether the brand stays coherent or fragments across teams. Agents tend to treat guidelines as a list of assets, write rules that are too vague to enforce, or document the brand so exhaustively that no one reads it. The harm is a brand that drifts: inconsistent voice across channels, logos misused by partners, and an identity that looks different everywhere it appears.

Use this skill before finalizing brand documentation, usage rules, or governance. The goal is to prevent the agent from writing guidelines that cannot be followed, from leaving usage boundaries undefined, or from creating documentation so heavy that teams ignore it and improvise.

## Core Rules

### Document The Brand's Core Before Its Rules

Brand guidelines that jump straight to logo sizes and color hex values miss the foundation. The rules only make sense in light of what the brand stands for, who it is for, and how it should feel. Without the core documented, teams apply the visual assets without the judgment that makes them work together.

Establish the core first:

- define the brand's purpose, mission, and the problem it exists to solve;
- articulate the brand personality and the values that shape decisions;
- describe the target audience and the relationship the brand wants with them;
- state the positioning relative to alternatives.

The visual and verbal rules that follow should be traceable to this core. A rule with no connection to the brand's identity is arbitrary and will be questioned or ignored.

### Define Voice And Tone As Actionable Guidance

Voice is the brand's personality expressed in words; tone is how that voice adapts to context. Guidelines often describe voice with adjectives ("friendly, professional, witty") but stop there, leaving writers to guess what those mean in practice. Actionable voice guidance gives examples and boundaries.

Make voice guidance usable:

- define voice attributes with do and do-not examples for each;
- show how tone shifts across contexts (a billing error versus a welcome email) while voice stays consistent;
- provide before-and-after rewrites that demonstrate the voice in action;
- address specific channels where voice is tested: error messages, marketing, support, legal.

Voice described only as adjectives produces inconsistent writing because every writer interprets the adjectives differently. Examples and boundaries make voice reproducible.

### Specify Usage Rules With Clear Do And Do-Not Examples

A rule like "use the logo correctly" is unenforceable because it does not define correct. Effective guidelines show exactly what is allowed and what is forbidden, with visual examples. People follow rules they can see far better than rules they must interpret.

For each usage rule:

- state the rule in one clear sentence;
- show a correct (do) example;
- show incorrect (do-not) examples covering the common mistakes;
- explain briefly why the rule exists, so teams understand the intent.

Do-not examples are often more instructive than do examples, because they show the mistakes teams actually make. Cover the realistic errors: stretching the logo, putting it on busy backgrounds, recoloring it, adding effects.

### Define Clear Space, Minimum Size, And Background Rules

The logo is the most misused brand asset, and its misuse is usually preventable with clear technical rules. Without defined clear space, minimum size, and acceptable backgrounds, the logo gets cramped, shrunk until illegible, or placed on surfaces that destroy its legibility.

Specify logo technical rules:

- define clear space: the minimum padding around the logo, often based on a unit derived from the logo itself;
- set minimum sizes for digital and print, below which the logo becomes illegible;
- list acceptable background colors and treatments, and forbid those that reduce contrast;
- provide variant logos (full color, single color, reversed) and when each is appropriate.

These rules are not aesthetic preferences; they protect the logo's function of being recognizable. A logo that cannot be read is not a logo.

### Govern Color, Typography, And Imagery As A System

A brand is more than its logo. Color, typography, and imagery carry the identity across every surface, and inconsistency in any of them fragments the brand. Guidelines must treat these as a coordinated system with the same rigor as the logo.

Govern the full identity system:

- define the color palette with primary, secondary, and neutral roles, and the contexts for each;
- specify typeface families, weights, and the hierarchy they form, including web-safe or licensed alternatives;
- establish imagery direction: photography style, illustration approach, iconography treatment, and what to avoid;
- show the elements together in example compositions so teams see how the system composes.

A brand with a disciplined logo but random imagery and ad-hoc color still feels inconsistent. The system is only as strong as its least-governed element.

### Make Guidelines Accessible And Maintainable

Guidelines that live in a heavy PDF that no one opens are effectively absent. The format and location of the guidelines determine whether they are used. A living, accessible, searchable reference is far more effective than a beautiful document that lives in a folder.

Ensure usability of the guidelines:

- host guidelines where teams work, in a searchable, linkable format;
- provide assets (logos, colors, fonts) for direct download alongside the rules;
- keep the documentation concise enough to scan, with deeper detail available on demand;
- assign ownership and a review cadence so the guidelines stay current.

Guidelines that are not maintained become wrong, and wrong guidelines are worse than none because they mislead with confidence. Treat the guidelines as a product with an owner and a lifecycle.

### Plan For External Partners And Edge Cases

Internal teams are not the only audience. Agencies, partners, sponsors, and the press all apply the brand, often with less context and more pressure to deviate. Guidelines must anticipate external use and the edge cases where deviation is tempting.

Address external and edge cases:

- provide a condensed partner-facing version with the essential rules and assets;
- define co-branding rules: how the brand appears alongside other logos;
- address sponsorship and event usage, where backgrounds and formats vary;
- specify an approval process for usage that falls outside the rules.

Without external guidance, partners improvise, and the brand appears in forms the company did not intend. A clear, shareable guide prevents the most damaging misuses.

## Common Traps

### Skipping The Brand Core

Guidelines that jump to visual rules without documenting purpose, personality, and audience produce rules teams cannot interpret with judgment.

### Voice As Adjectives Only

Describing voice with adjectives but no examples leaves writers guessing; provide do and do-not examples and context-specific tone guidance.

### Rules Without Do-Not Examples

A rule like "use the logo correctly" is unenforceable; show the specific mistakes teams make with visual do-not examples.

### Undefined Logo Technical Rules

Without clear space, minimum size, and background rules, the logo gets cramped, shrunk, or placed on illegible surfaces.

### Governing Only The Logo

A brand with a disciplined logo but random color, typography, and imagery still fragments; govern the full identity system.

### Guidelines In An Inaccessible Format

A heavy PDF no one opens is effectively absent; host guidelines where teams work in a searchable, linkable format.

### No External Or Co-Branding Guidance

Without partner-facing rules and co-branding guidance, external parties improvise and the brand appears in unintended forms.

### Guidelines That Are Never Maintained

Unmaintained guidelines become wrong and mislead with confidence; assign ownership and a review cadence.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The brand's core (purpose, personality, audience, positioning) is documented before the visual and verbal rules.
- [ ] Voice and tone are defined with do and do-not examples, context-specific tone shifts, and before-and-after rewrites.
- [ ] Each usage rule has a clear statement, a correct example, incorrect examples of common mistakes, and a brief why.
- [ ] Logo technical rules (clear space, minimum size, backgrounds, variants) are specified to protect legibility.
- [ ] Color, typography, and imagery are governed as a coordinated system, shown together in example compositions.
- [ ] Guidelines are hosted accessibly, searchable, with downloadable assets, and concise enough to scan.
- [ ] A condensed partner-facing version and co-branding rules address external and edge-case usage.
- [ ] The guidelines have an owner, a review cadence, and a version history so they stay current.
