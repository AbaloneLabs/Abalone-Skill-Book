# Composition

Composition is the arrangement of elements into a whole that feels intentional. It looks like placing things on a canvas, but it is really a set of decisions about where the eye goes first, how elements relate, where tension and rest live, and whether the result feels balanced or chaotic. Agents tend to treat composition as filling space, align things to a grid and stop, or pack a screen until every pixel is used. The harm is a layout that technically contains everything but reads as cluttered, unfocused, or arbitrary, leaving users unsure where to look or what matters.

Use this skill before finalizing a screen layout, focal structure, or spatial relationships. The goal is to prevent the agent from composing by habit, from filling whitespace reflexively, or from creating layouts that contain the right elements in the wrong relationships.

## Core Rules

### Establish A Clear Focal Point

Every screen should answer one question first: where should the eye go? Without a focal point, the eye wanders and the user feels uncertainty about what matters. A focal point is created through contrast in size, color, weight, position, or whitespace, and it should correspond to the screen's primary purpose.

Create deliberate focal points:

- identify the single most important element or action on the screen;
- give it visual prominence through size, color, isolation, or position;
- ensure secondary elements support rather than compete with it;
- avoid multiple equally prominent elements that split attention.

A screen with no focal point makes the user do the work of figuring out what matters. A screen with too many focal points does the same. Restraint creates clarity.

### Use Whitespace As An Active Element

Whitespace (negative space) is not empty space waiting to be filled. It is an active compositional element that creates breathing room, separates groups, emphasizes what remains, and gives the layout a sense of calm. Agents often treat whitespace as wasted space and fill it, producing dense, stressful screens.

Treat whitespace deliberately:

- use generous whitespace around focal elements to amplify their importance;
- use whitespace between groups to clarify structure and reduce cognitive load;
- resist the pressure to fill every pixel; emptiness is a feature, not a gap;
- vary whitespace intentionally: more around important things, less within tight groups.

A screen that uses whitespace well feels confident and clear. A screen that fills every gap feels anxious and overwhelming. Whitespace is a tool, not a deficit.

### Apply Gestalt Principles To Communicate Grouping

The eye perceives structure through relationships, and the gestalt principles describe how. Proximity, similarity, continuity, closure, and figure-ground are not academic concepts but practical tools for making a layout read as organized rather than scattered. Ignoring them forces users to infer structure that the design should make obvious.

Apply gestalt principles:

- proximity: place related elements close together and unrelated elements apart, so grouping is perceived instantly;
- similarity: use consistent color, shape, or size to signal that elements belong together;
- continuity: align elements along lines or curves so the eye follows a path;
- closure: suggest complete shapes with partial outlines so the mind fills the rest;
- figure-ground: ensure the subject stands out from its background through contrast.

When grouping is unclear, users cannot tell what relates to what. Gestalt principles make structure perceptual, not intellectual.

### Balance The Composition Deliberately

Balance is the distribution of visual weight across the layout. It can be symmetrical (formal, stable) or asymmetrical (dynamic, modern), but it must be intentional. An unbalanced layout feels off without the user being able to name why, creating subtle discomfort.

Achieve intentional balance:

- understand that visual weight comes from size, color saturation, density, and position;
- symmetrical balance mirrors elements across an axis for a formal, calm feel;
- asymmetrical balance offsets a large element with a smaller, visually heavier one (darker, more saturated) for dynamic tension;
- check balance by squinting at the layout or viewing it blurred, which reveals weight distribution without detail.

Accidental balance is rare; accidental imbalance is common. Make the balance a decision, not a byproduct.

### Create Rhythm And Repetition For Cohesion

Rhythm is the repetition of elements that creates a sense of movement and cohesion. Consistent spacing, recurring alignment, repeated shapes, and patterned structure give a layout a beat that the eye follows. Without rhythm, a layout feels like a collection of unrelated pieces.

Build rhythm deliberately:

- repeat spacing intervals to create vertical and horizontal rhythm;
- align elements to consistent edges and axes;
- reuse component shapes and treatments so the eye recognizes patterns;
- vary rhythm intentionally to create emphasis: break the pattern where you want attention.

A layout with consistent rhythm feels designed and trustworthy. A layout with random spacing and alignment feels arbitrary.

### Use Contrast To Separate And Emphasize

Contrast is the difference between elements that makes each distinguishable and directs attention. Without sufficient contrast, elements blur together and hierarchy collapses. Contrast exists in many dimensions: size, color, weight, shape, texture, and spacing.

Apply contrast purposefully:

- use size contrast to separate headings from body and primary from secondary;
- use color contrast to draw the eye to actions and away from structure;
- use weight contrast to emphasize key terms within text;
- ensure contrast is sufficient for accessibility, not just aesthetics;
- avoid weak contrast (slightly different grays) that fails to separate or emphasize.

Weak contrast produces layouts where everything is similar and nothing stands out. Strong, intentional contrast creates clarity.

### Align Everything To A Shared Structure

Alignment is the invisible structure that makes a layout feel orderly. Elements aligned to shared edges, centers, or a grid create a sense of intentionality, while misaligned elements create subtle visual noise that reads as sloppy. Alignment is often the difference between amateur and professional work.

Enforce alignment:

- align elements to a grid or consistent edges rather than by eye;
- choose one alignment strategy per group (left, center, right) and apply it consistently;
- avoid centering everything, which creates weak, ambiguous relationships;
- check alignment at the pixel level, since small misalignments are perceptible even if not consciously noticed.

Alignment is free and high-impact. It costs nothing but discipline and dramatically improves perceived quality.

### Compose For The Reading Direction And Scan Pattern

Users do not read screens; they scan them in patterns (often an F-pattern for text-heavy pages or a Z-pattern for simpler layouts). Composing against these patterns fights how users actually consume content, while composing with them guides the eye naturally.

Design for scan patterns:

- place important content where the eye lands first (top-left in left-to-right languages);
- structure content to follow the natural scan path rather than forcing jumps;
- use focal points and contrast to pull the eye off the default path where needed;
- account for right-to-left reading direction in localized products, which mirrors the scan pattern.

A layout that ignores scan patterns forces users to hunt. One that respects them feels effortless.

## Common Traps

### No Clear Focal Point

A screen without a dominant element makes the user work to find what matters; establish one focal point through contrast and position.

### Treating Whitespace As Waste

Filling every gap produces dense, stressful screens; use whitespace as an active element that emphasizes and separates.

### Ignoring Gestalt Grouping

When proximity and similarity do not communicate structure, users cannot tell what relates to what; apply gestalt principles deliberately.

### Accidental Imbalance

Layouts that feel off usually have unintentional weight distribution; check balance by blurring the view and adjust deliberately.

### Weak Contrast

Slightly different sizes or grays fail to separate or emphasize; use strong, intentional contrast for clarity and accessibility.

### Misalignment

Small misalignments create visual noise that reads as sloppy; align everything to a shared structure at the pixel level.

### Composing Against Scan Patterns

Forcing content against the natural reading direction makes users hunt; compose with scan patterns and localize for reading direction.

### Filling Space Reflexively

Adding elements because there is room, rather than because they serve the composition, produces clutter; let emptiness serve the design.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The screen has a clear focal point that corresponds to its primary purpose, created through contrast and position.
- [ ] Whitespace is used as an active element to emphasize, separate, and create calm, not filled reflexively.
- [ ] Gestalt principles (proximity, similarity, continuity, closure, figure-ground) are applied to make grouping perceptual.
- [ ] The composition is deliberately balanced, either symmetrically or asymmetrically, verified by a blurred view.
- [ ] Rhythm and repetition create cohesion through consistent spacing, alignment, and recurring patterns.
- [ ] Contrast in size, color, weight, and spacing separates elements and directs attention, meeting accessibility thresholds.
- [ ] All elements align to a shared grid or consistent edges, with no accidental misalignment.
- [ ] The layout respects the natural scan pattern and reading direction, with content placed where the eye lands.
