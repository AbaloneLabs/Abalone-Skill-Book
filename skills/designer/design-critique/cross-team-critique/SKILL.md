# Cross-Team Critique

A critique among designers is hard enough; a critique that brings together designers, engineers, product managers, accessibility specialists, and stakeholders is harder, because each discipline sees the work through a different lens and values different outcomes. Engineers see feasibility and risk; product sees scope and goals; stakeholders see strategy and brand; designers see craft and user experience. Left unstructured, cross-team critique devolves into a competition of perspectives where the loudest voice or the highest rank wins, and the design emerges as a compromised average rather than an improved whole. The judgment problem is structuring critique so that each discipline contributes what it uniquely knows, resolving conflicts through shared goals rather than power, and preventing the session from becoming design-by-committee. Agents tend to fail by letting critique become a vote, by allowing disciplines to weigh in outside their expertise, and by failing to translate between the languages each function speaks.

Use this skill when running or participating in a critique that spans disciplines: structuring the session, managing participation, resolving conflicting feedback, or synthesizing cross-functional input into design decisions. The goal is critique that uses each perspective's strength without letting any one dominate.

## Core Rules

### Define The Shared Goal Before The Critique

Cross-functional conflict often stems from each discipline optimizing for a different goal without realizing it. Before critique begins, establish what the work is trying to achieve, so feedback can be evaluated against a shared standard.

Establish the shared goal:

- the user need the design addresses and the audience it serves;
- the business or product objective the work supports;
- the constraints, technical, timeline, brand, that bound the solution;
- the stage of the work and what kind of feedback is wanted.

When the goal is shared, conflicting feedback can be resolved by asking which option better serves it. When the goal is not shared, conflict resolves by power, which produces worse design.

### Give Each Discipline A Clear Role And Lens

Each function in a cross-team critique has a perspective no other can provide, and the critique is strongest when each contributes its unique knowledge rather than all weighing in on everything. Define what each discipline is there to assess.

Assign lenses:

- designers: craft, usability, consistency, and user experience quality;
- engineers: feasibility, technical risk, performance, and edge cases;
- product managers: alignment with goals, scope, and user value;
- accessibility specialists: standards, real-user impact, and inclusive design;
- stakeholders: strategic fit, brand, and business risk.

Invite each to speak to their lens. Discourage disciplines from authoritatively weighing in outside their expertise, where their input is no better than opinion.

### Structure The Session To Prevent Dominance

In unstructured critique, the senior, the loud, or the design-literate dominate, and quieter or more technical voices never surface the risks only they can see. Structure counters this.

Structure by:

- giving the designer time to present context and the questions they want answered;
- collecting feedback discipline by discipline or round-robin, so all voices are heard;
- separating problem identification from solution prescription, so the team aligns on issues first;
- assigning a facilitator to manage time, participation, and focus.

Structure is not bureaucracy; it is what ensures the engineer who sees a fatal feasibility issue actually gets to say so before approval.

### Separate Problem Identification From Solution Prescription

Cross-team critique fails when participants jump straight to prescribing solutions, because solution preferences trigger disagreement that has nothing to do with whether the design works. Aligning on the problem first is more productive.

Sequence the critique:

- first, identify what is and is not working, and why, against the shared goal;
- then discuss whether the current direction addresses those problems;
- only then entertain alternative solutions, and weigh them against the same criteria.

Jumping to solutions turns critique into a preference contest. Starting with problems turns it into a diagnostic discussion the team can align on.

### Translate Between Disciplinary Languages

Each discipline uses vocabulary and reasoning that others may not share, and un-translated feedback creates misunderstanding and conflict. A designer's talk of hierarchy, an engineer's talk of state, and a product manager's talk of scope can describe the same issue in mutually incomprehensible terms.

Translate by:

- asking participants to explain the user or system consequence of their feedback, not just the jargon;
- restating feedback in shared terms to confirm understanding;
- surfacing the underlying concern when feedback seems to conflict, since it often agrees at the root;
- using concrete examples and the design itself as a shared reference point.

Much cross-functional conflict is linguistic. Translation often reveals agreement that argument concealed.

### Resolve Conflicts Through Goals And Evidence, Not Power

When disciplines disagree, the temptation is to resolve by rank or volume. The productive path is to resolve by reference to the shared goal, user evidence, and principles.

Resolve by:

- asking which option better serves the user need and the defined goal;
- seeking evidence, user research, data, technical assessment, rather than assertion;
- distinguishing principle-based disagreements, which have a right answer, from trade-off disagreements, which require a judgment call;
- escalating only genuine strategic conflicts to a decision-maker, with the trade-off framed clearly.

Not every conflict has an objective answer, but every conflict is better resolved by goals and evidence than by who spoke last or loudest.

### Prevent Design-By-Committee

A cross-team critique that tries to incorporate every piece of feedback produces a design that satisfies no one fully and serves the user poorly. The designer, not the committee, must own the synthesis and the decisions.

Prevent design-by-committee by:

- treating feedback as input to the designer's judgment, not as votes to be tallied;
- allowing the designer to reject feedback with reasoned justification;
- distinguishing must-fix issues, principle or feasibility violations, from optional suggestions;
- keeping the decision authority clear, with the designer accountable for the outcome.

Critique informs the design; it does not create it by consensus. Preserve a single owner of the decisions.

### Close The Loop On Feedback

Cross-team critique loses credibility when feedback is given and then seemingly ignored, because participants cannot tell whether their input was considered. Closing the loop sustains trust and engagement.

Close the loop by:

- documenting the feedback received and the decisions made in response;
- explaining when feedback was not incorporated and why;
- sharing the revised design with participants so they see the impact of their input;
- acknowledging contributions that meaningfully shaped the outcome.

Participants who see their feedback taken seriously contribute more valuable critique next time. Participants who feel ignored stop engaging.

## Common Traps

### Critique As A Vote Or Consensus

Tallying preferences produces compromised averages. The designer must own the synthesis.

### Disciplines Weighing In Outside Their Expertise

Authoritative-sounding feedback outside a discipline's knowledge is just opinion. Keep lenses clear.

### Unstructured Sessions Where The Loud Dominate

Without structure, senior or vocal voices crowd out the risks only quiet experts can see. Facilitate.

### Jumping To Solutions Before Problems

Solution preferences trigger preference contests. Align on problems against the shared goal first.

### Untranslated Jargon Creating Conflict

Disciplinary vocabulary obscures agreement. Translate to shared terms and consequences.

### Resolving Conflicts By Power

Rank and volume produce worse design than goals and evidence. Resolve by reference to the shared goal.

### No Closure On Feedback

Unexplained rejection of feedback erodes trust and future engagement. Document decisions and close the loop.

### Conflating Principle Issues With Trade-Offs

Principle violations have a right answer; trade-offs require judgment. Distinguish them before resolving.

## Self-Check

- [ ] A shared goal, user need, business objective, and constraints, is established before critique.
- [ ] Each discipline has a clear lens and contributes its unique expertise, not opinions outside it.
- [ ] The session is structured to ensure all voices are heard, with a facilitator managing participation.
- [ ] Problem identification precedes solution prescription, aligning the team on issues first.
- [ ] Feedback is translated into shared terms so disciplinary language does not create false conflict.
- [ ] Conflicts are resolved by reference to goals and evidence, not rank or volume.
- [ ] The designer owns the synthesis and decisions; feedback is input, not a vote.
- [ ] Must-fix issues are distinguished from optional suggestions.
- [ ] Feedback decisions and rationale are documented and shared with participants.
- [ ] The loop is closed so contributors see how their input shaped the outcome.
