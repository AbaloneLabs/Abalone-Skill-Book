# Design Review Process

A design review process is the structure that determines whether critique happens consistently, at the right moments, with the right people, and to the right standard. Without a process, reviews are ad hoc: some work gets rigorous critique, some gets none, and the quality of feedback depends entirely on who happens to be in the room. With a bad process, reviews become theater or bottlenecks: gates that slow work without improving it, meetings that generate noise instead of decisions, and criteria so vague that approval is arbitrary. The judgment problem is designing a review process that improves the work without strangling it, matching review depth to risk and stage, and keeping reviews focused on criteria rather than opinion. Agents tend to fail by making every review a full critique, by lacking defined criteria so approval is subjective, and by treating the process as bureaucracy rather than as a quality lever.

Use this skill when establishing, revising, or running a design review process: defining stages, criteria, participants, cadence, or gates. The goal is a process that consistently improves design quality and alignment without becoming a bottleneck.

## Core Rules

### Define What The Review Process Is For

A review process must have a clear purpose, or it becomes a ritual performed for its own sake. Define what the process is meant to achieve before designing it.

Clarify the purpose:

- improve the quality of the work against user needs and standards;
- ensure alignment across teams, brands, and platforms;
- catch risks, accessibility, consistency, and feasibility issues before they ship;
- develop designers through feedback and shared standards;
- create a record of decisions and rationale.

A process serving all these purposes looks different from one serving only approval. State the purpose, and let it shape the process.

### Match Review Depth To Risk And Stage

Not all work needs the same level of review, and treating it uniformly either over-reviews low-risk work or under-reviews high-risk work. Calibrate review depth to what is at stake.

Calibrate by:

- high-risk work, new patterns, major flows, brand changes: full review with senior reviewers;
- moderate-risk work, component variations, established patterns: standard review;
- low-risk work, minor updates, copy changes: lightweight or self-review against criteria;
- exploratory work: informal critique, not approval gates.

A process that subjects a typo fix to the same review as a redesign wastes time. A process that lets a major flow ship without review courts disaster. Match depth to risk.

### Establish Explicit Review Criteria

The most common process failure is reviewing against unstated criteria, which makes approval subjective and inconsistent. Different reviewers apply different standards, and designers cannot predict what will pass.

Define criteria such as:

- usability: does the design meet user needs and follow sound interaction principles;
- accessibility: does it meet accessibility standards and support assistive technology;
- consistency: does it align with the design system and existing patterns;
- feasibility: can it be built within constraints and performance budgets;
- brand and voice: does it reflect the brand's identity and tone;
- completeness: are all states, edge cases, and error paths resolved.

Criteria make review consistent and give designers a target. Without them, review is opinion.

### Decide Who Reviews What, And Why

Review quality depends on having the right perspectives, and the right perspectives depend on the work. A review with only designers may miss feasibility; one with only engineers may miss usability.

Compose review by need:

- design peers and seniors, for craft, consistency, and quality;
- engineers, for feasibility, data, and edge cases;
- product managers, for alignment with goals and scope;
- accessibility specialists, for standards and real-user impact;
- stakeholders, for strategic and brand alignment, at appropriate stages.

Not every review needs everyone. Match the reviewers to the questions the work raises.

### Set Cadence And Workload That Sustain Quality

A review process that demands too much of reviewers produces shallow feedback and burnout; one that demands too little lets work slip through. Cadence and workload must be sustainable.

Balance by:

- scheduling reviews at meaningful milestones, not on every minor change;
- keeping review groups small enough for depth but large enough for perspective;
- distributing review load so it does not fall on one or two people;
- allowing async review to reduce meeting load and include thoughtful contributors;
- protecting time for reviewers to actually engage, not just rubber-stamp.

A process that exhausts its reviewers degrades into approval theater. Design it to be sustainable.

### Separate Exploration Reviews From Approval Reviews

Exploratory work needs critique to improve; finished work needs approval to ship. Conflating the two produces a process that either approves unfinished work or blocks exploration with premature gates.

Separate by:

- exploration reviews: open critique, no approval, focused on direction and options;
- refinement reviews: feedback on near-complete work against criteria;
- approval reviews: a final gate confirming the work meets standards and is ready to ship.

Each type has a different purpose, tone, and outcome. Mixing them confuses designers about whether they are being helped or judged.

### Document Decisions And Rationale

A review process that does not record its decisions loses its value over time, because the same issues recur and the rationale for choices is lost. Documentation turns one-time review into organizational learning.

Document by:

- recording what was reviewed, what was decided, and why;
- capturing key feedback and the resulting changes;
- noting unresolved questions and their owners;
- making decisions searchable so future work benefits.

A review record is an asset for the next designer facing the same question. Without it, every review starts from scratch.

### Keep The Process From Becoming A Bottleneck

A review process that becomes a bottleneck does not improve quality; it delays it, and teams route around it. Monitor and adjust the process to keep work flowing.

Prevent bottlenecks by:

- setting target turnaround times for reviews;
- allowing work to proceed at calculated risk when reviews are delayed;
- regularly reviewing whether the process is catching real issues or just adding latency;
- simplifying or removing steps that do not add value.

A process exists to serve the work, not the reverse. If the process is the bottleneck, fix the process.

## Common Traps

### Review As Theater Without Purpose

A process performed for its own sake adds latency without quality. Define and serve the purpose.

### One Review Depth For All Work

Subjecting low-risk work to full review wastes time; skipping high-risk review courts risk. Calibrate depth.

### Unstated, Subjective Criteria

Review without criteria is opinion and produces inconsistent approvals. Define explicit standards.

### Wrong Reviewers For The Work

Design-only review misses feasibility; engineering-only misses usability. Match reviewers to questions.

### Unsustainable Reviewer Workload

Overloaded reviewers give shallow feedback or burn out. Balance cadence and distribute load.

### Conflating Exploration And Approval

Mixing critique with gates confuses designers about whether they are helped or judged. Separate the types.

### No Documentation Of Decisions

Undocumented reviews force every issue to be relitigated. Record decisions and rationale.

### Process As Bottleneck

A process that delays work gets routed around. Monitor and streamline to keep work flowing.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The review process has a defined purpose that shapes its design.
- [ ] Review depth is calibrated to the risk and stage of the work.
- [ ] Explicit criteria cover usability, accessibility, consistency, feasibility, brand, and completeness.
- [ ] Reviewers are selected to match the questions the work raises, not by default attendance.
- [ ] Cadence and workload are sustainable and distributed, not concentrated on a few.
- [ ] Exploration reviews are separated from approval reviews.
- [ ] Decisions, rationale, and unresolved questions are documented and searchable.
- [ ] Target turnaround times exist, and the process is monitored for bottlenecks.
- [ ] The process is regularly reviewed for whether it catches real issues or adds latency.
- [ ] The process improves quality and alignment without strangling the work.
