# Design Review And Critique

A design critique is a structured act of finding problems in work that someone cares about, in a setting that can easily become either a vague approval ritual or a demoralizing attack. The judgment problem is not how to list things you dislike. It is how to frame feedback so it is specific and actionable, how to separate genuine defects from personal preference, how to keep the session psychologically safe enough that the designer hears the hard feedback instead of defending against it, and how to avoid the most common failure mode of all: reviewers imposing their own solution instead of describing the problem. Agents tend to fail by offering vague praise or vague criticism, by confusing critique with approval or with redesign, by critiquing the designer instead of the work, and by letting the loudest voice in the room set the direction while quieter, equally valid perspectives are lost.

Use this skill when preparing for, leading, or participating in a design review or critique, when giving feedback on design work, or when structuring a session to evaluate design. The goal is feedback that improves the work without closing the design space or damaging the collaboration that produces good design over time.

## Core Rules

### Frame Critique As Problem-Finding, Not Solution-Imposing

The core discipline of useful critique is describing what is wrong or unclear without dictating how to fix it. When a reviewer says "make this a modal" or "move the button to the right," they are doing the designer's job and removing the designer's agency. When a reviewer says "I am not sure what action to take next on this screen" or "the primary action does not stand out from the secondary ones," they describe a problem the designer can solve in many ways. Problem-finding preserves the design space; solution-imposing collapses it to the reviewer's idea.

Practice framing feedback as:

- what you observed or experienced as a viewer;
- what was unclear, hard, or slow;
- what assumption the design seems to make that may not hold;
- what user consequence you predict, tied to evidence.

Reserve solution suggestions for when they are explicitly requested, and even then offer them as options, not directives.

### Make Feedback Specific And Locatable

Vague feedback cannot be acted on. "It feels off," "this needs more polish," and "I do not love the header" leave the designer guessing what to change. Useful feedback points at a specific element, names the specific issue, and ideally states the user consequence. The designer should be able to look at the feedback and know exactly which part of the work it addresses.

For each piece of feedback, include:

- the specific screen, element, flow, or interaction;
- the specific issue, not a general impression;
- the reason it matters, ideally a user consequence or a principle;
- enough context that the designer can find and act on it without follow-up.

Specific feedback is faster to act on and far less likely to be misinterpreted or dismissed.

### Separate Defects, Preferences, And Questions

Not everything said in a critique is the same kind of statement, and conflating them corrupts the feedback. A defect is a real problem with a user consequence: a control that is unreachable, a label that is ambiguous, a flow that dead-ends. A preference is a reviewer's taste: a color choice, a spacing value, a typographic style. A question is an attempt to understand intent: "why did you choose this layout?" These three require different responses and carry different weight.

Label feedback by type:

- defects should be addressed, because they cause user harm;
- preferences should be noted but not treated as mandatory, because design has legitimate variation;
- questions should be answered to align understanding, not scored as criticism.

Treating preferences as defects overwhelms the designer with debatable changes. Treating defects as preferences lets real problems slip through.

### Establish Psychological Safety Before Hard Feedback

A designer who feels attacked will defend the work instead of hearing the feedback, and the critique produces nothing. Safety is not softness; it is the condition under which hard feedback can actually land. The reviewer's job is to make clear that the critique is about the work, not the designer, and that finding problems is the intended outcome, not a sign of failure.

Build safety by:

- framing the session around improving the work, not judging the designer;
- asking what kind of feedback the designer wants before starting;
- acknowledging what is working before moving to problems;
- critiquing the work and the decisions, never the person;
- inviting the designer to push back and clarify.

A critique where the designer feels safe to hear "this part is not working" is worth far more than one where everyone is polite and nothing real is said.

### Clarify What Kind Of Feedback Is Wanted

Design work at different stages needs different feedback. Early exploratory work needs direction-level feedback: is this the right problem, the right approach, the right audience. Late, near-final work needs execution-level feedback: spacing, states, edge cases, polish. Giving execution feedback on early exploration kills ideas before they develop; giving direction feedback on near-final work forces rework that cannot be absorbed. Ask the designer what stage the work is at and what feedback would help, then calibrate.

Match feedback to stage:

- early: problem framing, approach, alternatives, audience fit;
- middle: structure, flow, hierarchy, major interaction choices;
- late: detail, states, consistency, edge cases, polish.

Mismatched feedback is a leading cause of frustration on both sides of a critique.

### Prevent The Loudest Voice From Dominating

In a group critique, the most confident or senior reviewer often shapes the consensus before others have spoken, and quieter perspectives, which may include the most useful observations, are lost. Structure the session so multiple independent perspectives surface before convergence. Have reviewers note observations individually before discussion, go around the group so each voice is heard, and treat disagreement as signal rather than noise to be suppressed.

Independent observation followed by structured sharing finds more problems and avoids the false consensus that emerges when the first speaker anchors everyone else.

### Close The Loop With Captured, Prioritized Feedback

A critique that produces no recorded, prioritized output is forgotten by the next day. Capture the feedback, separate defects from preferences, prioritize by severity and user impact, and confirm with the designer which items will be addressed. This turns the session from a conversation into an actionable set of decisions and prevents the same problems from being raised again next time.

## Common Traps

### Solution-Imposing Instead Of Problem-Finding

Reviewers who redesign the work remove the designer's agency and close alternatives. Describe the problem; let the designer solve it.

### Vague Feedback

"It does not feel right" cannot be acted on. Point at the specific element, the specific issue, and the user consequence.

### Confusing Preferences With Defects

Treating taste as mandatory buries real problems under debatable changes. Label feedback by type and weight defects above preferences.

### Critiquing The Designer, Not The Work

Personal criticism makes the designer defensive and ends the usefulness of the session. Keep all feedback about the work and the decisions.

### Mismatched Feedback For The Stage

Execution feedback on early work kills exploration; direction feedback on late work forces unabsorbable rework. Calibrate to the stage.

### Dominance By The Loudest Voice

When the first confident speaker anchors the room, quieter and often more useful observations never surface. Collect independent input before converging.

### Approval Ritual With No Real Feedback

A critique that only offers praise helps no one and lets real problems ship. The purpose is to find what is not working.

### No Captured Output

Feedback that is not recorded and prioritized evaporates. Capture, label, prioritize, and confirm next steps.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Feedback describes problems and user consequences rather than imposing specific solutions.
- [ ] Each piece of feedback points at a specific element, a specific issue, and a reason it matters.
- [ ] Defects, preferences, and questions are labeled separately and weighted differently.
- [ ] The session established psychological safety so the designer could hear hard feedback without defending.
- [ ] The kind of feedback matched the stage of the work: direction early, execution late.
- [ ] Multiple independent perspectives were collected before convergence, preventing dominance by the loudest voice.
- [ ] What is working was acknowledged before moving to problems.
- [ ] Feedback was captured, prioritized by severity and user impact, and confirmed as actionable next steps.
- [ ] No feedback attacked the designer rather than the work.
- [ ] The critique found real problems rather than functioning as an approval ritual.
