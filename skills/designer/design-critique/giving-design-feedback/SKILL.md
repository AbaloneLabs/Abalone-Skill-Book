# Giving Design Feedback

Feedback is the fuel of design iteration, but most feedback is low quality: vague, personal, unactionable, or aimed at the wrong layer of the work. "I don't like it," "make it pop," and "can it be more modern" are not feedback; they are reactions dressed as guidance, and they leave the designer with no idea what to change or why. Good feedback is a skill, and it is harder than it looks, because it requires understanding the work's goals, diagnosing what is and is not working against those goals, and communicating that diagnosis in a way the recipient can actually use. The judgment problem is making feedback specific, goal-oriented, and actionable, separating taste from principle, and delivering critique that strengthens the work rather than demoralizing the designer. Agents tend to fail by offering reactions instead of analysis, by confusing their preferences with universal principles, and by giving feedback so vague or so harsh that it cannot improve the work.

Use this skill when preparing or delivering feedback on design work, in any format: written comments, live critique, async review, or stakeholder response. The goal is feedback that the recipient can act on to make the work better.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Work's Goals Before Critiquing

Feedback that ignores what the work is trying to achieve is worse than useless, because it judges the design against the reviewer's implicit goals rather than the project's actual ones. Before critiquing, establish what the design is for.

Clarify the goals:

- what problem is the design solving, and for whom;
- what stage is the work at, exploration, refinement, or final;
- what constraints, brand, technical, audience, apply;
- what questions the designer is asking the reviewer to answer.

Feedback calibrated to goals and stage is useful. Feedback that assumes the wrong goals sends the designer in the wrong direction.

### Be Specific And Actionable

The defining quality of useful feedback is that the designer knows exactly what to do with it. Vague feedback, however well-intentioned, cannot be acted on.

Make feedback actionable by:

- pointing to specific elements, screens, or moments, not the whole design;
- describing what is happening and why it is a problem, not just that it is wrong;
- offering direction or options, not prescribing a single fix unless asked;
- connecting the issue to a goal, user need, or principle.

"I'm not sure users will find the primary action" is feedback. "The primary action button lacks visual weight relative to the secondary options, so the eye does not land on it first" is better feedback, because it names the element, the mechanism, and the consequence.

### Separate Taste From Principle

Much feedback is the reviewer's personal preference presented as universal law. Distinguishing taste from principle is essential, because taste feedback is optional while principle feedback is not.

Distinguish by asking:

- is this a violation of a usability, accessibility, or consistency principle, or a preference;
- does this feedback serve the user and the goals, or my aesthetic sensibility;
- would a different competent designer reasonably disagree;
- am I describing a problem or asserting a preference.

Label taste feedback as such ("personally, I would lean toward...") so the designer can weigh it appropriately. Reserve authoritative language for actual principles.

### Critique The Work, Not The Person

Feedback aimed at the designer rather than the design is both unkind and ineffective, because it triggers defensiveness that prevents the designer from hearing the substance. Frame feedback around the work.

Frame around the work by:

- describing what the design does or fails to do, not what the designer did wrong;
- using the design's language, "the hierarchy here is unclear," not personal language;
- assuming good intent and effort from the designer;
- acknowledging what is working before addressing what is not.

A designer who feels attacked defends; a designer who feels the work is being examined improves. The framing changes the outcome.

### Address The Right Layer Of The Work

Design has layers: concept, structure, interaction, visual, and detail. Feedback aimed at the wrong layer wastes effort, because it asks for visual polish when the structure is unresolved, or nitpicks detail when the concept is in question.

Match feedback to layer by:

- checking whether the concept and structure are sound before critiquing visuals;
- not demanding pixel-perfect feedback when the work is at sketch stage;
- not reopening concept questions when the work is at final polish;
- sequencing feedback from foundational to detail.

Feedback at the wrong layer cannot be acted on, because the designer is working on a different set of decisions. Meet the work where it is.

### Balance What Works With What Does Not

Feedback that lists only problems leaves the designer uncertain about what to preserve, and feedback that is only praise leaves them uncertain about what to change. Both extremes fail.

Balance by:

- identifying specifically what is working, so it is preserved and built on;
- identifying specifically what is not, so it can be addressed;
- prioritizing the most important issues rather than listing every minor one;
- making clear which feedback is critical versus nice-to-have.

A designer who knows what to keep and what to change iterates efficiently. One who knows only one or the other flounders.

### Adapt Feedback Style To The Recipient And Context

Different recipients and contexts need different feedback styles. The same feedback delivered the same way to a junior designer, a peer, and an executive will land very differently.

Adapt by:

- offering more direction and explanation to less experienced designers;
- treating peers as collaborators and inviting dialogue;
- framing feedback for executives in terms of goals, risk, and decisions;
- matching the formality to the setting, live critique differs from written async review.

Feedback that ignores the recipient's needs and context may be correct and still useless, because it is not heard.

### Invite Dialogue Rather Than Issuing Verdicts

The best feedback opens a conversation, because the reviewer may be missing context the designer has, and the designer may have considered and rejected the apparent fix for good reason.

Invite dialogue by:

- asking questions where you are uncertain about the designer's intent;
- explaining the reasoning behind the feedback, not just the conclusion;
- being open to the designer's response and adjusting your view;
- treating feedback as a contribution to the work, not a ruling.

Feedback delivered as a verdict shuts down the thinking that produces the best solutions. Feedback delivered as dialogue improves the work and the relationship.

## Common Traps

### Reacting Instead Of Analyzing

"I don't like it" is a reaction, not feedback. Diagnose what is and is not working and why.

### Ignoring The Work's Goals And Stage

Feedback against the wrong goals or at the wrong stage sends the designer astray. Calibrate first.

### Vague, Unactionable Comments

"Make it pop" tells the designer nothing. Be specific about what, why, and the direction.

### Taste Presented As Principle

Personal preference is not universal law. Label taste feedback as optional.

### Personal Rather Than Work-Focused Framing

Feedback aimed at the designer triggers defensension. Frame around the work.

### Feedback At The Wrong Layer

Visual feedback on unresolved structure, or concept questions at final polish, wastes effort. Match the layer.

### Only Problems Or Only Praise

Listing only issues or only strengths leaves the designer uncertain. Balance both.

### Verdicts Instead Of Dialogue

Feedback as a ruling shuts down thinking. Invite conversation.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The work's goals, audience, constraints, and stage were understood before feedback was given.
- [ ] Feedback is specific, pointing to elements, mechanisms, and consequences, not vague reactions.
- [ ] Taste feedback is labeled as preference, distinct from principle-based feedback.
- [ ] Feedback is framed around the work, not the person, assuming good intent.
- [ ] Feedback addresses the layer the work is currently at, foundational to detail.
- [ ] What is working is identified alongside what is not, with clear priorities.
- [ ] Feedback style is adapted to the recipient's experience and the context.
- [ ] Feedback invites dialogue and reasoning rather than issuing verdicts.
- [ ] The most important issues are prioritized over a comprehensive list of minor ones.
- [ ] The designer could act on the feedback to improve the work.
