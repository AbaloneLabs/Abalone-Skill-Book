# Prototype Audience And Purpose

Every prototype is built for a reason and an audience, and the single most common cause of wasted prototyping effort is failing to define either clearly. A prototype built "to show the new design" serves no one well, because different audiences need to extract different things from a prototype, and a single artifact cannot simultaneously invite open critique from designers, win executive approval, prove feasibility to engineers, and elicit realistic behavior from test users. The judgment problem is that prototypes are expensive, and building one without a crisp purpose and a defined audience produces artifacts that look impressive but answer no question decisively.

Agents frequently default to building a broad, polished prototype that tries to satisfy everyone, because it feels safer and more complete. The result is a prototype that is too finished to invite critique, too vague to resolve an engineering question, and too generic to test a specific hypothesis. This skill helps the agent define the audience and purpose of a prototype before building, so the effort is targeted and the feedback is usable.

## Core Rules

### Define the single decision the prototype must inform

A prototype exists to move a decision forward. Before building, state in one sentence what decision the prototype is meant to inform: whether a concept direction is viable, whether a flow is understandable, whether an interaction feels right, whether the design is feasible to build, whether stakeholders will fund the work. If you cannot state the decision, the prototype is not yet scoped. A prototype without a decision to inform becomes an exercise in production rather than a tool for learning.

### Identify the primary audience and design the prototype for how they engage

Different audiences engage with prototypes differently and extract different value. Define who the primary audience is and shape the prototype accordingly. Designers and product partners can read low fidelity and focus on structure; executives often need higher fidelity to engage and are evaluating whether to invest; engineers need enough detail to assess feasibility, data, and edge cases; end users in testing react to what they see and behave realistically only when fidelity is sufficient. A prototype designed for one audience will fail with another. When multiple audiences matter, build separate targeted prototypes rather than one compromised artifact.

### Match the prototype's scope to the question, not to the whole product

A prototype that tries to cover the entire product answers every question shallowly. Scope the prototype to the specific question: a single flow, a critical interaction, an edge case, a state transition, a comparison of alternatives. Narrowing scope lets you go deep where it matters and produces decisive feedback. Resist the pressure to prototype everything; a focused prototype that resolves one key question is more valuable than a broad one that leaves every question half-answered.

### Separate exploratory prototypes from validation prototypes

Prototypes serve different roles at different stages, and conflating them causes problems. Exploratory prototypes are built to generate ideas and invite critique; they should be rough, cheap, and disposable, and their purpose is to open options. Validation prototypes are built to confirm a near-final direction; they should be realistic enough to test and their purpose is to reduce risk before building. Mixing the two — treating an exploratory prototype as if it were validated, or polishing a validation prototype while still exploring — produces misleading confidence or wasted polish. Be explicit about which role the prototype plays.

### Decide what feedback you need and design the prototype to elicit it

The prototype must be shaped to produce the kind of feedback that informs the decision. If you need open critique of a concept, roughness invites challenge; if you need approval, polish signals readiness; if you need behavioral data, realism drives authentic interaction; if you need feasibility judgment, technical detail and edge cases matter. Designing a prototype without considering what feedback it should produce often results in feedback you cannot use, because the prototype did not invite the right kind of response.

### Align the audience's expectations with the prototype's maturity

Audiences misjudge prototypes when their expectations do not match the prototype's maturity. An executive who expects a finished product will reject a rough concept prototype; a user who encounters broken states will give feedback about the breakage rather than the concept. Set expectations explicitly before the session: what the prototype is meant to show, what is unfinished, and what feedback is wanted. Managing expectations prevents the audience from fixating on the wrong things.

### Plan for disposal or evolution, not permanence

Prototypes are means, not ends. Decide upfront whether the prototype is disposable (built to answer a question and then discarded), evolutionary (iterated toward the final design), or a throwaway that informs a clean build. Treating a disposable prototype as permanent leads to shipping prototype-quality code; treating an evolutionary prototype as disposable wastes the learning. Clarity about the prototype's lifespan shapes how much to invest and how to hand off.

## Common Traps

### Building one prototype for everyone

A single prototype trying to satisfy designers, executives, engineers, and users satisfies none well. Each audience needs a different artifact; targeting one primary audience per prototype produces sharper feedback.

### Prototyping without a defined decision

A prototype built "to show the design" has no success criterion and cannot decisively move the work forward. State the decision the prototype informs before building.

### Scope creep into the whole product

Prototyping the entire product shallowly answers no question well. Narrow the scope to the specific question and go deep where it matters.

### Conflating exploration and validation

Treating an exploratory prototype as validated produces false confidence; polishing a prototype while still exploring wastes effort. Be explicit about the prototype's role.

### Mismatched audience expectations

Showing a rough prototype to an audience expecting polish, or a polished one when seeking critique, produces unusable feedback. Set expectations about maturity and desired feedback before the session.

### Designing for feedback you did not plan to elicit

A prototype built without considering what feedback is needed often produces feedback you cannot act on. Shape the prototype to invite the specific response that informs the decision.

### Treating prototypes as permanent

Shipping prototype-quality work, or discarding learning from an evolutionary prototype, both waste effort. Decide the prototype's lifespan upfront.

## Self-Check

- Can you state in one sentence the specific decision the prototype is meant to inform?
- Have you identified the primary audience and shaped the prototype for how they engage and what they need to extract?
- Is the prototype scoped to the specific question rather than attempting to cover the whole product shallowly?
- Is it explicit whether the prototype is exploratory (inviting critique) or validation (confirming a direction)?
- Have you designed the prototype to elicit the specific kind of feedback that informs the decision?
- Have you set the audience's expectations about the prototype's maturity and what feedback is wanted?
- Have you decided whether the prototype is disposable, evolutionary, or a throwaway informing a clean build?
- Are you building separate targeted prototypes for different audiences rather than one compromised artifact?
