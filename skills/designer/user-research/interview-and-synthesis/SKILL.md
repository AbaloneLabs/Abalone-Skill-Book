# Interview And Synthesis

An interview is not a conversation that happens to be recorded. It is a structured attempt to surface what users actually do, think, and need, and its value is destroyed by leading questions, by the interviewer talking too much, and by synthesis that imposes a narrative the data does not support. The hard work is split across two stages that are equally easy to get wrong: conducting the interview so that it produces honest, specific, behavior-grounded data, and synthesizing the results so that themes emerge from the evidence rather than being projected onto it.

Agents tend to fail by asking what users want instead of what they do, by accepting rationalized accounts as fact, and by generalizing from vivid quotes without checking whether the pattern holds across participants.

## Core Rules

### Ask About Specific Past Behavior, Not About The Future Or About Preferences

People are reliable witnesses to their recent past and unreliable predictors of their future. Ask about concrete, specific events: "Tell me about the last time you did X," not "Would you use a feature that did Y."

Avoid these question types:

- hypothetical questions about the future, which produce optimistic answers that do not predict behavior;
- preference questions, "do you like A or B," which reflect what sounds good, not what the user would actually choose;
- generic questions that invite generalization rather than a specific instance.

Anchor every question in a real, recent, specific instance. When a participant generalizes, gently pull them back to a concrete example.

### Write A Guide, But Follow The Participant

Prepare an interview guide with your key questions ordered by priority, so that if you run out of time you have covered what matters most. But treat the guide as a safety net, not a script.

Balance structure and flexibility:

- the guide ensures coverage of the priority questions;
- flexibility ensures depth, because the most valuable findings often come from following an unexpected thread;
- when a participant says something surprising or mentions a workaround, probe it with follow-up questions even if it was not in the plan.

Note which guide questions you skipped or shortened so you can assess coverage after the session.

### Avoid Leading Questions And Loaded Language

The phrasing of a question determines the answer. "How frustrating is it when the app crashes" assumes frustration and a crash; "tell me about a time the app did not work as you expected" does not.

Review every question for:

- embedded assumptions that hand the participant the expected answer;
- loaded words like "easy," "difficult," "confusing," "helpful," and "annoying," which cue agreement;
- double-barreled questions that ask two things at once.

Neutral, open phrasing is harder to write and far more reliable. If you must ask a direct evaluation question, ask both directions, what worked and what did not, to avoid forcing a positive or negative frame.

### Listen More Than You Talk

The interviewer's job is to create space for the participant to speak. A common failure is filling silences, finishing the participant's sentences, or explaining the product.

Keep your own talking limited to:

- introductions and setup;
- clarifications;
- probes that open rather than close a topic.

Silence is a tool: after a participant answers, a pause often produces the deeper, more honest follow-up. If you find yourself explaining or defending the design, stop; the interview is not the place to justify decisions, and doing so contaminates the data.

### Capture Verbatim Quotes And Behavioral Detail

During synthesis, the difference between a strong insight and a weak one is often the specificity of the evidence.

Capture during the session:

- exact wording in quotes, because paraphrasing smooths away the texture that reveals what the user actually meant;
- behavioral detail: hesitations, workarounds, confusion, the tools and steps the participant actually used;
- the participant's environment and context, especially in contextual interviews.

Raw notes that are vague or paraphrased cannot support strong synthesis later, because the detail that would have revealed the pattern is already lost.

### Synthesize From The Data, Not Toward A Conclusion

Synthesis is the process of letting themes emerge from the evidence. The failure mode is the reverse: deciding what the answer should be and then selecting the quotes that support it.

Synthesize bottom-up:

- break the raw data into discrete observations;
- group observations by affinity;
- name the themes that the groups actually represent;
- actively search for disconfirming evidence, looking for participants or moments that contradict each theme.

Report the contradictions rather than hiding them. A theme that survives an honest search for counterexamples is far more trustworthy than one assembled from confirming quotes.

### Separate Observations From Interpretations

Conflate what you saw with what you think it means, and the synthesis becomes uncheckable. Keep two layers distinct.

Keep these layers separate:

- observations, what the participant said or did, as evidence;
- interpretations, what you believe it signifies, as hypotheses.

A participant spending five minutes finding a setting is an observation; "the settings are hard to navigate" is an interpretation. Present observations as evidence and interpretations as hypotheses, so that readers can weigh the interpretation against the evidence and reach their own judgment.

### Preserve The Count And The Distribution

Qualitative synthesis that reports themes without saying how many participants exhibited them invites overgeneralization. For each theme, record how many participants contributed evidence and how strongly.

Reporting distribution does not turn qualitative research into quantitative research; it keeps the reader from mistaking a single voice for a chorus. Where a theme is held by a minority, say so, because minority needs can matter even when they are not dominant.

## Common Traps

### Asking What Users Want

Users are designers of their own problems, not of solutions. Asking what feature they want produces wish lists that do not reflect real needs. Ask about problems and behaviors instead.

### Accepting Rationalized Explanations

When users explain why they did something, they are often reconstructing a plausible story, not reporting their actual reasoning. Treat explanations as hypotheses and probe for the concrete sequence of events.

### Leading With Loaded Questions

Questions that embed the expected answer produce that answer. Review and rewrite every question for embedded assumptions.

### Interviewer Talking Too Much

Explaining, defending, or filling silences contaminates the data and steals time from the participant's account. Listen.

### Synthesizing Toward A Preferred Conclusion

Selecting confirming quotes and ignoring disconfirming ones produces a convincing but false synthesis. Actively search for counterexamples.

### Paraphrasing Away The Evidence

Vague notes and smoothed-over quotes cannot support strong themes. Capture verbatim wording and behavioral detail.

### Generalizing A Vivid Quote To Everyone

One memorable participant can dominate the synthesis if their distribution is not reported. Preserve the count and the contradictions.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Questions ask about specific past behavior, not hypothetical futures or abstract preferences.
- [ ] An interview guide exists and is prioritized, but the interviewer follows unexpected threads.
- [ ] Every question was reviewed for leading phrasing and loaded language and rewritten to be neutral.
- [ ] The interviewer listened more than they talked and did not explain or defend the design.
- [ ] Raw notes capture verbatim quotes and behavioral detail, not paraphrases.
- [ ] Synthesis worked bottom-up from observations to themes, not top-down toward a conclusion.
- [ ] Observations are presented separately from interpretations, so readers can weigh the evidence.
- [ ] Each theme reports how many participants contributed evidence and notes contradictions.
- [ ] Disconfirming evidence was actively sought and reported, not hidden.
- [ ] A skeptical reader could trace each theme back to the raw data behind it.
