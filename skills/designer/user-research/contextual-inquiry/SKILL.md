# Contextual Inquiry

A contextual inquiry is an interview conducted in the user's real workplace while they do real work. It is not a chat in a meeting room and not a usability test of a prototype. The defining move is that meaning lives in context: the artifacts on a desk, the windows left open, the interruptions, the workaround a user performs without mentioning it, and the second screen they switch to under pressure. A stripped interview in a lab recovers none of this, and a design built from decontextualized self-report routinely misses the work that actually drives the product.

Agents tend to fail contextual inquiry in predictable ways. They treat it as a sit-down interview that merely happens to occur in the user's office, so they never look at the work. They ask users to describe what they do and trust the description, even though people routinely omit steps they consider obvious, embarrassing, or habitual. They observe one user and generalize a whole feature around it. Or they gather rich field notes and then summarize them into vague themes that lose the concrete detail that made the visit useful in the first place.

Use this skill before running a contextual inquiry, while in the field, and while interpreting the data. The goal is to gather observations that are faithful to the work as it happens, to separate what users say from what they do, and to convert field detail into design implications without overclaiming from a small sample.

## Core Rules

### Run The Inquiry In The Real Environment, On Real Work

The method's value comes from the setting. A contextual inquiry that is moved to a conference room, a video call with no screen share, or a generic "tell me about your process" conversation has discarded the method's core advantage.

Insist on the real environment and real work:

- conduct the session where the work actually happens, with the real tools, artifacts, and people present;
- ask the user to perform recent or representative work, not a rehearsed demo or a toy task;
- keep the artifacts visible: the open tabs, the sticky notes, the spreadsheet, the paper form, the second monitor;
- capture the interruptions, because they reveal what competes for the user's attention.

If access to the real environment is impossible, name the compromise explicitly and downgrade the claims accordingly. A remote screen-share session with think-aloud is a useful but weaker substitute, and its findings should not be presented as full contextual inquiry.

### Follow The Master-Apprentice Partnership Model

The classic framing is that the user is the master doing the work, and the researcher is the apprentice learning by watching and asking. This reframing matters because it changes who leads. In a standard interview the researcher leads with questions; in contextual inquiry the user leads by working, and the researcher interrupts to understand.

Apply the partnership by:

- letting the user drive the work at their own pace, not steering them through a script;
- interrupting at concrete moments to ask "what are you doing right now and why," anchored to the action in front of you;
- deferring abstract or opinion questions until after you have watched the concrete work;
- treating the user as the expert on their own work, not as a subject to be evaluated.

When the researcher reverts to leading, the session collapses into a recall interview and the context disappears.

### Observe What They Do, Then Ask About What You Saw

Self-report is unreliable for habitual work. Users omit steps they consider obvious, invent reasons for actions that are actually habitual, and describe the official process rather than the real one. The discipline is to watch first, then interrogate the gap between what was said and what was done.

Separate observation from interpretation:

- record the concrete actions and artifacts, not your conclusions about them;
- note the workarounds, the copy-paste, the "let me just" steps, and the tools used outside the official system;
- when the user says one thing and does another, ask about the discrepancy without implying fault;
- capture the trigger for each task: what made them start it, and how they knew it was time.

The richest findings usually live in the workarounds and the unofficial tools, because those are where the official system is failing the user.

### Decide What To Capture Before You Arrive

Field sessions generate more signal than anyone can remember. Decide in advance what you are capturing and how, so the session produces analyzable artifacts rather than vague memories.

Plan capture deliberately:

- decide whether audio, video, screen recording, photography of artifacts, or field notes are appropriate, and obtain consent for each;
- capture the artifacts in situ: the desk layout, the printed checklist, the annotated document;
- use a structured note template keyed to the work sequence, so notes map to tasks rather than to chronology;
- record exact quotes for later grounding, because paraphrase erases the user's framing.

Consent and confidentiality are part of capture, not an afterthought. Some workplaces forbid recording; some artifacts are sensitive. Agree the boundaries before the session starts, and never record what you were not permitted to record.

### Interpret Through Concrete Models, Not Vague Themes

A pile of field notes is not a finding. Contextual inquiry is paired with an interpretation step that builds concrete models of the work: the sequence of tasks, the artifacts involved, the physical and cultural environment, and the breakdowns where the current tools fail. Summarizing field notes into "users want it to be faster" throws away the structure that makes the method worth running.

Build interpretation artifacts:

- sequence models that show the steps, triggers, and order of a task;
- artifact models that show what objects the work produces and consumes;
- breakdown models that capture where the user was stopped, confused, or forced into a workaround;
- cultural and environmental models that capture the constraints of the setting.

Interpretation is stronger when done with the team rather than alone, because multiple observers catch different breakdowns and resist a single researcher's bias.

### Bound The Generalization To What You Observed

Contextual inquiry usually involves a small number of sites. A handful of visits can reveal how work happens and where breakdowns occur, but it cannot establish how common a breakdown is across the whole population. Overgeneralizing from a few vivid visits is the most common analytic error.

Bound the claims:

- treat findings as revelations of possibility (this breakdown can happen, this workaround exists) rather than measures of prevalence;
- use the visits to generate hypotheses, then verify prevalence with surveys, analytics, or a larger sample;
- be explicit about which roles, sites, and contexts you visited, and resist claims about contexts you did not observe;
- revisit the decision of whether more sites are needed before generalizing.

A small contextual inquiry is excellent for discovering the shape of a problem and terrible for sizing it. Use it for the former and pair it with another method for the latter.

### Sequence Contextual Inquiry Early, Before Solution Design

Contextual inquiry is generative. It is most valuable before the team has committed to a solution, because its purpose is to reveal the problem space. Running it after the design is fixed usually degenerates into validating the existing idea against the field, which wastes the method.

Place it deliberately in the research plan:

- run contextual inquiry during discovery, before flows and screens are locked;
- use its output to define the problem and the work to be supported, not to ratify a chosen solution;
- if the design is already fixed, be honest that the inquiry is evaluative and limited, and adjust claims accordingly.

## Common Traps

### Treating It As An Interview That Happens To Be On Site

If the user never performs real work and the researcher only asks questions, the context contributes nothing. The method requires watching work happen.

### Trusting Self-Report Over Observation

Users describe the official process and omit the habitual workarounds. Trust the actions and the artifacts, and interrogate the gaps.

### Generalizing From One Vivid Visit

A single compelling site can dominate the team's mental model. One visit reveals what is possible, not what is common.

### Summarizing Notes Into Vague Themes

Collapsing rich field data into "users want simplicity" discards the sequence, artifact, and breakdown models that made the visit worth running.

### Stepping Outside The Real Work

Asking the user to demonstrate a fake task or walk a prototype turns the session into a demo or usability test, losing the context of real triggers and interruptions.

### Ignoring Consent And Confidentiality Boundaries

Recording without agreement, or photographing sensitive artifacts, breaches trust and can end the research program. Agree capture boundaries before starting.

### Running It After The Design Is Fixed

Late contextual inquiry tends to confirm the existing design rather than reveal the problem space. Use it early, or relabel it as evaluation.

### Letting The Researcher Lead

When the interviewer drives with a question script, the user stops working and the context disappears. The user leads by working; the researcher learns.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The session was conducted in the user's real environment, on real and recent work, not a rehearsed demo or a decontextualized interview.
- [ ] The master-apprentice partnership was maintained: the user drove the work, and the researcher interrupted to understand concrete actions.
- [ ] Observation was separated from interpretation, and concrete actions, artifacts, workarounds, and triggers were captured.
- [ ] Capture methods and consent were decided in advance, and only permitted media were recorded.
- [ ] Findings were interpreted through concrete models (sequence, artifact, breakdown, environment) rather than collapsed into vague themes.
- [ ] Claims are bounded to the sites and roles observed, with prevalence treated as a hypothesis rather than a measured fact.
- [ ] The inquiry was sequenced before solution design, or, if run late, is explicitly labeled as evaluative.
- [ ] The gap between what users said and what they did was investigated, not ignored.
- [ ] Workarounds and unofficial tools were captured, because they locate where the current system fails.
- [ ] No finding is overclaimed beyond what a small field sample can support.
