# Scald Protection and Temperature Regulation

Hot water temperature is a safety decision, not a comfort setting: water at 140°F causes a full-thickness scald burn in approximately 3 seconds, while water at 120°F takes 5 minutes or more to cause the same injury, and the difference is the entire margin between a household accident and a hospital admission. The judgment problem is that the same temperature that controls Legionella bacteria (140°F storage) is the temperature that scalds, and the resolution requires deliberate engineering — a tempering valve that stores hot and delivers safe — not a simple thermostat setting. A plumber who treats hot water temperature as a dial to be set by occupant preference is installing a scald hazard. This skill covers the temperature regulation decisions that protect occupants from scalds while controlling Legionella.

## Core Rules

### Understand the Scald Time-Temperature Relationship and Design for the Safe Margin

Scald injury severity is determined by both temperature and exposure time, and the relationship is steep: at 120°F, a full-thickness burn requires roughly 5 minutes of exposure; at 130°F, 30 seconds; at 140°F, 3 seconds; at 150°F, 1 second; at 160°F, instantaneous. Children, elderly, and disabled occupants have thinner skin and shorter burn times at every temperature. The safe delivery temperature at fixtures is 120°F or below, and the maximum allowed by code for residential fixtures is typically 120°F. The judgment problem is that 120°F feels "warm" to a healthy adult and may be perceived as inadequate, leading occupants to request higher temperatures — but the higher temperature is the scald hazard. The disciplined rule is to deliver at or below 120°F at all fixtures, regardless of occupant preference, and to explain the scald risk rather than accommodate a dangerous request.

### Store Hot, Deliver Safe, Using a Tempering Valve

The resolution of the Legionella-scald tradeoff is to store water at 140°F (which kills or controls Legionella in the tank and piping) and deliver at 120°F (which is scald-safe) using a tempering valve (also called a mixing valve or thermostatic mixing valve). The tempering valve blends cold water with the hot supply to achieve the set delivery temperature, and it must be installed at the water heater outlet so that all delivered water passes through it. The trap is setting the heater to 120°F directly (Legionella risk in the tank) or to 140°F without a tempering valve (scald risk at fixtures). The disciplined rule is to install a tempering valve whenever storage temperature exceeds the safe delivery temperature, to set the valve to 120°F delivery, and to verify the delivery temperature at the farthest fixture with a thermometer.

### Install Thermostatic or Pressure-Balance Valves at Showers and Tub-Shower Combinations

Showers and tub-shower combinations present a special scald risk: when a toilet flushes or a cold faucet opens elsewhere in the building, the cold-water pressure to the shower drops, and without a compensating valve the shower delivers near-pure hot water at the storage temperature, scalding the bather. The code requires pressure-balance or thermostatic mixing valves at all shower and tub-shower fixtures, which compensate for pressure or temperature changes to maintain a stable delivery temperature. The trap is installing a standard shower valve without pressure-balance or thermostatic function, or installing the valve but failing to set the temperature limit stop, which allows the valve to deliver above the safe temperature. The disciplined rule is to install a code-compliant pressure-balance or thermostatic valve at every shower and tub-shower, set the temperature limit stop per the manufacturer (typically 115 to 120°F), and verify the delivery temperature under simulated pressure changes.

### Verify Delivery Temperature at Every Fixture, Not Just at the Heater

The temperature set at the water heater or tempering valve is not the temperature that arrives at fixtures — heat loss through piping reduces temperature at distant fixtures, and recirculation return temperatures may differ from supply. The disciplined approach is to verify the delivery temperature at every fixture with a thermometer, particularly the farthest fixture from the heater and any fixture served by a recirculation loop. The trap is setting the tempering valve to 120°F at the heater and assuming all fixtures deliver 120°F, when in fact distant fixtures may deliver 110°F (adequate) or, if the valve is set high, near fixtures may deliver 125°F (marginal scald risk). The disciplined rule is to measure at every fixture and adjust the tempering valve so that the hottest fixture delivers at or below 120°F.

### Address Legionella Control as Part of the Temperature Strategy

Legionella bacteria proliferate in warm water (77°F to 115°F) and are controlled by temperatures above 122°F, with rapid kill at 140°F and above. A water heater set to 120°F allows Legionella growth in the tank and in the piping (particularly in long recirculation loops that cool below 120°F), presenting a risk to immunocompromised occupants and in healthcare and institutional settings. The resolution is to store at 140°F (or per the institutional protocol, sometimes higher with periodic superheat-and-flush cycles) and deliver at 120°F with a tempering valve, and to ensure the recirculation return temperature remains above 122°F to prevent growth in the loop. The judgment problem is that Legionella control and scald protection are coupled and must be designed together, not set independently. The disciplined rule is to design the temperature strategy as a system: storage, delivery, recirculation return, and fixture verification, all coordinated.

## Common Traps

### Setting the Heater to 120°F Directly Without Considering Legionella

A plumber sets the water heater to 120°F for scald safety, and Legionella proliferates in the tank and piping, particularly in a long recirculation loop that cools below 120°F. The trap is that the scald-safe setting creates a Legionella risk. The mechanism is that 120°F is within the Legionella growth range. The false signal is that "the temperature is safe for scalds." The harm is Legionella contamination, dangerous to immunocompromised occupants. The defense is to store at 140°F and deliver at 120°F with a tempering valve, and to maintain recirculation return above 122°F.

### Setting the Heater to 140°F Without a Tempering Valve

A plumber sets the water heater to 140°F for Legionella control and connects the supply directly to the fixtures, delivering 140°F water that scalds in 3 seconds. The trap is that the Legionella-safe setting creates a scald hazard. The mechanism is the steep scald time-temperature curve. The false signal is that "the temperature controls Legionella." The harm is scald injuries, particularly to children and elderly. The defense is to install a tempering valve delivering 120°F whenever storage exceeds the safe delivery temperature.

### Installing a Shower Valve Without Pressure-Balance or Thermostatic Function

A plumber installs a standard shower valve without pressure-balance or thermostatic compensation, and when a toilet flushes the cold pressure drops and the shower delivers near-pure hot water at storage temperature, scalding the bather. The trap is that the valve "works" under steady pressure but fails under the pressure changes common in residential plumbing. The mechanism is that pressure changes alter the hot-cold mix ratio. The false signal is that "the shower delivers at the set temperature." The harm is pressure-change scalding. The defense is to install a code-compliant pressure-balance or thermostatic valve at every shower and tub-shower and to set the temperature limit stop.

### Setting the Tempering Valve Without Verifying Fixture Temperature

A plumber sets the tempering valve to 120°F at the heater and assumes all fixtures deliver 120°F, but a distant fixture delivers 110°F (adequate) while a near fixture delivers 125°F (marginal scald risk), because the valve setting does not account for piping heat loss distribution. The trap is that the setpoint is correct at the valve but not at the fixtures. The mechanism is that piping heat loss varies by distance. The false signal is that "the valve is set to 120°F." The harm is over-temperature at near fixtures. The defense is to measure the delivery temperature at every fixture and adjust the valve so the hottest fixture is at or below 120°F.

### Failing to Set the Temperature Limit Stop on a Shower Valve

A plumber installs a pressure-balance shower valve but does not set the temperature limit stop, allowing the valve to deliver above the safe temperature when the handle is turned to full hot. The trap is that the valve compensates for pressure changes but does not limit the maximum temperature unless the stop is set. The mechanism is that the limit stop is the maximum-temperature control. The false signal is that "the valve is pressure-balanced." The harm is that full-hot delivers storage-temperature water. The defense is to set the limit stop per the manufacturer (typically 115 to 120°F) and to verify the maximum delivery temperature with the handle at full hot.

## Self-Check

- Did I store water at 140°F (or per the institutional protocol) for Legionella control and deliver at 120°F using a tempering valve, rather than setting the heater directly to either temperature?
- Is a tempering valve installed at the water heater outlet whenever the storage temperature exceeds the safe delivery temperature?
- Did I install a code-compliant pressure-balance or thermostatic mixing valve at every shower and tub-shower fixture?
- Did I set the temperature limit stop on each shower valve per the manufacturer (typically 115 to 120°F), and did I verify the maximum delivery temperature with the handle at full hot?
- Did I verify the delivery temperature at every fixture with a thermometer, particularly the farthest fixture and any fixture on a recirculation loop?
- Is the hottest fixture delivery temperature at or below 120°F, and did I adjust the tempering valve accordingly?
- For recirculation systems, is the return temperature maintained above 122°F to prevent Legionella growth in the loop?
- For healthcare, nursing home, or other high-risk occupancies, did I follow the institutional Legionella protocol (storage temperature, periodic superheat, monitoring)?
- Did I explain the scald risk to the occupant and decline to raise the delivery temperature above 120°F, documenting the reason?
- Did I document the storage temperature, delivery temperature, tempering valve setting, shower valve limit stops, and fixture verification readings?
