# Water Heater Selection and Sizing

Water heater selection determines whether a building has adequate hot water for its demand pattern, and the wrong choice produces either chronic shortages (undersized) or chronic energy waste (oversized) — and in the case of fuel and venting choices, can introduce carbon monoxide risk. The judgment problem is that water heater sizing depends on the demand pattern (peak draw, recovery, simultaneous fixture use), not on a simple gallon capacity, and the correct type (tank, tankless, heat pump, indirect) depends on the fuel availability, the building's thermal envelope, the usage pattern, and the installation location constraints. A plumber who sizes by "a 50-gallon for a family of four" will get some houses right and many wrong, because the demand pattern varies far more than the household size suggests. This skill covers the selection and sizing decisions that match a water heater to the actual demand.

## Core Rules

### Size Tank Heaters by First-Hour Rating, Not by Gallon Capacity

A tank water heater's ability to deliver hot water during peak demand is measured by its first-hour rating (FHR) — the gallons of usable hot water it can deliver in the first hour starting with a full hot tank — which combines the stored volume and the recovery rate (the burner or element's ability to heat incoming cold water). Two 50-gallon heaters with different burners have very different FHRs, and the FHR, not the gallon capacity, must be matched to the peak hour demand. The peak hour demand is estimated by totaling the hot water use of fixtures and appliances likely to run simultaneously during the busiest hour (showers, washing machine, dishwasher, faucets), using the fixture-use tables in the code or the DOE method. The trap is sizing by gallon capacity alone — "a 50-gallon for a 3-bathroom house" — which ignores recovery rate and demand pattern, producing either shortages (low burner, high demand) or waste (high burner, low demand). The disciplined approach is to calculate peak hour demand and select a heater whose FHR meets or exceeds it.

### Size Tankless Heaters by Gallons-Per-Minute at the Required Temperature Rise

Tankless (on-demand) water heaters have no stored volume; they heat water as it flows, and their capacity is measured in gallons per minute (GPM) at a specific temperature rise (the difference between the incoming cold water temperature and the required delivery temperature). A unit rated 5 GPM at a 70°F rise may deliver only 3 GPM at a 90°F rise in cold climates, because the colder incoming water requires more heat per gallon. The sizing method is to identify the simultaneous fixture flow (typically two showers plus a faucet, roughly 3 to 5 GPM), determine the required temperature rise from the local groundwater temperature, and select a unit whose GPM at that rise meets the simultaneous demand. The trap is sizing by the unit's headline GPM (often quoted at a 35°F rise, which is unrealistic in cold climates) without checking the GPM at the actual local rise, producing a unit that cannot keep up in winter. The disciplined approach is to use the local groundwater temperature, calculate the rise to 120°F delivery, and verify the unit's GPM at that rise.

### Match the Heater Type to the Demand Pattern and Building Constraints

Tank heaters are best for moderate, regular demand and for installations where simultaneous flow is moderate and recovery time is acceptable. Tankless heaters are best for continuous low-to-moderate flow and where space is constrained, but they struggle with high simultaneous flow (multiple showers plus appliances) and with low-flow fixtures (the minimum flow rate to activate the burner may not be met by a trickle). Heat pump water heaters are best for warm, unconditioned or semi-conditioned spaces (they extract heat from the surrounding air and cool it) and are inefficient in cold spaces. Indirect water heaters (fired by a boiler through a heat exchanger) are best when a boiler is already present and sized for the combined load. The judgment problem is that no type is universally best, and the choice depends on the demand pattern, the fuel, the climate, the installation space, and the building's thermal characteristics. The trap is defaulting to one type for every job. The disciplined approach is to evaluate the demand pattern and constraints and select the type that fits.

### Account for Recovery, Standby Loss, and Off-Peak Demand

Tank heater sizing must account for recovery rate (how fast the burner reheats the tank after a draw) and standby loss (the heat lost through the tank jacket when no water is drawn), both of which affect the effective capacity over a multi-hour demand period. A high-recovery heater with a small tank may outperform a low-recovery heater with a large tank over a busy morning. The trap is sizing only for the peak hour and ignoring the recovery between peaks, which can leave the tank depleted for the second peak (the evening demand). The disciplined approach is to evaluate the demand over the full day, account for recovery between peaks, and select a heater whose recovery and storage together meet the daily pattern, not just the single peak hour. For commercial applications with high and continuous demand, recovery dominates and storage is secondary.

### Address Scald Protection and Legionella Control Together

Water heater temperature setting involves a tradeoff: higher storage temperature (140°F) controls Legionella bacteria growth but presents a scalding risk at fixtures; lower temperature (120°F) is scald-safe but allows Legionella growth in the tank and piping. The resolution is to store at 140°F (for Legionella control) and deliver at 120°F (for scald safety) using a tempering (mixing) valve, which blends cold water with the hot to achieve the safe delivery temperature. The trap is setting the heater to 120°F directly (Legionella risk) or to 140°F without a tempering valve (scald risk), each of which solves one problem and creates the other. The disciplined rule is to set storage temperature and delivery temperature separately, using a tempering valve where storage exceeds the safe delivery temperature, and to verify the actual delivery temperature at fixtures with a thermometer.

## Common Traps

### Sizing a Tank Heater by Gallon Capacity Alone

A plumber installs a 50-gallon heater for a 3-bathroom house based on a rule of thumb, but the heater has a low burner and the household takes four showers each morning, depleting the tank and leaving the last showerer cold. The trap is that the gallon capacity looks adequate but the recovery rate is insufficient for the demand pattern. The mechanism is that FHR, not gallons, determines peak-hour delivery. The false signal is that "the tank is 50 gallons." The harm is chronic morning shortages. The defense is to calculate peak hour demand and select by FHR.

### Sizing a Tankless Heater by Headline GPM

A plumber selects a tankless unit rated "7 GPM" without checking that the rating is at a 35°F rise, and the local groundwater is 40°F requiring an 80°F rise to deliver 120°F, at which the unit produces only 3.5 GPM — insufficient for two simultaneous showers in winter. The trap is that the headline GPM is quoted at an unrealistic rise. The mechanism is that GPM falls as the required rise increases. The false signal is the headline rating. The harm is inadequate hot water in cold weather. The defense is to size by the GPM at the local temperature rise, using the local groundwater temperature.

### Defaulting to One Heater Type for Every Job

A plumber installs a tankless heater in every house because "tankless is more efficient," but in a house with high simultaneous demand (five family members, two showers plus appliances) the tankless unit cannot keep up, or in a cold-climate garage the unit's minimum activation flow is not met by a trickle faucet. The trap is that no type is universally best, and the default fails in applications where the type's weaknesses matter. The mechanism is that each type has demand-pattern and installation constraints. The false signal is that "tankless is efficient." The harm is inadequate hot water or nuisance activation problems. The defense is to evaluate the demand pattern and constraints and select the type that fits.

### Setting the Heater to 120°F Without Considering Legionella, or 140°F Without a Tempering Valve

A plumber sets the heater to 120°F for scald safety, allowing Legionella to grow in the tank and piping, or sets it to 140°F for Legionella control and delivers 140°F water to fixtures, presenting a scald hazard. The trap is that each setting solves one problem and creates the other. The mechanism is the temperature tradeoff between Legionella control and scald safety. The false signal is that "the temperature is set." The harm is either Legionella risk or scald injuries. The defense is to store at 140°F and deliver at 120°F with a tempering valve, verifying the delivery temperature at fixtures.

### Ignoring Recovery Between Demand Peaks

A plumber sizes a tank heater for the morning peak hour but ignores that the tank must recover before the evening peak, and the household's evening demand exceeds the recovered capacity. The trap is that the single-peak sizing is correct for the morning but inadequate for the full daily pattern. The mechanism is that recovery between peaks determines available capacity for later peaks. The false signal is that "the morning peak is met." The harm is evening shortages. The defense is to evaluate the full daily demand pattern and account for recovery between peaks.

## Self-Check

- Did I size the tank water heater by first-hour rating (FHR) matched to the calculated peak hour demand, rather than by gallon capacity alone?
- Did I size the tankless water heater by the GPM at the local temperature rise (using the local groundwater temperature), rather than by the headline GPM at an unrealistic rise?
- Did I evaluate the demand pattern (peak draw, simultaneous fixtures, recovery between peaks) and the building constraints (fuel, climate, space, thermal envelope) before selecting the heater type?
- For tank heaters, did I account for recovery rate and standby loss in addition to storage volume, and does the selected heater meet the full daily demand pattern, not just the single peak hour?
- Did I address scald protection and Legionella control together, storing at 140°F (or per code) and delivering at 120°F with a tempering valve where storage exceeds the safe delivery temperature?
- Did I verify the actual delivery temperature at fixtures with a thermometer after installation?
- For tankless installations, did I confirm the gas supply (or electrical service) is adequate for the unit's firing rate, and that the minimum activation flow is compatible with the fixtures?
- For heat pump water heaters, did I confirm the installation space has adequate volume and temperature for efficient operation?
- Did I confirm the venting (for fuel-fired units) is correctly sized and categorized for the appliance, and that combustion air is provided per code?
- Did I document the demand calculation, the selected heater's ratings, the temperature setpoints, and the tempering valve configuration so the installation is verifiable?
