# T&P Relief Valve and Expansion Safety

The temperature and pressure (T&P) relief valve on a water heater or boiler is the last line of defense against catastrophic failure — a water heater that overheats and over-pressurizes without a functional relief valve becomes a bomb, and water heaters have launched through roofs and killed people when the relief was absent, plugged, or improperly sized. The judgment problem is that the T&P valve is a safety device that must be installed correctly, sized correctly, and discharged to a safe location, and a plumber who treats it as a fitting to be threaded in and forgotten has disabled the protection. A T&P that weeps is also a diagnostic signal, not a nuisance to be silenced by replacing the valve. This skill covers the installation, sizing, discharge routing, and diagnostic decisions that keep T&P relief functional.

## Core Rules

### Install a T&P Valve Rated for the Heater's BTU Input and Pressure, and Never Plug It

Every water heater and boiler must have a T&P relief valve with a pressure rating below the vessel's working pressure (typically 150 psi for residential water heaters) and a temperature rating (typically 210°F) and, critically, a BTU-per-hour discharge capacity that meets or exceeds the heater's input rating. The valve nameplate states the BTU rating, and if the heater input exceeds the valve capacity, the valve cannot relieve fast enough to prevent overpressure during an uncontrolled firing. The trap is installing a generic T&P without checking the BTU rating against the heater, or plugging a weeping T&P to stop the drip (which disables the protection entirely). The disciplined rule is to verify the valve's pressure, temperature, and BTU ratings against the heater, to never plug or cap a T&P, and to replace a defective valve with one of equal or greater rating.

### Route the Discharge to a Safe, Visible Location With an Air Gap and No Shutoff

The T&P discharge must be routed to a safe location — typically within 6 inches of the floor (to prevent scalding spray), to a floor drain, to an indirect waste receptor, or to the exterior — with an air gap at the termination (to prevent back-siphonage of drain water into the heater) and with no shutoff valve in the discharge pipe (a shutoff would allow the protection to be defeated). The discharge pipe must be the same diameter as the valve outlet (not reduced), made of a material rated for high temperature (copper, CPVC rated for hot discharge, or steel — not regular PVC which softens), and routed to drain by gravity (no traps, no upward runs). The trap is reducing the discharge pipe diameter, installing a shutoff, using PVC which softens and collapses, or routing the discharge to a concealed location where a leak goes unnoticed. The disciplined rule is to route full-diameter, high-temperature, gravity-drained, with an air gap and no shutoff, to a visible or drain-connected location.

### Diagnose T&P Weeping as a Symptom, Not a Defect in the Valve

A T&P valve that weeps or drips is almost always responding to a real condition — thermal expansion pressure spikes in a closed system, excessive temperature from a faulty thermostat, or excessive supply pressure — and replacing the valve without diagnosing the cause silences the alarm without fixing the problem. The diagnostic sequence is: measure the system pressure over a heating cycle with a logging gauge (to detect thermal expansion spikes above 150 psi), measure the water temperature at a faucet (to detect thermostat failure above 130°F), and measure the static supply pressure (to detect excessive municipal pressure above 80 psi). The trap is replacing the weeping T&P with a new one, which weeps for the same reason, and repeating the cycle. The disciplined rule is to diagnose the cause before replacing the valve, and to fix the cause (install an expansion tank, replace the thermostat, install a PRV) so the new T&P does not weep.

### Install a Thermal Expansion Tank on Every Closed System

A closed system (one with a PRV, a meter check valve, or a backflow preventer) cannot relieve thermal expansion back to the main, so every water heater heating cycle raises the pressure until the T&P opens. The repeated T&P opening wastes water and energy, stresses the valve (causing premature failure and leakage), and stresses every fitting in the system. The expansion tank absorbs the expanded volume and keeps the pressure stable. The trap is installing a PRV without an expansion tank, or installing an undersized or incorrectly pre-charged tank. The disciplined rule is that every closed system with a water heater must have a correctly sized and pre-charged expansion tank, and the T&P weeping in such a system is the symptom of the missing or failed tank.

### Test the T&P Periodically and Replace It per the Manufacturer Schedule

T&P valves should be test-operated periodically (typically annually) by lifting the lever to confirm the valve opens and discharges, and reseats cleanly when released. Valves that do not open, do not reseat, or weep after the test should be replaced. Manufacturers recommend replacement at intervals (often 3 to 5 years for residential, more frequently in hard-water areas where mineral deposits accumulate in the valve). The trap is never testing the T&P, so a stuck or mineral-plugged valve remains in service, silently failed. The disciplined rule is to test the T&P at every service visit, to replace valves that fail the test or that weep, and to follow the manufacturer replacement interval in hard-water areas.

## Common Traps

### Plugging or Capping a Weeping T&P

A homeowner or plumber plugs or caps a weeping T&P to stop the drip, disabling the only overpressure protection on the heater. The trap is that the drip stops (the symptom is silenced) but the protection is gone, and the next overpressure event (thermostat failure, thermal expansion) has no relief, potentially rupturing the heater. The mechanism is that the T&P is the last line of defense, and plugging it removes the defense. The false signal is that "the drip stopped." The harm is catastrophic heater failure. The defense is to never plug or cap a T&P, to diagnose the weep cause, and to replace a defective valve with one of equal or greater rating.

### Reducing the Discharge Pipe Diameter

A plumber reduces the T&P discharge pipe from 3/4 inch to 1/2 inch to fit a convenient routing, restricting the discharge flow. The trap is that the reduced pipe cannot carry the valve's rated discharge flow, so during a relief event the pressure downstream of the valve rises, reducing the effective relief capacity and potentially allowing the heater to overpressure. The mechanism is that discharge pipe restriction reduces relief capacity. The false signal is that "the discharge is piped." The harm is inadequate relief during an overpressure event. The defense is to maintain the full valve outlet diameter through the entire discharge run, with no reductions.

### Installing a Shutoff in the Discharge Pipe

A plumber installs a shutoff valve in the T&P discharge pipe "for maintenance," allowing the protection to be defeated if the shutoff is closed. The trap is that the shutoff, if closed (accidentally or intentionally to silence a weep), completely disables the relief. The mechanism is that a closed shutoff in the discharge is equivalent to plugging the valve. The false signal is that "the discharge is piped." The harm is total loss of overpressure protection. The defense is to never install a shutoff in the T&P discharge, ever.

### Using PVC for the Discharge Pipe

A plumber uses standard PVC for the T&P discharge pipe, and when the valve discharges (at 210°F), the PVC softens, collapses, and restricts or blocks the discharge. The trap is that PVC is convenient and approved for cold DWV, but it is not rated for the 210°F discharge temperature. The mechanism is that PVC loses structural integrity at high temperature. The false signal is that "the pipe is installed." The harm is discharge restriction or blockage during a relief event. The defense is to use a high-temperature material (copper, CPVC rated for hot discharge, steel) for the T&P discharge.

### Replacing a Weeping T&P Without Diagnosing the Cause

A plumber replaces a weeping T&P with a new valve, which weeps for the same reason (thermal expansion, excessive temperature, excessive pressure), and the cycle repeats. The trap is that the new valve is not defective; the system condition that caused the weep is unaddressed. The mechanism is that the weep is a symptom. The false signal is that "the valve was replaced." The harm is continued weeping, wasted water, and the underlying condition (which may be dangerous, such as a thermostat failure) going unaddressed. The defense is to diagnose the cause (measure pressure over a heating cycle, measure temperature, measure static pressure) before replacing the valve, and to fix the cause.

## Self-Check

- Is the T&P valve's pressure rating (typically 150 psi), temperature rating (typically 210°F), and BTU-per-hour discharge capacity verified against the heater's input rating, and does the valve capacity meet or exceed the heater input?
- Is the T&P valve never plugged, capped, or obstructed, and is there no shutoff valve anywhere in the discharge pipe?
- Is the discharge pipe the same diameter as the valve outlet (no reductions), made of a high-temperature material (copper, CPVC rated for hot discharge, or steel — not PVC), and routed to drain by gravity with no traps or upward runs?
- Does the discharge terminate within 6 inches of the floor, to a floor drain, to an indirect waste receptor, or to the exterior, with an air gap at the termination?
- If the T&P is weeping, did I diagnose the cause (thermal expansion pressure spikes, excessive temperature, excessive supply pressure) by measuring with appropriate gauges before replacing the valve?
- For a closed system (PRV, meter check, or backflow preventer), is a correctly sized and pre-charged thermal expansion tank installed, and is the pre-charge verified to match the system cold pressure?
- Did I test-operate the T&P by lifting the lever to confirm it opens, discharges, and reseats cleanly, and did I replace the valve if it failed the test?
- In hard-water areas, am I following the manufacturer replacement interval (often 3 to 5 years) for the T&P, given mineral accumulation?
- Did I verify that the system pressure remains below the T&P setpoint (typically 150 psi) during water heater heating cycles, using a logging gauge?
- Did I document the T&P ratings, discharge routing and material, expansion tank size and pre-charge, and any diagnostic readings, so the safety system is verifiable?
