# Radiant Manifold Balancing and Air Purging

Commissioning a radiant system — making it actually deliver the heat it was designed to deliver — is where the design meets reality, and it is where most radiant systems fail to perform. The judgment problem is that a radiant manifold with multiple loops will not self-balance: the shortest, lowest-resistance loop takes disproportionate flow while the longer loops starve, and any air trapped in a loop blocks flow entirely, creating a cold zone that no amount of balancing can fix. An installer who turns on the system, feels warm water at the manifold, and walks away has not commissioned the system — the loops may be wildly imbalanced, air-locked, or short-circuiting, and the floor will have hot and cold stripes for the life of the building. This skill covers flow balancing, air purging, the air separator, and the role limits that place complex commissioning with hydronic specialists.

## Core Rules

### Balance Each Loop to Its Design Flow Using the Manifold Flow Meters

A radiant manifold has a flow meter (a sight glass with a float) on each loop's return, and a balancing valve on each loop's supply. The correct commissioning method is to set each loop's balancing valve so its flow meter reads the design flow for that loop (determined from the loop's heat output and the design temperature drop, typically 10 to 20°F). The trap is opening all balancing valves fully and assuming the manifold self-distributes — it does not, because the loops have different lengths and resistances, and the shortest loop short-circuits most of the flow while the longer loops get little. The disciplined approach is to determine each loop's design flow, close the balancing valves, then open and adjust each one until its flow meter reads the design value, re-checking the others as you go (because adjusting one loop affects the others on a common circulator). Iterate until all loops read their design flow. Document the final flow settings.

### Purge Air From Every Loop Before and After Start-Up

Air in a hydronic loop is the enemy of flow and heat: an air pocket in a radiant loop blocks the water from circulating, and that loop goes cold regardless of the manifold balance or the circulator. Air enters the system during fill and construction, and it is released from the water as it heats (cold water holds more dissolved air than hot). The disciplined purging method is to fill the system slowly, use the manifold's purge valves (a drain on the supply and return of each loop, or a purge station) to force water through each loop individually until no air bubbles appear in the discharge, and then run the system through a heat-up cycle and purge again as the dissolved air comes out of solution. The trap is filling the system, running the pump briefly, and assuming the air separator will catch everything — it will not catch a large air pocket trapped in a loop, and that loop will run cold. Purge each loop individually with a hose to a drain, confirm no bubbles, and repeat after the first heat-up.

### Install and Locate the Air Separator and Expansion Tank Correctly

Beyond loop purging, a hydronic system needs an air separator (a device that removes micro-bubbles and dissolved air from the flowing water) installed at the point of highest temperature and lowest pressure — typically just after the heat source on the supply main, where the water is hottest and the air is most likely to come out of solution. The expansion tank (which absorbs the volume change as water heats and cools) is installed at the same point, connected to the air separator's connection, because this is the point of lowest pressure and the tank functions correctly there. The trap is installing the air separator in a cool return location where little air comes out of solution, or omitting it and relying on manual purging forever. The disciplined approach is to install a micro-bubble air separator at the hottest, lowest-pressure point (supply out of the heat source), connect the expansion tank there, and confirm the separator has the required flow and orientation per the manufacturer.

### Set the Circulator and Mixing Valve for the Design Conditions

The circulator must run at the design flow (set during balancing), and on a multi-zone system it should be a variable-speed or delta-P circulator that maintains flow as zone valves open and close. The mixing valve (or mixing block) must be set to deliver the design supply temperature to the radiant loops — not the boiler temperature. The trap is setting the mixing valve by guess or leaving it at a default, so the radiant supply is too hot (overheating the floor and risking damage, especially to hardwood) or too cold (under-heating the space). The disciplined approach is to set the mixing valve to the design supply temperature (measured at the manifold supply with an accurate thermometer), verify it holds that temperature across the operating range, and use outdoor reset to adjust the supply temperature with the outdoor conditions. Confirm the circulator delivers the total design flow at the balanced condition.

### Diagnose Uneven Floor Temperature by Loop, Not by Guess

When a radiant floor has cold spots or cold rooms, the diagnosis must be systematic, by loop. Check each loop's flow meter at the manifold: a loop reading zero flow is air-locked or has a closed valve; a loop reading far below design is restricted or imbalanced; a loop reading above design is over-balanced and stealing flow from others. Measure the supply and return temperature at each loop: a loop with a large temperature drop (return much cooler than supply) has low flow; a loop with a small drop has high flow or is short-circuiting. The trap is guessing ("the boiler must be too small" or "the tubing is broken") without checking the manifold flows and temperatures, leading to wrong and expensive conclusions. The disciplined approach is to read every loop's flow and temperature at the manifold, compare to design, and the problem (air, imbalance, restriction) usually reveals itself.

### Respect the Role Limits — Complex Commissioning Needs Hydronic Specialists

Balancing and purging a simple single-zone radiant system is within a licensed plumber or radiant installer's scope. Multi-zone systems, systems with persistent air or balance problems, and combination systems (radiant plus radiators, snow-melt integration) may require a hydronic specialist with the instruments and experience to diagnose and resolve complex commissioning issues. Confirm scope before taking on difficult commissioning, and escalate complex problems to a hydronic specialist.

## Common Traps

### Assuming the Manifold Self-Balances

The installer opens all balancing valves, feels warm water at the manifold, and walks away. The trap is that loops have different resistances and the flow distributes by resistance, so the short loops short-circuit and the long loops starve. The mechanism is that flow follows the path of least resistance. The false signal is "all the loops have water." The harm is hot and cold stripes in the floor, with the long/high-resistance loops running cold. The defense is to balance each loop to its design flow using the manifold flow meters.

### Filling the System and Assuming the Air Separator Catches Everything

The installer fills the system, runs the pump, and assumes the air separator will remove all the air. The trap is that a large air pocket trapped in a loop cannot reach the air separator (it cannot flow through the loop), and that loop goes cold. The mechanism is that the separator only removes air that reaches it in the flowing water. The false signal is "the system is full and running." The harm is one or more cold loops that no amount of balancing fixes. The defense is to purge each loop individually through the purge valves until no air appears, and to repeat after the first heat-up.

### Air Separator in the Wrong Location

The installer puts the air separator on the cool return line, where little air comes out of solution. The trap is that air separates most effectively at high temperature and low pressure (the supply out of the heat source), and a return-mounted separator catches little. The mechanism is that dissolved air stays dissolved in cool water. The false signal is "there's an air separator." The harm is chronic micro-bubble air in the system, noisy operation, and reduced flow. The defense is to install the air separator at the hottest, lowest-pressure point.

### Guessing the Diagnosis Instead of Reading the Manifold

A cold room prompts a guess ("the boiler is too small" or "the tubing is damaged") without checking the loop's flow and temperature at the manifold. The trap is that the manifold's flow meters and temperature readings usually reveal the problem (air, imbalance, restriction) immediately, and a guess leads to a wrong, expensive conclusion. The mechanism is that the symptom (cold room) has several possible causes that the manifold data distinguishes. The false signal is "the room is cold, so the system is undersized." The harm is an unnecessary boiler replacement or floor demolition when the real problem was an air-locked loop. The defense is to read every loop's flow and temperature at the manifold before concluding.

## Self-Check

- Did I determine each loop's design flow (from the heat output and design temperature drop), and did I set each balancing valve so its flow meter reads the design value, iterating until all loops are balanced?
- Did I purge each loop individually through the purge valves until no air bubbles appeared, and did I repeat the purge after the first heat-up cycle when dissolved air came out of solution?
- Is the air separator installed at the hottest, lowest-pressure point (supply out of the heat source), with the expansion tank connected at the same point, and is it the correct size and orientation per the manufacturer?
- Is the circulator delivering the total design flow at the balanced condition, and on a multi-zone system is it a variable-speed or delta-P circulator that maintains flow as zones open and close?
- Is the mixing valve set to the design supply temperature (measured at the manifold), does it hold that temperature across the operating range, and is outdoor reset used to adjust with outdoor conditions?
- For any cold spot or cold room, did I read the affected loop's flow meter and supply/return temperature at the manifold and compare to design before concluding the cause?
- Did I confirm my scope covers this commissioning, and did I escalate complex multi-zone or persistent-problem systems to a hydronic specialist?
- Are the design flow rates, the final balanced flow settings, the purge procedure, the mixing temperature, and the diagnostic readings documented for the owner and future service?
