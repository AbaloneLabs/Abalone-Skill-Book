# Pipe Lining Versus Bursting Versus Open-Cut Decision

Rehabilitating an underground line is a decision among three fundamentally different methods, and choosing the wrong one turns a manageable project into a failed liner, a stuck bursting head, or an unnecessarily expensive excavation. The judgment problem is that each method has hard constraints that agents often learn only after mobilizing: cured-in-place pipe (CIPP) lining requires a host pipe that is structurally intact enough to hold the liner and cannot fix a collapsed or off-set section; pipe bursting requires a continuous host pipe to pull through and a receiving geometry that tolerates expansion; open-cut excavation is the only method that can change alignment, replace a fully collapsed section, or install a larger main, but it carries the full cost of surface disruption. Agents miss the diagnosis because they favor the method they know, or they accept a CCTV report at face value without confirming whether the defects are structural or merely joint and infiltration issues. This skill covers method selection by pipe condition, the structural-versus-non-structural lining distinction, diameter and upsizing limits, surface-disruption and depth tradeoffs, and the lateral-reinstatement planning that determines whether a trenchless job is even feasible.

## Core Rules

### Select the Method From the Pipe Condition, Not From the Preferred Technique

The starting point is the CCTV inspection and the structural verdict on the existing pipe, because each method has a hard precondition. CIPP lining (non-structural or semi-structural) requires a host pipe that is continuous, roughly round, and free of large holes, collapses, or major offsets; it rehabilitates an existing pipe by creating a new interior surface but cannot restore a pipe that has lost its shape. Pipe bursting replaces the host pipe by fracturing it outward and pulling a new pipe behind the bursting head, so it tolerates cracks, roots, and moderate deformation but still needs a continuous host to pull through and breaks down if it hits a fully collapsed section or an unreinforced concrete encasement. Open-cut excavation is the universal method: it can replace any condition, change alignment, increase diameter freely, and install a brand-new main, at the cost of full surface disruption. The discipline is to read the CCTV for structural failure first, and route any collapsed, heavily deformed, or off-set section to bursting or open-cut rather than lining.

### Decide Structural Versus Non-Structural Lining Before Specifying a Liner

Liners fall into two classes and the choice must match the host pipe's remaining strength. A non-structural liner (a thin CIPP or spray-on coating) seals infiltration and improves hydraulics but assumes the host pipe continues to carry the soil and load; if the host pipe is corroding or losing wall, a non-structural liner delays but does not prevent failure. A structural or fully structural liner (a thick CIPP designed to AWWA or ASTM standalone capacity, or a close-fit slip liner) is designed to carry the full soil, traffic, and groundwater load independently of the host pipe, and is required when the host is significantly degraded. The judgment is to assess the host pipe's remaining structural capacity from the CCTV (wall loss, corrosion, deformation) and the loading (shallow traffic load versus deep non-traffic), and to specify a structural liner where the host can no longer carry the load. The trap is specifying a cheap non-structural liner on a host pipe that is already failing structurally, which gives a few years of false security.

### Account for Diameter Loss With Lining and Upsizing Limits With Bursting

Every liner reduces the internal diameter, and the reduction matters for hydraulics. A typical CIPP liner reduces the diameter by the liner thickness (commonly 3 to 12 mm depending on size and structural class), and while the smoother liner wall partly recovers flow capacity through a lower Manning's n, a line already near capacity can lose meaningful flow. The discipline is to compute the post-lining capacity (new diameter and the smoother friction coefficient) and confirm it still meets the design flow, especially on near-flat gravity sewers where a small loss of slope-effective diameter matters. Pipe bursting, by contrast, can upsize the pipe by one or two nominal sizes (commonly bursting a 4-inch to a 4- or 6-inch, within the expansion the soil and geometry allow), which is the only trenchless method that increases capacity. Open-cut has no diameter constraint. The rule is to size the method to the hydraulic requirement: lining for seal-and-smooth where capacity is adequate, bursting for capacity increase, open-cut for the largest changes.

### Weigh Surface Disruption, Depth, and Access Against Cost

The economic case for trenchless methods is avoiding surface disruption, and the weight of that case scales with what is above the pipe. CIPP lining and pipe bursting need only an entry pit and an exit pit (and for bursting, a pulling shaft), so under a building slab, a busy roadway, a landscaped yard, or mature trees, trenchless is often dramatically cheaper than open-cut once restoration is included. But trenchless economics erode with depth and access: very deep lines need large pits, long pulls exceed the bursting machine's capability, and lines with many bends or sharp changes of direction may not be burstable or may need multiple liners. Open-cut becomes competitive or cheaper on shallow lines in open ground, short runs, or where the surface is already being disturbed. The discipline is to cost the full installed-and-restored price for each method, including pavement, landscaping, and traffic management, and to choose trenchless where the restored cost favors it and the geometry permits.

### Plan Lateral Reinstatement and Confirm It Is Feasible Before Committing to Lining

Main-line lining and bursting both seal off the service laterals that connect properties to the main, and reinstating those laterals is a defining cost and risk of any lining project. After a CIPP main liner is installed and cured, each active lateral must be robotically cut open from inside the main, and each cut must be located precisely and verified by camera; a missed or mis-cut lateral silently stops a property's service and causes a backup. Pipe bursting shears off the old lateral connections, which must then be reconnected by excavation at each property or by a reconnection fitting, also a significant cost. The discipline is to map every active lateral from the pre-lining CCTV, confirm each one's location and condition, count the reinstatement work into the project cost, and confirm that the lateral geometry (angle, condition) permits robotic reinstatement. A lining project with many laterals on a busy street can have reinstatement costs that rival the lining itself, and missing this step turns a trenchless job into a series of emergency excavations.

## Common Traps

### Specifying CIPP Lining on a Collapsed or Heavily Deformed Pipe

A CCTV shows infiltration and root intrusion, and the plumber specifies CIPP lining, but the report also shows a partially collapsed section that the liner cannot bridge, and the liner wrinkles, bridges, or fails at the collapse. The trap is that lining is approved on a non-structural defect report. The mechanism is that a liner needs a continuous, roughly round host to support it during inversion and cure. The false signal is that "the pipe is damaged and lining fixes damage." The harm is a failed liner, wasted mobilization, and eventual excavation anyway. The defense is to read the CCTV for structural failure (collapse, offset, deformation beyond roughly 10 percent) and route those sections to bursting or open-cut.

### Choosing a Non-Structural Liner for a Host Pipe That Has Lost Wall Strength

A corroded cast-iron sewer is lined with a thin non-structural CIPP to stop infiltration, but the host pipe continues to corrode and loses structural capacity, and within a few years the liner deforms as the host fails around it. The trap is that the infiltration stops, suggesting success. The mechanism is that a non-structural liner relies on the host for load. The false signal is that "the leak is sealed." The harm is delayed structural failure. The defense is to assess remaining host wall from the CCTV and loading, and to specify a structural (standalone-capacity) liner where the host can no longer carry the load.

### Ignoring Diameter Loss and Reducing an Already-Marginal Sewer's Capacity

A near-flat 6-inch sewer at capacity is lined, losing roughly half an inch of diameter, and although the liner is smoother, the net capacity drops enough to cause backups in wet weather. The trap is that the lining "improves" the pipe while reducing its effective size. The mechanism is that smoother walls recover some friction loss but cannot fully offset a diameter reduction on a flat grade. The false signal is that "a new liner is installed." The harm is recurring surcharging. The defense is to compute post-lining capacity with the reduced diameter and the lower Manning's n, and to choose bursting or open-cut if capacity is marginal.

### Choosing Bursting on a Line With Bends, Encasements, or a Collapsed Section

A line is specified for pipe bursting to upsize capacity, but the run has two sharp bends and a section of concrete encasement, and the bursting head seizes partway, requiring an emergency excavation to recover it. The trap is that bursting is approved on geometry it cannot traverse. The mechanism is that bursting heads cannot negotiate sharp bends or fracture heavy encasements. The false signal is that "bursting replaces the pipe." The harm is a stuck head and a costly recovery dig. The defense is to confirm from the CCTV and records that the run is straight enough, free of heavy encasement, and continuous, and to switch to open-cut or segmental replacement where it is not.

### Underestimating Lateral Reinstatement Cost and Missing a Lateral

A main is lined and the contractor reinstates the obvious laterals, but one active lateral is misidentified on the CCTV and left sealed, and the property backs up days later. The trap is that reinstatement is treated as routine rather than critical. The mechanism is that lining seals every connection, and a missed cut is a silent service loss. The false signal is that "the main is rehabilitated." The harm is a property backup and an emergency excavation. The defense is to map every active lateral before lining, robotically cut and camera-verify each one after cure, and dye-test any uncertain connection.

## Self-Check

- Did I base the method choice on the structural condition from CCTV (collapse, offset, deformation beyond roughly 10 percent), routing failed sections to bursting or open-cut rather than lining?
- Did I decide between non-structural and structural lining based on the host pipe's remaining wall strength and the soil and traffic loading, specifying a standalone-capacity liner where the host is degrading?
- Did I compute the post-lining hydraulic capacity with the reduced diameter and the smoother friction coefficient, and confirm the line still meets design flow, especially on near-flat gravity sewers?
- If upsizing is required, did I confirm pipe bursting can achieve the target diameter within the soil and geometry limits, or specify open-cut where it cannot?
- Did I cost each method on a fully installed and restored basis (pavement, landscaping, traffic management) and choose trenchless only where restored cost and geometry favor it?
- For pipe bursting, did I confirm the run is free of sharp bends, heavy concrete encasement, and fully collapsed sections that would seize the head?
- Did I map every active service lateral from the pre-lining CCTV, count the reinstatement work into the project cost, and confirm each lateral's geometry permits robotic reinstatement?
- Did I distinguish between main-line and lateral work, and confirm whether a main-to-lateral (T-liner) connection is needed at the interface?
- Did I verify access pit and pulling shaft locations are feasible and that depth does not make the pits uneconomic?
- Did I document the structural verdict, the method rationale, the diameter and capacity check, and the lateral reinstatement plan so the method choice is defensible and verifiable?
