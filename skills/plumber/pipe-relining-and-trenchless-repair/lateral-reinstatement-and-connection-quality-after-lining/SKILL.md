# Lateral Reinstatement and Connection Quality After Lining

When a sewer main is lined, the cured liner seals every service lateral connection along its length, and each active lateral must be reopened from inside the main and verified before any property is returned to service. The judgment problem is that a missed or mis-cut lateral is a silent failure: the property's drain is blocked, the backup may not appear for hours or days, and the cause is invisible on a quick main-line camera pass. Agents miss the risk because reinstatement looks like a routine robotic step, but it is in fact a precision operation where a few inches of error cuts the lateral at the wrong angle, leaves a flap of liner obstructing flow, or seals the wrong connection entirely. This skill covers mapping laterals before lining, robotic cutting technique and verification, the main-to-lateral interface seal (T-liner), post-lining flow testing, and the documentation that proves every connection was found and opened, to prevent the silent service losses and property backups that define a failed reinstatement.

## Core Rules

### Map Every Active Lateral Before Lining and Confirm Each Connection

Reinstatement begins before the liner is installed, with a pre-lining CCTV survey that identifies every lateral connection along the main, marks its station (distance from the upstream access), records its clock position (for example, 10 o'clock or 2 o'clock), and confirms whether each is active. Active laterals are those serving a connected property; abandoned or capped laterals are sealed and left alone. The map is the work order for reinstatement: each active lateral must be cut open after cure, and the count must reconcile. The discipline is to build the lateral map during pre-lining inspection, cross-check it against property records (every building on the main should have a connection), and treat any unaccounted connection as a risk to investigate. A reinstatement crew working without a confirmed map will miss laterals, because the liner obscures the original connections and only the map tells them where to cut.

### Cut Each Lateral Robotically With the Correct Angle and Full Opening

After the liner cures, a robotic cutter is launched into the main and navigated to each mapped lateral station, where it cuts through the liner to reopen the host lateral connection. The cut must be centered on the lateral, sized to the lateral diameter (commonly 4 to 6 inches for residential service), and angled to match the lateral's entry into the main (a lateral entering at 10 o'clock is cut at that angle, not straight in). A cut that is too small restricts flow, a cut off-center leaves a liner flap that catches debris, and a cut at the wrong angle damages the host lateral invert. The discipline is to locate each lateral precisely (using the station and clock position from the map, and confirming by the shadow or depression at the connection), cut to the full lateral diameter, and remove the cut liner coupon so it does not wash downstream and obstruct. Each cut is then immediately camera-verified from the main.

### Verify Each Reinstated Lateral by Camera and Dye Test, Not by Assumption

A cut that looks open on the main-line camera can still be partially obstructed by a liner flap, or can be the wrong connection (an abandoned lateral reopened while the active one stays sealed). The verification is two-step: a main-line camera pass confirming a clean, full-diameter opening at each station, and a dye test from the property side (fluorescent dye introduced at the property's cleanout or fixture, observed entering the main at the correct station through the camera). The dye test confirms the connection is actually the property's active drain and that flow passes unobstructed. The discipline is to dye-test every reinstated lateral, especially on mains with many connections or where the property records are uncertain, and to document each test with the station, the property, and the camera image. A lateral "reinstated" without dye verification is an assumption, and assumptions produce silent backups.

### Seal the Main-to-Lateral Interface Where Infiltration Is a Risk

Cutting the liner to reopen a lateral leaves an annular gap between the new liner edge and the host lateral, and on lines with high groundwater or on public mains subject to infiltration standards, that gap is a path for groundwater and root intrusion that defeats part of the lining benefit. The seal is a main-to-lateral connection liner (often called a T-liner or Lateral Connection Liner), a short CIPP liner that lines the lateral for a few feet and overlaps the main liner, creating a continuous sealed connection. T-liners are specified where infiltration at the connection is a design concern (wet ground, high water table, regulatory infiltration limits) and are installed after the main cut, cured in place, and camera-verified. The discipline is to decide during design which connections need a T-liner (based on groundwater and infiltration requirements), install and verify them, and not rely on the open cut alone where infiltration must be controlled.

### Run a Post-Lining Flow Test and Document Every Reinstated Connection

The final proof of reinstatement is a flow test that confirms the system drains as designed, combined with documentation that records every active lateral, its station, the cut, the dye verification, and any T-liner installed. The flow test may be a main-line flush or a property-by-property fixture drain, observing that each property's flow reaches the main without backup. The documentation is the deliverable that protects both the contractor and the owner: it proves which connections were found and opened, supports any later service call, and is required by most municipal lining programs. The discipline is to run the flow test with all laterals in service, to resolve any backup immediately (it indicates a missed or obstructed lateral), and to deliver a reinstatement log (station, clock position, property, cut size, dye result, T-liner yes or no) for every connection on the main.

## Common Traps

### Reinstating Without a Pre-Lining Lateral Map and Missing Connections

The crew reinstates the obvious laterals visible on a quick post-cure camera, but two active connections were obscured by the liner and are not on any map, and those properties back up within days. The trap is that reinstatement is treated as a visual step rather than a map-driven step. The mechanism is that the liner hides the original connections, and only the pre-lining map locates them. The false signal is that "the visible laterals were cut." The harm is silent service loss at the unmapped properties. The defense is to build and confirm the lateral map during pre-lining CCTV, cross-check against property records, and cut every active lateral on the map.

### Cutting the Lateral Off-Center or Undersized, Leaving a Liner Flap

The robotic cutter is positioned slightly off the lateral center, and the cut leaves a flap of liner that projects into the lateral, catching solids and causing repeated blockages. The trap is that the cut "looks open" from the main. The mechanism is that an off-center or undersized cut leaves liner material in the flow path. The false signal is that "the lateral is reinstated." The harm is recurring blockages at the property. The defense is to locate each lateral by station and clock position, cut to the full lateral diameter at the matching angle, remove the coupon, and camera-verify the opening is clean and full-size.

### Reopening an Abandoned Lateral While the Active One Stays Sealed

Two connections are close together on the main, the crew cuts the more visible one, but it is an abandoned lateral, while the property's active connection a few feet away remains sealed. The trap is that a cut was made and looks correct. The mechanism is that proximity and visibility mislead the location. The false signal is that "a lateral was reinstated at that station." The harm is the active property backs up while the crew believes the work is done. The defense is to dye-test every reinstated lateral from the property side, confirming the connection actually serves the property.

### Skipping the T-Liner Where Infiltration at the Connection Must Be Controlled

The main is lined to stop infiltration, but the lateral connections are cut open with no seal, and groundwater enters through the annular gap at each connection, defeating the infiltration reduction the lining was meant to achieve. The trap is that the main is "sealed." The mechanism is that the open cut leaves a gap between the liner and the host lateral. The false signal is that "the main lining is complete." The harm is continued infiltration and root intrusion at the connections. The defense is to specify and install T-liners at connections where groundwater or infiltration standards require, and to verify each by camera.

### Declaring Success Without a Flow Test or Reinstatement Documentation

The crew cuts and camera-verifies each lateral, packs up, and the project is signed off, but no flow test is run and no reinstatement log is delivered, and a partially obstructed lateral goes unnoticed until the property backs up weeks later. The trap is that camera verification is mistaken for functional verification. The mechanism is that a camera shows an opening but not whether flow passes freely under load. The false signal is that "all laterals were cut and verified." The harm is a delayed backup and no record to diagnose it. The defense is to run a post-lining flow test with all laterals in service and to deliver a reinstatement log for every connection.

## Self-Check

- Did I build a lateral map during pre-lining CCTV, recording each connection's station, clock position, and active status, and cross-check it against property records so no active lateral is unaccounted?
- Did I locate each lateral by station and clock position during reinstatement, cut to the full lateral diameter at the matching angle, and remove the cut liner coupon so it does not obstruct downstream?
- Did I camera-verify each cut from the main, confirming a clean, full-diameter opening with no liner flap or obstruction?
- Did I dye-test every reinstated lateral from the property side (fluorescent dye at the cleanout or fixture, observed entering the main at the correct station) to confirm it is the property's active drain?
- Where infiltration at the connection is a design concern (high groundwater, regulatory infiltration limits), did I specify and install a T-liner at each such connection and verify it by camera?
- Did I confirm that abandoned or capped laterals were intentionally sealed and not cut, and that the count of cut laterals matches the count of active properties on the main?
- Did I run a post-lining flow test with all laterals in service, observing that each property's flow reaches the main without backup?
- Did I resolve any backup immediately as a probable missed or obstructed lateral, rather than attributing it to other causes?
- Did I deliver a reinstatement log (station, clock position, property, cut size, dye result, T-liner yes or no) for every connection on the main?
- Did I confirm that the robotic cutter did not damage the host lateral invert and that no cut coupon washed downstream to obstruct the main?
