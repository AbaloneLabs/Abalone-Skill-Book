# Cured-In-Place Pipe Lining Process and Cure Verification

Cured-in-place pipe (CIPP) lining creates a new structural pipe inside an old one by saturating a felt or fiberglass tube with resin, inverting or pulling it into place, and curing the resin into a rigid shell, and the entire process is a chain in which any weak link produces a failed liner. The judgment problem is that a liner can look perfect on a final CCTV while being uncured in spots, saturated unevenly, wrinkled at bends, or contaminated with uncured styrene that leaches into potable water. Agents miss the failures because the visible surface is only the outside of the cure, and the real quality is in the resin chemistry, the temperature profile over time, and the bond to the host pipe, none of which are visible on a camera pass. This skill covers pre-cleaning and inspection, saturation and inversion control, cure method and the temperature-time profile, cool-down discipline, and the final verification (CCTV plus thickness and bond checks) that must precede return to service, to prevent the uncured-liner and potable-contamination failures that follow a rushed or under-verified cure.

## Core Rules

### Pre-Clean, Inspect, and Remove All Obstructions Before Introducing the Liner

A liner cannot cure correctly over debris, standing water, roots, or offset joints, and a liner installed over an obstruction wrinkles, bridges, or cures with a void behind it. The pre-lining workflow is high-pressure jet cleaning (for sewers) or mechanical cleaning and drying (for potable lines), followed by a CCTV inspection to confirm the host is clean, continuous, and free of standing water, roots, protruding laterals, and major offsets. Any offset, protrusion, or partial collapse that the liner cannot bridge must be removed or the section routed to a different method. The discipline is to treat the pre-lining CCTV as a go/no-go gate: if the host is not clean, dry, and structurally acceptable, the liner does not go in. Skipping the pre-clean to save time produces a liner that fails the final inspection and must be re-done, often after the access pits are already closed.

### Control Resin Saturation and Inversion to Avoid Dry Spots and Wrinkles

The liner must be uniformly saturated with the correct resin volume before installation, and installed by a method (inversion or pull-in) that does not stretch, fold, or wrinkle it. Dry spots (areas where the resin did not fully wet the felt) cure as weak or porous patches and are a primary failure mode; wrinkles (from pulling a too-long liner or inverting too fast) create flow obstructions and stress concentrators. The discipline is to verify the resin batch volume against the liner's calculated wet-out requirement, to saturate under controlled vacuum or pressure per the manufacturer process, and to invert or pull at the controlled rate that keeps the liner taut against the host. For inversion lining, the inversion pressure and rate must be maintained per the manufacturer table; for pull-in-and-inflate, the liner must not be dragged across sharp host edges that could tear it. The trap is accepting a liner that "looks okay" entering the host without verifying saturation uniformity.

### Match the Cure Method to the Liner and Verify the Temperature-Time Profile

Cure is driven by heat (or UV light for UV-cured fiberglass liners), and each liner specification defines a cure temperature and a dwell time that the resin must reach to fully crosslink. Steam cure and hot-water cure are the thermal methods: hot water is gentler and common for larger diameters, steam is faster and common for smaller diameters, and both require the entire liner length to reach the specified cure temperature for the specified dwell. UV cure uses a light train pulled through a translucent fiberglass liner and is faster and more controllable but only works on UV-rated liners. The discipline is to specify the cure method per the liner, to monitor the temperature (or UV intensity and speed) continuously along the liner, and to record the temperature-time profile as proof of cure. A liner that reached temperature for too short a dwell, or that had a cold spot at an invert or a bend, is undercured at that spot regardless of how the rest looks.

### Enforce Cool-Down Before Service and Before Any Pressure or Flow

A cured liner is still thermally stressed and the resin continues to develop final strength as it cools, and introducing flow or pressure while the liner is hot causes movement, wrinkling, or delamination. The cool-down discipline is to reduce the cure temperature gradually (per the manufacturer schedule, often a controlled drop rather than a sudden drain), to allow the liner to reach near ambient temperature before the bulkheads are removed or flow is restored, and to keep the liner under a small holding pressure during cool-down so it stays in contact with the host. For potable lines, the cool-down must be complete before any flushing, because hot resin can release residual monomer into the water. The trap is restoring service as soon as the cure timer ends, while the liner is still hot, which produces a liner that looks cured but has relaxed away from the host or wrinkled at the invert.

### Verify Cure by Final CCTV Plus Thickness and Bond Checks, and Certify Potable Liners

The final verification is more than a CCTV pass. The CCTV confirms the liner is smooth, free of wrinkles and lifts, fully round, and that all laterals are open (or sealed where intended), but it cannot confirm the liner is fully cured, the correct thickness, or bonded to the host. The additional checks are thickness measurement (by a coupon cut from a bulkhead or by a calibrated ultrasonic gauge), confirmation of the structural class against the design, and for potable liners, certification to NSF/ANSI 61 (the drinking water system component health effects standard) and a flush-and-test for residual styrene or monomer before return to service. The discipline is to require the temperature-time record, the thickness measurement, the final CCTV, and (for potable) the NSF 61 certification and contaminant test as a complete package before signing off, and to reject any liner missing any element.

## Common Traps

### Installing the Liner Over Standing Water, Roots, or an Offset Joint

The host pipe is jet-cleaned but not dewatered, and the liner is inverted over a pool of standing water at a low point, curing with a void and a wrinkle at the invert. The trap is that cleaning is mistaken for drying. The mechanism is that water and debris prevent resin contact with the host and distort the liner during cure. The false signal is that "the pipe was cleaned." The harm is a liner with voids and wrinkles that fails early. The defense is to CCTV after cleaning and dewatering, confirm the host is dry and continuous, and treat any offset or obstruction as a no-go until removed.

### Accepting Dry Spots From Incomplete Resin Saturation

The resin volume is short of the wet-out requirement, and areas of the felt cure dry and porous, visible only as a discolored patch on close CCTV. The trap is that the liner inverts and cures "normally." The mechanism is that under-saturation leaves unwet felt that cannot crosslink. The false signal is that "the liner cured." The harm is a structurally weak, leak-prone patch. The defense is to verify resin batch volume against the calculated wet-out, saturate under controlled vacuum, and inspect the saturated liner for uniform wetting before installation.

### Declaring Cure From the Timer Alone Without the Temperature-Time Profile

The crew runs the cure for the scheduled time, but a cold spot at a bend or invert never reached cure temperature, and that section is undercured and soft. The trap is that the schedule is treated as proof. The mechanism is that cure requires temperature and time together; time alone is insufficient. The false signal is that "the cure timer elapsed." The harm is an undercured section that deforms under load. The defense is to monitor temperature along the entire liner, record the temperature-time profile, and confirm every section reached the specified temperature for the specified dwell.

### Restoring Service While the Liner Is Still Hot

The cure timer ends and flow is restored immediately, while the liner is still at cure temperature, and the liner relaxes away from the host or wrinkles at the invert as it cools under flow. The trap is that "cured" is read as "ready." The mechanism is that the resin continues to develop strength during cool-down and must stay in contact with the host. The false signal is that "the cure is complete." The harm is a deformed liner and lost bond. The defense is to cool down per the manufacturer schedule under holding pressure, and to restore flow only after the liner reaches near ambient temperature.

### Returning a Potable Liner to Service Without NSF 61 Certification and a Contaminant Test

A potable water line is lined and flushed briefly, then returned to service, and residual styrene or uncured monomer leaches into the supply. The trap is that "the liner is cured and flushed." The mechanism is that potable liners can release residual monomer until fully cured and flushed. The false signal is that "the water runs clear." The harm is chemical contamination of the potable supply. The defense is to require NSF/ANSI 61 certification for the liner and resin system, to flush per the specification, and to test for residual styrene or monomer before return to service.

## Self-Check

- Did I jet-clean (or mechanically clean and dry) the host and run a pre-lining CCTV confirming it is clean, dry, continuous, and free of roots, offsets, and standing water before introducing the liner?
- Did I verify the resin batch volume against the liner's calculated wet-out requirement and confirm uniform saturation before installation?
- Did I invert or pull the liner at the controlled rate that keeps it taut, avoiding stretch, fold, or wrinkle, and protected from sharp host edges?
- Did I specify the cure method (steam, hot water, or UV) per the liner, and record the continuous temperature-time (or UV intensity and speed) profile along the entire liner?
- Did I confirm every section of the liner reached the specified cure temperature for the specified dwell time, with no cold spots at bends or inverts?
- Did I enforce controlled cool-down per the manufacturer schedule under holding pressure, and restore flow only after the liner reached near ambient temperature?
- Did I run a final CCTV confirming the liner is smooth, round, free of wrinkles and lifts, and that all intended laterals are open?
- Did I measure the liner thickness (coupon or ultrasonic gauge) and confirm the structural class against the design?
- For a potable liner, did I confirm NSF/ANSI 61 certification for the liner and resin, flush per specification, and test for residual styrene or monomer before return to service?
- Did I assemble the complete verification package (pre-clean CCTV, temperature-time record, thickness measurement, final CCTV, and potable certification and test) before signing off the installation?
