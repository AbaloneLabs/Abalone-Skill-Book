# Water Quality and Treatment for Wells

Private well water is untreated groundwater, and its quality varies house to house depending on the aquifer, the geology, and nearby contamination sources — a well can be pristine or it can carry bacteria, iron, hardness, sulfur, arsenic, or nitrate, and the only way to know is to test. The judgment problem is that well water treatment is not one decision but a sequence of decisions: what to test for, what the results mean, what treatment each problem requires, and in what order to install the equipment so that each stage works and does not foul the next. A plumber who installs treatment by guess — a softener for "hard water" without a test, a filter for "bad taste" without identifying the contaminant — builds a system that may not address the actual problem and may make others worse. This skill covers the diagnostic and design decisions that make well water treatment effective and maintainable.

## Core Rules

### Test Before Treating, and Test for the Right Contaminants

The first step in any well water treatment decision is a comprehensive water test from a certified lab, and the test panel must cover the contaminants likely in the area, not just the ones the owner can taste. A basic panel includes coliform bacteria (and E. coli), nitrate, pH, total dissolved solids, hardness, iron, manganese, and turbidity; an extended panel adds arsenic, lead, radon, uranium, volatile organics, and pesticides where geology or nearby land use suggests risk. The disciplined approach is to test for the full panel relevant to the region, to re-test annually for bacteria and nitrate (which vary with season and surface influence), and to test any time the taste, odor, color, or supply changes. The trap is treating based on a single "hardness" reading or on owner description without a lab test, which addresses the wrong contaminant or misses a health-based one like arsenic or nitrate.

### Address Bacterial Contamination First and With the Right Method

Bacterial contamination (coliform or E. coli positive) is a health hazard and must be addressed before any aesthetic treatment, because no iron filter or softener removes bacteria. The two standard disinfection methods are ultraviolet (UV) disinfection and chlorination. UV is a point-of-entry physical disinfectant that kills organisms as water passes a UV lamp, requires clear water (low turbidity and iron, which shield organisms from the UV light), and needs pre-treatment if turbidity or iron is high. Chlorination (a chemical disinfectant, injected or via a retention tank) is effective against a broader range of organisms and provides residual protection through the plumbing, but requires contact time, a retention tank, and often dechlorination downstream. The disciplined choice depends on the water: UV for clear water with intermittent bacterial risk, chlorination for chronic contamination, high turbidity, or where residual protection is desired. The trap is installing UV on cloudy or iron-laden water without pre-treatment, which shields organisms and lets them through.

### Sequence Treatment So Each Stage Protects the Next

Well water treatment is almost always multi-stage, and the order matters: each stage must deliver water that the next stage can handle. The general sequence is: sediment filtration first (to protect downstream equipment from particulate), then disinfection or iron/manganese treatment (which often requires oxidation and filtration), then water softening (for hardness, after iron is removed because iron fouls softener resin), then point-of-use treatment (reverse osmosis at the sink for drinking). The disciplined approach is to lay out the sequence so that fouling contaminants (sediment, iron, manganese) are removed before they reach sensitive equipment (UV, softener, RO), and to size each stage for the flow and the contaminant load. The trap is installing a softener before the iron filter, which fouls the softener resin with iron and ruins it within months.

### Size Treatment Equipment to Peak Flow and Contaminant Load

Treatment equipment is sized to two parameters: the peak flow rate (so the equipment can serve simultaneous fixtures) and the contaminant concentration (so the media or lamp can handle the load). A UV system sized to peak flow but with a lamp underrated for the water's UV transmittance (cloudy or iron-laden water) will not disinfect; a softener sized to flow but undersized to the hardness and the daily water use will exhaust its capacity between regenerations and deliver hard water. The disciplined approach is to size each unit to both parameters using the manufacturer's sizing method, and to confirm the peak flow (from fixture units) and the contaminant load (from the lab test). The trap is sizing by household size or pipe size without checking flow and concentration, which undersizes the equipment.

### Plan Maintenance From the Start: Media, Lamps, Salt, and Testing

Well water treatment requires ongoing maintenance, and a system installed without a maintenance plan will fail silently and deliver untreated water. UV lamps lose intensity and must be replaced annually (even if they still glow); the quartz sleeve must be cleaned of scale quarterly. Softeners and iron filters require salt or potassium and periodic resin or media replacement. Cartridge filters require scheduled changes based on pressure drop. The disciplined approach is to specify the maintenance schedule for each component at installation, to provide the owner with a written schedule and the replacement part numbers, and to recommend annual re-testing for bacteria and nitrate. The trap is installing the equipment and leaving the owner with no maintenance guidance, so the UV lamp runs two years past its life, the softener runs out of salt, and the system delivers untreated water.

### Distinguish Aesthetic From Health-Based Contaminants and Prioritize

Well water contaminants divide into aesthetic (hardness, iron, manganese, sulfur — unpleasant but not acutely harmful at typical levels) and health-based (bacteria, nitrate, arsenic, lead, radon — harmful and requiring treatment for safety). The disciplined approach is to prioritize health-based contaminants first, treating them to safe levels, and to address aesthetic contaminants based on owner preference and budget. The trap is spending the treatment budget on a softener for hard water while leaving an arsenic or nitrate level above the health limit untreated, because the owner could taste the hardness but not the arsenic. The disciplined rule is to communicate the health-based results clearly and to ensure they are treated before or alongside the aesthetic treatment.

## Common Traps

### Treating Based on a Single Reading or Owner Description Without a Lab Test

A plumber installs a water softener based on the owner's complaint of "hard water spots" and a single hardness reading, without testing for iron, manganese, bacteria, or arsenic, and the softener fouls with iron within months while an undetected arsenic level remains a health risk. The trap is that the owner's description points to one symptom and the single reading confirms it, but the full water quality is unknown. The mechanism is that well water carries multiple contaminants, and treating one without testing for the others leaves health risks and fouls the equipment. The false signal is that the softener "addresses the complaint." The harm is fouled equipment, an unresolved health risk, and wasted money. The defense is to require a comprehensive lab test before specifying any treatment, covering at least bacteria, nitrate, pH, hardness, iron, manganese, and region-specific health contaminants.

### Installing UV on Cloudy or Iron-Laden Water Without Pre-Treatment

A plumber installs a UV disinfection system directly on well water with high turbidity and iron, and the UV light cannot penetrate the cloudy, iron-stained water, so organisms shielded by particles pass through unharmed and the "disinfected" water is still contaminated. The trap is that UV is marketed as a simple, chemical-free disinfectant and the installer omits the pre-treatment that makes it effective. The mechanism is that UV disinfection requires the light to reach every organism, and particles and iron absorb or scatter the light, shielding organisms in their shadow. The false signal is that the UV lamp "is on." The harm is a false sense of safety — the system appears to disinfect but does not — and continued bacterial exposure. The defense is to pre-treat for turbidity and iron before UV (sediment filter, iron filter), to confirm the water's UV transmittance meets the lamp's requirement, and to use chlorination where the water cannot be made clear enough for UV.

### Putting the Softener Before the Iron Filter and Fouling the Resin

A plumber installs a water softener upstream of an iron filter, reasoning that the softener "treats the water first," and the iron fouls the softener resin, reducing capacity and eventually ruining the resin bed within months. The trap is that the sequence seems logical ("soften then filter") but the softener resin cannot handle iron load, and iron must be removed first. The mechanism is that iron oxidizes and precipitates in the resin bed, coating the resin beads and blocking the ion exchange, so the softener loses capacity and channels. The false signal is that the softener "is regenerating." The harm is ruined resin, hard water breakthrough, and a costly resin or softener replacement. The defense is to sequence treatment correctly: sediment first, then iron and manganese removal, then softening, so the softener sees only hardness.

### Sizing Equipment by Household Size Without Checking Flow and Concentration

A plumber sizes a UV system and a softener "for a family of four" without calculating the peak flow or checking the contaminant concentration, and the UV cannot disinfect at peak flow (two showers and a laundry), while the softener exhausts its capacity between regenerations on high-hardness water. The trap is that household size is a rough proxy and feels like a sizing input, but the actual sizing depends on peak flow (fixture units) and contaminant load (lab test). The mechanism is that each unit has a flow-dependent and load-dependent capacity, and a guess undersizes one or both. The false signal is that the equipment "is installed." The harm is inadequate treatment at peak or between cycles, delivering contaminated or hard water despite the equipment. The defense is to size each unit to peak flow and contaminant concentration using the manufacturer's method, and to confirm the peak flow and the lab results.

### Leaving the Owner With No Maintenance Plan and Silent Treatment Failure

A plumber installs a UV system, a softener, and an iron filter, hands the owner the manuals, and leaves, and within a year the UV lamp is past its life, the softener is out of salt, and the iron filter media is exhausted, all delivering untreated water silently. The trap is that treatment equipment requires active maintenance and the owner often does not understand the schedule, so the system fails silently. The mechanism is that UV lamps fade, softener salt depletes, and filter media exhausts, and none of these failures are obvious without monitoring. The false signal is that the equipment "is running." The harm is untreated water delivered to the building, a false sense of safety, and equipment damage from neglect. The defense is to provide a written maintenance schedule with replacement intervals and part numbers, to recommend annual re-testing for bacteria and nitrate, and to offer a service contract or reminder system.

### Prioritizing Aesthetic Treatment Over a Health-Based Contaminant

A plumber spends the owner's treatment budget on a softener and an iron filter to address staining and hardness, while an arsenic or nitrate level above the health limit goes untreated because the owner could not taste it and the plumber did not emphasize it. The trap is that the aesthetic problems are visible and the owner demands them fixed, while the health risk is invisible and easy to under-prioritize. The mechanism is that the budget is finite and the visible problems crowd out the invisible health risk. The false signal is that the owner "is happy with the water." The harm is continued exposure to a health-based contaminant that was detected but not treated. The defense is to communicate all lab results clearly, to prioritize health-based contaminants first in the treatment design, and to document the recommendation if the owner declines health-based treatment.

## Self-Check

- Did I require a comprehensive lab test (bacteria, nitrate, pH, hardness, iron, manganese, plus region-specific health contaminants) before specifying any treatment?
- For bacterial contamination, did I choose UV (with pre-treatment for turbidity and iron) or chlorination based on the water quality and the need for residual protection?
- Did I sequence treatment so that sediment, iron, and manganese are removed before UV, softening, and reverse osmosis, preventing fouling of downstream equipment?
- Did I size each treatment unit to both the peak flow (from fixture units) and the contaminant concentration (from the lab test), using the manufacturer's method?
- Did I prioritize health-based contaminants (bacteria, nitrate, arsenic, lead, radon) before aesthetic ones (hardness, iron, sulfur), and communicate the health results clearly?
- Did I provide a written maintenance schedule with replacement intervals and part numbers for UV lamps, quartz sleeves, salt, resin, and filter cartridges?
- Did I recommend annual re-testing for bacteria and nitrate, and re-testing whenever the taste, odor, color, or supply changes?
- For UV disinfection, did I confirm the water's UV transmittance meets the lamp's requirement, and install pre-treatment for turbidity and iron?
- Did I avoid installing a softener upstream of an iron filter, which fouls the resin?
- Did I document the lab results, the treatment sequence, the equipment sizing, and the maintenance schedule so the system is verifiable and the owner can maintain it?
