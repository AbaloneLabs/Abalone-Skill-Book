# Well Pump and Pressure Tank Systems

A private well system replaces the municipal supply with a pump and a pressure tank, and the pump-tank-control triangle is the most failure-prone part of rural plumbing because the three components must be matched to each other and to the well's capacity. The judgment problem is that a well pump is sized to the well's yield and the building's demand, the pressure tank is sized to the pump's flow to prevent short-cycling, and the pressure switch coordinates the two — and a mismatch in any one causes rapid cycling, premature pump failure, pressure fluctuations, or a system that cannot keep up. A plumber who swaps a pump by horsepower or a tank by size, without checking the well yield and the control settings, builds a system that fails within years. This skill covers the decisions that make well pump and pressure tank systems reliable and durable.

## Core Rules

### Select Submersible or Jet Pump Based on Well Depth and Yield

The pump type is determined primarily by the well's static water level and depth. A submersible pump (pushing from the bottom of the well) is the standard for wells deeper than about 25 feet to the static water level, and is the choice for virtually all modern drilled wells because it is efficient, quiet, and can lift from hundreds of feet. A jet pump (pulling from the top, using a venturi) is limited to about 25 feet of suction lift for a shallow-well single-pipe jet, or to about 80 to 100 feet for a deep-well two-pipe jet with a down-hole ejector, and is used for shallow wells, cisterns, and some older installations. The disciplined approach is to determine the static water level and the well depth, confirm the well's sustainable yield (in gpm), and select the pump type that fits the geometry. The trap is selecting a jet pump for a well whose static level is below 25 feet (it will not pull) or a submersible for a well that cannot house one (too small a casing).

### Size the Pump to the Well Yield and Building Demand

A well pump must deliver the building's peak demand without exceeding the well's sustainable yield, and oversizing the pump relative to the well is a common and damaging error. The disciplined approach is to obtain the well's yield (from the well log or a pump test, in gpm), confirm it exceeds the building's peak demand (from fixture units), and select a pump whose rated flow at the required head (depth plus lift plus building pressure) is within the well's yield. If the pump's rated flow exceeds the yield, the pump will draw the well down, suck air, and cavitate, damaging the pump and potentially dewatering the aquifer. The trap is selecting the pump by horsepower or "for a 4-inch well" without checking the yield, which overpumps a low-yield well. The disciplined rule is to size the pump to the lesser of the building demand and the well yield, and to install low-yield protection (a pump protector or a storage tank with a slow-pump fill) where the yield is marginal.

### Size the Pressure Tank to the Pump Flow to Prevent Short-Cycling

The pressure tank stores pressurized water so the pump does not start on every fixture opening, and its size determines the pump's cycle frequency. A pump that short-cycles (starts and stops rapidly) destroys its motor and control through repeated inrush current and mechanical shock. The disciplined rule is to size the tank so the pump runs a minimum of one minute per cycle (the industry standard for motor life), using the formula based on the pump's flow rate and the pressure switch differential: the tank's drawdown (the usable water volume between cut-in and cut-out) should equal the pump's gpm flow. For a 10 gpm pump, the drawdown should be about 10 gallons, which typically requires a 40 to 50 gallon total tank (drawdown is roughly 20 to 30 percent of tank volume for a 40/60 switch). The trap is installing an undersized tank (a small tank that fits the space) which causes rapid cycling. The disciplined rule is to size the tank to the pump flow for one-minute minimum cycles.

### Set the Pressure Switch Cut-In and Cut-Out and Match the Tank Pre-Charge

The pressure switch controls the pump: it starts the pump at the cut-in pressure (commonly 30 or 40 psi) and stops it at the cut-out (commonly 50 or 60 psi), and the standard settings are 30/50 or 40/60. The pressure tank's air pre-charge must be set 2 to 4 psi below the cut-in pressure (so 28 psi for a 30/50 switch, 38 psi for a 40/60 switch), so the tank has water available down to the cut-in and the pump starts before the tank empties. The disciplined approach is to set the switch to the desired range, confirm the cut-in and cut-out with a gauge, drain the tank, and set the pre-charge with a compressor to 2 to 4 psi below cut-in. The trap is leaving the tank at the factory pre-charge (often 38 psi) on a 30/50 switch, which means the pre-charge is above the cut-in, the tank empties before the pump starts, and the pressure drops to zero at the fixture before the pump kicks in. The disciplined rule is to match the pre-charge to the cut-in, always.

### Diagnose Short-Cycling Before Replacing Components

Short-cycling (the pump starts and stops every few seconds) is the most common well system complaint, and it is almost always a pressure tank problem (a waterlogged tank with no air charge), not a pump or switch problem. The diagnostic method is to tap the tank: a waterlogged tank sounds solid (full of water) where a healthy tank sounds hollow at the top (air). Confirm by draining the tank and checking the air charge; if no air is present or water comes out the air valve, the bladder is failed and the tank must be replaced. The disciplined rule is to diagnose the tank first when cycling is the complaint, because replacing the pump or adjusting the switch does not fix a waterlogged tank. The trap is replacing the pressure switch or the pump when the tank is the failed component.

### Provide Well Protection: Low-Yield, Dry-Well, and Lightning Protection

A well pump is an expensive down-hole component, and it must be protected from the conditions that destroy it. Low-yield and dry-well protection (a pump protector that senses motor load or water flow and shuts the pump off when the well is drawn down) prevents the pump from running dry and burning out. Lightning and surge protection (a surge arrester at the pressure switch and the control box) prevents the common rural failure of a pump motor fried by a nearby strike. The disciplined approach is to install low-yield protection on any marginal well and surge protection on every submersible installation, because the cost of the protection is a fraction of the cost of a pump replacement. The trap is omitting protection to save cost, then losing the pump to a dry well or a strike within the warranty period.

## Common Traps

### Installing a Jet Pump on a Well Whose Static Level Is Below the Suction Limit

A plumber installs a shallow-well jet pump on a well whose static water level is 30 feet below the pump, and the pump cannot pull water — it runs dry, cavitates, and delivers nothing, despite being "correctly sized" by horsepower. The trap is that a jet pump's suction lift is physically limited to about 25 feet at sea level (less at altitude), and a static level below that cannot be served by a jet pump regardless of its power. The mechanism is that atmospheric pressure can only lift water about 25 feet in a suction pipe, and no amount of pump power overcomes that limit. The false signal is that the pump "is rated for the depth." The harm is a pump that cannot deliver, a dry system, and a costly re-do with a submersible. The defense is to determine the static water level before selecting the pump type, and to use a submersible for any static level below 25 feet.

### Oversizing the Pump Relative to the Well Yield

A plumber installs a high-capacity submersible pump "for good pressure" on a low-yield well, and the pump draws the well down, sucks air, cavitates, and eventually burns out, while the building experiences intermittent pressure loss. The trap is that a bigger pump feels like "more pressure" and the well yield is invisible until the pump overruns it. The mechanism is that the pump's flow exceeds the aquifer's recharge rate, the water level in the well drops to the pump intake, the pump sucks air and runs dry, and the motor overheats. The false signal is that the pump "delivers great pressure initially." The harm is premature pump failure, intermittent supply, and potential aquifer damage. The defense is to obtain the well yield, size the pump's flow to the lesser of the building demand and the yield, and install low-yield protection on marginal wells.

### Undersizing the Pressure Tank and Short-Cycling the Pump

A plumber installs a small pressure tank to fit a tight space, and the pump short-cycles — starting and stopping every few seconds as the small tank fills and empties — destroying the motor and the pressure switch within months. The trap is that the small tank fits and "works," but its drawdown is too small for the pump flow, so the pump cycles hundreds of times per day. The mechanism is that each start draws high inrush current and shocks the motor windings and the switch contacts, and rapid cycling accumulates damage until failure. The false signal is that the system "delivers pressure." The harm is premature pump and switch failure, frequent service calls, and energy waste. The defense is to size the pressure tank so its drawdown equals the pump's gpm flow, giving a minimum one-minute run per cycle.

### Leaving the Tank Pre-Charge at the Factory Setting Without Matching the Switch

A plumber installs a pressure tank straight from the box (factory pre-charge 38 psi) on a 30/50 pressure switch, and the pre-charge is above the cut-in, so the tank empties completely before the pump starts, the pressure drops to near zero at the fixtures, and the system feels like it "runs out of water" between cycles. The trap is that the pre-charge is invisible and the tank "holds pressure," but the mismatch means the tank's air cushion is not available down to the cut-in. The mechanism is that the pre-charge must be below the cut-in for the tank to deliver water until the pump starts; a pre-charge above cut-in means the tank runs out of water before the switch trips. The false signal is that the tank "is pressurized." The harm is pressure fluctuations, near-zero pressure at the fixture before the pump starts, and a system that feels unreliable. The defense is to set the tank pre-charge 2 to 4 psi below the cut-in pressure, with the tank drained, before start-up.

### Replacing the Pump or Switch When the Tank Is Waterlogged

A homeowner reports the pump "clicks on and off constantly," and the plumber replaces the pressure switch, but the cycling continues because the actual cause is a waterlogged pressure tank with a failed bladder, and the new switch cycles just as fast. The trap is that the symptom (rapid cycling) looks like a switch problem, but the cause is almost always the tank. The mechanism is that a waterlogged tank has no air cushion, so the pressure rises and falls instantly with the pump, and the switch cycles as fast as the pump can pressurize the small water volume. The false signal is that the switch "was the problem." The harm is an unnecessary switch replacement and a continued cycling problem that destroys the new switch and the pump. The defense is to diagnose the tank first (tap test, drain and check air charge) when cycling is the complaint, and to replace a waterlogged tank before changing the switch or pump.

### Omitting Lightning and Low-Yield Protection on a Submersible Pump

A plumber installs a submersible pump without surge protection or low-yield protection to save cost, and within the first storm season a nearby lightning strike fries the motor, or within the first drought the well draws down and the pump runs dry and burns out. The trap is that the protections add a small cost and the threats are intermittent, so they are easy to omit, but a submersible pump is a costly down-hole component whose failure requires pulling the well. The mechanism is that a voltage surge from a nearby strike destroys the motor insulation, and a dry well lets the pump run without cooling flow until the motor overheats. The false signal is that the pump "has been fine." The harm is a costly pump replacement and well-pulling, often outside warranty. The defense is to install surge protection at the pressure switch and control box on every submersible, and low-yield protection on any marginal well, as standard practice.

## Self-Check

- Did I determine the well's static water level and depth before selecting the pump type, and use a submersible for any static level below 25 feet rather than a jet pump?
- Did I obtain the well's sustainable yield and size the pump's flow to the lesser of the building demand and the yield, with low-yield protection on marginal wells?
- Did I size the pressure tank so its drawdown equals the pump's gpm flow, giving a minimum one-minute pump run per cycle?
- Did I set the pressure switch to the desired range (30/50 or 40/60), confirm the cut-in and cut-out with a gauge, and set the tank pre-charge 2 to 4 psi below the cut-in with the tank drained?
- For a short-cycling complaint, did I diagnose the pressure tank (tap test, drain and check air charge) before replacing the switch or pump?
- Did I install surge protection at the pressure switch and control box on every submersible pump?
- Did I confirm the pump's rated flow at the required head (depth plus lift plus building pressure) is within the well's yield?
- Did I verify the system delivers stable pressure across the cut-in to cut-out range, with no near-zero pressure at the fixtures between cycles?
- Did I document the well yield, the pump model and settings, the tank size and pre-charge, and the switch cut-in and cut-out so the system is verifiable and serviceable?
- If the well yield is marginal, did I recommend a storage tank with a slow-pump fill rather than overpumping the aquifer?
