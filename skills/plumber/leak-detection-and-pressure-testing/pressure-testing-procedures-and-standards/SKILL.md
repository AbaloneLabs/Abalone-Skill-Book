# Pressure Testing Procedures and Standards

A pressure test is the verification that a plumbing system is leak-free before it is concealed, placed in service, or turned over to the owner, and the quality of the test determines whether leaks are caught cheaply (during rough-in) or expensively (after the walls are closed and the building is occupied). The judgment problem is that a pressure test is only as good as its procedure — the test pressure, the duration, the gauge calibration, the boundary isolation — and a test that "passes" with a short hold, an uncalibrated gauge, or an unisolated boundary may miss slow leaks that appear later. A plumber who tests to "good enough" rather than to the code procedure is providing false assurance. This skill covers the pressure testing decisions that produce reliable leak verification for each plumbing system type.

## Core Rules

### Test Each System Type at the Code-Specified Pressure and Duration

Each plumbing system type has a code-specified test pressure and duration, and the test must meet or exceed the code requirement. For water supply piping (PEX, copper, CPVC): typically 1.5 times the working pressure (often 100 psi minimum for residential, higher for commercial) for at least 15 minutes, though longer holds (30 minutes to several hours) are more reliable for detecting slow leaks. For DWV: a water test (filling the system with water to the highest point) for at least 15 minutes, or an air test at 5 psi for DWV (where air testing is permitted). For gas piping: air or nitrogen at 1.5 times the working pressure (commonly 10 psi minimum for residential low-pressure, higher for larger systems) for at least 10 minutes, with longer holds for larger systems. For hydronic heating: 1.5 times the working pressure (commonly 30 to 60 psi) for at least 15 minutes. The trap is testing at a lower pressure or for a shorter duration than the code requires, or testing at "whatever pressure the pump produces." The disciplined rule is to identify the system type, look up the code test requirement, and test at the specified pressure and duration, documenting both.

### Use a Calibrated Gauge and Verify It Before and After the Test

The pressure gauge is the test instrument, and its accuracy determines the test validity. A gauge that reads high (showing 100 psi when the system is at 80 psi) produces a false pass; a gauge that reads low (showing 80 psi when the system is at 100 psi) over-pressurizes the system. Gauges must be calibrated (within their calibration interval, typically annual), and the gauge should be verified against a known reference before and after the test. For gas tests, a calibrated pressure gauge (often a diaphragm or digital gauge rated for the test gas) is required; for water tests, a calibrated Bourdon gauge. The trap is using any gauge on hand, or a gauge that has been dropped or is past its calibration date, and trusting its reading. The disciplined rule is to use a calibrated gauge, to verify the calibration date, and to record the gauge identification and calibration date with the test result.

### Isolate the Test Boundary and Exclude Appliances, Meters, and Fixtures

The test boundary must be carefully defined to include the piping being tested and to exclude components not rated for the test pressure or not part of the work. Water supply tests exclude fixtures (which are tested separately), water heaters (which have their own T&P), and the meter (which is the utility's). Gas tests exclude the meter and the appliances (which are tested separately by leak-checking after connection). Hydronic tests exclude the boiler's relief valve (which would open at 30 psi) unless the boiler is isolated or the relief is removed and plugged for the test. The trap is including a component not rated for the test pressure — testing a water heater at 100 psi (the T&P opens at 150, but the heater is not designed for the test), or testing a gas system with the meter included (the meter is damaged by the test pressure). The disciplined rule is to define the test boundary explicitly, to cap or plug the boundary points, to exclude appliances and meters, and to isolate or remove relief valves that would open below the test pressure.

### Test in Stages: Rough Before Concealment, Final Before Service

Every system should be tested in stages, not only at the end. The rough test (before the walls are closed, the floors are covered, the trenches are backfilled) verifies the concealed piping is leak-free while leaks are cheap to fix. The final test (after fixtures and appliances are connected, before service) verifies the connections are leak-free. The trap is testing only at the end of the job, after concealment, when a leak requires destructive access. The disciplined rule is to rough-test before concealment, to final-test before service, to document both, and to treat any leak found at either stage as a defect to be repaired and re-tested, not a "monitor" item.

### Interpret Pressure Drops Correctly: Temperature, Trapped Air, and True Leaks

A pressure drop during a test may indicate a leak, but it may also indicate a temperature change (water and air expand and contract with temperature, changing the pressure) or trapped air (air dissolves into water over time, reducing the gas volume and the pressure). A small pressure drop over a long hold may be a temperature or air effect, not a leak; a rapid or continuous drop is a leak. The diagnostic approach is to hold the test long enough to distinguish a temperature transient (which stabilizes) from a continuous leak (which continues), and to inspect every joint visually for moisture. For gas tests (air or nitrogen), trapped air is not a factor (the test medium is gas), and any pressure drop is a leak. The trap is declaring a small drop "just temperature" without verifying, or panicking over a small drop that is actually temperature. The disciplined rule is to interpret pressure drops carefully, to hold long enough to distinguish transients from leaks, to inspect visually, and to re-test after any repair.

## Common Traps

### Testing at a Lower Pressure or Shorter Duration Than the Code Requires

A plumber tests a water supply system at 60 psi for 5 minutes because the test pump is slow and time is short, when the code requires 100 psi for 15 minutes, and a slow leak that would appear at 15 minutes is missed. The trap is that the test "passes" at the lower standard but does not meet the code requirement. The mechanism is that test pressure and duration determine leak detection sensitivity. The false signal is that "the system held pressure." The harm is a slow leak that appears after concealment. The defense is to identify the code requirement and test at the specified pressure and duration, documenting both.

### Using an Uncalibrated Gauge

A plumber uses a gauge that has been dropped, is past its calibration date, or is of unknown provenance, and trusts the reading. The trap is that the gauge may read high or low, producing a false pass or an over-pressurization. The mechanism is that the gauge is the test instrument, and its accuracy determines the test validity. The false signal is the gauge reading. The harm is a false pass or system damage. The defense is to use a calibrated gauge, to verify the calibration date, and to record the gauge identification with the test result.

### Including Appliances or Meters in the Test Boundary

A plumber pressure-tests a gas system with the meter included, or a water system with the water heater included, damaging the meter or tripping the heater's T&P. The trap is that the test boundary was not defined to exclude components not rated for the test. The mechanism is that appliances and meters have lower pressure ratings than the test pressure. The false signal is that "the system tested." The harm is damage to the meter or appliance. The defense is to define the test boundary explicitly, to cap or plug the boundary points, and to exclude appliances and meters from the test.

### Testing Only at the End of the Job

A plumber completes the entire system, conceals it, and tests only at the end; a leak is found in a concealed wall, requiring destructive access. The trap is that end-of-job testing is technically compliant but defeats the purpose, which is to find leaks while they are cheap to fix. The mechanism is that concealment hides leaks. The false signal is that "I tested it." The harm is expensive rework. The defense is to test in stages — rough before concealment, final before service — and to document both.

### Dismissing a Pressure Drop as Temperature Without Verifying

A plumber observes a small pressure drop over the test hold, dismisses it as temperature change, and declares the test passed, when in fact the drop is a slow leak. The trap is that temperature changes do cause small drops, and the dismissal feels reasonable, but it may mask a real leak. The mechanism is that temperature transients and slow leaks both produce small drops. The false signal is that "it's just temperature." The harm is a concealed slow leak. The defense is to hold the test long enough to distinguish a transient (which stabilizes) from a continuous leak (which continues), to inspect visually, and to re-test if there is any doubt.

## Self-Check

- Did I identify the system type (water supply, DWV, gas, hydronic) and test at the code-specified pressure and duration, documenting both?
- Did I use a calibrated pressure gauge, verify the calibration date, and record the gauge identification with the test result?
- Did I define the test boundary explicitly, cap or plug the boundary points, and exclude appliances, meters, and relief valves not rated for the test pressure?
- Did I test in stages — rough test before concealment and final test before service — and document both?
- Did I hold the test long enough to distinguish a temperature transient (which stabilizes) from a continuous leak (which continues)?
- Did I inspect every joint visually for moisture during and after the test?
- For DWV testing, did I use a water test (filling to the highest point) or an air test (where permitted) at the code pressure and duration?
- For gas testing, did I use air or nitrogen (never gas itself) at the code pressure and duration, with the meter and appliances excluded?
- For hydronic testing, did I isolate or remove the relief valve (which opens below typical test pressure) or test below the relief setpoint?
- Did I document the test medium, pressure, duration, gauge identification, boundary definition, and result (pass/fail) for each system, so the test is verifiable and the record is complete?
