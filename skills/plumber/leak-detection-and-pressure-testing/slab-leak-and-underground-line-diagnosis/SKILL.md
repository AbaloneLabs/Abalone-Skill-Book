# Slab Leak and Underground Line Diagnosis

Under-slab and underground leaks are among the highest-stakes diagnostics in plumbing: the leak is invisible, the access is destructive and expensive, and the wrong repair strategy (reroute versus tunnel versus jackhammer) can cost the owner tens of thousands of dollars and disrupt occupancy for weeks. The judgment problem is that a slab leak must be confirmed, isolated to a specific section, and located precisely before any repair strategy is chosen, and the repair strategy itself depends on the leak type (a single pinhole versus a corroding system), the floor finish (carpet versus tile), the building configuration (single-story versus multi-story above), and the long-term prognosis of the remaining piping. A plumber who jackhammers a slab without confirming and isolating the leak, or who recommends a strategy without considering alternatives, is making an expensive and disruptive decision without adequate diagnosis. This skill covers the diagnostic and repair-strategy decisions for under-slab and underground leaks.

## Core Rules

### Confirm the Slab Leak With Hydrostatic Testing Before Any Access

Before any destructive access, confirm that an under-slab leak exists using hydrostatic testing: isolate the under-slab piping (typically by capping or plugging at the risers), fill the isolated section with water to a known pressure or to a standing column, and observe whether the pressure holds or the water level drops over a timed hold (commonly 15 to 30 minutes). A pressure drop or a level drop confirms a leak in the isolated section. The trap is assuming a slab leak based on symptoms (a damp floor, a warm spot, a high water bill) without hydrostatic confirmation, and jackhammering a slab that has no leak (the symptom is condensation, an exterior intrusion, or a fixture leak). The disciplined rule is to hydrostatically test the isolated under-slab section before any access, and to document the test result.

### Isolate the Leak to a Specific Section Before Locating

Once a leak is confirmed in the under-slab system, isolate it to a specific section (a specific bathroom group, a specific branch, a specific radiant loop) by testing sections individually — capping and testing each branch or loop separately, and identifying which section drops. This narrows the search area before the precise locating (acoustic, tracer gas, or thermal) and avoids opening the slab in the wrong room. The trap is skipping the section isolation and going straight to locating or access over the entire under-slab area, which is slow and may miss the leak. The disciplined rule is to isolate the leak to a section by individual testing, then pinpoint within that section.

### Pinpoint the Leak Location Before Choosing a Repair Strategy

Within the isolated section, pinpoint the leak location using acoustic listening (for pressurized leaks), tracer gas (for difficult or deep leaks), or thermal imaging (for hot-water or radiant leaks), to within a few inches. The precise location determines the access strategy: a precisely located single leak may be repairable through a small targeted opening, while a poorly located or systemic leak may warrant a reroute. The trap is choosing a repair strategy (jackhammer, tunnel, reroute) before pinpointing the leak, leading to an access in the wrong location or an oversized strategy for a single leak. The disciplined rule is to pinpoint the leak precisely, then choose the repair strategy based on the location, the floor finish, and the system prognosis.

### Evaluate Reroute Versus Tunnel Versus Jackhammer Based on the Full Situation

The three repair strategies for an under-slab leak are: jackhammer through the slab at the leak location (direct, but damages the floor finish and the slab, and requires finish repair); tunnel under the slab from an exterior or crawlspace access (avoids interior finish damage, but is labor-intensive and limited by depth and soil); and reroute the leaking section overhead or through the attic/walls (abandons the under-slab section, avoids slab access entirely, but may be long and may affect other finishes). The choice depends on the leak type (a single pinhole in an otherwise sound system favors jackhammer or tunnel; a corroding system with multiple expected leaks favors reroute), the floor finish (carpet is easy to lift; tile is expensive to replace), the building configuration (a second story above limits tunneling and reroute), and the owner's tolerance for disruption. The trap is defaulting to jackhammer without considering tunnel or reroute, or recommending reroute for a single leak in a sound system. The disciplined rule is to evaluate all three strategies against the full situation and to present the tradeoffs to the owner.

### Assess the System Prognosis Before Recommending a Spot Repair

A critical decision is whether the leak is an isolated event (a single pinhole in an otherwise sound copper system, a single fitting failure) or a symptom of a deteriorating system (a copper system with widespread pinhole corrosion from aggressive water, a galvanized system scaling and restricting throughout, a radiant loop with multiple failing tubes). For an isolated leak in a sound system, a spot repair (jackhammer or tunnel) is appropriate. For a deteriorating system, a spot repair fixes one leak and the next appears shortly, so a system reroute or re-pipe is the cost-effective long-term strategy. The trap is recommending a spot repair for a deteriorating system, leading to recurring leaks and repeated disruption. The disciplined rule is to assess the system prognosis (inspect exposed piping for corrosion, consider the water chemistry and the pipe age and material) and to recommend a spot repair only for isolated leaks in sound systems, with a reroute or re-pipe for deteriorating systems.

## Common Traps

### Jackhammering a Slab Without Hydrostatic Confirmation

A plumber, called for a damp floor or warm spot, jackhammers the slab based on the symptom, without hydrostatically confirming a leak, and finds no leak (the cause is condensation, an exterior intrusion, or a fixture leak). The trap is that the symptom pointed to a slab leak but the cause is elsewhere. The mechanism is that damp and warm spots have multiple causes. The false signal is the floor symptom. The harm is destructive access for no benefit. The defense is to hydrostatically test the isolated under-slab section before any access.

### Skipping Section Isolation and Searching the Entire Slab

A plumber confirms a slab leak but does not isolate it to a section, and searches the entire under-slab area with acoustic or tracer gas, which is slow, noisy, and may miss the leak in a large search area. The trap is that the search area is too large without section isolation. The mechanism is that section isolation narrows the search. The false signal is that "the leak is somewhere under the slab." The harm is wasted time and a missed or imprecise location. The defense is to isolate the leak to a section by individual testing before pinpointing.

### Choosing a Repair Strategy Before Pinpointing the Leak

A plumber recommends a jackhammer or a reroute before pinpointing the leak location, and the access is in the wrong place or the strategy is oversized for a single leak. The trap is that the strategy was chosen without the precise location. The mechanism is that the location determines the optimal strategy. The false signal is that "the leak is confirmed." The harm is an access in the wrong location or an oversized repair. The defense is to pinpoint the leak precisely before choosing the repair strategy.

### Defaulting to Jackhammer Without Considering Tunnel or Reroute

A plumber defaults to jackhammering the slab for every under-slab leak, without considering tunneling (which avoids interior finish damage) or rerouting (which avoids slab access entirely), and the owner pays for finish repair or experiences disruption that an alternative strategy would have avoided. The trap is that jackhammer is the default but not always the best strategy. The mechanism is that each strategy has tradeoffs by situation. The false signal is that "jackhammer is how you fix a slab leak." The harm is unnecessary finish damage or disruption. The defense is to evaluate all three strategies (jackhammer, tunnel, reroute) against the full situation and present the tradeoffs.

### Recommending a Spot Repair for a Deteriorating System

A plumber recommends a spot repair (jackhammer and fix one pinhole) for a copper system with widespread pinhole corrosion from aggressive water, and the next pinhole appears within months, requiring repeated disruption. The trap is that the spot repair fixes one symptom of a deteriorating system. The mechanism is that aggressive water corrodes the entire system. The false signal is that "the leak is fixed." The harm is recurring leaks and repeated disruption. The defense is to assess the system prognosis (inspect exposed piping, consider water chemistry and age) and recommend a reroute or re-pipe for deteriorating systems.

## Self-Check

- Did I hydrostatically test the isolated under-slab section (capping at the risers, filling, observing for pressure or level drop over a timed hold) before any destructive access?
- Did I isolate the leak to a specific section (branch, bathroom group, radiant loop) by testing sections individually before pinpointing?
- Did I pinpoint the leak location precisely (acoustic, tracer gas, or thermal) to within a few inches before choosing a repair strategy?
- Did I evaluate all three repair strategies (jackhammer, tunnel, reroute) against the leak type, floor finish, building configuration, and owner tolerance, and present the tradeoffs?
- Did I assess the system prognosis (inspect exposed piping for corrosion, consider water chemistry, pipe age, and material) before recommending a spot repair?
- For a deteriorating system (widespread corrosion, multiple expected leaks), did I recommend a reroute or re-pipe rather than a spot repair?
- For a single isolated leak in a sound system, did I choose the least-disruptive access (targeted jackhammer, tunnel, or reroute) based on the situation?
- After the repair, did I hydrostatically re-test the repaired section to confirm the repair is leak-free before closing the access?
- Did I restore the slab, vapor barrier, and floor finish per the original construction, addressing any structural or waterproofing concerns?
- Did I document the hydrostatic test, the section isolation, the pinpointed location, the repair strategy and rationale, and the post-repair test, so the work is verifiable and the prognosis is recorded?
