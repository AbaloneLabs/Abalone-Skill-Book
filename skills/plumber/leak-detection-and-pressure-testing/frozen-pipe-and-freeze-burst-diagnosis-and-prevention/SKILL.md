# Frozen Pipe and Freeze-Burst Diagnosis and Prevention

Freezing is the mechanism by which a plumbing system destroys itself in cold weather: water expands as it freezes, and the expanding ice exerts enormous pressure on the pipe wall, splitting copper, cracking PEX fittings, and shattering PVC and CPVC, so that when the ice thaws the released water floods the building. The judgment problem is that freeze damage is invisible until the thaw, the burst is often far from the frozen section (ice dams the pipe and the pressure splits the weakest point), and the prevention is a combination of insulation, heat, routing, and drainage that must be designed for the coldest expected condition, not the average winter. A plumber who thaws a frozen pipe without finding the burst, or who insulates a pipe without addressing the heat source that keeps it above freezing, will return to the same failure. This skill covers the risk identification, safe thawing, burst location, and prevention decisions that freeze damage demands.

## Core Rules

### Identify Freeze-Prone Pipes by Location, Not by Material Alone

A pipe freezes when the surrounding air temperature drops below freezing for long enough to cool the water in the pipe to freezing, and the risk is determined by the pipe's location relative to cold air, not by the pipe material (though material affects the burst consequence). The high-risk locations are: pipes in exterior walls (particularly north-facing walls and walls with poor insulation), pipes in unheated crawlspaces, attics, and garages, pipes in vacant or seasonally unheated buildings, pipes in exterior hose bibbs and sillcocks not drained for winter, pipes in floor and ceiling cavities adjacent to vented soffits or cold outside air, and pipes in slab-on-grade perimeters where the slab edge is exposed. The trap is assuming "PEX doesn't burst" (PEX expands and resists splitting better than copper, but the fittings can still fail and the connected copper or fittings can burst) or "this wall faces south so it's fine" (a south wall with poor insulation and a cold-air leak still freezes). The disciplined rule is to survey every pipe run by location, identify those exposed to air that can drop below freezing, and prioritize those for insulation, heat, rerouting, or drainage.

### Thaw Frozen Pipes Safely, Never With an Open Flame, and Watch for the Hidden Burst

Thawing a frozen pipe must be done with a safe heat source (warm towels, a hair dryer, a heat gun on low, electrical heat tape applied per the manufacturer, or a commercial pipe thawing machine), starting from the open faucet end and working toward the frozen section, never with an open flame (torch, propane heater) which can ignite the building and overheat the pipe. The critical safety practice is to open the faucet downstream of the freeze before thawing, so that as the ice melts the water has a path out and does not pressurize, and to watch for the burst that is often hidden: the ice dams the pipe, the pressure splits the pipe at the weakest point (often a fitting or a thin spot), and the burst may be anywhere along the frozen run, not at the visible ice. The trap is thawing a frozen pipe, seeing water flow, and leaving without inspecting the full run for a burst that will leak when the system re-pressurizes. The disciplined rule is to thaw from the open end with a safe heat source, to inspect the full frozen run for splits and cracks before re-pressurizing, and to pressure-test the repaired section before concealment.

### Locate the Burst by Systematic Inspection, Not by Where the Ice Was

The burst from a freeze is often not at the location where the pipe froze; the ice forms a dam, the pressure between the dam and the closed faucet (or the closed main) builds, and the pipe splits at its weakest point, which may be a fitting, a thin section, or a section with a defect, anywhere along the pressurized run. The systematic method is to isolate the frozen section (close the nearest shutoffs), inspect the full run from the frozen section to the faucet (and to the main), looking for splits (in copper, a longitudinal crack), cracks (in plastic, a brittle fracture), and fitting failures (a cracked solder joint, a split PEX fitting), and to pressure-test the section to confirm there are no hidden bursts before restoring service. The trap is repairing the visible split at the frozen section and missing a second burst further along the run, which leaks when the system re-pressurizes. The disciplined rule is to inspect the full pressurized run systematically and to pressure-test before restoring service.

### Design Freeze Protection as a System: Routing, Insulation, Heat Source, and Drainage

Freeze prevention is a system, not a single measure, and the components work together. Routing: locate pipes in heated spaces where possible, and avoid exterior walls, crawlspaces, and attics; where routing through cold spaces is unavoidable, route along the warm side of the insulation. Insulation: insulate pipes in cold spaces with pipe insulation sized to the pipe, extending the full run with no gaps, and in extreme cold, increase the insulation thickness. Heat source: for pipes that cannot be kept above freezing by routing and insulation alone, provide heat (electrical heat tape listed for the pipe material, installed per the manufacturer with a thermostat; or a controlled heat source in the space). Drainage: for seasonal and exterior piping (hose bibbs, irrigation, seasonal cabins), slope the piping to drain and install freeze-proof hose bibbs that drain back when shut off. The trap is insulating a pipe without addressing the heat source (insulation slows heat loss but does not add heat, so a pipe with no heat source eventually freezes however well insulated). The disciplined rule is to design freeze protection as a system: route in heated spaces, insulate where cold exposure is unavoidable, add heat where insulation is insufficient, and drain seasonal piping.

### Protect Seasonal and Vacant Buildings by Draining or Heating, Not by "Leaving a Drip"

Seasonal cabins, vacant buildings, and buildings subject to winter power loss must be either fully drained (all water removed from the piping, the water heater, and the traps, with antifreeze in the traps and the toilet bowls) or heated (the building maintained above freezing with a monitored heat source and a low-temperature alarm). The common advice to "leave a faucet dripping" is a partial measure that helps in a marginal freeze (the moving water resists freezing and the open faucet relieves pressure) but is unreliable in a hard freeze or a long power outage, and it wastes water and relies on the drain not freezing. The trap is relying on a dripping faucet to protect a vacant cabin through a cold week, which fails when the power goes out or the cold is sustained. The disciplined rule is to drain seasonal and vacant buildings fully (with antifreeze in traps and bowls) or to heat them with a monitored source and a low-temperature alarm, and to treat the dripping faucet as a marginal measure only, not a freeze-protection strategy.

## Common Traps

### Thawing a Frozen Pipe With an Open Flame

A plumber or homeowner thaws a frozen pipe with a propane torch, reasoning that "the flame is fast." The trap is that the open flame can ignite adjacent combustibles (framing, insulation, the pipe's combustible surroundings) and can overheat the pipe (a copper pipe can be melted or weakened, a plastic pipe can be ignited or melted), and the method has caused numerous structure fires. The mechanism is that an open flame applies uncontrolled high heat to the pipe and its surroundings. The false signal is that "the ice melts fast." The harm is a structure fire or pipe damage. The defense is to thaw only with a safe heat source (warm towels, hair dryer, heat gun on low, listed heat tape, or a commercial thawing machine), never an open flame.

### Thawing a Pipe Without Opening the Downstream Faucet

A plumber thaws a frozen pipe without opening the faucet downstream of the freeze, and as the ice melts the water has no path out, the pressure between the ice dam and the closed faucet builds, and the pipe splits at a weak point, or the thawed water sprays when the ice finally releases. The trap is that the closed faucet turns the thaw into a pressurization event. The mechanism is that the ice forms a dam, and the thawing water needs a relief path. The false signal is that "the pipe is thawing." The harm is a pressure-induced burst or a spray when the ice releases. The defense is to always open the downstream faucet before thawing, so the thawing water has a path out.

### Repairing the Visible Split and Missing a Second Burst

A plumber finds and repairs a visible split at the frozen section, restores water, and leaves, but a second burst further along the run (at a fitting or a thin spot) leaks when the system re-pressurizes, causing a concealed flood. The trap is that the visible split was the obvious damage, but the pressure that caused it may have split the pipe elsewhere along the run. The mechanism is that ice dams the pipe and the pressure splits the weakest points, which may be multiple. The false signal is that "the visible split is repaired." The harm is a concealed leak from the missed burst. The defense is to inspect the full pressurized run systematically for splits, cracks, and fitting failures, and to pressure-test the section before restoring service and concealment.

### Insulating a Pipe Without a Heat Source

A plumber insulates a pipe in a cold crawlspace with thick pipe insulation, reasoning that "insulation prevents freezing." The trap is that insulation slows heat loss but does not add heat, so a pipe with no heat source (no warm water flow, no warm air, no heat tape) eventually cools to the crawlspace temperature and freezes however well insulated. The mechanism is that insulation is a thermal resistance, not a heat source. The false signal is that "the pipe is insulated." The harm is a freeze despite the insulation. The defense is to design freeze protection as a system: route in heated spaces, insulate where cold exposure is unavoidable, and add heat (listed heat tape or a warm-air source) where insulation alone is insufficient.

### Relying on a Dripping Faucet to Protect a Vacant Building

A homeowner leaves a faucet dripping in a vacant cabin for the winter, reasoning that "moving water doesn't freeze." The trap is that the dripping faucet is a marginal measure that helps in a light freeze but fails in a hard freeze or a long power outage (the heating stops, the building cools, the drip freezes, and the pipe bursts), and it wastes water and relies on the drain not freezing. The mechanism is that a drip provides minimal freeze protection and no protection against a sustained cold or a power loss. The false signal is that "the water is moving." The harm is a freeze and burst in a vacant building, discovered (or not) when the owner returns. The defense is to drain vacant and seasonal buildings fully (with antifreeze in traps and bowls) or to heat them with a monitored source and a low-temperature alarm, treating the dripping faucet as a marginal measure only.

## Self-Check

- Did I survey every pipe run by location and identify the freeze-prone pipes (exterior walls, crawlspaces, attics, garages, vacant buildings, exterior hose bibbs, slab perimeters), rather than assuming a material or a wall orientation is safe?
- When thawing a frozen pipe, did I use only a safe heat source (warm towels, hair dryer, heat gun on low, listed heat tape, or a commercial thawing machine), never an open flame?
- Did I open the downstream faucet before thawing, so the thawing water has a path out and does not pressurize the pipe?
- After thawing, did I inspect the full pressurized run systematically for splits, cracks, and fitting failures (not just the visible ice location), and did I pressure-test the section before restoring service?
- For freeze prevention, did I design protection as a system: route pipes in heated spaces where possible, insulate where cold exposure is unavoidable (full run, no gaps, adequate thickness), add heat (listed heat tape with thermostat) where insulation is insufficient, and drain seasonal and exterior piping?
- Did I install freeze-proof hose bibbs that drain back when shut off, and did I slope and drain seasonal irrigation and supply piping?
- For seasonal and vacant buildings, did I fully drain the piping, water heater, and traps (with antifreeze in traps and toilet bowls), or heat the building with a monitored source and a low-temperature alarm, rather than relying on a dripping faucet?
- Did I verify that insulation is on the cold side of the pipe (between the pipe and the cold air), with no gaps, and that heat tape is listed for the pipe material and installed per the manufacturer with a thermostat?
- Did I address the cold-air infiltration (seal gaps, insulate walls) that caused the freeze, not just the pipe, so the condition does not recur?
- Did I document the freeze-prone locations, the prevention measures (routing, insulation, heat, drainage), the thawing and repair, and the pressure test, so the freeze protection is verifiable and the system is protected against the next cold event?
