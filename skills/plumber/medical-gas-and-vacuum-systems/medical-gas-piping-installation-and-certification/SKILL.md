# Medical Gas Piping Installation and Certification

Medical gas systems deliver life-critical gases (oxygen, medical air, nitrous oxide, nitrogen, carbon dioxide, and medical vacuum) to patient care outlets, and a contamination, leak, or misconnection in the piping can injure or kill a patient — delivering the wrong gas to an outlet labeled for another gas, or delivering a gas contaminated with particulate, oil, or moisture. The judgment problem is that medical gas piping is the most strictly regulated plumbing work, governed by NFPA 99 (Health Care Facilities Code), requiring dedicated clean materials, specific brazing procedures, high-purity purging, rigorous pressure testing, and third-party verification and certification by a credentialed inspector — and a plumber who treats medical gas piping like ordinary copper water piping (using standard fittings, brazing without purge, testing to water-pipe pressures) creates a system that will fail verification and may harm patients. This skill covers the installation and certification decisions that produce a verifiable, safe medical gas system.

## Core Rules

### Use Only Medical Gas-Certified Materials and Maintain Cleanliness

Medical gas piping uses Type L or Type K copper tube (ASTM B88) and wrought copper fittings, but the materials must be specifically cleaned and capped for medical gas service (labeled "medical gas" or "MG"), sealed against contamination during transport and storage, and kept capped until brazed. Standard plumbing copper and fittings are not acceptable because they may carry manufacturing oils, flux residues, or particulate that would contaminate the gas stream. The trap is using standard copper fittings or reusing fittings, which introduces contaminants that fail verification and harm patients. The disciplined rule is to use only medical gas-certified, cleaned, and capped materials, to keep all open ends capped until the moment of brazing, and to handle materials with clean gloves.

### Braze With an Oxygen-Free Nitrogen (OFN) Purge and Silver Brazing Alloy

Medical gas joints are brazed (not soldered) with a silver brazing alloy (BCuP or BAg series, with a flux appropriate for the alloy where required, though many medical gas brazes use phosphorus-bearing BCuP alloys that are self-fluxing on copper-to-copper joints), and a continuous purge of oxygen-free nitrogen (OFN) through the pipe during brazing prevents internal oxidation and scale formation that would contaminate the gas stream. The purge must be flowing before brazing begins, maintained throughout, and continued until the joint cools. The trap is brazing without the nitrogen purge (forming internal oxide scale that flakes into the gas stream), or using standard soldering flux (which is corrosive and contaminating). The disciplined rule is to use a silver brazing alloy appropriate for medical gas, to maintain a continuous OFN purge during brazing and cooling, and to avoid flux on copper-to-copper joints where a self-fluxing alloy is used.

### Pressure-Test to NFPA 99 Requirements With an Extended Hold

Medical gas systems are pressure-tested to NFPA 99 requirements, which are far more rigorous than ordinary plumbing: the test uses medical-grade nitrogen (not compressed air, which introduces moisture and contaminants), the test pressure is specific to the gas system (e.g., oxygen at 150 psi, or higher per the code edition), the test must hold for a minimum duration (typically 24 hours, with no pressure drop beyond a code-allowed tolerance for temperature), and the test gauge must be calibrated and of appropriate range. The trap is testing to water-pipe pressures (too low), testing with compressed air (contaminating), or testing for a short duration (insufficient to detect slow leaks). The disciplined rule is to test to the NFPA 99 pressure for the specific gas system, with medical-grade nitrogen, for the required duration (typically 24 hours), with a calibrated gauge, and to document the initial and final pressures and temperatures.

### Purge and Cross-Test Before Activation, Then Label Outlets

After pressure testing, the system is purged with the specific medical gas (or medical-grade nitrogen) to remove the test gas and any residual moisture, and each outlet is cross-tested to confirm that the gas delivered at each outlet matches the gas labeled at that outlet — a critical verification that prevents the catastrophic harm of delivering the wrong gas to a patient outlet. Each outlet is then labeled with the gas name, the pressure, and a unique identifier, color-coded per NFPA 99 (e.g., green for oxygen, yellow for medical air), and the system is documented with as-built drawings and outlet schedules. The trap is omitting the cross-test (which is the verification that the right gas reaches the right outlet) or mislabeling an outlet. The disciplined rule is to purge the system, cross-test every outlet to confirm gas identity, label every outlet per NFPA 99 color and text, and document the as-built system.

### Arrange Third-Party Verification by a Credentialed Inspector

Medical gas systems require third-party verification and certification by an ASSE 6030 (or equivalent) Medical Gas Inspector, who is independent of the installing contractor, before the system is placed in service. The inspector verifies the materials, the brazing purge documentation, the pressure test results, the purge and cross-test, the outlet labeling, the alarm functions, and the source equipment, and issues a certification document. The installing plumber must coordinate the inspection, provide all documentation (material certifications, brazing procedures, purge records, pressure test records, cross-test records, as-built drawings), and correct any deficiencies before certification. The trap is attempting to place the system in service without third-party verification, or performing the verification in-house (a conflict of interest). The disciplined rule is to arrange third-party verification by a credentialed inspector, to provide complete documentation, and to obtain certification before activation.

## Common Traps

### Using Standard Plumbing Copper and Fittings for Medical Gas

A plumber uses standard plumbing copper fittings or reuses fittings for a medical gas system, introducing manufacturing oils and particulate that fail verification and contaminate the gas stream. The trap is that the materials "look the same" but are not cleaned for medical gas. The mechanism is that standard fittings carry contaminants. The false signal is that "it is copper, it will work." The harm is verification failure and patient harm from contamination. The defense is to use only medical gas-certified, cleaned, and capped materials, kept capped until brazing.

### Brazing Without an Oxygen-Free Nitrogen Purge

A plumber brazes medical gas joints without the nitrogen purge, and internal oxide scale forms, flakes into the gas stream, and contaminates the system, failing verification. The trap is that the joint looks sound externally but is scaled internally. The mechanism is that oxygen in the hot tube forms oxide scale. The false signal is that "the joint is brazed." The harm is internal contamination and verification failure. The defense is to maintain a continuous OFN purge during brazing and cooling, and to avoid flux on copper-to-copper joints.

### Pressure-Testing With Compressed Air or to Water-Pipe Pressures

A plumber pressure-tests a medical gas system with compressed air (which introduces moisture and contaminants) or to water-pipe pressures (which are too low to meet NFPA 99), and the test does not meet the code. The trap is that the test "passes" but does not meet the medical gas standard. The mechanism is that the test medium and pressure are code-specified. The false signal is that "the system holds pressure." The harm is a non-compliant system that fails verification. The defense is to test with medical-grade nitrogen to the NFPA 99 pressure for the specific gas system, for the required duration, with a calibrated gauge.

### Omitting the Outlet Cross-Test or Mislabeling an Outlet

A plumber omits the outlet cross-test (the verification that the right gas reaches the right outlet) or mislabels an outlet, and a patient receives the wrong gas at an outlet labeled for another gas, a catastrophic harm. The trap is that the cross-test seems redundant but is the critical verification. The mechanism is that a piping error or a manifold error could deliver the wrong gas. The false signal is that "the piping is pressure-tested." The harm is delivering the wrong gas to a patient. The defense is to cross-test every outlet to confirm gas identity, to label every outlet per NFPA 99, and to document the cross-test.

### Placing the System in Service Without Third-Party Verification

A plumber places a medical gas system in service without third-party verification by a credentialed inspector, or performs the verification in-house, violating NFPA 99 and exposing patients to an uncertified system. The trap is that the system "works" but is not certified. The mechanism is that independent verification is a code requirement. The false signal is that "the installer tested it." The harm is an uncertified, non-compliant system and patient risk. The defense is to arrange third-party verification by an ASSE 6030 inspector, provide complete documentation, and obtain certification before activation.

## Self-Check

- Did I use only medical gas-certified, cleaned, and capped Type L or K copper tube and wrought fittings, kept capped until brazing?
- Did I braze with a silver brazing alloy appropriate for medical gas, and maintain a continuous oxygen-free nitrogen purge during brazing and cooling, avoiding flux on copper-to-copper joints?
- Did I pressure-test each gas system to the NFPA 99 pressure with medical-grade nitrogen, for the required duration (typically 24 hours), with a calibrated gauge, and document the initial and final pressures and temperatures?
- Did I purge the system with the specific medical gas or medical-grade nitrogen after pressure testing, and cross-test every outlet to confirm gas identity?
- Did I label every outlet per NFPA 99 (gas name, pressure, unique identifier, color code) and document the as-built drawings and outlet schedule?
- Did I arrange third-party verification by an ASSE 6030 (or equivalent) credentialed inspector, independent of the installing contractor?
- Did I provide complete documentation (material certifications, brazing procedures, purge records, pressure test records, cross-test records, as-built drawings) to the inspector?
- Did I correct all deficiencies identified by the inspector before certification?
- Did I confirm the alarm functions (master alarm, area alarms) and the source equipment (manifolds, medical air compressor, vacuum pump) as part of the verification?
- Did I obtain the certification document from the inspector before placing the system in service, and is the certification on file for the facility?
