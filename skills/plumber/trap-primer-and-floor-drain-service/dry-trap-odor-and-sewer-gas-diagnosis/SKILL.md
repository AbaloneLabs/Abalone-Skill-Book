# Dry Trap Odor and Sewer Gas Diagnosis

A sewer gas or odor complaint in a building is most often traced to an evaporated trap seal on an infrequently-used fixture, and it is one of the most misdiagnosed plumbing problems because the odor is diffuse, the source is invisible, and the temptation is to chase venting, drain cleaning, or chemical additives before confirming the simplest cause: a dry trap. The judgment problem is that a dry trap is an open pipe to the sewer, allowing hydrogen sulfide, methane, sewer-organism aerosols, and in stagnant drains potential Legionella and other opportunistic pathogens to enter the occupied space, and the diagnosis requires distinguishing trap-seal evaporation from venting failures (which siphon the seal), drain fouling (which is an odor source itself), and non-plumbing odors, then refilling the seal and specifying a primer or vent repair that prevents recurrence. Seasonal and HVAC effects accelerate evaporation, and a trap that holds in a humid spring can run dry in a dry, air-conditioned summer. This skill covers the diagnostic path, the evaporation and siphonage mechanisms, and the long-term solutions that protect occupants from sewer gas exposure.

## Core Rules

### Confirm a Dry Trap Before Chasing Venting or Drain Odors

The first diagnostic step in a sewer gas or odor complaint is to identify and refill every infrequently-used trap in the affected area, because a dry trap is the most common and most easily fixed cause, and confirming or eliminating it before investigating venting, drain fouling, or non-plumbing sources saves time and avoids unnecessary work. Pour a quart of water into each suspect floor drain, unused fixture, and rarely-used sink, then assess whether the odor resolves over the next hours to days; if it does, the cause was a dry trap and the fix is a primer or regular use. If the odor persists with full traps, escalate to venting, drain fouling, and non-plumbing causes. The trap is skipping the trap-check and going straight to drain cleaning or vent work, which is expensive and does not address a dry trap. The disciplined rule is to refill every suspect trap first, observe, and escalate only if the odor persists.

### Distinguish Evaporation From Siphonage and Back-Pressure as the Depletion Mechanism

Trap seals deplete by three mechanisms, and the mechanism determines the fix. Evaporation removes water gradually and affects infrequently-used traps; the fix is a primer or regular use. Siphonage removes the seal in a single event when a fixture discharge downstream creates negative pressure that pulls the seal out, and the cause is a missing, undersized, or obstructed vent; the fix is vent repair. Back-pressure (positive pressure) pushes the seal out the fixture side, also a venting problem. The diagnostic signs differ: an evaporated trap is dry and gradual; a siphoned trap may gurgle or show a low seal after a nearby discharge; a back-pressured trap may bubble. The trap is assuming every dry trap is evaporation and installing a primer when the real cause is a venting defect that will siphon the primer's water too. The disciplined rule is to identify the depletion mechanism (evaporation, siphonage, back-pressure) by history and testing, and to apply the matching fix.

### Account for Seasonal and HVAC-Driven Evaporation Rates

Evaporation rate depends on temperature, humidity, and airflow over the trap, and these vary seasonally and with HVAC operation: a warm, dry, mechanically-ventilated mechanical room can evaporate a 2-inch trap seal in two to four weeks, while a cool, humid basement may take months. Air-conditioning dries building air and accelerates evaporation, so a trap that holds in spring may run dry in summer; heating season can also dry air. The trap is diagnosing a dry trap in summer and assuming the primer is broken, when the primer's rate is simply too low for the summer evaporation. The disciplined rule is to estimate the evaporation rate for the space (temperature, humidity, airflow), confirm the primer rate exceeds it with margin, and adjust the primer schedule or rate seasonally if needed.

### Investigate Anti-Siphon and Venting Causes When the Seal Repeatedly Empties

If a trap is refilled and empties again quickly despite adequate priming, the cause is likely siphonage or back-pressure from a venting defect, not evaporation. Investigate the vent: is the trap vented at all, is the vent sized per code, is it obstructed (bird nest, ice, debris), is it too far from the trap (long wet vent), or is the vent shared in a way that allows one fixture to siphon another's trap? A drain cleaning or camera inspection can confirm an obstruction. The fix is to add, resize, clear, or reroute the vent so the trap seal is protected. The trap is repeatedly refilling a siphoned trap and blaming the primer, when the venting defect will continue to empty the seal. The disciplined rule is to investigate venting when a refilled trap empties quickly, and to repair the vent rather than increase priming.

### Refill, Sanitize, and Specify a Long-Term Solution Including Stagnant-Drain Pathogen Risk

When a dry trap is confirmed, the immediate fix is to refill the seal, but a long-dry drain may harbor biofilm, organic buildup, and opportunistic pathogens including Legionella in the stagnant water and biofilm, so the drain should be flushed and, where risk warrants, sanitized (per the facility's water-management program, especially in healthcare, hospitality, and large buildings). The long-term solution depends on the mechanism: a primer for evaporation, a vent repair for siphonage, and regular fixture use or removal of the unused fixture. In healthcare and hospitality, document the dry trap and the remediation in the water-management plan. The trap is refilling the seal and walking away, leaving the biofilm and the recurrence risk. The disciplined rule is to refill, flush and sanitize where risk warrants, specify the matching long-term solution, and document the remediation.

## Common Traps

### Chasing Venting or Drain Cleaning Before Checking the Traps

A plumber responds to an odor complaint by cleaning the drains or inspecting the vent before refilling the suspect traps. The trap is that the simplest cause (a dry trap) is overlooked. The mechanism is that a dry trap is an open sewer path. The false signal is that "the drains were recently cleaned." The harm is wasted work and continued odor. The defense is to refill every suspect trap first and observe before escalating.

### Assuming Every Dry Trap Is Evaporation

A plumber installs a primer on a trap that repeatedly empties, assuming evaporation, when the real cause is a venting defect siphoning the seal. The trap is that siphonage and evaporation need different fixes. The mechanism is that a venting defect empties the seal in a single event. The false signal is that "the trap is dry, so it evaporated." The harm is a primer that cannot keep up with siphonage. The defense is to identify the depletion mechanism by history and testing before specifying the fix.

### Ignoring Seasonal and HVAC Effects on Evaporation

A plumber diagnoses a dry trap in summer and blames the primer, when the summer HVAC drying has simply outpaced the primer rate. The trap is that evaporation rate varies with conditions. The mechanism is that warm dry air accelerates evaporation. The false signal is that "the primer worked last season." The harm is a primer adjustment that is wrong for the season. The defense is to estimate the evaporation rate for the space and confirm the primer rate exceeds it.

### Refilling a Siphoned Trap Without Repairing the Vent

A plumber refills a siphoned trap and leaves, and the trap empties again after the next nearby discharge. The trap is that the venting defect was not addressed. The mechanism is that siphonage empties the seal on each discharge. The false signal is that "the trap is full now." The harm is repeated sewer gas entry. The defense is to investigate and repair the vent when a refilled trap empties quickly.

### Refilling and Walking Away Without Sanitizing or a Long-Term Fix

A plumber refills a long-dry trap and leaves, ignoring the biofilm and pathogen risk and the recurrence. The trap is that a long-dry drain harbors organisms. The mechanism is that stagnant water and biofilm support pathogens including Legionella. The false signal is that "the seal is restored." The harm is ongoing odor and pathogen exposure. The defense is to flush and sanitize where risk warrants, specify the matching long-term solution, and document the remediation.

## Self-Check

- Did I refill every suspect infrequently-used trap in the affected area before investigating venting or drain fouling?
- Did I observe whether the odor resolves after refilling, confirming or eliminating the dry-trap cause?
- Did I identify the depletion mechanism (evaporation, siphonage, back-pressure) by history and testing, rather than assuming evaporation?
- For a siphoned or back-pressured trap, did I investigate the vent (presence, sizing, obstruction, distance, sharing) and repair it?
- Did I estimate the evaporation rate for the space (temperature, humidity, airflow) and confirm the primer rate exceeds it with margin?
- Did I account for seasonal and HVAC effects (air-conditioning dries air and accelerates evaporation)?
- For a long-dry drain, did I flush and sanitize where risk warrants (healthcare, hospitality, large buildings) per the water-management program?
- Did I specify the matching long-term solution (primer for evaporation, vent repair for siphonage, regular use or removal of unused fixtures)?
- Did I consider and rule out non-plumbing odor sources (HVAC, dead animals, chemical storage) if the odor persists with full traps?
- Did I document the diagnosis, mechanism, remediation, and long-term solution, especially in healthcare and hospitality water-management plans?
