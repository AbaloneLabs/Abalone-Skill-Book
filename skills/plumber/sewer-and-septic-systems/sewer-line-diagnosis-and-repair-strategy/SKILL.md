# Sewer Line Diagnosis and Repair Strategy

A sewer line problem — recurring backups, slow drains throughout the building, or a visible sewage overflow — is a high-stakes diagnostic because the symptom (a backup) has many causes (a clog, root intrusion, a belly, a collapsed pipe, a broken joint) and the correct repair depends entirely on the actual cause, which is underground and invisible without camera inspection. The judgment problem is that snaking a recurring backup without camera inspection clears the immediate clog but leaves the underlying cause (roots, a belly, a collapse) active, guaranteeing recurrence, and a plumber who snakes and leaves is selling a recurring problem rather than a fix. A plumber who recommends sewer replacement without camera inspection is guessing at a very expensive repair. This skill covers the diagnostic and repair-strategy decisions for main sewer lines.

## Core Rules

### Camera-Inspect Every Recurring or Significant Sewer Problem

Every recurring sewer backup (more than one in a short period), every significant backup affecting multiple fixtures, and any sewer problem in a line over approximately 20 years old should be video camera-inspected to identify the cause: a clog (clearable), root intrusion at a joint (clearable but recurring), a belly (a low spot where water and solids accumulate, requiring repair), an offset or sheared pipe (a misalignment that blocks flow and roots intrude), a collapsed pipe (requiring replacement), or grease buildup (clearable with jetting). The trap is snaking a recurring backup without camera inspection, clearing the clog, and leaving the underlying cause (roots, belly, collapse) active for the next recurrence. The disciplined rule is to camera-inspect every recurring or significant sewer problem, to record the video, and to base the repair strategy on the actual cause.

### Locate the Problem Precisely Along the Run and Document the Depth

Camera inspection must include a locator (a transmitter sonde in the camera head, located from the surface with a receiver) to pinpoint the problem along the run (the distance from the cleanout) and the depth below grade. This location and depth determine the repair access (where to dig or where to position a trenchless machine) and the cost. The trap is camera-inspecting without a locator, knowing only "there is a problem somewhere out there," which is inadequate for repair planning. The disciplined rule is to use a locating sonde with the camera, to mark the problem location at the surface, and to record the distance and depth for repair planning.

### Match the Repair Strategy to the Specific Problem

The repair strategy must match the specific problem identified by camera inspection. A simple clog: snake or hydro-jet to clear, with no repair. Root intrusion at a joint: snake or jet to clear, followed by a root inhibitor treatment and a recurring maintenance plan, or repair if the joint is severely damaged. A belly (low spot): repair is required (the pipe must be re-bedded to the correct slope), because jetting clears the accumulation but the belly refills. An offset or sheared pipe: repair or replacement is required. A collapsed pipe: replacement is required. Grease buildup: hydro-jet to clear, with a recurring maintenance plan and a grease management review. The trap is applying the same strategy (usually snaking) to every problem, which clears clogs and root masses but does not fix bellies, offsets, or collapses. The disciplined rule is to match the repair strategy to the specific problem and to explain to the owner why a clearing-only strategy is inadequate for structural problems.

### Evaluate Trenchless Versus Open-Cut for Replacement

When replacement is required, evaluate trenchless methods (pipe bursting, where a new pipe is pulled through the old, breaking it outward; or cured-in-place pipe lining, where a resin-impregnated liner is inverted and cured inside the old pipe) against traditional open-cut excavation. Trenchless methods minimize surface disruption (no long trench through landscaping, driveways, or streets) and are often cost-competitive for long runs under obstacles, but they have limitations: pipe bursting requires adequate access pits and may not be feasible through collapsed or severely offset sections; lining reduces the interior diameter slightly and requires clean lateral connections; both require the existing line to be relatively intact or clearable. Open-cut allows direct inspection and repair of the line and connections but requires extensive excavation and surface restoration. The trap is defaulting to open-cut without considering trenchless, or recommending trenchless for a line that is too damaged to burst or line. The disciplined rule is to evaluate both methods against the line condition, the access, the surface disruption, and the cost, and to present the tradeoffs to the owner.

### Address the Cause of Recurring Problems, Not Just the Symptom

Recurring sewer backups almost always have an underlying cause that must be addressed: root intrusion (from nearby trees, requiring root cutting, chemical inhibition, or pipe repair), a belly (requiring re-bedding), a structural failure (requiring repair or replacement), grease accumulation (requiring a grease management program and jetting), or a poorly designed system (a too-long run, an inadequate slope). The trap is treating each backup as an independent event (snake and leave) without addressing the underlying cause, guaranteeing recurrence and eroding the owner's trust. The disciplined rule is to identify the underlying cause by camera inspection, to propose a strategy that addresses the cause (not just the symptom), and to be honest with the owner about whether a clearing-only strategy is a maintenance plan or a deferral of an inevitable repair.

## Common Traps

### Snaking a Recurring Backup Without Camera Inspection

A plumber responds to a sewer backup, snakes the line, clears the clog, and leaves, without camera inspection; the backup recurs because the underlying cause (roots, a belly, a collapse) is active. The trap is that the snaking clears the symptom but not the cause. The mechanism is that structural and root problems recur after clearing. The false signal is that "the line is clear." The harm is recurring backups and eroded trust. The defense is to camera-inspect every recurring or significant sewer problem and to base the repair strategy on the cause.

### Camera-Inspecting Without a Locator

A plumber camera-inspects a sewer line, identifies a problem, but does not use a locating sonde, and cannot tell the owner where the problem is or how deep. The trap is that the problem is identified but not located, which is inadequate for repair planning. The mechanism is that the sonde provides the surface location and depth. The false signal is that "the problem is on the video." The harm is repair planning without location or depth. The defense is to use a locating sonde, to mark the surface location, and to record the distance and depth.

### Applying the Same Strategy to Every Problem

A plumber snakes every sewer problem, regardless of whether the cause is a clog, roots, a belly, or a collapse, and structural problems recur after snaking. The trap is that snaking clears clogs and root masses but does not fix structural problems. The mechanism is that the strategy must match the problem. The false signal is that "the line is clear for now." The harm is recurrence of structural problems. The defense is to match the repair strategy to the specific problem and to explain why clearing-only is inadequate for structural problems.

### Defaulting to Open-Cut Replacement Without Considering Trenchless

A plumber recommends open-cut excavation and replacement for every sewer replacement, without considering trenchless methods (pipe bursting or lining) that could minimize surface disruption and cost. The trap is that open-cut is the default but not always the best strategy. The mechanism is that trenchless methods have different tradeoffs. The false signal is that "replacement means excavation." The harm is unnecessary surface disruption and cost. The defense is to evaluate trenchless and open-cut against the line condition, access, and surface disruption, and present the tradeoffs.

### Treating Each Recurring Backup as Independent Without Addressing the Cause

A plumber responds to each recurring backup as an independent event, snaking and leaving, without identifying and addressing the underlying cause, and the backups recur. The trap is that each event is treated in isolation. The mechanism is that the underlying cause is unaddressed. The false signal is that "the line was cleared each time." The harm is recurring backups and an unaddressed structural or root problem. The defense is to identify the underlying cause by camera inspection and propose a strategy that addresses the cause.

## Self-Check

- Did I camera-inspect every recurring or significant sewer problem, and did I record the video for the owner and the record?
- Did I use a locating sonde with the camera to pinpoint the problem along the run and the depth below grade, and did I mark the surface location?
- Did I identify the specific problem (clog, root intrusion, belly, offset, collapse, grease) and match the repair strategy to that problem?
- For structural problems (belly, offset, collapse), did I recommend repair or replacement rather than clearing-only, and did I explain why clearing is inadequate?
- For replacement, did I evaluate trenchless methods (pipe bursting, cured-in-place lining) against open-cut excavation, considering line condition, access, surface disruption, and cost?
- Did I present the tradeoffs of each strategy to the owner and document the recommendation?
- For root intrusion, did I propose a root management strategy (cutting, inhibition, repair) rather than one-time clearing?
- For grease accumulation, did I propose a grease management program and recurring jetting rather than one-time clearing?
- After any repair or replacement, did I camera-inspect to confirm the line is clear and structurally sound, and did I record the post-repair video?
- Did I document the camera inspection, the problem location and depth, the repair strategy and rationale, and the post-repair verification, so the work is verifiable and the line condition is recorded?
