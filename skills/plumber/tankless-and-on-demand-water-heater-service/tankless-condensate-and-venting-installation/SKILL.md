# Tankless Condensate and Venting Installation

A condensing tankless water heater extracts so much heat from the flue gases that the water vapor in them condenses inside the unit, producing a steady stream of acidic liquid and a low-temperature, pressure-positive exhaust that must be vented in listed corrosion-resistant material. The judgment problem is that the condensate is acidic enough to dissolve standard drain components, the vent is pressure-positive and must be gas-tight, and the combustion air and intake must be protected from recirculation and freezing — and an agent who treats the vent and drain as generic plumbing will create carbon monoxide (CO) risk, corrode the drain or the heat exchanger, or freeze-lock the unit. Agents miss this because the unit "fires and runs" on the test, masking that the condensate is eating the trap, the vent joints are not sealed, or the intake is inhaling its own exhaust. This skill covers the condensate, venting, combustion-air, and freeze decisions that keep a condensing tankless safe and running.

## Core Rules

### Drain Condensate Through a Neutralizer to an Approved Receptor

Condensing-tankless condensate is acidic (pH commonly 3.0 to 4.5) because the flue gases contain carbon dioxide and traces of nitrogen oxides that dissolve in the water. Discharging it directly to a cast-iron drain, a copper trap, or a standard PVC run (where code disallows it) corrodes piping and can violate code. The disciplined rule is to route condensate through a neutralizer (a cartridge of limestone or magnesium oxide media) that raises the pH to near neutral before discharge to an approved receptor (floor drain, utility sink, or indirect waste with an air gap), to size the neutralizer to the unit's condensate rate (commonly 1 to 2 gallons per hour for a 199,000 BTUH unit at full fire), and to replace or recharge the media on interval as the manufacturer specifies. Confirm the discharge path cannot freeze and is not shared with a pressure-relief line without an air gap.

### Use the Listed Vent Category, Material, and Diameter

Condensing tankless units are Category IV (pressure-positive, condensing) appliances, and the vent must be the manufacturer's listed material at the listed diameter — typically a listed AL29-4C stainless, a listed PVC/CPVC, or polypropylene per the manual, never a generic B-vent (Category I) or a random plastic pipe. Concentric venting (an exhaust pipe inside an intake pipe, terminating in one roof or wall penetration) is common and simplifies intake/exhaust proximity but must still meet the listed length and clearance limits. The trap is substituting material "because it fits" or downsizing the diameter "because the run is short." The disciplined rule is to use only listed material at the listed diameter, to seal all joints per the manual (cement for PVC/CPVC, listed seals for stainless), and to support the vent per code so it cannot sag and trap condensate.

### Limit Vent Length, Slope the Vent to Drain, and Size Combustion Air

Every tankless manual states a maximum equivalent vent length (straight runs plus fitting equivalents, commonly 35 to 50 feet for a 3-inch vent, more for 4-inch) and a maximum number of elbows. Exceeding the length or elbow count causes the fan to struggle, condensate to pool, and the unit to lock out. The vent must slope (commonly 1/4 inch per foot) back toward the unit so condensate drains to the unit's condensate trap (not out the termination), and combustion air must be sized to the firing rate (50 cubic feet per 1,000 BTUH for unconfined space, or a dedicated ducted/intake pipe). The disciplined rule is to lay out the vent within the listed equivalent length, slope it back to the unit, and to provide combustion air by volume or by duct per code and the manual.

### Maintain Intake-to-Exhaust and Termination Clearances

The intake must not inhale the exhaust. Manufacturers and codes (IFGC/NFPA 54) specify minimum separation between the intake and exhaust terminations (commonly 12 inches for side-by-side, more if the exhaust faces the intake), clearance from windows and doors (commonly 12 inches for intake, 12 to 48 inches for exhaust depending on size), clearance from combustion-air intakes for other appliances, and clearance from snow lines and grade (commonly 12 inches above grade or expected snow). The trap is mounting the intake and exhaust too close, or pointing the exhaust at the intake, so the unit recirculates its own flue gases, runs lean, and produces CO. The disciplined rule is to lay out both terminations to the manual's separation and clearance table, to orient the exhaust away from the intake, and to confirm snow-line clearance for the climate.

### Protect the Intake and Condensate Path From Freezing

In cold climates the intake can draw in snow or subfreezing air that freezes the combustion-air path or the condensate trap, locking the unit out. The intake termination should be located out of direct snowfall and drift paths, raised above the expected snow line, and (where the manual allows) fitted with an intake screen to block debris. The condensate trap and drain must be in a conditioned space or heat-traced and insulated where they run through freezing areas, because a frozen condensate trap backs up condensate into the unit, locks it out, and can spill. The disciplined rule is to route both the intake and the condensate path clear of freezing exposure, to insulate or heat-trace any condensate run in unconditioned space, and to confirm the intake termination is above the snow line.

## Common Traps

### Discharging Acidic Condensate Without a Neutralizer

A plumber runs condensate straight to a cast-iron or copper drain with no neutralizer. The trap is that the acidic condensate (pH 3.0 to 4.5) corrodes the drain over months. The mechanism is that the acids dissolve metal and some plastics; the false signal is "the drain works at startup." The harm is corroded piping, leaks, and code violations. The defense is to install a neutralizer sized to the condensate rate and to discharge to an approved receptor with an air gap.

### Substituting Vent Material or Downsizing the Diameter

A plumber uses leftover B-vent or undersized PVC because it fits. The trap is that a condensing tankless is Category IV (pressure-positive, condensing) and needs listed AL29-4C, listed PVC/CPVC, or polypropylene at the listed diameter. The mechanism is that the vent is pressure- and corrosion-rated; the false signal is "it's a metal/plastic vent." The harm is CO leakage, joint failure, and lockout. The defense is to use only the manufacturer's listed material and diameter and to seal joints per the manual.

### Exceeding the Listed Equivalent Vent Length

A plumber runs a long vent with many elbows to reach an exterior wall. The trap is that the manual limits equivalent length and elbow count, and exceeding them starves the fan and pools condensate. The mechanism is that fan capacity is finite; the false signal is "the vent reaches outside." The harm is lockouts and condensate pooling. The defense is to lay out the vent within the listed equivalent length and elbow count, upsizing diameter only where the manual permits.

### Mounting Intake and Exhaust Too Close or Mis-Oriented

A plumber mounts the intake and exhaust side by side with the exhaust pointing at the intake. The trap is that the unit recirculates flue gases, runs lean, and produces CO. The mechanism is that exhaust is drawn back into the intake; the false signal is "both terminations are outside." The harm is CO production and nuisance lockouts. The defense is to meet the manual's intake-exhaust separation and clearance table and to orient the exhaust away from the intake.

### Letting the Condensate Trap or Intake Freeze

A plumber routes the condensate drain through an unconditioned crawlspace and mounts the intake at grade in a snowy climate. The trap is that the trap or intake freezes and locks the unit out. The mechanism is that condensate freezes at 32°F and snow blocks the intake; the false signal is "it ran in the fall." The harm is no-heat/no-hot-water calls in winter and possible spillage. The defense is to keep the condensate path conditioned or heat-traced and to raise the intake above the snow line.

## Self-Check

- Is condensate routed through a neutralizer (limestone or magnesium oxide media) sized to the unit's condensate rate (commonly 1 to 2 GPH at full fire), with media replacement on the manufacturer's interval?
- Does the neutralized condensate discharge to an approved receptor (floor drain, utility sink, indirect waste) with an air gap, and is the path protected from freezing?
- Is the vent the manufacturer's listed Category IV material (AL29-4C stainless, listed PVC/CPVC, or polypropylene) at the listed diameter, with no B-vent or random substitutions?
- Are all vent joints sealed per the manual (cement for PVC/CPVC, listed seals for stainless), and is the vent supported per code so it cannot sag and trap condensate?
- Is the vent within the manual's maximum equivalent length and elbow count, and is it sloped (commonly 1/4 inch per foot) back toward the unit so condensate drains to the trap?
- Is combustion air sized to the firing rate (commonly 50 cubic feet per 1,000 BTUH for unconfined space, or dedicated ducted/intake pipe)?
- Do the intake and exhaust terminations meet the manual's separation (commonly 12 inches side-by-side) and clearance from windows, doors, and other intakes?
- Is the exhaust oriented away from the intake so the unit cannot recirculate its own flue gases and run lean or produce CO?
- Is the intake termination located above the expected snow line and out of direct snowfall and drift paths, with a screen where the manual allows?
- Are condensate traps and drains in unconditioned space insulated or heat-traced so they cannot freeze and back up into the unit?
