# Tankless Heater Sizing and Gas Supply Requirements

A tankless water heater is sized and served completely differently from a tank: it has almost no stored hot water, so its ability to deliver is set entirely by its firing rate against the temperature rise required at the moment of the draw. The judgment problem is that a tankless unit that "is rated 11 GPM" will deliver far less in winter when ground water is cold, and the same unit often demands more gas than the existing pipe and meter were ever built to supply. Agents miss this because the marketing GPM number is at a tiny 35°F rise, the gas pipe "looks big enough," and the unit fires on the test run — masking that two showers plus winter ground water will starve the burner, lock out, or deliver lukewarm water. This skill covers the sizing, gas-supply, combustion-air, and cascade decisions that determine whether a tankless install actually meets the load.

## Core Rules

### Size by GPM at the Required Temperature Rise, Not by the Label Number

The governing equation is GPM equals BTU per hour divided by (temperature rise times 500). A 199,000 BTUH unit at 80 percent efficiency delivers about 159,000 BTUH of output; at a 70°F rise (40°F ground water to 110°F delivery) that is roughly 4.5 GPM, and at a 100°F rise (40°F to 140°F) it is about 3.2 GPM. The label "11 GPM" is at a 35°F rise, a condition that essentially never occurs in a whole-house winter installation. The disciplined rule is to look up the local coldest ground-water temperature (commonly 40 to 50°F in cold regions, 50 to 60°F in mild), set the required delivery temperature (commonly 110 to 120°F), compute the rise, and size the unit to the simultaneous fixture load (two showers at 2.0 GPM each plus a lav at 1.0 GPM equals 5.0 GPM) at that rise. Always derate the label GPM for the real rise.

### Verify the Gas Pipe and Meter Can Deliver the Full Firing Rate

A whole-house tankless typically fires at 160,000 to 199,000 BTUH, far more than the 40,000 BTUH tank it often replaces. The existing gas pipe and meter, sized for the old tank plus a furnace, are frequently too small, and undersized gas supply causes nuisance lockouts, low flame, and code 11/12 errors under peak load. The disciplined rule is to perform a gas-pipe sizing calculation (longest run plus equivalent length, per IFGC/NFPA 54), confirm the meter and regulator are rated for the added load (often requiring a meter upgrade by the utility), and to size the tankless gas connection per the manual (commonly 3/4-inch or 1-inch at the unit even if the run is shorter, because the unit's regulator needs the volume). Never assume the existing pipe is adequate because the old appliance worked.

### Confirm Combustion Air and Venting Match the Firing Rate

A 199,000 BTUH tankless needs a large volume of combustion air (commonly 50 cubic feet per 1,000 BTUH for an unconfined indoor space, or dedicated outdoor/ducted air), and the vent must be the manufacturer's listed material and diameter (typically Category III stainless or a listed PVC/CPVC for condensing units, never a generic B-vent unless listed). Combustion air and venting are not interchangeable between tank and tankless; a tankless installed into a closet with the old tank's vent and no combustion air will lock out, spill, or fail inspection. The disciplined rule is to size combustion air to the firing rate per the manual and code, to use only listed vent material at the listed diameter and length, and to confirm the vent termination clearances (windows, intake proximity, snow line) before installation.

### Use the Ground-Water Temperature Table to Set Realistic Winter Performance

Ground-water temperature varies by latitude and season and directly sets the temperature rise the unit must produce. A unit that delivers 5.0 GPM at a 45°F rise (southern summer, 70°F ground water to 115°F) may deliver only 3.5 GPM at a 75°F rise (northern winter, 40°F ground water to 115°F). The disciplined rule is to obtain the local design ground-water temperature (DOE map or local utility data), compute the rise to the delivery setpoint, and to communicate the realistic winter GPM to the occupant so expectations match performance. For cold-climate whole-house service, a single 199,000 BTUH unit may be insufficient and a larger or cascaded arrangement may be required.

### Plan Multi-Unit Cascade for High Load and Redundancy

Where a single unit cannot meet the load (large homes, high simultaneous draw, commercial service), multiple tankless units can be cascaded (staged on as flow increases) and/or paralleled (modulating together). Cascade requires a manufacturer-approved communication cable so the units stage in order and share the load, and the gas supply must serve the combined firing rate. The disciplined rule is to size the cascade to the peak simultaneous load at the winter rise, to stage the units so the lead unit is not short-cycled (rotate the lead if the controller supports it), and to confirm the gas pipe and meter serve the combined BTU input, not just one unit.

## Common Traps

### Quoting the Label GPM Without Derating for the Real Rise

A plumber quotes "11 GPM" from the brochure for a winter installation. The trap is that the 11 GPM is at a 35°F rise, and the real winter rise is 70 to 100°F, cutting delivery to 3 to 5 GPM. The mechanism is that GPM is inversely proportional to temperature rise at fixed BTU; the false signal is "the label says 11." The harm is a system that cannot run two showers in winter and an unhappy customer. The defense is to compute GPM at the local winter ground-water rise and quote that number.

### Assuming the Existing Gas Pipe Is Adequate Because the Old Tank Worked

A plumber connects the tankless to the existing 1/2-inch gas pipe that served a 40,000 BTUH tank. The trap is that the tankless draws four to five times the gas, and the pipe cannot deliver the volume. The mechanism is that gas-pipe capacity scales with pipe size and length, and the old pipe was sized for a small load; the false signal is "gas is connected and the unit fires." The harm is nuisance lockouts under peak load and code 11/12 errors. The defense is to run a gas-pipe sizing calculation for the new firing rate and to upgrade pipe and meter as needed.

### Undersizing Combustion Air in a Closet Install

A plumber installs the tankless in a small closet with no dedicated combustion air. The trap is that the unit consumes the closet's oxygen and locks out or burns lean. The mechanism is that a 199,000 BTUH unit needs far more combustion air than a tank; the false signal is "it fires on the test." The harm is lockouts, sooting, and failed inspection. The defense is to size combustion air to the firing rate per code and the manual, ducted or louvers to a large space if needed.

### Using Unlisted Vent Material or Diameter

A plumber vents the tankless with leftover B-vent or undersized PVC. The trap is that tankless vents are Category III (stainless, pressure-positive) or listed PVC/CPVC for condensing units, and the wrong material or diameter causes spillage, condensate pooling, or failure. The mechanism is that the vent is pressure-rated and diameter-specific to the unit; the false signal is "it's a metal vent." The harm is CO risk, corrosion, and lockout. The defense is to use only the manufacturer's listed vent at the listed diameter and length.

### Forgetting the Meter and Regulator When Adding a Tankless

A plumber upgrades the gas pipe to the tankless but not the meter or regulator. The trap is that the utility meter and house regulator were sized for the old load and cannot pass the new volume. The mechanism is that meter and regulator capacity is finite and often lower than the new combined load; the false signal is "the pipe is big enough." The harm is gas starvation across all appliances under peak load. The defense is to confirm meter and regulator capacity with the utility and upgrade them with the pipe.

## Self-Check

- Did I size the unit by computing GPM at the required temperature rise (GPM equals output BTUH divided by rise times 500), using the local design ground-water temperature, rather than quoting the label GPM?
- Is the BTU input (commonly 160,000 to 199,000 BTUH for whole-house) sufficient for the simultaneous fixture load at the winter rise?
- Did I run a gas-pipe sizing calculation (longest run plus equivalent length, per IFGC/NFPA 54) for the new firing rate, and is the pipe sized to deliver full volume at the unit?
- Did I confirm the gas meter and regulator are rated for the added load, and did I request a meter upgrade from the utility if needed?
- Is combustion air sized to the firing rate per the manual and code (commonly 50 cubic feet per 1,000 BTUH for unconfined space, or dedicated ducted/outdoor air)?
- Is the vent the manufacturer's listed material (Category III stainless, or listed PVC/CPVC for condensing units) at the listed diameter and length, with no unlisted substitutions?
- Are the vent termination clearances (windows, doors, intake proximity, snow line, mechanical intake) met per the manual and code?
- Did I obtain the local design ground-water temperature and communicate the realistic winter GPM to the occupant so expectations match performance?
- For a multi-unit cascade, is the staging controller wired per the manufacturer, the lead rotated to avoid short-cycling, and the gas supply sized to the combined firing rate?
- Did I document the sizing calculation, ground-water temperature, gas-pipe and meter sizing, combustion air, venting, and (if applicable) cascade configuration so the installation is verifiable?
