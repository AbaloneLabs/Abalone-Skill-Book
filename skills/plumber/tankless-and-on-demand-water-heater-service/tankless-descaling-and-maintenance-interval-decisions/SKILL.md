# Tankless Descaling and Maintenance Interval Decisions

A tankless water heater heats water on demand through a tightly wound, small-diameter heat exchanger, so any hardness in the supply deposits directly onto the surfaces that must transfer heat. The judgment problem is that scale accumulation is invisible and progressive — the unit keeps firing until the flow sensor is choked, the outlet temperature sags, or the heat exchanger overheats and fails — and an agent who treats "no hot water" calls as unrelated to maintenance, or who flushes on a fixed calendar regardless of hardness, either misses the root cause or wastes service. Agents miss this because the unit "ran fine for years," masking that every gallon of hard water was narrowing the waterways. This skill covers the descale-interval, flushing-procedure, component-cleaning, and error-code decisions that keep a tankless unit delivering rated flow and surviving its design life.

## Core Rules

### Set the Descale Interval by Hardness and Runtime, Not by Calendar Alone

Scale forms in proportion to the volume of hard water heated and the temperature setpoint (hotter delivery deposits faster). A unit on 15 grains-per-gallon (GPG) water at 120°F needs descaling far more often than the same unit on 3 GPG water. A practical rule is to descale every 12 months on moderately hard water (3 to 7 GPG), every 6 months on hard water (7 to 15 GPG), and every 3 to 4 months or install a softener on very hard water (over 15 GPG); on softened water, extend toward 18 to 24 months and focus instead on the inlet screen and burner. The disciplined rule is to test the supply hardness, set the interval from the hardness and the setpoint, and to log each descale so the interval can be tuned to the actual rate of scale formation (measured by flush time, flow recovery, or a flow-sensor reading).

### Flush the Heat Exchanger With Isolation Valves, a Pump, and the Right Solution

A proper descale uses a set of isolation valves (service valves) on the hot and cold connections, a small submersible pump, two hoses, and a descaling solution (white vinegar for routine scale, or a dedicated sulfamic-acid descaler for heavy scale). The circuit runs from the cold service valve, through the pump, into the hot service valve (reverse flow through the heat exchanger), and back to the bucket, circulating for 45 to 90 minutes until effervescence stops and flow is restored. The trap is flushing forward (cold in, hot out) which can pack scale into the outlet, or using harsh acids that attack the heat exchanger. The disciplined rule is to follow the manufacturer's flush procedure, circulate reverse-flow through the heat exchanger with a mild acid, rinse thoroughly with fresh water before returning to service, and to verify flow is restored before leaving.

### Clean the Flow Sensor, Inlet Screen, and Burner on Every Service

Scale and debris do not only coat the heat exchanger. The flow sensor (the turbine or hall-effect device that tells the burner how much water is flowing) can foul or stick, causing the unit to under-fire or not fire at all; the inlet screen can plug, reducing flow and triggering LC (low flow) codes; and the burner and flame rod can coat with dust or condensate, causing ignition or flame-sensing failures. The disciplined rule is to remove and clean the flow sensor and inlet screen at every descale, to inspect and clean the burner and flame rod per the manual (light brushing, no abrasive), and to verify the fan and air intake are clear. These are the components whose fouling produces the nuisance codes that a descale alone will not fix.

### Interpret Error Codes as Symptoms of an Underlying Condition

Tankless error codes point to a system, not a single part. Code 11 (ignition failure) points to gas supply, spark, flame rod, or combustion air; code 12 (flame loss during run) points to gas starvation, a fouled flame rod, or a venting/combustion-air problem; and LC codes (low flow, often LC1 through LC4) point to scale in the heat exchanger, a plugged inlet screen, or a stuck flow sensor. The trap is clearing the code and leaving, or replacing the named part without diagnosing why. The disciplined rule is to treat each code as a symptom: for LC codes, verify flow before and after a descale; for code 11/12, verify gas pressure, flame rod, combustion air, and venting before condemning a part; and to confirm the underlying condition is resolved before returning the unit to service.

### Verify Flow and Temperature Recovery After Every Service

A descale is only successful if flow and outlet temperature recover to spec. Before leaving, run the unit at a known fixture flow (a bucket and stopwatch, or a flow meter), measure the outlet temperature at steady state, and confirm the unit modulates without LC codes or short-cycling. Compare to the pre-service readings; if flow or temperature did not recover, the scale may be beyond flushing (heat exchanger replacement) or another fault (flow sensor, gas supply) remains. The disciplined rule is to measure flow and outlet temperature before and after every descale, to document the recovery, and to escalate to component diagnosis or replacement when a flush does not restore performance.

## Common Traps

### Flushing on a Fixed Calendar Regardless of Hardness

A plumber descales every 12 months whether the water is 3 GPG or 18 GPG. The trap is that the calendar ignores the actual rate of scale formation. The mechanism is that scale is proportional to hardness and runtime; the false signal is "we descale annually." The harm on hard water is a choked heat exchanger between services; the harm on soft water is wasted service. The defense is to test hardness, set the interval from hardness and setpoint, and tune it to measured scale.

### Flushing Forward and Packing Scale Into the Outlet

A plumber flushes cold-in to hot-out, pushing loosened scale into the outlet restriction. The trap is that forward flushing can worsen the blockage. The mechanism is that the heat exchanger's narrowest waterways are near the outlet; the false signal is "we ran solution through it." The harm is reduced flow after the service. The defense is to flush reverse-flow (hot valve in, cold valve out) per the manufacturer so scale is pushed back out the inlet path.

### Using Harsh Acid That Attacks the Heat Exchanger

A plumber uses muriatic or other strong acid to "clean faster." The trap is that strong acids attack copper or stainless heat exchangers and gaskets. The mechanism is that the heat exchanger is a thin-walled alloy rated for mild descalers only; the false signal is "it cleaned up fast." The harm is pinhole leaks and premature failure. The defense is to use vinegar or a manufacturer-approved sulfamic-acid descaler at the recommended dilution and dwell time.

### Clearing an LC Code Without Verifying Flow Recovery

A plumber descales, the LC code clears, and the plumber leaves without measuring flow. The trap is that the code can clear while the heat exchanger is still restricted. The mechanism is that the LC threshold is a flow ratio, and partial recovery can reset the code; the false signal is "the code is gone." The harm is a repeat call and continued overheating. The defense is to measure flow and outlet temperature before and after and to confirm recovery to spec.

### Treating Code 11 or 12 as a Part Failure Without Diagnosing Cause

A plumber sees code 11 (ignition failure) or code 12 (flame loss) and replaces the ignition pack or gas valve. The trap is that these codes are usually caused by gas supply, flame-rod fouling, combustion air, or venting, not the named part. The mechanism is that the code points to a system; the false signal is "the code names the part." The harm is a costly misdiagnosis and a recurring fault. The defense is to verify gas pressure, flame rod, combustion air, and venting before condemning a part.

## Self-Check

- Did I test the supply hardness (GPG) and set the descale interval from hardness and delivery setpoint (e.g., 12 months at 3 to 7 GPG, 6 months at 7 to 15 GPG, 3 to 4 months or a softener above 15 GPG)?
- Did I flush reverse-flow through the heat exchanger (hot service valve in, cold service valve out) using isolation/service valves, a pump, and a manufacturer-approved solution (vinegar or sulfamic acid)?
- Did I circulate for the full dwell (commonly 45 to 90 minutes) until effervescence stopped, then rinse thoroughly with fresh water before returning to service?
- Did I remove and clean the flow sensor and inlet screen, and inspect and clean the burner and flame rod per the manual (no abrasive)?
- For an LC code, did I measure flow before and after the descale and confirm recovery to spec, rather than clearing the code and leaving?
- For code 11 (ignition failure) or code 12 (flame loss), did I verify gas pressure, flame rod condition, combustion air, and venting before condemning a part?
- Did I avoid strong acids (muriatic) and use only vinegar or a manufacturer-approved descaler at the recommended dilution and dwell?
- Did I run the unit at a known fixture flow after service and measure steady-state outlet temperature to confirm modulation without LC codes or short-cycling?
- Did I log the pre- and post-service flow and temperature readings so the descale interval can be tuned to the actual rate of scale formation?
- Did I recommend a water softener where hardness exceeds roughly 15 GPG, since descaling alone cannot keep pace with the scale formation rate?
