# Labor and Overhead Calculation

A plumbing bid is not just material plus a markup; it is material plus labor plus overhead plus contingency plus profit, and the labor and overhead portions are where most estimates go wrong because they require converting a plan into hours and then loading those hours with the true cost of running a business. The judgment problem is that labor is task-based and variable (a rough-in bath takes a different number of hours than a main replacement), overhead is fixed and easy to under-allocate, and contingency for unknown conditions is a judgment call that low-bidders omit and disciplined estimators include. A plumber who bids labor by a flat square-foot or fixture-unit rate, without breaking down the tasks and loading the overhead, produces a bid that looks competitive and loses money on every job. This skill covers the discipline that makes labor and overhead estimates accurate and profitable.

## Core Rules

### Estimate Labor Task by Task From the Scope, Not by a Flat Rate

Labor is consumed by tasks, and the disciplined estimate breaks the scope into tasks (rough-in DWV, rough-in supply, underground, set fixtures, pressure test, trim-out, service call) and assigns a labor-hour estimate to each based on the quantity and the historical productivity for that task. The unit rates come from the estimator's own history (hours per fixture for rough-in, hours per linear foot for pipe, hours per fixture for trim) or from a published cost guide (RSMeans, PHCC) adjusted to the local market and the crew's actual productivity. The disciplined approach is to build the labor estimate from the bottom up: scope to tasks, tasks to quantities, quantities to hours using unit rates. The trap is bidding labor by a flat rate per fixture or per square foot, which fits some jobs and loses money on others because the task mix differs.

### Size the Crew to the Schedule and the Task Sequence

Labor hours convert to duration through the crew size, and the crew size must fit the schedule and the task sequence. The disciplined approach is to determine the required duration from the project schedule (how many days of rough-in before the walls close), divide the total labor hours by the available crew-hours per day, and confirm the resulting crew size is practical (a one-day rough-in with 80 labor hours needs a 10-person crew, which may be impractical). The crew size also affects productivity: a crew too large for the workspace gets in its own way; a crew too small misses the schedule. The trap is bidding labor hours without checking whether the crew size they imply fits the schedule and the workspace, leading to either a schedule miss or an unproductive crew.

### Allocate Overhead Based on Real Cost, Not a Flat Markup

Overhead is the cost of running the business that is not directly attributable to a single job — the shop, the truck, the office staff, the insurance, the tools, the estimator's salary, the owner's non-billable time — and it must be recovered across the volume of work. The disciplined approach is to total the annual overhead cost, divide by the annual billable labor hours (or the annual revenue) to get an overhead rate per hour (or per dollar of revenue), and apply that rate to each job's labor (or revenue). The trap is applying a flat "10 percent overhead" markup that bears no relation to the actual overhead cost, which under-recovers on small jobs and over-recovers on large ones, distorting the bid. The disciplined rule is to calculate the real overhead rate from the real costs and apply it consistently.

### Include Labor Burden in the Labor Cost, Not Just the Wage

The cost of an hour of labor is more than the wage; it includes the payroll taxes, workers' compensation, unemployment insurance, health insurance, retirement, paid time off, and tool allowance — collectively the "labor burden," which often adds 40 to 70 percent to the base wage. The disciplined approach is to calculate the fully burdened labor rate for each worker classification (journeyman, apprentice, foreman) and use that rate in the estimate, not the bare wage. The trap is bidding labor at the wage rate and absorbing the burden from the margin, which under-prices the labor and erodes profit on every job. The disciplined rule is to use the fully burdened rate in every labor estimate.

### Build Contingency for Unknown Conditions Proportional to the Risk

Every estimate carries risk that the actual conditions will differ from the assumed — concealed materials in a remodel, an undocumented underground conflict, a fixture substitution, a schedule compression — and contingency is the line item that covers that risk. The disciplined approach is to assess the risk of unknowns for each job (low for a defined new-construction rough-in, high for a remodel with concealed conditions) and to include a contingency proportional to that risk (a few percent for defined work, 15 to 25 percent for a risky remodel). The trap is omitting contingency to make the bid look competitive, which means every unknown comes out of the margin. The disciplined rule is to include a risk-proportional contingency and to document the assumptions it covers.

### Set Profit as a Conscious Margin, Not Whatever Is Left

Profit is the return for the risk and the capital, and it should be a conscious decision based on the market, the risk, and the firm's goals, not the residual after material, labor, and overhead. The disciplined approach is to set a target profit margin (commonly 10 to 20 percent for plumbing, varying with risk and market), apply it to the fully-loaded cost (material plus burdened labor plus overhead plus contingency), and confirm the resulting price is competitive. The trap is pricing to win (lowering profit until the bid is low) rather than pricing to profit, which wins work at a margin too thin to absorb any problem. The disciplined rule is to set profit consciously and to walk away from work that cannot be done at a profitable margin.

## Common Traps

### Bidding Labor by a Flat Rate Per Fixture or Per Square Foot

A plumber bids a residential rough-in at a flat rate per fixture, wins the job, and loses money because the job's task mix (a long main run, a complex island vent, an unusually deep underground) consumed more hours than the flat rate assumed. The trap is that the flat rate fits an "average" job and the actual job is not average. The mechanism is that labor is consumed by tasks, and the task mix varies by job, so a flat rate under-bids the atypical jobs. The false signal is that the rate "won the bid." The harm is a margin loss on every atypical job, which accumulates across a year. The defense is to estimate labor task by task from the scope, using unit rates applied to the actual quantities.

### Using the Bare Wage Instead of the Fully Burdened Labor Rate

A plumber bids labor at the journeyman's wage of $30 per hour, ignoring the $18 per hour of taxes, workers' comp, insurance, and benefits, and recovers only the wage from the bid while the burden comes out of the margin, silently eroding profit on every job. The trap is that the wage is the visible number and the burden is back-office, so the bid uses the wage and the burden is "absorbed." The mechanism is that the burden is a real cost of every labor hour, and bidding without it under-prices the labor by 40 to 70 percent of the wage. The false signal is that the labor "is priced." The harm is a systematic margin erosion that the owner cannot explain, because every job "makes its number" on the bare wage but the business struggles. The defense is to calculate the fully burdened labor rate for each classification and use it in every estimate.

### Applying a Flat Overhead Markup That Does Not Match the Real Cost

A plumber applies a flat 10 percent overhead to every bid, but the real annual overhead (shop, trucks, office, insurance, estimator) is 18 percent of revenue, so every job under-recovers overhead by 8 percent of revenue and the business loses money on a full book of work. The trap is that the flat markup is a round number that "everyone uses" and the real overhead is not calculated, so the bid under-recovers systematically. The mechanism is that overhead is a real fixed cost that must be recovered across the volume, and a guessed markup that is below the real rate loses money on every job. The false signal is that the bids "are competitive." The harm is a business that wins work and loses money, unable to understand why. The defense is to total the annual overhead, divide by the annual billable hours or revenue, and apply the resulting real rate to every estimate.

### Omitting Contingency to Make the Bid Look Competitive

A plumber omits contingency on a remodel bid to come in low, wins the job, and when the concealed conditions differ from the assumption (galvanized instead of copper, a hidden structural conflict) the cost comes out of the margin and the job loses money. The trap is that contingency is invisible in the bid and omitting it lowers the price, which feels like a competitive advantage, but the risk is real and the cost falls on the plumber. The mechanism is that unknown conditions are probable in a remodel, and without a contingency line every unknown erodes the margin. The false signal is that the bid "won." The harm is a job that loses money on the first surprise, and a pattern of winning risky work at thin margins. The defense is to include a risk-proportional contingency (high for remodels and unknown conditions, low for defined new work) and to document the assumptions it covers.

### Bidding Labor Hours Without Checking the Crew Size Against the Schedule

A plumber bids 200 labor hours for a rough-in that must be done in five days, which implies a 5-person crew at 8 hours, but the workspace fits only 3 productive workers, so the job takes longer than the schedule and incurs delay or overtime cost. The trap is that the labor hours "fit the bid" but the implied crew size does not fit the workspace or the schedule. The mechanism is that labor hours convert to duration through crew size, and a crew too large for the space is unproductive while a crew too small misses the schedule. The false signal is that the hours "are estimated." The harm is a schedule miss, overtime cost, or an unproductive crew that erodes the margin. The defense is to check the implied crew size (hours divided by available crew-hours) against the workspace and the schedule, and to adjust the crew, the duration, or the scope accordingly.

### Pricing to Win Rather Than Pricing to Profit

A plumber lowers the profit margin on every bid until the bid is the lowest, wins a full book of work, and discovers at year-end that the 2 to 3 percent margins cannot absorb a single problem, and one bad job wipes out the year. The trap is that winning feels like success and the thin margin feels like the cost of staying busy, but a margin too thin to absorb risk is a path to a bad year from a single problem. The mechanism is that profit is the buffer for the inevitable problem, and pricing it away removes the buffer. The false signal is that the business "is busy." The harm is a business that operates at break-even and is one problem from loss. The defense is to set profit consciously based on risk and market, to know the minimum acceptable margin, and to walk away from work that cannot be done profitably.

## Self-Check

- Did I estimate labor task by task from the scope, using unit rates applied to the actual quantities, rather than a flat rate per fixture or per square foot?
- Did I use the fully burdened labor rate (wage plus taxes, workers' comp, insurance, benefits) for each worker classification, not the bare wage?
- Did I calculate the real overhead rate from the annual overhead cost and the annual billable hours or revenue, and apply it to the estimate rather than a flat guessed markup?
- Did I size the crew to the schedule and the workspace, checking that the labor hours imply a practical crew size and duration?
- Did I include a contingency proportional to the risk of unknown conditions (high for remodels, low for defined new work), and document the assumptions it covers?
- Did I set profit as a conscious margin based on risk and market, applied to the fully-loaded cost, rather than pricing to win?
- Did I confirm the resulting price is competitive, and if not, identify whether the issue is scope, productivity, or market rather than cutting profit?
- Did I break the labor estimate into phases (underground, rough-in, trim, service) so progress can be tracked against the estimate?
- Did I document the labor unit rates, the burden calculation, the overhead rate, the contingency, and the profit margin so the bid is defensible and re-priceable?
- Did I compare the estimate to actual costs on completed jobs to refine the unit rates and the overhead rate for future bids?
