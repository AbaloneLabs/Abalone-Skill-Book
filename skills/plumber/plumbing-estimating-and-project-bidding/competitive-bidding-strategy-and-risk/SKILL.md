# Competitive Bidding Strategy and Risk

Winning a bid is not the same as winning profitable work, and the most expensive mistake in plumbing estimating is winning a job at a price that cannot be delivered without loss. The judgment problem is that a bid is a contract offer made under information asymmetry — the owner and the general contractor know the schedule, the payment terms, and the real scope, while the plumber knows only what the documents show — and the competitive pressure to be low rewards bidders who under-read the scope, omit the exclusions, and ignore the risk. A plumber who bids to win rather than to profit fills the schedule with work that erodes the business. This skill covers the strategic decisions that make a bid both competitive and defensible, and the discipline to walk away from work that cannot be done profitably.

## Core Rules

### Clarify Scope Before Bidding and Document Every Exclusion

A bid must state exactly what is and is not included, because ambiguity in scope becomes a loss when the general contractor or owner interprets it against the plumber. The disciplined approach is to read the scope documents completely, list every inclusion explicitly (the fixtures, the rough-in, the testing, the permits, the trenching, the coordination), and list every exclusion explicitly (the items that look like plumbing but are not — the fire sprinkler, the gas line if separate, the site utility beyond the building, the fixtures supplied by the owner, the patch-back if excluded). The trap is a bid that says "plumbing per the plans" without listing inclusions and exclusions, leaving the plumber vulnerable to scope creep and dispute. The disciplined rule is to document scope and exclusions in the bid letter, in writing, before the price.

### Document Assumptions and Conditions in the Bid

Every bid rests on assumptions — the fixture models are as specified, the existing conditions are as shown, the schedule is as stated, the access is as implied — and undocumented assumptions become the plumber's loss when they prove wrong. The disciplined approach is to state the assumptions and conditions in the bid: the bid is based on the documents dated X, the fixtures are the specified models, the existing conditions are as shown in the demolition plans, the schedule is the stated duration, the access and the workspace are as implied. The trap is a bid that is a price with no stated basis, so when a condition differs the plumber has no defense and must absorb the cost. The disciplined rule is to document the basis of the bid so changes to it justify a change order.

### Include Change-Order Provisions That Protect Against Scope and Condition Changes

Change orders are how a plumber recovers the cost of work outside the original scope, and the provisions governing them must be in the bid or the contract before the work starts. The disciplined approach is to include provisions stating that work outside the documented scope, changes to the fixtures or the specifications, concealed conditions differing from the documented assumptions, and owner- or GC-directed changes will be priced as change orders at the bid labor rates plus material and overhead. The trap is a bid with no change-order provision, so when the scope changes the plumber has no agreed mechanism to recover the cost and must negotiate from weakness mid-project. The disciplined rule is to state the change-order pricing basis in the bid or the contract.

### Recognize the Low-Bid Trap and the Cost of Winning at the Wrong Price

The low bid wins the work but also takes the risk, and a bid that is low because it omitted scope, under-estimated labor, or ignored risk is a trap that converts the project into a loss. The disciplined approach is to recognize the signals of a low-bid trap: a scope that is ambiguous or incomplete, a schedule that is compressed, payment terms that are unfavorable, a general contractor with a reputation for scope games, and a bid that came in lower than the estimator's own gut check. When these signals appear, the disciplined response is to re-check the estimate, to clarify the scope in writing, and if the bid still requires an unsustainable price, to walk away. The trap is treating the low bid as a win rather than as a warning. The disciplined rule is to know the minimum profitable price and to refuse to bid below it.

### Evaluate the Client and the Project, Not Just the Price

The price is only one term of a contract; the client's reliability, the payment terms, the schedule, the project's definition, and the working relationship determine whether a profitable price actually yields profit. The disciplined approach is to evaluate the client (do they pay on time, do they honor change orders, do they scope-creep), the payment terms (pay-when-paid, retainage, the payment cycle), the schedule (is it realistic, does it allow productive sequencing), and the project definition (are the plans complete, are the fixtures specified, are the existing conditions documented). The trap is bidding on price alone to a client who pays late, denies change orders, and runs a chaotic schedule, so a profitable price yields a loss in cash flow and rework. The disciplined rule is to evaluate the whole project, not just the price.

### Know When to Walk Away

The discipline to decline a project is as important as the discipline to bid one, and a plumber who cannot walk away fills the schedule with unprofitable or unmanageable work. The disciplined approach is to establish the walk-away criteria in advance: a scope that cannot be clarified, a price below the minimum profitable margin, a client with a documented payment or change-order problem, a schedule that is physically impractical, a risk that exceeds the contingency. When the criteria are met, the disciplined response is to decline the bid or to submit a no-bid with a brief explanation, preserving the relationship and the reputation. The trap is bidding every project regardless of risk, because "we need the work," which fills the schedule with losses. The disciplined rule is to walk away from work that cannot be done profitably or safely.

## Common Traps

### Bidding "Plumbing Per the Plans" Without Listing Inclusions and Exclusions

A plumber submits a bid that says "plumbing per the plans, lump sum $X," wins the job, and the general contractor hands over a scope that includes the gas line, the site utility, and the patch-back, none of which the plumber priced, because "per the plans" was ambiguous and the GC interprets it broadly. The trap is that the lump-sum phrase feels complete and the exclusions feel like they invite argument, but the ambiguity is the plumber's loss. The mechanism is that an undocumented scope is interpreted by the other party, usually against the plumber, and the lump sum locks in the price for whatever scope is asserted. The false signal is that the bid "won." The harm is scope creep absorbed at the plumber's cost, eroding or eliminating the margin. The defense is to list every inclusion and every exclusion explicitly in the bid letter, in writing, before the price.

### Omitting Assumed Conditions From the Bid and Absorbing the Surprise

A plumber bids a remodel assuming the existing pipe is copper (as shown in the partial demolition photos), does not state the assumption, opens the wall to find galvanized steel that must be fully replaced, and absorbs the triple-cost replacement because the bid had no stated basis to justify a change order. The trap is that the assumption felt safe and stating it felt like hedging, but the undocumented assumption is the plumber's loss when it proves wrong. The mechanism is that an undocumented assumption cannot justify a change order, so the cost of the surprise falls on the plumber. The false signal is that the bid "was accepted." The harm is a margin wiped out by a concealed condition that could have been a change order. The defense is to state every assumption (existing materials, fixture models, schedule, access, workspace) in the bid, so a differing condition justifies a change order.

### Bidding With No Change-Order Provision and Negotiating From Weakness

A plumber submits a bid with no change-order provision, the scope changes mid-project (the owner upgrades the fixtures, the GC adds a mop sink), and the plumber has no agreed mechanism to price the change, so the GC imposes a price that barely covers cost. The trap is that the change-order provision feels like a confrontational detail to omit in a competitive bid, but without it the plumber has no leverage when the scope changes. The mechanism is that a change order without a pre-agreed pricing basis is negotiated under duress mid-project, and the party with the schedule leverage (the GC) sets the price. The false signal is that the original scope "is what was bid." The harm is unpaid or underpaid scope changes that erode the margin. The defense is to include a change-order pricing provision (bid labor rates plus material and overhead) in the bid or the contract before the work starts.

### Treating the Low Bid as a Win Rather Than a Warning

A plumber's estimate comes in higher than the apparent market, so the plumber trims the labor, drops the contingency, and cuts the profit to win the bid, then delivers the job at a loss because the trimmed estimate could not absorb the real conditions. The trap is that winning feels like success and the trimming feels like the cost of competitiveness, but a bid trimmed below the profitable price is a guaranteed loss. The mechanism is that the estimate reflected the real cost, and trimming it below that cost does not change the cost, only the recovery. The false signal is that the bid "won." The harm is a job delivered at a loss, a damaged margin, and a pattern of winning unprofitable work. The defense is to know the minimum profitable price, to refuse to bid below it, and to treat a bid that requires trimming below it as a signal to walk away.

### Bidding on Price Alone to a Client Who Pays Late or Denies Change Orders

A plumber bids a competitive price to a new general contractor to "get the work," wins the project, and discovers the GC pays at 90 days, denies legitimate change orders, and runs a chaotic schedule that causes rework, so the profitable price yields a cash-flow crisis and a loss. The trap is that the price felt profitable and the client evaluation was skipped because "we need the work," but the client's behavior determines whether the price yields profit. The mechanism is that payment terms, change-order integrity, and schedule quality convert a profitable price into a loss through cash flow and rework. The false signal is that the bid "was profitable." The harm is a cash-flow crisis, unpaid change orders, and rework cost that the margin cannot absorb. The defense is to evaluate the client's payment history, change-order behavior, and schedule realism before bidding, and to decline or to price the risk into the bid.

### Bidding Every Project Because "We Need the Work"

A plumber, fearing a gap in the schedule, bids every project that comes in regardless of scope clarity, risk, or client quality, wins a mix of underpriced and unmanageable jobs, and fills the schedule with work that loses money and prevents taking profitable work when it appears. The trap is that the fear of idle time drives bidding on bad projects, and the bad projects consume the capacity that should go to good projects. The mechanism is that capacity is finite, and filling it with unprofitable work crowds out profitable work. The false signal is that the schedule "is full." The harm is a business that is busy but unprofitable, and unable to pursue or deliver the work that would be profitable. The defense is to establish walk-away criteria, to decline or no-bid projects that fail them, and to keep capacity available for profitable work.

## Self-Check

- Did I read the complete scope documents and list every inclusion and every exclusion explicitly in the bid letter, in writing, before the price?
- Did I state the assumptions and conditions the bid is based on (documents dated, fixture models, existing conditions, schedule, access) so a differing condition justifies a change order?
- Did I include a change-order pricing provision (bid labor rates plus material and overhead) in the bid or the contract before the work starts?
- Did I recognize the signals of a low-bid trap (ambiguous scope, compressed schedule, unfavorable payment terms, GC reputation, estimate below gut check) and respond by clarifying, re-checking, or walking away?
- Did I evaluate the client (payment history, change-order behavior), the payment terms, the schedule, and the project definition, not just the price?
- Did I know the minimum profitable price for the project, and refuse to bid below it?
- Did I establish walk-away criteria in advance, and decline or no-bid the project if they were met?
- Did I confirm the plans are complete, the fixtures are specified, and the existing conditions are documented before bidding, and request clarification on any gap?
- Did I document the scope, exclusions, assumptions, and change-order provision so the bid is defensible and the contract is clear?
- Did I treat a bid that required trimming below the profitable price as a signal to walk away rather than as a win?
