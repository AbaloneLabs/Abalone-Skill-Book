# Lift Station Basin and Pump Selection for Site Conditions

A sanitary lift station is the mechanical heart of any plumbing system that must move sewage up from below the gravity sewer, and its basin depth, pump selection, and wet-well volume together determine whether the station runs for decades or burns out its pumps in months. The judgment problem is that a lift station is a coupled hydraulic and mechanical system: the basin depth is set by the invert elevation and the pump's required shutoff head, the wet-well volume sets the pump cycle time (and thus motor life), the pump's flow and solids handling must match the load, and the control strategy (float or transducer) sets how reliably the level is sensed. An agent who sizes the basin by pit diameter, who selects the pump by horsepower, or who ignores the cycle-time math will create a station that short-cycles the pumps to death, overflows at peak flow, or strands a pump inaccessible beneath a finished floor. This skill covers the basin, pump, and control selection decisions that make a lift station reliable — and the role limits that place large-station design with a licensed engineer.

## Core Rules

### Set the Basin Depth by Invert Elevation and the Pump's Required Shutoff Head

The basin depth is not a guess; it is set by two hard constraints. First, the invert elevation of the incoming sewer (the elevation at which sewage enters the basin) sets the highest operating level — the basin must extend below that invert to provide the active volume (the volume between pump-on and pump-off) plus the pump submergence and a reserve below the inlet. Second, the pump's required shutoff head (the total dynamic head at zero flow, which is the static lift from the lowest pump-off level to the discharge point plus friction and residual) sets how deep the pump can be — the pump must be able to lift from its lowest operating level to the discharge. The basin depth must satisfy both: deep enough for the active volume and submergence below the invert, and shallow enough (or the pump powerful enough) that the pump can lift from the bottom. The trap is digging a basin to a convenient depth without calculating the shutoff head, leaving a pump that cannot lift from the bottom or a basin too shallow for the active volume. Calculate the invert, the active volume, the submergence, and the shutoff head, and set the depth to satisfy all four.

### Size the Wet-Well Volume for the Required Cycle Time and Starts-Per-Hour

Pump motor life is governed by the number of starts (each start imposes inrush current and heating), so the wet-well volume must be sized to keep the pump's cycle time and starts-per-hour within the motor's limits. The active volume (between pump-on and pump-off) divided by the pump flow gives the run time per cycle, and the active volume divided by the inflow rate gives the fill time; the cycle time is the sum, and the starts-per-hour is 60 divided by the cycle time in minutes. Industry guidance targets a minimum run time per cycle (commonly at least 5 minutes for submersible motors to cool and clear solids) and a maximum starts-per-hour (commonly 10 starts per hour for submersible pumps, fewer for larger motors). The trap is installing a basin with too small an active volume, so the pump short-cycles (turning on and off every minute or two), burning the motor windings and the controls; or too large an active volume, so the sewage sits and turns septic. Size the active volume so the pump runs at least the minimum cycle time and does not exceed the maximum starts-per-hour at the typical inflow rate.

### Select the Pump by Flow at Total Dynamic Head and Solids Handling, Not Horsepower

Pump capacity is the flow (gpm) the pump delivers at the total dynamic head (TDH), and TDH is the static lift (from the pump-off level to the discharge point) plus the friction loss in the discharge pipe and fittings plus any residual pressure at the discharge. The pump's published curve shows the flow at each head, and the selected pump must deliver the required peak flow (sized to the fixture load or the inflow rate, often 15 to 40 gpm for residential, far more for commercial) at the calculated TDH. Solids handling matters for sewage: the pump must pass the solids in the stream (commonly 2-inch spheres for a sewage ejector, with grinder pumps macerating solids into a slurry for small-diameter discharge). The trap is selecting the pump by horsepower ("a 1/2 hp ejector"), which says nothing about the flow at the actual head; the harm is a pump that runs but cannot keep up at peak flow, overflowing the basin. Calculate the TDH, identify the required flow and solids handling, and select a pump whose curve delivers that flow at that head.

### Choose Submersible or Dry-Pit by Access, Environment, and Redundancy Needs

The pump configuration — submersible (pump and motor in the wet well) or dry-pit (pumps in a separate dry vault beside the wet well) — is driven by access, environment, and station size. Submersible pumps are the default for residential and small commercial stations: they are simpler, cheaper, and the motor is cooled by submergence; they are lifted out on guide rails for service. Dry-pit pumps suit larger stations where the pumps must be accessible for frequent service without entering the wet well, where the environment is corrosive or abrasive, or where the station serves a critical facility that cannot tolerate downtime — the dry pit allows pump service without taking the station offline and without confined-space entry into the sewage. The trap is using a submersible pump in a large or critical station where service requires dewatering and confined-space entry, or a dry-pit in a small station where the added cost and complexity are unjustified. Match the configuration to the station size, the service frequency, the environment, and the criticality.

### Select Float or Transducer Control by Reliability and the Station's Criticality

The level control — how the basin senses pump-on, pump-off, and high-water alarm — is either mechanical float switches or a pressure/bubbler/submersible transducer reading the level continuously. Float switches are simple, cheap, and reliable for small stations, but they are mechanical (the contacts wear, the floats tangle, the cables fatigue) and they sense only discrete levels (on, off, alarm). Transducers (or bubbler systems) read the level continuously, enable variable-frequency drive and lead-lag alternation logic, and suit larger or more critical stations, but they are electronic (sensitive to lightning, moisture, and calibration drift) and require a control panel. The trap is using floats in a large station where continuous level reading and alternation logic are needed, or a transducer in a small station where simplicity matters; the harm is unreliable level sensing, short-cycling, or undetected high water. Match the control to the station's size and criticality, and for any critical station provide redundant level sensing (a transducer backed up by a high-water float).

## Common Traps

### Digging the Basin to a Convenient Depth Without Calculating Shutoff Head

The agent digs the basin to a round depth (4 feet, 6 feet) without calculating the pump's required shutoff head from the lowest pump-off level to the discharge. The trap mechanism is that the pump must be able to lift from the bottom of the basin, and a basin deeper than the pump's shutoff head leaves the pump unable to clear the lowest water, while a basin too shallow for the active volume leaves no reserve below the invert. The false signal is that "the basin is deep enough." The harm is a pump that cannot lift from the bottom or a basin that overflows at the invert. The defense is to calculate the invert, the active volume, the submergence, and the shutoff head, and set the depth to satisfy all four.

### Sizing the Wet Well Too Small, Causing Short-Cycling

The agent installs a small-diameter basin with a few gallons of active volume, and the pump turns on and off every minute or two as fixtures trickle in. The trap mechanism is that short-cycling imposes repeated inrush current and heating on the motor windings (burning them), wears the float or transducer contacts, and prevents the pump from running long enough to clear solids from the discharge. The false signal is that "the pump keeps up." The harm is premature pump and control failure and solids accumulation. The defense is to size the active volume so the pump runs at least the minimum cycle time (commonly 5 minutes) and does not exceed the maximum starts-per-hour (commonly 10) at the typical inflow rate.

### Selecting the Pump by Horsepower Instead of Flow at Total Dynamic Head

The agent installs a "1/2 hp ejector" because it is standard, without calculating the TDH. The trap mechanism is that pump flow falls as head rises, and a 1/2 hp pump may deliver 40 gpm at 10 feet but only 15 gpm at 20 feet; if the installation has 20 feet of head and the peak inflow is 25 gpm, the pump cannot keep up and the basin overflows. The false signal is that "the pump runs." The harm is a basin overflow under peak load. The defense is to calculate the TDH (static lift plus friction plus residual), identify the required flow, and select a pump whose curve delivers that flow at that head.

### Using a Submersible Pump in a Large or Critical Station Requiring Frequent Service

The agent installs submersible pumps in a large commercial or critical-facility station, reasoning that submersibles are standard. The trap mechanism is that submersible pump service requires lifting the pump on guide rails (dewatering the wet well and confined-space entry for the larger stations), which is costly and slow, and a critical station that needs frequent service suffers downtime and confined-space risk that a dry-pit configuration would avoid. The false signal is that "submersibles are simpler." The harm is costly service, downtime, and confined-space exposure. The defense is to match the configuration to the station size, service frequency, environment, and criticality, using dry-pit for large or critical stations.

### Using Floats Without Redundancy in a Critical Station

The agent controls a critical station with a single set of floats and no backup level sensing. The trap mechanism is that floats are mechanical and fail (tangled cables, worn contacts, fatigued tethers), and a single float failure in a critical station causes an undetected overflow or a short-cycling pump; the station has no redundant level input to catch the failure. The false signal is that "the floats work." The harm is an undetected sewage overflow into occupied space. The defense is to provide redundant level sensing (a transducer backed up by a high-water alarm float) in any critical station, and to test the high-water alarm on schedule.

## Self-Check

- Did I set the basin depth by calculating the incoming invert elevation, the active volume, the pump submergence, and the pump's required shutoff head — not by a convenient round depth?
- Does the wet-well active volume size the pump cycle time so the pump runs at least the minimum run time per cycle (commonly 5 minutes for submersibles) and does not exceed the maximum starts-per-hour (commonly 10) at the typical inflow rate?
- Did I calculate the total dynamic head (static lift from pump-off level plus discharge friction plus residual) and select a pump whose published curve delivers the required peak flow at that head, rather than selecting by horsepower?
- Does the pump's solids handling match the stream (commonly 2-inch spheres for a sewage ejector, or grinder maceration for small-diameter discharge)?
- Did I choose submersible or dry-pit configuration based on the station size, service frequency, environment, and criticality — using dry-pit for large or critical stations requiring frequent service?
- Did I select float or transducer control based on the station's size and criticality, and for any critical station did I provide redundant level sensing (a transducer backed up by a high-water alarm float)?
- Is the pump accessible for service (guide rails for submersibles, dry-pit access for dry-pit pumps), and is the basin sealed with a gasketed cover and vented to the plumbing vent or outdoor air?
- Did I confirm my licensing scope covers the station size (plumber for small stations, licensed engineer for large or engineered stations), and did I escalate engineered-station design?
- Are the invert elevation, basin depth, active volume, cycle-time calculation, TDH, pump curve reference, configuration, and control strategy documented for inspection and maintenance?
