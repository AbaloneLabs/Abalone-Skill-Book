# Transition Fittings and Dissimilar Metal Connections

Connecting one pipe material to another is a routine part of plumbing — a remodel that ties new PEX into old copper, a repair that joins PVC to cast iron, a water heater connection that joins copper to steel — and these transitions are a frequent source of failures because dissimilar metals corrode galvanically and dissimilar materials require specific transition fittings. The judgment problem is that a transition that "works" initially can corrode, leak, or fail prematurely because the materials are incompatible or the wrong fitting was used, and the failure is concealed and progressive. A plumber who joins dissimilar materials with whatever fitting is available, without considering galvanic corrosion and material compatibility, is installing a future leak. This skill covers the transition fitting and dissimilar material decisions that produce durable connections.

## Core Rules

### Use Dielectric Unions or Dielectric Nipples Between Copper and Steel

When copper (a noble metal) is joined directly to steel or galvanized steel (a less noble metal) in a water system, the contact creates a galvanic cell: the steel corrodes preferentially, weakening the joint and producing rust that restricts flow and stains fixtures. The prevention is a dielectric union (a fitting with an insulating gasket and washer that breaks the metal-to-metal contact) or a dielectric nipple (a steel nipple with an internal plastic or ceramic lining that isolates the water from the steel). Dielectric protection is required at every copper-to-steel connection in a water system, particularly at water heater connections (copper to steel tank nipples). The trap is joining copper directly to steel with a brass or copper fitting, which does not break the galvanic cell and allows corrosion. The disciplined rule is to use a dielectric union or dielectric nipple at every copper-to-steel transition, and to verify the dielectric barrier is intact (the insulating gasket and washer are present and undamaged).

### Use the Correct Transition Fitting for Each Material Combination

Each material combination has a correct transition fitting. Copper to PEX: a copper-to-PEX adapter (solder or press on the copper side, crimp or expansion on the PEX side). Copper to PVC or CPVC: a copper-to-plastic adapter (solder or compression on the copper side, solvent weld or threaded on the plastic side) or a threaded transition with the correct sealant. PVC to cast iron: a no-hub coupling (for hubless cast iron) or a donut gasket (for hub-and-spigot cast iron). PEX to PB (old polybutylene): a specific PEX-to-PB transition fitting, because the materials are not directly compatible. Copper to brass: a direct solder or threaded connection is acceptable (brass and copper are close on the galvanic series). The trap is improvising a transition with the wrong fitting — a copper-to-steel connection with a brass fitting (no dielectric), or a PVC-to-copper connection with a rubber coupling not rated for pressure. The disciplined rule is to use the specific transition fitting designed for the material combination, listed for the application, and to verify the fitting is rated for the pressure and temperature.

### Use Listed Flexible Couplings for DWV Transitions of Different Materials and Sizes

In DWV systems, transitions between materials (cast iron to PVC, clay to PVC) and sizes (4-inch to 3-inch) are made with flexible couplings (no-hub couplings, Fernco-style couplings) — rubber sleeves with stainless steel clamps that accommodate the material and size difference. These couplings must be listed for the application: a no-hub coupling is for hubless cast iron to hubless cast iron (or to PVC with a shielded coupling rated for the combination); a Fernco-style coupling is for transitions between different materials and sizes (cast iron to PVC, clay to PVC) and is rated for underground or aboveground as marked. The trap is using a coupling not rated for the application — a Fernco aboveground where a shielded coupling is required, or a coupling rated only for underground used aboveground. The disciplined rule is to use the listed coupling for the specific material and size transition, to verify the coupling is rated for the location (underground or aboveground), and to torque the clamps to the manufacturer specification.

### Address Galvanic Corrosion in All Dissimilar-Metal Contact, Not Just Copper-Steel

Galvanic corrosion occurs whenever two dissimilar metals are in contact in the presence of an electrolyte (water), and the less noble metal corrodes. The galvanic series, from noble (cathode) to less noble (anode), includes (roughly): graphite, platinum, gold, silver, titanium, stainless steel (passive), copper, brass, bronze, tin, lead, steel, galvanized steel, aluminum, zinc, magnesium. The risk is greatest when the metals are far apart on the series and the less noble metal is small relative to the noble (a small steel nipple connected to a large copper system corrodes rapidly). The trap is assuming only copper-steel contact is a galvanic risk, when in fact copper-aluminum, copper-galvanized, and stainless-steel contacts also corrode. The disciplined rule is to identify all dissimilar-metal contacts, to use dielectric isolation where the metals are far apart on the series, and to prefer compatible metals or listed transition fittings.

### Verify the Transition Is Rated for the Pressure, Temperature, and Location

Transition fittings have specific ratings for pressure, temperature, and location (concealed, exposed, underground, aboveground), and the fitting must be rated for the application. A copper-to-PEX adapter is rated for potable water pressure and temperature; a Fernco coupling is rated for DWV (low pressure) and for underground or aboveground as marked; a dielectric union is rated for potable water pressure and temperature. The trap is using a fitting rated for DWV in a pressure application, or a fitting rated only for aboveground in a concealed or underground location. The disciplined rule is to verify the transition fitting's pressure rating, temperature rating, and location approval against the application, and to select a fitting that meets or exceeds all three.

## Common Traps

### Joining Copper Directly to Steel Without a Dielectric Union

A plumber connects copper supply lines directly to a steel water heater nipple with a brass fitting or a copper flex line, without a dielectric union or dielectric nipple, and the steel nipple corrodes galvanically, producing rust and eventual failure. The trap is that the connection "works" initially but the galvanic cell is active. The mechanism is that copper and steel in contact form a galvanic cell, and the steel corrodes. The false signal is that "the connection is solid." The harm is corrosion, rust staining, and eventual failure. The defense is to use a dielectric union or dielectric nipple at every copper-to-steel transition.

### Improvising a Transition With the Wrong Fitting

A plumber connects PVC to copper with a rubber Fernco coupling rated only for DWV, in a pressure water application, and the coupling blows off under pressure. The trap is that the fitting "fits" the materials but is not rated for the application. The mechanism is that DWV couplings are not rated for pressure. The false signal is that "the fitting connects the materials." The harm is a pressure failure. The defense is to use the specific transition fitting rated for the application, listed for the pressure and temperature.

### Using a DWV Coupling in a Pressure Application

A plumber uses a Fernco-style coupling (rated for DWV only) to join two pressure water lines, and the coupling restricts or blows off under pressure. The trap is that the coupling looks like a pressure fitting but is rated only for low-pressure DWV. The mechanism is that DWV couplings are not pressure-rated. The false signal is that "the coupling fits." The harm is a pressure failure and water damage. The defense is to verify the coupling's pressure rating and to use only pressure-rated fittings in pressure applications.

### Using an Aboveground-Only Coupling Underground, or Vice Versa

A plumber uses a Fernco coupling rated only for aboveground in an underground burial, or a shielded no-hub coupling rated only for aboveground underground, and the coupling deteriorates in the soil. The trap is that the coupling "fits" but is not rated for the soil environment. The mechanism is that soil chemistry and groundwater attack the rubber or the clamps. The false signal is that "the coupling is installed." The harm is premature underground failure. The defense is to verify the coupling is rated for the location (underground or aboveground) and to use only underground-rated fittings underground.

### Ignoring Galvanic Corrosion Beyond Copper-Steel

A plumber addresses the copper-steel transition with a dielectric union but ignores a copper-aluminum or stainless-steel contact elsewhere in the system, and that contact corrodes. The trap is that galvanic corrosion applies to all dissimilar-metal contacts, not just copper-steel. The mechanism is the galvanic series. The false signal is that "the copper-steel is protected." The harm is corrosion at the unaddressed contact. The defense is to identify all dissimilar-metal contacts and to isolate or use compatible metals throughout the system.

## Self-Check

- At every copper-to-steel transition (particularly water heater connections), did I install a dielectric union or dielectric nipple with an intact insulating barrier?
- For each material combination, did I use the specific transition fitting designed for that combination (copper-to-PEX adapter, copper-to-PVC adapter, no-hub or Fernco for cast iron to PVC), listed for the application?
- For DWV transitions of different materials or sizes, did I use a listed flexible coupling (no-hub for hubless cast iron, Fernco for material/size transitions), rated for the location (underground or aboveground)?
- Did I verify each transition fitting's pressure rating, temperature rating, and location approval against the application, and does the fitting meet or exceed all three?
- Did I identify all dissimilar-metal contacts in the system (copper-steel, copper-galvanized, copper-aluminum, stainless-steel) and provide dielectric isolation or compatible metals where the metals are far apart on the galvanic series?
- For water heater connections, did I use dielectric nipples or a dielectric union between the copper supply and the steel tank nipples?
- Did I avoid using DWV-rated couplings in pressure applications, and pressure-rated fittings in DWV applications where a DWV fitting is appropriate?
- Did I torque flexible coupling clamps to the manufacturer specification, and did I verify the clamps are positioned correctly over the pipe stops?
- For concealed and underground transitions, did I verify the fitting is rated for concealed or underground installation per the code?
- Did I document the transition fittings used, their ratings, and their locations so the connections are verifiable and the materials are traceable?
