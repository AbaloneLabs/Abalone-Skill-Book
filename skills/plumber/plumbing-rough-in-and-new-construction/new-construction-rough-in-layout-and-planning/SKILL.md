# New Construction Rough-In Layout and Planning

Rough-in layout is the moment when a plumbing system is committed to the building, and once framing is closed the cost of correction multiplies tenfold. The judgment problem is that rough-in is not just placing pipes where the plan shows them; it is a three-dimensional coordination problem where drain slope, vent rise, fixture clearance, structural integrity, and the work of every other trade all compete for the same concealed cavities. A plumber who lays out by the fixture schedule alone, without accounting for floor joist direction, bearing wall locations, and the mechanical and electrical runs that share the chase, produces a system that fits on paper but collides in the wall. Worse, layout errors discovered at final inspection force walls open, delay the project, and erode the plumber's credibility with the builder. This skill covers the planning decisions that make rough-in correct the first time and inspection-ready.

## Core Rules

### Establish the Drain Path and Slope Before Any Other Layout

Every drain line has a non-negotiable geometry: it must slope continuously downhill from fixture to sewer at a minimum of 1/4 inch per foot for pipes up to 3 inches, and it must reach that grade without sagging, bellying, or dipping. This slope requirement is the master constraint of rough-in because it dictates where pipes can run and how deep they must sit in the floor system. Layout must therefore begin with the drain, not the fixture: trace the path from the building drain up through every branch, confirm the cumulative rise, and confirm the available depth in the floor joist bay or slab. If the geometry does not work — for example, a long bathroom group run that cannot achieve 1/4 inch per foot within the joist depth — the layout must be revised before any pipe is cut, which may require relocating fixtures, reorienting the bathroom group, or requesting an engineered joist modification. Never commit a fixture location until the drain path has been verified for continuous slope.

### Plan Wall and Floor Penetrations Around Structure, Not Against It

Drain and vent pipes routinely need to pass through studs, plates, and joists, and every penetration weakens the member. The disciplined approach is to plan penetrations in the structural context: locate holes in the middle third of a stud depth (not near the edge), keep notches out of the tension zone of joists, and never drill or notch a manufactured I-joist flange or the engineered LVL beam. Where a pipe run requires penetrations that exceed code boring limits (typically one-third of stud depth for a single hole, with restrictions on multiple holes), the layout must be revised to route around the member, or an engineered solution (a sistered stud, a squash block, a header) must be coordinated with the framer and, where required, the building official. A plumber who drills structure freely and lets the framer discover the damage at framing inspection creates rework for everyone.

### Verify Fixture Spacing and Clearances Against the Adopted Code and the Fixture Schedule

Fixture spacing is governed both by the manufacturer's rough-in dimensions (which define where the waste and supply connections must land) and by the plumbing and building code (which define minimum clearances between fixtures, from fixtures to walls, and for accessibility). Layout must reconcile both. A toilet roughed at 12 inches from the finished wall per the manufacturer may still violate code if the adjacent vanity leaves less than the required center-to-center or side clearance. The disciplined approach is to lay out every fixture from the finished wall lines (not the rough framing), confirm the manufacturer rough-in dimensions, and then confirm code clearances — including the 21-inch (IPC) or 24-inch (UPC) front clearance at a toilet, the minimum lavatory spacing, and any accessibility requirements if the building is within the accessibility scope. Never rough to the stud face; always rough to the finished wall, accounting for the wall finish thickness.

### Coordinate Rough-In With Other Trades Before and During Installation

Plumbing does not own the concealed cavities; it shares them with HVAC ducts and refrigerant lines, electrical cables and boxes, low-voltage runs, and fire sprinkler piping. The disciplined approach is to attend the pre-construction coordination meeting, walk the framing with the mechanical and electrical leads, and agree on chase assignments and crossing rules before rough-in begins. In practice this means identifying which bays are plumbing-dominant, which are shared, and where crossing conflicts will be resolved by priority (typically drain slope governs over duct and wire, but a main duct run may require rerouting the plumbing). Document the agreements and re-walk at key milestones, because framing changes and trade drift between layout and installation are common. A plumber who roughs in isolation and expects others to route around the pipes will lose conflicts at framing inspection and pay for the reroute.

### Sequence the Work to Match the Inspection Schedule and the Builder's Milestones

Rough-in is inspected in stages, and the layout must anticipate the sequence so that testable, inspectable work is complete at each milestone. The typical sequence is: underground (below-slab) drain and water service before slab pour; above-floor DWV and water supply rough-in before wall close; shower pan and tub installation before the wall behind it is closed; and a final pressure and inspection test before insulation and drywall. Layout must ensure that cleanouts, test tees, and inspection access points land in accessible locations at each stage, and that the test plugs and gauges can be installed without dismantling framing. The disciplined rule is to plan backward from each inspection: identify what must be testable and visible at each inspection, and ensure the layout supports it. Never close a wall or pour a slab before the corresponding inspection has passed.

### Leave Cleanouts, Access, and Test Points That Future Work Will Need

A layout that omits cleanouts at every required change of direction and the base of stacks, or that buries shutoff valves and test ports behind finished walls, is a future maintenance liability and often a code violation. The disciplined approach is to place cleanouts at every change of direction greater than 45 degrees, at the base of every stack, and at intervals not exceeding code maximums (typically 100 feet in the IPC for horizontal runs). Confirm each cleanout lands in an accessible, code-compliant location with the required clearance for rodding, not buried in a cabinet or behind a fixed appliance. Provide access panels for any concealed valve, PRV, filter, or equipment connection. A layout optimized only for first-cost installation will be expensive to own.

## Common Traps

### Laying Out to the Rough Framing Instead of the Finished Wall

A plumber measures the toilet rough-in from the stud face, forgetting that the finished wall will add half-inch drywall plus tile or wainscot, and the toilet ends up an inch off the finished wall or the tank hits a tile bump-out. The trap is that the stud face is the visible reference during framing, while the finished wall is months away and easy to forget. The mechanism is that finish thickness accumulates — drywall, mortar bed, tile — and a 12-inch rough measured to the stud becomes a 10.5-inch rough to the finished tile, violating the fixture's clearance and the code. The false signal is that the pipe is "where the plan shows it." The harm is a toilet that does not fit, a vanity that cannot be pushed to the wall, or a shower valve trim that will not seat, all requiring expensive rework after finishes are installed. The defense is to always rough to the finished wall plane, calculating finish thickness from the finish schedule, and to mark the finished wall line on the framing for every fixture.

### Committing a Fixture Location Before Verifying the Drain Slope

A plumber sets the toilet flange and lavatory stubs to the architect's bathroom layout, then discovers the drain run to the stack cannot achieve 1/4 inch per foot within the available joist depth, and the only fix is to relocate the bathroom group or reframe the floor. The trap is that the plan looks dimensionally correct in two dimensions but does not account for the third dimension — the slope the drain requires over its run. The mechanism is that slope consumes floor depth linearly with run length, and a long run in a shallow joist bay simply cannot fit. The false signal is that the layout "matches the drawings." The harm is a drain installed flat or back-pitched (which will clog chronically and fail inspection) or a costly reframing change order after rough-in has started. The defense is to verify drain slope geometry for every fixture group before committing any pipe location, and to raise the conflict with the designer early if the geometry does not work.

### Drilling Structure Without Regard to Boring and Notching Limits

A plumber runs a 3-inch vent stack through three studs in a row, boring a 2-inch hole in each, and at framing inspection the building official flags the wall as structurally compromised because the holes exceed the boring limit and are not in the permitted zone. The trap is that drilling studs feels routine and the limits are easy to ignore under schedule pressure, but the code limits exist to preserve wall strength against wind and gravity loads. The mechanism is that each hole removes cross-section, holes near the edge concentrate stress, and multiple holes in adjacent studs create a weak link in the wall. The false signal is that the pipe fits and the wall is still standing. The harm is a failed framing inspection, a requirement to sister studs or reframe, and a dispute over who pays. The defense is to know the boring and notching limits for the adopted code, to keep penetrations in the middle third of studs, to avoid notching the tension edge of joists, and to coordinate any unavoidable structural impact with the framer and building official before drilling.

### Routing Pipes Through Another Trade's Dominant Chase Without Coordination

A plumber runs a 3-inch drain through the chase assigned to the HVAC return duct, assuming the duct can simply route around, and at the coordination walk the mechanical contractor refuses to redesign the duct and demands the plumbing move. The trap is that chase assignments are not self-evident and a plumber working in isolation will naturally use the easiest path, which is often the path another trade has already claimed. The mechanism is that concealed cavities are finite and shared, and uncoordinated routing guarantees collision. The false signal is that the pipe fits in the bay. The harm is rework, schedule delay, and a conflict that escalates to the general contractor for resolution, often at the plumber's expense. The defense is to participate in pre-construction coordination, agree on chase ownership and crossing rules, and re-walk the framing with the affected trades before and during rough-in.

### Omitting Cleanouts to Save Pipe and Fittings

A plumber omits the cleanout at the base of a stack and at a long horizontal run to save material and labor, and years later a blockage in the line cannot be rodded without opening the finished wall. The trap is that cleanouts add cost now and provide no visible benefit at installation, so they are tempting to skip under bid pressure. The mechanism is that drains clog, and without cleanouts the only access is through the roof vent or by cutting the pipe behind finished surfaces. The false signal is that the installation passes inspection in jurisdictions with weak cleanout enforcement. The harm is a maintenance nightmare for the owner and, where enforced, a failed inspection requiring finished surfaces to be opened. The defense is to install cleanouts at every code-required location and at every practical access point, and to ensure each is accessible with the required rodding clearance.

### Closing Walls or Pouring Slab Before the Inspection Has Passed

Under schedule pressure, a plumber allows the slab to be poured over the underground DWV before the inspector has signed off, or allows drywall to close the walls before the rough pressure and DWV test, and a leak or code deficiency is discovered only after it is concealed. The trap is that the schedule rewards speed and punishes the plumber who insists on inspection, but concealing uninspected work transfers all risk to the plumber. The mechanism is that once work is concealed it cannot be visually inspected, and any defect becomes a demolition problem. The false signal is that the work "tested fine" on the plumber's own gauge. The harm is a failed final inspection, mandatory wall or slab opening, and a credibility loss with the inspector and builder. The defense is to never permit concealment of any plumbing rough-in before the corresponding inspection has passed, and to make this a written condition with the general contractor.

## Self-Check

- Did I establish the drain path and verify continuous minimum slope (1/4 inch per foot for pipes up to 3 inches) for every fixture group before committing any pipe location?
- Did I lay out every fixture to the finished wall plane, accounting for the full finish thickness from the finish schedule, rather than to the rough stud face?
- Did I confirm that every wall and floor penetration complies with the boring and notching limits of the adopted code, and coordinate any unavoidable structural impact with the framer and building official?
- Did I verify fixture spacing and clearances against both the manufacturer rough-in dimensions and the code minimums, including front and side clearances and any accessibility requirements?
- Did I participate in pre-construction coordination with HVAC, electrical, and sprinkler trades, agree on chase ownership and crossing rules, and re-walk the framing during rough-in?
- Did I sequence the work to match the inspection milestones (underground, above-floor DWV and supply, shower/tub, final pressure test) so that each stage is testable and inspectable before concealment?
- Did I install cleanouts at every code-required change of direction and stack base, with each in an accessible location meeting rodding clearance, and provide access panels for all concealed valves and equipment?
- Did I confirm that no wall, floor, or slab was closed or poured before the corresponding plumbing inspection passed, and is this condition documented with the general contractor?
- Did I mark the finished wall lines and fixture centerlines on the framing so that other trades and the finish work can verify alignment?
- Did I document the verified drain slopes, fixture rough-in dimensions, and any coordination agreements so the design intent and as-built layout are traceable?
