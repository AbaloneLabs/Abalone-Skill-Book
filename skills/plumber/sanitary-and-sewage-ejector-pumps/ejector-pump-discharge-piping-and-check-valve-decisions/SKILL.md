# Ejector Pump Discharge Piping and Check Valve Decisions

The discharge piping on a sewage ejector system is where preventable failures — backflow, pump deadhead, siphoning, and unservicable arrangements — are designed in or out, and the decisions are easy to get wrong because the pipe "works" on the first cycle. The judgment problem is that the discharge pipe must never be smaller than the pump outlet (a reduction deadheads the pump and erodes the impeller), the check valve must pass solids (a wrong type jams on the first rag), and the service arrangement (union, gate valve, check valve order) determines whether the pump can be pulled without flooding. Agents also omit the backwater valve where a gravity path could let the sewer back up into the basin, set the discharge below the flood rim so a stoppage floods back, and ignore anti-siphon so the pump loses its seal and short-cycles. This skill covers discharge-pipe sizing, check-valve type and selection, service-valve arrangement, backwater valves, discharge elevation, and anti-siphon protection.

## Core Rules

### Size the Discharge Pipe Never Smaller Than the Pump Outlet

The discharge pipe must be at least as large as the pump outlet, and is often upsized one nominal size for long runs to reduce friction. A reduction at the pump outlet restricts flow, raises the pump's operating head (moving the duty point left on the curve toward shutoff), increases velocity wear at the reduction, and can deadhead the pump if combined with a check-valve restriction. The pipe is sized for the design GPM at an acceptable friction loss (commonly under 2 to 3 psi per 100 feet), with the static lift (vertical rise to the gravity connection) added to get total dynamic head. The trap is reducing to match an existing smaller line. The disciplined rule is to keep the discharge at or above the pump outlet size, compute the TDH including friction, and upsize for long runs.

### Choose the Check Valve Type for Solids Passage and Service

The check valve on a sewage discharge must pass solids — a standard swing check (with a hinged flap) is the common choice for horizontal sewage lines because the full-port opening passes a 2-inch sphere; a spring-loaded check may be required for vertical runs (where a swing check would not seat) but must be a solids-passing spring type, not a small-port spring check that jams on the first rag. The check valve is installed in the correct orientation (swing checks horizontal with the hinge pivot up per the manufacturer; spring checks in any orientation marked on the body), with the flow arrow toward the sewer. The trap is using a small-port or non-solids check that jams. The disciplined rule is to use a full-port solids-passing check (swing for horizontal, spring for vertical), oriented per the manufacturer, and to verify it passes the rated sphere.

### Arrange the Union, Gate Valve, and Check Valve for Service

The service arrangement allows the pump to be pulled or the check valve to be serviced without flooding the basin or the space. The standard order, from the pump upward, is: pump discharge, a union (or flanged connection) to disconnect the pump, a gate or full-port ball valve to isolate the discharge line, and the check valve to prevent backflow when the valve is open for service. The gate valve lets the line be closed so the check valve or pump can be removed without the discharge column draining back into the basin; the union lets the pump be disconnected without cutting pipe. The trap is omitting the gate valve or the union, or ordering them so the check valve cannot be serviced. The disciplined rule is to install union, isolation valve, and check valve in an order that allows pump removal and check-valve service without back-flooding.

### Install a Backwater Valve Where a Gravity Exit Could Back Up

Where the discharge connects to a gravity sewer that could back up (a combined sewer in a heavy storm, a sewer prone to stoppage), a backwater valve on the discharge line (or on the building drain it serves) prevents sewage from flowing back through the discharge into the basin and out the cover. The backwater valve is a one-way valve, distinct from the check valve (which protects against the discharge column draining back), and is required by code in some jurisdictions where the fixtures are below the next upstream manhole. The backwater valve must be accessible for inspection and cleaning. The trap is relying on the check valve alone where a sewer-backup path exists. The disciplined rule is to evaluate the sewer-backup risk, install a backwater valve where a gravity backflow path exists, and keep it accessible.

### Set Discharge Elevation Above the Flood Rim and Provide Anti-Siphon

The discharge must connect to the gravity sewer above the flood rim of the lowest fixture it serves (or per local code), so a stoppage in the gravity system cannot back up through the discharge into the basin. The discharge loop should rise to above the flood rim before dropping to the connection, and an anti-siphon device (or a loop that prevents siphon) prevents the discharge column from siphoning the basin down past the pump-off level (which breaks the pump's seal and short-cycles the next start) or from pulling sewer gas back through the line. The trap is dropping the discharge below the flood rim or omitting anti-siphon. The disciplined rule is to connect above the flood rim, provide a high loop or anti-siphon device, and confirm the basin holds its level with the pump off.

## Common Traps

### Reducing the Discharge Pipe Below the Pump Outlet Size

The plumber reduces the discharge to tie into an existing smaller line. The trap is that the restriction raises the pump head and erodes the reduction. The mechanism is that a smaller pipe increases velocity and friction and moves the duty point toward shutoff. The false signal is that "it fits the existing line." The harm is deadhead, impeller wear, and inadequate flow. The defense is to keep the discharge at or above the pump outlet size, compute the TDH, and upsize for long runs.

### Using a Small-Port or Non-Solids Check Valve That Jams

The plumber installs a standard small-port spring check or a non-solids check on a sewage discharge. The trap is that the first rag or solids plug jams the valve. The mechanism is that a non-full-port check obstructs solids passage. The false signal is that "a check valve is a check valve." The harm is a jammed-open valve (backflow) or jammed-closed valve (deadhead) and pump failure. The defense is to use a full-port solids-passing check (swing for horizontal, spring for vertical), oriented per the manufacturer.

### Omitting the Gate Valve or Union, Making the Pump Unservicable

The plumber omits the isolation valve or the union to save fittings. The trap is that the pump or check valve cannot be serviced without flooding. The mechanism is that the discharge column drains back when the line is opened. The false signal is that "it runs, so it's fine." The harm is an unservicable system and a flood on the first repair attempt. The defense is to install a union, an isolation valve, and the check valve in an order that allows pump removal and check-valve service without back-flooding.

### Relying on the Check Valve Where a Sewer-Backup Path Exists

The plumber installs only the check valve on a discharge tied to a sewer that can back up in a storm or stoppage. The trap is that the check valve is not rated for sewer-backup protection. The mechanism is that a combined or stoppage-prone sewer can pressurize back through the discharge. The false signal is that "the check valve stops backflow." The harm is sewage backing into the basin and out the cover. The defense is to evaluate the sewer-backup risk, install a backwater valve where a gravity backflow path exists, and keep it accessible.

### Dropping the Discharge Below the Flood Rim or Omitting Anti-Siphon

The plumber connects the discharge below the flood rim or runs a straight drop with no anti-siphon loop. The trap is that a stoppage floods back or the column siphons the basin. The mechanism is that a below-rim connection admits backflow and a siphon breaks the pump seal. The false signal is that "the discharge is connected." The harm is back-flooding or short-cycling from a siphoned basin. The defense is to connect above the flood rim, provide a high loop or anti-siphon device, and confirm the basin holds its level with the pump off.

## Self-Check

- Is the discharge pipe at least as large as the pump outlet, upsized for long runs, and sized for the design GPM at an acceptable friction loss (under 2 to 3 psi per 100 feet)?
- Is the check valve a full-port solids-passing type (swing for horizontal, spring for vertical), oriented per the manufacturer with the flow arrow toward the sewer?
- Is the service arrangement — union, isolation (gate or full-port ball) valve, and check valve — ordered so the pump can be pulled and the check valve serviced without back-flooding?
- Did I evaluate the sewer-backup risk and install an accessible backwater valve where a gravity backflow path exists (combined sewer, stoppage-prone line, below the upstream manhole)?
- Does the discharge connect to the gravity sewer above the flood rim of the lowest fixture served, so a stoppage cannot back up through the discharge into the basin?
- Did I provide a high loop or anti-siphon device so the discharge column cannot siphon the basin below the pump-off level?
- With the pump off, does the basin hold its level (no siphon drain-down), confirming the anti-siphon and check-valve seal?
- Are the discharge piping and valves supported independently so the pump and basin do not carry pipe weight?
- Did I confirm the total dynamic head (static lift plus friction) is within the pump's curve at the design GPM, so the pump is not deadheaded?
- Did I document the discharge size, check-valve type and orientation, valve arrangement, backwater valve, discharge elevation, and anti-siphon provision in the installation record?
