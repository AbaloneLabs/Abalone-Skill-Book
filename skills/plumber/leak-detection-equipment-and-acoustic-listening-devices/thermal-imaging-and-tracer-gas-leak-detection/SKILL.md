# Thermal Imaging and Tracer Gas Leak Detection

When a leak is small, silent, buried in a wall or slab, or on a line too deep or too quiet for acoustic detection, the tools that find it are thermal imaging and tracer gas, and each has a narrow band where it works and a large band where it misleads. The judgment problem is that a thermal camera shows surface temperature, not water, so a warm patch can be a hot pipe, a heating cable, a sunlit wall, or a slow hot leak, and only a disciplined reading distinguishes them; tracer gas (a helium or hydrogen-nitrogen mix) escapes through the leak and is detected at the surface, but it is defeated by deep burial, impermeable surfaces, and background gas, and a single reading can be a false positive from a permeable joint or a trapped pocket. Agents miss leaks by trusting one method in isolation, by reading a thermal image as proof of water, or by declaring a tracer-gas peak without ruling out background, and the result is a dry excavation or a leak that continues to damage the structure. This skill covers when each method applies, how to read thermal signatures, tracer-gas injection and detection discipline, background and sensitivity limits, and the combination strategy that confirms a leak before any wall or slab is opened.

## Core Rules

### Match the Method to the Leak Type: Thermal for Hot and Radiant, Tracer Gas for Small and Silent

Thermal imaging detects temperature differences, so it excels at hot-water leaks (a failed tube in a wall feeding a hot tap), radiant-floor heating leaks (a loop leak under a finished floor), and condensate or moisture that changes surface temperature, and it is useless on cold lines at ambient temperature where the leak does not change the surface. Tracer gas detection excels where the leak is small, silent, cold, or deep enough that acoustics and thermal fail: it pressurizes the line with a gas that escapes through the leak and is detected at the surface, and it works on any line that can be isolated and pressurized. The selection rule is to use thermal first when the leak is hot or the system is radiant (because it is fast and non-invasive), and to use tracer gas when the leak is cold, small, silent, or when thermal is inconclusive. The trap is using thermal on a cold line (no signature) or tracer gas on a hot radiant leak (thermal would have found it faster and cheaper), so the method must match the leak physics.

### Read Thermal Signatures as Temperature, Not as Proof of Water

A thermal camera displays surface temperature as color, and a warm patch on a wall or floor is evidence of warmth, not of water. The disciplined reading distinguishes the patterns: a hot-water leak typically shows a diffuse, irregular warm area that spreads from a source and may show drip patterns or a warm streak following a stud or pipe route; an intact hot pipe shows a clean linear warm trace along the pipe route; a radiant loop shows a regular serpentine warm pattern; a sunlit or HVAC-affected wall shows a broad, even warm zone unrelated to pipe routes. The discipline is to interpret the shape, the spread, and the relationship to known pipe routes, to confirm with a second indicator (a moisture meter on the surface, or a cold reading after the hot water has been off long enough for a real leak to cool), and never to open a wall on a thermal image alone. A warm patch that does not cool when the water is shut off is not a leak; it is a pipe, a cable, or an environmental effect.

### Inject Tracer Gas Correctly and Detect With a Controlled Sweep

Tracer gas leak detection works by pressurizing the suspect line with a gas (commonly a 5 percent hydrogen in 95 percent nitrogen mix, or helium) that is small-molecule, mobile, and detectable by a sensitive sensor, then sweeping the surface above the line with the detector to find where the gas emerges. The injection discipline is to isolate the line, drain or vent it as appropriate, pressurize with the tracer gas to a modest pressure (commonly 15 to 50 psi for residential lines, higher only where the system rating permits), allow time for the gas to travel to and through the leak and percolate to the surface, then sweep the detector slowly along the line at the surface. The detector reads gas concentration, and a leak produces a localized peak that intensifies as the detector approaches and falls off as it moves away. The discipline is to sweep methodically, to mark the peak, and to re-sweep from a perpendicular approach to confirm, because a true gas leak peak is consistent in location while a surface pocket or permeable joint is not.

### Control for Background, Surface Permeability, and Depth Before Trusting a Reading

Tracer gas readings are confounded by three factors that produce false positives and false negatives, and each must be controlled. Background gas (helium or hydrogen present in the environment, or escaping from a neighboring line or a previous test) produces a reading with no leak; the discipline is to take a baseline reading before injection and to ventilate the area. Surface permeability matters: gas emerges readily through soil, gravel, and permeable tile, but is blocked by sealed concrete, vinyl, or standing water, so a leak under an impermeable surface may produce no reading at the surface while accumulating at the slab edge; the discipline is to drill small detection holes through impermeable surfaces or to test at the slab edge and cracks where gas can escape. Depth defeats tracer gas as it defeats acoustics: a deep leak may take hours to percolate, or may never reach the surface in detectable concentration. The discipline is to allow sufficient time, to test at cracks and edges, and to treat a negative reading under impermeable cover as inconclusive rather than as proof of no leak.

### Combine Methods to Confirm Before Opening Any Wall or Slab

No single non-invasive method is certain, and the standard before opening a finished wall or slab is confirmation by a second method. A thermal candidate should be confirmed by a moisture meter, a tracer-gas test, or exposure of a small inspection area; a tracer-gas peak should be confirmed by correlation, a thermal check (if any hot component), or a small exploratory opening; an acoustic candidate should be confirmed by correlation. The combination strategy is to use the fastest appropriate method to find a candidate, then a second independent method to confirm, and only then to open the structure. The discipline is to treat any single-method location as a candidate, not a confirmation, and to document both the candidate and the confirmation before cutting. This is what separates a precise, low-damage repair from a series of exploratory holes.

## Common Traps

### Reading a Warm Patch as a Leak When It Is an Intact Hot Pipe or an Environmental Effect

A thermal scan shows a warm streak in a wall, and the operator opens the wall expecting a leak, but the streak is an intact hot supply line or a sunlit warm zone. The trap is that temperature is read as water. The mechanism is that an intact hot pipe and a hot leak both read warm. The false signal is that "there is a warm patch." The harm is an unnecessary wall opening. The defense is to interpret the shape and spread against the known pipe route, to shut off the hot water and re-scan (a real leak cools slowly and shows a spreading plume; a pipe cools quickly and uniformly), and to confirm with a moisture meter before opening.

### Using Thermal on a Cold Line and Declaring No Leak

A cold supply line under a slab is leaking, and the operator scans with a thermal camera, sees no signature, and declares no leak, because cold leaks at ambient do not change surface temperature. The trap is that thermal is the wrong tool for a cold line. The mechanism is that thermal requires a temperature difference, and a cold leak at ambient produces none. The false signal is that "no thermal signature means no leak." The harm is a missed leak and continued damage. The defense is to recognize thermal's limit and to use tracer gas or correlation for cold, silent leaks.

### Trusting a Tracer-Gas Peak Without a Baseline or Perpendicular Confirmation

The operator injects tracer gas, sweeps, marks the first peak, and excavates, but the peak is background gas or a permeable joint, and the dig is dry. The trap is that a single peak is treated as the leak. The mechanism is that background and permeable pockets produce localized gas. The false signal is that "the detector peaked here." The harm is a dry excavation. The defense is to take a baseline before injection, ventilate, and re-sweep from a perpendicular direction, accepting only a peak that is consistent in location across approaches.

### Declaring No Leak Because Gas Did Not Surface Through an Impermeable Slab

A leak under a sealed concrete slab produces no tracer-gas reading at the surface because the slab blocks the gas, and the operator declares no leak, while the gas accumulates beneath. The trap is that an impermeable surface produces a false negative. The mechanism is that gas cannot penetrate sealed concrete, vinyl, or standing water. The false signal is that "no surface reading means no leak." The harm is a missed leak under the slab. The defense is to drill small detection holes through the slab, to test at cracks and slab edges where gas can escape, and to treat a negative reading under impermeable cover as inconclusive.

### Excavating on a Single Method Without Confirmation

The operator finds a candidate by thermal alone, or by tracer gas alone, and opens the wall or slab without a second method, and the candidate is wrong, producing an unnecessary repair and continued leak. The trap is that a single method is treated as confirmation. The mechanism is that each method has confounders (thermal reads temperature; gas has background and surface limits). The false signal is that "a location was found." The harm is a dry opening and wasted cost. The defense is to confirm every candidate with a second independent method before opening any finished surface.

## Self-Check

- Did I select the method to match the leak physics, using thermal for hot or radiant leaks and tracer gas for cold, small, or silent leaks, rather than applying one method to every case?
- When reading a thermal image, did I interpret the shape, spread, and relationship to known pipe routes, and confirm with a moisture meter or a cool-down re-scan before treating a warm patch as a leak?
- Did I recognize that thermal imaging is ineffective on cold lines at ambient temperature and switch to tracer gas or correlation rather than declaring no leak?
- For tracer-gas detection, did I isolate and pressurize the line correctly, allow sufficient time for the gas to reach and percolate through the leak, and sweep the detector methodically along the line?
- Did I take a baseline gas reading before injection, ventilate the area, and re-sweep from a perpendicular direction to confirm that a peak is consistent in location?
- Did I account for surface permeability, drilling small detection holes through impermeable slabs and testing at cracks and slab edges where gas can escape, rather than declaring no leak under sealed cover?
- Did I allow for depth, recognizing that deep leaks may take hours to percolate or may not reach the surface in detectable concentration, and treat a deep negative reading as inconclusive?
- Did I confirm the candidate from one method with a second independent method (thermal plus moisture meter, tracer gas plus correlation, acoustic plus correlation) before opening any finished wall or slab?
- Did I document both the candidate location and the confirmation method, so the decision to open the structure is defensible?
- Did I avoid false confidence by treating every single-method reading as a candidate rather than a confirmed leak?
