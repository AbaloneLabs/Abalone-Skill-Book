# Gas Appliance Combustion Analysis and CO Diagnosis

Combustion analysis is the measurement of the flue gas a fuel-burning appliance produces, and it is the only objective way to confirm that an appliance is burning cleanly, venting safely, and operating efficiently rather than merely firing. The judgment problem is that an appliance can fire, produce heat, and appear to operate correctly while producing elevated carbon monoxide (CO), sooting internally, and wasting fuel, because the symptoms of bad combustion develop slowly and are invisible to the occupant until CO has accumulated or the heat exchanger has failed. A plumber who lights an appliance, confirms the burner fires, and leaves without measuring the flue gas is guessing at safety and efficiency, and the guess will eventually be wrong. This skill covers the measurement, interpretation, and adjustment decisions that confirm a gas appliance is burning cleanly and venting safely.

## Core Rules

### Measure Flue Gas With a Calibrated Analyzer, Never by Flame Appearance

Combustion quality is measured by sampling the flue gas (before any dilution air, at the appliance flue collar or heat exchanger outlet for Category I, or at the vent outlet for sealed-combustion units) with a calibrated digital combustion analyzer that reports oxygen (O2), carbon dioxide (CO2), carbon monoxide (CO), flue temperature, and combustion efficiency. The analyzer must be calibrated on its schedule (typically annually, with sensor replacement as needed), zeroed in fresh air before each use, and allowed to stabilize in the flue stream before the reading is recorded. The historical method of judging combustion by flame appearance (a hard blue flame, no yellow tipping) is a rough indicator only; a flame that looks acceptable can still produce elevated CO under poor combustion-air or gas-pressure conditions. The trap is adjusting an appliance by eye, declaring it "burning clean," and leaving, when the analyzer would show high CO or low efficiency. The disciplined rule is to measure the flue gas with a calibrated analyzer at every appliance service, and to record the readings.

### Interpret O2, CO2, CO, and Stack Temperature Together, Not in Isolation

The four key flue gas measurements form a coupled picture of combustion quality. Oxygen (O2) is the excess air indicator: too much O2 (above roughly 8 to 10 percent for a typical atmospheric appliance) means too much excess air, which cools the flame and lowers efficiency; too little O2 (below roughly 3 to 4 percent) means insufficient air, which produces CO and soot. Carbon dioxide (CO2) is the inverse of O2 and rises as excess air falls; a typical target is 6 to 9 percent CO2 for atmospheric appliances, higher for power-burner and condensing units. Carbon monoxide (CO) is the key safety reading: undiluted CO should be below 100 to 400 ppm (the threshold varies by standard and appliance), and any reading above the threshold indicates incomplete combustion that must be corrected. Stack temperature (flue temperature) indicates heat extraction: a high stack temperature means heat is being lost up the vent (low efficiency); a stack temperature below the dew point in a non-condensing appliance causes condensation and corrosion. The trap is reading one number (often CO) and ignoring the others, which misses the cause: high CO with low O2 is insufficient air; high CO with high O2 is a flame impingement or burner problem; high stack temperature with low CO2 is poor heat transfer. The disciplined rule is to read all four together and to diagnose the cause from the pattern.

### Diagnose the Cause of High CO Before Adjusting the Appliance

Elevated CO is a symptom with several causes, and adjusting the appliance (turning the gas pressure down, closing the air shutter) without diagnosing the cause can move the CO but not fix the problem, or can create a new problem. The common causes are: insufficient combustion air (the appliance is starved, often because the room is tight or the air openings are blocked); incorrect gas pressure (the manifold pressure is out of spec, over-firing or under-firing the burners); dirty or misaligned burners (the flame pattern is disrupted, causing impingement and incomplete combustion); a cracked or sooted heat exchanger (the heat transfer surface is compromised, the flame is cooled, and CO rises); or a blocked or oversized vent (the flue gases are not leaving, the combustion air is displaced, and the flame burns dirty). The diagnostic sequence is to verify combustion air (by the code method and by observation), measure the manifold gas pressure against the nameplate, inspect the burners and heat exchanger, and check draft and venting, before adjusting anything. The trap is turning down the gas pressure to reduce CO when the cause is a blocked vent, which lowers the CO but leaves the vent problem and the appliance under-fired. The disciplined rule is to diagnose the cause of high CO, correct the cause, and re-measure to confirm the CO dropped.

### Adjust Combustion by the Manufacturer's Procedure, in the Correct Order

When combustion must be adjusted (after a cause is corrected, or as part of setup), the adjustment follows the manufacturer's procedure, typically: set the manifold gas pressure to the nameplate value with a manometer, adjust the air shutter or blower to achieve the target O2/CO2, and verify the CO is below threshold and the stack temperature is in range. The order matters: setting gas pressure first establishes the firing rate, then air adjustment tunes the excess air, then CO and stack temperature confirm the result. The trap is adjusting the air shutter to chase a CO reading without first setting the gas pressure, which produces an unstable tune that drifts. The disciplined rule is to follow the manufacturer's sequence, to set gas pressure first, and to re-measure all four readings after any adjustment.

### Verify Draft and Spillage Under the Same Conditions as the Combustion Test

Combustion analysis and draft verification are coupled: a combustion reading is only valid if the appliance is venting correctly during the test, and an appliance that spills flue gas during the test is producing CO in the occupied space regardless of the flue gas reading. For Category I appliances, verify draft at the draft hood with a smoke or flame test and confirm no spillage after warmup; for induced-draft appliances, confirm the pressure switch stays closed. The trap is recording a clean combustion reading while the appliance is spilling, which documents a flue gas composition that is not actually reaching the vent. The disciplined rule is to verify draft and spillage under the same operating conditions as the combustion test, and to record both.

## Common Traps

### Judging Combustion by Flame Appearance and Skipping the Analyzer

A plumber adjusts an appliance until the flame "looks good" (hard blue, no yellow) and declares it burning clean, without sampling the flue gas. The trap is that flame appearance is a rough indicator that misses high CO under conditions the eye cannot detect: a slightly rich burn with insufficient air can look acceptable while producing CO above the safe threshold, and a sooting heat exchanger can produce a clean-looking flame while the flue gas is laden with CO. The mechanism is that the eye integrates over the visible flame and cannot measure the products of combustion. The false signal is the acceptable flame. The harm is elevated CO that goes undetected until an occupant is symptomatic or a CO alarm sounds. The defense is to measure the flue gas with a calibrated analyzer at every service and to record the readings, never relying on flame appearance alone.

### Reading CO in Isolation and Ignoring O2, CO2, and Stack Temperature

A plumber reads a CO of 350 ppm, declares the appliance "producing CO," and turns down the gas pressure to reduce it, without reading the O2 or stack temperature. The CO drops to 150 ppm, but the O2 is now 2 percent (the appliance is starved) and the stack temperature has risen (efficiency has fallen), and the underlying cause (a blocked combustion air opening) is unaddressed, so the CO will rise again. The trap is that a single reading does not diagnose the cause, and adjusting to chase one number moves the symptom without fixing the mechanism. The mechanism is that the four readings are coupled and the pattern reveals the cause. The false signal is the falling CO. The harm is a temporarily improved reading that masks the real problem and degrades efficiency. The defense is to read all four measurements together, diagnose the cause from the pattern, correct the cause, and re-measure all four.

### Turning Down Gas Pressure to Reduce CO When the Cause Is a Blocked Vent

A plumber finds high CO on a furnace and reduces the manifold gas pressure to lower the firing rate, which lowers the CO reading. The actual cause is a blocked or undersized vent that is not evacuating the flue gas, displacing the combustion air and causing the dirty burn. The trap is that the CO reading improved, so the adjustment "worked," but the vent blockage remains, the appliance is now under-fired (producing less heat and running longer), and the CO will rise again under different conditions. The mechanism is that gas pressure reduction masks the symptom without addressing the vent failure. The false signal is the lower CO reading. The harm is an unresolved vent problem that will recur, plus an under-fired appliance. The defense is to diagnose the cause of high CO (verify combustion air, check draft and venting, inspect burners and heat exchanger) before adjusting, and to correct the cause rather than masking the symptom.

### Recording a Clean Combustion Reading While the Appliance Is Spilling

A plumber samples the flue gas at the draft hood of a Category I water heater, records a clean reading, and leaves, without verifying that the appliance is drafting and not spilling into the room. The trap is that the sampled gas is diluted by room air drawn into the draft hood, and if the appliance is spilling, the flue gas is entering the room regardless of the composition reading. The mechanism is that the sample point and the draft behavior are coupled, and a spillage condition invalidates the reading as a safety confirmation. The false signal is the clean reading. The harm is CO in the occupied space while the record says the appliance is clean. The defense is to verify draft and spillage under the same conditions as the combustion test, to sample at the correct point (before dilution air for undiluted readings), and to record both the combustion readings and the draft verification.

### Failing to Recalibrate or Zero the Analyzer Before Use

A plumber uses a combustion analyzer that is past its calibration date or was not zeroed in fresh air before the test, and records readings that do not reflect the appliance's actual combustion. The trap is that an out-of-calibration analyzer drifts, and the readings may be high or low in any direction, producing a false pass or a false fail. The mechanism is that the electrochemical sensors degrade over time and the zero drifts with temperature and barometric pressure. The false signal is the reading on the display. The harm is a safety decision made on invalid data, either alarming on a good appliance or passing a dangerous one. The defense is to calibrate the analyzer on its schedule, to zero it in fresh air before each use, and to replace sensors as they reach end of life.

## Self-Check

- Did I measure the flue gas with a calibrated combustion analyzer at every appliance service, and did I record O2, CO2, CO, and stack temperature, rather than judging by flame appearance?
- Is the analyzer within its calibration interval, was it zeroed in fresh air before use, and were the sensors replaced as they reached end of life?
- Did I sample the flue gas at the correct point (before dilution air for undiluted readings, at the appliance flue collar or heat exchanger outlet)?
- Did I read all four measurements (O2, CO2, CO, stack temperature) together and diagnose the cause of any abnormality from the pattern, rather than reading CO in isolation?
- Is the undiluted CO below the applicable threshold (commonly 100 to 400 ppm, varying by standard and appliance), and did I correct the cause rather than mask the symptom if CO was elevated?
- Before any adjustment, did I verify combustion air (by the code method), measure the manifold gas pressure against the nameplate, inspect the burners and heat exchanger, and check draft and venting?
- Did I follow the manufacturer's adjustment procedure in the correct order (set gas pressure first, then air, then verify CO and stack temperature), and did I re-measure all four after any adjustment?
- Did I verify draft and spillage under the same operating conditions as the combustion test, and did I record the draft result alongside the combustion readings?
- For condensing appliances, did I confirm the stack temperature is above the dew point (to avoid condensation in a non-condensing unit) or that condensation is occurring and draining correctly (in a condensing unit)?
- Did I document the analyzer model and calibration date, the sample point, the four readings, the draft verification, any adjustments made and their cause, so the combustion record is verifiable and the appliance is confirmed safe and efficient?
