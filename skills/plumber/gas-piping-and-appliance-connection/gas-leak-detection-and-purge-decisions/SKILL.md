# Gas Leak Detection and Purge Decisions

A gas odor call is the plumbing equivalent of a fire alarm: the consequences of a wrong decision are immediate, catastrophic, and irreversible, and the window for the correct decision is measured in seconds to minutes. The judgment problem is that natural gas and propane are intentionally odorized so that they are detectable by smell at concentrations far below the explosive limit, which means a strong odor does not necessarily mean imminent explosion — but it always means immediate action, and the only safe default is to treat every odor call as a real leak until proven otherwise. Plumbers also routinely purge air from new or repaired gas lines, and the purge itself is a hazardous operation that has killed workers who followed the gas with an open flame or purged into a confined space. This skill covers the decisions involved in detecting, confirming, and responding to gas leaks, and in purging gas lines safely.

## Core Rules

### Treat Every Odor Call as a Real Leak Until Proven Otherwise

The first decision on a gas odor call is whether to evacuate and shut off, and the answer is always yes until investigation proves the call is a false alarm. The trap is the false-alarm fatigue: most odor calls are minor (a pilot out, a small fitting weep, mercaptan odor carried on clothing), and a plumber who has responded to fifty minor calls may delay evacuation on the fifty-first, which is the real one. The disciplined rule is that evacuation and gas shutoff precede investigation, not the other way around. Do not use the phone inside the building, do not switch any electrical device on or off, do not light a match, and do not assume the odor is residual. Evacuate occupants, shut off the gas at the meter or tank if it can be done safely, ventilate, and only then begin systematic detection with a calibrated combustible gas detector.

### Detect With a Calibrated Instrument, Never With a Flame

The historical method of finding gas leaks with a match or lighter is not merely outdated — it is the direct cause of explosions and burns, and it is forbidden by every modern code and standard. A combustible gas detector (CGD), properly calibrated to the target gas, is the only acceptable detection instrument for confirmation. Soap solution is acceptable for pinpointing a suspected fitting after the general area is identified, but it is not a primary detection method for an unknown leak because it only checks fittings you already suspect. The disciplined sequence is: ventilate, sweep the area with the CGD starting near appliances and working outward along the piping, identify the highest reading, then soap-test individual fittings to pinpoint. Never use a flame for any purpose during a gas investigation, including lighting a pilot — the pilot can be relit only after the system is confirmed leak-free.

### Understand LEL Versus Odor Threshold Versus Explosive Concentration

The odorant in natural gas is detectable by smell at roughly 1% of the lower explosive limit (LEL), which means a noticeable odor can be present at a concentration that is not yet explosive. This is a safety margin, not a license to ignore the odor. The explosive range for natural gas in air is approximately 5% to 15% by volume, and for propane approximately 2.1% to 9.5%. A CGD reading in percent LEL tells you how close the atmosphere is to the explosive threshold: 10% LEL is the evacuation threshold in most standards, and 20% to 25% LEL is the threshold for immediate withdrawal and ignition-source elimination. The judgment problem is that a plumber who does not understand these numbers may read "5% LEL" and conclude the situation is minor, when in fact 5% LEL on a rising trend is an emergency. Always read the trend, not just the instantaneous value, and always evacuate at or before 10% LEL.

### Purge Air Before Appliance Light-Off, and Purge Safely

A new or repaired gas line contains air, and the first flow of gas pushes that air out ahead of it. If an appliance is lit while the line still contains an air-gas mixture, the burner will receive a combustible mixture at the wrong concentration and may flash back or fail to light cleanly. The purge must be done before any appliance is operated, and it must be done in a way that does not create a hazard. The safe purge method is to open the farthest downstream outlet (with the appliance disconnected or the gas valve isolated), allow gas to flow until the air is displaced, verify with a CGD that pure gas is present at the outlet, and only then close the outlet and light appliances in sequence from the meter outward. Never purge into a confined space, never purge with an open flame nearby, and never purge for longer than necessary — the goal is displacement, not continuous venting.

### Confirm Leak-Free Before Returning to Service

After any repair, the system must be pressure-tested to confirm integrity before gas is restored, and after gas is restored, every joint that was disturbed must be soap-tested or CGD-checked. The trap is the "it's just one fitting" assumption — a plumber replaces a single valve, opens the gas, sniffs the new fitting, and walks away, never testing the adjacent fittings that were stressed during the repair. Gas piping is a mechanical assembly, and torquing one fitting can disturb another. The disciplined practice is to pressure-test the repaired section, restore gas, and then systematically check every fitting in the work zone, not just the new one. Document the test pressure, duration, and result.

## Common Traps

### Using a Flame to Find a Leak

A plumber, taught by an older tradesperson, uses a lighter to check fittings for gas leaks, watching for a flicker. The trap is that the method "works" in the sense that a small leak will indeed cause a flame response — until the leak is large enough that the flame ignites a cloud, at which point the plumber is standing in it. The mechanism is that the method has no upper bound: a small leak produces a controlled flicker, but a larger leak produces an uncontrolled flash, and the plumber cannot know which they have until they apply the flame. The false signal is years of successful flame-checking, which is actually years of luck. The harm is severe burns and structural explosions. The defense is absolute: never use a flame for leak detection, ever, and treat any tradesperson who does as a safety hazard.

### Ignoring the Trend in LEL Readings

A plumber reads 3% LEL on a CGD and concludes the situation is minor because 3% is far below 100%. The trap is that a single reading is meaningless without a trend: 3% and falling after ventilation is a recovering situation; 3% and rising with the gas on is an emergency heading toward the explosive range. The false signal is the low absolute number, which feels safe. The harm is that the plumber relaxes, continues investigating, and the concentration climbs past the LEL while they are still in the space. The defense is to always read the trend over several minutes, to ventilate aggressively, and to evacuate at 10% LEL regardless of whether the number "feels" low.

### Purging Into a Confined or Ignition-Rich Space

A plumber purges a new gas line by opening the outlet in a mechanical room with operating boilers, or in a crawlspace with limited ventilation. The displaced air-gas mixture accumulates, finds an ignition source, and ignites. The trap is that the purge feels routine — gas has to go somewhere — and the plumber does not consider where the displaced gas is going or what is operating in the space. The false signal is "I've purged lines before," which ignores the specific conditions of this purge. The harm is explosion and fire. The defense is to always purge to a well-ventilated outdoor location or through a hose routed outdoors, to eliminate ignition sources in the purge zone, and to monitor the atmosphere with a CGD during the purge.

### Trusting Smell Alone After a Repair

A plumber repairs a fitting, restores gas, sniffs the area, smells nothing, and leaves. The trap is that olfactory fatigue sets in within minutes of exposure to mercaptan — the nose desensitizes, and a plumber who has been working in a slightly gassy environment may not smell a leak that a fresh nose would catch immediately. The false signal is "I don't smell anything," which reflects nose fatigue rather than absence of gas. The harm is a leak that goes undetected until an occupant or a different tradesperson catches it, by which point gas may have accumulated. The defense is to never rely on smell alone for confirmation; always use a CGD or soap test, and always re-check after stepping out and letting the nose reset.

### Relighting Pilots Without Confirming the Cause of Outage

A plumber is called because a pilot is out, relights it, and leaves without determining why it went out. The trap is that a pilot outage is often a symptom — a drafting problem, a failing gas valve, a cracked heat exchanger, or a low-level leak that extinguished the flame — and relighting without diagnosis treats the symptom while leaving the cause active. The false signal is the relit pilot, which appears to solve the problem. The harm is a recurrence, or worse, a CO production issue or a leak that continues. The defense is to always investigate the cause of any pilot outage before relighting: check draft, check the gas supply pressure, check the appliance for sooting or damage, and document the finding.

## Self-Check

- On a gas odor call, did I evacuate occupants and shut off the gas before beginning investigation, rather than investigating first and evacuating only if I found something?
- Did I eliminate ignition sources (no phone use inside, no switching electrical devices, no flames) before and during the investigation?
- Did I use a calibrated combustible gas detector to locate the leak, and did I avoid any use of an open flame for detection or pilot lighting during the investigation?
- Did I read the LEL trend over several minutes rather than relying on a single instantaneous reading, and did I evacuate at or before 10% LEL?
- If I purged air from a new or repaired line, did I purge to a well-ventilated location (preferably outdoors via hose), eliminate ignition sources in the purge zone, and monitor the atmosphere with a CGD?
- After any repair, did I pressure-test the affected section before restoring gas, and did I soap-test or CGD-check every fitting in the work zone, not just the new fitting?
- Did I avoid relying on smell alone for leak confirmation, recognizing olfactory fatigue, and did I step out and reset before any final sniff check?
- For any pilot outage call, did I investigate and document the cause (draft, pressure, appliance condition) before relighting, rather than relighting and leaving?
- Did I record the LEL readings, test pressures, purge method, and leak locations so that the response is documented for liability and follow-up?
- Did I communicate the gas-shutoff status and any do-not-use instruction clearly to the occupant before leaving the site?
