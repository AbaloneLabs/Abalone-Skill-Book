# Gas Appliance Connection and Venting Decisions

Connecting a gas appliance is the point where the gas piping system meets combustion, and the decisions made at the appliance — connector type, valve location, venting category, combustion air supply — determine whether the appliance burns cleanly and vents safely or produces carbon monoxide and spills flue gases into the occupied space. The judgment problem is that an appliance can appear to operate correctly while venting poorly, because the symptoms of bad draft (sooting, condensation, intermittent spillage) develop slowly and are invisible to the occupant until CO has accumulated. A plumber who connects appliances by rote, without evaluating venting and combustion air, is installing potential CO sources. This skill covers the connection, venting, and combustion air decisions that determine whether a gas appliance is safe to operate.

## Core Rules

### Use the Correct Connector or Hard Pipe for the Appliance and Location

Appliance connectors are flexible listed connectors designed for a specific gas type and appliance movement. A range, dryer, or other movable appliance uses a listed flexible connector; a stationary appliance such as a furnace or water heater is typically hard-piped with a sediment trap and a shutoff valve, though a short listed connector is permitted for vibration isolation in some jurisdictions. The trap is using the wrong connector type: a flexible connector not listed for the appliance, a connector used where hard pipe is required, or a connector run through a wall or concealed space (which is prohibited because concealed connectors cannot be inspected for damage). The disciplined rule is to verify the connector listing against the appliance and gas type, never run a flexible connector through a wall, floor, or concealed space, and always include a shutoff valve within reach of the appliance and a sediment trap downstream of the valve where required by code.

### Install a Sediment Trap and an Accessible Shutoff Valve

Every gas appliance should have a sediment trap (also called a drip leg) — a short downward-facing tee or nipple that catches pipe scale and moisture before it reaches the appliance gas valve — and an accessible shutoff valve within 6 feet of the appliance. The sediment trap is installed at the appliance, downstream of the shutoff valve, and the appliance connector connects to the trap outlet. The trap is not optional in most codes, and its omission is a common inspection failure. The shutoff valve must be accessible — not behind the appliance, not concealed in a cabinet, not requiring a tool to reach — so that the occupant or emergency responder can shut off the gas quickly. The judgment problem is that an accessible valve is sometimes aesthetically inconvenient, and the temptation to tuck it behind the appliance must be resisted.

### Match Venting Category to the Appliance, and Size the Vent for the Input

Gas appliances are categorized by vent pressure and temperature: Category I is negative pressure and non-condensing (the traditional atmospheric furnace or water heater venting into a B-vent or masonry chimney); Category II is negative pressure and condensing (rare); Category III is positive pressure and non-condensing (some tankless units); Category IV is positive pressure and condensing (high-efficiency furnaces and boilers, venting in PVC or specialized stainless). The vent material, sizing method, and termination are entirely different for each category, and mixing them is dangerous. A Category IV appliance must never vent into a B-vent designed for Category I; the condensate will corrode the vent and the positive pressure will push flue gases through joints. A Category I appliance vented into an oversized or unlined masonry chimney will have inadequate draft and will spill. The vent must be sized to the appliance input using the vent sizing tables in the fuel gas code, considering the height, lateral run, and number of appliances common-vented.

### Provide Combustion Air by the Code Method, Not by Guess

Every fuel-burning appliance needs combustion air, and the code provides specific methods: the standard method (1 square inch of opening per 1000 BTU/hr, with one opening within 12 inches of the ceiling and one within 12 inches of the floor of the confined space), the known air infiltration rate method, or mechanical supply. The trap is assuming that a "big basement" or a "leaky room" provides enough air, which is not a code method and which fails when the house is tightened up (new windows, weatherstripping) and the combustion air that was adequate yesterday becomes inadequate today, causing spillage and CO. The disciplined rule is to calculate combustion air by a code method, install the required louvers or ducts, and never rely on assumed infiltration. For high-efficiency direct-vent appliances, follow the manufacturer's combustion air pipe requirements exactly, because these appliances are not drawing room air and the pipe sizing is critical.

### Evaluate Draft and Spillage Before Leaving the Appliance in Service

After connecting and lighting a gas appliance, the plumber must verify that it drafts correctly: a smoke test or flame test at the draft hood (for Category I) should show steady updraft within minutes of startup, and there should be no spillage of flue gases into the room. For appliances with induced draft (Category III and IV), verify that the vent pressure switch closes and stays closed. The trap is lighting the appliance, confirming the burner fires, and leaving without checking draft — the burner firing is not evidence of safe venting. Spillage under startup conditions is common and brief; spillage that persists after warmup is a serious defect indicating inadequate draft, a blocked vent, or an oversized common vent. Always test draft under operating conditions and document the result.

## Common Traps

### Using a Flexible Connector Where Hard Pipe Is Required, or Concealing a Connector

A plumber uses a flexible appliance connector for a stationary furnace because it is faster than threading pipe, or runs a connector through a cabinet wall to reach a remote gas valve. The trap is that flexible connectors are listed for specific uses (appliance movement, vibration isolation) and are prohibited in concealed spaces or where they cannot be inspected. The mechanism is that a concealed connector can chafe, kink, or corrode without being seen, and a connector used beyond its listing has no proven integrity. The false signal is that the connector holds pressure during the test. The harm is a leak that develops later in an inaccessible location. The defense is to hard-pipe stationary appliances, reserve flexible connectors for movable appliances, and never run a connector through a wall, floor, or concealed cabinet.

### Omitting the Sediment Trap

A plumber connects the appliance directly to the gas valve without a sediment trap, because the trap "never catches anything" in their experience. The trap is that the sediment trap's value is invisible in normal operation — it catches pipe scale and moisture only when those contaminants are present, which is intermittent — and its omission appears harmless until a slug of scale reaches the appliance gas valve and causes a failure or a flame disturbance. The false signal is years of trouble-free operation without a trap. The harm is appliance malfunction and, in some cases, flame instability that produces CO. The defense is to always install the sediment trap as required by code, treating it as non-optional regardless of perceived need.

### Common-Venting a High-Efficiency Appliance With a Conventional One

A plumber adds a new Category IV condensing furnace to an existing B-vent that also serves a Category I water heater, assuming that "a vent is a vent." The trap is that the categories are incompatible: the Category IV appliance produces positive pressure and condensate, which will be pushed down the B-vent and out through the Category I appliance's draft hood, spilling flue gases into the room and corroding the vent. The mechanism is the pressure and condensate incompatibility between categories. The false signal is that the appliances appear to vent during a brief test. The harm is CO spillage, corrosion, and eventual vent failure. The defense is to never common-vent across categories, to vent each appliance per its category, and to liner or replace the vent when an appliance is upgraded to a different category.

### Assuming a Large Room Provides Enough Combustion Air

A plumber installs a furnace in a basement and assumes the basement volume provides adequate combustion air without calculating or installing openings. The trap is that the assumption holds while the house is leaky, but fails when the homeowner tightens the building (new windows, insulation, weatherstripping) and the combustion air that was incidental becomes insufficient, causing spillage and CO. The mechanism is that combustion air is a function of room volume and infiltration rate, both of which can change after the appliance is installed. The false signal is years of operation without spillage. The harm is CO production and spillage that appears only after the building is tightened. The defense is to calculate combustion air by a code method and install the required openings or mechanical supply, never relying on assumed infiltration.

### Lighting the Appliance Without Verifying Draft

A plumber connects a water heater, turns on the gas, lights the pilot, confirms the main burner fires, and leaves. The trap is that a firing burner is not evidence of safe venting; the flue gases may be spilling into the room because the vent is cold, blocked, oversized, or improperly terminated. The mechanism is that draft develops over the first few minutes as the vent warms, and a brief spillage during cold-start is normal, but persistent spillage after warmup is a serious defect. The false signal is the firing burner. The harm is CO in the occupied space. The defense is to always test draft under operating conditions — smoke or flame test at the draft hood, verify pressure switch closure on induced-draft units — and to document the result before leaving the appliance in service.

## Self-Check

- Did I select a connector or hard pipe appropriate to the appliance (movable vs. stationary) and gas type, and did I avoid running any flexible connector through a wall, floor, or concealed space?
- Did I install an accessible shutoff valve within reach of the appliance and a sediment trap downstream of the valve where required by code?
- Did I confirm the appliance venting category (I, II, III, or IV) and select vent material, sizing, and termination appropriate to that category, avoiding any common-venting across incompatible categories?
- Did I size the vent to the appliance input using the fuel gas code vent tables, accounting for height, lateral run, and the number of common-vented appliances?
- Did I calculate combustion air by a code method (standard opening ratio, known infiltration, or mechanical supply) and install the required louvers, ducts, or supply, rather than assuming room volume or infiltration is adequate?
- For direct-vent appliances, did I follow the manufacturer's combustion air pipe sizing and termination exactly?
- After lighting the appliance, did I verify draft under operating conditions — smoke or flame test at the draft hood for Category I, pressure switch closure for induced-draft units — and confirm no persistent spillage after warmup?
- Did I check for sooting, flame irregularity, or condensate issues that would indicate incomplete combustion or venting problems?
- Are the vent termination clearances from windows, doors, intakes, and property lines met per code?
- Did I document the draft test result, combustion air method, and vent configuration so that the appliance is confirmed safe for service and the design basis is recorded?
