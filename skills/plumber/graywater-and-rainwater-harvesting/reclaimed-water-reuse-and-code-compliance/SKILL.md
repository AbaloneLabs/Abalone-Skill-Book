# Reclaimed Water Reuse and Code Compliance

Reclaimed water reuse — sending treated graywater or rainwater to toilets, urinals, irrigation, or cooling-tower makeup through a dedicated non-potable piping system — is the point where a harvesting system becomes a building plumbing system, and it is where the regulatory and safety stakes rise sharply. The judgment problem is that a reuse system introduces a second, non-potable water supply throughout the building, and the plumbing code (IPC Chapter 13, NPC, or local reclaimed-water rules) imposes strict requirements to ensure that this non-potable water can never be confused with, or cross-connected to, the potable supply. An installer who uses the wrong pipe color, who omits the required backflow protection on make-up water, or who fails to label every outlet will create a system that works hydraulically but is a latent cross-connection hazard — and one that will fail inspection and may be illegal to operate. This skill covers dual-supply plumbing, cross-connection control, labeling, treatment for reuse, and the role limits that place permitted reuse-system design with qualified designers and health-authority coordination.

## Core Rules

### Use Dedicated, Identified Non-Potable Piping Throughout

Reclaimed and non-potable water piping must be a completely separate system from the potable supply, and it must be identified as non-potable at every point. The plumbing code specifies pipe identification: purple piping (or purple markings/labels on other colors) with "non-potable water" or specific wording at intervals along every run, at every wall penetration, and at every fixture connection. Fixtures supplied by non-potable water (toilets, urinals, hose bibbs) must be marked as non-potable. The trap is running non-potable PVC or PEX alongside potable piping in the same color, or marking only the tank and not the runs, so a future plumber cannot distinguish the two systems and accidentally connects them — or a homeowner drinks from a non-potable hose bibb. The disciplined approach is to use purple pipe or apply code-compliant labels at the required intervals on every non-potable run, mark every non-potable fixture and hose bibb, and document the system on the as-built drawings. There is no such thing as over-labeling a non-potable system.

### Protect the Potable Make-Up With an Air Gap or Approved Backflow Preventer

Most reuse systems include a potable water make-up connection to top up the non-potable tank when rainwater or graywater is insufficient — and this connection is the single highest-risk cross-connection point in the system. The make-up must be protected by a code air gap (a physical break between the potable supply and the non-potable tank's flood rim, typically twice the pipe diameter) or an approved backflow preventer (often a reduced pressure zone assembly) at the connection. The trap is running a potable line directly into the non-potable tank below the water line, or with only a check valve, so that during a pressure loss the non-potable tank water back-siphons into the building's potable supply — a serious contamination event. The disciplined approach is to provide a verified air gap on every potable make-up connection (preferred, because it is fail-safe), or an RPZ backflow preventer where an air gap is infeasible, and to confirm the air gap dimension meets code.

### Treat Reuse Water to the Standard Required for Its End Use

The level of treatment required depends on what the reclaimed water touches. Subsurface irrigation and toilet flushing typically require basic treatment: screening, settling, and often filtration and disinfection (UV or chlorination) to meet the code's water-quality standard for the end use. Surface irrigation, spray irrigation, and cooling-tower makeup have stricter standards. Potable reuse (rare, highly regulated) requires advanced treatment and ongoing monitoring. The trap is sending raw or minimally treated graywater to toilet-flushing toilets without disinfection, exposing occupants to aerosolized pathogens when the toilet flushes, or sending untreated rainwater to a cooling-tower makeup and introducing biological growth to the HVAC system. The disciplined approach is to identify the end use, determine the code-required water-quality standard for that end use, specify the treatment (filtration, disinfection, and any required testing), and document that the treated water meets the standard. Arrange for the ongoing monitoring the code requires.

### Install Treatment, Pump, and Controls With Redundancy and Alarms

A reuse system supplying fixtures (toilets, cooling towers) must be reliable — if the non-potable supply fails, there must be a fallback. This means the treatment and pump system should include a potable make-up (air-gapped, as above) that activates when the non-potable tank is low, a pump with pressure switch and expansion tank to supply fixture pressure, and ideally an alarm or indicator for high-tank, low-tank, pump-failure, and treatment-failure conditions. The trap is a single-pump, single-tank system with no redundancy, so a pump failure or a dry rainwater tank takes the toilets out of service. The disciplined approach is to design the make-up, pump, and controls so that a non-potable supply failure silently falls back to potable (via the air-gapped make-up) without interrupting fixture service, and to provide indicators so the owner knows the system is running on make-up.

### Coordinate Permits, Inspection, and Health-Authority Approval

Reclaimed water reuse systems are regulated by the plumbing code and, in most jurisdictions, by the environmental health authority or water utility, and they typically require a permit, plan review, inspection, and sometimes ongoing reporting. The requirements vary dramatically by state and utility — some allow simple residential graywater-to-irrigation with minimal permitting, others require engineered plans and operational permits for any reuse. The trap is installing a reuse system without confirming the local regulatory path, then facing a stop-work order, a failed inspection, or an order to remove the system. The disciplined approach is to confirm the permitting and code requirements before design, submit plans for review where required, schedule inspections at the rough-in and final stages, and obtain the operational approval before the system is put in service. Document everything for the health authority and future service.

### Respect the Role Limits — Permitted and Commercial Reuse Needs Specialists

Simple residential graywater-to-subsurface-irrigation and rainwater-to-irrigation systems are within a licensed plumber's scope in many jurisdictions. Systems that supply building fixtures (toilets, cooling towers), commercial systems, and any potable reuse require design by a qualified designer (often a licensed engineer or certified water professional), treatment specification, and health-authority coordination. Confirm the scope and regulatory requirements before taking on reuse work, and escalate permitted and commercial systems to specialists.

## Common Traps

### Unlabeled Non-Potable Piping Mistaken for Potable

The installer runs non-potable PEX or PVC in the same color as the potable supply, with labels only at the tank. The trap is that a future plumber or homeowner cannot tell the systems apart at a fixture connection or a mid-run repair, and may cross-connect them or drink from a non-potable outlet. The mechanism is that visual identification is the primary safeguard against cross-connection, and inadequate labeling defeats it. The false signal is "the systems are separate." The harm is an accidental cross-connection or exposure. The defense is to use purple pipe or apply code-compliant labels at the required intervals on every run and mark every non-potable fixture and hose bibb.

### Potable Make-Up Without an Air Gap Back-Siphons

The installer runs a potable top-up line into the non-potable tank below the water line, with a check valve "for safety." The trap is that a check valve is not an approved backflow preventer for this high-hazard connection, and during a municipal pressure loss the non-potable tank water can back-siphon through a failed or leaking check valve into the potable supply. The mechanism is that the submerged inlet eliminates the physical break. The false signal is "there's a check valve." The harm is contamination of the building's potable supply with non-potable water. The defense is to provide a verified code air gap (or an RPZ backflow preventer) on every potable make-up connection.

### Untreated Graywater to Toilets Exposes Occupants

The installer sends screened-but-undisinfected graywater to toilet-flushing toilets. The trap is that toilet flushing aerosolizes droplets, and undisinfected graywater contains skin bacteria and organics that become an inhalation or contact exposure, and the organics also grow biofilm in the toilet tank. The mechanism is that the end use (toilet flushing) requires a water-quality standard that raw graywater does not meet. The false signal is "it's just flushing toilets." The harm is occupant exposure and biofilm/odor in the fixtures. The defense is to treat reuse water to the code-required standard for the end use, including disinfection where required.

### No Permit, No Inspection, and an Order to Remove

The installer builds a reuse system supplying toilets without confirming the local regulatory path, and the health authority or building department orders it removed or modified after the fact. The trap is that reuse regulations vary dramatically and are often stricter than the installer assumes, and an unpermitted system is illegal to operate. The mechanism is that the regulatory requirement exists independently of the installer's knowledge. The false signal is "it works hydraulically." The harm is a stop-work order, removal, fines, and rework. The defense is to confirm permitting and code requirements before design and to obtain permits and inspections.

## Self-Check

- Is all non-potable piping a dedicated, separate system from the potable supply, identified with purple pipe or code-compliant labels at the required intervals, with every non-potable fixture and hose bibb marked?
- Is every potable make-up connection protected by a verified code air gap (preferred) or an approved RPZ backflow preventer — never a submerged inlet or a mere check valve?
- Is the reuse water treated to the code-required standard for its end use (subsurface irrigation, toilet flushing, surface irrigation, cooling-tower makeup), including filtration and disinfection where required?
- Does the treatment, pump, and control system include redundancy — an air-gapped potable make-up that activates on low tank, a pump with pressure tank, and alarms or indicators for high/low tank, pump failure, and treatment failure?
- Did I confirm the local permitting and code requirements before design, submit plans for review where required, and schedule rough-in and final inspections with the health authority or building department?
- Did I confirm my scope covers this system, and did I escalate fixture-supply, commercial, or potable reuse systems to a qualified designer and the health authority?
- Are the as-built drawings, treatment spec, water-quality monitoring plan, permits, and inspection records documented for the owner, inspector, and health authority?
- Is the owner educated on the system's operation, the non-potable labeling, and the maintenance required (filter cleaning, lamp replacement, tank inspection)?
