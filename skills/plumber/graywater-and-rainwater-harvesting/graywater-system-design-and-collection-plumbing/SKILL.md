# Graywater System Design and Collection Plumbing

Graywater is the relatively clean wastewater from showers, bathtubs, lavatories, and clothes washers, recovered for subsurface irrigation or (with treatment) toilet flushing. It is governed by the plumbing code's graywater provisions (IPC Chapter 13 or local equivalents, which vary dramatically by state) and by environmental health rules, and the judgment problem is that graywater sits in a regulatory and sanitary gray zone: it is not sewage (it has no toilet waste), but it is not clean water either — it carries soap, skin bacteria, lint, hair, and warmth that make it biologically active and capable of causing disease, odor, and clogging if mishandled. An installer who sources the wrong fixtures, who connects a prohibited discharge, or who designs a surge tank or distribution system without accounting for the biological load will create a system that smells, clogs, contaminates, or fails inspection. This skill covers sourcing, collection plumbing, surge capacity, and the role limits that place large or treated graywater systems with designers and environmental-health coordination.

## Core Rules

### Source Only Permitted Fixtures and Exclude All Prohibited Discharges

The first design decision is which fixtures contribute to the graywater system, and the plumbing code is specific: graywater typically includes showers, bathtubs, lavatories (bathroom sinks), and clothes washers. It excludes kitchen sinks (grease, food particles, high bacterial load), dishwashers (food and high solids), and — critically — any toilet, urinal, or bidet discharge (which is blackwater, not graywater). The trap is including the kitchen sink or dishwasher "because it's just drain water," which introduces fats, oils, grease, food solids, and high bacterial loading that the graywater distribution system and the irrigation field cannot handle, leading to rapid clogging, odor, and potential pathogen exposure. The disciplined approach is to map every fixture in the building, classify each as graywater-permitted or prohibited, and plumb only the permitted fixtures to the graywater system. Where a clothes washer is sourced, confirm the detergent used is graywater-compatible (low salt, no boron, no chlorine bleach) and educate the owner, because the chemicals sent down the washer end up in the landscape.

### Size the Surge Tank for Peak Flow and Settling

Graywater is generated in surges (a shower, a laundry cycle) and consumed slowly by the irrigation field, so a surge tank (or the surge capacity of the system) is required to buffer the difference. The tank must hold the peak simultaneous input without overflowing to the sewer and without being so large that the water turns septic from long residence (graywater goes anaerobic and malodorous in 24 to 48 hours). The trap is sizing the surge tank by average daily flow and ignoring that two showers plus a laundry load can deliver 50 to 80 gallons in 30 minutes, far exceeding the irrigation field's acceptance rate. The disciplined approach is to compute peak inflow (sum of simultaneous sourced fixtures at their drain rates), subtract the field's instantaneous acceptance rate, and size the surge capacity to hold the difference with margin. For residential systems, a 50 to 100 gallon surge capacity is common; larger systems require detailed calculations. Include a screened overflow to the sanitary sewer (or septic) for the events that exceed the tank, so graywater never backs up into the building.

### Install a Three-Way Diverter Valve for Bypass and Maintenance

Every graywater system must be able to be diverted back to the sanitary sewer — for maintenance, for when the landscape cannot accept water (saturated soil, freezing weather, vacation), and for when the graywater system itself is being serviced. This requires a three-way diverter valve (manual or motorized) on the graywater line upstream of the surge tank or treatment system, with a clear, labeled, accessible actuator. The trap is hard-piping the graywater line to the tank with no bypass, so the first time the system clogs or the owner goes on vacation, the graywater has nowhere to go and backs up into the fixtures. The disciplined approach is to install a three-way diverter at the collection point, route the bypass to the sanitary sewer, label the valve positions (graywater / sewer), and verify the owner knows how to operate it. For motorized valves, provide a manual override.

### Prevent Cross-Connections Between Graywater and Potable Supply

Graywater is a non-potable water source, and the plumbing code treats it with the same cross-connection rigor as any other reclaimed water: every graywater fixture outlet, irrigation point, and tank must be clearly identified as non-potable, the piping must be labeled (purple or the code-specified color, with "non-potable" or "graywater" markings at intervals), and any make-up water connection to a graywater tank (for toilet flushing systems that need potable top-up) must include a code air gap or approved backflow preventer. The trap is running graywater PVC alongside potable PVC without labeling, or connecting a potable make-up line to a graywater tank without an air gap, creating a cross-connection that can back-siphon graywater into the building's potable supply during a pressure loss. The disciplined approach is to label all graywater piping per code, use the required pipe color or markings, provide air-gapped make-up where required, and never allow a direct connection between graywater and potable piping.

### Design the Distribution for Subsurface Application and Clogging

Graywater irrigation is almost always subsurface (surface discharge is generally prohibited due to pathogen and odor risk), and the distribution system must tolerate the lint, hair, soap scum, and biological solids that graywater carries. The trap is designing a distribution system with small emitters or fine pores that clog within weeks; graywater's solids load is incompatible with drip irrigation without robust filtration. The disciplined approach is to use a coarse filtration (a removable mesh screen or mulch basin) ahead of the distribution, use large-orifice distribution (mulch basins, infiltration chambers, or large-pore subsurface grids) rather than fine emitters, and design the field for the daily graywater volume and the soil's percolation rate. Size the field so it can accept the daily volume without surfacing or ponding, and locate it the code-required distance from foundations, property lines, and the water table.

### Respect the Role Limits — Treated Systems and Large Installations Need Specialists

Simple residential laundry-to-landscape and surge-tank-to-subsurface systems are within a licensed plumber's scope in many jurisdictions (some states require a permit and inspection even for these). Larger systems, systems that treat graywater for toilet flushing (which require disinfection, often filtration, and a separate non-potable supply piping system throughout the building), and commercial systems require design by a qualified designer and coordination with the environmental health authority. Confirm the local code's permitting and licensing requirements before starting, and escalate treated and commercial systems to specialists.

## Common Traps

### Sourcing the Kitchen Sink or Dishwasher

The installer includes the kitchen sink or dishwasher in the graywater collection to "increase the water captured." The trap is that kitchen wastewater carries grease, food solids, and high bacterial loading that the graywater system is not designed to handle. The mechanism is that the grease coats and clogs the distribution, the food solids rot anaerobically in the surge tank, and the bacterial load creates a health hazard. The false signal is "it's drain water, so it's graywater." The harm is a clogged, malodorous, and potentially hazardous system that fails inspection. The defense is to source only code-permitted fixtures and exclude kitchen sinks, dishwashers, and any toilet/bidet discharge.

### Surge Tank Sized for Average, Not Peak

The installer sizes the surge tank for the daily graywater volume divided by some factor, but two morning showers plus a laundry load deliver 60 gallons in 30 minutes, exceeding the tank and overflowing to the sewer (or, worse, backing up into the fixtures). The trap is that graywater is generated in surges, not steadily, and the tank must buffer the peak. The mechanism is that the irrigation field accepts water slowly, so the instantaneous inflow exceeds the outflow and the difference must be stored. The false signal is "the daily volume fits." The harm is overflow, fixture backup, or an undersized field that surfaces water. The defense is to size the surge capacity for peak simultaneous inflow minus field acceptance rate.

### No Bypass, So the First Clog Floods the House

The installer hard-pipes the graywater line to the surge tank with no diverter. The trap is that graywater systems require periodic maintenance (screen cleaning, field flushing) and must be taken offline during vacations, freezing weather, or saturated soil — and without a bypass, the graywater has nowhere to go but back up the drains. The mechanism is that a system with no bypass fails the moment it cannot accept water. The false signal is "the system works when new." The harm is a backup into the showers and lavatories the first time the field saturates or the owner travels. The defense is to install a labeled three-way diverter to the sanitary sewer upstream of the tank.

### Unlabeled Graywater Piping Creates a Cross-Connection Hazard

The installer runs graywater PVC alongside the potable supply without labeling, or ties a potable make-up line into a graywater tank without an air gap. The trap is that graywater looks like any other drain line, and a future plumber or homeowner cannot distinguish it — and a direct potable connection without an air gap can back-siphon graywater into the building supply. The mechanism is that the lack of identification and backflow protection defeats the cross-connection safeguards. The false signal is "the pipes are separate." The harm is accidental cross-connection and potential pathogen exposure via the potable supply. The defense is to label all graywater piping per code, use the required color/markings, and air-gap any potable make-up connection.

## Self-Check

- Did I map every fixture and source only code-permitted graywater fixtures (showers, bathtubs, lavatories, clothes washers), excluding kitchen sinks, dishwashers, and all toilet/bidet discharges?
- Did I size the surge tank or surge capacity for peak simultaneous inflow minus the irrigation field's acceptance rate, with a screened overflow to the sanitary sewer for exceedances?
- Is a labeled, accessible three-way diverter valve installed upstream of the tank so the system can be bypassed to the sanitary sewer for maintenance, vacation, or saturated/frozen conditions?
- Is all graywater piping labeled per code (required color and "non-potable"/"graywater" markings), and is any potable make-up connection air-gapped or protected by an approved backflow preventer?
- Did I design the distribution for subsurface application with coarse filtration and large-orifice distribution (not fine emitters), sized to the daily volume and the soil's percolation rate?
- Is the irrigation field located the code-required distance from foundations, property lines, and the seasonal high water table, and can it accept the daily volume without surfacing?
- Did I confirm the local permitting and licensing requirements, obtain permits where required, and escalate treated (toilet-flushing) and commercial systems to a qualified designer?
- Are the fixture sourcing map, surge sizing, diverter, labeling, and field design documented for the owner, the inspector, and future service?
