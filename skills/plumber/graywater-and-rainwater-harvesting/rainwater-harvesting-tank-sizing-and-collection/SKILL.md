# Rainwater Harvesting Tank Sizing and Collection

Rainwater harvesting collects runoff from a roof, stores it in a cistern, and supplies it for irrigation, toilet flushing, or (with treatment) potable use. The judgment problem is that a rainwater system's value depends on matching tank size to both the supply (roof area × rainfall, which is intermittent and seasonal) and the demand (irrigation or fixture use, which is steady and may peak in dry periods when the tank is empty). An installer who sizes the tank by rule of thumb, who collects from the wrong roof surface, or who omits first-flush diversion and screening will deliver a system that overflows constantly while failing to bridge dry spells, or one that delivers debris-laden, contaminated water that clogs pumps and irrigation. This skill covers catchment yield, tank sizing, collection plumbing, first-flush and screening, and the role limits that place potable rainwater systems with specialists and public-health coordination.

## Core Rules

### Calculate Catchment Yield From Roof Area and Local Rainfall

The available water is a function of the roof catchment area and the local rainfall, and both must be quantified. The basic yield formula is: gallons collected = roof area (sq ft) × rainfall (inches) × 0.62 (conversion) × runoff coefficient (about 0.8 to 0.9 for hard roofs, accounting for wetting loss and inefficiency). A 2000 sq ft roof in a region with 40 inches of annual rainfall yields roughly 40,000 to 45,000 gallons per year — but that water arrives in storms, not steadily, and the monthly distribution matters as much as the annual total. The trap is sizing the system by annual yield and ignoring that in many climates the rainfall is concentrated in a wet season followed by months of drought, so the tank must be large enough to store wet-season surplus to bridge the dry season, or the system will overflow all winter and run dry all summer. The disciplined approach is to obtain monthly rainfall data for the site, compute monthly yield, compare to monthly demand, and size the tank to bridge the dry months using a water-balance (behavioral) analysis, not a single annual number.

### Size the Tank for Supply, Demand, and the Dry-Season Bridge

Tank sizing is a balance: too small and the system overflows during storms and cannot bridge dry periods; too large and the capital cost is wasted on capacity rarely filled. The correct method is a monthly water-balance that tracks tank fill level: add each month's catchment yield, subtract each month's demand, cap at the tank's capacity (overflow is lost), and floor at empty (no negative). Iterate tank sizes until the chosen capacity meets the desired reliability (e.g., meets demand 90 percent of months) without excessive idle capacity. The trap is picking a tank size by "a 1000-gallon tank is common" without the water-balance, then discovering it overflows in every storm and empties in the first dry month. The disciplined approach is to run the water-balance with site-specific rainfall and realistic demand, and select the smallest tank that meets the reliability target — recognizing that diminishing returns set in quickly, and a tank much larger than the largest single storm capture is rarely justified by irrigation demand alone.

### Select the Catchment Surface for Water Quality

The roof material affects water quality dramatically. Metal, tile, and smooth composite roofs yield clean water suitable for irrigation and, with treatment, for potable use. Asphalt shingles shed granules and petrochemical compounds; wood shakes harbor biological growth and leach tannins; flat roofs with accumulated debris and bird droppings yield contaminated water. The trap is collecting from an asphalt-shingle or debris-laden flat roof and using the water for toilet flushing or (worse) potable supply without acknowledging the contamination, so the tank fills with sediment and the downstream pumps and fixtures clog or are exposed to contaminants. The disciplined approach is to evaluate the catchment surface, prefer metal or tile, and — for any roof — install screening and first-flush diversion. For potable rainwater systems, the catchment surface, treatment, and testing requirements are stringent and typically require a specialist and health-department approval.

### Install First-Flush Diversion and Screening on Every System

The first portion of runoff from a roof carries the accumulated dust, bird droppings, pollen, and debris, and diverting this "first flush" dramatically improves stored water quality. A first-flush diverter captures the initial runoff (commonly 0.5 to 1 gallon per 100 sq ft of roof) and sends it to drain or landscape, after which cleaner water flows to the tank. Inlet screening (a leaf screen or basket at the gutter downspout or tank inlet) removes leaves and large debris. The trap is omitting first-flush diversion and screening, so every storm washes the roof's accumulated contamination straight into the tank, where it settles, decomposes, and degrades water quality and breeds mosquitoes. The disciplined approach is to install a first-flush diverter sized to the roof area, screen every inlet, and provide a mosquito-proof, screened overflow. Maintain the diverter and screens — they fill with debris and must be cleaned regularly.

### Provide a Calmed Inlet, Floating Extract, and Screened Overflow

Inside the tank, design details govern water quality. A calmed inlet (a down-turned pipe that introduces water below the surface, rather than splashing in at the top) prevents disturbing the settled sediment and the biofilm layer that helps digest organics. A floating extract (a suction or overflow draw from just below the surface, where the water is cleanest) avoids drawing sediment from the bottom or floating debris from the top. A screened, mosquito-proof overflow (sized to pass the maximum inflow without tank pressurization) prevents the tank from flooding and excludes vectors. The trap is a simple top-fill, bottom-draw tank with an undersized overflow, so the tank stirs up sediment with every storm, draws cloudy water, and either floods or breeds mosquitoes. The disciplined approach is to install a calmed inlet, a floating extract for the supply pump, and a generously sized screened overflow.

### Respect the Role Limits — Potable Systems Need Specialists and Approval

Irrigation and toilet-flushing rainwater systems are within a licensed plumber's scope in many jurisdictions, subject to permit and inspection. Potable rainwater systems (where the rainwater is treated for drinking) require treatment design by a water specialist, ongoing water-quality monitoring, and health-department approval, and in many jurisdictions are restricted or require a licensed water operator. Confirm the local code and permitting requirements before starting, and escalate potable and large commercial systems to specialists and the health authority.

## Common Traps

### Sizing by Annual Rainfall, Not Monthly Water-Balance

The installer computes annual yield (say 40,000 gallons), picks a 5000-gallon tank, and declares the system sized. The trap is that the rainfall arrives in storms concentrated in a wet season, and a 5000-gallon tank overflows in every large storm while running dry weeks into the dry season. The mechanism is that annual yield is irrelevant to storage; what matters is the mismatch between monthly inflow and monthly demand. The false signal is "the annual yield is 40,000 gallons." The harm is a system that wastes overflow water and fails to supply demand when it is most needed. The defense is to run a monthly water-balance and size the tank to bridge the dry months.

### Collecting From a Contaminating Roof Without Treatment

The installer collects from an asphalt-shingle or debris-covered flat roof and uses the water for toilet flushing or irrigation without screening or first-flush. The trap is that these surfaces shed granules, petrochemicals, bird waste, and debris that load the tank with sediment and contamination. The mechanism is that every storm washes the roof's accumulation into storage. The false signal is "it's rainwater, so it's clean." The harm is a tank full of sediment, clogged pumps and fixtures, and potential exposure to contaminants. The defense is to evaluate the catchment surface, prefer metal or tile, and install first-flush diversion and screening on every system.

### Omitting First-Flush Diversion and Screening

The installer runs the downspout directly into the tank to "capture every drop." The trap is that the first runoff carries the roof's accumulated dust, pollen, bird droppings, and debris, and sending it to the tank degrades the stored water quality and breeds mosquitoes. The mechanism is that the first flush is the dirtiest water, and diverting it is the single most effective quality measure. The false signal is "more water captured." The harm is sediment, odor, mosquitoes, and clogged downstream equipment. The defense is to install a first-flush diverter sized to the roof area and screen every inlet, with a maintenance schedule.

### Undersized or Unscreened Overflow Floods or Breeds Mosquitoes

The installer provides a small overflow pipe without a screen, sized for "normal" storms. The trap is that a large storm can deliver inflow far exceeding the overflow capacity, pressurizing and flooding the tank, or that an unscreened overflow admits mosquitoes and small animals that colonize the tank. The mechanism is that the overflow must pass the maximum inflow and exclude vectors. The false signal is "there's an overflow pipe." The harm is tank flooding, property damage, or a mosquito-breeding public-health hazard. The defense is to size the overflow for maximum inflow and screen it mosquito-proof.

## Self-Check

- Did I compute catchment yield from the actual roof area and local monthly rainfall (not annual average), using a realistic runoff coefficient?
- Did I run a monthly water-balance comparing inflow to demand, and size the tank to bridge the dry-season months at the target reliability without excessive idle capacity?
- Did I evaluate the catchment surface material, prefer metal or tile, and acknowledge the contamination risk of asphalt shingles, wood shakes, or debris-laden flat roofs?
- Is a first-flush diverter installed, sized to the roof area (commonly 0.5 to 1 gallon per 100 sq ft), and are all inlets screened, with a maintenance schedule for cleaning?
- Inside the tank, is there a calmed inlet (below-surface, to avoid disturbing sediment), a floating extract for the supply pump, and a generously sized, mosquito-proof screened overflow?
- Is the tank material, location, and support appropriate (UV-resistant, structurally rated for the water weight, and accessible for maintenance)?
- Did I confirm the local permitting and code requirements, obtain permits where required, and escalate potable or large commercial systems to a specialist and the health authority?
- Are the catchment yield, water-balance, tank sizing, first-flush and screening design, and maintenance schedule documented for the owner and inspector?
