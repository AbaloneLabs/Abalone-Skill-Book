# Condensate Drain Sizing and Trap Design

An air conditioner or condensing furnace produces condensate continuously while it runs, and that water must leave the cabinet through a drain that is correctly sized, correctly trapped, and correctly sloped — or it backs up into the cabinet, floods the pan and the ceiling below, or lets the blower suck air through the drain and upset the airflow. The judgment problem is that the trap, the slope, and the secondary drain are not optional details: a negative-pressure cabinet (most upflow and horizontal units) needs a trap deep enough to seal against the blower's suction, an undersized or flat drain backs up under peak condensate, and a dry trap in winter lets conditioned air escape. Agents miss this because the drain "works" in mild weather, masking that the trap is too shallow, the slope is flat, or there is no secondary protection — and the first hard cooling day floods the ceiling. This skill covers the sizing, trap, slope, secondary-drain, and material decisions that keep condensate drainage reliable.

## Core Rules

### Size the Drain to the Condensate Rate With Margin

Condensate production scales with the unit's capacity and the latent load (humidity). A rule of thumb is roughly 1 pint per hour per ton of cooling at typical conditions, peaking far higher in humid weather, so a 3/4-inch drain is the practical minimum for units up to about 5 tons and a 1-inch drain for larger units, per most codes (IMC/UPC) and manufacturer manuals. The disciplined rule is to size the drain from the unit's rated condensate rate with margin (a 3/4-inch minimum even for small units, upsizing for long runs or high latent loads), to confirm the drain is never reduced below the unit's outlet, and to avoid horizontal runs that are under-sized for the peak rate. A drain that is "barely enough" on a dry day will back up on the first humid day.

### Design the Trap Depth Against the Blower's Static Pressure

The trap exists to stop the blower from pulling air through the drain, and its depth must exceed the cabinet's negative static pressure (for negative cabinets) or be matched to the positive pressure (for positive cabinets). A negative-cabinet trap that is too shallow is blown open by the blower's suction: the water seal is drawn out, the drain then sucks air, and condensate cannot enter the drain, so it overflows the pan. The disciplined rule is to size the trap depth to at least twice the cabinet's static pressure (commonly a 2- to 4-inch seal for typical residential negative cabinets, deeper for high-static commercial units), to make the trap removable or cleanable, and to confirm the outlet of the trap is below the drain pan weir so the seal is maintained. A positive-cabinet (draw-through return) unit still needs a trap sized to its positive pressure.

### Prevent Trap Prime Loss, Dry-Out, and Algae Blockage

A trap only works if it holds water, and a trap that dries out (in winter, or on a unit that runs intermittently) loses its seal and lets air bypass. A trap can also clog with algae, slime, or debris, blocking flow. The disciplined rule is to size the trap so it holds enough water to survive the longest off-cycle, to provide a cleanout at the trap so it can be flushed, and (in installations where the trap may dry out) to prime the trap at startup and to consider a trap primer or a deeper trap for seasonal units. Routine service should include a flush or a biodegradable algae tablet to keep the trap and line clear, because a biologically clogged trap backs up exactly like an undersized one.

### Slope the Drain Continuously and Avoid Low Spots and Traps in the Run

Condensate drains by gravity, so the horizontal run must slope continuously downhill — commonly 1/8 inch per foot minimum (1/4 inch per foot preferred) — with no sags, low spots, or secondary traps that can collect water and block flow. The disciplined rule is to slope the entire horizontal run at 1/8 to 1/4 inch per foot, to support the pipe so it does not sag between hangers, to avoid any reduction in diameter along the run, and to terminate the drain at an approved location (an indirect waste, a floor drain, a condensate pump receptor, or outdoors with an air gap) — never directly into a sanitary sewer without an air gap or approved receptor. Verify the slope with a level, not by eye.

### Provide a Secondary or Auxiliary Drain and a Safety Switch

Where the primary drain's failure would cause damage (an attic or closet install, a unit over finished space), a secondary (auxiliary) drain or a safety (float) switch is required by code (IMC 307.2.3 and equivalents) and by common sense. The secondary drain pan under the unit, with its own piped drain to a conspicuous location, or a float switch in the primary pan that shuts the unit off before overflow, prevents a ceiling flood when the primary drain clogs. The disciplined rule is to install a secondary drain pan with an independent drain to a visible location whenever the unit is over finished or concealed space, to add a float (safety) switch wired to shut the unit down on high water, and to route the secondary drain's discharge where its dripping will be noticed (so a clog is seen before it floods).

## Common Traps

### Undersizing the Drain or Running It Flat

A plumber runs a 1/2-inch drain or slopes a 3/4-inch run flat by eye. The trap is that the drain cannot carry the peak condensate rate and backs up. The mechanism is that condensate flow is gravity-driven and limited by diameter and slope; the false signal is "it drains when I pour water in." The harm is a flooded pan and ceiling damage on the first humid day. The defense is to use a 3/4-inch minimum, slope 1/8 to 1/4 inch per foot verified by level, and support the pipe against sagging.

### Using a Trap Too Shallow for a Negative Cabinet

A plumber installs a 1-inch trap on a negative-static cabinet. The trap is that the blower's suction draws the water seal out and the drain then sucks air, blocking condensate entry. The mechanism is that the seal depth must exceed the cabinet static pressure; the false signal is "a trap is installed." The harm is pan overflow and water damage. The defense is to size the trap depth to at least twice the cabinet static (commonly a 2- to 4-inch seal).

### Omitting the Trap on a Negative Cabinet Entirely

A plumber runs the condensate line straight off the cabinet with no trap. The trap is that the blower pulls air through the drain, preventing condensate from leaving the pan. The mechanism is that there is no seal to resist the suction; the false signal is "the drain is open." The harm is overflow and possible airflow imbalance. The defense is to install a trap sized to the cabinet static on every negative-cabinet unit.

### Skipping the Secondary Drain or Safety Switch Over Finished Space

A plumber installs an attic unit with only the primary drain. The trap is that when the primary clogs (algae, debris), the pan overflows into the ceiling. The mechanism is that a single drain has no redundancy; the false signal is "the primary drains." The harm is ceiling and property damage. The defense is to install a secondary drain pan with an independent, visible discharge and a float switch wired to shut the unit off.

### Using Unlisted Material or No Cleanout

A plumber runs condensate in non-listed tubing or builds a trap with no cleanout. The trap is that the material is not approved or the trap cannot be serviced, so a clog cannot be cleared. The mechanism is that condensate drains clog with biological growth and need routine cleaning; the false signal is "the pipe is plastic." The harm is recurring blockages and overflow. The defense is to use PVC (or listed CPVC/pipe) per code and to include a cleanout at the trap.

## Self-Check

- Is the drain sized to the unit's condensate rate with margin (3/4-inch minimum up to about 5 tons, 1-inch for larger units), with no reduction below the unit's outlet?
- Is the trap depth sized to at least twice the cabinet's static pressure (commonly a 2- to 4-inch seal for negative cabinets, deeper for high-static commercial), and is the trap removable or cleanable?
- Is the trap outlet below the drain-pan weir so the water seal is maintained, and is the trap primed at startup and protected from dry-out on seasonal units?
- Does the horizontal run slope continuously at 1/8 to 1/4 inch per foot (verified by level), with no sags, low spots, secondary traps, or diameter reductions?
- Is the drain supported against sagging between hangers, and does it terminate at an approved indirect location (floor drain, utility sink, condensate pump receptor, or outdoors with an air gap)?
- For a unit over finished or concealed space, is a secondary drain pan with an independent drain to a conspicuous location installed, and is a float (safety) switch wired to shut the unit off on high water?
- Is the drain material PVC or listed CPVC/pipe per code (condensate is mildly corrosive but PVC is approved), and is there a cleanout at the trap?
- Did I include routine service provisions (a flush or biodegradable algae tablet) to keep the trap and line clear of biological growth?
- Did I verify the drain carries the peak condensate rate (roughly 1 pint per hour per ton, higher in humid conditions) without backing up?
- Did I document the drain size, trap depth and static-pressure basis, slope, secondary drain and switch, material, and termination so the design is verifiable?
