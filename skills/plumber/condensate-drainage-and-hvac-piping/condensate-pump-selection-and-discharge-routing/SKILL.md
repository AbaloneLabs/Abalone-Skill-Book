# Condensate Pump Selection and Discharge Routing

When an HVAC unit, condensing furnace, or condensing boiler sits below the available gravity drain, a condensate pump is the only way to lift the water out — and a misapplied pump, a misrouted discharge, or an ignored alarm will flood the equipment room, freeze the line, or back-contaminate the potable or sanitary system. The judgment problem is that the pump must be sized to the peak condensate rate (not the average), the discharge must run to an approved indirect receptor with an air gap (never a hard pipe into a sanitary sewer), acidic condensate from a condensing boiler must be neutralized, and the reservoir's float and alarm must be reliable because a failed pump with no alarm is a guaranteed flood. Agents miss this because the pump "keeps up" on a mild day, masking that it is undersized for peak humidity, that the discharge is frozen or back-pressured, or that there is no alarm to warn of failure. This skill covers the selection, reservoir, discharge-routing, freeze, and acidic-condensate decisions that keep pumped condensate reliable and safe.

## Core Rules

### Select the Pump for Peak Condensate Rate and Lift Head

A condensate pump must move the unit's peak condensate production (not the average) against the vertical lift and the horizontal run's friction. Cooling condensate can peak at roughly 1 pint per hour per ton in humid conditions, and a condensing boiler can produce several gallons per hour at full fire, so the pump's rated capacity (commonly in GPH or mL/min) must exceed the peak with margin. The pump must also overcome the vertical lift (each foot of lift is roughly 0.43 psi of back-pressure) plus the friction of the horizontal run and any fittings. The disciplined rule is to confirm the unit's peak condensate rate, select a pump whose rated capacity at the installed lift exceeds that rate by at least 50 to 100 percent margin, and to verify the pump's maximum lift covers the vertical rise to the discharge point.

### Size the Reservoir, Float, and Alarm for Run Time and Failure Warning

The pump's reservoir holds condensate between pump cycles, and its volume sets the cycle frequency (a larger reservoir cycles less often, extending switch and motor life). The float switch must be reliable and positioned so the pump starts before the reservoir overflows and stops before it runs dry. For any installation whose failure would cause damage (finished space, equipment rooms, attics), an alarm — a high-water float wired to an audible or remote signal, or a safety switch that shuts the unit off — is essential, because a pump that fails with no alarm will flood until someone notices. The disciplined rule is to choose a reservoir large enough to limit cycling, to verify the float starts and stops correctly, and to install a high-water alarm or unit-shutoff switch on every pumped condensate system over finished or equipment space.

### Route the Discharge to an Approved Indirect Receptor With an Air Gap

Condensate is not clean water — it carries dust, biological growth, and (from condensing boilers) acid — so it must discharge to an approved indirect waste receptor (a floor drain, a mop sink, a hub drain, or a dedicated receptor) through an air gap, never hard-piped into a sanitary sewer or a fixture trap. The air gap (commonly 1 to 2 inches, or twice the discharge pipe diameter) prevents a backed-up sewer from contaminating the condensate system or the equipment. The disciplined rule is to terminate the discharge with a visible air gap above an approved receptor, to confirm the receptor can accept the pump's flow without overflowing, and never to hard-connect the discharge to the sanitary system.

### Avoid Freezing, Backflow, and Long Horizontal Runs in the Discharge

The discharge line runs where the pump can push it, which often means through unconditioned or outdoor space where it can freeze. A frozen discharge line backs condensate up into the reservoir and overflows the pump, shutting the unit down or flooding. Backflow (from a discharge routed downhill then up, or into a receptor that can back up) defeats the pump. The disciplined rule is to route the discharge by the shortest practical path to the receptor, to keep the line in conditioned space where possible, to insulate or heat-trace any run through freezing areas, to pitch the line so it drains between cycles (no low spots that hold water and freeze), and to add a check valve at the pump only where the manual requires one (some pumps need it to hold prime, others are damaged by back-pressure).

### Neutralize Acidic Condensate From Condensing Boilers and Furnaces

Condensing-boiler and condensing-furnace condensate is acidic (pH commonly 3.0 to 4.5) because the flue gases dissolve into the water. Discharging it directly to a cast-iron drain, a copper trap, or a concrete floor (and in some jurisdictions to any drain without treatment) corrodes piping and violates code. The disciplined rule is to route condensing-appliance condensate through a neutralizer (a cartridge of limestone or magnesium oxide media) sized to the appliance's condensate rate before the pump or the receptor, to replace or recharge the media on the manufacturer's interval (as the media dissolves), and to verify the discharge pH is near neutral. The neutralizer can sit before or after the pump, but must be in the path before the drain.

## Common Traps

### Undersizing the Pump to the Average, Not the Peak, Rate

A plumber selects a pump rated just above the unit's average condensate rate. The trap is that peak humidity or full-fire boiler operation exceeds the pump's capacity and the reservoir overflows. The mechanism is that condensate rate varies with load and humidity; the false signal is "the pump keeps up today." The harm is a flood on the first humid or full-fire day. The defense is to size the pump to the peak rate at the installed lift with 50 to 100 percent margin.

### Omitting the High-Water Alarm on a Pumped System Over Finished Space

A plumber installs a pump with no alarm over a finished ceiling. The trap is that when the pump fails (motor, float, or frozen line), the reservoir overflows silently. The mechanism is that a pump failure with no alarm is invisible until the flood; the false signal is "the pump runs." The harm is ceiling and property damage. The defense is to install a high-water alarm or unit-shutoff float on every pumped system over finished or equipment space.

### Hard-Piping the Discharge Into the Sanitary Sewer

A plumber hard-connects the pump discharge into a sanitary sewer or a fixture trap with no air gap. The trap is that a sewer backup can contaminate the condensate system and the equipment. The mechanism is that there is no air gap to break the cross-connection; the false signal is "it drains into the sewer." The harm is contamination and code violation. The defense is to terminate with a visible air gap above an approved indirect receptor.

### Routing the Discharge Where It Can Freeze

A plumber runs the discharge line through an unconditioned crawlspace or to an outdoor termination in a cold climate. The trap is that the line freezes and backs condensate up into the reservoir. The mechanism is that standing water in the line freezes at 32°F; the false signal is "it drained in the fall." The harm is a no-heat/no-cool call and possible flood. The defense is to keep the line conditioned, insulate or heat-trace freezing runs, and pitch the line to drain between cycles.

### Discharging Condensing-Boiler Condensate Without a Neutralizer

A plumber runs acidic condensing-boiler condensate straight to a drain with no neutralizer. The trap is that the acidic condensate (pH 3.0 to 4.5) corrodes the drain over months. The mechanism is that the acids dissolve metal and concrete; the false signal is "the drain works at startup." The harm is corroded piping, leaks, and code violations. The defense is to install a neutralizer sized to the appliance's condensate rate and to replace the media on interval.

## Self-Check

- Is the pump's rated capacity at the installed lift greater than the unit's peak condensate rate (roughly 1 pint per hour per ton for cooling, several GPH for a condensing boiler at full fire) by at least 50 to 100 percent margin?
- Does the pump's maximum lift cover the vertical rise to the discharge point (each foot of lift is roughly 0.43 psi of back-pressure)?
- Is the reservoir sized to limit cycling, is the float verified to start before overflow and stop before dry-running, and is a high-water alarm or unit-shutoff float installed on every system over finished or equipment space?
- Does the discharge terminate with a visible air gap (commonly 1 to 2 inches, or twice the pipe diameter) above an approved indirect receptor (floor drain, mop sink, hub drain)?
- Is the receptor able to accept the pump's flow without overflowing, and is the discharge never hard-piped into the sanitary sewer or a fixture trap?
- Is the discharge routed by the shortest practical path, kept in conditioned space where possible, and insulated or heat-traced through any freezing area?
- Is the discharge line pitched to drain between cycles (no low spots that hold water and freeze), and is a check valve installed only where the pump manual requires one?
- For a condensing boiler or condensing furnace, is a neutralizer (limestone or magnesium oxide media) installed in the path before the drain or pump, sized to the appliance's condensate rate?
- Is the neutralizer media on a replacement interval, and is the discharge pH verified near neutral?
- Did I document the pump capacity and lift basis, reservoir and alarm provisions, air-gap discharge, freeze protection, and neutralizer so the installation is verifiable?
