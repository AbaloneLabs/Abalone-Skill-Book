# Faucet and Valve Installation

Faucet and valve installation is where the plumbing system meets the occupant — the point of use — and the quality of the installation determines whether the fixture works smoothly for years or leaks, drips, and resists operation from the start. The judgment problem is that faucets and valves contain precision internal components (cartridges, ceramic disks, seats, springs) that are damaged by debris, overtightening, and incorrect supply connection, and a plumber who installs them quickly without flushing the supplies, without using the correct connection type, or without setting the cartridge correctly will produce a fixture that fails prematurely. A faucet that drips within months of installation is almost always an installation error, not a product defect. This skill covers the installation and diagnostic decisions that produce reliable faucet and valve operation.

## Core Rules

### Flush the Supply Lines Before Connecting the Faucet

Before connecting a faucet or valve to the water supply, the supply lines must be flushed to remove debris — solder beads, Teflon tape fragments, pipe dope, copper burrs, PEX crimp ring shavings — that would otherwise be driven into the faucet cartridge or valve seat and cause immediate leakage or hard turning. The flushing procedure is to remove the aerator, open the shutoff valves briefly with the supplies directed into a bucket, and confirm the water runs clear. The trap is connecting the faucet directly to newly piped supplies without flushing, then turning the water on and finding the faucet drips or will not shut off because a solder bead is lodged in the cartridge. The disciplined rule is to flush every supply line before connecting the fixture, and to flush through the faucet with the aerator removed for the first run.

### Match the Supply Connection Type to the Faucet and the Code

Faucets connect to the water supply by several methods: rigid supply tubes (chrome or copper, with compression fittings), flexible braided supply lines (listed connectors with the correct end fittings), or integral supplies (some high-end faucets have flexible lines built in). The connection must match the faucet's inlet (typically 3/8-inch or 1/2-inch compression, or a specific manufacturer fitting), the shutoff valve outlet, and the local code (some jurisdictions restrict flexible connectors in concealed spaces or require specific listings). The trap is using the wrong connector type — a rigid tube that does not quite reach and is bent to fit (stressing the fitting), or a flexible connector not listed for the application. The disciplined rule is to use the correct length and type of listed connector, to avoid bending rigid tubes to fit (use the correct length instead), and to verify the connector listing against the code.

### Install Shutoff Stops at Every Fixture, Accessible and Functional

Every fixture should have individual shutoff valves (angle stops or straight stops) on both hot and cold supplies, located accessible to the fixture, so that the fixture can be serviced without shutting off the building water. The stops must be functional (they must actually close and hold), which means they must be operated during installation to confirm they work — a stop that is frozen open from mineral buildup is no better than no stop. The trap is installing stops that are inaccessible (behind a finished wall or under a cabinet that cannot be opened), or installing stops but never operating them, so a frozen stop is discovered only when the fixture needs service. The disciplined rule is to install accessible stops at every fixture, to operate each stop during installation to confirm function, and to replace any stop that does not operate freely.

### Set the Cartridge or Valve Correctly and Lubricate With the Approved Lubricant

Modern faucets use cartridges (single-handle), ceramic disks (two-handle), or compression stems (older two-handle), and each must be installed per the manufacturer: the cartridge oriented correctly (the hot-cold indicator and the internal ports aligned), the mounting nut torqued to specification (overtightening cracks ceramic disks, undertightening allows play and leakage), and the internal seals lubricated with the manufacturer-approved lubricant (typically a silicone or plumbers grease, not petroleum jelly which degrades rubber over time). The trap is installing the cartridge in the wrong orientation (hot and cold reversed, or the cartridge rotated so the ports do not align), overtightening the mounting nut (cracking the ceramic disk), or using the wrong lubricant (degrading the seals). The disciplined rule is to follow the manufacturer's installation procedure exactly, to torque to specification, and to use only the approved lubricant.

### Diagnose Leaks and Hard Operation by Component, Not by Replacing the Faucet

A faucet that drips, leaks at the base, or turns hard has a specific failed component, and the diagnostic approach is to identify the component (the cartridge, the O-rings, the seats, the aerator, the supply connection) rather than replacing the entire faucet. A drip from the spout is a cartridge or seat failure; a leak at the base is an O-ring or body gasket failure; hard turning is a cartridge or stem binding from debris or mineral buildup; a spray from the spout is an aerator restriction. The trap is replacing the entire faucet when a $10 cartridge would fix the problem, or replacing the cartridge without identifying why it failed (debris, overtightening) and having the new cartridge fail the same way. The disciplined rule is to diagnose the failed component, replace it, and address the cause (flush debris, correct the torque, install a filter) so the failure does not recur.

## Common Traps

### Connecting the Faucet Without Flushing the Supplies

A plumber solders and assembles the supply piping, connects the faucet directly, and turns on the water; a solder bead or pipe dope fragment lodges in the cartridge and the faucet drips or will not shut off. The trap is that the debris is invisible until the water flows, and the failure appears to be a product defect. The mechanism is that debris in the supply is driven into the precision cartridge. The false signal is that "the piping is clean." The harm is a failed faucet and a warranty claim. The defense is to flush every supply line into a bucket before connecting the faucet.

### Bending a Rigid Supply Tube to Make It Fit

A plumber uses a rigid chrome supply tube that is slightly too long or the wrong angle, and bends it to fit between the stop and the faucet, stressing the compression fitting and the tube wall. The trap is that the bent tube looks acceptable but the stress causes a slow leak or a fatigue crack over time. The mechanism is that rigid tubes are not designed to be field-bent to fit. The false signal is that "it fits." The harm is a slow leak under the sink. The defense is to use the correct length and angle rigid tube, or a flexible listed connector of the correct length.

### Installing Stops but Never Operating Them

A plumber installs angle stops at a lavatory but never opens or closes them during installation, and years later when the faucet needs service the stop is frozen open from mineral buildup and cannot shut off the fixture. The trap is that the stop "is installed" but is non-functional, discovered only when needed. The mechanism is that stops seize from disuse and mineral accumulation. The false signal is that "the stops are there." The harm is a fixture that cannot be isolated for service. The defense is to operate each stop during installation to confirm function and to replace any stop that does not operate freely.

### Overtightening the Cartridge Mounting Nut

A plumber installs a two-handle faucet and overtightens the cartridge mounting nut "to be sure it does not leak," cracking the ceramic disk inside. The trap is that the overtightening feels like good workmanship but destroys the precision component. The mechanism is that ceramic disks are brittle and crack under excess compression. The false signal is that "the nut is tight." The harm is a faucet that drips immediately or soon after installation. The defense is to torque the cartridge nut to the manufacturer specification, using a torque wrench where specified, and to rely on the internal seals rather than overtightening.

### Replacing the Faucet Instead of Diagnosing the Component

A plumber replaces an entire dripping faucet when a $10 cartridge would fix the problem, or replaces the cartridge without identifying that debris caused the failure, and the new cartridge fails the same way. The trap is that the replacement addresses the symptom without the diagnosis, wasting money and time and recurring if the cause is unaddressed. The mechanism is that the failed component is specific and the cause is identifiable. The false signal is that "the faucet was bad." The harm is unnecessary cost and recurring failure. The defense is to diagnose the failed component, replace it, and address the cause.

## Self-Check

- Did I flush every supply line into a bucket before connecting the faucet, to remove debris that would damage the cartridge?
- Did I use the correct length and type of listed supply connector (rigid or flexible), avoiding bending rigid tubes to fit?
- Did I install accessible shutoff stops on both hot and cold supplies at every fixture, and did I operate each stop during installation to confirm it opens and closes freely?
- Did I install the cartridge or valve per the manufacturer — correct orientation, torque to specification, and lubricated only with the approved lubricant?
- Did I avoid overtightening the cartridge mounting nut, relying on the internal seals rather than excess compression?
- After installation, did I flush the faucet with the aerator removed to clear any residual debris, then reinstall the aerator?
- Did I verify hot and cold orientation (left hot, right cold, or per the manufacturer indicator) and correct any reversed supply?
- For leaks or hard operation, did I diagnose the failed component (cartridge, O-ring, seat, aerator) rather than replacing the faucet, and did I address the cause?
- Did I confirm that all connections are dry after the system is pressurized, checking under the fixture and at the stops?
- Did I document the fixture make and model, the cartridge or stem type, and any special installation requirements so future service is straightforward?
