# Fixture Selection and Rough-In Dimensions

Fixture selection and rough-in layout determine whether a bathroom or kitchen works for the occupant for decades or is a chronic source of crowding, code violations, and accessibility failures. The judgment problem is that fixtures have specific rough-in dimensions (the location of the water supplies, the drain, the closet flange) and code-required clearances (the space around and between fixtures), and a layout that ignores these produces a fixture that does not fit, a clearance that violates code, or an installation that fails accessibility requirements. A plumber who roughs in by "standard dimensions" without checking the specific fixture and the code clearances will eventually install a toilet too close to a wall, a lavatory without knee clearance, or a shower too small to meet code. This skill covers the fixture selection and rough-in decisions that produce a functional, code-compliant, accessible installation.

## Core Rules

### Verify the Specific Fixture's Rough-In Before Framing the Walls

Every fixture model has specific rough-in dimensions published by the manufacturer: the toilet closet flange distance from the finished wall (typically 12 inches, but 10 and 14 inches exist), the lavatory supply heights and spread (4-inch centers, 8-inch centers, or single-hole), the shower drain location and curb size, the bathtub drain and overflow location. The trap is roughing in to "standard" dimensions before the fixture is selected, then discovering the selected fixture needs a different rough-in, requiring rework of concealed piping. The disciplined rule is to select the fixture (at least the model) before rough-in, obtain the manufacturer's rough-in sheet, and lay out the piping to the specific fixture dimensions. Where the fixture is not yet selected, use the most common rough-in but document the assumption and verify before wall close-in.

### Meet the Code Minimum Clearances Between and Around Fixtures

Plumbing codes specify minimum clearances: the center of a toilet to any obstruction (wall, tub, vanity) is typically 15 inches minimum, 18 inches recommended; the clearance in front of a toilet is typically 21 inches minimum; the spacing between two lavatories is typically 4 inches minimum between edges; the shower interior minimum dimension is typically 30 inches, with a minimum 900 square inches of interior area. These clearances are code minimums, not recommendations, and a layout that violates them fails inspection. The trap is laying out fixtures by aesthetic arrangement without checking the code clearances, producing a toilet 13 inches from a wall or a shower 28 inches wide. The disciplined rule is to lay out every fixture against the code clearance minimums, to measure from finished wall surfaces (not framing), and to verify the layout before rough-in.

### Address ADA and Accessibility Requirements Where Applicable

In commercial, public, and some residential (visitability) applications, fixtures must meet ADA (Americans with Disabilities Act) accessibility requirements: toilet seat height 17 to 19 inches, grab bars at specific heights and locations, lavatory knee clearance (29 inches high, 30 inches wide, 27 inches deep at the toe), lavatory rim height 34 inches maximum, shower seat and grab bar requirements, and turning space (60-inch circle or T-shaped). The trap is installing standard fixtures in an ADA-required space, or installing "ADA" fixtures without meeting all the dimensional requirements (a common error is the lavatory that is at the right height but lacks knee clearance because of the trap or supply locations). The disciplined rule is to identify whether ADA applies (public accommodations, commercial restrooms, accessible residential), to follow the ADA dimensional requirements for each fixture, and to verify the installation against the requirements after rough-in and after finish.

### Coordinate the Fixture Type With the Water Supply and Drain Configuration

Different fixture types require different supply and drain configurations: a wall-mount toilet needs a concealed carrier and a different rough-in than a floor-mount; a vessel sink needs a different drain and supply height than an undermount; a sensor-flush toilet needs an electrical supply; a shower with a thermostatic valve needs a different rough-in than a pressure-balance valve; a bidet needs a hot and cold supply and a specific drain. The trap is selecting a fixture without verifying its supply and drain requirements, then discovering the rough-in does not match. The disciplined rule is to confirm the fixture type and its supply, drain, and (where applicable) electrical requirements before rough-in, and to coordinate with the electrical trade for sensor, pump, or heated fixtures.

### Plan the Layout Against the Framing, Structure, and Other Trades

Fixture layout must coordinate with the building structure: a toilet flange cannot land on a floor joist; a wall-mount lavatory needs blocking in the wall; a shower drain must clear the framing below; a tub must fit between studs and clear the floor joists; supply and drain piping must route through stud bays without weakening studs beyond the boring limits. The trap is laying out fixtures without checking the framing, then discovering the drain lands on a joist or the supply requires a stud to be over-bored. The disciplined rule is to lay out fixtures on the framing plan, to verify drains and supplies clear structural members, to add blocking where fixtures need support, and to coordinate with the framing trade before rough-in.

## Common Traps

### Roughing In to "Standard" Dimensions Before Selecting the Fixture

A plumber roughs in a toilet at 12 inches and a lavatory at 8-inch centers based on standard dimensions, then the homeowner selects a 10-inch rough toilet and a single-hole vessel sink, and the rough-in must be reworked in the finished wall. The trap is that "standard" dimensions cover the majority but not all fixtures, and the assumption fails for the specific selection. The mechanism is that fixtures vary in rough-in. The false signal is that "the rough-in is standard." The harm is concealed rework. The defense is to select the fixture model before rough-in and to lay out to the manufacturer dimensions.

### Laying Out Fixtures Without Checking Code Clearances

A plumber arranges a bathroom by aesthetics and installs a toilet 14 inches from a vanity and a shower 28 inches wide, both violating the code minimum clearances, and the installation fails inspection. The trap is that the layout looks balanced and functional but violates the minimums. The mechanism is that clearances are code requirements, not aesthetic choices. The false signal is that "the layout looks good." The harm is inspection failure and rework. The defense is to lay out every fixture against the code clearance minimums, measured from finished surfaces.

### Installing "ADA" Fixtures Without Meeting All Dimensional Requirements

A plumber installs a lavatory at the correct ADA height but without knee clearance because the trap and supplies block the knee space, or installs grab bars at the wrong height or spacing. The trap is that meeting one ADA dimension (height) while missing another (knee clearance, grab bar placement) fails the accessibility requirement as surely as installing a standard fixture. The mechanism is that ADA is a set of coupled dimensional requirements. The false signal is that "the fixture is ADA height." The harm is a non-compliant accessible installation and liability. The defense is to follow all the ADA dimensional requirements for each fixture and to verify after finish.

### Selecting a Fixture Without Verifying Supply and Drain Requirements

A plumber selects a wall-mount toilet or a sensor-flush fixture without verifying that the rough-in needs a carrier or an electrical supply, and the installation cannot proceed without rework. The trap is that the fixture type implies supply and drain requirements that differ from standard. The mechanism is that fixture type determines rough-in. The false signal is that "it's a toilet." The harm is rework. The defense is to confirm the fixture type and its supply, drain, and electrical requirements before rough-in.

### Laying Out Fixtures Without Checking the Framing

A plumber lays out a shower drain that lands on a floor joist, or a wall-mount lavatory without wall blocking, or a toilet flange over a joist, and the rough-in requires structural modification. The trap is that the layout looks correct on the floor plan but conflicts with the framing. The mechanism is that fixtures must coordinate with structure. The false signal is that "the layout fits the room." The harm is structural rework. The defense is to lay out fixtures on the framing plan and verify drains, supplies, and supports clear the structure.

## Self-Check

- Did I select the fixture model (or document the assumed model) before rough-in, and did I lay out to the manufacturer's specific rough-in dimensions?
- Did I verify that every fixture meets the code minimum clearances (toilet center-to-wall, front clearance, lavatory spacing, shower minimum dimension), measured from finished wall surfaces?
- Where ADA or accessibility requirements apply, did I follow all the dimensional requirements (seat height, grab bar placement, knee clearance, rim height, turning space), not just one?
- Did I confirm the fixture type and its supply, drain, and (where applicable) electrical requirements before rough-in, and coordinate with the electrical trade?
- Did I lay out fixtures on the framing plan and verify that drains, supplies, and supports clear structural members without requiring over-boring or structural modification?
- Did I add wall blocking where fixtures (wall-mount lavatories, grab bars, heavy mirrors) need support?
- Did I verify that the closet flange lands on a finished floor (not on subfloor alone) and is at the correct height for the finished floor thickness?
- For showers and tubs, did I verify the drain location, the curb or threshold dimensions, and the waterproofing coordination with the tile trade?
- Did I confirm that the fixture finishes (chrome, brushed nickel, etc.) and the trim match the selected rough-in valves?
- Did I document the fixture selections, rough-in dimensions, code clearances, and ADA compliance so the installation is verifiable and the layout can be inspected?
