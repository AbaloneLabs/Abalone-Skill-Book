# Grease Waste Pumping and Code Compliance

A grease interceptor that is not pumped on time overflows FOG into the sewer, and the resulting municipal violation, fine, and sewer backup are entirely preventable with disciplined pumping and record-keeping. The judgment problem is that pumping frequency is governed by a measurable threshold (the FOG and solids depth), not a calendar guess, and the compliance burden — manifests, hauler licensing, sampling ports, prohibited-discharge rules, and record retention — is what the municipality audits when a violation occurs. Agents set a generic quarterly pump and walk away, let kitchen staff dump concentrated oils or chemicals that should be hauled as waste, and fail to keep the manifests that prove the interceptor was maintained. This skill covers the 25 percent FOG depth rule, manifest and hauler documentation, pumping access, sampling ports, prohibited discharge, and record retention.

## Core Rules

### Pump on the 25 Percent FOG Depth Rule, Not a Generic Calendar

The standard maintenance threshold for a grease interceptor is 25 percent of the wetted depth occupied by FOG and settled solids: when the floating grease layer plus the bottom solids layer reaches one-quarter of the liquid depth, the interceptor's separation capacity is compromised and it must be pumped. This is measured with a core sampler or a sludge judge at each service. Pumping too early wastes money; pumping too late allows FOG breakthrough into the sewer. The interval to reach 25 percent depends entirely on the kitchen's FOG load — a high-volume fry kitchen may hit it in four weeks, a low-volume cafe in four months — so the frequency must be set by measurement, not a default. The trap is pumping quarterly regardless of load. The disciplined rule is to measure the FOG and solids depth at each service, pump at 25 percent, and adjust the interval to the measured rate.

### Manage Hauler Licensing and Manifest Documentation

Grease waste is a regulated waste stream, and the hauler must be licensed by the state or municipality to transport it, and must provide a manifest (a tracking document) for every pumping that records the generator (the kitchen), the hauler, the volume pumped, the date, and the receiving disposal facility. The manifest is the proof that the waste was legally removed and disposed, and the generator (the kitchen owner, often via the plumber or FOG service) must retain copies. Using an unlicensed hauler, or pumping without a manifest, exposes the generator to liability for illegal dumping if the hauler mismanages the waste. The trap is using the cheapest pumper without checking licensing or manifests. The disciplined rule is to use only licensed haulers, to require a manifest for every pumping, and to retain the manifests.

### Ensure Pumping Access With Removable Lids and No Permanent Obstruction

An interceptor cannot be pumped if it cannot be opened and reached. Indoor hydromechanical units need their lids clear of counters, shelving, and equipment; outdoor gravity interceptors need their lids removable (not sealed, not paved over) and within hose reach of the pumping truck (typically 50 to 100 feet of hose, with truck access to the site). Permanent obstructions — a built-in counter over an indoor unit, a parked dumpster or structure over an outdoor vault — make pumping impossible or force a partial pump that leaves FOG behind. Access also means the inlet and outlet baffles can be inspected and the chamber fully evacuated, not just the surface skimmed. The trap is boxing in the interceptor after installation. The disciplined rule is to keep lids removable and clear, maintain truck access for outdoor units, and ensure the full chamber can be evacuated.

### Provide Sampling Ports Where the Municipality Requires Them

Many municipalities require a sampling port (a clean, accessible access on the interceptor effluent or the building sewer downstream of the interceptor) so the inspector can draw a representative sample of the discharge to test for FOG concentration, pH, and other parameters. The sampling port must be located per the local FOG ordinance (often on the effluent line within a set distance of the interceptor), be accessible, and be kept clear. Without it, the municipality may require installation before issuing or renewing the FOG permit, and may sample at a less representative point. The trap is omitting the sampling port on a new install or renovation. The disciplined rule is to verify the local FOG ordinance, install the sampling port where required, and keep it accessible and clear.

### Prevent Prohibited Discharge and Retain Records

Concentrated waste oils (deep-fryer oil, shortening, large quantities of cooking oil) must be collected in a separate waste-oil container and hauled by a renderer, not poured down the drain or into the grease interceptor — the volume overwhelms the interceptor and constitutes prohibited discharge. Chemicals (strong caustics, solvents, bleach in quantity) must not be dumped into the interceptor because they emulsify the grease (causing breakthrough) and may violate discharge limits. Food solids belong in solids separation or trash, not the interceptor. Record retention is the compliance backbone: manifests, pumping logs, FOG-depth measurements, cleaning records, and any sampling results must be retained per the local ordinance (commonly three years) and produced on inspection. The trap is treating the interceptor as a catch-all drain. The disciplined rule is to route concentrated oils to a renderer, keep chemicals and solids out, and retain all records for the required period.

## Common Traps

### Pumping on a Calendar Schedule Regardless of FOG Depth

The kitchen pumps quarterly whether the interceptor needs it or not. The trap is that a high-load kitchen hits 25 percent FOG in weeks, and a low-load one wastes money quarterly. The mechanism is that FOG accumulation rate depends on kitchen volume and menu. The false signal is that "quarterly is the rule." The harm is breakthrough and sewer overflow on the high end, wasted cost on the low end. The defense is to measure FOG and solids depth at each service, pump at 25 percent, and adjust the interval to the measured rate.

### Using an Unlicensed Hauler or Skipping the Manifest

The kitchen uses the cheapest pumper without verifying licensing, or accepts pumping without a manifest. The trap is that the generator remains liable for the waste. The mechanism is that grease waste is a regulated stream requiring licensed transport and documented disposal. The false signal is that "it was pumped, so it's handled." The harm is liability for illegal dumping and municipal penalties. The defense is to use only licensed haulers, require a manifest for every pumping, and retain the manifests.

### Boxing In the Interceptor So It Cannot Be Fully Pumped

A counter is built over an indoor unit or a structure placed over an outdoor vault, blocking the lid. The trap is that the interceptor cannot be opened or fully evacuated. The mechanism is that pumping requires full lid access and hose reach. The false signal is that "the pumper can work around it." The harm is partial pumping that leaves FOG, leading to breakthrough. The defense is to keep lids removable and clear, maintain truck access, and ensure the full chamber can be evacuated.

### Omitting the Sampling Port the Municipality Requires

The installer omits the sampling port on a new kitchen or renovation. The trap is that the municipality cannot monitor compliance and may require retrofit. The mechanism is that the FOG ordinance specifies a sampling access point on the effluent. The false signal is that "the interceptor is the compliance device." The harm is permit denial, retrofit cost, or sampling at an unrepresentative point. The defense is to verify the local FOG ordinance, install the sampling port where required, and keep it accessible.

### Dumping Concentrated Oils or Chemicals Into the Interceptor

Kitchen staff pour fryer oil or caustic cleaner down the drain into the interceptor. The trap is that the volume or chemistry overwhelms the interceptor. The mechanism is that concentrated oil exceeds separation capacity and caustics emulsify the trapped grease. The false signal is that "the interceptor catches it." The harm is breakthrough, FOG overflow into the sewer, and discharge-limit violations. The defense is to route concentrated oils to a renderer, keep chemicals and food solids out of the interceptor, and train staff on prohibited discharge.

## Self-Check

- Did I establish the pumping interval by measuring FOG and settled-solids depth at each service, and pump when the combined layer reaches 25 percent of the wetted depth, rather than a calendar default?
- Is the hauler licensed by the state or municipality, and is a manifest issued for every pumping recording generator, hauler, volume, date, and disposal facility?
- Are the interceptor lids removable and clear of counters, shelving, equipment, and structures, so the full chamber can be evacuated?
- For outdoor gravity interceptors, is there truck access and hose reach (typically 50 to 100 feet) to the unit?
- Did I verify the local FOG ordinance and install the required sampling port on the effluent, accessible and clear?
- Are concentrated waste oils (fryer oil, shortening) collected in a separate container and hauled by a renderer, not poured into the interceptor?
- Are strong caustics, solvents, and bleach kept out of the interceptor to prevent grease emulsification and discharge-limit violations?
- Are food solids routed to solids separation or trash rather than the interceptor?
- Are manifests, pumping logs, FOG-depth measurements, cleaning records, and sampling results retained for the required period (commonly three years) and producible on inspection?
- Did I document the pumping frequency rationale (measured FOG depth), hauler licensing, manifest numbers, and sampling-port location for the compliance record?
