# Commercial Kitchen Drain Line Design and Maintenance

Commercial kitchen drain lines carry FOG (fats, oils, grease), food solids, and hot wash water, and they fail by a mechanism residential lines do not: grease solidifies as the water cools downstream, building a narrowing crust that closes the pipe over months. The judgment problem is that the design decisions that prevent this — adequate slope, correct cleanout spacing, indirect (air-gap) waste on food-prep sinks, and the right cleaning frequency — are easy to get wrong because they look like over-engineering on a clean new pipe, and the maintenance decisions (hot-water flushing, line jetting frequency) can actively make grease worse if done without heat recovery downstream. Agents also connect food-prep sinks directly to the sewer (a health-code violation requiring an air gap) and route mop-sink waste through the grease system (which the chemicals defeat). This skill covers slope and cleanout requirements, direct versus indirect waste, cleaning frequency, hot-water-flush risks, sink-drainage configuration, and mop-sink separation.

## Core Rules

### Design FOG-Laden Lines With Adequate Slope and Cleanout Spacing

Grease-laden lines need enough slope to keep solids moving but not so much that the water outruns the grease. The IPC and UPC prescribe minimum slopes: 1/4 inch per foot for pipes up to 3 inches, and 1/8 inch per foot for pipes 4 inches and larger for sanitary drainage; FOG lines should favor the higher slope (1/4 inch) to keep grease and solids in suspension. Cleanouts are required at every change of direction greater than 45 degrees and at intervals not exceeding 100 feet (and often less by local amendment for kitchen lines), with access for powered routing and jetting equipment. The trap is running a long FOG line at minimum slope with no intermediate cleanout, so the first blockage is unreachable. The disciplined rule is to slope FOG lines at 1/4 inch per foot, install cleanouts at every direction change and at intervals, and size cleanouts for the equipment used.

### Use Indirect (Air-Gap) Waste for Food-Prep Sinks and Direct-Connect Only Where Code Allows

Food-prep sinks, pot sinks, and other fixtures that contact food or food-soiled utensils generally require an indirect waste (an air gap) to the drainage system: the fixture discharges through an air gap (typically 1 to 2 inches, or twice the pipe diameter) into a floor sink or floor drain, preventing a backup from contaminating the fixture and the food. Direct connection of food-prep sinks to the sewer is a health-code violation in most jurisdictions. The grease interceptor itself receives the indirect discharge downstream. Fixtures that may be direct-connected (where code allows) include hand-washing sinks and some equipment drains, but food-contact fixtures must be indirect. The trap is direct-connecting a three-compartment sink for simplicity. The disciplined rule is to use indirect waste (air gap to a floor sink or drain) for all food-contact fixtures, and to verify the local direct-connect exceptions.

### Set Cleaning Frequency by FOG Load, Not by a Calendar Default

Grease-line cleaning frequency depends on the FOG load and the volume, not a generic quarterly schedule. A high-volume fry kitchen may need jetting every 4 to 6 weeks; a low-volume cafe may need it quarterly. The right frequency is the one that keeps the line open between cleanings, established by inspection (camera or by the rate of slow-drain calls). Cleaning methods include mechanical routing (for blockages), hydro-jetting at temperatures that keep grease in solution (and with a downstream interceptor that can receive it), and biological/enzyme dosing (a supplement, not a replacement for physical cleaning). The trap is waiting for a backup to clean. The disciplined rule is to set the cleaning interval by load and inspection, to jet before slow drains appear, and to record each cleaning.

### Understand the Risks of Hot-Water Flushing on Grease Lines

Hot water flushes and chemical grease-cutters dissolve grease locally, but the grease re-solidifies wherever the water cools — often further down the line or in the city main, where it causes a worse blockage. A hot-water flush that clears a kitchen line can push the grease into the building sewer or the municipal main and create a downstream stoppage that is harder to reach. Hot-water jetting is effective only when the grease can be captured at a downstream interceptor or pulled back to the access point. Chemical treatments (caustics, enzymes) can help maintain flow but do not replace physical removal and can damage pipes or upset the interceptor biology if overused. The trap is repeated hot-water flushing that moves the problem downstream. The disciplined rule is to use hot-water jetting with a capture point, to avoid blind flushing that pushes grease into the city main, and to rely on physical removal and pumping.

### Configure Multi-Compartment Sink Drainage and Separate Mop Sink Waste

Three-compartment sinks (wash, rinse, sanitize) drain through a common indirect waste to a floor sink, with each compartment trapped or combined under a single trap per code; the drainage must not bypass the grease interceptor. Two-compartment sinks follow the same indirect rule. Mop sinks (floor-mounted service sinks for dirty wash water) must drain to the sanitary system, not to the grease interceptor, because the mop water contains chemicals, dirt, and solids that defeat FOG separation and fill the interceptor. Hand-washing sinks drain to the sanitary system (not the grease line). The trap is combining mop-sink or hand-sink waste with the grease line for piping convenience. The disciplined rule is to route food-contact fixtures indirectly through the grease system, and to route mop sinks, hand sinks, and sanitary waste directly to the sanitary system.

## Common Traps

### Running a Long FOG Line at Minimum Slope With No Intermediate Cleanout

The plumber runs a 60-foot grease line at 1/8 inch per foot with cleanouts only at the ends. The trap is that the first blockage is in the middle and unreachable. The mechanism is that low slope lets grease and solids settle, and long runs without access defeat routing. The false signal is that "it drains fine new." The harm is a blockage that requires cutting the pipe to reach. The defense is to slope FOG lines at 1/4 inch per foot and install cleanouts at every direction change and at intervals not exceeding 100 feet (or less by local code).

### Direct-Connecting a Food-Prep Sink to the Sewer

The plumber runs a three-compartment sink directly into the drain without an air gap. The trap is that a backup can contaminate the food-contact fixture. The mechanism is that a direct connection has no backflow prevention. The false signal is that "it drains faster direct." The harm is a health-code violation and contamination risk. The defense is to use indirect waste (air gap to a floor sink or drain) for all food-contact fixtures and verify local exceptions.

### Waiting for a Backup to Clean the Grease Line

The kitchen cleans the line only when it backs up. The trap is that a backup means the line is already narrowed by months of grease crust. The mechanism is that grease builds continuously between cleanings. The false signal is that "it's draining, so it's fine." The harm is recurring emergencies and eventual line replacement. The defense is to set the cleaning interval by FOG load and inspection, jet before slow drains appear, and record each cleaning.

### Blind Hot-Water Flushing That Pushes Grease Into the City Main

The plumber repeatedly hot-flushes a slow kitchen line without a downstream capture point. The trap is that the grease re-solidifies downstream. The mechanism is that dissolved grease drops out wherever the water cools. The false signal is that "the line is clear now." The harm is a worse stoppage in the building sewer or municipal main and potential municipal liability. The defense is to jet with a capture point (interceptor or pull-back), avoid blind flushing, and rely on physical removal and interceptor pumping.

### Combining Mop-Sink or Hand-Sink Waste With the Grease Line

The plumber ties the mop sink or hand sink into the grease line for piping convenience. The trap is that mop chemicals and solids defeat FOG separation. The mechanism is that the mop water fills and emulsifies the interceptor contents. The false signal is that "it's all drain water." The harm is rapid interceptor fill, breakthrough, and FOG discharge. The defense is to route mop sinks, hand sinks, and sanitary waste directly to the sanitary system, and to reserve the grease line for FOG-generating fixtures.

## Self-Check

- Did I slope FOG-laden lines at 1/4 inch per foot (or per local code for the pipe size) to keep grease and solids in suspension?
- Did I install cleanouts at every change of direction greater than 45 degrees and at intervals not exceeding 100 feet (or less by local amendment), sized for routing and jetting equipment?
- Did I use indirect waste (air gap to a floor sink or floor drain) for all food-contact fixtures (three-compartment, pot, pre-rinse sinks), and reserve direct-connect for fixtures the code allows?
- Did I set the grease-line cleaning frequency by FOG load and inspection (camera, slow-drain rate), rather than a generic calendar schedule, and record each cleaning?
- When hot-water jetting, did I provide a downstream capture point (interceptor or pull-back) rather than blind-flushing grease into the building sewer or municipal main?
- Did I route the three-compartment and two-compartment sink drainage through the grease interceptor via indirect waste, with proper trapping per code?
- Did I route mop-sink, hand-sink, and sanitary waste directly to the sanitary system, not into the grease line?
- Did I verify that no food grinder discharges into the grease line, and that solids are handled separately?
- Did I confirm the line size accommodates peak kitchen flow (fixture units) without excessive velocity or grease drop-out?
- Did I document the slope, cleanout locations, indirect-waste arrangement, connected fixtures, and cleaning schedule for the permit and maintenance record?
