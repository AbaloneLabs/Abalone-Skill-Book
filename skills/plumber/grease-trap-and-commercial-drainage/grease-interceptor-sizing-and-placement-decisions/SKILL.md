# Grease Interceptor Sizing and Placement Decisions

A grease interceptor that is undersized, mislocated, or connected to the wrong fixtures will back up the kitchen within weeks and trigger municipal FOG (fats, oils, grease) discharge violations and fines. The judgment problem is that sizing is a hydraulic calculation (flow rate in GPM and fixture capacity), not a guess based on sink count, and the choice between a hydromechanical interceptor (small, under-sink, flow-controlled) and a gravity interceptor (large, outdoor, residence-time-based) depends on the load and the municipality's code. Agents also connect fixtures that must not enter the interceptor (food grinders, sanitary waste, toilets) and omit fixtures that must (pot sinks, three-compartment sinks), and they forget the flow restrictor that makes a hydromechanical unit work. This skill covers sizing by PDI and IPC methods, interceptor-type selection, fixture-connection rules, flow-restrictor sizing, and placement decisions that prevent backups and FOG violations.

## Core Rules

### Size by Flow Rate (GPM) and Fixture Capacity, Using PDI or IPC Methods

Hydromechanical grease interceptors (HGIs) are sized by peak flow in GPM, derived from the fixture dimensions and the drain outlet, using the Plumbing and Drainage Institute (PDI) G101 method or the International Plumbing Code (IPC) method. The PDI method sums the fixture volumes (length times width times depth to the flood rim, in cubic inches, divided by 231 for gallons) times a fill factor (typically 75 percent for a sink that is three-quarters full when drained) times a drainage load factor, divided by a one- or two-minute drainage period, yielding the GPM rating. The IPC method uses flow-control devices and rates the interceptor at the controlled flow. An undersized interceptor passes grease through (breakthrough) because residence time is too short; an oversized one is wasteful but functional. The trap is sizing by sink count. The disciplined rule is to calculate the GPM from fixture dimensions per PDI or IPC and select the rated interceptor.

### Choose Hydromechanical, Gravity, or Outdoor Gravity by Load and Code

Hydromechanical interceptors (HGIs, 5 to 100 GPM) are small, installed indoors near the fixtures, rely on flow control and baffles, and suit moderate loads; they need frequent maintenance (weekly to monthly) but are accessible. Gravity (passive) interceptors, typically 500 to 2500 gallons and installed outdoors below grade, use residence time (typically 30 minutes) to separate FOG by density, suit high loads, and are pumped on a schedule (weeks to months). Many municipalities now require an outdoor gravity interceptor for new commercial kitchens regardless of indoor units. The choice depends on the FOG load, the available space, the local code (which may mandate outdoor), and the maintenance regime the operator can sustain. The trap is defaulting to an under-sink HGI for a high-volume kitchen. The disciplined rule is to match the interceptor type to the load and the local code, and to verify the municipal requirement before selecting.

### Connect the Required Fixtures and Exclude the Prohibited Ones

Only fixtures that generate FOG should drain to the interceptor: three-compartment sinks, pot sinks, pre-rinse sinks, wok stations, and soup kettles. Fixtures that must not connect include food (garbage) grinders (the solids overwhelm the interceptor and should discharge to a solids separator or the sanitary line), mop sinks (the chemicals and solids interfere with FOG separation and belong on the sanitary line), and any toilet or urinal (sanitary only). Dishwashers are code-dependent: some jurisdictions require dishwasher flow through the interceptor, others prohibit it because the heat and chemicals emulsify grease and defeat separation. The trap is connecting everything for simplicity or omitting a pot sink for convenience. The disciplined rule is to connect only FOG-generating fixtures, to verify the dishwasher rule locally, and to route grinders, mop sinks, and sanitary waste to the sanitary line.

### Size and Install the Flow Restrictor for Hydromechanical Interceptors

A hydromechanical interceptor works only at its rated flow, which is enforced by a flow restrictor (a plate or device with a calibrated orifice) installed on the inlet, ahead of the interceptor. The restrictor limits the flow to the interceptor's GPM rating, ensuring the residence time needed for separation; without it, a sudden dump from a large sink overwhelms the interceptor and pushes grease through. The restrictor must match the interceptor rating (a 25 GPM unit takes a 25 GPM restrictor), be installed vertically or as the manufacturer specifies, and be kept clear of debris. The trap is omitting the restrictor or installing the wrong size. The disciplined rule is to install the matching flow restrictor on every HGI, verify its size, and confirm it is clear at every service.

### Place the Interceptor for Access, Venting, and Code Clearance

Placement determines whether the interceptor gets maintained. An indoor HGI goes under the fixture or nearby, with the lid accessible for cleaning and the air intake (for air-relief-type units) clear; an outdoor gravity interceptor goes below grade in a location accessible to the pumping truck (hose reach and truck access), with the lid removable and not under permanent obstruction (no built-in counters or equipment over indoor units, no parked equipment over outdoor vaults). Venting is required: the interceptor needs a vent on the inlet and outlet to prevent siphoning and to carry sewer gases away. The trap is burying an outdoor interceptor where the truck cannot reach or boxing in an indoor unit. The disciplined rule is to place for pumping and cleaning access, with removable lids, proper venting, and no permanent obstruction over the unit.

## Common Traps

### Sizing the Interceptor by Sink Count Instead of Calculated GPM

The plumber counts the sinks and picks a "20 GPM" or "50 GPM" unit by feel. The trap is that fixture dimensions and drainage period drive the flow, not the count. The mechanism is that a single large three-compartment sink can demand more GPM than several small hand sinks. The false signal is that "more sinks means bigger unit." The harm is an undersized interceptor that breaks through and a FOG discharge violation. The defense is to calculate the GPM from fixture dimensions per PDI or IPC and select the rated interceptor.

### Defaulting to an Under-Sink HGI for a High-Volume Kitchen

The plumber installs a small under-sink HGI for a busy restaurant because it is cheap and easy. The trap is that the FOG load overwhelms a small unit within days. The mechanism is that hydromechanical units have limited residence time and capacity. The false signal is that "any grease trap satisfies code." The harm is frequent breakthrough, kitchen backups, and municipal penalties. The defense is to match the interceptor type to the load and the local code, and to verify whether the municipality requires an outdoor gravity interceptor.

### Connecting a Food Grinder or Mop Sink to the Interceptor

The plumber connects the food grinder or mop sink to the interceptor for plumbing simplicity. The trap is that solids and chemicals defeat FOG separation. The mechanism is that grinder solids fill the interceptor and mop-sink chemicals emulsify the grease. The false signal is that "it all goes to the same place." The harm is rapid fill, breakthrough, and interceptor failure. The defense is to connect only FOG-generating fixtures, route grinders and mop sinks to the sanitary line, and verify the local dishwasher rule.

### Omitting or Mis-sizing the Flow Restrictor on a Hydromechanical Unit

The plumber installs the HGI without the flow restrictor or with the wrong size. The trap is that the interceptor cannot control flow without it. The mechanism is that the restrictor sets the rated flow that gives separation residence time. The false signal is that "the interceptor is rated 25 GPM." The harm is grease breakthrough on the first large dump. The defense is to install the matching flow restrictor on the inlet, verify its size, and confirm it is clear at every service.

### Burying an Outdoor Interceptor Where the Pumping Truck Cannot Reach

The plumber places the outdoor gravity interceptor in a courtyard or behind a permanent structure where the pumper hose cannot reach. The trap is that the interceptor cannot be pumped. The mechanism is that pumpers have a finite hose length and need truck access. The false signal is that "it is below grade, so location is flexible." The harm is skipped pumping, FOG overflow into the sewer, and municipal penalties. The defense is to place for pumping-truck access (hose reach and approach), with a removable lid and no permanent obstruction.

## Self-Check

- Did I size the interceptor by calculating the GPM from fixture dimensions (length times width times depth to flood rim, fill factor, drainage period) per the PDI or IPC method, rather than by sink count?
- Did I select the interceptor type (hydromechanical, gravity, outdoor gravity) to match the FOG load and the local municipal code, and verify whether the jurisdiction requires an outdoor gravity unit?
- Did I connect only FOG-generating fixtures (three-compartment, pot, pre-rinse, wok, soup kettle) and route grinders, mop sinks, and sanitary waste to the sanitary line?
- Did I verify the local rule for dishwasher flow through the interceptor before connecting or excluding it?
- Did I install the matching flow restrictor on the inlet of every hydromechanical interceptor, verify its size against the rating, and confirm it is clear?
- Is the indoor interceptor placed with the lid accessible for cleaning, the air intake clear, and no permanent obstruction (counter, equipment) over it?
- Is the outdoor gravity interceptor placed for pumping-truck access (hose reach and approach), with a removable lid and no permanent obstruction?
- Is the interceptor vented on inlet and outlet to prevent siphoning and carry sewer gases away?
- Did I confirm the interceptor's rated capacity meets or exceeds the calculated peak GPM, including any future fixture additions?
- Did I document the sizing calculation, interceptor rating, connected fixtures, flow-restrictor size, and placement for the permit and maintenance record?
