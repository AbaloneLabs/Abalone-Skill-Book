# Grease Interceptor and Pretreatment Sizing

Commercial kitchens, food service, and industrial facilities discharge waste that cannot go directly to the sanitary sewer: fats, oils, and grease (FOG) from kitchens (which clog sewer lines), oil and petroleum from vehicle service areas, sand and grit from car washes, and acid from laboratory or process waste. These require pretreatment — grease interceptors, oil separators, sand traps, and acid neutralization — sized and selected for the specific waste stream, flow rate, and facility. The judgment problem is that an undersized or misapplied interceptor fails to remove the contaminant (grease passes through to the sewer, oil bypasses the separator), and the facility faces sewer backups, code violations, and sewer-use surcharges. A plumber who sizes a grease interceptor by "a standard 50-gallon" without calculating the flow and the fixture load installs a future failure. This skill covers the sizing and selection decisions for grease interceptors and industrial pretreatment.

## Core Rules

### Size the Grease Interceptor by Flow Rate and Fixture Capacity

Grease interceptors are sized by the flow rate they must handle (in gallons per minute, GPM) and the capacity of the fixtures discharging to them. Two sizing approaches are common: the flow rate method (calculating the GPM from the fixture drain sizes, the flow rates, and a 1-minute or 2-minute retention time), and the fixture capacity method (summing the fixture volumes, such as a 3-compartment sink, a mop sink, a tilting skillet, and a dishwasher, and sizing the interceptor to hold a fraction of the total with adequate retention). Hydromechanical grease interceptors (HGIs, the smaller under-sink or near-sink units, rated by GPM flow) are sized by the flow rate; gravity grease interceptors (GGIs, the larger outdoor or below-grade concrete tanks, rated by volume) are sized by the flow rate and the retention time (typically 30 minutes). The trap is sizing by a "standard" size without calculating the flow or the fixture capacity. The disciplined rule is to size the interceptor by the calculated flow rate and fixture capacity, using the manufacturer's sizing method or the code sizing method, and to document the sizing.

### Choose Hydromechanical Versus Gravity Interceptors by the Application

Hydromechanical grease interceptors (HGIs) are smaller, flow-control-based units (typically 10 to 100 GPM) that use a baffle and a vent to separate grease, suitable for smaller kitchens or where space is limited; they require frequent maintenance (weekly to monthly pumping) and are less effective at high or surge flows. Gravity grease interceptors (GGIs) are larger (typically 500 to 2000 gallons or more) concrete or fiberglass tanks that use retention time and gravity to separate grease, suitable for larger kitchens and providing longer service intervals (monthly to quarterly pumping); they require outdoor or below-grade space. The choice depends on the kitchen size, the available space, the maintenance capability, and the local code (some jurisdictions require GGIs for all commercial kitchens; others allow HGIs for smaller operations). The trap is choosing an HGI for a large kitchen (inadequate capacity, frequent pumping, grease bypass) or a GGI for a small kitchen (unnecessary cost and space). The disciplined rule is to choose the interceptor type by the application, the kitchen size, the space, and the code, and to document the rationale.

### Locate the Interceptor Accessibly and on the Correct Fixtures

The grease interceptor must be located accessibly for maintenance (pumping, cleaning, and inspection), and it must receive the discharge from the grease-producing fixtures (3-compartment sink, prep sinks, mop sink, tilting skillet, wok stations, combination ovens) — but not from non-grease fixtures (toilets, urinals, hand-washing lavatories, which go directly to the sanitary sewer, not through the interceptor). Routing non-grease fixtures through the interceptor overloads it with unnecessary flow and dilutes the retention; routing grease fixtures around the interceptor defeats its purpose. The trap is routing the wrong fixtures to the interceptor (overloading it with clean water, or bypassing it with grease fixtures). The disciplined rule is to route only the grease-producing fixtures to the interceptor, to route non-grease fixtures directly to the sewer, and to locate the interceptor accessibly for maintenance.

### Address Other Pretreatment: Oil, Sand, and Acid Waste

Beyond grease, industrial and specialty facilities require other pretreatment: oil separators (for vehicle service areas, parking garages, and machine shops, to remove petroleum oil before the sewer), sand traps (for car washes and vehicle service, to settle sand and grit), and acid neutralization tanks (for laboratories, dental offices, and industrial processes with acid waste, to neutralize the pH before the sewer). Each pretreatment device is sized and selected for the specific waste stream, flow rate, and code requirement, and each requires periodic maintenance (oil removal, sand cleanout, neutralizing media replacement). The trap is omitting the pretreatment for a specialty waste stream (oil, sand, acid), allowing the untreated waste to enter the sewer, violating the sewer-use code and damaging the sewer. The disciplined rule is to identify the waste streams in the facility, to provide the appropriate pretreatment for each, and to size and maintain each device per the code and the manufacturer.

### Maintain the Interceptor and Document the Pumping

A grease interceptor or pretreatment device only works if it is maintained: a grease interceptor that is full of grease passes grease through to the sewer; an oil separator full of oil passes oil through; an acid neutralization tank with exhausted media passes acid through. The maintenance frequency depends on the loading (a busy restaurant may need monthly grease pumping; a light kitchen quarterly), and the maintenance must be documented (pumping records, waste manifests) for the sewer utility and the inspector. The trap is installing the interceptor and neglecting the maintenance, which allows it to fill and fail. The disciplined rule is to establish the maintenance frequency based on the loading, to maintain the interceptor or device per the frequency, and to document the maintenance.

## Common Traps

### Sizing by a "Standard" Size Without Calculating Flow or Fixture Capacity

A plumber installs a "standard 50-gallon" grease interceptor without calculating the flow rate or the fixture capacity, and the interceptor is undersized for a busy kitchen, passing grease to the sewer. The trap is that the standard size works for a small kitchen but fails for a large one. The mechanism is that the interceptor must match the flow and fixture load. The false signal is that "an interceptor is installed." The harm is sewer backups and code violations. The defense is to size by the calculated flow rate and fixture capacity using the manufacturer or code method.

### Choosing an HGI for a Large Kitchen or a GGI for a Small Kitchen

A plumber chooses a hydromechanical interceptor for a large, busy kitchen, and the HGI is overwhelmed, requiring constant pumping and passing grease; or chooses a gravity interceptor for a small kitchen, wasting cost and space. The trap is that the type must match the application. The mechanism is that HGIs and GGIs have different capacities and service intervals. The false signal is that "an interceptor is selected." The harm is inadequate treatment or unnecessary cost. The defense is to choose the interceptor type by the application, kitchen size, space, and code.

### Routing Non-Grease Fixtures Through the Interceptor or Bypassing Grease Fixtures

A plumber routes hand-washing lavatories or toilets through the grease interceptor, overloading it with clean water and diluting the retention, or bypasses the interceptor with a grease fixture (a tilting skillet), allowing grease to the sewer. The trap is that the wrong fixtures are routed. The mechanism is that only grease fixtures should route to the interceptor. The false signal is that "the fixtures are connected." The harm is interceptor overload or grease bypass. The defense is to route only grease-producing fixtures to the interceptor and non-grease fixtures directly to the sewer.

### Omitting Pretreatment for Oil, Sand, or Acid Waste

A plumber omits the oil separator, sand trap, or acid neutralization for a facility with those waste streams, and the untreated waste enters the sewer, violating the code and damaging the sewer. The trap is that the pretreatment is omitted because the waste stream is overlooked. The mechanism is that specialty waste requires specialty pretreatment. The false signal is that "the waste goes to the sewer." The harm is sewer damage and code violations. The defense is to identify the waste streams and provide the appropriate pretreatment for each.

### Installing the Interceptor and Neglecting the Maintenance

A plumber installs a grease interceptor and the facility neglects the maintenance, allowing the interceptor to fill and pass grease to the sewer. The trap is that the interceptor is installed but not maintained. The mechanism is that maintenance is required for function. The false signal is that "the interceptor is in place." The harm is sewer backups and the interceptor's failure. The defense is to establish the maintenance frequency, maintain per the frequency, and document the maintenance.

## Self-Check

- Did I size the grease interceptor by the calculated flow rate (GPM) and the fixture capacity (the 3-compartment sink, mop sink, tilting skillet, dishwasher), using the manufacturer or code sizing method?
- Did I choose the interceptor type (hydromechanical for smaller or space-limited, gravity for larger) based on the application, kitchen size, space, and code?
- Did I route only the grease-producing fixtures to the interceptor, and route non-grease fixtures (toilets, lavatories) directly to the sewer?
- Did I locate the interceptor accessibly for maintenance (pumping, cleaning, inspection)?
- For facilities with oil, sand, or acid waste, did I provide the appropriate pretreatment (oil separator, sand trap, acid neutralization tank), sized for the waste stream and flow?
- Did I confirm the pretreatment device is sized and selected per the code and the manufacturer for the specific waste stream?
- Did I establish the maintenance frequency for the interceptor and each pretreatment device based on the loading?
- Did I document the maintenance (pumping records, waste manifests) for the sewer utility and the inspector?
- Did I verify that the interceptor and pretreatment discharge meets the local sewer-use limits (FOG concentration, oil concentration, pH)?
- Did I document the sizing calculations, the device selection, the routing, and the maintenance plan, so the installation is verifiable and maintainable?
