# Preaction System Detection and Activation Logic

A preaction sprinkler system holds the piping dry (or partially dry) and charges it with water only after a separate fire detection system confirms a fire, combining the freeze protection of a dry system with a defense against accidental water damage in high-value spaces such as data centers, museums, and freezer storage. The judgment problem is that the activation logic — single-interlock versus double-interlock — defines how many independent signals are required before water enters the piping, and getting it wrong means either too little protection (water arrives too slowly or not at all in a fire) or too little defense against accidental discharge (a single fault floods a server room). An agent who treats preaction as "a dry pipe valve with a detector," who lets the sprinklers themselves serve as the detection, or who ignores the supervisory and anti-flood logic will produce a system that either fails to suppress a fire or floods a facility on a false signal. This skill covers the detection and activation decisions that determine whether a preaction system protects both the hazard and the property — and the role limits that place this work with licensed fire protection personnel.

## Core Rules

### Choose Single-Interlock or Double-Interlock by the Accidental-Discharge Tolerance

Preaction systems come in two principal logics. Single-interlock preaction admits water to the piping when one fire signal occurs — the detection system (smoke or heat detectors) actuates, the preaction valve opens, water fills the piping, and water flows only from sprinklers whose glass bulb has fused (so a single detection event primes the system but does not discharge water unless a head opens). Double-interlock preaction requires two independent signals before water enters the piping: both the detection system must actuate and a sprinkler head must fuse (loss of piping supervisory pressure), and only then does the valve open. Double-interlock is used where accidental discharge is unacceptable (data centers, freezer storage, archives) because a single fault — a false detector or a single broken head — cannot flood the space. The trap is choosing single-interlock where the contents cannot tolerate an accidental prime-and-discharge, or double-interlock where the added delay in water delivery lets a fire grow beyond control. Match the interlock type to the value and the hazard, and document the rationale.

### Keep Detection Independent of the Sprinklers

In a preaction system, the fire detection that triggers the valve must be a separate, independent system — smoke detectors, heat detectors, flame detectors, or a pneumatic pilot line — not the sprinkler heads themselves. The detection system exists to give an early, deliberate signal before a head fuses, and it must be wired or piped to the preaction valve's release panel or actuator through its own circuit. The trap is using the sprinkler line pressure (or the dry-pipe network's own air pressure) as the sole "detection," which makes the system a dry-pipe system in disguise and removes the early signal; the harm is delayed water delivery and the loss of the preaction's accidental-discharge defense. Install detection listed for the application, position it per the spacing rules for the hazard (typically more sensitive and denser than sprinkler spacing), and keep its wiring or tubing fully separate from the sprinkler supervisory circuit.

### Configure Supervisory Pressure and Anti-Flood Logic for Double-Interlock

Double-interlock preaction supervises the sprinkler piping with low-pressure air or nitrogen (typically a few psi, monitored by a pressure switch) so that a fused head or a broken pipe is detected as a pressure loss; the valve opens only when both the detection signal and the supervisory pressure-loss signal are present. This supervisory pressure must be maintained by a reliable air source (compressed air or nitrogen, with a listed compressor or cylinder and a pressure-maintenance device), and the pressure switch must be set to alarm on a defined drop. Anti-flood logic prevents the valve from opening on a single signal: the releasing panel or mechanical interlock requires both inputs simultaneously (or within a defined window) to actuate. The trap is a leaky supervisory line that drifts down and, combined with a detector false alarm, trips the valve; the harm is an accidental flood in the very space the double-interlock was meant to protect. Maintain supervisory pressure integrity, set the pressure switch with margin above the trip point, and test the anti-flood logic by simulating each signal alone (no trip) and both together (trip).

### Weigh Preaction Versus Dry Pipe by Speed, Freeze Risk, and Damage Tolerance

Preaction and dry-pipe both serve freeze-sensitive spaces, but they differ in water-delivery speed and accidental-discharge defense. A dry-pipe valve trips when a head fuses and the piping air pressure drops; water delivery is delayed by the time to vent the air (NFPA 13 limits this to 60 seconds to deliver water at the remote inspector's test for most systems). A single-interlock preaction can deliver water faster (the detection primes the piping before a head fuses), while a double-interlock preaction is typically slower than dry pipe because it waits for two signals. The tradeoff: preaction adds accidental-discharge protection (valuable in high-value spaces) at the cost of complexity, detection maintenance, and — for double-interlock — slower delivery. The trap is specifying double-interlock preaction for a high-hazard space where the delivery delay lets the fire exceed design size, or specifying dry pipe for a data center where an accidental discharge is catastrophic. Choose the system type by matching freeze risk, hazard growth rate, water-delivery requirement, and accidental-discharge tolerance.

### Coordinate Activation Sequencing With Alarm, Shutdown, and Restoration

Preaction activation does not happen in isolation: when the valve trips, the fire alarm must signal, associated equipment may need to shut down (power to a data center, air handlers that would spread smoke), and the system must be restored manually after inspection. The releasing panel must be integrated with the building fire alarm (the preaction trip is a supervisory or alarm signal), and any interlocks (door release, elevator recall, HVAC shutdown) must sequence correctly. After a trip, the system is not automatically restored: the cause must be investigated, the piping drained, the valve reset, the detection and supervisory restored, and the system returned to service by trained personnel. The trap is leaving the interlocks uncoordinated so that, for example, a preaction trip fails to shut down air handlers that then spread water and smoke, or failing to restore the supervisory pressure after a test so the system is left in a degraded state. Coordinate all interlocks at commissioning, test the full sequence, and follow the manufacturer's restoration procedure after every trip or test.

## Common Traps

### Choosing Single-Interlock Where Contents Cannot Tolerate Accidental Discharge

The designer specifies single-interlock preaction for a data center because it is simpler and faster, reasoning that the detection "rarely false-trips." The trap mechanism is that single-interlock primes the piping on a single detection signal, and if a head has been damaged or a fitting has failed (or a head fuses from a non-fire cause), water flows immediately — flooding the very space the preaction was meant to protect. The false signal is that "the system is preaction, so it is safe." The harm is a catastrophic flood in a high-value space from a single fault. The defense is to choose double-interlock where accidental discharge is unacceptable, accepting the delivery delay and the added complexity.

### Letting Sprinkler Pressure Serve as the Sole Detection

The installer wires the preaction valve to trip on sprinkler piping air-pressure loss alone, without an independent detection system. The trap mechanism is that this removes the early signal and the accidental-discharge defense, making the system functionally a dry-pipe system, and it loses the preaction benefit while retaining the preaction complexity. The false signal is that "the valve is a preaction valve." The harm is delayed water delivery (no early prime) and the loss of the two-signal defense. The defense is to install independent detection (smoke, heat, flame, or pneumatic pilot) listed for the application, separate from the sprinkler supervisory circuit.

### A Leaky Supervisory Line Plus a False Detector Trips the Valve

The double-interlock system's supervisory air pressure drifts down over weeks due to a slow leak, and a false smoke alarm provides the detection signal, tripping the valve and flooding the space. The trap mechanism is that double-interlock requires both signals but does not distinguish a real fire from a coincidental pair of faults, and a degrading supervisory line combined with any detector false alarm produces a flood. The false signal is that "two signals means it must be a real fire." The harm is an accidental discharge in a high-value space. The defense is to maintain supervisory pressure integrity, alarm on pressure drift well above the trip point, and investigate any drift or false alarm promptly.

### Specifying Double-Interlock for a Fast-Growing Hazard

The designer chooses double-interlock preaction for a high-hazard storage area to protect against accidental discharge, but the added water-delivery delay (waiting for both detection and a fused head) lets the fire grow beyond the design size before water arrives. The trap mechanism is that double-interlock trades speed for accidental-discharge protection, and for a fast-growing hazard the trade is wrong — the fire exceeds what the sprinklers can control. The false signal is that "preaction is more sophisticated, so it is better." The harm is an uncontrolled fire. The defense is to weigh preaction versus dry pipe (or even wet pipe) by the hazard growth rate and the water-delivery requirement, not by a preference for sophistication.

### Failing to Coordinate Interlocks or Restore Supervisory After a Test

After a trip test, the technician resets the valve but forgets to restore the supervisory air pressure or to re-arm the releasing panel, leaving the system in a state where it cannot trip in a real fire; or the interlocks were never coordinated, so a real trip fails to shut down air handlers that spread the water. The trap mechanism is that preaction restoration is a multi-step sequence and the interlocks are easy to overlook, and a partially restored or uncoordinated system is silently degraded. The false signal is that "the valve was reset." The harm is a system that fails to operate or causes collateral damage in the next event. The defense is to follow the manufacturer's full restoration procedure, verify supervisory pressure and panel status, and test the complete interlock sequence at commissioning and after every trip.

## Self-Check

- Did I choose single-interlock or double-interlock preaction based on the contents' tolerance for accidental discharge and the hazard's growth rate, and is the rationale documented?
- Is the fire detection that triggers the preaction valve an independent system (smoke, heat, flame, or pneumatic pilot) listed for the application, fully separate from the sprinkler supervisory circuit?
- For double-interlock, is the sprinkler piping supervised by maintained low-pressure air or nitrogen with a pressure switch set to alarm on a defined drop, with margin above the trip point?
- Did I test the anti-flood logic by simulating each signal alone (detection only, supervisory loss only — no trip) and both together (trip), confirming the valve opens only on the coincident pair?
- Did I weigh preaction versus dry pipe (and wet pipe) by freeze risk, hazard growth rate, water-delivery requirement, and accidental-discharge tolerance, rather than defaulting to preaction?
- Is the releasing panel integrated with the building fire alarm, and are all interlocks (HVAC shutdown, door release, elevator recall, power shutdown) coordinated and tested at commissioning?
- After any trip or test, did I follow the manufacturer's full restoration procedure — investigate cause, drain piping, reset valve, restore detection and supervisory pressure, re-arm panel — and verify the system is back in service?
- Did I confirm my licensing scope covers preaction system work, and did I escalate design and acceptance testing to a NICET-certified designer and licensed fire protection contractors?
- Are the interlock type, detection design, supervisory settings, anti-flood test results, interlock sequence, and restoration procedure documented for AHJ review and the NFPA 25 record?
