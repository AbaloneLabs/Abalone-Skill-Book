# Dry-Pipe and Preaction Trip Test Procedures

A dry-pipe or preaction valve that has never been tripped, or that was tripped long ago, may take far too long to deliver water — and the only way to know is the NFPA 25 trip test, which measures how fast the valve opens and how quickly water reaches the most remote outlet. The judgment problem is that dry and preaction valves are differential devices whose trip speed depends on priming pressure, air pressure, valve condition, and the length and volume of the piping, and a valve that trips in 30 seconds when new can drift to 90 seconds or fail to trip at all as seals harden, ports clog, and air pressure creeps up. An agent who runs a partial test instead of a full one, who measures trip time at the valve instead of water delivery at the remote inspector's test, or who restores the system without fully draining and re-priming will produce a test record that says "passed" while the system would fail a real fire. This skill covers the full and partial trip-test procedures, the 60-second delivery requirement, trip ratio, restoration, and documentation — and the role limits that place this work with licensed fire protection personnel.

## Core Rules

### Perform the Full Trip Test on the NFPA 25 Cycle, Not Just Partial Tests

NFPA 25 requires a full trip test of dry-pipe and preaction valves on a defined cycle (commonly annually for the full trip, with partial tests more frequently), during which the valve is actually opened and water is allowed to flow to the inspector's test connection. The full trip exercises the valve's differential mechanism under real conditions, measures the water-delivery time, and reveals degradation (slow trip, failure to reset, leaking clappers) that a partial or no-flow test cannot show. A partial trip (actuating the valve without full water flow) is a useful interim check but is not a substitute for the full trip. The trap is performing only partial trips year after year, recording "tested," and never discovering that the valve has slowed or stuck; the harm is a valve that fails to deliver water in a fire. Perform the full trip test on the required cycle, with the supply fully pressurized and water flowing to the inspector's test.

### Measure Water Delivery at the Remote Inspector's Test, Not Trip Time at the Valve

The performance metric that matters is how fast water reaches the hydraulically most remote outlet — the inspector's test connection at the end of the system — because that is how long a fire waits for suppression. NFPA 13 limits this delivery time to 60 seconds for most dry-pipe systems (and for preaction systems designed to that standard), measured from the moment the test connection is opened (venting the air) to the moment water flows at the remote inspector's test. Trip time at the valve itself (the moment the clapper lifts) is a useful diagnostic but is not the metric — a valve that trips fast but delivers water slowly (due to long piping, restricted ports, or excessive air volume) still fails the requirement. The trap is recording valve trip time and calling it delivery time; the harm is a system that trips fast but delivers water too slowly to control the fire. Always measure water delivery at the remote inspector's test with a stopwatch, and record both the valve trip time and the delivery time.

### Record the Trip Ratio to Detect Degradation Over Time

The trip ratio is the relationship between the priming pressure (or air pressure) at which the valve trips and the supply pressure, and it is a key diagnostic of valve condition. A dry-pipe valve is designed to trip at a specific differential (commonly a 1:5 or 1:6 ratio of air to water, depending on the valve), and as the valve ages the actual trip point drifts — a valve that should trip at 6:1 may not trip until 4:1, indicating a worn latching mechanism, hardened seals, or a compromised differential chamber. Recording the trip ratio at each full trip test lets the maintainer track degradation and predict when the valve needs service before it fails the 60-second delivery requirement. The trap is recording only "pass/fail" without the trip ratio; the harm is silent degradation that is not detected until the valve fails a test or, worse, a fire. Record the supply pressure, the air/priming pressure at trip, the calculated trip ratio, and the delivery time at every full trip test.

### Restore the System Fully: Drain, Inspect, Reset, Re-Prime

After a full trip test, the system is full of water and must be restored to service through a precise sequence: close the supply, fully drain the system (open all drain valves, including the main drain and auxiliary drains at low points, and verify no water remains), inspect the valve interior (clapper, seat, latching mechanism) for wear or debris, reset the valve (re-engage the latch or differential), re-prime the priming chamber to the correct pressure, restore the air pressure to the design setpoint, and slowly reopen the supply while monitoring for leaks or a false trip. Trapped water in a dry system corrodes the piping, freezes in cold spaces, and causes false trips when it eventually drains into the valve; trapped air in the priming chamber prevents reset. The trap is a partial drain and a quick reset, leaving water in low points or air in the chamber; the harm is corrosion, freeze damage, or a valve that will not hold. Follow the manufacturer's full restoration procedure, drain every low point, verify priming and air pressures, and confirm the valve holds before leaving it in service.

### Sequence the Preaction Trip Test to Verify Detection and Interlock Logic

A preaction valve trip test is more complex than a dry-pipe test because it must exercise the detection and interlock logic as well as the valve. The test sequence typically: simulate the fire detection signal (test a detector or the release circuit), confirm the releasing panel responds and the preaction valve actuates (for single-interlock, the valve opens and primes the piping; for double-interlock, also simulate the supervisory pressure loss), confirm water delivers at the inspector's test within the required time, confirm the alarm and interlocks operate (fire alarm, HVAC shutdown, etc.), and then restore the system per the manufacturer's procedure (drain, reset valve, restore detection and supervisory, re-arm panel). The trap is testing only the valve and skipping the detection and interlock verification; the harm is a valve that trips but whose detection or interlocks are degraded, so the system would not trip in a real fire or would cause collateral damage. Test the full sequence — detection, valve, delivery, alarm, interlocks — and document each step.

## Common Traps

### Running Partial Trip Tests Year After Year Instead of the Full Trip

The technician performs a partial trip (actuating the valve without full water flow) every year because it is faster and does not require draining, and records the system as tested. The trap mechanism is that a partial trip does not exercise the valve under full supply pressure, does not measure water-delivery time, and does not reveal the degradation (slow trip, stuck clapper, leaking seat) that only a full-flow test exposes. The false signal is that "the valve tripped." The harm is a valve that has silently degraded and fails to deliver water in a fire. The defense is to perform the full trip test on the NFPA 25 cycle, with the supply fully pressurized and water flowing to the inspector's test.

### Measuring Valve Trip Time Instead of Water Delivery at the Remote Test

The technician records the time from opening the test connection to the valve clapper lifting, and reports "delivered water in 25 seconds." The trap mechanism is that the metric that matters is water delivery at the hydraulically most remote inspector's test (60-second maximum), not the valve trip time — a valve can trip fast but deliver water slowly due to long piping, restricted ports, or excessive air volume, and the system still fails. The false signal is that "the valve tripped fast." The harm is a system that meets a trip-time metric but cannot deliver water to the fire within the required window. The defense is to measure water delivery at the remote inspector's test with a stopwatch and record both trip time and delivery time.

### Recording Pass/Fail Without the Trip Ratio

The technician records "full trip test: pass" without noting the supply pressure, air/priming pressure at trip, or trip ratio. The trap mechanism is that the trip ratio is the key diagnostic of valve degradation, and without it the maintainer cannot detect a valve whose trip point has drifted toward failure until it actually fails a test or a fire. The false signal is that "the test passed." The harm is silent degradation that crosses the failure threshold undetected. The defense is to record the supply pressure, air/priming pressure at trip, calculated trip ratio, and delivery time at every full trip test, and to trend the ratio over years.

### Partial Drain and Quick Reset Leaving Water or Air Trapped

After the trip test, the technician drains the main valve, resets the latch, re-primes, and returns the system to service without draining the low points or fully evacuating the priming chamber. The trap mechanism is that trapped water in low points corrodes the piping, freezes in cold spaces, and can drain into the valve and cause a false trip; trapped air in the priming chamber prevents the valve from holding and causes repeated false trips. The false signal is that "the valve was reset and is holding." The harm is corrosion, freeze damage, or chronic false trips. The defense is to follow the manufacturer's full restoration procedure, drain every low point, verify priming and air pressures, and confirm the valve holds before leaving it in service.

### Testing the Preaction Valve but Skipping Detection and Interlock Verification

The technician trips the preaction valve by manually venting the priming line, confirms water delivery, and records the test complete — without simulating the detection signal or verifying the alarm and interlocks. The trap mechanism is that a preaction system's reliability depends on its detection and interlock logic as much as on the valve, and a valve-only test cannot reveal a degraded detector, a failed releasing panel, or a mis-coordinated interlock. The false signal is that "the valve tripped and delivered water." The harm is a system that trips in a test but would not trip in a real fire, or that causes collateral damage when interlocks fail. The defense is to test the full sequence — detection, valve, delivery, alarm, interlocks — and document each step.

## Self-Check

- Did I perform the full trip test on the NFPA 25 required cycle (not merely partial trips), with the supply fully pressurized and water flowing to the inspector's test connection?
- Did I measure water delivery at the hydraulically most remote inspector's test (60-second maximum for most systems) with a stopwatch, recording both the valve trip time and the delivery time?
- Did I record the supply pressure, the air or priming pressure at trip, the calculated trip ratio, and the delivery time, so degradation can be trended over years?
- After the trip test, did I follow the manufacturer's full restoration procedure — close supply, drain all low points, inspect the valve interior, reset, re-prime, restore air pressure, slowly reopen supply — and verify the valve holds?
- Did I drain every low point and auxiliary drain so no water remains to corrode, freeze, or cause a false trip, and did I verify the priming chamber is fully evacuated of air before re-priming?
- For a preaction system, did I test the full sequence — simulate detection, confirm releasing panel and valve actuation, confirm delivery at the inspector's test, confirm alarm and interlocks — rather than tripping the valve manually?
- For double-interlock preaction, did I verify the anti-flood logic by simulating each signal alone (no trip) and both together (trip), in addition to the full trip test?
- Did I confirm my licensing scope covers dry-pipe and preaction trip testing, and did I perform the test under permit and with AHJ witness where required?
- Is the full trip test documented — date, personnel and certifications, supply and air pressures, trip ratio, delivery time, restoration steps, and any valve service performed — for AHJ review and the NFPA 25 record?
