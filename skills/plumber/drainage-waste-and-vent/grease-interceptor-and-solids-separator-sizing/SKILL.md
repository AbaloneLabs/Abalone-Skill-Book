# Grease Interceptor and Solids Separator Sizing

Grease and solids interceptors are the devices that keep fats, oils, grease (FOG), sediment, and other debris out of the building drain and the public sewer, and an undersized or misapplied interceptor causes sewer blockages, backflows, municipal surcharges, and in commercial kitchens, health-department shutdowns. The judgment problem is that interceptor sizing is governed by the flow rate and the grease load of the connected fixtures, not by the pipe size or the kitchen's square footage, and the choice between a hydromechanical grease interceptor (HGI, small, indoor, flow-controlled) and a gravity grease interceptor (GGI, large, outdoor, residence-time-based) depends on the application, the space, and the maintenance plan. A plumber who sizes an interceptor by rule of thumb ("a 50 gpm for a restaurant") will install a device that either overflows grease into the sewer (undersized) or takes up excessive space and is never maintained (oversized). This skill covers the sizing, selection, location, and maintenance decisions that make grease and solids interceptors effective.

## Core Rules

### Size Hydromechanical Grease Interceptors by Flow Rate From the Connected Fixture Load

A hydromechanical grease interceptor (HGI) is rated in gallons per minute (gpm) of flow it can treat while retaining grease, and the flow rate is determined by the connected fixtures, not by the drain pipe size. The standard sizing method (the PDI G101 or the UPC Appendix H method) totals the flow from each fixture: for each fixture, multiply the length by width by depth (in inches) of the sink or compartment, divide by 231 to convert to gallons, apply a fill-and-drain factor (commonly 0.75 for a single compartment, 1.0 for multiple compartments draining together), and convert to a flow rate (gpm) using the drain time assumption (commonly a 1-minute drain, or a 2-minute drain for large sinks). The fixture flow rates are summed to give the total required interceptor capacity, and an HGI with a gpm rating at or above that total is selected. The trap is sizing by the drain pipe size (a 2-inch drain "needs" a 50 gpm unit) or by the number of fixtures, which ignores the actual fixture volumes and drain rates. The disciplined rule is to calculate the flow from each fixture by volume and drain time, sum the flows, and select an HGI rated for that total gpm.

### Size Gravity Grease Interceptors by Residence Time and Grease Storage Capacity

A gravity grease interceptor (GGI) is a large outdoor tank (commonly 500 to 2000 gallons or more) that separates grease by residence time: the wastewater enters, slows, and the grease rises while the water exits, with the grease accumulating in a storage zone. GGI sizing is based on the peak flow rate and the required residence time (commonly 20 to 30 minutes for the grease to separate) plus a grease storage volume sized to the expected grease load between pumpings. The sizing methods (commonly the UPC 30-minute retention or a flow-based formula) produce a tank volume that provides the residence time at peak flow. The trap is sizing a GGI by "a 1000-gallon for a restaurant," which ignores the actual peak flow and the grease load, producing a tank that either does not provide adequate residence time (undersized) or is excessive and never pumped (oversized, anaerobic, and odorous). The disciplined rule is to calculate the peak flow from the connected fixtures (or the fixture-unit method), apply the required residence time, add the grease storage volume, and select a GGI of that total capacity.

### Match the Interceptor Type to the Application, Space, and Maintenance Plan

The choice between an HGI and a GGI depends on the application and the maintenance reality. An HGI is indoor, compact, flow-controlled (with a flow restrictor to prevent exceeding the rated flow), and requires frequent maintenance (weekly to monthly pumping in a busy kitchen); it is suited to smaller kitchens and to retrofit installations where outdoor space is unavailable. A GGI is outdoor, large, provides long residence time and large grease storage, and requires less frequent maintenance (monthly to quarterly pumping); it is suited to larger kitchens and new construction with outdoor space. A misapplication is installing an HGI where a GGI is required (the HGI overflows grease under high load) or a GGI where an HGI would suffice (excessive cost and space). The disciplined rule is to match the type to the kitchen size, the grease load, the available space, and the maintenance frequency the owner will realistically perform, and to document the maintenance schedule.

### Locate the Interceptor on the Fixture Drain Before the Building Sewer, and Exclude Non-Grease Fixtures

A grease interceptor must be located on the drain from the grease-producing fixtures (two- and three-compartment sinks, pot sinks, pre-rinse sinks, wok stoves, combi ovens, floor drains in food prep areas) before those drains join the building sewer, and non-grease fixtures (toilets, handwash sinks, mop sinks without grease) must be routed around the interceptor. Routing non-grease fixtures through the interceptor overloads it with flow (exceeding the rated gpm and pushing grease through) and adds nothing to the grease load the interceptor is meant to capture. Routing grease fixtures around the interceptor (a common error when a toilet is added to a kitchen) defeats the interceptor entirely. The trap is connecting all kitchen drains (including the toilet and handwash sink) to the interceptor, which overloads it. The disciplined rule is to identify every grease-producing fixture, route only those to the interceptor, and route all other fixtures directly to the building sewer.

### Size Solids and Sand/Oil Separators by the Contaminant and the Flow

Solids interceptors (for sediment, food waste, plaster, hair) and sand/oil separators (for automotive and industrial applications) are sized by the contaminant load and the flow rate, using the manufacturer sizing method or the code table. A solids interceptor must have a basket or baffle sized to the expected solids volume between cleanings, and a flow capacity that does not allow the solids to be carried through. A sand/oil separator (for a car wash or garage) must provide residence time for the oil to rise and the sand to settle, sized to the peak flow. The trap is installing a generic "trap" without sizing to the contaminant, which clogs, overflows, or passes the contaminant through. The disciplined rule is to identify the contaminant and the flow, size the separator per the manufacturer or code method, and schedule the cleaning frequency based on the expected load.

## Common Traps

### Sizing a Hydromechanical Interceptor by Drain Pipe Size

A plumber sizes an HGI by the drain pipe size, installing a 50 gpm unit because the drain is 2 inches, without calculating the actual fixture flow. The connected fixtures (a three-compartment sink plus a pre-rinse) actually produce 75 gpm at peak drain, and the undersized 50 gpm unit overflows grease into the sewer whenever both fixtures drain together. The trap is that the drain pipe size correlates loosely with flow but does not determine the interceptor capacity. The mechanism is that the interceptor's gpm rating is the flow it can treat, and exceeding it pushes grease through. The false signal is that "the unit is rated for a 2-inch drain." The harm is grease in the sewer, blockages, and municipal violations. The defense is to calculate the flow from each fixture by volume and drain time, sum the flows, and select an HGI rated for the total.

### Omitting the Flow Restrictor on a Hydromechanical Interceptor

A plumber installs an HGI without the flow restrictor (the orifice plate that limits the incoming flow to the rated gpm), reasoning that "the restrictor slows the drain." The trap is that without the restrictor, the fixtures can drain faster than the interceptor's rated flow, and the grease is pushed through the interceptor into the sewer, defeating the separation. The mechanism is that the HGI's separation depends on the flow not exceeding the rated gpm, and the restrictor is what enforces that. The false signal is that "the drain is fast now." The harm is grease passing through the interceptor. The defense is to always install the manufacturer flow restrictor sized to the interceptor's gpm rating, accepting the slower drain as the design condition.

### Routing All Kitchen Drains (Including Non-Grease Fixtures) Through the Interceptor

A plumber connects the kitchen's toilet and handwash sink to the grease interceptor along with the sinks, overloading the interceptor with flow that carries no grease. The trap is that the extra flow exceeds the rated gpm whenever the toilet flushes during sink draining, pushing grease through, and the interceptor fills with non-grease flow that adds nothing to its purpose. The mechanism is that the interceptor is sized for the grease-fixture flow only. The false signal is that "all the kitchen drains go to the interceptor." The harm is grease overflow and unnecessary interceptor loading. The defense is to route only grease-producing fixtures to the interceptor and to route toilets, handwash sinks, and mop sinks directly to the building sewer.

### Sizing a Gravity Interceptor by a Round Number Instead of Flow and Residence Time

A plumber installs a 1000-gallon gravity grease interceptor for a restaurant because "1000 gallons is standard," without calculating the peak flow or the required residence time. The connected kitchen actually produces a peak flow that requires a 1500-gallon tank for 30-minute residence, and the undersized 1000-gallon tank does not provide adequate residence time, so grease passes through. The trap is that the round number feels substantial but does not match the hydraulic requirement. The mechanism is that gravity separation requires residence time, and an undersized tank does not provide it. The false signal is that "the tank is 1000 gallons." The harm is grease in the sewer and recurring blockages. The defense is to calculate the peak flow, apply the required residence time, add the grease storage volume, and select a GGI of that total capacity.

### Installing an Interceptor With No Maintenance Plan, Allowing It to Fill and Bypass

A plumber installs a correctly sized grease interceptor but provides no maintenance schedule, and the owner never pumps it. The interceptor fills with grease, the inlet is blocked, and the incoming flow either backs up into the fixtures or bypasses through a failed seal, discharging grease to the sewer. The trap is that a full interceptor is worse than no interceptor, because it blocks the drain or bypasses the grease. The mechanism is that interceptors have finite storage, and a full unit stops functioning. The false signal is that "the interceptor is installed." The harm is fixture backups, sewer discharge violations, and health-department issues. The defense is to size the interceptor with a realistic maintenance frequency, to document the pumping schedule, and to educate the owner that the interceptor must be pumped on schedule or it fails.

## Self-Check

- Did I size each hydromechanical grease interceptor by calculating the flow from each connected fixture (volume by drain time), summing the flows, and selecting an HGI rated at or above the total gpm, rather than sizing by drain pipe size?
- Did I install the manufacturer flow restrictor on every HGI, sized to the interceptor's gpm rating, to prevent the rated flow from being exceeded?
- Did I size each gravity grease interceptor by the peak flow and the required residence time (commonly 20 to 30 minutes) plus the grease storage volume, rather than by a round-number tank size?
- Did I match the interceptor type (HGI vs GGI) to the kitchen size, grease load, available space, and the maintenance frequency the owner will realistically perform?
- Did I route only grease-producing fixtures (compartment sinks, pre-rinse, wok, combi, food-prep floor drains) to the interceptor, and route toilets, handwash sinks, and mop sinks directly to the building sewer?
- For solids and sand/oil separators, did I identify the contaminant and the flow, size the separator per the manufacturer or code method, and schedule the cleaning frequency based on the expected load?
- Did I locate the interceptor on the grease-fixture drain before the building sewer, with adequate access for maintenance (pumping, basket cleaning, inspection)?
- Did I provide a documented maintenance schedule (pumping frequency, basket cleaning frequency) sized to the interceptor's storage and the expected grease or solids load?
- Did I verify the interceptor installation meets the plumbing code and the local sewer-use ordinance (which may specify a minimum size or type), and coordinate with the health department for commercial kitchens?
- Did I document the fixture flow calculations, the selected interceptor type and capacity, the connected fixtures, the maintenance schedule, and the code/ordinance compliance, so the installation is verifiable and the interceptor is maintainable?
