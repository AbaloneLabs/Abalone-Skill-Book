# Whole-House Filtration and Contaminant Removal

Whole-house (point-of-entry) filtration treats all the water entering a building, and the judgment problem is that "filtration" is not one technology but a family of them, each effective against specific contaminants and useless or harmful against others. A carbon tank removes chlorine and some VOCs but does nothing for hardness, iron, or bacteria; an oxidizing filter removes iron and sulfur but not arsenic or lead; a specialty resin removes tannins but not sediment. The installer who recommends "a whole-house filter" without a water test, or who sequences the stages in the wrong order, will deliver a system that looks complete, passes water, and yet leaves the actual contaminants in the water — or worse, creates a new problem like bacterial growth in an oversized carbon tank. This skill covers matching media to contaminants, sequencing stages, sizing for flow and contact time, and the role limits that place complex or regulated-contaminant treatment with water treatment specialists and public-health coordination.

## Core Rules

### Start With a Comprehensive Water Test and Match Media to Contaminants

The single most important step is a water test that identifies the actual contaminants present, because media selection follows directly from the test results. Municipal supplies need testing for chlorine/chloramine, disinfection byproducts, lead (from service lines or plumbing), and any local contaminants (PFAS, industrial solvents). Well supplies need a broader panel: coliform bacteria, hardness, iron, manganese, pH, total dissolved solids, nitrate, arsenic, radon, uranium, and any regional contaminants (agricultural chemicals, petroleum, saltwater intrusion). The trap is recommending a filter based on the customer's complaint ("the water smells") without a test, then installing a carbon tank for a sulfur smell that actually requires an oxidizing iron/sulfur filter, or installing a sediment filter for staining that is actually iron. The disciplined approach is to require a comprehensive test (a certified lab for wells, the utility's consumer confidence report plus targeted testing for municipal), match each detected contaminant to a proven treatment technology, and document the match. Never promise removal of a contaminant the selected media does not treat.

### Sequence Treatment Stages in the Correct Order

When multiple contaminants require treatment, the order of the stages matters and is not interchangeable. The general sequence is: sediment filtration first (to protect downstream media from particulate fouling), then oxidation/filtration for iron, sulfur, and manganese (which would foul a softener or carbon if not removed first), then water softening (for hardness, and to protect downstream carbon and RO from scale), then carbon adsorption (for chlorine, VOCs, taste/odor), then specialty media (for arsenic, nitrate, PFAS, lead), then a final polish filter, and finally UV or chlorination for microbial control if required. The trap is putting carbon ahead of an iron filter, so the iron fouls the carbon bed within weeks; or putting a softener ahead of a sediment filter, so sediment loads the resin. The disciplined approach is to map the contaminants to media, then order the stages so each stage protects the next: sediment before everything, oxidation before softening and carbon, softening before carbon and RO, and microbial disinfection last.

### Size for Flow Rate and Contact Time, Not Just Tank Volume

Whole-house filters must pass the building's peak flow rate (commonly 10 to 15 gpm for a residential home with multiple fixtures, far higher for commercial) without excessive pressure drop, and adsorptive and catalytic media must provide adequate contact time (empty-bed contact time, EBCT) for the reaction to occur. Carbon tanks typically need 3 to 5 minutes EBCT for chloramine removal; oxidizing iron filters need a backwash flow rate the well pump can sustain; specialty lead or arsenic media have manufacturer-specified EBCT and flow limits. The trap is sizing the filter by tank diameter alone ("a 10-inch tank should be fine") without checking that the media bed provides the required contact time at peak flow, or that the backwash flow is available. The disciplined approach is to compute peak flow, select a tank and media volume that delivers the required EBCT at that flow, and verify the backwash flow rate is available (well pump capacity, or municipal pressure). An undersized contact time means the contaminant passes through untreated.

### Plan for Backwash and Drain Capacity

Many whole-house filters (oxidizing iron/sulfur filters, multi-media depth filters, some carbon tanks) are backwashing systems that periodically reverse flow to flush accumulated sediment and oxidized material to drain. Backwash flow rates are significant — often 5 to 15 gpm for several minutes — and the drain must accept this without overflow. The trap is installing a backwashing filter with the drain line run to an undersized standpipe or a distant floor drain, so the backwash overflows during regeneration. The disciplined approach is to verify the backwash flow rate from the manufacturer, confirm the drain and standpipe can accept it with an air gap, and ensure the well pump or municipal supply can sustain the backwash flow for the full cycle. Provide a code air gap on all backwash drains.

### Address Microbial Contaminants Explicitly

If the water test shows coliform or E. coli, filtration alone is not the answer — disinfection is required, typically UV sterilization (for whole-house bacterial control) or shock chlorination of the well followed by continuous chlorination if the source is contaminated. UV systems require low-tDS, low-turbidity, iron-free water to function (the UV light must penetrate the water), so prefiltration and often softening are required ahead of UV. The trap is installing a UV unit on raw well water with iron and turbidity, so the UV lamp is shielded within weeks and the disinfection fails silently. The disciplined approach is to pretreat to the UV manufacturer's water-quality spec (typically <5 NTU turbidity, low iron, low hardness), install the UV as the final stage, and replace the lamp annually (UV output declines over time even if the lamp still glows) with a quartz sleeve cleaning schedule.

### Respect the Role Limits — Regulated Contaminants Need Specialists and Coordination

Treatment for regulated contaminants (arsenic, nitrate, radon, uranium, PFAS, lead service line replacement) may trigger public-health notification, permitting, and ongoing monitoring requirements, and the system design is the province of water treatment specialists and sometimes licensed water operators. A plumber installing a lead filter or an arsenic system should coordinate with the local health department, use certified (NSF/ANSI) treatment components, and arrange for post-installation verification testing. Confirm scope and regulatory obligations before taking on regulated-contaminant work, and escalate to specialists and public-health authorities where required.

## Common Traps

### Recommending a Filter Without a Water Test

The installer hears "the water smells bad" and installs a carbon tank, but the smell is hydrogen sulfide, which carbon removes poorly and which requires an oxidizing filter. The trap is that the customer's complaint is a symptom, not a diagnosis, and the "filter" is a guess. The mechanism is that carbon adsorbs some compounds but not others, and sulfur passes through. The false signal is "I installed a filter." The harm is money spent on a system that does not address the actual contaminant, and the customer's problem persists. The defense is to require a comprehensive water test before recommending any treatment.

### Wrong Stage Sequence Fouls Downstream Media

The installer puts a carbon tank ahead of an iron filter, or a softener ahead of a sediment filter, because "the customer wanted taste/odor first." The trap is that iron and sediment foul the downstream-acting media rapidly: iron coats the carbon bed, sediment loads the resin. The mechanism is that each medium has a contaminant it cannot tolerate upstream, and the wrong order destroys the downstream stage. The false signal is "all the stages are installed." The harm is premature media replacement and degraded performance. The defense is to sequence stages so each protects the next: sediment, oxidation, softening, carbon, specialty, disinfection.

### Undersized Contact Time at Peak Flow

The installer selects a filter tank by diameter and installs it, but at the home's peak flow (two showers plus laundry) the empty-bed contact time falls below what the media needs to remove the target contaminant. The trap is that contact time is a function of flow rate and media volume, and a tank that works at low flow fails at peak flow. The mechanism is that the water passes through the media too fast for the adsorption or catalytic reaction to complete. The false signal is "the filter handles the flow." The harm is the contaminant passing through untreated during peak use. The defense is to compute EBCT at peak flow and size the media volume accordingly.

### UV on Untreated Water Fails Silently

The installer puts a UV unit on raw well water with iron and turbidity, with no prefiltration. The trap is that UV disinfection requires clear water, and iron and turbidity shield microbes from the UV light, so the unit "runs" but does not disinfect. The mechanism is that the quartz sleeve fouls within weeks and the UV penetration drops below the lethal dose. The false signal is "the UV lamp is on." The harm is undetected bacterial contamination of the whole-house supply. The defense is to pretreat to the UV manufacturer's water-quality spec, install UV last, and maintain the lamp and quartz sleeve on schedule.

## Self-Check

- Did I obtain a comprehensive water test (certified lab for wells; utility report plus targeted tests for municipal) before recommending any treatment, and did I match each detected contaminant to a proven treatment technology?
- Did I sequence the treatment stages so each protects the next: sediment first, then oxidation for iron/sulfur/manganese, then softening, then carbon, then specialty media, then microbial disinfection last?
- Did I size each filter and media bed for the building's peak flow rate with the required empty-bed contact time (EBCT), and did I verify the backwash flow rate is available and the drain can accept it with an air gap?
- If coliform or E. coli is present, did I specify disinfection (UV or chlorination) with the required pretreatment to the disinfection system's water-quality spec, and a lamp/sleeve maintenance schedule?
- For regulated contaminants (arsenic, nitrate, PFAS, lead, radon, uranium), did I use NSF/ANSI-certified components, coordinate with the health department, and arrange post-installation verification testing?
- Did I avoid promising removal of any contaminant the selected media does not treat, and did I document the contaminant-to-media match for the owner?
- Did I confirm my scope covers this system, and did I escalate regulated-contaminant or complex multi-contaminant systems to a water treatment specialist?
- Are the water test, stage sequence, sizing calculations, backwash/drain provisions, and maintenance schedule documented for the owner and future service?
