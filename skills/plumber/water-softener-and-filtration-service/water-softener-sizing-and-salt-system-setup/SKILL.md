# Water Softener Sizing and Salt System Setup

A water softener that is undersized for the hardness and the daily water use will exhaust its resin capacity before the next regeneration, letting hard water through to scale the water heater, fixtures, and appliances, while an oversized unit regenerates too infrequently and stagnates, and both errors trace to a sizing step that was skipped or guessed. The judgment problem is that softener sizing depends on the feedwater hardness (in grains per gallon, GPG), the daily water use (gallons per person per day, typically 75), the number of people, and the regeneration efficiency target, and a plumber who picks a unit by household size or pipe size without the hardness-and-use calculation will mis-size the system. The salt system adds its own decisions: salt type (sodium chloride versus potassium chloride for sodium-restricted households), brine tank sizing, an air-gap drain for regeneration discharge, a bypass valve for service and for non-softened uses (irrigation), and the recharge frequency and salt-efficiency setting. This skill covers the sizing, salt, drain, and control decisions that produce a softener that actually delivers soft water reliably.

## Core Rules

### Size the Softener From Hardness, Daily Water Use, and Regeneration Frequency

Softener sizing begins with the feedwater hardness (in GPG, from a water test, not a guess) and the daily water use (gallons per person per day, typically 75 for residential; commercial uses vary widely). Compute the daily grain load: hardness (GPG) x daily gallons, then multiply by the days between regenerations (commonly 3 to 7 days for salt efficiency and resin health) to get the required grain capacity between regenerations. Select a softener whose rated grain capacity (e.g., 24,000, 32,000, 48,000, 64,000 grain) exceeds the required capacity with margin, and confirm the resin volume and flow rate meet the peak household demand (a too-small unit cannot deliver peak flow even if its capacity is adequate). The trap is sizing by bedroom count or pipe size without the hardness-and-use calculation, which mis-sizes the unit. The disciplined rule is to test the hardness, compute the daily and multi-day grain load, and select a unit whose capacity and flow rate both meet the demand.

### Choose Salt Type by Water Quality and Household Sodium Restrictions

The regenerant salt is usually sodium chloride (NaCl), which is inexpensive and effective, but it adds a small amount of sodium to the softened water (roughly 30 mg sodium per liter per 10 GPG of hardness removed), which matters for sodium-restricted households (hypertension, low-sodium diets, infants). Potassium chloride (KCl) is the alternative for sodium-restricted households; it regenerates the resin similarly but adds potassium instead of sodium and is more expensive and slightly less soluble. The choice is a health and taste decision, not just a cost one, and should be confirmed with the household. The trap is defaulting to NaCl without asking about sodium restrictions, or recommending KCl without explaining the cost and solubility tradeoffs. The disciplined rule is to ask about sodium restrictions, recommend NaCl for unrestricted households and KCl for restricted ones, and document the choice.

### Size the Brine Tank and Provide Salt Storage for the Regeneration Interval

The brine tank holds the salt and the brine solution used to regenerate the resin, and it must be sized so that the salt charge lasts a reasonable interval (commonly a month or more for residential) and so the brine concentration reaches saturation (about 26 percent NaCl by weight) for an effective regeneration. An undersized brine tank needs frequent refills and may under-concentrate the brine; an oversized tank is fine but takes more space. The brine tank must sit level, be accessible for refilling, and be protected from freezing and from water that would float or flood the salt. The trap is installing a brine tank too small for the interval or in an inaccessible spot, leading to frequent refills or under-regeneration. The disciplined rule is to size the brine tank for the salt use over the target interval, install it level and accessible, and protect it from freezing and flooding.

### Install a Bypass Valve and an Air-Gap Drain for Regeneration Discharge

Every softener must have a bypass valve (manual or automatic) so the unit can be isolated for service without shutting off building water, and so that non-softened uses (irrigation, outdoor hose bibbs, sometimes drinking water) can bypass the softener to save salt and avoid sodium addition. The regeneration discharge (brine backwash) must drain through an air gap to an approved receptor (a floor drain or laundry tub), never hard-piped to the sewer, because a clogged drain can back up into the brine tank and contaminate the unit, and a hard-pipe is a cross-connection. The air gap must be at least twice the pipe diameter or 1 inch, whichever is larger, and visible. The trap is omitting the bypass or hard-piping the regeneration drain, which creates a service problem or a cross-connection. The disciplined rule is to install a bypass valve at every softener and route the regeneration discharge through a visible air gap to an approved receptor.

### Set Recharge Frequency and Salt Efficiency to Balance Capacity and Waste

The recharge (regeneration) frequency and salt dose set the tradeoff between capacity, salt efficiency, and water waste: a higher salt dose per regeneration yields more capacity per cycle but lower salt efficiency (more salt per grain removed), while a lower dose yields higher efficiency but requires more frequent regeneration. Modern units allow efficiency settings that minimize salt and water use while maintaining soft water; the target is to regenerate every 3 to 7 days (often enough to keep the resin from stagnating, infrequent enough to be efficient), with a salt dose sized to the unit capacity and the hardness. The trap is leaving the unit on the factory default, which may be set for capacity rather than efficiency, wasting salt and water. The disciplined rule is to set the recharge frequency and salt dose for the actual hardness and use, favoring efficiency, and to verify the setting produces soft water at the end of each cycle.

## Common Traps

### Sizing by Bedroom Count or Pipe Size Without the Hardness Calculation

A plumber selects a 32,000-grain unit for a 4-bedroom house without testing the hardness or computing the grain load. The trap is that household size does not determine capacity; hardness and use do. The mechanism is that grain load = hardness x daily gallons x days. The false signal is that "the unit is rated for a family." The harm is hard water breakthrough or a unit that regenerates too often. The defense is to test hardness, compute the grain load, and size the unit to capacity and flow.

### Defaulting to Sodium Chloride Without Asking About Restrictions

A plumber fills the brine tank with NaCl without asking about sodium-restricted household members. The trap is that NaCl adds sodium to the water. The mechanism is that ion exchange replaces hardness ions with sodium. The false signal is that "salt is salt." The harm is excess sodium intake for restricted household members. The defense is to ask about sodium restrictions and recommend KCl where needed.

### Omitting the Bypass Valve or Hard-Piping the Regeneration Drain

A plumber installs the softener with no bypass and hard-pipes the regeneration drain to the sewer. The trap is that the bypass is needed for service and the air gap is needed to prevent backup. The mechanism is that a clogged drain can back up into the brine tank. The false signal is that "the drain is connected." The harm is brine contamination and no way to isolate the unit. The defense is to install a bypass valve and route the discharge through a visible air gap.

### Leaving the Unit on Factory Default Salt and Recharge Settings

A plumber sets up the unit and leaves the factory default recharge and salt settings, which may be tuned for capacity rather than efficiency. The trap is that defaults often waste salt and water. The mechanism is that capacity-mode uses more salt per regeneration. The false signal is that "the unit is programmed." The harm is excessive salt and water use. The defense is to set the recharge frequency and salt dose for the actual hardness and use, favoring efficiency.

### Installing the Brine Tank Inaccessibly or in a Freeze-Prone Spot

A plumber installs the brine tank in a tight corner or an unheated garage where it is hard to refill and may freeze. The trap is that the brine tank needs access and freeze protection. The mechanism is that brine and salt are affected by freezing and flooding. The false signal is that "the tank fits." The harm is frequent difficult refills or frozen brine. The defense is to install the brine tank level, accessible, and protected from freezing and flooding.

## Self-Check

- Did I test the feedwater hardness (GPG) and compute the daily and multi-day grain load (hardness x daily gallons x days between regenerations)?
- Did I select a softener whose rated grain capacity and peak flow rate both meet the household demand?
- Did I ask about sodium restrictions and recommend sodium chloride or potassium chloride accordingly?
- Did I size the brine tank for the salt use over the target interval and install it level, accessible, and protected from freezing and flooding?
- Did I install a bypass valve (manual or automatic) so the unit can be isolated for service and so non-softened uses can bypass?
- Did I route the regeneration discharge through a visible air gap (at least twice the pipe diameter or 1 inch) to an approved receptor?
- Did I set the recharge frequency and salt dose for the actual hardness and use, favoring efficiency over the factory default?
- Did I verify that the unit delivers soft water (0-1 GPG) at the end of each cycle and just before regeneration?
- Did I confirm that irrigation and outdoor hose bibbs bypass the softener to save salt and avoid sodium addition?
- Did I document the hardness, sizing calculation, salt type, brine tank size, drain air gap, and control settings so the system is verifiable and maintainable?
