# Filtration Staging and Contaminant Targeting

Water filtration is a treatment problem, not a product selection, because every filter medium targets a specific class of contaminant and is inert or ineffective against the others, and a system assembled from generic "water filter" cartridges without a water test and a staging plan will either remove the wrong things, exhaust prematurely, or, in the case of carbon and fine filters held too long between changes, become a breeding ground for bacteria that makes the treated water worse than the raw. The judgment problem is that effective treatment requires a water test that identifies the actual contaminants and their concentrations, a staging sequence that protects downstream stages (sediment before carbon, carbon before membrane, softening before RO), media matched to the specific contaminants (iron needs oxidation or a dedicated resin, hydrogen sulfide needs catalytic carbon or oxidation, arsenic and PFAS need specialized media or RO), and flow-rate and capacity matched so the filter is not exhausted or bypassed at peak demand. NSF certification confirms the medium is tested for the claim. This skill covers the test, staging, contaminant targeting, capacity, certification, and maintenance decisions that produce treatment that actually works.

## Core Rules

### Test the Water Before Specifying Any Treatment

No filtration system should be specified without a water test that identifies the contaminants present and their concentrations, because treatment is contaminant-specific and a system chosen without a test treats the wrong things. For private wells, test at least for hardness, iron, manganese, pH, total dissolved solids, nitrate, coliform bacteria, and any local contaminants (arsenic, hydrogen sulfide, radon, uranium, PFAS); for municipal supplies, obtain the consumer confidence report and test for lead (at the tap), disinfection byproducts, and any taste or odor concern. Match each detected contaminant to a treatment stage. The trap is installing a generic "whole-house filter" without a test, which cannot address contaminants that were not measured and may be pointless. The disciplined rule is to test first, map each contaminant to a treatment stage, and specify the system from the test results.

### Stage Treatment in the Correct Sequence to Protect Downstream Stages

Treatment stages must be sequenced so each stage protects the next: sediment (particulate) filtration first, to protect carbon and membranes from fouling; then carbon (for chlorine, taste, odor, and some organics), which protects RO membranes from chlorine damage; then softening or specialty media (for hardness, iron, hydrogen sulfide); then a final-stage polish or RO for drinking water. Reverse osmosis, the finest stage, must be last and must be preceded by sediment and carbon to protect the membrane. Skipping the sediment stage loads the carbon with particulate and shortens its life; skipping carbon before RO lets chlorine destroy the membrane. The trap is installing stages out of order or omitting a protective stage, which fouls or destroys the downstream stages. The disciplined rule is to sequence sediment, carbon, softening or specialty, and RO, with each stage protecting the next.

### Target Specific Contaminants With the Correct Medium

Each contaminant requires a specific treatment medium or process, and a generic carbon cartridge does not treat all of them. Iron and manganese require oxidation (air, chlorine, permanganate) followed by filtration, or a dedicated greensand or Birm filter, depending on concentration and form (ferrous versus ferric, dissolved versus particulate). Hydrogen sulfide (rotten-egg odor) requires catalytic carbon or oxidation and filtration. Arsenic requires adsorptive media (ferric oxide, titanium dioxide) or RO. Lead is addressed at the tap by NSF-53-certified filters or by removing the lead source. PFAS requires activated carbon, ion exchange, or RO sized for the low discharge concentrations. Hardness is addressed by ion-exchange softening, not filtration. The trap is using a carbon cartridge for iron, arsenic, or PFAS, which it does not effectively remove. The disciplined rule is to match each contaminant to the tested medium and to verify the medium is rated for the concentration.

### Match Flow Rate and Filter Capacity to Peak Demand and Replacement Interval

A filter must deliver the peak flow rate the household or fixture demands (a whole-house filter must support the simultaneous shower plus laundry plus toilet load; an RO unit must support the drinking-water dispenser) and must have a capacity (gallons or months) that matches the use so it is changed before exhaustion or bacterial colonization. An undersized filter restricts flow at peak demand, causing pressure drop and fixture under-performance; an exhausted filter either passes contaminants (carbon exhausted of chlorine reduction) or, worse, channels and dumps trapped contaminant. Carbon and fine filters held too long between changes grow bacteria, so the replacement interval must be enforced by a calendar or a gallon counter, not by guess. The trap is sizing the filter by pipe size without checking flow and capacity, leading to restriction and exhaustion. The disciplined rule is to size the filter for peak flow and capacity, set a documented replacement interval, and enforce it.

### Require NSF Certification and Document Maintenance Intervals

Treatment media and systems should carry NSF (or equivalent) certification for the specific contaminant reduction claim (NSF 42 for aesthetic effects, NSF 53 for health effects, NSF 58 for RO, NSF 44 for softeners, NSF 401 for emerging contaminants including some PFAS), because certification confirms the medium was tested to deliver the claimed reduction under standard conditions, not just that it contains carbon or resin. Uncertified products may claim reduction without testing. Maintenance intervals (cartridge replacement, media replacement, sanitization) must be documented and scheduled, because an unmaintained filter is worse than no filter (exhausted carbon, bacterial growth, channeling). The trap is installing an uncertified product that claims broad reduction without testing, or omitting a maintenance schedule. The disciplined rule is to require NSF certification for the specific claim, document the replacement intervals, and schedule maintenance.

## Common Traps

### Installing a Generic Filter Without a Water Test

A plumber installs a whole-house carbon filter without testing the water, assuming carbon treats everything. The trap is that carbon does not remove hardness, iron, dissolved metals, or many contaminants. The mechanism is that each medium targets a contaminant class. The false signal is that "a filter is installed." The harm is ineffective treatment and false security. The defense is to test first and specify treatment from the results.

### Staging Filters Out of Order

A plumber installs an RO membrane before a carbon filter, and chlorine destroys the membrane. The trap is that stages must protect downstream stages. The mechanism is that chlorine degrades polyamide RO membranes. The false signal is that "the stages are installed." The harm is a destroyed membrane and wasted cost. The defense is to sequence sediment, carbon, softening or specialty, then RO.

### Using Carbon for Iron, Arsenic, or PFAS

A plumber uses a carbon cartridge to treat iron or arsenic, which carbon does not effectively remove. The trap is that carbon is not a universal medium. The mechanism is that each contaminant needs a specific medium or process. The false signal is that "carbon filters water." The harm is continued contaminant exposure and false security. The defense is to match each contaminant to the tested medium.

### Sizing by Pipe Size Without Flow and Capacity

A plumber selects a filter by the pipe size without checking peak flow or capacity, and the filter restricts flow or exhausts early. The trap is that pipe size does not determine filter performance. The mechanism is that flow rate and capacity govern serviceability. The false signal is that "the filter fits the pipe." The harm is pressure drop, exhaustion, and contaminant breakthrough. The defense is to size for peak flow and capacity and set a replacement interval.

### Holding Carbon and Fine Filters Too Long, Growing Bacteria

A plumber installs a carbon filter and leaves it past its replacement interval, and the filter grows bacteria that degrade the treated water. The trap is that an unmaintained filter is worse than none. The mechanism is that carbon and fine filters colonize bacteria over time. The false signal is that "the filter is still passing water." The harm is bacterial contamination of the treated water. The defense is to set and enforce a documented replacement interval.

## Self-Check

- Did I obtain a water test identifying the contaminants and concentrations before specifying any treatment?
- Did I map each detected contaminant to a specific treatment medium or process?
- Did I stage treatment in the correct sequence (sediment, then carbon, then softening or specialty, then RO) so each stage protects the next?
- For iron, manganese, hydrogen sulfide, arsenic, lead, and PFAS, did I select the specific tested medium rather than a generic carbon cartridge?
- Did I size each filter for the peak flow rate and the capacity (gallons or months) matching the use?
- Did I set a documented replacement interval for each cartridge and media bed, and schedule the maintenance?
- Did I require NSF (or equivalent) certification for the specific contaminant reduction claim (NSF 42, 53, 58, 44, 401)?
- Did I verify that the system delivers the required flow at peak demand without excessive pressure drop?
- Did I provide a means to monitor filter exhaustion (pressure gauge, gallon counter, calendar) so the replacement is enforced?
- Did I document the test results, staging, media, flow and capacity sizing, certification, and maintenance intervals so the treatment is verifiable and maintainable?
