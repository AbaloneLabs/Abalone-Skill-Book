# Pipe Freeze Risk Assessment and Vulnerable Location Identification

Freeze protection begins with finding the pipes that will actually freeze, and that requires a location-by-location assessment against the coldest expected condition — not a blanket assumption that "insulated walls are safe" or "PEX won't burst." The judgment problem is that a pipe freezes when the air around it drops below 32F for long enough to cool the water to freezing, and the risk is driven by where the pipe runs relative to cold air, the local winter design temperature, the building's thermal envelope, and whether the pipe has any heat source or drainage path. An agent who insulates every pipe indiscriminately without finding the cold-air leaks, who treats a south-facing wall as safe, or who abandons a seasonal line without slope-to-drain will return to the same burst pipe and the same water-damage and mold cleanup. This skill covers the risk-assessment and location-identification decisions that must precede any freeze-protection work, and the role limits that keep a plumber within their license scope.

## Core Rules

### Survey Every Pipe Run by Location Against the Cold-Air Exposure

Freeze risk is a function of the air temperature surrounding the pipe, so the first step is a location-by-location survey of every water pipe against the cold air it is exposed to. The high-risk locations are consistent across climates: pipes in exterior walls (especially north-facing walls and walls with poor insulation or air leaks), pipes in unheated crawlspaces and basements, pipes in attics (particularly near vents and soffits where cold air pools), pipes in unheated garages and outbuildings, exterior hose bibbs and sillcocks not drained for winter, pipes in floor and ceiling cavities adjacent to vented soffits or outside air, and pipes in slab-on-grade perimeters where the slab edge is exposed. Each run must be evaluated for the coldest air it will see, not the average winter temperature. The trap is surveying by memory or by building type ("it's a modern house, it's fine") and missing a pipe run in a cold chase, a garage attic, or behind a poorly insulated wall. Physically trace every run, document the location, and flag every exposure to air that can drop below freezing.

### Use the Local Winter Design Temperature and Freeze Depth, Not "Average Winter"

Freeze protection must be designed for the coldest expected condition, which is defined by the local winter design temperature (the temperature a location drops to or below on a defined percentage of hours, published in climate data and code tables) and the local freeze depth (the depth to which ground freezes, which governs buried service lines and shallow piping). A pipe in a vented attic in a region with a -10F design temperature needs far more protection than the same pipe in a region with a 20F design temperature. The trap is designing to "average winter" or to a generic insulation thickness; the harm is a pipe that survives an average winter but bursts in the first cold snap that reaches the design temperature, which is by definition a regular event. Look up the local design temperature and freeze depth for the specific site, design to those values with margin, and document the design basis so the protection can be reviewed if the climate or the building changes.

### Distinguish Insulation R-Value From Heat Trace by Whether the Pipe Has a Heat Source

Pipe insulation and heat trace solve different problems and are not interchangeable. Insulation (closed-cell foam, fiberglass, or mineral wool sized to the pipe) slows heat loss from the pipe to the cold air, extending the time a pipe with warm water or warm surroundings stays above freezing — but it adds no heat, so a pipe with no heat source (no warm water flow, no warm air, no heat trace) eventually cools to the surrounding temperature and freezes however well insulated. Heat trace (electrical heating cable listed for the pipe material) adds heat to the pipe and is the solution for pipes that cannot be kept above freezing by routing and insulation alone, such as pipes in unheated spaces with no warm-water flow. The trap is insulating a pipe in a cold crawlspace and assuming it is protected; the harm is a freeze despite the insulation because there was no heat source. For each vulnerable pipe, decide whether routing and insulation suffice (pipe has a warm source) or whether heat trace is required (no warm source), and design accordingly.

### Map the Thermal Envelope and Find the Cold-Air Leaks, Not Just the Pipes

A pipe freezes because cold air reaches it, and the most effective freeze prevention often fixes the cold-air path rather than the pipe. The thermal envelope — the boundary between heated and unheated space — should be mapped, and every breach (gaps around pipe penetrations, unsealed top plates, missing insulation, vented soffits open to wall cavities, broken or open crawl-space vents) identified and sealed. A pipe in an exterior wall behind missing insulation, with cold air leaking through an unsealed penetration, will freeze even if the wall is "insulated" elsewhere. The trap is treating the pipe in isolation and adding heat trace or insulation while the cold-air leak continues to drive the pipe toward freezing; the harm is recurring freezes and wasted energy. Map the envelope, find the leaks (with an infrared camera or a smoke pencil where available), seal the air paths, restore the insulation, and then decide whether the pipe still needs insulation or heat trace.

### Plan Slope-to-Drain for Seasonal and Abandoned Piping

For seasonal piping (irrigation, outdoor showers, seasonal cabins) and for piping being abandoned, the freeze-protection strategy is drainage: slope every run so water drains out when the supply is shut off, install drain valves at all low points, and use freeze-proof hose bibbs and sillcocks that drain back into the warm wall when shut. A line that holds water over winter will freeze and burst at the low points even if the rest is drained. Abandoned piping that is left full and pressurized (or full and trapped) is a hidden freeze and leak risk and should be drained, sloped, and disconnected from the supply. The trap is shutting off a seasonal line without draining it, or abandoning a line in place full of water; the harm is a burst at the first freeze and a flood when the system is re-pressurized or the ice thaws. Slope seasonal and abandoned piping to drain, install drain valves at low points, and confirm no water remains before leaving the system for winter.

## Common Traps

### Assuming a Material or Wall Orientation Is Safe Without a Location Survey

The agent assumes "PEX doesn't burst" or "this wall faces south so it's fine" and skips the location survey. The trap mechanism is that PEX resists splitting better than copper but its fittings can still fail and connected copper can burst, and a south-facing wall with poor insulation and a cold-air leak still freezes — material and orientation are poor proxies for the actual air temperature around the pipe. The false signal is that "the pipe is PEX" or "the wall faces south." The harm is a freeze and burst in a pipe that was assumed safe. The defense is to physically trace every run, document the location, and flag every exposure to air that can drop below freezing, regardless of material or orientation.

### Designing to Average Winter Instead of the Local Design Temperature

The agent insulates to a generic thickness or installs heat trace "for our typical winter," without looking up the local design temperature or freeze depth. The trap mechanism is that the design temperature is, by definition, a regular event (the temperature the site drops to on a defined percentage of hours), and protection sized to average winter fails at the first design-temperature cold snap. The false signal is that "it hasn't frozen in years." The harm is a burst during the first cold snap that reaches the design temperature. The defense is to look up the local design temperature and freeze depth, design to those values with margin, and document the basis.

### Insulating a Pipe That Has No Heat Source

The agent wraps a pipe in a cold crawlspace with thick insulation, reasoning that "insulation prevents freezing." The trap mechanism is that insulation slows heat loss but adds no heat, so a pipe with no warm-water flow, no warm air, and no heat trace eventually cools to the crawlspace temperature and freezes however well insulated. The false signal is that "the pipe is insulated." The harm is a freeze despite the insulation. The defense is to distinguish pipes with a heat source (routing and insulation may suffice) from pipes without (heat trace is required), and to add heat trace where insulation alone cannot keep the pipe above freezing.

### Fixing the Pipe but Leaving the Cold-Air Leak

The agent adds insulation or heat trace to a freezing pipe but never finds and seals the cold-air leak (a gap around the pipe penetration, missing wall insulation, an open soffit) that is driving the pipe toward freezing. The trap mechanism is that the cold-air path continues to cool the pipe and the surrounding cavity, so the freeze recurs or the heat trace runs continuously wasting energy, and the leak also wastes building heat. The false signal is that "the pipe is protected." The harm is recurring freezes, energy waste, and possible concealed mold from the air leak's condensation. The defense is to map the thermal envelope, find and seal the cold-air leaks, restore the insulation, and then decide whether the pipe still needs additional protection.

### Abandoning or Shutting Off a Seasonal Line Without Slope-to-Drain

The agent shuts off and abandons a seasonal irrigation or cabin supply line in place, full of water, without draining it or sloping it to drain. The trap mechanism is that the water left in the line freezes at the low points, splitting the pipe and the fittings, and the damage appears as a flood when the system is re-pressurized in spring or when the ice thaws into an occupied space. The false signal is that "the line is shut off." The harm is a burst and flood from the trapped water. The defense is to slope seasonal and abandoned piping to drain, install drain valves at all low points, use freeze-proof hose bibbs that drain back, and confirm no water remains before winter.

## Self-Check

- Did I physically trace every water pipe run and document its location, flagging every exposure to air that can drop below freezing (exterior walls, crawlspaces, attics, garages, hose bibbs, slab perimeters, cold chases)?
- Did I evaluate each run by the actual air temperature it will see, not by material ("PEX is safe") or wall orientation ("south-facing is fine")?
- Did I look up the local winter design temperature and freeze depth for the specific site, and design the freeze protection to those values with margin?
- For each vulnerable pipe, did I determine whether it has a heat source (warm-water flow, warm air) such that routing and insulation suffice, or whether heat trace is required because there is no heat source?
- Did I map the thermal envelope, find and seal the cold-air leaks (penetrations, missing insulation, open soffits, broken vents), and restore insulation before deciding on pipe-level protection?
- For seasonal and abandoned piping, did I slope every run to drain, install drain valves at all low points, use freeze-proof hose bibbs that drain back, and confirm no water remains before winter?
- Did I verify that insulation is on the cold side of the pipe (between the pipe and the cold air), with no gaps, sized to the pipe, and adequate for the design temperature?
- Where heat trace is used, is it listed for the pipe material, installed per the manufacturer with a thermostat, and protected by GFPE?
- Did I confirm my licensing scope covers the freeze-protection work (plumbing and, where relevant, electrical heat trace under the appropriate permit), and did I escalate beyond-scope work?
- Are the vulnerable locations, design temperature, protection measures (routing, insulation, heat trace, drainage), and any repairs documented so the freeze protection is verifiable and maintainable?
