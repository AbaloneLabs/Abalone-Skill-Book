# Fire Pump Weekly Test and Acceptance Criteria

A fire pump that is never tested is an assumption, and a fire pump that degrades silently between tests is a pump that will fail in the only moment it matters. The judgment problem is that NFPA 25 requires a weekly no-flow (churn) run and an annual certified flow test, the acceptance criteria are specific numeric thresholds (churn pressure, rated pressure, and the pressure at 150 percent of rated flow), and the weekly test also checks the packing gland drip rate and the electrical and mechanical health of the pump. A tester who runs the pump for two minutes without checking pressures, ignores a hot bearing or a dry packing gland, or skips the annual flow test will not detect degradation until the pump fails during a fire. The harm is an undetected pump failure in a fire event. This skill covers the weekly test, the annual flow test, the acceptance criteria, the mechanical checks, and the documentation decisions that keep a fire pump verifiably ready.

## Core Rules

### Run the Weekly No-Flow (Churn) Test per NFPA 25 and Record Pressures

NFPA 25 requires a weekly no-flow run of the fire pump (typically 10 minutes for electric pumps, longer for diesel) to confirm the pump starts automatically, runs without distress, and produces churn pressure (the pressure at zero flow, with the discharge closed). The test verifies the automatic start (by dropping pressure with the test drain until the controller starts the pump), records the churn pressure and suction pressure, and confirms the pump reaches stable operation. The trap is a token two-minute run with no pressure recorded, which proves nothing. The disciplined rule is to run the full duration, to record suction and discharge (churn) pressure, to confirm automatic start, and to compare churn pressure to the nameplate and prior readings. A falling churn pressure trend is the earliest sign of pump wear.

### Perform the Annual Certified Flow Test and Evaluate the Full Characteristic Curve

The annual flow test (NFPA 25, performed by qualified personnel) measures the pump's performance across its characteristic curve: churn pressure (0 percent flow, 100 to 140 percent of rated pressure depending on pump type), rated pressure (100 percent flow, 100 percent pressure), and the pressure at 150 percent of rated flow (which must be at or above 65 percent of rated pressure per NFPA 20, the acceptance floor). The test uses the test return line and flowmeter or playpipe to measure flow at each point. The trap is testing only at rated flow and missing that the 150 percent point has collapsed, which indicates impeller or ring wear. The disciplined rule is to test and record all three points, to plot them against the nameplate curve, and to flag any point that has dropped more than the NFPA tolerance (commonly a 5 percent degradation from baseline triggers investigation).

### Check the Packing Gland Drip Rate and Mechanical Condition During Every Run

The packing gland on a centrifugal fire pump must drip steadily (commonly a rate of roughly 40 to 60 drops per minute, per manufacturer guidance) to lubricate and cool the packing; a dry packing gland burns the packing and scores the shaft sleeve, while a flooding gland wastes water and indicates over-tightening or worn packing. The weekly run is also when to check the mechanical condition: bearing temperatures (by touch or sensor), pump and motor for unusual noise or vibration, oil level in the pump bearings and the motor, and (for diesel) the engine oil, coolant, battery, and fuel levels. The trap is running the pump without inspecting these, so a hot bearing or dry gland is missed. The disciplined rule is to check the packing drip, bearings, oil levels, and (for diesel) engine readiness during every weekly run, and to record the findings.

### Record Electrical and Mechanical Readings and Compare to Baselines

For electric pumps, the weekly and annual tests include electrical readings: the running voltage and amperage at the controller (to detect a voltage drop or rising current that indicates mechanical loading or electrical problems), and the phase balance (significant imbalance indicates a supply or motor issue). For diesel pumps, the engine readings (oil pressure, coolant temperature, battery voltage, run hours) are recorded. The disciplined rule is to record the readings every test, to compare them to the nameplate and prior baselines, and to investigate any trend: rising amperage at the same flow indicates increasing mechanical load (wear, obstruction); falling voltage indicates a supply problem. A single reading means little; the trend is the diagnostic.

### Document All Tests and Submit Results to the AHJ and Facility Records

NFPA 25 requires that weekly and annual tests be documented and retained, and that the annual flow test results be submitted to the AHJ and the facility's records (and often the insurer). The documentation must include the date, the personnel, the suction and discharge pressures at each flow point, the flow rates, the electrical or diesel readings, the packing drip and mechanical condition, and any deficiencies and corrections. The trap is a test log with "OK" and no numbers, which cannot detect trend degradation and cannot defend the program after a fire. The disciplined rule is to record every numeric reading, to plot the annual flow test against the nameplate and prior years, to report deficiencies and corrections, and to submit the annual results to the AHJ within the required timeframe.

## Common Traps

### A Token Weekly Run With No Pressures Recorded

The weekly test is run for two minutes with "OK" logged and no suction or churn pressure recorded. The trap is that the run proves the pump spins, not that it performs. The mechanism is that NFPA 25 requires pressure recording to detect degradation. The false signal is that "the pump ran." The harm is undetected pressure drop and pump wear. The defense is to run the full duration, record suction and churn pressure, and compare to nameplate and prior readings.

### Testing Only at Rated Flow and Missing the 150 Percent Point

The annual test measures only rated flow, and the 150 percent point has collapsed undetected. The trap is that the rated point can look fine while the curve has degraded. The mechanism is that the 150 percent point is the wear-sensitive indicator. The false signal is that "rated pressure is normal." The harm is a pump that cannot deliver at high demand during a fire. The defense is to test churn, rated, and 150 percent points and plot the full curve.

### Ignoring a Dry or Flooding Packing Gland

The packing gland runs dry (burning the packing and scoring the sleeve) or floods (wasting water), and no one adjusts it. The trap is that the drip is a maintenance item, not cosmetic. The mechanism is that packing needs a controlled drip to lubricate. The false signal is that "the pump runs." The harm is packing and shaft-sleeve damage and eventual leakage. The defense is to check and adjust the drip rate (roughly 40 to 60 drops per minute) during every run.

### Missing Rising Amperage or a Hot Bearing

The pump amperage creeps up or a bearing runs hot, and the trend is ignored until failure. The trap is that a single reading looks fine. The mechanism is that the trend indicates mechanical loading or wear. The false signal is that "the reading is in range." The harm is progressive damage and eventual in-service failure. The defense is to record and trend electrical and mechanical readings and investigate any rise.

### A Test Log With "OK" and No Numbers

The test log records "OK" with no pressures, flows, or readings, so trend degradation is invisible and the record cannot defend the program. The trap is that the log asserts readiness without evidence. The mechanism is that NFPA 25 requires numeric documentation. The false signal is a completed log. The harm is undetected degradation and an undefendable record after a fire. The defense is to record every numeric reading, plot annual flow tests, and submit results to the AHJ.

## Self-Check

- Did I run the weekly no-flow (churn) test for the full NFPA 25 duration (typically 10 minutes electric, longer diesel), and confirm automatic start?
- Did I record suction and discharge (churn) pressure, and compare churn pressure to the nameplate and prior weekly readings for trend?
- For the annual flow test, did I measure and record churn pressure, rated pressure, and the pressure at 150 percent of rated flow?
- Is the 150 percent flow pressure at or above 65 percent of rated pressure (the NFPA 20 acceptance floor), and did I flag any point degraded beyond tolerance?
- Did I plot the annual flow test points against the nameplate curve and prior years to detect trend degradation?
- During every run, did I check and adjust the packing gland drip rate (roughly 40 to 60 drops per minute) and record it?
- Did I check bearing temperatures, oil levels, noise, and vibration, and for diesel pumps the engine oil, coolant, battery, and fuel?
- Did I record electrical readings (voltage, amperage, phase balance) or diesel readings, and compare them to baselines for trend?
- Did I record every numeric reading in the test log (not just "OK"), report deficiencies and corrections, and retain the records?
- Did I submit the annual flow test results to the AHJ and facility (and insurer where required) within the required timeframe?
