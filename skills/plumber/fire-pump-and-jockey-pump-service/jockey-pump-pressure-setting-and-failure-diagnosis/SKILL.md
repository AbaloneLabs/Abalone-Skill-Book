# Jockey Pump Pressure Setting and Failure Diagnosis

A jockey pump is a small maintenance pump whose only job is to keep the fire-protection piping pressurized and to make up small leaks so the main fire pump does not start on every minor pressure drop. The judgment problem is that the jockey cut-in and cut-out pressures must be set above the fire pump start pressure (commonly the jockey cut-in is set about 10 psi above the fire pump start), the jockey must be sized to small GPM makeup (not to fight fires), and a jockey that cannot keep up, or whose check valve or pressure switch fails, will either cause false fire pump starts or mask a real system leak until the system is empty. The harm is unnecessary wear and false starts of the fire pump, or an undetected leak that leaves the system underpressurized when a fire occurs. This skill covers the setting, sizing, and diagnostic decisions that keep the jockey doing its narrow job.

## Core Rules

### Set Jockey Cut-In Above the Fire Pump Start, Typically by About 10 psi

The jockey pump must start before the fire pump, so its cut-in pressure is set above the fire pump start pressure, commonly about 10 psi higher (for example, if the fire pump starts at 130 psi, the jockey cuts in around 140 psi and cuts out around 160 psi). The fire pump stop is set below the jockey cut-out. This staging ensures the jockey makes up small losses and only a real demand (a large pressure drop) starts the fire pump. The trap is setting the jockey cut-in at or below the fire pump start, which causes the fire pump to start on every small leak, wearing the fire pump and alarming the facility. The disciplined rule is to set jockey cut-in about 10 psi above fire pump start, jockey cut-out above that, and to verify the sequence by test: a small bleed should start only the jockey, a large bleed should start the fire pump.

### Size the Jockey for Small GPM Makeup, Not Fire Flow

The jockey is sized to make up normal leakage and minor pressure losses, typically a few gallons per minute, not to deliver sprinkler demand. Oversizing the jockey lets it mask a significant leak or even a small system breach by keeping up with flow that should trigger the fire pump. The disciplined rule is to size the jockey to the expected leakage (often under 10 to 20 GPM for most systems), to confirm it cannot keep up with a sprinkler head or hose flow (so the fire pump starts on real demand), and to document the sizing basis. A jockey that can out-pump a single sprinkler head defeats the fire pump's automatic start.

### Diagnose the Jockey Before Blaming the Fire Pump for Frequent Starts

When the fire pump starts frequently or the jockey runs constantly, the cause is usually a jockey problem or a system leak, not a fire pump problem. The diagnostic sequence is: first check the jockey pressure switch setpoints (a drifted switch can let pressure fall to the fire pump start before the jockey cuts in), then check the jockey check valve (a leaking check valve lets jockey discharge flow back and the jockey cycles or cannot build pressure), then look for a system leak (a weeping drain, a leaking joint, or a hidden leak in the underground main). The trap is repeatedly resetting the fire pump controller without diagnosing why pressure keeps dropping. The disciplined rule is to follow the diagnostic sequence and find the root cause.

### Distinguish a Small Leak (Jockey Handles It) From a Large Leak (Fire Pump Starts)

The behavior of the system tells you the leak size. A small leak (a weep, a tiny joint leak) drops pressure slowly, the jockey starts periodically and restores pressure, and the fire pump never starts; this is normal and the jockey is doing its job. A large leak (a broken joint, an open drain, a failed fitting) drops pressure faster than the jockey can recover, pressure falls to the fire pump start, and the fire pump starts; this is the system signaling a real problem. The trap is ignoring a jockey that runs more and more frequently, which is the early warning of a growing leak. The disciplined rule is to track jockey run frequency, to investigate any increasing trend, and to confirm whether the jockey can still recover pressure between cycles.

### Maintain the Jockey Check Valve, Pressure Switch, and Run Log

The jockey's reliability depends on three components: the check valve (must hold so the jockey builds pressure and does not cycle against backflow), the pressure switch (must cut in and out at the setpoints without drift), and the run log (frequency and duration of jockey runs reveals leak trends). The trap is a jockey with no maintenance record, so a drifted switch or leaking check valve goes unnoticed until the fire pump starts falsely or the system is found empty. The disciplined rule is to test the pressure switch setpoints periodically, to verify the check valve holds (the jockey should not cycle immediately after cut-out), and to log jockey run frequency as a leading indicator of system leakage. A jockey that suddenly runs every few minutes has a story to tell.

## Common Traps

### Jockey Cut-In Set at or Below the Fire Pump Start

The jockey cut-in is set at the same pressure as the fire pump start, so the fire pump starts on every small leak. The trap is that the jockey never gets the chance to do its job. The mechanism is that pressure staging must separate the two pumps. The false signal is that "both pumps are wired." The harm is unnecessary fire pump starts, wear, and facility alarms. The defense is to set jockey cut-in about 10 psi above fire pump start and verify the sequence by test.

### An Oversized Jockey That Masks a Real Leak

The jockey is sized large enough to keep up with a sprinkler head, so a real breach never triggers the fire pump. The trap is that the jockey hides the demand. The mechanism is that the jockey should only make up small leakage. The false signal is that "pressure is holding." The harm is an undetected system breach and a fire pump that never starts when it should. The defense is to size the jockey to small GPM makeup and confirm it cannot out-pump a single head.

### A Leaking Jockey Check Valve Causing Cycling

The jockey check valve passes, so discharge flows back, the jockey cycles or cannot build pressure, and the fire pump may start. The trap is that the jockey is blamed when the check valve failed. The mechanism is that the check valve must hold to build pressure. The false signal is that "the jockey runs constantly." The harm is wear, false fire pump starts, and unresolved pressure loss. The defense is to verify the check valve holds and inspect or replace a passing valve.

### Pressure Switch Drift Starting the Fire Pump

The jockey pressure switch drifts, so it cuts in after the fire pump start pressure, and the fire pump starts on small leaks. The trap is that the switch setpoints are assumed stable. The mechanism is that switches drift with age and temperature. The false signal is that "the settings are correct." The harm is false fire pump starts. The defense is to test the switch setpoints periodically and recalibrate or replace a drifted switch.

### Ignoring an Increasing Jockey Run Frequency

The jockey runs more and more often, signaling a growing leak, and no one investigates until the system fails. The trap is that the trend is the early warning. The mechanism is that run frequency tracks leak rate. The false signal is that "the jockey is working." The harm is an undetected leak that empties or underpressurizes the system. The defense is to log jockey run frequency and investigate any increasing trend.

## Self-Check

- Is the jockey cut-in set about 10 psi above the fire pump start pressure, with the jockey cut-out above that, and the fire pump stop below the jockey cut-out?
- Did I verify the staging by test: a small bleed starts only the jockey, and a large bleed starts the fire pump?
- Is the jockey sized for small GPM makeup (typically under 10 to 20 GPM), and can it not out-pump a single sprinkler head?
- When the fire pump starts frequently or the jockey runs constantly, did I diagnose the jockey pressure switch, jockey check valve, and system leak before blaming the fire pump?
- Is the jockey check valve holding, so the jockey builds pressure and does not cycle immediately after cut-out?
- Are the jockey pressure switch setpoints tested periodically and recalibrated or replaced if drifted?
- Am I logging jockey run frequency and duration, and did I investigate any increasing trend as a sign of a growing leak?
- Did I distinguish a small leak (jockey recovers pressure, fire pump never starts) from a large leak (pressure falls to fire pump start)?
- Is there a hidden leak (weeping drain, underground main leak) if the jockey runs frequently with no visible cause?
- Did I document the jockey sizing, setpoints, check-valve and switch tests, and run-log trend so the system is verifiable and maintainable?
