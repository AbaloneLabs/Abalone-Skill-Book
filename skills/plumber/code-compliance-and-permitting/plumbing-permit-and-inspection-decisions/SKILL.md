# Plumbing Permit and Inspection Decisions

Plumbing permits and inspections exist because plumbing failures (leaks, contamination, gas leaks, sewer backups) cause property damage, health hazards, and deaths, and the permit-and-inspection process is the mechanism by which the authority having jurisdiction (AHJ) verifies that the work meets the minimum code before it is concealed or placed in service. The judgment problem is that the permit and inspection requirements are often seen as bureaucratic friction to be minimized or avoided, but skipping or rushing them conceals non-compliant work, creates liability for the plumber and the property owner, and can void insurance and complicate property sales. A plumber who skips a permit to save the owner money, or who conceals work before inspection, is taking on personal liability for a code failure that the inspection was designed to catch. This skill covers the permit and inspection decisions that keep work legal, verifiable, and insurable.

## Core Rules

### Determine Permit Requirements Before Starting Any Non-Trivial Work

Most non-trivial plumbing work requires a permit: new installations, replacements of water heaters or fixtures (in most jurisdictions), sewer replacements, gas line installations or extensions, backflow preventer installations, repipes, and any modification to the drainage, water, or gas system. Minor repair work (clearing a clog, replacing a faucet washer, replacing a single fixture in some jurisdictions) may be permit-exempt, but the exemption thresholds vary by jurisdiction. The disciplined rule is to determine the permit requirement before starting work, by consulting the local building department or the code, and to obtain the permit before beginning the work. The trap is assuming a job is "too small" for a permit or skipping the permit to save cost, which makes the work illegal and uninspectable.

### Schedule Rough-In Inspection Before Concealing Any Drain, Water, or Gas Line

The rough-in inspection (also called the rough plumbing inspection) occurs after the drain, waste, vent, water, and gas piping is installed but before it is concealed behind walls, under floors, or above ceilings (and before fixtures are set). The inspector verifies the pipe sizing, slope, support, material, venting, and protection, and may require a pressure test (for water and gas lines) or a flood-level test (for DWV). Work must not be concealed before the rough-in inspection passes, because concealing uninspected work removes the ability to verify compliance. The disciplined rule is to schedule the rough-in inspection before any concealment, to have the system test-ready (under pressure or flooded as required), and to not conceal any work until the inspection passes. The trap is concealing work before inspection (covering walls, pouring a slab over under-slab piping) to keep the schedule moving, which forces the inspector to accept the work unseen or to require uncovering.

### Schedule the Final Inspection After Fixtures Are Set and the System Is Operational

The final inspection occurs after the fixtures are set, the fixtures and faucets are operational, the water heater is installed and connected, the gas appliances are connected and operational, and the system is ready for use. The inspector verifies the fixture installation, the drain and vent connections, the water heater installation (T&P valve, drain pan, seismic strapping where required, gas connection and venting), the gas appliance connections, and the overall system function. The final inspection must pass before the building can be occupied or the system placed in service (in most jurisdictions, the certificate of occupancy depends on the final plumbing inspection). The disciplined rule is to schedule the final inspection after the system is complete and operational, to have all fixtures and appliances set and functional, and to address any deficiencies before the certificate of occupancy.

### Coordinate Inspections With the AHJ and Plan for Hold Points

Plumbing inspections are hold points — the work cannot proceed past the inspection until it passes — and the plumber must coordinate the inspection scheduling with the AHJ (the building department), plan the work sequence around the hold points, and build the schedule with inspection lead time (typically 24 to 48 hours' notice for an inspection). The rough-in inspection is a hold point before concealment; the final inspection is a hold point before occupancy; and additional inspections may be required for under-slab work (before the slab is poured), for backflow preventers, and for gas pressure tests. The trap is not planning for the inspection hold points, scheduling the drywall or the slab pour without the inspection, and then having to delay or to cover uninspected work. The disciplined rule is to identify all inspection hold points in the schedule, to request inspections with adequate lead time, and to not proceed past a hold point until the inspection passes.

### Document the Permit, the Inspections, and the Approvals

The permit, the inspection records, and the certificate of occupancy (or final approval) are the legal record that the work was permitted and inspected, and they protect the plumber and the owner from liability and from future disputes (e.g., a property sale that requires proof of permitted work). The plumber should keep a copy of the permit, the inspection cards (signed by the inspector), and any correction notices and their resolution, and provide them to the owner. The trap is treating the permit and inspection records as disposable after the job, which leaves no proof of compliance if a problem arises. The disciplined rule is to document the permit, the inspections, the approvals, and any corrections, and to provide the records to the owner and retain a copy.

## Common Traps

### Skipping the Permit to Save Cost or Time

A plumber skips the permit for a water heater replacement or a repipe to save the owner the permit fee and the inspection time, and the work is unpermitted and uninspectable; a later failure or a property sale reveals the unpermitted work, creating liability and requiring uncovering or replacement. The trap is that skipping the permit saves short-term cost but creates long-term liability. The mechanism is that the permit is the legal authorization for the work. The false signal is that "the work is done correctly." The harm is liability, uninsurability, and complications in property sales. The defense is to determine the permit requirement before starting and to obtain the permit.

### Concealing Work Before the Rough-In Inspection

A plumber conceals plumbing work (covers walls, pours a slab) before the rough-in inspection, to keep the schedule moving, and the inspector must accept the work unseen or require uncovering, which is costly and contentious. The trap is that the concealment saves schedule but removes the verification. The mechanism is that the rough-in inspection verifies the concealed work. The false signal is that "the work is ready to cover." The harm is uninspectable work, uncovering cost, or concealed non-compliance. The defense is to schedule the rough-in inspection before any concealment and to not cover work until the inspection passes.

### Scheduling the Final Inspection Before the System Is Complete

A plumber schedules the final inspection before the fixtures are set or the appliances are connected, hoping to get the inspection "started," and the inspector fails the inspection for incompleteness, wasting a trip and delaying the occupancy. The trap is that the early scheduling seems efficient but wastes the inspection. The mechanism is that the final inspection requires a complete, operational system. The false signal is that "most of the work is done." The harm is a wasted inspection and schedule delay. The defense is to schedule the final inspection only after the system is complete and operational.

### Not Planning for Inspection Hold Points in the Schedule

A plumber does not plan for the inspection hold points (rough-in before concealment, final before occupancy) and schedules the subsequent trades (drywall, slab, occupancy) without the inspection, causing delays or concealed uninspected work. The trap is that the schedule ignores the hold points. The mechanism is that inspections are mandatory hold points. The false signal is that "the work is on schedule." The harm is schedule disruption and concealed non-compliance. The defense is to identify the hold points in the schedule, request inspections with lead time, and not proceed past a hold point until the inspection passes.

### Discarding the Permit and Inspection Records After the Job

A plumber discards the permit and inspection records after the job is complete, leaving no proof of compliance, and a later failure or property sale cannot prove the work was permitted and inspected. The trap is that the records seem disposable but are the legal proof. The mechanism is that the records document compliance. The false signal is that "the job is done." The harm is inability to prove compliance, liability, and property sale complications. The defense is to document the permit, inspections, approvals, and corrections, and to provide the records to the owner and retain a copy.

## Self-Check

- Did I determine the permit requirement before starting the work, by consulting the local building department or the code, and obtain the permit before beginning?
- Did I schedule the rough-in inspection before any concealment (walls, floors, ceilings, slabs), and was the system test-ready (under pressure or flooded as required)?
- Did I refrain from concealing any work until the rough-in inspection passed?
- Did I schedule the final inspection only after the system was complete and operational (fixtures set, water heater installed, gas appliances connected)?
- Did I identify all inspection hold points in the schedule (under-slab, rough-in, final, backflow, gas pressure test) and plan the work sequence around them?
- Did I request inspections with adequate lead time (typically 24 to 48 hours) and not proceed past a hold point until the inspection passed?
- Did I address all correction notices from the inspector and document the resolution?
- Did I document the permit, the inspection cards, the approvals, and any corrections, and provide the records to the owner?
- Did I retain a copy of the permit and inspection records for my files?
- Did I confirm that the certificate of occupancy (or final approval) was issued, indicating the work passed the final inspection?
