# Pressure Regulator and PRV Selection Installation

A pressure reducing valve (PRV) is the component that tames an overpressure municipal supply down to a safe building pressure, and it is also the component that, by closing the system, creates the thermal expansion problem that ruins water heaters and trips relief valves. The judgment problem is that selecting, sizing, and setting a PRV is a coupled decision with the expansion tank, the water heater, and the building's fixture demand, and a PRV chosen by pipe size alone or set by the factory dial will deliver the wrong pressure and omit the expansion control the system now requires. A plumber who installs a PRV without measuring incoming pressure, without sizing to peak flow, and without the mandatory expansion tank builds a system that damages itself. This skill covers the decisions that regulate building pressure correctly and accommodate the closed system a PRV creates.

## Core Rules

### Measure Incoming Static Pressure Before Deciding Anything

The first data point in any pressure regulation decision is the actual incoming static pressure, measured with a gauge on a hose bibb as close to the meter as practical, at a time representative of normal supply (not at peak neighborhood demand, not at a dead-end main). Municipal pressure varies widely — 30 psi in low zones, over 150 psi near pumping stations or at the bottom of elevation zones — and the decision to install a PRV, and its sizing, depends entirely on this measurement. A PRV is required whenever static pressure exceeds 80 psi (the code maximum for residential supply in most jurisdictions), and is recommended whenever pressure exceeds about 60 psi to protect fixtures and reduce water hammer. The disciplined rule is to measure first, with a calibrated gauge, and to record the reading; never assume pressure from the address or the neighborhood. If pressure is marginal or variable, log it over 24 hours to capture peak and trough.

### Size the PRV to Peak Demand, Not Just to the Pipe Size

A PRV has a flow capacity that depends on its size and design, and an undersized PRV becomes a flow restriction that drops pressure dramatically under demand even though the static setting looks correct. The disciplined approach is to size the PRV to the building's peak demand (estimated from fixture units, as in water service sizing), to confirm the manufacturer's flow curve supports the peak flow at the desired residual pressure, and to upsize the PRV if the curve indicates excessive drop. The trap is selecting a PRV by matching the inlet pipe size (a 1-inch main gets a 1-inch PRV) without checking the flow curve, which can deliver adequate static pressure and inadequate residual pressure under simultaneous fixture use. The disciplined rule is to verify the PRV's rated flow at the desired output pressure exceeds the building peak demand.

### Install a Thermal Expansion Tank on Every Closed System a PRV Creates

A PRV with an internal check valve (which most modern PRVs have) creates a closed system: water can flow in but cannot flow back to the main. In a closed system with a water heater, the heated water expands, the pressure rises rapidly — often to 150 psi or more within a heating cycle — and the only relief is the water heater's T&P valve, which drips, or a fitting, which fails. The disciplined rule is that every closed system with a water heater must have a thermal expansion tank, sized to the heater volume and the system pressure per the manufacturer's chart, pre-charged to the system cold pressure, and installed per code. The trap is installing the PRV without the expansion tank because "the old system didn't have one," not recognizing that the old system was open (no check valve) and the new PRV has closed it. This is the single most common PRV installation error.

### Set the PRV Output With a Gauge, Not by the Dial or Turn Count

The PRV's output pressure must be set and verified with a gauge on a downstream hose bibb, because the dial indication and the actual delivered pressure differ due to manufacturing variation and downstream fixture mix. The disciplined approach is to install a gauge (or a permanent pressure gauge at the PRV), open a downstream hose bibb, and adjust the PRV until the gauge reads the target pressure — typically 50 to 60 psi for residential, which provides adequate fixture performance without excessive stress. Confirm the pressure at the top floor as well, accounting for elevation loss (0.433 psi per foot), to ensure upper-floor fixtures have adequate residual pressure. The trap is setting the PRV by counting turns from the factory setting or trusting the dial, which can deliver 50 or 75 psi at a "60 psi" indication. The disciplined rule is to set with a gauge and document the delivered pressure.

### Select a PRV With the Right Features for the Application

PRVs vary in features that matter for the application: a built-in check valve (standard on most, creating the closed system), an integral bypass (which allows backflow on pressure drop and can eliminate the need for a separate expansion tank in some open-system jurisdictions, though codes increasingly require the tank regardless), a replaceable cartridge (for easier service), a strainer (to protect the seat from debris), and a pressure gauge port. The disciplined approach is to select a PRV with a strainer (essential for municipal supply with sediment), a replaceable seat or cartridge (for serviceability), and the appropriate bypass or check configuration for the local code. The trap is selecting the cheapest PRV without a strainer, which fouls the seat and causes pressure drift or failure within years.

### Locate the PRV for Access, Strainer Maintenance, and Expansion Tank Proximity

The PRV must be installed in an accessible location — not buried behind a finished wall or under a low deck — because the strainer requires periodic cleaning and the seat requires service. The disciplined layout places the PRV downstream of the main shutoff, with a union on each side for removal, a shutoff valve downstream for isolation, and the expansion tank nearby (typically at the water heater, on the cold side). Provide a drain or drip for the strainer cleanout, and leave clearance for gauge installation and adjustment. The trap is locating the PRV in a tight, inaccessible spot during new construction, which converts a five-minute strainer cleaning into a demolition project years later.

## Common Traps

### Installing a PRV Without an Expansion Tank on the Closed System

A plumber installs a PRV with an internal check valve to bring 120 psi municipal pressure down to 60 psi, omits the thermal expansion tank because the old system didn't have one, and within weeks the water heater T&P valve is dripping on every heating cycle and the fittings are stressed. The trap is that the old system was open (no check valve, allowing backflow to the main) so expansion did not build pressure, while the new PRV has closed the system. The mechanism is that the PRV's check valve prevents expanded water from returning to the main, so every heating cycle raises the pressure until the T&P opens. The false signal is that the system "works" initially. The harm is chronic T&P discharge, fitting stress, energy and water waste, and eventual fitting failure. The defense is to always install a correctly sized and pre-charged expansion tank with any PRV or closed-system backflow preventer.

### Sizing the PRV by Pipe Size Without Checking the Flow Curve

A plumber matches the PRV to the 1-inch main without checking the manufacturer's flow curve, and the static pressure reads 60 psi but drops to 30 psi when two fixtures and a shower run simultaneously, starving the fixtures. The trap is that pipe-size matching feels correct and the static reading looks fine, but the PRV's flow capacity may be less than the building's peak demand, causing a large pressure drop under flow. The mechanism is that every PRV has a flow-dependent pressure drop, and an undersized PRV drops more pressure at higher flow. The false signal is that the "pressure is set to 60." The harm is inadequate residual pressure under simultaneous use, weak showers, and complaints that are difficult to diagnose after the PRV is installed. The defense is to size the PRV to the building peak demand using the manufacturer's flow curve, confirming the residual pressure at peak flow meets the fixture minimum.

### Setting the PRV by the Dial or Turn Count Without a Gauge

A plumber sets the PRV by counting turns from the factory setting, trusting the dial indication, and the actual delivered pressure is 75 psi — above the safe range and stressing every fixture — or 45 psi, starving the upper floor. The trap is that the dial and the actual output differ, and setting without a gauge is a guess. The mechanism is that manufacturing variation and downstream fixture mix shift the actual output away from the dial indication. The false signal is that the PRV is "set to 60." The harm is either excessive pressure (above 80 psi, causing fixture damage, water hammer, and code violation) or inadequate pressure (below 40 psi, causing weak fixtures). The defense is to always set the PRV with a gauge on a downstream hose bibb, confirm the delivered pressure in the 50 to 60 psi range, and verify the top-floor residual accounting for elevation loss.

### Omitting the Strainer or Choosing a PRV Without One

A plumber installs a PRV without a strainer to save cost, and within a few years sediment from the municipal main fouls the PRV seat, causing pressure drift, chatter, and eventual failure. The trap is that the strainer adds a small cost and the sediment problem is invisible at installation, so it is easy to omit. The mechanism is that municipal water carries sediment that accumulates on the PRV seat and prevents full closure, causing the downstream pressure to creep up toward the inlet pressure. The false signal is that the PRV "works fine" initially. The harm is pressure drift that overpressurizes the system, premature PRV failure, and a service call to clean or replace the valve. The defense is to select a PRV with an integral strainer, to install it upstream of the PRV seat, and to schedule periodic strainer cleaning.

### Locating the PRV in an Inaccessible Spot

A plumber installs the PRV in a tight crawlspace corner or behind a finished wall during new construction, and years later the strainer needs cleaning and the PRV needs service, but the access requires demolition. The trap is that the inaccessible location is convenient during construction and the service need is years away, so the consequence is invisible at installation. The mechanism is that the PRV is a service component with a strainer and a seat that require periodic attention, and concealing it converts routine service into demolition. The false signal is that the PRV "is installed." The harm is a service nightmare and a PRV that goes unserviced until it fails. The defense is to locate the PRV in an accessible utility area, with unions, a downstream shutoff, clearance for gauge and adjustment, and a drain for the strainer cleanout.

### Using the Wrong Expansion Tank Pre-Charge

A plumber installs an expansion tank straight from the box, factory pre-charged to 40 psi, on a system running at 60 psi, and the tank's acceptance volume is reduced because the lower pre-charge lets water in at rest, so it cannot absorb the full thermal expansion and the pressure still spikes. The trap is that the pre-charge is invisible and the tank "looks installed," but the pre-charge must match the system cold pressure for the tank to have its full acceptance volume available. The mechanism is that the air-side pre-charge sets the water level in the tank at rest; a low pre-charge fills the tank partially with water at rest, leaving less volume for expansion. The false signal is that the tank "is in place." The harm is inadequate expansion control and continued pressure spikes despite the tank. The defense is to check the pre-charge with a tire gauge before installation, adjust it to the measured system cold pressure, and re-check periodically.

## Self-Check

- Did I measure the incoming static pressure with a calibrated gauge at a representative time before deciding whether a PRV is required, and is a PRV installed if the pressure exceeds 80 psi?
- Did I size the PRV to the building peak demand using the manufacturer's flow curve, confirming the residual pressure at peak flow meets the fixture minimum, rather than sizing by pipe size alone?
- Did I install a thermal expansion tank on the closed system the PRV creates, sized to the water heater volume and system pressure per the manufacturer chart?
- Did I check and adjust the expansion tank pre-charge to the measured system cold pressure before installation, rather than using the factory pre-charge?
- Did I set the PRV output with a gauge on a downstream hose bibb to deliver 50 to 60 psi, and verify the top-floor residual pressure accounting for elevation loss?
- Did I select a PRV with an integral strainer, a replaceable seat or cartridge, and the appropriate check or bypass configuration for the local code?
- Did I locate the PRV in an accessible utility area with unions, a downstream shutoff, clearance for gauge and adjustment, and a drain for the strainer cleanout?
- Did I confirm the system pressure remains below 80 psi at all fixtures, including during water heater heating cycles, by measuring with a logging gauge?
- If a T&P valve is discharging, did I investigate thermal expansion pressure spikes before replacing the valve?
- Did I document the measured incoming pressure, the PRV size and model, the set pressure, the expansion tank size and pre-charge, and any logged readings?
