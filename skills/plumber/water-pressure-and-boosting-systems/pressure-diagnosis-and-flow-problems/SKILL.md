# Pressure Diagnosis and Flow Problems

A "low pressure" complaint is the most common plumbing service call, and it is also the most misdiagnosed, because the symptom — weak flow at a fixture — has at least a dozen causes and only one of them is actually low supply pressure. The judgment problem is that pressure and flow are related but distinct quantities, and a fixture that delivers weak flow can have perfectly adequate static pressure but a restriction that kills the flow under demand. A plumber who diagnoses by feel, turning up the PRV or prescribing a booster pump without measuring, treats the symptom and often worsens the problem. This skill covers the diagnostic discipline that identifies the actual cause of a pressure or flow complaint before any component is changed.

## Core Rules

### Measure Static and Residual Pressure Before Changing Anything

The first step in any pressure complaint is to measure, with a gauge, both the static pressure (no flow) and the residual pressure (under a known flow) at representative points in the system. Static pressure at a hose bibb near the meter tells you the supply; residual pressure at the same point while a fixture runs tells you whether the supply holds up under demand; static and residual at the top floor tells you the effect of elevation and friction. The disciplined rule is to measure before adjusting any valve, replacing any component, or prescribing any solution, and to record the readings. The trap is adjusting the PRV or prescribing a booster based on the complaint alone, which addresses the wrong cause. Pressure is a measured quantity; diagnose it with a gauge.

### Distinguish a Pressure Problem From a Volume Problem

A weak shower can be low pressure (the supply cannot deliver) or low volume (a restriction limits the flow despite adequate pressure), and the two require different fixes. The diagnostic method is to measure static pressure at the fixture (adequate or not), then measure flow rate at the fixture with a bucket and stopwatch (gallons in a known time), then compare to the fixture's rated flow. If static pressure is adequate but flow is low, the problem is a restriction — a clogged aerator or showerhead, a faulty valve cartridge, a corroded riser, a partially closed shutoff, an undersized pipe. If static pressure is low, the problem is the supply — low municipal pressure, a faulty PRV, an undersized main, elevation loss. The disciplined rule is to separate pressure from volume before prescribing: pressure problems need supply-side fixes; volume problems need restriction-side fixes.

### Check the Simple and Common Causes First

Most "low pressure" complaints have simple causes that are cheap to check: a clogged aerator or showerhead (mineral scale, especially in hard water), a partially closed shutoff valve (a gate valve stuck half-open, a ball valve not fully turned), a faulty pressure-balance or thermostatic shower valve (a failing cartridge that restricts flow), or a whole-house filter or softener that is clogged or bypassed. The disciplined approach is to check these first — remove and inspect the aerator, verify every valve is fully open, check the filter pressure drop — before measuring deeper or prescribing supply-side fixes. The trap is jumping to the expensive diagnosis (a booster pump, a main replacement) before checking the five-minute, five-dollar causes. The disciplined rule is to clear the simple causes before escalating.

### Identify Whether the Problem Is Isolated or Whole-House

A pressure or flow problem at one fixture has a different cause than the same problem at every fixture, and the scope of the complaint narrows the diagnosis. An isolated problem (one weak shower, one slow faucet) points to a fixture-level cause — a clogged aerator, a faulty cartridge, a restricted supply stop. A whole-house problem (every fixture weak) points to a system-level cause — low municipal pressure, a faulty PRV, an undersized or corroded main, a partially closed main shutoff. The disciplined approach is to determine the scope first (check multiple fixtures on multiple floors), then narrow the diagnosis to the level the scope indicates. The trap is treating a whole-house problem as if it were isolated (changing one shower cartridge) or an isolated problem as if it were whole-house (prescribing a booster for one weak shower).

### Locate Hidden Restrictions With a Systematic Pressure Survey

When the simple causes are clear and the problem persists, the cause is often a hidden restriction — a corroded galvanized riser, an undersized branch, a partially closed valve inside a wall, a kinked supply tube. The diagnostic method is a systematic pressure survey: measure static and residual pressure at successive points from the meter toward the affected fixture, and locate the point where the residual pressure drops disproportionately under flow. The drop localizes the restriction. For example, if the residual holds at the basement hose bibb but drops at the second-floor fixture, the restriction is between those points — a corroded riser, an undersized branch, or a hidden valve. The disciplined rule is to trace the pressure drop along the path to localize the restriction before opening walls or replacing pipe.

### Verify the Fix With Measurement, Not by Owner Impression

After a repair, verify the result with the same measurements used in diagnosis — static and residual pressure, fixture flow rate — and compare to the pre-repair readings and to the fixture's rated performance. The disciplined rule is to confirm the fix objectively, not by asking the owner "how does it feel," because owner impression is subjective and a fix that "feels better" may not have changed the measured flow. The trap is closing the call on owner impression and having the complaint recur because the actual cause was not addressed. Document the before and after readings so the fix is verifiable.

## Common Traps

### Diagnosing by Feel and Adjusting the PRV Without Measuring

A homeowner complains of a weak shower, and the plumber turns up the PRV by a turn or two without measuring, which raises the whole-house pressure, stresses every fitting, and does not fix the shower because the cause was a clogged showerhead. The trap is that the adjustment "feels like doing something" and the homeowner may report it "feels better" briefly, but the actual cause is unaddressed and the system is now overpressurized. The mechanism is that the symptom (weak shower) has many causes, and adjusting the PRV addresses only one (low supply pressure) while ignoring the rest. The false signal is that the homeowner "is happier." The harm is an unresolved root cause, a system running above 80 psi, and accelerated fixture and fitting wear. The defense is to always measure static and residual pressure before adjusting any control, and to clear simple causes before touching the PRV.

### Confusing a Volume Restriction With a Pressure Problem

A plumber diagnoses a slow-running lavatory as "low pressure" and prescribes a booster pump, when the actual cause is a clogged aerator restricting the flow despite adequate static pressure, and a five-minute aerator cleaning would have fixed it. The trap is that the symptom (slow flow) feels like low pressure, but pressure and flow are distinct, and a restriction kills flow without changing static pressure. The mechanism is that a restriction creates a pressure drop only under flow, so static pressure measures fine but residual at the fixture is low. The false signal is that the flow "is weak, so pressure must be low." The harm is an expensive, unnecessary booster pump that does not fix the restriction, plus the energy and noise of the pump. The defense is to measure static and residual pressure and the fixture flow rate, and to distinguish a pressure problem (low static) from a volume problem (low flow with adequate static).

### Skipping the Simple Causes and Jumping to the Expensive Fix

A plumber faced with a whole-house low-flow complaint prescribes a main replacement or a booster pump without first checking the main shutoff, the filter, the softener, and the PRV, and the actual cause is a main shutoff that is partially closed or a clogged whole-house filter. The trap is that the expensive fix "addresses the system" and feels thorough, while the simple checks feel trivial, but the simple causes are the most common. The mechanism is that a partially closed valve or a clogged filter restricts flow to the whole house while leaving static pressure nearly unchanged. The false signal is that the problem "is everywhere, so it must be the main." The harm is an expensive, unnecessary repair that does not fix a closed valve or a dirty filter. The defense is to check the simple causes first — verify every valve is fully open, check the filter and softener pressure drop, inspect aerators and showerheads — before escalating.

### Treating an Isolated Problem as Whole-House or Vice Versa

A plumber treats one weak shower as a whole-house problem and prescribes a booster, when every other fixture in the house is fine and the shower's cause is a faulty pressure-balance cartridge. Or conversely, a plumber treats a whole-house problem as isolated and changes one shower cartridge, when every fixture is weak and the cause is a faulty PRV. The trap is that the scope of the complaint was not determined before the diagnosis, so the fix targets the wrong level. The mechanism is that isolated and whole-house problems have different cause sets, and conflating them leads to the wrong fix. The false signal is that the fix "addresses the complaint." The harm is an unnecessary component change that does not resolve the actual cause. The defense is to determine the scope first by checking multiple fixtures on multiple floors, then narrow the diagnosis to the level the scope indicates.

### Ignoring a Hidden Restriction and Prescribing a Supply-Side Fix

A plumber diagnoses a weak second-floor shower as low supply pressure and prescribes a booster, when the actual cause is a corroded galvanized riser that restricts flow to the upper floor, and the booster would raise the static pressure without fixing the restriction. The trap is that the static pressure at the meter looks adequate and the residual at the fixture is low, but the cause is the pipe between them, not the supply. The mechanism is that a corroded or undersized pipe creates a flow-dependent pressure drop that a booster cannot overcome efficiently. The false signal is that "more pressure will fix it." The harm is an expensive booster that marginally helps and a corroded riser that continues to worsen until it fails. The defense is to perform a systematic pressure survey from the meter to the fixture, localizing the drop to identify the restriction, before prescribing a supply-side fix.

### Closing the Call on Owner Impression Without Verifying the Fix

A plumber replaces a shower cartridge based on a low-flow complaint, asks the owner "how does it feel," gets a positive answer, and closes the call, but the measured flow is unchanged because the actual cause was a clogged showerhead, and the complaint recurs in a week. The trap is that owner impression is subjective and a new cartridge "feels different" even if the flow did not change, so the positive answer is unreliable. The mechanism is that the verification was subjective rather than measured, so the actual cause was never confirmed fixed. The false signal is that the owner "is satisfied." The harm is a recurring complaint, a callback, and a credibility loss. The defense is to verify every fix with the same measurements used in diagnosis — static and residual pressure, fixture flow rate — and to document the before and after readings.

## Self-Check

- Did I measure static and residual pressure with a gauge at representative points (near the meter, at the top floor) before adjusting any valve or replacing any component?
- Did I distinguish a pressure problem (low static pressure) from a volume problem (low flow with adequate static pressure) by measuring both pressure and fixture flow rate?
- Did I check the simple and common causes first — clogged aerator or showerhead, partially closed shutoff, faulty valve cartridge, clogged filter or softener — before escalating?
- Did I determine whether the problem is isolated (one fixture) or whole-house (every fixture), and narrow the diagnosis to the level the scope indicates?
- For a persistent problem, did I perform a systematic pressure survey from the meter toward the affected fixture to localize a hidden restriction?
- Did I avoid adjusting the PRV or prescribing a booster without measurement, especially for a complaint that may be a local restriction?
- Did I verify the fix with the same measurements used in diagnosis (static and residual pressure, fixture flow rate), not by owner impression alone?
- Did I document the before and after readings so the diagnosis and fix are verifiable?
- If the cause is a corroded galvanized or undersized pipe, did I recommend pipe replacement rather than a supply-side fix that masks the restriction?
- Did I confirm the whole-house static pressure remains below 80 psi after any adjustment, to avoid overpressurizing the system?
