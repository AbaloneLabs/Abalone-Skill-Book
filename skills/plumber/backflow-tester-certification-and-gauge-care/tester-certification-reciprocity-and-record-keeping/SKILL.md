# Tester Certification Reciprocity and Record Keeping

A backflow test is only legally valid if the tester held a current, recognized certification at the moment of the test, in the jurisdiction where the assembly sits, and the record of that test must survive long enough to defend the tester and the purveyor in a contamination lawsuit that may surface years later. The judgment problem is that certification is not a single national credential: it is issued by various bodies (state health departments, ABPA, ASSE, local water purveyors), it expires on a fixed cycle (commonly three years), it requires continuing education units for renewal, and reciprocity between jurisdictions is partial and conditional. A tester who lets a cert lapse, who tests outside a jurisdiction that does not reciprocate, or who discards records after one year produces tests that are legally void and personal exposure that insurance may not cover. This skill covers the certification, reciprocity, record-retention, and liability decisions that keep a tester's work defensible.

## Core Rules

### Confirm Certification Is Current and Jurisdiction-Recognized Before Every Test

A backflow test is invalid if the tester's certification was expired, suspended, or not recognized by the jurisdiction or purveyor at the test date. Certification cycles vary (often three years), and the renewal often requires a documented number of continuing education units (CEUs) plus a practical or written re-exam. The disciplined rule is to verify, before each job, that the certification is unexpired, that it covers the assembly types to be tested (some certs restrict by size or type), and that the issuing body is accepted by the local purveyor or AHJ. Record the certification number and expiration on the test form. A tester who cannot prove current, recognized certification has no business signing a form, and a form signed under a lapsed cert is void even if the procedure was perfect.

### Treat Reciprocity as Conditional and Verify It Before Crossing Jurisdictions

Reciprocity is not automatic. One state may accept ABPA or ASSE certification outright; a neighboring state may require a separate application, a fee, and proof of CEUs; a city purveyor may require its own tester registration on top of any state cert. The trap is assuming that "certified in one place means certified everywhere." The disciplined rule is to confirm reciprocity in writing with the receiving jurisdiction before testing there, to carry the reciprocity letter or registration card, and to record that recognition reference on the form. When reciprocity is unclear or denied, the tester must either obtain the local credential or decline the work; testing without recognition produces records the purveyor cannot accept and exposes the tester to practicing without qualification.

### Retain Records for the Longest Applicable Period, Typically Three to Ten Years

Test records are the legal evidence relied on after a contamination event, and retention periods vary by jurisdiction and hazard level: many states require three years, some require up to ten, and high-hazard (medical, industrial) assemblies often carry the longest retention. The disciplined rule is to retain records for the longest period that applies to the tester's work (a safe default is ten years for all records), to store them in a retrievable system (paper or electronic with backup), and to include the assembly identification, readings, gauge calibration date, and tester certification number on every record. The retention clock starts at the test date, not the billing date. Destroying records before the period expires destroys the only defense in a lawsuit and may violate purveyor rules.

### Report Test Results to the Water Purveyor Within the Required Timeframe

Most purveyors require the completed test form within a fixed window after the test (commonly 10 to 30 days), and some require immediate notification of a failed assembly so the supply can be protected. The disciplined rule is to know each purveyor's reporting deadline and format (paper form, portal upload, or both), to submit within the window, and to flag any failed assembly that was not repaired and re-tested so the purveyor can act. Late or unreported tests leave the purveyor blind to an unprotected hazard, and a contamination event traced to an unreported failure converts a routine test into a liability claim. Document the submission date and method.

### Carry Liability Insurance and Document Repairs and Re-Tests to Limit Exposure

A tester who passes a failed assembly, or who damages an assembly during testing, can be named in a contamination or property-damage lawsuit. Professional liability (errors and omissions) and general liability insurance are the tester's financial defense, and many purveyors and commercial clients require proof of coverage. The disciplined rule is to carry adequate insurance, to document every repair with parts replaced and the re-test readings, to photograph assemblies when relevant, and to never sign a form asserting a test that was not fully performed. If a contamination event is suspected, preserve all records and notify the insurer promptly. Falsified or incomplete records are the fastest path to an uncovered claim and loss of certification.

## Common Traps

### Letting the Certification Lapse and Testing Anyway

A tester's three-year cert expired last month, but they continue testing because "the renewal is in the mail." The trap is that every test signed under a lapsed cert is void. The mechanism is that certification validity is a precondition of a legal test. The false signal is a signed form that looks complete. The harm is that in a contamination event the tester has no qualified standing, the purveyor cannot rely on the record, and the tester may face fines or debarment. The defense is to track expiration, complete CEUs early, and stop testing the day the cert lapses until renewal is confirmed.

### Assuming Reciprocity Where None Exists

A tester certified in State A crosses to State B and tests assemblies, assuming the cert transfers. The trap is that State B requires a separate application that was never filed. The mechanism is that reciprocity is jurisdiction-by-jurisdiction and conditional. The false signal is that the tester "is certified." The harm is records the State B purveyor cannot accept and potential citation for unqualified practice. The defense is to confirm reciprocity in writing before testing in a new jurisdiction and to carry the proof.

### Destroying Records After One Year

A tester or firm shreds test records after a year to save space, not realizing the jurisdiction requires three to ten years. The trap is that the only evidence of a properly performed test is gone. The mechanism is that retention is a legal and purveyor requirement. The false signal is a clean filing cabinet. The harm is that a contamination lawsuit filed three years later has no defense records, and the destruction itself may be a violation. The defense is to retain records for the longest applicable period, defaulting to ten years, in a retrievable system.

### Missing the Purveyor Reporting Deadline

A tester completes the test but sits on the form for six weeks, missing a 30-day reporting window, and a failed assembly sits unprotected in the meantime. The trap is that the test was performed but not communicated. The mechanism is that the purveyor acts on submitted records, not on tests performed in silence. The false signal is a completed form in the truck. The harm is an unprotected hazard and a late-filing violation. The defense is to submit within the required window and to immediately notify the purveyor of any unrepaired failure.

### Signing Forms Without Insurance or After Incomplete Tests

A tester signs a form asserting a full test that was only partially performed, or operates without liability insurance because "nothing ever happens." The trap is that the signature is a legal attestation and the lack of insurance is uncovered exposure. The mechanism is that the form is relied on as proof of protection. The false signal is a clean pass. The harm is that a contamination event converts the incomplete test into an uncovered liability claim and possible certification revocation. The defense is to carry insurance, perform the full procedure, and never sign for a test not fully completed.

## Self-Check

- Did I verify that the tester certification is current, unexpired, and covers the assembly types and sizes being tested?
- Is the issuing body of the certification recognized by the local water purveyor or AHJ, and did I record the certification number and expiration on the form?
- If testing in a new jurisdiction, did I confirm reciprocity or registration in writing and carry that proof?
- Did I complete the required CEUs and renewal before the certification expiration, and did I stop testing if the cert lapsed?
- Am I retaining test records for the longest applicable period (default ten years), in a retrievable system with backup?
- Does every retained record include assembly identification, readings, gauge serial and calibration date, tester certification number, and pass/fail?
- Did I submit the completed test form to the purveyor within the required timeframe (commonly 10 to 30 days)?
- Did I immediately notify the purveyor of any failed assembly that was not repaired and re-tested?
- Do I carry professional and general liability insurance adequate to my work, and can I produce proof of coverage if requested?
- For any repair, did I document the parts replaced and the re-test readings, and did I avoid signing any form asserting a test I did not fully perform?
