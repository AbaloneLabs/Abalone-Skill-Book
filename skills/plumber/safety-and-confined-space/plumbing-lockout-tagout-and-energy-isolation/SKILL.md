# Plumbing Lockout-Tagout and Energy Isolation

Plumbing service and installation often involves equipment and systems that carry hazardous energy: electrical power (pumps, controls, water heaters, boilers), gas (fuel piping, appliances), steam and high-temperature water (boilers, hydronic systems), hydraulic and pressurized water (water mains, pressure tanks), and thermal energy (hot water and steam lines). The judgment problem is that the unexpected release of this energy during service — a pump that starts on a float switch, a gas valve that is opened, a steam line that is energized, a pressurized water line that is opened — causes shocks, burns, cuts, and crush injuries, and the energy sources are often multiple and not obvious (a pump has electrical power, may have a remote start, and is connected to pressurized water). A plumber who services a pump by flipping the local disconnect without locking it out, or who opens a gas line without verifying the supply is off, is exposed to an unexpected energization. This skill covers the lockout-tagout and energy isolation decisions that prevent energy-release injuries.

## Core Rules

### Identify All Energy Sources Before Beginning Service

Before servicing any plumbing equipment or system, identify all energy sources: electrical (the power supply to pumps, controls, water heaters, boilers, and the location of the disconnects — local and remote, including automatic starts from float switches, timers, or building automation); gas (the fuel supply to appliances and the location of the shutoff valves); steam and high-temperature water (the supply and return, and the isolation valves); pressurized water (the supply pressure, the isolation valves, and any stored pressure in tanks or elevated lines); and thermal (hot water and steam lines that retain heat after isolation). The trap is identifying only the obvious energy source (the local electrical disconnect) and missing the others (a remote start, a gas supply, stored pressure, residual heat). The disciplined rule is to identify all energy sources — electrical, gas, steam, pressurized water, thermal — and the location of each isolation point, before beginning service.

### Apply Lockout-Tagout to Each Energy Source

Each identified energy source is isolated and locked out with a personal lock and a tag identifying the worker and the date. Electrical disconnects are switched off and locked; gas valves are closed and locked (or the valve is disabled per the procedure); steam and water valves are closed and locked (or chained and locked); stored pressure is relieved (draining or venting tanks and lines); and residual thermal energy is allowed to cool or is protected against. Each worker applies their own personal lock (each worker, each lock — no shared locks, no master keys), and in group lockout, each worker applies a personal lock to a group lockout device (a hasp or a box). The trap is isolating without locking (the disconnect is off but not locked, and a coworker or a remote start re-energizes it), or using a shared lock or a tag without a lock (a tag is a warning, not a physical barrier). The disciplined rule is to apply a personal lock and a tag to each energy source's isolation point, and to use a group lockout device for multi-worker service.

### Verify Zero Energy Before Beginning Work

After lockout-tagout, verify that the energy is actually isolated — "zero energy" — before beginning work: try to start the equipment from its normal controls (the start button, the float switch) and confirm it does not start; check the electrical voltage at the equipment (with a verified voltage tester) and confirm it is de-energized; check the gas pressure downstream of the isolation and confirm it is zero; check the water or steam pressure and confirm it is zero (and that the line is cool, for thermal). The verification catches isolation failures (the wrong disconnect, a bypassed valve, a backfeed from another source). The trap is assuming the isolation worked without verifying, and beginning work on an energized or pressurized system. The disciplined rule is to verify zero energy (try-start, voltage test, pressure test, temperature check) at each isolated source before beginning work.

### Coordinate Group Lockout With Other Trades on Commercial and Industrial Sites

On a commercial or industrial site, multiple trades may service the same equipment or system (a boiler may have the plumber, the electrician, and the controls technician all working on it), and group lockout coordinates the isolation: each worker applies a personal lock to a group lockout device (a hasp on the disconnect, or a lock box holding the keys to the isolation locks), so that no worker can re-energize the equipment while another is still working. The coordination requires a clear procedure, a designated authorized person managing the group lockout, and clear communication when workers join or leave the group. The trap is each trade isolating independently without group coordination, so one trade re-energizes while another is still working. The disciplined rule is to use a group lockout device for multi-trade service, with each worker applying a personal lock, and to coordinate through a designated authorized person.

### Restore Service Safely After Service

After service, the restoration is the reverse of the isolation: each worker removes their personal lock and tag (only the worker who applied the lock removes it — no one removes another's lock except in a specific, documented procedure for an absent worker); the work area is inspected for tools, materials, and personnel; the equipment is checked for proper reassembly and safe condition; and the energy is restored in the correct sequence (electrical, gas, water, steam) with the equipment in a safe state (controls in off, valves positioned). The trap is restoring energy before the work area is clear, or restoring in the wrong sequence (e.g., energizing a boiler before the water is restored, damaging it). The disciplined rule is to remove locks and tags only by the applying worker, to inspect the work area, to verify the equipment is safe, and to restore energy in the correct sequence.

## Common Traps

### Identifying Only the Obvious Energy Source

A plumber identifies the local electrical disconnect for a pump but misses the remote start (a float switch, a building automation signal) and the pressurized water, and the pump starts or the water sprays during service. The trap is that the obvious source is not the only source. The mechanism is that equipment has multiple energy sources. The false signal is that "the disconnect is off." The harm is an unexpected start or a pressure release. The defense is to identify all energy sources (electrical, gas, steam, pressurized water, thermal) and the location of each isolation point.

### Isolating Without Locking, or Using a Tag Without a Lock

A plumber isolates a disconnect by switching it off but does not lock it, and a coworker or a remote start re-energizes it during service; or uses a tag without a lock, which is a warning but not a physical barrier. The trap is that the isolation seems sufficient but is not physically secured. The mechanism is that a lock is a physical barrier and a tag is not. The false signal is that "the disconnect is off." The harm is re-energization and injury. The defense is to apply a personal lock and a tag to each isolation point.

### Assuming Isolation Worked Without Verifying Zero Energy

A plumber applies lockout-tagout but does not verify zero energy, and begins work on an energized or pressurized system (the wrong disconnect was locked, or a valve was bypassed). The trap is that the isolation seems correct but is not verified. The mechanism is that isolation can fail (wrong point, backfeed). The false signal is that "the lock is on." The harm is energization or pressure during work. The defense is to verify zero energy (try-start, voltage test, pressure test, temperature check) before beginning work.

### Each Trade Isolating Independently Without Group Coordination

On a multi-trade service, each trade isolates independently without group coordination, and one trade re-energizes while another is still working. The trap is that the independent isolation lacks coordination. The mechanism is that group lockout coordinates multi-trade service. The false signal is that "each trade has its own lock." The harm is re-energization while a trade is working. The defense is to use a group lockout device with each worker's personal lock and to coordinate through a designated authorized person.

### Restoring Energy Before the Work Area Is Clear or in the Wrong Sequence

A plumber restores energy before the work area is inspected for tools and personnel, or restores in the wrong sequence (energizing a boiler before the water is restored), causing injury or equipment damage. The trap is that the restoration is rushed or mis-sequenced. The mechanism is that restoration requires a clear area and the correct sequence. The false signal is that "the service is done." The harm is injury or equipment damage. The defense is to remove locks only by the applying worker, inspect the work area, verify the equipment is safe, and restore energy in the correct sequence.

## Self-Check

- Did I identify all energy sources (electrical, gas, steam, pressurized water, thermal) for the equipment or system before beginning service?
- Did I identify the location of each isolation point (disconnects, valves, relief points)?
- Did I apply a personal lock and a tag to each isolation point (no shared locks, no tags without locks)?
- Did I verify zero energy (try-start from normal controls, voltage test, gas pressure test, water/steam pressure test, temperature check) at each isolated source?
- For stored pressure (tanks, elevated lines), did I relieve the pressure before opening the system?
- For thermal energy (hot water, steam lines), did I allow the system to cool or protect against the residual heat?
- For multi-trade service, did I use a group lockout device with each worker's personal lock, coordinated through a designated authorized person?
- Did I communicate clearly when workers joined or left the group lockout?
- After service, did I remove my locks and tags myself (or follow the documented absent-worker procedure)?
- Did I inspect the work area for tools and personnel, verify the equipment is safe, and restore energy in the correct sequence (electrical, gas, water, steam), and document the lockout-tagout and the restoration?
