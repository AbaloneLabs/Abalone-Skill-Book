# Combustible Gas Detector Use and Leak Isolation

Locating a gas leak with an instrument is a measurement problem, not a sniff test, and the wrong measurement method turns a controlled search into an ignition event or a missed leak. The judgment problem is that the human nose detects mercaptan odorant at roughly 1 percent of the lower explosive limit (LEL), which is good enough to know gas is present but useless for locating the source or judging severity — and agents commonly substitute a flame, a lighter, or an uncalibrated nose for a calibrated combustible gas detector (CGD), any of which can ignite the mixture or miss a slow leak. Agents also misread the instrument: a single snapshot reading is meaningless because gas migrates and stratifies, and the 10 percent LEL threshold is an action level that demands evacuation, not a "safe" reading to work under. This skill covers CGD selection and use, LEL interpretation, systematic sweep technique, soap-bubble pinpointing, and the calibration and bump-test discipline that keeps the readings trustworthy.

## Core Rules

### Use a Calibrated Combustible Gas Detector, Never a Flame or Nose Alone

The only acceptable instrument for locating a gas leak is a calibrated, bump-tested combustible gas detector (CGD) tuned to the target gas — natural gas (methane) or propane — and reading in percent LEL. A flame, lighter, or match is never acceptable as a leak-location tool; the explosive range (5 to 15 percent for natural gas, 2.1 to 9.5 percent for propane) is wide enough that a leak area can be inside it without obvious warning. The nose is a trigger to evacuate and get the instrument, not a locating tool, because odor fatigue desensitizes the sense within minutes and mercaptan distribution does not match gas concentration. The trap is using a lighter "just to check a joint," which can ignite a pocket already in the explosive range. The disciplined rule is to bring a calibrated CGD to every reported odor, to never introduce an open flame for leak location, and to evacuate if the reading demands it before continuing the search.

### Interpret LEL, Odor Threshold, and Explosive Range Correctly

The LEL is the lowest concentration in air that will burn (5 percent for methane, 2.1 percent for propane); the upper explosive limit (UEL) is the highest (15 percent methane, 9.5 percent propane); between them the mixture is explosive. A CGD reads in percent LEL, so 100 percent LEL equals the LEL concentration. The 10 percent LEL threshold (0.5 percent methane, 0.21 percent propane in air) is the widely adopted action level at which personnel evacuate and ventilate — it is not a "safe to keep working" reading. Odor threshold sits near 1 percent LEL, so a detectable smell already implies a reading worth monitoring. The trap is treating a 9 percent LEL reading as "almost safe" or reading percent LEL as percent gas and underestimating severity. The disciplined rule is to know the LEL/UEL of the gas in question, to read percent LEL as an action scale, and to evacuate and ventilate at 10 percent LEL.

### Read Trends, Not Snapshots, and Sweep Systematically

A single CGD reading is uninformative because gas migrates along pipes, through wall cavities, and (for propane) along floors, and concentrations shift with airflow. Read the trend: watch whether the reading climbs or falls as you move, and how it responds to ventilation. Sweep systematically from the known appliance outward — start at gas connectors, valve unions, and regulator vents, then trace the house line back toward the meter, checking every fitting, and for propane check low points (basements, crawl spaces, sumps) where the heavier gas pools. The trap is chasing the highest single reading, which may be a migration accumulation far from the source. The disciplined rule is to map the reading gradient, to follow the rising trend back to the source, and to check low areas for propane and high areas for natural gas.

### Pinpoint with Soap Solution After the Area Is Identified

Once the CGD narrows the leak to a fitting or component, confirm and pinpoint with a non-corrosive soap solution (50/50 dish soap and water, or a commercial leak-detector fluid) brushed onto the fitting — escaping gas forms bubbles at the exact leak point. Soap is precise, safe, and leaves no ignition risk, and it reveals small leaks that a CGD gradient can only approximate. Apply to all fittings in the suspect zone, wait 30 to 60 seconds for bubble formation on slow leaks, and inspect from multiple angles. The trap is skipping soap and tightening or replacing a fitting based only on the CGD gradient, which can miss the actual leaking joint and "repair" the wrong one. The disciplined rule is to use the CGD to localize the area and soap to pinpoint the exact fitting, and to re-test with both after any repair.

### Maintain Calibration and Bump-Test Discipline

A CGD is trustworthy only if calibrated to the target gas on the manufacturer schedule (typically annual factory calibration) and bump-tested with a known gas before each day of use. A bump test exposes the sensor to a known concentration and confirms the detector responds within tolerance; a sensor that fails the bump test is out of service until recalibrated. Catalytic-bead sensors (common in percent-LEL detectors) can be poisoned by silicone, lead, or sulfur compounds and lose sensitivity without warning, which is why the bump test is mandatory. The trap is trusting last year's calibration or skipping the bump test because the detector "turned on," and missing leaks with a desensitized sensor. The disciplined rule is to calibrate on schedule, to bump-test before each use, to record the bump test, and to take any detector that fails the bump test out of service.

## Common Traps

### Using a Flame or Lighter to Locate a Leak

The responder uses a lighter or match to check a suspected joint, believing a small flame is controlled. The trap is that the joint area can already be within the explosive range. The mechanism is that any concentration between the LEL and UEL ignites from the flame. The false signal is that "a tiny flame is safe." The harm is an explosion and burn injury. The defense is to never introduce an open flame for leak location, to use a calibrated CGD, and to pinpoint with soap solution.

### Reading Percent LEL as Percent Gas and Underestimating Severity

The responder reads 10 percent LEL and interprets it as 10 percent gas, concluding the concentration is far below explosive. The trap is that percent LEL is a fraction of the LEL concentration, not a gas percentage. The mechanism is that 10 percent LEL equals 0.5 percent methane, the action level for evacuation. The false signal is that "the number looks low." The harm is continuing to work in an atmosphere that should trigger evacuation. The defense is to understand the percent-LEL scale, to know the LEL concentration of the gas, and to evacuate at 10 percent LEL.

### Chasing the Highest Single Reading Instead of the Gradient

The responder sees a high reading in one spot and focuses the repair there, ignoring the gradient that points back to the true source. The trap is that the high reading is a migration accumulation, not the leak. The mechanism is that gas migrates along pipes and through cavities and pools away from the source. The false signal is that "the detector pegged here." The harm is repairing the wrong fitting and leaving the actual leak active. The defense is to read the trend and gradient, follow the rising reading back to the source, and pinpoint with soap before repairing.

### Skipping Soap and Repairing Based Only on the CGD Gradient

The responder tightens or replaces a fitting based on the CGD gradient without soap-confirming the exact leaking joint. The trap is that the gradient localizes an area, not a joint. The mechanism is that a CGD reading is an approximation of the leak zone. The false signal is that "the reading is highest near this fitting." The harm is repairing a non-leaking joint and leaving the leak active. The defense is to brush soap on every fitting in the suspect zone, wait for bubbles, and pinpoint the exact leak before any repair, then re-test after.

### Trusting a Detector That Was Not Bump-Tested Today

The responder uses a CGD that turns on and shows zero, assuming it works, without a same-day bump test. The trap is that a poisoned or aged catalytic-bead sensor reads falsely low while appearing functional. The mechanism is that silicone, lead, or sulfur compounds desensitize the sensor without an error indication. The false signal is that "the detector turned on and zeroed." The harm is missing a real leak because the sensor under-responds. The defense is to bump-test with a known gas before each day of use, to record the test, and to take any detector that fails out of service for recalibration.

## Self-Check

- Did I bring a calibrated combustible gas detector tuned to the target gas (methane or propane) to the reported odor, and did I avoid any open flame for leak location?
- Did I confirm the detector's factory calibration is current and perform a same-day bump test with a known gas before relying on its readings?
- Do I know the LEL and UEL of the gas in question (5 to 15 percent methane, 2.1 to 9.5 percent propane) and read percent LEL as an action scale?
- Did I evacuate and ventilate when any reading reached 10 percent LEL rather than continuing to work?
- Did I sweep systematically from the appliance outward, checking connectors, valve unions, regulator vents, and every fitting, and did I check low points for propane and high points for natural gas?
- Did I read the trend and gradient rather than a single snapshot, following the rising reading back toward the source?
- Did I pinpoint the exact leaking fitting with a non-corrosive soap solution, waiting 30 to 60 seconds for bubble formation on slow leaks?
- Did I check all fittings in the suspect zone, not just the one nearest the highest reading?
- After any repair, did I re-test the area with both the CGD and soap to confirm the leak is eliminated?
- Did I record the bump test, the readings taken, and the leak location for the service record?
