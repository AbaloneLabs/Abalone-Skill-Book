# Emergency Shutdown and Evacuation Decisions

A reported gas odor is a time-critical, life-safety event where the wrong first move can ignite an explosion or cause asphyxiation. The judgment problem is that the safest action (immediate evacuation and calling the fire department) feels passive, while the instinct to "find the leak fast" pulls the responder toward actions that create sparks — flipping a light switch, using a phone indoors, starting a fan — any of which can detonate a flammable mixture already inside the explosive range. Agents also misjudge where to isolate: shutting the interior appliance valve may not stop gas feeding from a leaking supply line upstream, and on propane the heavier-than-air gas pools in low spaces long after the source is closed. This skill covers the first-response decisions — evacuate-versus-investigate thresholds, correct isolation point, ignition-source discipline, utility coordination, re-entry criteria, and documentation — that prevent explosion, asphyxiation, and liability exposure.

## Core Rules

### Treat Any Confirmed Odor as an Immediate Evacuation Decision, Not an Investigation Decision

The default action on a reported gas odor inside a structure is to evacuate all occupants and not attempt to locate the source while people remain inside. Mercaptan odorant is detectable by the human nose at roughly 1 percent of the lower explosive limit (LEL), so by the time gas is smelled the concentration is already approaching a hazardous fraction of the explosive range (5 to 15 percent for natural gas, 2.1 to 9.5 percent for propane). The decision threshold is: if the odor is strong, persistent, or widespread, evacuate first and investigate only after the building is cleared and the gas is isolated. Investigating in place is justified only for a faint, localized odor near a known appliance where the occupant can stand at a distance and the responder can isolate without disturbing switches. The trap is inverting the order — investigating first, evacuating only if it "looks bad" — because the act of investigating (moving through gas, operating controls) is itself the ignition risk. The disciplined rule is to default to evacuation on any strong or building-wide odor, to evacuate before investigating, and to treat a "quick check" as the highest-risk action.

### Isolate at the Meter or Tank, Not Only at the Appliance

The correct isolation point for an interior gas leak is the main shutoff at the meter (natural gas) or at the tank service valve (propane), not the appliance valve, unless the leak is unambiguously confined to the appliance connector behind that valve. Shutting only the appliance valve leaves the upstream supply piping pressurized, so any leak in the house line, the riser, or a fitting continues to feed gas into the structure. The meter valve is a quarter-turn lug valve on the inlet side; a wrench turns the lug parallel to the pipe to close. On propane, the tank service valve closes the entire system. The trap is assuming the appliance valve stops the problem because the odor seems local, when the actual leak is in the concealed house line. The disciplined rule is to isolate at the meter or tank for any leak not visibly confined to the appliance connector, to know the meter-valve orientation before an emergency, and to never re-open the meter valve yourself — that is the utility's action after pressure testing and relight.

### Eliminate Ignition Sources Without Creating New Ones

Eliminating ignition sources is mandatory, but the methods themselves must not spark. Do not operate light switches, thermostats, fans, or appliances (off or on — both can arc). Do not use a landline or cell phone inside the gas area. Do not start a vehicle in an attached garage. Do not light a match or lighter to "find" the leak. Leave doors open on the way out only if it aids evacuation without disturbing sources; do not go back in to open windows. The ignition energy required to ignite natural gas is extremely low (roughly 0.25 millijoules), so even static discharge or a doorbell can be enough. The trap is using an electronic device or switch to "be safe" (calling for help from inside, turning off a light to prevent a spark) and creating the very arc that ignites the mixture. The disciplined rule is to leave the building without touching electrical devices, to call 911 and the gas utility from outside or from a neighbor's phone, and to keep all personnel and vehicles upwind and clear of the building.

### Coordinate with the Fire Department and Utility, and Do Not Re-Enter Until Cleared

Once occupants are out and gas is isolated at the meter or tank, call 911 (fire department) and the gas utility. The fire department brings atmospheric monitoring (combustible gas indicators calibrated to percent LEL), ventilation capability, and authority to control the scene; the utility owns the meter, the service line, and the relight. Do not re-enter the building to "check" until the fire department or utility has monitored and declared it safe, because propane pools in basements and natural gas can linger in wall cavities and re-accumulate from a continuing source. Re-entry is safe only when monitoring shows 0 percent LEL throughout the space, the source is isolated and repaired or capped, and the space has been ventilated. The trap is the occupant or plumber re-entering after the smell fades, because odor fatigue and ventilation can mask a still-hazardous concentration. The disciplined rule is to treat the scene as hot until instrument-confirmed cold, to defer re-entry to the authority having jurisdiction, and to never restore gas until the system is pressure-tested and the utility relights.

### Document the Incident for Liability and Follow-Up

Every gas emergency response must be documented contemporaneously: time of call, who reported the odor, location and strength of odor, evacuation actions, isolation point and time, agencies notified (fire, utility), readings taken (if any), and the condition left. This documentation protects against later claims that the response was delayed or the leak left active, and it creates the record needed for the repair scope, insurance, and code compliance. Photograph the meter valve position, the appliance, and any visible damage once it is safe to do so. The trap is relying on memory after a chaotic emergency, which leaves the responder unable to prove what was done and when. The disciplined rule is to record times, actions, readings, and notifications in writing at the scene, to retain the record per company policy, and to ensure the repair and pressure-test results are appended before the job is closed.

## Common Traps

### Investigating Before Evacuating on a Strong Odor

The responder smells strong gas and begins searching for the source while occupants remain inside, believing a fast find reduces risk. The trap is that moving through and operating in a gassed space is the ignition risk. The mechanism is that any switch, phone, or motor arc can detonate a mixture already in the explosive range. The false signal is that "finding it fast makes everyone safer." The harm is an explosion that would have been avoided by evacuating first. The defense is to evacuate on any strong or widespread odor before investigating, and to isolate at the meter from outside or with no electrical manipulation.

### Shutting Only the Appliance Valve on a Concealed House-Line Leak

The responder shuts the appliance valve, the odor near the appliance fades, and everyone assumes the leak is controlled. The trap is that the actual leak is in the concealed supply piping upstream of that valve. The mechanism is that the appliance valve isolates only the appliance, not the house line, riser, or fittings feeding it. The false signal is that "the smell went down." The harm is continued gas accumulation from the upstream leak, leading to explosion or asphyxiation after the responder leaves. The defense is to isolate at the meter or tank for any leak not visibly confined to the appliance connector, and to leave the meter off for the utility to verify.

### Using a Phone or Switch Indoors to "Be Safe"

The responder reaches for a cell phone or flips a light switch to call for help or darken the area, believing the action reduces risk. The trap is that the act of operating an electrical device creates the arc that ignites the gas. The mechanism is that gas-air mixtures ignite at very low energy, and switches and phones produce sparks or RF energy. The false signal is that "calling for help is the safe move." The harm is detonation of the accumulated mixture. The defense is to leave the building without touching electrical devices and to call 911 and the utility from outside or from a neighbor's phone.

### Re-Entering After the Smell Fades

Occupants or the plumber re-enter after the odor seems to dissipate, assuming the danger has passed. The trap is that odor fatigue and partial ventilation can mask a still-hazardous concentration, and propane pools in low spaces. The mechanism is that the nose adapts to mercaptan and gas continues to enter or stratify. The false signal is that "I can't smell it anymore." The harm is asphyxiation or explosion on re-entry into an unsafe atmosphere. The defense is to treat the scene as hot until a calibrated combustible gas indicator confirms 0 percent LEL throughout, and to defer re-entry to the fire department or utility.

### Restoring Gas Without a Pressure Test and Utility Relight

After a repair, the plumber re-opens the meter valve and relights appliances to "get the customer back on," skipping the pressure test and the utility's verification. The trap is that the repaired system may still leak, and an untested relight can introduce gas into a confined space. The mechanism is that a repair without a code pressure test (for example, 10 psi for 15 minutes minimum per many jurisdictions) is unverified, and the utility owns the meter and relight. The false signal is that "the joint is tight." The harm is a recurrence of the leak or a flashback from residual air in the line. The defense is to pressure-test the repaired system to code, purge air to the outdoors, confirm pure gas at the outlet, and defer the meter re-open and relight to the utility.

## Self-Check

- Did I treat the reported odor as an evacuation-first decision, evacuating all occupants before investigating when the odor was strong, persistent, or building-wide?
- Did I isolate at the meter (natural gas) or tank service valve (propane) rather than only at the appliance valve, unless the leak was visibly confined to the appliance connector?
- Did I leave the building without operating any light switch, thermostat, fan, appliance, phone, or vehicle, and call 911 and the gas utility from outside?
- Did I keep all personnel and vehicles upwind and clear of the building, and avoid any action (matches, lighters, doorbells) that could create an ignition source?
- Did I notify the fire department and the gas utility, and did I refrain from re-entering until the scene was declared safe by instrument (0 percent LEL) by the authority having jurisdiction?
- Did I ensure no one re-entered based on the smell fading, recognizing odor fatigue and propane pooling in low spaces?
- Did I refrain from restoring gas myself, deferring the meter re-open and relight to the utility after a code pressure test?
- Did I document the time of call, reporter, odor location and strength, evacuation, isolation point and time, agencies notified, readings, and condition left, in writing at the scene?
- Did I photograph the meter valve position, appliance, and any visible damage once it was safe to do so?
- Did I append the repair scope and pressure-test results to the incident record before closing the job?
