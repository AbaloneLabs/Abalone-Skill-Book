# Post-Repair Pressure Testing and Purge to Return to Service

Returning a gas piping system to service after a repair or new installation is the moment when an unverified joint, trapped air, or a rushed light-off can produce a flashback, an explosion at the appliance, or a leak that goes unnoticed until the building re-fillss. The judgment problem is that the repair feels finished when the last fitting is tight, so the pressure test is treated as a formality and the purge is rushed — but the test is the only objective evidence the system holds, and the purge is the only way to remove the air-gas mixture that flashes back when an appliance is first lit. Agents also tend to test only the fitting they touched, missing a disturbed joint upstream, and to relight appliances before confirming pure gas at the outlet, trusting the hiss of gas as proof. This skill covers the code pressure test, the safe purge procedure, the pure-gas confirmation, the full work-zone fitting check, and the documentation that makes the return-to-service defensible.

## Core Rules

### Pressure-Test the Entire Affected System Before Restoring Gas

Before any gas is reintroduced, the repaired or new piping must be pressure-tested to confirm it holds with no loss. The test gauge must read in the appropriate range (a 0 to 5 or 0 to 15 psi gauge for low-pressure residential tests), the system is pressurized with air or an inert gas (never with fuel gas for the test), and the pressure is held for the code-specified duration — commonly a minimum of 10 psi for 15 minutes for residential work in many jurisdictions, with stricter requirements (for example, 1.5 times working pressure or longer holds) for larger or commercial systems per the International Fuel Gas Code (IFGC) or local amendments. The test must cover the entire affected system, not just the new fitting, because disturbing one joint can crack another. The trap is testing only the repaired section at a low pressure for a minute and calling it good. The disciplined rule is to test the whole affected system to the code pressure and duration, to use a calibrated gauge, and to record the start pressure, end pressure, and hold time.

### Purge Air to the Outdoors Safely With No Ignition Sources

When a line has been opened, repaired, or newly installed, it contains air, and reintroducing fuel gas creates an air-gas mixture that passes through the explosive range (5 to 15 percent for natural gas, 2.1 to 9.5 percent for propane) as the line fills. Purging that mixture through an appliance burner is exactly how a flashback occurs. The safe purge vents the line to the outdoors through a hose connected to a downstream point, with no ignition sources in the area, while monitoring the discharge with a combustible gas detector. The purge continues until the detector reads pure gas (100 percent gas, 0 percent air) at the discharge. The trap is bleeding the mixture into the room or through the appliance to "let it clear," which puts an explosive mixture next to a pilot or igniter. The disciplined rule is to purge to the outdoors via a hose, eliminate ignition sources, and monitor with a CGD until pure gas is confirmed.

### Confirm Pure Gas at the Outlet Before Any Light-Off

Before any appliance is lit, the gas at the furthest outlet must be confirmed as pure fuel gas, not an air-gas mixture. This is verified with the CGD during the purge (reading 100 percent gas at the discharge) and confirmed again at the appliance when the connector is reattached. Lighting an appliance on a mixture is the classic flashback cause: the burner lights, the flame travels back through the mixture in the line, and the flashback damages the appliance or injures the person lighting it. The trap is hearing gas hiss and assuming the line is clear, when the first gas to arrive is a mixture. The disciplined rule is to confirm 100 percent gas at the furthest outlet with a CGD before any pilot or main burner is lit, and to purge each branch individually if the system has multiple outlets.

### Check All Fittings in the Work Zone, Not Just the New One

A repair or addition disturbs the system beyond the joint being worked on: tightening a new fitting loads the adjacent pipe, an old pipe nipple can crack, and a disturbed valve packing can weep. After the pressure test and purge, soap-test every fitting in the work zone — the new joint and every fitting a couple of joints upstream and downstream — and confirm no bubbles. The trap is testing only the fitting just made up, which can pass while a disturbed neighbor weeps. The disciplined rule is to soap-test all fittings within the zone of disturbance, to re-test with a CGD around the appliance and meter set after gas is restored, and to treat any bubble or reading as a failed test requiring rework.

### Document Test Results and the Return-to-Sequence

Every return-to-service must be documented: the test pressure, hold duration, gauge reading at start and end, the purge method and confirmation reading, the fittings soap-tested, the appliances relit and by whom, and the final leak survey. This is the liability record and the code-compliance evidence; in many jurisdictions the pressure test result is required to be reported to the authority having jurisdiction or the utility before the meter is released. The trap is treating the test as a pass/fail in the moment and leaving no record, which makes a later incident undefendable. The disciplined rule is to record the test parameters and results in writing at the scene, to retain the record per company and code requirements, and to ensure the utility performs the meter turn-on and relight where the utility owns the meter.

## Common Traps

### Testing Only the Repaired Fitting at Low Pressure for a Short Hold

The plumber pressurizes just the new section to a few psi for a minute, sees no drop, and restores gas. The trap is that a disturbed upstream joint is excluded and a short hold misses a slow leak. The mechanism is that the test must cover the whole affected system and hold long enough to reveal a slow loss. The false signal is that "the gauge didn't move." The harm is a leak from an untested joint after gas is restored. The defense is to test the entire affected system to the code pressure (commonly 10 psi minimum) for the code duration (15 minutes minimum), with a calibrated gauge.

### Purging the Air-Gas Mixture Through the Appliance Burner

The plumber opens the valve and lets the appliance purge itself through the burner, then lights it. The trap is that the mixture in the line passes through the explosive range at the burner. The mechanism is that the first gas to reach the burner is an air-gas mixture, and any ignition source flashes it back. The false signal is that "gas is reaching the burner." The harm is a flashback that damages the appliance or injures the lighter. The defense is to purge to the outdoors through a hose with no ignition sources, monitor with a CGD, and confirm pure gas before light-off.

### Lighting the Appliance Before Confirming Pure Gas at the Outlet

The plumber hears gas hissing at the burner and lights it, assuming the line is clear. The trap is that the hiss is an air-gas mixture, not pure gas. The mechanism is that the leading gas front is a mixture until the line is fully purged. The false signal is that "gas is flowing." The harm is a flashback through the mixture. The defense is to confirm 100 percent gas at the furthest outlet with a CGD before lighting any pilot or main burner, and to purge each branch separately.

### Soap-Testing Only the New Fitting and Missing a Disturbed Neighbor

The plumber soap-tests the joint just made up, sees no bubble, and leaves. The trap is that the adjacent fitting, disturbed by the work, is the one weeping. The mechanism is that making up a new joint loads the pipe and can crack or loosen a neighboring joint. The false signal is that "my joint is clean." The harm is a leak from the untested neighbor fitting after gas is restored. The defense is to soap-test every fitting in the work zone, upstream and downstream, and to re-survey with a CGD after gas is restored.

### Restoring Gas Without a Written Record of the Test

The plumber passes the test, restores gas, and leaves no documentation of pressure, duration, or results. The trap is that the test result exists only in memory and cannot be proven later. The mechanism is that a later incident triggers a records request, and none exists. The false signal is that "it passed, that's enough." The harm is undefendable liability and code-violation exposure. The defense is to record the test pressure, hold time, gauge readings, purge confirmation, fittings tested, appliances relit, and final survey in writing at the scene, and to retain it per code.

## Self-Check

- Did I pressure-test the entire affected system — not just the repaired fitting — to the code pressure (commonly 10 psi minimum) for the code duration (15 minutes minimum) with a calibrated gauge?
- Did I use air or an inert gas for the test, never fuel gas, and record the start pressure, end pressure, and hold time?
- Did I purge the air-gas mixture to the outdoors through a hose with no ignition sources in the area, rather than through the appliance burner?
- Did I monitor the purge discharge with a combustible gas detector and continue until it read 100 percent gas (0 percent air)?
- Did I confirm pure gas at the furthest outlet with the CGD before lighting any pilot or main burner?
- For multi-branch systems, did I purge each branch individually and confirm pure gas at each outlet?
- Did I soap-test every fitting in the work zone — the new joint and the neighboring fittings upstream and downstream — not just the fitting I made up?
- After restoring gas, did I re-survey the appliance, meter set, and work zone with a CGD to confirm no leak?
- Did I defer the meter turn-on and appliance relight to the utility where the utility owns the meter, rather than restoring gas myself?
- Did I document the test pressure, hold duration, gauge readings, purge method and confirmation, fittings soap-tested, appliances relit and by whom, and the final leak survey, in writing at the scene?
