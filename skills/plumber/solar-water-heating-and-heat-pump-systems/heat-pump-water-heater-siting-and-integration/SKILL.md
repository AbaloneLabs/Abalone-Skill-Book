# Heat Pump Water Heater Siting and Integration

A heat pump water heater (HPWH) is an appliance that heats water by moving heat out of the surrounding air, so its performance, longevity, and occupant acceptance depend almost entirely on where it is placed and how it is integrated with the room and the existing plumbing. The judgment problem is that an HPWH installed like a conventional electric tank — dropped into any closet, with no thought to air volume, intake clearance, condensate, or the room's heating load — will underperform, short-cycle, deliver cold complaints, or fail to reheat after a large draw. Agents miss this because the unit looks like a tank and the box only says "up to 4.0 EF," masking that the rated efficiency assumes a warm, large, unrestricted room. This skill covers the siting and integration decisions that determine whether an HPWH actually performs as rated.

## Core Rules

### Verify Room Volume, Intake Clearance, and Air Supply Match the Unit

An HPWH needs a large volume of air to draw heat from, and the manufacturer states a minimum room volume (commonly 700 to 1,000 cubic feet for a 50- to 80-gallon unit) or a minimum ducted supply. The unit also needs clearance around the intake and outlet (commonly 6 to 12 inches at the top and sides, per the manual) so air can circulate without recirculating its own cold discharge. Installing in a small closet without ducting causes the unit to cool its own intake air, dropping COP and tripping the compressor on low-evaporator-temperature limits. The disciplined rule is to confirm the room meets the manual's volume and clearance minimums, to add ducting (intake from a large space, outlet to a return or large space) when it does not, and to never block the top outlet where most units discharge cold air.

### Predict COP Against the Actual Ambient Temperature, Not the Rated Number

The rated COP (commonly 3.5 to 4.0) is measured at 67.5°F ambient (DOE test condition). COP falls as ambient falls: a unit rated 3.8 at 67°F may deliver roughly 2.5 at 50°F and may refuse to run the compressor near 40 to 45°F, falling back to the resistive element. In a cool basement or garage the year-round average COP is far below the label. The trap is promising the rated COP in a cold space. The disciplined rule is to estimate the seasonal ambient of the install location, predict the seasonal COP from the manufacturer's performance table, and to size the tank and operating mode so the compressor carries the load during the warmest part of the day or the warmest season. In consistently cold spaces, consider ducting intake from a warm buffer zone.

### Plan Condensate Drainage From the First Day of Layout

An HPWH produces condensate whenever it runs the compressor — typically 1 to 4 pints per hour in humid conditions, drawn from the room air. The condensate connection must drain by gravity to an approved receptor (floor drain, utility sink, condensate pump) with a trap or air gap as required, and the drain must not be shared with a pressure-relief discharge without an air gap. The trap is forgetting condensate entirely, so the first humid cooling-season run floods the pan or the floor, or routing the drain where it can freeze or back up. The disciplined rule is to plan the condensate drain as a primary system, sized and pitched (minimum 1/8 inch per foot), with a safe discharge point and a secondary path for high-humidity operation.

### Decide Conditioned Versus Unconditioned, and Ducted Versus Free Discharge

Where the HPWH sits changes both its COP and the building's load. In a conditioned space, the HPWH cools and dehumidifies the room (a benefit in summer, a penalty in winter, since the home's heating system must replace the removed heat). In an unconditioned garage or attic, the unit avoids cooling the house but suffers low COP in winter and may fall back to the element. Ducting the cold discharge to a return or outdoors can manage the cold-air complaint but adds static pressure that can reduce airflow and COP if the ducts are too long or small. The disciplined rule is to make the conditioned/unconditioned and ducting decisions deliberately, weighing COP, building heating/cooling interaction, and occupant comfort, and to keep duct runs within the manufacturer's maximum length and diameter.

### Stage the Backup Element and Set Operating Mode to Preserve Efficiency

Most HPWHs have one or two resistive elements as backup. If the unit is left in "high demand" or "electric" mode, or if the element staging is set so the upper element fires first on every recovery, the unit behaves like a standard electric tank and the COP advantage disappears. The disciplined rule is to set the operating mode to heat-pump-first (often "efficiency" or "heat pump only" for maximum savings, or "auto/hybrid" for balanced recovery), to confirm the compressor carries the normal load and the element only assists during large draws or low-ambient conditions, and to size the tank large enough (often 65 to 80 gallons) that the compressor, not the element, recovers the daily load.

## Common Traps

### Installing in a Small Closet Without Ducting

A plumber drops the HPWH into a small utility closet and walks away. The trap is that the room is below the minimum volume, so the unit recirculates its own cold discharge, dropping the evaporator temperature until the compressor locks out on low suction. The mechanism is that the unit consumes the heat in the small air volume faster than the room can replace it; the false signal is "it runs for a while." The harm is poor COP, long recovery, and eventual element-only operation. The defense is to verify room volume against the manual and to duct intake and discharge where the room is too small.

### Promising the Label COP in a Cold Space

A plumber quotes the 3.8 label COP for a unit installed in a 50°F garage. The trap is that the label is at 67.5°F and COP falls steeply with ambient. The mechanism is that heat-pump efficiency is proportional to the temperature lift between the air source and the water; the false signal is "it's a heat pump, so it's efficient." The harm is an unhappy customer whose energy savings are far below the quote. The defense is to estimate seasonal ambient, read the manufacturer's COP table at that ambient, and quote the realistic seasonal performance.

### Forgetting the Condensate Drain

A plumber sets the HPWH and leaves the condensate port uncapped or undrained. The trap is that condensate only appears when the compressor runs in humid air, so the system "works dry" for weeks and then floods. The mechanism is that dehumidification condenses room moisture on the evaporator; the false signal is "no water at startup." The harm is a flooded pan, floor damage, or a rusted base. The defense is to plan and connect the condensate drain before commissioning and to confirm discharge to an approved receptor.

### Leaving the Unit in Electric or High-Demand Mode

A plumber commissions the HPWH in the factory "high demand" or "electric" default to guarantee fast recovery, and never switches it to heat-pump mode. The trap is that the resistive element carries the load and the COP advantage is lost. The mechanism is that the element is a direct-resistance heater at COP 1.0; the false signal is "the water is hot." The harm is energy bills as high as the old electric tank. The defense is to set the operating mode to heat-pump-first and to confirm the compressor, not the element, recovers the daily load.

### Retrofitting Onto an Existing Tank Without Checking the Space

A plumber replaces a failed electric tank with an HPWH in the same tight closet, assuming a one-for-one swap. The trap is that the HPWH is taller, needs top and side clearance, and needs air volume and condensate drainage that the old tank did not. The mechanism is that an HPWH has fundamentally different siting requirements than a resistance tank; the false signal is "it fits in the same footprint." The harm is poor performance, code or clearance violations, and callbacks. The defense is to evaluate every retrofit against the HPWH's volume, clearance, condensate, and electrical requirements before the swap.

## Self-Check

- Does the install room meet the manufacturer's minimum volume (commonly 700 to 1,000 cubic feet), or is ducting provided to a larger space?
- Is the intake and outlet clearance met (commonly 6 to 12 inches top and sides per the manual), with the top cold-air discharge unblocked?
- Did I estimate the seasonal ambient of the location and predict the seasonal COP from the manufacturer's performance table, rather than quoting the 67.5°F label number?
- Is the condensate drain planned, connected, pitched at least 1/8 inch per foot, and discharged to an approved receptor with a trap or air gap as required?
- Did I make a deliberate conditioned-versus-unconditioned and ducted-versus-free-discharge decision, weighing COP, building load interaction, and occupant comfort?
- If ducting is used, are the duct length and diameter within the manufacturer's maximum, so static pressure does not reduce airflow and COP?
- Is the operating mode set to heat-pump-first (efficiency, auto/hybrid), and did I confirm the compressor carries the normal daily load with the element only as backup?
- Is the tank sized (often 65 to 80 gallons) so the compressor, not the resistive element, recovers the daily draw?
- For a retrofit, did I verify height, clearance, condensate path, and electrical circuit (often a dedicated 30-amp circuit) before removing the old tank?
- Did I document the room volume, clearance, ambient, condensate path, mode, and expected seasonal COP so the integration is verifiable?
