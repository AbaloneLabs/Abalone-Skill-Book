# Incident Documentation And Follow Up

Incident Documentation And Follow Up is a fitness-coaching judgment problem inside acute symptom response, stop rules, emergency action plans, staff roles, incident documentation, and follow-up. The agent is not merely choosing exercises; it is deciding what is safe to attempt, what evidence is missing, what the client can recover from, and where trainer scope ends. A polished program can still be weak if it ignores symptoms, medications, pain behavior, confidence, access, recovery, consent, or the client real life constraints.

Use this skill before giving training advice that could affect health, injury risk, adherence, body image, privacy, or professional boundaries. Strong guidance should keep the client agency intact, avoid diagnosis or treatment claims, make progression measurable, and identify when a medical, rehabilitation, nutrition, mental-health, or other qualified professional should be involved.

## Core Rules

### Start With The Client, Not The Exercise
Frame incident documentation and follow up around the client goal, history, preferences, readiness, environment, and constraints. The same drill, load, cue, or progression can be appropriate for one client and wrong for another. Identify what decision is being made today: screening, exercise selection, load change, regression, referral, behavior target, test, class option, or communication boundary. If the answer cannot say who it is for and why now, it is too generic.

### Read The Emergency Response Context As A Safety System
The practical context here is acute symptom response, stop rules, emergency action plans, staff roles, incident documentation, and follow-up. Check the available evidence: symptoms, emergency contacts, AED location, staff roles, incident notes, and escalation path. Missing context should change confidence. Do not fill gaps with intensity, novelty, or trainer preference. Ask what would reduce uncertainty: intake question, movement observation, symptom clarification, lower-risk trial set, medical clearance, environment change, or referral. The goal is useful training, not proving that the client can survive the planned session.

### Stop First, Then Document And Follow Up
Emergency guidance should define symptoms that stop a session, who calls emergency services, where equipment such as an AED is located, how staff communicate, and what documentation follows. Acute chest symptoms, fainting, severe shortness of breath, neurologic signs, uncontrolled bleeding, head injury, heat illness signs, or severe allergic reaction should not be managed as normal training discomfort. Afterward, record facts without speculation and adjust future participation only within scope.

### Control The Environment Before Loading The Client
Facility guidance should verify machine adjustment, equipment condition, floor space, traffic flow, spotting, storage, sanitation, emergency access, and incident reporting. Many injuries come from setup rather than programming. A trainer should not load a client into a machine that does not fit, a rack without clear space, or equipment with obvious maintenance issues. Hygiene and maintenance are part of safety, not housekeeping trivia.

### Screen For Readiness Before Designing The Workout
Training guidance should begin with health history, goals, experience, medications, symptoms, injuries, pregnancy status where relevant, prior training response, and informed consent. A session plan is premature if the trainer does not know who is in front of them. Clarify what the trainer can decide, what requires medical clearance, and what the client believes the service includes. This protects autonomy and prevents hidden risk from becoming a workout problem.

### Respect Medical Boundaries And Red Flags
Fitness trainers can modify exercise and coordinate with healthcare professionals, but they should not diagnose, treat, prescribe, or override medical advice. Watch for chest pain, fainting, unexplained shortness of breath, neurologic signs, severe or worsening pain, uncontrolled blood pressure concerns, acute injury, infection, or symptoms that do not behave like normal exercise fatigue. Medication timing, blood glucose awareness, and clinician restrictions can change intensity, exercise order, and stop rules.

### Make Progression Observable And Reversible
For incident documentation and follow up, define how progress will be seen: better technique, lower perceived effort at the same load, more confidence, improved repeatable metric, fewer symptoms, better recovery, or stronger adherence. Also define how to reverse course. Good training includes regressions, substitutions, rest, referrals, and stop rules before they are needed. This protects the client from a plan that only works when every week is ideal.

### Document What A Future Trainer Would Need
Record the goal, relevant history, consent, exercise choices, loads, cues that worked, symptoms, modifications, client feedback, referrals, incidents, and next steps. Documentation is not bureaucracy; it protects continuity, privacy, professional boundaries, and learning. If an emergency, pain report, or sensitive conversation occurs, record facts without diagnosis or judgment and follow the facility or service policy.

## Common Traps

### Debating A Stop Rule During The Event
Emergency decisions should be made before adrenaline and social pressure arrive. If the plan depends on improvising, it is not a plan.

### Assuming Familiar Equipment Is Safe Today
Benches loosen, cables fray, pins go missing, floors get wet, and rooms crowd. Familiarity can hide changing hazards.

### Skipping Intake Because The Exercise Looks Basic
A basic squat, walk, or machine circuit can be inappropriate for a client with symptoms, contraindications, fear, medication effects, or a recent medical event. Simplicity of the exercise does not remove screening responsibility.

### Turning A Health Concern Into A Coaching Challenge
Motivation is not the answer to dizziness, chest symptoms, neurologic signs, severe pain, or unexplained breathlessness. Treating red flags as mental toughness can create serious harm.

### Optimizing The Workout While Losing The Client
Incident Documentation And Follow Up can look successful when the session is hard, novel, or impressive. That is a trap if the client leaves anxious, ashamed, flared up, confused, or unlikely to return. The useful program is the one the client can perform, recover from, understand, and repeat.

### Letting Trainer Identity Drive The Recommendation
Trainers often prefer certain lifts, styles, metrics, nutrition beliefs, or motivational language. Preference should not outrank client goals, safety, access, culture, pain response, medical context, or enjoyment. Explain why the chosen method fits this client rather than the trainer brand.

### Hiding Uncertainty Behind Fitness Confidence
Fitness advice often sounds certain because exercise names are familiar. Uncertainty should be named when symptoms, medical history, technique quality, recovery, or client context are unclear. A lower-risk trial, referral, or narrower answer is stronger than a confident guess.

- [ ] Is the incident documentation and follow up recommendation specific to the client goal, history, current capacity, environment, and constraints?
- [ ] Does the answer use evidence from acute symptom response, stop rules, emergency action plans, staff roles, incident documentation, and follow-up rather than generic exercise preferences?
- [ ] Emergency response includes stop symptoms, staff roles, communication, equipment location, documentation, and return-to-session boundary.
- [ ] Equipment setup, fit, spacing, spotting, sanitation, maintenance, emergency access, and incident reporting are checked.
- [ ] The plan reflects health history, goals, consent, readiness, scope boundaries, and referral or clearance needs.
- [ ] Medical red flags, medication implications, clearance needs, stop rules, and referral boundaries are explicit.
- [ ] Are medical red flags, pain behavior, medication or condition implications, pregnancy or age considerations, and referral boundaries explicit where relevant?
- [ ] Are technique standards, progression criteria, regression options, stop rules, and recovery expectations concrete enough to apply during a session?
- [ ] Does the guidance protect consent, confidentiality, body image, autonomy, inclusion, and professional boundaries?
- [ ] Are records, follow-up markers, and triggers for changing the plan clear enough for another trainer or qualified professional to understand?
