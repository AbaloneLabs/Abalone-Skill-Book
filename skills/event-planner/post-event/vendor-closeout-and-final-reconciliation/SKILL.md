# Vendor Closeout And Final Reconciliation

Vendor closeout is where event promises, contracts, actual delivery, and money meet. It is not just paying final invoices. Rentals may be missing, overtime may be disputed, damage claims may appear, deposits may need release, sponsor deliverables may require proof, and lessons learned should affect future vendor choices. Agents often treat closeout as administrative cleanup, which can leave money, disputes, and accountability unresolved. This skill helps the agent close vendors with evidence and control.

## Core Rules

### Build Closeout From The Contract And Final Scope

Start with the executed contract, statement of work, change orders, venue rules, production schedule, delivery records, and onsite decisions. Compare what was contracted, what changed, what was delivered, and what was invoiced. Do not review final invoices in isolation.

Scope often changes onsite: extra labor, extended hours, added rentals, weather moves, menu count changes, rush deliveries, damage, replacement equipment, or client-requested additions. Determine whether each change was authorized, by whom, and under what pricing rule. If authorization was informal, document the facts before approving payment.

### Reconcile Deposits, Minimums, Overtime, And Fees

Event vendor invoices commonly include deposits, retainers, minimums, service charges, taxes, gratuities, delivery fees, setup fees, strike fees, corkage, security, fuel, cleaning, waste, damages, equipment loss, and overtime. Check math, dates, quantities, rates, and contract terms. Confirm deposits are credited correctly.

Overtime deserves special review. Was overtime caused by vendor delay, venue access, client scope change, weather, late program, or planner decision? The cause determines whether the charge is expected, disputable, or a learning point for future schedules.

### Confirm Physical And Digital Returns

Closeout includes returning radios, badges, keys, linens, rentals, furniture, decor, AV gear, costumes, signage, credentials, devices, charging equipment, storage items, and data access. Missing physical assets can become charges; missing data controls can become privacy risk.

For technology and registration vendors, revoke access, export agreed reports, close admin accounts, archive recordings, transfer files, and delete temporary data according to policy. Vendor closeout is not only physical.

### Collect Proof Of Deliverables

For sponsors, exhibitors, speakers, media, production, catering, decor, and marketing vendors, gather proof: photos, reports, lead counts, scan data, menu service confirmation, staffing rosters, delivery receipts, run-of-show completion, recordings, social posts, signage placement, and attendee feedback. Proof supports sponsor reporting, invoice approval, and future vendor evaluation.

If deliverables were partially missed, document the gap and remedy. Remedies may include credit, make-good, corrected report, future discount, replacement asset, or formal dispute. Avoid vague dissatisfaction; tie issues to contracted deliverables or agreed expectations.

### Resolve Disputes With Evidence

Disputes should be factual: contract clause, approved quote, email, change order, time log, photo, delivery receipt, incident report, witness, or venue record. Keep tone professional. The goal is fair resolution, not punishment. Escalate legal or insurance issues for damages, injury, cancellation, or major breach.

Do not delay undisputed payment unnecessarily while one line item is disputed if partial payment is appropriate and approved. Maintaining vendor relationships matters, but so does protecting the client budget.

### Evaluate Vendor Performance For Future Use

Closeout should feed procurement. Evaluate responsiveness, accuracy, staffing, quality, safety, flexibility, documentation, billing clarity, and onsite conduct. Separate vendor failure from planner-caused scope changes or venue constraints. A vendor who performed well under difficult conditions should not be penalized for problems outside their control.

Capture whether to rehire, re-bid, require different terms, change scope, or improve briefing next time. Vendor memory should not live only in one planner's head.

### Close Open Risk Before Archiving

Before archiving the event, check whether any vendor issue could still create risk: injury reports, property damage, missing certificates, music licensing, data exports, unpaid subcontractors, rental loss, food safety complaints, labor disputes, or insurance notices. These items should have owners and deadlines even if final invoices are paid. Financial closeout without risk closeout can leave the client exposed after the event appears finished.

## Common Traps

- Paying final invoices without comparing contract, change orders, and actual delivery.
- Forgetting to credit deposits or reconcile minimums and service charges.
- Approving overtime without understanding cause and authorization.
- Missing rental returns, keys, radios, devices, or storage items.
- Closing technology vendors while leaving admin access or temporary attendee data active.
- Lacking proof for sponsor or vendor deliverables.
- Treating dissatisfaction as a dispute without tying it to evidence or scope.
- Holding entire payments hostage for small disputed items without a payment strategy; failing to capture vendor performance lessons for future procurement

## Self-Check

- Are contracts, SOWs, change orders, onsite decisions, and final invoices compared?
- Are deposits, minimums, taxes, service charges, delivery, overtime, damages, and fees reconciled?
- Is each scope change tied to authorization and pricing basis?
- Are physical rentals, keys, radios, devices, badges, and materials returned or accounted for?
- Are vendor data access, exports, recordings, and temporary files closed according to policy?
- Is proof of deliverables collected for sponsors, vendors, and client reporting?
- Are missed deliverables documented with remedy or dispute path?
- Are disputes handled with contract evidence and professional escalation?
- Are undisputed amounts and disputed amounts separated where appropriate?
- Is vendor performance captured for future selection and contracting?; are unresolved risk items assigned before the event file is archived?
