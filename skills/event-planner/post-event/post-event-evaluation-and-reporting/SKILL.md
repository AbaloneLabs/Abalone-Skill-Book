# Post Event Evaluation And Reporting

Post-event evaluation is not a vanity recap. It is the point where event objectives, budget, attendee experience, sponsor value, operational reliability, and stakeholder expectations are compared against evidence. Agents often summarize what happened without distinguishing outcomes from activity, or they focus on positive highlights while ignoring root causes and future decisions. This skill helps the agent create post-event reporting that supports accountability and better next events.

## Core Rules

### Start With The Original Objectives

Evaluate the event against the goals that justified it: revenue, attendance, donor cultivation, employee alignment, product launch, education, community trust, sponsor value, press coverage, lead generation, retention, celebration, or behavior change. If objectives were vague, say so and report against the clearest available success criteria rather than inventing success after the fact.

Do not let activity metrics replace outcomes. Number of attendees, posts, badges scanned, meals served, or sessions held may matter, but they do not prove the event worked. Connect metrics to intent: whether the right audience came, stayed, engaged, donated, learned, bought, renewed, or expressed trust.

### Reconcile Quantitative And Qualitative Evidence

Use multiple evidence types: registration, check-in, no-show rate, session attendance, revenue, expenses, sponsor leads, survey results, app activity, helpdesk issues, social sentiment, media coverage, staff debriefs, vendor reports, safety incidents, accessibility feedback, and client observations. Each source has bias. Survey respondents may be unusually pleased or upset; badge scans may miss informal engagement; staff anecdotes may overrepresent crises.

Make evidence limits visible. If data is incomplete, late, or inconsistent, report that. A strong post-event report can say "we cannot accurately measure this because session scanning was not enabled" and recommend a fix. Weak certainty creates false learning.

### Separate Results, Causes, And Recommendations

A report should distinguish what happened, why it likely happened, and what should change. Low attendance may result from weak promotion, wrong audience, bad date, high price, poor value proposition, or competition. Long food lines may result from station placement, insufficient staff, menu complexity, or release timing. Do not jump from symptom to solution without cause analysis.

Recommendations should be actionable and prioritized. "Improve communication" is too broad. "Send arrival instructions 48 hours earlier and move parking details into the first screen" is useful. Include owner, timing, cost or effort, and expected effect where possible.

### Include Operational And Risk Performance

Post-event reporting should cover more than audience satisfaction. Review load-in, registration, AV, catering, staffing, security, accessibility, weather, transportation, vendor performance, command center issues, incident response, and closeout. Operational failures may be invisible to sponsors but still create risk and cost.

Include near misses. A blocked accessible route that staff fixed quickly, a radio dead zone, a delayed medical response, or an almost-missed speaker cue should be captured. Future events benefit from learning about risks that did not become public problems.

### Report Financially With Context

Budget reporting should compare planned, forecast, actual, committed, unpaid, and disputed costs. Include revenue, sponsorship, ticketing, donations, refunds, chargebacks, deposits, cancellation fees, overtime, contingency use, and in-kind contributions. A final event report is weak if finance is still waiting on invoices or disputes.

Explain variances. Overages may be justified by scope changes, safety needs, attendance growth, weather contingency, or client decisions. Savings may reflect good negotiation or underservice. Do not label a budget variance good or bad without context.

### Tailor Reporting To Stakeholders

Executives may need outcomes and decisions; sponsors need proof of benefits; operations teams need root causes; finance needs reconciliation; community partners may need impact; volunteers may need thanks and feedback. Use one source of truth, but tailor emphasis and detail.

Avoid hiding bad news. Stakeholders can handle a balanced report if it is factual and improvement-oriented. Overly positive reports reduce trust and prevent better planning.

## Common Traps

- Reporting what happened without comparing against original objectives.
- Treating attendance or social posts as proof of success without outcome connection.
- Using only survey results and ignoring operations, finance, and incident evidence.
- Hiding data gaps instead of explaining measurement limits.
- Making recommendations that are too broad to act on.
- Ignoring near misses because guests did not notice them.
- Reporting budget before final invoices, refunds, deposits, and disputes are reconciled.
- Blaming one vendor or team without examining process and decision causes.
- Creating one generic report for every stakeholder.

## Self-Check

- Are results evaluated against the original event objectives or clearly stated success criteria?
- Are activity metrics connected to actual outcomes?
- Are quantitative and qualitative evidence sources reconciled with limitations stated?
- Are results, root causes, and recommendations separated?
- Are recommendations specific, prioritized, and assigned where possible?
- Are operations, risk, safety, accessibility, staffing, vendor, and command issues included?
- Are near misses captured for future prevention?
- Are financial results reconciled or marked as provisional with missing items listed?
- Are stakeholder versions consistent with the same source of truth?
- Does the report help the next event improve rather than merely summarize the last one?
