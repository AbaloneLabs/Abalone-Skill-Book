# Site Visit And Operational Fit Assessment

A site visit is not a tour. It is the planner's chance to test whether the venue can support the event under real operating conditions. Agents often treat site visits as visual inspections and leave with photos of ballrooms but no answers about loading, queues, power, restrooms, staff routes, weather backup, or emergency flow. A strong site visit verifies assumptions, finds constraints while they are still negotiable, and creates evidence for layout, budget, and contract decisions.

Use this skill before conducting, summarizing, or acting on a venue walkthrough or operational site assessment. The goal is to turn a visit into a practical feasibility record.

## Core Rules

### Walk The Guest Journey In Sequence

Inspect the venue in the order guests will experience it: approach, parking or drop-off, entrance, security, registration, coat check, wayfinding, main room, breakouts, food service, restrooms, sponsor areas, outdoor areas, departure, and rideshare or shuttle pickup. This reveals friction that a room-by-room tour can hide.

Notice visibility, lighting, bottlenecks, stairs, elevator waits, door widths, confusing turns, weather exposure, signage needs, and staff positions. If guests arrive in waves, simulate the peak. If guests include VIPs, families, press, or mobility-device users, walk their routes separately.

### Inspect Back-Of-House And Vendor Flow

Event success depends on what guests do not see. Inspect loading docks, freight elevators, service corridors, green rooms, kitchens, staging, storage, waste handling, staff rooms, production mix positions, security rooms, and vendor parking. Ask how vendors enter, where they wait, when they can load in, and whether guest paths cross service paths.

A venue with elegant guest spaces but weak back-of-house may create expensive labor, slow service, and visible clutter. Back-of-house constraints should be documented as clearly as ballroom features.

### Verify Physical Measurements And Buildout Assumptions

Do not rely only on marketing floorplans. Confirm room dimensions, ceiling heights, door widths, elevator sizes, dock heights, stage dimensions, built-in furniture, column locations, rigging points, power drops, outlet locations, and sightline obstructions. For outdoor or temporary builds, mark tent footprints, slope, drainage, generator location, restroom placement, and emergency access.

If the event includes stage, booths, buffet stations, bars, dance floor, camera risers, registration, or accessibility seating, sketch or request layouts during the visit. Confirm whether the venue's proposed layout includes all necessary elements.

### Test Technical Infrastructure Early

Ask specific questions about electrical capacity, generator rules, internet bandwidth, Wi-Fi density, cellular service, AV tie-ins, house sound, lighting control, rigging, streaming, recording, and backup power. For hybrid events, demos, ticket scanning, cashless bars, or sponsor activations, network and power are not minor details.

Where feasible, run speed tests, check cellular dead zones, inspect network closets or access points, and ask for prior event examples. Technical weaknesses discovered after contract can be expensive or impossible to fix.

### Assess Accessibility In Practice

Code compliance does not guarantee a good accessible event. During the site visit, check accessible parking, curb cuts, step-free entrances, elevator reliability, route slopes, door widths, restroom access, seating options, stage access, registration height, service animal relief, quiet spaces, caption screen visibility, and staff knowledge.

Walk the accessible route rather than asking whether the venue is accessible. If the accessible entrance is hidden, far away, locked, or through service areas, the guest experience may be unacceptable. Document gaps and ask what the venue can provide.

### Surface Venue Rules And Operational Limits

Use the site visit to ask about rules that affect design and budget: load-in times, noise limits, flame restrictions, confetti, signage, drilling, hanging, candles, alcohol, outside catering, exclusive vendors, union labor, security, guest screening, overtime, cleaning, waste, deliveries, storage, and insurance. Rules discovered later become change orders.

Ask what has caused problems in past events. Venue managers often know the real constraints: late-night noise complaints, elevator limits, kitchen bottlenecks, parking overflow, weak Wi-Fi, neighbors, or difficult weather.

### Bring The Right People Or Questions

The planner should ensure the site visit includes the people who can evaluate critical areas: production, catering, security, accessibility, transportation, decorator, registration, sponsor operations, or client decision maker. If they cannot attend, gather their questions in advance and capture photos or measurements for them.

Do not let a venue salesperson's tour determine the agenda. Prepare a checklist based on the event's operating model, then guide the visit through the risks that matter.

### Convert The Visit Into A Decision Record

After the visit, create a concise record: photos, measurements, open questions, risks, cost implications, assumptions confirmed, assumptions disproven, required follow-up, and recommendation. Mark which issues affect contract negotiation, budget, layout, timeline, or go/no-go.

Site visit notes should be usable by vendors and stakeholders. If the visit only produces impressions such as "nice space," it has not done its job.

## Common Traps

### Letting The Venue Lead A Sales Tour

Sales tours highlight attractive spaces and may skip loading, restrooms, power, staff routes, and constraints. The planner must lead with operational questions.

### Forgetting Peak Flow

A quiet walkthrough does not show what happens when 400 guests arrive, a session turns over, or everyone leaves at once. Simulate flow mentally and ask for past event patterns.

### Ignoring Back-Of-House

Kitchen access, freight elevators, storage, service corridors, and vendor parking often determine labor cost and service quality.

### Assuming Floorplans Are Accurate Enough

Marketing floorplans may not include columns, furniture, door swings, ADA seating, production mix, or real buffet footprints. Verify dimensions.

### Accepting "We Have Wi-Fi" As Technical Proof

Wi-Fi for casual guests is not the same as event-grade connectivity for registration, streaming, payments, sponsor demos, and staff operations.

### Treating Accessibility As A Yes/No Question

Accessible in principle may be awkward in practice. Walk routes and inspect details.

### Leaving Without Open Issue Owners

Site visits often reveal questions. Each open issue needs an owner and deadline before contract decisions.

### Not Sharing Findings With Vendors

Production, catering, security, and registration vendors need site realities. Holding the information in planner notes creates preventable problems.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Was the guest journey walked in sequence from arrival through departure?
- [ ] Were VIP, accessible, staff, vendor, emergency, and service routes inspected separately where relevant?
- [ ] Were loading docks, freight elevators, storage, kitchens, green rooms, waste, and vendor parking reviewed?
- [ ] Were room dimensions, ceiling heights, door widths, elevator sizes, power, rigging, and obstructions verified?
- [ ] Were technical infrastructure, Wi-Fi, cellular, power, streaming, AV, and backup needs assessed?
- [ ] Was accessibility checked by actual route and practical guest experience?
- [ ] Were venue rules, exclusive vendors, labor, noise, alcohol, signage, storage, and overtime limits surfaced?
- [ ] Were the right specialists involved or their questions captured?
- [ ] Does the site visit record include photos, measurements, risks, cost impacts, open issues, and owners?
- [ ] Can the visit findings support a contract, layout, budget, or go/no-go decision?
