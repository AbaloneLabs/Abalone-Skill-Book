# Procurement Deposits And Payment Risk

Event procurement creates financial commitments long before the event happens. Deposits, cancellation clauses, attrition, minimums, payment schedules, rush fees, and nonrefundable vendor costs can lock the client into risk before the full plan is mature. Agents often focus on selecting attractive vendors and comparing headline prices while missing payment timing, contract authority, insurance requirements, purchase order rules, tax treatment, vendor reliability, and what happens if attendance, weather, sponsorship, or leadership priorities change. Strong event procurement controls who can commit money, when cash leaves, and what risk is accepted.

Use this skill before recommending vendors, signing contracts, paying deposits, issuing purchase orders, or promising payment terms. The goal is to make financial commitment and vendor accountability visible.

## Core Rules

### Verify Buying Authority Before Commitment

Before any deposit or contract, clarify who has authority to spend and sign. Corporate, nonprofit, government, campus, and public-sector events may require purchase orders, preferred vendors, competitive bids, tax forms, insurance certificates, legal review, board approval, grant restrictions, or procurement thresholds. Private events may still require agreement from the payer, host, or family decision maker.

Do not let a planner, assistant, sponsor, or enthusiastic stakeholder create unauthorized obligations. A vendor hold, signed proposal, or paid deposit can become binding even when the broader organization has not approved it. Confirm the approval path and document authorization.

### Compare Vendors On Risk, Not Only Price

Vendor selection should consider capability, reliability, scope clarity, exclusions, staffing, insurance, references, communication, financial stability, venue familiarity, contingency depth, and fit with the event's risk profile. A lower price may hide missing labor, weaker equipment, poor staffing, no backup plan, limited insurance, or fragile capacity.

Ask what the vendor is responsible for and what they assume others will provide. In event work, gaps between vendors are common: production expects power from venue, caterer expects rentals from planner, decorator expects overnight access, entertainment expects green room and stage management. Procurement should expose gaps before contract.

### Understand Deposit And Cancellation Exposure

Deposits are not just payment timing; they are risk allocation. Identify which amounts are nonrefundable, which become nonrefundable over time, what cancellation dates apply, what minimums or attrition clauses exist, and what costs are due if the event is postponed, reduced, or cancelled.

Build a cancellation exposure schedule. It should show what the client loses or owes at each milestone. This is essential for weather-prone, sponsorship-dependent, ticketed, travel-heavy, outdoor, or leadership-sensitive events. Stakeholders should know when financial risk increases.

### Align Payment Timing With Cash And Approval Process

Vendors may require deposits before the client's internal payment process can move. If purchase orders, finance approval, W-9 or tax forms, vendor onboarding, wire setup, grant restrictions, or board approvals take time, the planner must plan payment lead time. Late payment can lose holds, damage vendor relationships, or trigger rush fees.

Track payment due dates, approval owners, invoice requirements, accepted payment methods, card fees, bank details, and proof of payment. Do not assume a vendor is booked until contract and payment conditions are satisfied.

### Tie Payment To Deliverables Where Possible

For complex services, payment milestones should align with deliverables: design approval, production drawings, equipment reservation, staffing plan, rehearsal, event completion, final reconciliation, or deliverable proof. Some deposits are unavoidable, but the client should avoid paying too much before receiving clear value or protection.

Use retainage, final payment after service, or documented acceptance where appropriate. For small vendors or artists, balance protection with fair cash needs. The point is not to starve vendors; it is to avoid paying in ways that remove accountability or create unrecoverable exposure.

### Check Insurance, Tax, And Compliance Requirements

Event vendors may need certificates of insurance, additional insured language, liquor liability, workers' compensation, auto coverage, cyber coverage, permits, licenses, food safety certifications, background checks, music rights, union compliance, tax forms, or accessibility obligations. These requirements can affect whether a vendor is eligible and how quickly they can be onboarded.

Confirm requirements before selection, not after. A vendor that cannot meet venue or client requirements may force a late change. Payment should not proceed if mandatory compliance documents are missing unless an authorized risk owner approves an exception.

### Avoid Uncontrolled Rush And Change Fees

Late decisions create rush production, overtime, expedited freight, revised design, additional labor, and rebooking fees. Procurement should identify which vendor costs are sensitive to deadlines and what change fees apply. This helps stakeholders understand the price of delay.

Include change-order language in contracts or scopes of work. A vendor should not proceed with billable changes based on vague verbal requests unless the client has authorized the cost.

### Plan Final Reconciliation

The final invoice often differs from the estimate because of consumption, overtime, guest count changes, damage, rentals, gratuities, shipping, or taxes. Define how final charges will be documented and reviewed. For venues and caterers, review banquet event orders, guarantees, bar consumption, service charges, and labor. For production, review crew hours, equipment substitutions, and strike time.

Hold enough documentation to reconcile deposits, payments, refunds, credits, and chargebacks. Closeout is part of procurement, not an afterthought.

## Common Traps

### Treating A Quote As A Complete Scope

Quotes often exclude required labor, equipment, delivery, power, taxes, permits, insurance, or overtime. A low quote may simply be incomplete.

### Paying Deposits Before Authority Is Clear

Unauthorized deposits create conflict when finance, legal, family, board, or procurement later objects. Approval should precede commitment.

### Missing Nonrefundable Milestones

Cancellation exposure often increases over time. If stakeholders do not know the dates, they may delay decisions until the event has quietly become expensive to change.

### Ignoring Vendor Onboarding Lead Time

Corporate or public entities may take days or weeks to onboard a new vendor. A vendor cannot be paid on time if forms, insurance, banking, or procurement steps start too late.

### Comparing Vendors With Different Assumptions

One proposal may include delivery, setup, and strike while another excludes them. Normalize assumptions before comparing price.

### Letting Verbal Changes Become Billable

Vendors may act quickly on verbal requests and invoice later. Change orders protect both sides by confirming scope, cost, and approval.

### Overpaying Before Performance

Large upfront payments reduce leverage if the vendor underperforms, disappears, or cannot deliver. Balance fair deposits with staged accountability.

### Forgetting Final Invoice Review

Final invoices can include legitimate but unexpected charges. Without review against contracts, BEOs, time sheets, and approvals, overbilling or misunderstandings may pass unnoticed.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is spending and signing authority verified before deposits, holds, or contracts are issued?
- [ ] Are vendors compared on scope, reliability, compliance, insurance, exclusions, and contingency depth as well as price?
- [ ] Is deposit, cancellation, attrition, minimum, and nonrefundable exposure documented by date?
- [ ] Are payment due dates aligned with purchase order, finance, tax, vendor onboarding, and approval lead times?
- [ ] Are payment milestones tied to deliverables or service completion where possible?
- [ ] Are insurance, permits, licenses, tax forms, and compliance requirements checked before commitment?
- [ ] Are rush fees, overtime, change fees, and late-decision costs visible?
- [ ] Is there a change-order process for billable vendor changes?
- [ ] Is final reconciliation planned with required documentation?
- [ ] Could the client explain what financial risk has already been accepted and what risk is still avoidable?
