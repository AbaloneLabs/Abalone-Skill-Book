# Projection Sound Rights And Content Security

A screening succeeds only if the content plays correctly, legally, and securely. Projection, sound, DCP delivery, KDM windows, captions, subtitles, rights, anti-piracy, and playback backups are specialized details that can ruin a premiere if treated like ordinary AV. Agents often plan arrivals and guest experience while assuming the theater will handle the film. This skill ensures the screening itself is technically and contractually ready.

## Core Rules

### Confirm screening rights and usage scope

Verify who grants permission, what title/version is approved, date, venue, audience type, ticketing or free status, number of screenings, recording limits, publicity rights, sponsor association, and Q and A or livestream permissions. Rights may differ for festival, educational, press, private, or commercial screenings.

Do not assume possession of a file or disc means permission to screen. Rights should be documented before invitations are issued.

### Control content version and delivery

Identify format: DCP, ProRes, Blu-ray, encrypted screener, streaming link, file download, or other playback. Confirm version, runtime, aspect ratio, frame rate, audio mix, language, subtitles, captions, watermark, and delivery deadline.

Use one source of truth for final version. Multiple "final" files create risk of wrong cut, missing captions, or unapproved content.

### Manage KDM and encryption windows

Encrypted DCPs require KDMs matched to the server, projector, venue, and screening window. Confirm equipment certificates, KDM delivery, validity start and end time, time zone, test window, and emergency contact for reissue.

Do not schedule the first playback test inside the public screening window. KDM failure is common enough to plan for.

### Require technical rehearsal with actual content

Test playback on the exact projector, server, audio system, screen, and content path. Check image, sound, sync, subtitles, captions, masking, brightness, surround channels, volume, trailer package, house lights, and microphone handoff if Q and A follows.

A generic projector test is not enough. The actual file and actual audio mix must be checked.

### Plan accessibility and language access

Screenings may need open captions, closed captions, audio description, assistive listening, subtitles, sign language interpretation for Q and A, accessible seating, and companion seating. Confirm availability and how guests request devices.

Do not treat captions as optional decoration. For some audiences, they are essential access and may be contractually or legally required.

### Protect content security and anti-piracy

Premieres and pre-release screenings require content custody, limited file access, watermarking, bag or phone policy if required, night-vision monitoring if used, staff confidentiality, no-recording announcements, and secure disposal of screeners or drives.

Balance security with guest experience. Heavy-handed phone enforcement may be necessary for some screenings but should be communicated and staffed properly.

### Brief audience and staff on device policy

If phones, cameras, laptops, smart watches, or recording devices are restricted, communicate before arrival and again onsite. Plan bag check, phone pouching, device storage, exemptions, accessibility needs, press rules, and what happens if a guest refuses. Staff should know the difference between venue policy, distributor anti-piracy policy, and press access.

A device policy that surprises guests at the door can delay seating and create conflict. Anti-piracy needs an operating plan, not only a warning slide.

### Prepare playback contingencies

Have backup content where rights allow, backup player, spare cables, projectionist contact, distributor contact, KDM emergency contact, caption backup, microphone backup, and delayed-start communication. Decide how long to troubleshoot before delaying, replacing, or canceling.

If the screening is high-profile, escalation authority should be clear. The projection booth should not be negotiating with publicists in the lobby.

### Coordinate show flow around content

Intros, trailers, sponsor slides, festival bumpers, house lights, Q and A, credits, and post-screen movement must align with content rights and technical cues. Some filmmakers may require full credits to play before lights.

Do not let sponsor slides or unauthorized recordings violate rights. Show flow is part of content control.

## Common Traps

- Assuming a file delivery equals screening permission.
- Playing the wrong version, cut, subtitle file, or audio mix.
- Waiting until show day to discover KDM window or server mismatch.
- Testing the projector but not the actual content package.
- Forgetting captions, subtitles, audio description, and assistive listening.
- Treating anti-piracy rules as optional because guests are invited; announcing phone or camera restrictions only at the door with no storage, exemption, or refusal process
- Giving too many people access to pre-release files; having no backup plan for KDM, audio, captions, microphones, or playback failure
- Adding sponsor slides or recording rights not approved by the distributor; turning lights up before credits when filmmaker or rights terms require otherwise

## Self-Check

- Are screening rights documented for title, version, date, venue, audience, ticketing, publicity, and recording limits?
- Is the exact content version, runtime, aspect, frame rate, audio, language, subtitles, captions, and watermark confirmed?
- Are KDM, equipment certificates, time zone, validity window, and emergency reissue contacts tested?
- Has the actual content played through the exact projection, server, audio, caption, and microphone setup?
- Are captions, subtitles, audio description, assistive listening, Q and A interpretation, and accessible seating handled?
- Are content custody, file access, watermarking, no-recording policy, and anti-piracy staffing clear?
- Are audience and staff prepared for device restrictions, storage, exemptions, press rules, and refusal handling?
- Are backup playback, KDM, cables, mics, captions, contacts, and delay decisions ready?
- Does show flow respect trailers, slides, credits, lights, Q and A, and rights limits?
