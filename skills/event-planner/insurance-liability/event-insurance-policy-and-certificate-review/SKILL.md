# Event Insurance Policy And Certificate Review

Event insurance review is easy to reduce to collecting certificates, but a certificate of insurance is only evidence of certain coverage information on a date. It does not prove that every contract requirement is satisfied, that exclusions are acceptable, that additional insured status is granted, or that the policy will respond to the event's real risks. The agent should help compare contractual requirements, event activities, vendor responsibilities, and coverage evidence while escalating legal or broker conclusions to qualified reviewers.

## Core Rules

### Start with the event's risk profile and contracts

Insurance requirements should follow the event's activities, not a generic checklist. Identify venue type, expected attendance, alcohol service, food handling, staging, rigging, pyrotechnics, vehicles, shuttles, volunteers, minors, sports, animals, medical services, exhibitors, public attendance, ticketing, VIPs, international components, and load-in/load-out hazards. Then compare those facts against the venue contract, client agreement, vendor contracts, municipal permit conditions, sponsor requirements, and lender or landlord requirements.

Do not assume one policy solves all exposures. Commercial general liability, hired and non-owned auto, workers compensation, employer liability, liquor liability, event cancellation, cyber, crime, equipment, inland marine, umbrella, professional liability, and special accident coverage serve different purposes. An event with volunteers serving alcohol, rented vehicles, temporary structures, and attendee data collection may need several layers of evidence and responsibility assignment.

### Read certificate evidence against contract requirements

When reviewing a certificate of insurance, compare named insured, insurer, policy type, policy number, effective dates, limits, cancellation language, certificate holder, description of operations, and whether the event date including load-in and strike falls inside the policy period. Check whether the certificate holder matches the contract, including legal names and addresses. A certificate with a high limit is not enough if the wrong vendor is insured, the event date is outside the period, or the required policy type is missing.

Be cautious with the description box. It may state that a venue is additional insured, but actual additional insured status usually depends on policy wording or endorsement. If the contract requires additional insured status, waiver of subrogation, primary and noncontributory wording, completed operations, or specific endorsements, request the endorsement or broker confirmation rather than relying on certificate text alone.

### Match limits and deductibles to the event's severity

Policy limits should be assessed against credible loss severity, contractual requirements, and stacked exposure. A small private reception has a different risk profile from a public festival with alcohol, temporary stages, crowd queues, and road closures. Umbrella or excess liability may be needed when venue or municipal requirements exceed the base policy. Deductibles, self-insured retentions, and aggregate limits matter because a vendor may technically have coverage but lack the ability to absorb retained risk.

Do not treat minimum required limits as a statement that risk is acceptable. The contract may set a floor, not the optimal risk posture. If the event involves high-severity hazards, recommend broker or risk manager review even if certificates appear to meet minimum requirements.

### Identify exclusions and activity mismatches

Certificates rarely reveal important exclusions. Ask whether the activities include alcohol, assault and battery exposure, participant injury, athletic activities, inflatable attractions, amusement devices, pyrotechnics, drones, medical services, communicable disease, professional services, pollution, fireworks, weapons, animals, water activities, or high-risk installation work. These may require endorsements, separate policies, or explicit vendor responsibility.

Vendor scope matters. A caterer with food liability may not cover bartending if a separate bar vendor serves alcohol. A production company may cover its crew but not subcontracted riggers. A transportation provider may have auto coverage but not enough passenger liability or vehicle schedule evidence. Review insurance by the actual service performed at the event.

### Track evidence as an operational dependency

Insurance evidence must be collected early enough to fix gaps. Build deadlines into vendor onboarding, service orders, and final production meetings. Track missing COIs, expired policies, wrong certificate holders, insufficient limits, missing endorsements, and subcontractor gaps. Do not wait until load-in to discover that a vendor cannot satisfy venue requirements.

The agent should not make final legal coverage determinations. Instead, prepare a clean issue list for the broker, risk manager, counsel, venue, or client: requirement, evidence received, gap, consequence, and decision needed. This turns insurance review from document collection into risk management.

## Common Traps

- Treating a COI as a contract amendment. A certificate usually does not change policy terms.
- Accepting "additional insured" wording in the description box without endorsement support when the contract requires it.
- Checking event day only and missing setup, rehearsal, delivery, strike, or offsite storage dates.
- Ignoring vendor subcontractors. The prime vendor's certificate may not cover independent crews, drivers, installers, or specialty operators.
- Confusing general liability with workers compensation, auto, liquor liability, professional liability, or event cancellation.
- Assuming a venue's insurance covers the planner, client, sponsors, exhibitors, or vendors. Venue coverage usually protects the venue's interests.
- Letting expired or wrong-name certificates sit unresolved because the event is still weeks away. Broker corrections can take time.
- Giving legal assurances that coverage will respond. The agent can identify gaps and evidence; coverage decisions require policy review.

## Self-Check

- Have I identified the event activities and contractual insurance requirements before reviewing certificates?
- Does each COI match the correct legal entity, event dates including load-in and strike, certificate holder, policy type, and required limits?
- Where additional insured, waiver, primary and noncontributory, or endorsement wording is required, have I flagged evidence beyond description-box text?
- Have I checked whether alcohol, vehicles, workers, volunteers, temporary structures, pyrotechnics, drones, medical services, or other special activities need separate coverage?
- Are subcontractor and independent operator insurance responsibilities clear?
- Have I tracked gaps with owner, due date, consequence, and decision needed rather than merely collecting files?
- Did I avoid presenting certificate review as a guarantee of coverage?
- Have I escalated material policy interpretation, exclusion, or contract compliance questions to a broker, risk manager, counsel, or authorized decision maker?
