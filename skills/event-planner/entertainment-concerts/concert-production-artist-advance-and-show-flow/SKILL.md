# Concert Production Artist Advance And Show Flow

Concerts are deadline-driven productions where artist expectations, venue rules, technical requirements, audience flow, and curfews meet in real time. Agents often focus on booking talent and selling tickets while underplanning the artist advance, soundcheck, changeovers, hospitality, stage access, and show timing. A strong concert plan turns the contract and rider into an executable day-of schedule with clear ownership.

## Core Rules

### Start with contract, rider, and venue constraints

Review artist contract, technical rider, hospitality rider, stage plot, input list, billing, set length, support acts, curfew, radius restrictions, merchandise terms, photo policy, recording rights, settlement terms, and cancellation clauses. Compare these with venue capacity, stage size, power, rigging, labor, sound limits, backstage, dressing rooms, and load-in.

Do not assume venue equipment satisfies the rider. Any shortfall should be advanced, approved, rented, or clearly rejected before show day.

### Run a real artist advance

Advance arrival time, tour manager contact, vehicle count, trailer or bus parking, load-in door, credentials, dressing rooms, hospitality, meal times, guest list, soundcheck, backline, merchandise, security needs, runner, payment, and settlement process. Confirm who makes decisions on site.

An advance is not a casual email. It is the operating bridge between contract expectations and local reality.

### Build a show-day timeline with buffers

Include load-in, stage setup, line check, soundcheck, doors, support sets, changeovers, headliner set, encore, curfew, meet-and-greet, merchandise close, load-out, and settlement. Add buffers for late arrivals, technical fixes, and audience ingress.

Curfew and union or labor rules can create real cost and legal exposure. If a show runs long, know who can approve overtime or set cuts.

### Define show authority during live changes

Concerts change in motion: artist arrival slips, opener runs long, technical faults appear, weather threatens, crowd lines back up, or curfew approaches. Define who can hold doors, delay show start, cut a set, extend changeover, approve overtime, cancel meet-and-greet, or move a performance.

This authority map should include promoter, tour manager, production manager, venue manager, security lead, and stage manager. A live show cannot wait for a committee with unclear power.

### Coordinate technical and hospitality needs together

Artists experience the event through both stage and backstage. Technical readiness, clean dressing rooms, meals, water, towels, Wi-Fi, security, private bathrooms, and runner support affect performance and relationships.

Hospitality should be realistic and respectful. If rider items are impossible or unreasonable, negotiate substitutions before arrival, not at 5 p.m.

### Manage support acts and changeovers

Openers, DJs, local bands, hosts, and special guests need stage plots, inputs, set lengths, backline sharing rules, dressing space, settlement, guest lists, and merch rules. Changeovers should be timed and staffed.

Do not let support acts discover limits during soundcheck. Ambiguity creates delays and conflict with headliner priorities.

### Protect audience flow and show start

Doors, ticket scanning, security, bars, merchandise, restrooms, coat check, accessible seating, and opening act start are connected. A late box office line can make the room feel empty for the opener. A slow bar can delay audience movement.

Production should know front-of-house status before starting critical moments. Communication between FOH manager, stage manager, security, and promoter matters.

### Plan recording, photography, and content rights

Artists may restrict photography, flash, pit access, livestream, recording, fan video, house photographer use, and social clips. Sponsors or media may expect content. Resolve rights before doors.

Security and photographers need the same policy. A policy not communicated at the barricade will fail.

### Close with load-out and settlement

After the show, manage artist settlement, merchandise count, tax forms if applicable, receipts, overtime, damage, lost items, hospitality wrap, load-out order, bus departure, and venue close. Settlement should be private, documented, and aligned with contract.

Do not let the emotional high of the show obscure financial and operational closeout.

## Common Traps

- Booking talent without reconciling rider needs with venue capabilities.
- Treating artist advance as optional or informal.
- Building a show timeline with no buffer for soundcheck, changeover, ingress, or technical fixes.
- Failing to define who can delay, cut, extend, or cancel elements when live conditions change.
- Ignoring curfew, labor overtime, sound limits, and neighborhood restrictions.
- Letting hospitality substitutions become day-of surprises; underplanning support act technical needs and backline sharing
- Starting the show without knowing FOH, security, and box office status; failing to communicate photo and recording rules to security and media
- Letting settlement happen without documentation or privacy; forgetting load-out, bus parking, lost items, and post-show venue obligations

## Self-Check

- Are contract, rider, stage plot, input list, hospitality, settlement, and venue constraints reconciled?
- Has a real artist advance confirmed contacts, arrival, parking, load-in, credentials, hospitality, soundcheck, guest list, merch, and decision authority?
- Does the timeline cover load-in through load-out with buffers and curfew awareness?
- Is live-change authority clear for doors, start time, set cuts, overtime, meet-and-greet, weather, and safety calls?
- Are technical and hospitality needs coordinated with approved substitutions where needed?
- Are support acts, changeovers, backline, dressing rooms, set lengths, and merch rules clear?
- Is FOH, ticketing, security, bar, restroom, accessibility, and audience ingress status tied into show start?
- Are photo, recording, livestream, sponsor, and media rights communicated to all relevant teams?
- Are settlement, merch count, overtime, damage, tax forms, and load-out documented after the show?
