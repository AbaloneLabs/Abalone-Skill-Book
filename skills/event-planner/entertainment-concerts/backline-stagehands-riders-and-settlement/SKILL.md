# Backline Stagehands Riders And Settlement

The least glamorous concert details often decide whether the show works: drum kits, amps, keys, DI boxes, risers, stagehands, runners, towels, meals, receipts, and settlement. Agents can book artists and venues while leaving backline and labor assumptions unresolved. This skill focuses on translating riders into equipment, staffing, cost control, and financial closeout so the performance does not depend on improvisation.

## Core Rules

### Parse riders into required, preferred, and negotiable items

Technical and hospitality riders include essentials, preferences, brand requests, comfort items, and sometimes outdated boilerplate. Work with production, artist representative, and venue to classify what is required for performance, what can be substituted, and what is not feasible.

Do not ignore the rider because it looks excessive. Also do not fulfill every item without question. The advance should produce an agreed version.

### Verify backline specifications early

Backline needs exact models, acceptable substitutes, quantities, tuning, drum sizes, cymbals, guitar amps, bass amps, keyboards, stands, risers, power, inputs, and who supplies each item. Local rental availability may be limited, especially in smaller markets.

If the artist carries some gear, confirm what travels, what is shared, and what remains local. Backline gaps discovered at soundcheck create expensive rushes.

### Build labor calls around real workload

Stagehands, loaders, audio, lighting, backline techs, runners, wardrobe, security, and hospitality staff need call times tied to load-in, setup, soundcheck, show, changeover, and load-out. Venue labor rules, union requirements, minimum calls, meal breaks, and overtime matter.

Understaffing saves money on paper and costs time onsite. Overstaffing without role clarity wastes budget and creates confusion.

### Coordinate patch, line check, and change documentation

Backline changes affect audio patching, monitor mixes, stage plots, and changeover timing. If an amp, keyboard, drum setup, wireless channel, DI, or input count changes, update the audio team and stage manager immediately. Do not rely on verbal comments scattered during load-in.

Maintain a current stage plot and input list at the console, stage, and production desk. Small backline substitutions can become show-stopping problems when they alter power, space, or signal routing.

### Manage hospitality and runners operationally

Hospitality riders need dressing rooms, meals, beverages, towels, ice, dietary needs, bus stock, green room setup, showers, laundry, and runner errands. Confirm what is included, who buys it, receipts required, and where it is staged.

Runners need authority limits, vehicle access, parking, payment method, and local knowledge. A runner with no budget or unclear task list cannot solve day-of needs.

### Track production expenses against deal terms

Artist deals may be guarantee, versus percentage, door split, backend, promoter profit, or flat fee. Production expenses, house nut, ticketing fees, taxes, facility fees, comps, guest list, merch percentage, and settlement deductions must align with contract.

Do not wait until after the show to learn what can be deducted. Finance assumptions should be reviewed before doors.

### Prepare settlement documentation

Settlement should include ticket counts, gross, comps, holds, tax, facility fees, promoter expenses, artist guarantee, overage, merch sales, merch commission, receipts, W-9 or tax forms if needed, payment method, and signatures or approvals.

Keep settlement private and controlled. Public or rushed settlement can create errors and relationship damage.

### Control merchandise logistics

Merch needs table location, lighting, power, seller, inventory count, price list, payment methods, tax handling, venue percentage, artist percentage, settlement, security, and load-out. Merch lines can block exits or bars if poorly placed.

Clarify whether the venue, artist, or promoter supplies sellers and payment devices. Count in and count out.

### Close equipment and damage accountability

After load-out, check rented backline, venue gear, stage, dressing rooms, green rooms, and artist areas for damage or missing items. Document issues before vendors leave. Track who is responsible under contract.

Equipment accountability protects future supplier relationships and prevents surprise invoices.

## Common Traps

- Treating riders as either absolute demands or meaningless boilerplate.
- Accepting vague backline substitutes without artist approval.
- Discovering local rental gaps at soundcheck.
- Understaffing load-in and load-out because the show seems simple.
- Letting backline substitutions change inputs, monitors, power, or stage layout without updating production documents.
- Forgetting labor minimums, meal breaks, overtime, and union rules; sending runners without budget, receipts, parking, or authority limits
- Misunderstanding deal terms and settlement deductions; placing merch where it blocks exits, bars, or crowd flow
- Skipping merch count-in, count-out, and payment reconciliation; not inspecting rented equipment and dressing rooms before closeout

## Self-Check

- Are rider items classified as required, preferred, negotiable, or infeasible with artist approval?
- Are backline models, substitutes, quantities, suppliers, and shared gear confirmed?
- Are labor calls matched to load-in, setup, soundcheck, show, changeover, load-out, breaks, and overtime?
- Are stage plot, input list, patch changes, monitor impacts, and backline substitutions documented for audio and stage teams?
- Are hospitality, runner tasks, receipts, payment method, dietary needs, and staging clear?
- Do production expenses and deductions match the artist deal and promoter budget?
- Is settlement documentation ready for ticket counts, expenses, guarantee, overage, merch, tax, and payment?
- Are merch location, seller, prices, payment, taxes, commission, security, and inventory counts controlled?
- Are equipment, venue, dressing room, and damage checks completed before final closeout?
