# Exhibitor Service Manual Freight And Drayage

The exhibitor service manual is the operating bridge between booth sales and show-site reality. If deadlines, forms, freight rules, drayage, labor jurisdictions, electrical orders, insurance, and move-in instructions are unclear, exhibitors arrive angry and the show floor becomes a logistics problem. Agents often treat the service manual as administrative paperwork while missing that it controls cost, safety, freight flow, and exhibitor trust. This skill helps prepare exhibitor operations guidance that is actionable and enforceable.

## Core Rules

### Make The Service Manual Action-Oriented

Exhibitors need to know what to order, by when, from whom, at what cost, and what happens if they miss deadlines. The manual should cover booth rules, deadlines, freight, advance warehouse, direct-to-show, material handling, labor, power, internet, furnishings, carpet, signage, insurance, badges, sampling, demos, fire rules, lead retrieval, cleaning, security, and move-out.

Do not bury critical deadlines in dense text. Use a deadline summary, exhibitor checklist, contact list, and clear links. Exhibitors vary in experience; the manual should help first-timers without insulting experienced teams.

### Distinguish Advance Warehouse, Direct-To-Show, And Hand Carry

Freight timing affects cost and congestion. Advance warehouse gives earlier receiving and controlled delivery but adds storage and material handling. Direct-to-show may be cheaper or necessary for some freight but creates dock peaks. Hand carry rules vary and are often misunderstood.

State addresses, labels, receiving windows, target move-in, carrier instructions, overtime rules, marshaling yard, prohibited deliveries, and consequences of early/late freight. Clarify what exhibitors can carry themselves and what requires official material handling.

### Explain Drayage And Material Handling Transparently

Drayage is often the most disputed exhibitor cost because exhibitors do not understand it until the invoice arrives. Explain what material handling includes, how it is calculated, minimums, overtime, special handling, small package rules, empty storage, re-delivery, and outbound loading.

Avoid hiding costs behind jargon. If the event uses an official service contractor, make pricing and rules accessible. Exhibitors should know how palletization, carrier choice, and deadline compliance affect their charges.

### Coordinate Labor Jurisdictions And Booth Build Rules

Union rules, venue rules, contractor rules, booth height, hanging signs, rigging, electrical, fire code, tools, ladders, floor covering, structural drawings, and EAC or exhibitor-appointed contractor requirements can affect installation. Exhibitors often assume they can build anything their booth vendor designed.

State what exhibitors may do themselves, what requires official labor, what requires drawings, what needs approval, and when EAC forms are due. Do not let booth designs reach show site before rule conflicts are discovered.

### Control Move-In And Move-Out Sequence

Move-in and move-out are safety and congestion events. Freight, forklifts, crates, exhibitors, decorators, electricians, cleaners, and venue staff compete for aisles and docks. Target move-in times, aisle carpet timing, power installation, inspection deadlines, and empty crate return must be sequenced.

Create schedules by booth size, freight type, and hall zone where needed. For move-out, communicate when dismantle begins, when empties return, carrier check-in deadlines, forced freight rules, and outbound paperwork. Early booth teardown should be governed if it affects attendee experience.

### Require Insurance, Certificates, And Compliance Early

Exhibitors and EACs may need certificates of insurance, waivers, sampling permits, food permits, alcohol approval, fire certificates, vehicle fuel rules, hazardous material disclosures, hanging sign approvals, and data/privacy rules for lead capture. Late compliance issues can stop booth setup.

List requirements by exhibitor activity. A standard tabletop exhibitor and a live cooking demo do not need the same review. Escalate regulated or risky demos early.

### Support Exhibitors Without Becoming Their Operations Team

Good exhibitor service reduces confusion, but exhibitors remain responsible for many orders and deadlines. Provide help desks, office hours, webinars, reminders, and contact paths. Track common questions and update FAQs.

Avoid making custom exceptions that undermine rules or create unfairness unless approved. If an exhibitor misses a deadline, explain options and consequences clearly.

### Reconcile Post-Show Freight And Service Issues

After the show, exhibitors may dispute material handling, damage, missing freight, forced freight, labor, electrical, or cleaning charges. Records matter: inbound receipts, photos, empty labels, outbound bills, carrier check-in, booth orders, and service tickets.

Capture issues and feed them into contractor review. Exhibitor service quality affects renewal and reputation.

## Common Traps

### Treating The Manual As A Document Dump

Exhibitors need clear actions, deadlines, contacts, and consequences.

### Hiding Drayage Details

Material handling surprises create anger and post-show disputes.

### Failing To Clarify Hand Carry

Exhibitors often misunderstand what they may bring through public entrances.

### Discovering Booth Rule Violations Onsite

Height, rigging, fire, labor, and EAC approvals must be reviewed before move-in.

### Underplanning Move-Out

Outbound carrier deadlines, empties, forced freight, and paperwork need clear control.

### Applying One Compliance List To Every Exhibitor

Sampling, vehicles, cooking, alcohol, demos, and hazardous materials require different reviews.

### Making Informal Exceptions

Untracked exceptions create unfairness and operational risk.

### Not Keeping Freight Evidence

Damage and missing freight disputes require receiving, photos, and outbound records.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Does the service manual clearly state actions, deadlines, contacts, costs, and consequences?
- [ ] Are advance warehouse, direct-to-show, hand carry, small package, and prohibited delivery rules separated?
- [ ] Are drayage/material handling pricing, minimums, overtime, special handling, empty storage, and outbound rules understandable?
- [ ] Are booth build, labor, EAC, electrical, rigging, height, fire, and structural approval rules clear?
- [ ] Are move-in and move-out schedules sequenced for docks, aisles, empties, inspections, and carrier deadlines?
- [ ] Are insurance, permits, sampling, food, alcohol, vehicles, hazardous materials, and risky demos reviewed early?
- [ ] Are exhibitor reminders, webinars, FAQs, and help contacts planned without uncontrolled exceptions?
- [ ] Are onsite service tickets, freight receipts, photos, and outbound bills retained for disputes?
- [ ] Are first-time exhibitors able to follow the process without prior trade show knowledge?
- [ ] Would contractor, venue, decorator, and exhibitor instructions all match on show site?
