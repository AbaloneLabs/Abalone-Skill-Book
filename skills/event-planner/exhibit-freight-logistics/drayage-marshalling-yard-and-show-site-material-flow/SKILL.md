# Drayage Marshalling Yard And Show Site Material Flow

Show-site freight has its own logic. Drayage, marshaling yards, target move-in times, docks, forklifts, empty storage, union jurisdictions, and move-out procedures can control when a booth is actually buildable. Agents may know that freight shipped but not understand how it moves through the event system. This skill helps manage material flow from truck arrival to booth placement and back out again.

## Core Rules

### Understand drayage scope and cost drivers

Drayage may include receiving freight, unloading, weighing, moving to booth, storing empties, returning empties, moving freight to dock, and loading outbound carrier. Fees can depend on weight, minimums, overtime, special handling, small packages, uncrated items, mixed shipments, late arrival, and warehouse versus show-site delivery.

Explain cost exposure early. Exhibitors and clients are often surprised because shipping cost and drayage cost are separate.

### Follow target move-in and marshaling rules

Large shows often assign target dates and times for freight arrival. Trucks may need to check in at a marshaling yard before dock access. Missing target windows can cause waiting fees, overtime, or delayed booth setup.

Coordinate carriers with target times and provide complete show information. A driver who arrives at the wrong dock or skips the marshaling yard can lose hours.

### Coordinate dock, forklift, and labor needs

Booth components may require forklifts, pallet jacks, riggers, carpet labor, electricians, carpenters, decorators, or union crews. Determine who can move freight from aisle to booth and when. Some freight cannot be handled by exhibitor staff.

Plan labor calls around freight availability. A build crew waiting for crates is expensive; crates arriving before labor may block aisles.

### Protect aisle and safety flow

Move-in floors are crowded with forklifts, crates, ladders, carpet, electrical work, and people. Material placement should not block fire aisles, neighboring booths, emergency exits, or access to utilities. Empty crates need labels and removal process.

Safety rules are not paperwork. Poor freight staging can create injuries and delay multiple booths.

### Track freight at piece level

Use piece counts, crate numbers, photos, labels, tracking, dock receipts, and booth delivery confirmation. Partial deliveries are common. A booth may receive five of six crates and not notice until a critical counter or monitor is missing.

Assign someone to verify arrivals against the freight list during move-in, not after build starts.

### Keep a show-site freight command log

During move-in, maintain a simple log of truck check-in, dock release, pieces delivered, service desk tickets, missing items, forklift requests, labor calls, empty labels applied, and decisions made. This log helps resolve disputes and keeps shift changes clean.

The log does not need to be elaborate. It needs to show current status, owner, next action, and time. Show floors are too chaotic to rely on memory.

### Manage empty crate storage

Empty crates and cases are usually labeled and removed by the general contractor, then returned after show close. This takes time. Materials needed during the show should not be packed into empties. Return timing after close can delay outbound packing.

Label empties correctly and photograph labels. Mislabeling can cause expensive delays during move-out.

### Plan move-out before show close

Outbound material flow needs carrier, bills of lading, labels, pickup time, target dock, packing crew, empty return timing, material handling forms, and forced freight risk. If the carrier misses the deadline, show contractor may ship at higher cost.

Do not wait until the aisle carpet is being rolled up to decide outbound logistics. Move-out is faster and more chaotic than move-in.

### Communicate with exhibitors and booth staff

Exhibitors need to know what they can hand-carry, when freight arrives, what not to pack in empties, where to get service desk help, and who approves labor or material handling orders. Booth staff may not understand show logistics.

Clear instructions reduce conflict with contractors and prevent accidental rule violations.

## Common Traps

- Treating drayage as the same thing as shipping.
- Missing target move-in, marshaling yard, or dock check-in rules.
- Sending carriers without booth, event, contractor, and target-time information.
- Scheduling labor before freight can reach the booth.
- Blocking aisles, utility access, or neighboring booths with crates.
- Tracking shipments only by carrier tracking instead of piece-level booth delivery; running move-in from memory instead of a freight command log with tickets, owners, and timestamps
- Packing needed show materials into empty crates that are removed; mislabeling empties and losing them before move-out
- Waiting until show close to arrange outbound carrier and bills of lading; letting exhibitors violate hand-carry or labor rules because instructions were unclear

## Self-Check

- Are drayage scope, minimums, overtime, special handling, warehouse, and show-site fees understood?
- Are target move-in, marshaling yard, dock, carrier check-in, and waiting-time rules communicated?
- Are forklift, labor, union, rigging, electrical, and booth build needs aligned with freight timing?
- Does freight staging protect aisles, exits, neighboring booths, utilities, and worker safety?
- Is freight tracked by piece count, crate number, photos, labels, dock receipts, and booth confirmation?
- Is there a freight command log for truck check-in, dock release, service tickets, forklift requests, and next actions?
- Are empty crate labels, removal, storage, and return timing planned?
- Are outbound carrier, bills of lading, labels, packing crew, and forced freight risks planned before close?
- Do exhibitors and booth staff know hand-carry, service desk, labor approval, and material handling rules?
