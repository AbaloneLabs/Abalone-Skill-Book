# Event Emergency Action Plan And Command Roles

An event emergency action plan is only useful if people know what to do, who decides, and how to communicate under pressure. It should connect venue procedures, public agency requirements, event staffing, guest communication, medical response, security, production, and client authority into one workable system. Agents often write emergency plans as generic safety documents instead of live operating plans. This skill helps the agent build emergency readiness around command clarity, realistic triggers, and staff behavior.

## Core Rules

### Build From The Venue And Event Risk Profile

Start with the venue's emergency procedures, evacuation routes, shelter locations, fire alarm policy, AED locations, medical room, security contacts, public address system, emergency vehicle access, and incident reporting process. Then add event-specific risks: crowd size, alcohol, VIPs, children, weather, temporary structures, pyrotechnics, water, animals, protests, controversial content, late-night departures, and accessibility needs.

Do not copy a generic emergency template without adapting it to the site and program. The plan for a hotel ballroom, outdoor festival, campus event, private estate, convention center, theater, and public street closure differs. If the event uses a nontraditional venue, confirm which party owns fire, medical, security, and evacuation planning.

### Define Command Roles And Authority

Name the incident lead, venue lead, security lead, medical lead, production lead, client decision-maker, communications lead, and backups. Clarify who can stop the show, pause entry, evacuate, shelter in place, call emergency services, remove an attendee, cancel a segment, activate weather plan, or issue public messaging. Authority should not depend on the most senior person being reachable.

Use a simple command structure. During an incident, staff should know whether they report to event command, venue security, production, or public authorities. If outside agencies take control, the event team should know how to support rather than interfere. Avoid parallel chains where client leaders, venue managers, and producers give conflicting instructions.

### Create Observable Triggers

Emergency decisions should be tied to observable conditions where possible: fire alarm, blocked exit, medical incident requiring ambulance, lightning within threshold, severe weather warning, structural concern, credible threat, crowd surge, lost child, power loss, active harassment or violence report, or unsafe intoxication. Exact thresholds may come from venue policy, public agencies, or qualified professionals, but the event plan should not rely on improvisation.

Triggers should state immediate action and escalation. For example: stop music, clear lane for medical response, hold doors, move guests to shelter, notify command, or prepare announcement. Staff cannot execute vague instructions such as "monitor situation" without knowing what changes the response.

### Design Communication Channels And Language

Emergency communication needs redundancy: radio, phone, text group, command center, public address, screens, app push, staff runners, and venue alarms. Assign channels by role and keep them simple. Radio protocol should include location, issue, requested action, and whether emergency services are needed.

Prepare scripts for likely scenarios: medical pause, severe weather delay, evacuation, shelter, room closure, security hold, missing person, and cancellation. Scripts should be plain, calm, and action-oriented. Avoid euphemisms that confuse attendees. If the event includes multilingual or accessibility needs, plan how critical messages will reach those attendees.

### Train Frontline Staff For First Actions

Most incidents are first seen by ushers, registration staff, volunteers, catering staff, security, or attendees. Frontline staff need simple first actions: call command, identify location, keep access clear, do not move injured people unless trained and necessary, direct guests away from hazards, preserve privacy, and wait for qualified responders.

Brief staff on emergency routes, AEDs, exits, shelter areas, radio language, escalation contacts, and what not to do. Volunteers should not be asked to physically intervene, investigate serious complaints, handle intoxicated guests alone, or make medical judgments. Give them observation and escalation roles.

### Include Recovery And Documentation

After an incident, decide whether the event resumes, relocates, cancels, or modifies the program. The plan should include who assesses readiness, how guests are updated, how vendors are informed, and how media or stakeholder questions are handled. A small incident can become a larger crisis if the restart decision is careless.

Document incident time, location, people involved, actions taken, responders contacted, witness information where appropriate, and follow-up owner. Records support claims, learning, and duty-of-care review. Protect sensitive information, especially medical, youth, harassment, or security incidents.

## Common Traps

- Writing a polished emergency plan that frontline staff never see or practice.
- Naming departments instead of actual decision owners and backups.
- Assuming the venue will handle all emergencies without event-specific coordination.
- Creating triggers that are too vague to act on under pressure.
- Letting the client, producer, venue, and security lead all believe someone else has stop-show authority.
- Relying on one communication channel such as radio or PA only.
- Forgetting accessible emergency communication, egress, and shelter support.
- Assigning volunteers to roles that require trained security, medical, or legal judgment; restarting the event after an incident without a readiness decision and documentation

## Self-Check

- Is the plan based on the actual venue procedures and event-specific risks?
- Are incident lead, venue lead, security, medical, production, client, communications, and backups named?
- Is authority clear for stop-show, evacuation, shelter, emergency calls, removal, cancellation, and public messaging?
- Are triggers observable and tied to immediate actions?
- Are communication channels, radio language, scripts, and backup methods defined?
- Are frontline staff briefed on first actions, exits, AEDs, shelter, escalation, and limits of their role?
- Are accessibility, language, mobility, sensory, and medical needs included in emergency communication and movement?
- Is there a process for deciding whether to resume, modify, relocate, or cancel after an incident?
- Are incident documentation and privacy controls defined?
- Would a new staff member know who to call and what to do in the first two minutes?
