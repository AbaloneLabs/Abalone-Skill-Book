# Crisis Communications Media And Stakeholder Updates

Event crisis communication must move faster than rumor while staying accurate, lawful, and humane. A crisis may involve safety, weather, cancellation, protest, medical incident, harassment report, speaker controversy, technology outage, security issue, foodborne illness, data breach, or public criticism. Agents often draft polished statements without defining who approves them, what facts are known, what must stay private, and which audiences need different updates. This skill helps the agent plan crisis messaging as part of incident response.

## Core Rules

### Separate Audiences And Purposes

Identify who needs information: attendees, staff, volunteers, speakers, performers, sponsors, vendors, venue, client leadership, board, media, public agencies, neighbors, virtual attendees, ticket holders, families, and internal executives. Each audience may need different timing, detail, and action. Attendees need what to do. Staff need instructions. Media need a statement. Families may need reassurance. Regulators may need formal reporting.

Do not send one vague message to everyone if actions differ. A weather shelter instruction, sponsor update, media holding statement, and staff radio message should align but not be identical. Message design should reduce confusion and protect privacy.

### Establish Approval And Spokesperson Authority

Crisis communication needs a clear approval path before the crisis. Name who drafts, who approves, who posts, who speaks to media, who updates attendees, who informs sponsors, and who coordinates with legal, venue, security, medical, or public authorities. If approval is too slow, inaccurate messages or silence will fill the gap.

Use holding statements for early uncertainty. A good holding statement confirms awareness, states immediate safety action if relevant, avoids speculation, promises updates, and names the next channel. Do not wait for perfect information before telling attendees what action to take.

### Communicate Facts, Actions, And Timing

Crisis messages should distinguish confirmed facts, actions being taken, instructions for recipients, and next update time. Avoid speculation, blame, medical details, names of affected individuals, and legal conclusions. If information is uncertain, say so plainly. Accuracy matters more than appearing complete.

Give practical instructions: where to go, whether to stay away, how to get refunds, when to return, how to reach support, what entrance is closed, whether sessions continue, or how virtual attendees can access recordings. Emotional tone should be calm and respectful, but it must not bury the action.

### Protect Privacy And Legal Sensitivity

Incidents involving medical emergencies, minors, harassment, assault, security threats, employee issues, VIPs, donor data, or attendee personal data require strict privacy. Do not identify individuals or share details beyond need-to-know. Coordinate with legal, HR, security, medical, or safeguarding leads where relevant.

Media pressure can be intense. Staff should know not to speculate, post personal commentary, share photos, or confirm details without authorization. Social media monitoring can identify misinformation, but responses should be controlled.

### Coordinate Channels

Use the channels that match urgency: PA, screens, SMS, email, app push, website, social media, helpdesk scripts, staff radio, signage, ticketing platform, press release, and direct stakeholder calls. Make sure channels agree. If cancellation appears on social media before ticket holders receive email, support volume and frustration rise.

Accessibility and language matter. Critical instructions should be available to attendees who are deaf or hard of hearing, blind or low vision, non-native speakers, offline, or not using the event app. Do not rely only on audio announcements in a loud environment or only on digital messages where connectivity is weak.

### Follow Through After The Immediate Crisis

Post-incident communication may include refunds, rescheduling, apology, investigation status, safety actions, support resources, sponsor updates, vendor instructions, insurance documentation, and lessons learned. Avoid overpromising outcomes before review. If commitments are made, assign owners and timelines.

Review communication performance after the event. Did messages reach people? Were they clear? Did staff repeat the same language? Did misinformation spread? Did privacy hold? Use the review to improve future crisis playbooks.

## Common Traps

- Drafting messages during the crisis with no approval path or spokesperson authority.
- Sending vague reassurance when attendees need immediate instructions.
- Speculating about cause, blame, injury, security, or legal responsibility.
- Sharing personal details about medical, youth, harassment, or security incidents.
- Letting unauthorized staff answer media or post incident commentary.
- Updating social media, website, email, app, and onsite signage inconsistently.
- Relying on one communication channel that some attendees cannot access.
- Forgetting sponsors, vendors, speakers, virtual attendees, or families as distinct audiences; apologizing or promising refunds, investigations, or safety changes without authority and follow-through

## Self-Check

- Are crisis audiences separated by information need and action required?
- Is there a named approval path, spokesperson, channel owner, and backup?
- Are holding statements prepared for early uncertainty?
- Do messages separate confirmed facts, actions, instructions, and next update time?
- Are speculation, blame, private personal details, and legal conclusions avoided?
- Are legal, venue, security, medical, HR, safeguarding, or public-agency coordination points defined?
- Are staff instructed how to handle media, social posts, rumors, and attendee questions?
- Are PA, SMS, email, app, website, social, signage, helpdesk, and stakeholder calls coordinated?
- Are accessibility, language, offline, and connectivity limitations considered?
- Is there a post-incident follow-up owner for refunds, rescheduling, support, investigation updates, and lessons learned?
