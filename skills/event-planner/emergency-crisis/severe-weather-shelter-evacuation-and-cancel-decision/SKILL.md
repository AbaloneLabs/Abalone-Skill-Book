# Severe Weather Shelter Evacuation And Cancel Decision

Weather decisions are difficult because safety, money, guest experience, vendor commitments, and client emotion collide. Outdoor and semi-outdoor events are especially exposed, but indoor events can also face travel disruption, power loss, flooding, smoke, heat, or evacuation constraints. Agents often recommend a rain plan but miss decision authority, thresholds, shelter capacity, communication, accessibility, and cancellation timing. This skill helps the agent design weather decisions that are made before pressure peaks.

## Core Rules

### Identify Weather Hazards Specific To The Site

Weather risk includes lightning, wind, rain, flooding, snow, ice, heat, cold, poor air quality, wildfire smoke, high surf, rough water, dust, tornado warnings, hail, and reduced visibility. The relevant hazards depend on geography, season, venue, structures, ground conditions, transportation, attendee demographics, and event activities.

Map vulnerable elements: tents, stages, temporary structures, power, generators, signage, decor, trees, water features, parking fields, shuttles, exposed queues, elderly attendees, children, performers, animals, equipment, food safety, and accessible routes. A weather plan should address the parts of the event that fail first.

### Use Qualified Forecasting And Monitoring

Assign a weather monitor and sources. For larger or higher-risk events, consider professional weather services or venue/public agency guidance. Define how often weather is checked and what source is authoritative. Social media rumors and casual radar checks are not enough for serious weather decisions.

Use thresholds from qualified sources where possible: lightning radius, wind speed for structures, heat index, air quality index, flood warnings, tornado warnings, snow accumulation, or road closure. The planner should not invent technical thresholds for structures or safety; use venue, engineer, fire marshal, tent company, public agency, or professional guidance.

### Decide Delay, Shelter, Evacuation, Or Cancellation In Advance

Weather response is not one choice. Delay may work for passing storms. Shelter may be safer than evacuation during lightning or tornado conditions. Evacuation may be required for flooding, structural risk, fire, or unsafe site conditions. Cancellation may be needed when safe operation cannot be restored. Define the decision tree before the event.

Name who decides and when. If the call is delayed too long, guests may already be onsite, vendors may have exposed equipment, and safe movement may be harder. Include financial and contractual impacts, but do not let sunk cost override safety. A strong plan gives leaders permission to make the hard call.

### Confirm Shelter And Egress Capacity

Shelter plans must be real. Identify where attendees, staff, vendors, performers, VIPs, and people with mobility needs will go. Check capacity, distance, route safety, restrooms, ventilation, power, communication, and whether shelter is accessible. A small indoor room cannot shelter a large outdoor crowd.

Egress routes can fail in weather. Rain creates mud and slippery floors; darkness reduces visibility; wind can move signage; flooding blocks parking; elevators may be unavailable; shuttles may stop. Plan how people move safely and where they wait. If evacuation means sending people to cars, check whether the parking area is safe.

### Protect Staff, Vendors, And Equipment

Weather plans often focus on attendees but ignore workers. Stagehands, caterers, security, parking staff, performers, drivers, and volunteers may be outside longer than guests. Define stop-work conditions, break areas, hydration, warming or cooling, rain gear, and when staff may leave exposed posts.

Equipment and structures need protection: stages, tents, power, cables, generators, speakers, lighting, screens, decor, food, and rented furniture. Decide what can be covered, lowered, powered down, moved, or abandoned safely. Do not ask staff to secure equipment during dangerous conditions.

### Communicate Early And Repeatedly

Weather communication should reach attendees before travel, at arrival, onsite, and during delays. Use email, SMS, app, website, signage, PA, staff scripts, and social channels consistently. Messages should state status, action, timing, safety instructions, and next update time.

For cancellation or postponement, include refund, ticket, reschedule, travel, vendor, and media messaging plans. If the event continues with changes, explain what changed: entrance, footwear, parking, shuttle, coat check, indoor location, schedule, or prohibited items. Ambiguity creates support burden and unsafe movement.

## Common Traps

- Treating "rain plan" as the whole weather plan.
- Making weather decisions based on optimism, sunk cost, or client emotion rather than thresholds.
- Planning shelter without checking capacity, accessibility, restrooms, route safety, and communication.
- Evacuating during conditions where shelter would be safer, or sheltering where flooding or structural risk requires evacuation.
- Ignoring staff and vendor exposure during setup, show hours, and strike.
- Assuming tents, stages, and temporary structures are safe in wind without professional limits.
- Sending cancellation updates through one channel only.
- Forgetting parking, shuttles, rideshare, mud, darkness, and road conditions; waiting until attendees are onsite to announce foreseeable weather changes

## Self-Check

- Are site-specific weather hazards identified beyond rain?
- Are vulnerable structures, routes, equipment, queues, staff posts, and attendee groups mapped?
- Is a qualified weather source and monitoring owner assigned?
- Are safety thresholds based on venue, public agency, engineer, tent, or professional guidance where needed?
- Is the delay/shelter/evacuate/cancel decision tree defined with owners and timing?
- Are shelter locations real, accessible, large enough, and supportable?
- Are egress, parking, shuttle, rideshare, lighting, mud, flood, and road issues addressed?
- Are staff, vendors, performers, and volunteers protected by stop-work and exposure rules?
- Are communication channels, scripts, next-update timing, and cancellation messages ready?
- Does the plan let leaders choose safety before financial pressure dominates?
