# Vendor Schedule Loadin And Onsite Coordination

Vendor coordination is where the event plan becomes a physical operation. A strong contract can still fail if vendors arrive in the wrong order, cannot access the dock, lack badges, block each other, miss power, or do not know who can approve changes onsite. Agents often list vendor arrival times without testing dependencies, venue access, labor limits, staging space, and strike sequence. Strong onsite coordination turns many separate vendor plans into one integrated operating schedule.

Use this skill when preparing vendor schedules, load-in plans, event-day operations, or teardown coordination.

## Core Rules

### Build The Schedule From Dependencies

Vendor timing should follow operational dependencies, not vendor preference alone. Flooring may need to go in before furniture. Rigging may precede lighting. Power may precede registration. Rentals may need to arrive before catering setup. Decor may need access after production heavy work but before photography. Security may need to be present before high-value items arrive.

Map what each vendor needs before they can start and what they must finish before another vendor can work. Then create a sequence that protects safety, efficiency, and critical deadlines. A schedule with everyone arriving at 8 a.m. is usually not a plan.

### Confirm Access, Credentials, And Site Rules

Each vendor needs practical access information: delivery address, dock entrance, parking, check-in process, badges, insurance approval, union rules, freight elevator, vehicle height, loading zone, security screening, contact number, and allowed work areas. Missing access details cause late starts and venue conflict.

Send vendor instructions early and reconfirm before load-in. Include venue rules for floor protection, waste, smoking, noise, adhesives, power, storage, restrooms, and meal areas. Vendors should not discover rules from security at the door.

### Coordinate Dock, Freight, And Staging Space

Loading docks, elevators, service corridors, and staging areas are scarce resources. Large deliveries, trucks, rentals, catering, production cases, florals, signage, sponsor materials, and waste removal can collide. Assign delivery windows and staging locations.

For complex events, use a dock schedule and a load-in manager. Identify what can be delivered early, what must be just-in-time, what needs refrigeration, what is high value, and what must remain accessible. Keep guest areas clear and preserve emergency routes.

### Define Onsite Communication And Authority

Vendors need to know who runs the event, who controls the venue, who approves changes, who handles emergencies, and how to reach each other. Establish radio channels, phone list, command post, escalation path, and decision authority before doors open.

Clarify which decisions the onsite planner can make and which require client approval: overtime, substitutions, layout changes, safety stops, weather moves, guest-facing changes, or added costs. Unclear authority slows response and creates billing disputes.

### Protect Rehearsal, Testing, And Inspection Time

Load-in schedules often underestimate testing: soundcheck, lighting focus, livestream test, registration scan test, kitchen timing, room reset, signage review, accessibility route check, fire marshal walkthrough, and sponsor activation test. If testing is squeezed, problems surface in front of guests.

Build test and inspection windows into the schedule. Do not let setup work consume all buffer. If a vendor runs late, decide what testing is protected and what scope can be simplified.

### Plan Strike And Load-Out With Equal Care

Teardown can create damage, lost items, overtime, safety incidents, and vendor disputes. Strike sequence matters: guests depart, valuables secure, rentals counted, waste removed, production powers down, catering clears, sponsor materials packed, and venue inspected. Some vendors cannot leave until another vendor finishes.

Define strike times, freight routes, disposal, storage, staff responsibilities, damage inspection, rental counts, and who signs venue closeout. If labor or venue access ends at a hard time, plan backward.

### Manage Vendor Meals, Breaks, And Working Conditions

Long event days require realistic vendor care. Crew meals, water, rest areas, breaks, parking, restrooms, and safe work conditions affect performance. Ignoring vendor working conditions can create delays, errors, morale problems, and labor issues.

Plan vendor meals and breaks where appropriate, especially for production, security, registration, transportation, catering, and staff working through meal periods. Respect labor rules and safety requirements.

### Keep A Live Issue Log

During load-in and event day, issues appear constantly. Track issue, owner, decision needed, deadline, status, and cost impact. This prevents problems from living in scattered texts and helps close out after the event.

After the event, use the log to review vendor performance, approve change orders, dispute invoices, and improve future plans.

## Common Traps

### Scheduling Vendors Independently

Each vendor may optimize for themselves. The planner must integrate dependencies and shared resources.

### Ignoring Dock And Elevator Limits

Access constraints can delay the whole day. Dock schedules and staging plans are necessary for complex events.

### Forgetting Credentialing And COIs

Vendors without approved documents or badges may be denied entry. Confirm before load-in.

### Squeezing Testing Time

Sound, lights, registration, livestream, kitchens, and signage need testing. Setup completion is not readiness.

### Leaving Cost Approval Ambiguous

Onsite overtime and changes happen fast. Know who can approve paid changes.

### Treating Strike As Cleanup Only

Strike has safety, damage, rental, labor, and venue deadline risks. Plan it deliberately.

### Not Feeding Vendors

Long shifts without meals or breaks reduce performance and can violate labor expectations.

### Losing Issues In Text Messages

Text threads do not replace an issue log. Track ownership and resolution.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is vendor timing based on dependencies rather than independent arrival preferences?
- [ ] Are access, parking, dock, badges, insurance, security, freight elevator, and venue rules communicated?
- [ ] Are dock, freight, staging, refrigeration, high-value items, and emergency routes coordinated?
- [ ] Is onsite communication defined with contacts, radio channels, command post, and escalation path?
- [ ] Is authority clear for overtime, substitutions, layout changes, safety decisions, and added cost?
- [ ] Are rehearsal, technical testing, registration testing, inspections, and accessibility checks protected?
- [ ] Is strike sequenced with rental counts, waste, damage review, storage, and venue closeout?
- [ ] Are vendor meals, breaks, water, restrooms, and safe working conditions planned?
- [ ] Is there a live issue log with owners and deadlines?
- [ ] Could a vendor arrive onsite and know exactly where to go, who to call, and what to do?
