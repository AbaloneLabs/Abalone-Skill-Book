# Staff Meals Breaks And Back Of House Care

Staff care is operational infrastructure. Hungry, dehydrated, exhausted, or unsupported staff make mistakes, treat guests poorly, miss safety signals, and leave early. Agents often plan attendee hospitality in detail while assuming crew, vendors, volunteers, and security can manage themselves. A strong back-of-house plan protects people who deliver the event and keeps operations reliable across long shifts.

## Core Rules

### Identify every group needing care

Map planners, production crew, AV, stagehands, security, registration staff, volunteers, speakers, interpreters, drivers, catering staff, cleaners, medical, photographers, vendors, union labor, and client team. Each group may have different call times, meal breaks, location limits, and contractual requirements.

Do not assume a vendor's employer handles meals. Contracts may require client-provided meals after certain hours, or crews may be unable to leave post.

### Build meals into shift plans

Meal breaks should align with coverage needs. Registration cannot all leave during peak arrival; security posts need relief; AV needs breaks around show cues; volunteers may need shorter shifts. Plan staggered meals and relief coverage.

A meal plan that requires abandoning a critical position is not a plan. Break coverage should be assigned, not improvised.

### Count accurately and include dietary needs

Staff meal counts should include vendors, late crew, overnight crew, load-in, show day, strike, and people who are not on the guest count. Track allergies, vegetarian, vegan, halal, kosher, gluten-free, and medical needs where feasible.

Underfeeding staff creates resentment and operational risk. Overordering without storage and waste plan also matters.

### Choose food that fits work conditions

Crew food should be practical: easy to eat, not too messy, available at the right temperature, with protein, hydration, caffeine where appropriate, and options for long shifts. Avoid only sugary snacks or heavy meals before physical work.

Provide water stations, electrolyte options in heat, and warm drinks in cold. Staff often cannot access attendee bars or buffets.

### Provide rest, storage, and sanitation

Back-of-house should include seating, shade or heat, restrooms, handwashing, trash, secure bag storage, phone charging, and a quiet place for short breaks. Volunteers and temporary staff need somewhere to leave personal items safely.

Do not place staff rest areas so far from posts that breaks become impossible. Convenience determines whether care is used.

### Respect labor rules and contracts

Union labor, venue staff, security, and contracted crews may have required breaks, meal penalties, turnaround times, and overtime. Know who tracks compliance and who approves extensions. Ignoring rules can create cost and relationship issues.

Volunteer programs also need humane limits. Unpaid enthusiasm does not remove the need for breaks and food.

### Monitor welfare during the event

Supervisors should watch for missed breaks, heat stress, dehydration, low blood sugar, unsafe lifting, emotional fatigue, harassment, and staff who are afraid to leave posts. Provide a way for staff and volunteers to request relief without feeling they are failing the team.

Back-of-house care should be active. A meal table in a distant room does not help someone who cannot leave a radio post or registration line.

### Plan for load-in and strike

Care is often weakest before and after the guest-facing event. Load-in and strike can be physically demanding, late, cold, hot, or wet. Provide meals, water, PPE if needed, lighting, and break plans for these phases.

Do not spend the entire hospitality budget on showtime while the hardest labor happens unfed.

### Communicate care details clearly

Staff should know where meals are, when they can eat, what is available, where to refill water, where to take breaks, and who handles issues. Supervisors should monitor whether people actually get breaks.

Back-of-house care is not successful because food was ordered; it is successful when people can use it.

## Common Traps

- Counting only event staff and forgetting vendors, security, interpreters, drivers, and strike crew.
- Scheduling meal breaks during peak operational moments.
- Providing no relief coverage for fixed posts.
- Underestimating dietary needs and allergen labeling.
- Feeding staff with leftover attendee food that arrives too late.
- Forgetting water, electrolytes, warm drinks, and weather-appropriate care; providing no secure storage, seating, restrooms, or charging for staff
- Ignoring union meal penalties, overtime, and turnaround rules; failing to monitor missed breaks, heat stress, dehydration, unsafe lifting, harassment, and inability to leave posts
- Taking care of show-day staff but not load-in and strike teams; assuming food delivery equals usable breaks

## Self-Check

- Are all staff, vendor, volunteer, crew, security, driver, interpreter, and strike groups included in care planning?
- Are meals and breaks built into shift plans with relief coverage?
- Are counts, allergies, dietary needs, late crews, overnight crews, and strike teams included?
- Does food fit the work, timing, weather, and energy needs?
- Are water, restrooms, seating, storage, charging, shade, heat, trash, and handwashing available?
- Are labor rules, meal penalties, overtime, and volunteer limits respected?
- Are supervisors actively monitoring welfare and giving staff a usable relief path?
- Are load-in and strike care planned, not only guest-facing hours?
- Do staff know where and when to eat, refill, rest, and report issues?
