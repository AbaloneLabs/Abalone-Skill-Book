# Union Labor Exclusive Vendor And House Rule Review

Venue house rules can matter as much as rental price. They define who is allowed to work, which vendors must be used, when work can happen, what equipment can be brought in, and what fees apply when the event deviates from the venue's standard operating model. Agents often ask whether a space is available and overlook union labor jurisdiction, exclusive catering or AV, required security, rigging rules, freight access, overtime, noise limits, storage, and vendor approval processes. These rules can change budget, timeline, creative options, and feasibility.

Use this skill before recommending a venue, comparing venue costs, contracting vendors, or finalizing production, catering, decor, exhibitor, or load-in plans.

## Core Rules

### Identify Exclusive And Required Vendors

Many venues require specific vendors for catering, beverage, AV, rigging, security, electrical, internet, cleaning, furniture, valet, ticketing, or waste. Some allow outside vendors with fees, approvals, insurance, or supervision. Others prohibit outside services entirely.

List all exclusive, preferred, and restricted categories. For each, identify price implications, service quality, flexibility, approval process, deadlines, and whether exceptions exist. A venue with low rental but mandatory high-cost vendors may not be cost-effective.

### Understand Union Labor Jurisdiction

Convention centers, hotels, arenas, theaters, and major venues may have union labor rules that govern who can move freight, plug in equipment, hang signs, operate lifts, set stage, run AV, handle rigging, install booths, or load trucks. Work jurisdiction affects cost, schedule, vendor selection, and onsite control.

Ask which tasks require union labor, minimum calls, overtime rules, meal penalties, holidays, steward requirements, cancellation terms, and who orders labor. Ensure vendors understand what they can and cannot do. A nonunion vendor may still need to work through house labor.

### Review Load-In, Load-Out, And Access Rules

House rules often limit when and how vendors can enter. Check dock access, freight elevator size, vehicle height, marshaling yard rules, delivery windows, security check-in, floor protection, freight routes, storage, overnight holds, and strike deadlines. These rules affect labor hours and production feasibility.

A short rental window may require overnight labor or additional days. A small freight elevator may require more trips. A venue with no storage may require just-in-time delivery. Build these realities into budget and schedule.

### Check Technical And Rigging Restrictions

Venues may restrict rigging, power tie-ins, open flame, haze, pyrotechnics, drones, confetti, decals, adhesives, drilling, hanging, floor loads, vehicle displays, wireless frequencies, or outside AV. House engineers may be required for power, lighting, sound, or network changes.

Production design should be tested against these rules early. Do not let creative concepts assume attachments, ceiling points, or special effects that the venue will not allow. If approval is needed, assign owner and deadline.

### Review Food, Beverage, And Alcohol Rules

Catering exclusivity, minimums, corkage, outside food restrictions, sampling rules, alcohol service, bartender ratios, last-call times, cash bar rules, sponsor beverage rights, health permits, and donation of leftover food can shape event experience and cost. Food-related rules also affect cultural, religious, allergen, and accessibility needs.

Clarify whether menus can be customized, whether outside specialty vendors are allowed, how tastings work, how dietary requests are handled, and what service fees apply. If sponsor sampling or food trucks are planned, confirm permission and permits.

### Understand Security, Staffing, And Supervision Mandates

Venues may require house security, ushers, police, fire watch, medical staff, parking attendants, cleaners, coat check, restroom attendants, engineers, or event managers. Requirements may scale with attendance, alcohol, VIPs, public access, or late hours.

These mandates should be treated as fixed operating costs and duty-of-care requirements. If the client wants to reduce staffing, check whether the venue permits it and whether risk remains acceptable.

### Translate Rules Into Budget And Timeline

House rules are only useful if translated into cost and schedule. For each rule, identify cost driver, lead time, approval requirement, responsible party, and risk if missed. Include overtime, minimum calls, supervision, service charges, permit fees, and required staff in the budget.

Compare venues using rule-adjusted costs. A venue that includes strong infrastructure may be cheaper overall than a venue with low rental and many required add-ons.

### Communicate Rules To Vendors And Stakeholders

Vendors, sponsors, exhibitors, decorators, speakers, and internal stakeholders need to know venue rules before designing deliverables. Provide rule summaries for load-in, signage, sampling, booth setup, power, internet, freight, alcohol, and teardown. Hidden rules create conflict onsite.

For trade shows or sponsor-heavy events, include rules in exhibitor manuals and sponsor agreements. Confirm that third parties cannot promise activations the venue prohibits.

## Common Traps

### Comparing Venue Rental Fees Without Required Vendor Costs

Mandatory vendors and labor can exceed the rental fee. Compare fully loaded costs.

### Assuming Outside Vendors Can Work Freely

Venues may require house supervision, union labor, approved vendor status, or insurance even when outside vendors are allowed.

### Discovering Labor Jurisdiction Onsite

If vendors perform work reserved for union or house labor, work can stop and costs can rise. Clarify early.

### Ignoring Minimum Calls And Overtime

Labor minimums, meal breaks, holidays, and overtime can make short tasks expensive. Schedule realistically.

### Designing Decor That Violates House Rules

Adhesives, hanging, candles, confetti, vehicles, haze, and floor loads are common restrictions. Check before creative approval.

### Underestimating Dock And Elevator Constraints

Small docks, limited elevators, and narrow service corridors increase labor and time. They are not minor details.

### Forgetting Sponsor And Exhibitor Rules

Sponsors may want sampling, signage, retail, lead capture, or buildouts that require venue approval. Communicate limits early.

### Treating Required Staffing As Optional

Venue-mandated security, cleaning, engineers, ushers, or fire watch may be contract requirements and safety controls.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Are exclusive, preferred, restricted, and required vendors identified by service category?
- [ ] Are union labor jurisdiction, minimum calls, overtime, meal penalties, holidays, and ordering process understood?
- [ ] Are load-in, load-out, dock, freight elevator, storage, vehicle, and access rules reflected in schedule and budget?
- [ ] Are rigging, power, AV, internet, special effects, signage, floor, and attachment restrictions checked?
- [ ] Are catering, alcohol, sampling, outside food, dietary, and service fee rules understood?
- [ ] Are security, ushers, police, medical, cleaning, engineering, and supervision mandates included?
- [ ] Are all rules translated into cost, timeline, approval owner, and operational risk?
- [ ] Are venue rules communicated to vendors, sponsors, exhibitors, and internal stakeholders before they design work?
- [ ] Are exceptions captured in writing by an authorized venue representative?
- [ ] Is the venue still feasible after house rules and required services are included?
