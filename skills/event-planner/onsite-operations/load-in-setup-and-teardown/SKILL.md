# Load In Setup And Teardown

Load-in and teardown are the physical construction and deconstruction of the event. They determine whether the venue is ready before guests arrive and whether the site is returned safely, on time, and without damage. Agents often focus on the guest-facing schedule and underestimate dock limits, sequence dependencies, labor rules, safety checks, storage, waste, rentals, and strike deadlines. Strong load-in planning turns vendor work into a sequenced operation with owners, buffers, and inspection points.

Use this skill when planning event build, setup, venue turnover, strike, or site restoration.

## Core Rules

### Sequence Work By Physical Dependencies

Setup order matters. Floor protection may precede heavy equipment. Rigging may precede lighting. Stage build may precede decor. Power may precede registration. Rentals may precede table settings. Catering may need kitchen access before service areas are dressed.

Create a sequence that respects dependencies, shared spaces, and safety. Avoid having too many vendors in the same area at once. If the venue offers limited load-in time, identify what can be prebuilt, delivered early, simplified, or cut.

### Control Access, Docks, And Freight Movement

Load-in often fails at the dock. Vehicles need arrival windows, addresses, height clearances, freight elevators, marshaling, security check-in, parking, union labor, and staging areas. Without control, trucks queue, vendors block each other, and labor goes idle.

Use a delivery schedule for complex events. Assign freight routes, elevator times, vehicle contacts, and staging zones. Keep emergency routes open. Confirm venue rules for floor protection, carts, pallets, disposal, and overnight storage.

### Build In Inspection And Readiness Checks

Setup is not ready until inspected. Check stage stability, power, cables, trip hazards, signage, seating, accessibility routes, fire exits, food service, restrooms, registration, lighting, sound, sponsor areas, VIP rooms, and emergency equipment. Safety and accessibility checks should happen before doors, not after guests find problems.

Use readiness checkpoints: venue handover, production ready, catering ready, registration ready, front-of-house ready, security ready, and final walkthrough. Assign owners and deadlines.

### Protect Venue And Property

Events can damage floors, walls, elevators, lawns, docks, furniture, and restrooms. Protect surfaces, define approved attachment methods, control food and beverage spills, manage waste, and document pre-existing damage. For outdoor sites, protect turf, landscaping, utilities, and drainage.

Take pre-event photos where damage disputes are possible. During strike, inspect with venue representatives and document any incidents honestly.

### Manage Waste, Returns, And Leftover Materials

Teardown creates trash, recycling, compost, rentals, sponsor materials, florals, food leftovers, signage, carpet, pallets, packaging, and lost-and-found. Decide what is discarded, donated, shipped, stored, returned, or reused. Waste rules may be part of venue contract or sustainability commitments.

Do not leave strike decisions to tired staff at midnight. Label materials and assign owners before the event. For sponsor or exhibitor materials, communicate pickup deadlines and disposal consequences.

### Plan Strike As A Separate Operation

Teardown is not simply setup in reverse. Guests depart, staff fatigue is high, vendors rush, valuables remain, and venue deadlines loom. Sequence strike to protect safety and property: secure cash and valuables, clear guests, remove food, count rentals, power down safely, pack sponsor materials, remove decor, load trucks, clean, and inspect.

Define who can leave when. Some vendors depend on others. If strike crosses overnight or union overtime, include labor and cost. If venue access ends at a hard time, plan backward.

### Coordinate Staff Welfare And Safety

Load-in and strike can involve heavy lifting, ladders, vehicles, forklifts, cables, hot equipment, sharp objects, fatigue, and poor lighting. Plan PPE, meal breaks, water, restrooms, lighting, first aid, radios, and supervisor coverage. Do not allow rushed strike to create injury risk.

If volunteers are involved, assign them appropriate tasks and supervision. Volunteers should not handle high-risk technical, rigging, electrical, or heavy-load work unless trained and authorized.

### Keep A Live Status Board

During setup, track areas, tasks, owners, blockers, deadlines, and readiness. A status board or command sheet lets the event lead see which work is late and what decisions are needed. Verbal updates alone get lost.

Update the run of show and issue log as setup realities change. If a sponsor booth is late or a stage element is cut, front-of-house and programming teams need to know.

## Common Traps

### Giving Every Vendor The Same Arrival Time

Crowding the dock and floor wastes labor. Sequence by dependency.

### Treating Setup Complete As Guest Ready

Readiness requires testing, inspection, cleaning, signage, staff briefing, and safety checks.

### Forgetting Venue Protection

Damage disputes can be expensive. Protect surfaces and document condition.

### Underplanning Strike

Teardown has safety, labor, rental, waste, and venue deadline risks. Plan it as carefully as setup.

### Ignoring Waste And Leftovers

Unassigned materials become trash, cost, or conflict. Decide before event day.

### Blocking Emergency Routes During Build

Load-in clutter must not block exits, fire lanes, or accessible routes.

### Using Volunteers For Risky Work

Untrained helpers should not do electrical, rigging, heavy lifting, or unsafe tasks.

### Losing Status In Verbal Updates

Complex setups need written status and issue tracking.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is setup sequenced by physical dependencies and shared-space constraints?
- [ ] Are dock, freight, vehicle, elevator, security, parking, and staging rules scheduled?
- [ ] Are readiness inspections defined for production, catering, registration, accessibility, safety, and front-of-house?
- [ ] Are venue protection, attachment methods, floor covering, turf, and pre-existing damage documented?
- [ ] Are waste, recycling, donations, rentals, sponsor materials, lost-and-found, and leftovers assigned?
- [ ] Is strike sequenced with owners, deadlines, safety, labor, and venue closeout?
- [ ] Are staff welfare, PPE, breaks, lighting, first aid, and supervision planned?
- [ ] Are volunteers limited to appropriate supervised tasks?
- [ ] Is a live status board or issue log used during setup and strike?
- [ ] Could the team prove the venue was returned correctly and any damage was documented?
