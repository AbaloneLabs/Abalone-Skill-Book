# Backstage Command Center And Radio Protocol

On event day, communication becomes infrastructure. Staff, vendors, security, production, catering, transportation, registration, and leadership all need a shared way to coordinate without flooding each other. Agents often create contact lists but miss channel discipline, decision authority, radio etiquette, command center staffing, issue logging, and emergency escalation. Strong onsite communication gives the event a nervous system: information reaches the right person quickly, decisions are recorded, and teams avoid chaos.

Use this skill when planning command center operations, backstage coordination, radio plans, show calling, or event-day communications.

## Core Rules

### Define The Command Function

A command center is not just a table with radios. It is the place or function that receives information, prioritizes issues, dispatches resources, escalates decisions, and maintains situational awareness. For small events, one experienced lead may perform this role. For large events, a staffed operations center may be needed.

Define who sits in command, what information they track, what decisions they can make, and when they escalate. Include operations, production, security, medical, venue, transportation, registration, and client representation as appropriate.

### Build A Channel Plan

Radio channels should separate workstreams without isolating critical information. Common channels include command, production, security, registration, catering, transportation, facilities, and medical. Small events may use fewer channels; large events need disciplined separation.

Do not put everyone on one channel if traffic will be heavy. Do not create so many channels that teams cannot reach each other. Define who monitors which channel and how cross-channel messages are relayed.

### Establish Radio Etiquette And Message Structure

Radio communication should be concise and clear. Train staff to identify themselves, call the recipient, wait for acknowledgment, state the issue, location, need, and urgency, and avoid unnecessary chatter. Sensitive matters should move to phone or private channel.

Use plain language where possible. Avoid codes that volunteers or vendors may not know unless the event requires them and everyone is trained. For emergencies, clarity matters more than cleverness.

### Define Escalation Levels

Not every issue belongs in command, but some must escalate immediately: injury, fire, severe weather, security threat, missing child, VIP incident, media crisis, overcrowding, power loss, stage failure, alcohol incident, or accessibility failure. Define severity levels and escalation paths.

Staff should know what they can solve locally and what must be reported. A spilled drink does not need executive attention; a blocked fire exit does. Escalation rules prevent both noise and silence.

### Maintain An Issue Log

Command should track material issues: time, location, description, owner, status, decision, cost impact, and closeout. Without a log, problems disappear into radio chatter and cannot be reviewed, billed, or learned from.

The log should be simple enough to maintain under pressure. It supports post-event reporting, vendor accountability, insurance, and improvement.

### Coordinate Backstage And Front-Of-House

Backstage decisions affect guests, and front-of-house conditions affect the show. If registration lines are long, doors may need to hold. If catering is late, the program may need to adjust. If a speaker is missing, front-of-house may need guest messaging.

Create links between stage management, command, registration, catering, security, and host. Do not let production run a perfect stage while guest operations fail outside the room.

### Prepare For Communication Failure

Radios may have dead zones, dead batteries, wrong channels, interference, or insufficient units. Phones may lose signal. Internet-based tools may fail. Test coverage before doors. Plan spare batteries, chargers, check-in points, runners, and backup phone trees.

Assign radio numbers and collect them at end of day. Label devices and train staff before the rush begins.

### Protect Sensitive Information

Radio traffic can be overheard by guests, vendors, media, or unauthorized people. Avoid broadcasting names, medical details, security-sensitive information, celebrity movements, payment issues, or private complaints. Use discreet language and switch to secure channels when needed.

Privacy and reputation are part of communication discipline.

## Common Traps

### Treating A Contact List As A Communication Plan

Phone numbers help, but live operations need channels, monitoring, etiquette, and escalation rules.

### Putting Everyone On One Channel

One busy channel becomes noise. Teams miss critical information.

### Creating Channels No One Monitors

Separate channels only work if responsible people actively listen.

### Letting Radio Chatter Replace Decisions

Discussion is not resolution. Assign owners and log outcomes.

### Sharing Sensitive Details Over Radio

Medical, security, VIP, and personal information should be protected.

### Failing To Test Coverage

Dead zones and battery issues appear at the worst time. Test and provide backups.

### Not Linking Front And Back Of House

Stage, registration, catering, transportation, and security must share key timing changes.

### Leaving Command Unstaffed During Peaks

Command must be strongest during doors, meal transitions, program peaks, and departure.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is the command function defined with roles, authority, and information responsibilities?
- [ ] Is there a radio or communication channel plan sized to event complexity?
- [ ] Are channel monitors assigned and cross-channel relay procedures clear?
- [ ] Are radio etiquette, message format, plain language, and sensitive-information rules briefed?
- [ ] Are escalation levels defined for safety, security, medical, VIP, media, weather, and operational issues?
- [ ] Is a live issue log maintained with owner, status, decision, and closeout?
- [ ] Are backstage, production, front-of-house, registration, catering, transportation, venue, and security connected?
- [ ] Are radio coverage, batteries, chargers, labels, spare units, and backup communication tested?
- [ ] Are privacy and reputation protected in live communications?
- [ ] Could staff know whom to call and what to say during a time-sensitive issue?
