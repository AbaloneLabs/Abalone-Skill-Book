# Day Of Issue Triage And Decision Making

Event day problems are inevitable; uncontrolled decision-making is optional. Late vendors, missing speakers, weather, AV failures, guest complaints, medical incidents, room overcrowding, catering delays, and VIP surprises all compete for attention. Agents often respond to the loudest issue or search for perfect answers while time-sensitive problems grow. Strong day-of triage classifies issues by severity, protects safety and core outcomes first, assigns owners, and records decisions quickly enough for the event to keep moving.

Use this skill when handling onsite issues, event-day escalation, or operational recovery decisions.

## Core Rules

### Triage By Safety, Time, And Event Impact

Not all issues are equal. First identify whether the issue affects life safety, security, accessibility, legal compliance, crowd control, or medical response. Those outrank comfort, aesthetics, and convenience. Next assess time sensitivity: does a decision need to happen in minutes, before doors, before a session, or after the event?

Then assess impact on the event objective. A missing keynote speaker may be more important than minor decor damage; a blocked accessible route may be more important than a sponsor sign placement. Triage prevents the team from solving visible but low-value issues while critical risks go unmanaged.

### Assign One Owner Per Issue

Every material issue needs one owner responsible for next action and status. Many people can help, but one person should carry the issue until closed or handed off. Without ownership, teams assume someone else is acting.

The owner should know decision authority, deadline, resources, and escalation path. The command center or event lead should track status. Avoid group chats where issues are discussed but not owned.

### Use Decision Frames Under Pressure

Fast decisions need a simple frame: What is happening? What is at risk? What options exist? What does each option cost or compromise? Who can approve? By when must we decide? What communication is needed?

For example, if catering is delayed, options may be delaying program, serving snacks, opening bars, adjusting speeches, or moving guests. Each affects cost, guest experience, and schedule. The planner should frame options, not simply report panic.

### Protect The Core Event Objective

When something must give, protect the event's main purpose. If the goal is donor fundraising, preserve the ask and donor hospitality. If the goal is training, preserve learning outcomes and required attendance. If the goal is public safety, reduce program ambition before reducing safety controls.

Use the brief and priorities established before the event. Day-of decisions should not be random; they should reflect what the event was built to achieve.

### Communicate Changes To Affected Teams Only As Needed

Over-communicating every issue creates noise. Under-communicating creates misalignment. Identify who needs to act, who needs awareness, and who should not be distracted. Production, registration, catering, security, transportation, speakers, client leads, and guest services may need different messages.

Guest-facing communication should be calm, accurate, and useful. Do not promise a fix before it is confirmed. If delays affect guests, tell them what to do, where to go, and when to expect updates.

### Document Decisions And Costs

Day-of changes often create costs: overtime, extra rentals, premium delivery, refunds, complimentary service, replacement equipment, or vendor credits. Record who approved the decision, why, time, cost estimate, and follow-up needed.

Documentation supports closeout, invoice review, insurance, vendor accountability, and lessons learned. It also prevents later disputes about why a decision was made.

### Know When To Stop Or Slow The Event

Some issues require stopping, delaying, evacuating, or cancelling part of the event: severe weather, fire alarm, crowd crush risk, medical emergency, structural issue, security threat, power failure, or unsafe stage condition. The planner should know who has authority and what triggers action.

Do not let reputational pressure override safety. A delayed program is better than preventable harm.

### Run A Short Post-Issue Review

After a major issue is stabilized, check whether secondary effects remain. A delayed speaker may affect meal timing and transportation. A power failure may affect registration data. A medical incident may require privacy handling and incident report.

After the event, review major issues for root cause and future prevention. Day-of triage is also a learning system.

## Common Traps

### Responding To The Loudest Person

Urgency and importance differ. Triage by safety, time, and event impact.

### Leaving Issues Without Owners

Broadcasting a problem is not solving it. Assign one owner.

### Hiding Bad News Too Long

Delayed escalation removes options. Surface material issues early with options.

### Overcorrecting Minor Problems

Spending major resources on low-impact issues can harm the core event. Match response to impact.

### Making Guest Promises Before Confirmation

Do not announce fixes, timing, refunds, or replacements until confirmed.

### Ignoring Secondary Effects

One fix may affect catering, transport, staffing, security, or agenda. Check dependencies.

### Forgetting Cost Approval

Emergency spending needs authority and documentation unless life safety requires immediate action.

### Letting Safety Become Negotiable

Unsafe conditions require action even when inconvenient or embarrassing.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Are issues triaged by safety, security, accessibility, compliance, time sensitivity, and event objective impact?
- [ ] Does each material issue have one owner, deadline, and escalation path?
- [ ] Are options framed with cost, compromise, risk, and approval need?
- [ ] Are decisions tied to the event's core objective and priority order?
- [ ] Are affected teams informed without creating unnecessary communication noise?
- [ ] Are guest-facing updates accurate, calm, and actionable?
- [ ] Are day-of decisions, approvals, costs, and follow-up items documented?
- [ ] Are stop, delay, evacuation, and cancellation triggers understood?
- [ ] Are secondary effects checked after the immediate issue is stabilized?
- [ ] Are major issues reviewed after the event for root cause and prevention?
