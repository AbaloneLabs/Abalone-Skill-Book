# Exhibitor Onboarding Deadlines And Service Orders

Exhibitor success depends on a long chain of deadlines and small decisions. A sold booth is not ready until the exhibitor understands rules, orders services, submits certificates, ships freight, registers staff, prepares lead retrieval, and knows onsite procedures. Agents often assume exhibitors will read the manual and self-serve. A stronger event operation treats exhibitor onboarding as guided readiness, especially for first-time or high-value sponsors.

## Core Rules

### Build a deadline map from the service manual

Extract critical dates for booth design approval, insurance certificates, electrical, internet, furniture, carpet, rigging, hanging signs, lead retrieval, badge registration, program listings, freight to advance warehouse, direct-to-site freight, catering, labor orders, and cancellation penalties. Put them in one exhibitor-facing timeline.

Do not rely on a dense service manual as the only communication. Exhibitors miss deadlines when information is buried across contractor portals, PDFs, and sponsor agreements.

### Segment exhibitors by support need

First-time exhibitors, international exhibitors, small sponsors, complex booth builders, healthcare or regulated exhibitors, and major sponsors need different support. Identify exhibitors who need proactive help before deadlines pass.

High-value exhibitors should not discover onsite that they forgot power or ordered the wrong booth package. Support level should match revenue, complexity, and risk.

### Clarify booth package versus extra orders

Exhibitors often confuse what is included with what must be ordered: pipe and drape, table, chairs, carpet, wastebasket, electrical, internet, monitor, lead retrieval, booth cleaning, labor, shipping, storage, and material handling. Provide a plain-language inclusion matrix.

Hidden extras create resentment and onsite service desk overload. Make cost exposure visible early.

### Control exhibitor data and public listings

Collect company name, description, logo, website, booth contacts, onsite contacts, emergency phone, certificate holder, booth staff, dietary needs where relevant, accessibility needs, and public listing information. Proof names and logos before publishing.

Separate public marketing data from operational contacts. An onsite logistics phone should not automatically become public attendee-facing information.

### Manage service order verification

Track whether exhibitors have ordered electrical, internet, furniture, freight, labor, lead retrieval, badges, and insurance. Where possible, get reports from the general contractor and registration provider. Do not assume that an order was completed because an exhibitor said they "handled it."

Create exception reports before the discount deadline and before move-in. Missing orders are cheaper and easier to fix early.

### Use a readiness cadence, not one-time reminders

Exhibitor onboarding should have a cadence: welcome, service manual release, discount deadline reminder, listing proof, badge deadline, freight reminder, final readiness check, and move-in briefing. Track readiness by exhibitor so staff can see who is missing insurance, badges, power, freight, or listings.

This cadence prevents last-minute service desk overload. It also gives sales and sponsor teams time to intervene with important exhibitors before problems become public.

### Explain show rules in operational terms

Rules may cover booth height, sightline, noise, giveaways, food sampling, alcohol, live demos, drones, animals, raffles, fire safety, hanging signs, storage, staffing, teardown timing, and union labor. Translate rules into "what you may do" and "what requires approval."

Exhibitors may bring plans from other shows that do not fit this venue. Rule education prevents conflict on the floor.

### Prepare move-in and onsite support

Exhibitors need arrival instructions, dock access, marshaling, hand-carry rules, labor desk, service desk, badge pickup, Wi-Fi help, lead retrieval help, empty crate labels, and emergency contacts. Provide a pre-show readiness reminder focused on onsite action.

Onsite exhibitor support should have authority paths. Staff at the desk need to know who can approve late orders, exceptions, and dispute resolution.

### Close exhibitor issues after the event

Track complaints, missing services, lead retrieval issues, freight problems, badge disputes, sponsor deliverable gaps, and renewal signals. Send post-show service feedback and reconcile any refunds or credits.

Exhibitor onboarding improves when closeout lessons feed the next manual and communication cadence.

## Common Traps

- Assuming exhibitors read and understand the service manual.
- Communicating deadlines across too many PDFs, portals, and emails.
- Treating all exhibitors as equally capable of self-service.
- Failing to distinguish booth package inclusions from extra-cost orders.
- Publishing unproofed company names, logos, and descriptions.
- Believing service orders are complete without contractor or provider reports; sending one generic reminder instead of tracking exhibitor readiness through multiple deadline gates
- Explaining rules only in legalistic manual language; leaving move-in questions to onsite staff without escalation authority
- Not warning exhibitors before discount deadlines and late-fee dates; failing to capture exhibitor service issues for the next show cycle

## Self-Check

- Is there a single deadline map for all exhibitor orders, approvals, certificates, badges, listings, and freight?
- Are exhibitors segmented by complexity, value, experience, and risk for proactive support?
- Are booth inclusions and extra-cost orders clear in plain language?
- Are public listings, logos, onsite contacts, and operational data collected and proofed appropriately?
- Are service orders verified through reports, not only exhibitor statements?
- Is exhibitor readiness tracked through welcome, discount, listing, badge, freight, and final move-in checkpoints?
- Are booth rules translated into practical permissions and approval requirements?
- Are move-in, hand-carry, dock, service desk, badge, lead retrieval, and empty crate instructions clear?
- Are post-event exhibitor issues captured for refunds, credits, renewal, and next-year improvement?
