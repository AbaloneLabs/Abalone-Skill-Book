# Tent Power Restroom And Site Infrastructure

Outdoor events are temporary facilities. Tents, power, water, restrooms, lighting, flooring, waste, fencing, vendor utilities, and access routes must be designed as an operating site, not a collection of rentals. Agents often list equipment without checking load calculations, ground conditions, permits, ADA routes, generator placement, restroom servicing, emergency access, and load-in sequence. This skill helps plan outdoor infrastructure so the event can actually function safely.

## Core Rules

### Start With Site Conditions And Permissions

Infrastructure depends on site ownership, permits, ground surface, slope, drainage, underground utilities, wind exposure, fire lanes, vehicle access, noise limits, protected areas, neighborhood impact, and local codes. A park, farm, beach, street, rooftop, plaza, and private home each has different constraints.

Before selecting rentals, confirm site rules, permit requirements, load limits, staking permissions, utility locates, fire marshal needs, tent permits, health department rules, alcohol rules, generator rules, and restoration obligations. Do not assume a temporary event can use a site like a blank field.

### Treat Tents As Engineered Structures

Tents must be sized, weighted or staked, permitted, inspected, and rated for wind, rain, occupancy, exits, fire safety, lighting, flooring, HVAC, and sidewalls. Tent failure can be catastrophic. Decoration, hanging loads, lighting, signage, and AV rigging add requirements.

Use qualified tent vendors and local code guidance. Confirm staking or ballast method, wind thresholds, evacuation rules, exits, aisle widths, flame retardancy, emergency lighting, and weather monitoring. Do not treat tent walls as a complete weather solution; they affect airflow, heat, condensation, and egress.

### Calculate Power Loads And Distribution

Temporary power must support catering, refrigeration, lighting, sound, video, registration, POS, Wi-Fi, medical, security, restrooms, heaters, fans, charging, vendor booths, and back-of-house. Underpowered events fail through tripped circuits, food safety risk, dark walkways, and production failures.

Build a power schedule with wattage, voltage, phase, location, operating times, peak loads, cable runs, distribution, grounding, weather protection, generator capacity, fuel plan, and backup. Separate critical loads where possible. Do not let vendors plug in unapproved equipment without load review.

### Place Generators And Utilities For Safety And Service

Generators need ventilation, fuel access, noise control, distance from guests, cable protection, grounding, fire safety, weather protection, and security. Cables need ramps or overhead runs to prevent trips and vehicle damage. Water, greywater, and waste need service access.

Plan utility corridors and service routes. Keep generators away from air intakes, tents, and guest congregation where required. Ensure refueling can occur safely without crossing guest flow. Provide lighting for utility areas used after dark.

### Size Restrooms For Attendance, Duration, Alcohol, And Servicing

Restroom needs depend on attendance, event duration, alcohol service, gender mix, families, accessibility, staff, VIPs, food service, and local requirements. Too few restrooms create lines, complaints, sanitation issues, and accessibility failures. Portable restrooms may require servicing during long events.

Provide accessible units, handwashing or sanitizer, lighting, mirrors where appropriate, baby-changing if relevant, staff or vendor restrooms, and servicing access. Place restrooms near flow but not where odors or lines interfere with food, stages, or ceremonies. Consider weather and night visibility.

### Build Accessible Routes Across The Site

Outdoor sites often have grass, gravel, mud, slopes, curbs, cables, uneven surfaces, and long distances. Accessibility requires routes from parking or drop-off to entry, restrooms, seating, food, bars, stages, medical, and exits. Temporary mats, ramps, cable covers, lighting, and signage may be needed.

Do not claim an outdoor event is accessible because it has one accessible restroom. Test the route under expected weather and crowd conditions. If rain could turn grass to mud, the accessible route may fail without flooring.

### Coordinate Load-In, Vendor Access, And Site Protection

Outdoor infrastructure must be installed in sequence: tents, flooring, power, lighting, AV, catering, fencing, restrooms, signage, decor, vendors, and inspections. Vehicle access can damage turf or block other work. Late deliveries can conflict with guests or safety inspections.

Create a load-in plan with vendor arrival times, vehicle routes, staging, credentials, ground protection, forklift or cart needs, inspection windows, and restoration. For public sites, neighborhood or pedestrian management may be required.

### Plan Waste, Water, Lighting, And Back-Of-House

Temporary sites need waste streams, recycling/compost, greywater, potable water, ice, handwashing, dishwashing, lighting, staff areas, vendor storage, security, lost and found, and cleaning. These basics affect guest satisfaction and health compliance.

Place back-of-house where vendors can work without blocking guest paths. Ensure waste removal and restroom servicing can happen without crossing critical program moments. Lighting should support safety, wayfinding, security, and teardown.

## Common Traps

### Renting Equipment Before Understanding The Site

Permits, staking, ground, access, utilities, and code can change the entire infrastructure plan.

### Treating Tents As Weather Guarantees

Wind, lightning, drainage, heat, exits, and evacuation still matter.

### Guessing Power Needs

Uncalculated loads lead to outages, food safety risk, and production failures.

### Hiding Generators Too Far Away Or Too Close

Distance affects cable loss and service; proximity affects noise, fumes, and safety.

### Under-Sizing Restrooms

Alcohol, duration, family needs, and servicing can change requirements materially.

### Forgetting Accessible Paths

Grass, gravel, mud, slopes, cables, and distance can make a site unusable for some guests.

### Letting Load-In Become First-Come Chaos

Outdoor sites need sequence, access routes, inspections, and ground protection.

### Ignoring Back-Of-House

Vendors need water, waste, storage, lighting, and service access to operate.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Are site permissions, permits, ground, slope, drainage, utilities, fire lanes, and local codes checked?
- [ ] Are tents engineered for size, ballast/staking, wind, exits, fire safety, lighting, sidewalls, and evacuation?
- [ ] Is there a power schedule with loads, timing, distribution, grounding, cable protection, generator capacity, and backup?
- [ ] Are generators, fuel, cables, water, greywater, and utility corridors placed for safety and service?
- [ ] Are restrooms sized for attendance, duration, alcohol, accessibility, staff, servicing, lighting, and handwashing?
- [ ] Are accessible routes continuous from arrival through restrooms, seating, food, stages, medical, and exits?
- [ ] Is load-in sequenced with vendor access, vehicle routes, credentials, ground protection, and inspections?
- [ ] Are waste, recycling, water, ice, lighting, storage, cleaning, and back-of-house needs included?
- [ ] Can servicing happen without disrupting guests or blocking emergency access?
- [ ] Would the site still function if rain, darkness, or a delayed vendor stresses the infrastructure?
