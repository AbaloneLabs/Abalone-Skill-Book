# AV Production Rentals And Technical Delivery

AV production is where an event's promise becomes audible, visible, and technically reliable. It is not just ordering microphones and screens. The correct plan depends on room shape, audience size, content type, presenter behavior, lighting, acoustics, power, rigging, internet, union rules, load-in access, rehearsal time, and failure tolerance. Agents often treat AV as a vendor checklist after the agenda is set. This skill helps the agent notice production constraints early enough to prevent weak sound, unreadable visuals, late setup, unsafe staging, and expensive last-minute rentals.

## Core Rules

### Start With The Experience And Room Conditions

Define what the audience must see, hear, and feel. A board meeting, awards dinner, keynote, panel, training, concert, hybrid town hall, product demo, and fundraising gala all require different technical choices. Audience size alone is not enough. Consider room length, ceiling height, sightlines, ambient light, acoustics, seating layout, camera positions, speaker movement, content detail, and whether attendees need to read small text.

Request floor plans, ceiling plans, rigging limits, power diagrams, house sound details, internet options, load-in path, elevator dimensions, dock access, and venue AV rules. A beautiful room may be technically poor if screens cannot be seen, sound reflects, daylight washes out projection, or the only power is far from stage. Production feasibility should influence venue layout, not simply adapt to it later.

### Translate Agenda Into Technical Requirements

Build the AV plan from the run of show. Each segment should identify speakers, format, microphones, playback, slides, videos, walk-on music, confidence monitors, teleprompter, translation, captions, recording, livestream, lighting looks, camera shots, and transitions. Panels need microphone strategy and stage furniture; awards need stings and winner reveal controls; demos may need network and device switching; training may need audience mics or breakout support.

Do not accept vague requirements like "standard AV." Ask what content will be shown, who controls it, what devices connect, whether audio is embedded, whether presenters move, whether Q&A is live or moderated, and whether any content is confidential. Technical delivery fails when assumptions remain implicit until rehearsal.

### Clarify Vendor Scope And Venue Constraints

Different providers may control house AV, outside production, lighting, staging, rigging, power, internet, decor, translation, livestream, and labor. Define who supplies, installs, operates, tests, strikes, insures, and repairs each element. Confirm exclusivity rules, union labor, required house technicians, corkage or patch fees, overtime, meal breaks, and approval deadlines.

Vendor quotes should be compared by deliverable, not only total cost. Check equipment quality, labor hours, setup time, rehearsal support, standby coverage, backup equipment, project management, CAD or diagram support, and onsite lead authority. A lower quote that omits show labor, cable ramps, backup microphones, confidence monitors, or strike overtime may be more expensive in practice.

### Protect Power, Internet, And Signal Paths

Power and connectivity are production dependencies. Confirm dedicated circuits, generator needs, cable paths, distro, battery backup for critical equipment, load calculations, and who may tie into house power. Avoid placing power and signal cables where they create trip hazards, block accessible routes, or conflict with catering and decor.

Internet-dependent AV requires special review. Livestream, remote speakers, polling, check-in, demos, translation, captioning, video playback from cloud sources, and event apps can fail under shared Wi-Fi. Use wired connections where possible, test bandwidth and latency, define backup media playback, and avoid relying on a presenter's personal hotspot for critical moments.

### Schedule Load-In, Testing, And Rehearsal Realistically

AV needs time for delivery, room access, rigging, cable runs, screen placement, focus, sound check, lighting focus, video testing, presentation loading, presenter rehearsal, and troubleshooting. A room that turns over from lunch to general session may not support a complex production change. Build load-in and rehearsal time into the venue contract and program schedule.

Test from the actual presenter position and audience seats. A microphone that works at the tech table may fail when a speaker turns their head. A slide deck that looks fine on a laptop may be unreadable on a wide screen. A video may play without audio because the signal path was not tested end to end. Rehearsal is not ceremony; it is risk discovery.

### Plan Redundancy For Critical Moments

Identify moments where failure would materially harm the event: keynote audio, CEO video, award reveal, donor ask, product demo, livestream, remote speaker, translation, safety announcement, or music cue. For those, plan redundancy: spare microphones, backup laptops, local video files, duplicate adapters, printed scripts, alternate playback method, backup internet, and a manual show-calling fallback.

Redundancy should be operational, not just available in a case somewhere. Staff should know where backup gear is, who can swap it, and when to abandon the primary method. If a demo is fragile, rehearse the failure path as well as the success path.

## Common Traps

- Ordering "standard AV" without translating the agenda into segment-by-segment technical requirements.
- Choosing screens or projection without checking sightlines, ambient light, ceiling height, and content detail.
- Comparing AV quotes by total price while ignoring labor, rehearsal, backup gear, overtime, and excluded essentials.
- Assuming venue house AV can support livestream, hybrid speakers, captions, demos, or complex show flow.
- Leaving power, internet, and cable paths to be solved during load-in.
- Scheduling rehearsals after the room is already needed for guests, catering, or sponsor setup.
- Relying on cloud video playback or personal devices for critical program moments.
- Forgetting accessible cable management, sightlines, hearing support, and caption or interpretation needs; treating AV vendor arrival as the start of setup while dock access, freight elevator, security, and union rules add time

## Self-Check

- Is the AV plan tied to audience experience, room conditions, and the exact run of show?
- Are microphones, screens, projection, playback, lighting, staging, recording, and support needs defined by segment?
- Have venue constraints, exclusivity rules, union labor, rigging, power, internet, and load-in paths been confirmed?
- Are vendor scopes compared by deliverables, labor, testing, rehearsal, backup, and onsite authority?
- Are power load, cable routing, trip hazards, accessibility, and signal paths planned?
- Is internet dependency minimized or backed up for critical functions?
- Is load-in, technical testing, presenter rehearsal, and troubleshooting time realistic?
- Are content devices, adapters, audio playback, video files, and slide formats tested end to end?
- Are critical moments protected with operational backups and named owners?
- Does the final plan reduce technical uncertainty before guests enter the room?
