# Lyric and Narrative Craft

Lyrics are where songs become specific, and they are also where songwriters most often settle for vagueness that sounds poetic but communicates nothing. The judgment problem is that abstract, general language feels safe and "universal" while writing — "we went through some things," "time passes," "I feel alive" — but it fails to make a listener see, feel, or remember anything, because the listener is given no concrete image or specific emotion to hold. Great lyrics are not vague; they are specific in a way that unlocks universality, the way a single described detail ("the kitchen tile was cold") lets every listener supply their own kitchen. A second failure is the reverse: lyrics so personal and referential that no outsider can decode them. The craft is finding the specific detail that resonates generally, the concrete image that carries the abstract emotion, and the voice that sounds like a person rather than a poem. This skill covers the decisions that govern lyric writing: point of view, imagery and concreteness, rhyme and meter, verse-chorus content division, and the relationship of words to melody and singability.

## Core Rules

### Establish a Clear Point of View and Voice

Before writing lines, decide who is speaking, to whom, and from what moment in time. First person present ("I am standing here"), first person past ("I remember when"), second person direct ("you said"), and third person observational each create different intimacy and distance. The decision is to choose a point of view that serves the song's emotional intent and to maintain it consistently, unless a shift is deliberate. The trap is drifting between perspectives without realizing it, which confuses the listener about who is speaking. The voice should sound like a specific person — with a stance, a vocabulary, a relationship to the subject — not like generic lyrical English. Define the speaker and the situation in a sentence before drafting lines.

### Use Concrete Imagery Instead of Abstract Statements

The most common lyric failure is telling the listener how to feel ("I was so sad," "it was beautiful") instead of showing a concrete image that makes the listener feel it. The decision is to replace abstract emotional language with specific, sensory details: instead of "we were close," describe the physical proximity or shared object that proves the closeness; instead of "I miss you," describe the specific absence. The criterion is the "show, don't tell" test: can the listener see, hear, smell, or touch what you describe? Concrete imagery is more emotionally powerful than abstract statement because it lets the listener inhabit the moment rather than receive a label. When you find yourself writing "I feel X," ask what specific image or action demonstrates X, and write that instead.

### Match Rhyme and Meter to the Song's Emotional Rhythm

Rhyme scheme and metrical rhythm are not decorative; they carry emotional information. Regular rhyme and meter create predictability and comfort; broken or irregular patterns create tension and surprise; internal rhyme and consonance add density. The decision is to choose rhyme and meter that serve the song's emotional rhythm, not to force every line into a predetermined scheme. The trap is sacrificing meaning to complete a rhyme — choosing a word because it rhymes rather than because it is right — which produces lyrics that sound forced and communicate less. Favor slant rhyme and varied meter where strict rhyme would compromise the line, and let the rhythm of the words match the rhythm of the emotion.

### Differentiate Verse and Chorus Content Functionally

Verses and choruses do different narrative work, and confusing them weakens both. Verses advance the story, provide details, and build context; the chorus delivers the central emotional statement, the hook, the thing the song is "about." The decision is to give verses specific, developing content (each verse adds new information or perspective) and to give the chorus the distilled, memorable, repeatable core. The trap is a chorus that is just another verse — developing new information rather than delivering the emotional center — or verses that repeat the chorus's idea without advancing. Test each section: does the verse move the narrative forward, and does the chorus deliver the emotional thesis?

### Ensure Lyrics Are Singable and Phrased for the Melody

Lyrics exist to be sung, and words that read well on the page may fail in performance. The decision is to test lyrics by singing them against the melody, checking that the natural stress of the words aligns with the musical stress, that vowels are singable on sustained notes (open vowels project; closed vowels like "ee" and "oo" are harder to sustain loudly), and that consonant clusters do not create awkward tonguing. The trap is setting important words on weak beats or forcing a word against its natural emphasis ("for-GOT-ten" with stress on the wrong syllable), which sounds awkward and undermines the lyric. Sing every line and revise where the words fight the melody; the lyric and the melody are one instrument.

### Revise Without Losing the Original Spark

First drafts often contain a genuine spark — an emotional truth or striking image — buried under filler and vagueness. The decision is to revise ruthlessly to protect and clarify that spark, cutting lines that do not serve it, sharpening vague language, and tightening meter, while preserving the voice and feeling that made the draft worth saving. The trap is either refusing to revise (treating the first draft as sacred) or revising the life out of the song by smoothing every edge into competence. Identify what the song is really about — its core — and revise everything else in service of that core, cutting what does not serve it even if the lines are individually good.

## Common Traps

### Abstract Language That Sounds Poetic but Says Nothing

The writer uses general emotional words — "feelings," "time," "forever," "heart" — that read as lyrical but create no image. The false signal is that the language sounds poetic. The harm is lyrics that communicate no specific emotion or scene, leaving the listener unmoved because they have nothing concrete to feel. The mechanism is mistaking abstraction for depth. The remedy is concrete, sensory imagery.

### Forcing Rhyme at the Expense of Meaning

A line ends with a word chosen purely because it rhymes, even though it is not the right word. The false signal is that the rhyme scheme is intact. The harm is a lyric that sounds contrived and communicates less, because the forced word breaks the line's truth. The mechanism is prioritizing rhyme pattern over meaning. The fix is slant rhyme, varied scheme, or rewriting the line.

### Drifting Point of View Without Realizing

The song starts in first person, shifts to second person address, then to third person observation, with no deliberate reason. The false signal is that each line works individually. The harm is listener confusion about who is speaking and to whom, which undermines emotional engagement. The mechanism is inattention to perspective consistency. The remedy is to define and maintain the point of view.

### Chorus That Just Adds Another Verse

The chorus introduces new narrative information rather than delivering the emotional center, functioning as a third verse. The false signal is that the chorus has content. The harm is a song without a clear hook or emotional thesis, because the chorus does not distill or repeat the core idea. The mechanism is not differentiating section function. The fix is to make the chorus the distilled thesis and the verses the developing narrative.

### Setting Words Against Their Natural Stress

Important words are placed on weak beats, or words are forced against their spoken emphasis. The false signal is that the words fit the syllable count. The harm is awkward phrasing that sounds wrong even to listeners who cannot articulate why, undermining the lyric's impact. The mechanism is treating lyrics as text rather than as sung sound. The remedy is to sing every line and align word stress with musical stress.

### Over-Revising Until the Spark Is Gone

The writer polishes every line until the lyric is competent but lifeless, having removed the specific, slightly rough element that gave it character. The false signal is that the lyric is "tight." The harm is a song that is correct but forgettable, because the revision killed what made it worth writing. The mechanism is optimizing for flawlessness over truth. The fix is to identify the core spark and protect it through revision.

## Self-Check

- Have I established a clear point of view — who is speaking, to whom, from what moment — and maintained it consistently, with a voice that sounds like a specific person?
- Have I replaced abstract emotional statements with concrete, sensory imagery that lets the listener see, hear, or feel the moment, passing the "show, don't tell" test?
- Do the rhyme scheme and meter serve the song's emotional rhythm, with no lines where meaning was sacrificed to complete a rhyme?
- Does each verse advance the narrative with new information, while the chorus delivers the distilled, repeatable emotional thesis?
- Have I sung every line against the melody, confirming word stress aligns with musical stress, vowels are singable, and consonants do not create awkward phrasing?
- Have I revised to protect the original spark — cutting what does not serve the core, sharpening vague language — without smoothing the life out of the song?
- If a stranger read only the lyrics, would they understand what the song is about and feel something specific, or would they find it vague and generic?
