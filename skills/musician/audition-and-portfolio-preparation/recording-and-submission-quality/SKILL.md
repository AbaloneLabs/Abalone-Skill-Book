# Recording and Submission Quality

A recorded submission — for a school, competition, festival, or job — is often the only evidence a panel will see of your work, and it will be judged alongside submissions from people who will be heard live. The judgment problem is that strong performers produce weak submissions because they treat the recording as an afterthought (a phone recording in a practice room) when panels expect a baseline of audio and visual quality that lets them judge the musicianship rather than struggle through poor capture. At the same time, over-production (heavy editing, pitch correction, unnatural reverb) signals dishonesty and undermines credibility. The agent must capture clean, honest, well-presented recordings that represent the performance accurately, must curate the portfolio to show range without padding, and must meet every technical specification exactly.

## Core Rules

### Capture Clean, Accurate Audio and Video That Represents the Performance

A submission recording's job is to let the panel judge the musicianship, not to impress with production. This means clean audio (a good microphone or stereo pair in a favorable acoustic, appropriate levels, no distortion or noise) and clear video (well-lit, framed to show the performer and instrument, stable camera). The recording should represent the performance honestly — the dynamic range, the tone, the nuance — without flattering compression or reverb that misrepresents the acoustic. A recording that sounds better than the performer (through heavy processing) will be exposed at a live audition; a recording that sounds worse (through poor capture) undersells the performer.

**Decision criteria:** Does the recording capture the performance accurately, with clean audio and clear video? Does it represent the musician's actual sound, without processing that flatters or degrades? Could a panel judge the musicianship fairly from this recording?

### Meet Every Technical Specification Exactly

Submissions have specifications: file format, resolution, audio sample rate, duration limits, accompaniment rules, recording date restrictions, and naming conventions. Failing to meet any specification — submitting an MOV when MP4 is required, exceeding the duration limit, using a recording older than allowed — can disqualify the submission or signal carelessness. Read the specifications carefully, map each to a compliance check, and verify before submitting. Technical non-compliance is the most preventable form of submission failure.

**Decision criteria:** Has every technical specification (format, resolution, sample rate, duration, date, naming) been met exactly? Is there a compliance checklist that has been verified before submission?

### Curate the Portfolio to Show Range Without Padding

A portfolio or demo reel should show range and strength without padding. Include work that demonstrates your best abilities across relevant styles or contexts; exclude weaker work that dilutes the impression. A short, strong portfolio (5-8 minutes of excellent material) outperforms a long, uneven one (20 minutes including mediocre work). Panels have limited time and will judge the weakest included piece as representative. Curate ruthlessly: every included piece should be one you are proud to have represent you. Quality over quantity, always.

**Decision criteria:** Does every piece in the portfolio represent your best work? Is the portfolio long enough to show range but short enough that every piece is strong? Has weak or padding material been excluded?

### Avoid Over-Editing That Signals Dishonesty

Light editing (selecting the best take, trimming silence, balancing levels) is expected and acceptable. Heavy editing (comping the best fragments of many takes into a "perfect" performance, pitch correction, rhythm quantization) produces a recording that no live performance could match and signals to the panel that the recording does not represent real ability. When the panel later hears the performer live (at a callback or admission), the gap between the over-edited recording and reality damages credibility. Edit honestly: the recording should represent a performance you can actually deliver.

**Decision criteria:** Does the editing represent an achievable live performance? Is there comping, pitch correction, or quantization that produces a recording you could not match live? If so, reduce the editing to an honest level.

### Present the Recording and Accompanying Materials Professionally

The recording is part of a submission package that includes program notes, repertoire list, resume, and any required forms. All materials should be professionally presented: clear labeling (performer name, piece, composer, date on the recording file and in the video), accurate program notes, a clean and current resume, and complete forms. Sloppy or incomplete accompanying materials undermine the impression made by a good recording. Treat the package as a professional presentation, because the panel evaluates the whole package, not just the audio.

**Decision criteria:** Are all materials (recording, program notes, resume, forms) professionally presented, complete, and accurate? Is the recording clearly labeled with all required information?

### Record in a Favorable but Honest Acoustic

The recording space affects the result profoundly. A dead, dry room makes any performance sound flat; a reverberant space flatters but can obscure detail. Choose a space with a favorable but honest acoustic — supportive but not masking. For classical submissions, a recital hall or chapel acoustic is often ideal; for jazz or contemporary, a drier but not dead space. Avoid spaces with obvious problems (HVAC noise, traffic, poor isolation). The space is part of the capture; choose it deliberately.

**Decision criteria:** Was the recording made in a space with a favorable, honest acoustic, free of noise and unfavorable reflections? Does the space support the performance without masking it?

## Common Traps

### Phone or Practice-Room Recordings That Undersell the Performance

A smartphone recording in a dead practice room captures neither the tone nor the dynamic range of the performance, making a strong performer sound weak. The trap mechanism is that the recording "captures" the performance, so it seems sufficient. The harm is submissions that undersell ability and lose to better-captured competitors. The corrective is using adequate microphones and a favorable acoustic.

### Technical Non-Compliance With Specifications

Submitting the wrong format, exceeding duration, or violating recording-date rules can disqualify an otherwise strong submission. The trap mechanism is that the music is what matters. The harm is disqualification or a carelessness signal. The corrective is a compliance checklist verified before submission.

### Padding the Portfolio With Weak Material

Including weaker pieces to fill out a portfolio dilutes the impression, because the panel judges the weakest included work as representative. The trap mechanism is that more material feels more comprehensive. The harm is a portfolio that appears uneven or mediocre. The corrective is ruthless curation for quality.

### Over-Editing to a "Perfect" but Dishonest Recording

Heavy comping and correction produce a recording that cannot be matched live, damaging credibility when the performer is heard in person. The trap mechanism is that the edited recording sounds flawless. The harm is a credibility gap at callbacks or admission. The corrective is honest editing that represents an achievable performance.

### Sloppy or Incomplete Accompanying Materials

A good recording undermined by missing program notes, an outdated resume, or incomplete forms signals unprofessionalism. The trap mechanism is that the recording is the main thing. The harm is a negative overall impression. The corrective is a complete, professional package.

### Recording in an Unfavorable Acoustic

A dead, dry room or a noisy, reverberant space makes any performance sound worse than it is. The trap mechanism is that the room seems fine to the performer. The harm is a recording that undersells the performance. The corrective is choosing a favorable, honest acoustic.

## Self-Check

- Does the recording capture the performance accurately, with clean audio and clear video that let the panel judge the musicianship?
- Does the submission meet every technical specification (format, resolution, duration, date, naming) exactly, verified by a checklist?
- Does the portfolio show range with every piece representing your best work, with weak material excluded?
- Is the editing honest, representing an achievable live performance without heavy comping or correction?
- Are all accompanying materials (program notes, resume, forms) professionally presented, complete, and accurate?
- Was the recording made in a favorable, honest acoustic, free of noise and unfavorable reflections?
- Could a panel fairly judge your musicianship from this submission, and could you match it in a live setting?
