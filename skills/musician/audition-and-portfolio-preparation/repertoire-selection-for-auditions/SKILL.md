# Repertoire Selection for Auditions

Audition repertoire selection is a strategic decision that determines whether a performer's abilities are accurately represented to a panel under severe time constraints. The judgment problem is that musicians often choose repertoire they love or that is impressive on paper rather than repertoire that showcases their specific strengths within the audition's format and time limits, with the result that strong performers underperform in auditions because their selections hide their best qualities or expose their worst. A panel hears a small sample and generalizes; the performer's job is to make sure that sample is maximally favorable. The agent must know their own strengths honestly, must match selections to the specific audition's requirements and panel expectations, and must prioritize clean, confident execution over ambitious risk.

## Core Rules

### Choose Repertoire That Showcases Your Strengths, Not Your Aspirations

An audition is not the place to demonstrate range at the cost of quality. The panel generalizes from a small sample, so every piece should present you at your best. If your strength is lyrical phrasing, choose repertoire that features it; if your strength is virtuosic command, choose repertoire that displays it. Avoid pieces that expose a known weakness, even if they are impressive on paper. An ambitious piece played imperfectly scores lower than a simpler piece played beautifully. Honesty about your strengths — and the discipline to choose for them — is the foundation of audition success.

**Decision criteria:** Does each piece showcase a genuine strength? Are any pieces included for ambition or impressiveness rather than because you can execute them beautifully? Could you perform each piece at your best under pressure?

### Match Repertoire Precisely to the Audition Requirements

Every audition has requirements: specified pieces, era or style requirements, time limits, accompaniment rules, and memorization expectations. Failing to match these — bringing a piece outside the required era, exceeding the time limit, requiring accompaniment when none is provided — signals unprofessionalism and may disqualify. Read the requirements carefully, map each requirement to a specific selection, and verify that the program as a whole complies. When requirements are open, choose strategically within the freedom; when they are specific, follow them exactly.

**Decision criteria:** Does every piece meet a specified requirement? Does the program comply with time limits, era/style requirements, and accompaniment/memorization rules? Have the requirements been read and mapped precisely?

### Program for Contrast Within the Time Limit

A strong audition program demonstrates range through contrast: different eras, styles, tempi, and characters, within the time limit. A program of four fast, virtuosic pieces shows one dimension; a program that includes lyrical, virtuosic, rhythmic, and introspective work shows a complete musician. Contrast also keeps the panel engaged. But contrast must fit the time limit — do not cram so many pieces that none is fully presented. Choose fewer pieces with clear contrast over many pieces rushed. The panel remembers contrast and character, not quantity.

**Decision criteria:** Does the program offer contrast in era, style, tempo, and character? Is the number of pieces appropriate to the time limit, with each fully presented rather than rushed?

### Prioritize Clean, Confident Execution Over Ambitious Risk

The panel rewards control, musicality, and confidence more than raw difficulty. A piece played cleanly and musically at a slightly lower difficulty level outperforms a harder piece with errors and strain. Assess each candidate piece honestly: can you perform it reliably, under pressure, with musicality? If the answer is not a confident yes, choose something you can perform at your best. Ambition that produces errors costs more than it gains. The exception is when a specific audition rewards virtuosity explicitly; even then, the execution must be secure.

**Decision criteria:** For each piece, can you perform it reliably and musically under pressure? Is there any risk of significant errors that would undermine the impression? When in doubt, choose the piece you can perform most securely.

### Build the Program Around Opening and Closing Impressions

Panels remember the first and last pieces most. Open with something that immediately establishes your command and musicality — confident, flattering, and within secure execution. Close with something memorable that leaves a strong final impression. Place the riskiest or most exposed material in the middle, where it is bracketed by stronger impressions. The ordering of the program is a strategic choice that shapes the panel's overall impression. Do not leave the strongest piece for an uncertain later slot or open with the weakest.

**Decision criteria:** Does the program open with a strong, secure, flattering piece and close memorably? Is the riskiest material placed in the middle, bracketed by stronger pieces?

### Prepare Backup and Alternate Repertoire for Contingencies

Auditions have contingencies: the panel may ask for a specific piece from a list, cut the program short, request a different style, or (for some auditions) ask for sight-reading. Prepare backup repertoire that covers likely requests, and have the full required list ready even if you expect to play only part of it. For competitions and festivals, prepare more repertoire than you expect to perform. Arriving with only the minimum, unprepared for the panel's requests, forfeits opportunities. Over-prepare within the realistic scope.

**Decision criteria:** Is backup and alternate repertoire prepared for likely panel requests? Is the full required list ready even if only part is expected to be performed? Are you prepared for contingencies (cuts, style requests, sight-reading)?

## Common Traps

### Choosing Impressive-on-Paper Repertoire That Exposes Weaknesses

Selecting a famous, difficult piece to seem impressive, when it exposes a technical or musical weakness, produces errors that score lower than a simpler piece played well. The trap mechanism is that the piece feels impressive on the resume. The harm is a poor audition that misrepresents your ability. The corrective is choosing for strengths and secure execution.

### Ignoring or Misreading Audition Requirements

Bringing repertoire outside the required eras or styles, exceeding time limits, or failing memorization/accompaniment rules signals carelessness and may disqualify. The trap mechanism is that the requirements seem secondary to the playing. The harm is disqualification or a negative professionalism impression. The corrective is precise requirement matching.

### Programming Without Contrast

A program of similar pieces shows one dimension and loses the panel's engagement. The trap mechanism is that each piece is strong individually. The harm is a program that fails to demonstrate range. The corrective is contrast in era, style, tempo, and character.

### Prioritizing Difficulty Over Execution

Choosing the hardest piece you can almost play, rather than a piece you can play beautifully, produces errors that undercut the impression. The trap mechanism is that difficulty feels like a stronger statement. The harm is a lower score than secure execution would earn. The corrective is prioritizing clean, confident, musical performance.

### Weak Opening or Closing Choices

Opening with an exposed or risky piece, or closing with something forgettable, wastes the panel's strongest memory positions. The trap mechanism is that ordering feels less important than selection. The harm is a weaker overall impression. The corrective is strategic opening and closing.

### Arriving With Only the Minimum Repertoire

Bringing only the pieces you expect to perform, with no backup, forfeits opportunities when the panel requests more. The trap mechanism is that the minimum feels sufficient. The harm is inability to respond to panel requests. The corrective is over-preparing within scope.

## Self-Check

- Does each piece in the program showcase a genuine strength, with none included for ambition over secure execution?
- Does the program match every audition requirement (specified pieces, eras, time limits, accompaniment, memorization)?
- Does the program offer contrast in era, style, tempo, and character, within the time limit?
- Can each piece be performed reliably and musically under pressure, with no significant risk of errors?
- Does the program open strongly and close memorably, with the riskiest material in the middle?
- Is backup and alternate repertoire prepared for likely panel requests and contingencies?
- Could you explain, for each piece, why it is in the program and what it demonstrates about your musicianship?
