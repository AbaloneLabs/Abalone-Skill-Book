# Part Preparation for Performers

A performer's part is their only interface to the music during a session or concert. Unlike the conductor's full score, the part shows only their line, and it must contain everything they need to play their role without consulting anyone else. The judgment problem is that parts are often extracted from the score as an afterthought, missing the cues, markings, transposition details, and practical information that a player needs under pressure. A poorly prepared part costs studio time, causes wrong notes, and erodes the performer's confidence in the music director. A well-prepared part lets the player focus on music-making rather than navigation. The agent must prepare parts from the performer's perspective, anticipating what they will need to know and when.

## Core Rules

### Include Cues From Other Parts at Rests and Long Passages

A player who has 20 measures of rest needs to know when to re-enter, and counting measures alone is error-prone, especially in complex meters or when the conductor's beat is hard to follow. Cues — snippets of other instruments' lines notated in the part — allow the player to track where they are and enter confidently. Cues should be placed at significant rests and before entries, should be clearly labeled with the instrument they represent, and should be sized smaller than the player's own notes to avoid confusion. The absence of cues is the single most common cause of missed entries.

**Decision criteria:** For every rest of more than a few measures, does the part include cues that allow the player to follow the music and anticipate their entry? Are cues labeled and visually distinct from the player's own line?

### Provide Clear Rehearsal Marks and Measure Numbers Throughout

Rehearsal marks (letters or numbers at structural points) and measure numbers are the addressing system that lets the conductor say "from letter C" and have everyone find it instantly. Every part must have rehearsal marks at all structural divisions and measure numbers at the start of every system (and ideally every measure in complex music). Without these, rehearsal becomes a slow hunt for "the place after the thing." Rehearsal marks must align exactly across the score and all parts.

**Decision criteria:** Are rehearsal marks present at all structural divisions and aligned across score and parts? Are measure numbers visible at the start of every system, allowing instant navigation to any point?

### Ensure Transposition Is Correct and Clearly Indicated

Transposing instruments (B-flat trumpet, F horn, A clarinet, etc.) read from parts written in their transposition, not concert pitch. A part must be correctly transposed for the instrument it is written for, and the instrument name and transposition must be clearly indicated at the top of the part. Errors in transposition produce wrong notes that may not be caught until the session. Double-check every transposing part against the concert-pitch score, and verify that the key signature and accidentals reflect the correct transposition.

**Decision criteria:** For every transposing part, has the transposition been verified against the concert score? Is the instrument and transposition clearly labeled? Are there any transposition errors that would produce wrong pitches?

### Add Practical Performance Markings the Player Will Need

Beyond the notes, a part should include markings that aid performance: bowings for strings (in consultation with the concertmaster or section), breath marks for winds, fingering suggestions where helpful, mute changes with adequate warning, instrument changes (e.g., "to piccolo") with time to switch, and page turns placed at rests. These markings reflect collaboration with the performers and respect their practical needs. Omitting them forces the player to make decisions under time pressure or to interrupt the session to ask.

**Decision criteria:** Does the part include bowings, breath marks, mute and instrument change warnings, and feasible page turns? Were practical markings developed in consultation with the relevant performers where possible?

### Verify That Score and Parts Match Exactly

A common and serious error is divergence between the conductor's score and the individual parts — a dynamic in the score missing from the part, an articulation in the part not in the score, a pitch or rhythm edited in one but not the other. These divergences cause the conductor and players to disagree about what is being played, wasting rehearsal time and undermining authority. After extracting parts, systematically verify that every pitch, rhythm, dynamic, articulation, and mark in the score appears identically in the corresponding part.

**Decision criteria:** Has every part been checked against the score for exact correspondence of pitches, rhythms, dynamics, articulations, and marks? Are there any divergences between score and parts?

### Organize and Label the Part Folder for the Contractor and Session

Parts must be organized so that the contractor or music director can distribute them efficiently and each player finds their part instantly. This means each part labeled with the instrument (and doubling, e.g., "Flute 1 / Piccolo"), the project name, the composer/arranger, and a part number. The folder should be organized by instrument in a consistent order, with a master list. For sessions, each player's part should be in a folder or bound to prevent page chaos. Disorganization at the session costs time and signals unprofessionalism.

**Decision criteria:** Is every part clearly labeled with instrument, project, composer, and part number? Is the folder organized with a master list so any part can be found instantly? Are parts bound or foldered to prevent page disarray?

## Common Traps

### Extracting Parts Without Adding Cues at Rests

Software can extract a part from a score automatically, but it will not add cues at rests unless told to. The trap mechanism is that the extraction produces a part that looks complete, so the missing cues go unnoticed. The harm is missed entries during the session, with players counting long rests and losing their place. The corrective is manually adding cues at every significant rest before the session.

### Rehearsal Marks or Measure Numbers Missing or Misaligned

If rehearsal marks are in the score but did not transfer to the parts, or if they are at different measures in different parts, the conductor's "from letter C" sends players to different places. The trap mechanism is that the marks seem to be present but are inconsistent. The harm is wasted rehearsal time and confusion. The corrective is verifying that rehearsal marks and measure numbers are present and aligned in every part and the score.

### Transposition Errors in Transposing Instrument Parts

A B-flat trumpet part written at concert pitch, or an F horn part transposed to the wrong key, produces wrong notes that may not be caught until the session, when fixing them costs studio time. The trap mechanism is that the part looks like notation and the error is invisible without checking against the concert score. The harm is wrong pitches in the recording or performance. The corrective is verifying every transposing part against the concert score.

### Divergence Between Score and Parts After Edits

When a change is made to the score after parts were extracted, the parts may not be updated, or vice versa. The trap mechanism is that the change feels complete in one place. The harm is the conductor and players disagreeing about what to play. The corrective is a discipline that any edit updates both score and parts, followed by a verification pass.

### Delivering Loose, Unlabeled Parts to a Session

A stack of unbound, unlabeled parts handed to a contractor at the session guarantees confusion and lost time as players sort out whose part is whose. The trap mechanism is that the parts are "done" in the engraver's mind. The harm is an unprofessional impression and session time lost to logistics. The corrective is organizing, labeling, and binding parts before delivery.

## Self-Check

- Does every part include cues at significant rests so players can track their entries?
- Are rehearsal marks and measure numbers present in every part and aligned exactly with the score?
- Has every transposing part been verified against the concert-pitch score for correct transposition?
- Does every part include practical markings (bowings, breath marks, mute/instrument changes, feasible page turns)?
- Have score and parts been verified to match exactly in pitches, rhythms, dynamics, articulations, and marks?
- Is every part labeled with instrument, project, composer, and part number, and is the folder organized with a master list?
- Are parts bound or foldered so they will not disarray during the session?
