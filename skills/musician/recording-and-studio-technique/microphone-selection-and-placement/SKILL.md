# Microphone Selection and Placement

The microphone is where the recording is made or lost, and no amount of mixing can fully recover a signal that was captured wrong. The judgment problem is that engineers often reach for a familiar mic by habit and position it by visual guess, then spend hours in mixing trying to fix problems — mud, harshness, bleed, lack of body — that were created at the source. Microphone choice and placement are decisions about what sound to capture before any processing, and they interact with the source's frequency content, the room's acoustics, and the other instruments playing simultaneously. A mic that is perfect for a bright vocalist will sound harsh on a dark one; a placement that captures a full drum sound in a dead room will sound boomy in a live room. This skill covers the decisions that govern capturing sound: microphone type and pattern selection, placement distance and angle, phase relationships in multi-mic setups, and the management of bleed and room interaction.

## Core Rules

### Match the Microphone's Character to Complement or Contrast the Source

Every microphone has a sonic signature: frequency response, transient response, and self-noise. Condensers generally capture detail and high-frequency air; dynamics handle high sound pressure and offer a colored, robust character; ribbons offer a smooth, natural high end with a figure-eight pattern. The decision is to choose a mic whose character complements the source: a dark source (low-mid-heavy voice, warm amp) may benefit from a brighter mic to add clarity; a bright or harsh source may need a darker, smoother mic to tame edge. The trap is using the "best" or most expensive mic by default, which may be the wrong tool for a given source. Listen to the source first, identify what it needs (more body, less harshness, more detail), and choose the mic that provides it.

### Choose the Polar Pattern for the Capture and Rejection You Need

A microphone's polar pattern determines what it captures and what it rejects. Cardioid captures the front and rejects the rear, useful for isolating a source and managing proximity effect. Omnidirectional captures equally from all directions, offering the most natural sound and no proximity effect but no rejection. Figure-eight captures front and rear while rejecting the sides, useful for two facing sources or for room capture. The decision criterion is the balance between capturing the desired source and rejecting unwanted sound (bleed from other instruments, room reflections, monitors). In a live room with multiple players, choose patterns and angles that reject the other sources; in an isolated booth, an omni may capture a more natural sound. Decide the pattern based on the acoustic environment, not habit.

### Use Distance and Angle to Shape Frequency and Room Balance

Mic distance and angle are the most powerful and underused placement variables. Moving a mic closer increases proximity effect (bass boost on directional mics) and intimacy while reducing room sound; moving it away adds room ambience and natural balance but reduces presence and isolation. Angling the mic off-axis reduces high-frequency harshness and can tame a bright source; on-axis placement maximizes clarity and high end. The decision is to move the mic — not reach for EQ — to shape the captured tone. Walk the room with one ear covered to find where the source sounds balanced, then place the mic there. For instruments, small changes in angle and distance dramatically change the captured timbre: a few inches toward the bridge of a guitar darkens the sound; a few inches toward the sound hole adds boom.

### Check Phase Coherence in All Multi-Microphone Setups

Whenever two or more microphones capture related sound (top and bottom of a snare, close and room mics, multiple drum overheads), their signals interact based on timing differences. If the mics are at different distances from the source, the waveforms arrive at slightly different times, causing phase cancellation that hollows out frequencies — often a thin, weak sound lacking body. The decision is to manage phase deliberately: use the three-to-one rule (place mics at least three times as far apart as they are from the source), align mic distances where possible, and check phase by summing to mono and listening for cancellations. Flip polarity on individual mics and keep whichever setting produces fuller low end. Never assume multi-mic setups are phase-coherent; verify by ear and measurement.

### Manage Bleed Deliberately, Not as an Accident

Bleed — sound from other sources entering a mic intended for one source — is sometimes a problem and sometimes an asset. The decision is whether bleed serves the recording or harms it. In a tight, controlled studio recording, bleed is usually minimized through isolation, pattern choice, and placement. In a live-in-the-room recording, bleed contributes to the cohesive, energetic sound and is embraced. The criterion is intentionality: bleed that is planned and consistent becomes part of the sound; bleed that is unplanned and variable muddies the mix and limits processing options. Decide your bleed strategy for the session and execute it consistently, rather than letting it happen by default.

### Audition the Mic in Context, Not in Isolation

A mic that sounds great on a soloed source may disappear or clash in the full mix. The decision is to evaluate mic choice and placement in the context of the arrangement, not just on the isolated track. A guitar tone that is full and warm alone may mask the vocal in the mix; a snare sound that is punchy alone may get lost under cymbals. The criterion is how the captured sound fits the sonic space the arrangement creates. When possible, make final mic decisions during a full-band playback or reference mix, and adjust placement based on how the source occupies its frequency and dynamic space relative to the other instruments.

## Common Traps

### Reaching for the Same Mic Out of Habit

The engineer uses the same vocal mic on every singer because it worked once. The false signal is that the mic is "good." The harm is that the mic's fixed character complements some voices and fights others; a bright mic on a bright singer sounds harsh, and a dark mic on a dark singer sounds muddy. The mechanism is substituting familiarity for source-appropriate choice. The remedy is to listen to the source first and choose the mic that complements its character.

### Fixing Placement Problems With EQ

The captured sound is muddy or harsh, so the engineer reaches for EQ to carve it. The false signal is that EQ "fixes" the tone. The harm is that EQ cannot restore frequencies that were never captured or remove problems baked into the phase and room interaction; heavy EQ often introduces phase shift and artifacts that degrade the signal. The mechanism is treating capture as fixed and processing as the solution. The remedy is to move the mic first and reserve EQ for fine-tuning.

### Ignoring Phase in Multi-Mic Setups

Two mics are placed at different distances, and the engineer does not check phase because each mic sounds fine alone. The false signal is that individual tracks sound acceptable. The harm is that summed to mono — or even in stereo — the tracks cancel, producing a thin, hollow sound that no amount of EQ fixes. The mechanism is evaluating tracks individually rather than in combination. The fix is to check phase coherence by summing to mono and adjusting distance or polarity.

### Placing the Mic Where It Looks Right Rather Than Where It Sounds Right

The mic is positioned by visual convention — a foot from the singer, on-axis to the amp grill — without listening to the result at different positions. The false signal is that the placement follows a standard. The harm is a captured tone that is merely acceptable rather than optimal, because the best position varies with the source, the room, and the desired character. The mechanism is trusting convention over listening. The remedy is to move the mic while monitoring and choose by ear.

### Letting Bleed Happen by Default

In a room with multiple players, the engineer places mics without considering bleed, then discovers in mixing that the drum bleed into the vocal mic cannot be separated. The false signal is that close placement "isolates" the source. The harm is uncontrolled bleed that muddies the mix and prevents processing, because the bleed is inconsistent and variable. The mechanism is treating bleed as a passive consequence rather than a deliberate decision. The fix is to decide the bleed strategy and use pattern, angle, and placement to execute it.

### Evaluating Mic Choice Soloed Rather Than in the Mix

The engineer chooses a mic based on how the soloed track sounds, then finds the source disappears or clashes in the full mix. The false signal is that a great soloed sound is a great mixed sound. The harm is re-recording or heavy processing to make the track fit, when a different mic or placement would have fit from the start. The mechanism is optimizing for isolation rather than integration. The remedy is to evaluate mic decisions in the context of the arrangement.

## Self-Check

- Did I choose the microphone based on how its character complements or contrasts the specific source, rather than by habit or cost?
- Did I select the polar pattern based on the acoustic environment and the needed balance of capture versus rejection, rather than defaulting to cardioid?
- Did I use distance and angle to shape the captured tone, moving the mic to find the best sound before reaching for EQ?
- In every multi-mic setup, did I check phase coherence by summing to mono, applying the three-to-one rule, and verifying polarity for fullest low end?
- Did I decide the bleed strategy deliberately for the session — minimizing or embracing — and execute it consistently through pattern, angle, and placement?
- Did I evaluate the final mic choices in the context of the full arrangement, confirming each source occupies its space well rather than just sounding good soloed?
- If I solo the captured tracks, do they sound like the source in the room, and do they fit together without heavy corrective processing?
