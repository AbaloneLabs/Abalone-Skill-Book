# Co-Writing and Credit Negotiation

Co-writing is where creative collaboration and legal ownership collide. A song written by two or more people is jointly owned from the moment it is fixed, and the splits agreed (or not agreed) during the session determine who is paid and credited forever after. The judgment problem is that the creative energy of a writing session feels incompatible with the cold transactional conversation about splits, so the conversation is deferred, and deferred conversations become disputes when the song earns money. The agent must learn to hold both the creative openness of collaboration and the discipline of clear credit agreement, and must understand that "who gets credit" is governed by contribution and custom, not by goodwill or who spoke first.

## Core Rules

### Agree on Splits Before or Immediately After the Session, Not Later

The single most important rule is to determine and document songwriting splits while all contributors are present and the memory of who did what is fresh. The industry standard is that splits are decided at the end of the session and written on a split sheet. Deferring "until we see how the song does" sounds fair but creates maximum conflict, because once money is on the table, memories diverge and goodwill evaporates. A split decided in good faith at the session is almost always respected; a split negotiated after success is almost always contested.

**Decision criteria:** Before leaving any co-writing session, confirm and document the splits in writing (a split sheet signed or acknowledged by all contributors). If splits are genuinely deferred, document the deferral and the agreed process for deciding later.

### Understand the Distinction Between Songwriting and Other Contributions

Songwriting credit (the composition) is traditionally earned by contributing to the melody, lyrics, chord progression, or fundamental structure of a song — the elements that define the underlying work. Production choices, arrangement, instrumentation, and performance are generally not songwriting contributions under standard practice, though some producers negotiate a writing credit as part of their deal. The trap is conflating "I contributed to the recording" with "I am a songwriter." The line is not always sharp, especially in beat-driven genres where the track is the song, but the distinction matters enormously for revenue.

**Decision criteria:** For each contributor, identify whether their contribution was to the composition (melody, lyric, harmony, structure) or to the production/arrangement/performance. Credit composition contributors as songwriters; compensate others through production fees, points, or master ownership as appropriate.

### Match Split Logic to Genre Custom and to the Specific Session

Split conventions vary by genre and context. In Nashville and traditional pop, even splits among all writers in the room is common regardless of individual contribution. In hip-hop and electronic music, the producer/beatmaker often takes a substantial or majority split. In some contexts, the top-line writer (melody and lyric) takes the majority. There is no universal rule. The agent must know the custom of the genre and the expectations set before the session, and must clarify if conventions differ among the writers in the room. Assuming your convention is universal is a fast route to resentment.

**Decision criteria:** Before or at the start of a session, confirm the split convention in play (even splits, contribution-weighted, producer-majority, etc.). If writers come from different genre traditions, surface and resolve the difference explicitly.

### Document Everything in Writing With a Split Sheet

Memory is unreliable and goodwill is not a contract. A split sheet — a simple document listing the song title, all writers, their publisher and PRO (performing rights organization) information, and the agreed percentage splits — is the protection against future disputes. It should be signed or explicitly acknowledged by all writers. This document is what publishers, administrators, and PROs use to register and pay the song. Without it, registration is delayed and disputes are unresolvable.

**Decision criteria:** For every co-written song, does a completed split sheet exist with all writers' names, publisher/PRO info, and agreed percentages, acknowledged by all parties? If not, the song is not properly documented.

### Address Producer Credits and Points Explicitly

Producers may be owed a production credit on the master, a royalty "point" (a percentage of master revenue), and sometimes a songwriting credit. These are separate considerations that must be negotiated explicitly. A producer who "helped with the melody" may deserve a songwriting split; a producer who only shaped the sound may deserve a fee and points but no writing credit. Conflating these or leaving them vague leads to disputes at release. The producer agreement should be in writing before the master is exploited.

**Decision criteria:** For any producer involvement, confirm in writing: production fee, any master points/royalty, and whether any songwriting credit is included. Do not release a track with producer terms undefined.

### Register the Song With the Correct PROs and Publishers

Once splits are agreed and documented, the song must be registered with each writer's performing rights organization (ASCAP, BMI, SESAC, PRS, GEMA, etc.) and with the publishers. Registration is what generates performance and mechanical royalties. Unregistered songs earn nothing regardless of the splits agreed. Each writer (or their publisher) registers their own share. Errors or omissions in registration cause revenue to go uncollected or to the wrong party.

**Decision criteria:** After splits are documented, confirm each writer has registered their share with their PRO and that publisher information is correct. Verify registration before release so royalties flow from day one.

## Common Traps

### Deferring the Splits Conversation Because It Feels Awkward

The creative warmth of a good writing session makes raising money and credit feel crass, so writers defer the conversation. The trap mechanism is that deferral feels like preserving goodwill, but it actually destroys it, because the conversation becomes harder and more contested the longer it waits. The harm is that a song that succeeds becomes a source of litigation and broken relationships. The corrective is treating the split conversation as a normal, expected part of the session, not an intrusion.

### Assuming Even Splits Is the Universal Default

Writers from a Nashville even-split tradition may assume all co-writes are split evenly, while writers from a producer-driven tradition assume the beatmaker takes the majority. When these assumptions collide silently, the result is a dispute. The trap is that each writer believes their convention is obvious and universal. The harm is resentment when splits are finally discussed and assumptions differ. The corrective is surfacing the convention explicitly at the start of every session with writers from different backgrounds.

### Conflating Production Contribution With Songwriting Contribution

A producer who improved the arrangement, suggested a drum sound, or even rewrote a bridge may believe they deserve a songwriting credit, but under standard practice these are production contributions unless they contributed to the core composition. The trap mechanism is that the contribution feels significant (it changed the song), so the producer expects credit. The harm is inflated writer counts that dilute the actual writers' revenue and create registration disputes. The corrective is clearly distinguishing composition contribution from production contribution and compensating each appropriately.

### Relying on Verbal Agreements and Memory

A handshake agreement about splits, made in the heat of a session, is relied upon until one party's memory differs — and memories always differ once money is involved. The trap is that verbal feels sufficient among friends. The harm is an unresolvable dispute because there is no record. The corrective is the split sheet, signed or acknowledged in writing, before the session ends.

### Failing to Register the Song With PROs After Agreeing Splits

Even with a perfectly agreed and documented split, if the song is not registered with the writers' PROs, no performance or mechanical royalties are collected. The trap mechanism is that the split sheet feels like the finish line, so registration is forgotten. The harm is songs that earn nothing despite being released and streamed. The corrective is treating PRO registration as a required step before release, with each writer confirming their share is registered.

## Self-Check

- Were songwriting splits discussed and documented (split sheet) before or immediately after the session, while all contributors were present?
- For each contributor, did I distinguish composition contribution (songwriting) from production/arrangement/performance contribution?
- Was the split convention (even, weighted, producer-majority) explicitly confirmed, especially with writers from different genre traditions?
- Does a completed split sheet exist with all writers' names, publisher/PRO info, and agreed percentages, acknowledged by all parties?
- For any producer involvement, are production fee, master points, and any songwriting credit documented in writing?
- Has each writer registered their share with their PRO, and has registration been verified before release?
- Could every writer in the session, asked independently today, name the agreed splits and confirm they agree?
