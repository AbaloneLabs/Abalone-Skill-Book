# Mixing for Picture and Loudness Targets

Mixing for picture is fundamentally different from mixing music because it must serve the dialogue first, integrate music and effects around it, and meet precise loudness and dynamic range specifications set by delivery platforms. Agents advising on post-production mixing often apply music mixing instincts, maximizing loudness and fullness. But picture mixing prioritizes clarity, dynamic range, and spec compliance over loudness, and failing to meet specs can cause rejection of the deliverable. This skill covers the judgment needed to mix for picture and hit loudness targets.

## Core Rules

### Prioritize the Dialogue in the Mix Hierarchy

In picture mixing, dialogue is king. Music and effects must be balanced around the dialogue so it is always clear, ducking under it and rising only when the voice is absent. Recommend structuring the mix so dialogue sits at a consistent, clear level and everything else is balanced relative to it, because a picture mix where the music or effects compete with dialogue fails its primary purpose of delivering the voice.

### Understand and Hit the Delivery Loudness Specification

Every delivery platform, broadcast television, streaming services, cinema, has specific loudness specifications measured in integrated loudness, typically around negative twenty-three to negative twenty-seven LUFS depending on the platform, and true peak limits. Recommend identifying the target specification before mixing and monitoring loudness throughout, because a mix that does not meet spec is rejected or is automatically normalized by the platform, often sounding worse than intended.

### Manage Dynamic Range for the Delivery Context

Different delivery contexts expect different dynamic ranges: cinema allows wide dynamics; broadcast and streaming require controlled dynamics so quiet content is audible and loud content does not exceed limits. Recommend matching the dynamic range to the delivery context, using compression and limiting to control peaks where required, because a cinema-dynamic mix delivered to streaming will have inaudible quiet parts and may be penalized for excessive loud parts.

### Balance the Three Mix Stems: Dialogue, Music, Effects

Picture mixes are organized into stems, primarily dialogue, music, and effects, which must be balanced individually and together. Recommend mixing each stem coherently and then balancing the stems so dialogue leads, music supports, and effects sit appropriately, because the stem balance defines the mix's clarity and impact, and the stems must also be delivered separately for international versioning.

### Use Automation to Manage the Balance Moment to Moment

The balance among dialogue, music, and effects changes constantly: music ducks when dialogue speaks, rises when it does not, effects highlight specific actions. Recommend using automation extensively to ride levels moment to moment, because static balances cannot serve a picture that changes every second, and automation is the primary tool for keeping every element appropriately present throughout.

### Check the Mix on Representative Playback Systems

Picture audio is heard on diverse systems: theaters, home theaters, soundbars, phones. Recommend checking the mix on representative systems, especially small speakers where dialogue clarity and low-end balance are challenged, because a mix that works only on large monitors fails for audiences who hear it on compromised systems.

## Common Traps

### Applying Music Mixing Loudness Instincts to Picture

This trap arises because mixers bring music mixing habits, maximizing loudness and fullness. The harm is a mix that is too loud, too compressed, and that buries dialogue under dense music and effects. The false signal is that the mix sounds big; the result is a deliverable that fails picture mixing's priorities and may violate loudness specs, because loudness maximization works against picture mixing goals.

### Ignoring the Loudness Specification Until Delivery

Mixers complete the mix and then check loudness, discovering it does not meet spec. The trap is that loudness seems like a final technicality. The harm is a mix that must be revised or that is rejected or normalized by the platform, because the loudness was never a design constraint, and late loudness correction often degrades the carefully balanced mix.

### Static Balance That Does Not Serve the Changing Picture

Mixers set levels and leave them, without automating the balance as the picture changes. The trap is that static levels feel stable. The harm is moments where dialogue is buried or music is too loud, because the balance was not adjusted to serve the changing content, and the mix fails to keep every element clear throughout.

### Failing to Check Translation to Small Speakers

Mixers perfect the mix on large monitors and deliver without checking small speakers. The trap is that the mix sounds excellent in the studio. The harm is dialogue that is inaudible and low end that is overwhelming or absent on phones and soundbars, because the mix was never tested on the systems most audiences use.

## Self-Check

- Did I prioritize dialogue and balance music and effects around it?
- Did I identify and monitor the delivery loudness specification throughout the mix?
- Did I manage dynamic range appropriate to the delivery context?
- Did I balance the dialogue, music, and effects stems coherently?
- Did I use automation to manage the balance moment to moment?
- Did I check the mix on representative playback systems including small speakers?
