# Ethics and Boundaries in Therapy

Music therapy carries ethical risks that purely musical work does not, because the medium of music, intimate, emotional, and often nonverbal, can blur relational boundaries that verbal therapies keep more visible. Agents often focus on the musical content of a session and miss that the therapeutic relationship itself is a clinical instrument requiring the same care as the music. Ethical failures in this field rarely come from malice; they come from drift, from small boundary compromises that felt kind in the moment but accumulated into harmful or unprofessional conduct.

## Core Rules

### Establish Informed Consent as an Ongoing Process

Consent is not a signed form collected once at intake. It is a continuing agreement that the client understands what music therapy is, what the interventions involve, what the risks and benefits are, and that they may withdraw at any time. For clients with limited capacity, consent involves guardians or advocates and must be supplemented with assent, the client's observable willingness to participate. Revisit consent when the treatment plan changes significantly, because consent given for one intervention does not automatically cover another.

### Respect Confidentiality Beyond the Session

Confidentiality extends to the fact that a person is a client, to the content of sessions, and to any musical products such as recordings or compositions that contain personal expression. A song a client wrote about their grief is protected health information. Sharing recordings for supervision, teaching, or promotion requires specific written consent, and even then identifying details must be minimized. The casual sharing that feels harmless in a close professional community can constitute a serious breach.

### Avoid and Actively Manage Dual Relationships

A dual relationship occurs when the therapist has another role with the client, such as teacher, performer, friend, or family member. In small music communities these overlaps are common and not always avoidable, but they always require active management because they impair objectivity and create power imbalances. Document the dual relationship, consult a supervisor, and establish clear boundaries about how each role will be conducted. Never assume a second role is harmless simply because both parties seem comfortable.

### Stay Within Scope of Practice

Music therapists are trained to use music to address clinical goals, not to diagnose unrelated conditions, provide psychotherapy for issues outside their competence, or offer medical advice. When a client presents needs beyond scope, the ethical action is referral, not quiet expansion of one's own role. Practicing outside scope exposes the client to harm and the therapist to legal and professional liability, and it often happens gradually as a trusted therapist takes on more without noticing the line has been crossed.

### Recognize the Power Asymmetry in the Therapeutic Relationship

The therapist holds authority through expertise, the institutional setting, and the client's vulnerability. This asymmetry means that even seemingly mutual interactions, accepting gifts, socializing, or sharing personal stories, carry weight the therapist may underestimate. The standard is not whether the interaction feels balanced to the therapist but whether it preserves the client's autonomy and the relationship's clinical integrity. When in doubt, consult before acting.

### Document Objectively and Timely

Clinical notes are legal and ethical records. Write them contemporaneously, focus on observable behavior and clinical reasoning rather than interpretation or judgment, and avoid speculative language about the client's character. Late, vague, or editorialized notes fail to protect both client and therapist if the treatment is ever questioned. Documentation that reads as advocacy rather than observation can undermine credibility.

### Use Supervision and Consultation Proactively

Ethical practice is not solitary. Regular supervision provides a structured space to surface boundary concerns, countertransference, and clinical uncertainty before they become ethical violations. Waiting until a situation has escalated to seek consultation means missing the window in which a small adjustment could have prevented harm. Treat consultation as routine professional hygiene, not as an admission of inadequacy.

## Common Traps

### The Kindness Trap of Boundary Drift

Small accommodations, extending sessions, accepting gifts, sharing personal contact details, feel compassionate in the moment and are often welcomed by the client. The trap is that each compromise feels minor in isolation, but together they erode the professional frame that keeps the client safe. The harm appears later, when the blurred boundary produces dependency, role confusion, or an inability to end treatment cleanly, and the therapist cannot point to the moment it went wrong.

### Assuming Comfort Implies Consent

A client who smiles, engages eagerly, or never objects is not necessarily giving informed consent, particularly when capacity is limited or the power differential is large. The false signal is visible cooperation; the reality may be compliance, desire to please, or inability to refuse. Relying on apparent willingness without checking understanding can lead to interventions the client did not genuinely agree to.

### Confidentiality Leaks Through Performance and Recordings

Music therapists naturally want to showcase client progress, and recordings or performances feel like positive, consensual sharing. The trap is that recordings capture deeply personal expression and can be re-shared beyond the original consent, especially online. A client's enthusiastic consent at one moment does not account for how they will feel about the material years later, and once it is public it cannot be retracted.

### Scope Creep Through Trust

Clients often ask a trusted music therapist for help with problems outside music therapy's scope, and refusing can feel like abandonment. The trap is that gradual expansion feels responsive and caring, but it moves the therapist into unqualified territory where harm is likely and accountability is unclear. The ethical response, referral with warm handoff, can feel cold but protects the client.

### Treating Supervision as Optional or Remedial

When supervision is treated as something only needed when problems arise, therapists stop bringing forward the early, ambiguous concerns that most need outside perspective. The harm is that the very situations most likely to become ethical violations, subtle boundary questions, countertransference, uncertain capacity, are the ones least likely to be raised. Routine supervision catches what self-reflection misses.

## Self-Check

- Is informed consent documented as an ongoing process, including assent for clients with limited capacity?
- Are recordings, compositions, and any client-identifying material protected and shared only with specific written consent?
- Have all dual relationships been identified, documented, and discussed in supervision?
- Are you operating strictly within your trained scope, with referrals made for needs outside it?
- Have you examined recent interactions for power-asymmetry effects rather than relying on how they felt to you?
- Are clinical notes written contemporaneously, objectively, and free of speculative judgment?
- Have ambiguous boundary or countertransference concerns been brought to supervision before they escalated?
