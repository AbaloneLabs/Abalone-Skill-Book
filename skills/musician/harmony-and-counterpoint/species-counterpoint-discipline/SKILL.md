# Species Counterpoint Discipline

Species counterpoint is a pedagogical discipline that trains a composer to write independent lines under tight constraints, and the judgment problem is that the constraints are not arbitrary hurdles — each rule encodes a perceptual principle about how lines achieve independence and how dissonance must be controlled to sound intentional. An agent writing or evaluating species exercises faces two failure modes: treating the rules as a checklist to satisfy mechanically (which produces correct but lifeless exercises), and treating the rules as obstacles to evade (which produces exercises that fail the discipline's purpose). The deeper judgment problem is that species counterpoint is a style — Palestrina-era Renaissance polyphony — and its rules are conventions of that style, not universal laws. This skill covers the decisions that govern species work: dissonance treatment, melodic contour constraints, rhythmic relationships between the voices, and the balance between rule-adherence and musicality.

## Core Rules

### Treat Dissonance as a Controlled, Prepared Event

In species counterpoint, dissonance is never freely introduced on a strong beat; it must be prepared (approached by step) and resolved (left by step in the opposite direction), or it must be a passing or neighbor tone on a weak beat that resolves immediately. The decision criterion is that dissonance must always sound intentional and transient, never like an error. The judgment problem is that a dissonance that is theoretically correct (a passing tone on the weak portion of the beat) can still sound wrong if it is approached or left by leap, because the ear loses the sense of stepwise preparation. Before approving a dissonance, verify not just its beat placement but the shape of its approach and resolution.

### Build Melodic Contour From Consonant Steps and Small Leaps

The cantus firmus and the counterpoint line should be built primarily from stepwise motion and leaps of a third or fourth, with larger leaps (fifth, octave) used sparingly and always followed by stepwise motion in the opposite direction to recover. The decision rule is that a melody of mostly steps with occasional small leaps sings naturally, while a melody of frequent large leaps sounds angular and instrumental rather than vocal. The trap is writing lines that are technically consonant but melodically unconvincing because they leap without recovery. After writing a line, trace its contour and confirm that every leap larger than a fourth is followed by stepwise contrary motion.

### Avoid the Prohibited Parallel and Direct Perfect Intervals

Parallel fifths and octaves — two voices moving in the same direction to land on a perfect fifth or octave — are prohibited because they destroy the independence of the lines by fusing them into a single perceptual stream. Direct (hidden) fifths and octaves — where the outer voices approach a perfect interval by similar motion, especially with a leap in the soprano — are also restricted because they create an unintended accent on the perfect interval. The decision is to check every vertical interval against the previous one and flag any approach to a perfect fifth or octave by parallel or direct similar motion. The judgment problem is that these errors are easy to miss when focusing on one voice at a time; the remedy is to scan interval-by-interval between each voice pair.

### Manage the Rhythmic Relationship Between Species

Each species defines a specific rhythmic relationship between the cantus firmus and the counterpoint: first species is note-against-note, second species adds two notes against one, third species uses four notes against one, fourth species introduces syncopation and suspensions, and fifth species combines the previous rhythms freely. The decision is to honor the species's rhythmic definition strictly, because the rhythmic constraint is what creates the specific contrapuntal problem the exercise is meant to solve. The trap is drifting into a freer rhythmic relationship (sneaking a syncopation into second species) because it sounds easier; the discipline requires staying within the defined rhythm.

### Secure the Cadence With a Standard Contrapuntal Close

Species exercises conventionally cadence with the leading tone ascending to the tonic in one voice while the supertonic descends to the tonic in the other, producing a unison or octave close. The decision is to plan the cadential approach several notes before the end, because a last-minute scramble to reach the close often produces parallel octaves or an unresolved leading tone. The judgment criterion is that the cadence should feel inevitable and earned, not abrupt; map the final three or four intervals deliberately and verify they avoid prohibited motion.

### Prioritize Musicality Within the Constraints

The highest goal of species counterpoint is not rule-compliance but musicality achieved within the constraints. A line that satisfies every rule but has no contour, no climax, and no sense of direction has failed the exercise's purpose. The decision is to evaluate the exercise as music after evaluating it as rule-compliance: does the counterpoint line sing, does it have a high point, does it interact with the cantus firmus in a way that creates interest? The trap is treating the rules as the entire standard; the remedy is to revise for musical shape once the rules are satisfied.

## Common Traps

### Satisfying the Rules Mechanically Without Seeking Musical Shape

A student checks every interval, avoids every parallel fifth, resolves every dissonance correctly, and produces an exercise that is technically flawless but melodically inert — a line that wanders without a climax, with no sense of where it is going. The false signal is that the exercise passes a rule-checker; the harm is that the discipline's actual goal — training the ear to write singable, independent lines — is never reached. The mechanism is reducing counterpoint to compliance rather than craft, and the remedy is to evaluate the line as a melody after the rules are met.

### Missing Parallel Octaves by Checking One Voice at a Time

The composer traces the soprano against the cantus firmus, then the alto against the cantus firmus, and concludes there are no parallels, but never checks the soprano against the alto, where a hidden parallel octave lurks. The false signal is that each pair looks clean in isolation; the harm is an error that survives the check because not every voice pair was examined. The mechanism is incomplete interval scanning; the fix is to systematically check every voice pair at every interval change, not just the most obvious pairs.

### Introducing a Dissonance on a Strong Beat by Mistake

A passing tone is placed on the second half of the beat, which the composer believes is always weak, but in compound meter or at certain tempos that portion is accented enough that the dissonance sounds like an error rather than a passing tone. The false signal is theoretical beat-placement correctness; the harm is a dissonance that sounds unprepared to the ear. The mechanism is trusting metric theory over perceptual reality; the remedy is to listen to the exercise and relocate any dissonance that sounds unprepared regardless of its theoretical beat position.

### Leaping to or From a Dissonance

A passing tone or neighbor tone is correctly placed on a weak beat but is approached or left by a leap rather than by step, so it loses the stepwise preparation that makes it sound intentional. The false signal is that the dissonance is on the correct beat; the harm is a dissonance that sounds like a wrong note rather than a controlled expressive event. The mechanism is treating beat placement as sufficient while ignoring the melodic shape of the approach and resolution; the fix is to ensure every dissonance is both approached and resolved by step.

### Drifting Out of the Species Rhythm for Convenience

The exercise is nominally second species (two notes against one) but the composer inserts a syncopated suspension because it solves a voice-leading problem more easily, violating the species definition. The false signal is that the line sounds smoother; the harm is that the specific contrapuntal skill the species is meant to develop is never actually practiced. The mechanism is prioritizing ease over discipline; the remedy is to solve the problem within the species's rhythmic constraint, even if it requires more effort or a less obvious solution.

## Self-Check

- Is every dissonance both prepared (approached by step) and resolved (left by step in the opposite direction), with no dissonance introduced by leap or left unresolved?
- Does the melodic contour consist mostly of steps and small leaps, with every leap larger than a fourth followed by stepwise contrary motion to recover?
- Have I checked every voice pair (not just the obvious ones) for parallel and direct perfect fifths and octaves at every interval change?
- Does the exercise stay strictly within the rhythmic definition of its species, with no drift into syncopation or freer rhythms that belong to another species?
- Is the cadence planned several intervals in advance, with the leading tone ascending and the supertonic descending to a secure close, and no prohibited motion in the final intervals?
- Beyond rule-compliance, does the counterpoint line sing with a coherent shape, a discernible climax, and an interesting relationship to the cantus firmus?
- Have I listened to the exercise (not just inspected it visually) to catch dissonances that are theoretically correct but sound unprepared?
