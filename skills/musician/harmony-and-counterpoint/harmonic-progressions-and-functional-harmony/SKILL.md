# Harmonic Progressions and Functional Harmony

Functional harmony is the system by which chords create a sense of direction — the feeling that the music is moving toward, away from, or resting on a tonal center. The judgment problem is that the same chord can serve radically different functions depending on context, and the difference between a progression that compels and one that meanders is rarely about which chords are chosen but about whether each chord performs a clear functional role. Beginners tend to reach for richer or more unusual chords when the real problem is that the functional skeleton is weak, and they reach for familiar clichés when the real problem is that the harmony never builds enough tension to make resolution satisfying. This skill covers the decisions that govern functional progressions: role assignment, directional motion, substitution, modulation, and the calibration of complexity to genre and emotional goal.

## Core Rules

### Assign a Functional Role to Every Chord Before Refining

Before selecting voicings, extensions, or inversions, determine the functional role of each chord: tonic (rest and stability), predominant (preparation and departure), or dominant (tension and drive toward resolution). The decision criterion is directional clarity: if you strip the progression to its root-position triads and remove the melody, can you still hear where the phrase is going? A progression with clear functional roles creates expectation; a sequence of chords with no functional logic creates stasis regardless of how sophisticated the chords appear. Compose the functional map first — establish tonic, move through predominant, arrive at dominant, resolve to tonic — then layer substitutions and color.

### Build Tension Through Predominant-to-Dominant Motion

The engine of harmonic direction is the movement from predominant function (ii, IV, vi) through dominant function (V, vii) toward tonic. The decision is how much weight to give each stage: a strong predominant (ii or IV in root position) sets up the dominant with expectation; the dominant, especially with a chord seventh, creates maximum tension that demands resolution to tonic. The trap is skipping the predominant stage and going straight from tonic to dominant, which produces a weaker, less prepared arrival. Plan the predominant-to-dominant-to-tonic arc as the spine of each phrase, and use substitutions (iv, ii–V, secondary dominants) to enrich each stage without losing the functional skeleton.

### Use Substitutions to Enrich Without Obscuring Function

A functional substitution replaces a chord with another that serves the same role but adds color: a vi substituting for tonic at a deceptive cadence, a tritone sub (flat-II) replacing dominant, a iii replacing tonic for a brighter rest. The decision criterion is that the substitution must preserve the underlying functional direction; a substitution that changes the perceived function is a modulation, not a color choice. The trap is stacking substitutions until the listener can no longer perceive the functional skeleton. Apply substitutions one at a time, and after each, confirm that the phrase still moves toward its intended cadence.

### Plan Modulations as Deliberate Tonal Journeys

A modulation — a change of tonal center — is one of the most powerful tools for large-scale direction, but it must be prepared and confirmed rather than slipped in accidentally. The decision is the method: a pivot-chord modulation uses a chord common to both keys to transition smoothly; a direct modulation simply jumps, creating surprise; a sequential modulation uses a repeating pattern to walk the tonal center to a new location. The judgment criterion is that the new key must be established (confirmed by a cadence in the new key) shortly after arrival, or the listener perceives tonal ambiguity rather than a journey. Plan the modulation point, the pivot, and the confirming cadence as three deliberate decisions.

### Calibrate Harmonic Rhythm to Emotional Intent

Harmonic rhythm — how frequently chords change — is a separate parameter from which chords are used, and it often carries more emotional weight than the harmony itself. Fast harmonic rhythm (one chord per beat or per half-bar) creates urgency and instability; slow harmonic rhythm (one chord per four or eight bars) creates space, weight, and immersion. The decision is to choose the rate of change deliberately and to vary it across sections, because constant fast rhythm becomes a wash and constant slow rhythm becomes static. A common strong move is to slow the harmonic rhythm approaching a cadence to amplify the arrival, or to accelerate it during a build to increase tension.

### Match Complexity to Genre and Emotional Goal

The appropriate harmonic vocabulary depends on genre expectations and the intended emotional effect. A folk song may be best served by three diatonic chords; a jazz standard may require extended harmonies, secondary dominants, and tritone substitutions; a film cue may need modal interchange to shift color. The decision criterion is not that more complex is better but that the complexity must serve the emotional narrative and meet or thoughtfully subvert genre expectations. Adding ninths, elevenths, and altered dominants to a song that needs simplicity obscures the melody; restricting a jazz piece to triads when it needs color sounds thin. Calibrate deliberately, and be willing to simplify when a passage feels cluttered.

## Common Traps

### Reharmonizing to Fix a Weak Functional Skeleton

A progression fails to build toward its cadence, so the composer swaps in richer chords — a flat-II, a secondary dominant, a tritone sub — expecting the added color to create direction. The result is a progression that sounds sophisticated in isolation but still does not move the listener, because the underlying functional skeleton was never established. The false signal is that the chords are more interesting; the harm is wasted effort adding color to a structure that lacks direction. The mechanism is mistaking vertical color for horizontal function, and the fix is to strengthen the tonic-predominant-dominant map before adding any substitutions.

### Changing Chords Every Beat to Sound Active

A composer believes frequent chord changes make music feel energetic, so they set a harmonic rhythm of one chord per beat throughout a verse. The false signal is that busy harmony feels productive while writing it. The harm is that constant change prevents any single harmony from registering, the listener cannot anchor to a tonal center, and the phrase feels restless without building real tension because there is no contrast to define it. Energetic harmonic rhythm only reads as energy against a backdrop of slower rhythm elsewhere; without that contrast, it becomes an undifferentiated wash.

### Defaulting to the Same Cadence for Every Phrase

Every phrase ends on a V–I authentic cadence because it feels safe and final. The false signal is that each phrase lands correctly. The harm is that uniform cadence types flatten the formal shape — every arrival feels equally conclusive, so no arrival stands out, and the listener loses the sense of building toward a destination. Varying cadence type by formal function (half cadence at the end of an open phrase, authentic at the true close, deceptive to extend) is what gives a multi-phrase section its contour and dramatic shape.

### Stacking Substitutions Until Function Disappears

The composer adds a tritone sub, then a secondary dominant to approach it, then a borrowed chord from the parallel minor, until the listener can no longer perceive where the phrase is going. The false signal is that each added chord is individually interesting. The harm is a progression that sounds sophisticated but directionless, because the functional skeleton has been buried under color. The mechanism is additive reharmonization without periodic functional auditing; the fix is to strip the progression back to triads periodically and confirm the functional direction survives.

### Modulating Without Confirming the New Key

The composer pivots to a new key but moves on to new material before establishing the tonal center with a cadence, so the listener perceives tonal drift rather than a deliberate modulation. The false signal is that the pivot chord was correct. The harm is a section that sounds harmonically lost, because the new tonic was never confirmed. The mechanism is treating the pivot as the complete modulation rather than as the first of two required steps (pivot then confirm); the remedy is to plan a confirming cadence in the new key immediately after the pivot.

## Self-Check

- Have I assigned a functional role (tonic, predominant, dominant) to every chord, and does the progression have clear directional motion even when stripped to root-position triads with the melody removed?
- Does each phrase build through a predominant-to-dominant-to-tonic arc, with no important arrival reached without adequate preparation?
- Does every substitution preserve the underlying functional direction, and have I periodically stripped the progression back to confirm the skeleton survives the added color?
- Is each modulation planned with a pivot point and a confirming cadence in the new key, rather than slipped in as tonal drift?
- Is the harmonic rhythm chosen deliberately and varied across sections, so changes in chord rate create perceptible shifts in energy?
- Is the harmonic complexity calibrated to the genre and emotional goal, and have I considered whether simplification would serve the piece better than added color?
- Do the cadence types vary by formal function, so that open phrases, conclusive arrivals, and extensions are distinguished rather than uniformly final?
