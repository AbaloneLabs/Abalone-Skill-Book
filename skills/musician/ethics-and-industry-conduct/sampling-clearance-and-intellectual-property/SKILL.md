# Sampling Clearance and Intellectual Property

Sampling — incorporating a portion of an existing recording into a new work — creates legal and ethical obligations that, mishandled, can lead to lawsuits, pulled releases, and destroyed income. The judgment problem is that the ease of sampling (any audio can be chopped and flipped in a DAW) is utterly disconnected from the difficulty and cost of clearing it (negotiating with publishers and master owners, often for substantial fees and splits), so producers build tracks on uncleared samples, release them, and face consequences that range from takedowns to judgments that seize all revenue. The agent must understand the difference between composition and master rights, must know when clearance is required and when it is not, and must never release uncleared material as if it were cleared.

## Core Rules

### Understand the Two Layers of Rights: Composition and Master

Every recorded song has two separate copyrights: the composition (the underlying song — melody, lyrics, chord progression, owned by the songwriter and their publisher) and the master (the specific sound recording, owned by the record label or artist). Sampling a recording uses both layers: the master (you are using the actual recording) and the composition (you are using the underlying song). Clearing a sample requires clearing both — the master from the label, the composition from the publisher. Clearing only one leaves you exposed on the other. This two-layer structure is the foundation of all sample clearance.

**Decision criteria:** For any sample, have you identified both the composition (songwriter/publisher) and master (label/recording owner)? Is clearance being sought for both layers, not just one?

### Clear Samples Before Release, Not After

The most common and catastrophic error is releasing a track with an uncleared sample and planning to "clear it later if it blows up." This fails because: (1) if the track succeeds, the rights holders can demand exorbitant fees or refuse clearance, forcing you to pull the track and surrender all revenue; (2) even before success, rights holders can issue takedowns and sue; (3) "later" clearance is always more expensive and less certain than pre-release clearance. Clearance must be secured before release. If clearance is uncertain or unaffordable, do not release the track with the sample, or replace the sample with an original or pre-cleared element.

**Decision criteria:** Is every sample cleared (both composition and master) before release? If clearance is uncertain or unaffordable, is the sample replaced rather than released uncleared?

### Distinguish Sampling From Interpolation and Understand Each's Requirements

Sampling (using the actual recording) requires clearing both master and composition. Interpolation (re-recording the melody or portion yourself, without using the original recording) requires clearing only the composition, not the master — because you are not using the original sound recording. Interpolation is often cheaper and easier to clear because it involves only the publisher, not the label. The choice between sampling and interpolation has significant clearance implications. Know which you are doing and clear accordingly. Re-recording a portion to avoid master clearance is a legitimate strategy but still requires composition clearance.

**Decision criteria:** Are you sampling (using the recording, requiring both clearances) or interpolating (re-recording, requiring only composition clearance)? Is clearance being pursued for the correct layer(s) based on which you are doing?

### Treat "Small" and "Unrecognizable" Samples as Still Requiring Clearance

A common myth is that samples under a certain duration (e.g., "under 6 seconds" or "under 2 seconds") or that are unrecognizable do not require clearance. This is false under current law. Any use of a portion of a copyrighted recording, however small or transformed, technically requires clearance, and rights holders have successfully sued over brief, heavily processed samples. The only safe assumption is that any sample requires clearance. "Transformative" use (heavily chopping, pitching, reversing) does not eliminate the clearance obligation, though it may affect whether the sample is recognized and pursued.

**Decision criteria:** Are any samples being treated as "too small" or "unrecognizable" to require clearance? Is the assumption that all samples require clearance being applied, regardless of duration or transformation?

### Understand Fair Use's Narrow Application to Music

Fair use is a legal defense (not a permission) that allows limited use of copyrighted material for purposes like criticism, commentary, parody, and education. Its application to music sampling is narrow and uncertain: courts have generally held that commercial music sampling does not qualify as fair use, even when transformed. Relying on fair use to justify an uncleared sample in a commercial release is legally risky and likely to fail if challenged. Fair use is not a reliable clearance strategy for music; treat all samples as requiring clearance unless a qualified entertainment attorney advises otherwise for a specific case.

**Decision criteria:** Is fair use being relied upon to justify an uncleared sample? Is it recognized that fair use is a narrow, uncertain defense for commercial music sampling, not a reliable clearance strategy?

### Manage Remixes, Mixtapes, and Covers With Appropriate Licensing

Remixes (using the original master) require master and often composition clearance from the rights holders, who may grant or refuse permission. Mixtapes using copyrighted beats or samples are, despite the culture, generally unauthorized and expose the creator to takedown and liability. Covers (re-performing a copyrighted song) do not require permission but require a mechanical license (compulsory for digital and physical distribution in many jurisdictions) and payment of the statutory mechanical rate. Each of these uses has specific requirements; treating them casually leads to legal exposure. Know the requirements for each use type and comply.

**Decision criteria:** For remixes, is master and composition clearance obtained? For mixtapes with copyrighted material, is the unauthorized status recognized and managed? For covers, is a mechanical license obtained and the statutory rate paid?

## Common Traps

### Releasing Uncleared Samples and Planning to Clear Later

Releasing first and clearing later exposes the creator to takedowns, lawsuits, and exorbitant post-success clearance demands that can seize all revenue. The trap mechanism is that pre-release clearance seems unnecessary if the track is small. The harm is catastrophic loss if the track succeeds. The corrective is pre-release clearance.

### Clearing Only the Master or Only the Composition

Clearing one layer but not the other leaves the creator exposed on the uncleared layer. The trap mechanism is that partial clearance feels sufficient. The harm is liability on the uncleared layer. The corrective is clearing both.

### Believing Small or Transformed Samples Do Not Need Clearance

The myth that brief or unrecognizable samples are exempt leads to uncleared releases that are still legally exposed. The trap mechanism is that the sample seems too small to matter. The harm is lawsuits over brief samples. The corrective is the assumption that all samples require clearance.

### Relying on Fair Use for Commercial Sampling

Fair use is narrow and uncertain for commercial music and is likely to fail if challenged. The trap mechanism is that fair use feels like a permission. The harm is failed defense and liability. The corrective is treating fair use as unreliable for music sampling.

### Treating Mixtapes and Remixes as Exempt From Clearance

The culture of unauthorized mixtapes and remixes does not change the legal requirement for clearance. The trap mechanism is that the practice is culturally accepted. The harm is takedowns and liability. The corrective is understanding and complying with the requirements for each use type.

### Ignoring Composition Clearance When Interpolating

Interpolation avoids master clearance but still requires composition clearance, which creators sometimes overlook. The trap mechanism is that interpolation feels exempt because the recording is not used. The harm is composition-layer liability. The corrective is clearing composition for interpolations.

## Self-Check

- For every sample, have both the composition (publisher) and master (label) rights been identified?
- Is every sample cleared (both layers) before release, with uncleared samples replaced rather than released?
- Is the distinction between sampling (both clearances) and interpolation (composition only) understood and applied correctly?
- Are all samples, regardless of duration or transformation, treated as requiring clearance?
- Is fair use recognized as a narrow, uncertain defense for commercial music, not relied upon as a clearance strategy?
- Are remixes, mixtapes, and covers handled with their specific licensing requirements (master/composition clearance, mechanical licenses)?
- Could you document, for every copyrighted element in a release, the clearance obtained or the basis for non-infringement?
