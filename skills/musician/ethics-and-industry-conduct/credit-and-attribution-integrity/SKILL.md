# Credit and Attribution Integrity

Credit is the currency of reputation in music. Every album, single, film, and session generates credits that determine who is recognized, hired, paid, and remembered for the work. The judgment problem is that credits are often assigned carelessly, politically, or dishonestly — ghost producers omitted, minor contributors over-credited, engineers and session musicians erased, and metadata filed incorrectly — with the result that the record of who did what is inaccurate, and careers are built or broken on false attribution. The agent must treat credit as an ethical and professional obligation, must assign it accurately based on actual contribution, and must ensure that the public and industry record reflects the truth, because inaccurate credits are not a victimless oversight but a theft of reputation and opportunity.

## Core Rules

### Assign Credit Based on Actual Contribution, Not Politics or Convenience

Credits should reflect what each person actually contributed. A producer who shaped the sound deserves producer credit; a friend who was present does not. An engineer who recorded and mixed deserves engineering credit; an assistant who set up microphones may deserve assistant credit. Session musicians who performed deserve performer credit. The temptation to assign credit for political reasons (to please a label executive, to include a famous name, to appease a difficult collaborator) or to omit it for cost (avoiding royalties by not crediting a contributor) corrupts the record. Assign credit by contribution, document the basis, and resist pressure to inflate or deflate attribution.

**Decision criteria:** For each credited (and uncredited) person, does the credit accurately reflect their actual contribution? Is any credit assigned for political or financial reasons rather than contribution? Is any contributor omitted to avoid payment or recognition?

### Credit All Material Contributors, Including "Invisible" Roles

Many contributors to a recording are systematically under-credited or erased: session musicians, background vocalists, engineers, assistants, programmers, arrangers, copyists, and samples' original creators. The practice of omitting these roles — especially of marginalized contributors, who are disproportionately erased — distorts the historical record and denies people recognition and opportunity. Make a comprehensive list of everyone who contributed materially and ensure each is credited appropriately. The "invisible" roles are often where erasure happens; audit for them deliberately.

**Decision criteria:** Are all material contributors credited, including session musicians, engineers, programmers, arrangers, and others often omitted? Are there contributors who were present and contributing but uncredited? Is there an audit for commonly erased roles?

### Ensure Metadata and Liner Notes Are Accurate and Complete

Credits exist in two forms: the human-readable liner notes and the machine-readable metadata (ID3 tags, ISWC, ISRC, PRO registrations, DSP credits). Both must be accurate and complete, because metadata drives royalties, search, and the industry databases that track who did what. Inaccurate or missing metadata means contributors go unpaid and uncredited in the systems that matter. Metadata errors (misspelled names, wrong role codes, missing writer splits) are common and consequential. Audit both liner notes and metadata before release, and verify that they match.

**Decision criteria:** Are liner notes complete and accurate? Is metadata (writer credits, performer credits, ISRC/ISWC, PRO registrations) complete, accurate, and matched to the liner notes? Has metadata been audited before release?

### Address Ghost Production and Uncredited Work Honestly

Ghost production — where a producer or writer creates work for which another person takes public credit — is common and is ethical only when agreed transparently by both parties (usually for compensation). When ghost production is undisclosed and the credited person did not do the work, it is a misrepresentation that harms the actual creator and deceives the public. If you are using ghost producers or uncredited writers, ensure the arrangement is transparent and compensated. If you are the ghosted creator, ensure you have agreed to the terms knowingly. Covert ghosting that denies creators recognition is a credit integrity failure.

**Decision criteria:** Is any ghost production or uncredited work transparent and agreed by both parties? Is there covert ghosting that denies creators recognition? Are the terms of any ghost arrangement fair and knowingly agreed?

### Handle Credit Disputes Through Contribution Evidence, Not Power

Credit disputes arise when contributors disagree about who deserves what. These should be resolved through evidence of contribution (what each person actually did, documented) and the applicable conventions (genre split customs, role definitions), not through power (who has more industry leverage, who is more aggressive). A contributor with less power who did the work deserves the credit; a powerful contributor who did less does not deserve more. Resolving disputes by power entrenches inequity and produces inaccurate records. Document contributions as they happen so disputes can be resolved with evidence.

**Decision criteria:** When credit disputes arise, are they resolved through contribution evidence and conventions, or through power and leverage? Are contributions documented as they happen to support fair resolution?

### Correct Historical Credit Errors When Discovered

Many older recordings have inaccurate credits — erased contributors, misattributed work, omitted samples — that persist in the record. When errors are discovered (through research, contributor testimony, or evidence), the ethical response is to correct them, through reissued liner notes, metadata updates, public acknowledgment, and compensation where appropriate. Defending inaccurate credits because "that is how it was done then" or to protect a legacy perpetuates the harm. Correction may be imperfect (some contributors are dead, some royalties are unrecoverable), but the effort to set the record right is an obligation.

**Decision criteria:** When historical credit errors are discovered, are they corrected through reissue, metadata, acknowledgment, and compensation where possible? Or are they defended to protect legacy or avoid disruption?

## Common Traps

### Omitting Credits to Avoid Payment or Recognition

Excluding a contributor from credits to avoid paying royalties or sharing recognition is a direct theft of reputation and income. The trap mechanism is that omission is easy and invisible. The harm is denied livelihood and erased contribution. The corrective is crediting all material contributors regardless of cost.

### Over-Crediting for Political Reasons

Adding credits for people who did not contribute (to please an executive, include a famous name, or appease a collaborator) dilutes the record and misrepresents the work. The trap mechanism is that the credit seems harmless. The harm is a distorted record and denied recognition for actual contributors. The corrective is crediting by contribution only.

### Erasing "Invisible" Roles and Marginalized Contributors

Session musicians, engineers, programmers, and arrangers — disproportionately women and people of color — are systematically under-credited, erasing their work from the record. The trap mechanism is that these roles are seen as secondary. The harm is compounded career disadvantage. The corrective is auditing for and crediting all material roles.

### Inaccurate or Missing Metadata

Credits that are correct in liner notes but wrong or missing in metadata mean contributors go unpaid and uncredited in the systems that drive royalties and industry databases. The trap mechanism is that liner notes feel sufficient. The harm is lost income and recognition. The corrective is metadata auditing before release.

### Covert Ghost Production That Denies Recognition

Ghost production that is not transparently agreed denies the actual creator recognition and deceives the public. The trap mechanism is that the arrangement is convenient for the credited party. The harm is erased contribution and deception. The corrective is transparent, agreed ghost arrangements.

### Resolving Disputes by Power Rather Than Contribution

When credit disputes are resolved by who has more leverage rather than who did the work, the record becomes inaccurate and inequitable. The trap mechanism is that power feels like authority. The harm is entrenched inequity and false records. The corrective is contribution-evidence-based resolution.

## Self-Check

- Does every credit accurately reflect actual contribution, with none assigned for political or financial reasons?
- Are all material contributors credited, including commonly erased roles (session musicians, engineers, programmers, arrangers)?
- Are liner notes and metadata both complete, accurate, and matched, audited before release?
- Is any ghost production or uncredited work transparent and agreed, with no covert denial of recognition?
- When disputes arise, are they resolved through contribution evidence and conventions, not power?
- When historical errors are discovered, are they corrected through reissue, metadata, acknowledgment, and compensation where possible?
- Could each contributor, asked independently, confirm that their credit accurately reflects their work?
