# Professional Conduct and Boundaries

The music industry has long tolerated unprofessional, exploitative, and abusive behavior under the cover of "creative genius," late nights, alcohol, and the power asymmetry between established artists and the musicians, engineers, and students who depend on them for work. The judgment problem is that musicians constantly operate in informal, emotionally charged, high-stakes environments — tours, studios, lessons, co-writes — where formal workplace protections feel distant, yet where conduct and boundary decisions have real consequences for careers, safety, and the integrity of the profession. The agent must recognize power dynamics, must hold professional standards even when the culture normalizes their violation, and must understand that "how things have always been done" is not a defense for harm.

## Core Rules

### Recognize and Respect Power Asymmetries

Music work is rife with power asymmetries: the bandleader over the side-person, the producer over the artist, the teacher over the student, the label over the signer, the veteran over the newcomer. These asymmetries mean that a subordinate's agreement, silence, or participation is not freely given consent — it may be coerced by the need for work, credit, recommendation, or career advancement. Professional conduct requires the person with more power to actively account for the asymmetry: to not exploit it for sexual, financial, or creative gain; to create space for dissent; to not punish refusal. Consent given under power imbalance is suspect, and the powerful party bears responsibility for ensuring decisions are genuinely voluntary.

**Decision criteria:** In any interaction, who holds more power (economic, reputational, gatekeeping)? Is the less powerful party's agreement genuinely voluntary, or shaped by fear of consequence? Is the power asymmetry being respected rather than exploited?

### Maintain Clear Boundaries Between Professional and Intimate Relationships

The music world blurs personal and professional lines — bands become families, collaborators become partners, tours create intense bonds. But boundary blurring creates risk: romantic or sexual relationships within power structures (teacher-student, producer-artist, bandleader-side-person) are inherently problematic because the power asymmetry undermines genuine consent, and a relationship's end can destroy the professional relationship and the dependent party's career. Professional conduct requires maintaining clear boundaries: avoiding romantic or sexual relationships where a power asymmetry exists; disclosing and recusing where a relationship arises between peers; separating the professional from the personal in ways that protect both parties. When boundaries are uncertain, the conservative choice is to maintain distance.

**Decision criteria:** Does a romantic or sexual relationship involve a power asymmetry (teacher/student, producer/artist, employer/employee)? If so, is the relationship being avoided or, if it arises, disclosed and managed with recusal where needed?

### Do Not Tolerate or Enable Harassment, Discrimination, or Abuse

Harassment (sexual, verbal, racial, gender-based), discrimination, and abuse are not "part of the business" regardless of how common they have been. Professional conduct requires that musicians neither commit nor tolerate such behavior: that they intervene when they witness it, support those subjected to it, and refuse to work with those who perpetrate it when they have the power to do so. Enabling — through silence, through continuing to collaborate with known abusers, through dismissing complaints as "drama" — perpetuates harm. The standard is not merely personal non-offense but active non-acquiescence in a culture of harm.

**Decision criteria:** When harassment or abuse is witnessed or reported, is it addressed rather than ignored or minimized? Are known perpetrators held accountable (not hired, not collaborated with) when the musician has the power to do so? Is complaining treated as legitimate rather than as "drama"?

### Honor Commitments, Contracts, and Professional Obligations

Professional conduct includes honoring the mundane obligations: showing up on time, being prepared, fulfilling contractual terms, paying side-people as agreed, delivering work by deadlines, and communicating changes honestly and early. The music world's informality does not excuse unreliability. A musician's reputation — the foundation of future work — is built on whether they are dependable. Breaching commitments, even small ones, accumulates into a reputation for flakiness that closes doors. When commitments cannot be met, the professional response is early, honest communication and renegotiation, not avoidance or last-minute cancellation.

**Decision criteria:** Are commitments (sessions, shows, payments, deadlines) honored or, when impossible, communicated early and renegotiated honestly? Is reliability treated as a core professional virtue?

### Protect Vulnerable Parties: Students, Minors, and Newcomers

Teachers, mentors, and established musicians have heightened obligations toward vulnerable parties: students (whose learning and evaluation depend on them), minors (who cannot consent and require safeguarding), and newcomers (who depend on established figures for access and opportunity). Professional conduct requires that these parties be protected, not exploited: that teaching relationships remain non-exploitative, that minors be supervised appropriately and never placed in compromising situations, that newcomers be given honest guidance rather than manipulated for labor or compliance. The obligation is heightened because the vulnerable party may not recognize exploitation or may lack the power to resist it.

**Decision criteria:** In interactions with students, minors, or newcomers, is the heightened obligation to protect (rather than exploit) being met? Are minors safeguarded appropriately? Is mentorship honest and non-manipulative?

### Address Conflict Through Direct, Respectful Communication

Musical collaboration generates conflict — creative differences, credit disputes, interpersonal friction. Professional conduct requires that conflict be addressed through direct, respectful communication rather than passive aggression, gossip, sabotage, or escalation. Direct communication means naming the issue honestly, listening to the other perspective, and seeking resolution; respect means attacking the problem, not the person. Gossip and sabotage destroy bands and reputations; escalation (legal threats, public callouts) should be reserved for genuine misconduct, not creative disagreement. Most musical conflict is resolvable through honest conversation if addressed early.

**Decision criteria:** Is conflict addressed directly and respectfully, or through gossip, passive aggression, or premature escalation? Are creative differences separated from personal attacks?

### Separate Substance Use From Professional Obligation

Substance use is pervasive in music culture, but professional conduct requires that it not impair performance, endanger others, or create pressure on non-using colleagues. Showing up impaired to a session, show, or lesson is unprofessional and, in performance contexts, can endanger the ensemble and the audience. Pressuring colleagues to drink or use is a boundary violation. Musicians in recovery must have their sobriety respected. The standard is not abstinence but separation: substance use must not interfere with professional obligation or coerce others, and impaired work is unacceptable.

**Decision criteria:** Does substance use impair professional performance or endanger others? Are non-using or recovering colleagues respected and not pressured? Is impaired work recognized as unacceptable?

## Common Traps

### Treating Power Asymmetry Consent as Freely Given

When a powerful figure (producer, teacher, bandleader) obtains agreement from a dependent party, the agreement may reflect fear of consequence rather than genuine consent. The trap mechanism is that the powerful party assumes the subordinate's participation is voluntary. The harm is exploitation that the subordinate cannot refuse without career cost. The corrective is that the powerful party bears responsibility for ensuring decisions are genuinely voluntary.

### Romantic Relationships Within Teaching or Production Power Structures

Relationships between teachers and students, producers and artists, or bandleaders and side-people are compromised by the power asymmetry that undermines genuine consent and, at the relationship's end, can destroy the dependent party's career. The trap mechanism is that the relationship feels mutual in the moment. The harm is coercion and career destruction. The corrective is avoiding such relationships or, if they arise between peers, disclosing and recusing.

### Enabling Abuse Through Silence and Continued Collaboration

Witnessing harassment or abuse and remaining silent — or continuing to work with known perpetrators — perpetuates harm and signals tolerance. The trap mechanism is that intervention feels costly or risky to one's own career. The harm is that perpetrators face no consequences and victims are isolated. The corrective is active non-acquiescence: intervention, support, and refusal to collaborate when one has the power.

### Excusing Unreliability as "Artistic Temperament"

Treating lateness, no-shows, broken commitments, or unpaid side-people as acceptable quirks of creative genius erodes professional standards and damages those who depend on the unreliable party. The trap mechanism is that creativity is used to excuse unprofessionalism. The harm is a culture of flakiness and real economic harm to collaborators. The corrective is treating reliability as non-negotiable.

### Exploiting Students, Minors, or Newcomers Because They Cannot Resist

Vulnerable parties — students who depend on evaluation, minors who cannot consent, newcomers who depend on access — are targets for exploitation precisely because they lack power. The trap mechanism is that the vulnerable party's compliance is mistaken for willingness. The harm is exploitation the victim may not even recognize. The corrective is the heightened obligation to protect rather than exploit.

### Addressing Conflict Through Gossip or Premature Escalation

Resolving creative or interpersonal conflict through gossip, passive aggression, or immediate legal or public escalation destroys bands and reputations without resolving the issue. The trap mechanism is that indirect or escalated responses feel safer than direct conversation. The harm is destroyed relationships and reputations. The corrective is direct, respectful communication, with escalation reserved for genuine misconduct.

## Self-Check

- In every interaction, have you identified who holds more power and ensured the less powerful party's agreement is genuinely voluntary?
- Are romantic or sexual relationships within power structures (teaching, production, employment) avoided, or disclosed and managed with recusal?
- When harassment or abuse is witnessed or reported, is it addressed rather than ignored, and are known perpetrators held accountable?
- Are professional commitments — sessions, shows, payments, deadlines — honored or renegotiated honestly and early when impossible?
- Are vulnerable parties (students, minors, newcomers) protected rather than exploited, with minors safeguarded appropriately?
- Is conflict addressed through direct, respectful communication rather than gossip, sabotage, or premature escalation?
- Is substance use kept separate from professional obligation, with non-using and recovering colleagues respected and impaired work recognized as unacceptable?
