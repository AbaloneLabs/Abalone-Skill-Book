# Routing and Schedule Planning

A tour's success depends as much on the routing and schedule as on the performances. Agents advising on touring often build routes that look efficient on a map but ignore travel time, rest, and the human limits of the artists and crew. A poorly routed tour produces exhausted performers, missed or compromised shows, and wasted money. Routing and scheduling is a judgment problem about geography, time, energy, and cost. This skill covers how to plan tours that are feasible and sustainable.

## Core Rules

### Build the Route Around Geographic Logic

Tour routing should minimize backtracking and long jumps between distant cities. Recommend planning the route as a logical geographic path, moving in a direction that reduces total travel distance and time, because inefficient routing with cross-country jumps wastes fuel, time, and energy, and a geographically coherent route is the foundation of a feasible tour.

### Account for Real Travel Time, Not Only Distance

The time between cities includes driving plus loading, traffic, weather, and rest stops, and these often exceed the raw driving time. Recommend calculating realistic travel times with buffers, and ensuring the schedule allows arrival with time to load in, soundcheck, and rest before the show, because schedules built on optimistic drive times produce late arrivals, missed soundchecks, and stressed, underprepared performances.

### Build in Rest and Recovery Time

Touring is physically and vocally demanding, and performers need sleep and recovery to perform well. Recommend building adequate overnight stops, limiting overnight drives, and scheduling lighter days or days off in demanding stretches, because a tour without rest produces diminishing performances, illness, and burnout, and sustainable pacing protects both the artists and the quality of the shows.

### Sequence Show Days and Travel Days Realistically

A show day that also requires long travel is far harder than a show day with short travel. Recommend sequencing the schedule so that long travel happens on off days or before early shows, and clustering shows geographically to reduce travel on performance days, because asking artists to drive six hours and perform that night consistently produces compromised shows.

### Plan Around Venue Load-In and Soundcheck Windows

Venues have specific load-in and soundcheck windows that constrain the schedule. Recommend confirming these windows when booking and building the schedule around them, ensuring arrival allows the full production setup and soundcheck, because missing the window delays the entire evening and may force abbreviated or skipped soundchecks that compromise the show.

### Build Contingency for Delays and Problems

Travel delays, vehicle problems, weather, and venue issues are normal on tour, not exceptions. Recommend building buffer time into the schedule and having contingency plans for common problems, because a schedule with no buffer turns every delay into a crisis, and realistic touring schedules assume that something will go wrong and leave room to absorb it. Factor in season and terrain: winter routing through mountain passes, remote rural venues with limited fuel stops, and border crossings each add risk that flat-map planning completely hides, so adjust buffers to the specific geography of each leg.

## Common Traps

### Optimistic Drive Times Without Buffers

This trap arises because mapping tools give raw drive times and planners use them directly. The harm is schedules that are impossible in reality, producing chronic lateness, missed soundchecks, and stressed artists. The false signal is that the route looks efficient; the result is constant crisis, because the real travel time, with stops and delays, was never accounted for.

### Backtracking and Inefficient Geographic Routing

Planners book shows based on availability rather than geography, producing routes that jump back and forth across a region. The trap is that each show seems bookable individually. The harm is excessive travel that wastes money and exhausts the crew, because the route was never planned as a coherent path, and the cumulative travel is far greater than necessary.

### No Rest Days in Demanding Stretches

Planners fill every available date with shows to maximize revenue. The trap is that more shows equal more money. The harm is exhausted, ill performers whose shows decline in quality, because the tour had no recovery time, and the later, worse shows may earn less and damage reputation more than a rest day would have cost.

### Ignoring Venue and Production Constraints

Planners schedule without confirming load-in and soundcheck windows. The trap is that the show is booked and the logistics seem secondary. The harm is production problems, delayed doors, and compromised shows, because the schedule did not account for the real time the venue and production require.

## Self-Check

- Did I build the route around geographic logic to minimize travel?
- Did I calculate realistic travel times with buffers for stops and delays?
- Did I build in adequate rest and recovery time for the artists?
- Did I sequence show days and travel days so long travel does not precede shows?
- Did I confirm and schedule around venue load-in and soundcheck windows?
- Did I build contingency buffer for delays and common problems?
