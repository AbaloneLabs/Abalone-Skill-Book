# Gear and Production Management

Touring gear and production management is the discipline of getting the right equipment to the right place, working, every night, across changing venues and conditions. Agents advising on touring often focus on the performances while treating gear and production as background logistics. But gear failures, missing equipment, and poor production coordination derail shows and cost money. This skill covers the judgment needed to manage gear and production reliably on the road.

## Core Rules

### Maintain Detailed Gear Inventories and Documentation

Every piece of gear must be tracked: what it is, where it goes, and its condition. Recommend maintaining a detailed inventory with serial numbers, and documentation including stage plots and input lists sent to every venue in advance, because missing or misunderstood gear requirements produce show-night chaos, and clear documentation lets venues prepare what you need.

### Plan for Venue Variability and Provide Backline Specs

Venues vary wildly in what they provide and what they expect you to bring. Recommend confirming exactly what each venue provides, specifying your backline and technical requirements in advance, and bringing what venues will not provide, because arriving without needed gear or with wrong assumptions about venue provision produces compromised or impossible shows.

### Build Redundancy for Critical Failure Points

Identify the gear whose failure would stop the show, cables, key instruments, power supplies, computers, and ensure backups exist. Recommend carrying spares and redundant systems for critical paths, because a single point of failure on tour is guaranteed to fail eventually, and the cost of a spare is trivial compared to a canceled show.

### Standardize Setup and Striking Procedures

A repeatable setup process saves time and reduces errors across venues. Recommend standardizing the setup and striking procedures, with labeled cables, consistent stage positions, and a documented order of operations, because a standardized process lets the crew work efficiently in every venue and reduces the errors that rushed, ad hoc setups produce.

### Manage Power and Electrical Requirements

Touring productions have specific power needs, and inadequate or incorrect power damages gear and stops shows. Recommend confirming power availability with venues, carrying appropriate distribution and protection, and never assuming venue power is adequate or correct, because power problems destroy equipment and end shows, and electrical safety is non-negotiable.

### Coordinate With Venue and Local Crew Effectively

Every venue has local crew and technical staff who are essential to a smooth show. Recommend communicating clearly and respectfully with venue staff, providing advance information, and integrating local crew into your setup process, because venue staff who are informed and respected help solve problems, while staff who are ignored or mistreated can make the night difficult. The local crew knows the room and its quirks better than you do, so treat their knowledge as an asset: asking about stage sightlines, power locations, and known acoustic problems often prevents issues that your stage plot alone cannot anticipate.

## Common Traps

### No Backups for Critical Gear

This trap arises because carrying spares costs money and space, and nothing has failed yet. The harm is a show-stopping failure with no recovery, because the single cable, power supply, or instrument that failed had no backup. The false signal is that the gear has been reliable; the result is eventual disaster, because tour conditions guarantee failures that redundancy would have covered. Vibration, temperature swings, humidity changes, and rough loading all stress equipment far more than home or studio use, so reliability under gentle conditions is no predictor of reliability on the road.

### Unclear or Wrong Advance Information to Venues

Touring productions arrive without having confirmed gear and technical needs with the venue. The trap is that advance communication seems like bureaucracy. The harm is venues unprepared for the production's actual needs, producing last-minute scrambles, missing gear, and compromised shows, because the requirements were never clearly communicated in advance.

### Ad Hoc Setup Without Standardized Procedures

The crew sets up differently at every venue, figuring it out each time. The trap is that flexibility feels adaptive. The harm is slow, error-prone setups that eat into soundcheck and rest time, because the lack of standardization forced the crew to reinvent the process nightly rather than execute a proven procedure.

### Assuming Venue Power Is Adequate

Productions plug into venue power without checking capacity or condition. The trap is that power is power. The harm is damaged gear, tripped circuits, or dangerous conditions, because the venue's electrical system was not verified to handle the production's load, and power problems can destroy equipment and injure people.

## Self-Check

- Did I maintain a detailed gear inventory and provide stage plots and input lists?
- Did I confirm venue provision and specify backline requirements in advance?
- Did I build redundancy and carry spares for critical failure points?
- Did I standardize setup and striking procedures for consistency across venues?
- Did I confirm power availability and carry appropriate distribution and protection?
- Did I coordinate clearly and respectfully with venue and local crew?
