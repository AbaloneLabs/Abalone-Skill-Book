# Budget and Financial Management

A tour's financial success depends on disciplined budgeting and expense management, because touring has many costs that accumulate invisibly and can turn a seemingly successful tour into a loss. Agents advising on touring often focus on booking shows without building the financial model that determines whether the tour makes money. Tour finance is a judgment problem about forecasting revenue, controlling costs, and making decisions based on real numbers. This skill covers how to manage tour finances to profitability.

## Core Rules

### Build a Comprehensive Budget Before Committing

Before booking or committing to a tour, build a complete budget forecasting all revenue and all expenses, including travel, lodging, food, gear, crew, visas, insurance, and contingencies. Recommend modeling the tour financially first and evaluating whether it is viable, because a tour that looks busy may lose money once all real costs are included, and the budget reveals the truth before commitment.

### Track Per-Show Economics Honestly

Each show has its own economics: the guarantee or door split, the travel cost to reach it, and the local expenses. Recommend calculating whether each show covers its marginal cost, because some shows lose money once travel and expenses are included, and a tour full of shows that individually lose money is a losing tour regardless of how busy it looks.

### Distinguish Fixed From Variable Costs

Some tour costs are fixed, paid once regardless of show count, such as gear and crew salaries, and others are variable, scaling with shows and travel, such as fuel and per diems. Recommend understanding which costs are fixed and which are variable, because adding shows spreads fixed costs but adds variable costs, and the economics of adding or dropping a show depend on this distinction.

### Control the Costs That Spiral

Certain costs, food, fuel, last-minute purchases, and discretionary spending, spiral out of control on tour if unmanaged. Recommend setting and enforcing per diems and spending limits, and tracking expenses daily against budget, because uncontrolled discretionary spending silently erodes tour profitability, and daily tracking catches overspending before it becomes unmanageable.

### Understand Deal Structures: Guarantees Versus Door Splits

Shows are compensated through guarantees, a fixed fee, or door splits, a percentage of ticket revenue, or a combination. Recommend understanding each deal structure and its risk profile, because guarantees provide certainty while door splits offer upside but carry risk, and the right deal depends on your draw in each market and your risk tolerance.

### Build Contingency Into the Budget

Tours encounter unexpected costs: vehicle repairs, medical needs, canceled shows, gear replacement. Recommend building a contingency fund into the budget, typically ten to twenty percent, because a tour with no contingency is one problem away from a loss, and the unexpected is normal on tour, not exceptional. Track actual spending against the contingency line daily, because a contingency fund that gets quietly consumed by ordinary overspending is no longer available when the genuine emergency arrives.

## Common Traps

### Booking Shows Without Modeling the Full Tour Economics

This trap arises because booking feels like progress and each show seems like revenue. The harm is a tour that loses money because the full costs, travel, lodging, crew, were never modeled against the revenue. The false signal is a full calendar; the result is a financial loss, because the shows were booked without a viable financial model.

### Ignoring Per-Show Marginal Cost

Planners add shows because they fill dates, without checking whether each show covers the cost of getting to it. The trap is that more shows seem like more money. The harm is shows that lose money individually, dragging down the tour's overall profitability, because the travel and expense to reach them exceeded what they earned.

### Uncontrolled Discretionary Spending

Tour members spend on food, drinks, and purchases without limits or tracking. The trap is that these costs seem small individually. The harm is cumulative overspending that erodes the tour's profit margin, because the daily discretionary spending was never budgeted or tracked, and it silently consumed the margin the performances earned.

### No Contingency Fund for Inevitable Problems

The budget has no margin for the vehicle repair, the canceled show, or the medical expense. The trap is that contingency seems like unnecessary padding. The harm is that the first significant problem turns a profitable tour into a loss, because there was no reserve to absorb the cost that touring reliably produces.

## Self-Check

- Did I build a comprehensive budget forecasting all revenue and expenses before committing?
- Did I calculate per-show economics to ensure each show covers its marginal cost?
- Did I distinguish fixed from variable costs when evaluating adding or dropping shows?
- Did I set and enforce per diems and track expenses daily against budget?
- Did I understand the deal structures and risk profiles of each show's compensation?
- Did I build a contingency fund into the budget for inevitable problems?
