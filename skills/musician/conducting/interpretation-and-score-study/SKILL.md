# Interpretation and Score Study

An interpretation is not a layer of personal expression applied on top of the notes — it is a coherent reading of the score that emerges from understanding the music's structure, its harmonic and formal logic, its historical context, and its dramatic arc, and then making decisions about tempo, phrasing, dynamics, and emphasis that serve that understanding. The judgment problem is that a conductor who has not studied the score deeply has no basis for interpretation except personal whim, producing performances that are either generic (following unexamined defaults) or arbitrary (imposing gestures the score does not support). Deep score study reveals what the music wants — where the climax is, which voices are primary, how the harmony directs the phrasing — and the conductor's job is to realize that, not to override it. This skill covers the decisions that govern interpretation and score study: structural analysis, tempo and character decisions, voice prioritization, historical context, and forming a coherent dramatic reading.

## Core Rules

### Analyze the Score's Structure Before Forming an Interpretation

Before deciding on tempos, phrasings, or dynamics, analyze the score's architecture: the formal sections, the harmonic progression and its tension-resolution points, the thematic material and its development, the climaxes and cadences. The decision criterion is structural grounding: every interpretive decision should be traceable to something in the score. The judgment problem is that conductors often form interpretations from general impressions or from recordings they admire, without analyzing why the music behaves as it does, producing readings that feel right but are not grounded. Study the score as a structure first; let the interpretation emerge from the structure, not the reverse.

### Determine Tempo From Character and Dance-Type Evidence

Tempo markings (Allegro, Andante, Adagio) indicate character as much as speed, and metronome markings, dance types (waltz, sarabande, gigue), and historical conventions provide evidence for the appropriate range. The decision is to gather all tempo evidence — the marking, the dance type, the harmonic rhythm, the text (if vocal), the historical convention — and choose a tempo that satisfies all of it. The judgment criterion is coherence: does the tempo realize the character the marking indicates, at a speed the harmonic rhythm and the text can support? The trap is choosing tempo by what feels comfortable or by the inertia of a familiar recording, without checking it against the score's evidence — inertia from a familiar recording is not evidence.

### Identify Which Voices Are Primary at Each Moment

In a full score, not all voices are equal: at any given moment, one voice carries the primary melodic material, others provide harmonic support, and others are accompanimental. The decision is to identify the primary voice at each moment and to balance the ensemble so that voice is heard, while the supporting voices recede appropriately. The judgment criterion is textural clarity: can the listener perceive the primary line, or is it buried by voices the score treats as secondary? The trap is treating all voices as equal, which produces a muddy texture where nothing is primary. Mark the primary voice in the score and shape the balance to feature it.

### Ground Interpretive Decisions in Historical Context

The appropriate performance style depends on the music's historical period and original performance context: a Baroque suite demands different articulation, ornamentation, and vibrato than a Romantic symphony; a Classical symphony demands different balance and phrasing than a twentieth-century work. The decision is to research the conventions of the era — instrumentation, tempo practice, articulation, ornamentation — and to calibrate the interpretation to historical evidence, while acknowledging where informed departure is justified. The judgment criterion is informed performance: are your choices grounded in knowledge of how this music was originally performed, or are you applying generic modern defaults? Study the period's performance practice before deciding.

### Form a Coherent Dramatic Reading of the Whole Work

A performance should have a dramatic arc — a shape of tension and release, building and receding, that carries the listener from beginning to end — and that arc should be planned, not left to chance. The decision is to map the work's intensity across its entire span: where is the main climax, where are the secondary peaks, where are the moments of repose, and how does each section relate to the whole? The judgment criterion is large-scale coherence: does the performance tell a story with a destination, or does it move from moment to moment without accumulation? Plan the dramatic shape before the first rehearsal, so every local decision serves the whole.

### Reconcile Personal Vision With Fidelity to the Score

An interpretation is neither slavish obedience to the notation (which ignores the fact that notation is incomplete and requires the performer's choices) nor free rein over the music (which ignores the composer's intent). The decision is to find the balance: respect the score's explicit markings and structural logic, while making the choices the score leaves open (exact tempo within the marking's range, precise dynamic shaping within the marked level, phrasing within the notated rhythm). The judgment criterion is faithful creativity: are your personal choices in the service of the music the score presents, or are they imposed on it? Let the score constrain your choices, then express yourself within those constraints.

## Common Traps

### Forming an Interpretation From Recordings Rather Than the Score

A conductor listens to a respected recording, internalizes its tempo, phrasing, and balance choices, and reproduces them without analyzing whether the score supports those choices, producing a derivative performance rather than an interpretation. The false signal is that the performance sounds polished. The harm is an interpretation that is second-hand, carrying another conductor's decisions without understanding, and that may misread the score if the model recording did. The mechanism is substituting listening for studying; the remedy is to form the interpretation from score analysis first, then consult recordings to test and refine, not to replace.

### Choosing Tempo by Comfort or Habit Rather Than Evidence

The conductor chooses a tempo that feels comfortable to conduct or that matches a familiar recording, without checking it against the score's tempo marking, dance type, harmonic rhythm, or text, and the tempo may be wrong for the music's character. The false signal is that the tempo feels natural. The harm is a performance whose character does not match the music's intent, because the tempo was chosen by convenience rather than evidence. The mechanism is unexamined default; the fix is to gather all tempo evidence from the score and choose a tempo that satisfies it.

### Treating All Voices as Equal and Producing a Muddy Texture

The conductor balances the ensemble so all voices are equally audible, expecting fairness to produce clarity, but the result is a muddy texture where no voice is primary and the main melodic line is buried. The false signal is that all parts are present. The harm is a performance where the listener cannot perceive the music's hierarchy, because the score's voice prioritization was ignored. The mechanism is conflating equality with clarity; the remedy is to identify the primary voice at each moment and balance the ensemble to feature it.

### Imposing Dramatic Gestures the Score Does Not Support

The conductor adds extreme ritardandos, exaggerated dynamic contrasts, or tempo changes that feel dramatic but that the score's structure and markings do not support, producing a performance that feels imposed rather than emergent. The false signal is that the gestures are expressive. The harm is a performance that distorts the music's own dramatic logic, substituting the conductor's drama for the composer's. The mechanism is ego overriding fidelity; the fix is to ground every dramatic gesture in the score's structure and markings, and to let the music's drama lead.

### Ignoring Historical Performance Practice

The conductor applies modern orchestral defaults — heavy vibrato, legato phrasing, equal-tempered tuning, large string sections — to music of a period where different conventions prevailed, producing a performance that is technically clean but stylistically anachronistic. The false signal is that the ensemble sounds good. The harm is a performance that misrepresents the music's intended character, because the period's conventions were not consulted. The mechanism is applying one performance aesthetic universally; the remedy is to research the period's practice and calibrate the interpretation to historical evidence.

## Self-Check

- Did I analyze the score's formal, harmonic, and thematic structure before forming interpretive decisions, so every choice is traceable to the score?
- Is my tempo grounded in the score's evidence (marking, dance type, harmonic rhythm, text, historical convention), rather than chosen by comfort or habit?
- Have I identified the primary voice at each moment and planned the ensemble balance to feature it, rather than treating all voices as equal?
- Are my performance choices calibrated to the music's historical period and original performance context, rather than applying generic modern defaults?
- Have I mapped the dramatic arc across the entire work — climax, secondary peaks, repose — so the performance tells a coherent story with a destination?
- Are my personal interpretive choices in the service of the score's structure and markings, or are some imposed on the music without justification?
- If asked why I chose this tempo, this phrasing, or this balance, can I point to specific evidence in the score, or do my reasons reduce to preference?
