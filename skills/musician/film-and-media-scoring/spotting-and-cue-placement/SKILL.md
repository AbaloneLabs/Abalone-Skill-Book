# Spotting and Cue Placement

Spotting is the collaborative process of deciding, scene by scene, where music plays in a film or media project, where it does not, and what role it serves in each moment. The judgment problem is that inexperienced composers want to score everything — to fill every scene with music — when the most powerful scoring decisions are often about absence: where to leave the scene to dialogue, sound design, or silence. Over-scoring drowns the film; under-scoring leaves it emotionally unsupported. The agent must think like a dramatist, not just a composer, must align with the director's vision before writing a note, and must build a precise cue list that becomes the working contract for the score.

## Core Rules

### Decide Where Music Should Be Absent Before Deciding Where It Should Play

The most common spotting error is scoring too much. Music is most powerful when it is not constant; its entry has impact precisely because it was absent, and its exit creates space. Before deciding what to score, walk the cut and identify the scenes that should have no score: scenes carried by dialogue, scenes where sound design or source music is the point, and scenes where silence creates tension or intimacy. The absence of music is a deliberate choice that should be made consciously, not by default. A film where music never stops becomes emotionally monotonous no matter how good the cues are.

**Decision criteria:** For every scene, ask whether music serves the drama or whether it competes with it. Identify and protect the scenes that should be silent or source-only before scoring the rest.

### Align With the Director's Vision Before Composing

The spotting session with the director is where the creative contract is established: what each cue should feel like, what it should emphasize or withhold, whether it should be diegetic or non-diegetic, and what the emotional arc of the film demands. Composing before this alignment risks delivering cues that miss the director's intent and must be redone. The spotting session should produce, for each cue, a shared understanding of function, emotion, and boundaries (entry and exit points). Document the decisions; do not rely on memory.

**Decision criteria:** Has a spotting session (or equivalent detailed conversation) occurred for every cue, producing a documented agreement on function, emotion, and boundaries? Is there a shared understanding before composing begins?

### Define Each Cue's Dramatic Function, Not Just Its Emotion

A cue's job is not simply to create an emotion; it serves a specific dramatic function: establishing location or time, building tension before a reveal, accompanying action, commenting ironically on a scene, connecting scenes via a thematic bridge, or providing a character's subjective interior. Naming the function sharpens the composition, because different functions demand different musical approaches. A tension cue and a location-establishment cue may both feel "dark," but they are built differently. Define the function of each cue in the cue list.

**Decision criteria:** For each cue, can you state its dramatic function in a phrase (e.g., "build dread before the door opens," "establish the 1940s nightclub")? Does the function, not just the emotion, guide the composition?

### Set Precise Entry and Exit Points With Timecode

Every cue must have defined entry and exit points, referenced to the locked cut's timecode (SMPTE or frame-accurate). "Starts when she enters" is not a cue point; "01:14:22:06" is. Vague boundaries cause sync problems, last-minute edits, and disputes. The cue list should record, for each cue: cue number, title, timecode in, timecode out, duration, and function. This list is the working document that the composer, editor, and music editor all reference. Any change to the cut requires re-spotting affected cues and updating the list.

**Decision criteria:** Does every cue have frame-accurate entry and exit timecode recorded in a cue list? Are durations calculated and verified against the locked cut?

### Respect the Cut and the Sound Design as Collaborators

Music does not exist in isolation in film; it shares the soundtrack with dialogue, sound effects, and ambience, and it shares the rhythm with the editing. A cue that fights the cut (entering on a weak beat against an edit, or conflicting with a key sound effect moment) undermines both. Spotting should account for where dialogue is dense (music may need to thin out), where sound design carries the moment (music may need to exit or simplify), and where edits create natural musical boundaries. The composer who ignores the rest of the soundtrack produces cues that are mixed down or cut.

**Decision criteria:** For each cue, have you considered the dialogue density, key sound effects, and edit rhythm? Does the cue cooperate with the rest of the soundtrack rather than competing?

### Plan Thematic Deployment Across the Cue List

A film score is not a collection of unrelated cues; it is a network of themes and motifs deployed strategically across the narrative. Spotting is when this deployment is planned: where the main theme first appears, where it returns, where a character's motif is introduced, where themes combine or transform. A cue list without thematic planning produces a score that feels like background wallpaper rather than a dramatic structure. Map the themes to the cues during spotting, so each cue knows what motivic material it carries.

**Decision criteria:** Does the cue list indicate which themes or motifs each cue carries, and is the deployment planned across the narrative arc? Is there a thematic map, not just a list of cues?

## Common Traps

### Scoring Everything and Drowning the Film

The instinct to fill every scene with music produces a soundtrack that never breathes, where music loses its impact because it is constant, and where the audience tunes it out as background. The trap mechanism is that each scene seems to "need" music, so the composer adds it everywhere. The harm is emotional monotony and a film that feels musically suffocated. The corrective is consciously choosing silence and source-only scenes.

### Composing Before Aligning With the Director

A composer who writes cues based on their own reading of the film, before a detailed spotting conversation, often delivers work that misses the director's intent and must be redone. The trap mechanism is that composing feels like the real work and spotting feels like a meeting. The harm is wasted effort and a strained relationship. The corrective is treating the spotting session as the foundation.

### Vague Cue Boundaries Without Frame-Accurate Timecode

Cue boundaries described as "around when she sits down" guarantee sync problems and disputes when the cut is conformed. The trap mechanism is that the description feels clear in the room. The harm is last-minute fixes and conform errors. The corrective is frame-accurate timecode in the cue list.

### Ignoring Dialogue and Sound Design Density

A cue that is dense and loud under heavy dialogue, or that competes with a key sound effect, will be mixed down until it is inaudible or will be cut entirely. The trap mechanism is that the cue sounds great in isolation. The harm is a cue that does not survive the mix. The corrective is accounting for the rest of the soundtrack during spotting and composition.

### No Thematic Plan, Just Cue-by-Cue Scoring

Writing each cue fresh, without reference to a thematic plan, produces a score that feels like a collection of unrelated pieces rather than a coherent dramatic structure. The trap mechanism is that each cue is satisfying in isolation. The harm is a score that lacks the connective tissue that makes film music powerful. The corrective is planning thematic deployment during spotting.

## Self-Check

- Have I consciously identified scenes that should have no score, and protected them from musical filling?
- Was a spotting session held with the director, producing documented agreement on each cue's function, emotion, and boundaries?
- Does each cue have a stated dramatic function (not just an emotion) that guides the composition?
- Does every cue have frame-accurate entry and exit timecode recorded in a cue list?
- Have I accounted for dialogue density, sound design, and edit rhythm in each cue's placement and density?
- Is there a thematic map showing which motifs each cue carries and how they deploy across the narrative?
- If the cut changes, is there a process to re-spot affected cues and update the cue list?
