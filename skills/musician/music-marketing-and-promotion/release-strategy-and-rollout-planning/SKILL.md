# Release Strategy and Rollout Planning

A release is not the day music appears on streaming platforms; it is a multi-week campaign designed to build anticipation, capture pre-saves, generate press and playlist attention, and convert listener interest into sustained engagement. The judgment problem is that independent artists routinely treat the release date as the finish line, push everything live at once, and watch the work vanish into algorithmic silence within 48 hours. A good rollout sequences content so that each asset compounds the last, aligns with platform behavior (which rewards sustained activity over a spike), and gives press, radio, and playlist curators lead time measured in weeks, not hours. The agent must plan backward from the release date with realistic lead times and must resist the temptation to dump everything at once because it feels like momentum.

## Core Rules

### Plan Backward From the Release Date With Realistic Lead Times

Every asset — audio masters, artwork, music video, press photos, bio, press release, pitch emails — must be finished weeks before release because distributors and curators require lead time. Distributors typically need 2-4 weeks to process and deliver to all platforms. Playlist curators and press need 3-6 weeks of lead time to consider and schedule coverage. A release planned with a two-week runway will miss every external opportunity. Build the timeline by listing every dependency and its required lead time, then working backward from the target date.

**Decision criteria:** For each external party (distributor, curator, journalist, radio), confirm their required lead time and ensure the asset is delivered before that deadline. If any lead time cannot be met, move the release date, not the lead time.

### Sequence Singles to Build Algorithmic and Audience Momentum

Modern streaming platforms reward sustained activity. Releasing one single every 3-6 weeks before an album drop keeps the artist in algorithmic favor, accumulates pre-saves, and gives each song its own promotional window. Dumping an entire album at once front-loads all activity into one spike that the algorithm interprets as a brief event, not sustained growth. The single-by-single approach also lets the artist learn which songs resonate and adjust the campaign.

**Decision criteria:** Default to a singles-led rollout for album campaigns. Reserve full-album drops for artists with an existing large audience where the album itself is the event. Space singles to maintain momentum without exhausting the audience (typically 3-6 weeks apart).

### Build a Pre-Save and Pre-Release Conversion Funnel

Pre-saves convert intent into action before release day, when attention is highest. A pre-save campaign captures listeners who discover the music during the promotional window and ensures the track lands in their library on day one, which boosts first-day streams and signals to the algorithm. The funnel should include a pre-save link, an email list signup, and social follow prompts. Without this, attention generated during promotion is lost because there is no mechanism to capture it.

**Decision criteria:** Every release should have a pre-save link live at least 2-3 weeks before release, promoted at every content touchpoint. Track conversion and follow up with captured contacts.

### Give Press and Playlists Exclusive Advance Access With an Angle

Journalists and curators will not cover a release they hear about the day it drops; they need to hear it early and they need a reason to write about it. An advance stream (private link, watermarked promo, or embargoed release) with a clear angle — a story, a premiere offer, a visual asset — gives them material to work with. A generic "here's my new song" email with no angle and no lead time will be ignored. The pitch must answer why this matters now and to whom.

**Decision criteria:** For every press or playlist target, prepare an advance with at least 3-4 weeks lead time and a specific angle (premiere, feature, interview, playlist addition). Do not send the public link as the pitch.

### Coordinate Assets So Nothing Is the Bottleneck

A rollout fails when one asset is late and blocks the rest. The video isn't done, so the premiere can't be pitched, so the press runs dry, so the single underperforms. Map every asset's dependency: masters must be done before distribution submission; artwork before distributor submission; video before premiere pitch; press photos before press campaign. Identify the critical path and ensure the slowest asset sets a realistic date, rather than promising a date and missing assets.

**Decision criteria:** List every asset and its dependencies. The release date must be later than the latest-finishing critical asset plus the required lead time. Never set a date that assumes an asset will be finished "on time" without buffer.

### Define Success Metrics Before the Campaign Starts

Without defined metrics, every release feels like a failure because expectations are undefined. Define what success looks like for this specific release and artist stage: first-week streams, pre-saves, playlist additions, press features, ticket sales, follower growth. These should be realistic for the artist's current audience size, not benchmarked against major-label artists. Documenting the target lets the team evaluate the campaign honestly and learn for the next one.

**Decision criteria:** Before launch, write down 2-4 concrete success metrics with target numbers based on the artist's historical data or comparable artists. After release, compare actuals to targets and document what worked.

## Common Traps

### Treating Release Day as the Goal Instead of the Starting Line

Many artists pour all energy into the weeks before release and go quiet on release day, assuming the work is done. In reality, release day is when the promotional work intensifies: the algorithm needs signals, playlists need nudging, and the audience needs repeated reminders. The trap mechanism is that the buildup feels like the whole campaign, so the post-release period is neglected. The harm is a release that peaks on day one and decays immediately because no sustained push followed. The corrective is to plan at least 2-4 weeks of post-release content and outreach.

### Dumping All Content at Once for a "Big Splash"

Releasing the album, all videos, all social posts, and all press at the same moment creates a single spike that the algorithm and audience both interpret as a brief event. The false signal is a busy release day that feels like momentum. The harm is that there is nothing left to sustain attention in the following weeks, and the algorithm deprioritizes the artist after the spike. The corrective is to stage content over weeks so activity is sustained.

### Setting the Release Date Before Assets Are Ready

Artists often pick a meaningful date (a birthday, an anniversary) and then scramble to finish masters, art, and video in time, submitting to the distributor days before with no lead time. The trap is that the date feels important, so it overrides operational reality. The harm is botched distribution (tracks missing on release day), no press or playlist coverage (no lead time), and a campaign that looks amateurish. The corrective is to set the date based on asset readiness plus lead time, not sentiment.

### Pitching Press and Playlists With No Angle and No Lead Time

A mass email saying "my new single is out Friday, please cover it" sent on Wednesday will be ignored by every serious curator and journalist. The trap mechanism is that sending the pitch feels like doing the work, but without an angle and lead time it has near-zero effect. The harm is wasted effort and a false conclusion that "press doesn't work for independent artists." The corrective is targeted pitches with specific angles and 3-4 weeks lead time.

### Ignoring Post-Release Data and Repeating the Same Strategy

After a release underperforms, the temptation is to move on to the next one without analyzing what happened. But the data — where streams came from, which playlists drove discovery, which content converted, where listeners dropped off — is the most valuable output of the campaign. The trap is treating each release as an isolated event rather than a learning cycle. The harm is repeating the same ineffective strategy indefinitely. The corrective is a structured post-release review against the defined success metrics.

## Self-Check

- Did I plan the timeline backward from the release date with realistic lead times for the distributor (2-4 weeks) and press/playlists (3-6 weeks)?
- Is the rollout sequenced (singles leading to album) to build sustained momentum rather than a single spike?
- Is there a pre-save campaign live at least 2-3 weeks before release, promoted at every content touchpoint?
- Did every press and playlist pitch include a specific angle and advance access with adequate lead time?
- Have I mapped all asset dependencies and confirmed the release date accounts for the slowest critical asset plus lead time?
- Did I define 2-4 concrete success metrics with target numbers before the campaign launched?
- Is there a planned 2-4 week post-release content and outreach schedule, not just pre-release buildup?
