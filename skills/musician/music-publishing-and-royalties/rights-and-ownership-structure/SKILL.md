# Rights and Ownership Structure

Music rights are complex, split between composition and recording, and divided among songwriters, performers, publishers, labels, and producers. Agents advising musicians on rights often give vague guidance that leaves ownership ambiguous, which leads to disputes when money appears. Understanding and structuring rights clearly from the start is essential legal and business hygiene. This skill covers the judgment needed to navigate rights and ownership without creating future conflicts.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Composition Rights From Master Rights

There are two distinct copyright bundles in every recorded song. The composition, the underlying melody and lyrics, generates publishing royalties. The master, the specific sound recording, generates recording royalties. Recommend always separating these two in any discussion of ownership and revenue, because they are owned, licensed, and monetized differently, and confusing them produces agreements that fail to capture or correctly assign the right revenue streams.

### Document Splits Before the Work Is Released

Agree and document who owns what percentage of the composition and the master before the music is released or generates revenue. Recommend using split sheets signed by all contributors before or immediately after the writing or recording session, because undocumented splits become he-said-she-said disputes when money appears, and memories and intentions diverge under the pressure of unexpected income. Early documentation prevents later conflict.

### Understand the Role of Publishers and Administration

A publisher manages and monetizes composition rights, collecting mechanical, performance, and sync royalties in exchange for a percentage. Administration deals collect and distribute royalties without taking ownership. Recommend understanding what you are giving up in a publishing deal versus an admin deal, and whether the services justify the cost, because signing away publishing ownership is a significant long-term decision that should not be made casually.

### Account for Producer and Collaborator Points

Producers, featured artists, and other contributors often receive points, a percentage of master royalties or ownership, for their work. Recommend agreeing on and documenting these contributions in writing before release, including whether they apply to composition or master, because informal handshake deals with producers become disputes when the record succeeds, and unclear contributor rights block deals and revenue.

### Register Works With the Correct Organizations

Songs and recordings must be registered with the appropriate organizations, performance rights organizations for composition performance royalties, mechanical rights organizations for mechanical royalties, and similar bodies in each territory, to ensure royalties are collected. Recommend registering every work promptly and accurately with metadata identifying all writers and their shares, because unregistered works do not generate the royalties they earn, and the money is lost or delayed.

### Get Agreements in Writing, Always

Verbal agreements about rights and splits are unenforceable in practice and lead to disputes. Recommend putting every ownership, split, and revenue-sharing agreement in writing, signed by all parties, before the work is exploited, because the cost of documentation is trivial compared to the cost of a dispute, and written agreements protect all parties by making expectations explicit and enforceable.

## Common Traps

### Leaving Splits Undocumented Until Money Appears

This trap arises because discussing splits feels awkward and the music is not earning yet. The harm is that when the music does earn, contributors' memories and claims diverge, producing disputes that can destroy relationships and block revenue. The false signal is that everyone agrees now; the result is conflict later, because the agreement was never written down while goodwill existed.

### Confusing Composition and Master Rights

Musicians and advisors treat all rights as one thing, failing to distinguish the composition from the recording. The trap is that the distinction seems technical. The harm is agreements and deals that assign or retain the wrong rights, because the two copyrights were conflated, and revenue streams are misallocated or lost entirely.

### Signing Away Publishing Without Understanding the Terms

Musicians sign publishing deals because they feel like validation or advancement, without understanding what ownership they are giving up. The trap is that the deal feels like success. The harm is long-term loss of composition revenue and control, because the publisher now owns a share of the songs potentially forever, and the terms were never evaluated against alternatives like administration deals.

### Failing to Register Works and Losing Royalties

Musicians release music without registering it with the appropriate rights organizations. The trap is that registration seems like bureaucracy. The harm is that the music earns royalties that are never collected, because the works were never registered, and the money sits unclaimed or is distributed to others, representing permanent loss of income the music genuinely earned.

## Self-Check

- Did I distinguish and address composition rights and master rights separately?
- Did I document splits with signed split sheets before release?
- Did I understand the difference between publishing and administration deals?
- Did I account for and document producer and collaborator points?
- Did I register every work with the correct rights organizations with accurate metadata?
- Did I put every ownership and revenue-sharing agreement in writing?
