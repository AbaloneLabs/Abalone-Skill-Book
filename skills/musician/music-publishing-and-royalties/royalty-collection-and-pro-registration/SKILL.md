# Royalty Collection and PRO Registration

Musicians lose enormous amounts of earned income not because their music does not generate royalties but because they fail to register, submit incorrect metadata, or do not know which organizations collect which royalties. Agents advising on royalties often give general encouragement to register without walking through the specific, fragmented system of collection. Royalty collection is a bureaucratic discipline where accuracy and completeness determine whether earned money actually arrives. This skill covers the judgment needed to capture royalties effectively.

## Core Rules

### Register With the Correct Organizations for Each Royalty Type

Different royalties are collected by different organizations: performance rights organizations collect public performance royalties for composition; mechanical rights organizations collect mechanical royalties for reproduction; neighboring rights organizations collect royalties for the public performance of the master recording. Recommend identifying and registering with every organization relevant to your works and territory, because each collects a different stream, and registering with one does not capture the others.

### Submit Accurate and Complete Metadata With Every Registration

Royalties are matched to works and payees using metadata: song title, writer names, publisher names, ISWC and ISRC codes, split percentages. Recommend ensuring every registration includes complete, accurate, consistent metadata, because errors and inconsistencies cause royalties to be unmatched, delayed, or paid to the wrong party, and metadata problems are the single largest cause of lost royalties.

### Register Works Before They Are Exploited, Not After

Registration should happen before the work is publicly performed, streamed, sold, or synced, because royalties generated before registration may be lost or become very difficult to claim retroactively. Recommend registering works immediately upon completion or before release, because the systems match royalties to registered works, and unregistered works earn money that goes unclaimed or is distributed elsewhere.

### Understand International Collection and Sub-Publishing

Royalties are generated in every territory where the music is used, and collecting internationally requires either direct registration in each territory or sub-publishing and neighboring rights deals that cover multiple territories. Recommend planning international collection, especially for music with global streaming or performance, because international royalties are substantial and are lost without territory-specific registration or administration.

### Track and Audit Royalty Statements

Royalty statements contain errors, and uncollected or misallocated royalties are common. Recommend regularly reviewing royalty statements, cross-checking against known usage, and querying discrepancies, because passive trust in the system results in lost income, and active tracking catches errors that would otherwise go unnoticed and uncorrected.

### Keep Registration Current as Catalog and Collaborations Change

As new works are created, new collaborations occur, and splits are adjusted, registrations must be updated. Recommend maintaining a current, organized record of all works, splits, and registrations, and updating organizations promptly when changes occur, because outdated registrations cause royalties to be paid according to old or wrong information, and the system does not correct itself. When co-writers disagree on splits or fail to register their share, the entire work's payment can be held or misrouted, so confirm splits in writing with all collaborators before registration and resolve disputes early.

## Common Traps

### Registering With One Organization and Assuming All Royalties Are Covered

This trap arises because registering with a performance rights organization feels like the complete step. The harm is that mechanical, sync, and neighboring rights royalties go uncollected, because they are handled by different organizations that were never engaged. The false signal is that registration is done; the result is partial collection that leaves substantial earned income on the table.

### Submitting Inconsistent Metadata Across Registrations

Works are registered with slightly different titles, writer names, or splits across organizations. The trap is that minor inconsistencies seem harmless. The harm is unmatched royalties, because the systems use metadata to match usage to works, and inconsistencies cause failures that route money to the wrong place or leave it unclaimed.

### Registering After the Work Is Already Earning

Musicians release first and register later, assuming they can backdate. The trap is that the music is already generating royalties. The harm is lost income, because many organizations do not pay retroactively or have claim windows that expire, and royalties generated before registration are forfeited or extremely difficult to recover.

### Ignoring International Royalties

Musicians focus on domestic collection and ignore the territories where their music streams or performs internationally. The trap is that international collection seems complex and optional. The harm is the loss of all international royalties, which for music with global reach can equal or exceed domestic income, because no mechanism was set up to collect across territories.

## Self-Check

- Did I register with every organization relevant to each royalty type and territory?
- Did I submit complete, accurate, consistent metadata with every registration?
- Did I register works before they are publicly exploited, not after?
- Did I plan international collection through direct registration or sub-publishing?
- Did I regularly review and audit royalty statements for errors?
- Did I keep registrations current as the catalog and collaborations change?
