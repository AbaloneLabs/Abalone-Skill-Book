# Sync Licensing and Placement Deals

Sync licensing, placing music in film, television, advertising, and games, is one of the most lucrative revenue streams for musicians, but it requires understanding the deal structures, the pitch process, and the rights clearances that make placement possible. Agents advising on sync often encourage pitching without ensuring the music is clearable or the deals are understood. Sync is a business where clearance readiness and deal literacy determine success. This skill covers the judgment needed to pursue and negotiate sync placements effectively.

## Core Rules

### Ensure Music Is Clearable Before Pitching

Supervisors will not use music they cannot clear quickly, and unclearable music is useless for sync regardless of quality. Recommend ensuring every track you pitch has clear ownership, documented splits, all samples cleared, and all participants' approvals, because a track that cannot be cleared in the timeframe the production needs will be rejected, and burned bridges with supervisors who wasted time are hard to rebuild.

### Understand Sync Fees Versus Ongoing Royalties

A sync deal typically involves a sync fee, a one-time payment for the right to synchronize the music, and ongoing performance royalties generated when the placed music is broadcast or streamed. Recommend understanding both, negotiating the sync fee appropriately, and ensuring performance royalties are registered so they are collected, because focusing only on the sync fee misses the ongoing royalty stream that often exceeds the initial fee over time.

### Prepare Metadata and Instrumental Versions

Supervisors need clean metadata, accurate tempo, mood, and instrumentation tags, and instrumental versions of vocal tracks, because they often need music without vocals to sit under dialogue. Recommend preparing professional metadata and instrumental versions for every track before pitching, because unprepared music, no matter how good, is passed over for prepared music that the supervisor can use immediately.

### Evaluate Exclusive Versus Non-Exclusive Representation

Sync libraries and pitch services may offer exclusive deals, taking the right to represent and place your music exclusively, or non-exclusive deals allowing you to pitch elsewhere. Recommend evaluating the tradeoff: exclusive deals may come with better pitching and catalog prestige but limit your options, while non-exclusive deals preserve flexibility but may mean less focused pitching. The choice depends on the specific deal and your catalog strategy.

### Build Relationships With Supervisors and Pitch Appropriately

Sync is a relationship business, and supervisors work with catalogs and writers they trust. Recommend building genuine relationships by pitching relevant, well-targeted music rather than mass-submitting, because supervisors ignore mass submissions and respond to targeted pitches that show understanding of their project's needs, and a reputation for relevant pitching earns future attention.

### Read and Understand Every Deal Term

Sync deals contain terms about usage scope, duration, territories, media, and fees that determine what the placement is worth and what rights you are granting. Recommend reading and understanding every term, and consulting a professional for significant deals, because terms that seem standard may grant broad rights for low fees, and the value of a placement depends entirely on the terms you agree to.

## Common Traps

### Pitching Unclearable Music and Burning Supervisor Relationships

This trap arises because musicians want their music placed and pitch eagerly without ensuring clearance. The harm is that supervisors waste time on music they cannot use and stop considering future pitches from that source. The false signal is that pitching more increases chances; the result is damaged relationships, because the music was not clearable and the supervisor's time was wasted.

### Focusing Only on the Sync Fee and Missing Royalties

Musicians negotiate the sync fee and forget to register or track the ongoing performance royalties. The trap is that the fee feels like the complete payment. The harm is the loss of ongoing royalty income that often exceeds the fee over time, because the performance royalties generated by broadcast and streaming were never registered or collected.

### Submitting Without Metadata or Instrumental Versions

Musicians submit raw tracks without metadata or instrumental versions. The trap is that the music speaks for itself. The harm is immediate rejection, because supervisors need searchable metadata and instrumental versions to use the music, and unprepared submissions cannot be evaluated or used efficiently.

### Signing Exclusive Deals Without Evaluating the Tradeoff

Musicians sign exclusive representation deals because they feel like opportunity, without evaluating what they are giving up. The trap is that exclusivity feels like commitment from the library. The harm is locked-up music that cannot be pitched elsewhere, potentially earning less than it would under non-exclusive representation, because the deal's terms were not weighed against alternatives.

## Self-Check

- Did I ensure every pitched track is fully clearable with documented rights?
- Did I understand both the sync fee and the ongoing royalty structure?
- Did I prepare professional metadata and instrumental versions for all tracks?
- Did I evaluate exclusive versus non-exclusive representation for my situation?
- Did I build supervisor relationships through targeted, relevant pitching?
- Did I read and understand every deal term before agreeing?
