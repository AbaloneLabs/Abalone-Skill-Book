# Timing and Edit to Picture

In commercial music, the music must fit a precise duration and align with specific visual moments: cuts, product reveals, logo appearances, and emotional beats. Agents advising on scoring to picture often compose freely and then struggle to fit the music to the edit. But scoring to picture is a constraint problem where the timing is fixed and the music must serve it. Getting this right requires planning tempo, structure, and hit points before composing freely. This skill covers the judgment needed to fit music to picture precisely.

## Core Rules

### Identify the Key Sync Points Before Composing

Before writing, identify the visual moments the music must support: the hard cuts, the product reveal, the logo, the emotional turn. Recommend listing these sync points with their timecodes and planning the music's structure around them, because music that hits these points feels integrated with the picture while music that misses them feels disconnected, and the sync points are non-negotiable constraints.

### Choose a Tempo That Fits the Duration and Cuts

The tempo determines where musical beats fall relative to the picture, and the right tempo places beats naturally near the key cuts. Recommend calculating tempos that align the musical meter with the edit, often by dividing the duration by the desired number of bars and adjusting, because a tempo chosen without regard for the picture produces beats that fight the cuts, while a calculated tempo makes sync feel effortless.

### Plan the Musical Structure to Land on Sync Points

Once the tempo and sync points are known, plan where phrases, sections, and hits fall so that important musical moments coincide with important visual moments. Recommend mapping the musical form to the picture structure, placing downbeats, cadences, and accents at key cuts, because structure planned to the picture lands naturally while structure imposed after the fact requires awkward adjustments.

### Build Flexibility for Last-Minute Edit Changes

Commercials change: cuts move, durations shift, the client revises the edit. Recommend building the music with modular sections and flexible transition points so that changes can be accommodated without rewriting everything, because rigidly constructed music breaks when the picture changes, and the ability to adapt quickly is essential in commercial work.

### Shape Hits and Accents to Visual Action

At key moments, the music should accent the visual action: a sting on a logo reveal, a swell on an emotional peak, a hit on a hard cut. Recommend designing these hits deliberately, matching their character to the visual moment, because well-placed hits unify music and picture while generic music playing under key moments wastes the opportunity to reinforce the visual.

### Account for Dialogue and Voiceover Frequency Space

Commercials usually contain voiceover or dialogue, and the music must leave space for it. Recommend carving the music's frequency range so the voice sits clearly on top, avoiding instruments and frequencies that compete with speech, because music that masks the voiceover undermines the commercial's message, regardless of how good it sounds.

## Common Traps

### Composing Freely Then Forcing the Fit

This trap arises when composers write music they like and then try to squeeze it to the picture. The harm is awkward timing, missed sync points, and music that feels imposed on the visual rather than integrated with it. The false signal is that the music sounds good in isolation; the result is a spot where the music and picture fight each other, because the timing was never a design constraint.

### Choosing Tempo Without Regard for Cuts

Composers pick a tempo that feels right musically but that places beats between the cuts rather than on them. The trap is that the tempo seems fine musically. The harm is that no musical moment aligns with a visual moment, and the music feels disconnected from the picture, because the tempo was not chosen to serve the edit.

### Rigid Construction That Breaks on Edit Changes

Composers build music tightly synced to one version of the edit, with no flexibility. The trap is that tight sync feels precise. The harm is that when the client changes a cut, the music no longer fits and must be rebuilt, because there was no modularity to absorb the change, and the revision becomes expensive.

### Music Masking the Voiceover

Composers fill the frequency spectrum with rich music, leaving no space for the voice. The trap is that the music sounds full. The harm is an unintelligible voiceover, because the music's frequencies mask the speech, and the commercial's message is lost under the music that was supposed to support it.

## Self-Check

- Did I identify the key sync points and timecodes before composing?
- Did I choose a tempo that aligns the musical meter with the cuts?
- Did I plan the musical structure so important moments land on visual moments?
- Did I build modular flexibility to accommodate edit changes?
- Did I design hits and accents to match key visual actions?
- Did I carve frequency space for the voiceover or dialogue?
