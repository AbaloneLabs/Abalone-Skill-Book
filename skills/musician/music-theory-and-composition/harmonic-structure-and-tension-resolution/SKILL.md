# Harmonic Structure and Tension-Resolution

Harmony is the engine of musical direction. A composer who treats chords as decorative colors underneath a melody will write music that wanders; a composer who treats harmony as a tension-resolution system will write music that moves the listener through time with intention. The judgment problem is that the same chord can function in several ways depending on context, and the difference between a progression that compels and one that meanders is rarely about which chords are chosen — it is about how tension is built, how long it is sustained, and where and how it resolves. Beginners often reach for "interesting" chords when the real problem is weak functional direction, and they reach for familiar clichés when the real problem is that the harmony never establishes enough tension to make the resolution satisfying. This skill covers the decisions that govern harmonic structure: functional roles, harmonic rhythm, voice leading, cadence planning, and the deliberate management of expectation.

## Core Rules

### Establish Functional Direction Before Choosing Chords

Before selecting specific voicings or extensions, determine the functional role of each chord in the phrase. Ask whether each harmony is tonic (rest), predominant (preparation), or dominant (tension), and whether the phrase is moving away from tonic, toward tonic, or resting on tonic. A progression with clear functional direction creates expectation; a sequence of chords with no functional logic creates stasis regardless of how colorful the chords are. The decision criterion is directional: if you remove the melody, does the harmony still tell you where the phrase is going? If not, the functional skeleton is too weak and no amount of reharmonization will fix it. Compose the functional map first — I, IV, V, I — then layer substitutions and color.

### Manage Harmonic Rhythm as an Independent Variable

Harmonic rhythm — how frequently chords change — is a separate compositional parameter from which chords are used, and it often does more emotional work than the chords themselves. Fast harmonic rhythm (one chord per beat) creates urgency and instability; slow harmonic rhythm (one chord per four bars) creates space and weight. The trap is letting harmonic rhythm be an accidental byproduct of the melody rather than a deliberate choice. A common strong move is to slow the harmonic rhythm approaching a cadence to amplify the arrival, or to accelerate it during a build to increase tension. Decide the rate of change deliberately, and vary it across sections so the listener perceives shifts in energy.

### Plan Cadences to Control the Sense of Arrival

A cadence is the punctuation of a harmonic phrase, and its type determines how final the arrival feels. An authentic cadence (V–I) asserts closure; a plagal cadence (IV–I) offers a softer, amen-like rest; a half cadence (ending on V) suspends and asks a question; a deceptive cadence (V–vi) denies the expected resolution and extends the phrase. The decision is not which cadence is "best" but which degree of closure the form requires at that moment. A section that should feel open and unresolved should not end on an authentic cadence; a final chorus that should feel conclusive should not end on a half cadence. Map cadence types to formal function before finalizing the progression.

### Voice Lead Smoothly to Avoid Unintentional Roughness

Voice leading — how individual lines move from chord to chord — determines whether a progression sounds smooth and connected or choppy and accidental. The general principles are to move voices by step where possible, to retain common tones between adjacent chords, to avoid parallel fifths and octaves in styles where they weaken independence, and to lead the leading tone upward to tonic and the seventh downward in dominant resolutions. The judgment problem is that strict voice-leading rules belong to specific historical styles, and applying them dogmatically to pop, jazz, or film music can produce stiff, academic results. The real criterion is whether each line sings independently and whether transitions sound intentional. When a transition sounds rough, diagnose the voice leading before changing the chord.

### Use Non-Chord Tones Deliberately to Shape Tension

Non-chord tones — passing tones, neighbor tones, suspensions, appoggiaturas, anticipations — are the primary tool for adding expressive tension and color without changing the underlying harmony. A suspension on a strong beat creates yearning; an appoggiatura leaning into a chord tone creates melodic emphasis. The trap is sprinkling non-chord tones randomly because they "sound nice," which produces clutter rather than direction. Each non-chord tone should have a reason: it prepares, prolongs, or resolves tension in service of the phrase. Decide where tension tones fall relative to the beat and where they resolve, and treat the preparation-resolution pair as a single musical event.

### Match Harmonic Complexity to Genre and Emotional Goal

The appropriate harmonic vocabulary depends on genre expectations and the intended emotional effect. A folk song may be best served by three diatonic chords; a jazz ballad may require extended harmonies and secondary dominants; a film cue may need modal interchange to shift color. The decision criterion is not "more complex is better" but "does this harmonic choice serve the emotional narrative and meet or thoughtfully subvert genre expectations?" Adding ninths, elevenths, and tritone substitutions to a song that needs simplicity obscures the melody; restricting a jazz piece to triads when it needs color sounds thin. Calibrate complexity to purpose, and be willing to simplify when a passage feels cluttered.

## Common Traps

### Reharmonizing to Fix a Weak Functional Progression

A progression fails to build toward its cadence, so the composer swaps in richer chords — adding a flat-II, a secondary dominant, a tritone sub — expecting the added color to create direction. The result is a progression that sounds sophisticated in isolation but still does not move the listener, because the underlying functional skeleton was never established. The mechanism of the trap is mistaking vertical color for horizontal direction; the harmony is prettier but the phrase still does not go anywhere. The fix is almost always to strengthen the functional map (clear predominant-to-dominant-to-tonic motion) before adding substitutions, not the reverse.

### Changing Chords Every Beat to Sound "Active"

A composer believes that frequent chord changes make music feel energetic and sophisticated, so they set a harmonic rhythm of one chord per beat throughout a verse. The false signal is that busy harmony feels productive while writing it. The harm is that constant change prevents any single harmony from registering, the listener cannot anchor to a tonal center, and the phrase feels restless without building real tension because there is no contrast. Energetic harmonic rhythm only reads as energy against a backdrop of slower rhythm elsewhere; without that contrast, it becomes a wash.

### Defaulting to the Same Cadence for Every Phrase

Every phrase ends on a V–I authentic cadence because it feels safe and final. The false signal is that each phrase "lands correctly." The harm is that uniform cadence types flatten the formal shape — every arrival feels equally conclusive, so no arrival stands out, and the listener loses the sense of building toward a destination. Varying cadence type by formal function (half cadence at the end of an open phrase, authentic at the true close, deceptive to extend) is what gives a multi-phrase section its contour.

### Ignoring Voice Leading Because the Chords Are "Right"

The chord symbols are functionally correct, but the voicing creates awkward leaps, parallel octaves, or a clashing inner line. The composer blames the chord choice and reharmonizes, when the actual defect was in how the voices moved between chords. The mechanism is conflating harmonic correctness with linear smoothness; a correct progression can still sound bad if the voices jump unnecessarily. The harm is wasted effort changing harmonies that were fine, while the real roughness persists.

### Overusing Chromatic Bass Motion as a Crutch

Descending or ascending chromatic bass lines feel dramatic and are easy to generate, so a composer uses them repeatedly across sections to create movement. The false signal is that chromatic motion sounds "cinematic." The harm is that the device loses impact through overuse, the tonal center becomes ambiguous, and the listener fatigues because every phrase relies on the same trick. Chromatic bass motion is most powerful when reserved for a single high-stakes moment.

### Treating Tension as Always Desirable

A composer maximizes dissonance and harmonic ambiguity throughout a piece, believing that more tension equals more emotional power. The mechanism of the trap is that tension only registers against release; without stretches of stability, the listener has no baseline, and the dissonance becomes the norm rather than a special event. The harm is a piece that feels uniformly anxious and never delivers catharsis. Deliberate stretches of consonance and clear tonic function are what make the tense moments land.

## Self-Check

- Did I map the functional role (tonic, predominant, dominant) of each chord before adding color, and does the progression have clear directional motion even with the melody removed?
- Did I choose the harmonic rhythm deliberately and vary it across sections so that changes in chord rate create perceptible shifts in energy?
- Does each phrase end with a cadence type whose degree of closure matches the formal function of that phrase (open, conclusive, extended)?
- Did I voice lead smoothly, checking for unnecessary leaps, common-tone retention, and proper resolution of leading tones and sevenths, rather than blaming chord choice for roughness?
- Does each non-chord tone have an identifiable expressive purpose tied to preparation and resolution, rather than being scattered randomly?
- Is the harmonic complexity calibrated to genre and emotional goal, and have I considered whether simplification would serve the piece better than added color?
- Have I balanced tension with sufficient release so that dissonant or ambiguous moments register as special rather than as the default texture?
