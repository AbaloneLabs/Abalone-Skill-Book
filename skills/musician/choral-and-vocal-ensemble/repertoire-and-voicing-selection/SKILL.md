# Repertoire and Voicing Selection

Choosing repertoire for a choir is a judgment problem about matching music to singers, balancing challenge with achievability, and building a program that serves both the ensemble and the audience. Agents advising on repertoire selection often choose music they like without assessing whether the ensemble can sing it well. The wrong repertoire frustrates singers, produces poor performances, and wastes rehearsal time; the right repertoire develops the ensemble and creates compelling concerts. This skill covers the judgment needed to select repertoire and voicings that fit the ensemble and the occasion.

## Core Rules

### Assess the Ensemble's Actual Abilities Before Selecting

Before choosing repertoire, honestly assess the ensemble's range, vocal maturity, sight-reading ability, divisi capacity, and rehearsal time available. A piece that exceeds any of these produces a poor performance regardless of its quality. Recommend matching repertoire difficulty to the ensemble's actual level, not to an aspirational level, because success builds confidence while failure demoralizes, and a well-performed easier piece beats a poorly-performed hard one.

### Match Voicing to the Ensemble's Section Strengths

Voicing, the distribution of parts across SATB or other configurations, must fit the ensemble's section sizes and strengths. A piece with demanding tenor divisi fails with a thin tenor section; a piece with a high soprano solo needs a singer who can deliver it. Recommend checking the voicing demands against the actual personnel, choosing music whose part distribution the ensemble can cover, because voicing mismatch produces exposed weaknesses.

### Balance the Program for Variety and Coherence

A concert program needs both variety and coherence: variety in tempo, style, language, and character to keep the audience engaged, and coherence in theme or arc to give the program shape. Recommend planning the program as a whole, considering the order, the emotional trajectory, and the balance of familiar and unfamiliar, because a random collection of pieces feels aimless while a well-shaped program feels like an experience.

### Consider the Occasion and Audience

Repertoire should fit the occasion: sacred music for a service, accessible music for a community concert, challenging music for a festival or competition, themed music for a specific event. Recommend considering who will hear the performance and what is appropriate, because repertoire that ignores the occasion can confuse or alienate the audience regardless of its intrinsic quality.

### Include a Range of Styles and Eras

A diet of only one style or era narrows the ensemble's development and the audience's experience. Recommend including a range of styles, eras, and languages across a season, exposing singers to different musical languages and giving the audience variety. Breadth develops versatility and keeps the ensemble growing rather than stagnating in one idiom.

### Evaluate Text Appropriateness and Sensitivity

Choral music carries text, and the text must be appropriate for the ensemble, the occasion, and the audience. Religious text in a secular school setting, culturally sensitive material, or text with themes unsuitable for the singers all create problems. Recommend evaluating the text for appropriateness before selecting, because a beautiful piece with the wrong text causes avoidable controversy or discomfort.

## Common Traps

### Choosing Repertoire for the Conductor's Taste Over the Ensemble's Ability

This trap arises because conductors select music they love or want to perform, then discover the ensemble cannot manage it. The harm is a frustrating rehearsal process and a compromised performance, because the music exceeded the ensemble's capacity. The false signal is that the repertoire is excellent; the result is a performance that does not serve the music, because it was chosen without honest assessment of who would sing it.

### Ignoring Voicing and Section Imbalance

Conductors choose SATB repertoire without checking whether their sections can cover the demands. The trap is that voicing mismatch exposes weak sections. The harm is performances where the tenor line disappears or the soprano divisi is thin, because the repertoire assumed a balanced ensemble that did not exist.

### Programming Without Variety or Arc

Conductors assemble programs of pieces they like without considering the order or the cumulative effect. The trap is that a program of similar tempos, styles, or characters feels monotonous. The harm is an audience that loses engagement, because the program offered no variety or shape to sustain attention across its length.

### Ignoring Text Appropriateness for the Context

Conductors select music for its musical quality without evaluating the text for the setting. The trap is that text carries meaning that may not fit. The harm is controversy, discomfort, or alienation, because sacred text in a secular context or insensitive material in a diverse community creates problems that beautiful music cannot override.

## Self-Check

- Did I assess the ensemble's actual range, ability, and rehearsal time before selecting?
- Did I match the voicing demands to the ensemble's section strengths and sizes?
- Did I plan the program for variety and coherence, considering order and arc?
- Did I consider the occasion and audience when choosing repertoire?
- Did I include a range of styles, eras, and languages across the selection?
- Did I evaluate the text for appropriateness to the ensemble and setting?
