# Analytical Method and Evidence

Musical analysis is an argument, not a description. To analyze is to make a claim about how a piece works and to support that claim with evidence drawn from the notes. Agents often produce analysis that amounts to labeling, naming chords, identifying forms, listing themes, without ever establishing why those observations matter or whether the chosen method actually illuminates the piece at hand. The judgment problem is to select a method whose assumptions fit the music, to gather evidence that genuinely supports the claim, and to distinguish a finding from an imposition.

## Core Rules

### Match the Method to the Music and the Question

No single analytical framework fits all music. Schenkerian voice-leading graphs assume tonal repertoire organized around prolongation; Roman-numeral harmony assumes functional common-practice syntax; set theory assumes post-tonal pitch organization; motivic analysis assumes developmental coherence. Choosing a method whose premises the music does not share produces analysis that is internally consistent but meaningless, like applying functional harmony to a melody that does not function that way. State the question first, then choose the method whose assumptions the music satisfies.

### Distinguish Observation From Interpretation

An observation is what the score literally contains: a pitch, a rhythm, a register, an instrumentation. An interpretation is what that observation means structurally or expressively. Conflating the two, presenting an interpretive claim as if it were a visible fact, is the most common analytical error. Keep the evidentiary layer, the things anyone could verify in the score, separate from the argumentative layer, the significance you are proposing, so that readers can evaluate each on its own terms.

### Let the Music Suggest the Analytical Unit

Analysts often impose units that distort the music: forcing phrase boundaries at four-bar intervals when the music breathes differently, or treating every motif as a transformation of a single cell when independent material is present. Identify boundaries by attending to cadences, harmonic rhythm, textural change, and surface articulation, and accept that real music frequently uses irregular or overlapping units. Imposed regularity produces analysis that looks elegant but describes a piece that does not exist.

### Require Evidence Proportional to the Claim

A modest claim, "this passage modulates to the dominant," needs only a cadence and a key signature. A strong claim, "the entire movement is unified by a single descending third," requires demonstrating the third's presence and structural role across the whole, not merely locating it once or twice. Match the burden of proof to the claim's reach. Sweeping claims supported by a few cherry-picked instances are not analysis but assertion.

### Account for Counterexamples and Ambiguity

Strong analysis acknowledges passages that resist the proposed reading. If your framework predicts a clear half cadence and the music gives an evaded or deceptive one, the exception is analytically informative, not an embarrassment to ignore. Addressing ambiguity demonstrates that the method is engaging the actual piece rather than selecting only the evidence that fits.

### Consider Multiple Analytical Lenses When Warranted

A piece may reward harmonic, motivic, formal, and timbral analysis simultaneously, and no single lens captures everything. Insisting on one framework when the music operates across several produces a partial picture presented as complete. When the music's salient features span dimensions, say so, and let complementary lenses each carry the part of the argument they can support.

### Test the Analysis Against Hearing

Analysis that cannot be heard raises a question about what it explains. This does not mean every analytical feature must be consciously perceptible on first listening, but the structural claims should be auditable by an attentive listener or at least should not contradict what the ear reliably reports. Analysis that survives only on paper, disconnected from sonic experience, often indicates a method stretched beyond its valid domain.

## Common Traps

### Labeling Instead of Arguing

Naming a chord, a form, or a motif feels like analysis because it uses analytical vocabulary, but labeling alone establishes no claim about how the piece works or why it matters. The trap is that a page full of correct labels looks rigorous while actually making no argument. The harm is that the analysis never advances from inventory to insight, leaving the reader with a list and no understanding.

### Method Mismatch

Applying a method whose assumptions the music violates is seductive because the method produces tidy results regardless of fit. Roman numerals can be forced onto almost any verticality, and set theory can classify any collection, but the classifications are meaningless if the music does not organize itself by those principles. The false signal is that the analysis "works" technically; the reality is that it works only because the method cannot fail by construction.

### Cherry-Picking Supporting Passages

Selecting only the moments that confirm the thesis and silently passing over those that complicate it produces an analysis that looks airtight but is actually unfalsifiable. The trap is that confirmation feels like thoroughness when it is really selective evidence. The harm is that the argument cannot be trusted because the reader has no way to know what was excluded.

### Over-Interpreting a Single Feature

Finding a motive, an interval, or a pitch class in several places and declaring it the structural key to the whole piece extrapolates from limited data. The trap is that recurrence feels significant, but recurrence can be coincidental, generic, or a byproduct of tonal syntax rather than a deliberate unifying device. Without demonstrating structural function, not just presence, the claim overreaches the evidence.

### Ignoring What the Ear Contradicts

Analysis that declares a passage strongly unified when listeners experience it as contrastive, or that identifies a structural downbeat where the surface rhythm points elsewhere, disconnects from the actual hearing of the piece. The trap is that the analytical diagram looks compelling in isolation. The harm is that the analysis fails to explain the experience it was supposed to illuminate, reducing analysis to an abstract exercise.

## Self-Check

- Does the chosen analytical method's assumptions actually hold for the music being analyzed?
- Are observations of score fact kept distinct from interpretive claims about their meaning?
- Are analytical units, phrases, motifs, sections, derived from the music's own articulation rather than imposed?
- Is the evidence proportional in scope to the strength of the claim being made?
- Have passages that resist the proposed reading been acknowledged and addressed?
- Where the music spans multiple dimensions, have complementary analytical lenses been used rather than forcing one?
- Can the central structural claims be audited by an attentive listener, or do they contradict reliable hearing?
