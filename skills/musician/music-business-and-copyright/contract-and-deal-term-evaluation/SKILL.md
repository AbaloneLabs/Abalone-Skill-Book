# Contract and Deal Term Evaluation

Music contracts determine who owns the work, who earns from it, and who controls the career for years after signing, and they are written by lawyers for the party that drafted them. The judgment problem is that artists, eager for validation and advancement, often sign contracts they do not fully understand — advances that are fully recoupable from royalties, rights assignments that are perpetual, options that bind them to unfavorable terms for multiple albums — then discover years later that the deal they celebrated has structured their income against them. The advance that felt like a gift is a loan repaid from the artist's own royalties; the "partnership" language describes a one-sided rights transfer. Music contracts are not standardized; every clause is negotiable, and the artist who signs the first draft accepts terms drafted to favor the other side. This skill covers the decisions that govern contract evaluation: understanding recoupment and rights, evaluating deal types, identifying red-flag clauses, the necessity of qualified legal review, and the long-term thinking required before signing.

## Core Rules

### Understand That Advances Are Recoupable, Not Free Money

An advance is a payment against future royalties — effectively a loan that the artist repays through their royalty earnings before receiving any additional income. The decision is to understand that every advance (from a label, publisher, or distributor) must be recouped from the artist's share before the artist earns more, which means a large advance can keep the artist earning nothing for years if royalties are slow. The trap is treating the advance as income or generosity, signing for the largest advance without considering the recoupment terms and royalty rate that determine whether the artist ever earns beyond it. The criterion is that you understand the recoupment structure: what is recoupable, from what income stream, at what rate, and how long until you might earn beyond the advance. Model the recoupment before signing.

### Evaluate Which Rights Are Being Assigned and for How Long

Music deals assign rights — master rights, publishing rights, distribution rights — and the scope and duration of those assignments determine the artist's control and income for years. The decision is to understand exactly which rights the deal assigns, whether the assignment is exclusive, and for how long (a fixed term, or in perpetuity, or until recoupment plus options). The trap is signing away rights broadly and permanently for short-term benefit, then being unable to reclaim control of your own work. The criterion is that you know what you are giving up: is the master assignment perpetual? Is the publishing deal for a fixed term or your entire catalog? Are the rights exclusive in a way that prevents other deals? Prefer limited-term, defined-scope assignments over broad perpetual transfers.

### Scrutinize Options and Term Limits

Many deals grant the label or publisher options to extend the deal for additional albums or terms, often on terms they set, which can bind the artist to an unfavorable deal for far longer than the initial term. The decision is to scrutinize option clauses: how many options, on what conditions, with what advance and royalty terms for option periods. The trap is focusing on the initial term and missing that options can extend the deal for multiple albums, during which the artist remains bound to terms they may want to escape. The criterion is that you understand the total potential commitment, not just the first period. Negotiate option limits, option exercise conditions, and terms for option periods.

### Identify Red-Flag Clauses That Transfer Control or Income

Certain clauses systematically disadvantage artists: broad cross-collateralization (one album's unrecouped balance charged against the next), deduction-laden royalty calculations (packaging, breakage, free goods, reduced rates on new media), control over creative decisions assigned to the label, and reversion rights that never trigger. The decision is to identify these clauses and negotiate or reject them. The trap is accepting standard-sounding deductions that compound to take a large share of income, or granting creative control that lets the label shelve or alter the work. The criterion is that you understand how each clause affects your income and control over the deal's life. Flag deductions, control clauses, and reversion terms for negotiation.

### Obtain Qualified Entertainment Attorney Review Before Signing

Music contracts are complex and industry-specific, and an artist without legal training cannot reliably identify unfavorable terms. The decision is to have any contract reviewed by a qualified entertainment attorney before signing — not a family lawyer, not a friend, but an attorney who works in music and knows the industry's norms and traps. The trap is signing to save the attorney fee or because the deal "seems fine," which accepts terms a professional would have flagged. The criterion is that every contract is reviewed by qualified counsel whose obligation is to protect your interests. Budget for legal review as a non-negotiable cost of any deal.

### Think Long-Term, Not Just About the Immediate Deal

The decision to sign is a multi-year commitment that shapes the artist's career trajectory, income, and control. The decision is to evaluate the deal against long-term goals: does this deal move you toward where you want to be in five years, or does it trade long-term control and income for short-term validation and advance? The trap is signing for the immediate feeling of being signed, the advance, or the prestige, without assessing whether the deal's terms serve a career that may look very different in three years. The criterion is that the deal supports your long-term interests, not just your immediate desire for a deal. Walk away from deals that mortgage the future for the present.

## Common Traps

### Treating the Advance as Income

The artist celebrates the advance without understanding it is recoupable from their royalties. The false signal is a big check and validation. The harm is years of earning nothing beyond the advance, because royalties repay it first, often at unfavorable rates. The mechanism is misunderstanding recoupment. The remedy is modeling recoupment before signing.

### Signing Away Rights Perpetually for Short-Term Benefit

The deal assigns master or publishing rights in perpetuity for an advance and immediate support. The false signal is partnership and backing. The harm is permanent loss of control and income from the work, long after the advance is repaid and the relationship has ended. The mechanism is prioritizing the immediate over the durable. The fix is limited-term assignments.

### Missing Option Clauses That Extend the Deal

The artist focuses on the initial term and does not account for options. The false signal is a defined first album deal. The harm is being bound for multiple albums under terms the artist cannot renegotiate, because options were exercised. The mechanism is overlooking option mechanics. The remedy is option scrutiny and negotiation.

### Accepting Standard Deductions That Compound

Royalty deductions — packaging, breakage, free goods, reduced new-media rates — are accepted as standard. The false signal is that everyone accepts these terms. The harm is a large share of income lost to deductions that compound across the deal, leaving far less than the headline rate suggested. The mechanism is not modeling the net royalty. The fix is deduction scrutiny.

### Skipping Attorney Review to Save Money or Time

The artist signs without qualified review, trusting the other party or their own reading. The false signal is that the deal is understandable. The harm is accepting terms a professional would have flagged — unfavorable recoupment, broad rights, control clauses — that shape the career negatively for years. The mechanism is underestimating contract complexity. The remedy is qualified attorney review.

### Signing for Validation Rather Than Career Strategy

The artist signs because being signed feels like success, without assessing whether the deal serves long-term goals. The false signal is the emotional reward of a deal. The harm is a career shaped by a deal that does not serve it, trading control and income for short-term validation. The mechanism is prioritizing feeling over strategy. The fix is long-term evaluation.

## Self-Check

- Do I understand that the advance is recoupable from my royalties, and have I modeled when (or whether) I might earn beyond it given the royalty rate and deductions?
- Do I know exactly which rights the deal assigns, whether exclusively, and for how long — and are the assignments limited in scope and term rather than broad and perpetual?
- Have I scrutinized the option clauses, understanding the total potential commitment and the terms for option periods?
- Have I identified red-flag clauses — cross-collateralization, deduction-laden royalties, creative control transfers, non-triggering reversion — and negotiated or rejected them?
- Has the contract been reviewed by a qualified entertainment attorney whose obligation is to protect my interests, before I sign?
- Does this deal serve my long-term career goals, or does it trade long-term control and income for short-term validation and advance?
- If this deal's terms governed my career for the next seven years, would I still sign it, or would the terms work against where I want to be?
