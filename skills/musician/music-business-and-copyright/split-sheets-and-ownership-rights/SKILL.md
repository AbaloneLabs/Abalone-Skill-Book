# Split Sheets and Ownership Rights

Song ownership is determined at the moment of creation, but it is documented — or not — at the moment of release, and the gap between those moments is where careers are made or damaged by paperwork. The judgment problem is that splits (the percentage of songwriting ownership each contributor holds) are often left informal during the creative excitement, decided by handshake or assumption, and then become contested when the song earns money or gets placed. A collaborator who said "sure, whatever" in the studio may claim a larger share when the song generates royalties; a contributor who added one line may believe they own half the song. The defense is the split sheet, a simple document signed before release that records who owns what percentage and why. Musicians avoid this paperwork because it feels transactional and trust-eroding in the creative moment, but avoiding it is what erodes trust — and fortunes — later. This skill covers the decisions that govern splits and ownership: allocating percentages by contribution, documenting with split sheets, separating writer from publisher shares, and handling disputes and changes.

## Core Rules

### Determine Splits by Actual Creative Contribution, Not Default Assumptions

Songwriting splits should reflect each contributor's actual creative input to the song's essential elements — melody, lyric, chord progression, structure — not assumptions like "we always split evenly" or "whoever brought the idea owns it." The decision is to assess each contributor's contribution to the copyrightable elements and allocate percentages accordingly. The trap is defaulting to even splits regardless of contribution, which is simple but unfair when contributions were unequal, and which breeds resentment when the song succeeds. The criterion is that the split reflects what each person actually created. Discuss contributions honestly as the song develops, and agree on splits while the contributions are fresh and undisputed.

### Document Splits on a Signed Split Sheet Before Release

A split sheet is a simple document listing the song title, each writer and publisher, their respective percentages, and signatures — and it must be completed and signed before the song is released or shopped. The decision is to make split sheets a non-negotiable part of every collaboration, completed before any commercial use, because once money is involved, memories and agreements shift. The trap is treating the split sheet as paperwork to do later, which becomes paperwork to dispute later. The criterion is that every released song has a signed split sheet on file. Create the split sheet as the song nears completion, get every contributor's signature, and store it where it can be retrieved.

### Separate Writer Shares From Publisher Shares

Song ownership has two sides: the writer share (paid to the songwriter) and the publisher share (paid to the publisher, which may be the writer's own company or an outside publisher). Together they total 200% of the song (100% writer, 100% publisher). The decision is to understand and document both shares, because a writer who does not have a publishing deal still owns their publisher share, and assigning it to a publisher is a separate decision from the writer split. The trap is conflating writer and publisher shares or failing to document the publisher side, which causes royalty collection problems. Document both the writer split and the publisher split on the split sheet, and understand what you are assigning if you sign a publishing deal.

### Address Samples, Interpolations, and Pre-Existing Material

If the song incorporates samples, interpolations of existing melodies, or lyrics from other sources, the original creators own a portion of the new song, and this must be cleared and documented before release. The decision is to identify any pre-existing material in the song, clear it with the original rights holders, and document the resulting ownership split (which reduces the new writers' percentages). The trap is releasing a song with an uncleared sample or interpolation, which leads to takedowns, lawsuits, and forced retroactive splits that may be far less favorable than a pre-clearance negotiation. Audit the song for pre-existing material before release, clear everything, and document the splits including the original rights holders.

### Revisit Splits When Contributions Change

Splits agreed early may not reflect later contributions: a co-writer who was going to write a verse does not, or a producer adds significant melodic material late in the process. The decision is to revisit and adjust splits when contributions change materially, rather than holding to an agreement that no longer reflects reality. The trap is rigidly enforcing an early agreement when the actual contributions diverged, which is unfair and breeds dispute. The criterion is that the documented split reflects the final contributions. Revisit the split sheet when the song is final, confirm it matches actual contributions, and update signatures if anything changed.

### Register Splits With PROs and Publishing Administrators

A split sheet documents the agreement, but the royalties flow only if the splits are registered with the performing rights organization (PRO: ASCAP, BMI, SESAC, PRS, etc.) and any publishing administrator. The decision is to register each song's splits with the relevant PROs and administrators, ensuring that each writer and publisher is correctly listed with their percentages. The trap is completing the split sheet but never registering it, so the royalties are not collected or are collected incorrectly. The criterion is that every released song is registered with the correct splits at the PROs. Register promptly after release, and verify the registration shows the correct percentages.

## Common Traps

### Leaving Splits Informal During the Creative Process

The collaborators agree to "figure out splits later" because the creative moment feels wrong for business. The false signal is trust and focus. The harm is contested ownership when the song earns money, because the informal agreement is unverifiable and memories diverge. The mechanism is avoiding the transactional conversation. The remedy is the split sheet before release.

### Defaulting to Even Splits Regardless of Contribution

Splits are divided equally among everyone in the room regardless of who created what. The false signal is fairness and simplicity. The harm is resentment when contributions were unequal, and disputes when the song succeeds, because the split did not reflect reality. The mechanism is substituting a formula for an honest contribution assessment. The fix is contribution-based splits.

### Conflating Writer and Publisher Shares

The writer and publisher sides of the song are treated as one, or the publisher side is not documented. The false signal is that the writer split is the whole picture. The harm is royalty collection errors and confusion when a publishing deal is involved, because the two shares were not separated. The mechanism is incomplete understanding of song ownership. The remedy is documenting both shares.

### Releasing Songs With Uncleared Samples or Interpolations

A sample or interpolation is included without clearance, assuming it is small enough to ignore. The false signal is that it will go unnoticed. The harm is takedowns, lawsuits, and forced retroactive splits that are far worse than pre-clearance, plus damage to reputation and release momentum. The mechanism is underestimating detection and consequence. The fix is pre-release clearance.

### Holding to an Early Agreement When Contributions Changed

The split agreed at the start is enforced even though later contributions diverged. The false signal is honoring the agreement. The harm is unfair ownership that breeds dispute, because the split no longer reflects who actually created the song. The mechanism is rigidity. The remedy is revisiting splits when contributions change.

### Completing Split Sheets but Never Registering With PROs

The split sheet is signed and filed, but the song is never registered with the PRO. The false signal is that the paperwork is done. The harm is uncollected or misallocated royalties, because the PRO does not know the splits. The mechanism is stopping at documentation. The fix is PRO registration.

## Self-Check

- Are the splits determined by actual creative contribution to copyrightable elements, discussed and agreed while contributions are fresh?
- Is there a signed split sheet for every released song, completed and stored before any commercial use?
- Are both the writer shares and the publisher shares documented and understood, with the publisher side addressed even without a publishing deal?
- Have I audited the song for samples, interpolations, and pre-existing material, cleared everything, and documented the resulting ownership including original rights holders?
- Did I revisit and update the split sheet when the song was final, confirming it reflects actual final contributions?
- Is every released song registered with the correct splits at the relevant PROs and publishing administrators, verified to show the correct percentages?
- If this song earned significant money tomorrow, would the ownership be undisputed and documented, or would there be a dispute?
