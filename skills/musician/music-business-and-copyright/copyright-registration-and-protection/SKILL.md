# Copyright Registration and Protection

Copyright is the legal foundation that lets musicians own and earn from their work, and it is also the area where misunderstanding is most common and most costly. The judgment problem is that musicians often hold folk beliefs about copyright — "I mailed it to myself," "it's protected once I write it down," "posting it online proves I made it" — that provide no real protection, then discover the gap between belief and law when their work is copied. In fact, copyright exists from the moment of fixation, but registration provides the enforcement powers (statutory damages, attorney's fees, the ability to sue) that make protection practical rather than theoretical. Compounding the confusion, copyright has two sides for music — the composition (the song itself) and the sound recording (the specific master) — and protecting only one leaves the other exposed. This skill covers the decisions that govern copyright protection: what is protectable, registration strategy, composition versus master rights, enforcement, and royalty collection through PROs and neighboring rights.

## Core Rules

### Understand What Is and Is Not Protectable

Copyright protects original expression fixed in tangible form — the specific melody, lyric, chord progression as combined, and arrangement of a composition; the specific recorded performance of a sound recording. It does not protect ideas, titles, chord progressions in isolation, common musical building blocks, or concepts. The decision is to understand the boundary of protectability, so you protect what you own and do not claim protection for what is in the public domain or common usage. The trap is believing a chord progression or a song title is protectable, leading to unenforceable claims or, conversely, failing to protect a genuinely original melody because you assumed it was too simple. The criterion is that you know what your copyright actually covers.

### Register Copyrights for Commercial Works Before Release

While copyright exists from fixation, registration with the copyright office (U.S. Copyright Office or the equivalent in your territory) provides critical enforcement benefits: the ability to sue, statutory damages and attorney's fees for timely registrations, and a public record of ownership. The decision is to register commercially significant works before or shortly after release, treating registration as part of the release preparation. The trap is relying on fixation alone, which leaves you with a copyright you cannot practically enforce, especially against well-funded infringers. The criterion is that works you intend to monetize or protect are registered. Register collections of works to reduce cost, and register before release where possible.

### Separate Composition Copyright From Sound Recording Copyright

Music involves two distinct copyrights: the composition (the underlying song, owned by writers and publishers) and the sound recording (the specific master recording, owned by the artist or label). The decision is to understand, protect, and manage both separately, because they generate different royalties (composition: performance and mechanical; master: reproduction and neighboring rights) and can be owned by different parties. The trap is protecting only one — typically registering the composition but not the master, or vice versa — which leaves half the value unprotected. The criterion is that both the composition and the sound recording of each commercial release are registered and their ownership documented.

### Register With PROs to Collect Performance Royalties

Performance royalties — paid when your music is performed publicly, broadcast, or streamed — are collected by performing rights organizations (ASCAP, BMI, SESAC, PRS, GEMA, etc.) and distributed to writers and publishers. The decision is to affiliate with a PRO and register every commercial composition, so that performance royalties are collected. The trap is releasing music without PRO registration, which means performance royalties go uncollected or, in some systems, into a pool that does not reach you. The criterion is that every released composition is registered with your PRO under the correct writer and publisher splits. Register promptly after release and verify the registration.

### Collect Neighboring Rights and Mechanical Royalties

Beyond performance royalties, music generates neighboring rights royalties (paid to sound recording owners and performers for broadcast and streaming, collected by neighboring rights organizations) and mechanical royalties (paid for reproductions, collected by mechanical rights organizations or administratively via DSPs). The decision is to understand and register for these revenue streams, which are often missed by independent artists. The trap is collecting only performance royalties and leaving neighboring rights and mechanicals uncollected, which can represent significant income especially for streamed music. Affiliate with the relevant organizations, register your masters and compositions, and ensure all revenue streams are captured.

### Enforce Rights Through Cease-and-Desist and Takedown, Not Vigilantism

When infringement occurs, the practical enforcement tools are cease-and-desist letters, DMCA takedown notices to platforms, and, for significant cases, legal action through an attorney. The decision is to use these formal tools rather than informal confrontation or public accusation, which can create legal exposure and damage reputation. The trap is responding to infringement emotionally — public call-outs, threats — which may itself create liability and rarely resolves the issue efficiently. Document the infringement, send a formal notice or takedown, consult an attorney for significant cases, and reserve public statements for after resolution.

## Common Traps

### Relying on Poor Man's Copyright or Fixation Alone

The musician mails a copy to themselves or relies on the fact of creation, believing this proves ownership. The false signal is that evidence of creation equals enforceable copyright. The harm is no practical enforcement power, because without registration you cannot access statutory damages or attorney's fees, making enforcement against infringers impractical. The mechanism is believing folk copyright myths. The remedy is formal registration.

### Protecting Only the Composition or Only the Master

One copyright is registered while the other is neglected. The false signal is that the registered side is protected. The harm is the unprotected side's value is exposed — unregistered compositions cannot collect via some channels, unregistered masters cannot collect neighboring rights. The mechanism is not understanding the two-copyright structure of music. The fix is registering both.

### Releasing Without PRO Registration

The music is released without registering the compositions with a PRO. The false signal is that streaming and broadcast will find the writer. The harm is uncollected performance royalties that never reach the writer, because the PRO does not have the registration. The mechanism is skipping the administrative step. The remedy is prompt PRO registration.

### Missing Neighboring Rights and Mechanical Royalties

Only performance royalties are collected; neighboring rights and mechanicals are not registered. The false signal is that performance royalties are the main income. The harm is significant uncollected income, especially from streaming, where neighboring rights and mechanicals represent large shares. The mechanism is incomplete revenue stream awareness. The fix is affiliating with all relevant organizations.

### Believing Titles, Chord Progressions, or Ideas Are Protectable

The musician claims infringement based on a similar title, chord progression, or general idea. The false signal is that the similarity feels like copying. The harm is unenforceable claims that waste effort and, if public, create reputation damage, because these elements are not protectable. The mechanism is misunderstanding protectability. The remedy is understanding what copyright covers.

### Vigilante Enforcement Instead of Formal Process

The musician confronts the infringer publicly or threatens informally. The false signal is standing up for your rights. The harm is legal exposure (defamation, tortious interference) and inefficient resolution, because formal tools were not used. The mechanism is emotional response. The fix is formal notices and attorney guidance.

## Self-Check

- Do I understand what copyright protects (original expression fixed in tangible form) and what it does not (ideas, titles, common building blocks), so my claims are accurate?
- Are my commercially significant works registered with the copyright office before or shortly after release, to secure enforcement benefits?
- Are both the composition copyright and the sound recording copyright of each release registered and documented, not just one?
- Is every released composition registered with my PRO under the correct writer and publisher splits, verified after registration?
- Am I affiliated with the organizations that collect neighboring rights and mechanical royalties, with my masters and compositions registered to capture all revenue streams?
- If my work were infringed, would I use formal tools (cease-and-desist, DMCA takedown, attorney) rather than informal confrontation?
- If I audited my catalog, would every commercial release have its composition and master registered across all relevant rights organizations, or are there gaps losing income?
