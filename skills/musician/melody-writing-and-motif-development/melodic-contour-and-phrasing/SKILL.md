# Melodic Contour and Phrasing

Contour is the shape of a melody as it rises and falls through time, and phrasing is how that shape is organized into perceivable units separated by breathing points. The judgment problem is that contour is what makes a melody feel directed or aimless, yet it is often left to chance — a composer follows chord tones or wanders by feel, producing a line that never builds toward a goal the listener can anticipate. A melody with deliberate contour has a sense of arrival; a melody without it is a random walk. Phrasing is equally neglected: phrases that run too long lose the listener, phrases chopped too short feel stilted, and the natural breathing points that make a melody singable are often ignored when composing at a screen rather than with the voice or instrument. This skill covers the decisions that govern contour and phrasing: arc design, climax placement, phrase length, breathing points, and the relationship of contour to register and singability.

## Core Rules

### Plan the Contour Arc With a Goal Note and a Climax

Effective melodic contour moves toward a high point — a climax — that is prepared by preceding ascent and resolved by subsequent descent, creating an arc the listener can feel. The decision is where the climax falls: at a structurally important moment, not accidentally in the first phrase or only at the very end without preparation. A climax placed too early leaves the rest of the melody anticlimactic; one placed too late feels unearned. The judgment criterion is dramatic shape: map the contour across the whole melody and confirm it rises to a single peak and resolves, rather than wandering up and down without accumulation.

### Align Phrase Lengths With Breathing and Form

Melodic phrases should align with natural breathing points and with the formal structure of the piece. A phrase that runs too long without a rest or cadential arrival loses the listener; a phrase chopped too short feels stilted. Standard phrase lengths (four-bar antecedent-consequent pairs, eight-bar periods) exist because they match how listeners group musical information. The decision is not to always use four-bar phrases but to be conscious of phrase length and to use deviations (elisions, extensions, irregular three- or five-bar phrases) deliberately for expressive effect. Mark phrase boundaries explicitly and confirm they coincide with harmonic and rhythmic arrivals.

### Respect Range, Tessitura, and Singability

A melody's range (lowest to highest note) and tessitura (where it predominantly sits) determine who can perform it and how it feels to hear. A melody that sits too high for too long fatigues a singer; one that stays in a narrow low register sounds inert. For vocal melodies, consider the crossover points between registers (chest to head voice) and whether the melody exploits or fights them. For instrumental melodies, consider the idiomatic range and timbral changes across the instrument's register. The decision criterion is fitness for the intended performer and emotional effect. Test melodies by singing or playing them, not just by entering notes in a score, because a melody that looks fine on the staff may fight the instrument's physiology.

### Use Stepwise Motion With Occasional Leaps for Expressive Impact

Strong melodies move predominantly by step (adjacent scale tones), which creates a singable, connected line, and reserve leaps (skips of a third or more) for moments of expressive emphasis. The decision criterion is the ratio and placement: too many leaps make a melody angular and hard to sing; too few make it scale-like and undifferentiated. A common strong move is to follow a leap with stepwise motion in the opposite direction, which fills in the gap and makes the leap sound intentional rather than accidental. After writing a melody, trace its motion and confirm that leaps are followed by recovery rather than by another leap in the same direction.

### Relate Contour to Harmony Through Chord Tones and Tension

A melody's notes are either chord tones (stable) or non-chord tones (tension), and the relationship shapes the melody's expressive character. A melody that hits only chord tones can sound square; one that avoids them entirely sounds aimless. Strong melodies often emphasize key chord tones on strong beats and use non-chord tones (especially suspensions and appoggiaturas) on strong beats to create expressive tension. The decision criterion is the balance of stable and unstable tones across the phrase, and whether the melody's strongest notes reinforce or contradict the underlying harmony. When a melody feels weak, check whether its strong-beat notes are fighting the harmony before rewriting the contour.

### Vary Contour Density Across Sections for Formal Contrast

If every phrase has the same contour shape — rising arcs, similar ranges, identical climax placements — the melody becomes monotonous across a piece. The decision is to vary contour characteristics by section: a verse may use a narrow, conversational range; a chorus may open to a wider range with a higher climax; a bridge may invert the contour or explore a new register. The judgment criterion is that the listener should perceive formal contrast partly through contour change, not only through harmony or instrumentation. Plan contour as a section-level parameter, not only a phrase-level one.

## Common Traps

### Writing a Contour That Wanders Without a Peak

The melody moves up and down in small intervals, following chord tones but never building toward a high point. The false signal is that the notes are correct against the harmony. The harm is a melody that feels flat and directionless, with no moment the listener anticipates or remembers. The mechanism is the absence of a planned climax; without a goal note or peak, the contour becomes a random walk that never accumulates energy. The fix is to plan the dramatic arc and place the climax at a structurally meaningful moment.

### Placing the Climax Too Early or Too Late

The highest or most intense note arrives in the first phrase, leaving the rest of the melody with nowhere to go, or it arrives only at the very end without preparation, feeling unearned. The false signal is that a dramatic high note is inherently powerful. The harm is a contour with no satisfying arc — either anticlimactic after an early peak, or arbitrary if the peak lacks build. The mechanism is failing to plan the dramatic shape across the whole melody. The remedy is to map the contour across all phrases and place the climax where it is prepared and where the form can absorb its impact.

### Ignoring Singability and Register Crossover

A melody is written at a piano or in a DAW and looks fine on the staff, but it sits across a singer's register break or demands sustained notes at the extreme of the range. The false signal is that the notes are within the singer's total range. The harm is a performance that sounds strained, tentative, or inconsistent in tone, because the melody fights the instrument's physiology. The mechanism is composing visually or digitally rather than physically; the remedy is to sing or play the melody yourself and feel where it is uncomfortable, then revise the register or the sustained-note demands.

### Making Phrases All the Same Length

Out of a sense of symmetry, the composer writes every phrase as a four-bar unit, producing a melody that feels mechanical and predictable. The false signal is that equal lengths look balanced. The harm is a melody that lacks the breathing variation that makes music feel alive; the listener comes to predict each phrase boundary and loses surprise. The mechanism is confusing visual symmetry with musical proportion. Varied phrase lengths — an extended consequent, an elided transition, an irregular three-bar phrase — create organic shape that rigid symmetry cannot.

### Leaping Without Recovery

The melody leaps up a sixth, then leaps up another fourth, then leaps down a fifth, creating an angular line that is hard to sing and sounds like it is reaching rather than flowing. The false signal is that leaps create drama. The harm is a melody that sounds disjointed and instrumental rather than vocal, because consecutive leaps without stepwise recovery prevent the line from connecting. The mechanism is overusing leaps without following the convention of recovery; the fix is to follow each leap with stepwise motion in the opposite direction to fill the gap.

## Self-Check

- Does the melodic contour have a deliberate arc with a planned climax placed at a structurally meaningful moment, rather than wandering up and down without a goal?
- Do the phrase lengths align with breathing points and formal structure, with any irregular lengths used deliberately for expressive effect rather than as miscounts?
- Is the range and tessitura appropriate for the intended performer, and have I sung or played the melody to confirm it is comfortable and exploits register color rather than fighting it?
- Does the melody move predominantly by step with leaps reserved for expressive emphasis, and is each leap followed by stepwise recovery rather than by another leap?
- Do the melody's strong-beat notes reinforce the harmony through chord tones and expressive non-chord tones, rather than fighting the underlying progression?
- Have I varied contour characteristics (range, climax placement, motion type) across sections so the listener perceives formal contrast through contour, not only through other parameters?
- If I remove the harmony and accompaniment, does the melody alone have a recognizable shape and direction that a listener could hum and remember?
