# Multi Touch Sequence Design

A sequence is not a pile of reminders. It is a series of relevant touches that test whether a buyer has a problem worth discussing. Agents often optimize for persistence while ignoring fit, timing, consent, and message progression. This skill helps the agent design sequences that are respectful, evidence-driven, and useful.

## Core Rules

### Start from a clear hypothesis

Define why this person or account may care now: trigger event, role pain, industry issue, account signal, funding, hiring, technology change, renewal, or known initiative. Without a hypothesis, sequencing becomes spam.

### Match message to persona and account and progress the narrative across touches

Executives, managers, practitioners, technical owners, and procurement contacts care about different outcomes and risks. Adapt the angle without inventing false personalization.

Each touch should add context, proof, question, or useful resource. Do not repeat the same ask with slightly different wording. Progression shows respect for attention.

### Design the sequence as a test and vary value, not just format

State what the sequence is testing: whether the persona owns the problem, whether the trigger matters, whether the account is active, or whether the message resonates. If no response arrives, the learning should inform targeting and messaging, not only prompt more sends.

Changing from email to phone to LinkedIn is not enough if the message value stays the same. Vary the business angle, proof, question, resource, or stakeholder hypothesis. The buyer should not feel chased by the same idea.

### Account for message fatigue and localize thoughtfully

Prospects may receive many vendor sequences. Shorten, slow, or stop when the account has heavy marketing touches, active sales conversation, customer support issue, or recent negative reply. Respect attention as a scarce resource.

Region, language, industry, seniority, and cultural norms affect tone and cadence. Localization is not just translation; it includes examples, proof, compliance, and communication norms.

### Review legal and policy constraints and define ownership of replies

Outbound rules, opt-out handling, data source rules, industry policies, and company guidelines may constrain sequence design. Build compliance into the sequence rather than treating it as afterthought.

Before launching, know who handles positive replies, objections, referrals, out-of-office messages, unsubscribes, and wrong-person responses. A sequence that generates replies without fast ownership wastes interest and can violate trust.

### Include a relevance reset and use channels deliberately

If the sequence continues after several touches, include a message that resets relevance or offers a graceful exit. This prevents the sequence from sounding like automated persistence with no learning.

Email, phone, LinkedIn, direct mail, event follow-up, and referral touches each have different norms. Choose channels based on buyer context, consent, and signal strength.

### Control cadence and frequency and include value before ask

Persistence can become pressure. Set a cadence that fits segment, urgency, and relationship. Avoid over-contacting, especially across multiple internal teams.

Offer a relevant insight, benchmark, question, resource, or observation. A meeting ask with no buyer value is weak.

### Define stop conditions and coordinate account-level outreach

Stop or change approach after negative reply, no fit, role mismatch, account conflict, customer status, compliance constraint, or sequence exhaustion. Respect opt-outs immediately.

Multi-threading should be planned, not chaotic. Avoid several sellers contacting different people with conflicting messages. Account-based sequencing needs shared narrative and owner.

### Measure quality, not only activity and refresh based on learning

Track replies, meetings, qualified conversations, opportunity creation, disqualification learning, and negative sentiment. Opens and sends are weak success measures.

Use responses, objections, and silence to improve targeting, hypothesis, message, and timing. Do not run a failing sequence unchanged.

## Common Traps

- Sequencing without a reason the buyer may care now.
- Sending the same message repeatedly; personalizing with shallow or false details
- Using every channel because it exists; over-contacting buyers or accounts
- Asking for meetings before offering value; ignoring stop conditions and opt-outs
- Creating uncoordinated multi-threading; measuring success by sends and opens
- Failing to learn from negative replies; treating sequence design as copywriting only
- Ignoring customer or partner ownership; running sequences without a learning hypothesis; varying channel while repeating the same value; ignoring buyer message fatigue
- Translating words without localizing context; treating compliance as an afterthought; launching sequences without reply ownership; continuing a cadence without a relevance reset or graceful exit

## Self-Check

- Is there a clear buyer-care hypothesis?
- Is messaging matched to persona and account?; does each touch add something new?
- Are channels selected deliberately?; is cadence respectful?
- Does the sequence provide value before asking?; are stop conditions defined?
- Is account-level outreach coordinated?; are quality metrics tracked?
- Is sequence learning used to revise the approach?; are ownership and compliance constraints respected?
- Would the buyer see the sequence as relevant rather than repetitive?; is the sequence designed to test a specific hypothesis?
- Does each touch vary value, not just format?; is message fatigue considered?
- Is localization appropriate?; are legal and policy constraints built in?
- Is reply ownership defined?; does the sequence include a relevance reset or graceful exit?
