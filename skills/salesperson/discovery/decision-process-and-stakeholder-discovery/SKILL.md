# Decision Process And Stakeholder Discovery

Deals are lost when sellers understand the problem but not the path to decision. Buyers may like the product while procurement, security, finance, legal, executives, or implementation owners quietly block progress. Decision discovery helps the seller understand how the organization actually buys, who must agree, and what evidence each stakeholder needs.

## Core Rules

### Ask how decisions like this are made

Do not settle for "my manager decides" or "we will discuss internally." Ask about previous purchases, required approvals, evaluation stages, procurement, budget timing, legal review, security review, technical validation, and executive signoff.

Past buying behavior often reveals the real process.

### Separate influence from authority

Champions, users, evaluators, budget owners, economic buyers, procurement, legal, IT, security, finance, and executives have different roles. Map who cares, who decides, who signs, who blocks, and who implements.

Confusing influence with authority creates false confidence.

### Identify decision criteria

Ask how the buyer will compare options, what risks must be resolved, what success proof is required, and what tradeoffs matter. Criteria may include price, integration, compliance, user adoption, ROI, support, vendor risk, and timing.

If criteria are not explicit, the seller cannot control evaluation quality.

### Understand internal politics carefully

Stakeholders may disagree about priority, budget, ownership, or current vendor. Capture disagreement respectfully. Do not ask the champion to expose sensitive politics beyond what is needed.

Political risk should be managed, not exploited.

### Test champion strength

A champion can articulate pain, access stakeholders, influence criteria, and take internal action. A friendly contact who only attends calls is not a champion.

Ask what they are willing to do next inside their organization.

### Identify blockers early

Blockers may be incumbent vendor, IT constraints, data security, budget freeze, legal terms, change fatigue, implementation capacity, procurement rules, or executive skepticism. Surface blockers before proposal stage.

Late blockers are often discovery failures.

### Connect decision process to timeline and record the process as a living map

Map dates for evaluation, technical review, procurement, budget approval, committee meeting, contract, implementation, and go-live. Check whether timeline is buyer-owned or seller-created.

Timeline without owners is wishful.

Update stakeholder roles, criteria, blockers, dates, and confidence as the deal evolves. Share relevant pieces with the buyer to confirm accuracy.

### Ask for decision artifacts and identify decision stalls and no-decision risk

Where appropriate, ask whether the buyer has templates, procurement checklists, security questionnaires, vendor onboarding steps, board approval requirements, budget request forms, or evaluation scorecards. These artifacts reveal the real process better than verbal summaries.

Artifacts also help the seller prepare legal, security, and procurement teams early.

The biggest competitor may be doing nothing. Ask what would cause the project to stall, lose sponsorship, miss budget, or get pushed to next quarter. Capture the internal failure modes, not only the approval steps.

No-decision risk should be managed like competitor risk.

### Confirm stakeholder access strategy and distinguish formal process from real process

For each important stakeholder, decide whether access will happen through the champion, direct outreach, executive alignment, technical workshop, procurement process, or later stage. Lack of access should be a risk, not an assumption.

The map should create an access plan.

Many buyers describe the official process, but the real process includes informal reviews, executive preferences, political vetoes, trusted advisors, budget timing, and prior vendor history. Ask respectfully where deals usually slow down or surprise teams.

The seller should track both formal steps and practical realities.

## Common Traps

- Accepting vague statements about internal discussion.
- Treating a friendly user as the decision maker.
- Not asking how similar purchases were made before; skipping legal, procurement, security, IT, or finance until late
- Letting decision criteria remain implicit; ignoring stakeholder disagreement because it is uncomfortable
- Overestimating champion strength based on enthusiasm; building timelines around seller forecast needs
- Failing to update stakeholder maps after new people appear; asking for political details in a way that reduces trust
- Relying on verbal process summaries when artifacts could reveal the real path; mapping approval steps but not no-decision or stall risks
- Listing stakeholders without a plan to reach or influence them; treating the formal procurement path as the whole decision process

## Self-Check

- Is the actual decision process described from prior comparable purchases or buyer confirmation?
- Are influence, authority, approval, signature, blocking, and implementation roles separated?
- Are decision criteria explicit?
- Are procurement, legal, security, IT, finance, executive, and technical validation steps known where relevant?
- Is champion strength evidenced by internal action, not friendliness?
- Are blockers identified early?
- Are stakeholder disagreements captured respectfully?; are timeline dates tied to owners and buyer events?
- Is the decision map updated as facts change?; has the buyer confirmed the process where possible?
- Are decision artifacts such as checklists, questionnaires, scorecards, or procurement steps requested where appropriate?; are stall and no-decision risks identified?
- Is there an access strategy for each important stakeholder?; are informal reviews, political vetoes, trusted advisors, budget timing, and prior vendor history considered?
