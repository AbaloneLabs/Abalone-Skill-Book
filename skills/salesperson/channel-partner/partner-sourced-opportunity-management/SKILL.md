# Partner Sourced Opportunity Management

Partner-sourced opportunities carry two relationships: the buyer relationship and the partner relationship. Agents often treat partner leads as either automatically qualified or as ordinary direct leads, missing attribution, role clarity, partner incentives, and buyer trust. This skill helps the agent manage partner-sourced deals without creating channel conflict or weak buyer experience.

## Core Rules

### Confirm source and ownership

Identify who sourced the opportunity, whether it is registered, who owns the buyer relationship, and what rules govern engagement. Do not assume partner involvement means the partner owns all communication or that direct sales can bypass them.

### Qualify the buyer independently and define partner role in the deal

A partner referral is not automatically a good-fit opportunity. Confirm buyer problem, role, authority, timing, budget path, technical fit, and next action. Partner enthusiasm may reflect partner incentives rather than buyer readiness.

Clarify whether the partner will source, influence, resell, implement, support, integrate, procure, advise, or simply introduce. Role clarity determines margin, responsibilities, handoff, and customer expectation.

### Validate partner qualification quality and confirm buyer consent for handoff

Ask what the partner actually confirmed with the buyer: pain, timeline, authority, budget path, current solution, decision process, and desired next step. Partner notes that only say "interested" are not enough. Qualification quality determines how much direct discovery is still needed.

If the partner is introducing the buyer to direct sales or another internal owner, confirm that the buyer expects the handoff. Surprise outreach can make both partner and vendor look uncoordinated.

### Align incentives before strategy and decide how forecast will treat partner dependence

Partners may care about services revenue, resale margin, marketplace transaction, implementation control, or long-term account ownership. Understand incentives before assuming the partner will support the seller's preferred strategy.

Partner responsiveness, buyer access through partner, partner delivery quality, and channel approval can affect probability and close date. Make partner dependence visible in forecast instead of treating it as normal sales work.

### Protect direct customer discovery and define update rhythm with the partner

Even when a partner has a strong relationship, the seller may still need direct access to validate fit, technical requirements, and value. Define what must be learned directly from the buyer and what can rely on partner input.

Agree how often the seller and partner will exchange status, what must be shared, and what remains confidential. Without a rhythm, the partner may feel excluded or the seller may lose visibility into buyer-side movement.

### Handle partner-introduced trust carefully and align buyer message

The buyer may trust the partner more than the vendor. Honor that trust by referencing the introduction accurately, avoiding abrupt takeover, and giving the partner credit where appropriate. A clumsy transition can weaken the warm path.

Buyer should hear one coherent story about value, scope, pricing path, implementation, and support. Direct seller and partner messages that diverge create distrust.

### Respect partner economics and protect customer fit

Understand referral fees, reseller margin, marketplace fees, services revenue, implementation scope, and discount constraints. Commercial decisions can affect partner motivation and fairness.

Partners may push deals that fit their services or quota but not the product. Validate use case and implementation readiness. Do not let partner pressure override fit discipline.

### Coordinate follow-up cadence and document attribution and commitments

Decide who follows up, when, with what message, and how updates will be shared. Duplicate or conflicting outreach makes the buyer feel mishandled.

Record source, partner role, registration, owner, commercial terms, commitments, buyer status, next steps, and any constraints. Partner disputes often come from poor records.

### Plan handoff if partner implements and review partner performance

If the partner will deliver services or support, confirm capability, scope, buyer responsibilities, escalation path, and customer success coordination before close.

Track lead quality, conversion, buyer experience, implementation quality, and conflict rate. Partner-sourced pipeline should be evaluated for quality, not just volume.

## Common Traps

- Treating partner referrals as qualified without buyer discovery.
- Bypassing partner ownership and creating conflict; assuming the partner's role is obvious
- Letting buyer hear different value or scope stories; ignoring partner economics in discount or pricing decisions
- Allowing partner incentives to override customer fit; creating duplicate buyer outreach
- Failing to document source and registration; discovering partner implementation gaps after close
- Measuring partner value only by lead count; confusing partner influence with buyer commitment
- Hiding channel complexity from forecast review; accepting partner qualification without checking evidence; contacting the buyer before consented handoff; ignoring partner incentives that shape behavior
- Forecasting partner-dependent deals like direct deals; outsourcing all discovery to the partner; having no partner update rhythm; mishandling the trust created by the partner introduction

## Self-Check

- Is source, registration, and ownership clear?
- Is buyer qualification independently verified?; is partner role defined?
- Are buyer-facing messages aligned?; are partner economics understood?
- Is customer fit protected from partner pressure?; is follow-up cadence coordinated?
- Are attribution and commitments documented?; is partner delivery role validated where relevant?
- Is partner performance measured by quality?; are channel risks visible in CRM and forecast?
- Would the buyer experience a coordinated team rather than channel confusion?; is partner qualification evidence strong enough?
- Did the buyer consent to the handoff or next contact?; are partner incentives understood?
- Is partner dependence reflected in forecast?; is direct buyer discovery protected where needed?
- Is partner update rhythm defined?; is partner-introduced trust handled carefully?
