# Champion Deck And Forwardable Summary

A forwardable summary is not a compressed sales deck. It is a tool the champion uses when the seller is not in the room. The material must preserve the business case, scope, evidence, and decision ask without relying on live persuasion. Agents often make these artifacts too polished, too product-heavy, or too optimistic, leaving the champion unable to handle internal questions. This skill helps the agent build buyer-facing summaries that travel accurately.

## Core Rules

### Write for the absent seller

Assume the seller will not be available when the champion forwards the material. Include the problem, impact, current state, recommended approach, expected value, assumptions, risks, and requested decision in language a buyer can repeat. Avoid shorthand that only makes sense after the sales call.

The champion should not have to translate vendor language into internal business language.

### Preserve buyer language and evidence

Use the buyer's confirmed pain, metrics, workflow facts, deadlines, and decision criteria. Distinguish confirmed facts from seller hypotheses. If the deck says "reduce review time," it should show whose review time, current baseline, why it matters, and what evidence supports the claim.

Forwardable material becomes weak when it sounds like a generic vendor claim.

### Equip the champion for objections

Champions need answers to predictable internal concerns: why change now, why this vendor, why this scope, why this price, what risks remain, what implementation effort is required, and what happens if the company does nothing. Include concise objection handling without attacking other stakeholders.

The goal is not to script the champion, but to make them safe and credible.

### Make the ask explicit

Every summary should name the decision or action requested: approve evaluation, join an executive call, validate business case, authorize technical review, start procurement, review contract, commit implementation resources, or decide not to proceed. A deck that ends with "next steps" but no buyer action is weak enablement.

### Keep scope and caveats visible

Include what is in scope, what is optional, what is deferred, what the buyer must provide, and what assumptions shape the recommendation. If the champion forwards a summary without caveats, internal stakeholders may hear a broader promise than the seller intended.

### Match the audience level

An executive summary should emphasize business consequence, priority, risk, funding path, and decision. A technical stakeholder may need architecture, integration, security, and implementation assumptions. Finance may need conservative value logic and cost timing. Procurement may need terms, process, and documents. Do not use one artifact for all audiences if it forces important details to disappear.

### Support the champion without overusing them

Ask whether the champion is willing and able to carry the message. A friendly contact may not have authority, credibility, political capital, or time. Provide materials, talking points, and suggested internal path, but do not make the champion do work that should be done by an executive sponsor, technical owner, or seller.

### Avoid confidential or sensitive overreach

Do not include sensitive internal buyer information, named stakeholder criticism, competitive gossip, unapproved discounts, security claims, roadmap commitments, or legal interpretations in a forwardable artifact unless it is appropriate and approved. Forwarded material can reach people far beyond the intended audience.

### Use brevity with enough substance and keep CRM and handoff aligned

A forwardable summary should be concise, but not thin. Prefer a clear one-page memo or short deck with decision-useful content over a long marketing deck. Remove feature inventory, broad brand claims, and decorative pages that bury the decision.

Record what was sent, to whom, what claims were made, what assumptions were included, and what internal action was requested. If the deal advances, implementation and customer success may rely on the same artifact to understand what was promised.

## Common Traps

- Creating a prettier version of the sales pitch instead of a buyer decision aid.
- Using vendor language that the champion cannot repeat naturally.
- Leaving out risks and assumptions because they make the deck less persuasive.
- Giving the champion a deck but no internal strategy or requested action.
- Assuming the champion has authority because they are enthusiastic; overloading one artifact for executive, technical, finance, procurement, and user audiences
- Including unapproved roadmap, legal, pricing, security, or implementation promises; hiding the cost of inaction or business consequence behind product screenshots
- Forgetting that forwarded material may reach skeptics, competitors, procurement, or legal; letting the deck drift from CRM, proposal, business case, or contract language
- Making the champion defend a value claim that the buyer has not validated; treating "forwarded internally" as progress without evidence of internal response

## Self-Check

- Can the champion explain the problem, value, scope, risk, and requested decision without the seller present?
- Does the material use buyer-confirmed language and evidence?
- Are facts, assumptions, hypotheses, and caveats separated?
- Does the artifact answer why change, why now, why this scope, why this vendor, and what happens if no action is taken?
- Is the requested buyer action explicit?
- Are likely executive, technical, finance, procurement, and user concerns covered or intentionally routed elsewhere?
- Is the champion's authority and willingness to carry the message verified?
- Are sensitive claims, pricing, legal terms, security statements, and roadmap references approved?
- Is the artifact concise without becoming content-free?; are scope boundaries, buyer responsibilities, and deferred items visible?
- Is the sent artifact recorded in CRM with claims and assumptions?; is there evidence that forwarding created internal movement, not only activity?
