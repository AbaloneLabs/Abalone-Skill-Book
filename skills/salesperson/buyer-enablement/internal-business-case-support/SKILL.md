# Internal Business Case Support

Internal business case support is different from sending an ROI slide. The seller is helping the buyer make a defensible argument inside their own organization. That argument must survive finance review, executive skepticism, competing priorities, implementation concerns, and procurement pressure. Agents often overstate savings, omit buyer effort, or create a case the champion cannot defend. This skill helps the agent support the buyer's case without turning uncertain value into a promise.

## Core Rules

### Identify the internal decision

Clarify what the business case must unlock: budget creation, executive sponsorship, project prioritization, procurement approval, risk acceptance, renewal defense, or stakeholder alignment. Different decisions need different evidence. Finance approval may require conservative assumptions, while executive sponsorship may require strategic fit and risk timing.

Do not build a generic ROI artifact before knowing the internal decision path.

### Co-author with buyer-owned data

Use buyer-provided or buyer-validated inputs wherever possible: volumes, labor time, error rates, current spend, revenue impact, risk frequency, churn, cycle time, customer impact, staffing constraints, and implementation effort. If the seller provides benchmarks or examples, label them as external references rather than buyer facts.

The business case belongs to the buyer, so the buyer must be able to defend the numbers.

### Separate benefit types

Distinguish hard savings, productivity capacity, revenue impact, avoided cost, risk reduction, compliance value, employee experience, customer experience, and strategic optionality. Each category needs a different standard of proof. Do not convert every benefit into hard-dollar ROI just because a spreadsheet wants a number.

Soft and strategic value can matter, but it should not be disguised as audited savings.

### Include cost, effort, and timing

Show subscription cost, services, implementation, integration, training, internal labor, data cleanup, change management, support, partner fees, taxes, and opportunity cost where relevant. Also show when costs begin and when benefits are expected to appear. A case can look strong annually while still failing near-term cash or resource constraints.

### Account for adoption and buyer responsibilities

Value often depends on the buyer assigning owners, changing process, cleaning data, training users, enforcing workflow, and reviewing metrics. Name those responsibilities. If the buyer cannot commit them, lower the expected value or propose a narrower phase.

### Use conservative ranges

Where uncertainty is meaningful, show conservative, expected, and upside cases. Identify the assumptions that most affect the result. A single payback number creates false certainty and may collapse when challenged.

### Connect the case to decision criteria and make the case forwardable

The business case should shape how the buyer evaluates vendors and scope. If the value depends on risk reduction, security, reliability, support, and implementation quality may matter more than lowest price. If the value depends on labor efficiency, workflow adoption and integration depth may matter more than a long feature list.

Write so an executive, finance partner, procurement reviewer, or skeptical stakeholder can understand the logic without seller narration. Include context, assumptions, sources, caveats, and the requested decision. Avoid marketing tone.

### Prepare the internal sponsor narrative and show priority against competing investments

The champion may need a short spoken narrative as much as a document. Help them explain why the problem matters now, what alternatives were considered, why the recommended scope is proportionate, what risks are controlled, and what decision is needed. A strong narrative lets the sponsor answer "why this, why now, why not wait" without sounding like they are repeating vendor copy.

Most internal business cases compete with other projects, not only with other vendors. Ask what else could use the same budget, executive attention, implementation capacity, or change management effort. Position the case against realistic alternatives such as hiring, internal build, incumbent expansion, process change, outsourcing, or doing nothing. If the business case cannot explain priority, it may be valid but not fundable.

### Avoid outcome promises beyond control and keep the case updated

Do not promise revenue growth, headcount reduction, compliance outcome, customer retention, or payback unless buyer data, adoption plan, and approved language support it. Use contribution and dependency language when the product is only one factor.

If scope, price, rollout, timeline, modules, services, or success metrics change, revise the business case. Old value attached to new scope is misleading and creates post-sale conflict.

## Common Traps

- Building the case before understanding the approval decision; treating seller benchmarks as buyer-validated data
- Mixing soft benefits into hard ROI without agreement; omitting implementation effort and internal labor
- Presenting one attractive ROI number with no sensitivity; ignoring adoption requirements and buyer-side work
- Writing for the seller's forecast instead of the buyer's internal approval; failing to connect business value to evaluation criteria
- Promising outcomes the product cannot control alone; leaving finance, operations, or metric owners out until late
- Reusing an old business case after scope or price changes; making the champion defend assumptions they did not help create
- Giving the champion numbers but no narrative for executive discussion; ignoring competing initiatives that may be more urgent than this purchase
- Treating "good ROI" as automatically equal to budget approval; comparing only against direct competitors and not against internal alternatives

## Self-Check

- Is the internal decision the business case must support clear?
- Are the main inputs buyer-provided or buyer-validated?
- Are seller benchmarks and assumptions labeled separately from buyer facts?
- Are hard savings, productivity, revenue, risk, compliance, experience, and strategic value separated?
- Are total cost, buyer effort, and timing included?; are adoption responsibilities and buyer owners named?
- Are conservative, expected, and upside cases considered where uncertainty matters?; are the most sensitive assumptions visible?
- Does the case influence vendor and scope decision criteria?; is the artifact forwardable to finance, executives, procurement, and skeptics?
- Are outcome claims bounded by what the product and buyer actions can control?; has the case been updated after scope, price, rollout, or timeline changes?
- Can the champion explain the case verbally without sounding like a vendor?; does the case address competing investments and opportunity cost?
- Are realistic alternatives such as hiring, internal build, incumbent expansion, outsourcing, process change, and doing nothing considered?; is the decision framed as fundable priority, not only positive ROI?
