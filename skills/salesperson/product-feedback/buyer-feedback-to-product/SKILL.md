# Buyer Feedback To Product

Buyer feedback is valuable only when it is translated from sales conversation into product evidence. A buyer's requested feature may represent a real market gap, a misunderstanding, a negotiation tactic, a one-off workflow, or a bad-fit account. Weak feedback forwards raw requests and asks product to build. This skill helps the agent capture buyer feedback in a way product can evaluate without distorting the sales process.

## Core Rules

### Capture the buyer problem behind the request

Record what the buyer is trying to accomplish, why current capability is insufficient, what process fails, and what outcome they need. Do not only capture the requested feature name.

Product teams prioritize problems better than solution demands.

### Separate signal type

Classify feedback as feature gap, usability issue, integration need, pricing or packaging issue, documentation gap, implementation concern, competitive pressure, compliance requirement, security concern, or market trend.

Different signal types need different owners.

### Include buyer segment and deal context

Capture industry, company size, persona, use case, region, deal stage, opportunity size, urgency, competitive context, and whether the request blocks purchase or is a preference.

This prevents one large but unusual deal from being mistaken for broad market demand.

### Label evidence strength

State whether feedback comes from one buyer, repeated accounts, strategic segment, lost deal, active evaluation, customer expansion, or pilot evidence. Avoid saying "customers need this" when only one prospect asked.

Confidence matters.

### Avoid roadmap promises

Tell buyers their feedback will be shared, not that it will be built, prioritized, or delivered by a date. Use approved roadmap language and escalate requests for commitments.

Sales feedback should not become product obligation.

### Preserve competitive insight carefully and note workaround quality

If the buyer compares competitors, capture what they value and why. Do not spread unverified competitor claims as fact. Separate buyer perception from confirmed competitor capability.

Perception still matters, but it should be labeled.

If a workaround exists, describe whether it is safe, scalable, manual, costly, unsupported, or acceptable. Workaround quality affects urgency.

Do not hide a fragile workaround to make product feedback sound stronger.

### Route feedback to the right place and close the loop to sales

Product, design, pricing, packaging, documentation, partnerships, security, compliance, implementation, or customer success may own the signal. Route based on problem domain.

When product responds, update positioning, qualification, objection handling, demo guidance, and roadmap-safe language. If product declines, sales still needs a truthful boundary.

### Distinguish market signal from deal tactic and include what the buyer tried today

Buyers sometimes raise feature requests to negotiate price, slow a decision, compare vendors, or avoid saying no. Capture the request seriously, but note whether the buyer has a real workflow dependency and whether they would still buy without it.

This helps product separate durable market demand from deal-specific pressure.

Product feedback is stronger when it includes current workaround, competing tool, manual process, spreadsheet, service provider, internal project, or "doing nothing" path. This shows how painful the gap is and what alternative the product must beat.

Do not report unmet demand without the current substitute.

### Protect confidentiality and deal context and track feedback outcome against sales motion

Some buyer feedback includes sensitive strategy, internal process, competitor pricing, security concern, or regulated use case. Redact or summarize appropriately before broad product sharing.

Product needs context, not unnecessary private deal detail.

After product accepts, declines, or defers feedback, update whether the affected accounts should continue, narrow scope, nurture, or disqualify. Product feedback should change sales behavior, not simply enter a backlog.

If the same feedback keeps appearing with no product action, sales needs clear positioning and qualification boundaries.

## Common Traps

- Forwarding feature requests without underlying problem.
- Treating one strategic prospect as proof of market need.
- Mixing product gaps, pricing issues, documentation gaps, and implementation problems; promising roadmap movement to keep a deal alive
- Presenting buyer perceptions of competitors as verified facts; hiding workaround quality
- Routing every signal to product when another owner fits better; failing to capture segment and deal context
- Letting product feedback disappear without sales guidance update; using feedback to justify a deal that should be disqualified
- Treating negotiation pressure as product demand without checking workflow dependency; omitting the buyer's current workaround or alternative
- Sharing sensitive deal details too broadly while trying to make the feedback persuasive; capturing feedback in product systems without changing sales motion, qualification, or positioning

## Self-Check

- Is the buyer problem behind the request clear?
- Is the signal type classified?
- Are segment, persona, use case, region, stage, deal size, urgency, and competitive context included?
- Is evidence strength labeled?
- Does buyer-facing language avoid roadmap promises?
- Are competitor claims separated from buyer perception?
- Is workaround quality described honestly?; is the feedback routed to the right product or operational owner?
- Does product response update sales guidance?; is feedback used to improve fit, not excuse bad deals?
- Is the request separated from negotiation tactic, delay tactic, and true workflow dependency?; is the buyer's current workaround or alternative captured?
- Is confidential deal context redacted or summarized appropriately?; did product response update whether affected deals continue, narrow, nurture, or disqualify?
