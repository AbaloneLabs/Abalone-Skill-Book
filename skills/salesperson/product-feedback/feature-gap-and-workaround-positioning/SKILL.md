# Feature Gap And Workaround Positioning

Feature gaps are moments of truth in sales. A seller can preserve trust by explaining limits clearly, or create future churn by making a workaround sound like a supported capability. Buyers can often accept a limitation if they understand impact and alternatives. This skill helps the agent position gaps and workarounds honestly while keeping the deal decision grounded.

## Core Rules

### Name the gap precisely

State what the product does not support, what is partially supported, what depends on configuration, and what requires services, partner work, or roadmap. Avoid vague "we can handle that" language.

Precision prevents misinterpretation.

### Understand why the gap matters

Ask what workflow, stakeholder, compliance need, integration, reporting requirement, or business outcome depends on the missing capability. A gap may be critical, minor, or irrelevant depending on context.

Do not assume every gap blocks the deal.

### Describe workaround status

Classify the workaround as supported, documented, manual, partner-led, services-based, temporary, risky, or unsupported. Explain tradeoffs: effort, reliability, scale, data quality, security, reporting, user experience, and maintenance.

A workaround is not the same as product fit.

### Avoid roadmap substitution

Do not use future roadmap to cover a current gap unless product has approved customer-facing language and commitment. Even then, separate current decision from future possibility.

Buyers should decide based on what they can rely on.

### Involve the right owner

Technical, implementation, product, legal, security, customer success, or partner teams may need to review a gap before the seller positions it. Escalate when the gap affects feasibility, contract, security, compliance, or success.

### Compare alternatives fairly and document gap acceptance

Sometimes the buyer should use another tool, keep an existing process, buy services, wait, or disqualify. Do not force a workaround when it creates poor customer outcome.

Fair positioning can protect long-term reputation.

If the buyer accepts a workaround or limitation, record who accepted it, what tradeoffs were explained, what assumptions apply, and how it will be handled post-sale.

Post-sale teams need this context.

### Monitor repeated gaps and make buyer acceptance explicit

Repeated gap conversations are product and ICP signals. Track which segments ask, whether gaps block deals, and whether workarounds succeed after sale.

If the deal proceeds despite a gap, confirm that the right buyer stakeholders understand the limitation and workaround. Acceptance from a champion is not enough if users, technical owners, legal, security, or executives will experience the risk.

Gap acceptance should be documented before proposal or close.

### Recheck pricing and value after a gap and avoid competitive overcorrection

A workaround may reduce value, increase services cost, require buyer effort, or shift risk to the customer. Revisit pricing, scope, success criteria, and implementation assumptions instead of pretending the original value case is unchanged.

The economics of the deal may change when the gap is real.

If a competitor has the missing feature, do not dismiss it automatically or attack the competitor. Acknowledge the difference, compare buyer impact, and return to the full decision criteria.

Credible positioning is more persuasive than defensiveness.

### Preserve the gap in handoff records and assign a review owner for risky gaps

If the buyer accepts a limitation, implementation and customer success must still know it exists. Record the gap, workaround, tradeoffs, owner, buyer acceptance, and any follow-up promise in the handoff.

Accepted gaps become post-sale risks if they are forgotten.

For gaps involving security, compliance, legal terms, data, integration, accessibility, service levels, or implementation effort, assign an internal owner to review the positioning before close. The seller should not be the only judge of acceptable risk.

Risky gaps need accountable review, not just optimistic messaging.

## Common Traps

- Saying "yes" to a requirement that is only possible through manual effort.
- Treating a fragile workaround as supported capability; assuming a gap is fatal without understanding buyer impact
- Using roadmap as a substitute for current fit; failing to involve technical or product owners for risky gaps
- Hiding maintenance burden from the buyer; letting the buyer accept a gap verbally without documentation
- Creating post-sale surprises for implementation or success teams; treating repeated gaps as isolated objections
- Continuing a deal that should narrow or disqualify; relying on one champion's acceptance when other stakeholders carry the limitation
- Keeping the same price, scope, or success case after a workaround materially changes value; reacting defensively when a competitor covers the gap better
- Letting an accepted gap disappear before implementation or customer success handoff; letting the seller alone approve positioning for high-risk gaps

## Self-Check

- Is the gap precisely named?
- Is partial support, configuration, services, partner work, and roadmap separated?
- Is the buyer impact of the gap understood?
- Is workaround status and tradeoff clear?
- Is roadmap language approved and separated from current capability?
- Are technical, product, implementation, legal, security, success, or partner owners involved where needed?; are alternatives and disqualification considered?
- Is buyer acceptance of limitation documented?; are post-sale handoff implications clear?
- Are repeated gaps tracked as product and ICP signals?; is gap acceptance confirmed with stakeholders who own risk and usage?
- Are pricing, scope, success criteria, and implementation assumptions revisited after material gaps?; is competitive positioning honest and tied to buyer decision criteria?
- Is the accepted gap preserved in handoff with workaround, tradeoffs, owner, and follow-up promises?; is there an accountable review owner for security, compliance, legal, data, integration, accessibility, SLA, or implementation-risk gaps?
