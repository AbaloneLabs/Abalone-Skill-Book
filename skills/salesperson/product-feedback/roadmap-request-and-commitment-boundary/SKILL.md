# Roadmap Request And Commitment Boundary

Roadmap questions are common in serious evaluations, but they are dangerous when sellers trade future certainty for present momentum. A buyer may need to know whether a gap will close, while the company must avoid promises it cannot keep. This skill helps the agent discuss roadmap direction with approved boundaries, evidence, and escalation.

## Core Rules

### Separate current product from future direction

Always distinguish what exists today, what is in beta, what is planned, what is being considered, and what is not committed. Buyers should know what they can rely on for their decision.

Current capability should anchor the deal.

### Use approved roadmap language

Roadmap statements should come from product-approved sources or authorized owners. Avoid dates, scope, priority, or guaranteed delivery unless explicitly approved for that buyer and context.

Unapproved roadmap statements can become contractual expectations.

### Understand why the roadmap matters

Ask what business process, decision criterion, stakeholder concern, or purchase condition depends on the future capability. A roadmap request may be curiosity, evaluation risk, negotiation leverage, or true blocker.

Do not treat all roadmap asks as blockers.

### Escalate commitment requests

If the buyer wants contractual language, delivery date, custom feature, design influence, beta access, or executive assurance, route to product, legal, leadership, or deal desk as required.

Frontline sellers should not invent commitment authority.

### Provide current alternatives honestly

If a current workaround, partner, configuration, or services path exists, explain tradeoffs. If no safe alternative exists, say so. Do not make a workaround sound like the future feature.

### Avoid roadmap-for-discount tradeoffs and document what was said

Do not imply that a discount, longer term, or faster close will secure roadmap delivery unless an approved commercial and product agreement exists.

Roadmap is not a casual concession.

Record exact roadmap language shared, source, approver, buyer dependency, open risks, and follow-up owner. This protects sales, product, legal, and post-sale teams.

### Reassess fit if roadmap is required and control written materials and contracts

If the buyer cannot succeed without future functionality, the deal may need to pause, narrow, or disqualify. Selling before capability exists may be irresponsible.

Roadmap risk often enters through proposals, mutual action plans, emails, security questionnaires, procurement portals, or redlines. Review written materials for accidental commitments such as "will support," "coming in Q3," or "included in roadmap."

If legal or procurement asks for future-functionality language, route it through approved contract review.

### Prepare a no-roadmap answer and monitor internal pressure

Sometimes the safest answer is that the company cannot commit. Prepare respectful language that explains current capability, available alternatives, feedback capture, and the buyer's decision options.

A clear no is better than a vague future that the buyer interprets as yes.

Large deals can create pressure on product to make exceptions. Capture revenue, strategic value, buyer dependency, and product cost, but do not let sales urgency override product governance.

Roadmap exceptions need explicit executive and product ownership.

### Reconfirm before close and keep internal roadmap notes separate from customer language

If a deal involved roadmap discussion, reconfirm before close what was and was not committed. Make sure proposal, contract, CRM, handoff, and customer-facing recap match the approved boundary.

The riskiest roadmap promises are often made early and forgotten until implementation.

Sales may have internal roadmap context that is useful for strategy but not approved for buyer communication. Label internal-only notes clearly and do not paste them into proposals, recap emails, or mutual plans.

Internal confidence is not the same as external commitment.

## Common Traps

- Blurring current product, beta, planned work, and ideas.
- Giving dates because the buyer is pushing; treating a seller's belief about roadmap as approved language
- Not asking why the future feature matters; promising beta access or influence without approval
- Using roadmap to overcome a current non-fit; trading discount or contract term for implied product delivery
- Failing to document roadmap statements; letting legal or procurement add future-functionality language unnoticed
- Closing a deal that depends on uncommitted capability; allowing proposals, emails, mutual action plans, or redlines to contain accidental roadmap commitments
- Avoiding a clear no when no commitment is approved; letting deal size create shadow roadmap commitments outside product governance
- Failing to reconfirm roadmap boundaries before contract, proposal, or handoff; copying internal-only roadmap notes into customer-facing materials

## Self-Check

- Are current, beta, planned, considered, and uncommitted capabilities separated?
- Is roadmap language approved for customer use?
- Are dates, scope, priority, and delivery commitments avoided unless authorized?
- Is buyer dependency on the roadmap understood?
- Are commitment requests escalated to product, legal, leadership, or deal desk?
- Are current alternatives and workarounds described with tradeoffs?; is roadmap kept separate from discount and commercial concessions?
- Is exact language, source, approver, dependency, risk, and owner documented?; does the deal still make sense if the roadmap item does not ship?
- Is pause, narrow scope, or disqualification considered where future capability is required?; were written materials and contract language checked for accidental future commitments?
- Is there approved language for declining roadmap commitment while preserving buyer respect?; are any roadmap exceptions owned explicitly by product and leadership?
- Before close, do proposal, contract, CRM, handoff, and customer recap match the approved roadmap boundary?; are internal-only roadmap notes clearly separated from approved customer-facing language?
