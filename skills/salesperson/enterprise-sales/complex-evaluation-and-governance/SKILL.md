# Complex Evaluation And Governance

Enterprise evaluations are rarely simple product comparisons. They are governed processes where a buyer tries to reduce technical, financial, legal, operational, political, and career risk before making a public commitment. Agents often treat an evaluation as a demo sequence or a checklist of requested documents, then miss the decision architecture underneath it. This skill helps the agent design and inspect the evaluation as a buyer-owned governance process with clear evidence, authority, accountability, and stopping rules.

Use this skill when the work involves evaluation plans, pilots, proof-of-concepts, enterprise trials, legal or security review, multi-stakeholder approval, procurement gates, technical validation, executive review, or any deal where progress depends on more than one person saying the product looks good. The agent should have enough freedom to propose structure, ask for missing evidence, narrow scope, and flag risks, but not enough freedom to invent buyer authority, fabricate proof, or promise delivery acceptance.

## Core Rules

### Define the decision the evaluation must support

Start by naming the specific decision the buyer must make at the end of the evaluation. Strong evaluation design answers questions such as: approve purchase, select vendor, validate integration, confirm compliance, justify budget, retire incumbent, expand deployment, or reject the project. A weak evaluation only lists activities such as "run demo," "complete security questionnaire," or "hold technical workshop."

Tie every activity to a decision criterion. If a task does not produce evidence for a decision, remove it or label it as relationship-building rather than evaluation. This matters because complex evaluations expand easily. Buyers may request extra demos, documents, calls, or pilot features without clarifying whether those requests change the decision. The agent should protect both sides from endless evaluation by asking what evidence would be enough, who will accept it, and what happens if the evidence is positive, mixed, or negative.

### Map governance before momentum

Enterprise deals often appear to move quickly until they hit a governance gate that was invisible to the seller. Map economic buyer approval, user acceptance, technical review, security review, privacy review, legal review, procurement, finance, implementation signoff, executive steering, and any board or public-sector process where relevant. For each gate, identify the owner, required artifact, review standard, expected timing, blockers, and escalation path.

Do not treat a champion's confidence as governance clearance. A champion can coordinate, translate, and advocate, but they may not control budget, risk acceptance, contract language, security exceptions, or implementation staffing. If the champion cannot name the approver, process, or objection likely to appear at a gate, the evaluation is not governed yet.

### Convert seller tasks into buyer commitments

A seller-controlled plan can create activity without progress. A stronger evaluation plan includes mutual commitments: what the seller will provide, what the buyer will test, which stakeholders will attend, what data will be supplied, how findings will be scored, when review meetings happen, and who confirms the next decision. Every major step should have a buyer action, not only a seller follow-up.

Be precise about ownership. "Customer to review" is not enough. Name the function or person, the artifact they review, the decision they can make, and the due date. If the buyer will not commit to a reasonable action, treat that as evidence about priority or authority instead of hiding it behind polite language.

### Set evidence standards by risk

Not all evaluation evidence has the same weight. A lightweight workflow demo may be enough for a low-risk use case, while a regulated deployment may require security documentation, data-processing terms, audit evidence, integration testing, accessibility review, disaster recovery review, and implementation capacity checks. Match proof to the buyer's risk, not to the seller's convenience.

Separate observed evidence from asserted evidence. "The integration worked with sample data in a sandbox" is different from "the integration will support production load under the buyer's identity and access model." "Legal has the draft" is different from "legal has approved the terms." The agent should write and forecast based on the strongest confirmed evidence, then label remaining assumptions.

### Control pilot and proof-of-concept scope

A pilot should test the riskiest assumptions, not become a free implementation. Define use case, user group, data, success criteria, technical dependencies, support model, timeline, exit criteria, cost responsibility, privacy constraints, and what commercial decision follows. If a pilot has no decision owner or no agreed success standard, it can consume resources while postponing the real buying question.

Do not let the buyer add production obligations through informal pilot requests. Requests for custom work, sensitive data handling, integrations, service levels, admin access, performance guarantees, or expanded users may require security, legal, product, or implementation review before the seller agrees. The agent should flag when evaluation scope has crossed into delivery commitment.

### Maintain a decision and assumption log

Complex evaluations create many small agreements across calls, emails, documents, and internal notes. Keep a decision log that records criteria, owners, evidence accepted, open questions, commitments, deadlines, approvals, and assumptions. This is not bureaucracy for its own sake. It prevents the seller, buyer, and delivery team from remembering different versions of the same process.

Update the log when conditions change. If the buyer adds a stakeholder, changes the use case, extends the pilot, raises a compliance issue, or delays budget approval, the evaluation plan should reflect the changed risk. A stale mutual action plan is worse than no plan because it gives false confidence.

### Forecast governance completion, not enthusiasm

Enterprise forecast confidence should rise when governance gates are passed, not when meetings feel positive. Polite engagement, high attendance, executive curiosity, or a successful demo can still end in no decision if budget, legal, procurement, security, or implementation remains unresolved. Inspect forecast by asking which required approvals are complete, which are scheduled, which are blocked, and which have not yet been discovered.

Stage progression should require buyer-verified evidence. If the opportunity is marked late-stage but no economic buyer has confirmed value, no technical owner has validated feasibility, or no procurement path is known, the forecast is inflated. The agent should surface that gap plainly.

### Protect the post-sale handoff during evaluation

Evaluation artifacts often become the seed of implementation expectations. Capture promised outcomes, known limitations, workaround assumptions, configuration decisions, integration constraints, data conditions, security exceptions, timeline dependencies, and support needs. Delivery teams should not learn after signature that the deal was sold on an unreviewed pilot condition or executive promise.

When a risk is accepted to win the deal, name who accepted it and how it will be managed. Silence at handoff turns sales risk into customer success debt.

## Common Traps

- Treating a long evaluation plan as proof of buyer commitment. Length can hide weak authority, unclear value, or avoidance of a commercial decision.
- Letting the seller own every next step. If the buyer does not contribute data, stakeholders, criteria, or decisions, the evaluation may be vendor theater.
- Accepting "legal is reviewing" or "security is reviewing" as a milestone without knowing reviewer, standard, timeline, and blockers.
- Designing a pilot around impressive features instead of the riskiest assumptions that could stop purchase or implementation.
- Using a champion's optimism to skip economic buyer, procurement, or security validation.
- Allowing evaluation scope to drift into implementation, customization, or production support without approval.
- Forecasting based on meeting sentiment rather than completed governance gates.
- Forgetting to update handoff records when evaluation findings change what was promised.

## Self-Check

- Is the final buyer decision named, and does every evaluation activity produce evidence for that decision?
- Are all relevant governance gates mapped with owner, artifact, review standard, timing, and escalation path?
- Does the plan include buyer-owned commitments, not only seller follow-ups?
- Are evidence standards matched to the buyer's risk level and deployment context?
- Are observed facts separated from assumptions, hopes, and unconfirmed approvals?
- If there is a pilot or proof of concept, are scope, success criteria, data, users, timeline, exit criteria, and commercial next step explicit?
- Are legal, security, privacy, procurement, finance, technical, and implementation risks routed to the right owners before promises are made?
- Does the forecast reflect completed governance gates rather than enthusiasm or activity volume?
- Are changes to criteria, stakeholders, use case, or timeline reflected in the evaluation plan and CRM record?
- Would customer success or implementation understand exactly what was tested, promised, limited, and still unresolved?
