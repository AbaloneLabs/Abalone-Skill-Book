# LinkedIn Engagement And Credibility

Social selling is not public prospecting spam. Professional networks expose seller judgment, credibility, and respect. Agents often rush to pitch after a connection or leave generic comments that add no value. This skill helps the agent use LinkedIn-style engagement to build relevance and trust before asking for time.

## Core Rules

### Start by listening

Review the prospect's role, posts, company context, mutual connections, industry themes, and public signals. Do not engage based only on title.

### Add value in public and do not fake familiarity

Comments should be specific, thoughtful, and relevant. Avoid generic praise, self-promotion, or hijacking the post. Public engagement should make the seller look credible even to observers.

A shared group, post view, or connection request is not a relationship. Be honest about the context and avoid implying personal connection that does not exist.

### Treat connection requests as small asks and build credibility before conversion

A connection request should have a reason when the person is not already familiar. Keep it brief, relevant, and low pressure. Do not use the request note as a full pitch.

Credibility comes from useful comments, relevant content, accurate profile claims, and consistent professionalism. If the seller's public activity is thin or self-promotional, direct outreach may feel less trustworthy.

### Avoid public disagreement traps and use mutual connections ethically

If a prospect posts something questionable or competitive, do not argue in public to prove expertise. Ask thoughtful questions or move sensitive disagreement to private conversation when appropriate.

Do not imply endorsement from a mutual connection unless that person has actually provided one. Mutuality can provide context, not automatic trust.

### Keep social activity aligned with account plan and avoid automation that looks automated

Social engagement should support account strategy: stakeholder mapping, trigger monitoring, credibility, or warm path. Random engagement can consume time without creating useful evidence.

Connection requests, comments, and messages that are obviously templated reduce credibility. If automation is used, it must still respect relevance, timing, consent, and human review for high-value accounts.

### Know when not to engage and separate thought leadership from prospecting

Some posts are personal, political, sensitive, crisis-related, or outside the seller's expertise. Silence can be the more professional choice. Social selling includes restraint.

A seller's posts can build credibility at scale, but comments and direct messages should still be buyer-specific. Do not assume someone who likes thought leadership has invited a sales motion. Move from content engagement to prospecting only when relevance is clear.

### Keep a consistent professional point of view and connect engagement to buyer relevance

Credibility grows when public activity shows a coherent perspective on the buyer's domain, not random promotional bursts. A scattered or purely self-serving presence weakens later outreach.

If moving to a message, reference a real signal and offer a relevant idea, question, or resource. Do not jump from a public comment to a hard pitch without context.

### Respect personal-professional boundaries and use profile credibility responsibly

Avoid using sensitive personal posts, family information, health events, politics, or tragedies as sales hooks. Professional relevance matters.

Seller profile, content, and activity shape trust. Claims, case studies, and expertise should be accurate and not overstate authority.

### Coordinate account-level social activity and watch compliance and company policy and know when to move off-platform and track useful signals

Multiple sellers engaging the same account should align on narrative and avoid crowding the buyer's feed. Social engagement can become noisy.

Regulated industries, employment rules, endorsement rules, and company social policies may constrain claims, testimonials, or outreach. Use approved language.

Move to a meeting, email, or formal path when the buyer shows relevant interest. Do not keep the relationship in vague public engagement forever.

Record meaningful public signals, not every like. Use social insights to improve account research, timing, and messaging.

## Common Traps

- Treating LinkedIn as another cold email channel.
- Commenting with generic praise; pitching immediately after connection
- Pretending weak social contact is a relationship; using personal-sensitive content as a sales trigger
- Overstating credibility on profile or posts; having several sellers crowd the same buyer
- Making unapproved claims publicly; staying in public engagement without a next step
- Recording every social action as intent; arguing publicly with prospects or competitors; ignoring buyer's preferred communication style; sending connection requests that read like full pitches
- Trying to convert before building any credibility; creating public disagreement that damages trust; imlying mutual connection endorsement without permission; spending social time disconnected from account strategy
- Using obvious automation that damages credibility; engaging on sensitive posts where restraint would be wiser; treating thought leadership likes as sales consent; maintaining a scattered or purely promotional public presence
