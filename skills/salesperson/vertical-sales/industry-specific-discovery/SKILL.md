# Industry Specific Discovery

Industry-specific discovery is not sprinkling vertical buzzwords into generic questions. Different industries have different workflows, economics, regulations, seasonality, risk tolerance, buying committees, and proof standards. Agents often overgeneralize from one customer or use industry claims without understanding the buyer's actual operating model. This skill helps the agent adapt discovery to the vertical while staying evidence-driven.

## Core Rules

### Learn the buyer's operating model

Ask how the organization creates value, serves customers, manages risk, measures performance, and makes operational decisions. Industry labels are broad; two companies in the same vertical can work very differently.

### Use industry language carefully

Use buyer language where confirmed, but do not overuse jargon to appear credible. If a term has specific meaning in the industry, verify that the buyer uses it the same way.

### Identify industry-specific workflows

Map the workflows the product may affect: claims, patient intake, loan origination, field service, inventory, procurement, enrollment, logistics, compliance reporting, or store operations. Discovery should reach the actual work, not just strategic themes.

### Understand economic drivers and check regulatory and compliance context

Ask which metrics matter in the vertical: margin, throughput, utilization, loss ratio, readmission, conversion, shrink, cycle time, compliance cost, downtime, retention, or service level. Value should map to metrics the buyer recognizes.

Regulated buyers may need security, privacy, auditability, data residency, retention, accessibility, procurement rules, or specific approvals. Do not provide legal conclusions; identify requirements and route to qualified owners.

### Validate sources of industry understanding and ask about industry constraints that shape change

Use buyer statements, credible customer examples, internal subject matter experts, implementation history, product data, and current documentation. Do not rely only on generic industry summaries or outdated assumptions. If the source is weak, treat the industry insight as a hypothesis.

Some verticals have seasonal peaks, staffing shortages, union rules, clinical workflows, production downtime windows, public budgeting cycles, academic calendars, or audit periods. These constraints may shape timeline and adoption more than product interest.

### Identify domain-specific stakeholders and calibrate proof standard

Industry deals may involve roles outside generic sales maps: compliance officer, clinical leader, plant manager, superintendent, risk officer, claims director, store operations lead, grants manager, or data governance committee. Discovery should identify who owns the relevant domain risk.

Some industries require peer proof, security evidence, workflow demonstration, pilot data, public references, regulatory review, or board-ready business case before trust exists. Ask what proof the buyer considers credible.

### Avoid stereotype-based discovery and explore industry buying norms

Industry patterns are useful but can become stereotypes. Ask how this buyer differs from common industry patterns. The agent should use vertical knowledge to ask sharper questions, not to assume answers.

Some verticals rely on RFPs, committees, grants, seasonal budgets, pilots, unions, boards, clinical review, vendor credentialing, or public procurement. Discovery should include the buying process shaped by the industry.

### Avoid assuming vertical proof transfers and identify incumbent and ecosystem constraints

A case study from the same industry may still differ by size, region, workflow, maturity, or regulatory environment. Use proof as a starting point, then validate fit.

Vertical buyers may depend on core systems, integrators, channel partners, EHRs, ERPs, payment networks, data standards, or specialized vendors. Ecosystem fit can make or break the deal.

### Adapt risk conversation and capture vertical learning

Industry risks differ: patient safety, financial exposure, operational downtime, student data, supply chain disruption, public scrutiny, or regulatory penalties. Discovery should ask what failure would mean in that context.

Record validated industry patterns, buyer language, proof needs, objections, and constraints. Feed learning into targeting, messaging, demos, and qualification.

## Common Traps

- Treating a vertical label as enough context.
- Using jargon without understanding buyer meaning; asking generic discovery questions that miss industry workflow
- Mapping value to metrics the buyer does not use; giving legal or compliance certainty without qualification
- Missing vertical-specific buying process; assuming all industry case studies transfer
- Ignoring incumbent ecosystem constraints; using generic risk language in high-consequence verticals
- Overfitting to one customer and calling it industry truth; failing to capture vertical learning
- Confusing industry trend awareness with account-specific discovery; relying on weak or outdated sources for industry assumptions
- Missing seasonal, labor, audit, or operational constraints; using generic stakeholder maps in specialized domains; applying the wrong proof standard; turning industry patterns into stereotypes

## Self-Check

- Is the buyer's operating model understood?
- Is industry language used accurately and not performatively?
- Are relevant workflows mapped?
- Are value drivers tied to recognized industry metrics?; are regulatory and compliance needs identified and routed appropriately?
- Are industry buying norms understood?; is proof checked for context fit?
- Are ecosystem and incumbent constraints mapped?; is risk framed in industry-specific consequences?
- Are validated vertical learnings recorded?; are assumptions separated from buyer-confirmed facts?
- Would the buyer experience the discovery as informed but not presumptive?; are sources for industry assumptions credible and current?
- Are industry constraints that shape timing and adoption identified?; are domain-specific stakeholders mapped?
- Is the buyer's proof standard understood?; has the buyer been asked how they differ from common industry patterns?
