# Domain Proof And Use Case Relevance

Domain proof is persuasive only when it is relevant. A logo from the same industry can be misleading if the use case, scale, region, maturity, regulation, or workflow differs. Agents often reach for impressive examples instead of the proof that answers the buyer's real risk. This skill helps the agent choose and frame proof with precision.

## Core Rules

### Match proof to the buyer concern

Select proof based on the question the buyer needs answered: workflow fit, implementation risk, compliance, ROI, adoption, integration, support, scalability, or peer trust. A famous logo is weak if it does not address the concern.

### Check use-case similarity and check scale and complexity

Compare workflow, users, data, systems, process maturity, decision criteria, and success metrics. Same industry does not mean same use case. Be explicit about what is similar and what is different.

A proof point from a small team may not transfer to an enterprise rollout. An enterprise case may overwhelm a small buyer. Scale, number of users, integrations, regions, and governance affect relevance.

### Respect regulatory and regional differences and avoid overstating outcomes

Compliance, privacy, procurement, labor, accessibility, and data requirements may vary by region and sector. Do not imply proof in one context guarantees acceptability in another.

Use approved claims and caveats. If a case study reports savings, adoption, or time-to-value, do not generalize the result unless the buyer context supports it. Outcomes depend on buyer actions.

### Use negative relevance honestly and prepare the proof narrative

Sometimes the best proof is not available. Say what is missing and propose alternative validation: technical review, pilot, reference with adjacent context, security review, or custom business case.

Explain why the example matters: starting problem, constraints, chosen approach, implementation path, measured result, and what can or cannot transfer. Proof without narrative becomes logo-dropping.

### Use a proof hierarchy and match proof to decision stage

Prefer proof closest to the buyer's context: same use case and similar operating model, then same vertical, then adjacent vertical, then general capability proof. Explain when a weaker proof type is being used and what additional validation is needed.

Early discovery may need a short example to spark discussion. Technical validation may need architecture and implementation proof. Executive approval may need business outcome and risk proof. Procurement may need compliance and contractual proof. Do not use one proof asset for every stage.

### Prepare for proof challenges and avoid proof overload

Buyers may ask whether the reference is current, whether results were typical, what implementation effort was required, and whether the customer is comparable. Prepare caveats and source details so proof survives scrutiny.

Too many examples can make the seller sound unfocused. Choose a small set of relevant proof points and connect each one to a buyer concern. Quality beats volume.

### Convert proof into validation questions and avoid proof that creates the wrong expectation

After presenting proof, ask what matches, what differs, what risk remains, and what the buyer would need to see next. This turns proof into discovery rather than persuasion theater.

Some proof points showcase capabilities, timelines, services, or outcomes that are not included in the buyer's proposed scope. If using such proof, state the difference clearly. Proof should not accidentally promise a premium implementation, custom feature, or exceptional result.

### Coordinate proof with references and protect confidentiality and permissions

If proof may lead to a reference call, make sure the reference is appropriate, available, permissioned, and prepared for the buyer's likely questions. A poorly matched reference can weaken trust more than no reference.

Only use references, logos, metrics, and customer names according to permission. Do not reveal sensitive details to make proof more compelling.

### Combine domain proof with buyer discovery and keep proof current

Proof should support discovery, not replace it. After presenting proof, ask what matches the buyer's situation, what differs, and what they would need to validate.

Markets, products, regulations, and customer outcomes change. Use current proof and avoid outdated examples that no longer reflect capability or policy.

## Common Traps

- Choosing the most impressive logo instead of the most relevant proof.
- Treating same industry as same use case; ignoring scale and complexity differences
- Overlooking regional or regulatory differences; generalizing another customer's results
- Pretending missing proof exists; dropping logos without explaining relevance
- Using customer names or metrics without permission; letting proof replace discovery
- Reusing stale case studies; failing to state caveats
- Assuming peer proof answers every buyer risk; using weak proof without explaining its limits; reusing the same proof regardless of decision stage; being unprepared when the buyer challenges comparability
- Overloading the buyer with many weak examples; presenting proof without turning it into validation questions; using proof that implies scope or outcomes not included in the current offer; offering a reference that is not matched or permissioned

## Self-Check

- Is proof matched to the buyer's specific concern?
- Is use-case similarity assessed?; are scale, integrations, regions, and complexity comparable?
- Are regulatory and regional differences considered?; are outcome claims approved and caveated?
- Is missing proof acknowledged with an alternative validation path?; does the proof narrative explain why the example matters?
- Are confidentiality and permissions respected?; does proof lead to better discovery questions?
- Is the proof current?; are similarities and differences explicit?
- Would the buyer see the proof as relevant, not merely impressive?; is proof selected from the closest available context?
- Does proof match the decision stage?; are proof limitations and comparability caveats ready?
- Is the number of proof points focused?; does proof generate validation questions?
- Could the proof imply an unsupported promise?; is any reference matched, permissioned, and prepared?
