# Conflict Of Interest And Gift Policy

Conflicts of interest can damage trust, violate policy, or disqualify a deal even when no one intended wrongdoing. Gifts, meals, travel, referral incentives, personal relationships, partner benefits, and side arrangements require caution. Agents often treat these as relationship gestures without checking buyer rules. This skill helps the agent identify and route conflict risk before acting.

## Core Rules

### Identify the benefit or relationship and check both sides' policies

Name what is being offered or involved: meal, gift, event ticket, travel, speaking fee, referral fee, discount, job discussion, family relationship, partner incentive, or personal favor. Vague "relationship building" is not enough.

The seller's company and buyer's organization may each have rules. Public-sector, healthcare, education, finance, and procurement contexts may be especially strict. Follow the stricter applicable path.

### Consider timing and role and check appearance, not only intent

Benefits offered during evaluation, procurement, renewal, or negotiation can look different from ordinary hospitality. Benefits to decision makers, procurement, evaluators, or public officials carry higher risk.

Even if the seller's intent is harmless, the benefit may look improper to a procurement officer, auditor, competitor, journalist, or the buyer's manager. Evaluate how the situation would appear if disclosed.

### Handle conferences and events carefully and separate education from inducement

Meals, receptions, travel stipends, booth giveaways, raffles, and VIP events may be normal in some contexts and prohibited in others. Public-sector, regulated, and active procurement attendees need special caution.

Educational events and briefings should provide legitimate business value and not be disguised rewards. If attendance benefits the seller more than the buyer, review the offer.

### Watch charitable and community contributions and respond to buyer requests for benefits carefully

Donations, sponsorships, scholarships, or community contributions connected to a buyer can create conflict concerns. Route them through approved programs and avoid tying them to deal outcomes.

If a buyer asks for something unusual, do not comply casually. Pause, document, and route to legal, compliance, or management. A buyer request does not make the benefit appropriate.

### Include family, employment, and investment links and keep competitive fairness in mind

Conflicts can arise from family relationships, job discussions, consulting work, advisory roles, investments, board seats, or side businesses. These may need disclosure even if no gift is offered.

If a benefit would not be acceptable if offered by a competitor or disclosed to all bidders, it is likely risky. This test is especially useful in procurement and public-sector contexts.

### Document declined benefits and avoid anything that influences improperly

If a benefit is declined or redirected because of policy, record enough context to show the issue was handled. This protects the seller and buyer if questions arise later.

Do not offer anything intended to secure unfair advantage, change evaluation, bypass process, or reward a decision. Even appearance can matter.

### Use approved thresholds and reporting and be careful with travel and entertainment

Gift value limits, meal limits, pre-approval, disclosure, and reporting requirements should be followed. Do not split benefits to avoid thresholds.

Travel, lodging, premium events, and entertainment are high-risk. Route for approval and confirm buyer policy before offering.

### Manage personal relationships transparently and watch partner and referral incentives

If a seller has a personal, family, financial, or prior employment relationship with a buyer stakeholder, disclose internally and follow guidance. Hidden relationships create credibility risk.

Partner payments, referral fees, rebates, and side benefits should follow approved agreements. Do not create informal compensation paths.

### Keep records and choose trust-preserving alternatives

Document approvals, value, recipient, business purpose, policy check, and buyer acceptance where required. Records protect both sides.

When unsure, use lower-risk options: educational content, standard events, public webinars, modest refreshments within policy, or no benefit. A deal should stand on value.

## Common Traps

- Treating gifts as harmless relationship gestures; checking only seller policy and not buyer policy
- Offering benefits during active procurement; giving benefits to decision makers or evaluators without review
- Splitting gifts to avoid thresholds; assuming travel or entertainment is fine because competitors do it
- Hiding personal relationships; creating informal referral or partner payments
- Failing to record approvals; using generosity to compensate for weak value
- Ignoring appearance of impropriety; assuming public-sector rules are flexible
- Considering only intent and not appearance; treating event hospitality as automatically safe; disguising inducement as education; overlooking conflict risk in donations or sponsorships
- Complying casually with unusual buyer benefit requests; ignoring family, employment, investment, or advisory links; forgetting competitive fairness as a practical test; failing to document declined or redirected benefits

## Self-Check

- Is the benefit or relationship clearly identified?; have both seller and buyer policies been checked?
- Does timing or recipient role increase risk?; could the benefit appear to influence improperly?
- Are thresholds, approvals, disclosures, and reporting followed?; are travel and entertainment reviewed carefully?
- Are personal relationships disclosed internally?; are partner and referral incentives approved?
- Are records complete?; is a lower-risk alternative available?
- Would the interaction withstand buyer audit or public scrutiny?; does the deal still stand on value without the benefit?
- Has appearance of impropriety been considered?; are event benefits checked against buyer context?
- Is the activity genuinely educational or business-relevant?; are donations or sponsorships routed properly?
- Are unusual buyer requests paused and reviewed?; are family, employment, investment, and advisory conflicts checked?
- Would the benefit seem fair if disclosed to competitors?; are declined or redirected benefits documented where appropriate?
