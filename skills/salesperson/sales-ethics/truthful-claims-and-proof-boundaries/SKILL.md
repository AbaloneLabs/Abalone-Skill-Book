# Truthful Claims And Proof Boundaries

Sales claims shape buyer decisions and future obligations. A claim can be directionally true but still misleading if it omits conditions, scope, or uncertainty. Agents often overstate product capability, ROI, security, roadmap, or customer outcomes to reduce friction. This skill helps the agent keep claims accurate, bounded, and supported.

## Core Rules

### Classify the claim type

Identify whether the claim is about product capability, implementation, ROI, security, compliance, legal terms, roadmap, customer proof, support, pricing, competitor comparison, or availability. Each type has different proof and approval requirements.

### Require evidence before certainty and state scope and conditions

Use confirmed product documentation, approved collateral, customer data, buyer-validated assumptions, legal language, security materials, or specialist input. If evidence is incomplete, use qualified language.

Claims may depend on package, configuration, region, data quality, buyer actions, integration, services, support tier, or future release. Include the conditions that make the claim true.

### Use uncertainty deliberately and distinguish capability from entitlement

Words such as may, can, typically, expected, estimated, planned, and guaranteed carry different meanings. Choose wording that matches proof. Do not use soft language to hide a weak claim or strong language for an uncertain one.

The product may be technically capable of something that is not included in the buyer's package, not generally available, not supported, or requires services. State whether the capability is included, configurable, custom, roadmap, or unsupported.

### Preserve proof source and avoid claims by omission

For important claims, record where the proof came from: product documentation, security response, legal approval, customer data, buyer assumption, or specialist answer. This makes later review and correction possible.

Leaving out a material limitation can be misleading even if every sentence is technically true. Ask what the buyer would reasonably assume after hearing the claim.

### Encourage specialist review early and review buyer interpretation

If a claim will appear in proposal, RFP, contract negotiation, executive deck, or regulated-buyer response, route it early. Late review creates pressure to accept bad wording.

The ethical standard is not only what the seller literally said; it is what a reasonable buyer would understand. If a claim could be heard as broader than intended, narrow the language or add caveats.

### Keep claim discipline under competition and make corrections visible to affected parties

Competitive pressure, procurement questions, or aggressive buyer demands can tempt sellers to stretch. Do not lower proof standards because a competitor claims more. If proof is missing, say what can be verified.

If a claim has already influenced a buyer, proposal, forecast, or handoff, correct the same audience where practical. Quietly fixing an internal note is not enough when the buyer still holds the wrong belief.

### Teach repeatable claim boundaries and avoid roadmap promises

When a risky claim appears repeatedly, update enablement, snippets, templates, or approval guidance. Repeated claim drift is a system problem, not only an individual wording mistake.

Future features, timelines, and commitments should use approved roadmap language. Do not turn direction, exploration, or planned work into a promise.

### Treat ROI conservatively and route regulated claims

Separate hard savings, productivity, revenue, risk reduction, and strategic value. Use buyer-validated assumptions and caveats. Do not imply guaranteed financial outcomes.

Security, privacy, compliance, legal, medical, financial, public-sector, and accessibility claims should be reviewed by appropriate owners. Sales confidence is not approval.

### Compare competitors fairly and correct errors quickly and align artifacts and preserve trust over convenience

Use verified, current, and approved information. Do not spread rumors, exaggerate gaps, or imply facts not known.

If an incorrect claim was made, correct it in writing with appropriate context and update CRM. Silence allows misunderstanding to become expectation.

Decks, proposals, emails, contracts, RFP responses, and CRM notes should not contain conflicting claims. Inconsistency creates risk.

Truthful boundaries may slow a deal, but they protect customer success and reputation. A deal won through misleading claims is not a quality win.

## Common Traps

- Making broad claims from narrow evidence.
- Omitting package, region, or configuration limits; turning roadmap direction into commitment
- Presenting ROI as guaranteed; answering compliance or legal questions casually
- Repeating competitor rumors; failing to correct inaccurate statements
- Letting proposal and contract claims diverge; using customer proof beyond context
- Saying yes to reduce discomfort; treating "technically possible" as "included and supported."; forgetting buyer responsibilities in outcome claims; using wording that overstates or hides uncertainty
- Confusing capability with entitlement; losing proof source for important claims; misleading by omitting material limits; waiting too long for specialist review
- Focusing on literal wording while ignoring likely buyer interpretation; stretching claims because of competitive pressure; correcting internally while the buyer still believes the inaccurate claim; treating repeated claim drift as isolated mistakes

## Self-Check

- Is claim type identified?
- Is evidence strong enough for the wording?; are scope and conditions stated?
- Is roadmap language approved?; are ROI claims conservative and assumption-based?
- Are regulated claims routed?; are competitor claims verified?
- Are errors corrected promptly?; are artifacts aligned?
- Does the claim protect trust?; are buyer responsibilities included where outcomes depend on them?
- Would post-sale teams be able to honor the claim?; does wording match certainty level?
- Is capability distinguished from entitlement?; is proof source recorded for important claims?
- Are material limitations included?; has specialist review happened early enough?
- Would a reasonable buyer interpret the claim correctly?; are proof standards maintained under competitive pressure?; are corrections visible to affected parties?; are repeated risky claims fed into enablement or template fixes?
