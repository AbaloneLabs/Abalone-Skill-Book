# Sales Presentation Structure

A sales presentation is not a place to unload everything the seller knows. It should help the buyer understand a decision: why the problem matters, why the proposed approach fits, what tradeoffs exist, and what action should follow. Agents often build decks around company credibility and product features rather than buyer reasoning. This skill helps the agent structure presentations that guide decisions.

## Core Rules

### Start with buyer context

Open with the buyer's situation, trigger, current process, pain, impact, and objective. If the buyer does not recognize themselves in the first minutes, the presentation becomes generic.

### State the decision the presentation supports and use a problem-to-outcome arc

Know whether the presentation should create discovery alignment, technical validation, executive sponsorship, proposal approval, procurement readiness, renewal decision, or expansion interest. Structure follows decision.

Move from current state to consequence, desired outcome, proposed approach, proof, risk, and next action. Avoid jumping from problem to feature inventory.

### Limit product detail to decision relevance and include tradeoffs and assumptions

Show features only when they explain how the buyer's outcome is achieved or risk is reduced. Do not include every capability because the product team is proud of it.

Name assumptions about data, integration, buyer responsibilities, timeline, support, scope, and adoption. Presenting only upside makes the deck less credible.

### Structure for the primary stakeholder and use slide hierarchy deliberately

A user audience needs workflow relevance; an executive audience needs business consequence; a technical audience needs feasibility and risk; finance needs value assumptions; procurement needs scope and terms. If several stakeholder types attend, structure the deck so each group gets what it needs without overwhelming the others.

Each slide should have one decision point or message. Details, examples, and proof should support that point. If a slide requires the seller to explain why it exists, it may not belong or needs a clearer heading.

### Show the path from evidence to recommendation and plan interaction points

Do not jump from discovery facts to seller recommendation. Show how buyer evidence leads to criteria, how criteria lead to proposed scope, and how scope leads to next decision. This makes the presentation feel reasoned rather than pitched.

Include moments where the buyer confirms, corrects, or chooses. For example, validate current state, rank criteria, confirm risk, or select next evaluation step. Interactive structure keeps the buyer from becoming passive.

### Rehearse transitions and time and create a safe appendix

A good structure can fail if transitions are unclear or time runs out before the ask. Rehearse how each section connects and what can be skipped if the buyer changes priority.

Detailed product tables, security details, implementation steps, pricing assumptions, and proof can live in an appendix if they are approved and current. This keeps the main story focused while allowing deeper answers when needed. Do not hide risky or unapproved claims in backup slides.

### Define what should not be presented and use proof at the right moment

Some content may be irrelevant, premature, confidential, unapproved, or likely to distract from the decision. Decide what to omit as deliberately as what to include. A strong presentation is shaped by exclusion.

Place proof where it answers a buyer concern: peer case for credibility, demo for workflow, architecture for technical risk, ROI for finance, implementation plan for adoption. Proof should not be decorative.

### Design for discussion and make next decision explicit

Leave space for buyer questions, objections, and decisions. A presentation that fills the whole meeting with slides prevents discovery.

End with a clear ask: validate criteria, approve technical review, confirm business case, select scope, enter procurement, schedule executive meeting, or decide not to proceed.

### Prepare for internal forwarding and align with other artifacts

Slides may travel without the seller. Use clear headings, concise logic, caveats, and context. Avoid slides that require live narration to avoid misunderstanding.

Presentation claims should match proposal, contract, CRM, business case, demo, and security materials. Inconsistent language creates risk.

## Common Traps

- Opening with vendor overview instead of buyer context.
- Presenting without knowing the decision being supported; jumping from pain to product features
- Including product detail that does not affect the decision; hiding assumptions and tradeoffs
- Using proof as logo decoration; leaving no time for discussion
- Ending without a buyer decision or action; creating slides that cannot be forwarded safely
- Letting deck claims drift from proposal or contract; using the same deck for every stakeholder
- Measuring success by presentation completion instead of buyer movement; designing for the wrong primary stakeholder; building slides with no single decision message; recommending before showing the logic from evidence
- Making the buyer passive for the whole presentation; running out of time before the decision ask; putting all details in the main flow instead of controlled appendix; including distracting or premature content because it is available

## Self-Check

- Does the presentation start with buyer context?
- Is the supported decision explicit?; does the narrative move from problem to outcome to approach to proof to next action?
- Is product detail decision-relevant?; are assumptions and tradeoffs visible?
- Is proof placed where it answers a concern?; is discussion time protected?
- Is the next decision or action explicit?; can the deck be forwarded without distortion?
- Are claims aligned with other artifacts?; is the structure tailored to stakeholder and stage?
- Would the buyer leave clearer about how to decide?; is the primary stakeholder and audience mix reflected in structure?
- Does each slide have one clear message?; is the recommendation traceable to buyer evidence and criteria?
- Are interaction points planned?; are transitions and time rehearsed?
- Is appendix content approved, current, and used intentionally?; is omitted content deliberately excluded for relevance or risk reasons?
