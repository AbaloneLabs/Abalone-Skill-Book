# Demo Discovery Alignment

A demo should prove the parts of the buyer's problem that discovery made important. When a seller demos everything, the buyer learns little about fit and the seller learns little about risk. Weak demo alignment creates excitement without decision evidence, hides implementation gaps, and wastes stakeholder attention. This skill helps the agent turn discovery into a focused demonstration plan.

## Core Rules

### Start from the confirmed problem

Name the buyer problem, current process, impact, trigger, and desired outcome before choosing demo flow. If discovery is thin, the demo should include discovery checkpoints rather than pretend certainty.

The demo is not a replacement for discovery.

### Define the demo decision

Clarify what the buyer should be able to decide after the demo: whether the workflow fits, whether technical review is warranted, whether stakeholders agree, whether a pilot should begin, or whether the deal should narrow or stop.

Without a decision, a demo becomes entertainment.

### Match scenes to stakeholders

Executives need business outcome and risk reduction. Users need workflow clarity. Technical reviewers need integration and security implications. Managers need control, reporting, and adoption. Do not show the same depth to every audience.

If multiple stakeholders attend, structure the demo so each role sees what matters.

### Use buyer language and data shape

Mirror the buyer's terminology, process steps, roles, and success criteria. Use sample data that resembles their reality without exposing private customer data or implying custom work that is not supported.

Generic demo data makes the solution feel abstract.

### Show the critical path first

Lead with the workflow most tied to value. Avoid wandering through navigation, settings, or advanced features before proving the core use case.

The buyer should see the answer to "can this solve our problem" early.

### Expose assumptions and prerequisites and plan discovery moments inside the demo

Name required integrations, data quality, permissions, admin work, configuration, training, implementation services, or plan tier. Do not hide the setup burden until after excitement is created.

Transparent prerequisites prevent post-sale disappointment.

Use the demo to validate: "Is this how your approval works?" or "Who would own this step?" These questions reveal fit and missing stakeholders.

A good demo teaches the seller as well as the buyer.

### Avoid unsupported theater and connect to the next step

Do not hard-code a perfect path, imply unavailable features, skip known limitations, or present roadmap as current product. If a workaround is shown, state its support status and tradeoffs.

Demo trust matters more than polish.

Close by summarizing what the demo proved, what remains unproven, who needs to review, and what buyer action comes next. Tie the next step to success criteria.

### Capture demo evidence and decide what not to show

Record stakeholder reactions, confirmed fit, unresolved risks, feature gaps, technical questions, objections, and buyer commitments. This evidence should update qualification and forecast.

Demo alignment includes exclusions. Do not show features that distract from the buyer's problem, create unsupported expectations, invite unneeded stakeholders into irrelevant detail, or open roadmap questions the seller cannot handle. A shorter demo can be stronger when it protects the decision path.

If a buyer asks to see unrelated functionality, decide whether it is a genuine decision criterion, curiosity, or a signal of unclear discovery.

### Prepare role-specific proof artifacts and check whether demo results change qualification

A demo may need follow-up proof: workflow screenshots, technical notes, security documentation, ROI assumptions, implementation summary, or user-facing recap. Prepare artifacts based on the stakeholders in the room.

Do not rely on the live demo alone to carry the internal buying conversation after the meeting ends.

After the demo, reassess fit, urgency, stakeholder access, technical risk, success criteria, and next buyer action. A good demo may reveal that the deal should slow down, narrow, or disqualify.

Demo success is not applause; it is better evidence.

## Common Traps

- Running a generic feature tour after discovery revealed specific pain.
- Designing a demo with no decision at the end.
- Showing executive and technical stakeholders the same content.
- Using buyer-like data in a way that implies unsupported customization; saving the core value workflow until late
- Hiding prerequisites, implementation work, or limitations; treating the demo as one-way presentation instead of validation
- Showing roadmap, mockups, or workarounds as if generally available; ending with "we will follow up" instead of mutual next action
- Failing to update CRM with demo evidence; showing extra features because they are available, not because they support the buyer decision
- Assuming the live demo will be remembered accurately without forwardable proof artifacts; treating positive reactions as advancement without rechecking qualification evidence

## Self-Check

- Is the demo anchored in confirmed problem, current process, impact, trigger, and desired outcome?
- If discovery is thin, are discovery checkpoints built into the demo?
- Is the buyer decision after the demo explicit?
- Are demo scenes tailored by stakeholder role?
- Does terminology and data shape match the buyer without unsafe data use?
- Is the critical value workflow shown early?
- Are prerequisites, integrations, data, permissions, configuration, training, services, and plan limits clear?
- Does the demo validate assumptions through questions?; are limitations, roadmap, and workarounds represented honestly?
- Are proven points, unproven points, stakeholder reactions, risks, and buyer commitments recorded?; is there a clear reason for what was intentionally excluded from the demo?
- Are follow-up proof artifacts prepared for stakeholders who must sell or review internally?; did the demo results update qualification, risk, forecast, and next action?
