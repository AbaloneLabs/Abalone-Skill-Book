# Tailored Demo Storyline

A tailored demo is a buyer-specific argument, not a tour with the buyer's logo added. The storyline should show how the buyer moves from current pain to a better operating state, while making assumptions, limits, and next decisions visible. Weak storylines overwhelm buyers with features or hide the proof they need. This skill helps the agent build demos that are clear, relevant, and decision-oriented.

## Core Rules

### Build around a before-and-after path

Start with the current workflow and pain. Then show the future workflow, outcome, and what changes for the buyer. The storyline should make the improvement visible.

If the buyer cannot see the before-and-after difference, the demo is not tailored enough.

### Choose scenes by decision value

Every scene should answer a buyer question, reduce a risk, prove a criterion, or create a next action. Remove scenes that exist only because the feature is impressive.

Attention is limited. Spend it on decision evidence.

### Sequence from familiar to strategic

Begin with a workflow the buyer recognizes, then show leverage: automation, insight, control, risk reduction, scale, or executive visibility. Jumping straight to advanced features can create confusion.

The buyer should feel oriented before being impressed.

### Name the assumptions behind the story

If the storyline assumes clean data, specific integrations, user adoption, admin ownership, permissions, or process change, say so. Assumptions are not weaknesses if they are managed.

Hidden assumptions become implementation surprises.

### Tailor by audience mix

A demo for users should show daily work. A demo for executives should show business outcome. A demo for IT should show architecture and controls. Mixed rooms need signposts so each group knows when their concern is being addressed.

Do not let one stakeholder dominate the whole story.

### Include moments of buyer participation and make proof credible

Ask the buyer to compare the demo flow to their process, identify missing steps, and react to tradeoffs. Participation creates better evidence than passive approval.

The best demo is a working session where appropriate.

Use examples, customer stories, metrics, or workflows only when they match the buyer's context and are approved for use. Avoid exaggerated ROI, unsupported benchmarks, or irrelevant logo-dropping.

Proof that does not fit can reduce trust.

### Show limits and alternatives and end with decision recap

If a requested workflow is not supported, show the closest supported path and explain tradeoffs. Do not bury gaps behind a smooth script.

Buyers need to know whether fit is real.

Close with what the storyline demonstrated, what remains open, what evidence is needed, and what the buyer will do next. Tie the recap to the original success criteria.

### Prepare a fallback path and anchor the story in buyer consequence

If the demo environment fails or the buyer changes priority, know which scenes can be skipped, which screenshots or recordings can support the point, and which topics require follow-up rather than improvisation.

Each part of the storyline should connect to what happens if the buyer does not change: lost time, risk, missed revenue, poor experience, manual work, compliance exposure, or inability to scale. Consequence makes the story more than a product walkthrough.

If there is no consequence, the scene may not belong.

### Manage demo depth deliberately and make the storyline reusable by the champion

Too little detail feels hand-wavy; too much detail buries the business point. Decide where to go deep based on stakeholder role and decision risk. For example, go deep on a risky integration for technical reviewers but stay at outcome level for executives.

Depth should be a choice, not a habit.

A tailored demo should produce a narrative the champion can repeat internally: problem, proof, expected outcome, open risks, and next decision. If the champion cannot retell it, the seller has not made the story portable.

Portability is part of demo quality.

## Common Traps

- Calling a demo tailored because it includes the buyer's name.
- Showing impressive features with no decision purpose.
- Starting with advanced workflows before the buyer recognizes the problem.
- Hiding assumptions about data, integrations, permissions, or adoption; designing for one stakeholder while ignoring the rest of the room
- Treating silence as agreement; using proof points that do not match the buyer's context
- Scripting around product gaps instead of naming them; ending without a decision recap or buyer action
- Having no fallback if the demo path fails; showing scenes that have no consequence tied to buyer inaction
- Going too deep or too shallow by habit rather than stakeholder risk; creating a story that only works when the seller is in the room

## Self-Check

- Does the storyline show a clear before-and-after path?
- Does every scene reduce risk, prove criteria, answer a question, or create next action?
- Is the sequence easy for the buyer to follow?
- Are assumptions about data, integrations, ownership, permissions, and adoption named?
- Is the audience mix addressed with role-specific signposts?
- Are buyers asked to validate fit during the demo?
- Are proof points relevant and approved?
- Are unsupported requests handled honestly with alternatives or tradeoffs?; does the recap connect to success criteria and next action?
- Is there a fallback path for demo failure or priority change?; does each scene connect to a consequence of inaction or a decision criterion?
- Is demo depth chosen by stakeholder role and decision risk?; can the champion retell the storyline internally without the seller?
