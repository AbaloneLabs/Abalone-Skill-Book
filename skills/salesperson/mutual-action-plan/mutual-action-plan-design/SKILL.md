# Mutual Action Plan Design

A mutual action plan is not a seller's checklist with buyer names attached. It is a shared decision and execution map that tests whether both sides understand the path to a real outcome. Agents often make plans that are linear, seller-owned, or overly optimistic, which hides buyer dependencies and weak commitment. This skill helps the agent design plans that reveal risk and create mutual accountability.

## Core Rules

### Start from the buyer outcome

Define the business outcome the buyer wants and the decision the plan supports. The plan may lead to vendor selection, budget approval, contract signature, pilot completion, go-live, renewal, or expansion. If the plan starts from the seller's close date instead of the buyer outcome, it will feel like pressure rather than coordination.

### Include both buying and selling work

List buyer tasks and seller tasks separately. Buyer tasks may include stakeholder alignment, data access, security review, legal review, budget approval, technical validation, reference call, procurement, implementation resource assignment, and executive decision. Seller tasks may include proposal, security response, demo, technical design, business case support, redline review, and handoff preparation.

Progress requires movement on both sides.

### Name owners, not departments

"Legal" or "IT" is not an owner. Assign named people where possible, or identify the person responsible for finding the owner. Ambiguous ownership makes missed steps hard to diagnose and lets the plan become decorative.

### Tie dates to dependencies

Dates should reflect dependencies, not seller desire. Security review may depend on questionnaire completion. Contract signature may depend on legal redlines, PO, and budget approval. Go-live may depend on data readiness and admin availability. If a date is uncertain, mark it as estimated and identify what would make it firm.

### Put decision criteria in the plan

A mutual action plan should show what evidence the buyer needs to make the decision: business case accepted, technical requirements met, security approved, user workflow validated, pricing agreed, legal terms resolved, or executive sponsor aligned. Activities without decision criteria do not prove progress.

### Include exit and pause conditions and make the plan easy to update

Healthy plans define what would cause disqualification, scope reduction, pause, or redesign. If technical fit fails, budget is not approved, legal terms are unacceptable, or buyer resources are unavailable, the plan should not pretend the path is unchanged.

Use a simple structure with milestone, owner, date, dependency, status, and next action. A plan that is too complex will not be maintained; a plan that is too vague will not reveal risk. Update it after every meaningful buyer movement or missed commitment.

### Confirm mutual commitment explicitly and use the plan as a qualification tool

Do not send a plan and assume agreement. Review it with the buyer, ask what is missing, confirm owners and dates, and secure agreement that the plan reflects their process. A plan the buyer has not accepted is a seller artifact.

A buyer who will not review owners, dates, dependencies, or decision criteria may not be ready for an active sales cycle. Treat refusal, repeated avoidance, or vague agreement as signal. The right response may be to simplify the plan, return to value discovery, find a stronger owner, pause, or disqualify. Do not keep advancing a seller-owned plan after the buyer avoids accountability.

### Design around immovable events and show what can run in parallel

Some dates are truly fixed: budget expiration, board meeting, compliance deadline, incumbent renewal, launch date, funding cycle, or implementation blackout. Other dates are preferences. Mark which dates are immovable and build backward from them. If the required work cannot fit, surface the gap early instead of pretending the plan can compress later.

Legal, security, technical validation, business case review, procurement setup, and implementation planning may be able to overlap, but only if owners and inputs are ready. Identify which work can run in parallel and which must be sequential. Parallelization should be buyer-approved, not assumed by the seller.

### Align with forecast and CRM and carry the plan into handoff

The plan should support forecast evidence. If the CRM close date, stage, next step, or risk differs from the mutual action plan, reconcile them. Forecast confidence should come from buyer-validated milestones, not internal optimism.

If the plan extends beyond signature, preserve implementation expectations, buyer responsibilities, success criteria, and dates for customer success and implementation. Do not let a signature erase the commitments that made the deal closeable.

## Common Traps

- Building the plan around the seller's desired close date; listing only seller tasks and calling the plan mutual
- Assigning tasks to departments rather than accountable people; treating calendar dates as real without dependency evidence
- Tracking activities but not decision criteria; omitting pause, disqualification, or scope-change conditions
- Making the plan too detailed to maintain or too vague to guide action; sending the plan without buyer review and acceptance
- Letting CRM and the mutual action plan tell different stories; ignoring implementation and buyer responsibilities after signature
- Treating missed buyer commitments as minor scheduling noise; using the plan as pressure instead of shared coordination
- Keeping an active deal open when the buyer refuses to validate the plan; failing to distinguish fixed buyer events from preferred seller dates
- Assuming work can run in parallel without owner agreement or required inputs; treating the plan as complete before testing whether it changes buyer behavior

## Self-Check

- Is the buyer outcome and supported decision explicit?
- Are buyer tasks and seller tasks both represented?
- Are owners named or is there a clear owner-finding task?
- Are dates tied to dependencies and confidence level?
- Are decision criteria attached to major milestones?; are pause, exit, or scope-change conditions defined?
- Is the plan simple enough to maintain but specific enough to reveal risk?; has the buyer reviewed and accepted the plan?
- Do CRM stage, close date, next step, and forecast risk match the plan?; are missed commitments treated as evidence and investigated?
- Are implementation expectations and buyer responsibilities preserved for handoff?; does the plan create mutual accountability rather than seller pressure?
- Has buyer response to the plan been used as qualification evidence?; are immovable events separated from flexible target dates?
- Is backward planning used where deadlines are fixed?; are parallel workstreams explicit and owner-approved?
