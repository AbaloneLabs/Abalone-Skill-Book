# Timeline Critical Path And Owner Tracking

A sales timeline is not a list of hoped-for dates. It is a dependency map. Deals slip when sellers track visible meetings but miss critical path work such as budget approval, security review, legal redlines, procurement setup, executive signoff, or buyer resource assignment. This skill helps the agent distinguish real timeline evidence from optimism and keep owners accountable before slippage becomes unavoidable.

## Core Rules

### Identify the true end milestone

Clarify what the timeline is trying to reach: decision, signature, PO, pilot completion, kickoff, go-live, renewal, expansion, or value milestone. These are not interchangeable. A signed contract may not mean the buyer is ready for kickoff; a completed pilot may not mean procurement can start.

### Map the critical path

Identify the sequence of tasks that determines the earliest possible completion date. Include buyer tasks, seller tasks, third-party tasks, approvals, reviews, and waiting periods. Non-critical tasks still matter, but critical path tasks determine whether the date is real.

### Assign owners and backups

Every critical task needs an owner, and important buyer-side tasks may need a backup or escalation route. If the owner is unknown, the task is to identify the owner. Avoid vague ownership such as "team to review" or "procurement to handle."

### Verify duration, not only due date

Ask how long each step usually takes and what can shorten or lengthen it. Legal review, security assessment, vendor onboarding, PO creation, finance approval, and executive signature often have standard durations. A due date that ignores normal duration is not credible.

### Mark dependencies and blockers

For each milestone, name what must happen first. Security may need architecture details. Legal may need final order form. Finance may need approved business case. Procurement may need vendor forms. Implementation may need data access. A blocked dependency should change forecast confidence.

### Build backward from fixed dates and decide what can safely run in parallel

When the buyer has a fixed event such as a board meeting, renewal deadline, fiscal year close, grant date, compliance milestone, launch, or implementation freeze, build the timeline backward from that date. Show the last acceptable date for each prerequisite. If the path is already too tight, surface the decision early: accelerate with real resources, narrow scope, accept a later date, or stop pretending.

Parallel work can recover time, but only when prerequisites are ready and owners agree. Security review can sometimes run while business case is finalized; legal can sometimes review template terms before final price; implementation planning can sometimes begin before signature. But parallel work can waste resources if the deal is unqualified or if critical inputs will change.

### Track owner responsiveness and track evidence quality

Timeline quality depends not only on the plan but on whether owners respond, decide, and unblock. Record repeated delays, vague answers, unavailable approvers, and ownership gaps. Responsiveness is evidence about buyer priority and forecast risk.

Classify dates as buyer-confirmed, owner-confirmed, inferred, seller-targeted, or unknown. A timeline built on seller-targeted dates should be treated as risk. Update confidence when owners confirm or miss milestones.

### Detect slippage early and avoid compressing buyer work unrealistically

A delay in a critical path step should trigger a revised timeline or recovery decision. Do not keep the final date unchanged unless there is a credible compression plan. Compression may require parallel work, escalation, added resources, narrowed scope, or changed launch expectations.

Sellers often assume buyer legal, procurement, finance, or security can move faster near quarter-end. Some processes cannot be compressed, and pressure may damage trust. Ask what can realistically move in parallel and what cannot.

### Communicate timeline risk plainly and preserve timeline learning

Updates should show milestone status, owner, dependency, risk, confidence, and required decision. Avoid vague phrases like "on track" when critical evidence is missing. Leadership and buyers need the same reality.

After close, slip, or loss, compare expected versus actual timeline. Note which steps were underestimated, which owners were missing, and which dependencies should be surfaced earlier in similar deals.

## Common Traps

- Treating contract signature, PO, kickoff, and go-live as the same endpoint; listing milestones without identifying the critical path
- Assigning work to departments instead of accountable owners; tracking due dates without normal process duration
- Missing dependencies that make a date impossible; treating seller-targeted dates as buyer-confirmed dates
- Keeping the close date unchanged after critical path slippage; assuming quarter-end pressure can compress legal, finance, procurement, or security
- Reporting "on track" when blockers are unresolved; letting non-critical activity create false confidence
- Forgetting third-party, partner, or reseller dependencies; failing to learn from repeated timeline misses
- Planning forward from today when the buyer actually has a fixed deadline; running workstreams in parallel without prerequisites or owner agreement
- Ignoring owner responsiveness as a signal of priority and risk; treating a date as recoverable without naming the resource or scope change that recovers it

## Self-Check

- Is the endpoint precisely defined?
- Is the critical path identified?
- Are buyer, seller, third-party, approval, and waiting-period tasks included?
- Does each critical task have an owner or owner-finding action?
- Are normal durations known or explicitly uncertain?; are dependencies and blockers visible?
- Are dates classified by evidence quality?; has slippage changed the final date or produced a credible recovery plan?
- Are compression assumptions realistic and buyer-approved?; does communication include status, owner, dependency, risk, confidence, and decision needed?
- Are third-party and partner dependencies included?; has learning from prior slippage been captured for future deals?
- If there is a fixed buyer date, has the plan been built backward from it?; are last acceptable dates for critical prerequisites visible?
- Are parallel workstreams justified by readiness and owner agreement?; is owner responsiveness tracked as forecast evidence?
