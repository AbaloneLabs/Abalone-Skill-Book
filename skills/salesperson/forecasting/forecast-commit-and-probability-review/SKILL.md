# Forecast Commit And Probability Review

Forecasting is not prediction by optimism. It is a disciplined claim about what buyer evidence supports within a time period. Agents often confuse deal quality with timing confidence, or use probability fields as generic sentiment. This skill helps the agent review commit and probability using evidence, risk, and critical path.

## Core Rules

### Define the forecast question

Clarify whether the review asks "will this close," "will this close this period," "is this commit," "what is the risk-adjusted amount," or "what action improves confidence." These are different questions. A good deal can be a poor current-period forecast.

### Use category definitions consistently and separate probability from stage

Commit, best case, pipeline, omitted, upside, or equivalent categories should have clear criteria. Do not let each seller invent their own meaning. Category discipline matters more than mathematical precision.

Stage may describe process position; probability should reflect evidence quality, buyer commitment, timing, competition, risk, and critical path. A late-stage deal can have low probability if procurement, legal, or budget is unresolved.

### Inspect timing risk and separate date confidence from win confidence

Forecasting within a period depends on close path: approval, security, legal, procurement, PO, signature, and internal buyer deadlines. If any critical path item lacks owner or date, current-period confidence should drop.

A deal may be highly likely to win but unlikely to close this month. Another may be time-bound but commercially fragile. Review probability of winning and probability of closing in the forecast period separately before choosing category.

### Use scenarios, not only point estimates and inspect rollup concentration

For material deals, consider likely, upside, and downside outcomes for amount and date. Scenario thinking exposes whether the forecast depends on one fragile assumption. It also helps leadership understand range, not just a single number.

If forecast depends heavily on a few large deals, category discipline and risk inspection must be stricter. A small slip in a concentrated forecast can change the whole period. Deal size should influence inspection depth.

### Align probability with action plan and review forecast by failure mode

Probability should improve only when the action plan reduces uncertainty. A deal should not become more probable simply because time passed or the seller had another friendly call. Link probability changes to evidence and completed milestones.

For each material deal, name the most likely reason it will miss: no decision, date slip, amount reduction, legal issue, procurement delay, competitive loss, technical failure, budget issue, or implementation readiness. Probability becomes more useful when tied to a concrete failure mode and mitigation.

### Keep commit criteria stricter than optimism criteria and review pull-in and push-out risk

Commit should require buyer-validated path, not merely a seller belief that the deal is strong. The manager should be able to ask, "What buyer action makes this commit?" and hear a specific answer. If the answer is only confidence, the deal is not commit.

Some deals are forecasted because sellers hope they can pull them into the period. Others are at risk of pushing out despite strong value. Inspect what must compress, who agrees, and what tradeoff is required. Pull-in without buyer evidence should not drive forecast.

### Include amount and scope risk and account for negative evidence

Forecast risk includes whether the deal closes at the expected amount. Discounts, reduced scope, phased rollout, service changes, or procurement pressure may change forecast amount even if the deal closes.

Missed commitments, single-threading, weak business case, no economic buyer, no procurement path, competitive uncertainty, legal redlines, or implementation concerns should affect probability. Positive sentiment should not erase negative evidence.

### Calibrate with historical accuracy and define confidence-changing events

Review prior commit misses, stage slippage, seller tendencies, segment cycle time, procurement duration, and product-specific risks. Forecasting should learn from history rather than reset each week.

For each forecasted deal, name what would move it up, down, in, or out of commit: signed order form, returned redlines, executive approval, security pass, PO created, procurement date, or buyer resource commitment.

### Avoid sandbagging and happy ears and update forecast promptly

Both under-forecasting and over-forecasting damage planning. Encourage evidence-based changes rather than emotional safety. The goal is operating accuracy, not seller comfort.

When evidence changes, update category, amount, close date, and notes. Do not wait for the forecast call if the current record is wrong.

## Common Traps

- Forecasting whether the deal is good instead of whether it closes in the period.
- Letting each seller define commit differently; equating late stage with high probability
- Ignoring critical path timing risk; forecasting full amount despite likely scope or discount change
- Looking only at positive evidence; failing to use historical slippage and cycle-time data
- Naming no confidence-changing event; rewarding sandbagging or happy ears
- Delaying forecast updates after evidence changes; treating probability as a precise statistic when inputs are subjective
- Forgetting no-decision risk; combining win confidence and in-period timing confidence into one vague feeling; giving leadership only a point estimate when range is material; ignoring concentration risk in the forecast rollup
- Raising probability without an evidence-changing milestone; reviewing probability without naming the likely miss reason; letting commit mean "seller feels strong."; pulling deals into the period without buyer-confirmed compression

## Self-Check

- Is the forecast question clear?
- Are forecast category definitions applied consistently?; is probability separated from stage?
- Are period timing risks inspected?; are amount, scope, and discount risks included?
- Is negative evidence reflected?; does history inform confidence?
- Are confidence-changing events named?; are sandbagging and happy ears discouraged through evidence?
- Are category, amount, close date, and notes updated promptly?; would the forecast still make sense if reviewed by finance or leadership?
- Is no-decision risk considered?; are win confidence and date confidence reviewed separately?
- Are likely, upside, and downside scenarios considered for material deals?; is concentration risk visible?
- Is every probability change tied to evidence or milestone completion?; is the most likely failure mode named for material deals?
- Does commit have a buyer-validated path?; are pull-in and push-out assumptions buyer-confirmed?
