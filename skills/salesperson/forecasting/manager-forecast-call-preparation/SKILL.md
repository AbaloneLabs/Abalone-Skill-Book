# Manager Forecast Call Preparation

A forecast call should not be a storytelling session or a live CRM cleanup meeting. It should surface evidence, risk, action, and forecast changes clearly enough that managers can coach, allocate resources, and roll up accurate numbers. Agents often prepare narratives that defend the seller's view rather than inspect it. This skill helps the agent prepare concise, evidence-based forecast updates.

## Core Rules

### Prepare from the record first

Review CRM fields, notes, stage, amount, close date, next step, mutual action plan, proposal, procurement status, and prior forecast call commitments. If the record is wrong, update it before the call rather than explaining around it.

### Lead with changes and separate commit, best case, and risk

Managers need to know what changed since the last review: stage movement, close date shift, amount change, new risk, resolved blocker, buyer commitment, competitive event, or forecast category update. Repeating old background wastes inspection time.

Present each deal with category, evidence, risk, action, and confidence. Do not blend "I like the deal" with "it is commit this period." Category discipline improves rollup quality.

### Bring buyer evidence and name asks for management

Use buyer actions, confirmed dates, named owners, returned documents, approved business case, technical validation, procurement steps, or executive confirmation. Avoid vague phrases such as "they are excited" or "legal should be done soon."

If management help is needed, ask specifically: executive outreach, discount approval, legal escalation, security resource, deal strategy, objection handling, or disqualification support. Escalation without an ask creates noise.

### Prepare a concise deal brief and show rollup impact

For material deals, prepare a brief with account, amount, category, close date, buyer problem, decision process, current evidence, top risks, next buyer action, management ask, and confidence-changing event. This keeps the call from becoming a long narrative search.

Managers need to know whether a deal change affects individual forecast, team forecast, quota coverage, product mix, region, cash timing, or services capacity. A slipped large deal may require broader action than a small stage update.

### Bring decisions, not only updates and flag data confidence

Forecast calls should resolve choices: keep in commit or move out, escalate or wait, discount or hold, narrow scope or preserve amount, pause or push, involve executive or not. Prepare recommendations so the manager is not forced to design the plan from scratch.

If the record is incomplete, say so. A forecast update should distinguish high-confidence evidence from provisional assumptions. Managers can handle uncertainty better than false precision.

### Send pre-read when complexity is high and avoid surprise category changes

For complex or high-value deals, prepare a short pre-read before the call so the conversation can focus on judgment and decisions. Include only the evidence and choices that matter. A pre-read is especially useful when legal, procurement, executive escalation, or large forecast movement is involved.

If a material deal moves into or out of commit, prepare the reason before the call. The manager should not discover major forecast movement through vague conversation. Explain what evidence changed, when it changed, and what action is now required.

### Distinguish coaching issues from forecast issues and identify forecast-changing events

Some problems require deal coaching; others require forecast adjustment. A weak champion, for example, may need coaching and lower forecast confidence. Treating every issue as coaching can leave the forecast wrong; treating every issue as forecast failure can miss the chance to recover.

For each material deal, state what evidence before the next call would change forecast category, amount, or close date. This creates a focused follow-up path.

### Include downside and upside honestly and prepare slippage explanations with learning

Managers need to understand risk in commit deals and realistic upside in best-case deals. Avoid hiding downside to protect confidence or inflating upside to sound productive.

If a deal slipped, explain what assumption was wrong, what evidence was missed, what changed, and what will be done differently. Do not only report a new date.

### Keep the call action-oriented and close the loop after the call

The output should be decisions, actions, owners, and updates. If the conversation produces no change in plan, resource, or forecast, it may have been status theater.

Update CRM, notify owners, schedule actions, revise forecast, and confirm any management commitments. Forecast calls lose value when decisions stay verbal.

## Common Traps

- Using the forecast call to clean CRM live.
- Repeating full deal history instead of changes and evidence; blurring commit and best case
- Relying on seller sentiment instead of buyer evidence; escalating without a specific management ask
- Naming no forecast-changing event; hiding commit downside or exaggerating upside
- Reporting slippage without learning; leaving the call with no actions or owners
- Failing to update CRM after decisions; surprising the manager with risks that should have been flagged earlier
- Defending a forecast rather than inspecting it; arriving without a concise deal brief for material opportunities; ignoring rollup impact of a deal movement; providing updates without recommended decisions
- Presenting provisional assumptions as firm evidence; skipping pre-read for complex deals and using the call for discovery; surprising the manager with major category movement; confusing coaching needs with forecast category decisions

## Self-Check

- Is CRM accurate before the call?
- Are changes since last review clear?; are commit, best case, upside, omitted, and risk separated?
- Is buyer evidence ready for each material deal?; are management asks specific?
- Are forecast-changing events identified?; are downside and upside represented honestly?
- Does slippage explanation include missed assumptions and learning?; does the call produce actions, owners, and decisions?
- Are CRM and forecast updated after the call?; are risks surfaced early enough for management to help?
- Is the preparation designed for inspection rather than defense?; is there a concise brief for each material deal?
- Is rollup impact clear?; are recommended decisions prepared?
- Is data confidence labeled?; is there a pre-read for complex or high-value deals?
- Are major category changes explained by evidence?; are coaching issues and forecast issues separated?
