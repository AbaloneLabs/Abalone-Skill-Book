# Deal Inspection And Evidence Quality

Deal inspection is not asking whether the seller feels good about the opportunity. It is evaluating the quality of evidence behind stage, forecast, next step, and close date. Agents often accept confident explanations, busy activity, or positive sentiment as proof. This skill helps the agent inspect deals with a bias toward buyer-verifiable evidence.

## Core Rules

### Prefer buyer actions over seller claims

Strong evidence includes buyer-scheduled meetings, shared data, validated business case, technical owner engagement, procurement steps, returned redlines, executive confirmation, or named implementation resources. Seller statements such as "they like us" or "I think legal is close" are weaker.

### Inspect evidence freshness and separate facts, inference, and hope

Old evidence decays. A business pain confirmed three months ago may no longer drive urgency. A close date set before procurement discovery may be invalid. Check when evidence was last confirmed and by whom.

Deal notes should distinguish what happened, what the seller infers, and what the seller hopes. "CFO attended and asked about payback" is a fact. "CFO is supportive" may be inference. "Commit this quarter" may be hope unless supported by buyer action.

### Check decision process completeness and weight evidence by decision authority

Inspect economic buyer, champion, users, technical owner, legal, procurement, security, finance, signer, and implementation owner as relevant. Missing decision roles reduce evidence quality.

Evidence from the person who can decide, fund, block, or implement carries more weight than evidence from a friendly but low-authority contact. A user's excitement may prove workflow pain; it does not prove budget approval. An executive's interest may prove priority; it does not prove technical fit. Weight each signal according to the stakeholder's role.

### Inspect evidence independence and test for contradiction

Multiple signals from one champion are weaker than independent confirmation from separate stakeholders. Look for whether finance, technical owners, users, executives, and procurement independently support the deal. Single-threaded evidence can be enthusiastic and fragile.

Good inspection looks for inconsistent facts: buyer says timeline is urgent but misses meetings; champion says budget exists but finance is unknown; procurement starts but business case is unapproved; stage is late but technical fit is untested. Contradictions should become risk items.

### Link evidence to resource requests and compare internal and external momentum

When a seller asks for executive help, discount approval, legal escalation, or technical resource, inspect whether evidence justifies the resource. Weak evidence may mean the better action is more discovery or disqualification, not escalation.

Internal momentum can look strong while buyer momentum is weak. Internal work includes pricing approvals, solution design, proposal drafting, and leadership attention. External momentum includes buyer-owned actions, stakeholder access, approvals, returned documents, and decision commitments. Inspect both, but forecast primarily from external momentum.

### Check whether evidence supports the amount and require a recovery action for weak evidence

Evidence may support a deal closing but not at the forecasted amount. If modules, user counts, services, term, or discount are still uncertain, amount confidence should be lower than win confidence. Inspection should separate whether the buyer will buy from what they will buy.

If evidence is weak, the inspection should not end with "monitor." Name the action that will improve or disprove confidence: secure economic buyer confirmation, validate budget, confirm procurement path, test technical fit, or ask for a go/no-go decision. Weak evidence without recovery action becomes forecast drift.

### Evaluate next-step quality and test close date credibility

A high-quality next step has buyer owner, seller owner, date, purpose, and decision dependency. Low-quality next steps such as "follow up" should reduce confidence.

Ask what must happen before close, who owns each step, how long it takes, and what evidence shows the buyer agrees. A close date without critical path evidence is not forecast evidence.

### Look for negative evidence and compare stage against exit criteria

Missed commitments, no executive access, vague urgency, stalled security review, unvalidated ROI, single-threading, discount requests, no procurement path, or implementation concerns are evidence too. Do not inspect only confirming signals.

The opportunity stage should match recorded evidence. If the seller cannot show buyer proof for the current stage, regress or flag the opportunity.

### Ask what would change the forecast and document inspection outcome

Good inspection identifies the next evidence that would increase or decrease confidence. If no specific evidence is named, the forecast is not being managed.

Record evidence strength, gaps, risk, required action, owner, due date, and forecast implication. Inspection without record becomes a conversation with no operating value.

## Common Traps

- Accepting seller confidence as evidence.
- Treating old evidence as current; mixing fact, inference, and hope
- Ignoring missing decision roles; counting weak next steps as momentum
- Trusting close dates without critical path; looking only for positive evidence
- Keeping stage unchanged after evidence review; asking many questions but defining no next evidence
- Failing to record inspection outcome; confusing executive attendance with executive commitment
- Ignoring buyer silence because prior calls were positive; giving equal weight to low-authority and high-authority evidence; treating many signals from one champion as broad buying-committee support; ignoring contradictions between words and behavior
- Allocating scarce internal resources without evidence quality; confusing internal deal work with external buyer momentum; inspecting win likelihood but not amount confidence; leaving weak evidence with no recovery or disproof action

## Self-Check

- Is forecast evidence based on buyer actions?
- Is evidence fresh and recently confirmed?; are facts, inferences, and hopes separated?
- Are relevant decision roles identified?; is the next step high quality?
- Is close date supported by critical path evidence?; is negative evidence included?
- Does stage match exit criteria?; is the next confidence-changing evidence named?
- Is inspection outcome documented with owner and due date?; would another manager reach the same risk view from the record?
- Did inspection produce a change in action, stage, risk, or forecast where warranted?; is evidence weighted by stakeholder authority?
- Is there independent confirmation beyond one champion?; are contradictions identified as risks?
- Are resource requests justified by deal evidence?; is external buyer momentum separated from internal seller work?
- Does evidence support the forecasted amount, not only possible win?; is there a recovery or disproof action for weak evidence?
