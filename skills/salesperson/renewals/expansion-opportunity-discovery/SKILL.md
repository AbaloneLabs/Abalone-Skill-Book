# Expansion Opportunity Discovery

Expansion discovery is not asking a happy customer whether they want more licenses. It is understanding whether additional scope would create meaningful value and whether the customer is ready to absorb it. Agents often overread satisfaction, push expansion before renewal health is secure, or miss operational constraints. This skill helps the agent discover expansion responsibly.

## Core Rules

### Start with realized value

Expansion is strongest when current value is proven or credibly developing. Review adoption, success metrics, user feedback, business outcomes, and unresolved issues before proposing more. If the customer has not realized value, expansion may feel like pressure.

### Identify natural expansion triggers

Triggers may include new teams, higher usage, workflow maturity, executive initiative, compliance need, geographic rollout, product adoption milestone, support request, integration expansion, or strategic planning cycle. Expansion should connect to a customer event, not seller quota.

### Map adjacent use cases and check account health first

Explore which teams, processes, regions, or products share similar pain, data, users, or outcomes. Do not assume the first team's success transfers automatically. Each use case needs its own owner, value, and readiness check.

Support issues, unresolved implementation risk, low adoption, billing problems, or renewal uncertainty should shape expansion timing. Sometimes the right expansion move is to fix the base account.

### Build a white-space map with evidence and sequence expansion responsibly

Map potential teams, regions, products, workflows, and use cases, but attach evidence to each one: pain signal, stakeholder, current workaround, value owner, readiness, budget path, and timing. White space without evidence is only a seller wish list.

Not all expansion opportunities should be pursued at once. Prioritize based on value, readiness, stakeholder access, implementation effort, renewal impact, and risk. A clear sequence prevents the account from being overloaded.

### Protect the current champion relationship and look for expansion blockers

The champion may support expansion but may also feel exposed if a broader rollout fails. Give them evidence, internal narrative, and realistic scope. Do not use the champion merely as a door opener without considering their political risk.

Expansion blockers may include data silos, different procurement owner, separate budget, regional compliance, competing internal project, incumbent tool in another team, or lack of executive mandate. Identify blockers before proposing commercial terms.

### Use customer success as a guardrail and clarify whether expansion is land-and-expand or new sale

Customer success should be able to challenge expansion timing if adoption is weak, support sentiment is poor, or the customer is overloaded. Treat that challenge as risk evidence, not sales resistance. Healthy expansion should make CS more confident about the account.

Some expansions are simple extensions of current use; others are effectively new sales with different buyer, budget, risk, and implementation path. Choose discovery depth accordingly. Under-discovering a new-sale expansion creates bad scope and forecast risk.

### Involve customer success and validate buyer capacity

Customer success often knows adoption patterns, stakeholder sentiment, and risk. Expansion discovery should coordinate with them rather than surprise them with sales pressure.

Expansion requires budget, admin time, change management, training, technical work, and stakeholder attention. A customer can want more and still lack capacity to implement it.

### Separate user demand from economic approval and build value hypothesis for each expansion

Users may want more seats or features, but expansion needs budget owner, decision process, and commercial approval. Do not treat usage interest as purchase intent.

Define problem, current workaround, expected outcome, value owner, baseline, risk, and adoption path. Expansion should have its own business logic.

### Avoid expansion that harms retention and preserve account narrative

Selling too much too soon can create failed rollout, support burden, and renewal risk. Expansion should strengthen the relationship, not overload it.

Record why expansion is being explored, current value evidence, risks, stakeholders, timing, and next action. Future account teams need context.

## Common Traps

- Treating satisfaction as expansion readiness; pushing expansion before current value is proven
- Inventing expansion triggers from seller quota; assuming one team's success transfers to another
- Ignoring account health and support issues; surprising customer success with expansion pressure
- Underestimating buyer capacity; mistaking user interest for budget approval
- Using generic value stories for new use cases; creating expansion that weakens retention
- Forgetting to document expansion rationale; treating expansion as easier than new sales without evidence
- Building a white-space map without evidence per opportunity; pursuing too many expansion paths at once
- Exposing the champion politically without support; missing blockers unique to the new team, region, or product; treating customer success concern as friction instead of account health evidence; under-discovering expansion that is really a new sale

## Self-Check

- Is current realized value understood?
- Is there a customer-owned expansion trigger?
- Are adjacent use cases mapped with owners and readiness?; is account health strong enough for expansion?
- Is customer success aligned?; is buyer capacity realistic?
- Are user demand and economic approval separated?; does each expansion have a value hypothesis?
- Could expansion harm retention if mistimed?; is account narrative documented?
- Is next action buyer-owned?; does expansion serve customer value, not only seller growth?
- Does white-space map include evidence, owner, readiness, and timing?; is expansion sequence prioritized?
- Is champion political risk considered?; are blockers for the new scope identified?
- Has customer success had a real chance to challenge timing?; is this expansion a simple extension or a new sale in practice?
