# Trade Show Booth Conversation

Trade show booth conversations are short, noisy, and easy to overcount. A badge scan or friendly chat is not qualified demand. Agents often pitch too quickly or collect leads without context. This skill helps the agent use booth conversations to identify relevance, qualify interest, and create useful follow-up.

## Core Rules

### Open with context, not a pitch

Ask why the attendee is at the event, what they are exploring, what role they have, and what problem brought them to the booth. A fast pitch may miss the actual reason for engagement.

### Qualify lightly but meaningfully and match depth to signal

Capture company, role, use case, current process, urgency, authority, timing, and follow-up interest. Booth qualification should be short, but it should separate curiosity from potential opportunity.

High-fit buyers with active projects deserve deeper discovery or scheduled follow-up. Low-fit or casual visitors should receive lighter handling. Do not spend equal time on every badge.

### Define booth team roles and use a lightweight lead quality code

If several people staff the booth, assign greeter, qualifier, demo owner, executive handler, partner handler, customer handler, and note owner as needed. Clear roles prevent duplicate questions and missed high-value visitors.

Create simple codes for active project, target account, customer, partner, student/vendor, competitor, nurture, or disqualified. Codes help prioritize follow-up later when memory fades.

### Protect demo stations from crowd dynamics and handle existing customers carefully

Crowded booths can make demos shallow and distracting. Decide when to show a quick teaser, when to schedule a private demo, and when to route to a specialist. Do not let a crowd force an irrelevant demo.

Customers at the booth may raise support issues, expansion interest, renewal concerns, or reference potential. Route them differently from net-new prospects and avoid discussing sensitive account details publicly.

### Capture permissioned contact context and prepare booth discovery prompts

If the attendee agrees to follow-up, record preferred topic, channel, urgency, and any requested asset. A scan without context often leads to irrelevant outreach.

Use a few prompts that fit the event theme and target audience. Good prompts reveal role, current initiative, trigger, and whether the attendee is learning or evaluating. Avoid interrogating people, but do not let every conversation stay at brochure level.

### Know when to disengage politely and use demos selectively

Some visitors are poor fit, competitors, vendors, job seekers, or casual swag seekers. Provide a respectful exit and preserve time for qualified conversations. Booth discipline requires ending conversations as well as starting them.

On-site demos should answer the attendee's use case, not run the standard tour. If the environment is too noisy or the use case requires detail, schedule a dedicated session.

### Capture exact follow-up reason and respect privacy and consent

Follow-up notes should say why the person engaged and what they asked for. "Met at booth" is not useful. "Ops director evaluating vendor for Q3 workflow migration; wants security overview" is useful.

Badge scans, contests, and event lists may have consent rules. Be clear about follow-up and respect opt-out or regional privacy requirements.

### Identify partner and competitor signals and avoid overqualifying in public

Events reveal ecosystem information: partner interest, competitor claims, market themes, and buyer objections. Capture signals separately from lead notes.

Sensitive topics such as budget, vendor dissatisfaction, or internal problems may not be appropriate on a crowded floor. Offer a private follow-up when needed.

### Create immediate next actions and debrief daily

For strong conversations, schedule meeting, send relevant artifact, introduce specialist, or assign owner before the event energy fades. Delayed generic follow-up wastes event investment.

Review lead quality, common themes, high-priority follow-ups, messaging issues, and market signals while the event is active. Adjust booth questions and staffing accordingly.

## Common Traps

- Treating badge scans as qualified leads.
- Opening with a pitch before understanding the attendee; spending the same time on every visitor
- Running generic demos in a noisy booth; writing follow-up notes with no reason for engagement
- Ignoring privacy and consent constraints; losing partner, competitor, and market signals
- Asking sensitive questions in public; waiting too long to follow up on strong interest
- Failing to adapt during the event; letting booth traffic metrics replace opportunity quality
- Forgetting to route existing customers differently from prospects; staffing booths without clear roles; having no simple lead quality codes; letting crowd dynamics drive irrelevant demos
- Treating customers like generic booth traffic; capturing contact details without follow-up context; having no discovery prompts tailored to the event; spending too long with visitors who are clearly not sales-relevant
