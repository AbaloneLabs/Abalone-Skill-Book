# Buying Committee Mapping

Buying committees are often larger and less visible than sellers expect. A single enthusiastic contact can create false confidence while unseen stakeholders shape requirements, budget, risk, and timing. This skill helps the agent map who matters, what each person needs, and where the deal is exposed.

## Core Rules

### Identify roles, not just names

Map economic buyer, champion, users, decision maker, technical evaluator, security, legal, procurement, finance, executive sponsor, implementation owner, blocker, influencer, and partner. One person can hold multiple roles, and one role can involve several people.

Names without roles do not explain the buying path.

### Understand each stakeholder's stakes

For each role, capture what they care about, what risk they perceive, what evidence they need, and what would make them support or block the purchase. Stakeholders rarely evaluate the same value.

Messaging should change by role.

### Test access and influence

Knowing a stakeholder exists is not enough. Determine whether the seller can reach them directly, through a champion, through an executive, or not at all. Also assess whether the champion can influence them.

Hidden stakeholders create late-stage surprises.

### Map formal and informal power

Formal authority may sit with procurement or executives, while informal power may sit with a respected user, architect, finance analyst, or operations owner. Capture both.

Ignoring informal power can stall adoption and approval.

### Identify missing stakeholders early

If legal, security, IT, procurement, finance, implementation, or executive sponsors usually matter but are absent, name the gap. Ask the buyer how those groups will be involved.

Late introduction of risk owners often delays close.

### Track stakeholder sentiment and use the map to plan next moves

Mark stakeholders as supportive, neutral, skeptical, blocked, unknown, or actively opposed. Update sentiment based on evidence, not optimism.

Sentiment should reflect action, not politeness.

Stakeholder maps should drive discovery, demo attendees, business case, technical validation, executive outreach, champion coaching, and procurement preparation. If the map does not change the plan, it is decorative.

### Keep the map current and map information flow

Stakeholders change through reorgs, hiring, budget shifts, project changes, and champion departure. Update the map as new names and risks appear.

Know how information moves inside the buying committee: who forwards materials, who summarizes meetings, who controls the narrative, and who is absent from direct conversations. A stakeholder may never meet the seller but still shape the decision through internal summaries.

Information flow determines what proof must be forwardable.

### Identify consensus requirements and protect confidentiality and trust

Some organizations require formal consensus. Others need one economic buyer with veto checks. Determine whether the committee needs unanimity, executive decision, technical approval, procurement clearance, or informal agreement.

Consensus rules affect deal pacing and risk.

Stakeholder maps may include sensitive politics, objections, and influence notes. Keep internal notes factual and professional. Do not reveal one stakeholder's private concern to another without permission.

The map should help selling without damaging buyer trust.

### Identify stakeholder evidence gaps and plan proof by stakeholder

For each important stakeholder, mark whether the seller has direct evidence, champion-reported evidence, inference, or no evidence. A committee map full of inferred sentiment can create false confidence.

Evidence gaps should drive next discovery and access requests.

Each stakeholder should receive proof suited to their risk: business case for economic buyers, workflow fit for users, architecture for technical teams, risk controls for security, terms clarity for legal, and process readiness for procurement.

The map should determine what proof is still missing.

## Common Traps

- Treating the first contact as the buying committee.
- Listing names without roles, stakes, or influence; assuming the champion can reach everyone
- Ignoring informal power; waiting until procurement or security appears late
- Mistaking polite attendance for support; failing to map blockers and skeptics
- Building one message for all stakeholders; letting the map become stale after org changes
- Not using the map to plan deal actions; ignoring who controls internal information flow and meeting summaries
- Assuming consensus works the same across all accounts; recording political notes in a way that would damage trust if exposed
- Treating inferred stakeholder sentiment as confirmed fact; mapping stakeholders without identifying what proof each one needs

## Self-Check

- Are stakeholder roles identified, not only names?
- Are economic buyer, champion, users, technical, legal, procurement, finance, security, executive, implementation, blockers, influencers, and partners considered?
- Is each stakeholder's stake, risk, evidence need, and support condition known?
- Is access path to each stakeholder clear?
- Is champion influence tested?
- Are formal and informal power mapped?; are missing stakeholders named as gaps?
- Is sentiment based on evidence and updated?; does the map drive next deal actions?
- Is the map current after organizational or deal changes?; is internal information flow and narrative control mapped?
- Are consensus requirements and veto points understood?; are sensitive stakeholder notes factual, professional, and confidential?
- Are direct evidence, champion-reported evidence, inference, and unknowns separated for each key stakeholder?; is proof mapped by stakeholder risk and decision role?
