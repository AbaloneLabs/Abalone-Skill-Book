# Close Plan And Mutual Commitment

A close plan is not a countdown to the seller's target date. It is a final-stage evidence map showing what must be true for the buyer to make a responsible decision and for the seller to deliver what is being sold. Agents often over-focus on signature mechanics and miss unresolved value, authority, legal, procurement, implementation, or buyer-resource risk. This skill helps the agent make closing mutual, explicit, and handoff-safe.

## Core Rules

### Confirm the buyer's decision state

Clarify whether the buyer has selected the solution, is still evaluating, is waiting on approval, is negotiating terms, is seeking budget, or is only exploring. Different states require different close actions. Asking for signature when the buyer still lacks internal approval creates pressure without progress.

### Identify every required approval

List business owner, economic buyer, legal, procurement, security, finance, technical owner, executive signer, vendor management, and implementation sponsor where relevant. Confirm what each must approve and whether any approval is conditional.

One missing approver can invalidate an otherwise strong close plan.

### Link each step to buyer value

Close steps should not feel like administrative pressure. Connect contract, procurement, kickoff planning, implementation resources, and timeline decisions to the buyer's business outcome and cost of delay. This keeps the close plan grounded in the buyer's reason to act.

### Use mutual commitments

For each seller action, identify the buyer action it supports. For each buyer action, identify the seller action required. Examples include seller providing security answers so buyer can complete review, buyer scheduling legal so seller can respond to redlines, or buyer naming implementation owner so kickoff can be planned.

### Validate paper process details

Confirm paper type, order form, MSA, DPA, redline process, signature authority, purchase order requirement, vendor setup, payment terms, tax handling, and procurement portal use. Do not treat "legal is reviewing" as enough detail.

### Check implementation readiness before signature and resolve final risk openly

Closing a deal that cannot start well creates churn risk. Confirm buyer resources, admin access, data readiness, integration path, success criteria, kickoff timing, training expectations, and ownership. If readiness is weak, narrow scope or plan remediation.

End-stage risks may include budget uncertainty, executive hesitation, procurement tactics, unapproved concessions, technical gaps, security concerns, legal terms, lack of user buy-in, implementation load, or weak success metrics. Name each risk, owner, and resolution path.

### Confirm the buyer's no-surprise path and test commitment with buyer-owned actions

Ask what could still surprise the buyer's side before signature or kickoff. This may reveal a hidden signer, PO requirement, security committee, legal policy, budget freeze, internal announcement, implementation blackout, or executive concern. A no-surprise conversation is not pessimism; it is a way to protect both sides from late-stage embarrassment.

End-stage commitment should include buyer actions, not only seller paperwork. Examples include scheduling signer review, returning redlines, confirming PO path, naming kickoff owner, allocating implementation resources, approving the business case, or sending procurement requirements. If the buyer will not take buyer-owned actions, the close plan is not mutual.

### Decide what happens if timing slips and keep forecast tied to evidence

A close plan should include consequences and recovery paths for missed dates. If legal slips, does kickoff move? If PO creation takes longer, can implementation planning continue? If security requires an exception, who decides? Naming slip handling prevents each delay from becoming a fresh negotiation.

A close plan should support forecast confidence through buyer-confirmed milestones and completed dependencies. Do not forecast commit because the seller has sent paper. Forecast commit requires evidence that the buyer can and intends to complete the remaining steps.

### Avoid coercive urgency and prepare the post-signature handoff

Use buyer events, implementation windows, budget cycles, risk deadlines, or business consequences to explain timing. Avoid artificial pressure or disappearing discounts that are not policy-backed. Trust can be lost at the end of a deal.

Before signature, capture final scope, assumptions, exclusions, special terms, value promises, buyer responsibilities, success criteria, risks, and implementation contacts. Closing is not complete if post-sale teams must rediscover what was sold.

## Common Traps

- Treating the seller's desired close date as the buyer's decision date; asking for signature before confirming selection, approval, and funding state
- Missing a late approver such as finance, security, vendor management, or executive signer; listing close tasks without mutual buyer-seller commitments
- Treating "legal review" or "procurement" as a single step; closing without implementation readiness or buyer-side owners
- Hiding final risks to preserve forecast optimism; forecasting commit based on sent paperwork rather than buyer-confirmed dependencies
- Using artificial urgency that damages trust; letting final discounts or concessions bypass approval
- Forgetting special terms, assumptions, and success criteria in handoff; winning the signature while setting up a poor customer start
- Skipping a no-surprise conversation because the deal feels close; counting seller paperwork as mutual commitment
- Having no recovery plan for missed legal, procurement, PO, or kickoff dates; treating buyer silence near signature as harmless

## Self-Check

- Is the buyer's current decision state clear?
- Are all required approvers and conditional approvals identified?
- Is each close step tied to buyer value or decision quality?
- Are buyer and seller commitments paired?
- Are paper process details confirmed?; are implementation readiness, resources, and kickoff expectations checked?
- Are final risks named with owner and resolution path?; does forecast confidence come from buyer-confirmed evidence?
- Is urgency based on buyer consequence rather than seller pressure?; are concessions approved and documented?
- Are scope, assumptions, special terms, value promises, and success criteria ready for handoff?; would the buyer experience the close plan as coordination rather than pressure?
- Has the buyer been asked what could still surprise the process?; are there buyer-owned actions that prove mutual commitment?
- Is there a recovery path if legal, procurement, PO, or kickoff timing slips?; has buyer silence or delay near signature been investigated rather than ignored?
