# Bid No Bid And RFP Fit Review

Formal RFPs can look like qualified opportunities because the buyer has budget, timeline, and a written process. They can also be traps: the requirements may favor an incumbent, the decision may already be shaped, the salesperson may lack access, or the response may consume enormous team time with little chance of winning. Agents often treat any RFP as a proposal-writing task and miss the earlier decision: whether the deal deserves pursuit. This skill helps the agent evaluate bid fit before committing sales, technical, legal, security, and executive resources.

## Core Rules

### Establish whether the opportunity is real and open

Clarify why the RFP exists, who initiated it, whether there is an incumbent, whether the buyer has a preferred vendor, whether requirements were influenced by a competitor, and whether the process allows meaningful differentiation. A formal request does not guarantee an open competition.

If the seller had no prior relationship, no discovery, no access to decision makers, and no ability to shape criteria, treat the opportunity as high risk unless the requirements strongly match a known strength.

### Check strategic and commercial fit

Evaluate fit against ICP, use case, industry, company size, geography, required capabilities, implementation complexity, support obligations, margin, contract terms, payment risk, and long-term expansion potential. A large contract can still be bad if it requires heavy customization, weak margin, risky service promises, or unsupported geography.

Do not let logo value or revenue size override fit. Bad-fit wins can damage delivery teams and future references.

### Identify must-have requirements and disqualifiers

Separate mandatory requirements from preferences. Look for security certifications, regulatory obligations, data residency, integrations, uptime commitments, insurance levels, accessibility, public procurement rules, local presence, pricing format, implementation dates, and contract terms. Decide which gaps are acceptable, which need partners, and which should stop the bid.

Avoid responding "yes" to requirements that are roadmap items, custom work, or unsupported edge cases unless the commitment is approved.

### Assess access to the buying committee

Strong RFP pursuit usually requires access to business owners, technical evaluators, procurement, legal, security, finance, and champions. If the process blocks all dialogue, the seller needs stronger evidence from prior discovery or public materials.

Ask whether the seller can clarify requirements, test assumptions, understand scoring, and mobilize an internal champion. Without access, the response must win on paper alone, which raises the threshold for fit.

### Estimate response cost and opportunity cost

RFP responses can consume account executive time, solution consulting, product, security, legal, finance, executive review, partner input, and proposal operations. Compare this effort with probability of win and competing opportunities.

A low-probability bid can still be worth pursuing for a strategic account or market signal, but the reason should be explicit. Do not hide speculative pursuit behind routine pipeline optimism.

### Define the win strategy before writing

A bid should have a clear thesis: why this buyer should choose this vendor, what business outcome matters, where differentiation will be scored, how risks will be answered, and how price will be justified. If the team cannot state the win strategy, writing more answers will not fix the deal.

The response should not simply comply. It should guide evaluators toward the seller's strongest evidence within the rules.

### Confirm process rules and deadlines

Check submission deadline, format, portal rules, question deadline, word limits, attachment limits, pricing templates, required certifications, blackout periods, site visits, demos, oral presentations, BAFO stages, and disqualification rules. Missing a procedural requirement can lose an otherwise strong bid.

If the process is already too late for a quality response, recommend no-bid or a narrowed strategy rather than a rushed, weak submission.

### Make the decision explicit

Record bid, no-bid, or conditional bid with reasons, owners, required approvals, known gaps, and next decision gate. A conditional bid may depend on clarification answers, executive access, partner confirmation, pricing approval, or legal review.

The salesperson should not drift into a full response just because documents arrived.

## Common Traps

- Treating every RFP as a qualified opportunity because the buyer wrote requirements.
- Ignoring signs that requirements were written around an incumbent or competitor.
- Pursuing a bid with no champion, no discovery, and no evaluator access.
- Saying yes to mandatory requirements that need unapproved roadmap, custom work, or risky exceptions.
- Underestimating legal, security, technical, pricing, and executive effort.
- Letting a large contract value hide poor margin, delivery risk, or bad customer fit.
- Starting response writing before defining the win strategy; missing question deadlines, submission format, portal rules, or disqualification criteria
- Filling pipeline with RFPs that have low probability and little strategic value; failing to document no-bid rationale, causing the same poor-fit bid pattern to repeat

## Self-Check

- Is the RFP genuinely open, or does evidence suggest an incumbent or preferred vendor?
- Does the opportunity match ICP, use case, geography, margin, support model, and delivery capability?
- Are mandatory requirements, disqualifiers, and risky gaps identified?
- Are unsupported roadmap, customization, security, legal, or service commitments clearly escalated?
- Does the seller have access to decision makers, evaluators, procurement, or a champion?
- Is response effort weighed against win probability and opportunity cost?
- Is there a clear win strategy before response writing begins?
- Are process rules, deadlines, portal instructions, question windows, and disqualification risks understood?
- Is the decision recorded as bid, no-bid, or conditional bid with owners and gates?
- Would a no-bid preserve resources for stronger opportunities, or is there a documented strategic reason to pursue?
