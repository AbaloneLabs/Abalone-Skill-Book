# Sales Proposal

A sales proposal is not a polished recap of everything discussed. It is a decision document. It should show the buyer that the seller understands the problem, can deliver the proposed scope, has named assumptions and tradeoffs, and is asking for a specific next decision. Weak proposals are generic, overbroad, and full of unsupported value claims. This skill helps the agent write proposals that are clear, bounded, and handoff-safe.

## Core Rules

### Anchor the proposal in buyer-confirmed need

Start with the buyer's problem, current state, impact, decision criteria, and desired outcome. Use buyer language where possible. Do not lead with company boilerplate or product inventory.

The buyer should recognize their own situation.

### State the recommended scope

Name included products, services, modules, users, regions, integrations, support level, implementation path, timeline, and success metrics. Separate optional items and future phases.

Ambiguous scope becomes negotiation and delivery risk.

### List assumptions and exclusions

Include assumptions about buyer resources, data readiness, integrations, security review, legal approval, usage volume, timeline, and implementation responsibilities. State what is not included.

Exclusions prevent silent promises.

### Tie value to evidence

Summarize value drivers and expected outcomes using discovery evidence and clearly labeled assumptions. Avoid inflated ROI, generic savings, or claims the buyer did not validate.

Proposal value should be credible enough for internal forwarding.

### Address risks and mitigations and make commercial terms readable

Include known risks such as adoption, implementation, technical validation, security review, timeline, change management, data quality, or stakeholder alignment. Pair each with owner or mitigation.

Risk transparency builds trust.

Pricing, discounts, payment terms, renewal, contract length, expiration date, taxes, services, and optional fees should be clear. Do not hide important terms in footnotes or attachments.

Commercial clarity reduces late procurement friction.

### Align with approved promises and make next steps mutual

Check proposal language against product capability, legal terms, security statements, roadmap boundaries, implementation capacity, and discount approval. Do not include promises that delivery, product, legal, finance, or leadership has not accepted.

The proposal becomes a record.

State what the buyer and seller each do next: review, stakeholder meeting, technical validation, redlines, procurement, signature, kickoff, or decision deadline. Avoid seller-only follow-up.

### Write for internal circulation and separate recommendation from alternatives

The proposal may be read by people who never met the seller. Include enough context, proof, and definitions for internal champions to forward it without re-explaining.

Internal circulation changes the standard for clarity. A champion may forward the proposal to finance, security, procurement, legal, a technical owner, or an executive who has different concerns. Use headings, short rationale, defined terms, and explicit assumptions so the proposal survives outside the live sales conversation. Avoid language that requires the seller to interpret it later.

If there are multiple options, make the recommended option clear and explain why alternatives were not chosen. Alternatives may differ by tier, rollout pace, services, price, risk, or time to value. This helps the buyer understand tradeoffs instead of assuming the seller is merely pushing the largest package.

### Preserve decision history and keep legal and proposal language aligned

Reference the criteria, constraints, objections, and decisions that shaped the proposal. If a requested integration was deferred, a lower support tier was rejected, or a phased rollout was chosen, state the reason. This prevents old options from resurfacing late in procurement as if they were never discussed.

Do not use proposal wording that creates obligations the contract will not support. Phrases such as "guaranteed," "fully compliant," "custom integration included," "unlimited support," or "delivery by" may create risk unless they match approved legal and operational language. When in doubt, use bounded language and confirm with the responsible team.

### Design for decision, not decoration and preserve handoff value

A proposal can be visually polished and still weak. Prioritize decision-useful content: problem, value, scope, terms, risk, proof, next step, and owner. Remove filler pages, generic awards, broad feature inventory, and marketing copy that bury the decision.

The final proposal should help implementation and customer success understand what was sold, why, under what assumptions, and with what risks.

## Common Traps

- Using a generic proposal with buyer name swapped in.
- Listing features instead of buyer need and decision rationale; leaving scope, services, regions, integrations, users, or support level vague
- Omitting assumptions and exclusions; presenting ROI without evidence or caveats
- Hiding risks to make the proposal cleaner; making pricing hard to understand
- Including roadmap, security, legal, or implementation promises without approval; ending with seller follow-up but no buyer action
- Writing a proposal the champion cannot forward internally; creating a proposal that delivery teams cannot use
- Letting proposal language drift from CRM and contract terms; presenting several options without naming the seller's recommendation
- Reopening prior decisions because the proposal does not preserve rationale; using legally risky phrases that are not supported by contract terms; polishing the document while leaving decision-critical content vague; forgetting that finance, security, legal, and executives may each read for different risks

## Self-Check

- Is the proposal anchored in buyer-confirmed problem, impact, criteria, and outcome?
- Is recommended scope explicit?
- Are optional items and future phases separated?
- Are assumptions and exclusions listed?; are value claims evidence-based and caveated?
- Are risks and mitigations named with owners?; are commercial terms readable?
- Are product, legal, security, roadmap, implementation, finance, and leadership promises approved?; are buyer and seller next actions clear?
- Is the proposal forwardable to internal stakeholders?; is it useful for implementation and customer success handoff?
- Does it support a decision rather than merely look polished?; does the proposal survive internal forwarding without seller narration?
- Are alternatives and the recommended path clearly distinguished?; is the decision history preserved where it affects scope, risk, or price?
- Is proposal wording aligned with contract and approved operational commitments?; has generic filler been removed in favor of decision-useful content?
