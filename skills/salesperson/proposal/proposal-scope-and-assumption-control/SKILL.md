# Proposal Scope And Assumption Control

Proposal scope is where many delivery problems are created. A seller may intend one thing, the buyer may read another, and implementation may inherit a third version. Assumptions that remain implicit become promises. This skill helps the agent make scope, exclusions, dependencies, and responsibilities explicit before the buyer relies on the proposal.

## Core Rules

### Define included work precisely

State products, modules, services, integrations, users, environments, data scope, support level, training, onboarding, migration, reporting, and implementation activities. Avoid vague phrases such as "full setup" or "integration support" unless defined.

Specificity protects both buyer and delivery team.

### Separate required from optional

Some items are necessary for success; others are optional enhancements or later phases. Mark required, optional, future, buyer-owned, partner-owned, and not included.

This prevents optional work from becoming assumed scope.

### Make buyer responsibilities explicit

Name what the buyer must provide: data, admin access, security review, technical owner, stakeholder availability, training attendance, decisions, approvals, testing, procurement steps, and internal communications.

Buyer dependencies affect timeline and success.

### List assumptions and consequences

For each important assumption, state what happens if it is false. For example, poor data quality may extend timeline, missing API access may require a different integration path, or delayed legal review may move kickoff.

Assumptions without consequences are easy to ignore.

### Identify exclusions plainly and align scope with pricing

Exclusions may include custom development, unsupported integrations, data cleanup, legacy migration, on-site training, premium support, legal review, managed services, change management, or third-party fees.

Do not hide exclusions in fine print.

Check that price, discount, services fees, term, usage tier, support level, and optional add-ons match the proposed scope. If scope changes, pricing and approvals may need to change.

Scope and price must move together.

### Verify delivery acceptance and treat dependencies as schedule risks

If delivery, implementation, security, product, legal, finance, or partner teams must fulfill the scope, confirm they accept it. A seller's intent does not create delivery capacity.

Delivery acceptance should include capacity, timeline, prerequisites, support model, and known risks. A team may agree that something is technically possible but not agree that it can be delivered in the proposed timeline or at the proposed services price. Capture the exact nature of acceptance so later escalation does not depend on memory.

Buyer dependencies are not administrative details. Security review, data access, executive approval, test users, legal redlines, procurement forms, and integration credentials can determine the real timeline. Tie dependencies to dates and owners where possible. If the buyer cannot commit, avoid promising a fixed kickoff, go-live, or value milestone.

### Define change control before change happens and keep commercial and operational scope together

State how scope changes will be handled: new quote, revised services estimate, statement of work, change order, delayed milestone, extra partner fee, or future phase. Buyers may assume small changes are included unless the proposal explains the boundary.

Do not let a commercial order form say one thing while an implementation attachment says another. If there are multiple documents, check that product package, services scope, user count, usage limits, support level, implementation timeline, and assumptions match. Conflicting documents create leverage for the broadest interpretation.

### Use examples where terms are ambiguous and control versioning

If a phrase could be interpreted broadly, add a concrete example. "Standard data migration" may mean CSV import only, not cleanup, transformation, deduplication, historical backfill, or custom mapping. Examples make boundaries understandable to non-specialists.

Proposal versions should show what changed: scope, assumptions, price, dates, terms, or responsibilities. Avoid multiple conflicting proposal copies.

### Watch for buyer edits and preserve scope through handoff

Buyers may modify proposal language, procurement forms, order forms, or redlines in ways that expand scope. Review changes before accepting.

The final accepted scope should appear in CRM, contract, kickoff notes, and customer success handoff.

## Common Traps

- Using broad scope language that delivery cannot interpret.
- Letting optional items appear included; omitting buyer responsibilities
- Listing assumptions without consequences; hiding exclusions in small print
- Changing scope without changing price or approvals; selling services delivery has not accepted
- Sending multiple proposal versions with conflicting terms; missing buyer edits that expand scope
- Letting kickoff start from memory rather than final scope; treating partner work as included without partner confirmation
- Assuming "standard implementation" means the same thing to buyer and seller; confusing technical feasibility with delivery acceptance
- Treating buyer dependencies as outside the seller's timeline risk; waiting until a buyer asks for extras before defining change control; letting commercial documents and implementation documents drift apart; using familiar internal terms that the buyer reads more broadly

## Self-Check

- Is included work precisely defined?
- Are required, optional, future, buyer-owned, partner-owned, and excluded items separated?
- Are buyer responsibilities explicit?
- Are assumptions paired with consequences if false?; are exclusions plain and visible?
- Does pricing match scope and support level?; are scope changes reflected in approvals?
- Have delivery and other responsible teams accepted the scope?; are proposal versions controlled?
- Were buyer edits reviewed for scope expansion?; is final scope preserved in CRM, contract, kickoff, and handoff?
- Could implementation execute from the proposal without guessing?; does delivery acceptance cover capacity, timing, prerequisites, and support model?
- Are buyer dependencies tied to owners and dates where possible?; is change control defined before extra work appears?
- Do order form, proposal, SOW, CRM, and handoff agree?; are ambiguous terms illustrated with examples or exclusions?
