# Sales Proposal

A sales proposal is not a brochure with pricing attached. It should show that the salesperson understood the customer's problem, mapped the offer to that problem, explained value in the customer's terms, set clear scope, handled risks, and made the decision path easier. A proposal that is generic, overbroad, or disconnected from discovery can create confusion even when the product is a fit.

Use this skill before writing a proposal, quote, demo recap, business case, executive summary, mutual action plan, or follow-up recommendation. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing a polished document that lacks decision usefulness.

## Core Rules

### Anchor The Proposal In The Customer's Problem

Open with the customer's situation, not the seller's company history.

Include:

- problem or objective in the customer's words;
- current state;
- cost or risk of the current state;
- desired outcome;
- stakeholders affected;
- decision criteria discussed.

This proves the proposal is specific. If the proposal could be sent unchanged to many prospects, it is probably too generic.

### Connect Offer Components To Outcomes

Each part of the proposed solution should map to a customer need or decision criterion.

For every major component, state:

- what it does;
- which problem it addresses;
- what outcome it supports;
- what evidence or proof exists;
- what assumption or dependency matters.

Avoid long feature lists. The customer needs to understand relevance, not the full catalog.

### Define Scope And Boundaries

Ambiguous scope creates disappointment. State what is included, what is not included, and what may require additional work.

Clarify:

- products, services, modules, or deliverables included;
- implementation responsibilities;
- timeline assumptions;
- customer responsibilities;
- integrations included or excluded;
- support level;
- training;
- data migration;
- customization;
- renewal or expansion terms;
- expiration of pricing.

Scope clarity protects trust.

### Present Price In Context Of Value

Price should be understandable and tied to the value case. Do not hide important pricing conditions.

Explain:

- pricing unit;
- one-time and recurring costs;
- discounts and expiration;
- payment terms;
- usage limits;
- implementation fees;
- optional add-ons;
- assumptions behind the quote;
- expected return or value where responsibly estimated.

Avoid false ROI precision. Use transparent assumptions and separate hard savings from potential upside.

### Include Proof Matched To Risk

The buyer needs confidence. Proof should match the risk they feel.

Proof may include:

- relevant case study;
- reference;
- benchmark;
- demo evidence;
- pilot result;
- security documentation;
- implementation plan;
- service level commitment;
- customer testimonial;
- subject-matter explanation.

Do not overload the proposal with irrelevant proof. Choose evidence that answers the buyer's likely doubts.

### Make Decision And Next Steps Easy

A proposal should reduce ambiguity about what happens next.

Include:

- decision needed;
- who needs to approve;
- deadline or timeline;
- next meeting;
- procurement or legal step;
- implementation kickoff;
- mutual action plan;
- open questions;
- owner for each next step.

If the proposal ends with "let us know", it may leave too much work for the buyer.

### Avoid Overpromising

Do not promise timelines, outcomes, integrations, support levels, savings, or custom work unless the organization can deliver them. Sales promises become implementation obligations.

If something depends on customer data, internal approval, technical fit, third-party vendors, or behavior change, state the dependency.

### Tailor The Proposal To The Buying Group

Different stakeholders read proposals for different reasons. The economic buyer may look for business value and risk. The user may look for workflow fit. Technical reviewers may look for integration, security, and operational effort. Procurement may look for terms. Legal may look for obligations and liability. Customer success may later look for what was promised.

When the buying group is complex, include enough structure for each audience:

- executive summary for decision makers;
- use-case detail for users;
- implementation assumptions for operators;
- technical or security references for reviewers;
- commercial terms for procurement;
- open questions and next steps for the champion.

Do not let one stakeholder's preferred format erase the needs of the rest of the buying group.

## Common Traps

### Proposal Before Discovery

Sending a proposal too early often produces generic scope and price shock. Use discovery to earn specificity.

### Seller-Centric Structure

Company history, product awards, and feature catalogs can be useful, but they should not bury the customer's problem and decision.

### Hiding Costs

Surprise implementation fees, usage limits, renewals, or required add-ons damage trust.

### No Customer Responsibilities

Many solutions require customer data, approvals, access, training, or change management. Name these responsibilities.

### Treating All Proof As Equal

A logo slide may not answer a security concern. A testimonial may not answer financial risk. Match proof to the buyer's concern.

### Unclear Expiration Or Terms

Discount deadlines, price holds, and proposal expiration should be real and clear.

### Ignoring Internal Review Before Sending

Proposals often create commitments. If the document includes custom scope, unusual terms, security claims, legal language, implementation timing, or discounting, it should be reviewed by the right internal owner before it reaches the buyer.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The proposal starts from the customer's problem, current state, impact, desired outcome, and decision criteria.
- [ ] Each proposed component maps to a customer need or outcome.
- [ ] Scope, exclusions, customer responsibilities, implementation assumptions, support, and timeline are clear.
- [ ] Price is explained with units, one-time costs, recurring costs, discounts, terms, and assumptions.
- [ ] Value or ROI claims are transparent and not overstated.
- [ ] Proof is selected to address the buyer's specific risks and doubts.
- [ ] Open questions, approval steps, decision owner, and next actions are explicit.
- [ ] The proposal addresses the needs of economic buyer, users, technical reviewers, procurement, legal, and implementation stakeholders where relevant.
- [ ] The proposal does not promise outcomes, integrations, timelines, or services the organization cannot deliver.
- [ ] Custom scope, special terms, security claims, or unusual discounts received appropriate internal review.
- [ ] Terms, expiration, and conditions are clear enough to avoid surprise.
- [ ] The document helps the buyer decide, not merely admire the seller.
