# Business Case And ROI Summary

A business case is not a spreadsheet designed to make the deal look attractive. It is the buyer's internal argument for why this purchase deserves money, time, and risk. Weak ROI summaries use unvalidated assumptions, ignore implementation cost, and create promises customer success cannot prove. This skill helps the agent build business cases that are credible, transparent, and decision-ready.

## Core Rules

### Start with the buyer's decision

Know what the business case must support: budget approval, executive sponsorship, procurement, board review, finance validation, renewal defense, or project prioritization. The format and evidence should match that decision.

Do not create ROI theater for a buyer who needs risk clarity.

### Use buyer-validated inputs

Base calculations on buyer-provided or buyer-approved assumptions: volume, labor time, error rate, conversion, revenue, churn, cost, risk, adoption, and implementation timeline. If assumptions are estimates, label them.

The buyer must be able to defend the numbers internally.

### Separate benefit categories

Distinguish cost savings, productivity, revenue growth, risk reduction, compliance, customer experience, employee experience, and strategic optionality. Do not add soft benefits into hard ROI unless the buyer agrees.

Benefit categories need different proof.

### Include total cost and effort and show range and sensitivity

Account for subscription, services, implementation, integration, training, migration, internal labor, support, change management, taxes, partner fees, and opportunity cost. ROI that ignores cost is not credible.

Use conservative, expected, and upside cases where appropriate. Identify the assumptions that most affect the result. This helps buyers judge risk.

Single-point ROI can create false certainty.

### Involve finance or metric owners early and use risk-adjusted language

If the business case will be used for budget approval, ask which buyer-side person can validate the financial model. Finance, operations, analytics, or department leaders may each own different inputs. A champion's enthusiasm is not the same as finance acceptance. Early validation reduces late-stage rejection of assumptions.

When benefits depend on adoption, implementation date, workflow change, market behavior, or user compliance, describe the dependency. A benefit can be real but uncertain. Language such as "potential," "estimated," "based on current volume," or "assuming adoption by the claims team" preserves credibility.

### Make non-benefits explicit and compare timing of cost and benefit

Clarify what the business case does not count: unmeasured soft benefits, avoided risks that cannot be quantified, excluded departments, future phases, indirect effects, or benefits outside the contract scope. This prevents readers from assuming the model is either more complete or more certain than it is.

Costs may begin at contract signature while benefits arrive after implementation, training, adoption, or data maturity. Show when value is expected to appear and when the buyer will review progress. A positive annual ROI can still be cash-flow painful if timing is ignored.

### Prepare the case for challenge and tie value to success metrics

Assume finance, procurement, or an executive will challenge one or two inputs. Keep source notes, formulas, definitions, and caveats clear enough that the model can be defended. If the business case collapses when one assumption changes, the seller should know that before presenting it.

The business case should connect to post-sale measurement: baseline, owner, data source, cadence, and target. Otherwise the buyer cannot verify value later.

### Avoid unsupported payback claims and address non-financial reasons

Do not promise payback period, savings, revenue, or risk elimination unless the buyer data and implementation assumptions support it. Use caveats and approved language.

Some purchases are justified by compliance, risk, strategic alignment, customer trust, or operational resilience. Do not force every case into narrow financial ROI.

### Make it forwardable and update after scope changes

A champion should be able to share the summary with finance, executives, procurement, and implementation owners. Include assumptions, sources, owner, and caveats.

If price, modules, services, timeline, rollout, or success criteria change, update the ROI. Old numbers attached to new scope are misleading.

## Common Traps

- Creating a business case before knowing the buyer's internal decision.
- Using seller-assumed numbers the buyer cannot defend; mixing soft benefits into hard-dollar ROI without agreement
- Ignoring internal labor and implementation cost; presenting one ROI number without sensitivity
- Claiming payback from weak assumptions; omitting post-sale measurement plan
- Treating compliance or risk purchases as if only direct savings matter; making the summary too seller-centric for internal forwarding
- Failing to revise ROI after scope or price changes; hiding assumptions to make the case look stronger
- Letting the business case promise outcomes delivery cannot control; relying only on a champion's numbers when finance will approve the spend
- Using confident language for benefits that depend on uncertain adoption; omitting excluded benefits and model boundaries; ignoring timing differences between upfront cost and delayed benefit; building a model that cannot survive a basic assumption challenge

## Self-Check

- Is the buyer's internal decision known?
- Are inputs buyer-validated or clearly labeled estimates?
- Are benefit categories separated?
- Are subscription, services, internal labor, implementation, integration, training, migration, taxes, partner, and opportunity costs included?; are conservative, expected, and upside cases considered?
- Are sensitivity drivers visible?; are value claims tied to baseline, owner, data source, cadence, and target?
- Are payback and ROI claims caveated appropriately?; are non-financial reasons represented where relevant?
- Is the summary forwardable to finance, executives, procurement, and implementation?; is ROI updated after scope, price, or timeline changes?
- Could customer success later verify the promised value?; have finance, analytics, operations, or metric owners validated the inputs that matter?
- Is uncertainty visible where value depends on adoption, timing, or buyer action?; are excluded benefits and model boundaries named?
- Is cost-benefit timing clear?; could the case survive challenge from finance, procurement, or an executive?
