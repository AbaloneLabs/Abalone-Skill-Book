# Persona And Use Case Targeting

Persona targeting is not choosing a job title and writing a message around it. The same title can own different problems in different organizations, and the same use case can require different buyers, users, approvers, and blockers. Weak persona work creates irrelevant outreach and shallow discovery. This skill helps the agent connect persona, use case, business pain, and buying role before acting.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish persona from buying role

A persona may be user, champion, evaluator, economic buyer, technical approver, legal reviewer, procurement owner, security reviewer, finance approver, or executive sponsor. Do not assume the person who feels the pain can buy or the person who signs can define requirements.

Targeting should map likely influence and decision rights, not just job titles.

### Ground use cases in real workflow

State the job the buyer or user is trying to perform, the current process, where friction appears, what trigger makes it urgent, and what outcome would count as success. Avoid abstract labels like "productivity" or "visibility" without workflow detail.

Use cases should be specific enough to support discovery questions.

### Match message to persona stakes

Users care about daily friction. Managers care about team output and control. Executives care about business outcomes and risk. Technical stakeholders care about integration, security, reliability, and maintainability. Procurement cares about commercial terms and process.

Do not send one generic value proposition to all roles.

### Avoid title-based overconfidence

Job titles vary by company size, region, industry, and maturity. A "head of operations" at one company may own tooling; at another, they may be far from budget. Treat titles as hypotheses and validate responsibilities.

Use profile evidence, company context, and prior interactions to improve confidence.

### Account for multi-person use cases

Many sales use cases cross departments. A product bought by sales may affect finance, operations, legal, IT, support, or end users. Identify who gains value, who carries implementation work, who bears risk, and who can block.

Messaging that speaks only to the visible buyer may miss the real blockers.

### Watch for unsupported or low-fit use cases

Some use cases sound attractive but require features, service levels, integrations, compliance, data volume, customization, or implementation help the organization cannot support. Do not target personas with a promise the product cannot fulfill.

Use targeting to prevent bad pipeline, not just create more meetings.

### Use evidence from market learning

Refine personas and use cases from won deals, lost deals, discovery notes, demo questions, implementation outcomes, support tickets, churn reasons, and expansion patterns. If a persona often engages but cannot mobilize buying action, adjust targeting.

Persona quality should improve with field evidence.

### Respect privacy and boundaries

Personalization should use relevant professional context, not invasive personal details. Avoid assumptions about identity, age, family, health, politics, or sensitive life events. Keep outreach based on business context and publicly appropriate signals.

### Prioritize personas by buying sequence and test message-market fit by persona

Some personas are best for opening a conversation, others for validating pain, building consensus, approving budget, or clearing risk. Decide the sequence: start with user pain, enter through an executive trigger, validate with technical owner, or mobilize through a champion.

Poor sequencing causes sellers to ask the wrong person for the wrong commitment.

Track whether each persona responds, accepts meetings, provides useful discovery, mobilizes others, or stalls. A persona with high reply rate but no buying influence may be useful for learning but weak for pipeline creation.

Use outcomes to adjust targeting instead of celebrating activity alone.

## Common Traps

- Treating a title as proof of authority or pain ownership.
- Confusing user persona, champion, economic buyer, and approver.
- Targeting a broad use case without workflow, trigger, or success outcome.
- Using the same message for end users, executives, technical buyers, and procurement.
- Ignoring departments that will implement, approve, or block the use case; building campaigns around use cases the product cannot support reliably
- Continuing to target personas who take meetings but never mobilize decisions; using intrusive or sensitive personal details for personalization
- Forgetting regional and industry differences in role responsibility; failing to capture field learning back into targeting rules
- Asking a user for executive commitment or asking an executive for workflow detail too early; overvaluing personas that reply often but cannot mobilize buying action

## Self-Check

- Are persona and buying role separated?
- Are user, champion, economic buyer, technical approver, legal, procurement, finance, security, and executive roles considered where relevant?
- Is the use case grounded in workflow, current process, friction, trigger, and success outcome?
- Does messaging match the role's actual stakes?
- Are title-based assumptions labeled and validated?
- Are cross-functional beneficiaries, implementers, risk owners, and blockers mapped?
- Are unsupported use cases excluded or narrowed?
- Does targeting reflect evidence from wins, losses, discovery, demos, implementation, support, churn, and expansion?
- Is personalization limited to appropriate business context?; will the targeting produce better-fit conversations rather than just more activity?
- Is persona sequencing aligned with opening, pain validation, technical review, budget approval, and consensus building?; are persona response and mobilization outcomes used to refine targeting?
