# Success Criteria And Outcome Definition

Success criteria turn interest into an evaluable decision. Without them, demos become feature tours, pilots drift, proposals overpromise, and post-sale teams inherit vague expectations. This skill helps the agent define outcomes the buyer can measure and the seller can responsibly support.

## Core Rules

### Define success in buyer terms

Ask what would make the buyer say the project worked: faster cycle time, fewer errors, more revenue, lower cost, better compliance, easier reporting, improved customer experience, reduced risk, or smoother adoption.

Do not substitute the seller's preferred metrics for the buyer's.

### Separate business outcome from product usage

Logging in, configuring a feature, or completing a pilot task may be necessary but not sufficient. Connect product use to the business result the buyer cares about.

Usage is evidence only if it leads to outcome.

### Make criteria observable

Criteria should be measurable or at least verifiable: baseline, target, time window, data source, owner, and acceptable evidence. If precise measurement is impossible, define qualitative evidence and decision owner.

Vague success creates evaluation conflict.

### Include stakeholder-specific criteria

Executives, users, IT, finance, procurement, legal, security, and customer success may judge success differently. Identify which criteria are must-haves, tradeoffs, and nice-to-haves.

One stakeholder's success can be another's risk.

### Check feasibility and control

Some outcomes depend on buyer adoption, data quality, process change, integration, or external conditions. Do not promise outcomes the seller cannot control. State assumptions and buyer responsibilities.

Responsible success criteria include shared accountability.

### Use criteria to shape evaluation and avoid vanity metrics

Success criteria should guide demos, proof of concept, business case, technical validation, procurement, and implementation plan. If evaluation activities do not test the criteria, they are noise.

Metrics that look good but do not influence decision or value can distract: number of features shown, survey excitement, meeting attendance, or raw activity. Choose criteria that matter to the buying decision.

### Carry criteria into handoff and define failure criteria too

Post-sale teams need the success definition, baseline, promises, risks, and buyer responsibilities. Do not let success criteria disappear after close.

Ask what would make the buyer judge the project unsuccessful: missed adoption target, unresolved integration, poor user experience, compliance risk, high support burden, slow time to value, or weak executive reporting. Failure criteria reveal hidden risk.

If failure criteria are not discussed, the seller may miss the buyer's real fear.

### Align criteria with evaluation length and guard against moving goalposts

Some outcomes cannot be proven in a short pilot or pre-sale evaluation. Decide which criteria can be tested now, which require implementation, and which should become post-sale success metrics.

Do not promise proof during evaluation if the data will not exist until later.

Document criteria before demos, pilots, and proposals. If criteria change, record who changed them and why. Moving criteria may indicate new stakeholders, hidden objections, or weak initial discovery.

Stable criteria make evaluation fair.

### Tie criteria to decision ownership and connect criteria to disqualification

Every important criterion should have an owner who can judge it. If nobody owns "ease of use," "ROI," "security," or "implementation success," the deal can stall because success is undefined in practice.

Ownerless criteria are conversation topics, not decision criteria.

Success criteria should also define when the seller should walk away or narrow scope. If required outcomes cannot be measured, cannot be influenced, or depend on unsupported capabilities, the opportunity may need disqualification or expectation reset.

Criteria are useful only if they can change the deal path.

## Common Traps

- Defining success as product usage rather than business outcome.
- Accepting vague goals like "better visibility" without evidence; ignoring baseline, target, owner, and time window
- Letting one stakeholder's criteria hide another's risk; promising outcomes dependent on buyer behavior without stating assumptions
- Running pilots or demos that do not test success criteria; using vanity metrics because they are easy to show
- Changing success criteria late to make the seller look good; failing to document criteria in CRM and handoff notes
- Closing without post-sale ownership of the promised outcome; defining success without asking what failure would look like
- Choosing pilot criteria that cannot be tested before purchase; letting criteria change without documenting the new stakeholder or reason
- Defining criteria that no stakeholder owns or can judge; treating success criteria as aspirational wishes that never affect qualification or scope

## Self-Check

- Is success defined in buyer language?
- Is the business outcome separated from product usage?
- Are baseline, target, time window, data source, owner, and evidence defined?
- Are stakeholder-specific criteria mapped?
- Are must-haves, tradeoffs, and nice-to-haves separated?
- Are assumptions and buyer responsibilities explicit?; do demos, pilots, business cases, and validation steps test the criteria?
- Are vanity metrics avoided?; are criteria documented for proposal and handoff?
- Can post-sale teams understand what outcome was sold?; are failure criteria and hidden buyer fears captured?
- Are criteria separated into pre-sale testable, implementation-dependent, and post-sale success metrics?; are changes to criteria documented with owner and reason?
- Does each important criterion have a stakeholder owner who can judge it?; do criteria identify when to advance, narrow scope, reset expectations, or disqualify?
