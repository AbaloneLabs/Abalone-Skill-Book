# Current Process Mapping

Current process mapping prevents sellers from solving an imaginary workflow. Buyers often describe desired future state, but the real opportunity is hidden in today's handoffs, rework, delays, tools, approvals, and exceptions. This skill helps the agent map the current process well enough to understand pain, value, and implementation implications.

## Core Rules

### Map the process from trigger to outcome

Identify what starts the workflow, each major step, who acts, what system is used, what decision occurs, what output is produced, and how completion is recognized. Include manual and informal steps.

The hidden spreadsheet or Slack approval may matter more than the official system.

### Identify handoffs and ownership

Most friction appears between teams. Map who hands work to whom, what information moves, what gets lost, and who owns resolution when something fails.

Handoffs reveal stakeholders and implementation risk.

### Capture exceptions and edge cases

Ask what happens when the normal path breaks: missing data, rush request, failed approval, customer complaint, high-value account, compliance issue, integration failure, or manual override.

Exceptions often drive the strongest pain.

### Quantify volume and frequency

Estimate how often the workflow happens, how many people participate, how long it takes, how often errors occur, and how much rework appears. Directional estimates are useful if exact numbers are unavailable.

Volume turns process maps into business cases.

### Note tools and data sources

List systems of record, spreadsheets, integrations, exports, emails, shared drives, reports, and manual data entry. Identify duplicate entry, stale data, access gaps, and data quality issues.

Tool reality shapes implementation feasibility.

### Identify controls and approvals and separate observed process from desired process

Compliance, security, finance, legal, management, and customer commitments may impose approval steps. Do not propose a faster flow that bypasses required controls.

Controls may be valuable even when painful.

Buyers may describe how the process should work. Ask what actually happens. Capture both current reality and desired future state, but do not blur them.

Implementation depends on reality.

### Turn map into sales implications and capture emotional and political friction

Use the map to decide demo focus, value hypothesis, stakeholders, integrations, migration needs, rollout risk, success metrics, and qualification.

Process mapping should change the sales plan.

Processes are not only steps. They include frustration, blame, ownership disputes, shadow work, fear of losing control, and teams that benefit from the current process. Capture these carefully and respectfully.

Political friction often determines whether a solution will be adopted.

### Identify what should not change and validate the map with participants

Some current-process elements are working well or required for compliance, quality, customer trust, or risk control. A sales recommendation should not imply that all current work is waste.

Knowing what to preserve makes the solution more credible.

A manager may describe the official process while frontline users know the real process. Validate the map with people who perform, approve, and receive the work.

Process maps are more reliable when checked from multiple roles.

### Mark where the seller's product would enter and capture baseline evidence before proposing change

After mapping the current process, identify which steps the product would replace, augment, automate, integrate with, or leave unchanged. This prevents the demo from implying a full process replacement when only part of the workflow is in scope.

It also surfaces integration, migration, and change-management questions early.

If the seller later claims improvement, the baseline must be known. Capture current time, cost, error rate, volume, rework, customer impact, or risk level before proposing future state. If the buyer lacks data, define how baseline will be estimated.

Baseline evidence supports credible value selling and post-sale success.

## Common Traps

- Mapping only the official process and missing informal workarounds.
- Starting with the seller's solution instead of the buyer's trigger-to-outcome flow; ignoring handoffs where work breaks
- Failing to ask about exceptions; accepting vague volume and time claims without ranges
- Missing spreadsheets, exports, and duplicate data entry; proposing efficiency that bypasses required approvals
- Mixing current process with desired future state; producing a map that does not affect deal strategy
- Not validating the map with the buyer; ignoring emotional, political, and ownership friction inside the process
- Treating every current step as waste instead of identifying controls worth preserving; mapping only the manager's view and missing frontline reality
- Failing to show where the proposed product enters, leaves, or changes the current workflow; proposing improvement without baseline evidence or a plan to estimate it

## Self-Check

- Is the process mapped from trigger to outcome?
- Are steps, actors, systems, decisions, outputs, and completion signals clear?
- Are informal workarounds included?
- Are handoffs, lost information, and ownership gaps identified?
- Are exceptions and edge cases captured?
- Are volume, frequency, time, error, and rework estimates included?; are tools, data sources, integrations, spreadsheets, exports, and duplicate entry documented?
- Are controls and approvals respected?; are current reality and desired future state separated?
- Does the map inform demo, value, stakeholders, integration, rollout, success metrics, and qualification?; are emotional and political friction points captured respectfully?
- Are valuable controls or steps to preserve identified?; has the map been validated with users, managers, approvers, or recipients where possible?
- Are product entry points, unchanged steps, integrations, migrations, and change-management implications marked?; are current baseline measures or baseline-estimation methods captured before proposing change?
