# Needs Analysis And Problem Framing

Needs analysis turns messy discovery into a clear problem the buyer can recognize and act on. Weak framing repeats the buyer's feature request, ignores root causes, or makes the seller's solution sound inevitable before the need is understood. This skill helps the agent frame needs around business outcomes, evidence, and buyer language.

## Core Rules

### Use buyer language first

Start with how the buyer described the issue. Preserve phrases that reveal urgency, frustration, stakes, or internal goals. Then translate into a structured problem statement.

If the problem statement sounds like vendor marketing, it may not be buyer-owned.

### Separate needs from wants

Wants are requested features, formats, or preferences. Needs are outcomes, constraints, risks, and jobs that must be satisfied. A buyer may want a dashboard because they need executive visibility or audit confidence.

Do not overbuild around surface wants.

### Identify the primary problem

Deals often contain many pains. Choose the problem that most strongly connects to urgency, value, and decision authority. Secondary problems can support the case but should not blur the message.

A crowded problem frame weakens action.

### Tie need to measurable outcome

Frame the need in terms of cycle time, cost, revenue, risk, accuracy, compliance, adoption, customer experience, or scalability. If measurement is unavailable, define what evidence would show improvement.

Needs without outcomes are hard to prioritize.

### Include constraints

Budget, timeline, integrations, data readiness, security, procurement, staffing, region, support expectations, and change capacity shape what solution is viable. A need analysis that ignores constraints becomes fantasy.

Constraints can be decision criteria, not obstacles.

### Check fit and scope and align stakeholders around the frame

Determine which needs the offer can satisfy, which require services, which require roadmap, and which are outside scope. Be explicit about unsupported needs.

Honest framing prevents bad promises.

Users, managers, executives, technical teams, and procurement may define the need differently. Identify disagreements and decide which version should anchor the sales process.

Misaligned needs create late-stage stalls.

### Convert frame into next action and validate the frame with multiple sources

A problem frame should guide the next step: demo focus, business case, technical validation, stakeholder meeting, proposal scope, or disqualification.

If no next action follows, the analysis is not operational.

When possible, confirm the problem frame with more than one stakeholder or evidence source. A user's frustration, a manager's metric, and an executive priority may tell different stories. If only one source exists, label the frame as provisional.

Multi-source validation reduces the risk of building the deal around one person's view.

### Avoid premature solution anchoring and include consequences of solving the wrong problem

Do not frame the need so narrowly that only the seller's product appears possible before criteria are agreed. A buyer-centered frame leaves room to compare alternatives honestly while still highlighting where the seller fits.

Premature anchoring may win agreement early and lose trust later.

If the team solves a symptom rather than root need, the buyer may spend budget, disrupt users, and still fail. Note what risk exists if the problem frame is wrong and what discovery would reduce that risk.

This keeps analysis honest.

### Keep the frame narrow enough to sell and make buyer tradeoffs explicit

A problem frame should be broad enough to matter but narrow enough to evaluate. If the frame becomes "transform the business," the seller cannot prove value or scope the deal. Narrow it to the operational problem the buyer can act on now.

Clear scope helps proposals, demos, and success plans stay credible.

Solving one need may require tradeoffs in budget, timeline, process change, integrations, user effort, governance, or vendor risk. A strong problem frame states which tradeoffs the buyer appears willing to consider and which are unacceptable.

This prevents the seller from proposing a solution the buyer will not operationally accept.

## Common Traps

- Rewriting the buyer's problem into vendor-centered language.
- Treating feature wants as needs; trying to solve every pain at once
- Framing needs without measurable outcomes; ignoring constraints that determine feasibility
- Hiding unsupported needs to preserve momentum; letting one stakeholder's framing dominate without checking others
- Creating a problem statement that does not drive next action; confusing symptoms with root needs
- Overstating certainty when discovery evidence is thin; building the frame from one stakeholder's opinion without labeling uncertainty
- Framing the problem to make the seller's solution inevitable before criteria are clear; ignoring the cost of solving the wrong problem
- Framing the need so broadly that no buyer can evaluate or own it; ignoring buyer tradeoffs and constraints that determine whether the need can be acted on

## Self-Check

- Does the problem frame preserve buyer language?
- Are wants separated from needs?
- Is the primary problem clear?
- Is the need tied to measurable or observable outcome?
- Are constraints included?
- Are supported, service-dependent, roadmap-dependent, and unsupported needs separated?; are stakeholder disagreements identified?
- Does the frame guide demo, business case, validation, stakeholder meeting, proposal, or disqualification?; are evidence gaps labeled?
- Would the buyer agree that this is their problem?; has the frame been validated across stakeholders or evidence sources where possible?
- Does the frame avoid premature solution anchoring?; are risks of a wrong problem frame and next validation steps identified?
- Is the problem frame narrow enough to scope, prove, and assign ownership?; are buyer tradeoffs around budget, timeline, process change, integrations, effort, governance, and risk explicit?
