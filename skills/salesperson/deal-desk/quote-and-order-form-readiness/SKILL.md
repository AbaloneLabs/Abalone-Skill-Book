# Quote And Order Form Readiness

A quote or order form is not a clerical artifact at the end of a sale. It converts the sales conversation into the commercial record that finance, legal, billing, implementation, customer success, and the customer will rely on. Agents often check whether the price looks right and miss entity details, scope boundaries, product configuration, discount authority, billing terms, service commitments, renewal language, and dependencies that can create booking defects or post-sale disputes. This skill helps the agent prepare quote materials that are commercially accurate and operationally usable.

Use this skill when drafting, reviewing, or summarizing quotes, order forms, SKU configurations, subscription terms, service packages, add-ons, discounts, billing schedules, renewal terms, signature packets, or customer-facing commercial summaries. The agent should not invent approved terms; it should identify readiness gaps and route exceptions.

## Core Rules

### Reconcile the quote with the deal narrative

Before sending or reviewing a quote, compare it against the discovery record, proposal, buyer requirements, approved pricing, legal terms, implementation assumptions, and close plan. The quote should reflect what was actually sold, not only what the CPQ tool allowed.

Watch for silent drift: a product tier that omits a needed feature, an add-on discussed but missing, a service package assumed but not priced, a user count that changed, or a billing term that contradicts the buyer's procurement expectation. The commercial document should not surprise the buyer or internal teams.

### Verify customer entity and signer details

Correct legal entity, billing entity, address, tax treatment, purchase order requirement, signature authority, parent or subsidiary relationship, reseller or partner involvement, and currency matter. A quote to the wrong entity can delay procurement, invoicing, tax handling, and revenue recognition.

Do not assume the logo name is the contracting party. Enterprise groups, franchises, government agencies, nonprofits, and international buyers often have complex entities. If the buyer cannot confirm the entity, treat that as a readiness gap.

### Check product configuration and scope boundaries

Validate SKUs, plan tier, modules, seats, usage limits, regions, environments, support level, implementation services, training, integrations, data volume, and start date. If the buyer's desired outcome depends on something not included, flag it before signature.

Scope boundaries should be visible. If custom work, migration, premium support, security feature, service level, or onboarding help is excluded, say so clearly using approved language. Ambiguous scope is a future escalation.

### Tie discounts and concessions to approval

Discounts, free months, ramp pricing, special payment terms, renewal caps, cancellation rights, service credits, and bundled concessions need authority. Confirm approval source, expiration, conditions, and whether the concession is reflected correctly in the quote.

Do not treat a verbal manager comment as final approval if policy requires deal desk, finance, legal, or executive signoff. A concession without traceability can become a booking defect and a precedent problem.

### Align billing and service timing

Commercial documents should make timing clear: contract start, service start, billing start, invoice schedule, payment terms, implementation timeline, renewal date, ramp periods, free trial conversion, and cancellation window. Misaligned timing creates customer disputes and finance rework.

If service starts before billing, or billing starts before implementation readiness, make the decision explicit and approved. Avoid leaving timing assumptions buried in emails.

### Watch for revenue and booking blockers

The seller does not need to be a revenue accountant, but should recognize booking readiness risks: nonstandard terms, contingent acceptance, cancellation rights, side letters, future deliverables, customer-specific obligations, missing PO, unsigned order form, inconsistent dates, unsupported entity, or unresolved legal redline.

If a term could affect booking or revenue recognition, route to deal desk, finance, or legal. Do not promise a close date until blockers are known.

### Make customer-facing summaries match the document

Buyers often rely on a summary email more than the order form. Ensure summaries do not create extra commitments or simplify away conditions. If the summary says "includes onboarding," the order form should specify what onboarding means.

Use the summary to highlight key terms, not to renegotiate them casually. If the buyer asks for a change after the quote is issued, update the document rather than relying on side email language.

### Prepare for clean handoff after signature

Quote readiness includes downstream usability. Capture what was sold, why, key terms, billing details, implementation assumptions, support level, customer contacts, purchase order status, and any nonstandard approval. Customer success and finance should not have to reconstruct the deal from scattered messages.

If the quote includes unusual timing or commitments, make those visible before the deal is booked.

## Common Traps

- Assuming the quote is correct because it was generated by a system.
- Sending a quote that does not match the proposal, demo promise, or buyer's required outcome.
- Using the brand name instead of verifying the contracting and billing entity.
- Hiding excluded services or limitations until after signature.
- Treating discounts, free months, or payment terms as approved without traceable authority.
- Leaving start date, billing date, service date, or renewal date ambiguous.
- Creating side-email commitments that differ from the order form.
- Forecasting signature while booking blockers such as PO, legal terms, entity, or acceptance conditions remain unresolved.

## Self-Check

- Does the quote match discovery, proposal, approved pricing, scope, and implementation assumptions?
- Are legal entity, billing entity, address, tax, currency, PO, and signer details confirmed?
- Are SKUs, modules, seats, usage limits, support, services, regions, and start dates correct?
- Are exclusions and limitations visible enough to prevent customer confusion?
- Are discounts, concessions, and nonstandard terms tied to traceable approval?
- Are contract start, service start, billing schedule, renewal date, and payment terms aligned?
- Could any term create booking, revenue recognition, finance, or legal review risk?
- Does the customer-facing summary match the actual commercial document?
- Are late buyer changes reflected in the quote rather than side communication only?
- Would finance, billing, implementation, and customer success understand what was sold from the handoff record?
