# Booked Deal Quality And Revenue Handoff

Closed-won is not the end of operational responsibility. A deal that is signed but unclear can fail in booking, billing, implementation, customer success, or renewal. Agents often celebrate signature and move on before checking whether documents, approvals, terms, customer data, and promises are clean enough for revenue handoff. This skill helps the agent inspect the quality of a booked deal so the company can fulfill and bill what was sold.

Use this skill after signature, during booking review, when resolving booking defects, preparing revenue operations handoff, or checking closed-won CRM quality. The agent should make commercial truth easy for downstream teams to use.

## Core Rules

### Treat booking as a quality gate

Booking is not just uploading a signed document. It is the point where the company confirms that commercial terms are complete, authorized, and operationally executable. Check signature, entity, dates, product, term, price, discount, billing schedule, payment terms, purchase order, approvals, and required documents.

If the booking package has defects, fix them before downstream teams build on bad data. A rushed booking can create invoice disputes, revenue recognition issues, delayed access, or customer trust damage.

### Reconcile signed documents with systems

Compare the signed order form, contract, quote, CRM opportunity, approval records, billing system, provisioning request, and handoff notes. They should tell the same story. Watch for mismatched dates, product names, SKU counts, discount amounts, renewal terms, billing frequency, currency, legal entity, or service commitments.

Do not assume the signed PDF is correct if systems disagree. Find the authoritative source and route discrepancies to revenue operations, finance, legal, or deal desk.

### Surface nonstandard obligations

Closed-won deals often include obligations that matter after signature: special support, onboarding, implementation milestones, reporting, security commitments, renewal caps, price holds, service credits, notice periods, data restrictions, cancellation rights, or customer-specific billing. These must be visible to the teams that will perform or monitor them.

Do not bury nonstandard obligations in contract attachments or side emails. Summarize them in approved handoff fields with owner and due date where relevant.

### Confirm billing and collection readiness

Revenue quality depends on practical billing details: bill-to entity, invoice contact, PO number if required, tax information, payment method, billing frequency, start date, payment terms, portal submission requirements, and collections risk. A signature without billing readiness can delay cash and frustrate customers.

If a PO is pending or customer billing setup is incomplete, record whether service can begin, invoice can be issued, and who owns follow-up. Avoid assuming finance will solve missing details without context.

### Preserve implementation and provisioning context

Provisioning teams need to know what to activate, for whom, when, and under what constraints. Implementation teams need scope, start conditions, customer contacts, purchased services, success criteria, dependencies, integrations, and any sales commitments.

The handoff should not be a sales narrative; it should be operationally specific. If a buyer was promised a timeline, migration help, or configuration, the performing team must have accepted or reviewed it.

### Handle booking defects as process signals

Common defects include missing signatures, wrong entity, unapproved discount, mismatched order form, missing PO, inconsistent term, incomplete product configuration, undocumented side letter, invalid tax data, or unclear billing start. Track defects by source and root cause.

Do not treat defects as isolated admin mistakes if they repeat. They may indicate CPQ rules, sales training, approval workflow, proposal templates, or close process need improvement.

### Protect customer communication during handoff

If booking or provisioning is delayed, decide who tells the customer and what can be said. Avoid letting the customer hear conflicting messages from sales, finance, implementation, and support. If the delay is due to internal defect, communicate with ownership and a realistic next update.

Do not blame internal teams or expose unnecessary internal process. The customer needs a clear path, not organizational complexity.

### Close the sales loop with evidence

Before moving on, confirm the deal is booked, invoicing path is clear, provisioning or implementation is owned, CRM is accurate, approvals are attached, and customer next step is known. If any item is pending, the seller should know whether they still own it.

Closed-won quality should be inspected, not assumed. The best late-stage selling includes clean transfer of commercial truth.

## Common Traps

- Treating signature as completion even though booking data, approvals, or billing details are incomplete.
- Letting CRM, quote, order form, and approval records contradict each other.
- Hiding nonstandard obligations in side emails or contract attachments no one reads.
- Starting implementation with unclear scope or unaccepted sales promises.
- Assuming a missing PO, invoice contact, or tax detail is finance's problem without owner or timeline.
- Fixing booking defects one at a time without identifying repeated process causes.
- Letting customers receive different updates from sales, finance, and implementation.
- Marking the opportunity complete while downstream teams still lack the facts needed to execute.

## Self-Check

- Are signature, entity, dates, product, term, price, discount, billing, payment, PO, and approvals complete?
- Do signed documents, quote, CRM, approval records, billing data, and handoff notes match?
- Are discrepancies routed to the correct owner before booking assumptions are made?
- Are nonstandard obligations summarized with owner, scope, and timing?
- Are bill-to entity, invoice contact, PO, tax, payment method, billing frequency, and portal requirements known?
- Is service start or provisioning allowed if billing details are still pending?
- Does implementation or provisioning have scope, contacts, dependencies, timeline, and accepted commitments?
- Are booking defects tracked for root cause and process improvement?
- Is customer communication about delays owned and consistent?
- Can downstream teams fulfill and bill the deal without reconstructing commercial truth from scattered messages?
