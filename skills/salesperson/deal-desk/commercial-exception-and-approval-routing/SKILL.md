# Commercial Exception And Approval Routing

Commercial exceptions are not just ways to save a deal. They decide which risks and economics the business will accept outside standard policy. Agents often frame exceptions as "customer needs this" or "we need it to close," which gives approvers too little context and hides tradeoffs. This skill helps the agent prepare exception requests that are clear, evidence-based, and routed to the right authority.

Use this skill when handling discounts, payment terms, billing changes, free months, service credits, renewal caps, termination rights, custom support, implementation concessions, usage overages, nonstandard packages, side letters, or any commercial deviation from standard policy. The agent should make the business case visible without implying approval.

## Core Rules

### Identify the standard and the deviation

Start by stating the normal policy, pricing, term, package, support level, or approval path. Then state exactly what the customer requests and how it differs. An approver cannot evaluate an exception if the baseline is unclear.

Avoid vague labels such as "special pricing" or "flexibility." Name the actual change: discount percentage, dollar amount, payment delay, cancellation right, free service, cap, usage allowance, service level, or contract term. Precision prevents accidental approval of more than intended.

### Explain the business rationale and buyer evidence

A strong exception request explains why the deviation is commercially reasonable: strategic logo, competitive displacement, multi-year term, volume commitment, expansion path, payment acceleration, reference value, renewal protection, scope reduction, implementation readiness, or documented procurement constraint. Tie the rationale to buyer evidence, not seller hope.

If the rationale is only forecast pressure or end-of-quarter timing, say so honestly but expect scrutiny. Internal urgency is not the same as customer value.

### Show what the company receives in exchange

Concessions should usually trade for buyer movement: faster signature, longer term, larger scope, upfront payment, reduced services, reference rights, case study permission, cleaner legal terms, implementation commitment, or confirmed renewal path. A concession with no reciprocal value teaches the buyer to ask again.

If there is no trade, identify why the business should still accept the exception. The reason may be service recovery, contractual obligation, competitive necessity, or executive decision, but it should be explicit.

### Route to all affected owners

Commercial exceptions can affect finance, legal, revenue operations, billing, implementation, customer success, support, product, security, and leadership. A discount approval may not approve a nonstandard service promise. A legal approval may not approve margin impact. A finance approval may not approve delivery feasibility.

Map affected owners before presenting the exception as ready. The right approval path depends on deal size, risk type, customer segment, geography, product line, term length, and precedent.

### Make risk and precedent visible

Approvers need to know whether the exception is one-time, segment-specific, customer-specific, or likely to become a precedent. Consider margin, support load, service capacity, billing complexity, renewal risk, revenue recognition, customer fairness, partner conflict, and future negotiation behavior.

Do not hide precedent risk because the current deal is important. If the exception is approved, record the reason and limits so future teams do not treat it as standard.

### Avoid approval theater

Approval theater happens when a seller collects signatures after already promising the exception externally. That puts approvers under pressure and undermines controls. The agent should distinguish "requested," "recommended," "approved," and "communicated to customer."

Do not tell the buyer an exception is available until the approval is complete or until language clearly states that it is pending approval. If the buyer needs a tentative path, phrase it carefully.

### Keep exception records booking-ready

Approved exceptions should be reflected in the quote, order form, CRM, approval system, and handoff notes. Include approver, date, scope, expiration, conditions, and customer-facing wording. If the approval is conditional, track whether the condition was satisfied.

Unrecorded exceptions create disputes and handoff failures. A future renewal owner needs to know whether a concession was temporary or permanent.

### Revisit patterns, not only single requests

Repeated exceptions may indicate mispriced packaging, unclear policy, poor qualification, competitive pressure, product gaps, procurement mismatch, or sales habit. Track patterns by rep, segment, competitor, product, region, and deal size.

If every deal needs an exception, the policy or positioning needs review. Do not normalize workaround volume as standard execution.

## Common Traps

- Asking for approval without stating the standard position and exact deviation.
- Using "customer asked" or "deal will slip" as the whole business rationale.
- Giving discounts or terms without reciprocal buyer commitment.
- Getting approval from one owner while ignoring finance, legal, billing, delivery, or support impact.
- Promising the exception to the buyer before approval is complete.
- Treating a one-time concession as reusable precedent.
- Recording approval in chat or email but not in the commercial system or handoff.
- Failing to notice repeated exception patterns that reveal pricing, product, or process problems.

## Self-Check

- Is the standard policy or term clear before the requested exception is described?
- Is the exact deviation quantified or specified precisely?
- Is the business rationale supported by buyer evidence rather than seller hope alone?
- Does the request show what the company receives in exchange?
- Are all affected owners and approval thresholds identified?
- Are margin, booking, legal, billing, delivery, support, renewal, and precedent risks visible?
- Has the buyer not been promised the exception before approval?
- Are approval source, scope, expiration, conditions, and customer-facing wording recorded?
- Is the exception reflected consistently in quote, order form, CRM, and handoff notes?
- Are repeated exceptions tracked as potential policy, pricing, product, or qualification signals?
