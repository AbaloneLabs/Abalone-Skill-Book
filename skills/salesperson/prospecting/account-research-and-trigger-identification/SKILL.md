# Account Research And Trigger Identification

Account research is not collecting trivia. The useful question is whether something in the account's world makes the buyer more likely to care, act, and accept a relevant conversation now. Weak research produces flattering but irrelevant outreach. This skill helps the agent identify triggers that connect to pain, timing, and a plausible next conversation.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish trigger from background fact

A trigger is a change or condition that may create urgency: growth, leadership change, funding, hiring, new market, compliance pressure, merger, product launch, cost pressure, technology change, incident, public goal, or competitor movement. A static fact is useful only if it supports a specific hypothesis.

Do not treat any recent news as a trigger.

### Link trigger to a business problem

State how the trigger could create pain, risk, cost, opportunity, or operational complexity that the offering can address. The link should be plausible and role-specific.

If the link is weak, use the research for context rather than outreach personalization.

### Validate relevance to the target persona

The trigger should matter to the person being contacted. A funding announcement may matter to finance, hiring may matter to operations, a security incident may matter to IT, and expansion may matter to customer support or logistics.

Avoid sending the same trigger to every title.

### Check recency and source quality

Use reliable sources and note dates. Old news, duplicated press releases, scraped technology data, or vague intent signals can mislead. Confirm whether the event actually happened and whether it is still relevant.

Outreach based on stale triggers damages credibility.

### Avoid creepy or sensitive personalization

Use professional, publicly appropriate information. Avoid personal life events, health, family, politics, layoffs in a tone-deaf way, tragedies, or sensitive employee data. If a trigger involves harm, use restraint.

Personalization should show relevance, not surveillance.

### Form a testable hypothesis

Turn research into a hypothesis: "This expansion may make onboarding new field teams harder" or "This hiring plan may increase compliance workload." A hypothesis invites conversation without pretending certainty.

Do not assert internal pain as fact before discovery.

### Prioritize by actionability

The best triggers suggest a next question, helpful insight, or reason for the buyer to compare current process. If no next conversation follows, the research may not be worth using.

Actionability matters more than novelty.

### Record research for reuse and look for disqualifying research too

Store source, date, trigger, hypothesis, persona relevance, and confidence in CRM or prospecting notes. Future sellers should know what was inferred and what was confirmed.

Research should reveal reasons not to contact: hiring freeze, bankruptcy, recent layoff sensitivity, incompatible industry, no relevant use case, existing customer conflict, active support issue, competitor lock-in, public procurement constraint, or region where the product is unavailable.

Good research improves selectivity, not only personalization.

### Avoid overfitting to one source and convert research into a first question

One press release, intent spike, job posting, or profile change can be misleading. Look for corroborating evidence when the outreach claim is important. If a claim is based on a single weak signal, keep the language soft.

Confidence should shape the strength of the message.

Research is only useful if it creates a better first question. For each trigger, define what the seller should ask to validate the hypothesis. A question such as "How are you handling onboarding after the hiring push?" is stronger than a statement that assumes pain.

If no respectful first question follows from the research, do not use it in outreach.

## Common Traps

- Using company trivia as personalization without business relevance.
- Treating any news article as proof of urgency.
- Claiming the buyer has a problem based only on an external trigger.
- Sending a trigger to a persona who has no reason to care; using stale or low-quality data sources
- Referencing layoffs, incidents, or sensitive events in a self-serving way; sounding like the seller knows internal facts they do not know
- Researching endlessly without a clear outreach hypothesis; ignoring negative triggers that suggest bad timing or poor fit
- Failing to document source, date, and confidence; researching only reasons to contact and missing reasons to pause or disqualify
- Building a strong claim from one weak or ambiguous source; collecting research that never turns into a useful buyer question

## Self-Check

- Is the trigger a meaningful change or condition rather than trivia?
- Is the trigger linked to a plausible business problem, risk, cost, or opportunity?
- Is relevance specific to the target persona?
- Are source, date, and reliability checked?
- Is the personalization professionally appropriate and not invasive?
- Is the pain framed as a hypothesis, not a fact?
- Does the trigger suggest a useful question or next conversation?
- Are weak or stale triggers excluded from outreach?; are negative timing or fit signals considered?
- Is research documented with source, date, hypothesis, persona relevance, and confidence?; did research include disqualifying or pause signals?
- Is the outreach claim strength matched to source quality and corroboration?; does each trigger produce a respectful first question that validates the hypothesis?
