# Payment Terms And Credit Risk

Payment terms are not a harmless contract detail. They affect cash, collections, credit exposure, revenue operations, buyer commitment, and renewal expectations. Agents often use payment flexibility to save deals without understanding finance risk. This skill helps the agent discuss payment terms with commercial discipline and buyer clarity.

## Core Rules

### Identify the payment request

Clarify whether the buyer is asking for net terms, installments, delayed start, annual prepay change, milestone billing, PO dependency, credit card, wire transfer, local payment method, or special invoicing. Different requests create different risk.

### Separate affordability from cash timing and involve finance before promising

A buyer may value the solution but need payment timing flexibility, or may use payment terms to mask weak budget. Diagnose which issue exists before offering concessions.

Credit review, collections policy, revenue recognition, billing operations, tax, and risk approval may apply. Sales should not imply approval for non-standard payment terms before finance accepts them.

### Check buyer payment process and confirm who can approve payment terms

Understand PO requirement, vendor setup, invoice portal, approver, billing contact, payment cycle, tax documentation, and required invoice fields. A signed contract can still become a collections problem.

The person negotiating the purchase may not control payment policy. Identify finance, procurement, accounts payable, legal, and executive approvers for non-standard terms. A champion's acceptance is not enough if AP or finance will reject the invoice.

### Distinguish contractual term from operational practice and review impact on service access

A contract may say net 30 while the buyer's actual payment process takes longer because of PO, portal, batch payment, public-sector cycle, or approval queue. Forecast cash using operational reality, not only contract wording.

Payment terms may affect when service starts, whether implementation begins before payment, and what happens if payment is late. Align buyer expectation, contract language, and internal policy before close.

### Consider collections relationship tone and check reseller and marketplace payment paths

Aggressive collections after a friendly sale can damage trust if expectations were unclear. Sales should ensure the buyer understands payment obligations before finance has to enforce them.

If a partner, reseller, distributor, or marketplace bills the customer, credit and collections risk may shift but not disappear. Understand who invoices, collects, remits, and handles disputes.

### Model downside before approving and keep buyer-facing wording consistent

For large or unusual terms, ask what happens if the buyer pays late, disputes the invoice, downsizes, or fails. Consider cash exposure, service access, implementation cost already incurred, and whether the contract gives practical remedies. Downside modeling keeps payment flexibility from becoming hidden financing.

Payment language in email, quote, order form, contract, and invoice instructions should match. If the seller casually says "you can pay later" while the order form says otherwise, the buyer will remember the convenient version.

### Evaluate credit risk and trade terms for commitment

Consider company maturity, funding status, prior payment history, contract size, geography, public filings, reseller route, and unusual term requests. High credit risk may require prepay, shorter term, or credit approval.

If payment flexibility is granted, seek meaningful trade: longer term, upfront partial payment, faster signature, reduced scope, automatic payment method, or clear PO date. One-way payment concessions create risk.

### Make conditions explicit and align with procurement and billing reality

Document due dates, invoice timing, late payment handling, service start, suspension rights, tax responsibility, and conditions for discounts or payment plans. Ambiguity becomes collections conflict.

Buyer procurement may agree to terms that billing cannot execute, or billing may require details the buyer has not provided. Confirm operational feasibility before close.

### Preserve renewal implications and handoff collections context

Special payment terms can become expected at renewal. Document whether they are one-time, conditional, ramped, or standard.

Revenue operations, finance, customer success, and account owner should know payment conditions, billing contacts, risk, and buyer promises.

## Common Traps

- Treating payment terms as easy concessions.
- Confusing budget issue with cash timing; promising non-standard terms before finance approval
- Ignoring PO, vendor setup, and invoice portal details; failing to assess credit risk
- Giving payment flexibility without buyer commitment; leaving due dates and conditions vague
- Closing terms billing cannot execute; forgetting renewal precedent
- Hiding collections risk from post-sale teams; assuming signature means cash will arrive
- Using payment flexibility to avoid value discussion; accepting payment terms from someone without finance authority; forecasting cash from contract wording while ignoring operational payment practice; starting service before payment expectations and policy are aligned
- Creating collections friction through unclear sales communication; assuming partner or marketplace path removes all collections risk; approving payment flexibility without downside modeling; letting casual payment wording diverge from contract terms

## Self-Check

- Is the payment request clearly classified?
- Is affordability separated from cash timing?; has finance approved non-standard terms?
- Is buyer payment process understood?; is credit risk assessed?
- Is there a meaningful give-get for payment flexibility?; are conditions explicit?
- Can billing execute the terms?; are renewal implications documented?
- Is collections context handed off?; would finance understand and accept the risk?
- Can the deal close without creating avoidable collections surprise?; are payment-term approvers identified?
- Is operational payment practice understood, not only contract language?; is service access aligned to payment terms?
- Is collections tone protected by clear buyer expectations?; are reseller or marketplace payment paths understood?
- Is downside modeled for late payment, dispute, or non-payment?; is buyer-facing payment wording consistent across artifacts?
