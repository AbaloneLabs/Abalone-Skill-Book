# Deal Close Without Collections Surprise

Closed-won is not the same as collectible. A deal can be signed and still fail operationally if the buyer cannot issue a PO, rejects the invoice, disputes terms, delays vendor setup, or expected different billing. Agents often optimize for signature and leave finance to resolve cash friction. This skill helps the agent inspect collections readiness before close.

## Core Rules

### Confirm payment path before signature

Know how the buyer will pay, who approves, what documents are required, whether PO is needed, and when payment can be made. Do not wait until after signature to discover the path.

### Check invoice acceptance conditions and identify collections risk signals

Buyers may reject invoices without PO, correct entity, tax ID, line-item detail, portal submission, milestone proof, or approved vendor status. Confirm requirements early.

Signals include unusual term requests, funding uncertainty, startup cash issues, public-sector reimbursement, international payment complexity, prior late payments, or disagreement about start date. Escalate where needed.

### Align commercial terms with billing execution and confirm buyer internal handoff

Discounts, ramps, delayed start, milestone billing, usage tiers, services fees, and partner billing must be executable by finance systems. If systems cannot support the deal cleanly, revise terms before close.

The buyer's business sponsor may need to hand off the purchase to procurement, AP, finance, or a shared services center. Ask whether those teams have the documents and context they need. Collections surprise often starts inside the buyer organization.

### Test first-invoice scenario and treat disputes as pre-close risks

Walk through what happens after signature: who receives the invoice, what they check, what could cause rejection, when payment is scheduled, and who resolves questions. This scenario exposes gaps before they become overdue invoices.

If the buyer is unclear about tax, start date, services, usage, contract term, or cancellation, that is a future invoice dispute. Resolve before close rather than assuming finance can explain later.

### Align collections escalation with relationship owner and avoid hiding payment risk to protect commit

If payment is late, know whether finance, account owner, customer success, or executive sponsor contacts the buyer. Misaligned collections escalation can damage customer trust.

If payment path is weak, forecast and leadership should know. Hiding collections risk makes the deal look better than it is and shifts burden to finance.

### Check whether implementation costs precede collection and confirm customer-side invoice owner before close

If services, onboarding, partner work, or custom setup begin before payment, the company may incur cost before cash is secured. Make that exposure visible and approved. Collections risk is higher when delivery cost is front-loaded.

Ask who will confirm invoice accuracy and who can unblock payment if AP has questions. A billing contact alone may not have authority to resolve disputes.

### Clarify service start versus payment and communicate payment expectations to buyer

If service begins before payment, know policy, risk, and suspension rights. Do not create unapproved free service through billing delay.

Use clear language for invoice timing, payment due date, PO needs, tax, and billing contact. Avoid surprising the buyer with process after signature.

### Involve finance in risky deals and preserve customer trust

Finance should review high-value, non-standard, international, credit-risk, public-sector, partner, or delayed-payment deals. Collections risk is a commercial issue.

Collections conversations can damage relationships if sales promised one thing and finance executes another. Ensure buyer-facing expectations match internal process.

### Document risk and owner and learn from payment friction

Record payment path, open issues, owner, due dates, special terms, and escalation path in CRM and handoff. Collections surprise often comes from missing ownership.

Repeated collections issues may indicate poor qualification, weak order forms, bad routing, unclear billing handoff, or unpriced payment flexibility. Feed learning back.

## Common Traps

- Treating signed contract as collectible revenue.
- Discovering PO or vendor setup after close; missing invoice rejection conditions
- Ignoring credit and cash risk signals; creating terms finance cannot execute
- Starting service before payment policy is clear; failing to tell buyer what billing will require
- Keeping finance out of risky deals; letting sales and finance messages diverge
- Leaving payment risks ownerless; repeating collections issues without process fixes
- Treating collections as unrelated to sales quality; assuming the buyer sponsor has informed AP or procurement; failing to test the first-invoice scenario; treating likely invoice disputes as post-close issues
- Letting collections escalation surprise the relationship owner; hiding payment risk to preserve forecast commit; ignoring implementation cost incurred before collection; lacking a customer-side owner for invoice disputes

## Self-Check

- Is payment path confirmed before signature?
- Are invoice acceptance conditions known?; are collections risk signals identified?
- Can finance execute commercial terms?; is service start versus payment policy clear?
- Are payment expectations communicated to buyer?; is finance involved where risk is meaningful?
- Do sales and finance messages match?; are risk, owner, due date, and escalation documented?
- Are collections lessons fed back?; would the deal be collectible without heroic follow-up?
- Does close quality include cash readiness?; has buyer internal payment handoff been confirmed?
- Has first-invoice scenario been tested?; are potential invoice disputes resolved before close?
- Is collections escalation aligned with relationship owner?; is payment risk visible in forecast where relevant?
- Are pre-collection implementation costs visible and approved?; is customer-side invoice dispute owner identified?
