# Lead Scoring And Routing

Lead scoring and routing decide who receives attention and how fast. Bad scoring rewards noisy behavior, stale data, or vanity signals while good-fit buyers wait. Bad routing creates duplicate outreach, ownership conflict, and poor first response. This skill helps the agent interpret lead signals with fit, intent, timing, and ownership discipline.

## Core Rules

### Separate fit score from intent score

Fit reflects whether the account matches ICP, persona, use case, segment, region, and implementation potential. Intent reflects current behavior or timing. A high-intent bad-fit lead and a high-fit low-intent account need different motions.

Do not collapse all signals into one unexplained score.

### Check signal quality

Form fills, page visits, content downloads, webinar attendance, review-site activity, ad clicks, chat, email engagement, and third-party intent data vary in strength. Some signals are casual research; others indicate active buying.

Score based on behavior meaning, not just volume.

### Use recency and sequence

Recent repeated behavior across relevant pages or stakeholders is stronger than old isolated activity. Sequence matters: pricing page after technical docs may mean different intent than a top-of-funnel ebook download.

Stale activity should decay.

### Respect ownership and territory rules

Routing should account for account owner, territory, segment, partner, named account, reseller, existing customer, open opportunity, and customer success ownership. Incorrect routing creates customer confusion and internal conflict.

Search before assigning.

### Avoid over-automation

Automated routing can misread students, competitors, vendors, job seekers, consultants, investors, partners, or existing customers. Include exception checks for non-buyers and sensitive accounts.

Automation should accelerate good judgment, not replace it.

### Match speed to signal and document routing rationale

High-fit high-intent leads deserve fast response. Low-fit or low-intent leads may need nurture, self-serve, partner route, or disqualification. Speed matters, but so does relevance.

Fast irrelevant outreach wastes buyer attention.

Record why the lead went to a seller, nurture, partner, support, customer success, or disqualification. Include key score drivers and uncertainties.

This helps teams improve scoring models.

### Monitor false positives and false negatives and protect buyer experience during routing

Review leads that scored high but did not convert, and leads that scored low but became good opportunities. Adjust criteria based on evidence.

Lead scoring should learn from outcomes.

Routing should prevent duplicate touches, irrelevant handoffs, slow responses, and repeated discovery questions. If multiple teams may contact the buyer, choose one owner and share context.

The buyer should experience one coordinated company, not a routing experiment.

### Handle urgent and sensitive leads separately and audit routing speed and quality together

Some leads involve security incidents, legal requests, support issues, partnership inquiries, press, investors, procurement portals, or existing customer complaints. These should not be routed as ordinary sales opportunities.

Lead systems need exception paths for non-sales intent.

Fast routing is not useful if the wrong owner receives the lead or context is missing. Review response time together with acceptance rate, meeting quality, conversion, duplicate touches, reassignment, and buyer complaints.

Routing should improve both speed and relevance.

### Keep scoring explainable to sellers and govern manual overrides

If sellers cannot understand why a lead scored high or low, they will either ignore the score or follow it blindly. Show the main drivers, missing data, decay rules, and routing reason.

Explainability helps reps challenge bad data and trust useful signals.

Sales teams sometimes override routing because of executive interest, partner context, strategic account status, data error, or seller judgment. Overrides can be useful, but they need reason, owner, and review. Otherwise the scoring model becomes optional whenever someone wants a lead.

Track override outcomes so the model can improve or the override habit can be corrected.

## Common Traps

- Treating fit and intent as one opaque score.
- Overweighting clicks, opens, or downloads without context; ignoring recency decay
- Routing existing customers or open opportunities to the wrong seller; creating duplicate outreach from sales, partner, and customer success
- Letting automation route competitors, students, job seekers, or vendors as buyers; responding quickly with irrelevant messaging
- Sending low-fit leads to account executives to inflate activity; failing to document why a lead was routed
- Not reviewing false positives and false negatives; letting multiple sellers or teams contact the same buyer because routing ownership is unclear
- Sending support, legal, security, partner, press, investor, or complaint inquiries into normal sales cadence; celebrating fast routing while reassignment, duplicates, or low-quality first touches increase
- Using opaque lead scores that sellers cannot interpret or improve; allowing manual routing overrides without reason, owner, or outcome review

## Self-Check

- Are fit and intent scored separately?
- Are ICP, persona, use case, region, segment, and implementation fit considered?
- Are behavior signals interpreted by meaning, not only count?
- Are recency, sequence, and multi-stakeholder activity included?
- Are ownership, territory, named account, partner, reseller, customer success, and open opportunity rules checked?
- Are non-buyer and sensitive-account exceptions handled?; does response speed match signal quality and fit?
- Is the routing path appropriate: seller, nurture, partner, support, success, self-serve, or disqualify?; is routing rationale documented?
- Are scoring criteria updated from conversion and missed-opportunity evidence?; does routing create a coordinated buyer experience with one clear owner?
- Are urgent, sensitive, and non-sales intents routed through exception paths?; are routing speed and routing quality reviewed together?
- Are score drivers, missing data, decay rules, and routing reasons visible to the seller?; are manual overrides documented and reviewed against outcomes?
