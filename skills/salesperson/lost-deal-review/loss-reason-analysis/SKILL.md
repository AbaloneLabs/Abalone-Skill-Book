# Loss Reason Analysis

Loss reasons are often written too quickly, too defensively, or too neatly. A seller may mark price, competitor, timing, or no budget because those labels are familiar, acceptable, and easy to enter in CRM. The real cause may involve weak discovery, missing authority, poor differentiation, implementation fear, procurement timing, champion failure, bad fit, or a buyer who never intended to change. This skill helps the agent analyze a lost opportunity as evidence, not as a personal verdict or a cosmetic field update.

Use this skill when reviewing a closed-lost deal, drafting loss notes, preparing manager inspection, summarizing buyer feedback, comparing win-loss patterns, or deciding what the team should change. The agent should seek truth that can improve future work, while avoiding blame, invented certainty, or overgeneralization from one deal.

## Core Rules

### Separate label, story, and evidence

Treat the CRM loss reason as a starting label, not the conclusion. Separate the official label, the seller's narrative, buyer-stated feedback, observable behavior, timeline evidence, competitive facts, pricing history, and internal process gaps. A strong analysis shows how the evidence supports the reason and where uncertainty remains.

For example, "lost on price" may mean the buyer truly chose a cheaper vendor, lacked budget for the category, used price to avoid explaining low urgency, rejected the value case, or compared different scopes. "Lost to competitor" may mean the competitor was preferred, already embedded, better aligned to criteria, lower risk, or simply the only vendor with executive sponsorship. The agent should not let broad labels hide the mechanism.

### Reconstruct the deal timeline

Build a factual timeline from first engagement through close-lost. Include trigger event, discovery quality, stakeholders reached, criteria identified, demos, technical validation, proposal, pricing, procurement, executive conversations, delays, buyer commitments, seller commitments, and moments where the deal changed. Look for early signals that were ignored.

Timeline analysis prevents hindsight simplification. A deal that ended with procurement pressure may have been lost earlier when the seller failed to connect to business value. A deal that ended in no decision may have been unwinnable from the start because there was no compelling event. Record when the seller could reasonably have known more, not only what is obvious after the loss.

### Identify root cause and contributing factors

Most losses have one primary cause and several contributing causes. Name the root cause as the factor that most changed the outcome, then list the conditions that amplified it. Be careful with causes that feel satisfying but are not actionable. "They had no budget" is less useful than "budget owner was never engaged, and the project was not tied to a funded initiative."

Use categories that support learning: qualification error, weak pain, no compelling event, missing economic buyer, poor champion, competitor advantage, incumbent lock-in, solution gap, pricing mismatch, implementation risk, legal or security blocker, procurement issue, proposal scope mismatch, bad fit, seller execution, or external change. Do not force every deal into one taxonomy if the evidence points elsewhere.

### Seek buyer truth without arguing

When appropriate, ask the buyer for concise feedback after the loss. Make it safe for them to be honest: what mattered most, where the seller misunderstood, which risk remained, what competitor or status quo offered, and what would have changed the decision. Do not use the conversation to reopen the deal unless the buyer invites it.

Buyer feedback may still be incomplete or diplomatic. Compare it with behavior. If the buyer says "timing" but evaluated three competitors urgently, the deeper issue may be vendor fit. If they say "price" but refused to discuss value or scope, the seller may not have reached the real decision owner.

### Compare seller-controlled and buyer-controlled causes

Separate what the seller could influence from what they could only observe. Seller-controlled causes include poor qualification, missed stakeholders, weak discovery, late security review, unclear next steps, unsupported claims, weak business case, over-discounting, or poor follow-up. Buyer-controlled or external causes include budget cuts, acquisition, leadership change, internal freeze, regulatory shift, or an existing contract lock-in.

This distinction prevents both unfair blame and helplessness. Even when the buyer's situation changed, the review can ask whether the team identified risk early, forecasted accurately, and avoided over-investing.

### Inspect competitor and status quo losses differently

A competitor loss requires understanding criteria, proof, relationship, implementation risk, price, procurement fit, category framing, and incumbent dynamics. A status quo loss requires understanding change cost, urgency, internal priority, executive sponsorship, and whether the buyer believed the current pain was tolerable.

Do not lump status quo into competitor analysis. If no one won, the team should learn about urgency, value creation, and change management. If a competitor won, the team should learn about differentiation, criteria control, trust, proof, and commercial fit.

### Turn analysis into a decision

A good lost deal review should produce a decision about future behavior. Possible decisions include changing qualification criteria, revising discovery questions, improving competitive proof, adjusting pricing guidance, involving security earlier, improving champion coaching, changing demo flow, updating ICP, escalating a product gap, or creating better handoff between sales and solution engineering.

Avoid reviews that end with "do better discovery" or "build stronger relationships." Those phrases are not decisions. Name the specific behavior, artifact, owner, and feedback loop.

### Protect data quality and psychological safety

Loss analysis must be honest enough to be useful and fair enough that sellers will not game it. If managers punish every seller-controlled loss, the data will drift toward safe labels such as price, timing, or no budget. If teams avoid accountability entirely, the review becomes comforting fiction.

Write loss notes in a factual, professional tone. Avoid insults, speculation about buyer motives, or internal blame language that would be embarrassing if shared. CRM records may travel farther than expected.

## Common Traps

- Accepting the first CRM loss reason as the root cause.
- Marking "price" when the real issue was weak value, different scope, or missing economic buyer.
- Marking "competitor" when the buyer never had urgency to change.
- Rewriting the story after the fact to make the loss look inevitable.
- Treating buyer feedback as complete truth without comparing it to behavior and timeline evidence.
- Blaming seller execution for uncontrollable external changes, or blaming external changes to avoid inspecting seller execution.
- Ending the review with vague advice instead of a specific process, enablement, product, pricing, or qualification change.
- Writing emotional or speculative loss notes in systems that others may read later.

## Self-Check

- Is the official loss label separated from seller narrative, buyer feedback, observable behavior, and timeline evidence?
- Does the analysis explain the mechanism behind broad labels such as price, competitor, budget, timing, or no decision?
- Is there a factual timeline showing when key evidence appeared and when action was possible?
- Are root cause and contributing factors distinguished?
- Are seller-controlled, buyer-controlled, and external causes separated fairly?
- If buyer feedback was used, is it compared against buyer behavior and deal evidence?
- Is competitor loss distinguished from status quo or no-decision loss?
- Does the review identify what the team should change in qualification, discovery, proof, pricing, process, product feedback, or coaching?
- Are CRM notes factual, professional, and safe to share internally?
- Is uncertainty labeled where the evidence does not support a confident conclusion?
