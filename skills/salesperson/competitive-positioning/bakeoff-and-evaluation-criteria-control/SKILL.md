# Bakeoff And Evaluation Criteria Control

A bakeoff can look objective while measuring the wrong things. Buyers may compare vendors on long feature lists, demo polish, price alone, or criteria inherited from an incumbent. Agents often try to "control" the process by manipulating criteria, which damages trust. This skill helps the agent improve evaluation quality by tying criteria to buyer outcomes, risks, and decision evidence.

## Core Rules

### Understand the evaluation source

Ask who created the criteria, why they matter, and whether they reflect current business outcomes. Criteria may come from procurement templates, incumbent workflows, technical preference, executive concerns, user pain, analyst reports, or old requirements. The source affects fairness and relevance.

### Tie criteria to outcomes

Each criterion should connect to a business outcome, workflow need, risk, or constraint. If a criterion has no consequence, question its weight. This moves the evaluation from feature counting to decision quality.

### Separate must-have from nice-to-have

Mark requirements that are disqualifying versus preferences. Long scorecards often treat minor features and critical risks equally. Weighting matters because it shapes the decision.

### Include implementation and adoption

Evaluation should consider time to value, integration effort, data readiness, user adoption, admin burden, support model, change management, and buyer resource needs. A product can win a demo and fail in rollout.

### Make proof methods explicit and define scoring before vendors perform

Define how each criterion will be tested: demo, technical review, security documentation, reference call, pilot, data sample, architecture review, contract review, or business case. Do not let subjective impressions masquerade as evidence.

Ask how points, pass/fail items, weights, user feedback, executive judgment, price, and risk will be combined. If scoring is invented after demos, the process may favor the loudest stakeholder or the incumbent. Clear scoring protects the buyer from internal bias and protects the seller from moving goalposts.

### Include negative proof and account for evaluator experience

Evaluation should test what would disqualify a vendor or force scope change. Examples include unsupported integration, unacceptable security posture, weak adoption path, legal term conflict, missing data control, or implementation timeline mismatch. A process that only collects positive evidence may hide deal-breaking risk until late.

Users, technical evaluators, executives, procurement, and finance may experience the bakeoff differently. A flashy demo may impress executives but not daily users; a technical deep dive may ignore business value. Design the evaluation so each group tests what they are qualified to judge.

### Prevent proof-of-concept drift and watch for biased criteria

POCs and bakeoffs can expand without improving decision quality. Define scope, success criteria, data, timeline, owner, decision after completion, and what happens if results are mixed. Otherwise the evaluation becomes unpaid implementation or endless exploration.

Criteria may be biased toward incumbent architecture, a favored vendor, a procurement template, or a stakeholder's personal preference. Challenge bias respectfully by asking what outcome the criterion protects and whether another proof would be fairer.

### Control the demo narrative ethically and clarify decision process and tie-breakers

Do not perform disconnected feature theater. Align demo flow to the buyer's workflow and criteria. If the evaluation asks for irrelevant features, show how they relate or propose a better test.

Ask how scores will be combined, who can override, what happens if vendors tie, and how price, risk, and executive preference influence final selection. Hidden tie-breakers are common.

### Decline poor-fit bakeoffs when necessary and document agreed criteria

If criteria are unfair, irrelevant, impossible to test, or disconnected from value, consider pushing for redesign, narrowing participation, or walking away. Not every bakeoff deserves investment.

Record criteria, weights, proof method, owners, timeline, decision process, and open concerns. Evaluation memory should not depend on call notes alone.

## Common Traps

- Accepting inherited criteria without asking why they matter; treating all scorecard items as equally important
- Ignoring implementation and adoption in favor of feature demos; letting subjective reactions replace proof methods
- Attacking biased criteria instead of tying the conversation back to outcomes; running a generic demo against specific evaluation criteria
- Missing hidden tie-breakers such as executive preference, risk, or price; investing heavily in an unfair or low-intent bakeoff
- Letting procurement own criteria without business stakeholder validation; failing to document weights, owners, and proof methods
- Optimizing to win the test rather than solve the buyer problem; treating a high score as a closed deal without authority and budget
- Letting scoring be defined after vendor performance; testing only positive capabilities and not disqualifying risks
- Allowing the wrong evaluator group to dominate criteria they cannot judge; letting a POC expand without a decision rule

## Self-Check

- Is the source of evaluation criteria understood?
- Does each criterion tie to outcome, workflow, risk, or constraint?
- Are must-haves and nice-to-haves separated?
- Are implementation, adoption, support, and buyer resource needs included?
- Is the proof method for each criterion explicit?; are biased or inherited criteria challenged respectfully?
- Is the demo aligned to workflow and criteria?; are decision process, scoring, authority, and tie-breakers clear?
- Has declining or redesigning the bakeoff been considered if criteria are poor?; are criteria, weights, proof methods, owners, and timeline documented?
- Does the evaluation improve buyer decision quality?; is selection evidence connected to budget and authority?
- Is scoring defined before demos, POCs, or vendor responses?; are disqualifying risks and negative proof included?
- Are evaluator groups matched to the criteria they can judge?; does any POC have scope, success criteria, owner, timeline, and decision rule?
