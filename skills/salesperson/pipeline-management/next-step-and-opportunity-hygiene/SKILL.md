# Next Step And Opportunity Hygiene

Opportunity hygiene is not administrative neatness. It determines whether a seller, manager, or future owner can understand the deal, act on it, and judge risk. Agents often record vague next steps such as "follow up" or leave stale close dates because the deal still feels alive. This skill helps the agent keep opportunity records useful for action, coaching, forecast, and handoff.

## Core Rules

### Make the next step buyer-relevant

A strong next step includes buyer action, seller action, owner, date, purpose, and decision dependency. "Send recap" is incomplete unless it supports a buyer decision. "Ari to confirm security review owner by Tuesday; seller to send architecture summary today" is actionable.

### Distinguish task from outcome

Tasks are what people do; outcomes are what changes. A clean opportunity record should show both. If the next task completes but the buyer decision does not move, the opportunity may still be stalled.

### Keep close date evidence-based

Close date should reflect buyer process, critical path, approval timing, procurement, legal, signature, and implementation readiness. Do not leave a date because it was once hoped for. If the buyer cannot confirm path to close, mark uncertainty.

### Update stale opportunities aggressively

Stale opportunities consume attention and distort pipeline. Look for no buyer response, missed commitments, old next steps, stage age, inactive stakeholders, outdated close dates, or no current business reason. Decide to revive, nurture, regress, pause, or close lost.

### Diagnose why hygiene is poor and use a hygiene cadence

Bad hygiene is often a symptom. The seller may not know the buyer process, may be avoiding disqualification, may be waiting on a champion, may lack confidence in value, or may not understand stage criteria. Fixing fields without diagnosing the selling issue only makes the record look cleaner temporarily.

High-value or late-stage opportunities need more frequent hygiene review than early nurture deals. Establish a cadence based on forecast impact, stage, close date proximity, strategic value, and risk. Records become unreliable when hygiene is only reviewed during forecast pressure.

### Check cross-record consistency and make hygiene recoverable

Account notes, contact roles, activities, opportunity fields, quotes, proposals, tasks, and support or implementation records may each hold part of the truth. If these disagree, the agent should reconcile the source of truth before acting.

When a record is messy, do not guess silently. Add a recovery note: what is known, what is missing, what must be confirmed with the buyer, and which fields are provisional. This lets another owner see the uncertainty instead of inheriting false precision.

### Record decision process and owner and preserve key assumptions

An opportunity without decision process, economic owner, champion, technical owner, procurement path, or implementation owner may be too weak for its stage. Missing owners should become tasks, not ignored fields.

Record assumptions about scope, price, value, timeline, buyer responsibilities, dependencies, and risks. If assumptions are unverified, label them. Unlabeled assumptions become bad forecast evidence.

### Keep fields and narrative consistent and avoid private pipeline memory

Stage, amount, close date, forecast category, next step, notes, mutual action plan, proposal, and contract status should tell one story. Inconsistency forces managers to guess.

Important information in chat, email, or seller memory is not opportunity hygiene. If the deal matters, essential facts must live in the system of record or linked artifacts.

### Treat hygiene as buyer respect and use hygiene reviews for coaching

Accurate records reduce repeated questions, broken promises, bad handoffs, and irrelevant follow-up. Hygiene is not just for management reporting; it improves the buyer experience.

Poor hygiene often reveals skill gaps: weak discovery, fear of disqualification, single-threading, no mutual action plan, or unclear value. Use it to improve selling behavior, not only to clean data.

## Common Traps

- Writing "follow up" as a next step; recording seller tasks without buyer action or decision dependency
- Leaving outdated close dates to preserve pipeline appearance; treating stale opportunities as harmless
- Advancing deals without decision process or owner visibility; hiding assumptions inside optimistic notes
- Letting fields and notes contradict each other; keeping critical information in private memory
- Treating CRM hygiene as bureaucracy rather than buyer experience; cleaning fields without addressing the selling gap underneath
- Ignoring missed next steps because the account is strategic; making next steps too vague for another seller to execute
- Cleaning fields without diagnosing the underlying selling gap; reviewing hygiene only during forecast calls
- Letting account, contact, activity, proposal, and opportunity records contradict each other; guessing missing data instead of marking provisional fields

## Self-Check

- Does the next step include owner, date, purpose, and buyer relevance?
- Are task and outcome both clear?
- Is close date tied to buyer process and critical path evidence?
- Are stale opportunities revived, nurtured, regressed, paused, or closed?
- Are decision process and key owners recorded?; are assumptions labeled as verified or unverified?
- Do fields, notes, MAP, proposal, and forecast tell one story?; is essential information in the system of record?
- Does hygiene reduce buyer repetition and handoff risk?; are selling skill gaps visible from hygiene issues?
- Could another seller act from the opportunity record?; is forecast quality improved by the hygiene review?
- Has the cause of poor hygiene been diagnosed?; is hygiene cadence appropriate to stage and forecast impact?
- Are related records consistent?; are unknowns and provisional fields clearly labeled?
