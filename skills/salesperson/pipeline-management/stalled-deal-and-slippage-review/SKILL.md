# Stalled Deal And Slippage Review

A stalled deal is not always a dead deal, but it is always evidence. Slippage reveals something about value, urgency, authority, timing, process, risk, or seller execution. Agents often respond with more follow-up instead of diagnosing why the buyer stopped moving. This skill helps the agent review stalled deals honestly and decide the right recovery path.

## Core Rules

### Define the stall precisely

Name what stopped: buyer response, stakeholder meeting, technical validation, business case, procurement, legal, budget approval, executive decision, implementation planning, or signature. A vague "deal stalled" diagnosis produces vague action.

### Compare expected versus actual buyer movement

Review the mutual action plan, promised next steps, stage criteria, and close-date assumptions. Identify which buyer-owned action was missed and what dependency it blocked. Seller activity does not erase buyer inactivity.

### Diagnose root cause

Common causes include weak value, no compelling event, wrong stakeholder, hidden blocker, budget issue, competing priority, procurement delay, legal concern, technical risk, poor champion, implementation burden, or bad timing. Do not assume the first explanation is complete.

### Test whether urgency is real

Ask what happens if the buyer waits. If cost of inaction is weak or unowned, slippage may be rational. A real urgent deal should have buyer-side consequences for delay.

### Revisit status quo strength and check for stakeholder drift

Stalled deals often reveal that the status quo is stronger than the seller believed. The current process may be painful but familiar, cheaper, politically safe, or "good enough." Reassess whether the buyer has a compelling reason to disrupt it and whether the value case beats the comfort of no change.

The person who cared at discovery may no longer own the priority, or new stakeholders may have entered with different concerns. Slippage should trigger a fresh stakeholder check: champion strength, economic buyer attention, technical owner engagement, procurement path, and user support.

### Define re-entry triggers and avoid false revival

If the deal is paused or nurtured, document what future event would justify reactivation: budget cycle, renewal window, executive priority, compliance deadline, new stakeholder, product capability, funding, or failed incumbent initiative. Without a trigger, nurture becomes vague waiting.

A buyer reply after silence does not automatically revive a deal. Look for new evidence: decision owner, timeline, value, budget path, or agreed action. Otherwise the opportunity may return to pipeline without real change.

### Decide recovery path and use respectful directness

Options include re-engage with new insight, request a buyer decision, involve executive sponsor, rebuild value, reach a missing stakeholder, narrow scope, revise timeline, pause, nurture, or close lost. More follow-up is only one option.

When appropriate, name the stall with the buyer: "It looks like the timeline has shifted. Should we revisit priority, owners, or whether this is still worth active evaluation?" Direct language can save both sides time.

### Reforecast immediately and preserve relationship in exit paths

If a critical milestone slips, update close date, forecast category, stage, and risk. Do not wait for certainty. Forecast should reflect current evidence, not prior hope.

Closing lost or pausing is not failure if done professionally. Summarize value, reason for pause, future trigger, and any useful next step. A respectful exit can create future re-entry.

### Look for seller-caused slippage and feed learning into pipeline quality

Review whether the seller failed to create urgency, single-threaded, skipped economic buyer, sent weak follow-up, delayed documents, overpromised, or failed to clarify process. Slippage is not always buyer-caused.

Repeated slippage by segment, source, seller, stage, or product should inform qualification, enablement, ICP, pricing, implementation readiness, or forecast rules.

## Common Traps

- Treating all stalled deals as requiring another follow-up email; failing to identify the exact missed buyer action
- Accepting a surface explanation without root-cause diagnosis; assuming urgency exists because the seller wants the deal
- Keeping old close dates after critical slippage; avoiding direct conversation about priority and fit
- Treating close-lost as relationship damage; blaming the buyer before reviewing seller execution
- Ignoring repeated slippage patterns; letting strategic accounts remain indefinitely open
- Confusing legal or procurement delay with true business commitment; reviving a deal without new information or changed conditions
- Underestimating the comfort and politics of the status quo; missing stakeholder drift after a long stall
- Pausing without a future re-entry trigger; treating any buyer reply as revival evidence

## Self-Check

- Is the stall point precisely named?
- Is expected buyer movement compared with actual movement?
- Is root cause diagnosed beyond the first explanation?
- Is buyer urgency real and owned?
- Is the recovery path chosen deliberately?; has respectful directness been used where appropriate?
- Are close date, forecast category, stage, and risk updated?; is pause or close-lost handled with a future re-entry trigger where useful?
- Has seller execution been reviewed?; are slippage patterns captured for learning?
- Does the next action create new evidence rather than more activity?; is the deal still worth active pipeline attention?
- Has status quo strength been reassessed?; are current stakeholders and their roles still valid?
- Is there a concrete re-entry trigger if paused?; is revival based on new evidence rather than renewed conversation alone?
