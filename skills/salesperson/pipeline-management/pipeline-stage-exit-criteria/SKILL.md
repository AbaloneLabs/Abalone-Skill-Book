# Pipeline Stage Exit Criteria

Pipeline stages are not labels for seller activity. They are claims about buyer progress and deal evidence. Agents often advance opportunities because a meeting happened, a demo was delivered, or a proposal was sent, even when the buyer has not made a corresponding decision. This skill helps the agent use stage exit criteria to keep pipeline, forecast, and seller action grounded in evidence.

## Core Rules

### Define stages by buyer evidence

Each stage should require evidence of buyer movement: problem acknowledged, value confirmed, decision process identified, stakeholders engaged, technical fit validated, business case accepted, procurement started, or contract path confirmed. Seller work alone should not move a deal forward.

### Separate activity from progression

A discovery call, demo, email, proposal, or executive meeting may create progression, but only if it changes what the buyer knows, decides, commits to, or does next. Treat activity as input and buyer evidence as stage qualification.

### Make exit criteria observable

Criteria should be concrete enough that another person can inspect the opportunity and reach the same conclusion. "Strong interest" is weak. "Economic buyer confirmed the problem affects renewal cycle and agreed to review business case by Friday" is stronger.

### Include disqualification criteria

Good stage management defines when to exit backward or out: no owner, no problem, no budget path, bad fit, unsupported requirement, no decision process, repeated missed commitments, or implementation risk the buyer will not address. Disqualification protects focus and trust.

### Calibrate by segment and motion and treat evidence as decaying

Stage evidence may differ by enterprise, mid-market, SMB, partner-led, public-sector, renewal, expansion, or self-serve assist motion. An enterprise deal may require stakeholder map and security path before later stages; a high-velocity SMB deal may require simpler but still observable evidence. Calibrate criteria deliberately rather than copying one motion into another.

Buyer evidence has a shelf life. A pain confirmed last quarter, an old champion endorsement, or a stale implementation timeline may no longer justify the stage. Define when evidence must be reconfirmed, especially after stakeholder changes, budget cycles, competitor entry, product scope changes, or long inactivity.

### Require stage-specific risk review and handle exceptions explicitly

Each stage has different risks. Early stages risk weak pain and wrong persona. Middle stages risk missing stakeholders and unclear value. Late stages risk procurement, legal, technical validation, and implementation readiness. Stage exit should include the risk expected for that stage, not a generic "deal looks good" check.

Strategic accounts, executive-directed deals, channel opportunities, and renewals may not follow the standard path exactly. Exceptions should still have evidence, owner, reason, and review date. Do not let "strategic" become a reason to ignore stage discipline.

### Require next buyer action and align stage with forecast category

Every stage should have a buyer-owned next action or a clear reason why one is not possible. Seller-only next steps create false pipeline health. If the buyer has no action, the opportunity may be stalled or over-staged.

Stage and forecast category are related but not identical. A late stage with weak evidence should not create high forecast confidence. A strong early-stage deal may still be low forecast if timing, authority, or procurement is unresolved.

### Regress stages without shame and audit for stage inflation

If evidence weakens, move the opportunity backward or mark it stalled. Stage regression is not failure; it is better data. Keeping a deal over-staged creates poor coaching, resource allocation, and forecast judgment.

Look for opportunities advanced because of seller optimism, end-of-quarter pressure, executive attention, or CRM habit. Stage inflation hides risk and encourages the wrong actions.

### Connect stages to playbook actions and preserve evidence in CRM

Each stage should imply the next useful work: discovery, stakeholder mapping, technical validation, business case, proposal, procurement, legal, or handoff. If stage does not guide action, it is only reporting decoration.

Stage exit evidence should be recorded in notes, fields, attachments, or mutual action plans. If evidence is only in memory, managers and future owners cannot inspect it.

## Common Traps

- Advancing stage because the seller completed an activity; treating positive sentiment as buyer progress
- Using vague criteria that cannot be inspected; omitting disqualification and regression paths
- Letting seller-only next steps count as opportunity momentum; assuming late stage means high forecast confidence
- Avoiding stage regression because it feels embarrassing; allowing quarter-end pressure to inflate stages
- Having stages that do not suggest different actions; leaving stage evidence in private notes or memory
- Applying stage criteria inconsistently across segments; ignoring implementation readiness until after commercial stage movement
- Using one stage model for all sales motions without calibration; relying on old buyer evidence after the situation changes
- Applying the same risk review to every stage; letting strategic exceptions bypass evidence discipline

## Self-Check

- Is stage defined by buyer evidence rather than seller activity?
- Has activity been separated from true progression?
- Are exit criteria observable and inspectable?
- Are disqualification, pause, and regression criteria defined?
- Is there a buyer-owned next action?; does forecast category reflect evidence quality, not only stage?
- Is stage regression allowed when evidence weakens?; has stage inflation risk been checked?
- Does the stage imply the right next sales action?; is evidence preserved in CRM or related artifacts?
- Are segment-specific differences considered where relevant?; would another manager agree with the stage from the recorded evidence?
- Is stage evidence fresh enough for the current decision?; are stage-specific risks checked?
- Are exceptions documented with reason, owner, and review date?; would the stage still be valid if the champion left tomorrow?
