# Legal Redline And Contract Risk Handoff

Contract redlines are not merely paperwork on the way to signature. They decide which risks the company accepts, which promises become enforceable, which delivery teams must honor, and which future disputes will be hard to defend. Agents can be especially risky here because they may summarize legal language too casually, treat a redline as a sales obstacle, or promise that a term is "fine" without authority. This skill helps the agent support legal review while staying inside role boundaries and keeping business context visible.

Use this skill when handling customer paper, master service agreements, order forms, data processing terms, security addenda, service level terms, indemnity, liability caps, termination rights, renewal language, payment terms, assignment, audit rights, warranties, implementation obligations, or any contract exception that affects deal risk. The agent may organize facts, identify business impact, and route issues, but should not provide legal advice or approve legal risk unless explicitly authorized by the organization.

## Core Rules

### Preserve the boundary between sales context and legal advice

Sales can explain commercial context, buyer priority, negotiation history, business impact, timing, and tradeoffs. Legal decides legal acceptability and required wording. The agent should avoid interpreting clauses as safe, enforceable, standard, or harmless unless that position is drawn from approved guidance or counsel.

Use careful language: "The customer requested this change," "This appears to affect termination rights," "Legal review is needed," or "This deviates from our standard position." Do not say "we can accept this," "this is just boilerplate," or "this will not matter" when the authority is absent.

### Keep redlines tied to business context

Legal review is better when counsel understands why the buyer is asking, what value the deal has, which terms are deal-breakers, what alternatives exist, and what timing pressure is real. Provide deal size, product scope, customer segment, use case, data sensitivity, deployment model, renewal or expansion potential, competitive pressure, buyer deadline, and internal stakeholders affected.

Do not dump a marked-up document without context. Missing context can lead to over-negotiation, under-protection, or slow review. The salesperson's job is to make the business situation clear enough for legal and leadership to assess risk deliberately.

### Identify the clause's operational owner

Many redlines create obligations outside legal. Service levels affect support and operations. Security commitments affect security and engineering. Data processing terms affect privacy and compliance. Implementation commitments affect professional services and customer success. Payment terms affect finance. Product roadmap language affects product leadership.

For every material deviation, identify who must live with the obligation after signature. A legal approval alone may not be enough if the company cannot operationally perform the promise. The agent should route operational obligations before presenting them as acceptable.

### Distinguish negotiation position from approval

A fallback position, playbook note, or prior exception is not the same as approval for this deal. Check whether authority depends on deal size, product line, customer type, regulated industry, data category, geography, contract duration, liability amount, or delivery model. A term accepted once may have been tied to special facts that do not apply now.

When requesting an exception, state the standard position, the requested change, business rationale, risk, affected owners, proposed tradeoff, and deadline. Avoid exception requests that only say the customer wants it or the deal may slip.

### Watch for terms that change the sale itself

Some redlines alter what was sold. Examples include expanded usage rights, custom support hours, acceptance criteria, termination for convenience, refund rights, unlimited liability, most-favored-customer language, broad audit rights, nonstandard data retention, security obligations beyond current controls, implementation milestones, or service credits. These can change pricing, forecast quality, delivery risk, and customer expectations.

If a term changes scope or economics, revisit commercial approval. Do not let contract language quietly create a larger product, heavier service obligation, or lower-margin deal than the proposal described.

### Maintain version control and decision traceability

Contract negotiations can produce multiple drafts, side emails, comments, and verbal concessions. Track the current version, open issues, owner for each issue, latest position, decision, approval source, and buyer response. Avoid copying language between agreements without review.

Decision traceability matters after signature. If a customer later cites a special term, the company needs to know why it was accepted, who approved it, and what operational team was informed. A clean record also prevents sales, legal, and procurement from negotiating against different documents.

### Communicate risk without dramatizing or minimizing

The agent should make contract risk understandable to non-lawyers without converting it into legal advice. Translate practical impact: potential cost exposure, operational burden, timing risk, renewal risk, customer experience risk, compliance risk, delivery feasibility, or precedent risk. Use uncertainty where appropriate.

Do not use fear to force approval, and do not minimize legal concerns as "just contract stuff." Good communication helps the business choose between accepting, rejecting, trading, escalating, or narrowing the term.

### Close the loop into handoff

Signed terms matter to implementation, finance, support, customer success, security, privacy, and account management. After signature, ensure nonstandard obligations are visible in the handoff record. Include special service commitments, billing terms, notice requirements, data restrictions, reporting obligations, security commitments, renewal terms, termination rights, and any customer-specific process.

If the deal was closed with a legal or operational exception, name the owner who will monitor it. A hidden exception becomes a future customer trust problem.

## Common Traps

- Treating redlines as administrative friction instead of binding changes to risk, scope, and delivery.
- Summarizing legal language as acceptable without approval from the right owner.
- Sending legal a document without commercial context, buyer priority, or timing reality.
- Assuming a prior exception applies to a new customer with different size, data, geography, or use case.
- Letting contract language expand product scope, support duties, service credits, or implementation obligations without repricing or operational approval.
- Negotiating from outdated drafts or side emails that are not reflected in the current contract version.
- Pressuring legal with quarter-end urgency while hiding unresolved operational risk.
- Failing to pass nonstandard obligations to customer success, finance, security, support, or implementation after signature.

## Self-Check

- Is the agent staying within sales context and routing legal interpretation or approval to authorized counsel?
- Are standard position, customer redline, business rationale, risk, and requested decision clearly separated?
- Does legal have deal context: value, use case, scope, buyer deadline, data sensitivity, deployment, and competitive situation?
- Are operational owners identified for service, security, privacy, finance, implementation, product, and support obligations?
- Are exception requests tied to authority level and relevant deal facts rather than customer pressure alone?
- Could any redline change price, scope, support burden, delivery feasibility, liability, or renewal economics?
- Is the current contract version, open issue list, approval source, and buyer response traceable?
- Is risk explained in practical business terms without pretending to give legal advice?
- Are nonstandard signed terms included in handoff records and assigned to monitoring owners?
- Is any unresolved risk explicitly owned before signature?
