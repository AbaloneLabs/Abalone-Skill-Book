# Security Review And Vendor Questionnaire

Security review is not a formality. It is the buyer's attempt to decide whether trusting the vendor creates unacceptable data, operational, regulatory, privacy, or reputational risk. Agents often try to accelerate security review by answering from memory, copying old questionnaires, or smoothing over uncertainty. That can create false commitments and serious trust problems. This skill helps the agent support security review with accurate scope, approved evidence, and clear routing.

Use this skill when handling vendor questionnaires, security assessments, trust center packets, SOC reports, ISO evidence, penetration test summaries, data processing questions, AI usage questions, subprocessors, incident response, encryption, access control, retention, vulnerability management, business continuity, or security exceptions. The agent should coordinate and clarify, but should not invent technical or compliance answers.

## Core Rules

### Determine the review scope before answering

Security answers depend on product, edition, deployment model, data type, region, integrations, user roles, AI features, subprocessors, professional services, and implementation design. Before responding, determine what the buyer plans to use, what data will flow, who will access it, where it will be stored or processed, and which features are in scope.

Avoid generic answers that are true for one product but false for another. If the buyer asks about regulated data, production credentials, customer data, employee data, payment data, health data, government data, or children's data, route to the appropriate security, privacy, or legal owner.

### Use approved sources and current documents

Security questionnaires should be answered from approved knowledge bases, trust centers, security documents, standard responses, legal-approved terms, or named subject-matter experts. Check document dates, report periods, product scope, and confidentiality rules before sharing.

Do not copy from old customer questionnaires without verification. Security controls change, products change, subprocessors change, and report coverage can expire. A stale correct-sounding answer may be worse than a delayed answer because it creates reliance.

### Distinguish facts, commitments, and roadmap

Security reviewers ask both factual and commitment questions. "Do you encrypt data at rest?" asks about current controls. "Will you notify us within 24 hours?" may create contractual obligation. "Can data remain in a specific region?" may depend on product architecture and contract terms. Treat commitments carefully.

If an answer requires future change, roadmap feature, manual workaround, special configuration, or exception, label it clearly and route for approval. Never imply a roadmap or custom security commitment to pass a questionnaire.

### Answer the question asked without overexposing information

Provide enough detail to satisfy the buyer's risk review, but avoid unnecessary sensitive disclosure. Internal network diagrams, vulnerability details, unreleased audit findings, customer names, incident specifics, or security procedures may require NDA, portal access, redaction, or security approval.

If the buyer's questionnaire asks for information that policy does not allow sharing, explain the approved alternative such as summary, attestation, report under NDA, trust center access, or call with security. Do not improvise sensitive details in a sales email.

### Track ownership and turnaround

Security review often stalls because questions are spread across sales, security, privacy, legal, product, engineering, support, and implementation. Assign owner, due date, source of truth, blocker, and buyer recipient for each open item. Keep a single tracker where possible.

Tell the buyer what is complete, what is under review, who owns it, and when the next update will come. Avoid silence after receiving a large questionnaire. Process visibility can preserve trust even when answers take time.

### Watch for hidden contract terms

Questionnaires sometimes contain language that functions like contract terms: audit rights, breach notification timelines, data location commitments, deletion obligations, insurance requirements, service levels, subcontractor approval, vulnerability remediation deadlines, or indemnity-like assurances. Do not accept these through questionnaire answers unless policy allows it.

Route any answer that could create a binding obligation to legal or security leadership. If the contract says one thing and the questionnaire answer says another, the account may inherit ambiguity.

### Handle gaps and exceptions honestly

If a control is absent, partial, not applicable, or available only in a certain edition, say so through the approved process. Then identify compensating controls, implementation choices, roadmap status if approved, or risk acceptance path. Hiding gaps to pass review can create breach of trust and post-sale escalation.

For buyer-specific exceptions, record scope, expiration, approver, customer obligations, and handoff owner. A security exception that is not monitored can become a compliance problem later.

### Connect security status to deal strategy and forecast

Security review can be a critical-path item. Forecast should reflect questionnaire completion, security call status, open high-risk items, exception approvals, data processing terms, and buyer security signoff. A deal is not late-stage simply because the commercial buyer is enthusiastic.

If security is likely to fail or require costly controls, escalate early. The right answer may be to narrow scope, adjust deployment, exclude sensitive data, delay implementation, or disqualify.

## Common Traps

- Answering from memory because the question looks familiar.
- Reusing an old questionnaire without checking product, date, report period, or control changes.
- Giving one-size-fits-all answers across products, regions, editions, or deployment models.
- Treating notification, data residency, audit, remediation, or deletion answers as harmless when they may create obligations.
- Sharing sensitive security information outside approved channels or without required NDA.
- Hiding gaps, partial controls, or roadmap dependency to avoid slowing the sale.
- Letting security questions sit across teams without owner, deadline, or tracker.
- Forecasting a deal as closeable before buyer security signoff or exception approval.

## Self-Check

- Is the buyer's product, data, deployment, region, integration, and feature scope clear before answers are provided?
- Are responses sourced from approved, current materials or named subject-matter experts?
- Are facts, contractual commitments, exceptions, and roadmap items clearly separated?
- Are sensitive documents shared only through approved channels and under required confidentiality conditions?
- Are all open questionnaire items assigned to owners with due dates and source-of-truth references?
- Could any questionnaire answer create a legal, security, privacy, service, or operational obligation?
- Are gaps or partial controls handled through approved language and escalation rather than hidden?
- Are security exceptions documented with scope, approver, expiration, and handoff owner?
- Does the opportunity forecast reflect actual security review status and remaining blockers?
- Are security findings passed to legal, implementation, customer success, or product where they affect delivery?
