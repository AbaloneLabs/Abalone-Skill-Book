# Account Risk And Relationship Health

Account health is not whether the main contact is friendly. It is whether the account has value, trust, adoption, stakeholder coverage, executive alignment, and a manageable risk profile. Agents often overestimate health because one champion is responsive while hidden risks build elsewhere. This skill helps the agent inspect account relationships with evidence.

## Core Rules

### Evaluate health across dimensions

Review value realized, product adoption, support sentiment, stakeholder coverage, executive alignment, renewal path, commercial terms, implementation health, competitive pressure, and strategic fit. One strong dimension can hide weakness in another.

### Test champion strength and identify relationship concentration

A champion should have influence, access, credibility, personal stake, and willingness to act. Responsiveness alone is not enough. Check whether the champion can mobilize others, not just answer the seller.

If the account depends on one contact, one department, or one executive, relationship health is fragile. Map backup relationships and missing stakeholders.

### Inspect executive alignment and review adoption and outcome evidence

For strategic accounts, know whether executives understand value, risk, renewal stakes, and expansion potential. Executive absence may be acceptable in small accounts but risky in large or high-impact accounts.

Usage should connect to business outcomes. High logins may not equal value; low usage in a critical workflow may signal risk. Check whether adoption is broad, deep, and tied to success criteria.

### Compare relationship view across teams and identify health trend, not only current state

Sales, customer success, support, implementation, finance, and executives may each see different account signals. Compare their views before declaring health. A seller may see a friendly champion while support sees frustration, or CS may see adoption while finance sees payment issues.

Account health is directional. Improving adoption with open support issues is different from declining adoption after a resolved implementation. Track whether value, engagement, sentiment, and stakeholder coverage are improving, stable, or worsening.

### Define escalation thresholds and test relationship portability

Decide what conditions trigger executive outreach, support escalation, renewal intervention, account review, or commercial adjustment. Thresholds reduce delay when risk appears and prevent over-escalation for normal friction.

Ask what happens if the champion leaves, budget owner changes, or procurement reviews the account cold. Healthy accounts have documented value, multiple relationships, and institutional support beyond one person.

### Include expansion restraint and use direct customer evidence where possible

If relationship health is weak, expansion pressure can worsen trust. Account risk review should state whether to expand, stabilize, defend, or pause commercial asks.

Internal health opinions should be tested against customer statements, QBR feedback, renewal conversations, support comments, executive meetings, and adoption reviews. If the customer has not recently confirmed value or concern, label the health view as internal inference.

### Track recovery commitments and include support and service history

When health is weak, record what the seller or post-sale team committed to do, what the customer committed to do, and how improvement will be measured. Account recovery without commitments becomes hope.

Escalations, unresolved tickets, billing disputes, implementation pain, or slow responses shape relationship health. Relationship warmth can coexist with operational frustration.

### Watch for competitor or incumbent pressure and consider commercial fragility

Competitive risk may appear through benchmarking questions, procurement comparisons, new stakeholder requests, data export questions, or sudden executive scrutiny. Treat these as health signals.

Deep discounts, special terms, ramp pricing, unpriced support expectations, or unclear renewal baseline may create future friction. Relationship health includes commercial sustainability.

### Define risk actions and update health after events

Each risk should have owner, mitigation, date, and evidence of improvement. "Monitor account" is not enough. Actions may include executive review, value proof, support escalation, stakeholder mapping, adoption plan, or renewal path.

Reassess after org changes, renewal conversations, implementation milestones, support escalations, usage drops, expansion attempts, or competitor signals. Health is dynamic.

## Common Traps

- Equating friendly contact with healthy account.
- Ignoring value, adoption, support, and commercial dimensions; overestimating champion power
- Letting relationship concentration persist; missing executive absence in strategic accounts
- Treating usage volume as outcome proof; ignoring support and billing history
- Missing subtle competitor signals; calling special commercial terms a win without renewal risk
- Naming risks without mitigation owners; letting health scores go stale
- Separating customer success health from sales account strategy; trusting one team's view of health without cross-functional comparison; looking at current health without trend; waiting too long because escalation thresholds are undefined
- Assuming relationship health survives champion turnover; pushing expansion when the account needs stabilization; relying only on internal sentiment without customer evidence; starting account recovery without commitments and measures

## Self-Check

- Is account health reviewed across value, adoption, support, stakeholders, executive alignment, renewal, commercial, and competitive dimensions?
- Is champion strength evidenced by influence and action?; is relationship concentration identified?
- Is executive alignment appropriate for account size and risk?; does adoption connect to outcomes?
- Are support and billing histories included?; are competitor signals checked?
- Is commercial fragility considered?; does each risk have owner, mitigation, date, and evidence target?
- Is account health updated after material events?; are sales and customer success health views aligned?
- Would the account likely renew or expand if the main champion left?; are sales, CS, support, implementation, finance, and executive signals compared?
- Is health trend improving, stable, or worsening?; are escalation thresholds defined?
- Is relationship portability tested?; is the commercial motion expand, stabilize, defend, or pause?
- Is customer-confirmed evidence included or is the health view labeled as inference?; are recovery commitments and measures defined for unhealthy accounts?
