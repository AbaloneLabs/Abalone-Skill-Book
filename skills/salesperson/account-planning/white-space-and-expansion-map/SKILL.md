# White Space And Expansion Map

White-space mapping is not coloring empty boxes in an account grid. It is deciding which unserved parts of an account have real pain, value, readiness, access, and timing. Agents often mistake theoretical potential for an actionable opportunity. This skill helps the agent create expansion maps that guide discovery rather than inflate pipeline.

## Core Rules

### Define the unit of expansion

White space may be department, region, subsidiary, product line, workflow, persona, seat group, usage tier, integration, or adjacent product. Define the unit clearly before estimating potential. Ambiguous white space produces inflated account plans.

### Attach evidence to each area

For every potential expansion, record pain signal, current process, value hypothesis, stakeholder, budget path, readiness, competitive context, and timing. Empty areas without evidence should be labeled unknown, not opportunity.

### Separate known need from inferred need

If one team has a problem, another team may or may not share it. Similarity is a hypothesis, not proof. Mark whether need is confirmed, inferred from similarity, or unknown.

### Map buying centers and politics and consider current account health

Different departments may have different executives, procurement paths, technical owners, incumbents, and priorities. Expansion can fail if the seller assumes one champion can open every door.

Expansion should be shaped by adoption, support sentiment, renewal risk, implementation success, and value realization in the current footprint. Weak health may require stabilization before expansion.

### Prioritize by readiness and value and identify entry paths

Rank white-space opportunities using estimated value, urgency, access, product fit, implementation effort, buyer capacity, political risk, and renewal impact. High theoretical spend should not automatically be first.

Each white-space area should have a plausible entry path: current champion intro, executive sponsor, customer success insight, event interaction, partner route, usage signal, support conversation, or trigger-based outreach. A white-space box without an entry path is not ready for active pursuit.

### Define proof needed for each area and estimate customer capacity

Different areas may require different proof: peer case study, workflow demo, security validation, ROI estimate, technical workshop, reference, pilot, or implementation plan. Mapping proof needs prevents premature proposals.

The customer may not be able to evaluate or implement multiple expansions at once. Consider admin time, change fatigue, budget, executive attention, procurement load, and support health. White space should be sequenced by customer capacity, not only seller appetite.

### Separate commercial path from user interest and avoid internal-only white space

Users may want a capability, but expansion requires budget owner, approval process, contract path, and procurement readiness. Mark where user interest exists without commercial path.

If the map is based only on seller research, label it as hypothesis. Active opportunities require customer-confirmed pain, owner, or timing. Internal maps are useful for planning questions, not for forecast.

### Identify expansion blockers and use white space to guide discovery

Blockers may include separate budget, regional compliance, incumbent contracts, data silos, integration complexity, lack of executive mandate, procurement rules, or customer success concerns. Name blockers before creating forecast.

The map should produce questions and next actions: who owns this workflow, what process exists, what trigger matters, what current tool is used, what data is available, and what proof would justify expansion.

### Avoid pressure before value and update after new evidence

If the customer is still implementing or proving initial value, broad white-space pursuit may feel opportunistic. Expansion timing should respect trust and capacity.

White-space maps should change after discovery, usage analysis, support issues, renewal conversations, org changes, or competitive signals. Treat the map as a live hypothesis.

## Common Traps

- Calling every unpenetrated department an opportunity.
- Failing to define the expansion unit; treating inferred need as confirmed need
- Assuming one champion can sell across the whole account; ignoring current account health
- Prioritizing by theoretical spend alone; missing blockers unique to each buying center
- Creating forecast from white-space maps without buyer evidence; pressuring expansion before initial value is secure
- Letting the map go stale after org changes; using the map as internal ambition rather than discovery guide
- Ignoring customer success input on readiness; mapping white space with no entry path
- Proposing expansion before proof needs are known; ignoring customer capacity and change fatigue; treating user interest as commercial path; turning seller-only hypotheses into forecast

## Self-Check

- Is the unit of expansion clearly defined?
- Does each white-space area have evidence or an unknown label?
- Are confirmed, inferred, and unknown needs separated?
- Are buying centers, politics, and owners mapped?; is current account health considered?
- Are opportunities prioritized by value, readiness, access, effort, risk, and timing?; are blockers identified?
- Does the map create discovery questions and next actions?; is expansion timing respectful of trust and capacity?
- Is the map updated with new evidence?; is customer success input included where relevant?
- Would the map prevent wasted pursuit, not just increase ambition?; does each area have a plausible entry path?
- Is proof needed for each area defined?; is customer capacity considered in sequencing?
- Are user interest and commercial path separated?; are seller-only hypotheses labeled?
