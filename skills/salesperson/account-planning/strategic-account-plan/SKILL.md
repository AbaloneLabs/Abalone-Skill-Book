# Strategic Account Plan

A strategic account plan is not a profile of a company. It is a decision tool for where to invest seller time, executive attention, product help, customer success effort, and commercial strategy. Agents often fill account plans with facts that do not change action. This skill helps the agent build an account plan that identifies value, risk, timing, stakeholders, and the next moves that matter.

## Core Rules

### Start with the account objective

Define whether the plan is for new logo acquisition, renewal defense, expansion, displacement, executive alignment, risk recovery, or long-term relationship growth. The objective changes which facts matter. A renewal-risk plan needs different evidence than a white-space expansion plan.

### Use account facts that affect action

Capture business priorities, organizational structure, current initiatives, pain points, buying centers, technology environment, budget cycles, procurement norms, renewal dates, and known constraints. Avoid collecting generic company trivia unless it changes targeting, messaging, or timing.

### Map current and needed relationships

Identify champion, economic buyer, executive sponsor, technical owner, users, procurement, legal, finance, blockers, incumbent sponsors, and missing stakeholders. Mark relationship strength and evidence, not just names.

### Connect account strategy to value and identify timing windows

State the buyer outcomes the seller can credibly influence. Include current value, potential value, cost of inaction, business case assumptions, and proof needed. Account planning without value logic becomes account coverage theater.

Look for renewal dates, budget planning, executive initiatives, compliance deadlines, technology migrations, reorganizations, market changes, and procurement cycles. Timing determines whether outreach is relevant or premature.

### Prioritize opportunities inside the account and include risk and defense strategy

Not every possible use case deserves pursuit. Rank opportunities by value, fit, readiness, stakeholder access, implementation effort, competitive position, and timing. A plan should prevent scattered selling.

Strategic accounts can be lost through low adoption, executive turnover, competitor entry, support issues, contract dissatisfaction, or lack of value proof. Name risks, owners, and mitigation actions.

### Coordinate internal resources deliberately and build an account narrative

Executive outreach, solution engineering, product input, customer success, legal, partner support, and marketing attention should be tied to account evidence. Do not spend scarce resources because an account is famous.

The plan should have a concise narrative: why this account matters, what business change is happening, where the seller can create value, what relationship gaps exist, what risk could derail the plan, and what the next strategic move is. A narrative helps internal stakeholders align without reading every detail.

### Define decision gates and include customer success and delivery reality

Strategic accounts can absorb unlimited effort unless the plan defines gates. Decide what evidence would justify deeper investment, executive involvement, custom work, partner engagement, or disqualification. Gates prevent "strategic" from becoming a blank check.

If the account is already a customer, account planning must include customer success health, implementation history, support issues, adoption, and renewal risk. If the account is a prospect, include likely delivery constraints and fit. Growth strategy that ignores delivery reality creates future churn.

### Track competitive and incumbent strategy and define what not to pursue

Identify incumbent vendors, internal alternatives, competitor relationships, renewal dates, contract leverage, and likely counter-moves. Account planning should anticipate how the customer may compare options and what proof will be needed.

Good strategy excludes. Name departments, use cases, plays, or contacts that will not be pursued now because value, access, timing, or readiness is weak. This protects time and keeps the plan focused.

### Define plays and next actions and keep the plan current

Convert the plan into specific plays: executive alignment, renewal value review, technical workshop, stakeholder mapping, expansion discovery, reference strategy, competitive displacement, or risk recovery. Each play needs owner, date, and success signal.

Update after stakeholder changes, renewal movement, major meetings, support escalations, new initiatives, competitive signals, and opportunity shifts. A stale account plan can be worse than no plan because it creates false confidence.

## Common Traps

- Writing company research instead of a strategy.
- Planning without a clear account objective; listing names without relationship strength or role
- Treating all possible opportunities as equal; ignoring renewal, adoption, and support risk while chasing expansion
- Asking for internal resources without evidence; missing timing windows and budget cycles
- Creating plays that have no buyer-owned action; letting the plan become stale after organization changes
- Assuming a strategic logo deserves effort regardless of fit; separating account plan from CRM, forecast, and customer success context
- Confusing account coverage with account progress; lacking a concise account narrative that aligns internal resources
- Investing indefinitely without decision gates; ignoring customer success or delivery reality; underplanning incumbent and competitive counter-moves; refusing to name what will not be pursued

## Self-Check

- Is the account objective explicit?
- Are facts included because they affect action?
- Are current and needed relationships mapped with strength and evidence?
- Is value logic clear for current and future opportunities?; are timing windows identified?
- Are opportunities prioritized by value, fit, readiness, access, effort, competition, and timing?; are account risks and defense actions named?
- Are internal resources tied to evidence and expected impact?; are plays translated into owners, dates, and success signals?
- Is the plan updated after material changes?; does the plan align with CRM, forecast, renewal, and customer success context?
- Would the plan change what the seller does this week?; can the account narrative be stated clearly?
- Are investment decision gates defined?; are delivery and customer success realities included?
- Are incumbent and competitive moves anticipated?; does the plan explicitly deprioritize weak areas?
