# Pilot Proof Of Concept Design

A pilot is not a free sample. It should test specific buyer risks and decision criteria with enough structure that the buyer can decide what to do next. Poor pilots drift, consume technical resources, create unpaid consulting, and end with no decision. This skill helps the agent design pilots that are evidence-based, bounded, and mutually accountable.

## Core Rules

### Define the decision the pilot supports

State what the buyer will decide after the pilot: technical feasibility, user adoption, value proof, integration fit, executive approval, security readiness, or purchase. If no decision follows, the pilot may be a distraction.

Pilots need decision consequence.

### Set success and failure criteria

Define measurable or observable criteria, baseline, target, time window, data source, owner, and who judges success. Also define what would count as failure or non-fit.

Ambiguous pilots produce ambiguous outcomes.

### Limit scope deliberately

Choose use case, users, data set, integrations, workflows, environment, and support level. Avoid pilots that try to prove everything at once.

Scope should match the decision being tested.

### Require buyer commitment

The buyer should provide owners, data, technical access, user participation, evaluation meetings, timeline, and decision process. A pilot without buyer effort usually indicates low intent.

Mutual commitment protects seller resources.

### Separate pilot support from implementation and manage data and security safely

Clarify what help is included during pilot and what belongs to paid implementation, professional services, or post-sale onboarding. Do not let a pilot become unpaid production deployment.

Boundaries protect delivery teams.

Use approved environments, test data where possible, access controls, security review, privacy review, and data deletion or retention plan. Do not casually import sensitive production data.

Pilot shortcuts can create real risk.

### Plan evaluation cadence and capture proof and gaps

Schedule kickoff, checkpoints, issue review, user feedback, success measurement, and final decision meeting before the pilot begins. Waiting until the end creates drift.

Cadence keeps the pilot accountable.

Record what was proven, what failed, what remains untested, workarounds, user reactions, technical issues, and implementation assumptions. Use this evidence for proposal, disqualification, or next validation.

### Avoid pilot-to-nowhere patterns and prepare transition if successful

If the buyer repeatedly extends scope, misses meetings, withholds data, or avoids decision owners, reassess intent. The correct action may be pause, narrow, charge, or disqualify.

Define what happens after success: proposal, security review, contract, implementation plan, data migration, rollout, or customer success handoff.

### Decide whether the pilot should be paid and protect production boundaries

If the pilot requires significant services, custom configuration, data migration, technical support, or production-like work, evaluate whether it should be paid or contractually bounded. Free pilots can train buyers to consume delivery resources without commitment.

The price is less important than mutual seriousness and scope control.

Some buyers try to use a pilot as a live deployment. Define whether the pilot is sandbox, limited production, test data, controlled cohort, or full production. Clarify support level, liability boundaries, data handling, and what happens to pilot data afterward.

Production use requires stronger governance.

### Include exit criteria for no decision and preserve pilot learning for the handoff

State what happens if the buyer misses milestones, does not provide data, avoids final evaluation, or asks for indefinite extension. Options may include pause, paid extension, scope reduction, or closing the evaluation.

No-decision criteria protect both teams.

A pilot creates evidence about users, data, integrations, objections, adoption, support needs, and buyer responsibilities. Capture that evidence for proposal, implementation, customer success, and product teams.

Do not let pilot knowledge stay with the sales engineer or seller after close.

## Common Traps

- Starting a pilot without a decision it supports.
- Defining success as "they liked it."
- Letting pilot scope expand until nothing is conclusive; accepting no buyer owner, data, timeline, or decision meeting
- Providing unpaid implementation under the label of pilot; using sensitive production data without proper review
- Waiting until the end to discuss results; treating technical completion as business success
- Ignoring missed buyer commitments; ending a successful pilot without transition plan
- Running high-effort pilots for free without scope, contract, or commitment; allowing a pilot to become production use without governance
- Letting pilots extend indefinitely because no no-decision exit criteria exist; losing pilot evidence during proposal, implementation, or customer success handoff

## Self-Check

- Is the post-pilot decision explicit?
- Are success and failure criteria measurable or observable?
- Are baseline, target, time window, data source, and decision owner defined?
- Is pilot scope limited to the use case and risk being tested?
- Are buyer owners, data, access, users, meetings, timeline, and decision process committed?
- Are support, implementation, services, and post-sale boundaries clear?
- Are data, security, privacy, access, retention, and deletion handled safely?; is evaluation cadence scheduled before kickoff?
- Are proof, gaps, workarounds, reactions, and assumptions documented?; is there a transition plan after success or a stop path after failure?
- Has paid versus free pilot scope been considered based on seller effort and buyer commitment?; are sandbox, limited production, test data, support, liability, and data disposition boundaries clear?
- Are no-decision and missed-milestone exit criteria defined?; is pilot learning captured for proposal, implementation, customer success, and product handoff?
