# Technical Discovery And Fit Check

Technical discovery is where sales claims meet the buyer's operating reality. A deal can have strong business pain and still fail because of architecture, data, security, integration, scale, or implementation constraints. Weak technical discovery either goes too shallow and misses blockers or goes too deep before the business case is clear. This skill helps the agent validate technical fit at the right depth and with the right owners.

## Core Rules

### Tie technical questions to business use

Ask technical questions because they affect the buyer's desired workflow, risk, timeline, or success criteria. Do not collect architecture trivia. Know which technical facts determine whether the business outcome is feasible.

Technical discovery should serve the deal decision.

### Identify systems and data flow

Map source systems, destination systems, systems of record, data owners, integrations, APIs, exports, sync frequency, identity provider, reporting stack, and manual workarounds. Identify where data is created, transformed, approved, and consumed.

Data flow is often where hidden implementation work lives.

### Check security, privacy, and compliance constraints

Ask about security review, data residency, access controls, audit logs, encryption, SSO, permissions, retention, privacy requirements, regulatory obligations, and vendor risk process. Use approved language and route formal answers to qualified owners.

Do not improvise compliance claims.

### Validate scale and performance expectations

Understand user count, transaction volume, data volume, concurrency, latency needs, uptime expectations, reporting frequency, and peak periods. Some requirements may exceed standard configuration.

Scale questions should be checked before proposal commitments.

### Separate current capability from implementation work

Clarify what works out of the box, what is configurable, what requires services, what requires partner work, what depends on buyer resources, and what is unsupported. Avoid turning setup effort into a vague "yes."

Fit includes effort, not only possibility.

### Involve technical owners early enough

Bring sales engineering, implementation, security, product, or solutions teams when requirements affect feasibility, risk, or commitments. Do not wait until procurement or pilot to surface hard questions.

Specialists need context and a specific ask.

### Capture unknowns and proof needed

Technical discovery should produce a list of confirmed facts, assumptions, open questions, proof needed, owners, and due dates. Unknowns should not disappear from the deal narrative.

### Watch for non-fit and scope drift and translate technical findings into buyer language

Required custom development, unsupported integrations, strict data residency, impossible migration, or performance requirements beyond limits may require disqualification, scope change, or roadmap-safe product discussion.

Technical discovery should not stay as internal architecture notes. Summarize what the technical fact means for the buyer: timeline, cost, risk, user experience, implementation effort, security approval, or success criteria. This helps non-technical stakeholders make decisions.

For example, an API rate limit may mean delayed reporting, not just a technical constraint. A data residency requirement may mean contract review and deployment limitation, not just a checkbox.

### Confirm the buyer's technical owner and preserve formal answer boundaries

Identify who can validate technical facts, provide access, approve architecture, answer security questions, and commit implementation resources. If the seller only speaks with a business buyer, technical fit may be assumed rather than proven.

Technical owners should be involved before proposal commitments depend on their work.

Some questions require official documentation or specialist response: security, privacy, compliance, uptime, AI behavior, data processing, accessibility, and legal terms. Do not let casual technical discovery become a formal representation.

Route formal answers through the approved channel and record what was provided.

## Common Traps

- Asking technical questions disconnected from business outcome.
- Accepting "we integrate with that" without understanding data flow.
- Missing security, privacy, data residency, or vendor risk review until late.
- Treating scale and performance expectations as standard without checking; describing implementation effort as simple product capability
- Bringing specialists without context or a clear ask; losing technical unknowns after the call
- Hiding technical non-fit because business interest is strong; letting technical discovery become a feature debate before pain is qualified
- Making compliance, uptime, or architecture claims without approved evidence; keeping technical findings in internal jargon that business buyers cannot use
- Assuming technical fit without speaking to the buyer's technical owner; treating informal technical answers as formal security, privacy, compliance, or uptime commitments

## Self-Check

- Are technical questions tied to workflow, risk, timeline, or success criteria?
- Are systems, data flow, owners, integrations, APIs, identity, reporting, and workarounds mapped?
- Are security, privacy, compliance, data residency, access, audit, encryption, SSO, retention, and vendor review checked?
- Are scale, volume, latency, uptime, concurrency, and peak-period expectations captured?
- Are out-of-box, configurable, services, partner, buyer-owned, and unsupported work separated?
- Are specialists involved with context and specific ask where needed?
- Are confirmed facts, assumptions, unknowns, proof, owners, and due dates documented?
- Are non-fit requirements surfaced rather than hidden?; does technical discovery update scope, next step, and forecast risk?
- Are sensitive technical claims limited to approved evidence?; are technical findings translated into buyer impact, timeline, cost, risk, and implementation meaning?
- Is the buyer's technical owner identified and engaged where needed?; are formal-answer boundaries and approved evidence channels respected?
