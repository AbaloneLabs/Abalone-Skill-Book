# Handoff Ready CRM Record

A handoff-ready CRM record lets another owner continue work without rediscovering the deal. It is not enough for the opportunity to be closed or the required fields to be filled. Agents often leave value promises, scope assumptions, risks, buyer responsibilities, and special terms scattered across notes and attachments. This skill helps the agent prepare CRM records that protect the customer experience after ownership changes.

## Core Rules

### Summarize what was sold and why

Include buyer problem, desired outcome, selected scope, value drivers, success criteria, and decision rationale. Post-sale teams need to know the business reason behind the purchase, not only the products on the order form.

### Record stakeholders and roles and preserve promises and boundaries

Name champion, economic buyer, signer, admin, technical owner, procurement, legal, security, implementation owner, executive sponsor, and customer success counterpart where known. Include influence, concerns, and communication preferences when relevant.

Document approved commitments, assumptions, exclusions, special terms, support expectations, implementation scope, timeline, and buyer responsibilities. Also document what was explicitly not included. Boundaries prevent delivery surprise.

### Include value measurement plan and include a responsibility matrix

Record baseline, target metrics, data source, buyer owner, review cadence, and caveats. If value was sold but not measurable yet, state the missing baseline and plan to define it.

Handoff should show seller, implementation, customer success, support, technical, and buyer responsibilities for the first phase. Name who owns kickoff, data access, configuration, training, stakeholder communication, executive updates, and success review. Responsibility gaps cause early customer confusion.

### Highlight expectation risks and preserve commercial context for renewal

Flag places where buyer expectations may exceed contract or delivery reality: aggressive timeline, assumed customization, unclear support level, unconfirmed integration, broad success claim, or special executive promise. These risks should be managed before kickoff, not discovered in it.

Record discount rationale, ramp structure, expansion expectation, temporary concessions, payment terms, renewal baseline, and any condition that affects future commercial conversations. Renewal teams need to know whether the first contract was a standard deal or a strategic exception.

### Validate data and billing readiness and define handoff acceptance criteria

Make sure account, billing contact, invoice terms, tax details, start date, subscription term, services line items, and purchase order requirements are correct. Handoff quality includes revenue operations accuracy, not only customer success context.

The receiving owner should know what makes the handoff acceptable: complete stakeholder map, final contract links, implementation scope, success metrics, risks, commercial exceptions, billing readiness, and next action. If acceptance criteria are missing, handoff can become a meeting rather than a quality gate.

### Include customer-facing recap alignment and mark open questions separately from commitments

When appropriate, make sure the handoff record matches the final buyer recap. If internal teams see one version of scope and the customer heard another, kickoff will expose the gap. Customer-facing recap alignment reduces early trust risk.

Open questions are not promises. If data migration detail, integration design, admin availability, or success metric baseline is still unresolved, label it as open and assign an owner. This keeps post-sale teams from inheriting assumed commitments.

### Surface unresolved risks and link final artifacts

Handoff should include open risks: data readiness, adoption, integration, legal terms, security concerns, stakeholder alignment, budget sensitivity, implementation capacity, or executive expectations. Hiding risk makes post-sale teams start blind.

Attach or link final proposal, order form, contract, SOW, mutual action plan, business case, security responses, pricing approval, discount rationale, and important recaps. Use final versions, not drafts.

### Reconcile CRM fields and narrative and explain negotiation context

Products, amount, term, start date, services, support level, usage, renewal date, owner, and stage should match the written summary and contract artifacts. Conflicts create billing and delivery risk.

Record concessions, why they were granted, expiration dates, conditions, buyer sensitivities, and renewal implications. Customer success needs to know where expectations may be fragile.

### Make next post-sale action clear and validate with receiving owner

State kickoff owner, timing, required buyer preparation, internal preparation, and any immediate follow-up. Handoff is incomplete if the next owner must ask what to do first.

When possible, review the record with implementation or customer success before declaring handoff complete. Their questions reveal missing context.

## Common Traps

- Treating closed-won status as handoff readiness.
- Listing products without business rationale; omitting stakeholder roles and concerns
- Failing to document assumptions, exclusions, and buyer responsibilities; selling value without a measurement plan
- Hiding unresolved risks to make the deal look clean; linking draft artifacts instead of final versions
- Letting CRM fields conflict with contracts or summaries; forgetting negotiation concessions and renewal implications
- Leaving no clear first post-sale action; skipping receiving-owner review
- Making the customer repeat discovery after purchase; omitting responsibility ownership for the first phase; hiding expectation gaps to make the handoff look cleaner; losing commercial context needed for renewal
- Treating billing and revenue operations details as separate from handoff readiness; treating handoff as complete without acceptance criteria; letting internal handoff differ from the final customer recap; mixing open questions with confirmed commitments

## Self-Check

- Does the record explain what was sold and why?
- Are key stakeholders and roles recorded?; are promises, assumptions, exclusions, special terms, and buyer responsibilities visible?
- Is value measurement defined with baseline, owner, source, cadence, and caveats?; are unresolved risks surfaced?
- Are final artifacts linked?; do CRM fields, narrative, and contract artifacts agree?
- Is negotiation context documented?; is the next post-sale action clear?
- Has the receiving owner reviewed or been given a chance to question the record?; can implementation or customer success act without rediscovery?
- Would the customer experience continuity from sales to post-sale?; is there a clear responsibility matrix for seller, post-sale teams, and buyer?
- Are expectation risks highlighted before kickoff?; is renewal-relevant commercial context preserved?
- Are billing, tax, PO, start date, and services details accurate?; are handoff acceptance criteria explicit?
- Does the record match the final customer-facing recap?; are open questions separated from commitments with owners?
