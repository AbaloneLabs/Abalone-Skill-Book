# Free Trial Conversion And Sales Assist

A free trial is not automatically a buying process. It is a period where users test value, fit, setup effort, team interest, and risk. Agents often push for conversion based on time remaining or usage level, while missing whether the user has reached activation, whether blockers are product or support issues, and whether a buyer decision exists. This skill helps the agent support trial conversion by making the trial useful, measurable, and respectful.

Use this skill when assisting trials, freemium upgrades, trial extension requests, in-app purchase signals, product evaluations, or trial-to-paid conversations. The agent should help users reach value and clarify purchase path without applying pressure disconnected from product success.

## Core Rules

### Identify the trial's success criteria

Ask what the user or account needs to prove during the trial: workflow fit, team adoption, integration, reporting, security, time savings, collaboration, data import, admin control, or business case. If success criteria are unknown, conversion outreach becomes generic.

Trial success should be framed from the buyer's perspective. The goal is not simply "use more features"; it is to validate the outcome that would justify payment.

### Separate activation from purchase readiness

Activation means the user has experienced meaningful product value. Purchase readiness means the right person can buy, budget exists, risk is acceptable, and the plan fits. A user can be activated but unable to purchase, or ready to buy but blocked by setup.

Do not push paid conversion only because the trial clock is ending. Check value reached, blockers, decision owner, plan fit, and procurement or billing path.

### Diagnose blockers before selling

Common trial blockers include unclear setup, missing data, integration failure, permission issue, feature misunderstanding, unsupported workflow, low team adoption, security concern, pricing confusion, or lack of buyer authority. Treat blockers as diagnosis points.

If the user needs support, route or solve support first. Selling into frustration may produce a short-term conversion and long-term churn.

### Use trial extensions deliberately

An extension should have a reason, success criteria, owner, and next decision date. Extensions can help when setup was delayed, key stakeholder was unavailable, or evaluation needs a defined extra test. Extensions are weak when they only postpone an unclear decision.

Do not grant repeated extensions without learning what is preventing purchase. If the blocker is fit, authority, budget, or value, time alone will not solve it.

### Connect usage evidence to a buyer conversation

Usage can help tell the value story: active users, repeated workflow, completed tasks, collaboration, saved time, or avoided pain. Use evidence carefully and respectfully. Ask whether the observed usage reflects the buyer's intended process.

For team trials, identify who used the product, who owns the outcome, who pays, and who would be affected by rollout. Do not assume the most active user is the economic buyer.

### Recommend the right plan, not the fastest close

Plan recommendation should match use case, user count, data needs, admin control, security, support expectation, integration, and growth path. Overselling creates churn; underselling creates quick upgrade friction or missing capabilities.

If the buyer needs enterprise controls, custom terms, security review, or procurement, route out of a pure self-serve close. If the buyer only needs a small plan, do not force an enterprise motion.

### Set post-conversion expectations

Conversion is not success if onboarding, billing, data migration, or team rollout fails. Confirm what happens after upgrade: account activation, payment, invoice, admin setup, data retention from trial, support level, onboarding resources, and next milestone.

Avoid implying hands-on implementation or premium support unless included or approved.

### Learn from non-conversion

When trials do not convert, capture reason: no value, wrong use case, missing feature, price, no authority, security concern, setup friction, competitor, too early, or support failure. Aggregate by segment, source, feature, and activation milestone.

Trial loss data should improve product, onboarding, pricing, targeting, and sales-assist timing.

## Common Traps

- Treating trial expiration as urgency even when value has not been reached.
- Assuming high usage means purchase authority.
- Selling before diagnosing setup or support blockers.
- Granting extensions without success criteria or decision date.
- Using detailed usage data in a way that feels invasive.
- Recommending a higher plan because it closes more revenue rather than because it fits.
- Failing to explain what changes after paid conversion.
- Losing trial non-conversion reasons instead of feeding product and growth learning.

## Self-Check

- Are trial success criteria defined from the user's or account's perspective?
- Has the agent separated activation from purchase readiness?
- Are blockers diagnosed before conversion pressure is applied?
- If an extension is offered, does it have reason, owner, test, and decision date?
- Is usage evidence used respectfully and validated with the buyer?
- Are buyer, admin, active user, and economic owner distinguished?
- Is the recommended plan matched to actual use case, controls, support, and growth needs?
- Are enterprise needs routed out of a simple self-serve close when required?
- Are post-conversion expectations clear?
- Are non-conversion reasons captured for product, onboarding, pricing, and targeting improvement?
