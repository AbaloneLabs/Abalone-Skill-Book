# Call Review And Self Coaching

Call review is not judging whether a seller sounded polished. It is the practice of finding where buyer evidence was created, missed, misunderstood, or converted into the wrong next step. Agents often summarize calls by tone, topic coverage, or generic best practices, then miss the decisive moments: a buyer revealed weak urgency, a stakeholder gap appeared, a claim went unsupported, or the seller accepted a vague next step. This skill helps the agent review calls with enough precision to improve future behavior.

Use this skill when analyzing call recordings, transcripts, discovery notes, demos, qualification calls, negotiation calls, objection handling, or self-coaching reflections. The agent should use call evidence to identify a small number of behavior changes, not produce a long list of generic critiques.

## Core Rules

### Start with the call's intended job

Review the call against its purpose. A discovery call should create understanding of problem, impact, current state, decision process, authority, and next step. A demo should prove relevance against discovered criteria. A negotiation call should clarify interests, tradeoffs, and commitments. A renewal call should surface value, risk, and timeline.

Do not score every call using the same lens. A seller can run a pleasant discovery call and still fail if no decision process was uncovered. A demo can show the right features but fail if it does not connect to buyer pain. Name the call objective before judging execution.

### Use evidence, not impressions

Anchor observations to specific moments, buyer statements, missed questions, seller claims, next-step language, and changes in buyer behavior. If a transcript is available, use timestamps or short references. Avoid vague comments such as "seller needed more confidence" unless the behavior is observable.

Evidence-based review makes self-coaching possible. "At minute 17, the buyer said the CFO owns the budget, but no follow-up asked how the CFO evaluates the project" is better than "dig deeper." The agent should translate impressions into actions a seller can repeat or change.

### Separate buyer evidence from seller activity

Sellers often feel productive when they explain, present, handle objections, or schedule follow-up. Review whether the buyer actually supplied evidence: pain, impact, priority, authority, criteria, budget path, risk, timing, competitor, internal politics, or commitment. Seller activity without buyer evidence may not advance the deal.

Track buyer commitments separately from seller commitments. "Seller will send deck" is weaker than "buyer will share current workflow with operations director by Friday." A strong call creates mutual movement.

### Identify the missed fork in the conversation

Most calls have one or two moments where a different question or choice would have improved the outcome. Look for moments when the buyer gave an ambiguous answer, revealed a new stakeholder, raised a risk, avoided a commitment, showed urgency, or contradicted earlier assumptions. The best coaching often comes from these forks.

Do not rewrite the whole call. Focus on the missed moment that would have changed the next step or evidence quality. This keeps self-coaching actionable rather than overwhelming.

### Evaluate question quality and listening

Strong sales questions are not just open-ended; they move from situation to impact, from interest to priority, from preference to decision, and from objection to underlying concern. Review whether the seller asked follow-ups, let the buyer finish, summarized accurately, and tested assumptions.

Watch for leading questions that invite polite agreement, stacked questions that confuse the buyer, and rapid shifts to pitching after a surface pain. Listening quality shows up in whether the seller's next question uses the buyer's actual words.

### Inspect claims and proof boundaries

Calls are where unsupported promises often enter a deal. Review product claims, implementation timelines, security answers, roadmap hints, pricing assurances, customer outcome claims, and competitor statements. Mark whether each claim was supported, qualified, or routed to the right owner.

The goal is not to make sellers timid. It is to build credibility by matching confidence to evidence. Self-coaching should include claim discipline when the seller overreached or missed a chance to say, "I need to verify that."

### Reduce feedback to one or two practice targets

A call can reveal many improvement areas, but self-coaching works best with a narrow target. Choose the behavior that would most improve future calls: asking economic impact follow-ups, confirming decision process, pausing before demo, summarizing buyer language, testing next-step commitment, or qualifying disinterest earlier.

Define the next experiment. Example: "On the next three discovery calls, after any pain statement, ask for operational impact and current owner before describing product." A practice target is stronger than a general intention.

### Account for transcript and recording limits

Transcripts can misattribute speakers, miss tone, omit visual context, or distort technical terms. Recordings may not capture chat, screen share, pre-call context, or internal constraints. Use them carefully. If a moment is ambiguous, say so rather than treating the transcript as perfect truth.

Also respect consent and privacy rules for recorded calls. Do not include sensitive customer information in coaching summaries beyond what is necessary.

### Convert review into next-call preparation

The review is complete only when it affects future behavior. Update next steps, CRM notes, stakeholder map, open questions, and preparation for the next buyer interaction. If the call revealed a deal risk, it belongs in the opportunity record, not only in a coaching note.

Self-coaching should end with a specific action before the next call and a way to inspect whether the behavior improved.

## Common Traps

- Reviewing whether the seller sounded good instead of whether buyer evidence improved.
- Giving generic feedback such as "ask better questions" without naming the missed moment.
- Treating seller talk time, agenda completion, or slide coverage as proof of a successful call.
- Ignoring buyer commitments and tracking only seller follow-ups.
- Overloading the seller with ten improvement points instead of one or two practice targets.
- Criticizing personality or style when the issue is a specific behavior.
- Trusting transcripts blindly without checking ambiguity, speaker errors, or missing context.
- Keeping call learning separate from CRM, next-call prep, and opportunity risk.

## Self-Check

- Is the call reviewed against its actual objective and sales stage?
- Are observations tied to specific buyer statements, seller behaviors, or moments?
- Does the review distinguish buyer evidence from seller activity?
- Are missed forks in the conversation identified clearly?
- Are question quality, listening, and follow-up depth evaluated with examples?
- Are unsupported claims, roadmap hints, security answers, or delivery promises flagged?
- Does the review choose one or two practice targets rather than a long generic critique?
- Are transcript or recording limitations acknowledged where relevant?
- Are opportunity risks and next steps updated outside the coaching note?
- Is there a concrete next-call experiment and a way to inspect improvement?
