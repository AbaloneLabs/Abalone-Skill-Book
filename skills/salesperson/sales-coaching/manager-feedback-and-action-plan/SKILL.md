# Manager Feedback And Action Plan

Manager feedback should change seller behavior, not merely express a manager's reaction. Agents often turn feedback into broad advice, motivational language, or a list of everything wrong with a deal. That kind of feedback is hard to act on and easy to resist. This skill helps the agent create feedback that is grounded in evidence, focused on controllable behaviors, and converted into an action plan that can be inspected.

Use this skill when drafting coaching feedback, one-on-one notes, call review follow-up, pipeline inspection guidance, performance improvement actions, onboarding coaching, or manager summaries. The agent should help the manager be clear and fair without replacing human judgment about employment decisions, HR process, or sensitive personnel matters.

## Core Rules

### Ground feedback in observable evidence

Feedback should cite specific calls, CRM records, deal moments, emails, forecasts, stage movements, buyer statements, or outcomes. Avoid labels such as "not strategic," "low urgency," or "poor executive presence" unless they are translated into observable behavior.

Evidence-based feedback is easier to accept and easier to improve. "Three late-stage deals were forecast commit without confirmed procurement owner" gives the seller a behavior to fix. "You are too optimistic" invites debate about personality.

### Separate skill, will, process, and circumstance

Before designing an action plan, diagnose the likely source of the gap. Skill gaps require practice and examples. Will or priority gaps require expectation setting. Process gaps require tools, definitions, or stage criteria. Circumstantial gaps may involve territory quality, account timing, product limitation, market change, or internal support.

Do not coach every problem as if it were seller effort. A seller cannot fix an unclear compensation policy, bad routing, or missing product proof through better attitude. At the same time, do not hide controllable behaviors behind market excuses.

### Prioritize the behavior that changes outcomes

A manager may see many issues, but an action plan should focus on the highest-leverage behavior. Examples include confirming economic buyer early, asking impact questions, securing mutual next steps, improving CRM evidence, handling pricing with tradeoffs, or disqualifying poor-fit deals sooner.

Choose behaviors close enough to the seller's work that they can practice within days or weeks. "Become more consultative" is too broad. "On every discovery call, ask who owns the business outcome and what happens if nothing changes" is coachable.

### Make feedback balanced but not vague

Balanced feedback does not require equal praise and criticism. It requires accurate recognition of what worked, what did not, and what should change. Name strengths that can be repeated, not generic encouragement. Name risks plainly without making the feedback personal.

If the seller did something well in a deal that still lost, capture that. If a deal closed despite weak behavior, do not let the win hide the coaching need. Outcomes matter, but they do not fully explain skill.

### Convert feedback into a concrete action plan

An action plan should include target behavior, reason it matters, practice activity, live application, support owner, timeline, evidence of completion, and review date. It should be small enough to execute and specific enough to inspect.

Example structure: "For the next two weeks, before moving any opportunity to proposal, document economic buyer, decision criteria, procurement path, and buyer-owned next step. Manager will review three opportunities every Friday." This is stronger than "improve qualification."

### Define manager support, not only seller tasks

Good coaching requires manager action. The manager may provide call examples, role-play, pre-call planning, deal inspection, shadowing, feedback on emails, or removal of internal blockers. If the plan only assigns work to the seller, it may be performance management rather than coaching.

State what the manager will do and when. Also clarify when other teams such as enablement, solution engineering, revenue operations, or product marketing should help.

### Protect fairness and psychological safety

Feedback should be direct without being humiliating. Avoid sarcasm, public shaming, identity-based comments, speculation about motives, or comparisons that create defensiveness. In written notes, assume the seller, HR, or future managers may read them.

For serious performance issues, follow company process and involve HR or leadership where required. Do not use informal coaching notes to create hidden employment records outside policy.

### Inspect progress with evidence

A coaching plan should have review points and evidence. Evidence may include call moments, CRM fields, buyer commitments, stage conversion quality, forecast accuracy, activity-to-meeting conversion, proposal quality, or manager-observed behavior. Avoid relying only on lagging outcomes such as bookings.

If the behavior does not improve, revisit the diagnosis. The issue may be unclear expectations, insufficient practice, wrong territory, missing enablement, poor fit for role, or lack of accountability.

## Common Traps

- Giving personality feedback instead of behavior feedback.
- Listing every issue and leaving the seller unsure what to do first.
- Treating a won deal as proof the behavior was good or a lost deal as proof it was bad.
- Coaching seller effort when the root cause is process, territory, routing, product, or enablement.
- Writing action plans with vague verbs such as improve, own, be strategic, or communicate better.
- Assigning tasks to the seller without specifying manager support.
- Using pipeline pressure as a substitute for coaching.
- Creating written feedback that would appear unfair, speculative, or humiliating if reviewed later.

## Self-Check

- Is feedback tied to specific evidence rather than impressions or labels?
- Does the diagnosis separate skill, will, process, and circumstance?
- Is the action plan focused on one or two high-leverage behaviors?
- Are strengths named in a way the seller can repeat?
- Are risks stated directly without personality judgment?
- Does the plan include target behavior, practice, live application, owner, timeline, review date, and evidence?
- Is manager support clearly defined?
- Are HR-sensitive or serious performance issues routed through appropriate company process?
- Will progress be inspected with behavior evidence, not only final bookings?
- If improvement does not happen, is there a plan to revisit the diagnosis rather than repeat the same advice?
