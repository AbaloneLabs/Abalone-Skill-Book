# Transactional Close And Risk Check

A transactional close should be fast, but not careless. SMB buyers may have shorter cycles, smaller committees, and simpler contracts, yet the seller still must confirm fit, scope, authority, payment, terms, and expectations. Agents often treat small deals as too minor for risk review, which can lead to refunds, churn, support overload, billing disputes, or customers who bought the wrong thing. This skill helps the agent perform a lightweight final check before closing without turning a transactional sale into an enterprise process.

Use this skill when preparing order forms, checkout support, short-cycle proposals, end-of-call closes, SMB renewals, first purchase, plan upgrades, billing handoff, or final buyer confirmation. The agent should protect speed while catching the few risks most likely to create post-sale problems.

## Core Rules

### Confirm the buyer is buying the right thing

Before closing, restate the use case, plan, tier, users, key feature need, expected outcome, and known limitation. The buyer should understand what is included and what is not. A transactional sale often fails when the buyer assumes a feature, integration, support level, or service is included because no one corrected the assumption.

Do not close through confusion. If the buyer cannot explain how they will use the product or names a requirement the product cannot meet, pause, route, narrow scope, or disqualify.

### Check authority and payment readiness

Small deals still need someone who can buy. Confirm signer or checkout owner, billing entity, payment method, purchase order need, tax requirements, invoice contact, renewal preference, and any approval threshold. If the buyer says approval is informal, clarify what must happen after the call.

Payment failures, PO surprises, and invoice disputes can erase the value of a fast close. The final check should include practical billing readiness, not only verbal intent.

### Keep terms and commercial promises standard

Transactional motions rely on standard terms, pricing, packaging, onboarding, cancellation, support, and refund rules. Use approved language. If the buyer requests custom legal terms, special support, unusual billing, data commitments, custom implementation, or nonstandard cancellation rights, route to the appropriate path.

Do not create an exception because the deal seems small. Small exceptions are often poorly tracked and expensive to service.

### Test for hidden implementation or support burden

Ask what must happen after purchase for the buyer to succeed. Check setup complexity, data import, integrations, admin ownership, training needs, number of users, timeline, and support expectations. A buyer may be ready to pay but not ready to implement.

If the success path requires hands-on services, technical scoping, security approval, or migration help not included in the package, make that visible before closing. Selling a self-serve plan to a customer who needs managed implementation can create immediate dissatisfaction.

### Avoid pressure that creates regret

Transactional urgency can be legitimate: promotion end date, billing cycle, event deadline, limited implementation window, or budget timing. But urgency should be truthful and relevant. Do not use artificial scarcity, guilt, fear, or misleading discounts to force a quick signature.

Buyers who feel pushed may cancel, dispute charges, leave poor reviews, or avoid expansion. A clean close should make the buyer confident, not cornered.

### Capture a concise close record

Record the essentials: use case, plan, price, discount if any, term, payment or invoice status, buyer owner, key expectation, known limitation, promised materials, onboarding next step, and support or success handoff. In a fast process, this record is the protection against memory gaps.

The record should be short enough for a high-volume motion but clear enough for billing, support, customer success, or another salesperson to understand what was sold.

### Route exceptions deliberately

Some transactional deals are not truly transactional. Red flags include regulated data, security questionnaire, custom contract, procurement process, multi-location rollout, high user count, integration dependency, heavy migration, partner involvement, credit risk, unusual discount, or executive visibility. Define when to pause the close and route to another motion.

Routing is not failure. It prevents the wrong sales process from creating the wrong customer expectation.

### Confirm the first post-purchase step

A close is not complete when payment is captured or order form is signed. Confirm what happens next: account activation, admin setup, onboarding email, support contact, implementation call, invoice delivery, receipt, or training resource. The buyer should know where to go and who owns the next step.

If the next step belongs to another team or automated flow, make sure the buyer's expectation matches that process. Do not imply white-glove support when the plan is self-serve.

## Common Traps

- Treating small deal size as a reason to skip fit and expectation checks.
- Closing when the buyer's key requirement is assumed but not confirmed.
- Ignoring billing entity, payment method, PO, tax, or invoice contact until after the sale.
- Offering custom terms, support, or implementation in a standardized motion.
- Using discounts or deadline pressure that create buyer regret.
- Selling self-serve to buyers who clearly need scoped implementation or support.
- Failing to record what was promised because the deal was "simple."
- Completing signature or payment without confirming the buyer's first post-purchase step.

## Self-Check

- Can the buyer's use case, plan, included scope, excluded scope, and key limitation be stated clearly?
- Is the buyer's authority or approval path sufficient for the purchase?
- Are billing entity, payment method, PO need, tax requirement, and invoice contact confirmed where relevant?
- Are terms, pricing, onboarding, cancellation, support, and refund promises standard or properly routed?
- Has the agent checked for hidden implementation, migration, integration, training, or support needs?
- Is urgency truthful, relevant, and free of manipulative pressure?
- Are regulated, security, procurement, custom contract, large rollout, or credit-risk flags routed out of the transactional path?
- Is the close record concise but sufficient for billing, support, success, or future account ownership?
- Does the buyer know the first post-purchase step and who owns it?
- Would this close still look appropriate if reviewed after a refund, support escalation, or churn event?
