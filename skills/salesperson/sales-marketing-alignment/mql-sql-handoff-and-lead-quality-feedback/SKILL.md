# MQL SQL Handoff And Lead Quality Feedback

The MQL-to-SQL handoff is not just a status change in CRM. It is the point where marketing's demand signal becomes a sales decision about priority, fit, and next action. Agents often treat the handoff as a simple routing problem and miss the evidence that should travel with the lead, the reason sales accepts or rejects it, and the feedback marketing needs to improve targeting. This skill helps the agent inspect lead handoff as a shared learning loop rather than a blame exchange.

Use this skill when reviewing MQLs, SQL conversion, sales-accepted leads, lead routing, response SLAs, campaign-source quality, disqualification notes, or sales-to-marketing feedback. The agent should make handoff criteria and evidence visible.

## Core Rules

### Define the qualification boundary

Clarify what makes a lead marketing-qualified, sales-accepted, and sales-qualified. The criteria may include ICP fit, persona, intent signal, engagement level, firmographics, product interest, buying timeframe, region, account ownership, and exclusion rules. Without definitions, teams argue about lead quality after the fact.

Do not let a score alone define readiness unless the score's inputs are understood. A high score from content engagement may not mean buying intent; a low score from a strategic target account may still deserve sales attention.

### Preserve lead source and intent context

Sales needs to know why the lead was routed: demo request, pricing page, event attendee, webinar topic, referral, trial usage, content download, ad campaign, partner source, or account-based motion. The source changes the first conversation.

If source context is missing, the seller may start with a generic pitch and waste the signal marketing generated. Require enough context to tailor the opening without overwhelming the rep.

### Accept or reject with evidence

Sales should not reject leads with vague reasons such as "bad lead" or "not interested." Use evidence: wrong segment, student, vendor, competitor, unsupported region, no business email, existing customer routing, duplicate, no reachable contact, bad data, no relevant pain, or no response after defined attempts.

Likewise, marketing should not treat every rejection as sales avoidance. Inspect whether sales followed the SLA and used appropriate attempts before declaring the lead poor.

### Separate lead quality from follow-up quality

Poor conversion may come from bad targeting, weak routing, slow response, wrong message, bad data, poor rep execution, account conflict, offer mismatch, or unclear next step. Diagnose before assigning blame. Lead quality and follow-up quality are different problems.

Compare conversion by source, campaign, segment, persona, response time, rep, sequence, and account tier. Aggregated conversion hides useful patterns.

### Keep SLAs realistic and buyer-centered

Response SLA should depend on intent. A demo request may require rapid contact. A low-intent content download may need nurture. A target account engagement may require account-owner coordination before outreach.

Do not create SLAs that reward shallow activity. A rep who sends a generic email within five minutes may satisfy the clock while wasting the buyer signal. SLA quality should include relevance and next-step ownership.

### Close the feedback loop with marketing

Feedback should be structured enough to improve targeting, scoring, campaign messaging, forms, routing, content, and nurture. Useful feedback includes disqualification reason, buyer language, objections, missing fields, source quality, persona mismatch, or campaign promise mismatch.

Avoid anecdotal feedback that overweights one memorable lead. Use examples plus patterns.

### Watch for data and consent constraints

Marketing-sourced leads may have consent limits, source restrictions, or regional communication rules. Sales should know whether and how the contact may be followed up. Do not assume that a lead in CRM is contactable through every channel.

If consent or suppression status is unclear, resolve it before outreach.

### Maintain shared ownership

The handoff works only when marketing, SDR, AE, revenue operations, and sales leadership share definitions, routing rules, and review cadence. If one team changes scoring, campaigns, territory rules, or SLA expectations, the handoff should be updated.

Treat repeated handoff friction as a process design issue, not a personality conflict.

## Common Traps

- Treating a lead score as proof of sales readiness without understanding its inputs.
- Dropping campaign, content, or intent context before the seller engages.
- Rejecting leads with vague comments that marketing cannot act on.
- Blaming marketing for low conversion when sales response is slow or generic.
- Blaming sales for rejection when lead source quality or routing is poor.
- Using the same SLA for high-intent demo requests and low-intent content leads.
- Ignoring consent and suppression status for marketing-sourced contacts.
- Running feedback meetings from anecdotes instead of source and segment patterns.

## Self-Check

- Are MQL, sales-accepted, and SQL criteria clearly defined?
- Does the seller receive source, campaign, intent, persona, and relevant account context?
- Are acceptance and rejection reasons evidence-based and standardized?
- Is conversion analyzed by source, segment, persona, response time, rep, and message?
- Are lead quality issues separated from sales follow-up quality issues?
- Is the SLA matched to buyer intent and not only speed?
- Does marketing receive actionable feedback on targeting, scoring, forms, routing, and content?
- Are consent, suppression, and regional contact rules visible before outreach?
- Is there a shared cadence for reviewing handoff health?
- Would both sales and marketing agree the handoff record explains what happened?
