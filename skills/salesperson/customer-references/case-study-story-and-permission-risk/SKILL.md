# Case Study Story And Permission Risk

A case study is not a trophy. It is a public or semi-public claim about a customer's situation, actions, and outcomes. Agents often simplify stories until they become misleading, omit the customer's role in success, or use details beyond permission. This skill helps the agent use case studies in a way that is accurate, relevant, and respectful.

## Core Rules

### Confirm permission scope and preserve the real context

Know whether permission covers logo, name, quote, metrics, industry, use case, public website, sales deck, private reference, region, or time period. Permission is not all-or-nothing.

Include enough context for the story to be meaningful: starting problem, scope, buyer maturity, implementation path, customer responsibilities, timeline, and constraints. Over-simplified stories mislead buyers.

### Use approved metrics and claims and identify who owns approval

Only use approved numbers, timeframes, quotes, and outcomes. Do not round up, generalize, or combine metrics from different contexts.

Permission may come from customer marketing, legal, executive sponsor, procurement, communications, or account owner. Know who approved the story and who must approve changes. Informal customer enthusiasm is not formal permission.

### Separate internal story from external story and define anonymization risk

Sales may know implementation struggles, politics, or commercial context that should not appear externally. Keep internal learning separate from customer-approved story. Do not let internal detail leak into buyer-facing proof.

An anonymized story may still identify the customer through industry, geography, size, quote, metric, or unique workflow. Check whether anonymization is sufficient before sharing.

### Keep story aligned with current product and plan withdrawal or update

If the case involved old packaging, custom services, retired features, or exceptional support, state the difference or avoid using it. The story should not imply a current buyer gets the same conditions by default.

Customers may revoke permission, change relationship status, merge, rebrand, or experience new outcomes. Track how stories will be updated or retired.

### Align story with legal and brand review and avoid hero narratives that erase risk

Named stories may need approval from legal, brand, communications, product marketing, account owner, and the customer. Do not assume a sales-edited story can be used externally without review.

A strong story can still include constraints and lessons. Removing every difficulty may make the story less credible and may cause buyers to underestimate implementation effort. Use approved, balanced language.

### Define audience and distribution before drafting and separate product impact from customer work

A story for a private enterprise sales deck can include different detail than a public website, RFP response, press release, or analyst briefing. Decide audience and distribution early because permission, review, and risk depend on where the story will travel.

Success often depends on buyer process change, data cleanup, training, adoption, or executive sponsorship. Do not imply the product alone caused the result.

### Match story to buyer relevance and avoid exposing sensitive details

Select stories by use case, scale, industry, risk, region, and maturity. A great story can still be the wrong story.

Do not reveal internal systems, pricing, staffing, failures, security details, or strategy unless explicitly approved. Anonymized stories still need care if details identify the customer.

### Keep stories current and prepare caveats for sales use

Customer status, product capability, relationship health, and permission can change. Reconfirm old stories before heavy use.

Sales should know what the story proves, what it does not prove, and what conditions matter. Case studies should not become universal promises.

### Respect customer review rights and track story usage

If the customer requires review or approval before use, follow it. Do not modify quotes or context after approval without checking.

Record where named stories are used, especially in public, regulated, or strategic contexts. Overuse or misuse can create relationship risk.

## Common Traps

- Assuming logo permission includes all claims; simplifying story until it is misleading
- Using unapproved metrics or quotes; imlying product alone caused success
- Choosing impressive but irrelevant stories; revealing sensitive details
- Reusing stale case studies; letting sales turn story into guarantee
- Ignoring customer review rights; failing to track usage
- Making anonymized examples identifiable; using a case study from an unhealthy customer
- Treating informal enthusiasm as formal approval; mixing internal account details into external story; assuming anonymization is safe without checking identifiability; using old stories that imply unavailable product or support conditions
- Having no process to update or retire stories; skipping legal, brand, or customer review for named stories; making the story so polished that it hides real implementation constraints; drafting before audience and distribution are defined

## Self-Check

- Is permission scope confirmed?; is real context preserved?
- Are metrics and claims approved?; is customer contribution to success clear?
- Is story relevant to buyer context?; are sensitive details protected?
- Is the story current?; are caveats clear for sales use?
- Are review rights respected?; is usage tracked?
- Would the customer recognize and accept the story?; would the buyer understand what does and does not transfer?
- Is approval owner identified?; are internal and external story details separated?
- Is anonymization risk checked?; does story reflect current product and support conditions?
- Is update or withdrawal process clear?; are legal, brand, and customer reviews complete where needed?
- Does the story preserve enough constraint to stay credible?; are audience and distribution scope defined before use?
