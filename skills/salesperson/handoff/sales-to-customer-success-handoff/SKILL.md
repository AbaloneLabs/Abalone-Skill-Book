# Sales To Customer Success Handoff

Sales-to-customer-success handoff is where commercial intent becomes customer reality. A weak handoff forces customer success to rediscover value, rebuild trust, and uncover risks after the customer expects execution. Agents often summarize what was bought but omit why it was bought, what was promised, and what could go wrong. This skill helps the agent transfer enough context for customer success to deliver and renew the relationship.

## Core Rules

### Transfer the business story

Customer success needs the buyer problem, trigger event, desired outcomes, decision criteria, value drivers, cost of inaction, and why this solution was selected. Product line items alone do not explain success.

### Map stakeholders and relationships

Include champion, economic buyer, executive sponsor, users, admin, technical owner, procurement, legal, security, blockers, and supporters. Note relationship strength, concerns, and communication preferences.

### Document promises and boundaries and include value realization plan

List approved commitments, assumptions, exclusions, special terms, support expectations, implementation responsibilities, timeline, and buyer obligations. Customer success should know what was not promised as clearly as what was.

Share success metrics, baseline, target, review cadence, data source, buyer owner, and caveats. If value measurement is not yet defined, state the plan to define it.

### Provide a lifecycle view and include customer communication history

Customer success needs to understand not only kickoff but the first renewal path: expected time to value, adoption milestones, executive review points, expansion signals, renewal risks, and contract dates. A lifecycle view helps CS avoid optimizing only for launch while missing renewal health.

Share important recaps, unresolved questions, sensitive moments, prior objections, executive messages, and commitments already communicated to the customer. CS should know what the customer has heard and what tone has been established.

### Identify internal follow-up owners and mark confidence levels

If sales owes a document, legal owes clarification, product owes a roadmap response, finance owes billing setup, or implementation owes a technical review, name the owner and date. Handoff should not turn open sales obligations into vague CS work.

Some handoff facts are confirmed; others are inferred or buyer-reported. Label confidence so CS knows where to validate. This is especially important for value metrics, stakeholder influence, and implementation readiness.

### Attach source material, not only summaries and define what sales still owns after handoff

Summaries are useful, but customer success may need the final proposal, recap, MAP, business case, contract, SOW, and key customer emails. Source material lets CS verify nuance and avoid relying on a seller's compressed interpretation.

Some responsibilities may remain with sales for a short period: executive intro, commercial clarification, reference request, unresolved concession, or expansion context. Name what sales still owns so CS does not inherit everything by default.

### Surface risks honestly and preserve negotiation context

Name adoption risk, data issues, integration complexity, stakeholder politics, budget sensitivity, procurement scars, legal terms, support expectations, or weak readiness. Hiding risk makes customer success less effective.

Discounts, ramps, concessions, reference discussions, special terms, and renewal expectations affect future relationship management. Document why they happened and when they expire.

### Define first post-sale actions and avoid customer re-discovery

State who contacts the customer, when kickoff happens, what preparation is needed, and what internal work must happen first. Avoid ownership gaps immediately after signature.

Customer success may need to validate details, but should not ask the customer to repeat core discovery because sales failed to hand off. Repetition signals internal disorganization.

### Include expansion and renewal context and confirm handoff acceptance

Note departments not included, future use cases, expansion triggers, renewal risks, contract terms, and stakeholders who may matter later. Expansion context should not become pressure before value is delivered.

The receiving customer success owner should be able to ask questions and confirm readiness. Handoff is complete when the next owner can act, not when sales sends notes.

## Common Traps

- Summarizing products without business story; omitting stakeholder politics and relationship context
- Hiding assumptions, exclusions, and buyer responsibilities; selling value without handing off measurement plan
- Downplaying risks to protect deal optics; forgetting concessions and renewal implications
- Leaving no clear first post-sale action; making the customer repeat discovery
- Treating expansion signals as immediate upsell pressure; sending handoff notes without receiving-owner acceptance
- Keeping important context in private seller memory; treating handoff as administrative afterthought
- Handing off kickoff details without renewal and lifecycle context; omitting important customer communication history
- Leaving open internal obligations without owners; presenting inferred handoff facts as confirmed; providing only a summary without source artifacts; assuming sales owns nothing after handoff

## Self-Check

- Is the business story clear?
- Are stakeholders, roles, concerns, and relationship strength mapped?
- Are promises, assumptions, exclusions, special terms, and buyer responsibilities documented?; is value realization plan included?
- Are risks surfaced honestly?; is negotiation and renewal context preserved?
- Are first post-sale actions clear?; does handoff prevent unnecessary customer rediscovery?
- Are expansion signals and renewal risks included responsibly?; has the customer success owner accepted the handoff?
- Can customer success act from the record?; does the handoff protect trust after close?
- Does handoff include a lifecycle view through renewal?; is customer communication history included?
- Are open internal obligations assigned to owners?; are confidence levels labeled for uncertain facts?
- Are source artifacts attached or linked?; are any remaining sales-owned actions explicit?
