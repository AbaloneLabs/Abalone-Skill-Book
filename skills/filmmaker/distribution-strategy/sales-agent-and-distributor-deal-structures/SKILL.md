# Sales Agent And Distributor Deal Structures

Sales Agent And Distributor Deal Structures is a filmmaking judgment problem inside deal architecture, recoupment position, fees and expenses, term and exclusivity, approval rights, and the long relationship between a producer and the companies that sell the film. Agents often see only the visible creative or commercial request: get a sales agent, sign the distributor, take the MG, or close the deal. The harder work is identifying which invisible constraint actually controls the answer: recoupment position that determines who gets paid first, term and exclusivity that can lock up rights for years, expenses that are recouped before the producer sees money, approval authority that shifts creative or release control away from the filmmaker, and reporting transparency that determines whether the deal ever pays back.

Use this skill before giving deal advice that could lock a creative choice, spend money, expose people, or create a deliverable problem later. Good distribution guidance should preserve the intended film and its revenue potential while naming the tradeoffs that make the choice real. It should be specific enough for a producer, filmmaker, sales agent, entertainment lawyer, or financier to understand what evidence supports the recommendation and what still needs qualified legal and business review before any agreement is signed.

## Core Rules

### Read The Deal As A Cash Waterfall, Not A Headline Number
A distribution deal is defined by the order in which money flows, not by the size of the advance. Map the recoupment waterfall: gross receipts, distributor deductions for fees and expenses, MG recoupment position, sales agent commission and expenses, third-party participations, and finally the producer and investor share. Two deals with identical MGs can return radically different amounts to the producer depending on expense definitions, fee bases, and cross-collateralization. Always model the waterfall before comparing offers, and identify which deductions are capped, which are uncapped, and which are subject to approval.

### Separate MG Deals From Commission Deals
A minimum guarantee deal pays an advance against future revenues, which the distributor recoups before paying the producer, while a commission deal pays the producer a percentage of receipts after the distributor takes its fee. Each structure creates different incentives: an MG gives the distributor skin in the game and a motive to recoup, while a commission deal may leave the producer with more upside but less marketing commitment. Hybrid structures, such as a small MG with a higher backend, are common. Name which structure is on the table, what each incentivizes, and how the risk is distributed between the parties.

### Scrutinize Expenses And How They Are Recouped
Distribution expenses, including prints and advertising, marketing, dubbing, delivery, and festival costs, are often recouped before the producer share, and uncapped expenses can consume the entire revenue stream. Determine which expenses require producer approval, which are capped, which are cross-collateralized across territories or titles, and how they are documented. A deal that looks generous can return nothing if expenses are broadly defined and unsupervised. Demand reporting and audit rights, and require that expenses be itemized and reasonable before recoupment.

### Control Term, Exclusivity, And Rights Scope
The length of the deal, the exclusivity of the rights granted, and the scope of rights, such as theatrical, home entertainment, TV, SVOD, AVOD, and ancillary, determine how locked the film is and for how long. A long exclusive term with broad rights can prevent the producer from exploiting the film elsewhere even if the distributor is inactive. Match term and exclusivity to the distributor realistic commitment and the film shelf life. Build in performance triggers, such as minimum release commitments or reversion clauses, so rights do not sit unused. Never grant rights the distributor cannot meaningfully exploit.

### Define Approval Authority Over Release And Marketing
Distribution deals shift varying degrees of control over release date, marketing spend, trailer, key art, rating, and windowing. Identify which decisions the producer retains approval over, which are distributor discretion, and which require consultation. A deal that gives the distributor full release control can result in a window, spend, or positioning that undermines the film audience. Reserve approval on materials, premiere status, and key territories where the filmmaker has leverage, and accept distributor discretion where the filmmaker does not.

### Evaluate The Sales Agent Relationship Separately
A sales agent represents the film to territorial distributors and is distinct from those distributors. Review the sales agent term, commission rate, expense cap, minimum sales commitment, and the territories the agent retains versus sublicenses. A sales agent with a long term and no performance obligation can sit on the film. Confirm the agent has relationships in the territories that matter, a realistic assessment, and a marketing plan. The sales agent deal should align with the producer distribution strategy, not duplicate or conflict with direct deals.

### Require Reporting, Audit, And Transparency Rights
Revenue reporting is where distribution deals fail producers. Require regular reporting with gross receipts, deductions, and net proceeds broken down by territory and window, plus audit rights and a dispute mechanism. Without transparency, the producer cannot verify that the waterfall is being applied correctly. Specify reporting frequency, currency and exchange handling, and the right to inspect books. A deal without enforceable reporting is a deal whose returns cannot be trusted.

### Mark The Escalation Boundary
Deal structures require qualified legal and business affairs review. The agent should not draft or interpret binding terms, calculate final recoupment, or advise on tax, union, or securities implications. It should identify the structural risks, specify the questions a lawyer must answer, and keep the negotiation flexible until qualified review confirms the terms.

## Common Traps

### Judging The Deal By The MG Alone
A large advance with uncapped expenses and unfavorable recoupment can return less than a smaller MG with disciplined terms. The waterfall, not the headline, determines producer returns.

### Granting Broad Rights For A Long Term Without Triggers
Exclusive rights with no performance reversion can lock the film with an inactive distributor for years. Term and exclusivity must match realistic commitment.

### Letting Expenses Consume The Backend
Uncapped, cross-collateralized, or vaguely defined expenses can erase the producer share. Expense definitions and approval rights are as important as the revenue split.

### Confusing Sales Agent And Distributor Roles
A sales agent sells to distributors; a distributor exploits the film in a territory. Conflating the two can produce overlapping deals, double commissions, or gaps in coverage.

### Surrendering Release Control Without Leverage Analysis
Giving the distributor full discretion over release date, spend, and materials can produce a campaign that misses the film audience. Reserve approval where the filmmaker has leverage.

### Skipping Reporting And Audit Rights
Without enforceable reporting and audit, the producer cannot verify deductions or recoupment. Transparency terms must be negotiated before signing, not assumed.

### Treating The Deal As Done When Signed
Distribution deals require ongoing oversight: delivery, release compliance, expense review, and revenue tracking. A signed deal that is not monitored can underperform silently.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is the deal modeled as a full recoupment waterfall, with fees, expenses, MG position, cross-collateralization, and producer share identified?
- [ ] Is the deal structure, MG, commission, or hybrid, named with its incentives and risk distribution explained?
- [ ] Are expenses defined, capped where possible, subject to approval, and supported by reporting and audit rights?
- [ ] Do term, exclusivity, and rights scope match the distributor realistic commitment, with performance triggers or reversion where appropriate?
- [ ] Is approval authority over release date, marketing, materials, premiere, and windowing allocated based on filmmaker leverage?
- [ ] Is the sales agent relationship evaluated separately, with term, commission, expense cap, minimum commitment, and territory coverage confirmed?
- [ ] Are reporting frequency, territory and window breakdown, audit rights, and dispute mechanisms specified?
- [ ] Has the deal been flagged for qualified legal and business affairs review before signing?
- [ ] Does the recommendation separate what can be advised now from what needs legal, tax, or specialist signoff?
- [ ] Are remaining structural risks stated plainly rather than hidden behind confident deal language?
