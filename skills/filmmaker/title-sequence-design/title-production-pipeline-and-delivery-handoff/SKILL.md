# Title Production Pipeline And Delivery Handoff

A title sequence is one of the last elements to lock and one of the first the audience sees, and its production pipeline is usually planned while the cut, the music, and the credit list are all still changing. The agent is typically asked to plan or review the pipeline before the full constraints are visible: the vendor may not be chosen, the credit list may not be final, the music may not be cleared or recorded, and the delivery specs may be incomplete. The task is not to animate the titles; it is to build a pipeline that absorbs late change, protects text accuracy, syncs to sound, and conforms to every deliverable on a schedule that has no slack.

Title production also converts advice into hard deadlines and expensive renders. Titles finished late can block picture lock, delay the mix, or ship with text errors; an end crawl with a misspelled name or a missing credit creates real harm to real people; a render that fails a deliverable spec forces a re-render under deadline. Use this skill to slow the decision down enough to expose hidden constraints, then make the recommendation staged, documented, and appropriately conservative when schedule, text accuracy, music sync, and delivery conformance are involved.

## Core Rules

### Name The Deliverables, The Deadline, And The Dependencies
Start by stating exactly what title deliverables are required, when they are due in the finishing schedule, and what they depend on. Many weak pipeline answers treat titles as a self-contained design task. In reality the main title depends on picture lock and music, the end crawl depends on the final credit list and guild clearance, episode cards depend on the locked episode count, and every render depends on the final delivery spec. Map these dependencies before promising a schedule.

### Choose The Vendor And Scope Against Realistic Change
Whether titles are made in-house, by a specialist title house, or by a freelance motion designer, the scope and the change tolerance must be defined up front. Title work is vulnerable to late change because it sits at the end of the pipeline: the cut shifts, the music changes, credits are added. The agent should define what changes are in scope, how many revision rounds are included, what triggers an overage, and how change is version-controlled, because uncontrolled change is the most common cause of title overruns.

### Protect End Crawl Accuracy As A Safety Issue
The end crawl is where real people are named, and errors are personal and political, not just cosmetic. The agent should treat the credit list as a controlled document: a single authoritative source, guild and deal clearance before typesetting, proofreading against signed names and titles, and a sign-off step from the production office and, where required, guild representatives. A misspelled, omitted, or wrongly ordered credit causes lasting harm and is avoidable with process.

### Integrate Music And Sound Before Final Render
A title sequence is almost always married to music and sound, and the motion must be cut or conformed to the final audio, not a temp track. The agent should ensure the title vendor works to the final music (or a confirmed conformed temp), that the title handoff includes audio reference and sync points, and that the final render is married to the final mix in the correct loudness and channel spec, because a title cut to the wrong music will need to be reanimated.

### Conform To Every Delivery Spec And Aspect Ratio
Titles must conform to theatrical, broadcast, streaming, SDR and HDR, multiple aspect ratios and safe areas, and localized text variants. The agent should confirm the delivery matrix early, design within safe areas, render text as text or as replaceable layers where localization is required, and validate each deliverable against its spec, because a title that looks correct in one format can fail safe-area, aspect-ratio, or text-replacement rules in another.

### Build Schedule Slack And A Fallback
Because titles finish late, the schedule should build slack for credit-list changes, music changes, and spec revisions, and should identify a fallback: a simple card or static title that can ship if the bespoke sequence is not ready. The agent should never let the bespoke title be the only path to delivery, because a single late dependency can hold up the entire deliverable.

### Stage Approval And Preserve The Evidence Trail
Prefer a staged path: lock the delivery spec and dependencies, choose the vendor and scope, control the credit list, conform to final music, render and validate each deliverable, and retain a fallback. Record the delivery matrix, the vendor scope and revision log, the authoritative credit list and sign-offs, the music and sync references, the spec validation, and the version history. If the recommendation cannot say what should be recorded, it has not defined the pipeline clearly enough.

## Common Traps

### Treating Titles As A Self-Contained Design Task
Titles depend on picture lock, music, the credit list, and the delivery spec. A pipeline that ignores these dependencies will slip.

### Leaving Credit-List Control Loose
An end crawl typeset from an uncontrolled, unsigned, or uncleared list will contain omissions, misspellings, or order violations that harm real people.

### Cutting Titles To Temp Music
A sequence animated to a temp track will not sit correctly against the final music and will need rework or reanimation.

### Promising A Single Bespoke Path With No Fallback
If the bespoke title is the only delivery path, one late dependency can hold up the entire deliverable; a simple card fallback protects the schedule.

### Ignoring Safe Area, Aspect Ratio, And Localization
A title that looks correct in one format can fail safe-area, aspect-ratio, or text-replacement rules in another, forcing re-render.

### Underestimating Late Change
Because titles finish last, they absorb the cut, music, and credit changes of every upstream department, and a scope with no change tolerance will overrun.

### Presenting Delivery And Guild Advice As Production Judgment
Delivery specs, guild credit rules, and music clearance are contract- and platform-specific. The trap is locking a pipeline when the right next step is a delivery-spec review, a credit clearance, or a music supervisor.

### Hiding Uncertainty Behind A Confident Tone
Pipeline decisions often need action under uncertainty, but uncertainty should be named. State what is known, what is inferred, what must be verified, and what would change the recommendation.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is the exact title pipeline tied to specific deliverables, a deadline in the finishing schedule, and mapped dependencies on picture lock, music, credit list, and delivery spec?
- [ ] Is the vendor chosen and the scope defined against realistic change, with revision rounds, overage triggers, and version control specified?
- [ ] Is the end crawl credit list treated as a controlled document, with a single authoritative source, guild and deal clearance, proofreading, and sign-off?
- [ ] Is the title vendor working to final or conformed music, with audio reference and sync points in the handoff, and the final render married to the final mix in the correct loudness and channel spec?
- [ ] Does the title conform to theatrical, broadcast, streaming, SDR and HDR, aspect-ratio and safe-area, and localized text-replacement requirements across the delivery matrix?
- [ ] Is schedule slack built in for credit, music, and spec changes, and is a simple card fallback identified in case the bespoke sequence is not ready?
- [ ] Is approval staged, with delivery spec, vendor scope, credit control, music conformance, render validation, and fallback completed before final delivery?
- [ ] Are the delivery matrix, vendor scope and revision log, authoritative credit list and sign-offs, music and sync references, spec validation, and version history recorded?
- [ ] Does the output distinguish immediate dependency and scope steps from staged approval and the final delivery commitment?
- [ ] Are delivery-spec, credit-clearance, and music-supervisor referral boundaries explicit where the local answer changes the recommendation?
