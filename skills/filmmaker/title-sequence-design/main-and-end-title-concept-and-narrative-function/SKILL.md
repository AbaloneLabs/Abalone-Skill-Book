# Main And End Title Concept And Narrative Function

A title sequence is a contract with the audience about tone, genre, world, and point of view, and it is usually designed while the cut, the score, and the marketing position are still moving. The agent is typically asked for a concept or a critique before all the constraints are visible: the film may still be recut, the cast deals may restrict credit placement, the platform may impose length and accessibility rules, and the music may not be final. The task is not to propose a clever animation; it is to decide what narrative and emotional work the titles must do, what they must avoid, and what form serves the film and its delivery obligations.

Title design also converts advice into a fixed, expensive, and highly visible artifact. A sequence that overshadows the opening, leaks a spoiler, breaks the pacing, or fails a clearance or deliverable check will be reworked under deadline pressure, sometimes by replacing a bespoke design with a simple card. Use this skill to slow the decision down enough to expose hidden constraints, then make the recommendation staged, documented, and appropriately conservative when narrative integrity, credit obligations, spoiler risk, and delivery risk are involved.

## Core Rules

### Name The Function Before The Form
Start by stating what the title sequence must accomplish narratively and emotionally: establish tone and genre, set period and place, introduce a point of view, build dread or anticipation, deliver exposition, transition from a cold open, or simply get out of the way. Many weak title recommendations jump to a visual technique before the function is fixed. A cold-open drama, a genre procedural, an anthology, and a documentary each need different title work, and the correct form often follows once the function is named.

### Decide Designed, Edited, Or Simple Card On Evidence, Not Taste
A designed sequence (original motion graphics or live-action plates), an edited sequence (curated film clips or stills), and a simple card (text over a freeze or black) each carry different cost, risk, and narrative weight. The agent should match the choice to the film's budget, tone, and the value the titles add, not to fashion. A bespoke designed title on a restrained intimate drama can feel grandiose; a simple card on a sweeping genre film can feel cheap; the right choice is the one that serves the story and survives the budget and schedule.

### Treat Pacing And The First Minutes As Sacred
The opening minutes set the audience's trust. A title sequence that delays the hook, repeats information the cold open already delivered, or runs past the audience's patience can damage retention, especially on streaming where exit happens in seconds. The agent should test title length and placement against the real first-act rhythm, prefer shorter sequences when the cold open carries the hook, and never let the titles compete with the story the film is trying to start.

### Control Spoiler And Cast Visibility Exposure
Titles can leak information the film intends to withhold: a cast member's prominence, a location, a period shift, a character who appears later, or a visual motif that reveals a twist. Edited clip sequences are especially risky because they can preview shots from late in the film. The agent should map what each title element reveals against the film's intended withholdings, and prefer abstract or non-narrative material where revealing any story content would damage the experience.

### Respect Credit, Clearance, And Music Constraints
Title design is governed by obligations the filmmaker did not choose. Guild and union rules, cast and crew deals, and platform standards can dictate credit order, size, duration, placement, and even whether the director or writer receives a card. Music clearance can restrict whether a song may open the film, and visual material in an edited sequence may require its own clearance. The agent should verify the applicable credit and clearance rules before locking a concept, because a beautiful sequence that violates a deal or a guild rule will be changed.

### Match The Deliverable And Accessibility Spec
Titles must survive every deliverable: theatrical, broadcast, streaming, SDR and HDR, multiple aspect ratios, and localized versions where text must be replaced or translated. Designed sequences with embedded text, fine lines, rapid motion, or high-contrast strobing can fail accessibility checks for photosensitivity, legibility, or safe-area, and can be expensive to localize. The agent should confirm the title design meets the deliverable and accessibility specs before final approval, not after.

### Stage Approval And Preserve The Evidence Trail
Prefer a staged path: agree the function, test the form against budget and schedule, clear credit and music constraints, validate spoiler and pacing impact against a real cut, and confirm deliverable and accessibility specs before final render. Record the concept rationale, the credit and clearance confirmations, the spoiler and pacing review, the accessibility check, and the version history. If the recommendation cannot say what should be recorded, it has not defined the title decision clearly enough.

## Common Traps

### Designing The Look Before Naming The Function
A visually striking sequence that does the wrong narrative work, or no narrative work, is a costly ornament that the film may have to remove.

### Letting Titles Compete With The Opening
A long or loud title sequence placed after a strong cold open can drain the momentum the film just built, hurting retention and review.

### Spoiling Through Edited Clips
A clip-based sequence that previews late-film shots, locations, or character presence can quietly betray the film's structure to an attentive audience.

### Ignoring Guild And Deal Credit Rules
Credit order, size, and placement are often contractual. A designed sequence that violates them will be reworked, sometimes by scrapping the design entirely.

### Failing Photosensitivity Or Localization
Rapid motion, strobing, fine text, or baked-in language can fail accessibility review or make localization expensive, forcing a redesign under deadline.

### Assuming One Title Serves Every Deliverable
A sequence tuned for a dark theatre can be illegible on a phone, broken by aspect-ratio conversion, or illegal in a market with different credit rules.

### Presenting Legal And Guild Advice As Creative Judgment
Credit obligations, music clearance, and accessibility thresholds are contract- and platform-specific. The trap is locking a concept when the right next step is a credit clearance, music supervisor, or delivery spec review.

### Hiding Uncertainty Behind A Confident Tone
Title decisions often need action under uncertainty, but uncertainty should be named. State what is known, what is inferred, what must be verified, and what would change the recommendation.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is the exact title decision tied to a specific film, sequence position, and narrative function rather than described as a generic design brief?
- [ ] Is the choice between designed, edited, and simple card based on budget, tone, and narrative value rather than taste or fashion?
- [ ] Has the title length and placement been tested against the real first-act rhythm and the cold open, with a preference for brevity where the hook must land fast?
- [ ] Has spoiler and cast visibility exposure been mapped against the film's intended withholdings, with abstract or non-narrative material used where revealing content would damage the experience?
- [ ] Are guild, union, cast and crew credit obligations, and music and visual clearance constraints verified before the concept is locked?
- [ ] Does the title design meet theatrical, broadcast, streaming, HDR and SDR, aspect-ratio, accessibility, and localization deliverable specs?
- [ ] Is approval staged, with function, form, clearance, spoiler and pacing review, and deliverable checks completed before final render?
- [ ] Are the concept rationale, credit and clearance confirmations, spoiler and pacing review, accessibility check, and version history recorded?
- [ ] Does the output distinguish immediate concept and function steps from staged approval and the final render commitment?
- [ ] Are credit-clearance, music-supervisor, and delivery-spec referral boundaries explicit where the local answer changes the recommendation?
