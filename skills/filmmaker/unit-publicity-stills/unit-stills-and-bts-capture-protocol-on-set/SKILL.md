# Unit Stills And BTS Capture Protocol On Set

Unit stills and BTS capture happen inside the working machinery of a film set, where the first priority is always the performance and the safety of the take. The agent is typically asked to set or review the capture protocol while the director's tolerance, the cast's comfort, the AD's schedule, and the specific shots that will define the campaign are only partially known. The task is not to take good photographs; it is to build a protocol that places the photographer and BTS crew where they can capture the signature moments without disrupting the take, without endangering anyone, and without producing continuity-broken or unusable material.

On-set capture also converts advice into real-time, irreversible decisions. A stills photographer who fires during a quiet take, a BTS operator who crosses an eyeline or stands in a safety zone, or a unit that captures the wrong take cannot undo the disruption; the moment is gone. Use this skill to slow the decision down enough to expose hidden constraints, then make the recommendation staged, documented, and appropriately conservative when performance integrity, safety, continuity, and capture quality are involved.

## Core Rules

### Name The Shots, The Takes, And The Capture Priorities
Start by stating exactly which scenes and shots are the capture priorities for the campaign, and on which takes the stills and BTS should fire. Many weak protocols treat capture as continuous background activity. The correct plan identifies the signature shots in advance with the director and the marketing lead, confirms which takes will be "clean" for stills, and sequences the photographer's work around the performances that matter, so the campaign has the image it needs rather than the image that happened.

### Place The Photographer To Capture Without Disrupting
Unit stills require a physical position close enough to the performance to be useful, far enough to be silent and invisible, and never in the actor's eyeline or the camera's frame. The agent should coordinate placement with the AD and the DP, use a blimp or silent shutter where sound is sensitive, and respect that some takes are too fragile for any mechanical noise. A photographer who is well placed captures better material and is welcomed back; one who disrupts is banned from the set.

### Coordinate With The Director, Cast, And AD Before Rolling
BTS and stills capture should be agreed with the director, communicated to the cast, and scheduled by the AD, not improvised. Some directors welcome close BTS; others forbid it during emotional scenes; some actors are energized by a stills photographer and others are destabilized. The agent should confirm the director's and cast's tolerance in advance, mark which scenes are off-limits, and never surprise a performance with capture it did not expect.

### Respect Continuity, Slate, And Wardrobe Integrity
A still from the wrong take, the wrong angle, or a moment when continuity was broken can leak into the campaign and create errors the audience catches. The agent should ensure the stills photographer understands the scene's continuity, wardrobe, and prop state, captures images that match the filmed coverage, and flags any image that might be from a reset, a rehearsal, or a broken take, so that marketing does not promote a frame the film does not contain.

### Control Safety, Stunts, And Sensitive Content
On-set capture must never compromise safety. The stills and BTS crew must be briefed on the day's hazards, kept out of stunt zones, rigging paths, and hot work areas, and must wear the required personal protective equipment. Sensitive content such as minors, intimacy, nudity, or distress requires explicit, scene-specific consent and may require a closed set with no capture at all. The agent should treat safety and consent as hard gates that override any capture priority.

### Curate Take Selection And Metadata At Source
Capture is only useful if it can be found and trusted later. The agent should require slate and scene information on each capture, immediate culling of unusable frames, and metadata that ties each still or clip to its scene, take, and clearance status. Curation at source prevents the downstream library from drowning in duplicates and continuity-broken images, and protects against releasing an uncleared or wrong-take frame.

### Stage The Protocol And Preserve The Evidence Trail
Prefer a staged path: identify priority shots, agree director and cast tolerance, coordinate placement with AD and DP, brief safety and consent, capture with slate and metadata, and curate at source. Record the priority shot list, the director and cast agreements, the safety and consent briefings, the capture metadata, and the curation log. If the recommendation cannot say what should be recorded, it has not defined the protocol clearly enough.

## Common Traps

### Continuous Background Capture Without Priorities
Firing throughout the day without identifying the signature shots produces a large library that somehow lacks the one image the campaign needs.

### Disrupting A Fragile Take
A shutter or movement during a quiet or emotional take can break the performance, and the director will exclude the photographer from future scenes.

### Capturing The Wrong Take Or Broken Continuity
A still from a reset, rehearsal, or continuity-broken moment can leak into marketing and contradict the finished film.

### Crossing Safety Zones Or Sensitive Consent Lines
A BTS operator inside a stunt zone or a stills photographer on a closed intimate set endangers people and breaches consent, and ends the capture relationship.

### Improvising Without Director And AD Coordination
Surprising a director or cast with unannounced capture erodes trust and can get the unit pulled from the set entirely.

### Dumping Uncurated, Unslated Frames
A library of duplicates without scene, take, or clearance metadata buries the usable material and risks releasing an uncleared frame.

### Presenting Safety And Consent Judgment As Logistics
Stunt safety, minor and intimacy consent, and closed-set rules are production- and jurisdiction-specific. The trap is approving a capture plan when the right next step is the AD, the intimacy coordinator, or the safety officer.

### Hiding Uncertainty Behind A Confident Tone
Capture decisions often need action under uncertainty, but uncertainty should be named. State what is known, what is inferred, what must be verified, and what would change the recommendation.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is the exact capture protocol tied to specific priority shots, takes, and scenes rather than continuous background activity?
- [ ] Is the photographer placed to capture usefully without entering the actor's eyeline, the camera frame, or a sound-sensitive zone, using a blimp or silent shutter where required?
- [ ] Are director and cast tolerance, off-limit scenes, and any capture surprises agreed in advance and scheduled by the AD?
- [ ] Does the protocol protect continuity, wardrobe, and prop integrity, and flag images from resets, rehearsals, or broken takes?
- [ ] Are safety, stunt, rigging, and hot-work hazards briefed, and are minor, intimacy, nudity, and distress scenes treated as consent-gated closed sets?
- [ ] Is capture slated and metadata-tagged at source, with immediate culling of unusable frames and clearance status recorded?
- [ ] Is the protocol staged, with priority identification, tolerance agreement, placement coordination, safety and consent briefing, capture, and curation completed in sequence?
- [ ] Are the priority shot list, director and cast agreements, safety and consent briefings, capture metadata, and curation log recorded?
- [ ] Does the output distinguish immediate on-set capture steps from staged curation and library handoff?
- [ ] Are AD, intimacy-coordinator, and safety-officer referral boundaries explicit where the local answer changes the recommendation?
