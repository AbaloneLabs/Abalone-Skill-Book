# Author Order And Contributions

Once the set of authors is settled, the next question is order. Order is not cosmetic. In many fields it encodes who led the work, who supervised it, and who is accountable as the senior figure. When order is assigned by politics, seniority, or convenience, three harms follow. The person who actually led the work loses the recognition that drives promotions, grants, and jobs. Readers misjudge who is responsible, which distorts credit in citation networks. And disputes over order, left unresolved, can delay publication, fracture collaborations, and trigger integrity complaints.

The agent should use this skill when sequencing an author list, drafting or revising a contribution statement, deciding between competing claims for first or senior authorship, and deciding whether co-authorship markers are warranted. The goal is to keep the agent from treating order as a diplomatic compromise instead of a contribution-based decision that can be explained and defended.

## Core Rules

### Know The Ordering Convention Of The Target Field

Order means different things in different disciplines, and the same position can signal opposite roles. Before assigning order, identify the convention.

- First author as primary contributor, last author as senior investigator, common in biomedical, life, and many physical sciences.
- Alphabetical order with a stated note, common in some economics, mathematics, and theoretical physics traditions.
- Order by descending contribution with a contribution paragraph, common in some social science and interdisciplinary venues.
- Equal-contribution co-first or co-last markers when two people led or supervised equally.

Mixing conventions, or using order from one field while publishing in another, confuses readers. State the convention explicitly in the paper when there is any chance of ambiguity.

### Assign First Authorship To The Person Who Led The Intellectual Work

The first author is typically the person who conceived or drove the study, did the bulk of the analysis or experimentation, wrote the first draft, and coordinates revisions. If these roles were split, the decision becomes a judgment call, but it should still be based on who carried the largest share of the intellectual and execution load.

Do not assign first authorship as a reward for seniority, as a consolation for a future promise, or because someone insists. If two people genuinely led equally, use co-first authorship and explain it. If the load was unequal, order should reflect that even if it disappoints someone.

### Assign Senior Or Last Authorship Based On Leadership And Accountability

The last or senior author is usually the person who secured funding, led the research group, supervised the project, and takes long-term accountability. Senior authorship is not automatic for a department chair or principal investigator who was uninvolved. A principal investigator who provided only money and lab space, without intellectual supervision, may belong in acknowledgments rather than senior author position.

When two senior figures contributed equally, co-last authorship is acceptable. When the supervisory role shifted over the project, document this rather than guessing.

### Use A Contribution Taxonomy To Make Decisions Defensible

A contribution statement using a taxonomy such as CRediT turns a subjective ordering decision into a reviewable record. For each author, list which roles they filled.

- Conceptualization and methodology.
- Investigation and data curation.
- Formal analysis and software.
- Writing original draft versus review and editing.
- Visualization and supervision.
- Project administration and funding acquisition.

The statement should make the ordering self-evident. If the contribution statement does not support the chosen order, the order is probably wrong. Reconcile the two before submission rather than writing a vague statement to justify a predetermined list.

### Decide Co-Authorship Markers Honestly

Co-first and co-last markers exist to recognize genuine equality. They are sometimes abused to avoid difficult conversations or to inflate credit. Use them when two contributors are truly comparable in leadership or supervision, not to placate a strong personality.

If co-authorship is claimed, the contribution statement should show the equality. If one person clearly led, single first authorship is more honest, even if it costs a relationship. Have the difficult conversation early.

### Sequence Middle Authors By Contribution Within The Field's Logic

Middle author positions are less loaded but still meaningful in ordered fields. A common approach is descending contribution. In large consortia, middle authors may be listed by group, institution, or alphabetically within a subgroup, with the logic stated.

Avoid arbitrary middle-author ordering that hides who contributed more. If a junior researcher did substantial work, do not bury them behind senior names who contributed less.

### Reassess Order When Contributions Change

Contributions drift during a project. Someone planned to lead analysis but left. A student took over and rescued the study. A senior advisor stepped away. The order decided at the start may no longer match reality.

Reassess order at the drafting stage and again before submission. Updating order to match actual contribution is not betrayal; it is accuracy. Failing to update it is the betrayal, because it miscredits the person who actually did the work.

### Document And Communicate The Decision

Record the reasoning behind the order, share it with all authors, and invite objections before submission. A short written rationale prevents later disputes and gives the team a shared record. If someone objects, address it through the agreed process rather than ignoring it.

Corresponding authorship is separate from order. The corresponding author handles journal communication and reader inquiries. Assign it to someone responsive and accountable, not as a consolation for not getting first or senior position.

## Common Traps

### Defaulting First Authorship To The Most Senior Person

Senior people often already have strong records. Giving them first authorship on a junior-led study starves the person who needs the credit and misrepresents who did the work.

### Using Order To Avoid Conflict

Assigning co-first authorship or an ambiguous order to avoid a hard conversation produces a list that no one can defend and that may still trigger a dispute later.

### Writing A Vague Contribution Statement

A statement that says all authors contributed equally is rarely true and tells evaluators nothing. It also fails to justify the order. Be specific.

### Treating Middle Authorship As Meaningless

In large collaborations middle positions can blur, but evaluators still parse them. Burying a key contributor behind less-involved senior names is a form of miscredit.

### Promising Future Authorship To Resolve Current Disputes

Offering first authorship on a later paper to settle a current conflict creates a debt that may be impossible to repay and distorts the next study's credit.

### Ignoring Field Convention When Publishing Interdisciplinarily

A team from different fields may each assume their own ordering convention. Without an explicit agreement, everyone is surprised at submission.

### Forgetting That Order Affects Hiring And Grant Decisions

First and senior authorships are weighted heavily in academic evaluation. A careless order decision can cost someone a job or a grant. Treat order as a decision with career consequences, not a formatting detail.

### Changing Order Without Telling All Authors

Silently reordering the list before submission breeds distrust and can constitute a breach of collaboration norms. Every author should see and approve the final order.

## Self-Check

- Is the target field's ordering convention identified and stated in the paper?
- Is first authorship assigned to the person who led the intellectual and execution work?
- Is senior or last authorship assigned based on genuine supervision and accountability?
- Does a contribution statement using a recognized taxonomy support the chosen order?
- Are co-first or co-last markers used only when contributions are genuinely equal?
- Are middle authors sequenced by a stated logic rather than arbitrarily?
- Has the order been reassessed against actual contributions before submission?
- Have all authors seen, understood, and approved the final order and contribution statement?
- Is corresponding authorship assigned to a responsive and accountable person, separate from order politics?
- For contested or high-stakes ordering decisions, has a mentor, research integrity officer, or institutional authorship committee been consulted?
