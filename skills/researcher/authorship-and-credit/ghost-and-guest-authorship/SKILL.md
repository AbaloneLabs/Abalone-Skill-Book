# Ghost And Guest Authorship

Two opposite abuses distort the author list. Guest authorship, also called gift or honorary authorship, lists someone who did not contribute intellectually and cannot be accountable. Ghost authorship hides someone who did contribute, often an industry writer, sponsor, or external analyst, so that their involvement is invisible to readers. Both corrupt the credit system, both mislead readers about who is responsible for a study, and both can constitute research misconduct under many institutional and journal policies.

The agent should use this skill when reviewing an author list for integrity, when a sponsor or company is involved, when a manuscript arrives largely written by an outside party, when a senior figure requests inclusion without contribution, or when a contributor asks to remain unnamed. The goal is to keep the agent from accepting a convenient author list that hides responsibility or manufactures credit.

## Core Rules

### Recognize Guest Authorship In All Its Forms

Guest authorship is listing someone who fails the criteria of substantial intellectual contribution, critical revision, final approval, and accountability. Common shapes include the following.

- The department head or principal investigator added by default.
- A colleague included as a favor or in exchange for future inclusion.
- A senior name added to lend credibility or improve acceptance odds.
- A funder or sponsor representative listed without intellectual input.
- A friend or relative added to pad their record.

Each of these transfers credit without transferring accountability. If the study is later found to be flawed, the guest author is implicated in work they cannot defend, and the genuine contributors are underrepresented.

### Recognize Ghost Authorship And Its Drivers

Ghost authorship is the deliberate omission of someone who contributed substantially. It is most common when a commercial sponsor hires a medical communications company to draft a paper, then lists academic authors as if they had written it. It also occurs when an analyst who did the core statistical work is dropped to keep the author list short, or when a controversial contributor is hidden to avoid disclosure.

Drivers include marketing goals, desire to present a paper as independent, fear that disclosing industry authorship will reduce credibility, and pressure to keep academic authors prominent. Whatever the driver, hiding a real contributor misleads readers and violates most journal authorship policies.

### Apply Authorship Criteria Without Exception For Sponsors

Industry funding does not by itself create or remove authorship. A sponsor employee who contributed intellectually and accepts accountability should be an author. A sponsor employee who only provided funding or reviewed for compliance should be acknowledged, not authored. The same criteria apply regardless of employer.

Do not allow a sponsor to dictate the author list based on marketing considerations. If a contract gives a sponsor the right to approve or veto authors or content, disclose this in the manuscript, because such control affects interpretation of the work.

### Disclose Writing And Editorial Assistance

If an outside writer, editor, or communications firm helped draft or polish the manuscript, disclose this in the acknowledgments, including funding source for that assistance. Disclosed assistance is legitimate and common. Undisclosed writing assistance is ghost authorship even when the named authors contributed intellectually.

Disclosure should name the contributor or firm, describe the assistance, and state who paid for it. This lets readers judge independence and potential bias.

### Check For Undisclosed Conflicts On The Author List

Ghost and guest authorship often travel with undisclosed conflicts of interest. Review each author for the following.

- Employment or consulting with a sponsor or competitor.
- Equity or stock holdings in a relevant company.
- Patent or royalty interests.
- Board or advisory roles.
- Speaking or advisory fees.
- Grants from interested parties.

Require each author to complete a conflict of interest declaration before submission. Do not rely on memory. An undisclosed conflict that surfaces later can trigger retraction and regulatory review.

### Resist Pressure To Add Or Remove Names

Pressure can come from a senior colleague, a sponsor, a collaborator, or an institution. The right response is the same. Apply the criteria, document the decision, and escalate if needed.

- If asked to add a guest author, explain the criteria and offer acknowledgment instead.
- If asked to remove a genuine contributor, refuse and document the request.
- If threatened, involve the research integrity officer or ombudsperson.

Capitulating to pressure does not resolve the problem; it transfers it to the published record, where it is much harder to fix.

### Audit The Author List Before Submission

Before submission, run an explicit audit.

- Does each author meet all four criteria?
- Is anyone missing who contributed substantially?
- Is any outside writing or editorial assistance disclosed?
- Are conflicts of interest declared for every author?
- Does the contribution statement match the list?
- Are sponsor or industry roles disclosed and accurate?

Treat this audit as a required step, not a courtesy. Many ghost and guest authorship cases are caught only at this stage, and many slip through because no one checked.

### Know The Consequences And Escalate

Ghost and guest authorship can lead to retraction, correction, institutional investigation, loss of funding, and damage to careers. Some jurisdictions treat undisclosed industry ghostwriting in medical publications as a regulatory or legal matter. When the agent detects likely ghost or guest authorship and cannot resolve it through discussion, escalate to the responsible institutional authority before submission rather than after publication.

## Common Traps

### Assuming Senior Inclusion Is Harmless

Adding a senior name seems generous but implicates them in the work and erases the principle that authorship means accountability. It is not harmless.

### Treating Disclosed Writing Help As Ghostwriting

Disclosed, acknowledged writing assistance is legitimate and should not be conflated with ghost authorship. The harm is in the hiding, not in the help.

### Allowing A Sponsor To Control The List Silently

Contractual control over authors or content, if exercised, must be disclosed. Silent sponsor control is a classic ghost authorship pattern.

### Dropping A Contributor To Simplify The List

Removing an analyst or junior researcher who did core work, to keep the list tidy or to favor senior names, is ghost authorship by omission.

### Relying On Verbal Assurances About Contributions

Verbal claims that everyone contributed are not auditable. Use a contribution statement and conflict declarations as evidence.

### Confusing Acknowledgment With Authorship

Acknowledgment thanks people for help. Authorship assigns responsibility. Moving someone from author to acknowledgment to avoid a dispute, without basis, is a miscredit decision.

### Fearing Retaliation More Than Misconduct

The short-term discomfort of resisting pressure is smaller than the long-term cost of a retraction or investigation. Escalation exists precisely for these situations.

### Believing Small Papers Do Not Matter

Ghost and guest authorship on abstracts, supplements, or minor papers still distorts the record and can surface during later audits. Apply the same standards regardless of outlet prestige.

## Self-Check

- Does every listed author meet the criteria of substantial contribution, critical revision, final approval, and accountability?
- Is any substantial contributor missing from the list, including writers, analysts, or sponsor employees?
- Is all outside writing, editorial, or analytical assistance disclosed with its funding source?
- Has every author completed a current conflict of interest declaration?
- Does the contribution statement accurately match the author list and order?
- Are any sponsor or contractual controls over authorship or content disclosed in the manuscript?
- Has an explicit pre-submission audit of the author list been performed and documented?
- Has pressure to add or remove names been resisted and, where needed, escalated?
- Are the same standards applied to abstracts, supplements, and minor outputs as to main papers?
- For suspected ghost or guest authorship that cannot be resolved internally, has a research integrity officer, institutional compliance office, or qualified advisor been consulted before submission?
