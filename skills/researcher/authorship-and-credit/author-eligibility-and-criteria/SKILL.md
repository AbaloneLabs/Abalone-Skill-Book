# Author Eligibility And Criteria

Authorship is not a reward for participation, seniority, friendship, or funding. Authorship is a public claim of intellectual responsibility for a study, and with it comes accountability for the work's integrity, its interpretation, and its consequences. When authorship is assigned casually, three kinds of harm follow. First, unqualified people gain credit they did not earn and may later be held responsible for errors or misconduct they cannot defend. Second, genuine contributors are erased, which damages careers and distorts the scientific record of who produced knowledge. Third, readers and evaluators cannot trust that listed authors actually vouch for the work, which weakens the entire citation and credit system.

The agent should use this skill before finalizing an author list, before drafting a manuscript, before responding to a collaborator who asks to be added, and before a paper is submitted. The goal is to keep the agent from treating authorship as a social or political negotiation instead of a criteria-based decision about intellectual contribution and accountability.

## Core Rules

### Apply A Defined Authorship Standard

Use an explicit, recognized standard rather than a private custom. The most common is the ICMJE (International Committee of medical journal editors) four criteria, adapted across disciplines. A person should be an author only if they meet all of the following.

- Substantial contributions to the conception or design, or to data acquisition, analysis, or interpretation.
- Drafting or critically revising the manuscript for important intellectual content.
- Final approval of the version to be published.
- Agreement to be accountable for all aspects of the work.

In disciplines where the ICMJE model is not standard, still require that an author both contributed intellectually and agrees to be accountable. Purely technical, administrative, supervisory, funding, or acquisition-only contributions do not by themselves meet authorship criteria. They belong in an acknowledgments section.

### Separate Authors From Acknowledged Contributors

Many people help a study succeed without qualifying for authorship. Maintain a clear boundary.

- Authors take intellectual responsibility and can defend the work.
- Acknowledged contributors perform valued tasks but do not take responsibility for the conclusions.

Examples of acknowledged-only contributions include recruitment, data entry, laboratory technician work, editing for language, statistical programming under direction, providing a reagent or dataset, institutional leadership, and general departmental support. Acknowledged contributors must give consent to be named, because being listed can expose them or imply endorsement.

### Agree On Authorship Early And In Writing

The most common cause of authorship disputes is that expectations were never made explicit. Decide the authorship plan at the start of a project, revisit it as the work evolves, and record decisions in a shared document or consortium agreement.

Early discussion should cover.

- Expected criteria for joining the author list.
- Expected order of authors, if the field uses order.
- Expected roles of corresponding and senior authors.
- A process for adding or removing authors as contributions change.
- A process for resolving disagreements.

Revisit the plan at major milestones, because real contributions diverge from planned contributions. A person who planned to lead analysis but did not may no longer qualify, and a junior person who took over may.

### Decide Author Order By Field Convention

Author order carries meaning, but the meaning differs by field. Know the convention before assigning positions.

- Biomedical and many physical sciences use first author as primary contributor and last author as senior or corresponding investigator.
- Some fields list authors alphabetically and state this explicitly.
- Some fields use a contribution paragraph and treat order as less meaningful.
- Some fields allow co-first or co-last authorship for equal contributions.

Do not invent a private ordering rule and expect readers to understand it. If the field uses order, assign it by actual contribution. If two contributors are genuinely equal, use a recognized co-authorship marker and explain it in the contribution statement.

### Document Contributions Transparently

A growing number of journals require a contribution statement using a taxonomy such as CRediT (contributor roles taxonomy). Use it even when not required, because it forces honesty about who did what.

CRediT roles include conceptualization, methodology, software, validation, formal analysis, investigation, resources, data curation, writing original draft, writing review and editing, visualization, supervision, project administration, and funding acquisition. Assign each contributor the roles that are accurate, not the roles that look impressive. Funding acquisition alone does not earn authorship. Supervision alone does not earn authorship. They can be acknowledged, but authorship still requires intellectual contribution and accountability.

### Require Accountability From Every Author

Every author must be able to explain and defend the work, at least within their area of contribution. This is not optional. An author who cannot answer substantive questions about the part of the study they claim is not really an author under most criteria.

Before submission, confirm that each author has reviewed the manuscript, agrees with the conclusions, approves the final version, and accepts accountability. Keep records of approval. If an author refuses to approve or cannot be reached, do not list them without resolving the situation, and document the attempt.

### Handle Changes And Disputes Formally

Authorship can change during a project. Someone may leave, underperform, or join late. Handle changes through the agreed process, not through informal edits to the author list.

If a dispute arises, escalate within the institution or consortium before submission. Do not resolve disputes by quietly dropping a name or adding a name to avoid conflict. Many institutions have an ombudsperson, research integrity officer, or authorship committee. Using these channels protects everyone, including the person who raises the concern.

### Respect Institutional And Funder Rules

Some funders and institutions impose additional rules, such as open-access authorship policies, limits on gift authorship, requirements to report all affiliations, or rules about industry co-authorship. Check applicable policies before finalizing the list. Undisclosed conflicts, dual affiliations, or ghost industry authors can trigger retractions and regulatory action.

## Common Traps

### Adding The Supervisor By Default

Listing a senior person who did not contribute intellectually is gift authorship. It inflates their record, dilutes accountability, and can implicate them in problems they did not help create. Senior status is not authorship.

### Using Authorship As Currency

Trading authorship for samples, equipment, access, or favors corrupts the credit system. These contributions are real and should be acknowledged, but they are not intellectual contributions that carry accountability.

### Promising Authorship Before Knowing The Contribution

A promise made early can become wrong later. If a collaborator underdelivers, the team faces an awkward reversal. Make promises conditional on criteria being met, and revisit them honestly.

### Confusing Corresponding Authorship With Senior Authorship

The corresponding author handles communication with the journal and readers. The senior or last author provides intellectual leadership. These can be the same person but often are not. Assigning corresponding status as a consolation prize muddies responsibility.

### Forgetting Junior And Non-Academic Contributors

Students, research assistants, community partners, and technicians are frequently the people whose labor made the study possible. Review the contributor list actively to ensure that qualifying intellectual contributors are not erased because they are junior or outside the academic hierarchy.

### Splitting One Paper To Generate More Authorships

Salami publication, slicing one study into several papers to give more people first-author credits, distorts the literature and wastes review effort. Each paper must be a meaningful unit of work, not a credit-generation device.

### Ignoring Authorship When Contributions Diverge

Plans drift. The person who was supposed to analyze the data did not, and someone else did. If the author list is not updated, it becomes inaccurate by omission. Reassess at submission.

### Listing Group Authorship Without Specifying Members

Some consortium papers list a group name. Readers and evaluators still need to know which individuals are responsible and how to credit them. Provide the full member list, the writing group, and the contributors who take accountability.

## Self-Check

- Is a recognized authorship standard being applied, and is it stated explicitly?
- Does every listed author meet the criteria of substantial intellectual contribution, drafting or critical revision, final approval, and accountability?
- Are contributors who do not qualify for authorship placed in acknowledgments with their consent?
- Was the authorship plan agreed early, recorded, and revisited as contributions changed?
- Does author order follow a recognized field convention and reflect actual contribution?
- Is a contribution statement, such as CRediT, completed accurately for each author and contributor?
- Has every author approved the final manuscript and accepted accountability, with records kept?
- Are institutional, funder, and journal authorship rules checked and complied with?
- Have disputes been handled through formal channels rather than informal edits?
- For high-stakes or contested cases, has a research integrity officer, institutional authorship committee, or qualified advisor been consulted before the list is finalized?
