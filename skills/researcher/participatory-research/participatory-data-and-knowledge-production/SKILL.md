# Participatory Data And Knowledge Production

Data and findings are where participatory research most often stops being participatory. A study can be co-designed at the front end and still treat the community as a data source during collection and analysis, with the researcher carrying the raw material away to interpret alone. The judgment problem is that knowledge production carries power, and whoever controls the data, the analysis, and the interpretation controls what the research ultimately says about the community. When the community is excluded from these stages, several harms follow. Findings reflect the researcher's frame rather than the community's understanding, and they can misrepresent, pathologize, or stigmatize the people who provided the data. Data leaves the community and is stored, shared, and reused in ways the community never agreed to, including by parties who may harm them. Knowledge that the community co-produced is published under academic names, with the community's intellectual contribution erased. And the community ends up knowing less about itself than the researchers who studied it, which is the precise opposite of empowerment.

The agent should use this skill when deciding how to collect and analyze data with community members, how to co-produce findings, who owns the resulting data, how to honor data sovereignty, or how to assign authorship and ownership of outputs. The goal is to keep the agent from treating data and analysis as the researcher's exclusive domain, when in participatory work they are shared terrain where power, representation, and ownership must be negotiated openly.

## Core Rules

### Co-Collect Data Rather Than Extract It

In participatory research, data collection is not something done to the community but with it. The community should help gather the material that describes them.

- Train and resource community members to collect data themselves, including interviewing, observing, and documenting.
- Co-decide what is collected, from whom, and in what form, so the data reflects community-relevant categories rather than only academic ones.
- Avoid instruments that extract information the community finds meaningless or harmful to provide.
- Recognize that community collectors can reach people and contexts the outside researcher cannot, improving both access and trust.

Data gathered by the community is richer, more ethical, and more likely to be accurate, but only if collectors are trained, supported, and credited rather than treated as cheap labor.

### Co-Analyze Rather Than Interpret Alone

Analysis is where meaning is made, and it is the stage most often reclaimed by the researcher. Co-analysis keeps interpretation shared.

- Bring community members into coding, theme-building, and interpretation, not only into reviewing a finished analysis.
- Make analytical methods transparent and learnable, rather than treating them as expert mysteries the community cannot access.
- Hold space for the community to challenge or reject the researcher's reading of the data.
- Document disagreements in interpretation, because they are often the most important findings.

An analysis the community cannot see being done, or cannot contest, produces a reading of the community that the community had no hand in shaping.

### Co-Produce Knowledge That Integrates Community Expertise

Participatory knowledge production values lived experience and community knowledge alongside academic expertise, rather than treating them as raw material to be refined by the researcher.

- Treat community members as holders of expertise, not only as sources of data to be interpreted by experts.
- Integrate local knowledge, history, and context into the findings rather than filtering them out as "anecdote."
- Co-author the narrative of what the findings mean and what should be done, rather than presenting conclusions to the community for comment.
- Resist frames that pathologize the community or that measure it only against outside norms.

Knowledge that dismisses community understanding in favor of academic framing reproduces the very hierarchy participatory research is meant to disrupt.

### Establish Data Sovereignty From The Outset

Data sovereignty means the community holds authority over its data, including how it is stored, accessed, shared, and reused. This must be negotiated before collection, not after.

- Agree in writing who owns the data, where it is stored, and who can access it, with the community as a decision-maker.
- Respect community protocols and Indigenous data sovereignty principles, which may restrict how data is used or shared.
- Give the community the right to refuse secondary uses of the data, including by the researcher's own future projects.
- Avoid defaulting to open data mandates that override community consent, because open access can re-expose vulnerable communities.

Data treated as the researcher's property to archive and share freely is extracted data, regardless of how participatory the collection was.

### Negotiate Ownership Of Outputs And Authorship

Outputs, including papers, reports, tools, and databases, carry credit and control. Their ownership must be negotiated openly.

- Discuss authorship, ownership, and intellectual property early and explicitly, before outputs exist and disputes arise.
- Grant authorship to community members who meet the criteria, and credit contributions that authorship norms tend to erase.
- Decide jointly who controls derivative works, including commercial uses, datasets, and tools built from the research.
- Avoid clauses that assign all outputs to the researcher's institution by default.

Authorship and ownership decided after the fact default to the credentialed and the institution, quietly stripping the community of recognition and control.

### Protect Confidentiality In Participatory Settings

Participatory data has distinctive confidentiality risks, because community members collecting and analyzing data may know who is being described.

- Adapt de-identification for close-knit communities, where standard techniques fail because thin details reveal identity.
- Protect community co-researchers who collect sensitive data from pressure or retaliation within their own community.
- Consider whether even aggregate findings could identify a small community, and adjust publication accordingly.
- Control access to raw data so that community collectors are not exposed by the data they helped gather.

Standard confidentiality assumes outside researchers and anonymous subjects. Participatory research has neither, and the safeguards must change to match.

### Return Findings To The Community First

The community that produced the data deserves to learn from it before academic audiences do, in forms they can use.

- Share findings with the community before academic publication, and in accessible language and formats.
- Co-decide what is reported publicly, because some findings could harm the community if released.
- Offer the community the chance to correct misreadings before they are published.
- Provide material the community can use for its own advocacy, planning, or action, not only academic articles.

Findings published first in journals the community cannot access turn their own knowledge into a commodity they cannot use.

### Handle Sensitive And Potentially Harmful Findings Carefully

Some findings, even if accurate, can harm the community if released without care, by reinforcing stigma, exposing vulnerability, or arming adversaries.

- Assess the potential harm of findings before deciding how and whether to publish them.
- Co-decide with the community what is reported, what is withheld, and how it is framed.
- Avoid sensationalizing or pathologizing findings that could be weaponized against the community.
- Balance scientific openness against the community's right to protection.

A finding that is publishable is not necessarily one that should be published unchanged, and the community should help decide.

## Common Traps

### Extracting Data And Analyzing It Alone

Collecting data with the community but interpreting it in isolation reclaims the most powerful stage for the researcher. The trap is that solo analysis is faster and feels cleaner, but it produces a reading of the community the community did not shape and may not recognize.

### Dismissing Community Knowledge As Anecdote

Filtering out lived experience and local history in favor of academic categories strips the findings of meaning. The trap is that academic methods are built to discount non-standardized input, so defaulting to them erases community expertise without anyone noticing.

### Treating Data As The Researcher's Property

Archiving and sharing community data without ongoing community consent repeats extraction under an open-science banner. The trap is that open data feels virtuous, but for vulnerable communities it can mean loss of control and re-exposure to harm.

### Authorship Defaulting To Academics

Letting authorship norms assign all credit to credentialed researchers erases the community's intellectual contribution. The trap is that the default favors the institution, so doing nothing reproduces inequity while appearing neutral.

### Open Data Mandates Overriding Community Consent

Applying open-access requirements without exception can override the community's right to control its data. The trap is that funders reward openness, but blanket openness can harm communities whose data should not be public.

### Releasing Findings That Harm The Community

Publishing accurate but stigmatizing findings without community input can weaponize the research against the people who made it possible. The trap is that publishable findings feel obligatory, but some true things cause harm if released carelessly.

### Standard Confidentiality In Close-Knit Communities

Applying generic de-identification in small or interconnected communities fails because thin details reveal identity. The trap is that standard techniques satisfy ethics boards while leaving participants recognizable to their neighbors.

### Publishing Before The Community Sees The Findings

Releasing results to academic audiences before the community can review or use them turns the community's knowledge into a commodity they cannot access. The trap is that journal timelines pressure early release, but priority to the community is a baseline condition of ethical participatory work.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is data co-collected with trained and resourced community members, rather than extracted by the researcher alone?
- [ ] Does the community participate in analysis and interpretation, including the right to challenge the researcher's reading?
- [ ] Are lived experience and community knowledge integrated into the findings rather than filtered out as anecdote?
- [ ] Is data sovereignty established in writing before collection, with the community holding authority over storage, access, and reuse?
- [ ] Are authorship, ownership, and intellectual property negotiated early and explicitly, with authorship granted to community members who meet the criteria?
- [ ] Is confidentiality adapted for participatory and close-knit settings, protecting both participants and community co-researchers?
- [ ] Are findings returned to the community first, in accessible forms, before academic publication?
- [ ] Have potentially harmful findings been assessed jointly with the community for how and whether to publish them?
- [ ] Are open data and open access requirements checked against community consent rather than applied as blanket rules?
- [ ] For research involving Indigenous communities, vulnerable populations, or sensitive data, has an experienced community partner or data-ethics advisor reviewed the data sovereignty, ownership, and authorship arrangements before collection begins?

This skill provides methodological guidance on co-producing data and knowledge in participatory research. It is not a substitute for a formal data-sharing agreement, community partnership agreement, or ethics consultation. Participatory research that extracts data, analyzes it without the community, claims ownership the community did not agree to, or publishes findings that misrepresent or harm the community can damage trust, exploit partners, and cause lasting harm. Where the stakes are high or the situation uncertain, consult experienced community partners and ethics advisors before proceeding.
