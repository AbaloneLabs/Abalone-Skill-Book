# Power-Sharing And Capacity Building

The hardest test of participatory research is not whether the community was present, but whether it held power. Participation and power are not the same thing. A community can be consulted extensively, attend every meeting, and contribute labor, while the researcher retains every consequential decision, from what is studied to who is credited. That arrangement is tokenism dressed as partnership, and participants recognize it immediately. The judgment problem is that researchers tend to underestimate how much power they hold, because institutional authority, funding, credentials, and control of publication make the imbalance structural rather than personal. When power is not deliberately shared, several harms follow. Decisions reflect the researcher's or funder's agenda regardless of stated participatory intent. Community members invest time and social capital and receive neither influence nor benefit, which they experience as exploitation. Capacity stays concentrated in the research team, so the community is left no more able to act after the project than before. And the most marginalized members of the community, whose participation required the most effort to secure, are the first to be silenced when power is not redistributed.

The agent should use this skill when deciding what authority to cede to community partners, how to build lasting research capacity in the community, how to compensate and credit community contributions fairly, or how to recognize and prevent tokenism. The goal is to keep the agent from confusing presence with power, and from treating capacity-building as optional when it is the difference between empowerment and extraction.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Participation From Power, And Share Real Authority

There is a continuum from being informed, to being consulted, to sharing decisions, to leading the work. Tokenism lives at the shallow end, and most "participatory" projects quietly sit there.

- For each consequential decision, name honestly who holds the authority, including the research question, methods, budget, analysis, and publication.
- Cede genuine decision power over matters that affect the community, not only over implementation details or logistics.
- Avoid inviting input into decisions already settled, which is the most common form of tokenism.
- Match the promise of participation to what will actually be delivered, and never call consultation "partnership" when the community cannot change the outcome.

Power that is unexamined quietly ensures the research reflects the researcher's preferences regardless of participatory intent. Naming where power sits is the first step toward sharing it.

### Address Power Asymmetry Structurally, Not Personally

Power imbalance in participatory research is rarely about a single domineering researcher. It is structural, baked into funding, credentials, and institutional backing, and goodwill alone does not redistribute it.

- Recognize the researcher's institutional power and how it can silence dissent, even unintentionally.
- Create structures that let less powerful voices be heard, such as separate forums, anonymous input, or community-led sessions without the researcher present.
- Prevent local elites or gatekeepers from capturing the participatory process and speaking for those with less voice.
- Examine how funding flows concentrate control in the institution that holds the grant.

Structural problems require structural fixes. Relying on personal humility to neutralize systemic imbalance leaves the imbalance intact.

### Build Community Research Capacity, Not Just Collect Data

Capacity-building is what separates empowerment from extraction. If the community is no more skilled after the project than before, the research took without giving.

- Transfer concrete research skills to community members, including interviewing, analysis, facilitation, and project management.
- Design capacity-building as a core deliverable with a budget, not a side effect that happens if there is time.
- Let community members lead components of the work, with the researcher in a supporting role, rather than always the reverse.
- Avoid concentrating all expertise in the research team so that the community depends on the researcher to continue.

Research that builds no local capability leaves the community exactly as dependent as it was before, which is the opposite of empowerment.

### Compensate Community Contributions Fairly And Transparently

Community partners contribute time, knowledge, and social capital. Fair compensation recognizes this and prevents the research from being extractive.

- Budget for community partners' labor from the first grant, treating it as a cost of doing the work, not a favor.
- Set compensation relative to local norms and the time demanded, high enough to be fair but not so high it becomes coercive.
- Consider non-cash forms where cash is inappropriate, risky, or culturally mismatched, and let the community shape the form.
- Compensate fairly across participants, and be transparent about who is paid what, to avoid favoritism and suspicion.

Unpaid partnership transfers benefit from the community to the researcher. Fair compensation is not generosity; it is a baseline condition of ethical work.

### Share Credit And Authorship Equitably

Credit is a form of power, because it drives careers, reputations, and future opportunities. Hoarding it reproduces inequity.

- Recognize community partners' contributions in publications, presentations, and reports, not only in acknowledgments.
- Apply authorship criteria honestly, granting authorship to community members who meet the standard rather than defaulting all credit to academics.
- Avoid ghosting community contributors whose labor made the work possible but who lack the credential to be listed.
- Discuss credit and authorship expectations early and explicitly, before disputes arise.

A study co-produced by a community but published under only academic names is a quiet form of theft, however warm the relationship.

### Build Governance That Can Hold The Researcher Accountable

Power-sharing needs structures that outlast individual goodwill. Governance is how a community can constrain or redirect the researcher.

- Establish a community body with real authority over key decisions, not an advisory group that can only suggest.
- Give the community a documented right to halt, delay, or redirect the work, and mean it.
- Make decision-making transparent, so the community can see how its input did or did not change outcomes.
- Include mechanisms for the community to raise concerns without fear of retaliation.

A governance structure that cannot say no to the researcher is not power-sharing; it is consultation with extra steps.

### Plan For Sustainability Beyond The Project

Empowerment that evaporates when funding ends is not empowerment. Sustainability must be designed in from the start.

- Build local capacity to carry the work forward rather than centralizing skill in the research team.
- Embed structures and skills in existing community organizations rather than parallel project structures that collapse at exit.
- Hand over ownership of data, tools, and relationships gradually and explicitly, with agreed transitions.
- Avoid creating dependency on the researcher or on external resources that will not persist.

Change that dies when the researcher leaves is temporary relief, not capacity. Sustainability is a design criterion, not a hope.

### Recognize And Resist Tokenism Actively

Tokenism is not usually intentional; it emerges from defaulting to the easy, the familiar, and the controllable. It must be actively resisted.

- Audit regularly whether community input is actually changing decisions, or only being recorded.
- Watch for patterns where the same few community members are consulted repeatedly because they are agreeable.
- Be alert to participation that exists to satisfy a funder or ethics board rather than to share power.
- Treat community members' frustration or withdrawal as a signal of tokenism, not as ingratitude.

Tokenism feels like partnership from the inside, which is why it must be checked against outcomes rather than intentions.

## Common Traps

### Calling Consultation Partnership

Inviting input into already-settled decisions and labeling it partnership gives the appearance of involvement with no influence. The trap is that it generates records of engagement while changing nothing, and participants experience it as manipulation, breeding cynicism toward all future participation.

### Retaining All Real Decision Power

Ceding authority over logistics and implementation while keeping the question, methods, analysis, and publication under researcher control leaves the agenda untouched. The trap is that this feels like sharing power, but the consequential decisions never left the researcher's hands.

### Capacity-Building As An Afterthought

Treating skill transfer as optional or as something to do if time permits ensures the community gains no lasting capability. The trap is that researcher-led work feels efficient in the short term, but it produces dependency and leaves the community unable to continue.

### Unpaid Community Labor

Expecting community members to donate time while the researcher is paid and credited is extractive regardless of relationship warmth. The trap is that goodwill can mask the imbalance, but the benefit still flows one way, from the community to the researcher.

### Hoarding Credit And Authorship

Publishing co-produced work under only academic names while community contributors appear only in acknowledgments steals the recognition that drives careers. The trap is that authorship norms favor the credentialed, so defaulting to them reproduces inequity without anyone deciding to.

### Helicopter Research Without Sustained Presence

Dropping in to collect data and leaving without building relationships or capacity treats the community as a site, not a partner. The trap is that it is efficient and publishable, but it extracts knowledge and leaves nothing behind, damaging trust for years.

### Overburdening The Community Without Compensation

Asing the same committed community members to carry the partnership repeatedly, because they are reliable, burns them out without fair reward. The trap is that reliable partners are easy to lean on, but unpaid over-reliance exploits the very people most invested in the work.

### Performative Participation For Funders Or Boards

Structuring engagement to satisfy a funder's or ethics board's requirement rather than to share power produces participation that exists on paper only. The trap is that it clears procedural hurdles while leaving the power structure intact, and the community can tell the difference.

## Self-Check

- [ ] For each consequential decision, is it clear and honest who holds the authority, and has real power been ceded over matters that affect the community?
- [ ] Is power asymmetry addressed structurally, with mechanisms that let less powerful voices be heard and that prevent gatekeeper capture?
- [ ] Is capacity-building a budgeted core deliverable that transfers concrete research skills to community members?
- [ ] Are community partners compensated fairly and transparently from the first grant, not expected to donate labor?
- [ ] Is credit and authorship shared equitably, with authorship granted to community members who meet the criteria rather than defaulted to academics?
- [ ] Is there a governance structure with real authority, including the community's documented right to halt or redirect the work?
- [ ] Is sustainability planned so the community retains skills, data, and structures after the project ends?
- [ ] Has tokenism been actively audited by checking whether community input actually changed decisions, not merely whether input was collected?
- [ ] Are the same few agreeable community members being over-relied upon, and is burnout without compensation being avoided?
- [ ] For projects involving marginalized communities or severe power imbalances, has an experienced community partner or ethics advisor reviewed the power-sharing, capacity, and compensation plan before implementation?

This skill provides methodological guidance on sharing power and building capacity in participatory research. It is not a substitute for a formal community partnership agreement or for ethics consultation. Participatory research that retains all real power with the researcher, that extracts community labor without fair compensation or credit, or that performs participation without genuine authority can harm communities, exploit partners, and erode trust. Where the stakes are high or the situation uncertain, consult experienced community partners and ethics advisors before proceeding.
