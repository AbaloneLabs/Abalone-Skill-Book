# Community Collaboration And Co-Design

Participatory research begins long before data collection, in the relationships and decisions that decide what the research is even about. The judgment problem is that collaboration is easy to claim and hard to do honestly. Researchers frequently arrive with a question, a design, and a timeline already fixed, then invite the community to "participate" by endorsing what was already decided. That is not collaboration; it is recruitment with a friendlier label. When the partnership is superficial, several harms follow. The research targets what the researcher assumes matters rather than what the community lives with. Community members experience the process as extraction or manipulation, giving time and access in exchange for no influence and no benefit. Marginalized groups, who are hardest to reach and easiest to overlook, are excluded from the partnership entirely, while gatekeepers or local elites speak for them. And the trust that any future work would depend on is quietly eroded, sometimes for a generation.

The agent should use this skill when deciding whom to partner with, how to build the relationship before the study begins, how to co-design the research question and process with community members, how to choose a partnership structure, or how to avoid the common pattern of consulting after the real decisions are made. The goal is to keep the agent from treating community collaboration as a recruitment step, when it is the foundation on which the legitimacy and relevance of the whole study rest.

## Core Rules

### Build The Relationship Before The Research Question

Genuine partnership cannot be bolted onto a finished proposal. It must precede and shape the question itself.

- Invest time in relationship-building before asking anything of the community, including introductions, listening, and establishing mutual interest.
- Understand the community's history with research, which may include prior extraction, broken promises, or trauma from outside investigators.
- Learn the community's own priorities, structures, and ways of making decisions before proposing any study.
- Be transparent about who you are, who funds you, and what you can and cannot offer.

A question drafted in an office and then "validated" with the community is not co-designed. A genuine partnership lets the community reshape or reject the question before it is asked.

### Co-Design The Research Question With Those Affected

The research question is the single most consequential decision in a study, because it determines what counts as a relevant finding. It should not be the researcher's alone.

- Surface the community's own framing of the issue, which often differs from the academic or funder framing.
- Make space for the community to say the proposed question is the wrong question, and to redirect it.
- Translate between academic language and community language without stripping meaning from either.
- Document how the final question was negotiated, so the collaboration is traceable rather than asserted.

A question that the community helped shape will be more relevant, more ethical, and more likely to produce findings the community can actually use. A question imposed on the community produces data that serves everyone except the people who provided it.

### Choose Partners Deliberately, Not Just Conveniently

Whom you partner with shapes whose knowledge and interests the research reflects. Convenience is a poor basis for partnership.

- Map who is affected by the issue, including groups that are less visible, harder to reach, or historically excluded.
- Distinguish representative community organizations from self-appointed gatekeepers, and verify claims to represent the community.
- Avoid defaulting to the first contact, the English speakers, the people with email, or the people already connected to institutions.
- Recognize that "the community" is never singular; it contains conflicting interests, and partnering with one faction can silence others.

Partnering only with the easy-to-reach or the powerful reproduces existing inequities inside the research itself.

### Make The Process Co-Designed, Not Just The Question

Participation in framing the question is necessary but insufficient. The community should help shape how the research is done.

- Co-decide methods, timelines, and what data will be collected, in forms the community can assess.
- Build in community review of instruments, recruitment, and analysis plans before they are used.
- Respect community norms around time, seasons, ceremonies, labor, and decision-making pace, which rarely match academic timelines.
- Budget for the slower pace that genuine co-design requires, rather than forcing collaboration into a grant deadline.

A study whose question is co-designed but whose methods are imposed still treats the community as a setting rather than a partner.

### Agree On Governance And Decision-Making Up Front

Ambiguity about who decides what is where partnerships collapse. Make governance explicit before conflict arises.

- Define which decisions are shared, which are the community's, and which the researcher retains, and be honest about the difference.
- Establish a structure for ongoing dialogue, such as a community advisory group with real authority, not a rubber stamp.
- Agree on how disagreements will be handled, including the community's right to halt or redirect the work.
- Document the partnership in a written agreement the community helps draft and understands.

Vague or unspoken governance lets the researcher retain all real power while the community bears the appearance of influence.

### Commit Time And Resources To The Partnership

Partnership is not free for the community. It costs time, labor, and social capital, and these must be recognized.

- Budget for community partners' time from the first grant, not as an afterthought.
- Provide resources that let the community participate as equals, such as meeting costs, translation, childcare, or stipends.
- Recognize that relationship-building takes longer than data collection and fund it accordingly.
- Avoid expecting communities to donate labor to research that advances the researcher's career.

A partnership that depends on the community's unpaid goodwill is extractive, regardless of how warm the relationship feels.

### Sustain The Relationship Beyond The Project

A partnership that ends when funding ends was not a partnership; it was a transaction.

- Plan from the outset for what the community retains after the project, including skills, data, and relationships.
- Share findings with the community first, in accessible forms, before academic publication.
- Honor commitments made during the work, and avoid disappearing when the grant closes.
- Leave the community able to continue or build on the work without the researcher.

Sustainability is a design criterion, not a hope. Partnerships that collapse at project end damage the community's trust in all future research.

### Attend To Ethics And Consent In Participatory Settings

Participatory research changes what consent and ethics mean, because community partners are co-producers, not only subjects.

- Reconsider consent when community members are also researchers collecting data from their neighbors.
- Address risks to the community as a whole, not only to individual participants.
- Recognize that power within the community can pressure individuals to participate or to stay silent.
- Engage ethics review early, and treat it as a floor, not a ceiling.

Standard individual consent processes were designed for research on passive subjects, not for research with active co-researchers, and they need adaptation.

## Common Traps

### Recruiting Into A Finished Design And Calling It Partnership

Arriving with the question, methods, and timeline already set, then inviting the community to endorse them, is participation in name only. The trap is that it satisfies a funder's engagement requirement while leaving the agenda untouched, and the community experiences it as being used rather than partnered with.

### Mistaking A Single Workshop For Co-Design

Holding one consultation event and treating the input as sufficient collaboration underestimates how much sustained dialogue genuine co-design requires. The trap is that a workshop is easy to report but rarely shifts the actual research, leaving the design essentially unchanged.

### Partnering Only With The Convenient Or The Powerful

Defaulting to the first contact, the institution, or the English speakers excludes the very groups participatory research is meant to center. The trap is that convenient partners make the work easier in the short term while silencing marginalized voices and reproducing inequity inside the study.

### Letting Gatekeepers Speak For The Community

Treating a local leader or organization as the voice of all affected people silences those the gatekeeper does not represent. The trap is that gatekeepers are efficient to engage, so they become a substitute for the harder work of reaching people with less power.

### Imposing Academic Timelines On Community Pace

Forcing relationship-building and co-design into grant deadlines strips the collaboration of substance. The trap is that academic schedules are rigid and easy to prioritize, but rushing the partnership produces shallow engagement and erodes trust.

### Expecting The Community To Donate Its Labor

Relying on community members' unpaid time and goodwill while the researcher is paid and credited is extractive. The trap is that warm relationships can mask the imbalance, but unpaid partnership still transfers benefit from the community to the researcher.

### Overpromising What The Collaboration Will Deliver

Promising influence, benefits, or change to secure participation, then failing to deliver, breaches the trust the partnership was built on. The trap is that overpromising eases recruitment in the moment but poisons the relationship when expectations go unmet.

### Treating Relationship-Building As A Phase That Ends

Assuming the relationship is established once and then proceeding without ongoing dialogue lets the partnership drift into tokenism. The trap is that one-time relationship-building feels sufficient, but trust requires continuous maintenance or it decays.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is the relationship being built before the research question is fixed, rather than after the design is complete?
- [ ] Was the research question genuinely co-designed with affected community members, including the possibility that they redirected or rejected it?
- [ ] Do the partners include marginalized and harder-to-reach groups, not only the convenient, powerful, or well-connected?
- [ ] Is the research process itself co-designed, with community review of methods, instruments, and timelines?
- [ ] Is governance explicit, with clear agreement on which decisions are shared and how disagreements are handled?
- [ ] Are community partners' time and labor budgeted and resourced from the start, rather than expected as goodwill?
- [ ] Is there a plan to sustain the relationship and share findings with the community before academic publication?
- [ ] Has the ethics approach been adapted for participatory settings, addressing community-level risks and co-researchers?
- [ ] Are gatekeepers and single contacts being checked against the broader community they claim to represent?
- [ ] For partnerships with marginalized communities, historically exploited populations, or settings with significant power imbalances, has an experienced community partner or ethics advisor reviewed the collaboration and co-design plan before the study begins?

This skill provides methodological guidance on forming equitable partnerships and co-designing research with communities. It is not a substitute for a formal community partnership agreement or for ethics consultation. Participatory research that is done tokenistically, that imposes an outside agenda, or that extracts community knowledge without shared decision-making can harm communities, damage trust, and exploit partners. Where the stakes are high or the situation uncertain, consult experienced community partners and ethics advisors before proceeding.
