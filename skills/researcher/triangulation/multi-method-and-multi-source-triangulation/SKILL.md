# Multi-Method And Multi-Source Triangulation

Triangulation is often described as combining multiple methods or sources to confirm a finding, and that thin description is where most of the harm enters. Agents reach for "more" as if adding methods were inherently strengthening, then assemble components that look different but share the same blind spot, the same instrument, the same informant, or the same underlying assumption. The result is pseudo-triangulation: the appearance of corroboration with none of the independence that gives corroboration its force. A survey and a focus group drawn from the same self-selected respondents, coded by the same analyst using the same framework, do not triangulate; they repeat each other while borrowing each other's bias. The judgment problem at the design stage is not how many methods to stack but how to choose components that genuinely differ along the dimensions that matter, so that convergence means something and divergence is even possible.

This skill covers the selection and combination side of triangulation. Use it before data collection, when deciding what counts as a methodologically distinct source, which type of triangulation the question needs, and whether the planned components are independent enough to do the work asked of them. The goal is to prevent the agent from confusing repetition with triangulation, from counting components instead of evaluating their independence, and from building a design that can only ever agree with itself. The companion skills address how to interpret agreement and disagreement once data exist, and how to use triangulation to establish trustworthiness and report it.

## Core Rules

### Choose The Type Of Triangulation The Question Demands

Triangulation is an umbrella for four distinct strategies, and conflating them produces a design that solves the wrong problem. Name which one the study is actually doing.

Select by:

- data source triangulation, comparing data drawn from different people, times, places, or settings;
- investigator triangulation, having different analysts collect, code, or interpret the same data;
- theory triangulation, viewing the same phenomenon through competing theoretical lenses;
- methodological triangulation, using different methods, such as interview plus observation plus document analysis.

Each type answers a different threat. Data-source triangulation tests whether a finding holds across sources; investigator triangulation tests whether it survives a different analyst; theory triangulation tests whether it survives a different explanatory frame; methodological triangulation tests whether it survives a different way of seeing. Choosing the type implicitly, or claiming "triangulation" without specifying the type, hides which threat is and is not being addressed.

### Demand Genuine Independence, Not Surface Variety

Independence is the property that makes triangulation work, and it is the property most often assumed rather than verified. Two methods that share a hidden dependency corroborate nothing.

Verify independence by asking, for each pair of components:

- do they draw on the same informants, samples, or datasets?
- do they share an instrument, a coding scheme, or a measurement model?
- do they share an analyst, a team, or a sponsor with the same incentives?
- do they rest on the same theoretical assumption or construct definition?
- could a single shared flaw bias both in the same direction?

If a shared bias could move both components the same way, they are not independent for the purpose of triangulation. A self-report survey and a self-report interview about the same topic, from the same respondents, share the bias of self-report. They may be useful for richness, but they do not independently corroborate the accuracy of the self-report.

### Make The Components Differ On The Dimension Under Test

Independence is general; the more useful test is specific. Components should differ precisely along the dimension that the finding's credibility depends on.

Design by:

- if the threat is informant bias, vary the informants and their stakes;
- if the threat is recall error, vary the time frame or use records versus memory;
- if the threat is method artifact, vary the method so that no single method's distortion is shared;
- if the threat is analyst subjectivity, vary the analysts and their training;
- if the threat is theoretical tunnel vision, vary the theoretical frames brought to the data.

A design that varies a dimension irrelevant to the main threat produces triangulation that is decorative. The components must differ where the doubt lives.

### Distinguish Triangulation From Mere Repetition

Repetition strengthens reliability of measurement; triangulation strengthens credibility of the finding. They are not the same, and treating them as interchangeable inflates claims.

Separate by:

- repetition uses the same method on the same kind of source to check consistency of the procedure;
- triangulation uses a different method, source, investigator, or lens to check the finding from another angle;
- repetition asks whether the instrument is stable;
- triangulation asks whether the conclusion survives being approached differently.

Repeating the same interview protocol with similar respondents tells you the protocol behaves consistently. It does not tell you whether the respondents are accurate, or whether the interview method itself is capturing what matters.

### Treat Between-Method And Within-Method Triangulation Differently

Methodological triangulation comes in two forms with different inferential weights, and the choice changes what convergence can mean.

Distinguish by:

- within-method triangulation uses variations of one method, such as two survey formats or two observation schedules, and mainly tests method robustness;
- between-method triangulation combines methods of different families, such as quantitative survey with qualitative interview, and tests the finding across epistemological approaches;
- between-method carries stronger triangulation force because the methods' distortions are less likely to overlap;
- within-method is easier but offers weaker corroboration and should be labeled as such.

Claiming the strength of between-method triangulation while actually doing within-method variations overstates the design.

### Match The Combination To The Research Question And Stakes

Not every study needs every type of triangulation, and adding components that the question does not require wastes effort and dilutes focus.

Match by:

- the specific inferential threat the research question is most exposed to;
- the stakes of the conclusion, with higher stakes warranting stronger, more independent triangulation;
- the resources and access realistically available, so that fewer well-chosen components beat many shallow ones;
- the paradigm and tradition the study sits in, since the meaning of triangulation differs across paradigms.

A low-stakes exploratory study may be well served by modest data-source triangulation; a study whose findings will guide policy or clinical practice owes the reader genuinely independent, between-method corroboration on the central claims.

### Predefine What Counts As A Component And Why It Was Chosen

The credibility of triangulation depends on the components being chosen for principled reasons before results are known, not assembled post hoc to fit a desired story.

Predefine by:

- listing each component and the specific threat it is meant to counter;
- stating the dimension on which it differs from the others;
- justifying why this source, method, analyst, or lens rather than another;
- documenting the plan before looking at how the components align.

Post hoc triangulation, where components are added or emphasized because they agree with a favored finding, is selection dressed as corroboration. The design logic must precede the results.

### Preserve The Integrity Of Each Component Before Combining

Triangulation is undermined when components are forced into agreement during analysis. Each method or source must be allowed to speak in its own terms before synthesis.

Preserve by:

- analyzing each component fully within its own logic before comparing;
- not smoothing away the differences a method is designed to surface;
- using the appropriate quality criteria for each method, not a single blended standard;
- combining only at a clearly stated level of inference, with the synthesis logic made explicit.

When components are merged too early, the distinct angle each was meant to provide is lost, and the result is a single blurred source rather than multiple perspectives.

## Common Traps

### Sources That Share The Same Bias

Two sources that share a flaw, such as both relying on motivated self-report or both drawn from a single institution's records, will agree for the wrong reason. Agreement between dependent sources feels like corroboration but is actually shared error. This is the most common form of pseudo-triangulation, and it produces false confidence precisely because the sources appear to converge.

### Counting Components Instead Of Weighing Independence

Adding a fourth or fifth method does not strengthen triangulation if the additions share the bias of the first three. Agents equate quantity with rigor, but three dependent sources offer less triangulation than two genuinely independent ones. The unit of value is independence, not number.

### Within-Method Variations Sold As Between-Method Triangulation

Running two survey versions or two similar observation schedules is within-method triangulation. Presenting it as if it carries the force of combining survey and ethnography overstates the corroboration. The weaker form is legitimate but must be labeled honestly.

### Convenience Selection Of Sources And Methods

Choosing methods or sources because they are available, cheap, or already collected ignores the dimension-under-test logic. Convenient components that do not differ where the doubt lives add bulk without inferential value, and they can create the illusion of a robust design.

### Post Hoc Assembly To Match A Favored Finding

Selecting or emphasizing components after seeing that they agree, while quietly dropping or downplaying those that disagree, is cherry-picking. It converts triangulation into a rhetorical device and destroys the credibility the practice is meant to build.

### Forcing Components Into Agreement During Analysis

When analysts blend methods too early or reinterpret a dissenting source until it fits, the distinct perspective each component offered is erased. The synthesis then reflects the analyst's preferred conclusion rather than the independent voices of the methods.

### Assuming Different Methods Cannot Share A Blind Spot

Methods from different families can still share assumptions, such as a shared construct definition, a shared sampling frame, or a shared cultural lens held by one team. Believing that methodological difference guarantees independence is a trap; independence must be argued for the specific components, not assumed from their labels.

### Ignoring Paradigm Tensions In Mixed-Method Combination

Combining methods from different paradigms without acknowledging the epistemological tension can produce a design where the components cannot actually speak to each other. The synthesis then papers over a real disagreement about what counts as knowledge, weakening rather than strengthening the claim.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is the specific type of triangulation named, and does it match the inferential threat the research question faces?
- [ ] For each pair of components, has independence been argued rather than assumed, including against shared instrument, informant, analyst, and theory?
- [ ] Do the components differ on the dimension where the main doubt lives, not merely on irrelevant dimensions?
- [ ] Has triangulation been distinguished from repetition, with each component's purpose stated as checking the finding rather than the procedure?
- [ ] Where methodological triangulation is used, is between-method versus within-method correctly identified and the inferential weight matched to the label?
- [ ] Is the combination matched to the stakes and resources, with fewer well-chosen components preferred over many shallow ones?
- [ ] Was the set of components and the reason for each chosen before results were examined, avoiding post hoc selection?
- [ ] Is each component analyzed in its own terms before synthesis, with the combination logic made explicit?
- [ ] Is there an explicit argument that the methods do not share a blind spot, rather than an assumption that difference guarantees independence?
- [ ] For high-stakes conclusions, mixed-paradigm designs, or cases where independence is uncertain, has a methodologist or relevant domain expert been consulted to review the triangulation plan?
