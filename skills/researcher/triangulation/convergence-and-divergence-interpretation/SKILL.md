# Convergence And Divergence Interpretation

Once multiple methods or sources have been gathered, the harder judgment begins, and it is where triangulation most often fails in practice. The instinct is to read agreement as proof and disagreement as noise, and both readings are wrong. Convergence across sources can be the strongest possible support for a finding, or it can be the signature of a shared bias that every source inherited from the same root. Divergence can be embarrassing inconsistency to be explained away, or it can be the most informative result in the study, revealing that the phenomenon is context-dependent, that the methods measure different things, or that one source is simply wrong. The harm comes from treating the two outcomes asymmetrically: welcoming convergence uncritically while quietly pruning divergence until the story is clean. A clean story built on suppressed divergence is not triangulation; it is confirmation bias with extra steps.

This skill covers the interpretive side of triangulation. Use it during and after analysis, when comparing what the sources actually show, deciding whether agreement is meaningful, deciding what disagreement is telling you, and resolving contradictions without erasing them. The goal is to prevent the agent from equating agreement with truth, from treating divergence as failure, and from manufacturing false harmony by reinterpreting dissenting sources until they comply. The agent has freedom in how to weigh evidence, but convergence and divergence must be interpreted against explicit criteria, not against the desirability of a tidy conclusion.

## Core Rules

### Predefine What Convergence And Divergence Will Mean

Convergence is not self-defining. Sources can agree at several different levels, and agreement at one level can coexist with disagreement at another. Decide in advance what kind of agreement would count as corroboration.

Predefine by:

- the level of inference at which convergence is sought, such as raw scores, themes, directions of effect, or explanatory claims;
- the degree of agreement required, since perfect agreement is suspicious and partial agreement is normal;
- whether convergence must hold across all sources or a stated subset;
- what divergence at each level would be taken to indicate.

Without a predefined standard, the analyst tends to declare convergence at whatever level the sources happen to agree on, which is post hoc reasoning. A finding can fail to converge on numbers yet converge strongly on the direction and explanation, and that distinction must be decided deliberately.

### Interpret Convergence Conditionally On Independence

Convergence only corroborates to the extent that the converging sources are independent. Agreement among dependent sources is shared bias, not mutual confirmation.

Interpret by:

- re-examining whether the agreeing sources truly differ on the dimension under test;
- asking whether a single shared flaw could produce the agreement;
- weighting convergence between genuinely independent sources far more heavily than convergence among linked sources;
- downgrading convergence that appears only at a trivial level while sources disagree on the substantive claim.

The rule is simple and constantly violated: the inferential value of convergence equals the independence of the sources. If you cannot defend the independence, you cannot claim the corroboration.

### Treat Divergence As Data, Not As Failure

Divergence is frequently the most analytically valuable outcome of triangulation, because it points directly at a boundary, a mechanism, or a flaw that convergence would have hidden. Suppressing it discards the finding that triangulation was uniquely positioned to produce.

Treat divergence by:

- asking what the disagreement reveals about the phenomenon, such as context-dependence or heterogeneity;
- asking what it reveals about the methods, such as each measuring a different facet;
- asking what it reveals about the sources, such as one being unreliable or biased;
- recording the divergence and its candidate explanations rather than resolving it prematurely.

A study where every source agrees perfectly may simply have failed to vary the sources enough. Persistent, explicable divergence is often a more honest and more useful result than suspicious unanimity.

### Distinguish Types Of Divergence Before Resolving

Not all disagreement is the same, and the resolution path depends on the type. Misclassifying divergence leads to the wrong fix.

Distinguish by:

- complementary divergence, where sources illuminate different facets that together form a fuller picture;
- contradictory divergence, where sources make claims that cannot both be true at the same level;
- elevating divergence, where a deeper source reframes what a shallower source showed;
- noise divergence, where differences fall within expected error and carry no substantive meaning.

Complementary divergence is resolved by integration at a higher level. Contradictory divergence requires deciding which source is more credible and why, or reframing the level of claim. Elevating divergence changes the interpretation itself. Treating all divergence as noise, or all as contradiction, collapses these distinct situations into one and produces either false harmony or false conflict.

### Resolve Contradictions By Reframing The Level Of Inference

Many apparent contradictions dissolve when the claim is stated at the correct level. Two sources can disagree on a surface measure while agreeing on the underlying explanation, or vice versa.

Reframe by:

- checking whether the sources are actually making claims about the same construct at the same level;
- moving to a higher level of abstraction where both can be true, such as a mechanism that produces different surface results in different conditions;
- specifying the conditions under which each source is correct, turning a contradiction into a conditional finding;
- resisting the urge to pick one source as the winner when both can be partially right.

A contradiction that survives careful reframing is a real disagreement that must be reported, not hidden. A contradiction that dissolves under reframing becomes a richer, conditional finding.

### Investigate The Source Of Disagreement Before Choosing A Side

When sources genuinely conflict, the temptation is to trust the preferred method or the larger dataset. The disciplined move is to investigate why they conflict, because the reason is usually more informative than the choice.

Investigate by:

- examining each source's quality, sampling, and potential biases in turn;
- checking for range restriction, floor or ceiling effects, or differential sensitivity;
- considering whether the sources capture different populations, time frames, or aspects;
- seeking a third independent source or a theoretical reason that predicts the discrepancy.

Choosing a side without understanding the discrepancy inherits the bias of whichever source was preferred. Understanding the discrepancy often shows that both sources were right about different things.

### Report Convergence And Divergence Symmetrically

The credibility of a triangulated analysis depends on the reader seeing both the agreement and the disagreement, with the interpretive logic for each. Reporting only convergence misrepresents the evidence.

Report by:

- presenting where sources agree and at what level, with the independence argument;
- presenting where sources disagree and the candidate explanations;
- stating explicitly how contradictions were resolved or reframed;
- preserving any unresolved divergence as a limitation or a finding rather than deleting it.

A reader who cannot tell whether divergence existed, and how it was handled, cannot evaluate the claim. Transparent handling of divergence is itself a marker of trustworthy analysis.

### Resist The Pressure Toward A Single Tidy Conclusion

Triangulation does not obligate the analyst to produce one harmonized answer. Sometimes the honest conclusion is conditional, mixed, or explicitly uncertain.

Resist by:

- allowing conclusions of the form "this holds under condition A but not condition B";
- accepting mixed findings where convergence and divergence coexist at different levels;
- stating uncertainty explicitly when sources remain genuinely unresolved;
- valuing an honest conditional conclusion over a false unanimous one.

The pressure to resolve everything into one clean story is the enemy of accurate interpretation. Conditional and mixed conclusions are often the correct output of triangulation.

## Common Traps

### Treating Agreement As Proof Of Truth

Agreement corroborates only against the independence of the sources. When sources share a bias, agreement is evidence of the shared bias, not of the finding's truth. Assuming agreement equals truth is the central error of naive triangulation and produces overconfidence in findings that may rest on a common flaw.

### Suppressing Or Downplaying Divergence

Divergence is routinely treated as a problem to be cleaned up rather than as evidence. Reinterpreting a dissenting source until it fits, or omitting it as an outlier, manufactures false convergence and hides the very information triangulation was meant to surface. The suppressed divergence often points at the study's most important insight.

### Declaring Convergence At Whatever Level Happens To Agree

When sources disagree on numbers, analysts may declare convergence on themes; when they disagree on themes, on direction; and so on, descending until agreement appears. Without a predefined level, this is post hoc and inflates the appearance of corroboration. The level of convergence must be chosen before, not after, seeing the data.

### Resolving Contradictions By Trusting The Preferred Method

When two sources conflict, defaulting to the favored method, the larger sample, or the more prestigious data inherits that source's bias and discards the other's signal. The reason for the conflict is more informative than the choice, and ignoring it wastes the triangulation.

### Assuming Perfect Agreement Is The Goal

Perfect agreement across independent methods is unusual and often suspicious, suggesting the methods were not actually independent or that the phenomenon is trivially uniform. Chasing perfect agreement pushes the analyst toward dependent sources and away from the informative disagreements that triangulation should reveal.

### Confusing Complementary With Contradictory Divergence

Two sources that illuminate different facets are not contradicting each other, yet they are often forced into conflict and then awkwardly reconciled. Misclassifying complementary divergence as contradiction leads to false conflict; misclassifying contradiction as complementarity leads to false harmony. The type must be diagnosed before any resolution is attempted.

### Erasing Conditions By Forcing Unconditional Claims

When sources agree only under certain conditions, generalizing to an unconditional claim overstates the finding. The conditions under which convergence holds are part of the result, and dropping them to make a cleaner statement converts a careful conditional finding into an overclaim.

### Counting Heads Instead Of Weighing Independence

Treating three agreeing sources as automatically stronger than one dissenting source ignores that the three may be dependent while the one is independent and well-founded. Majority is not a valid rule when the voters have different inferential weights, and triangulation is not a vote.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is there a predefined standard for what convergence means, including the level of inference and the degree of agreement required?
- [ ] Is every claim of convergence accompanied by an argument for the independence of the converging sources?
- [ ] Is divergence treated as data with candidate explanations, rather than as noise to be removed or failure to be hidden?
- [ ] Are types of divergence distinguished, with complementary, contradictory, elevating, and noise cases handled differently?
- [ ] Are apparent contradictions checked for a reframing of the inference level before any source is declared wrong?
- [ ] Is the source of disagreement investigated, rather than resolved by defaulting to the preferred method or the majority?
- [ ] Are convergence and divergence reported symmetrically, with unresolved divergence preserved as a limitation or finding?
- [ ] Are conditional and mixed conclusions accepted where the evidence supports them, rather than forced into a single tidy claim?
- [ ] Is the level at which convergence is declared the one that was predefined, not the one that happens to look best after seeing the data?
- [ ] For high-stakes conclusions, persistent unexplained divergence, or cases where the interpretation is genuinely uncertain, has a methodologist or domain expert been consulted to review how convergence and divergence were weighed?
