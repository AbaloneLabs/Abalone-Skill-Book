# Critical Discourse Analysis

Critical discourse analysis (CDA) examines how language, text, and multimodal communication reproduce, naturalize, or challenge relations of power, ideology, and inequality. Unlike purely textual analysis, CDA insists that discourse must be read in relation to the social, political, and historical contexts that produce it and that it in turn helps produce. Its strength is its political and explanatory ambition; its danger is its susceptibility to confirmation bias. Because the analyst often begins with a commitment to exposing domination, it is easy to cherry-pick quotations that confirm the expected critique and to read ideology into textual features that admit other interpretations. The judgment problem is to connect textual analysis rigorously to social context, to subject one's own political reading to evidential discipline, and to avoid both apolitical description that ignores power and predetermined critique that finds only what it went looking for. A CDA that asserts ideology without demonstrating the textual mechanisms is editorial, not analysis; a CDA that over-reads every feature as domination loses credibility and explanatory power.

Use this skill when conducting critical discourse analysis. The goal is to prevent the agent from cherry-picking evidence, from conflating textual features with ideological effects, and from presenting a political reading as if it were self-evident rather than argued. The agent has freedom in theoretical framing, but the link between text and context must be demonstrated and the analysis must be open to disconfirmation.

## Core Rules

### Connect Textual Analysis To Social Context Rigorously

CDA's distinctiveness is the link between textual detail and social, political, and historical context. That link must be argued, not assumed.

Connect by:

- identifying the specific textual or multimodal features under analysis;
- tracing how those features relate to the social practices and structures of the context;
- showing the mechanism by which discourse reproduces or contests power;
- grounding contextual claims in evidence about the setting, not assertion.

A claim that a text "naturalizes inequality" must show which features do so and how, in relation to a documented context. Context invoked vaguely is not context analyzed.

### Make The Theoretical Framework Explicit

CDA rests on theoretical commitments about discourse, power, and ideology. These must be stated, not hidden.

Make explicit by:

- the discourse-analytic tradition being used, such as Fairclough's dialectical-relational or van Dijk's socio-cognitive approach;
- the theory of power and ideology the analysis assumes;
- how the framework guides what counts as evidence;
- the relationship between the framework and the claims.

Hiding the framework makes the analysis appear self-evident when it is theory-laden. Explicit framing lets readers assess the lens and its implications.

### Demonstrate Textual Mechanisms, Do Not Assert Effects

Claims about what discourse does must be shown in the textual mechanisms, not inferred from the analyst's politics.

Demonstrate by:

- citing specific lexical, grammatical, or multimodal features;
- showing how those features work to position subjects, naturalize assumptions, or obscure agency;
- considering alternative readings of the same features;
- linking mechanism to effect through argument, not assertion.

A text's ideological work is demonstrated through its mechanics. Asserting that a feature is ideological without showing how it functions is editorial commentary.

### Guard Against Confirmation Bias And Cherry-Picking

Because CDA often has political aims, the risk of finding only confirming evidence is acute and must be actively managed.

Guard by:

- analyzing the full corpus or a representative sample, not just vivid extracts;
- seeking and reporting disconfirming or counter-evidence;
- considering whether the features identified are peculiar to the case or widespread;
- subjecting the preferred reading to the same scrutiny as rival readings.

A CDA that quotes only the extracts that support the critique, and ignores those that complicate it, is advocacy dressed as analysis. The discipline of seeking disconfirmation is essential.

### Analyze Multimodality Where Relevant

Contemporary discourse is often multimodal: image, layout, sound, and text together. Analysis must match the data's modality.

Analyze by:

- how visual and verbal elements interact to produce meaning;
- layout, framing, and composition as part of the discourse;
- the relationship between text and image in positioning the viewer;
- not reducing multimodal data to its verbal content alone.

Analyzing only the words of a news broadcast, advertisement, or social media post misses how images and design do ideological work alongside language.

### Historicize The Discourse

Discourse is historically situated. Meanings and ideological effects depend on what came before and what is contemporaneous.

Historicize by:

- the genre and its conventions at the time of production;
- intertextuality and how the discourse draws on prior texts;
- shifts in discourse over time that the analysis is positioned within;
- the historical conditions that make specific meanings possible.

A feature that seems ideologically loaded may be conventional for its genre and time. Historicizing prevents anachronistic or decontextualized readings.

### Consider Reception, Not Just Production

Discourse has effects through how it is received, not only through what its producers intended. Where possible, reception matters.

Consider by:

- the likely audiences and their interpretive resources;
- evidence of how the discourse was taken up, circulated, or contested;
- the gap between intended and actual effects;
- avoiding the assumption that effects follow automatically from features.

A text does not guarantee its effects. Strong CDA considers, or at least flags, how reception mediates between discourse and ideological outcome.

### Acknowledge The Analyst's Positionality

CDA is not neutral, and neither is the analyst. Positionality must be acknowledged, not hidden.

Acknowledge by:

- the analyst's own political and theoretical commitments;
- how those commitments shape what is noticed and claimed;
- the steps taken to counterbalance them;
- transparency rather than pretense of neutrality.

Acknowledging positionality is not a confession of bias that invalidates the work; it is the honesty that lets readers assess the analysis. Hidden commitments are more distorting than acknowledged ones.

## Common Traps

### Asserted Ideology Without Demonstrated Mechanism

Claiming a text reproduces power without showing the textual features and how they work is editorial, not analysis.

### Cherry-Picked Quotations

Quoting only extracts that support the critique, while ignoring complicating evidence, is advocacy dressed as analysis.

### Vague Context Invocation

Invoking social context without analyzing it leaves the text-context link asserted rather than demonstrated.

### Hidden Theoretical Framework

Presenting a theory-laden reading as self-evident hides the lens and prevents readers from assessing it.

### Ignoring Multimodality

Analyzing only the words of multimodal data misses how images and design do ideological work.

### Dehistoricized Reading

Interpreting discourse without its genre, intertextual, and historical situation produces anachronistic or shallow claims.

### Assumed Effects Without Reception

Assuming ideological effects follow automatically from textual features ignores how reception mediates outcomes.

## Self-Check

- Is the link between textual features and social context argued through demonstrated mechanisms?
- Is the theoretical framework, including the theory of power and discourse, made explicit?
- Are ideological effects shown in specific textual or multimodal features rather than asserted?
- Is confirmation bias managed through full-corpus analysis and active search for disconfirming evidence?
- Where data are multimodal, are image, layout, and design analyzed alongside language?
- Is the discourse historicized within its genre, intertextuality, and time?
- Is reception considered or flagged, rather than effects assumed from features alone?
- Is the analyst's positionality acknowledged with steps taken to counterbalance it?
- Are rival readings of the same features considered rather than dismissed?
- Does the analysis earn its political claims through evidential discipline rather than assertion?
