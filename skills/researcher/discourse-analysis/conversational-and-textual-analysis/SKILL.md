# Conversational And Textual Analysis

Conversation analysis and the fine-grained analysis of text examine how meaning, action, and social order are built moment by moment in language. These methods treat talk and text not as a window onto pre-existing thoughts but as the site where social reality is constructed. They demand close attention to the mechanics of interaction: turn-taking, pauses, repairs, overlap, word choice, sequencing, and the design of utterances. Agents often treat conversational or textual data like any other qualitative data, summarizing what was said and ignoring how it was said. This throws away exactly what these methods are for. The judgment problem is to produce transcripts detailed enough to support fine-grained analysis, to analyze the sequential and design features of talk and text rather than their content alone, and to ground claims in the demonstrated mechanics of the data rather than in the analyst's gloss. An analysis that paraphrases what participants said, without showing how they said it and what that doing accomplished, is not conversational or textual analysis.

Use this skill when analyzing conversation, interaction, or text at the fine-grained level. The goal is to prevent the agent from collapsing interaction into summary, from using transcripts too thin to support the analysis, and from imposing interpretations not grounded in the demonstrated mechanics of the data. The agent has freedom in focus, but the analytic discipline of attending to sequence, design, and context must be sustained.

## Core Rules

### Produce Transcripts Detailed Enough For Fine-Grained Analysis

The transcript is the data for these methods. A transcript that records only words cannot support analysis of how talk works.

Produce by:

- Jeffersonian or equivalent conventions capturing pauses, overlaps, intonation, and repairs;
- consistent transcription standards applied throughout;
- enough detail to show timing, emphasis, and the mechanics of interaction;
- transparency about transcription decisions and their limits.

A clean, word-only transcript hides the very features, pauses, overlaps, rising intonation, that make interaction analyzable. The transcript must be fit for the analytic purpose.

### Analyze Sequence, Not Just Content

Meaning in interaction is built sequentially. What an utterance does depends on what came before and what comes after.

Analyze by:

- how each turn responds to and shapes the prior one;
- the sequential position of an utterance and what that position makes relevant;
- how participants display their understanding in their next turn;
- the trajectory of the interaction over a sequence.

An utterance's meaning is not in the words alone but in their sequential context. Ignoring sequence reduces analysis to paraphrase of decontextualized content.

### Attend To The Design Of Utterances

How something is said, the specific words, grammar, and delivery, is itself action. Design choices are data.

Attend by:

- why participants chose this formulation over possible alternatives;
- how grammar, lexis, and delivery accomplish specific actions;
- how turn design displays stance, alignment, or resistance;
- the relationship between design and the action being performed.

A request, an accusation, and an invitation may use similar words; their design and sequential position distinguish what they are doing. Design is not decoration but action.

### Use Next-Turn Proof As Core Evidence

In conversation analysis, how participants respond in the next turn is evidence for how they understood the prior turn.

Use by:

- treating the next turn as a participant's own analysis of the prior;
- using displayed understanding as evidence rather than the analyst's inference;
- checking interpretations against participants' demonstrated orientations;
- avoiding claims about hidden meanings not oriented to by participants.

The analyst's interpretation is strongest when it is backed by participants' own displayed understandings in the unfolding interaction, not imposed from outside.

### Ground Claims In Demonstrated Mechanics

Claims must be shown in the data's mechanics, not asserted from the analyst's impression.

Ground by:

- citing specific transcript features that demonstrate the claim;
- showing the pattern across multiple instances where possible;
- distinguishing what the data show from what the analyst infers;
- making the link from feature to interpretation explicit.

A claim that participants "resist" or "align" must be shown in the transcript's design and sequence, not merely asserted. The reader must be able to see the basis.

### Preserve The Context And Ecology Of The Data

Talk and text occur in specific contexts that shape what is possible and meaningful.

Preserve by:

- the setting, participants, and activity that frame the interaction;
- the institutional or mundane character of the setting;
- how context shapes what actions are relevant and possible;
- avoiding analysis that treats extracts as free-floating text.

The same utterance does different work in a medical consultation, a classroom, and a casual chat. Context is constitutive, not background.

### Collect Naturally Occurring Data Where Possible

These methods are strongest with naturally occurring talk and text, not data generated by the research encounter.

Collect by:

- recordings of interaction that would have happened without the researcher;
- recognition that interviews are themselves interaction, not neutral reports;
- attention to how recording and the researcher's presence affect the data;
- justification when simulated or elicited data are used.

Interview data can be analyzed interactionally, but they are the interaction of the interview, not a transparent window onto other settings. Naturally occurring data are the gold standard for these methods.

### Handle Ethics And Consent For Recorded Interaction

Recording people's naturally occurring talk raises specific ethical demands.

Handle by:

- informed consent for recording and analysis;
- anonymization of participants and identifying details;
- special care with sensitive, institutional, or vulnerable settings;
- the ethics of quoting recognizable voices or interactions.

Consent to be recorded is not always consent to be quoted or identified. The ethical handling must match the sensitivity and identifiability of the interaction.

## Common Traps

### Thin Transcripts

Word-only transcripts hide the timing and delivery features that make interaction analyzable.

### Content Summary Instead Of Mechanism

Paraphrasing what was said, ignoring how it was said and what it did, abandons the method's purpose.

### Ignoring Sequence

Analyzing utterances in isolation, without their sequential position, misses how meaning is built.

### Imposed Interpretations

Claims about hidden meanings not oriented to by participants are the analyst's gloss, not grounded findings.

### Decontextualized Extracts

Analyzing extracts without their setting and activity treats talk as free-floating text.

### Interview Data As Transparent Window

Treating interview talk as direct report rather than interaction distorts what the data can show.

### Ethics Of Recording Overlooked

Failing to handle consent and anonymization for recorded interaction can harm participants who never expected analysis.

## Self-Check

- Is the transcript detailed enough, with timing, overlap, and delivery, to support fine-grained analysis?
- Does the analysis examine sequence and position, not just the content of utterances?
- Is the design of utterances, the specific formulation and delivery, treated as action?
- Are interpretations backed by next-turn proof and participants' displayed understandings?
- Are claims grounded in demonstrated transcript features rather than analyst assertion?
- Is the context, setting, and activity of the interaction preserved and analyzed?
- Is the data naturally occurring where possible, with interview data recognized as interaction?
- Are ethics and consent for recorded interaction handled with attention to identifiability?
- Are patterns shown across multiple instances rather than asserted from a single extract?
- Does the analysis show how meaning and action are built, not just what was said?
