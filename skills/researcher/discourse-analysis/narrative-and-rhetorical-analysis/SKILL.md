# Narrative And Rhetorical Analysis

Narrative and rhetorical analysis examines how people and texts construct meaning, identity, and persuasion through the shaping of stories and arguments. A narrative analysis asks how a story is built, its structure, plot, characters, temporality, and the identity work it does for the teller. A rhetorical analysis asks how a text or speech persuades, the devices, figures, appeals, and strategies through which it moves an audience. Agents often treat these as simply reading for themes or summarizing the content of stories and speeches. This misses the point: the object of study is how the narrative or argument is constructed and what that construction accomplishes, not merely what it is about. The judgment problem is to analyze structure and strategy rather than content alone, to choose an analytic framework suited to the data, and to ground interpretive claims in the specific features of the text. A narrative analysis that reports what stories are told without examining how they are built, or a rhetorical analysis that lists devices without showing their persuasive work, produces description where analysis was wanted.

Use this skill when analyzing narratives or rhetoric. The goal is to prevent the agent from reducing stories and arguments to content summary, from applying frameworks without fit, and from asserting effects without demonstrating the textual basis. The agent has freedom in framework and focus, but the analysis must engage structure and strategy and ground claims in the data.

## Core Rules

### Analyze Structure And Strategy, Not Just Content

The object is how a narrative or argument is built and what that building does, not only what it depicts or states.

Analyze by:

- for narrative: plot structure, temporality, causality, character positioning, and teller identity work;
- for rhetoric: the argument's arrangement, appeals, figures, and strategies of persuasion;
- how form and content work together to produce meaning or effect;
- the work the construction does for the teller, writer, or audience.

Content summary reports what a story is about; analysis shows how its telling accomplishes something. The shift from what to how is the core of these methods.

### Choose A Framework Suited To The Data And Question

Narrative and rhetorical analysis have several frameworks, each with distinct assumptions. The framework must fit.

Choose by:

- for narrative: Labovian structure, Ricoeurian temporality, positioning theory, or life-story approaches;
- for rhetoric: classical rhetoric, Burkean dramatism, argumentation theory, or contemporary rhetorical criticism;
- the match between framework and the kind of narrative or argument under study;
- consistency in applying the chosen framework.

A framework imposed without fit produces a mechanical application that misses what the data can show. The choice should be justified against the question.

### Examine Narrative Structure And Temporality

Narratives organize time and causality. How they do so is central to their meaning.

Examine by:

- the plot arc and its evaluative point, why the story is worth telling;
- the ordering of events and departures from chronological sequence;
- how causality and agency are attributed within the narrative;
- how the teller positions themselves and others through the structure.

A story's meaning lies partly in its structure: what is foregrounded, what is omitted, how past and present are connected. Structure is not a container for content but part of the meaning.

### Attend To Identity Work In Narratives

People tell stories to construct and present selves. Narrative analysis should attend to this identity work.

Attend by:

- how the teller positions themselves as a certain kind of person;
- how characters are drawn and evaluated;
- how the narrative manages accountability, blame, or virtue;
- the audiences, real or imagined, that the identity work addresses.

Narratives are not just reports of events; they are acts of self-construction. The analyst should ask what self the telling performs.

### Analyze Rhetorical Appeals And Strategies

Rhetorical analysis examines how texts persuade through specific appeals and strategies.

Analyze by:

- ethos: how credibility and character are established;
- pathos: how emotion is evoked and directed;
- logos: how arguments, evidence, and reasoning are arranged;
- the rhetorical situation: audience, purpose, occasion, and constraints.

A rhetorical analysis that lists devices without connecting them to the persuasive situation and effect describes the surface without explaining the work.

### Ground Interpretive Claims In Textual Features

Claims about what a narrative or argument does must be shown in the text, not inferred from the analyst's response.

Ground by:

- citing specific structural, lexical, or figural features;
- showing how those features produce the claimed effect or meaning;
- considering alternative interpretations of the same features;
- distinguishing strong from speculative claims.

An interpretation that the analyst feels but cannot anchor in the text is impression, not analysis. The reader must be able to follow from feature to interpretation.

### Consider Context, Audience, And Circulation

Narratives and rhetoric operate in contexts and for audiences that shape their meaning and effect.

Consider by:

- the setting and occasion of the telling or text;
- the intended and actual audiences and their expectations;
- the genre conventions the narrative or argument draws on or breaks;
- how circulation and uptake shape meaning over time.

The same story or argument does different work in different settings and for different audiences. Context and audience are constitutive of meaning, not background.

### Preserve The Voice And Particularity Of The Data

Narrative and rhetorical analysis should not flatten data into generic categories. The particularity of expression matters.

Preserve by:

- quoting enough of the narrative or text to convey its voice;
- respecting the teller's or writer's own language and design;
- not reducing distinctive expression to abstract codes;
- balancing analytic abstraction with fidelity to the data.

An analysis that quotes only fragments stripped of voice, or that translates everything into framework categories, loses what makes the data worth studying.

## Common Traps

### Content Summary Instead Of Structural Analysis

Reporting what stories or arguments are about, without examining how they are built, abandons the method's purpose.

### Framework Without Fit

Applying a narrative or rhetorical framework mechanically, without justifying its fit, misses what the data can show.

### Ignoring Temporality And Structure

Treating narratives as content containers ignores how plot, time, and causality produce meaning.

### Devices Listed Without Effect

Cataloguing rhetorical devices without connecting them to persuasive work describes the surface, not the analysis.

### Ungrounded Interpretation

Asserting effects or meanings the analyst feels but cannot anchor in specific textual features is impression, not analysis.

### Decontextualized Text

Analyzing narratives or arguments without their setting, audience, and genre misses how context constitutes meaning.

### Flattening Voice Into Categories

Reducing distinctive expression to abstract codes loses the particularity that makes the data worth studying.

## Self-Check

- Does the analysis examine structure and strategy, not just content?
- Is the analytic framework chosen to fit the data and question, and applied consistently?
- For narratives, are plot structure, temporality, causality, and identity work examined?
- For rhetoric, are appeals, strategies, and the rhetorical situation connected to persuasive effect?
- Are interpretive claims grounded in specific textual or structural features?
- Are alternative interpretations of the same features considered?
- Are context, audience, genre, and circulation considered as constitutive of meaning?
- Is the voice and particularity of the data preserved through adequate quotation?
- Is the distinction between strong and speculative claims maintained?
- Does the analysis show how the narrative or argument works, not just what it says?
