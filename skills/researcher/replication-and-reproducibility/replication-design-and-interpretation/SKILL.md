# Replication Design And Interpretation

A replication tests whether a finding holds when the study is repeated. But replication is not simple repetition, and interpreting replication results is genuinely hard. A failed replication can mean the original was wrong, but it can also mean the replication was underpowered, the conditions differed in ways that matter, or the outcome was always variable. A successful replication can confirm a finding or can reflect shared biases. The judgment problem is designing replications that are fair tests and interpreting their results without overclaiming in either direction.

Use this skill when planning a replication, when choosing between direct and conceptual replication, when sizing a replication, when interpreting a replication result, and when synthesizing a mixed replication record. The goal is to keep the agent from treating replication as a simple pass-or-fail verdict and from using a single replication to settle a question it cannot settle alone.

This is a high-stakes domain because replication outcomes shape what a field treats as established, and misinterpretation in either direction wastes effort and misleads. When designing a high-stakes replication or interpreting a conflicted evidence base, the agent should consult a methodologist and the original authors rather than declaring a verdict unilaterally.

## Core Rules

### Choose The Replication Type Deliberately

Replication is not one thing. The type determines what the result can and cannot show.

Distinguish:

- direct replication, which repeats the original methods as closely as possible to test whether the finding reproduces under the same conditions;
- conceptual replication, which tests the same hypothesis with different methods to test whether the underlying claim generalizes;
- close replication with planned variations, which repeats the core method while changing elements known or suspected to matter.

Direct replication tests whether the original result was reliable; conceptual replication tests whether the underlying phenomenon is robust. Confusing the two leads to misreading a result. A failed conceptual replication does not refute the original finding the way a failed direct replication might.

### Ensure The Replication Is A Fair Test

A replication that is underpowered, carelessly executed, or subtly different in ways that matter cannot settle anything. Fairness is a precondition for interpretation.

Make the replication fair by:

- contacting the original authors for materials, code, and protocol details;
- following the original procedure closely for a direct replication, documenting any unavoidable deviations;
- powering the replication to detect the original effect size, not merely matching the original sample;
- using the same outcome measures and analysis where possible;
- preregistering the replication to prevent outcome-conditioned interpretation;
- conducting the replication in conditions comparable to the original, or documenting and justifying differences.

A replication designed to fail proves nothing. A replication designed to succeed at all costs proves little. Fairness is the standard.

### Power The Replication Appropriately

Original studies that barely reached significance are likely overestimates of the true effect. A replication powered to match the original sample is likely to fail even if the effect is real.

Plan for:

- sample sizes large enough to detect the effect if it exists, accounting for expected shrinkage from the original estimate;
- power analysis based on a realistic effect size, ideally the lower bound of a plausible range;
- attention to the statistical evidence the replication provides, not just the significance threshold;
- the possibility that the true effect is smaller than originally reported, which requires more power to detect.

Underpowered replications that fail are uninformative, and overpowered replications that succeed trivially can both mislead. Match power to the inferential goal.

### Interpret Replication Results In Probabilistic Terms

A single replication result is not a verdict. Significance or non-significance alone is a crude summary of what the data show.

Interpret using:

- the effect size and its confidence or credible interval, not just the p-value;
- comparison of the replication effect size to the original;
- the relative evidence the data provide for and against the hypothesis;
- whether the result is consistent with a range of plausible effect sizes;
- the cumulative evidence across the original and all replications.

A non-significant replication with a confidence interval that includes the original effect is not a refutation. A significant replication with a much smaller effect is not a clean confirmation. Probabilistic reading resists false verdicts.

### Consider Methodological And Contextual Differences

Replications almost never reproduce the original exactly. Differences can explain divergence without impugning either study.

Examine:

- whether the sample, setting, era, or population differ in ways the theory predicts would matter;
- whether procedural deviations were forced or chosen;
- whether the original effect depended on contextual conditions that changed;
- whether measurement differences altered what was captured;
- whether the replication was conducted by a team with relevant expertise.

Differences do not automatically excuse a failed replication, but ignoring them produces false refutations. Name the differences and assess their likely impact.

### Treat Replication As Cumulative, Not Episodic

A single replication, successful or failed, rarely settles a question. The record accumulates, and patterns across studies carry more weight than any one.

Build toward:

- synthesis of all available replications, direct and conceptual;
- meta-analytic combination where appropriate, with attention to heterogeneity;
- recognition that publication bias shapes which replications are visible;
- weighting replications by quality, power, and closeness to the original;
- updating beliefs in proportion to the cumulative evidence.

The question is rarely "did it replicate" but "what does the body of evidence now support." Framing a single replication as definitive overstates what it can show.

### Report And Publish Replications Regardless Of Outcome

The value of replication depends on seeing both successes and failures. Selective publication of confirming replications recreates the very bias replication is meant to correct.

Commit to:

- preregistered reporting regardless of outcome;
- submitting null or contradictory results, not shelving them;
- journals or outlets that accept replications on design quality, not result direction;
- transparent reporting of deviations and their justifications.

A replication that is run but never reported contributes nothing to the record and may mislead the team that ran it.

## Common Traps

### Treating A Failed Replication As Definitive Refutation

A single failed replication, especially if underpowered or methodologically distant, does not refute a finding. The cumulative record matters more.

### Treating A Successful Replication As Full Confirmation

A replication that reaches significance with a smaller effect confirms direction but not magnitude, and may share biases with the original.

### Powering The Replication To Match The Original

Original significant results tend to overestimate effects. Matching the original sample size sets the replication up to fail even when the effect is real.

### Ignoring Contextual Differences That Explain Divergence

Replications conducted in different settings may legitimately differ. Dismissing divergence as failure without examining context produces false refutations.

### Confusing Direct And Conceptual Replication

A failed conceptual replication does not impugn the original direct finding the way a failed direct replication would. Mixing the types muddies interpretation.

### Reading Significance As The Whole Story

Significant or not is a crude summary. Effect sizes, intervals, and cumulative evidence carry the real inferential weight.

### Shelving Null Replications

Replications that fail and are never reported distort the record and waste the effort of running them.

## Self-Check

- Is the replication type (direct, conceptual, close with variation) chosen deliberately and matched to the inferential goal?
- Is the replication a fair test, with original materials, close procedures, documented deviations, and preregistration?
- Is the replication powered to detect a realistic effect size, accounting for expected shrinkage from the original estimate?
- Are results interpreted probabilistically using effect sizes and intervals rather than reduced to significance alone?
- Are methodological and contextual differences examined for their likely impact on divergence?
- Is the replication treated as part of a cumulative record rather than as a single definitive verdict?
- Is there a commitment to report and publish the replication regardless of outcome?
- For high-stakes replications or conflicted evidence bases, has a methodologist and the original authors been consulted rather than declaring a verdict unilaterally?
