# Preregistration And Registered Reports

Preregistration and registered reports are tools for separating planned, confirmatory analysis from unplanned, exploratory work. The problem they address is that when researchers analyze data with knowledge of the results, they can, often unintentionally, shape analyses, outcomes, and thresholds to produce favorable findings, a flexibility that inflates false positives and overstates effects. By committing to hypotheses and analysis plans in advance, the research community can distinguish results that test a prediction from those that emerged from searching the data. Agents often treat preregistration as a bureaucratic hurdle or apply it superficially, locking in vague plans that do not actually constrain post-hoc flexibility, which defeats the purpose.

The harm this skill prevents is the proliferation of unfalsifiable, post-hoc findings presented as confirmatory, the erosion of credibility when flexible analyses produce non-reproducible results, and the waste of resources on studies whose conclusions cannot be trusted. The agent has freedom in research design, but the integrity requirements of genuine pre-commitment are binding.

## Core Rules

### Decide Preregistration Based On The Research Question And Design

Preregistration is most valuable for confirmatory research testing specific hypotheses with inferential statistics, where analytic flexibility could influence conclusions. It is less essential for purely exploratory, descriptive, or theory-generating work, though transparency still helps. Decide deliberately whether and how to preregister based on the work's nature.

Assess fit:

- confirmatory hypothesis tests: preregistration highly valuable;
- exploratory or descriptive work: preregistration optional but transparency encouraged;
- qualitative research: preregistration may not fit, but audit trails do;
- secondary data analysis: preregister the analysis plan since data exist;
- preregister what is genuinely planned, not what you wish you had planned.

### Specify Hypotheses, Outcomes, And Analysis Plans In Detail

A vague preregistration that does not actually constrain analysis provides no protection. The value lies in specificity: which hypotheses, which primary and secondary outcomes, which analyses, which covariates, which exclusion rules, and which thresholds for significance or equivalence.

Specify in detail:

- the hypotheses with direction where applicable;
- primary and secondary outcomes and their measures;
- the exact statistical models and tests;
- covariates and how they were chosen;
- inclusion and exclusion criteria;
- handling of missing data;
- criteria for outliers and exclusions;
- the decision rules for interpreting results.

### Distinguish Confirmatory From Exploratory Analysis Clearly

Preregistration does not forbid exploration; it requires honesty about which analyses were planned and which emerged from the data. Both are valuable when labeled correctly. The damage comes from presenting exploratory findings as if they were confirmatory.

Label analyses:

- confirmatory: those preregistered and reported as planned;
- exploratory: those devised after seeing data, clearly labeled as such;
- acknowledge that exploratory findings need replication;
- do not retroactively frame an exploratory result as a planned test;
- report deviations from the preregistration transparently.

### Consider Registered Reports For High-Stakes Or Costly Studies

Registered reports are a publication format where the protocol is peer-reviewed and provisionally accepted before data collection. This shifts the evaluation from novelty of results to the quality of the question and method, removing publication bias and incentives for p-hacking. They are especially valuable for studies that are costly, controversial, or difficult to publish as null results.

Consider registered reports when:

- the study is expensive or hard to repeat;
- null results are likely and important;
- the question is high-stakes or contested;
- you want the method peer-reviewed before commitment;
- the venue offers the format and fits the work.

### Make Preregistrations Verifiable And Time-Stamped

A preregistration's value depends on its being verifiably prior to data analysis. Use a registry or repository that provides a time-stamped, immutable record. A private document on your own computer cannot be verified and offers no protection.

Use verifiable platforms:

- public registries like the Open Science Framework, AsPredicted, or field-specific registries;
- clinical trial registries for medical studies;
- ensure the record is time-stamped and immutable;
- include a persistent identifier or DOI;
- link the preregistration in the final paper.

### Handle Deviations From The Preregistration Transparently

Real research often deviates from the plan: a measure fails, an assumption is violated, an unexpected issue arises. Deviations are not failures; hidden deviations are. Report what changed and why, and assess whether the deviation affects the confirmatory interpretation.

Report deviations:

- describe what was preregistered versus what was done;
- explain the reason for each deviation;
- report both the planned and deviant analyses;
- assess sensitivity of conclusions to the deviation;
- do not silently adopt a more favorable analysis.

### Preregister Analysis Plans For Existing Data Carefully

Secondary analysis of existing data presents a special challenge: the analyst may already know the data. Preregistration still helps by committing to the analysis before running it, but the integrity depends on honesty about prior exposure to the data.

For existing data:

- declare any prior exposure to the data or results;
- preregister the analysis plan before running it;
- use a frozen analysis file or audit trail;
- acknowledge the limits of preregistration when data are already known;
- consider holding out data or using split-sample approaches.

### Balance Pre-Commitment With Legitimate Flexibility

Preregistration is not meant to force researchers to follow a plan that turns out to be flawed. If the preregistered analysis is inappropriate given the data encountered, the right response is to report the issue, justify an alternative, and label it as such. The goal is transparency, not rigidity that produces worse science.

Balance:

- follow the plan when it remains appropriate;
- deviate transparently when the plan is flawed;
- never let pre-commitment produce a clearly wrong analysis;
- report both planned and revised analyses;
- let transparency, not rigidity, govern.

## Common Traps

### Vague Preregistration That Does Not Constrain

Plans too general to limit flexibility offer no protection. Specify outcomes, analyses, and rules.

### Presenting Exploratory Results As Confirmatory

Labeling a post-hoc finding as planned destroys credibility. Mark exploratory work honestly.

### Hidden Deviations From The Plan

Silently switching analyses is the very problem preregistration prevents. Report all deviations.

### Preregistration As A Box To Tick

Doing it superficially for compliance, without genuine commitment, defeats the purpose. Engage it substantively.

### Ignoring Registered Reports When They Fit

High-stakes studies that would benefit from in-principle acceptance miss a valuable format. Consider it.

### Unverifiable Private "Preregistration"

A document only you can see cannot be verified. Use time-stamped public registries.

### Rigid Adherence To A Flawed Plan

Following a preregistered analysis that is clearly wrong produces bad science. Deviate transparently when needed.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is the decision to preregister based on the research question and design?
- [ ] Does the preregistration specify hypotheses, outcomes, analyses, and exclusion rules in detail?
- [ ] Are confirmatory and exploratory analyses clearly distinguished and labeled?
- [ ] Has a registered report format been considered for high-stakes or costly studies?
- [ ] Is the preregistration on a verifiable, time-stamped, public platform?
- [ ] Are deviations from the preregistration reported transparently with reasons?
- [ ] For existing data, is prior exposure declared and analysis committed before running?
- [ ] Does the preregistration balance pre-commitment with legitimate, transparent flexibility?
- [ ] Is the preregistration linked in the final paper with a persistent identifier?
- [ ] Are both planned and deviant analyses reported so readers can judge robustness?
