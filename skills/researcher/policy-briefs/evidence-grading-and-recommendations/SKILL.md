# Evidence Grading And Recommendations

Not all evidence is equal, and recommendations must reflect that inequality. A recommendation built on a single observational study should not carry the same force as one built on multiple randomized trials, and decision makers need to know the difference. Evidence grading systems exist to make the strength and certainty of evidence explicit, so that recommendations are calibrated to what the evidence can actually support. Agents often produce recommendations without grading the underlying evidence, or apply grading systems mechanically without understanding the judgment involved, producing recommendations that either overstate or understate what is known.

The harm this skill prevents is recommendations that mislead decision makers about how much to trust the evidence, guidelines that collapse when weak foundations are exposed, and the erosion of evidence-informed practice through overconfident pronouncements. The agent has freedom in framework choice, but the discipline of transparent, calibrated evidence assessment is mandatory.

## Core Rules

### Use A Recognized Evidence Grading Framework

Ad hoc judgments about evidence quality are inconsistent and unaccountable. Recognized frameworks like GRADE and its counterparts provide structured, transparent methods for assessing evidence strength. Use one consistently and report it.

A framework provides:

- defined criteria for rating evidence quality;
- a transparent process others can audit;
- consistency across recommendations;
- a shared language for certainty;
- a basis for linking recommendations to evidence.

Choose the framework suited to the field and question, and apply it throughout.

### Rate Evidence Based On Multiple Dimensions, Not Just Study Design

Study design is the starting point, but evidence strength depends on more. A body of randomized trials can be downgraded for bias, inconsistency, or indirectness, and a body of observational studies can be upgraded for large effects or dose-response. Grade the whole body of evidence, not individual studies in isolation.

Assess dimensions including:

- risk of bias across studies;
- inconsistency of results across studies;
- indirectness of population, intervention, or outcomes;
- imprecision of estimates;
- publication bias;
- magnitude of effect;
- dose-response gradient;
- confounding direction.

### Distinguish Quality Of Evidence From Strength Of Recommendation

These are related but distinct. Evidence quality is about how confident we are in the estimate of effect. Recommendation strength is about how strongly the action is advised, which also depends on values, costs, acceptability, and the balance of benefits and harms. High-quality evidence does not always yield a strong recommendation, and vice versa.

Keep separate:

- certainty in the evidence (how true is the estimate);
- strength of recommendation (how strongly to act);
- the values and preferences that inform the recommendation;
- the resource and feasibility considerations.

### Balance Benefits And Harms Explicitly

A recommendation weighs desirable against undesirable outcomes. Even strong evidence of benefit may yield a weak recommendation if harms, costs, or burdens are substantial. Make this balancing explicit rather than implied.

Balance:

- magnitude and importance of benefits;
- magnitude and frequency of harms;
- burden on patients, providers, or systems;
- cost and resource use;
- distribution of benefits and harms across groups;
- uncertainty in both benefits and harms.

### Involve Stakeholders In Value Judgments

Because recommendations combine evidence with values, the people affected should inform the value judgments. Clinicians, patients, communities, and policymakers hold relevant perspectives on what outcomes matter and what tradeoffs are acceptable.

Involve stakeholders in:

- selecting which outcomes matter most;
- weighing benefits against harms;
- judging acceptability and feasibility;
- interpreting uncertain evidence;
- reviewing draft recommendations.

### Make Recommendations Actionable And Specific

A graded recommendation should tell the user what to do, for whom, and under what conditions, along with the strength and evidence basis. Vague recommendations cannot be implemented or audited.

Specify:

- the population the recommendation applies to;
- the intervention or action;
- the comparison or alternative;
- the strength (strong or weak, or equivalent);
- the evidence certainty behind it;
- any conditions or caveats.

### Report The Grading Transparently

The reasoning behind a grade should be visible. A reader should be able to see why evidence was rated as it was and how that rating led to the recommendation. Opaque grades undermine trust and accountability.

Report:

- the framework used;
- the evidence reviewed and its design;
- the ratings on each dimension;
- the reasoning for upgrades and downgrades;
- the final evidence certainty;
- how values and preferences entered the recommendation;
- areas of disagreement among the panel.

### Update Recommendations As Evidence Evolves

Evidence accumulates, and recommendations that are not updated become stale and potentially harmful. Build in review and update mechanisms so that recommendations track the current state of evidence.

Plan for updating:

- schedule periodic evidence reviews;
- monitor for new pivotal studies;
- version and date recommendations;
- flag recommendations likely to change;
- communicate updates to users.

## Common Traps

### Recommending Without Grading The Evidence

Ungraded recommendations leave users unable to judge how much to trust them. Grade the evidence transparently.

### Mechanical Grading Without Judgment

Applying a framework as a formula ignores the judgment dimensions require. Engage the reasoning, not just the scores.

### Conflating Evidence Quality With Recommendation Strength

High certainty does not automatically mean a strong recommendation. Keep them separate.

### Ignoring Harms And Burdens

Focusing only on benefits skews the balance. Weigh undesirable outcomes explicitly.

### Excluding Stakeholders From Value Judgments

Recommendations impose values; those affected should shape them. Involve stakeholders.

### Vague Recommendations

"Consider intervention X" tells the user nothing. Specify population, action, and strength.

### Static Recommendations That Never Update

Stale recommendations persist after evidence shifts. Build in review cycles.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is a recognized evidence grading framework applied consistently and reported?
- [ ] Is evidence rated across multiple dimensions (bias, consistency, indirectness, imprecision, publication bias)?
- [ ] Are evidence quality and recommendation strength kept distinct?
- [ ] Are benefits and harms balanced explicitly, including burdens and costs?
- [ ] Are stakeholders involved in the value judgments behind recommendations?
- [ ] Are recommendations specific about population, action, strength, and evidence certainty?
- [ ] Is the grading reasoning transparent and auditable?
- [ ] Are upgrades and downgrades explained with reasoning?
- [ ] Is there a mechanism to update recommendations as evidence evolves?
- [ ] Are areas of panel disagreement acknowledged?
