# Stakeholder Engagement

Research that affects people's lives is stronger when those people help shape it, and weaker, sometimes ethically compromised, when imposed from outside. Stakeholder engagement is the disciplined practice of involving the people and organizations affected by or able to act on research, not as subjects but as partners whose knowledge and priorities inform the work. Agents often treat engagement as a box to tick for grant requirements, producing token consultations that satisfy paperwork but change nothing, or they engage so broadly that the project loses focus. Meaningful engagement is a design choice with methodological and ethical consequences.

The harm this skill prevents is research that misses what matters to affected communities, extracts knowledge without benefit to participants, generates findings no one can use, and damages trust between researchers and the public. The agent has freedom in engagement methods, but the principles of respect, reciprocity, and genuine influence are binding.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Full Range Of Relevant Stakeholders Early

Stakeholders are not only the obvious beneficiaries. They include those affected by the problem, those who deliver services, those who hold decision power, those whose cooperation the research requires, and those historically excluded from similar research. Mapping stakeholders comprehensively prevents blind spots.

Map stakeholders:

- who experiences the problem the research addresses;
- who acts on or implements solutions;
- who holds resources or authority;
- who has been excluded from past research on this topic;
- who might be harmed or benefit from the findings;
- whose perspectives differ from the dominant view.

### Define The Level And Purpose Of Engagement

Engagement ranges from informing to consulting to collaborating to sharing power. Each level has different expectations, commitments, and risks. Be explicit about what level of engagement you are offering, and do not overpromise.

Clarify engagement level:

- informing: sharing findings with stakeholders;
- consulting: seeking input on predefined questions;
- involving: working together on design or interpretation;
- collaborating: shared decision making throughout;
- empowering: stakeholder-led with researcher support.

Match the level to the project's goals and the stakeholders' wishes, and state it honestly.

### Engage From The Beginning, Not Just At The End

The most valuable stakeholder input shapes the research question, design, and measures, not just the dissemination of results. Engaging only at the end to "translate findings" misses the chance to ask better questions and produces research stakeholders never wanted.

Engage early:

- involve stakeholders in defining the problem and question;
- consult on what outcomes matter to them;
- co-design measures and data collection;
- seek input on recruitment and consent;
- include stakeholders in interpreting findings.

### Build Reciprocity And Mutual Benefit

Stakeholders give time, knowledge, and access. Research that takes without giving back exploits the relationship and erodes trust. Design engagement so that stakeholders benefit, whether through compensation, capacity, access to findings, or influence on issues they care about.

Build reciprocity:

- compensate stakeholders for their time and expertise;
- share findings in accessible formats and timely ways;
- build capacity or skills where stakeholders value it;
- ensure the research addresses a stakeholder priority;
- credit stakeholders appropriately in outputs.

### Respect Diverse Knowledge And Lived Experience

Stakeholders hold knowledge that academic methods may not capture: lived experience, practitioner expertise, and community history. Treating this knowledge as inferior to formal evidence is both disrespectful and methodologically limiting.

Respect diverse knowledge by:

- valuing experiential and practical knowledge alongside academic;
- not requiring stakeholders to translate their knowledge into academic terms alone;
- recognizing that disagreement with the researcher may reflect insight, not ignorance;
- creating space for perspectives that challenge the research framing;
- acknowledging the limits of the researcher's own perspective.

### Manage Power Dynamics Deliberately

Research relationships are inherently unequal. Researchers hold resources, credentials, and agenda-setting power. Without deliberate attention, engagement replicates existing hierarchies and silences the least powerful voices.

Address power:

- be aware of your own positional power;
- create structures that amplify marginalized voices;
- avoid letting the loudest or most privileged stakeholders dominate;
- ensure consent and participation are genuinely voluntary;
- be transparent about what stakeholders can and cannot influence.

### Close The Loop And Sustain Relationships

Engagement that ends when funding ends, or that never reports back, damages trust and makes future collaboration harder. Plan for how the relationship continues and how stakeholders learn what came of their input.

Sustain engagement:

- report findings back to stakeholders, not only to funders and journals;
- explain how stakeholder input shaped (or did not shape) the research;
- share credit and authorship where appropriate;
- maintain relationships beyond a single project;
- be honest about constraints that limited influence.

### Document And Evaluate Engagement

Engagement is a methodological choice that should be documented and reflected upon, like any other part of the research design. This supports rigor, accountability, and learning for future work.

Document:

- who was engaged and how;
- what input was sought and received;
- how input influenced the research;
- what tensions or limitations arose;
- how engagement could improve.

## Common Traps

### Token Engagement To Satisfy Grant Requirements

A single consultation that changes nothing satisfies paperwork but exploits stakeholders. Engage meaningfully or not at all.

### Engaging Only At Dissemination

End-only engagement misses the chance to shape the question and design. Involve stakeholders from the start.

### Overpromising Influence

Telling stakeholders they are partners while retaining all decision power breeds cynicism. Be honest about the engagement level.

### Extracting Knowledge Without Reciprocity

Taking stakeholders' time and insight without giving back exploits the relationship. Build mutual benefit.

### Privileging Academic Knowledge Over Lived Experience

Dismissing experiential knowledge as unscientific limits the research and disrespects partners. Value diverse knowledge.

### Letting Powerful Voices Dominate

Without structure, the most privileged stakeholders set the agenda. Deliberately amplify marginalized perspectives.

### Disappearing When Funding Ends

Abandoning relationships after the project erodes trust. Plan for continuity and feedback.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Have you mapped the full range of relevant stakeholders, including historically excluded groups?
- [ ] Is the level and purpose of engagement explicit and honestly communicated?
- [ ] Are stakeholders engaged from the beginning, including in question and design?
- [ ] Is reciprocity built in through compensation, benefit, and accessible findings?
- [ ] Is diverse knowledge, including lived experience, respected alongside academic evidence?
- [ ] Are power dynamics acknowledged and structures created to amplify marginalized voices?
- [ ] Is there a plan to close the loop and report findings back to stakeholders?
- [ ] Are relationships sustained beyond a single funding cycle?
- [ ] Is engagement documented and reflected upon for rigor and learning?
- [ ] Have you avoided overpromising influence you cannot deliver?
