# Translating Research To Policy

Policy makers operate under constraints that researchers rarely face: tight timelines, political pressure, competing interests, limited attention, and accountability to constituents. Research that arrives as a dense academic argument, no matter how rigorous, often goes unused because it does not speak to the decision at hand. Translating research to policy is the discipline of distilling evidence into a form that is actionable, timely, and honest about what the evidence can and cannot support. Agents often produce briefs that read like journal abstracts or that overstate what the evidence permits, both of which fail the policy audience.

The harm this skill prevents is evidence being ignored when it could inform decisions, and policy being built on overclaimed research that later collapses, damaging the credibility of evidence-informed policy generally. The agent has freedom in framing and format, but the constraints of relevance, actionability, and evidentiary honesty are binding.

## Core Rules

### Start From The Decision, Not The Study

A policy brief serves a decision, not a publication record. The starting point must be the policy question or problem the decision maker faces, with the research positioned as evidence bearing on that question. Briefs that begin from "here is our study" fail to connect.

Frame around the decision:

- what specific decision or problem is this brief addressing;
- who has the authority to act on it;
- what is the timeline for that decision;
- what options are realistically on the table;
- what does the evidence say about each option.

### Lead With The Recommendation And Its Basis

Policy makers read the first paragraph and may read no further. The recommendation and the strength of evidence behind it must appear at the top, not buried after a literature review.

Front-load:

- the clear, specific recommendation in plain language;
- the strength and nature of the supporting evidence;
- the key caveat or condition on the recommendation;
- the consequence of acting or not acting;
- a one-line summary of the basis.

Detail and justification follow for those who read on.

### Be Honest About Evidence Strength And Limits

Policy audiences need to know how much weight to place on the evidence. Overstating certainty leads to policy built on sand; understating leads to inaction. Calibrate the recommendation to the actual strength of the evidence.

Convey evidence honestly:

- distinguish established findings from emerging or contested ones;
- note the quality of the underlying studies;
- acknowledge where evidence is absent or weak;
- separate what is known from what is assumed;
- flag where values, not evidence, drive the choice.

### Make Recommendations Specific And Actionable

Vague recommendations like "invest in prevention" are useless to a decision maker. Recommendations must specify what should be done, by whom, and with what expected effect.

Actionable recommendations specify:

- the concrete action or policy change;
- the actor or body responsible;
- the target population or scope;
- the mechanism by which it is expected to work;
- the resources or conditions required;
- how success would be measured.

### Address Implementation Feasibility

A recommendation that ignores feasibility is an academic exercise. Policy makers must weigh cost, political acceptability, administrative capacity, legal authority, and unintended consequences. Evidence-informed recommendations that engage these realities are far more useful.

Address feasibility:

- what the option costs and who pays;
- what administrative capacity it requires;
- what political or public resistance it may face;
- what legal or regulatory authority is needed;
- what unintended effects might follow;
- what evidence exists from similar implementations elsewhere.

### Use Format And Length Suited To The Audience

Policy briefs are short by necessity. A two-page brief, a one-page memo, and an oral briefing demand different compression. Match format to how the audience consumes information.

Format discipline:

- keep core briefs to one to four pages;
- use clear headers, bullets, and visual emphasis;
- include a short evidence summary or table;
- use plain language, not academic prose;
- provide references or a technical appendix for those who want depth.

### Separate Evidence From Values

Policy decisions combine evidence with values, and conflating them confuses the debate. Be explicit about which part of the recommendation rests on evidence and which rests on value judgments about what is desirable or just.

Clarify:

- what the evidence says will happen if an option is taken;
- what values or priorities determine whether that outcome is desirable;
- where reasonable people could disagree based on values, not evidence;
- what tradeoffs the recommendation accepts.

### Time The Input To The Decision Window

The best evidence is useless if it arrives after the decision is made. Policy windows open briefly. Engage early enough to influence the agenda and the options under consideration.

Time engagement:

- identify when the decision will be made;
- provide input during option formation, not after;
- build relationships before the urgent moment;
- be available for rapid response when asked;
- update briefs as the policy context evolves.

## Common Traps

### Writing A Journal Abstract Instead Of A Policy Brief

Academic framing does not serve decision makers. Frame around the decision and recommendation.

### Overstating Evidence To Drive Action

Inflated certainty leads to policy that collapses when evidence weakens, discrediting the enterprise. Calibrate to real strength.

### Vague Recommendations

"Invest more" or "raise awareness" tells the decision maker nothing actionable. Specify the action, actor, and mechanism.

### Ignoring Feasibility And Cost

Recommendations detached from political and administrative reality are ignored. Engage the constraints.

### Arriving After The Decision

Late evidence is useless. Time input to the decision window.

### Conflating Evidence With Values

Hiding value judgments inside "what the evidence shows" distorts the debate. Separate them explicitly.

### Burying The Recommendation

If the recommendation is not in the first paragraph, the reader may never find it. Front-load it.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is the brief framed around a specific decision or policy problem, not the study?
- [ ] Does the recommendation and its evidence basis appear in the first paragraph?
- [ ] Is the strength of evidence honestly conveyed, including gaps and weaknesses?
- [ ] Are recommendations specific, actionable, and assigned to a responsible actor?
- [ ] Does the brief address implementation feasibility, cost, and political constraints?
- [ ] Is the format and length suited to how the audience consumes information?
- [ ] Are evidence-based conclusions separated from value judgments?
- [ ] Is the input timed to the actual decision window?
- [ ] Are tradeoffs and unintended consequences acknowledged?
- [ ] Is the language plain and free of unnecessary academic prose?
