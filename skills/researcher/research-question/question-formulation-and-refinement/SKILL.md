# Question Formulation And Refinement

The single most common reason a study fails is that its question was never actually formulated. Researchers begin with a topic, an interest, or a dataset, and they carry that vague thing forward until the design, the analysis, and the conclusion all bend around an undefined target. A vague question cannot be matched to a design, cannot be operationalized into variables, and cannot be answered by evidence, because no one, including the researcher, knows what would count as an answer.

Use this skill when moving from a topic to a question, when refining a draft question, when judging whether a question is answerable and worth answering, and before committing to a design. The goal is to keep the agent from settling on a question that is too broad, too narrow, unfalsifiable, or already answered, and from disguising a topic as a question. The agent has latitude in framing and framework, but the question must be specific enough that evidence could confirm or disconfirm it.

## Core Rules

### Separate Topic From Question From Title

A topic is an area of interest; a question is a specific inquiry; a title is a label. Confusing them produces work that drifts.

Distinguish:

- topic, the broad domain, such as remote work and well-being;
- question, the specific inquiry, such as how does fully remote work affect the loneliness scores of new hires during their first year;
- aim, the intention of the study;
- objective, the concrete step toward the aim;
- hypothesis, the testable prediction where applicable;
- title, the communicative label for the finished work.

Write each of these separately and check that they align. A topic in the title with a question in the methods signals an unresolved formulation.

### Use A Structured Framework To Force Specificity

Frameworks exist because unaided formulation tends to stay vague. They force the researcher to name the elements that make a question answerable.

Apply a framework matched to the question type:

- PICO for quantitative intervention questions, population, intervention, comparator, outcome;
- PECO for exposure questions;
- SPIDER or SPICE for qualitative or mixed questions;
- FINER to judge whether a question is feasible, interesting, novel, ethical, and relevant;
- PICOT adding time, or PICOC adding context, where relevant.

The framework is a discipline, not a formality. If an element cannot be specified, the question is not yet ready.

### Specify Population, Context, And Time

A question without a defined population and context cannot be answered, because the answer depends on whom and where it concerns.

Define:

- the population, with inclusion boundaries;
- the setting or context;
- the time period;
- the level of analysis, individual, group, organization, or system;
- subgroups of particular interest;
- exclusion criteria and their rationale.

A question about "people" is not a question; a question about first-year university students in urban campuses during a specific semester is.

### Specify The Phenomenon, Comparator, And Outcome

The core of a researchable question names what is being examined, against what, and with what result.

Make explicit:

- the phenomenon, intervention, exposure, or experience;
- the comparator, such as a control, baseline, alternative, or prior state;
- the primary outcome or experience of interest;
- secondary outcomes where relevant;
- the direction or relationship expected, if any;
- the form of the answer sought, such as a difference, association, process, or meaning.

A question that lacks a comparator often cannot distinguish the phenomenon's effect from background change.

### Match The Question Type To The Kind Of Answer Sought

Different questions demand different evidence. The wording of the question should imply the kind of answer and therefore the design.

Identify:

- descriptive questions, asking what is the case;
- exploratory questions, asking what patterns or themes exist;
- explanatory or causal questions, asking why or what effect;
- predictive questions, asking what will happen;
- evaluative questions, asking how well something works;
- interpretive questions, asking what something means;
- comparative questions, asking how things differ.

A "why" question answered by a cross-sectional survey, or a "how much" question answered by interviews, signals a mismatch between question and method.

### Judge Feasibility, Novelty, Ethics, And Relevance Together

A well-formulated question can still be a bad question to pursue if it is infeasible, redundant, unethical, or irrelevant. Judge these before committing.

Assess:

- feasibility, given access, time, budget, skills, and data;
- novelty, against an honest literature search;
- ethics, given participants, data, and topic sensitivity;
- relevance, to theory, practice, policy, or affected communities;
- interest, sufficient to sustain the work and engage the field;
- the cost of the question relative to the value of the answer.

A question that fails one of these may still be answerable but may not be worth answering now, by this team, in this way.

### Refine Iteratively And Expose The Question To Falsification

A good question is the product of revision, not a first draft. Refinement should expose the question to the possibility of being wrong.

Refine by:

- writing the question in plain language and re-reading it cold;
- stating what evidence would confirm and what would disconfirm it;
- checking it against the literature for redundancy and gaps;
- testing it against alternative framings;
- asking whether a non-expert could identify the target;
- checking that it does not embed its answer;
- revising until it is specific, answerable, and falsifiable where applicable.

A question that no result could disconfirm is not a research question; it is a stance.

### Document The Question And Its Rationale

The question will be tested against drift throughout the study. A documented formulation protects against silent mutation.

Record:

- the final question in its structured form;
- the aim, objective, and hypothesis derived from it;
- the framework used and the elements specified;
- the rationale for the population, comparator, and outcome;
- the type of answer sought;
- the feasibility, novelty, ethics, and relevance assessment;
- the date and version, since questions evolve.

A dated, version-controlled question lets the team detect and report deviations honestly.

## Common Traps

### Confusing A Topic With A Question

"Remote work and productivity" is a topic. Without population, comparator, outcome, and time, it cannot be answered.

### Embedding The Answer In The Question

A question that assumes what it should test, such as "how does X improve Y," forecloses the inquiry before it begins.

### Skipping The Comparator

Without a comparator or baseline, observed change cannot be attributed to the phenomenon rather than to time, context, or chance.

### Mismatching Question Type To Method

A causal question answered by a descriptive design, or a meaning question answered by a mean, signals that the question was never refined.

### Pursuing A Question Without Checking Novelty

A question already answered by strong prior work is redundant unless it tests generalization, replication, or a new population.

### Ignoring Feasibility Until Too Late

A perfectly formulated question that the team cannot recruit for, measure, or analyze is a question that will fail.

### Letting The Question Drift Silently

Questions evolve, but unrecorded evolution becomes post hoc reframing. Document the question and its changes.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Are topic, question, aim, objective, hypothesis, and title distinguished and aligned?
- [ ] Has a structured framework been used to force specificity across all required elements?
- [ ] Are population, context, time period, and level of analysis explicitly defined?
- [ ] Are phenomenon, comparator, primary outcome, and expected direction or relationship specified?
- [ ] Does the question type match the kind of answer and therefore the design sought?
- [ ] Have feasibility, novelty, ethics, relevance, and interest been judged before commitment?
- [ ] Has the question been refined until it is specific, answerable, and falsifiable where applicable?
- [ ] Is there a stated form of evidence that would confirm and that would disconfirm the question?
- [ ] Is the final question documented with rationale, framework, and version for drift control?
- [ ] Has the question been checked against the literature to confirm it is not already answered?
