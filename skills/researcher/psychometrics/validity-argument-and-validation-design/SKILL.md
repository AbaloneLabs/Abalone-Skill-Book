# Validity Argument And Validation Design

Validity is the single most important property of a measure, and it is the most often reduced to a coefficient. The modern view treats validity as a unitary concept: the degree to which evidence and theory support the interpretations and uses of test scores. It is not a property of a test, not established by a single correlation, and not transferable across purposes. Agents frequently collect one or two forms of evidence (often a correlation with a criterion) and declare the measure valid. This collapses a structured argument into a statistic. A sound validity argument specifies the proposed interpretation and use, identifies the assumptions and inferences that interpretation requires, and gathers evidence that bears on each link in the chain. Validation is design, not a post-hoc calculation.

Use this skill when building a validity argument, planning validation studies, or judging whether a measure supports a claimed use. The goal is to prevent the agent from substituting isolated correlations for a structured argument and from ignoring the consequential and boundary assumptions that determine whether scores mean what is claimed.

## Core Rules

### Define The Specific Interpretation And Use Being Validated

Validity is always validity for some interpretation and use. A measure can be valid for one purpose and invalid for another.

Define by:

- the construct the scores are meant to represent;
- the population for whom the scores are intended;
- the decisions the scores will inform;
- the inferences the interpretation requires.

"Is this test valid?" is the wrong question. The right question is whether the evidence supports this interpretation for this population for this use.

### Build The Argument As A Chain Of Inferences

A validity argument is a chain: from observed performance to a score, from the score to the construct, from the construct to a decision, from the decision to a consequence. Each link is an assumption that needs support.

Structure by:

- laying out each inference in the chain;
- identifying the assumption each inference rests on;
- gathering evidence that bears on each assumption;
- locating the weakest links and prioritizing evidence there.

A correlation with a criterion supports one link. It does not validate the whole chain, and the weakest link determines the argument's strength.

### Gather Content Evidence For The Construct Domain

Content evidence addresses whether the items represent the construct domain. It is foundational and is established by judgment.

Gather by:

- expert review of the domain definition and item coverage;
- alignment of items to the construct's facets;
- identification of construct underrepresentation and contamination;
- documentation of the test development and review process.

A test whose items sample a narrow slice of the domain underrepresents the construct, regardless of how well it predicts.

### Gather Structural Evidence For Internal Consistency Of Meaning

Structural evidence addresses whether the internal structure of the test matches the theoretical structure of the construct.

Gather by:

- dimensionality analysis matching the hypothesized structure;
- factor structure consistent with the construct definition;
- item performance consistent with the measurement model;
- evidence that subscales, if claimed, are distinct.

A test claiming to measure one construct that shows a multidimensional structure has a structural validity problem.

### Gather Relations-To-Other-Variables Evidence

Evidence from relations to other variables addresses convergent, discriminant, and criterion validity. Each addresses a different assumption.

Gather by:

- convergent evidence: correlation with measures of the same construct;
- discriminant evidence: low correlation with measures of different constructs;
- criterion evidence: prediction of relevant outcomes;
- known-groups evidence: differences where theory says they should exist.

A single criterion correlation is one piece. Convergence and discrimination together test whether the construct is what it is claimed to be.

### Test The Boundaries Of Generalization Explicitly

Validity evidence supports interpretation within boundaries. Extrapolation beyond the studied population, setting, or use is an assumption.

Test by:

- the populations on which evidence exists versus those claimed;
- the settings and conditions of administration;
- the consequences of using scores in new contexts;
- measurement invariance across groups before generalizing.

Claiming validity for a population or use with no direct evidence generalizes beyond the data and weakens the argument.

### Include Consequential Evidence In The Argument

Modern validity theory includes the consequences of score use as part of the argument. This is the most often-omitted source.

Include by:

- intended consequences: do scores lead to better decisions?
- unintended consequences: bias, gatekeeping, teaching to the test;
- fairness across groups in high-stakes uses;
- monitoring of consequences after implementation.

A measure with strong content, structural, and criterion evidence that produces systematic adverse impact fails a consequential test for that use.

### Treat Validation As Iterative, Not One-Time

Validity is not established once and for all. New populations, uses, and evidence refine or challenge the argument.

Treat by:

- updating the argument as new evidence accumulates;
- re-examining the argument when the use changes;
- monitoring for changing populations or conditions;
- treating published validation as evidence for its context, not universal proof.

A validity argument from a decade ago may not support current uses with current populations.

### Separate Validity Evidence From Policy Justification

A measure may be valid and still not be the right policy choice. Validity evidence does not by itself justify a use.

Separate by:

- validity: do the scores support the interpretation?
- policy: is the use appropriate, fair, and worth the costs?
- the distinction between technical adequacy and value judgments;
- the stakeholders affected by the use.

Valid measurement is necessary but not sufficient for justifiable use. Conflating the two hides value judgments behind technical results.

## Common Traps

### Asking "Is This Test Valid?" Without Specifying Use

Validity is for an interpretation and use. The unqualified question treats validity as a fixed property and invites a single-coefficient answer.

### One Correlation Treated As A Validity Argument

A criterion correlation supports one inference in the chain. Treating it as the whole argument ignores content, structure, convergence, discrimination, and consequences.

### Ignoring The Weakest Link

A chain fails at its weakest point. Strong criterion evidence does not rescue a content-underrepresentation or consequential problem.

### Generalizing Beyond The Studied Population

Claiming validity for populations or uses without direct evidence extrapolates beyond the data and overstates the argument.

### Omitting Consequential Evidence

Consequences of use are part of modern validity. Ignoring adverse impact, bias, or unintended effects produces an incomplete argument.

### Treating Validation As Finished

Validity is iterative. Treating a published study as permanent proof ignores changing populations, uses, and conditions.

### Conflating Validity With Policy Justification

A valid measure may still be an unjustifiable policy choice. Technical adequacy does not settle fairness or worth.

## Self-Check

- Is the specific interpretation and use being validated stated, including construct, population, and decision?
- Is the validity argument structured as a chain of inferences, each with an identified assumption?
- Does content evidence address construct coverage, underrepresentation, and contamination?
- Does structural evidence confirm the internal structure matches the theorized construct?
- Are convergent, discriminant, criterion, and known-groups evidence gathered, not just one correlation?
- Are the boundaries of generalization stated, with measurement invariance tested before cross-group claims?
- Is consequential evidence included, covering intended and unintended effects and group fairness?
- Is validation treated as iterative, with the argument updated as populations and uses change?
- Is validity evidence kept distinct from policy justification of the use?
- Are the weakest links in the argument identified and prioritized for evidence?
