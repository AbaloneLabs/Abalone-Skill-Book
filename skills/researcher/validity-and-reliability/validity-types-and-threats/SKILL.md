# Validity Types And Threats

Validity is the warrant for a study's conclusions. A study can be carefully executed and still support no causal claim if its design allows alternative explanations, or can establish a local effect that fails to generalize. Researchers often collapse validity into a single idea of quality, but validity comes in distinct types, each with its own threats, and a design that secures one type can undermine another. The judgment problem is identifying which validity threats matter for the claim being made and choosing designs that address them.

Use this skill when appraising a study's validity, when identifying threats to internal, external, construct, or statistical conclusion validity, when designing controls, and when defending or limiting a causal claim. The goal is to keep the agent from treating validity as a generic virtue and to make the specific threats to the specific claim explicit and addressed.

This is a high-stakes domain because validity failures produce conclusions that mislead practice, policy, and further research. When a design cannot adequately address a serious validity threat, the agent should narrow the claim or consult a methodologist rather than overclaiming.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish The Four Validity Types And Their Competing Demands

Validity is not one thing. The standard framework separates four types, and designs that strengthen one can weaken another.

Separate:

- statistical conclusion validity, concerning whether covariation between variables is correctly estimated;
- internal validity, concerning whether the covariation reflects cause and effect rather than confounding;
- construct validity, concerning whether the operations represent the intended constructs;
- external validity, concerning whether the finding generalizes beyond the study.

A tightly controlled laboratory experiment may secure internal validity at the cost of external validity. A large diverse survey may support external validity but suffer internal validity threats. Name which type the claim depends on and design accordingly.

### Identify Internal Validity Threats Before Designing Controls

Internal validity asks whether the inferred cause actually produced the effect. Many threats can mimic a treatment effect.

Check for:

- selection, when groups differ before the intervention;
- history, when external events coincide with the intervention;
- maturation, when participants change naturally over time;
- testing and instrumentation, when repeated measurement or changed instruments alter scores;
- regression to the mean, when extreme scorers improve without any treatment;
- attrition, when differential dropout distorts comparisons;
- ambiguity about the direction of cause.

Identify the threats that are plausible for this design and population, then build controls such as randomization, comparison groups, pretests, and blinding to address them. A threat that is plausible but unaddressed weakens the causal claim.

### Judge External Validity Relative To The Target Population And Setting

External validity asks whether the finding applies beyond the study. It is not a property of the sample alone but of the match between sample and target.

Assess:

- the population to which generalization is claimed;
- whether the sample represents that population on relevant characteristics;
- whether the setting resembles the intended application contexts;
- whether the treatment as implemented resembles the treatment as it would be applied;
- whether outcome measures capture what matters in the target context;
- whether contextual moderators plausibly change the effect.

A convenience sample of undergraduates may support a narrow claim about that population but not a broad claim about adults generally. Match the generalization claim to the evidential basis for it.

### Evaluate Construct Validity For Both Causes And Outcomes

Construct validity asks whether the variables as measured represent the constructs as conceptualized. It applies to the independent variable, the outcome, and any covariates.

Examine:

- whether the manipulation represents the intended causal construct;
- whether the measure captures the intended outcome construct;
- whether the operations confound the intended construct with another;
- whether mono-operation bias limits the study to a single imperfect measure;
- whether the construct is culturally or contextually appropriate;
- whether the level of measurement supports the intended inference.

A study of "learning" measured by a single multiple-choice test has weak construct validity for learning broadly. The gap between operation and construct must be named.

### Address Statistical Conclusion Validity To Justify Inference

Statistical conclusion validity asks whether the study's statistical evidence supports the covariation claimed. Even a well-designed study can mislead through analytic errors.

Ensure:

- adequate power to detect the effect if it exists;
- appropriate assumptions for the chosen test or model;
- handling of multiple comparisons that could inflate false positives;
- treatment of missing data without introducing bias;
- correct effect size estimation with appropriate uncertainty;
- avoidance of overfitting in complex models.

A significant result from an underpowered study with uncorrected multiplicity provides weaker evidence than the significance suggests. Statistical conclusion validity is the floor for any causal claim.

### Recognize Tradeoffs Between Validity Types

Strengthening one validity type often weakens another. Ignoring the tradeoff produces designs that optimize the wrong thing.

Common tradeoffs:

- tight laboratory control increases internal validity but can reduce external validity;
- large diverse field samples increase external validity but can introduce confounding;
- multiple outcome measures improve construct validity but raise multiplicity concerns;
- long-term follow-up increases ecological validity but raises attrition threats.

Make the tradeoff explicit and choose based on the claim's priorities. There is no design that maximizes all validity types simultaneously.

### Match The Validity Argument To The Type Of Claim

Different claims depend on different validity types. A descriptive claim needs less internal validity than a causal claim; a theory-building claim may prioritize construct validity over external validity.

Align:

- causal claims with strong internal validity evidence;
- generalization claims with external validity evidence;
- measurement claims with construct validity evidence;
- effect estimation claims with statistical conclusion validity evidence.

Do not invoke external validity to defend a causal claim, or internal validity to defend a generalization. The validity argument must match the claim type.

## Common Traps

### Collapsing Validity Into A Single Quality Score

Treating validity as one property obscures the specific threats that matter for the specific claim. A study can be strong on one type and weak on another.

### Claiming Causality From A Design That Cannot Support It

Cross-sectional or observational designs cannot rule out confounding. Causal language in their conclusions overstates what the design shows.

### Generalizing From An Unrepresentative Sample

A convenience or self-selected sample may describe itself but cannot support broad claims about a different target population.

### Confusing The Measure With The Construct

A convenient proxy quietly becomes the construct in the conclusions. The gap between operation and concept goes unexamined.

### Strengthening Internal Validity While Ignoring External Costs

A pristine laboratory effect that fails in real settings answers a narrow question and may mislead applied decisions.

### Ignoring Statistical Conclusion Validity

Significance from underpowered, multiplicity-laden analyses provides weaker evidence than the p-value suggests.

### Listing Generic Threats Without Assessing Plausibility

A laundry list of every possible threat adds little. The value is in identifying the threats that are plausible for this study and addressing them.

## Self-Check

- Are the four validity types distinguished, with the claim's dependence on each type identified?
- Are internal validity threats plausible for the design and population identified and addressed through controls?
- Is external validity judged relative to a specified target population and setting, with the generalization claim matched to the evidence?
- Is construct validity evaluated for the cause, the outcome, and covariates, with operation-to-construct gaps named?
- Is statistical conclusion validity secured through power, assumptions, multiplicity handling, and missing data treatment?
- Are tradeoffs between validity types made explicit and resolved based on the claim's priorities?
- Does the validity argument match the type of claim being made (causal, descriptive, measurement, generalization)?
- Where a serious validity threat cannot be adequately addressed, has the claim been narrowed or a methodologist consulted rather than overclaiming?
