# Research Ethics And Publication Integrity

Research ethics is not a final approval stamp. It runs through question selection, recruitment, consent, data collection, analysis, authorship, publication, peer review, correction, and public communication. Researchers can harm participants, mislead readers, exploit collaborators, or distort evidence even when the project seems technically sound.

Use this skill before planning research involving people, sensitive data, collaborators, publication, peer review, authorship, or public claims. The goal is to make the agent check rights, approvals, responsibilities, conflicts, and reporting limits before presenting research as trustworthy.

## Core Rules

### Determine Whether Human Subjects Review Is Needed

Do not decide informally that a study is exempt or outside review when people, private information, biospecimens, interviews, surveys, interventions, or identifiable records are involved. Under the Common Rule, applicability depends on whether the activity is research, whether it involves human subjects, and whether an exemption applies.

Check:

- research purpose;
- interaction or intervention with people;
- identifiable private information;
- identifiable biospecimens;
- vulnerable populations;
- recruitment method;
- consent process;
- data sensitivity;
- funding or institutional policy;
- whether an IRB or ethics office must determine status.

If uncertain, ask the authorized institutional body. Researchers should not self-certify exemption unless policy allows it.

### Treat Consent As A Process

Informed consent is not just a signed form. Participants should understand what they are being asked to do and should have a real choice where consent is required.

Review:

- purpose;
- procedures;
- risks;
- benefits;
- alternatives;
- confidentiality;
- voluntary nature;
- withdrawal;
- compensation;
- contact information;
- data sharing;
- future use;
- understandable language;
- avoidance of coercion or undue influence.

Power relationships matter. Students, employees, patients, prisoners, and dependent communities may need additional safeguards.

### Protect Participants And Communities

Research can create harm through privacy loss, stigma, legal exposure, economic harm, emotional distress, physical risk, or community misrepresentation.

Plan:

- risk minimization;
- safety monitoring;
- referral process for distress;
- confidentiality controls;
- community consultation where appropriate;
- culturally respectful materials;
- compensation fairness;
- adverse event process;
- stopping rules;
- reporting obligations.

Do not treat approval as permission to ignore emerging harm. Monitor the study as it runs.

### Manage Conflicts Of Interest

Conflicts can be financial, personal, professional, political, institutional, or intellectual. A conflict does not automatically invalidate research, but it must be disclosed and managed.

Identify:

- funding source;
- sponsor role;
- consulting or equity interests;
- employment ties;
- personal relationships;
- advocacy commitments;
- data ownership;
- publication control;
- access to raw data;
- pressure to produce a result.

Disclose conflicts according to institutional, funder, and journal policies. Avoid sponsor control over unfavorable results unless the arrangement is openly managed and allowed.

### Set Authorship And Contribution Rules Early

Publication credit should reflect actual contribution and field standards. Ambiguity creates disputes and unfairness.

Discuss:

- authorship criteria;
- author order;
- corresponding author responsibilities;
- contributor roles;
- acknowledgements;
- data ownership;
- writing responsibilities;
- approval of final manuscript;
- responsibility for integrity of work;
- process for changes.

Do not add honorary authors or omit qualified contributors. Revisit authorship when contributions change.

### Avoid Fabrication, Falsification, And Plagiarism

Research misconduct policies vary, but fabrication, falsification, and plagiarism are central concerns across many systems.

Prevent:

- invented data;
- altered results;
- selective exclusion without rationale;
- image manipulation;
- unreported protocol deviations;
- copied text;
- copied ideas without credit;
- duplicate publication;
- salami slicing;
- undisclosed reuse of data;
- misrepresentation of methods.

Honest error and disagreement can happen, but poor documentation can make them hard to distinguish from misconduct.

### Report Methods And Limits Honestly

Readers need enough detail to evaluate the claim.

Report:

- question;
- design;
- sample;
- recruitment;
- measures;
- exclusions;
- missing data;
- analysis plan;
- deviations from plan;
- limitations;
- uncertainty;
- conflicts;
- funding;
- ethics approval or reason not required;
- data and code availability.

Do not make the abstract stronger than the evidence. Avoid causal, universal, or policy claims that the design cannot support.

### Respect Peer Review And Confidentiality

Peer review involves confidential manuscripts, grant proposals, ideas, data, and identities where blind review applies.

When reviewing:

- keep material confidential;
- do not use ideas or data;
- disclose conflicts;
- meet deadlines or decline;
- evaluate fairly;
- separate quality from disagreement;
- avoid personal attacks;
- follow journal or funder rules;
- do not delegate without permission.

If using AI tools in peer review, follow the journal or funder policy. Confidential material should not be pasted into unauthorized systems.

### Correct The Record

Research integrity includes responding when errors are found.

If an error appears:

- assess seriousness;
- notify coauthors;
- preserve evidence;
- consult institution if needed;
- contact journal or repository when appropriate;
- issue correction, expression of concern, or retraction if required;
- update data or code records;
- communicate limits to stakeholders.

Do not hide errors because they are embarrassing. Correction protects the research record.

## Common Traps

### Treating Ethics Approval As A One-Time Barrier

Ethics duties continue through recruitment, data collection, analysis, sharing, and reporting.

### Assuming Public Data Is Ethically Risk-Free

Publicly accessible data can still involve privacy, consent, platform terms, community harm, and re-identification risk.

### Letting The Sponsor Shape Results

Funding does not justify suppressing unfavorable findings, limiting access to data without disclosure, or overstating claims.

### Using Boilerplate Consent

Consent should match the actual study, risks, data use, future sharing, and participant population.

### Hiding Deviations

Protocol changes, exclusion changes, failed measures, and exploratory analyses should be reported transparently.

### Treating Authorship As Hierarchy

Seniority, funding, or lab membership alone should not determine authorship.

### Overclaiming In Public Communication

Press releases, policy briefs, and social media should preserve uncertainty and limitations.

## Self-Check

- Has the authorized body determined whether human subjects review, exemption, or approval is required?
- Is consent understandable, voluntary, and matched to actual procedures, risks, data use, and sharing?
- Are participant and community risks minimized and monitored?
- Are privacy, confidentiality, data protection, and re-identification risks handled?
- Are conflicts of interest disclosed and managed?
- Are authorship, contribution, author order, approval, and responsibility rules clear?
- Are fabrication, falsification, plagiarism, image manipulation, duplicate publication, and selective reporting risks addressed?
- Are methods, deviations, missing data, limitations, funding, ethics approval, and availability statements reported honestly?
- Are peer review confidentiality and conflict rules followed?
- Is there a plan to correct the record if errors are discovered?
