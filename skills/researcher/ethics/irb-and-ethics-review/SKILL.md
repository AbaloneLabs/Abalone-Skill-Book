# IRB And Ethics Review

Ethics review is not a paperwork obstacle. It is the structured moment when a project's risks to participants, communities, and the integrity of the record are examined before harm can occur. Researchers often underestimate review because they conflate intent with safety: a well-meaning study can still expose participants to privacy loss, stigma, coercion, distress, or legal risk. The review process exists precisely because researchers are poor judges of risk in their own work.

Use this skill before launching any project involving people, their data, or their biospecimens; when preparing an ethics submission; when classifying the level of review; when amending a protocol; and when handling adverse events or continuing review. The goal is to keep the agent from self-certifying exemption, from submitting incomplete protocols that delay protection, and from treating approval as a finish line rather than a continuing duty. The agent has latitude in study design, but must defer classification and approval decisions to the authorized body whenever the rules require it.

## Core Rules

### Determine Reviewability Before Assuming Exemption

Under frameworks such as the Common Rule, whether a project is "human subjects research," whether it is exempt, and which exemption applies are determinations that often belong to the IRB, not the investigator. Self-certifying exemption to avoid review is a recurring and serious error.

Assess:

- whether the activity meets the definition of research;
- whether it involves human subjects through intervention, interaction, or identifiable private information;
- whether identifiable biospecimens are involved;
- whether exempt categories plausibly apply;
- whether vulnerable populations or sensitive topics are present;
- whether the institution or funder requires review regardless of federal definitions;
- whether international or community partners require their own review.

When in doubt, submit for a determination. Document the outcome.

### Match The Level Of Review To The Risk

Review levels exist to calibrate scrutiny to risk. Misclassifying to obtain lighter review can expose participants and invalidate the project.

Consider:

- exempt review for minimal-risk categories defined by regulation;
- expedited review for minimal-risk studies falling into specified categories;
- full-board review for greater-than-minimal-risk studies, vulnerable populations, certain interventions, or sensitive topics;
- whether the study crosses categories that force full review;
- whether prisoners, pregnant people, neonates, children, or decisionally impaired adults are involved;
- whether deception, sensitive questioning, or biospecimen collection raises risk.

Risk is judged from the participant's standpoint, not the researcher's convenience.

### Prepare A Protocol That Reviewers Can Actually Evaluate

A vague protocol forces review cycles and signals that the team has not thought through the risks. Reviewers need enough detail to judge whether risks are minimized and justified.

Include:

- background, question, and design;
- population, sample size rationale, and recruitment;
- inclusion and exclusion criteria;
- procedures, interventions, and data sources;
- variables and measures;
- analysis plan;
- risk identification and minimization;
- data handling, security, retention, and destruction;
- consent process and materials;
- compensation and conflict management;
- data and safety monitoring if applicable;
- plans for adverse events and deviations.

Write for a reviewer who is methodologically literate but outside the subfield.

### Treat Consent And Recruitment As Reviewable Artifacts

Consent documents and recruitment scripts are not appendices. They are how participants understand and enter the study, and reviewers scrutinize them.

Ensure they cover:

- purpose and procedures in plain language;
- expected duration and burden;
- reasonably foreseeable risks and discomforts;
- potential benefits, and honest acknowledgment if none;
- alternatives to participation;
- confidentiality and its limits;
- mandatory reporting obligations;
- compensation and what happens if the study ends early;
- voluntary participation and right to withdraw;
- contacts for questions and rights;
- data sharing, future use, and biospecimen handling;
- avoidance of therapeutic misconception in research framed as treatment.

Avoid coercion in recruitment wording, especially with students, employees, patients, or communities dependent on the institution.

### Plan Data Protection Before Submission

Privacy risk is a core review concern. A protocol that cannot describe how data are protected will be sent back.

Specify:

- what data are collected, including identifiers;
- how identifiers are separated from research data;
- storage location, encryption, and access controls;
- who can access raw and analyzed data;
- retention period and destruction plan;
- sharing arrangements and data use agreements;
- re-identification risk and mitigation;
- cross-border transfer issues;
- platform terms of service for data scraped or collected online;
- secondary use and whether consent covers it.

Publicly accessible does not mean ethically risk-free. Re-identification, community harm, and platform terms still apply.

### Manage Amendments, Deviations, And Adverse Events

Approval is the start of an ongoing relationship with the review body, not the end. Changes and problems must be reported under the institution's rules.

Plan for:

- prospective amendments for design, recruitment, or consent changes;
- prompt reporting of unanticipated problems and adverse events;
- reporting of protocol deviations, intentional or not;
- continuing review for studies that require it;
- re-consent when new risks or information emerge;
- closure or final report when the study ends;
- coordination across multiple IRBs or ethics committees for multi-site work.

Silent changes to a protocol after approval are a serious integrity problem even when well-intentioned.

### Address Multi-Site, International, And Community Review

Studies that cross institutions, borders, or communities often need more than one review, and the requirements may conflict.

Check:

- single-IRB versus local review requirements;
- international ethics requirements and local law;
- community advisory boards or tribal review;
- permissions from clinics, schools, employers, or platforms;
- whether consent processes are culturally and linguistically appropriate;
- whether compensation is fair without being coercive locally;
- whether data can lawfully leave the country of collection.

Build the review timeline around the slowest and most demanding body, not the fastest.

### Coordinate Review With Other Obligations

Ethics review interacts with registration, data sharing mandates, funding conditions, and publication requirements. Treating them as separate streams creates gaps.

Align:

- study registration timing with approval;
- data sharing plan with consent language;
- funder reporting with continuing review;
- publication of protocol with final approved version;
- conflicts of interest disclosure with review submission;
- authorship agreements with contributor roles.

A consistent, dated record across these obligations protects both participants and the team.

## Common Traps

### Self-Certifying Exemption To Avoid Review

Researchers often assume their study is exempt because it is low-risk in their eyes. The determination usually belongs to the IRB, and getting it wrong can void the project and harm participants.

### Underestimating Risk From Identifiable Or Re-Identifiable Data

De-identification is fragile. Combined datasets, rare conditions, and rich variables can re-identify participants and expose them to stigma, legal, or economic harm.

### Submitting A Vague Protocol To Save Time

A thin protocol produces more review cycles, not fewer, and signals weak risk thinking. Reviewers cannot protect participants from risks the protocol does not name.

### Treating Approval As Permission To Stop Thinking

Approval does not suspend the duty to monitor harm, report problems, and amend when circumstances change. Ethics is continuous, not transactional.

### Quietly Changing The Protocol After Approval

Pilot-driven changes, recruitment tweaks, or added measures made without amendment violate the terms of approval and undermine the integrity of the record.

### Ignoring Recruitment And Consent Language

Coercive recruitment scripts and unreadable consent forms cause real harm and are a frequent reason for review delays and participant misunderstanding.

### Forgetting Multi-Site And International Requirements

Assuming the home IRB's approval satisfies all sites or countries leads to blocked data collection, legal exposure, and community harm.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has the authorized body determined whether the project is human subjects research and whether exemption applies?
- [ ] Is the level of review, including any full-board triggers, correctly matched to the risk and population?
- [ ] Does the protocol contain enough detail for a reviewer to evaluate risks, benefits, consent, and data protection?
- [ ] Do consent and recruitment materials cover procedures, risks, benefits, alternatives, confidentiality, withdrawal, and contacts in plain language?
- [ ] Is the data protection plan specific about identifiers, storage, encryption, access, retention, sharing, and re-identification risk?
- [ ] Are plans in place for amendments, adverse events, deviations, continuing review, and study closure?
- [ ] Are multi-site, international, community, and gatekeeper review requirements identified and sequenced?
- [ ] Is ethics review coordinated with registration, data sharing, funder, and publication obligations?
- [ ] Are vulnerable populations, sensitive topics, deception, and biospecimens given the extra scrutiny they require?
- [ ] Is there a dated record of the determination, approval, amendments, and final disposition?
