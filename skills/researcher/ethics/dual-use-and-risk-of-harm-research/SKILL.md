# Dual-Use And Risk Of Harm Research

Some research produces knowledge that could be used both for beneficial and harmful purposes. A study that reveals how a pathogen becomes more transmissible may advance vaccine design and also provide a blueprint for harm; an algorithm that finds software vulnerabilities may improve security and also enable exploitation; a dataset that reveals behavioral patterns may support public health and also enable surveillance. The dual-use dilemma is that the same knowledge can help or harm, and the standard research norm of open publication may, in some cases, increase the risk of harm. Ignoring this dilemma, treating all research as self-evidently beneficial and all publication as self-evidently good, can produce knowledge whose net effect is harmful. The judgment problem is recognizing dual-use risk, weighing benefits against foreseeable misuse, and choosing responsible paths for conduct, dissemination, and oversight.

Use this skill when evaluating research with dual-use potential, designing biosecurity or cybersecurity review, deciding on redaction or controlled dissemination, planning gain-of-function research, or weighing open publication against misuse risk. The goal is to prevent the agent from defaulting to unconditional openness without considering foreseeable harm, from overestimating benefits and underestimating misuse risk, or from imposing controls so heavy they smother legitimate inquiry.

## Core Rules

### Recognize Dual-Use Potential Early

Dual-use risk is easiest to manage when recognized at the design stage, not after the work is done and ready for publication. Many projects have no dual-use potential and need no special review; some do, and early recognition allows for responsible planning. The default should be a brief dual-use screen for projects involving pathogens, toxins, dangerous synthesis routes, critical infrastructure vulnerabilities, surveillance-enabling methods, or other foreseeable misuse pathways.

Screen early by:

- asking, at design stage, whether methods, data, or outputs could be misused to cause harm;
- considering whether the work could enable harm at scale, such as pandemic potential or infrastructure disruption;
- consulting institutional biosafety, biosecurity, or cybersecurity committees where relevant;
- documenting the screen and its conclusion, including rationale for no special review;
- revisiting the assessment if methods or aims change during the project.

A documented early screen catches genuine risks while avoiding burdening low-risk work with unnecessary review.

### Distinguish Genuine Dual-Use From Ordinary Research

Not all research is dual-use, and over-applying controls to ordinary work wastes effort and chills inquiry. Genuine dual-use research of concern typically involves enabling information: operational details that lower the barrier to harm, such as synthesis protocols, exploitation methods, or enhanced-potential-pandemic-pathogen characteristics. Research that merely describes a phenomenon, or whose misuse would require capabilities the findings do not provide, is usually not dual-use of concern.

Distinguish by:

- asking whether the findings provide actionable detail that would enable misuse, not just general knowledge;
- considering whether misuse would require resources, expertise, or access that the findings do not lower;
- recognizing that review articles and educational content rarely constitute dual-use of concern;
- reserving heightened review for research where the benefit-harm tradeoff is genuinely uncertain;
- documenting the distinction so that ordinary work proceeds without undue burden.

Conflating all risky-topic research with dual-use of concern over-regulates and erodes the credibility of genuine biosecurity review.

### Weigh Benefits Against Foreseeable Misuse

Where dual-use potential is genuine, the decision to conduct or publish requires weighing the benefits of the work against the foreseeable risk of misuse. This weighing is uncertain and value-laden, but it must be done explicitly rather than defaulted to open publication. The question is not whether misuse is possible, but whether the risk of misuse, discounted by its likelihood and manageability, outweighs the benefits.

Weigh by:

- estimating the benefits of the work, scientific, medical, security, or practical;
- estimating the foreseeable misuse pathways and their likelihood and severity;
- considering whether the benefits depend on the risky details or could be achieved with less dangerous methods;
- considering whether the work is likely to be replicated by others regardless, reducing the marginal risk of one's own publication;
- documenting the weighing and the decision, including who was consulted;
- erring toward caution where potential harm is catastrophic, such as pandemic potential, even if likelihood is low.

The weighing should be explicit, consultative, and documented, recognizing that reasonable people may disagree on catastrophic, low-probability risks.

### Use Institutional And Funder Review Processes

Dual-use review should not be a solitary judgment. Institutional biosafety committees, institutional review entities, funder review processes, and in some cases government review, provide structured oversight and distribute responsibility. Bypassing these processes, or treating them as rubber stamps, forfeits the scrutiny that dual-use decisions require.

Use review by:

- submitting proposed work to the relevant institutional and funder review before conduct where dual-use potential is identified;
- providing complete and honest information about aims, methods, and risks;
- respecting review conditions, including modifications, additional oversight, or non-approval;
- reporting concerns that arise during the work, not just at publication;
- recognizing that review may conclude the work should not proceed, and accepting that conclusion.

Review processes are imperfect, but they provide the structured, multi-perspective scrutiny that high-stakes dual-use decisions require, and they should be used in good faith.

### Consider Responsible Dissemination Options

Open publication is the norm, but where dual-use risk is significant, responsible dissemination may involve redaction, controlled access, or staged disclosure. These options have costs: they limit scientific scrutiny, slow beneficial applications, and may be circumvented by others. But in genuine cases of concern, they may be warranted. The choice between open publication and controlled dissemination should be explicit and justified.

Consider by:

- assessing whether the risky details are necessary for the scientific contribution, or whether they can be omitted without undermining the work;
- evaluating redaction, where operational details are removed while findings remain verifiable in outline;
- evaluating controlled access, where full details are available to vetted researchers under terms;
- recognizing the limits of these approaches, including that determined actors may reconstruct the work;
- documenting the dissemination decision and its rationale;
- preferring the least restrictive option that adequately addresses the risk.

Unconditional openness is a value, not an absolute, and responsible dissemination balances it against foreseeable harm.

### Be Transparent About Risk Management Decisions

Decisions about dual-use research, whether to proceed, modify, or restrict dissemination, should be transparent to the relevant communities. Secrecy about the decisions themselves, beyond what is needed to avoid enabling harm, erodes trust and prevents scrutiny of the risk management process. Transparency about the process, even when the risky details are restricted, allows the community to assess whether the decisions were reasonable.

Be transparent by:

- disclosing that a dual-use review occurred and its general conclusions, where this does not itself enable harm;
- explaining the rationale for restrictions, where restrictions are imposed;
- engaging with the relevant research community about dual-use norms and practices;
- acknowledging uncertainty and disagreement in the decision;
- avoiding both unwarranted secrecy and performative openness that ignores real risk.

Transparency about the decision process, distinct from disclosure of the risky details, supports legitimate scrutiny and community norms.

## Common Traps

### Defaulting To Unconditional Openness Without Considering Misuse

Open publication is a norm, not an absolute. Screen for dual-use potential where foreseeable misuse exists.

### Conflating All Risky-Topic Research With Dual-Use Of Concern

Over-regulation chills inquiry and erodes review credibility. Reserve heightened review for genuine enabling information.

### Solitary Judgment Without Institutional Review

Dual-use decisions are high-stakes and value-laden. Use institutional and funder review processes.

### Ignoring Catastrophic Low-Probability Risks

Catastrophic harms, such as pandemic potential, warrant caution even at low likelihood. Do not dismiss them as speculative.

### Redaction That Removes Scientific Verifiability

Redaction should preserve the scientific contribution where possible. Removing too much undermines scrutiny; removing too little fails to address risk.

### Performative Openness That Ignores Real Risk

Full disclosure that enables harm is not a virtue. Balance openness against foreseeable misuse.

### Failing To Revisit The Assessment As Work Evolves

Dual-use potential may emerge as methods or aims change. Revisit the screen throughout the project.
