# Community And Participatory Research Ethics

When research is conducted with a community rather than on subjects, the ethical unit shifts from the individual participant to the relationship between researcher and community, and the obligations extend far beyond informed consent and confidentiality. Community-based, participatory, and action research promise shared ownership and mutual benefit, but the promise is easy to make and hard to keep. Researchers can adopt the language of partnership while retaining control of the question, the data, the interpretation, and the credit, producing work that extracts from the community in the name of collaboration. The judgment problem is to design research that genuinely shares power and leaves capacity behind, rather than research that takes data and departs.

Use this skill when planning research with, in, or for a community, when negotiating roles and ownership, when designing consent and benefit-sharing, when appraising whether a project is genuinely participatory, or when resolving tension between academic demands and community priorities. The goal is to keep the agent from treating participation as a recruitment method or an ethics checkbox, and to keep it from imposing academic timelines and agendas on communities whose priorities may differ. The agent has freedom to choose the depth and form of participation, but must make the power-sharing real and the accountability specific.

## Core Rules

### Define What Participation Actually Means In The Project

Participation is a spectrum, not a binary. A project can be community-informed, community-consulted, community-involved, community-led, or community-owned, and each level carries different ethical obligations. Calling a project participatory without specifying where it sits on this spectrum hides how much power the community actually has.

Clarify:

- who defines the research question;
- who decides the methods and measures;
- who collects and analyzes the data;
- who interprets the findings;
- who owns the data and the outputs;
- who controls publication and dissemination;
- who benefits from the knowledge and any products.

State the level of participation honestly. A community-consulted study is legitimate if labeled as such; it becomes extractive only if presented as community-led.

### Establish Legitimate Community Representation

Community is not a single voice, and community partners do not automatically represent everyone affected. Leaders, elders, organizations, and gatekeepers may speak for some members and silence others, including women, youth, dissenters, or marginalized subgroups.

Assess:

- which individuals or bodies are treated as representing the community, and by what authority;
- whose voices are amplified and whose are excluded by that representation;
- whether the community is internally heterogeneous in ways that matter to the research;
- whether marginalized subgroups need separate consultation or consent pathways;
- how conflicts within the community will be handled without the researcher picking a side.

Do not treat the convenient or powerful community contact as the whole community. Representation is a judgment, and getting it wrong can reproduce existing inequalities within the group.

### Seek Consent At Both Individual And Community Levels

Individual consent is necessary but not sufficient when the unit of benefit and risk is the community. A study can have individual consent from every participant and still harm or misrepresent the community, or proceed without its collective approval.

Plan for:

- community-level consultation or consent through legitimate bodies, where they exist;
- individual consent that is consistent with, not contradictory to, community agreements;
- clarity about which decisions are individual and which are collective;
- what happens when an individual consents but the community objects, or vice versa;
- ongoing consent, since community approval at the start does not bind the community forever.

Community consent does not replace individual consent, and individual consent does not override legitimate community objection. Both layers matter.

### Make Ownership And Intellectual Property Explicit

Ambiguity about who owns the data, the findings, and any resulting products is a common source of conflict and harm. Academic norms often default ownership to the researcher or institution, which can conflict with community expectations of shared ownership.

Agree in advance on:

- who holds the data and where it is stored;
- who can access, use, and share it, and under what conditions;
- who owns interpretations, publications, and any interventions or products developed;
- how authorship and credit will be assigned, including community co-authors;
- how commercial or policy benefits will be shared;
- how the community can withdraw or restrict use of its contributions.

Put these agreements in writing, using community-developed data sovereignty principles where they exist, such as those articulated by Indigenous data sovereignty networks.

### Design Reciprocity And Benefit-Sharing Into The Project

Communities that contribute time, knowledge, and access deserve tangible benefit, not only the abstract promise that the research might someday help people like them. Reciprocity should be planned, not improvised.

Consider:

- what skills, tools, data, or capacity the project will leave behind;
- whether community members are paid fairly for their labor, including as co-researchers;
- whether the research addresses a question the community prioritizes;
- whether findings are returned in accessible, useful forms;
- whether the project builds local capacity to continue the work independently;
- whether benefits are distributed equitably within the community, not captured by its elites.

The test of reciprocity is what remains after the researcher leaves. If only publications and extracted data remain, the project was not reciprocal.

### Leave Capacity, Not Just Data

A strong participatory project strengthens the community's ability to act on its own behalf. This is the difference between research that depends on the community and research that empowers it.

Build capacity through:

- training community members in research methods and analysis;
- co-developing tools, measures, or interventions the community can use;
- sharing data in forms the community can access and apply;
- supporting community-led follow-up studies or action;
- documenting processes so they can be replicated or adapted;
- connecting the community to resources, networks, or decision-makers.

Capacity-building takes time and resources and should be budgeted as a core deliverable, not an afterthought.

### Navigate Academic And Community Timeline Conflicts Honestly

Academic schedules, driven by funding cycles, degrees, and publication pressure, move faster than community processes of deliberation, relationship-building, and collective decision-making. When the academic timeline wins, participation becomes tokenistic.

Acknowledge:

- that relationship-building cannot be rushed without damaging trust;
- that community review of methods and findings takes time;
- that funding deadlines may pressure premature decisions;
- that the researcher's career incentives may not align with the community's best interests;
- when a faster, less participatory design would be more honest than a rushed participatory performance.

If the timeline does not allow genuine participation, do a less participatory study and label it accurately, rather than performing participation under impossible constraints.

### Maintain Accountability After The Project Ends

Accountability does not end with publication. Findings can be misused, can stigmatize the community, or can be cited in ways the community opposes. The researcher retains a responsibility for how the work is used.

Plan for:

- how findings will be shared with the community before and after publication;
- how the community can raise concerns or request corrections;
- how the work will be framed to reduce risk of stigma or misuse;
- whether the researcher will advocate for the community's interests in policy or media;
- how the relationship will be sustained or responsibly concluded.

Walking away after data collection, leaving the community to deal with the consequences of the findings, is a failure of accountability.

## Common Traps

### Performative Participation

Adopting the language and structures of participatory research while retaining unilateral control of the question, data, interpretation, and credit. The community contributes; the researcher decides.

### Treating Community As A Recruitment Frame

Valuing the community for its access to participants rather than as a partner with its own priorities. The relationship ends when data collection does.

### Assuming One Community Contact Represents All

A single leader or organization may not represent marginalized subgroups, dissenters, or those most affected. Relying on one gatekeeper can silence the people the research claims to serve.

### Extractive Data Collection

Taking knowledge, stories, or biospecimens from the community and converting them into academic credit without leaving equivalent benefit behind. The community becomes raw material for publications.

### Imposing Academic Timelines

Letting funding or degree deadlines override the community's pace of deliberation and review, then presenting the result as collaborative. Rushed participation is tokenism.

### Ambiguous Or Default Ownership

Failing to negotiate data and intellectual property ownership in advance, then defaulting to institutional ownership that conflicts with community expectations and contributions.

### Publication Without Community Review

Publishing findings that affect the community without giving community partners the chance to review, contextualize, or object, exposing them to stigma or misrepresentation.

### Benefit Capture By Community Elites

Channeling benefits, credit, or capacity to the community's more powerful members while leaving marginalized subgroups unrewarded or further disadvantaged.

### Helicopter Research

Arriving, collecting data, and departing without sustained relationship or follow-through. The community bears the burden of participation and sees no return.

## Self-Check

- Is the level of participation, from informed to owned, stated honestly, with the distribution of power over question, methods, data, interpretation, and dissemination made explicit?
- Is community representation examined for legitimacy and coverage, including whether marginalized subgroups are included rather than silenced by the chosen representatives?
- Are both individual consent and community-level consultation or consent sought, with a plan for conflicts between the two?
- Are data ownership, intellectual property, authorship, access, sharing, and benefit-sharing agreed in writing in advance, using data sovereignty principles where applicable?
- Is reciprocity planned as tangible benefit and capacity-building that remains after the project, rather than only the abstract promise of future good?
- Are community members compensated fairly for their labor, including as co-researchers, and are benefits distributed equitably within the community?
- Are academic and community timeline conflicts acknowledged, with the design scaled to what genuine participation allows rather than rushed?
- Is there a plan for community review of findings before publication and for ongoing accountability afterward?
- Are risks of stigma, misuse, or misrepresentation of the community addressed in how findings are framed and shared?
- Does the project leave capacity, relationships, and tools behind, rather than only data and publications, avoiding extractive or helicopter patterns?
