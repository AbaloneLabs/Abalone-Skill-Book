# Conflicts Of Interest And Funding Ethics

Research is not conducted in a vacuum of interests. Funding, consulting relationships, equity holdings, career incentives, and political commitments can consciously or unconsciously bias the questions asked, the methods chosen, the analyses run, and the way findings are reported. The existence of a conflict of interest does not prove bias, but it is a risk factor for bias that must be managed transparently. Treating conflicts as irrelevant, disclosing them perfunctorily, or designing studies that hand control to sponsors, allows financial and political interests to distort the research record. The judgment problem is recognizing conflicts honestly, managing them through disclosure and structural safeguards, and detecting the subtle distortions that interested funding can introduce into design, analysis, and reporting.

Use this skill when managing conflicts of interest, designing safeguards for funded research, assessing whether sponsor influence has distorted a study, handling authorship and ghostwriting concerns, or ensuring integrity under commercial or political pressure. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating disclosure as sufficient, from accepting sponsor-controlled study designs, or from failing to detect the distortions that conflicts introduce.

## Core Rules

### Recognize Both Financial And Non-Financial Conflicts

Conflicts of interest are not only financial. Intellectual commitments to a theory, prior public stances, career dependence on a finding going a certain way, and political or ideological commitments can all create non-financial conflicts that bias research. Recognizing only financial conflicts misses substantial sources of bias, and researchers who declare no financial conflicts may still have strong non-financial interests that warrant disclosure.

Recognize by:

- disclosing financial conflicts, including funding, consulting, equity, patents, and speaker fees;
- disclosing non-financial conflicts, including intellectual commitments, prior public advocacy, and career stakes;
- considering the conflicts of all co-investigators, not just the principal investigator;
- updating disclosures as new relationships form during the project;
- recognizing that the appearance of conflict, not only actual conflict, warrants disclosure.

Disclosure should be broad enough to capture the full range of interests that could bias the work, not narrow enough to allow technical compliance that conceals real conflicts.

### Treat Disclosure As Necessary But Not Sufficient

Disclosure is the baseline, not the solution. It tells readers that a conflict exists so they can weigh the findings accordingly, but it does not eliminate the bias the conflict may introduce. Moreover, disclosure can backfire, with conflicted parties reasoning that having disclosed, they are free to act on the conflict. Disclosure must be paired with structural safeguards that reduce the conflict's influence on conduct and reporting.

Pair disclosure with:

- independent analysis, where data and analysis are controlled by researchers without the conflict;
- pre-registration of design and analysis to constrain post-hoc distortions;
- contractual guarantees of independence, including right to publish and control of the manuscript;
- separation of roles, so that the conflicted party cannot unilaterally shape design or reporting;
- independent replication, to test whether findings survive outside the conflicted context.

Disclosure without safeguards leaves the conflict free to operate; safeguards without disclosure are opaque. Both are needed.

### Preserve Researcher Control Over Design And Publication

A common pattern in industry-funded research is that the sponsor retains control over the data, the analysis, and the decision to publish, with researchers acting effectively as contractors. This structure allows sponsors to suppress unfavorable findings, shape analyses to produce favorable ones, and control the narrative. Contracts that guarantee researcher control of these elements are a primary safeguard against sponsor-driven distortion.

Preserve control by:

- securing contractual rights to access all study data, including raw data;
- securing contractual rights to conduct and publish the analysis, including unfavorable findings;
- ensuring the principal investigator, not the sponsor, controls the manuscript and its conclusions;
- requiring that any sponsor review be limited to factual correction and delay-bounded, not approval;
- walking away from funding whose terms compromise these controls.

Funding that requires surrendering control of data or publication is funding whose findings cannot be fully trusted, regardless of the researchers' integrity.

### Detect Sponsor Influence On Design And Reporting

Sponsor influence is often subtle, operating through design choices that bias the result rather than through overt suppression. Comparators chosen to make the sponsor's product look good, surrogate outcomes that favor the product, populations selected for responsiveness, analyses switched to produce favorable estimates, and selective reporting of favorable subgroups are all mechanisms by which design and reporting can be distorted without outright fraud.

Detect by:

- scrutinizing the comparator, is it the relevant alternative or a straw man;
- scrutinizing the outcomes, are they patient-important or surrogate markers that favor the product;
- scrutinizing the population, is it selected to maximize the product's apparent benefit;
- comparing the registered analysis plan to the published analysis, looking for switches;
- checking for selective reporting of subgroups, endpoints, or time points;
- looking for spin in the abstract and conclusions that overstate the findings.

These distortions are detectable in the published record, and their presence, especially in combination, is a strong signal that sponsor influence shaped the work.

### Manage Authorship And Ghostwriting Honestly

Industry-funded research and review articles have sometimes involved ghostwriting, where the named authors did not substantially conduct or write the work, and a sponsor's commercial writer drafted the manuscript. Ghostwriting misrepresents authorship and allows sponsor-controlled content to appear under the authority of independent academics. Honest authorship requires that named authors meet authorship criteria and take responsibility for the work.

Manage by:

- applying authorship criteria, substantial contribution, drafting or critical revision, and accountability;
- rejecting honorary, guest, or ghost authorship regardless of sponsor pressure;
- disclosing the contribution of any medical writer or contributor, with their name and role;
- ensuring named authors can vouch for the data and analysis, not just sign off on a draft;
- refusing arrangements where a sponsor provides a complete manuscript for academic signature.

Authorship is an accountability mechanism; when it is transactional, accountability is lost, and the work cannot be trusted as an independent academic product.

### Address Conflicts In Peer Review And Editorial Decisions

Conflicts of interest also affect peer review and editorial decisions. Reviewers with competing interests may provide biased reviews, and editors may be influenced by advertiser, funder, or institutional pressures. Managing these conflicts in the review and editorial process protects the integrity of publication decisions.

Address by:

- requiring reviewers to disclose conflicts and recusing themselves where significant;
- editors selecting reviewers without major competing interests;
- editors recusing themselves from decisions where their own conflicts are significant;
- disclosing editor and journal conflicts, including advertiser relationships;
- protecting against litigation or advertiser pressure on editorial decisions.

The integrity of the published record depends on managing conflicts not only in the research itself but throughout the review and editorial chain.

## Common Traps

### Disclosing Only Financial Conflicts

Non-financial conflicts, intellectual, career, ideological, also bias research. Disclose them too.

### Treating Disclosure As Sufficient

Disclosure is necessary but not sufficient. Pair it with structural safeguards and independent control.

### Accepting Sponsor-Controlled Study Designs

Control of data, analysis, and publication by sponsors enables distortion. Secure contractual researcher control.

### Missing Subtle Design Distortions

Comparator, outcome, population, and analysis choices can bias results without fraud. Scrutinize them.

### Honorary Or Ghost Authorship

Named authors must meet authorship criteria and be accountable. Reject transactional authorship.

### Ignoring Conflicts In Review And Editorial Decisions

Conflicts bias peer review and editing too. Manage them throughout the publication chain.

### Disclosure That Enables Rather Than Constrains

Having disclosed, conflicted parties may feel free to act on the conflict. Pair disclosure with safeguards.

### Perfunctory Disclosure With Vague Wording

Vague disclosures, such as "consulted for several companies," conceal more than they reveal. Disclose specifics.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Both financial and non-financial conflicts are disclosed, for all investigators, with specifics.
- [ ] Disclosure is paired with structural safeguards, not treated as sufficient on its own.
- [ ] Contracts guarantee researcher control of data, analysis, and publication, including unfavorable findings.
- [ ] The study design, comparator, outcomes, population, and analysis have been scrutinized for sponsor-favoring distortions.
- [ ] The registered analysis plan is compared to the published analysis for post-hoc switches.
- [ ] Authorship reflects substantial contribution and accountability, with no honorary or ghost authors.
- [ ] Contributions of writers or contributors are disclosed with names and roles.
- [ ] Peer review and editorial conflicts are managed, with recusal where significant.
- [ ] The work can be trusted as an independent academic product, not a sponsor-controlled narrative.
- [ ] The abstract and conclusions do not spin or overstate findings relative to the data.
