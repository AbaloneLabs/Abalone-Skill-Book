# Research Gap Typology And Formulation

A research gap is the justification for new work, but most gap statements are weak. "Few studies have examined X" is not a gap; it is an observation that could justify studying anything. A real gap explains what is missing, why it matters, what kind of evidence would fill it, and why the existing literature cannot already answer the question. Agents tend to manufacture gaps by under-searching, by treating the absence of their exact topic as a gap, or by claiming novelty for a combination of words that has not appeared together. The discipline is to find a gap that is real, consequential, and researchable, not one that merely sounds original.

Use this skill when identifying, classifying, or formulating a research gap, or when deciding whether a proposed contribution is genuinely needed. The goal is to prevent the agent from justifying work with a hollow or false gap.

## Core Rules

### Classify The Gap By What Is Missing

Different gaps imply different contributions. Naming the type of gap clarifies what kind of study would fill it and prevents vague novelty claims.

Common gap types:

- population gap: a group is under-studied or excluded;
- context gap: a setting, country, era, or condition is unexamined;
- method gap: a better or different method has not been applied;
- measurement gap: the construct is poorly or inconsistently measured;
- theoretical gap: existing theory does not explain a phenomenon;
- mechanism gap: how or why an effect occurs is unknown;
- implementation gap: something works in principle but not in practice;
- evidence conflict: findings disagree and the source is unresolved;
- replication gap: a widely cited result has not been independently tested;
- equity gap: outcomes or access differ across groups and are under-studied.

State which type of gap is being claimed. A vague gap supports a vague contribution.

### Establish That The Gap Is Real Through Adequate Search

A gap claim is only as strong as the search that failed to find the evidence. Under-searching manufactures false gaps.

Before claiming a gap:

- search multiple databases and grey literature;
- check theses, conference proceedings, and registries;
- consult experts who would know of unpublished work;
- search in relevant languages;
- distinguish "not found in my search" from "does not exist."

"No study exists" is a strong claim that requires a strong search. Qualify the scope where the search was limited.

### Show The Gap Is Consequential, Not Merely Unfilled

A gap matters only if filling it changes a decision, theory, practice, or understanding. Many unfilled spaces in a literature are unfilled because they are trivial.

Establish consequence by:

- identifying the decision, theory, or practice the missing evidence affects;
- showing what is currently assumed or done in the absence of the evidence;
- explaining the harm, cost, or error that the gap permits;
- connecting the gap to a live debate or unresolved tension.

A gap without consequence reads as filler and will not motivate a reader or reviewer.

### Avoid The "Exact Combination" Novelty Trap

Claiming novelty because a specific combination of variables, population, and setting has not been studied is a common but weak form of originality. Infinite unfilled combinations exist; most are unfilled for good reasons.

Test combination novelty by:

- asking whether the combination is theoretically motivated, not just untried;
- checking whether existing results already imply the answer;
- ensuring the combination tests a real prediction rather than fills a cell;
- avoiding "X has not been studied in Y context" without a reason X would differ in Y.

Novelty should be earned by reasoning, not by permutation.

### Link The Gap To A Specific Researchable Question

A gap must translate into a question a study can actually answer. Gaps that remain abstract cannot guide design.

Translate by:

- restating the gap as a question with population, comparison, and outcome;
- identifying the evidence that would fill it;
- checking feasibility of generating that evidence;
- specifying what would count as an answer for and against.

If the gap cannot be restated as a researchable question, it is not yet a contribution.

### Distinguish Absence Of Evidence From Evidence Of Absence

"Few studies" is not the same as "studies found no effect." Confusing them misstates what is known.

Clarify:

- whether the literature has examined the question and found nothing;
- whether the question has not been examined at all;
- whether existing studies were too small or flawed to detect an effect;
- whether the gap is in evidence or in certainty.

A gap framed as "nothing is known" when studies exist but are weak is a misrepresentation.

### Position The Gap Against The Strongest Existing Evidence

A gap is more credible when it engages the best existing work rather than the weakest. Ignoring strong studies to preserve a gap is a form of selective reading.

Engage by:

- identifying the most direct and rigorous existing studies;
- explaining precisely what they leave unanswered;
- showing how the proposed work extends rather than ignores them;
- acknowledging where existing evidence already partially answers the question.

A gap that survives engagement with the strongest evidence is defensible; one that depends on ignoring it is not.

### Make The Contribution Type Match The Gap

The kind of study proposed should match the kind of gap identified. A mismatch produces work that does not fill the gap it claims to.

Match by:

- a theoretical gap calls for theory development or testing;
- a method gap calls for applying or validating the method;
- a population gap calls for studying that population;
- a mechanism gap calls for explanatory or process evidence;
- a replication gap calls for a direct, rigorous replication.

Do not claim to fill a mechanism gap with a descriptive study, or a population gap with a different population.

## Common Traps

### "More Research Is Needed" As A Conclusion

This phrase justifies nothing. A gap must specify what is missing and why it matters.

### Manufacturing Gaps By Under-Searching

A weak search produces a false absence claim that collapses when a reviewer finds the existing studies.

### Exact-Combination Novelty

Permuting variables until an unfilled cell appears is not a contribution unless theoretically motivated.

### Ignoring Strong Existing Evidence

Preserving a gap by omitting the most rigorous prior work is selective and easily exposed.

### Confusing Absence Of Evidence With Evidence Of Absence

Claiming nothing is known when weak studies exist, or claiming no effect when none was studied, misstates the literature.

### Consequence-Free Gaps

A gap that changes no decision, theory, or practice will not motivate readers or reviewers.

### Mismatched Contribution And Gap

Proposing a study type that cannot fill the gap as described leaves the contribution unfulfilled.

## Self-Check

- Is the gap classified by type, and does that type imply a specific kind of contribution?
- Was the search to establish the gap adequate across databases, grey literature, languages, and expert input?
- Does the gap statement explain why filling it matters, not just that it is unfilled?
- Is novelty earned by reasoning rather than by an untried combination of variables?
- Can the gap be restated as a specific, researchable question with identifiable evidence?
- Is absence of evidence distinguished from evidence of absence?
- Does the gap engage and survive the strongest existing evidence, not just the weakest?
- Does the proposed contribution type match the type of gap identified?
- Is the scope of any absence claim qualified to match the search actually performed?
- Would a knowledgeable reader agree the gap is real and consequential, or only that it is unfilled?
