# Grounded Theory Coding And Theory Emergence

Grounded theory is a systematic method for developing theory from data, and its rigor lies in the disciplined movement between data and conceptual categories. The method is often reduced to its coding vocabulary (open, axial, selective) without understanding the analytic logic that connects them. Agents frequently code data, group codes into categories, and present the categories as a theory, when in fact they have produced a descriptive taxonomy, not a theory. A grounded theory must explain a process or phenomenon: it identifies a core category, specifies the conditions under which it operates, the strategies people use, and the consequences that follow. The judgment problem is to use coding not as labeling but as conceptual abstraction, to drive data collection through theoretical sampling until categories are saturated, and to recognize when the analysis has moved from description to explanatory theory.

Use this skill when applying grounded theory methods. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing descriptive category systems dressed as theory, from stopping analysis before saturation, and from imposing preconceived categories instead of letting them emerge.

## Core Rules

### Let Categories Emerge Rather Than Imposing Them

Grounded theory's central commitment is that theory emerges from data, not from preconceived frameworks.

Let emerge by:

- suspending prior theoretical frameworks during initial coding;
- coding close to the data before abstracting;
- remaining open to unanticipated categories;
- using existing literature as background, not as a coding template.

Imposing a framework from the literature turns grounded theory into theory confirmation. The method's contribution is to let the data's own structure surface.

### Use The Three Coding Phases With Their Distinct Purposes

Open, axial, and selective coding serve different analytic functions and must not be collapsed.

Use by:

- open coding: breaking data into discrete concepts close to the data;
- axial coding: relating categories to subcategories, specifying properties and dimensions;
- selective coding: integrating around a core category to form a theory;
- each phase building on, not replacing, the previous.

Treating all coding as one undifferentiated activity produces categories without integration, which is description, not theory.

### Write Analytic Memos Throughout

Memos are where conceptual development happens. They are the engine of theory building.

Write by:

- memos on emerging categories and their properties;
- memos on relationships between categories;
- memos on the core category as it emerges;
- using memos to guide further data collection.

Coding without memoing stays at the descriptive level. The move to theory happens in memos, where the researcher thinks conceptually about what the data mean.

### Use Theoretical Sampling To Saturate Categories

Data collection in grounded theory is driven by the emerging theory, not set in advance.

Use by:

- collecting data to develop and refine emerging categories;
- seeking instances that test and extend the categories;
- continuing until categories are saturated;
- letting the theory determine what data to collect next.

Theoretical sampling is not convenience sampling. Each new data source is chosen for its potential to develop the theory, and collection stops when saturation, not a quota, is reached.

### Define And Recognize Saturation Substantively

Saturation is the point at which new data no longer add to the categories' properties or relationships. It is a substantive judgment, not a count.

Define by:

- new data fitting existing categories without adding properties;
- the core category and its relationships stable;
- no new theoretical insights from additional data;
- saturation assessed per category, not as an overall average.

Stopping at a predetermined sample size before saturation produces underdeveloped theory; continuing past saturation wastes effort. Saturation is about conceptual completeness.

### Identify And Develop A Core Category

A grounded theory integrates around a core category that explains the main concern of participants.

Identify by:

- the category that recurs and connects others;
- the category that explains the central process;
- its relationship to conditions, strategies, and consequences;
- its power to integrate the analysis into a coherent theory.

Without a core category, the analysis remains a list of related themes. The core category is what turns categories into an explanatory theory.

### Distinguish Description From Theoretical Explanation

A frequent failure is stopping at descriptive categories and calling them theory.

Distinguish by:

- description: what is happening in the data;
- explanation: why and how it happens, the process and conditions;
- the level of abstraction that moves beyond the specific setting;
- the theory's ability to explain the phenomenon beyond the cases studied.

A set of well-described themes is a valuable descriptive product, but it is not a grounded theory unless it explains a process with specified conditions and consequences.

### Handle The Tension With Existing Literature Deliberately

Grounded theory delays engagement with literature to avoid imposing frameworks, but cannot ignore it entirely.

Handle by:

- initial openness to emergent categories;
- later integration of the emergent theory with existing literature;
- using literature as comparison, not template;
- justifying the timing and role of literature engagement.

Pretending the literature does not exist is naive; importing it too early defeats the method. The tension must be managed deliberately and transparently.

## Common Traps

### Imposing Preconceived Categories

Coding data into a framework from the literature turns grounded theory into confirmation and forfeits emergence.

### Coding Without Memoing

Coding without analytic memos stays at description and never develops the conceptual integration that constitutes theory.

### Fixed Sample Size Instead Of Theoretical Sampling

Stopping at a quota before saturation produces underdeveloped categories and incomplete theory.

### Saturation Assumed Rather Than Assessed

Treating saturation as reached because collection felt sufficient, without checking per category, underdevelops the theory.

### No Core Category

Presenting related themes without a core integrating category produces a taxonomy, not a theory.

### Description Mistaken For Theory

Stopping at descriptive categories and calling them theory overstates the analytic contribution.

### Premature Literature Engagement

Importing literature too early imposes frameworks and prevents categories from emerging from the data.

## Self-Check

- Are categories allowed to emerge from data rather than imposed from prior frameworks?
- Are open, axial, and selective coding used with their distinct purposes, not collapsed?
- Are analytic memos written throughout to drive conceptual development?
- Is theoretical sampling used to develop and saturate categories, with collection driven by the emerging theory?
- Is saturation defined and assessed substantively per category, not by a sample count?
- Is a core category identified that integrates the analysis and explains the central process?
- Does the analysis move beyond description to theoretical explanation of process, conditions, and consequences?
- Is the tension with existing literature managed deliberately and transparently?
- Does the theory explain the phenomenon at a level of abstraction beyond the specific cases?
- Are the relationships among categories specified, not just listed?
