# Grounded Theory Paradigms And Variants

Grounded theory is not a single method. It has split into variants, Glaserian (classic), Straussian, and constructivist, that differ in their paradigm assumptions, their procedures, and what they count as a valid outcome. Agents often apply procedures from one variant while citing another, or mix elements without recognizing that the variants encode incompatible assumptions about the nature of the theory to be produced and the role of the researcher. The classic and Straussian variants operate within a more objectivist frame, seeking to discover an emergent theory that reflects reality; the constructivist variant treats theory as an interpretation co-constructed by researcher and participants. The judgment problem is to choose a variant deliberately, align all procedural decisions with that variant's assumptions, and avoid the incoherence that comes from borrowing techniques across paradigms without reconciling them. The choice of variant is not cosmetic; it shapes sampling, coding, memoing, and the kind of claim the study can make.

Use this skill when choosing or applying a grounded theory variant. The goal is to prevent the agent from mixing incompatible procedures and from claiming a kind of theory the chosen variant cannot deliver.

## Core Rules

### Choose A Variant Deliberately, Not By Default

The choice among classic, Straussian, and constructivist grounded theory should be a conscious decision tied to the research question and paradigm.

Choose by:

- classic (Glaserian): emergence, theoretical coding families, minimal structure;
- Straussian: prescribed coding paradigm (conditions, actions, consequences), axial coding procedures;
- constructivist: co-construction of meaning, interpretive theory, researcher position explicit;
- the fit between variant and the kind of theory sought.

Defaulting to whatever procedures are familiar, without considering the variant's assumptions, produces methodological incoherence.

### Align Procedural Choices With The Variant's Paradigm

Each variant implies specific procedures. Borrowing across variants without reconciliation creates inconsistency.

Align by:

- coding paradigm: Straussian axial coding uses conditions-context-strategies-consequences;
- theoretical coding: Glaserian uses theoretical coding families, constructivist is less prescriptive;
- the role of the literature: classic delays engagement, constructivist integrates reflexively;
- the status of the emerging theory: discovered versus co-constructed.

Using the Straussian coding paradigm while claiming Glaserian emergence, or claiming objectivity while using constructivist co-construction, confuses readers and undermines the study's coherence.

### Handle The Role Of Existing Literature Per The Variant

Variants differ on when and how to engage with literature. This must be handled as the variant specifies.

Handle by:

- classic: delay literature engagement to avoid imposing frameworks;
- Straussian: limited prior engagement, more integration later;
- constructivist: reflexive engagement, acknowledging prior theory as part of the interpretive frame;
- transparency about the timing and rationale.

Pretending to suspend all literature while actually relying on tacit frameworks, or engaging literature heavily while claiming emergence, misrepresents the method.

### Specify The Status Of The Theory Being Produced

Variants differ on what the theory is and what it can claim.

Specify by:

- classic and Straussian: theory as a generalizable explanation of a process;
- constructivist: theory as an interpretive rendering, situated and co-constructed;
- the level of abstraction and generalization claimed;
- the relationship of the theory to the participants' meaning.

A constructivist grounded theory cannot claim to have discovered a general process independent of context; a classic grounded theory aims for just that. The claim must match the variant.

### Make The Researcher's Role Explicit

The variants differ on how the researcher's subjectivity is treated.

Make explicit by:

- classic: researcher as neutral instrument, subjectivity managed;
- Straussian: researcher role acknowledged but procedures provide structure;
- constructivist: researcher position and subjectivity as part of the analysis;
- reflexivity proportionate to the variant.

Hiding the researcher's role in a constructivist study misrepresents the method; over-claiming neutrality in a constructivist frame is incoherent.

### Reconcile Any Cross-Variant Borrowing

Some borrowing across variants is defensible if reconciled. Unacknowledged borrowing is not.

Reconcile by:

- stating which procedures come from which variant;
- justifying why the borrowed procedure fits the study;
- checking that borrowed procedures do not conflict with the dominant variant's assumptions;
- documenting the reconciliation.

Using a Straussian coding paradigm within a constructivist study is defensible if the researcher explains how the structure serves interpretive aims; doing so silently is incoherent.

### Match The Outcome To The Variant's Strengths

Each variant produces a characteristic kind of theory. The study's claims should reflect this.

Match by:

- classic: formal or substantive theory of a basic social process;
- Straussian: a conditional matrix or pathway explaining process;
- constructivist: an interpretive theory of how meaning is constructed;
- claims that the variant can actually support.

Promising a formal generalizable theory from a constructivist approach, or a situated interpretive rendering from a classic approach, mismatches outcome to method.

### Justify The Variant In The Research Context

The variant should fit the research question, the phenomenon, and the researcher's paradigmatic stance.

Justify by:

- why this variant fits the question;
- how the variant's assumptions align with the researcher's stance;
- the phenomenon's suitability for the variant's approach;
- the practical constraints and how they were managed.

A variant chosen without justification reads as arbitrary; a variant justified against the question and stance is defensible.

## Common Traps

### Mixing Procedures Across Variants Without Reconciliation

Using Straussian coding with Glaserian emergence claims, or constructivist co-construction with objectivist theory claims, produces incoherence.

### Defaulting To A Familiar Variant

Choosing a variant by habit rather than fit with the question and paradigm misuses the method.

### Mismatched Literature Engagement

Engaging literature heavily while claiming emergence, or claiming suspension while relying on tacit frameworks, misrepresents the method.

### Claiming A Theory Type The Variant Cannot Deliver

Promising formal generalizable theory from a constructivist approach, or situated interpretation from a classic one, mismatches claims to method.

### Hidden Researcher Role In A Constructivist Study

Failing to make the researcher's position explicit in a constructivist grounded theory misrepresents the interpretive nature of the work.

### Unacknowledged Cross-Variant Borrowing

Using procedures from multiple variants without explaining how they fit together undermines the study's coherence.

### Variant Chosen Without Justification

A variant selected without reference to the question, phenomenon, or paradigm reads as arbitrary and indefensible.

## Self-Check

- Is the grounded theory variant (classic, Straussian, constructivist) chosen deliberately and tied to the research question?
- Are procedural choices, coding paradigm, and theoretical coding aligned with the variant's paradigm?
- Is the role of existing literature handled as the variant specifies, with timing and rationale transparent?
- Is the status of the theory being produced, discovered or co-constructed, clearly specified?
- Is the researcher's role made explicit in a way proportionate to the variant?
- Is any cross-variant borrowing acknowledged and reconciled with the dominant variant?
- Does the study's claimed outcome match what the variant can deliver?
- Is the variant justified against the research question, phenomenon, and paradigmatic stance?
- Are the assumptions of the chosen variant stated and consistently applied?
- Would a reader understand which variant is in use and why?
