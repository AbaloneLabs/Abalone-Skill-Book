# Study Registration And Pre-Analysis Planning

Preregistration and pre-analysis planning are credibility tools, not bureaucratic rituals. Their purpose is to make the boundary between planned and unplanned analysis visible, so that readers can tell which results were tests of a prior commitment and which were generated by search. The value comes entirely from specificity declared before the data are examined: a vague preregistration protects nothing, and a plan that is quietly abandoned when results disappoint is worse than no plan because it lends false credibility. The judgment problem is to decide when preregistration helps, how much to specify, how to handle the inevitable deviations, and where preregistration does not fit the kind of study being done.

Use this skill when deciding whether and how to preregister, when writing a pre-analysis plan, when reporting deviations from a plan, or when judging whether preregistration is appropriate for an exploratory or qualitative design. The goal is to keep the agent from treating preregistration as a compliance step and from over- or under-specifying in ways that either strangle legitimate inquiry or fail to constrain the analyses that matter. The agent has latitude in how much to commit, but the commitment must be real, specific, and honored transparently.

## Core Rules

### Decide Whether Preregistration Fits The Study

Preregistration is most valuable where researcher degrees of freedom are high and the risk of result-driven inference is real. It is not equally suited to every design, and the decision to preregister should be reasoned, not automatic.

Consider:

- confirmatory hypothesis-testing studies benefit most, because many analysis choices could yield different conclusions;
- exploratory and hypothesis-generating studies benefit less from locking analyses, though registering the data and questions still helps;
- qualitative and emergent designs may preregister access, sampling logic, and questions, but should not pre-lock coding or themes;
- secondary analyses of existing data can preregister the analysis even though data already exist, provided the analyst has not yet examined the relevant results;
- replication studies should preregister to make the replication target unambiguous.

Where researcher degrees of freedom are low and the analysis is essentially determined by the design, preregistration adds less value but still aids discoverability and transparency.

### Specify What Must Be Fixed Before Data Are Examined

A preregistration earns its credibility only from the specificity of what it commits in advance. Vague intentions do not constrain post-hoc choices.

Lock in advance:

- the research question and hypotheses, including direction where relevant;
- the primary and secondary outcomes, named and operationalized;
- the treatment of the outcome, including transformations and thresholds;
- the comparison groups and the estimand;
- the analysis model, including covariates and their justification;
- the handling of missing data and exclusions;
- the decision rule, including thresholds and multiplicity correction;
- the sample size and stopping rule;
- any planned subgroup and sensitivity analyses.

Each locked element removes one researcher degree of freedom. The elements left unspecified remain free to be chosen after seeing results, and should be labeled exploratory when reported.

### Balance Flexibility Against Credibility

A pre-analysis plan can be too rigid or too loose. Over-specification can force an inappropriate analysis when reality differs from the plan; under-specification preserves flexibility but forfeits credibility. The skill is in choosing the right level of commitment.

Trade off:

- specifying the primary analysis tightly, while leaving clearly labeled exploratory analyses flexible;
- anticipating likely data problems, such as skew or missingness, and pre-specifying how each will be handled;
- pre-specifying a principled decision tree for deviations rather than leaving them ad hoc;
- distinguishing confirmatory tests, which must be locked, from exploratory probes, which may remain open.

The goal is not to eliminate judgment but to confine it to where it does not threaten the confirmatory inference.

### Plan For Deviations Before They Happen

Real studies deviate from their plans. Equipment fails, recruitment falls short, a measure behaves unexpectedly, an assumption is violated. The credibility of preregistration depends on how deviations are handled, not on pretending they never occur.

Handle deviations by:

- documenting each deviation and the reason it was necessary;
- distinguishing deviations forced by circumstances from those chosen for convenience;
- reporting both the planned analysis and the deviating analysis, so readers can see the effect of the change;
- pre-specifying a principled hierarchy for which deviations are acceptable;
- making the timing of decisions clear, especially whether a deviation was decided before or after seeing results.

A deviation that is reported transparently and justified substantively preserves most of the credibility of the plan. A deviation that is silently absorbed destroys it.

### Treat Registered Reports As A Stronger Model

Registered reports are a publication model in which the protocol is peer-reviewed and accepted before data collection, with a commitment to publish the results regardless of outcome. This realigns incentives away from novelty and significance toward rigor.

Consider registered reports when:

- the study is confirmatory and the question is important enough to warrant upfront review;
- the design and analysis can be specified with enough detail to review meaningfully;
- the field and journal support the format;
- the risk of publication bias or outcome reporting bias is high.

Registered reports do not eliminate all bias, because deviations and exploratory analyses remain possible, but they remove the largest source of distortion: the link between the result and the chance of publication.

### Distinguish Preregistration From Proof Of Quality

A preregistered study is not automatically a good study, and an unregistered study is not automatically bad. Preregistration constrains analysis choices; it does not guarantee a sound question, valid measures, adequate power, or correct inference.

Do not assume that:

- a preregistration badge means the results are credible;
- the preregistered analysis was the right one for the data;
- the study was adequately powered simply because it was planned;
- absence of preregistration means the work is unreliable.

Evaluate the preregistration on its specificity and on whether it was honored, and evaluate the study on its design and execution regardless of registration status.

### Know The Limits Of Preregistration For Exploratory And Qualitative Work

Preregistration was built for confirmatory quantitative inference, and applying it mechanically to emergent designs can distort them. Qualitative, grounded-theory, and exploratory work develop their categories and interpretations through engagement with data, which is precisely what preregistration is meant to prevent.

For such work, consider instead:

- preregistering the research questions, access, and sampling logic, not the codes or themes;
- documenting the analytic process and decisions as they unfold, in an audit trail;
- being explicit that the work is exploratory or emergent and should be read as such;
- avoiding the false credibility of a confirmatory preregistration applied to inherently flexible analysis.

The honest framing matters more than the formal act of registration. Calling exploratory work exploratory protects the literature better than a hollow preregistration.

## Common Traps

### Preregistering Vaguely

A plan that does not lock outcomes, exclusions, and analysis choices constrains nothing and lends false credibility to post-hoc decisions.

### Abandoning The Plan When Results Disappoint

Silently switching to a more favorable analysis after seeing the data destroys the value of preregistration and is a form of result-driven inference.

### Hiding Deviations

Unreported deviations make the published analysis look planned when it was not, misleading readers about the strength of the evidence.

### Treating Preregistration As A Quality Stamp

Registration constrains analysis choices; it does not validate the question, measures, power, or interpretation. A bad study can be preregistered.

### Over-Specifying To The Point Of Rigidity

Locking every analysis choice can force an inappropriate model when the data violate the plan's assumptions, unless a principled deviation process is pre-specified.

### Applying Confirmatory Preregistration To Emergent Work

Forcing qualitative or grounded-theory analysis into a pre-locked coding frame contradicts the logic of emergent inquiry and produces hollow compliance.

### Preregistering After Examining The Data

A preregistration filed after the analyst has seen the relevant results is not a prior commitment and provides no constraint; it is documentation dressed as preregistration.

### Confusing Registered Reports With Ordinary Registration

Registered reports commit to publication before results, which removes publication bias; ordinary registration does not. The two offer different protections.

## Self-Check

- Has it been reasoned whether preregistration fits this study, given its design and researcher degrees of freedom?
- Are hypotheses, primary and secondary outcomes, exclusions, analysis model, missing-data handling, decision rule, and multiplicity correction specified in advance?
- Is the level of commitment balanced, with confirmatory tests locked and exploratory analyses clearly labeled?
- Is there a pre-specified, principled process for handling deviations, and are deviations documented with timing and reasons?
- Are both planned and deviating analyses reported so readers can see the effect of changes?
- Has a registered report format been considered where the study is confirmatory and publication bias is a risk?
- Is preregistration treated as a constraint on analysis choices rather than as proof of study quality?
- For exploratory or qualitative work, is registration limited to questions and sampling logic, with the emergent nature made explicit?
- Is it clear that the preregistration was filed before the relevant results were examined?
- Are the limits of preregistration for this study acknowledged honestly in the conclusion?
