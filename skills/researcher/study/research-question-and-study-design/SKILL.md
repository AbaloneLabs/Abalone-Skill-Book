# Research Question And Study Design

Research design begins before data collection. A study can be technically impressive and still fail if the question is vague, the design cannot answer it, the sample is mismatched, the measures do not capture the concept, or the analysis plan is chosen after looking at results. The researcher should make the inferential target explicit before collecting, cleaning, or interpreting evidence.

Use this skill before proposing a study, narrowing a research topic, choosing methods, designing a survey, planning interviews, selecting a dataset, defining variables, or deciding whether existing evidence can answer a question. The goal is to keep the agent from turning a broad curiosity into a weak design that cannot support the claim it wants to make.

## Core Rules

### Turn The Topic Into A Researchable Question

A topic is not a question. "Remote work and productivity" is too broad. A researchable question identifies the phenomenon, population, context, comparison, and kind of answer sought.

Clarify:

- phenomenon or construct;
- population or case;
- setting;
- time period;
- comparison or contrast;
- outcome or experience;
- intended contribution;
- decision or theory the answer will inform.

Write the question in a form that implies evidence. If the question asks "why," the design must support causal or explanatory reasoning. If it asks "how," qualitative, process, or mechanism evidence may be needed. If it asks "how much," measurement and sampling become central.

### Define The Unit Of Analysis

Many studies fail because they mix levels. A question about individuals, teams, organizations, communities, documents, transactions, events, or countries needs data at the right level.

Identify:

- unit observed;
- unit analyzed;
- unit generalized to;
- nesting or clustering;
- repeated measures;
- aggregation rules;
- whether individual data can support group-level claims;
- whether group data can support individual-level claims.

Do not infer individual behavior from only aggregate data without acknowledging ecological limits. Do not infer organizational effects from a few individual anecdotes without framing them as exploratory.

### Match Design To Claim Type

Different claims need different designs. Descriptive, exploratory, explanatory, causal, predictive, evaluative, interpretive, and theory-building claims have different evidence requirements.

Design choices include:

- experiment;
- quasi-experiment;
- observational quantitative study;
- survey;
- interview study;
- ethnography;
- case study;
- archival analysis;
- content analysis;
- systematic review;
- mixed methods;
- simulation or modeling;
- design-based research.

If the study cannot establish causality, avoid causal language. If it is exploratory, state what it can generate: hypotheses, concepts, mechanisms, patterns, or future study directions.

### Operationalize Constructs Carefully

Concepts need observable definitions. A construct such as trust, engagement, resilience, quality, risk, learning, or inequality must be translated into measures or analytic indicators.

For each key construct, define:

- conceptual meaning;
- indicator or measure;
- source;
- validity evidence;
- reliability concern;
- measurement timing;
- scoring or coding rule;
- known limitations;
- cultural or language concerns.

Do not use a convenient proxy without explaining what it captures and what it misses. A proxy can be useful, but it should not quietly become the construct itself.

### Plan Sampling And Recruitment

Sampling affects what can be concluded. A sample can be large and still biased, or small and still appropriate for a focused qualitative question.

Plan:

- target population;
- sampling frame;
- inclusion criteria;
- exclusion criteria;
- recruitment method;
- sample size rationale;
- expected nonresponse;
- attrition risk;
- representation concerns;
- vulnerable or hard-to-reach groups;
- incentives;
- consent or permission requirements.

State whether the sample supports statistical generalization, analytic generalization, transferability, or only local insight.

### Identify Bias And Confounding Early

Bias is easier to reduce before data collection than to explain after results look strange.

Check for:

- selection bias;
- measurement bias;
- recall bias;
- social desirability bias;
- observer bias;
- missing data;
- confounding;
- survivorship bias;
- publication bias;
- researcher expectancy;
- instrumentation changes;
- seasonality;
- historical events.

For quantitative work, identify confounders before modeling. For qualitative work, identify positionality, access limitations, and interpretation risks.

### Predefine The Analysis Logic

The analysis plan should follow the question and design, not the most interesting result found later.

Specify:

- primary outcome or theme;
- comparison groups;
- statistical model or qualitative coding approach;
- covariates;
- subgroup analysis;
- missing data handling;
- sensitivity checks;
- stopping or exclusion rules;
- software;
- version control;
- preregistration if appropriate.

Exploratory analysis is legitimate when labeled as exploratory. Do not present post hoc discoveries as if they were planned tests.

### Consider Feasibility And Ethics Together

A design may be methodologically ideal but infeasible or unethical. Feasibility includes access, time, budget, skills, tools, approvals, recruitment, and data quality.

Check:

- human subjects review;
- privacy or confidentiality;
- consent;
- data access agreement;
- safety;
- community impact;
- compensation fairness;
- power imbalance;
- researcher qualifications;
- timeline;
- budget;
- equipment;
- field access.

If the design depends on restricted data, vulnerable participants, deception, biological specimens, or sensitive topics, consult the relevant institutional process before proceeding.

### Define Success And Failure Conditions

A study plan should say what results would support, weaken, or fail to answer the question.

Before data collection, ask:

- What would count as evidence for the claim?
- What would count as evidence against it?
- What result would be inconclusive?
- What missing data would break the study?
- What assumptions must hold?
- What limitations will remain even if the study succeeds?

This protects against overinterpreting ambiguous results.

## Common Traps

### Starting With Available Data Instead Of The Question

Existing data can be valuable, but it may not measure the right population, construct, time period, or outcome. Do not force a question into data that cannot answer it.

### Making Causal Claims From Descriptive Designs

Correlation, temporal co-occurrence, or participant belief may suggest a causal hypothesis. It does not prove causality without a design and assumptions that support causal inference.

### Treating Sample Size As Quality

Large samples do not remove measurement bias, confounding, bad proxies, or weak theory. Small samples can be rigorous when the design and claim are matched.

### Defining Variables After Seeing Results

Post hoc variable definitions can create false confidence. Record coding and exclusion rules before analysis when possible.

### Ignoring Negative Cases

Contradictory cases can refine theory and expose hidden assumptions. Do not discard them because they complicate the story.

### Overpromising Generalization

A local case study, convenience survey, or exploratory interview study can be useful without claiming universal truth. Match scope to evidence.

### Forgetting Operational Constraints

Recruitment failure, data access delay, missing permissions, software limits, or field conditions can break a design. Plan contingencies.

## Self-Check

- Is the topic turned into a specific researchable question?
- Are population, context, comparison, outcome, and intended contribution explicit?
- Is the unit of analysis clear and consistent with the claim?
- Does the study design match the type of claim being made?
- Are key constructs operationalized with measurement limitations stated?
- Is the sampling strategy aligned with the intended inference?
- Are major sources of bias, confounding, missingness, and researcher influence identified?
- Is the analysis logic defined before interpreting results?
- Are feasibility, approvals, ethics, privacy, safety, and data access considered?
- Are success, failure, inconclusive outcomes, assumptions, and remaining limitations defined?
