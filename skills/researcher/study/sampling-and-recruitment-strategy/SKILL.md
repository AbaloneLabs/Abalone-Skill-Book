# Sampling And Recruitment Strategy

A study's conclusions can only be as good as the sample on which they are built, and sampling is where many studies fail before data collection begins. A sample that is too small cannot detect real effects; a sample that is biased produces findings that do not generalize; a sample that excludes relevant populations produces conclusions that apply only to the studied subset; and a recruitment process that systematically reaches certain participants and not others introduces selection effects that distort every downstream analysis. These failures are often invisible in the final paper, which reports findings from "the sample" without examining whether the sample supports the claims made about it. The judgment problem is designing sampling and recruitment that fit the research question, that produce samples adequate to detect the effects of interest, that minimize bias, and that honestly represent the population to which conclusions will be generalized, rather than treating sampling as a procedural detail that can be sorted out later.

Use this skill when designing sampling strategy, determining sample size, planning recruitment, addressing sampling bias, recruiting underrepresented populations, or diagnosing generalizability problems. The goal is to prevent the agent from using convenience samples where representative ones are needed, from underpowering studies, from ignoring selection bias in recruitment, and from generalizing beyond what the sample supports.

## Core Rules

### Align Sampling Strategy With The Research Question And Claims

The sampling strategy must fit the research question and the claims the study intends to make. A study aiming to estimate population prevalence requires probability sampling; a study exploring mechanisms may work with purposive samples; a study comparing groups requires adequate representation of each. Mismatched sampling, using convenience where representativeness is needed, produces findings that cannot support the claims made.

Align by:

- identifying the population to which findings will be generalized;
- matching the sampling approach to the level of inference required, population-level versus exploratory;
- distinguishing studies that need representative samples from those that need informative ones;
- ensuring the sampling strategy can deliver the evidence the claims require.

A study whose sampling cannot support its claims has a conclusions section that overstates what the data show.

### Choose Probability Versus Non-Probability Sampling Deliberately

Probability sampling, where every member of the population has a known chance of selection, supports statistical generalization. Non-probability sampling, including convenience, purposive, and snowball, does not support formal generalization but is appropriate for exploratory work, hard-to-reach populations, or questions where representativeness is not the goal. The choice should be deliberate, not default.

Choose deliberately by:

- using probability sampling where population-level inference is the goal;
- using non-probability sampling where exploration, theory-building, or access to specific populations is the goal;
- being honest about the limits of non-probability samples for generalization;
- avoiding convenience sampling dressed up as if it supported population claims;
- documenting the sampling approach and its implications for inference.

Convenience sampling has legitimate uses, but presenting convenience sample findings as population-generalizable is a misrepresentation.

### Determine Sample Size Through Power Analysis, Not Guesswork

Sample size should be determined by the effect size the study needs to detect, the desired power, and the significance level, through formal power analysis. Underpowered studies fail to detect real effects, producing inconclusive findings that may be misinterpreted as evidence of no effect. Overpowered studies waste resources and may detect trivial effects as significant.

Determine by:

- conducting a power analysis based on the minimum effect size of interest;
- accounting for multiple comparisons, attrition, and clustering where they apply;
- distinguishing the effect size that is statistically detectable from the effect size that is substantively meaningful;
- avoiding sample sizes chosen for convenience that leave the study underpowered;
- pre-registering or documenting the sample size rationale.

An underpowered study that finds "no significant effect" has not shown there is no effect; it has shown the study could not detect one.

### Identify And Minimize Sampling Bias

Sampling bias occurs when the sample systematically differs from the population in ways related to the study variables. It arises from non-random selection, differential non-response, and recruitment through channels that reach certain participants preferentially. Sampling bias produces findings that are systematically off, regardless of sample size.

Identify and minimize by:

- examining whether recruitment channels reach the target population or a biased subset;
- assessing differential response rates across groups and their effect on representativeness;
- using weighting or post-stratification to adjust for known sampling biases where possible;
- recognizing that increasing sample size does not fix bias, it just produces a more precise biased estimate;
- documenting known biases and their likely direction.

A sample of ten thousand volunteers is still biased if volunteers differ systematically from non-volunteers on the study variables.

### Plan Recruitment To Reach The Intended Population

Recruitment determines who enters the sample, and recruitment through convenient channels, such as university participant pools or online platforms, produces samples that are convenient rather than representative. Planning recruitment to reach the intended population, including hard-to-reach groups, is essential for generalizable findings.

Plan by:

- identifying the channels through which the target population can be reached;
- using multiple recruitment channels to reduce over-reliance on a biased one;
- removing barriers to participation, such as timing, location, or technology, that exclude groups;
- recognizing that easy recruitment produces easy, often biased, samples;
- budgeting and scheduling for the harder recruitment that representative sampling requires.

A study that recruits only from an undergraduate pool cannot generalize to the general population, regardless of how many undergraduates participate.

### Recruit Underrepresented And Hard-To-Reach Populations Ethically

Including underrepresented and hard-to-reach populations is both a scientific and ethical imperative: scientific, because their exclusion limits generalizability; ethical, because their consistent exclusion from research means findings do not apply to them and they do not benefit from research. But recruitment of these populations requires care to avoid coercion, exploitation, and burden.

Recruit ethically by:

- using culturally appropriate recruitment channels and materials;
- building trust with communities through sustained engagement, not one-time extraction;
- ensuring incentives are appropriate, not coercive, relative to participants' circumstances;
- removing participation barriers, such as language, transportation, or scheduling;
- avoiding over-burdening populations already heavily researched for limited benefit;
- giving back to communities, through shared findings or tangible benefit, not only extracting data.

A population excluded from research for convenience is a population for whom evidence-based practice lacks an evidence base.

### Account For Attrition And Missing Data

Participants drop out, miss assessments, or provide incomplete data, and this attrition is rarely random. If attrition correlates with study variables, it introduces bias that compounds the sampling design. Planning for attrition and analyzing missing data appropriately are part of sampling strategy.

Account by:

- anticipating attrition rates based on similar studies and over-recruiting accordingly;
- minimizing attrition through engagement, reminders, and respect for participant burden;
- analyzing the pattern of missing data, random versus non-random, and its implications;
- using appropriate methods, such as multiple imputation, for missing data, not complete-case analysis that biases results;
- reporting attrition and missing data honestly.

A study that loses the participants most affected by the intervention produces findings biased toward those who stayed, who may differ systematically.

### Be Honest About Generalizability Limits

Every sample has limits on what it can generalize to, and honesty about those limits is essential. Overclaiming generalizability, extending conclusions beyond the population the sample represents, is a common and serious distortion. State the generalizability limits clearly.

Be honest by:

- defining the population the sample represents and the population it does not;
- avoiding conclusions about populations not represented in the sample;
- acknowledging selection factors that may limit generalizability;
- distinguishing statistical generalization, from probability samples, from theoretical transfer, from purposive samples;
- framing conclusions to match the sampling's actual support.

A study of volunteers cannot claim findings about the general population; a study of one clinic cannot claim findings about all clinics. The conclusions must match the sample.

## Common Traps

### Sampling Strategy Mismatched To Claims

Convenience sampling supporting population claims. Align sampling to the research question and intended inference.

### Underpowered Studies Interpreted As No Effect

Failure to detect an effect is not evidence of no effect. Use power analysis for sample size.

### Convenience Sampling Presented As Representative

Convenience samples have legitimate uses but do not support population generalization. Be honest.

### Bias Mistaken For Imprecision

Larger biased samples are precisely wrong. Address bias through design, not just size.

### Recruitment Through Biased Channels

Easy recruitment produces biased samples. Use multiple channels and plan for harder recruitment.

### Excluding Hard-To-Reach Populations For Convenience

Exclusion limits generalizability and equity. Recruit underrepresented populations ethically.

### Ignoring Attrition And Missing Data

Non-random attrition biases findings. Plan for attrition and analyze missing data appropriately.

### Overclaiming Generalizability

Conclusions must match the sample's support. State generalizability limits honestly.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The sampling strategy is aligned to the research question and the claims the study intends to make.
- [ ] Probability versus non-probability sampling is chosen deliberately based on the inference required.
- [ ] Sample size is determined through power analysis for the minimum meaningful effect size, with attrition accounted for.
- [ ] Sampling bias is identified through examination of recruitment and response, and minimized where possible.
- [ ] Recruitment is planned to reach the intended population, using multiple channels and removing barriers.
- [ ] Underrepresented and hard-to-reach populations are recruited ethically, with trust-building and appropriate incentives.
- [ ] Attrition and missing data are anticipated, minimized, and analyzed appropriately for their pattern.
- [ ] Generalizability limits are stated honestly, with conclusions matching the sample's support.
- [ ] No convenience sample is presented as supporting population-level claims.
- [ ] The population the sample represents and the population it does not are clearly defined.
