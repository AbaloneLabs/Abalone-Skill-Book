# Feasibility And Pilot Studies

A study can be theoretically excellent and practically impossible. Recruitment may never reach target, the intervention may be unacceptable to participants, the measure may be too burdensome, the data system may fail, or the cost may exceed the budget before the question is answered. Feasibility and pilot work exist to expose these failures early, while they are still cheap to fix. Researchers often skip this step out of optimism or urgency, then discover at full scale that the design cannot deliver the answer it promised.

Use this skill before launching a main study, when designing a pilot, when interpreting pilot results, and when deciding whether to proceed, revise, or stop. The goal is to keep the agent from confusing a pilot with a mini-trial, from overinterpreting tiny samples, and from treating feasibility as a box to tick rather than a decision gate. The agent has latitude in what to test, but must define what feasibility means before collecting evidence.

## Core Rules

### Define What Feasibility Means For This Study

Feasibility is not a single property. It is a bundle of questions about whether the study can run as designed and produce usable evidence. Define which dimensions matter before testing them.

Common dimensions:

- recruitment rate and reachable population;
- retention and attrition;
- eligibility criteria that are workable in the field;
- intervention acceptability and adherence;
- burden on participants and staff;
- data collection feasibility, including time and missingness;
- measure performance in this population;
- site and staffing capacity;
- cost per enrolled participant;
- timeline against funding;
- regulatory and approval burden;
- data system reliability;
- stakeholder or gatekeeper cooperation.

Rank these by risk to the main study. Test the riskiest first.

### Distinguish Pilot From Feasibility From Main Study

These terms are often used interchangeably, but they answer different questions, and conflating them distorts inference.

Clarify:

- a feasibility study asks whether the study can be done;
- a pilot study is a small-scale version run to test processes, often with the same procedures as the main trial;
- an internal pilot is embedded in the main trial and uses its data to refine the design;
- an external pilot stands alone and should not be pooled into the main analysis without strong justification;
- a main trial or main study tests the primary hypothesis.

State which kind of study is being run and what question it answers. A pilot is not powered to test the hypothesis, and its effect estimate should not be reported as if it were.

### Set Predefined Progression Criteria

A pilot without decision rules becomes a story about what happened. Progression criteria force the team to decide in advance what evidence would justify moving to the main study, revising it, or stopping.

Define criteria such as:

- recruitment rate above a stated threshold;
- retention above a stated threshold;
- adherence or completion above a stated threshold;
- data completeness above a stated threshold;
- fidelity to protocol above a stated threshold;
- absence of serious safety signals;
- acceptability ratings above a stated threshold;
- cost or time within budget envelope.

Use a traffic-light structure: green to proceed, amber to revise, red to stop. Without these, the team will rationalize whatever the pilot shows.

### Do Not Power A Pilot For Hypothesis Testing

Pilot samples are usually small because their purpose is to estimate processes, not to detect effects. Powering a pilot for the primary outcome misuses resources and invites misinterpretation.

Instead justify the pilot sample by:

- precision needed for feasibility parameters, such as recruitment rate;
- number needed to expose procedural problems;
- convention paired with explicit reasoning;
- desired width of confidence intervals around retention or adherence;
- saturation logic for qualitative components.

Do not report a pilot effect as "significant" or "non-significant." Report the estimate with a wide confidence interval and treat it as a planning input, not a finding.

### Use Pilot Data Cautiously For Main Study Planning

Pilot data can inform the design of the main study, but only for quantities that are stable enough to estimate from a small sample.

Reasonable uses:

- refining inclusion and exclusion criteria;
- estimating recruitment and retention rates;
- estimating variance for sample size calculations, with caution;
- revising the intervention or its delivery;
- revising measures and timing;
- estimating cost and burden;
- identifying missing data patterns.

Risky uses:

- treating the pilot effect size as the true effect;
- pooling pilot and main data without justification;
- using pilot variance to claim the main study is overpowered;
- changing the primary outcome based on which looked best.

Variance estimates from small samples are unstable. Consider adjusting the sample size calculation upward or using external data to confirm.

### Test Procedures, Not Just Outcomes

Feasibility is largely about whether the machinery of the study works. Outcomes are secondary in a pilot.

Test:

- recruitment scripts and channels;
- consent process and comprehension;
- randomization or assignment procedure;
- intervention delivery and training needs;
- data collection tools and platforms;
- coding or scoring procedures;
- monitoring and safety reporting;
- staff roles and handoffs;
- participant communication;
- retention strategies.

Document what broke and how it was fixed. This log is often more valuable than the outcome data.

### Plan For Ethics, Consent, And Data Limits In Pilots

Pilots are still research with human participants. They require the same ethical scrutiny as the main study, and sometimes more because procedures are untested.

Check:

- IRB or ethics review for the pilot;
- consent that reflects genuine uncertainty and untested procedures;
- data sharing and future use;
- compensation fairness for extra burden;
- stopping rules for safety signals;
- privacy for identifiable pilot data;
- whether pilot participants can enroll in the main study.

Do not treat the pilot as exempt because it is small. Size does not determine ethical obligation.

### Decide Explicitly Whether To Proceed

A pilot that ends without a decision has wasted its purpose. Convene the team against the progression criteria and record the decision.

Options:

- proceed to main study as designed;
- proceed with revisions, and document them;
- run a second pilot or feasibility iteration;
- stop and reconsider the question or design;
- abandon the approach if feasibility is poor.

Record the reasoning. Future reviewers and funders need to see why the design changed or did not.

## Common Traps

### Treating The Pilot As A Mini-Trial

Powering a pilot for the primary outcome and reporting its effect as a finding overstates tiny samples and misleads readers about the evidence.

### Using The Pilot Effect Size As The Truth

Effect estimates from small samples are noisy, often inflated, and biased toward the extremes. Plugging them straight into the main sample size calculation sets up disappointment or false confidence.

### Skipping Progression Criteria

Without green-amber-red rules, the team will declare success no matter what the pilot shows, defeating the purpose of testing feasibility.

### Confusing Feasibility With Efficacy

Showing that a study can be run is not the same as showing that the intervention works. Do not let a smooth pilot produce efficacy claims.

### Pooling Pilot And Main Data Quietly

Combining pilot and main participants without justification double-counts early adopters and biases estimates. Keep them separate or pre-specify a justified pooling rule.

### Ignoring What Broke

A pilot that exposes recruitment failure, measure burden, or data system gaps is valuable only if those problems are documented and addressed. Many teams note them verbally and then repeat them at scale.

### Underestimating Regulatory And Approval Burden

Ethics review, data agreements, site permissions, and registration can take longer than the pilot itself. Plan them as part of feasibility, not after.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Are the feasibility dimensions that matter most for this study defined and ranked by risk?
- [ ] Is the study clearly labeled as feasibility, pilot, internal pilot, or main, with the question it answers stated?
- [ ] Are progression criteria specified in advance, with green-amber-red thresholds for proceeding, revising, or stopping?
- [ ] Is the pilot sample justified by feasibility precision or process goals rather than hypothesis-testing power?
- [ ] Are pilot effect estimates reported with wide intervals and treated as planning inputs, not findings?
- [ ] Are procedures, recruitment, retention, adherence, data completeness, cost, and burden tested and documented?
- [ ] Is pilot variance used cautiously in sample size calculation, with sensitivity to small-sample instability?
- [ ] Are ethics review, consent reflecting uncertainty, compensation, and stopping rules planned for the pilot?
- [ ] Is there a recorded decision to proceed, revise, iterate, or stop, with reasoning tied to the criteria?
- [ ] Are pilot and main data kept separate or pooled only under a justified, prespecified rule?
