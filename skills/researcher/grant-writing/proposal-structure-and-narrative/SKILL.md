# Proposal Structure And Narrative

A grant proposal is a persuasive argument that a specific question matters, that the applicant can answer it, and that the funder should pay for the attempt. Unlike a paper, which reports completed work, a proposal asks for trust in advance. Reviewers read dozens of proposals under time pressure and rank them competitively, so a proposal must make its case efficiently, with a clear logic that a tired reviewer can follow. Agents often write proposals that bury the question, assume the significance is self-evident, or present methods without connecting them to aims, all of which lead to rejection even when the science is strong.

The harm this skill prevents is lost funding for worthwhile research, months of wasted effort on proposals that never had a coherent argument, and demoralization from repeated rejection rooted in structure rather than science. The agent has freedom in narrative style, but the requirements of clarity, feasibility, and persuasive logic are binding.

## Core Rules

### Build The Proposal Around A Clear, Compelling Question

A proposal without a sharp question reads as activity rather than inquiry. The specific aims must answer an overarching question that matters. Reviewers should be able to state the question and the contribution after reading the first page.

Define the question:

- what is the gap or problem the proposal addresses;
- why does it matter now, to whom;
- what specific question will each aim answer;
- how do the aims together address the overarching question;
- what would answering it change in the field.

### Make The Significance Explicit, Not Assumed

Reviewers will not infer importance. The proposal must argue why this problem matters, what is currently unknown, and how filling the gap advances understanding or practice. Significance is an argument, not a statement.

Argue significance:

- describe the problem and its stakes;
- summarize what is known and where knowledge stops;
- explain the consequences of the gap;
- connect the work to the funder's mission and priorities;
- avoid claiming significance without justification.

### Demonstrate Innovation Beyond Incremental Work

Funders seek work that advances the field, not merely extends it. The proposal must articulate what is genuinely new in the question, approach, method, or application. Innovation is not a buzzword; it requires a specific claim about what differs from prior work.

Articulate innovation:

- what is new about the conceptual approach;
- what methods or techniques are novel;
- how does this challenge or extend current paradigms;
- what would be newly possible if the work succeeds;
- how does it differ from the closest existing work.

### Connect Methods Tightly To Aims

Each aim needs methods that can actually answer it. A common failure is methods that describe techniques without showing how they produce the evidence each aim requires. Reviewers check whether the proposed work can deliver the promised findings.

For each aim:

- state the aim as a question or objective;
- describe the approach that answers it;
- identify the data or evidence that will result;
- explain how that evidence bears on the aim;
- note expected outcomes and alternatives.

### Establish Feasibility With Preliminary Data And Track Record

Reviewers must believe the applicant can do the work. Feasibility is demonstrated through preliminary data, prior publications, available resources, team expertise, and access to populations or data. A proposal that asks for trust without evidence of capability is rejected.

Demonstrate feasibility:

- present preliminary data showing the approach works;
- cite the team's relevant prior work;
- confirm access to required resources, data, or populations;
- show the team has the needed expertise, with roles defined;
- address potential obstacles and how they will be managed.

### Write For A Tired, Non-Specialist Reviewer

Reviewers are often experts in the field but not the specific topic, and they read under deadline. The proposal must be clear enough that a knowledgeable non-specialist can follow the logic without rereading. Dense, jargon-heavy writing loses reviewers.

Write accessibly:

- state the main point of each section clearly;
- use signposting and clear structure;
- define specialized terms when first used;
- use figures to convey complex ideas quickly;
- keep paragraphs focused with clear topic sentences;
- make the logic explicit rather than implied.

### Anticipate And Address Weaknesses Proactively

Reviewers find weaknesses. A proposal that acknowledges its limitations and explains how they will be managed demonstrates judgment and reduces the impact of criticism. Hiding weaknesses invites harsher judgment.

Address proactively:

- identify the most likely reviewer concerns;
- explain how the design mitigates them;
- describe contingency plans for key risks;
- acknowledge where evidence is preliminary;
- show awareness of alternative approaches and why the chosen one is best.

### Align With Funder Priorities And Guidelines

Funders have specific missions, priorities, and formatting requirements. A scientifically excellent proposal that does not fit the funder's aims or violates page limits and section requirements is rejected. Align content and format to the specific opportunity.

Align by:

- reading the funding announcement carefully;
- connecting the work explicitly to the funder's mission;
- following section structure and page limits exactly;
- using the funder's review criteria as an outline guide;
- checking eligibility and administrative requirements.

## Common Traps

### Burying The Question Under Background

Excessive literature review before the aims loses the reviewer. State the question early and clearly.

### Assuming Significance Is Self-Evident

Reviewers will not infer importance. Argue it explicitly.

### Methods Disconnected From Aims

Describing techniques without showing how they answer each aim fails the feasibility test. Connect them tightly.

### No Preliminary Data Or Track Record

Asking for trust without evidence of capability invites rejection. Show feasibility.

### Jargon-Dense Writing

Specialist-only prose loses non-expert reviewers. Write accessibly.

### Ignoring Funder Guidelines

Scientific merit cannot save a proposal that violates format or scope. Follow the announcement exactly.

### Hiding Weaknesses

Unacknowledged vulnerabilities invite harsher criticism. Address them proactively.
