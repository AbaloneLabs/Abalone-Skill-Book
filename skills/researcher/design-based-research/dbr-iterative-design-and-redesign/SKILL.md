# DBR Iterative Design And Redesign

Design-based research (DBR) is defined by its iterative movement between designing an intervention, enacting it in an authentic setting, analyzing what happened, and redesigning in light of the evidence. The judgment problem is that the iteration is easy to describe as a tidy loop and hard to run with discipline. Agents often collapse it into a single build-and-test pass, redesign on intuition rather than evidence, change so many things at once that no learning can be attributed, or freeze the design prematurely because the timeline demands a finished product. Each failure mode produces a different harm. Premature design freeze exports an under-tested intervention to other classrooms and learners. Undisciplined redesign produces a sequence of unrelated prototypes with no convergent understanding. Missing documentation means that when the intervention finally works, no one can reconstruct why, so the redesign knowledge, which is the real output of DBR, is lost.

The agent should use this skill when planning the iterative cycle of a design-based research project, deciding what each cycle tests and what counts as evidence to revise it, structuring the analysis that feeds redesign, documenting redesign decisions and their rationale, or judging whether the intervention has stabilized enough to stop iterating. The goal is to keep the agent from treating iteration as mere repetition or product development, and to ensure that each redesign is a defensible, evidence-based step toward both a better intervention and a better understanding of how it works.

## Core Rules

### Treat Each Cycle As Analysis-Design-Development-Evaluation, Not Just Build-And-Test

A DBR cycle is not a single try-it-and-see step. It has distinct phases that each must do real work.

- Analysis: examine the prior context, learners, and existing evidence to define the problem the design must solve and why prior approaches fall short.
- Design: articulate conjectures about what features will address the problem and the reasoning linking features to intended outcomes.
- Development: build or adapt the concrete intervention, materials, and supports so the conjectures are actually instantiated.
- Evaluation: collect evidence on what happened, compare it to the conjectures, and extract what was learned for the next cycle.

Collapsing these into a rapid build-and-test produces an intervention with no stated rationale and an evaluation with nothing to compare against. The four phases are what make iteration generative rather than reactive.

### Make Each Redesign Decision Evidence-Based And Explicitly Justified

Redesign is the heart of DBR, and it must be driven by evidence, not by preference, fatigue, or a deadline.

- Tie each change to a specific finding from the evaluation, stating which evidence motivated it.
- Distinguish changes made to fix a flaw from changes made to test a new conjecture.
- State the expected effect of each change so the next cycle can confirm or refute it.
- Resist redesigning from frustration when results disappoint; redesign from what the data show.

A redesign with no link to evidence is guesswork dressed as iteration. The credibility of the whole project rests on the audit trail from finding to change to expected outcome.

### Change One Substantive Thing At A Time Where Possible

When a redesign alters multiple features simultaneously, a later improvement cannot be attributed to any single change.

- Isolate the variable of interest where feasible, changing the feature under test while holding others stable.
- When multiple changes are unavoidable, document each one and predict its separate contribution.
- Use the cycle structure to decompose bundled changes across successive iterations.
- Recognize that confounded changes produce an intervention that works but an explanation that does not.

The point of DBR is not only a working artifact but a defensible account of why it works. Confounded redesigns forfeit that account.

### Document Conjectures, Evidence, And Redesign Rationale As A Running Record

The most valuable output of DBR is often not the final intervention but the documented reasoning behind its evolution.

- Keep a conjecture map or design rationale log linking conjectures to features to observed outcomes.
- Record what was tried, what was learned, and what was changed, with the evidence for each.
- Capture negative results and abandoned designs, not only successes.
- Preserve version history so any claim about the intervention can be traced to a specific version.

Without this running record, the redesign knowledge evaporates. A later researcher or practitioner cannot reconstruct why the design took its final shape, and the project collapses into a product demo.

### Decide When To Iterate By Convergence, Not By Schedule

The number of cycles should follow whether understanding has converged, not a predetermined count or grant deadline.

- Iterate when a cycle revealed a flaw, a surprise, or an incomplete account.
- Stop when additional cycles would only reconfirm what is understood or when the design has stabilized across cycles and settings.
- State an explicit convergence criterion so the work can end defensibly.
- Resist both premature freeze after one cycle and endless iteration that never concludes.

Predetermining the cycle count defeats the purpose of iteration, while iterating without a stopping rule produces activity without a defensible conclusion.

### Balance Intervention Fidelity With Intentional Adaptation

DBR unfolds in authentic settings where perfect fidelity is neither possible nor always desirable, but uncontrolled drift destroys interpretability.

- Define the core features that must be implemented faithfully for the conjecture to be testable.
- Distinguish planned adaptations, responsive to local context, from unplanned drift.
- Measure and document both fidelity and adaptation, and relate them to outcomes.
- Treat teacher or facilitator adaptations as data about the design's robustness, not merely as noise.

A study reporting no effect may reflect failed fidelity; one reporting an effect may reflect an intervention quite different from what was described. Fidelity and adaptation data are essential to interpret either.

### Work In Authentic Settings With Multiple Stakeholders

DBR is conducted in real classrooms, clinics, or workplaces, not stripped-down labs, and it depends on the people who live there.

- Engage practitioners, teachers, learners, and administrators as informants and co-designers, not just subjects.
- Treat their constraints, goals, and local knowledge as design input.
- Anticipate that stakeholder priorities will surface tensions the design must reconcile.
- Document how stakeholder input shaped each redesign.

Ignoring the people who enact the intervention produces designs that are elegant in theory and unworkable in practice. Authentic settings are not a complication to manage; they are the condition that makes DBR meaningful.

### Establish Trustworthiness Of The Iterative Analysis

Because DBR analysis is interpretive and the researcher is invested in the design, trustworthiness must be built in deliberately.

- Triangulate across data sources, methods, and observers.
- Keep an audit trail of decisions and their evidence.
- Use negative or discrepant case analysis to challenge emerging conclusions.
- Practice reflexivity about the researcher's stake in the design's success.

Without these safeguards, the analysis that drives redesign reflects the researcher's hopes more than the setting's reality.

## Common Traps

### Treating DBR As Mere Product Development

When the goal becomes shipping a polished intervention, the analysis and redesign rationale get trimmed to fit the timeline, and the project produces an artifact but no transferable understanding. This trap is seductive because a finished product is a visible, fundable deliverable, but it strips DBR of the design knowledge that makes it worth doing instead of ordinary product design.

### Redesigning From Intuition Rather Than Evidence

Changing the intervention because it feels wrong, or because a colleague suggested it, severs the link between finding and change. The trap feels efficient in the moment, but it turns iteration into guesswork and leaves no defensible account of why the design evolved as it did.

### Changing Too Many Features At Once

Bundled changes make later effects unattributable, so the project ends with a working intervention and no explanation. The trap arises because redesigning several things feels like faster progress, but it trades understanding for speed and forfeits the explanatory goal of DBR.

### Premature Design Freeze

Stopping iteration after one or two cycles, often under deadline pressure, exports an under-tested intervention and overstates what was established. The trap is that a clean early result is easier to report, but it risks harming learners in other settings who receive something never properly stabilized.

### No Documentation Of Redesign Rationale

When the reasoning behind changes is not recorded, the final design becomes opaque and the redesign knowledge is lost. The trap is common because documentation feels like overhead during fast iteration, yet it is precisely the artifact that distinguishes DBR from ad hoc prototyping.

### Confounding Design Changes With Effects

Attributing an improved outcome to the redesign when other things also shifted, such as the teacher, the cohort, or the season, overstates what the change accomplished. The trap is that the redesign is the most visible difference, so it gets the credit, but without controlling alternatives the inference is unsupported.

### Ignoring Fidelity And Adaptation Data

Reporting outcomes without knowing whether the intervention was actually delivered as designed makes both positive and negative results uninterpretable. The trap is that fidelity checks feel like a burden, but a no-effect finding from zero fidelity and an effect from heavy drift are equally meaningless without that data.

### Endless Iteration Without Convergence

Continuing to cycle because each round raises new questions never reaches a defensible conclusion and exhausts participants and resources. The trap is that openness to more learning feels rigorous, but without a convergence criterion the work never delivers actionable understanding.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Does each cycle include genuine analysis, design, development, and evaluation phases, each doing real work?
- [ ] Is every redesign decision explicitly tied to specific evidence from the evaluation, with expected effects stated?
- [ ] Are substantive changes isolated where possible, and where bundled, is each change and its predicted contribution documented?
- [ ] Is there a running record, such as a conjecture map or design rationale log, capturing conjectures, evidence, redesigns, and abandoned approaches?
- [ ] Is the decision to iterate or stop driven by an explicit convergence criterion rather than a fixed count or deadline?
- [ ] Are core features, fidelity, and adaptations defined, measured, and related to outcomes?
- [ ] Are authentic-setting stakeholders engaged as co-designers, and is their input documented in the redesign record?
- [ ] Is the iterative analysis made trustworthy through triangulation, audit trail, negative case analysis, and reflexivity?
- [ ] Is the project distinguishable from ordinary product development, producing transferable design knowledge rather than only a finished artifact?
- [ ] For high-stakes educational interventions, settings involving vulnerable learners, or cases where cycle design is uncertain, has an experienced design-based researcher or methodologist reviewed the iterative plan and redesign logic before implementation?

This skill provides methodological guidance on structuring iterative design and redesign in design-based research. It is not a substitute for experienced DBR mentorship or formal ethics consultation. DBR intervenes in real educational settings with real learners, and a cycle run poorly can freeze an under-tested design, mislead both practice and theory, and waste the effort and trust of the educators and students involved. Poorly documented or undisciplined iteration can legitimize interventions and design claims that were never properly established. Where stakes are high or the situation uncertain, consult experienced DBR researchers and ethics advisors before acting.
